011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large pre-trained vision-language models (e.g., CLIP) are vulnerable to adversar-
014            ial attacks in image-text retrieval (ITR). Existing works primarily focus on defense
015
               for image classification, overlooking two key aspects of ITR: multimodal manipu-
               lation by attackers, and the one-to-many relationship in ITR, where a single image
016
               can have multiple textual descriptions and vice versa (1:N and N:1). This is the
017
               first work that explores defense strategies for robust ITR. We demonstrate that our
018            proposed multimodal adversarial training, which accounts for multimodal pertur-
019            bations, significantly improves robustness against multimodal attacks; however, it
020            suffers from overfitting to deterministic one-to-one (1:1) image-text pairs in the
021            training data. To address this, we conduct a conprehensive study on leveraging
022            one-to-many relationships to enhances robustness, investigating diverse augmen-
023            tation techniques. Our findings reveal that diversity and alignment of image-text
024            pairs are crucial for effective defense. Specifically, text augmentations outper-
025            form image augmentations, which tend to create either insufficient diversity or
026
               excessive distribution shifts. Additionally, we find that cross-modal augmen-
               tations (e.g., image → text) can outperform intra-modal augmentations (e.g.,
027
               text → text) due to generating well-aligned image-text pairs. In summary, this
028
               work pioneers defense strategies for robust ITR, identifying critical aspects over-
029            looked by prior research, and offers a promising direction for future studies.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Image-text retrieval (ITR) is a fundamental Vision-Language (VL) task that involves cross-modal
035   representation alignment between vision and language modalities. It consists of retrieving the most
036   relevant text given an image query, and vice versa. One solution is using Vision-Language (VL)
037   models pre-trained on large-scale paired image-text data, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), which
038   learns a joint embedding space for images and texts. However, recent studies revealed that all VL
039
      models for ITR are vulnerable to adversarial attacks (Zhang et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023). Since
      adversarial attacks can deceive models with nearly negligible perturbations for humans, they pose
040
      significant risks of causing unintended consequences in real-world applications. For example, in
041
      e-commerce, retailers may add perturbations to the product images or descriptions to manipulate
042   the retrieval results of an ITR system, unfairly promoting or demoting specific products. As the
043   deployment of VL models in practical applications grows, understanding and mitigating their vul-
044   nerabilities against adversarial attacks has become crucial and urgent.
045
      While several defense strategies for VL models have been proposed (Mao et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
046
      2024b; Schlarmann et al., 2024), they primarily focus on image attacks, e.g., for robust zero-shot
047
      image classification, leaving defense strategies tailored for ITR fully unexplored. This is a con-
048   siderable oversight since ITR presents two challenges that make the problem much more complex
049   compared to image classification: (1) Multimodal manipulation: Adversarial attacks on ITR can
050   manipulate both image and text modalities, however, previous defense methods only consider image
051   perturbations. Such an increased attack capability in ITR requires more complex defense strategies.
052   (2) One-to-many (1:N) cross-modal alignment: Sentence-level text inputs in ITR exhibit a high
053   degree of variation and ambiguity, as a single image can be described in numerous ways, and vice
      versa. The one-to-many nature of image-text alignment in ITR (e.g., a single image is described as


                                                        1

054
      “a man with glasses is wearing a beer can crocheted hat” or “a man wears an orange hat and glasses”)
055   contrasts with simple and unambiguous text inputs in image classification tasks (e.g., all dog photos
056   are paired with “a photo of a dog”), making harder to achieve a robust image-text alignment. Thus,
057   the existing defense strategies for VL models aimed at image classification (Mao et al., 2022; Wang
058   et al., 2024b) overlook these two critical aspects, casting doubt on their effectiveness for ITR.
059
      To address this gap, we pioneer a study on defense strategies for VL models in ITR. Specifically, in
060
      this work, we study how to robustly fine-tune a large-scale vision-language model for downstream
061   ITR tasks.
062
063   First, by incorporating multimodal perturbations during adversarial training, our proposed multi-
064
      modal adversarial training (MAT) largely improves robustness against multimodal attacks. This
      highlights the need for defense strategies specifically tailored to multimodal threats in ITR, a re-
065
      quirement distinct vision-only defense strategies (Mao et al., 2022; Schlarmann et al., 2024).
066
067   However, we find that MAT suffers from overfitting to deterministic (1:1) image-text pairs in the
068   training data. To mitigate this issue, we investigate how to consider the one-to-many (1:N) rela-
069   tionships in ITR to enhance adversarial robustness. Inspired by works in cross-modal ambiguity
070
      modeling in ITR (Kim et al., 2023; Song & Soleymani, 2019), we explore various text and image
      augmentation techniques to create diverse one-to-many (1:N) and many-to-one (N:1) image-text
071
      pairs. Our in-depth analysis reveal that diversity and alignment of image-text pairs are crucial for
072
      effective defense. For instance, text augmentations outperform image augmentations, which tend
073   to create either insufficient diversity or excessive distribution shifts. Additionally, cross-modal aug-
074   mentations (e.g., image → text) can outperform intra-modal augmentations (e.g., text → text)
075   due to generating well-aligned image-text pairs. These findings are novel and unique for multi-
076   modal robustness settings, which has not been previously explored in unimodal adversarial training
077   literature.
078
      Our contributions are summarized as follows:
079
080          • First defense strategy for ITR: We demonstrate that existing defense methods for image
081            classification are suboptimal for robust ITR, and pioneer research in this new direction.
082          • Introduced multimodal adversarial training: Our multimodal adversarial training
083            largely improves robustness against multimodal attacks, highlighting the importance of
084            considering multimodal perturbations for ITR defense.
085
             • Comprehensive analysis of leveraging one-to-many relationships for robust ITR: We
086
               identify overfitting in multimodal adversarial training to deterministic one-to-one (1:1)
087            image-text pairs. Thus, we provide an in-depth analysis of diverse augmentations, cov-
088            ering both image and text modalities, as well as intra- and cross-modal augmentations. We
089            reveal that leveraging one-to-many (1:N) relationships improves robustness, with diversity
090            and alignment of augmented image-text pairs being crucial for defense—insights not rec-
091            ognized in unimodal adversarial training literature.

      2   R ELATED WORK
094
095
      Adversarial attacks on vision-language models. Adversarial attacks on VL models can be cat-
096   egorized into unimodal and multimodal attacks. Unimodal attacks, such as gradient-based image
097   attacks (Madry et al., 2017) and BERT-Attack for text (Li et al., 2020), manipulate a single modality
098   to mislead the models. On the other hand, recent studies have revealed that multimodal attacks,
099   which perturb both image and text modalities, are significantly more effective (Zhang et al., 2022;
100   Lu et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a). However, developing defense strategies against
101   multimodal attacks for ITR remains largely unexplored.
102   Adversarial defense for vision-language models. Existing defense strategies for VL models
103   mainly focus on vision robustness, where only the image modality is perturbed by adversarial at-
104   tacks. For example, Mao et al. (2022) and Wang et al. (2024b) have proposed robust fine-tuning
105   methods of CLIP for zero-shot image classification, which leverage adversarial training scheme to
106   improve the model’s adversarial robustness. Schlarmann et al. (2024) proposed a method to robustly
107   fine-tune a CLIP’s vision encoder aimed at applications to diverse vision-language tasks, only fo-
      cusing on image attacks. Unlike previous studies, we are the first to investigate adversarial defense


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      strategies for ITR tasks, where both image and text modalities can be manipulated by adversaries.
109   Distinct from existing defense strategies, we propose multimodal adversarial training to improve
110   robustness against multimodal attacks in ITR, and leverage the one-to-many (1:N) relationship in
111   ITR to enhance adversarial robustness.
112
      Leveraging the one-to-many (1:N) nature of image-text. To tackle robustness in VL models,
113
      we take inspiration from current works for ITR. These works aim to improve retrieval accuracy by
114
      modeling the ambiguity between image and text pairs; that is, although a sentence can have multiple
115   visual interpretations, normally only one is paired as the ground truth. Similarly, an image can be
116   described using multiple different captions, but only one is considered as its pair. Since such a 1:1
117   deterministic relationship is inconsistent with the 1:N nature of the data, ITR works propose repre-
118   senting image-text samples as probabilistic embeddings (Chun et al., 2021; Chun, 2024), considering
119   neighboring samples in the triplet loss (Thomas & Kovashka, 2020), and generating multiple and
120   diverse representations for each image-text sample (Song & Soleymani, 2019; Kim et al., 2023).
121   Among these solutions, the latter naturally fits the data augmentation strategy of adversarial train-
122   ing. We hypothesize that leveraging data augmentation to increase diversity in a 1:N manner leads
123
      to robustness against adversarial attacks.

127   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
128
129   VL models Radford et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021); Yang et al. (2022) are fundamentally built on
130
      image-text contrastive learning, where the training objective is to maximize the similarity between
      matching image-text pairs while reducing the similarity between non-matching pairs in a shared
131
      embedding space. Among them, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) is the representative VL model for
132
      ITR and is the foundation for many other VL models (Li et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022). Thus, we
133   focus on defense strategies for CLIP and conduct a comprehensive anlaysis on our proposed defense
134   strategy. Below, we provide a brief overview of CLIP, followed by an explanations of existing
135   adversarial attacks and defenses targeting CLIP.
136
      CLIP. Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (CLIP) consists of an image encoder Φ : I →
137

138

140   similarity score SΦ,Ψ (I, T ) = sim(Φ(I), Ψ(T )) (cosine similarity of image-text embeddings) for
141   correct image-text pairs, and minimize it for incorrect pairs. CLIP is based on image-text contrastive
142   learning using the InfoNCE loss, where paired image-text samples form positive pairs, and unpaired
143   image-text samples form negative pairs. For the batch of N paired image-text samples {(Ii , Ti )}Ni=1 ,
144   the InfoNCE loss (over images) is defined as:

180   3.2   M ULTIMODAL ADVERSARIAL TRAINING FOR MULTIMODAL ROBUSTNESS
181
182
      We aim to defend against adversarial attacks on CLIP for ITR, which involves multimodal attacks
      that manipulate both image and text modalities. To this end, distinct from existing CLIP’s adversarial
183
      fine-tuning methods (Mao et al., 2022; Schlarmann et al., 2024), which focus on vision robustness
184
      and fine-tune only CLIP’s vision encoder, we start by fine-tuning the whole CLIP model to obtain
185   robustness against multimodal perturbations.
186

212   3.3   L EVERAGING ONE - TO - MANY IMAGE - TEXT PAIRS FOR ROBUSTNESS GENERALIZATION
213
214   Existing works on cross-modal ambiguity in ITR (Kim et al., 2023; Song & Soleymani, 2019)
215   demonstrated the importance of modeling the one-to-many (1:N) relationship that exists between
      image and text descriptions. In our setting, a simple adversarial fine-tuning on a downstream ITR

      dataset would also easily overfit to deterministic (1:1) image-text pairs in the training data. There-
217   fore, the inherent ambiguity of image-text pairs in ITR needs to be considered to achieve a more
218   optimal adversarial robustness in ITR.
219
220
      To this end, we propose Multimodal Augmented Adversarial Training (MA2 T), leveraging data aug-
      mentation strategies to create diverse one-to-many (1:N) and many-to-one (N:1) image-text pairs to
221
      prevent overfitting in MAT. Our idea is streightforward: a single image can be described in numerous
222
      ways, and vice versa. Text augmentation can generate diverse text samples for a given image, cre-
223   ating 1:N image-text pairs. Similarly, image augmentation can generate diverse images for a given
224   text, creating N:1 image-text pairs. In this way, we can prevent overfitting to deterministic (1:1)
225   pairs and naturally model the ambiguity of image-text pairs in ITR during mutlimodal adversarial
226   fine-tuning, improving adversarial robustness.
227
      Thus, given an image-text pair (I, T ), we generate augmented images Iaug ← augI (I, T ) or aug-
228
      mented text Taug ← augT (I, T ), where augI and augT are image and text augmentation functions,
229   respectively. Then, the new augmented pairs (Iaug , T ) or (I, Taug ) are added to the training data for
230   the image or text augmentation scenarios. Note that we do not use the pairs (Iaug , Taug ) in the train-
231   ing process, as Iaug and Taug are not necessarily aligned with each other since they are generated
232   independently. Using these pairs empirically degrades the model’s performance.

      4.2       AUGMENTATION STRATEGIES
293
294
      Augmentation types: Intra-modal and Cross-modal. We consider two types of augmentation
295   techniques: intra-modal and cross-modal. Intra-modal augmentation enhances data points without
296   considering image-text interactions (i.e., text → text, image → image). For example, basic image
297   augmentation, such as random cropping, corresponds to intra-modal augmentation, as it does not
298   require any knowledge of the paired text data. In contrast, cross-modal augmentation enhances data
299   points by leveraging the other modality (i.e., image → text, text → image). An example is generating
300   plausible images from a given caption via a text-to-image generative model.
301   Text augmentations. We consider two text augmentation techniques for intra-modal augmentation:
302   Easy Data Augmentation (EDA) (Wei & Zou, 2019) and Language Rewrite (LangRW) (Fan et al.,
303   2024). EDA is a simple text augmentation technique that applies four types of operations: synonym
304   replacement, random insertion, random deletion, and random swap. LangRW is a more advanced
305   text augmentation technique that leverages a large language model to rewrite the original texts.
306   For cross-modal augmentation, we consider an image-to-text generative model, OFA (Wang et al.,
307   2022), which generates plausible captions from a given image. Additionally, we consider human
308   annotations, denoted as Human, as cross-modal augmentation: Flickr30k and COCO contain five
309
      captions per image, so we use the remaining four captions as augmented data points.
310   Image augmentations. For intra-modal augmentation, we consider two image augmentation tech-
311   niques: For intra-modal augmentation, RandAugment (RandAug; RA) (Cubuk et al., 2020). Ran-
312   dAug applies a series of image augmentations, such as random cropping, color distortion, and rota-
313   tion, to the original image. For cross-modal augmentation, we consider a text-to-image generative
314   model, Stable Diffusion (SD) (Rombach et al., 2022), which generates plausible images from a given
315
      caption.
316   Augmentation settings. For a fair comparison, we fix the number of augmented data points to
317   be five times the number of original data points (generating four augmented data points for each
318   original data point). Please refer to the Appendix A.1 for the detailed settings of each augmentation
319   technique.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
494
495
      This is the first work to study adversarial defense strategies for vision-language (VL) models in the
496   context of image-text retrieval (ITR). Existing defense methods for CLIP are not effective against
497   multimodal attacks, as they are designed to defend against image-only attacks and do not consider
498   the one-to-many (1:N) nature of images and texts. For this reason, we proposed a novel defense
499   framework, Multimodal Augmented Adversarial Training (MA2 T), which leverages one-to-many
500   (1:N) image-text pairs via augmentations to enhance robustness for ITR. Our comprehensive analy-
501   sis reveals that our framework can significantly improve adversarial robustness against multimodal
502   attacks, and that well-aligned and diverse augmentations are crucial for effective defense, which was
503   previously unexplored in the literature on unimodal adversarial defense. This work identifies novel
504
      challenges overlooked in previous works and provides a novel perspective on adversarial defense
      strategies for VL models in ITR, fostering future research for reliable and secure AI systems.
