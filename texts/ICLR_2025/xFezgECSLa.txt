009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We initiate a formal investigation into the design and analysis of LLM-based
012            algorithms, i.e. algorithms that contain one or multiple calls of large language
013            models (LLMs) as sub-routines and critically rely on the capabilities of LLMs.
014            While LLM-based algorithms, ranging from combinations of basic LLM calls
015            to complicated LLM-powered agent systems and compound AI systems, have
016            achieved remarkable empirical success, the design and optimization of them have
017            oftentimes relied on heuristics and trial-and-errors, which is largely due to a
018
               lack of formal and analytical study for these algorithms. To fill this gap, we
               start by identifying the computational-graph representation, task decomposition
019
               as the design principle, and some key abstractions, which then facilitate our
020
               formal analysis for the accuracy and efficiency of LLM-based algorithms, despite
021            the black-box nature of LLMs. Through extensive analytical and empirical
022            investigation in a series of case studies, we demonstrate that the proposed
023            framework is broadly applicable to a wide range of scenarios and diverse
024            patterns of LLM-based algorithms, such as parallel, hierarchical and recursive
025            task decomposition. Our proposed framework holds promise for advancing LLM-
026            based algorithms, by revealing the reasons behind curious empirical phenomena,
027            guiding the choices of hyperparameters, predicting the empirical performance of
028            algorithms, and inspiring new algorithm design. To promote further study, we
029
               include our source code in the supplementary materials.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      The rapid advancements of pre-trained large language models (LLMs) in the past few years (Bubeck
034
      et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022a; Schaeffer et al., 2023) have given rise to the new paradigm of
035
      algorithmic problem solving by utilizing LLMs as general-purpose solvers and prompting them with
036   task descriptions plus additional inputs that can help enhance their performance (Wei et al., 2022b;
037   Kojima et al., 2022). Meanwhile, it is also well recognized that even the best LLMs today still exhibit
038   various limitations, such as finite context window sizes and difficulty with complex reasoning. Some
039   of these limitations are fundamental (Merrill & Sabharwal, 2023; Peng et al., 2024; Thomm et al.,
040   2024; Hahn & Rofin, 2024; Wen et al., 2024; Merrill et al., 2024), and resolving them requires new
041   breakthroughs. Moreover, in resource-constrained scenarios where using the state-of-the-art LLMs
042   is not feasible, one might have to resort to smaller and weaker LLMs for solving complex tasks.
043   All these have motivated the developments of what we call LLM-based algorithms, namely,
044   algorithms that contain one or multiple LLM calls as sub-routines and fundamentally rely on the
045   capabilities of LLMs. In its most basic form, an LLM-based algorithm can be a combination of
046   multiple LLM calls (Yao et al., 2023a; Besta et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). More advanced
047   examples include LLM-powered agent systems (Mialon et al., 2023; Pezeshkpour et al., 2024;
048   Xi et al., 2023) and compound AI systems (Zaharia et al., 2024) that augment LLMs with
049   additional abilities like tool use and long-term memory, as well as the emerging paradigm of LLM
050
      programming (Schlag et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024).
051   LLM-based algorithms, in a similar spirit to neuro-symbolic programming (Chaudhuri et al., 2021;
052   Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023) and learning-augmented algorithms (Mitzenmacher & Vassilvitskii,
053   2022; Lindermayr & Megow, 2022), combine the advantages of both LLMs and traditional
      algorithms. An LLM-based algorithm, designed with human’s intelligence and knowledge of

      algorithmic problem solving, can exhibit much better controllability and interpretability, stronger
055   performance that is less reliant on delicate prompting or extensive trial-and-errors, and capabilities
056   that far exceed what could possibly be achieved by directly prompting the LLM for a solution.
057
058
      The rapid developments of LLM-based algorithms naturally raise the question: is it possible to
      provide any formal analysis or guarantee for LLM-based algorithms? This seems like a daunting
059
      task at first glance, due to the black-box nature of LLMs. Indeed, in prior works of this field,
060
      LLM-based algorithms have been typically designed in a heuristic manner and evaluated empirically
061   with measurements of some error or cost metrics on certain benchmarks. In contrast, formal
062   analysis of LLM-based algorithms, if it does exist, can bring various potential benefits, including
063   but not limited to revealing the reasons behind curious empirical phenomena, instructing choices
064   of hyperparameters, predicting the empirical performance of LLM-based algorithms, and even
065   inspiring new algorithm design.
066
067   Main contributions. The goal of this work is to initiate a formal investigation into the design and
068   analysis of LLM-based algorithms. Our contributions to filling this gap, and thereby advancing the
069   field of LLM-based algorithms, are summarized as follows.
070
             • Section 2 introduces a novel analytical framework. We start by formulating LLM-based
071
               algorithms as computational graphs, and identifying task decomposition as the design
072            principle. We further introduce some key abstractions, based on which formal analysis
073            for the accuracy and efficiency of LLM-based algorithms is developed.
074
             • Through a series of case studies in Sections 3, 4 and 5, we demonstrate the proposed
075
               framework in action for various patterns of LLM-based algorithms, including parallel,
076            hierarchical and recursive decomposition, in a wide range of diverse scenarios and tasks.
077
             • We derive novel insights from our analytical study, which are also validated by experiments.
078
               For example, considering the hyperparameter m that represents the granularity of parallel
079
               decomposition, our analysis explains why error and cost metrics of an LLM-based
080            algorithm are monotone in m in certain cases while non-monotone in others, which in
081            turn guides the choices of m for achieving the desired accuracy or efficiency. Our work
082            exemplifies how to leverage the proposed framework for systematic design and analysis of
083            practical LLM-based algorithms, which can potentially inspire future work in this area.

      2.1   D EFINITION , REPRESENTATION , AND DESIGN PRINCIPLE
090
091   Definition and computational-graph representation. Generally speaking, an LLM-based
092   algorithm is simply an algorithm that contains one or multiple LLM calls as its key components.
093   Examples of LLM-based algorithms range from one single LLM call, to LLM-powered agent
094   systems or compound AI systems consisting of a mixture of LLM calls and non-LLM programs.
095
      An LLM-based algorithm can be naturally formulated as a computational graph. Each graph node
096   takes some inputs from its predecessor nodes, executes certain operations, and returns some outputs.
097   The nodes can be categorized into two types, which we refer to as LLM nodes and non-LLM nodes,
098   demonstrated in Figure 1. Within an LLM node, the operations consist of a prompter that formats
099   a prompt based on the inputs, an LLM call that processes the prompt, and a parser that extracts
100   the targeted information from the LLM’s response. The prompter and parser, designed specifically
101   for the current node, serve as translators between natural language and traditional data structures.
102   Within a non-LLM node, the operations can be anything that does not involve LLMs, e.g. a symbolic
103   algorithm, an API call for a search engine, or a classical machine learning model.
104   Given such nodes, the computational graph is built by connecting them with directed edges that
105   specify the data flows within the LLM-based algorithm. For example, Figure 2a demonstrates the
106   pattern of parallel decomposition, which divides the input problem into parallel sub-tasks, solves
107   each with one LLM node, and aggregates the results for the final solution; Figure 2b illustrates an
      algorithm for book-length summarization with chunking and incremental updating (Chang et al.,

137   formulation is expressive enough to cover these diverse algorithms and facilitates unified analysis

139   static, or dynamically constructed at runtime. Configurations of an LLM-based algorithm include
140   how task decomposition is done (reflected by the graph topology and the sub-tasks of graph nodes),
141   the methods of prompting and parsing for each LLM node, configurations of the LLM(s) being used
142   (e.g. the LLM nodes within one graph might use the same or different backbone LLMs), and so on.

144   Design principle: task decomposition. As shown above, an LLM-based algorithm might utilize
145   multiple LLM calls and non-LLM programs to handle sub-tasks derived from the original problem,
146   whose outputs together give rise to the final solution. This naturally implies task decomposition as a
147   crucial principle. One key aspect of designing an LLM-based algorithm is to decompose the original
148
      task appropriately, so that each sub-task can be handled by one LLM call or non-LLM program
      accurately and efficiently, while the performance of the overall algorithm is also guaranteed.
149
150   Task decomposition, widely adopted in prior works on LLM-based algorithms, is beneficial and
151   oftentimes crucial for solving problems with LLMs. For example, a large document that is well
152   beyond the context window or long-context capability of the LLM might need to be divided into
153   chunks, each processed by one LLM call; or, a complex reasoning process might need to be
154
      decomposed into smaller steps, handled by multiple LLM calls in coordination. It is also possible
      that better efficiency might be achieved with task decomposition, e.g. when the input problem can be
155
      decomposed into multiple independent sub-tasks to be solved in parallel, with a smaller end-to-end
156
      latency than autoregressive decoding within one single LLM call.1 Despite all these benefits, it is
157   also possible that fine-grained task decomposition can incur higher errors or costs in certain cases.
158   Choosing the appropriate decomposition is crucial for achieving good performance of the overall
159   algorithm, be it a trade-off between accuracy and efficiency or the best of both worlds.

      Analysis: accuracy and efficiency. Given a task and the corresponding LLM-based algorithm,
163   we aim to analyze its performance, namely how accurately and efficiently the algorithm solves the
164   task, akin to analysis for any generic algorithm. This is done by analyzing for each LLM call or
165   non-LLM program first, and then for the overall algorithm. Given that LLMs are regarded as black-
166   box general-purpose problem solvers using natural language as input and output, we find it useful
167   and necessary to leverage certain abstractions, to be introduced in Section 2.2, in order to facilitate
168   formal analysis that will be presented in Section 2.3. One practical usage of formal analysis is to
169   predict the performance of an LLM-based algorithm before actually running it, which will in turn
170   help to optimize certain hyperparameters in the configurations or inspire better algorithm design.

      2.3   F ORMAL ANALYSIS : ACCURACY AND EFFICIENCY
208
209   Given the above formulations and abstractions, we are ready to conduct formal analysis for the
210   accuracy and efficiency of LLM-based algorithms. Our approach is to first analyze the error and
211   cost metrics for each individual node within the computational graph; these, combined with the
212   graph topology, lead to results about the error and cost metrics of the overall algorithm. Unless
213   otherwise specified, our analysis is deterministic, for a given task instance and fixed random seed(s).
214

215        Throughout this work, we use “accuracy” to refer to the broader concept of “quality”, and an “error metric”
      can be any metric that measures how much the output of an algorithm deviates from certain criteria.

      Analysis of error metrics. The error metrics of the output of each LLM node, calculated with
217   respect to what the output should have been if all nodes accomplish their tasks with exact accuracy,
218   depend on the characteristics (i.e. capabilities and limitations) of the LLM, as well as the specific
219   problem instance and random seed(s). For each node v, the error of its output y can be bounded by
220   some function fv of the errors of its inputs x1 , x2 , . . . , xk , i.e. the outputs of its predecessor nodes:
221

                                       E(y) ≤ fv E(x1 ), E(x2 ), . . . , E(xk ) .                                (1)
222

223

224   that passes all or the most test cases is chosen. More examples of fv will be demonstrated in our
225   case studies later in this work. Finally, the error metrics of the overall algorithm are exactly those of
226   the particular graph node that generates the final solution returned by the algorithm.

229   prefilling phase with a prompt of length Lpre , and a decoding phase that generates text of length
230   Ldec . In our framework, it is assumed that the cost C of one LLM call can be bounded by
231          C ≤ C(prefilling) + C(decoding) = Cpre (Lpre ) + Cdec (Lpre , Ldec ) =: CLLM (Lpre , Ldec ). (2)

      3     PARALLEL DECOMPOSITION
254
255
      This section focuses on parallel decomposition, a basic MapReduce-like (Dean & Ghemawat, 2008)
256
      pattern visualized in Figure 2a. An algorithm of this pattern divides the input problem into multiple
257   independent sub-tasks, solves each with one LLM node, and aggregates the results with an LLM or
258   non-LLM node for the final solution. The intermediate sub-tasks can be solved sequentially or in
259   parallel, which has impacts on certain cost metrics. This basic pattern of task decomposition can
260   be used as a building block for more sophisticated algorithms. Despite its simplicity, interesting
261   analysis and a wide variety of concrete tasks and algorithms can be derived from it.
262
      In the following, Section 3.1 introduces our general analysis for parallel decomposition, while
263   Sections 3.2 and 3.3 further demonstrate two concrete examples, namely counting and retrieval.
264   Analysis with the proposed framework for more concrete examples (including sorting, retrieval-
265   augmented generation, and long-text summarization) can be found in Appendix C.
266

366   3.3   E XAMPLE : RETRIEVAL
367
368   For another application of parallel decomposition, let us consider the task of question answering that
369
      requires retrieving some key information from a long piece of text, akin to the needle-in-a-haystack
      benchmark (Kamradt, 2023). For example, suppose that a key message (the needle) of the form
370
      “The passcode to the {targeted object, e.g. red door} is {6-digit passcode}” is randomly inserted
371
      into a piece of long text (the haystack), and the algorithm is asked to answer “What is the passcode
372   to the {targeted object}?”. To make this task more challenging and fun, we further assume that the
373   haystack consist of alike sentences of the form “The passcode to the {colored object} is {6-digit
374   passcode}”, with colored objects different from the targeted object. This allows us to investigate
375   both sides of retrieval capabilities of LLMs and LLM-based algorithms: retrieving the targeted
376   information correctly, while avoiding being confused or misled by background information that
377   might seem relevant to the question (Shi et al., 2023). In the following, we highlight our algorithm
      design and analysis of accuracy, with full details of this example deferred to Appendix C.3.


                                                           7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      We consider the algorithm below that follows the pattern of parallel decomposition. It first divides
379   the input text of length n into k chunks of lengths m1 , . . . , mk . Then for each chunk, one LLM
380   call is invoked to try to answer the question based on that chunk, or simply return “I don’t know”
381   if the LLM decides that the corresponding chunk does not contain sufficient information. The final
382   answer is generated by majority voting, with the “I don’t know” responses excluded.
383
      For our analysis, let us assume for simplicity that mi = m for all i ∈ [k], and focus on understanding
384
      how the accuracy of the overall algorithm is impacted by the hyperparameter m, i.e. the chunk size.
385   Following the approach in Section 2.3, this is achieve by first understanding each individual LLM
386   call. We start by identifying two failure modes of each LLM call for retrieval from one chunk:
387   (1) while the needle is contained in the chunk, the LLM might mistakenly return “I don’t know”
388   or an incorrect passcode; (2) while the chunk contains no needle, the LLM might hallucinate and
389   mistakenly return a passcode that it believes is the true answer, especially when the chunk contains
390   some objects that seem similar to the targeted object (e.g. “red lock” versus “red door”). It is
391   reasonable to expect that the first failure mode will occur more frequently for a larger chunk size m;
392   for the second failure mode, however, we observed empirically that some LLMs are more prone to it
393
      even when the value of m is small, while others are much less so. Based on these observations, we
      hypothetically categorize LLMs into two types: Type-1 LLMs are only prone to the first failure mode,
394
      while Type-2 LLMs are prone to both. Now we are ready to consider the accuracy of the overall
395
      algorithm. With a Type-1 LLM, a smaller value of m means the first failure mode is less likely to
396   occur in Step 2 of the algorithm, which implies higher accuracy for the final solution. Analysis with
397   a Type-2 LLM, on the other hand, is more complicated: a larger m means the first failure mode
398   is more likely to occur, while a smaller m implies a larger number of chunks k ≍ n/m, which
399   can potentially increase the chance of error in the final step of majority voting, due to the frequent
400   occurrence of the second failure mode in Step 2 of the algorithm. Consequently, the minimum error
401   of the overall algorithm might be obtained by some intermediate value of m that achieves a balance
402   between these two failure modes. If the input size n is too large, then there might not exist a good
403   value of m that can achieve a low error, as either failure mode must occur with high probability.
404
405   4   H IERARCHICAL DECOMPOSITION
406
407
      In this section, we apply the proposed analytical framework to the design and analysis of LLM-
408   based algorithms following a more expressive pattern named hierarchical decomposition, where the
409   original task is decomposed into multiple sub-tasks, and each of them can be further decomposed
410   into more lower-level sub-tasks. We outline our study for this pattern and highlight the key insights
411   in the following; the full version of this section can be found in Appendix D, which also includes
412   empirical validation of our analysis.
413
414   Task. The concrete example under consideration is a question-answering task that requires
415   retrieval of multiple needles from a large haystack and multi-hop reasoning over them. Suppose that
416   the targeted question is about finding the numeric value of a particular variable, while the haystack
417   consists of clues about the dependency between many variables, akin to the problem formulation
418   considered by Ye et al. (2024). The needles embedded in the haystack are logically related, and some
419
      of the needles are related to the targeted question only indirectly via connection to other needles. For
      example, the targeted question might be “What is the numeric value of A?”, while the needles are “A
420
      = B”, “B = C”, and “C = 100”, located separately in different chunks of the haystack. An algorithm
421
      with the pattern of parallel decomposition, e.g. retrieving the needles from the chunks independently
422   and then aggregating them for answering the targeted question, is doomed to fail in this case.
423
424
      Algorithm. We consider an LLM-based algorithm that involves multiple rounds of iterative
425
      retrieval and reasoning, visualized in Figure 13 in the appendix. It starts by dividing the input
426   haystack of size n into k chunks, each of size no larger than m ≍ n/k, and initializing an empty
427   list of references for storing the relevant clues retrieved by LLM calls. Then for each round, the
428   algorithm invokes k sequential (Figure 13a) or parallel (Figure 13b) LLM calls for retrieval from
429   k chunks based on the targeted question and references, followed by one LLM call for reasoning
430   about the updated references and deciding whether the algorithm is ready to answer the question
431   or need more rounds of retrieval and reasoning. Similar approaches have been widely adopted in
      prior works (Creswell et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2024; Qwen-Team, 2024). The resulting algorithm


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      exhibits a hierarchical structure: the original task is decomposed into multiple sequential rounds,
433   and each round is further decomposed into multiple steps of retrieval and reasoning.
434
435   Analysis and insights. We are particularly interested in two critical design choices: the option of
436   prompting the LLM calls responsible for reasoning and answering (“answer directly” or “think step
437   by step”), and the option of retrieval from multiple chunks within each round (sequentially or in
438   parallel). In the following, we let r denote the number of rounds (which is determined adaptively by
439   the algorithm itself at runtime), and ℓ denote the total length of the needles. We assume that, when
440   the LLM is given all needles and asked to answer the targeted question, the number of generated
441   tokens Ldec will be O(1) if it is prompted to answer directly, or O(ℓ) if it is prompted to think step
442
      by step before answering. The cost of such an LLM call is thus bounded by CLLM (Lsys + ℓ, 1 or ℓ).
443   For the case of sequential retrieval within each round (Figure 13a), we conclude that the total cost
444   of the overall algorithm can be bounded as follows (see Appendix D.3 for the derivation):

445
                        C ≤ r × k × CLLM (Lsys + m + ℓ, ℓ) + CLLM (Lsys + ℓ, 1 or ℓ) .                 (5)
446
447   In particular, this bound quantifies how the cost of LLM calls for reasoning and answering, namely
448   r × CLLM (Lsys + ℓ, 1 or ℓ), only occupies a small fraction of the total cost. These results also holds
449   for the case of parallel retrieval (Figure 13b), but a better bound can be obtained for the end-to-end
450
      latency with parallelism degree p, by replacing the k factor on the right-hand side of Eq. (5) with
      ⌈k/p⌉. Note that the concrete value of r in the case of parallel retrieval, where the list of references
451
      is updated only once at the end of each round, is typically larger than that in the case of sequential
452
      retrieval, where the list of references is updated immediately after each retrieval step.
453
454   We derive two major insights from the above analysis. (1) LLM nodes for reasoning and answering
455   only occupy a small fraction of the total cost, while playing a critical role in the output (and thus
456
      accuracy) of the overall algorithm. Our general recommendation is thus prompting them to think step
      by step before answering, which will boost the accuracy significantly with minor loss in efficiency.
457
      (2) Each option of retrieval has its own pros and cons. The sequential option requires fewer rounds
458
      of retrieval and reasoning (thanks to the timely updates to the references), with the downside that all
459   retrieval steps have to be executed sequentially. In contrast, the parallel option requires more rounds
460   and thus larger costs, but can leverage parallelism for achieving a smaller end-to-end latency.

      5   R ECURSIVE DECOMPOSITION
464
      This section studies recursive decomposition, a pattern that is vastly different from those in previous
465
      sections, yet still covered by our proposed framework. A recursive LLM-based algorithm starts from
466
      the original task and recursively generates intermediate sub-tasks, each of which can be solved by
467   aggregating the solutions to its own children tasks. In particular, decomposing and/or solving each
468   sub-task can be achieved by LLM calls, while the outline of recursive task decomposition remains
469   symbolic. Such LLM-based algorithms have been widely applied in the literature (Kazemi et al.,
470   2023; Schlag et al., 2023; Prasad et al., 2024; Schroeder et al., 2024; Lee & Kim, 2023; Khot et al.,
471   2023). We outline our study for this pattern and highlight some key results in the following; the full
472   version of this section (including empirical validation) can be found in Appendix E.
473
474   Task. We consider the same task from Section 4, which is about calculating a targeted variable
475   based on clues about the dependency between many variables. One major difference here is that
476
      we consider a much larger number of relevant variables. The complex reasoning required to answer
      the targeted question correctly, even if all relevant clues are given a priori, can be well beyond
477
      the capability of one single LLM call, which motivates decomposing the reasoning process into
478
      multiple LLM calls. The other major difference is, we assume that the clues can be accessed only
479   via querying a database: for each query, the database takes a name as input (say “A”), and returns a
480   clue for the variable of the same name, e.g. “A = B + C” if A is a non-leaf variable, or “A = 10” if A
481   is a leaf variable. Such a setting is motivated by (and can be regarded as an abstraction of) real-world
482   scenarios where an autonomous agent in the wild need to actively retrieve relevant information by
483   itself, via querying a real database, using a search engine, retrieving from documents, etc.
484

528   This work introduces an analytical framework for studying the design and analysis of LLM-based
529   algorithms. After identifying the computational-graph representation, task decomposition as the
530
      design principle, and other abstractions, we find it feasible to provide formal analysis for the
      accuracy and efficiency of generic LLM-based algorithms. Through extensive case studies, we
531
      demonstrate the proposed framework in action and derive novel insights for various scenarios.

      A    R ELATED WORKS
865
866
      LLM-based algorithms. The concept of LLM-based algorithms, as was explained in Section 1,
867   is fairly broad and general, ranging from a combination of one or multiple LLM calls with some
868   prompt engineering (Yao et al., 2023a; Besta et al., 2023; 2024; Lei et al., 2023; Saha et al., 2023;
869   Zhou et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Prasad et al., 2024; Chang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023;
870   2024b), to LLM-powered agent systems (Mialon et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024;
871   Gao et al., 2024a; Shen et al., 2023; Zhuge et al., 2024; Pezeshkpour et al., 2024; Xi et al., 2023; Qian
872   et al., 2024; Kapoor et al., 2024) and compound AI systems (Zaharia et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024)
873   that augment LLMs with additional abilities like tool use and long-term memory, and to the emerging
874   paradigm of LLM programming (Schlag et al., 2023; Khot et al., 2023; Khattab et al., 2024; Zheng
875
      et al., 2024; Kambhampati et al., 2024). Some elements of our analytical framework proposed
      in Section 2, such as the principle of task decomposition, the computational-graph representation,
876
      evaluation of and comparison between LLM-based algorithms with accuracy and efficiency taken
877
      into account simultaneously, etc., have already appeared in one way or another in these prior works.
878   It is primarily our unified, systematic and formal investigation into the design and analysis of generic
879   LLM-based algorithms that distinguishes the current work from this vast literature.
880
881   Scaling properties of LLM test-time computation. Recent works have started to investigate,
882   analytically or empirically, the scaling properties of LLM test-time computation.
883
      One line of research is concerned about the scaling properties of repeated sampling, e.g. randomly
884
      generating multiple sequences for the same prompt and then aggregating the results (Chen et al.,
885
      2024; Brown et al., 2024; Snell et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024), which might be regarded as a
886   stochastic version of parallel decomposition investigated in Section 3. Our work is orthogonal
887   and complementary to this line of research, as our analysis is deterministic and targeted at generic
888   patterns of LLM-based algorithms. Indeed, one potential direction for expanding the analytical
889   framework proposed in this work would be to augment it with stochastic decoding and repeated
890   sampling, along with relevant theoretical results from prior works.
891
      Another line of works is concerned about improving the output quality of an LLM call, albeit at
892   a higher cost, by generating more tokens autoregressively, e.g. via chains of thoughts (Wei et al.,
893   2022b) or step-by-step reasoning (Kojima et al., 2022). This idea has been investigated theoretically
894   (Feng et al., 2023; Merrill & Sabharwal, 2024; Li et al., 2024b), and further popularized recently by
895   the OpenAI o1 model (OpenAI, 2024a). As this approach of scaling up the test-time computation of
896   individual LLM calls is becoming more widely adopted, it is important to understand, analytically
897   and quantitatively, how it will impact the overall accuracy and efficiency when such LLM calls are
898   embedded within an LLM-based algorithm. Our work can be useful in achieving this, as illustrated
899   in the case studies in Appendices D and E, where the impacts of prompting LLM calls to “think step
900
      by step” versus “answer directly” can be characterized analytically with our proposed framework.
901
      Learning-augmented algorithms. Our work draws inspiration from the research area of
902
      algorithms with predictions / learning-augmented algorithms (Mitzenmacher & Vassilvitskii, 2022;
903
      Lindermayr & Megow, 2022). One standard paradigm of this area is to consider a specific task
904   (say sorting (Bai & Coester, 2023) or clustering (Ergun et al., 2022)), assume access to a black-
905   box machine learning model that satisfies certain properties (e.g. what additional computation or
906   information it can offer), propose a novel algorithm that leverages this ML model, provide theoretical
907   guarantees for its accuracy and efficiency, and show the improvements over traditional, purely
908   symbolic algorithms. Our work is similar in spirit to this line of research, but also substantially
909   different, in that our analytical framework is targeted at general tasks and LLM-based algorithms,
910   with assumptions on the capabilities, limitations and inference service of LLMs (regarded as
911   general-purpose problem solvers) that are quite different from typical assumptions for task-specific
912
      ML models in the literature of learning-augmented algorithms.

      B.1   D IRECTIONS FOR FUTURE WORK
921
922   Moving forward, we find it promising future research to further expand or apply the proposed
923   framework and thereby advance the field of LLM-based algorithms, from a theoretical or practical
924   perspective. For example:
925
926
             • We have been assuming that LLM nodes are state-less, and only considering the simplest
               and most straightforward usage of LLMs, i.e. sending a prompt and receiving a response for
927
               each LLM call. One potential extension is to consider LLM nodes with states, like short-
928
               term memory in agent systems, or other advanced ways of using LLMs, and understand
929            how accuracy and efficiency of LLM-based algorithms are impacted in such cases.
930
             • In our empirical study, we have mainly focused on the hyperparameter m that indicates the
931
               granularity of parallel task decomposition, or the option of prompting certain LLM calls
932
               to either answer directly or think step by step. Future work might place more emphasis
933            on other configurations of LLM-based algorithms, such as the choices of LLM models
934            (e.g. choosing a strong-but-expensive model for specific LLM calls and a weak-but-cheap
935            one for the others within the same algorithm), LLM decoding methods (e.g. greedy versus
936            stochastic (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Brown et al., 2024)), etc., and better
937            understand how they impact the accuracy and efficiency of LLM-based algorithms.
938          • Through our abstractions of multiple error and cost metrics for the same task, we have
939            touched upon the topics of multi-objective optimization and hyperparameter optimization.
940            Future work might try to formally investigate these aspects.
941          • From a more practical perspective, future work might adopt the proposed framework, or
942            some components and methodology within it, to assist the design, analysis, improvement
943            and application of new LLM-based algorithms, or for fair comparison between different
944            algorithms, with both accuracy and efficiency taken into account.
945
946   B.2   P RACTICAL CONSIDERATIONS
947
948   We make two comments about some practical considerations for the proposed framework.
949   First, regarding the capabilities and limitations of LLMs in a specific task, the black-box nature of
950   LLMs can make it challenging to analytically and accurately quantify these factors, in which case
951   one might resort to measuring and profiling in practice. On the positive side, it is oftentimes easier
952   to make certain qualitative assumptions. For example, in many tasks of interest, a larger problem
953   instance is harder than a smaller one, and thus incurs larger error metrics of an LLM call. For
954   practical purposes like optimizing certain hyperparameters of an LLM-based algorithm, such weak
955   assumptions might be sufficient already.
956   Second, understanding LLM inference service (Yuan et al., 2024; Pope et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,
957   2024), especially from a system perspective, is crucial for in-depth analysis of cost metrics. LLM
958   inference service can be diverse in practice: for example, LLMs might run on CPUs in a personal
959   laptop or on a distributed GPU cluster, inference might be compute-bound or memory-bound, the
960   complexity of long-sequence processing and generation might be linear or quadratic, parallelism
961   at various levels (e.g. multiple LLMs deployed on multiple machines, or batch inference with one
962   LLM) might be supported, and so on. All these are covered by our proposed framework in a unified
963
      manner.

       C.1   E XPERIMENTS FOR COUNTING
1027
1028   We validate our analysis in Section 3.2 for the counting example with numerical experiments. For

1031
1032   Results with Llama-3-8B. Our empirical results with a Llama-3-8B model are illustrated in
1033   Figure 5 and explained in the following.
1034   In Figure 5a, we vary the problem size n and set m = n in our algorithm, which means the
1035   algorithm becomes equivalent to a single LLM call. Unsurprising, error metrics in this counting
1036   task monotonely increase with n. The number of prefilling tokens increases linearly with n, while
1037   the number of decoding tokens is insensitive to n, since we prompt the LLM to output its answer
1038   directly without intermediate reasoning steps. The latency of one LLM call also increases linearly
1039   with n, which is likely due to the relatively small sequence lengths and the compute-bound nature
1040   of LLM inference by a Llama-3-8B model running on a CPU.
1041   In Figure 5b, we fix n = 200 and vary the hyperparameter m, namely the size of each sub-task in
1042   our proposed LLM-based algorithm. It is confirmed that decomposing the original task into smaller

       C.2     E XAMPLE : SORTING
1135
1136   For a more challenging example, let us consider the classical sorting problem: given a list of n
1137   numbers x ∈ Rn , the task is to sort it in ascending order.
1138
1139   C.2.1    A LGORITHM
1140
1141
       Below is an LLM-based algorithm for sorting a list, which generalizes the naive approach of sorting
       the list with one single LLM call:
1142
1143         1. Divide the input list x into k disjoint sub-lists x1 , . . . , xk of lengths m1 , . . . , mk ;
1144
             2. For each i ∈ [k], use one LLM call to sort the i-th sub-list, which returns a solution yi ;
1145
1146         3. Merge the sub-lists y1 , . . . , yk into a single list y using a symbolic algorithm.
1147
       We note two details about this algorithm. (1) To ensure efficiency and stability, for each LLM call,
1148
       we prompt the LLM to generate the sorted list directly, without intermediate reasoning steps. It has
1149
       been verified empirically that the LLMs considered in our experiments can follow such instructions,
1150   and generate text of length Ldec (m) = O(m) that can be easily parsed into a list. (2) Step 3 of the
1151   algorithm relies on a classical symbolic algorithm for merging two sorted lists, which maintains two
1152   moving pointers, one for each list, and chooses each entry of the merged list by comparing the values
1153   corresponding to the two pointers. Merging multiple sorted lists can be done by merging one pair
1154   of lists at a time, in an incremental or hierarchical manner. Python code for these procedures can be
1155   found in Listing 1 at the end of this subsection. Although they are designed under the assumption
1156   that the input lists are sorted, they can also be applied to input lists that are not fully sorted, which
1157   can possibly happen within the LLM-based algorithm since the input lists in Step 3 are generated by
1158
       LLM calls in Step 2.
1159
       C.2.2    A NALYSIS
1160
1161   Let us assume for notational convenience that mi = m for all i ∈ [k], and k = n/m is an integer.
1162
1163   Error metrics. Compared to counting, there are more diverse phenomena in the sorting task in
1164   terms of error metrics. In particular, multiple possible failure modes exist in sorting with an LLM-
1165   based algorithm:
1166
1167         1. The output list might not be monotone;
1168         2. The length of the output list might be larger or smaller than that of the input list;
1169         3. The output list might contain numbers that do not match exactly those of the input list.
1170
1171   Based on these failure modes, we define the following error metrics for sorting a list, where y
1172   denotes the solution returned by the algorithm and y ⋆ denotes the ground-truth solution:

1213
       We validate our analysis with numerical experiments. The input list of each problem instance is
1214
       generated by randomly sampling entries of the list from the uniform distribution over the interval
1215
       [0, 1] and then rounding each of them to two decimals.
1216
1217   Results with Llama-3-70B. Our empirical results with a Llama-3-70B model are illustrated
1218   in Figure 7 and explained in the following.
1219
       In Figure 7a, we vary the problem size n and set m = n in the LLM-based algorithm, in which case
1220
       the algorithm becomes equivalent to a single LLM call. We make the following observations:
1221
1222           • Unsurprisingly, all error metrics in this task monotonely increase with n.
1223           • While the LLM might output a list that deviates from the ground-truth solution, it is at least
1224             good at ensuring that the output list itself is sorted or has a very small non-monotonicity
1225             error.
1226           • The prefilling length grows linearly with n, while the growth of the decoding length and
1227             end-to-end latency slows down slightly for large values of n, which is mainly because the
1228             LLM is prone to returning a list that is shorter than the input list when n is large, as reflected
1229             in the length-mismatch error curve.
1230
1231
       In Figure 7b, we fix n = 200 and vary the sub-task size m. It is confirmed that decomposing the
       original task into smaller parallel sub-tasks with a smaller value of m implies lower error metrics
1232
       achieved by the overall algorithm, while increasing certain cost metrics. Two specific observations:
1233
1234           • The total number of decoding tokens decreases with m at a rate that matches the length-
1235             mismatch error curve. This does not contradict our previous analysis, which predicts an
1236             upper bound that is insensitive to the value of m.
1237           • Regarding the end-to-end latency with parallelism degree p = 4, the zigzag part of
1238             the curve might seems curious. In fact, a fine-grained analysis can well explain this
1239             phenomenon. If we approximate the latency for one LLM call solving a sub-task of
1240             size m by O(m), then the end-to-end latency of the overall algorithm is approximately
1241             O(m × ⌈k/p⌉). The numbers calculated in Table 2 for the concrete setting of this

       C.3     E XAMPLE : RETRIEVAL
1459
1460   We study a more realistic application of LLM-based algorithms, which is answering a given question
1461   that requires retrieving some key information from a long piece of text. Our design of this task
1462   draws inspiration from the needle-in-a-haystack benchmark (Kamradt, 2023) and other similar
1463   benchmarks that have been widely adopted for evaluating the long-context capability of LLMs as
1464   well as techniques of retrieval-augmented generation (Weston et al., 2015; Roucher, 2023; Kuratov
1465   et al., 2024; Mohtashami & Jaggi, 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a).
1466   Consider the following setting as an example. A key message (the needle) of the form “The passcode
1467   to the {targeted object, e.g. red door} is {6-digit passcode}” is randomly inserted into a piece of
1468   long text (the haystack). The algorithm is asked to answer the question “What is the passcode to
1469   the {targeted object}?”. To make the problem more challenging and fun, we let the haystack consist
1470   of alike sentences of the form “The passcode to the {colored object, e.g. red lock or green door}
1471   is {6-digit passcode}”, with colored objects different from the targeted object. This allows us to
1472
       investigate both sides of retrieval capabilities of LLMs and LLM-based algorithms: retrieving the
       targeted information correctly, while avoiding being confused or misled by background information
1473
       that might seem relevant to the question (Shi et al., 2023).
1474
1475   Note that while we use this concrete setting for our empirical study, the proposed algorithm and
1476   analysis in the following are actually applicable to generic settings of this retrieval task.
1477
1478   C.3.1    A LGORITHM
1479
       We consider the following LLM-based algorithm that follows the pattern of parallel decomposition:
1480
1481         1. Divide the input text of length n into k overlapping chunks of lengths m1 , . . . , mk ;
1482
             2. For each chunk, use one LLM call to try to answer the question based on that chunk;
1483
1484         3. Generate the final answer by majority voting.
1485
1486
       We note a few details about this algorithm. (1) All lengths involved here are measured by the number
       of characters. (2) In the first step of chunking, we let each pair of adjacent chunks share an overlap
1487
       that is larger than the length of the needle, to ensure that the needle will appears as a whole in at
1488
       least one chunk. (3) For each LLM call in the second step, the LLM is prompted to answer “I don’t
1489   know” if it believes that there is not sufficient information in the corresponding chunk, e.g. when
1490   the chunk simply does not contain the needle. Such answers will be excluded from the final step of
1491   the algorithm. (4) In the final step of majority voting, it is possible that there are multiple (say h)
1492   candidate solutions with the same frequency, in which case we let the algorithm return the list of
1493   such candidates. If this list contains the ground-truth solution, we calculate the exact-match error as
1494   1 − 1/h in our experiments.
1495
1496   C.3.2    A NALYSIS
1497
       Let us assume for concreteness that each pair of adjacent chunks share an overlap of length m/2,
1498
       and mi = m for all i ∈ [k − 1], while m/2 ≤ mk ≤ m. In this case, we have k = ⌈2n/m − 1⌉.
1499
1500
       Error metrics. Let us focus on how error metrics of the LLM-based algorithm are impacted by
1501
       the hyperparameter m. We start by identifying two failure modes of each LLM call for retrieving
1502   the targeted information from a chunk of size m in Step 2 of the algorithm:
1503
1504         1. The first failure mode is that, while the needle is contained in the chunk, the LLM might
1505            mistakenly return “I don’t know” or an incorrect passcode. It is reasonable to expect that
1506            this failure mode will occur more frequently for larger values of m. Our early experiments
1507            with various LLMs confirmed that this failure mode starts to occur when m exceeds a
1508            certain threshold specific to each LLM.
1509         2. The second failure mode is that, while the needle is actually absent from the chunk, the
1510            LLM might mistakenly return a passcode that it believes is the true answer to the question.
1511            We observed empirically that this is more likely to happen when the chunk contains some
                objects that seem similar to the targeted object (e.g. “red lock” or “green door” versus “red


                                                         28
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1512
                 door”), and that some LLMs are more prone to this failure mode even when the value of m
1513             is small, while others are less so.
1514
1515   Based on the above observations, we can hypothetically categorize LLMs into two types: Type-1
1516   LLMs are only prone to the first failure mode, while Type-2 LLMs are prone to both. It turns out that
1517   analysis of error metrics for the overall LLM-based algorithm is dependent on the type of the LLM
1518   being used.
1519
1520           • Analysis is simpler if a Type-1 LLM is used: a smaller value of m means the first failure
1521
                 mode is less likely to occur in Step 2 of the algorithm, which implies higher accuracy for
                 the final solution of the overall algorithm.
1522
1523           • Analysis is more complicated if a Type-2 LLM is used, since both failure modes can
1524             possibly occur in Step 2 of the algorithm. A larger value of m means the first failure
1525             mode is more likely to occur, while a smaller value of m implies a larger number of chunks
1526             k = ⌈2n/m − 1⌉, which can potentially increase the chance of error in the final step of
1527             majority voting, due to the frequent occurrence of the second failure mode in Step 2 of the
1528
                 algorithm. Consequently, the minimum error of the overall algorithm might be achieved by
                 some intermediate value of m that achieves a balance between the two failure modes. If n
1529
                 is too large, then there might not exist a good value of m that can achieve a low error, as
1530
                 either failure mode must occur with high probability.4
1531
1532
       Cost metrics. Our analysis of cost metrics for this task is largely the same as that for the counting
1533
       task, despite how different these two tasks might seem. Under some mild conditions explained in
1534
       Section 3.1, most cost metrics of interest are monotonely decreasing in m, except for the end-to-end
1535   latency with parallel LLM calls, which is increasing in m for parallelism degree p = ∞ and possibly
1536   non-monotone for finite p. One thing to note is that, due to the overlaps between consecutive chunks,
1537   the number of parallel sub-tasks in Step 2 of the algorithm is k = ⌈2n/m − 1⌉, rather than ⌈n/m⌉
1538   as in the counting task. This implies that the value of m minimizing the latency can be predicted by
1539   letting 2n/m − 1 ≈ p, namely m ≈ 2n/(p + 1).

              An informal probabilistic analysis is as follows. Given the sub-task size m, denote the probability of the
1553   first and second failure modes as p1 (m) and p2 (m) respectively. Then, the success rate of retrieval for the
1554   chunk containing the needle is 1 − p1 (m), while the expected number of “false positives” from the remaining
1555   chunks is approximately k × p2 (m) ≈ 2n × p2 (m)/m. One might opt for a relatively small value of m, which
1556   hopefully increases 1 − p1 (m) and hence mitigates the first failure mode. However, even if p2 (m)/m is very
       small, say 10−3 , the number of false positives can still be large if the size n of the original problem is large,
1557
       which will cause errors in the solution returned by majority voting.

       C.4     E XAMPLE : RETRIEVAL - AUGMENTED GENERATION
1729
1730   Our next example is a multiple-needle generalization of the previous retrieval task, which can be
1731   regarded as a synthetic and simplified version of retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis
1732   et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2024b). In particular, we consider fine-grained sentence-level retrieval by
1733   LLMs, rather than document-level or chunk-level retrieval by certain similarity measure of dense
1734   embedding vectors.
1735   More concretely, suppose that the input text is composed of sentences of the form “The {i}-th digit
1736   of the passcode to the {colored object}” is {digit}”, where i ∈ [6]. The algorithm is asked to answer
1737   the question “What is the 6-digit passcode to the {targeted object}?”. Compared with the previous
1738   single-needle retrieval task, here the algorithm need to retrieve multiple needles, each for one digit
1739   of the targeted object, in order to answer the question correctly; moreover, the final aggregation step
1740   requires certain capability of reasoning or summarization over the retrieved needles.
1741   Note again that while we focus on this specific setting in our experiments, the algorithm and analysis
1742   in the following are actually applicable to generic settings of this RAG task.
1743
1744   C.4.1    A LGORITHM
1745
1746   We consider the following LLM-based algorithm for solving this task:
1747
1748
             1. Divide the input text of length n into k overlapping chunks of lengths m1 , . . . , mk ;
1749         2. For each chunk, use one LLM call to retrieve sentences that can be useful for answering
1750            the question;
1751         3. Put the retrieved sentences together, based on which one LLM call is invoked for answering
1752            the question.
1753
1754   We note a few details about this algorithm. (1) For each chunk in Step 2, we prompt the LLM to
1755   retrieve relevant sentences, or return “None” if no relevant sentence is found in that chunk. Such
1756   “None” results will be excluded from Step 3 of the algorithm. (2) Unlike previous examples, the
1757   final aggregation step of this algorithm involves an LLM node, which adds to the cost metrics of
1758
       the overall algorithm. (3) For simplicity, we assume that the number of needles (i.e. length of the
       passcode) and the length of each needle are both O(1). For more general cases, the final aggregation
1759
       step might benefit from further task decomposition. (4) We allow the algorithm to return a partial
1760
       answer, by placing a special character in the digit(s) of the passcode that it is uncertain about.

1763

1768   example. In particular, there are two possible failure modes in the retrieval step, and conclusions for
1769   the errors of the final solution returned by the overall algorithm are dependent on whether a Type-1
1770   or Type-2 LLM is being used. For example, if a Type-1 LLM, which will not mistakenly retrieve
1771   irrelevant sentences from the input text, is used within the algorithm, then a smaller value of m
1772   implies higher accuracy of retrieval in Step 2 of the algorithm, which further leads to lower error

       metrics for the solution returned by the final aggregation step.

1838   For the final example of this section, we apply our analytical framework to the task of long-text
1839   summarization (Kryscinski et al., 2022; Chang et al., 2024), where generating a summary for the
1840   long input text with a single LLM call is infeasible. Algorithms that process the long text to be
1841   summarized by chunks have been proposed, such as hierarchical merging (Wu et al., 2021) and

1846

       D     H IERARCHICAL DECOMPOSITION ( EXTENDED )
1945
1946
       This section studies the pattern of hierarchical decomposition for LLM-based algorithms, where the
1947   original task is decomposed into multiple sub-tasks, and each of them can be further decomposed
1948   into more lower-level sub-tasks. This pattern is strictly more expressive than parallel decomposition
1949   studied in the previous section, and hence capable of solving more problems.
1950
       For concreteness, we investigate a challenging version of the RAG task studied in Appendix C.4 that
1951
       requires multi-hop reasoning (Yang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2024a). Recall that in the previous RAG
1952
       example, given a question and multiple text chunks, the algorithm following the pattern of parallel
1953   decomposition will first retrieve useful sentences from each chunk, and then invoke one LLM call
1954   to answer the question based on the retrieved information. Such a method might not be feasible
1955   in more challenging scenarios, where the needles embedded in the haystack are logically related,
1956   and some of the needles are related to the targeted question only indirectly via connection to other
1957   needles. For example, suppose that the question is “What is the numeric value of A?”, while the
1958   needles are “A = B”, “B = C”, and “C = 100”, located separately in different chunks. Retrieving
1959   needles from their corresponding chunks solely based on the targeted question will certainly fail in
1960   this case.
1961   To tackle this challenge, a natural idea is to extend the RAG algorithm considered in Appendix C.4,
1962   allowing it to perform multiple rounds of iterative retrieval and reasoning. For each round, the
1963   algorithm performs retrieval based on the targeted question as well as additional information from
1964   previous rounds, followed by reasoning about the retrieved sentences and deciding whether the
1965   targeted question can be answered, or further retrieval and reasoning is needed. Similar approaches
1966   have been widely adopted in prior work, e.g. in the Selection-Inference framework (Creswell et al.,
1967
       2023), in a RAG system with iterative follow-up questions for applications in medicine (Xiong
       et al., 2024), or as a technique of extending the effective context window of a LLM-powered agent
1968
       system (Qwen-Team, 2024). The resulting algorithm for reasoning with iterative retrieval exhibits
1969
       a hierarchical structure: the original task is decomposed into multiple sequential rounds, and each
1970   round is further decomposed into multiple steps of retrieval and reasoning.
1971
1972   In the rest of this section, we first elaborate the concrete setup and algorithms for this case study,
1973
       and then demonstrate our analysis of accuracy and efficiency based on the proposed analytical
       framework, as well as insights derived from it. Finally, numerical experiments are conducted to
1974
       validate the efficacy and scalability of the considered algorithm, as well as our analysis and insights.
1975
1976
1977
       D.1   C ONCRETE SETUP
1978
       For concreteness, let us consider the task of finding the numeric value of a targeted variable
1979   embedded within a grid, similar to the setting considered by Ye et al. (2024). A visualization can
1980   be found in Figure 12. Configurations of the grid include its depth d, width w, and degree g, which
1981   control the difficulty of this task. More specifically, the grid consists of d levels, each containing
1982   w variables; each variable at a certain level is a function (e.g. addition, substraction, maximum or
1983   minimum) of g variables at the next level, except for the leaf variables at the final level, whose
1984   numeric values are given. Such information is provided in clues of the form “A2 = B1 + B3” or “C1
1985   = 10”, each of which corresponds to one variable. As a result, the total number of clues is d × w, and
1986   each clue has length O(g). We refer to the clues that are necessary and sufficient for calculating the
1987
       targeted variable as the needles, which constitute a directed acyclic graph (DAG) embedded within
       the grid. It is assumed that the level of reasoning required in this case study is non-trivial yet also
1988
       simple enough, in the sense that one single LLM call with step-by-step reasoning is sufficient for
1989
       answering the targeted question correctly if all needles are given a priori.

       Remark 2. The algorithm under consideration exhibits the pattern of hierarchical decomposition,
2107   in that the overall task is decomposed into a sequence of rounds, and each round is further

2109   that its computational graph is constructed dynamically at runtime, since the number of rounds is

2112   D.3     A NALYSIS
2113
2114   D.3.1    E RROR METRICS
2115
       There are two major failure modes for this algorithm of reasoning with iterative retrieval:
2116
2117           • Mistakes in reasoning and answering. For example, the LLM call responsible for reasoning
2118             and answering might mistakenly decide that it is not yet ready to answer the targeted
2119             question, or make mistakes when doing the arithmetic and thus return a wrong numeric
2120             value, even when all needles have been successfully retrieved. It is also possible that the
2121             LLM call mistakenly returns a numeric value (e.g. the value of a variable whose name is
2122             similar to that of the targeted variable), while the needles have not been fully retrieved
2123
                 yet. The probability of this failure mode is particularly related to whether the LLM call
                 responsible for reasoning and answering is prompted to answer directly or think step by
2124
                 step before answering.
2125
2126           • Failure to retrieve all needles. As long as the ground-truth DAG of needles has a limited
2127
                 size, the probability of this failure mode is largely determined by the choice of the chunk
                 size. Since this has been thoroughly investigated in our previous retrieval (Appendix C.3)
2128
                 and RAG (Appendix C.4) examples, it will not be our focus in this section.
2129

2131
2132   Notations. Recall from Eq. (2) that CLLM (Lpre , Ldec ) = Cpre (Lpre ) + Cdec (Lpre , Ldec ) stands for
2133   the cost of one LLM call with a prompt of length Lpre and generated text of length Ldec . We also
2134   adopt some notations from Section 3.1 in our previous study of parallel decomposition: n represents
2135   the total length (in tokens or characters) of the input clues, m represents an upper bound for the
2136   chunk size, k ≲ n/m denotes the number of chunks, and Lsys denotes an upper bound for the
2137
       length of the system prompt for each LLM call.
2138   In addition, let r denote the number of rounds, which is determined adaptively by the algorithm
2139   itself at runtime. Finally, let ℓ be the total length of the needles, i.e. clues that are necessary and
2140   sufficient for answering the targeted question correctly. The value of ℓ can depend on the depth d,
2141   width w and degree g of the grid in various ways:
2142
               • If g = 1, then the needles form a chain of length d, in the form of “A = B”, “B = C”, “C =
2143
                 D”, and so on. In this case, we have ℓ ≲ d.
2144
2145
               • If the width w > dg−1 is sufficiently large, then the number of needles can be upper
                 bounded by the size of a tree with d levels and g children per node, namely 1 + g +
2146
                 g 2 + · · · + g d−1 = (g d − 1)/(g − 1). Since each needle has length O(g), we have ℓ ≲
2147
                 g × (g d − 1)/(g − 1).
2148
2149
               • If the width w ≪ dg−1 is limited, then a tighter bound for the number of needles will be
                 d × w, and thus ℓ ≲ g × d × w.

2196   D.3.3    I NSIGHTS
2197
       In sum, we derive two major insights from the above analysis of error and cost metrics:
2198
2199         1. LLM nodes responsible for reasoning and answering only occupy a small fraction of the
2200            costs of the overall algorithm, while playing a critical role in the output of the algorithm
2201            and thus in the error metrics. Therefore, our general recommendation is to prompt these
2202            LLM calls to think step by step before answering, instead of answering directly, which will
2203            most likely boost the accuracy significantly with negligible loss in efficiency.
2204         2. Regarding the “cyclic” and “parallel” options for retrieval, we conclude that each of them
2205            has its own pros and cons. With the “cyclic” option, fewer rounds of retrieval and reasoning
2206            are needed (thanks to the timely updates to the references), but the downside is that all
2207            retrieval steps have to be executed sequentially. In contrast, with the “parallel” option, the
2208            algorithm typically requires more rounds of retrieval and reasoning and thus larger costs
2209            in terms of most metrics, but can leverage parallelism of LLM calls for reducing the end-
2210            to-end latency. Therefore, whether the “cyclic” or “parallel” option is preferred largely
2211
                depends on which cost metric is of more concern.

2232

       E     R ECURSIVE DECOMPOSITION ( EXTENDED )
2431
2432
       This section studies LLM-based algorithms with recursive task decomposition, a pattern that is
2433   vastly different from those studied in previous sections, and yet still covered by the analytical
2434   framework proposed in Section 2. Roughly speaking, a recursive LLM-based algorithm starts from
2435   the original task of concern and recursively generates more intermediate sub-tasks, so that each sub-
2436   task can be solved by aggregating the solutions to its children tasks, while being unaware of other
2437   sub-tasks involved in the overall algorithm. In particular, solving and/or decomposing each sub-task
2438   can be achieved by LLM calls, while the outline of recursive task decomposition remains symbolic.
2439   Such LLM-based algorithms have been widely applied, e.g. in LAMBADA (Kazemi et al., 2023),
2440   LLM programs (Schlag et al., 2023), ADaPT (Prasad et al., 2024), THREAD (Schroeder et al.,
2441
       2024), Recursion of Thought (Lee & Kim, 2023), Decomposed Prompting (Khot et al., 2023), ReDel
       (Zhu et al., 2024), among many others.
2442
2443   In the following, we first introduce the concrete task under consideration, and then design a recursive
2444   LLM-based algorithm for solving it, highlighting the dynamic construction of its computational
2445   graph at runtime. We then provide analysis for its accuracy and efficiency using the proposed
2446   framework, which is further validated with numerical experiments; along the way, we derive a
2447
       formal guarantee for generic LLM-based algorithms, under the technical assumption of additive
       errors and bounded sensitivity.
2448
2449
2450
       E.1   C ONCRETE SETUP
2451   For concreteness, we consider the same task introduced in Appendix D.1 and visualized in Figure 12,
2452   which is about calculating a targeted variable based on clues about a grid of many variables.
2453
2454
       One major difference here is that we consider much larger depth d, width w and/or degree g for
       the grid of variables, which implies a larger DAG of needles, i.e. clues relevant to the targeted
2455
       question. Even if all relevant clues are given a priori, the complex reasoning required to answer
2456
       the targeted question correctly can be well beyond the capability of one single LLM call, which
2457   motivates decomposing the reasoning process into multiple LLM calls.
2458
2459   The other major difference is, we assume that the clues are not directly given in plain text, but rather
2460
       need to be accessed via querying a database. For each query, the database takes a name as input, and
       returns a clue for the variable of the same name, e.g. “A2 = B1 + B3” for input “A2” or “C1 = 10”
2461
       for input “C1”. The LLM-based algorithm need to decide by itself what to query from the database.
2462
       Such a setting is motivated by, and can be regarded as an abstraction of, practical scenarios where
2463   an autonomous agent in the wild need to actively retrieve relevant information by itself in order to
2464   accomplish a task (unlike in typical problem-solving benchmarks where such information is given),
2465   via querying a real database or knowledge graph, using a search engine, retrieving from documents,
2466   etc. In this case study, we assume that querying the database does not involve LLM calls, and thus
2467   neglect its costs in our analysis.
2468
2469   E.2   A LGORITHM
2470
2471   We consider the following recursive LLM-based algorithm for solving this task. The overall
2472   algorithm maintains a dictionary of variables that have been calculated throughout its execution,
2473
       and eventually outputs the value returned by applying a function named ProcessNode to the
       targeted variable.
2474
2475   The function ProcessNode, which takes the name of a variable as input and returns its numeric
2476   value, is defined in a recursive manner:
2477
2478
             1. If the input variable is found in the dictionary of calculated variables, then return its value
                directly.
2479
2480         2. Otherwise, query the database with the input variable, and invoke one LLM call with the
2481            returned clue, which is prompted to either answer with a numeric value or conduct further
2482
                task decomposition, i.e. identifying variables whose values will be necessary and sufficient
                for calculating the input variable.
2483
                 (a) If the LLM chooses to answer with a numeric value, then return it.


                                                         46
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2484
                 (b) Otherwise, invoke ProcessNode once for each variable identified by the LLM call,
2485                 collect the answers returned by these children tasks, and finally invoke one LLM call
2486                 to calculate and return the numeric value of the input variable.
2487
2488   We make a few comments about this recursive algorithm. (1) The computational graph of this
2489   algorithm, or a generic LLM-based algorithm following the pattern of recursive decomposition, is
2490   constructed dynamically in a depth-first-search style at runtime. See Figure 4 for a visualization.
2491   The final graph is symmetric about the nodes corresponding to the leaf variables (D, E); on the
2492
       left are LLM calls for identifying children variables for the non-leaf variables (A, B, C), while
       on the right are LLM calls for calculating their numeric values. (2) The process of complex
2493
       reasoning is decomposed recursively, so that each LLM call within this algorithm only need to
2494
       handle a small step of reasoning, whose complexity is irrelevant to that of the overall task. (3) One
2495   particular configuration of this algorithm, like in the previous case study, is about how the LLM calls
2496   (especially the ones responsible for calculation) are prompted, e.g. to answer directly or think step
2497   by step. The impact of this design choice on the accuracy and efficiency of the overall algorithm
2498   will be investigated analytically and empirically soon in this section.

2505

2507   specific variables. Empirical results during our early exploration suggested that LLMs make very

2509   limited arithmetic capabilities of current LLMs. Moreover, all mathematical operations considered

2536
2537   Let us first consider the cost of each LLM call. For each leaf variable, one LLM call is needed
       for finding its numeric value based on its corresponding clue of length O(1), which implies

2538
       Lpre ≤ Lsys + O(1) and Ldec ≤ O(1), and thus C(leaf) ≤ CLLM (Lsys + 1, 1). For each non-
2539   leaf variable, two LLM calls are needed. One of them is responsible for task decomposition,
2540   i.e. identifying its g children variables, which implies Lpre ≤ Lsys + O(g) and Ldec ≤ O(g),
2541   and thus C(decomposition) ≤ CLLM (Lsys + g, g). The other LLM call is responsible for calculating
2542   the numeric value of the current variable based on the values of its children variables, which has
2543   Lpre ≤ Lsys +O(g), and Ldec ≤ O(1) if the LLM is prompted to answer directly, or Ldec ≤ Lcalc (g)
2544   for some function Lcalc if it is prompted to do the calculation step by step (e.g. Lcalc (g) ≤ O(g)
2545   or O(g 2 ), depending on how the LLM executes the calculation with g variables). In sum, we have
2546   C(calculation) ≤ CLLM (Lsys + g, 1 or Lcalc (g)) for each LLM call responsible for calculation.
2547   The analysis above has also revealed the total number of LLM calls. Let n and nleaf be the number
2548   of relevant variables and the number of relevant leaf variables, respectively. These parameters are
2549   determined by d, w, g and other factors, as is the case for the ℓ parameter (total length of relevant
2550   clues) in Appendix D. Recall from above that each leaf variable requires one LLM call for finding
2551   its value from its corresponding clue, while each non-leaf variable requires two LLM calls, one for
2552   task decomposition and one for calculation. Assuming that task decomposition is done correctly by
2553
       the LLM for each non-leaf variable, the total number of LLM calls will be 2 × (n − nleaf ) + nleaf =
       2 × n − nleaf .
2554

2565   E.4   E XPERIMENTS
2566
2567   We validate our analysis via experiments with a Qwen2-72B-Instruct model (Yang et al.,
2568   2024), supported by vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) and running on a server with 4 Nvidia A100-80G
2569   GPUs. For each experiment, we vary the difficulty of the task through the depth d or degree g
2570   of the grid of variables, while comparing two options of prompting the LLM calls responsible for
2571   calculation, namely “answer directly” and “calculate step by step”.
2572   Empirical results are shown in Figures 17 and 18. For the former, we fix the width w = 6 and
2573   degree g = 4, while varying the depth d; for the latter, we fix the depth d = 3 and width w =
2574   100, while varying the degree g. The results confirm our previous analysis: compared to “answer
2575   directly”, prompting the LLM calls to “calculate step by step” significantly boosts the accuracy of
2576   the overall algorithm and leads to satisfactory performance for a wide range of task configurations,
2577   while incurring higher costs in the latency and total number of decoding tokens by a multiplicative
2578
       factor, and making no difference in the number of LLM calls or total number of prefilling tokens.
