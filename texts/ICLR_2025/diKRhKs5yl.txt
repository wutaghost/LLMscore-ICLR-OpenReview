009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In-context learning with large language models (LLMs) delivers strong few-shot
012            performance by choosing few-shot demonstrations from the entire training dataset.
013            However, previous few-shot in-context learning methods, which calculate similarity
014            scores for choosing demonstrations, incur high computational costs by repeatedly
015            retrieving large-scale datasets for each query. This is due to their failure to recog-
016
               nize that not all demonstrations are equally informative, and many less informative
               demonstrations can be inferred from a core set of highly informative ones. To this
017
               end, we propose FEEDER (FEw yet Essential Demonstration prE-selectoR), a novel
018
               pre-selection framework that identifies a core subset of demonstrations containing
019            the most informative examples. This subset, referred to as the FEEDER set (DFEEDER ),
020            consists of demonstrations that capture both the “sufficiency” and “necessity” infor-
021            mation to infer the entire dataset. Notice that DFEEDER is selected before the few-shot
022            in-context learning, enabling more efficient few-shot demonstrations choosing in a
023            smaller set (DFEEDER ). To identify DFEEDER , we propose a novel effective tree based
024            algorithm. Once selected, it can replace the original dataset, leading to improved
025            efficiency and prediction accuracy in few-shot in-context learning. Additionally,
026            DFEEDER also benefit fine-tuning LLMs, we propose a bi-level optimization method
027
               enabling more efficient training without sacrificing performance when datasets
               become smaller. Our experiments are on 6 text classification datasets, 1 reasoning
028
               dataset, and 1 semantic-parsing dataset, across 8 LLMs (ranging from 335M to 8B
029
               parameters), demonstrate that: (i) In few-shot inference, FEEDER achieves superior
030            (or comparable) performance while utilizing only half the input training data. (ii)
031            In fine-tuning, FEEDER significantly boosts the performance of LLMs.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs), e.g., GPT (Brown et al., 2020), Gemma (Team et al., 2024), and
037   Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), have demonstrated impressive performance across a wide range of tasks
038   by employing few-shot inference, often referred as in-context learning (Brown et al., 2020; Dong
039
      et al., 2022). This approach avoids the computational expense associated with fine-tuning LLMs.
      Here, the core challenge is how to select the most effective demonstrations from a large training set.
040
      Early methods (Qiu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; Rubin et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022) primarily
041
      selected demonstrations based on relevance, using similarity scores between each demonstration and
042   the input question. Recent studies (Levy et al., 2022; Köksal et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023) have also
043   incorporated diversity, uncertainty, or clustering based metrics along with similarity, acknowledging
044   that measuring each example in isolation is inefficient. This is because previous methods fail to
045   recognize that not all demonstrations contribute equally across different LLMs and domains. A small
046   set of highly informative examples can often capture enough information to infer many of the less
047   informative ones. By not focusing on this core set, prior approaches end up processing unnecessary
048   data, resulting in higher computational costs and lower efficiency in few-shot inference.
049   Our main idea is to identify the most informative subset that can effectively replace the entire original
050   dataset, which is grounded in the consistency of LLMs. As observed by (Jang & Lukasiewicz, 2023),
051   LLMs demonstrate strong performance in tasks such as transitive inference. On this promise, we
052   propose a demonstration pre-selector named FEEDER (FEw yet Essential Demonstration prE-selectoR).
053   Concretely, our FEEDER, served as a core subset selector over the training dataset, examines input
      demonstrations in terms of “sufficiency” and “necessity”. Sufficiency investigates whether prompting

      To efficiently select a FEEDER set from the training dataset, the exhaustive enumeration and evaluation
073

074   examine whether each demonstration is sufficient and necessary to represent other demonstrations.
075   Our identification of FEEDER sets can be characterized as a core-set selection approach, producing
076   a subset of training instances that is highly informative for downstream tasks, including in-context
077   learning and fine-tuning. In the in-context learning setting, our FEEDER can also benefit from the
078   use of various demonstration selectors, by utilizing a pre-selected FEEDER set as the retrieval pool
079   instead of the entire training dataset to generate n-shot demonstrations. Additionally, we demonstrate
080   that a FEEDER set also can enhance the fine-tuning process. Specifically, we show that fine-tuning
081   the performance of LLMs with a single epoch on the pre-selected subset proves to be more effective
082
      than doing so on the entire training dataset. The above observations collectively give rise to a novel
      bi-level framework, wherein we formulate the pre-selection of FEEDER sets and the fine-tuning of
083
      LLMs on the pre-selected subset as a unified bi-level optimization problem. It comprises an outer
084
      level for extracting a FEEDER set using a frozen LLM and an inner level for fine-tuning the LLM with
085   the fixed FEEDER set. This iterative process involves utilizing the tuned LLM for the new FEEDER
086   selection in the subsequent iteration.
087
088
      Our empirical evaluations span 6 text classification datasets, 6 LLM bases ranging from 335M to 7B,
      and 6 existing demonstration selectors (e.g., random, similarity-based, and diversity-based). Results
089
      consistently demonstrate that efficiency and effectiveness of FEEDER: In terms of efficiency, our
090
      pre-selected FEEDER saves nearly half of the data size. In terms of effectiveness, using FEEDER rather
091   than the full training dataset, consistently yields superior (or comparable) performance in the few-shot
092   inference. Moreover, results also indicate that fine-tuning LLMs on FEEDER consistently leads to
093   significant improvements compared to fine-tuning on the entire training dataset. The evaluation of
094   FEEDER is further expanded to 1 reasoning task and 1 semantic-parsing task, providing consistent
095   results with trends observed in the text classification task.

      3    C ONNECTIONS TO E XISTING W ORK
153
154
      With the growing capabilities of LLMs, data (often referred to as “demonstrations”) selection has
155
      gained prominence, which involves selecting suitable examples as the context for in-context learning
156   (Dong et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022) or filtering a subset from training examples
157   for fine-tuning (Sachdeva et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024). Previous solutions have revolved around
158   constructing either parameter-free selection mechanisms (Wang et al., 2022; Zemlyanskiy et al., 2022;
159   Gao et al., 2023) or neural-based selection methods (Pasupat et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Gupta et al.,
160   2021; Rubin et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023). Recent investigations (Xia et al., 2024; Marion et al., 2023)
161   focus on mining training examples for fine-tuning specific tasks, with (Wang et al., 2024) extending
      this approach to in-context learning. In contrast to previous methods that use LLMs as demonstration


                                                              3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      selectors, our work leverages the powerful few-shot inference capabilities of LLMs by employing
163   them as pre-selectors. Building on the observation from (Jang & Lukasiewicz, 2023) that LLMs excel
164   at high-level logical reasoning such as transitive inference, our approach examines “sufficiency” and
165   “necessity” to identify a core set of training examples. This pre-selection process remains consistent
166   regardless of test datasets, thereby eliminating the need for re-computation across different test sets.
167   The resulting FEEDER sets can serve a dual purpose: they can be used as candidate input contexts or
168   to fine-tune the LLM. In both scenarios, FEEDER can significantly reduce the computation costs by
169   substituting the entire training dataset with FEEDER sets.

173   Let X, C denote variables for the input and the context (i.e., selected demonstrations). We introduce
174   Y , a boolean variable, to represent whether the corresponding output is correct. For simplicity, we
175   use Yxn = 1 to denote Y = 1|X = xn , meaning that the LLM generates the correct output for
176   the input xn . Similarly, Yxn = 0, equivalent to Y = 0|X = xn , indicates that LLM produces an
177   incorrect output for xn .
178   For convenience, we introduce S, a variable to record the original status of the LLM before new
179   plug-in and unplug operations (denoted as plug(·) and unplug(·) respectively). The connections
180   between the above operations and the do(·) operation in causality are discussed in Appendix A1.

182   4.1    R ELATIONSHIP BETWEEN D EMONSTRATIONS : F ROM I NSTANCE LEVEL TO S ET LEVEL
183
184   We begin by considering the relationship between two examples, denoted as (xn , yn ) and (xm , ym ).
185   Sufficiency relationship is introduced to assess whether plugging in one data point is adequate for the
186   LLM to produce the correct answer to another data point. Formally, we define sufficiency as:
187
      Definition 1 (Sufficient Instance). Given tuple (X, Y, C, S), a training sample (xn , yn ) is considered
188
      sufficient for another one (xm , ym ), if the following equation holds:
189
190                            Yxm = 1|plug((xn , yn )); C = ∅, S = (Yxm = 0).                              (4)
191   It means that when plugging in (xn , yn ), it would correct the LLM’s answer to xm .
192
193   Necessity relationship is introduced to assess whether it is necessary to retain a particular plugged-in
194   data point to maintain the correct output of another data point. Its formal definition can be written as:
195   Definition 2 (Necessary Instance). Given tuple (X, Y, C, S), a training sample (xn , yn ) is consid-
196   ered necessary for another one (xm , ym ), if the following equation holds:
197
                        Yxm = 0|unplug((xn , yn )); C = ((xn , yn )), S = (Yxm = 1).                        (5)
198
199   It means that prior to unplugging (xn , yn ), the LLM’s output is correct. However, when we do unplug
200   (xn , yn ) from the context, it causes the LLM to offer an incorrect output.
201
202
      The above definitions of sufficiency and necessity metrics, operating on the instance level, are further
      clarified with examples in Appendix A2.1. Extending these definitions to the set level, a sufficient
203
      set signifies that plugging in a specific set is adequate to ensure the correct outputs for all examples
204
      in another set, while a necessary set implies that removing any example from this set would result
205   in incorrect answers for at least one example within another set. Formal definitions for the above
206   set-level metrics, along with examples, are available in Appendix A2.2.

356   D

358   We provide the proof of the above proposition in Appendix A3, which demonstrates that our ap-

360
      the resulting set remains sufficient to represent the entire training dataset. The above tree based
      approximation algorithm is summarized in Algorithm 2 in Appendix A3.
361
362   Additionally, we present another algorithm for finding an exact sufficient and necessary subset from
363   DTRAIN , along with its proof and deployment discussion, in Appendices A4.1 and A7. Moreover, our

365   components. Specifically, the resulting FEEDER set from one round can be used as the input for the
366
      subsequent round. This iterative process can also yield an exact sufficient and necessary subset, as

410   5.1     E VALUATING FEEDER IN THE I N - CONTEXT L EARNING S ETTING
411
412   Since our DFEEDER works as a pre-selector, when applied in the in-context learning setting, we propose
413   incorporating demonstration selectors into FEEDER. In other words, our evaluations follow an ablative
414   approach, with the baseline involving the direct application of these demonstration selectors on DTRAIN .
415   This baseline can be regarded as treating these methods both as pre-selectors and demonstration
416
      selectors. For ease of deployment, our DFEEDER is identified using only a one-shot inference check
      (i.e., K = 1) and a single-round run (i.e., R = 1), unless otherwise stated.
417
418   Concretely, we conducted an evaluation of FEEDER in conjunction with following 6 selectors: (i) RAN
419   is the random selector, which selects input demonstration randomly from the retrieval pool; (ii) SIM
420   is the similarity-based selector (Sorensen et al., 2022; Gonen et al., 2022), which selects relevant
421   demonstrations in terms of the cosine similarity metric over the embedding vectors generated by a
422
      sentence transformer (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019); (iii) DIV is the diversity-based selector (Ye et al.,
      2022), which selects similar and diverse demonstrations in terms of maximal marginal relevance
423
      (Carbonell & Goldstein, 1998); (iv) UNC is the uncertainty-based selector (Köksal et al., 2022) that
424
      conducts selections according to their uncertainty metric; (v) CLU is the clustering-based selector
425   (Zhou et al., 2023) that searches demonstrations by clustering. (vi) LVM uses LLMs as latent variable
426   models (Wang et al., 2024) to learn latent variables for down-streaming in-context learning. Please
427   refer to Appendix A5.1 for detailed descriptions of the above demonstration selectors.
428
      Experimental results regarding in-context learning performance are reported in Tables 1, 2 and 3. We
429
      also present the reduction of our FEEDER in Figure 4. Our findings are summarized as follows.
430
431   FEEDER is an effective demonstration pre-selector (i.e., compressor) and can benefit from diverse
      demonstration selectors. By combining the results from Table 1 and Figure 4, it is evident that

433   superior or comparable performance across popular demonstration selectors, including RAN, SIM,
434   and DIV. Experimental results using UNC, CLU, and LVM as demonstration selectors are depicted
435   in Table 2, providing additional evidence supporting the efficacy of FEEDER as a proficient data

511   5.3   C ASE S TUDY WITH A RTIFICIAL DATA P OINTS G ENERATED BY LLM S
512
513   Subsequently, we conduct a case study to substantiate the central proposition of this paper: whether
514   the assessment of the quality of demonstrations should depend on the specific LLM in use. We
515   consider the factual error made by Google Bard in the first demo1 . We further prompt gpt-3.5-turbo
516
      to generate 5 sufficient and necessary statements for the fact. We evaluate separately using these
      statements as a prompt to gpt-3.5-turbo, and find that either one of the generated statements is
517
      sufficient and necessary to answer the question “What took the very first pictures of a planet outside of
518
      our own solar system?” We then evaluate the performance of gpt-j-6b with the above 5 statements,
519   and find that only the 1-st or the 5-th statement is sufficient and necessary instance to answer the
520   above question. Combining the results of gpt-j-6b and gpt-3.5-turbo verifies one of the core
521   insights of our paper: the evaluation of prompting a demonstration should consider the specific LLM
522   in use. Please refer to the detailed description of prompts and outputs in Appendix A11.2.

      6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526
      In this paper, we present a novel demonstration pre-selector FEEDER, designed to leverage LLMs’
527   powerful transitivity inference capabilities to identify high-quality demonstration and provide an ap-
528   proximate approach for their discovery. Our experimental results showcase the significant advantages
529   of FEEDER across diverse LLM bases in both in-context learning and fine-tuning settings. Due to
530   budget limitations, our paper presents results only for LLMs with up to 10B parameters for in-context
531   learning evaluation and up to 2B parameters for the fine-tuning setting. In the future, it would be
532   valuable to explore the use of larger LLMs and extend the applications of FEEDER to areas such as
533   data safety and data management.

      A1     C ONNECTIONS TO E XISTING A PPROACHES

758
759   A1.1    C ONNECTIONS TO C AUSALITY
760
761
      The concepts of sufficiency and necessity have a broad application scope, especially in causality
      (Pearl, 1980; 2009), where sufficiency and necessity are proposed to define the causal relationship
762
      between two binary variables. Let X and Y denote a pair of variables. Then, the probability of
763
      sufficiency measures the capacity of setting X = true to produce Y = true, while the probability
764   of necessity measures the changing the value of X from X = true to X = false would cause the
765   value of Y changing from Y = true to Y = false
766
767
      In this paper, we adopt the concepts of sufficiency and necessity in the context of demonstration
      selection, where we investigate whether prompting certain data points is sufficient or necessary for
768
      the given LLM to generate correct answers for input questions. For this purpose, we introduce the
769
      plugging-in operation, denoted as plug(·), to examine sufficiency, and the unplugging operation,
770   denoted as unplug(·), to examine necessity. Both of these operations are analogous to the do
771   operation in causality, denoted as do(·), which indicates that the system operates under the condition
772   that certain variables are controlled by external forces. To be more specific, in our setting, the external
773   force can be explained as follows. We have the choice to either plug in or unplug certain data points,
774   thereby altering what is already plugged into the LLM. Our approach shares similarities with the
775   counterfactual idea in causality, which explores hypothetical scenarios by considering what might
776   happen if certain variables are set with different values. In our case, we investigate the impact of
777   plugged-in data that includes data points differing from the historical (i.e., factual) setting. Notably, a
778
      significant distinction between our approach and the counterfactual setting in causality lies in the
      fact that we do not need to estimate “counterfactual” situations; instead, we can directly conduct
779
      evaluations.
780

782   A1.2    C ONNECTIONS TO D EMONSTRATION S ELECTION
783
784   In the context of few-shot inference, a central challenge lies in selecting the appropriate training
785   samples as extra input during inference. These samples are often referred to as demonstrations or
786   prompts (Levy et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2022). The underlying assumption is that the
787   training dataset serves as a support set (Yosida, 2012) for test samples. Previous studies (Wang et al.,
788   2022; Rubin et al., 2021) have demonstrated that introducing similar training samples can enhance the
789
      performance of LLMs on test instances. (Gao et al., 2023) enhances these approaches by retrieving
      candidates whose ground label lies in top-2 zero-shot predictions. However, as pointed out in (Levy
790
      et al., 2022), existing methods often treat each data point in isolation, neglecting the collective impact
791
      of multiple data points. For instance, retrievers based on similarity metrics may select redundant
792   data points together. To address this limitation, (Levy et al., 2022) proposes to consider the diversity
793   among the data points, to avoid the case where too “similar” data points are selected together. Further,
794   (Rubin et al., 2021) trains an LLM as a contrastive scorer as well as a demonstration referrer, and (Li
795   et al., 2023) advances this framework through unified training across various datasets.
796
      In this paper, we present a novel perspective, asserting that the quality of demonstrations is contingent
797
      on the specific LLM in use. Namely, a high-quality demonstration for one LLM might be deemed
798   low-quality for another. Leveraging this insight, we introduce sufficiency and necessity as new set-
799   level metrics. Our approach offers several advantages: Firstly, sufficiency and necessity measure the
800   quality of data points based on the specific LLM, in contrast to generic similarity and diversity metrics.
801   Secondly, our proposed sufficiency and necessity extend to the set level, enabling the consideration
802   of data points as a cohesive whole. In our framework, “similarity” is akin to “sufficiency” signifying
803   that plugging in data points can enhance LLM performance, while “diversity” is akin to “necessity”
804   suggesting that each data point should play an indispensable role.
805   Recent studies (Xia et al., 2024; Marion et al., 2023) focus on mining training examples for fine-tuning
806   on specific tasks, while (Wang et al., 2024) extends this idea to in-context learning. Unlike these
807   approaches, which use LLMs to select demonstrations tailored to specific test datasets, our work
808   leverages LLMs as demonstration pre-selectors, identifying a core subset of the training data that
809   remains independent of the test datasets, thus eliminating the need for re-computation across different
      test datasets.

      A1.3    C ONNECTIONS TO C ORE S ET S ELECTION
811
812   Core-set selection (Feldman, 2020; Guo et al., 2022), a longstanding problem in machine learning,
813   focuses on identifying a subset of the most informative training samples. Previous research (Dor
814   et al., 2020) has surveyed and evaluated state-of-the-art approaches for models like BERT (Devlin
815   et al., 2018), encompassing strategies such as random sampling, uncertainty-sampling (using entropy
816   metric) (Lewis, 1995; Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) and diversity sampling (using diversity metric)
817   (Gissin & Shalev-Shwartz, 2019).
818   FEEDER, in contrast to these prior papers mainly using active learning, is designed to select core
819   sets, which can serve as additional input contexts (i.e., in-context learning setting) or be used for
820   fine-tuning LLMs (i.e., fine-tuning setting). FEEDER defines “informative training samples” as those
821   samples that specifically enhance the LLM’s performance on a given task.

823   A1.4    C ONNECTIONS TO P ROMPT O PTIMIZATION
824
825   Prompting provides a natural way for humans to interact with; and due to its flexibility, prompting
826   has been widely used as a genre method for various natural language processing tasks (Schick &
827   Schütze, 2020; Brown et al., 2020; Sanh et al., 2021). However, using prompting effectively with
828   LLMs requires careful design, either done manually (Reynolds & McDonell, 2021) or automatically
829
      (Gao, 2021; Shin et al., 2020), as LLMs do not interpret prompts in the same way humans do (Webson
      & Pavlick, 2021; Lu et al., 2021). While numerous successful methods (Liu et al., 2021; Lester
830
      et al., 2021; Qin & Eisner, 2021) for prompt tuning rely on optimizing a continuous space through
831
      gradient-based techniques, this approach becomes impractical as many powerful LLMs are only
832   accessible through APIs that may not offer gradient access.
833
834   Our FEEDER approach can be seen as a discrete pre-search method for prompts, distinct from existing
835
      methods for prompt generation (Gao, 2021; Ben-David et al., 2021), prompt scoring (Davison et al.,
      2019), and prompt paraphrasing (Jiang et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2021), which aim to optimize
836
      instructions by directly searching the natural language hypothesis space. Instead, our approach
837
      leverages the causal dependencies among candidate demonstrations, focusing on searching for the
838   most informative demonstrations as prompts, in terms of sufficiency and necessity.

1053   Definition 10 (Transitivity Inference). Noted by (Jang & Lukasiewicz, 2023) that LLMs excel at
1054
       transitive inference. We assume that sufficiency is transitive among sets. Formally, for any three sets,
       denoted as DA , DB , and DC , if DA is a sufficient set of DB and DB is a sufficient set of DC , then we
1055
       can conclude that DA is a sufficient set of DC .

1061

1062

1433
1434
       Notice that numerous real-world datasets are temporal and require frequent updates. Re-running the
       tree based approximation algorithm for FEEDER over all samples can be excessively time-consuming.
1435
       To address this, we design an incremental approach, treating the unchanged portion as a plug-and-play
1436
       FEEDER set and the LLM as a whole, forming a new “LLM”. Therefore, we can apply FEEDER solely
1437   to compute incremental data for the modified part, encompassing newly added and modified data
1438   points. Also, a significant challenge of FEEDER arises from the temporal nature of many real-world
1439   datasets, some of which require frequent updates, potentially on a daily basis. The conventional
1440   approach of recalculating a FEEDER over all unchanged and changed samples can be time-consuming
1441   in such dynamic scenarios. To address this challenge, we introduce an incremental update algorithm
1442   for FEEDER, enabling the efficient re-computation of only the changed portions, including newly
1443   added and modified samples.
1444   As depicted in Figure 7, once a FEEDER set for the original dataset is generated, we treat the unchanged
1445   part of plug-and-play plugged data and the LLM as a whole (depicted by the dashed box) as a new
1446   “LLM”. Subsequently, we apply FEEDER exclusively to compute incremental data for the changed
1447   part, covering newly added and modified data points. This strategy aims to enhance the efficiency
1448   and responsiveness of FEEDER in the context of evolving and temporal datasets.
