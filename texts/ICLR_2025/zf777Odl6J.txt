009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in
012            processing graph-structured data, but they often struggle with high-dimensional
013            features and complex, nonlinear relationships. To address these challenges, we
014            propose KA-GAT, a novel model that integrates Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks
015            (KANs) with Graph Attention Networks (GATs). KA-GAT leverages KAN to de-
016            compose and reconstruct high-dimensional features, enhancing representational
017            capacity, while a multi-head attention mechanism dynamically focuses on key
018
               graph components, improving interpretability. Experimental results on bench-
               mark datasets, including Cora and Citeseer, demonstrate that KA-GAT achieves
019
               significant accuracy improvements compared to baseline models like GAT, with
020
               a relative gain of 4.5% on Cora. These findings highlight KA-GAT’s robustness
021            and potential as an interpretable and scalable solution for high-dimensional graph
022            data, paving the way for further advancements in GNN research.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Graph-structured data is crucial in fields like social networks and bioinformatics. Graph Neural Net-
028   works (GNNs) have emerged as powerful tools for learning representations of such data, achieving
029   success in tasks like node classification and link prediction (Wu et al., 2021). However, traditional
030   GNNs struggle with high-dimensional features and complex relationships due to their reliance on
031   fixed functions and linearity. Recent advances in GNNs have introduced improved attention mech-
032   anisms (e.g. GATv2 (Brody et al., 2022)) and propagation schemes (e.g. APPNP (Klicpera et al.,
033   2019)), tackle some issues but still fall short in capturing intricate feature interactions.
034   Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs), based on Kolmogorov’s theorem, offer a way to decompose
035   functions into simpler parts, making them ideal for high-dimensional, nonlinear data. However,
036   integrating KANs into GNNs is largely unexplored. Existing approaches, such as GKAN (Kiamari
037   et al., 2024; Carlo et al., 2024) and KAGNN (Bresson et al., 2024), have demonstrated potential but
038   are often limited in scalability, generalizability, and performance on diverse datasets.
039   To address these limitations, we introduce KA-GAT, a novel GNN model that integrates KANs with
040   a multi-head attention mechanism. KA-GAT represents an innovative attempt to combine KAN’s
041   decomposition capabilities with GAT’s dynamic attention framework, enabling the model to effec-
042   tively process high-dimensional features and capture complex interactions within graph-structured
043   data. This integration not only enhances the model’s representational power but also improves its
044   interpretability by dynamically focusing on key graph components. KA-GAT demonstrates signifi-
045   cant improvements over baseline models and other new models combining KAN and GNN, such as
046
      GAT, GCN, GKAN and KAGCN, and provides a strong foundation for future research.
047   The main contributions of this study are as follows:
048
049         • Model Innovation: We propose KA-GAT, a GNN architecture that combines Kolmogorov-
050           Arnold layers with multi-head attention, addressing challenges in processing high-
051
              dimensional features and extending the flexibility of traditional GNNs.
052         • Theoretical Integration: KA-GAT bridges Kolmogorov-Arnold theory with GNN de-
053           sign, demonstrating how feature decomposition principles can enhance graph represen-
              tation learning.

068   2.1   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
069
070   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have transformed the processing of graph-structured data by en-
071   abling effective aggregation of neighborhood information (Gori et al., 2005; Scarselli et al., 2009).
072   Traditional GNNs, including Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) and Graph Attention Networks
073   (GATs), have shown strong performance in tasks such as node classification and link prediction.
074
      GCNs, introduced by Kipf and Welling (Kipf & Welling, 2017), employ a localized first-order ap-
      proximation of spectral graph convolutions to aggregate features from neighboring nodes. How-
075
      ever, GCNs often struggle to capture complex, non-linear relationships due to their reliance on
076
      fixed, linear transformations (Zhou et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2019). Recent GNN variants, such as
077   APPNP (Klicpera et al., 2019), address these limitations through improved propagation schemes
078   based on personalized PageRank, extending information flow across larger neighborhoods without
079   oversmoothing and increasing the model’s capacity for deep feature interactions.
080
081   2.2   G RAPH ATTENTION N ETWORKS
082
083   Graph Attention Networks (GATs) introduced an attention mechanism into GNNs, allowing models
084   to dynamically weigh the importance of neighboring nodes based on their features (Veličković et al.,
085   2018). This adaptive weighting mitigates some limitations of GCNs by enabling the model to focus
086   on the most relevant parts of the graph. The attention coefficients are calculated as:

091   Further advancements, such as GATv2 (Brody et al., 2022), allow the attention mechanism to ad-
092   just dynamically during training, improving adaptability in noisy or complex graph environments.
093   However, even with these improvements, GAT-based models often struggle with high-dimensional
094   features and capturing highly non-linear relationships. These limitations underscore the need for in-
095
      tegrating more sophisticated feature transformation methods, such as Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks
      (KANs), to enhance flexibility and expressiveness.

098   2.3   KOLMOGOROV-A RNOLD N ETWORKS
099

100   Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) for approximating non-linear functions (Liu et al., 2024). Inspired by the
101   Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem, KANs place learnable activation functions on edges
102   rather than nodes, with each weight parameterized as a spline. This configuration enhances the
103   expressiveness of neural networks, especially for tasks involving complex, high-dimensional data.
104   The mathematical formulation of KANs is given by:

      3.1   KOLMOGOROV-A RNOLD L AYER
137
138   The Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN) layer in the KA-GAT model leverages the Kolmogorov-
139   Arnold representation theorem to decompose complex, high-dimensional node features into simpler
140   univariate functions, which are then recombined to form richer, more informative representations.
141   By using univariate functions, the KAN layer effectively captures nonlinear relationships within the
142   node features, addressing a key limitation in traditional GNNs. This decomposition allows KA-GAT
143   to process high-dimensional data with greater flexibility, creating an expressive feature space that
144   enhances the model’s ability to represent complex graph structures.
145   In our implementation, the KAN layer consists of learnable transformations that map input fea-
146   tures to a higher-dimensional space, followed by nonlinear activations. The use of splines for the
147   univariate functions increases the flexibility in capturing intricate patterns, making the KAN layer
148   especially beneficial for datasets with complex interactions. This decomposition and recombination
149   process is essential to improving the expressiveness of KA-GAT, particularly for tasks requiring
150   robust nonlinear transformations.

          3.5    KA-GAT A RCHITECTURE
255
256       The overall architecture of KA-GAT integrates all these components to create a powerful GNN
257       capable of processing complex graph data. The forward pass of the KA-GAT model proceeds as
258       follows:
259
260              1. KAN Layer: Input node features are processed through the KAN layer, where they are
261                 decomposed and reconstructed into more informative representations.
262
                 2. Graph Convolutional Layers: The output from the KAN layer passes through multiple
263                 graph convolutional layers, combining Multi-Head Attention GNN, GCNConv, and GAT-
264                 Conv layers to aggregate local and global information.
265
266
                 3. Flatten and Fully Connected Layers: After the final graph convolutional layer, features
                    are flattened and processed through fully connected layers to produce the final output.
267
268              4. Multi-Head Attention: Throughout the graph convolutional layers, the attention mecha-
269                 nism enables KA-GAT to attend to different graph regions, capturing diverse node relation-
                    ships.

279   To evaluate KA-GAT’s performance, we conducted experiments on three widely used benchmark

      GNNs and existing KAN-GNN hybrids (Li et al., 2018; Fan et al., 2020; Alon & Yahav, 2021).

284           • Cora: This dataset consists of 2,708 scientific publications categorized into 7 classes, with
285             5,429 citation links. Each node is represented by a 1,433-dimensional feature vector, de-
286             rived from a bag-of-words model. Cora is widely used to evaluate models’ capacity to
287             capture high-dimensional and sparse features.
288
              • Citeseer: Comprising 3,327 scientific publications grouped into 6 categories, Citeseer fea-
289
                tures 4,732 citation links. Its 3,703-dimensional feature vectors make it one of the most
290             challenging datasets for models due to its sparse and high-dimensional nature.
291
292           • Pubmed: This dataset includes 19,717 publications from the medical domain, classified
293
                into 3 categories, with 44,338 citation links. Each node is described by a 500-dimensional

294
                Citeseer by emphasizing scalability.
295
296
      We adopted PyTorch Geometric’s (PyG) Planetoid class for standardized data loading and parti-
297

298   AdamW optimizer with an initial learning rate of 0.01, and early stopping was applied to prevent

300   performance was assessed with standard metrics: Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and F1-score.

409   In this study, we introduced KA-GAT, a model that integrates Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks
410   (KANs) and multi-head attention mechanisms to effectively address high-dimensional and com-
411   plex graph-structured data. The experimental results indicate that KA-GAT outperforms traditional

      GCN and GAT models on benchmark datasets like Cora and Citeseer, showcasing its robustness
      in handling intricate graph structures. In this section, we discuss the key findings, strengths, and
413
      limitations of KA-GAT and outline potential avenues for future research.

416   5.1   K EY I NSIGHTS AND P ERFORMANCE A NALYSIS
417
      KA-GAT demonstrated strong performance on high-dimensional datasets such as Cora and Cite-
418
      seer, achieving consistent improvements over baseline models and KAN-GNN hybrids. These re-
419
      sults highlight the effectiveness of the Kolmogorov-Arnold (KA) layer in capturing nonlinear rela-
420   tionships and decomposing complex high-dimensional features into more learnable representations.
421   Additionally, the multi-head attention mechanism dynamically focuses on key graph components,
422   enhancing the model’s ability to capture diverse and critical node relationships. These characteristics
423   position KA-GAT as a robust solution for tasks requiring rich feature representations and improved
424   interpretability, especially in scenarios with sparse or high-dimensional data.
425
      On the Pubmed dataset, which features lower-dimensional attributes and a larger graph scale, KA-
426   GAT’s performance gains were more limited. This discrepancy can be attributed to the dataset’s low
427   feature dimensionality, which reduces the advantages of the KA layer’s decomposition capabilities.
428   Furthermore, the dense graph structure of Pubmed places greater demands on the multi-head atten-
429   tion mechanism, making it challenging to scale effectively within resource-constrained conditions.
430   Increasing the number of attention heads to address Pubmed’s scale and complexity was infeasible
431   due to hardware limitations, as the training process already approached the full memory capacity of
      an NVIDIA A100 GPU.

442   and aggregation. The KAN layer’s ability to map high-dimensional features into univariate functions
443   reduces feature space complexity while preserving intricate nonlinear relationships. This decompo-

      sition is especially advantageous for complex graph data, as reflected in the improved performance

445
446   The multi-head attention mechanism further extends KA-GAT’s capabilities by processing multiple
447   feature subspaces in parallel, enabling the model to selectively focus on distinct node relationships.
448   This approach enhances KA-GAT’s flexibility and robustness by capturing diverse facets of graph
449   relationships, contributing to a more interpretable model. These features align KA-GAT with recent
450
      trends in improving GNN interpretability, offering valuable insights into model decision-making.

      5.4     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
473
474   While KA-GAT demonstrates strong performance on high-dimensional datasets like Cora and Cite-
