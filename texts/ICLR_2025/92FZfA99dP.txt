011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Medical image segmentation is essential in medical diagnostics but is hindered
014            by the scarcity of labeled three-dimensional imaging data, which requires costly
015            expert annotations. Semi-supervised learning (SSL) addresses this limitation by
016
               utilizing large amounts of unlabeled data alongside limited labeled samples. The
               Mean Teacher model, a prominent SSL method, enhances performance by em-
017
               ploying an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) of the student model to form a
018
               teacher model, where the EMA decay coefficient is critical. However, using a
019            fixed coefficient fails to adapt to the evolving training dynamics, potentially re-
020            stricting the model’s effectiveness. In this paper, we propose Meta Mean Teacher,
021            a novel framework that integrates meta-learning to dynamically adjust the EMA
022            decay coefficient during training. We incorporate the proposed Dynamic Decay
023            Modulation (DDM) module into our Meta Mean Teacher framework, which cap-
024            tures the representational capacities of both student and teacher models. DDM
025            heuristically learns the optimal EMA decay coefficient by taking the losses of the
026            student and teacher networks as inputs and updating it through pseudo-gradient
027
               descent on a meta-objective. This dynamic adjustment allows the teacher model
               to more effectively guide the student as training progresses. Experiments on two
028
               datasets with different modalities, i.e., CT and MRI, show that Meta Mean Teacher
029
               consistently outperforms traditional Mean Teacher methods with fixed EMA coef-
030            ficients. Furthermore, integrating Meta Mean Teacher into state-of-the-art frame-
031            works like UA-MT, AD-MT, and PMT leads to significant performance enhance-
032            ments, achieving new state-of-the-art results in semi-supervised medical image
033            segmentation. We will release the code if the paper is accepted.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Medical image segmentation, as a fundamental task in the medical field, lays the groundwork for
039   subsequent high-level tasks. However, three-dimensional medical imaging data pose a significant
040   annotation challenge, typically requiring the supervision of domain experts for accurate annotations,
041   making it difficult to provide large amounts of data. Semi-supervised learning aims to better utilize
042   extensive unlabeled data under the supervision of limited labeled data (Zhou et al., 2021; Zheng
043   et al., 2022; Rizve et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2023; Xin et al., 2019), effectively addressing limited data
044
      availability by fully leveraging unlabeled samples.
045   Mean Teacher (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017) is a classic work in the field of semi-supervised learning
046   and can significantly enhance performance in semi-supervised medical image segmentation. Early
047   work in the field, such as UA-MT, improved the Mean Teacher paradigm by introducing uncertainty,
048   thereby enhancing the model’s representation capabilities. More recent work, such as PMT (Gao
049   et al., 2024) and AD-MT (Zhao et al., 2023), demonstrated that using a more complex temporal
050
      framework, Mean Teacher can still improve the model’s representation capabilities.
051   The Mean Teacher model employs Exponential Moving Average (EMA) to generate a teacher model
052   from the student model without additional training, where the decay coefficient α plays a critical
053   role. As illustrated in Figure 1, varying values of α have a significant impact on model performance.
      To better harness the potential of the Mean Teacher framework, we propose Meta Mean Teacher,


                                                          1

054
      a framework that utilizes meta-learning and incorporates plug-and-play modules Dynamic Decay
055   Modulation (DDM) for optimization. By introducing DDM, we capture the training representations
056   of the model and explicitly adjust the α value of the Mean Teacher throughout the iterative process.
057
058
      This framework design is inspired by observations on the influence of EMA decay coefficient in the
      Mean Teacher method and insights from meta-learning in hyperparameter optimization (Finn et al.,
059
      2017; Li et al., 2017; Shu et al., 2019). One interpretation is that α determines the update intensity
060
      of the teacher model, a characteristic that should evolve as the model training progresses, which
061   DDM can capture effectively. Specifically, for the DDM module that allows dynamic adjustment of
062   α, we aim to enable the module to fully perceive the representational capacity of both student and
063   teacher models and to heuristically explore and learn the optimal α during training. Consistent with
064   previous approaches, we use the losses of both student and teacher models as inputs to the module.
065   Our DDM is trained on meta data unseen by the Mean Teacher architecture and is updated through
066   pseudo-gradient descent.

068   design of DDM, specifically focusing on the
069   choice of student guidance as the model up-

      the teacher is updated through the momentum

104   2.1   S EMI - SUPERVISED L EARNING
105
106   Semi-supervised learning leverages both labeled and unlabeled data to enhance model performance,
107   primarily through two paradigms: consistency regularization and pseudo label generation. Consis-
      tency regularization aims to maintain stable predictions under various perturbations. For instance,


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the Π model (Laine & Aila, 2016) applies image-level perturbations, while the Mean Teacher (MT)
109   model (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017) uses exponential moving average (EMA) to align outputs be-
110   tween teacher and student models. Advanced methods like SASSnet (Li et al., 2020) focus on
111   geometric shape regularity, and CPCL (Xu et al., 2022) establishes a cyclic framework for super-
112   vised and unsupervised training regularization. Further, DTC (Luo et al., 2021) introduces task-
113   level regularization with a dual-task consistency framework, and MCF (Wang et al., 2023) employs
114   heterogeneous models for model-level regularization. Pseudo label generation enhances the dis-
115   criminative ability of models by using labeled data to train a prior model, which then generates
116   pseudo labels for unlabeled data. Direct methods, such as the approach in (Lee et al., 2013), use
117
      high-confidence thresholds, while UA-MT (Yu et al., 2019) filters unreliable pseudo labels using un-
      certainty estimation. Indirect methods include Tri-Net (Dong-DongChen & WeiGao, 2018), which
118
      uses two subnetworks to generate pseudo labels for a third, and MCF (Wang et al., 2023), which
119
      dynamically generates pseudo labels with a heterogeneous network. These approaches collectively
120   advance semi-supervised learning by enhancing model stability and discriminative capacity through
121   innovative regularization and pseudo labeling techniques.
122

124   2.2   M ETA L EARNING
125
126   Meta-learning or “learning to learn” fundamentally involves optimizing hyperparameters to enhance
127   model adaptability across diverse tasks. This approach aims to create models that can quickly adapt
128   to new tasks with minimal data and computational resources. Several key works have approached
129   this from different angles, each contributing unique methodologies and insights to the field. One of
130
      the foundational works in this area is Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) (Finn et al., 2017),
      which optimizes initial model parameters to enable rapid adaptation with minimal data through a
131
      few gradient updates. MAML’s core idea is to find a set of initial parameters that can be fine-tuned
132
      quickly for any given task, making it highly versatile for various applications. Meta-SGD (Li et al.,
133   2017) extends this concept by learning not only the initialization but also the update direction and
134   learning rates. This allows for efficient single-step adaptation, further reducing the computational
135   burden and improving the speed of adaptation. Meta-Weight-Net (Shu et al., 2019) addresses biased
136   training data by learning a weighting function guided by unbiased meta-data, adjusting the impor-
137   tance of training samples to improve generalization. Other notable works also contribute to hyper-
138   parameter optimization through meta-learning. Learning to Optimize (Li & Malik, 2016) automates
139   optimization algorithm design using reinforcement learning. MetaQNN (Baker et al., 2016) auto-
140   mates CNN architecture design through Q-learning. Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017) learn
141
      a metric space for classification based on class prototypes. Meta Networks (MetaNet) (Munkhdalai
      & Yu, 2017) adjust inductive biases for rapid generalization. ALFA (Baik et al., 2020) enhances
142
      MAML by adaptively generating hyperparameters like learning rates and weight decay coefficients.

175   This Mean Teacher architecture leverages a more stable Teacher model to utilize a large amount of

179   For the key parameter α in the Mean Teacher framework, a naive understanding is that if α is too
180   large, the Teacher model will be too conservative and difficult to absorb new knowledge learned by
181   the Student model; if α is too small, the Teacher model may become too sensitive and easily affected
182   by noise. Figure 1 shows the significant impact of α on the performance of the Mean Teacher. On
183

      appropriate value such as 0.97 can bring an improvement of nearly 1 in dice loss, while an intuitively
184
      small value of 0.01 does not cause a significant performance degradation.

      To understand why our student-guided approach is more effective than a teacher-guided one, we
      provide a theoretical analysis based on function approximation in parameter space.
261
262   Let F be the space of all possible medical image segmentation functions, and p∗ ∈ F be the
263   true segmentation function. The student model can be represented as fs : Θs → F, where Θs
264   is the parameter space of the student model. Similarly, the teacher model can be represented as
265   ft : Θt → F.
266   Essentially, in the teacher-guided approach, the iterative process aims to have the teacher learn the
267   optimal interpolation result between the student and the previous teacher parameters. However, the
268   teacher can provide an unbiased estimate of the medical image segmentation only if the line segment
269   between the previous teacher and student parameters passes through the optimal parameters. In a
      high-dimensional parameter space, this probability approaches zero.

281

292   By updating the EMA based on the student model, the teacher model after EMA updates tends
293   to provide pseudo-labels that better help the student model approximate p∗ , thereby enhancing the
294   performance of the student model.
295
      This theoretical analysis provides crucial insights into the effectiveness of our student-guided ap-
296
      proach. By using the student model to guide the learning process, we shift the focus from attempting
297   to create an optimal teacher model (which is inherently limited by EMA constraints) to creating a
298   teacher model that best facilitates the student’s learning process.
299
300
      In other words, the DDM module in our approach doesn’t learn to produce a teacher model that
      directly approximates the true distribution—a goal that is theoretically unattainable due to EMA
301
      constraints. Instead, it learns to generate a teacher model that, while not necessarily optimal in
302
      isolation, is ideally suited to guide the student model towards the true distribution through pseudo-
303   labeling and other interactions.
304
305   In essence, our method transforms the role of the teacher model from a target to be reached into
306
      a guide to be followed. This paradigm shift allows us to overcome the theoretical limitations of
      EMA-based approaches while still benefiting from the stability and regularization effects that make
307
      Mean Teacher methods effective in semi-supervised learning scenarios.

464   5   C ONCLUSION
465
466   In this paper, we propose a semi-supervised medical image segmentation framework named Meta
467   Mean Teacher. Meta Mean Teacher introduces the DDM module to dynamically adjust the EMA
468
      decay coefficient, a critical hyperparameter in the Mean Teacher model. We demonstrate the effec-
      tiveness of the DDM model through simple theoretical derivations and experiments. Compared to a
469
      more straightforward approach, Teacher-guided, our Student-guided selection enhances the model’s
470
      representation capability. We argue that it is challenging for the Teacher model to fit the segmenta-
471   tion distribution, whereas it is simpler for the Teacher to learn how to provide pseudo-labels that are
472   more beneficial for the Student. In comparative experiments with other methods, the MT framework
473   methods incorporating DDM achieved state-of-the-art accuracy, significantly surpassing previous
474   methods and maintaining this advantage even with more limited data and more challenging tasks.
475   This indicates that our proposed DDM not only enhances the representation capability of the clas-
476   sic Mean Teacher but also, as a plug-and-play component, has extensibility and can generalize to
477   different models, demonstrating the versatility of our approach.
478   Limitations and Future Work. Despite the excellent performance of Meta Mean Teacher, the se-
479   lection of a more reasonable guidance method remains to be explored. Even with Student-guided
480   approach, the essence of the Mean Teacher still involves searching within a highly limited space,
481   which restricts the potential of the Mean Teacher. Investigating more reasonable guidance methods,
482   integrating them into the Meta Mean Teacher paradigm, and evaluating their potential to further
483   enhance performance is a topic worthy of future research. Additionally, applying this work to meth-
484   ods beyond medical imaging to further explore the generalizability of Meta Mean Teacher is also a
485
      necessary area of study.
