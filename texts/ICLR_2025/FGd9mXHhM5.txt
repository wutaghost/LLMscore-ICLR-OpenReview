009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Distributed approaches have many computational benefits, but they are vulnerable
012            to attacks from a subset of devices transmitting incorrect information. This paper
013            investigates Byzantine-resilient algorithms in a decentralized setting, where devices
014            communicate directly with one another. We investigate the notion of breakdown
015            point, and show an upper bound on the number of adversaries that decentralized
016            algorithms can tolerate. We introduce an algorithmic framework that recovers
017            ClippedGossip and NNA, two popular approaches for robust decentralized learn-
018
               ing, as special cases. This framework allows us to generalize NNA to sparse graph,
               and introduce CG+ , which is at the intersection of the two. Our unified analysis
019
               framework gives near-optimal guarantees for CG+ (and other approaches with
020
               additional assumptions). Experimental evidence validates the effectiveness of CG+
021            and the gap with NNA, in particular against a novel attack tailored to sparse graphs
022            that we introduce.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Distributed machine learning, in which the training process is performed on multiple computing
028   units (or nodes), responds to the increasingly distributed nature of data, its sensitivity, and the rising
029   computational cost of optimizing models. While most distributed architectures rely on coordination
030   from a central server, some communication networks favor peer-to-peer exchanges, and global
031   coordination can be costly in these cases. Besides, the decentralized setting has several other perks: it
032   mitigates the communication bottleneck and failure risk at the main server, and provides additional
033
      privacy guarantees since agents only have a local view of the system (Cyffers et al., 2022). However,
      distributing optimization over a large number of devices introduces new security issues: software may
034
      be faulty, local data may be corrupted, and nodes can be hacked or even controlled by a hostile party.
035
      Such issues are modeled as Byzantine node failures (Lamport et al., 1982), defined as omniscient
036   adversaries able to collude with each other.
037
038   Standard distributed learning methods are known to be vulnerable to Byzantine attacks (Blanchard
039
      et al., 2017), which has led to significant efforts in the development of robust distributed learning
      algorithms. From the first works tackling Byzantine-robust SGD (Blanchard et al., 2017; Yin
040
      et al., 2018; Alistarh et al., 2018; El-Mhamdi et al., 2020), methods have been developed to tackle
041
      stochastic noise using Polyak momentum (Karimireddy et al., 2021; Farhadkhani et al., 2022) and
042   mixing strategies to handle heterogeneous loss functions (Karimireddy et al., 2020; Allouah et al.,
043   2023). In parallel to these robust algorithms, efficient attacks have been developed to challenge
044   Byzantine-robust algorithms (Baruch et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2020). To bridge the gap between
045   algorithm performance and achievable accuracy in the Byzantine setting, tight lower bounds have
046   been constructed for the heterogeneous setting (Karimireddy et al., 2020; Allouah et al., 2024). Yet,
047   all these works rely on a trusted central server to coordinate training.
048   In contrast, the decentralized case has been less explored. In particular, it is still unclear how many
049   Byzantine nodes can be tolerated over a given communication network before aggregation protocols
050   fail. In fact, the network is often assumed to be fully connected (El-Mhamdi et al., 2021; Farhadkhani
051   et al., 2023), and most papers that go beyond this assumption by addressing sparse graphs either do
052   not give clear convergence rates or give weak guarantees on the asymptotic error (Peng et al., 2021;
053   Fang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023). While criteria for using SGD with generic robust decentralized
      aggregation rules have been proposed (Wu et al., 2023; Farhadkhani et al., 2023), Decentralized

068   SGD suffers from the same flaws. For instance, while (α, λ)-reduction (Farhadkhani et al., 2023)

070   only introduced and analyzed for fully-connected networks. ClippedGossip (He et al., 2022), which

072   mean estimation, is designed for sparse networks. Unfortunately, its clipping threshold requires
073

      small fraction of Byzantine failures.
074
075   Our work revisits both the ClippedGossip and NNA frameworks to solve the aforementioned
076   shortcomings. To do so, we carefully study the decentralized mean estimation problem. This
077   seemingly simple problem retains most of the difficulty of handling Byzantine nodes while allowing
078   us to derive strong convergence and robustness guarantees. We then tackle general (smooth non-
079
      convex) optimization problems through a reduction. Our contributions are summarized in Table 1,
      and the key points are the following:
080
081   1 - New upper bound on the breakdown point of robust algorithms. We show that in general,
082   Byzantine robust algorithms fail arbitrarily if the number of Byzantine neighbors per node exceeds a
083   given threshold. This threshold is expressed in terms of spectral quantities of the graph (the algebraic
084   connectivity), and recovers usual ones for fully-connected topologies.
085   2 - Unified algorithmic framework. We propose a unified algorithmic and analysis framework that
086   provides near-optimal breakdown guarantees for ClippedGossip and NNA and allows to naturally
087   introduce Gossip-NNA as well as CG+ , a more robust algorithm.
088
      3 - Stronger decentralized attacks. We propose a theoretically grounded attack, called Spectral
089   Heterogeneity, specifically designed to challenge decentralized algorithms by leveraging spectral
090   properties of the communication graph.

141
142   Similarly, the total number of honest nodes does not provide relevant information anymore, as the
143   results depend on how they are linked, i.e., the topology of the honest subgraph. Therefore, for sparse
144   topologies, we need to make an assumption about some property of the graph related to its topology,
145
      instead of simply the number honest neighbors. In the remainder of this paper, we consider spectral
      properties of the Laplacian of the honest subgraph as a relevant quantity for robustness analyses.
146

147
      subgraph, meaning that for a given graph, these properties change depending on the location of
148   Byzantine nodes. We introduce the following class of graphs to take this dependence into account.
149

153   In other words, we introduce a subset of all possible graphs, partitioning in terms of (i) their algebraic
154   connectivity, that is restricted to be larger than a minimal value µmin , and (ii) the maximal total

      3   F UNDAMENTAL LIMITS OF DECENTRALIZED COMMUNICATION SCHEMES
188
189
      In this section we provide an upper bound on the number of Byzantine neighbors that can be tolerated
190   by any algorithm running on a communication network in which the honest subgraph has a given
191   algebraic connectivity.
192
      Theorem 1. For any µmin ≥ 0, b ≥ 0, if µmin ≤ 2b, then for any H ≥ 2b, there exists G ∈ Γµmin ,b
193
      with H honest nodes such that no communication algorithm can be α-robust on G.
194
195
      Sketch of proof. The proof relies on considering a specific graph GH,b , decomposed as three cliques
196   of H/2 nodes, for an even H such that H/2 ≥ b. We choose that nodes in any of the three cliques
197   is neighbor to exactly b = k nodes in each of the two other cliques, in circular order. Finally, we
198   assume that two of the three cliques are honest, and the third one is composed of Byzantine nodes.
199
      First, GH,b ∈ Γµmin ,b . Indeed, the Laplacian matrix of the honest subgraph has an algebraic
200
      connectivity of 2b, i.e. µ2 ((GH,b )H ) = 2b, and 2b ≥ µmin by our assumption. Moreover, each honest
201
      node has exactly b-Byzantine neighbors. Second, we show that no algorithm can be α-robust on G.
202   Indeed, for any of the two honest cliques, the Byzantine clique is indistinguishable from the other
203   honest clique. As such, Byzantine nodes can send parameter values such that honest nodes cannot
204   hope to improve the global error in general. We refer the reader to Appendix C.1 for details.
205
206   It follows from Theorem 1 that, when aiming at obtaining a theoretical guarantee that quantifies the
207   robustness of the honest graph through µmin , we must have µmin > 2b. In the specific case of a fully
208   connected graph with unitary weights, where µmin = |H|, this condition boils down to requiring
209   |H| > 2|B|, which is aligned with common robustness criteria for distributed system (Lamport et al.,
210   1982; Vaidya et al., 2012; El-Mhamdi et al., 2021). As we show in Section 4, this upper bound on
211   b is tight up to a factor 2, in the sense that there exists an α-robust communication scheme over all
212
      G ∈ Γµmin ,b as soon as 4b ≤ µmin .
213   Algebraic connectivity as a robustness criterion. Note that Theorem 1 does not imply that all
214   aggregation methods fail as long as 2b ≥ µmin , but rather than since there exists a graph for which it
215   is the case, one cannot prove that an aggregation method works with b > µmin /2 Byzantine nodes
      for all graphs. Yet, one can still prove breakdown points using other graph-related quantities (which


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      might lead to tolerating b > µmin /2 Byzantine nodes for some graph architectures), or restricting the
217   graph topologies considered. This gap is standard in the optimization literature, since convergence
218   results often involve the spectral properties of the gossip matrix, whereas they do not naturally appear
219   when proving lower bounds on the number of iterations required to reach a certain accuracy. For
220   instance, the convergence rates of decentralized optimization algorithms depend on the (square root of
221   the) spectral gap of the Laplacian matrix of the communication graph (Scaman et al., 2017; Kovalev
222   et al., 2020), whereas iteration lower bounds are proven in terms of diameter. Yet, these decentralized
223   algorithms are termed as optimal as their guarantees match the lower bound on the path graph.
224   The Approximate Consensus Problem and dimension-dependent breakdown points. The design
225   of algorithms aiming at finding the average of parameters within a communication network is related
226   to the approximate consensus problem (ACP) (Dolev et al., 1986). In the standard ACP problem,
227   nodes need to converge to the same value while remaining within the convex hull of initial parameters.
228   Yet, communication-optimal methods for this problem are memory and computationally expensive
229   (Fekete, 1986). More recently, the work of LeBlanc et al. (2013) aims at designing communication
230   schemes that only use local information with computationally efficient aggregation rules. They show
231
      that standard robustness criterion of connectivity (Sundaram & Hadjicostis, 2010) does not properly
      reflect the robustness of a network for such methods. To mitigate this issue, they introduce the notion
232
      of r-robust networks. Vaidya (2014) generalizes this result by proving that the ACP cannot be solved
233
      using an algorithm with iterative communication on a system of m nodes with b Byzantine failures in
234   dimension d when m ≤ (d + 2)b + 1. This dependence on the dimension - intractable for ML usage -
235   derives from the requirement of staying within the convex hull of initial parameters for solving ACP:
236   staying in the convex hull of initial parameters is increasingly difficult as the dimension increases.
237   On the contrary, our definition of α-robustness only requires the algorithms to improve the average
238   squared distance to the target value. This relaxation of the consensus requirement allows us to prove
239   dimension independent breakdown point. Linking LeBlanc et al. (2013)’s robustness criterion with
240   algebraic connectivity is an interesting direction for future work.

433
434
      Similarly to robust aggregation methods, most proposed distributed attacks focus on centralized
435   communication networks. For instance, Baruch et al. (2019) and Xie et al. (2020) propose that all
436   Byzantine units send the same vector to the server to poison the update. However, the decentralized
437   setting offers an additional surface for attacks: all nodes have different parameters, and Byzantine
438   nodes can leverage this heterogeneity to disrupt learning even further. We show how to leverage this
439   in two different ways, and then compare robust algorithms against these attacks.
440
441   Changing the center of the attack Farhadkhani et al. (2023) implement existing centralized attacks
442   in a decentralized setting by making all Byzantine nodes declare the same attack vector to their
443   neighbors, namely, xtH + a where a is the attack direction. We make Byzantine nodes declare the
444
      parameter xti + a to node i instead. Hence, Byzantine nodes take the parameter of the honest node
      they attack as the reference point for the attack instead of the average of parameters. This reduces the
445
      likelihood of updates being clipped, but still pushes the overall system in the same direction.

528      6              C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
         This paper revisits robust averaging over sparse communication graphs. We provide an upper
         bound on the optimal breakdown point, and then introduce CG+ , a midpoint between NNA and
532
         ClippedGossip, which meets this optimal breakdown (unlike the two other). Our experiments show
533
         that NNA indeed fails before the optimal breakdown point. To obtain this result, we introduced a
534      new Spectral Heterogeneity attack that exploits the graph topology for sparse graphs. Now that we
535      have precisely quantified the impact of the topology, an interesting future direction is the precise
536      characterization of robustness when the constraint on the number of neighbors cannot be met globally,
537      but local convergence can be obtained by considering that honest nodes with too many Byzantine
538      neighbors are Byzantine themselves. Conversely, this opens up questions of which nodes should an
539      attacker corrupt to maximize its influence for a specific graph, under the light of our results.
