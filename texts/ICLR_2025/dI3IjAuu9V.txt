011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Modern retrieval systems often struggle with upgrading to new and more pow-
014            erful models due to the incompatibility of embeddings between the old and new
015            models. This necessitates a costly process known as backfilling, which involves
016
               re-computing the embeddings for a large number of data samples. In vision,
               Backward-compatible Training (BT) has been proposed to ensure that the new
017
               model aligns with the old model’s embeddings. This paper extends the concept of
018
               vision-only BT to the field of cross-modal retrieval, marking the first attempt to
019            address Cross-modal BT (XBT). Our goal is to achieve backward-compatibility
020            between Vision-Language Pretraining (VLP) models, such as CLIP, for the cross-
021            modal retrieval task. To address XBT challenges, we propose an efficient solution:
022            a projection module that maps the new model’s embeddings to those of the old
023            model. This module, pretrained solely with text data, significantly reduces the
024            number of image-text pairs required for XBT learning, and, once it is pretrained,
025            it avoids using the old model during training. Furthermore, we utilize parameter-
026            efficient training strategies that improve efficiency and preserve the off-the-shelf
027
               new model’s knowledge by avoiding any modifications. Experimental results
               on cross-modal retrieval datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of XBT and its
028
               potential to enable backfill-free upgrades when a new VLP model emerges.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      As the volume and variety of data grow exponentially in our multimedia-rich era, developing and
034
      maintaining efficient multi-modal retrieval systems becomes increasingly challenging. These systems,
035
      which provide data samples relevant to a user’s query, must handle diverse data types, from text and
036   images to audio and video. This growth puts a premium on the scalability and performance of these
037   systems, necessitating continuous advancements in algorithms and technology. Embedding-based
038   deep models for retrieval have emerged as a key solution, transforming high-dimensional data into
039   a lower-dimensional dense embedding space Rehman et al. (2012); Zhou et al. (2017); Wan et al.
040   (2014); Jang & Cho (2020); Hoe et al. (2021); Jang et al. (2022). These models capture the semantic
041   meanings of data samples, enabling the quantification of similarities for retrieval.
042   However, it is important to note that the embedding spaces generated by different deep models are not
043   inherently compatible with each other. This incompatibility restricts the reuse of an existing gallery
044   that has been embedded with an older model when a new, better-performing model is introduced.
045   Consequently, this necessitates “backfilling,” a process where the entire gallery must be rebuilt using
046   the embeddings from the new model. Such a requirement is resource-intensive and time-consuming,
047   posing a significant challenge when building retrieval systems.
048   Backward-compatible Training (BT) Shen et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2022a); Hu et al. (2022); Wang
049   et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2022b) has been developed to tackle this issue, specifically focusing
050   on image retrieval systems. The main objective of BT is to train a new model from scratch in a
051   manner that ensures its compatibility with an old model that was used to create the existing gallery.
052   A successful BT model must consequently demonstrate that retrieving from the old gallery using a
053   query embedded with the new model outperforms that using an embedding from the old model. This
      enhancement is crucial for justifying the application of BT.

072   Taking the BT problem a step further, we propose a new challenging task called Cross(X)-modal
073   Backward-compatible Training (XBT) as shown in Figure 1. Our focus here is on applying BT princi-
074
      ples within the realm of cross-modal retrieval, specifically exploring the interaction and compatibility
      between image and text embeddings of different Vision-Language Pretraining (VLP) models Radford
075
      et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022b); Jia et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022a); Singh et al. (2022).
076
077   To achieve XBT, just like the BT problem, we need to resolve the incompatibility between the
078   embeddings of the old, inferior model and the new, improved model. If we follow most prior
079   practices of training from scratch with an additional compatibility loss term, a substantial number
080
      of supervised image-text pairs would be needed. The ideal quantity would be in the hundreds of
      millions, approximating the amount utilized in the establishment of VLP models. However, accessing
081
      the original training samples is often impossible, and training on that scale is prohibitively expensive
082
      and impractical. We propose an efficient solution instead: a text-only pretrained projection module,
083   ϕ, to align a given pretrained new model’s embeddings with those of the old model.
084
085   Our focus is on using only text data to estimate the entire distribution of embeddings in the VLP space.
086
      Specifically, we train ϕ to learn the similarity between old and new text embeddings in a contrastive
      manner. By increasing the number of text samples, which is simpler than preparing image-text pairs,
087
      ϕ can approximate the oracle projection between the intra-modal distributions of both old and new
088
      embeddings for texts. Assuming that the intra-modal distribution of texts in the VLP embedding
089   space can mirror that of images, we can simply apply the same ϕ to the new image embeddings to
090   synthesize the corresponding old image embeddings.
091
092
      With these generated synthetic old image and text embeddings, which can be considered as aligned
      with the new embeddings, we aim to fine-tune the new model. In this process, far fewer supervised
093
      image-text pairs are required than in the original training dataset of the VLP. This also allows us to
094
      avoid using old model parameters during training, thereby enhancing training efficiency. In addition,
095   we incorporate parameter-efficient fine-tuning schemes Lester et al. (2021); Kim et al. (2021); Hu
096   et al. (2021) into the new model, which add small trainable parameters and do not harm any of the new
097   model’s original parameters during training. This not only accelerates the new model’s fine-tuning
098   process but also allows for an easy reversion to the original new-to-new retrieval by simply removing
099   the additional parameters.
100
      We demonstrate our approach on various cross-modal benchmarks, highlighting the effectiveness of
101   XBT in cross-modal retrieval protocols. In response to the rapid advancement of VLP models, XBT
102   offers an environmentally friendly solution by enabling backfill-free systems.

114   2      R ELATED W ORKS
115
116   Backward-compatible Training. The concept of Backward-compatible Training (BT) was first
117   introduced in the study (Shen et al. (2020)). This approach influences a new model with the learned
118
      classifier of the old model, thereby achieving backward compatibility between the old and new
      models. However, BT can degrade the original representational performance of the new model. To
119
      overcome this, Meng et al. (2021) proposed aligning class-wise centers presented by the old and
120
      new models. Another approach to achieve backward compatibility is to use an additional projection
121   to map the old embedding into the new embedding space by adding a lightweight transformation,
122   as suggested in Hu et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2020). The work of Ramanujan et al. (2022) further
123   adds an auxiliary feature in preparation for future updates, while Zhou et al. (2022) uses additional
124   dimensionality in the embedding to obtain compatibility. An online strategy that backfills the gallery
125   on the fly is explored in Zhang et al. (2022a), and Yan et al. (2021) addresses the model regression
126   problem. Despite this progress, the scenario considering cross-modal retrieval between image and
127   text, which has many real-world applications, remains unexplored. Our study on XBT in this paper
128   aims to fill this gap.
129   Vision-Language Continual and Transfer Learning The fields of continual learning Aljundi et al.
130   (2017); Chaudhry et al. (2019); De Lange et al. (2021) and transfer learning Lu et al. (2015); Zhuang
131   et al. (2020) bear similarities to backward-compatible representation learning, as all aim to update
132   an existing model to boost performance. In the realm of multi-modal representation learning for
133   cross-modal retrieval, continual learning approaches like Wang et al. (2021) propose methods to
134   prevent catastrophic forgetting across different modalities. Transfer learning approaches like Zhen
135
      et al. (2020) suggest strategies for transferring knowledge from previously labeled categories (source
      modality) to new, unlabeled categories (target modality). However, our proposed XBT stands apart
136
      from these methods as it specifically tackles the challenge of maintaining backward compatibility
137
      between old and new models. This unique attribute makes XBT ideally suited for backfill-free
138   retrieval scenarios, where the embeddings of the old model remain unchanged, yet we can still
139   leverage the enhanced performance of the new model.

      3.2   T EXT- ONLY P RETRAINING
203
204   The vast and diverse image-text pairs used to build VLP models significantly enhance their ability
205   to connect semantically similar visual content and natural language Chen et al. (2023). However,
206   this creates a complex embedding distribution that is difficult to predict and understand, thereby
207   complicating the XBT process. A straightforward solution is to use a large number of supervised
208   image-text pairs, similar to the approach used when building VLP models from scratch, to estimate
209   the entire distribution of image and text embeddings during XBT.
210   However, acquiring a sufficient number of accurate supervised pairs is extremely costly. To be far
211   more efficient, we employ a small sized projection module, ϕ, and train it exclusively with text
212   samples, as shown in Figure 2. We hypothesize that the distribution of text embeddings in VLP
213   models, which is determined by their semantic similarity, is similarly mirrored in the distribution of
214   their corresponding matched images. With this in mind, we aim to train ϕ to cover the broad spectrum
215   of the text embedding distribution between the new and old VLP models, making embeddings from
      the same text sample similar and others dissimilar in a contrastive way:

264   Efficient Training. The efficiency of XBT can be attributed to two design choices that avoid: (1) any
265   dependence on the old model when conducting XBT, and (2) updating the new VLP model off the
266
      shelf. We have already seen how ϕ can effectively achieve (1). (2) is simply facilitated by applying
      small-sized additional parameters, namely Soft prompt Lester et al. (2021) and LoRAHu et al. (2021).
267
268   To be more specific, we incorporate the concept of soft prompt tuning, as outlined in Lester et al.
                                                                                  I
269   (2021); Jia et al. (2022). Soft prompting is applied to the vision encoder Enew by adding trainable
      prompts as input, along with image patch tokens. We exclude soft prompt tuning on the text side, as

      we use the text-only pretrained module ϕ, which requires maintaining the original distribution of text
271   embeddings. We adopt the LoRA strategy Hu et al. (2021) for the new VLP model’s encoders Enew     I
272          T
      and Enew to avoid modifying original parameters. These two factors, in conjunction with ϕ, offer an
273   on-off solution: we can retrieve old samples using additional parameters, and we can easily revert to
274   the original model by removing these parameters for new-to-new retrieval.
275
      Further, regarding ϕ during XBT (Sec. 3.3), we only fine-tune the parameters of layer normalization.
276

278
279   In the end, our XBT framework offers a parameter-efficient solution that enables rapid convergence
280
      with fewer training iterations (a single epoch is sufficient); maintains the new model’s power; and
      requires far fewer supervised training samples than the scale of VLP model pretraining (approximately
281
      1% of the level required to train CLIP Radford et al. (2021) from scratch, which uses a 400M dataset,
282
      and 0.2% of the level required for LAION-2B based CLIP models Schuhmann et al. (2022), which
283   utilize a 2B dataset). All these help to avoid any full-scale retraining of the new VLP models, paving
284   the way for XBT to simply use new VLP models off-the-shelf as mentioned.

295   4.1       S ETUP
296
      Model Training. Our XBT process involves two distinct training stages: text-only pretraining
297
      (See Sec. 3.2) and image-text supervised training (See Sec. 3.3). For the text-only pretraining
298   stage, we utilize the text samples from a subset of the 115M filtered web-collected image-text paired
299   dataset from BLIP Li et al. (2022a), comprising around 67M available pairs (58.2% of the total).
300   We construct a text corpus DT in Eqn. 3 using these text samples. For the subsequent image-text
301   supervised training stage, we use a smaller subset of 4M image-text pairs from the same dataset to
302   construct a supervised dataset D in Eqn. 5. It is important to note that these subsets are significantly
303   smaller than the dataset scale (400M, 2B or more) used to build CLIP models Radford et al. (2021);
304   Schuhmann et al. (2022).
305   We utilize 8-A100-80GB GPUs to train and evaluate the models. For the text-only pretraining stage
306   in Sec. 3.2, the batch size is set to 8,192 (1,024 batch per GPU) and during this stage, the entire
307   set of trainable parameters of ϕ are trained while both VLP text encoders are fixed. Moving on
308   to the image-text supervised training in Sec. 3.3, the batch size is reduced to 1,024 (128 batch
309   per GPU), and in this stage, layer normalization is set to be the only trainable component for all
310   VLP image and text encoders with ϕ. Temperature hyper-parameters τpre , τX , and τN are fixed
311   at 2.6592. We employ the AdamW optimizer Loshchilov & Hutter (2017) with a fixed learning
312
      rate of 1e-4 and a weight decay of 0.01. For soft prompts, we use 10 prompts and apply 100 times
      larger learning rate, 1e-2. The training iteration is determined by the dataset size, and the entire
313
      pipeline is trained for a single epoch. For image augmentation, we begin with a random resized
314
      crop, adjusting the image scale between 0.5 and 1.0. Additionally, we apply a random horizontal
315   flip and make random adjustments to the image’s contrast, brightness, and sharpness. To incorporate
316   different perspectives and angles, we modify the image’s translation and rotation. The pretrained
317   weights provided by HuggingFace1 Wolf et al. (2020) are applied to baseline VLP models. We em-
318   ploy as: openai/clip-vit-base-patch32, openai/clip-vit-larget-patch14,
319   laion/CLIP-ViT-H-14-laion2B-s32B-b79K.

      (2021), based on a Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017) backbone, as our baseline VLP models. The
361
      models, listed in ascending order of scale and performance, are: CLIP-ViT-B32, CLIP-ViT-L14,
362   and CLIP-ViT-H14. For simplicity, we will refer to them as B32, L14, and H14, respectively.
363   Notably, while B32 and L14 are trained with the same dataset at 400M scale, H14 is trained with
364   a larger dataset at 2B scale. We apply additional LoRA Hu et al. (2021) parameters for each new
365   model encoder configured as follows: LoRAα = 16, rank = 16, and dropout = 0.1. The proposed
366   projection module ϕ consists of three Linear layers with layer normalization (LN) Ba et al. (2016) and
367   the GELU non-linearity function Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016). Detailed architecture is as follows:
368   Linear − LN − GELU − Linear − LN − GELU − Linear. Dropout is not applied as it was found
369   to degrade performance empirically. The intermediate hidden dimension of the Linear layer is set to
370
      be four times the dimensionality of the output embedding.

      against three baselines: Full-tune, LoRA-only, and Base, which we elaborate on below.
394
395   Naïve solution - Direct backward contrastive learning: The pretrained ϕ in Eqn. 3, which maps the
396   new embeddings to the old via text based contrastive learning, constitutes a major contribution of
397   XBT. We therefore would like to compare with a solution that does not utilize such a pretrained ϕ.
                                                                                   I         T
398   A straightforward solution for this is to fine-tune the new VLP encoders (Enew   and Enew  ) to be
399
      compatible with the old ones by minimizing the following loss:
400
401                                        LDirect = Ex,t∼D [Lc (v̄new , wold ; τN )
                                                                                                                                (6)
402                                                       +Lc (w̄new , vold ; τN )]
403
404   where Lc (·, ·) is the same contrastive loss used in Eqn. 5, and τN is a temperature. Here, we apply a
405   randomly initialized ϕ with the same configuration as XBT for projection, as per Eqn. 4, to generate
406   cross-modal backward compatible embeddings, w̄ and v̄.
407   Different training options: Based on Eqn. 6, we consider multiple training setups: (1) full fine-tuning
408   of all trainable components (Full-tune), (2) tuning LoRA parameters-only (LoRA-only), and (3)
409   starting from LoRA-only, adding an extra learnable prompt (Base). Models produced by Base would
                                                                                    I       T
410   therefore be akin to XBT without the pretrained ϕ. For these, we train the Enew    , Enew and ϕ using
411   LDirect with 4M image-text pairs. Note that, XBT only trains layer-normalization layers including
412   pretrained ϕ, however, Full-tune, LoRA-only and Base setups are training entire parameters of ϕ since
413   it is not trained before. The results of these baselines are presented in Table 1, tested with nocaps
414
      dataset. The performance of the original VLP models is also reported for a clear comparison. We
415    highlight our XBT method.
416
      In the context of cross-modal backward compatibility, where recall scores of w̄new /vold and
417   v̄new /wold should be higher than those of w/v and v/w of old model, respectively, Full-tune
418   significantly underperforms compared to the old VLP’s. This indicates that full fine-tuning is not
419   an appropriate solution. While both LoRA-only and Base improve performance over Full-tune,
420   they still fall short of the old VLP’s performance and fail to meet the criterion. In contrast, XBT
421   significantly outperforms these baselines and even surpasses the old VLP’s performance, satisfying
422   the cross-modal backward compatibility for all recall metrics (see Eqn. 2).
423   In terms of maintaining new VLP’s performance, where recall scores of w̄new /vnew and v̄new /wnew
424   could be similar to those of w/v and v/w of new model, respectively, all baselines show decent
425   performance in w̄new /vnew case but only XBT achieves better performance than new VLP model
426   in v̄new /wnew case. These results support that XBT not only maintains the performance of the new
427   VLP model but also enhances it in certain cases. The superior performance of XBT in both cases
428   underscores its promise as a robust solution for cross-modal retrieval tasks.

525
526   Potential Broader Impact and Limitation. This paper highlights our efforts to enhance the field of
527   Machine Learning, particularly in the area of multi-modal embedding-based representation learning.
528   Although our work may have societal implications, we do not believe there are any that require
529
      specific emphasis in this context. A potential limitation of the XBT system is that, despite the efficient
      learning approach reducing the need for image-caption pairs, its performance may still be limited by
530
      the quality, diversity, and representational richness of the data during training.
531
532
      Conclusion. In this paper, we introduced Cross-modal Backward-compatible Training (XBT),
533
      a novel task for cross-modal retrieval that focuses on the compatibility between image and text
534   embeddings of different Vision-Language Pretraining (VLP) models. We proposed an efficient
535   solution using a text-only pretrained projection module, ϕ, to align the new model’s embeddings
536   with those of the old model, thereby enhancing training efficiency. By integrating parameter-efficient
537   training schemes into the XBT framework, we were able to accelerate the model’s training while
538   preserving the original VLP’s zero-shot capabilities. Our approach, demonstrated on various cross-
539   modal benchmarks, effectively builds cross-modal retrieval systems without backfilling, offering an
      efficient and environmentally friendly solution in response to the VLP improvements.
