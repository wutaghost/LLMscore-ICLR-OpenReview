026                                                    A BSTRACT
027
028            3D semantic field learning is crucial for applications like autonomous navigation,
029            AR/VR, and robotics, where accurate comprehension of 3D scenes from limited
030            viewpoints is essential. Existing methods struggle under sparse view conditions,
031            relying on inefficient per-scene multi-view optimizations, which are impractical
032            for many real-world tasks. To address this, we propose SparseLGS, a feed-forward
033            method for constructing 3D semantic fields from sparse viewpoints, allowing di-
034            rect inference of 3DGS-based scenes. By ensuring consistent SAM segmentations
035
               through video tracking and using low-dimensional indexing for high-dimensional
               CLIP features, SparseLGS efficiently embeds language information in 3D space,
036
               offering a robust solution for accurate 3D scene understanding under sparse view
037
               conditions. In experiments on two-view sparse 3D object querying and segmenta-
038            tion in the LERF and 3D-OVS datasets, SparseLGS outperforms existing methods
039            in chosen IoU, Localization Accuracy, and mIoU. Moreover, our model achieves
040            scene inference in under 30 seconds and open-vocabulary querying in just 0.011
041            seconds per query.

044   1   I NTRODUCTION
045
046   In many real-world applications, such as autonomous navigation, AR, VR, and robotics, under-
047   standing and interacting with 3D environments using natural language is essential. In systems like
048   autonomous driving, where the number of onboard cameras is limited, efficient scene inference is
049   still required. Similarly, AR/VR applications often rely on limited perspectives to support accu-
050
      rate 3D scene understanding. Under these sparse-view conditions, achieving fast and accurate 3D
      semantic field learning becomes both critical and challenging.
051
052   While methods like LERF (Kerr et al., 2023) and LangSplat (Qin et al., 2024) have made signif-
053   icant advances in 3D language scene learning, they rely heavily on dense multi-view information,
      which makes them unsuitable for scenarios with limited viewpoints. When only a few views are


                                                                 1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      available, it becomes challenging to reconstruct detailed 3D geometry and maintain consistent lan-
055   guage information across those views. This hinders the system’s ability to accurately perform 3D
056   language field learning, as the semantic information cannot be reliably integrated across different
057   perspectives. Additionally, existing methods often require per-scene optimization, which is not only
058   time-consuming but impractical for real-world applications, such as robotics, autonomous naviga-
059   tion, or industrial monitoring, where rapid inference is necessary. In such cases, the inability to
060   perform direct inference on 3D scenes limits their utility in providing efficient semantic understand-
061   ing of the environment.
062   To overcome these limitations and enable the broader application of semantic fields in real-world
063   scenarios, we focus on improving inference speed and open-vocabulary query quality through three
064   key innovations. First, inspired by recent feed-forward approaches, we can adopt a feed-forward
065   framework that allows for direct inference without the need for per-scene optimization after model
066   convergence, greatly reducing time costs. Additionally, We can leverage 3D Gaussian Splatting
067   (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) for its fast and photorealistic rendering to accelerate the rendering pro-
068   cess in our model. Moreover, to enable open-vocabulary query, related works learn high-dimensional
069
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) features or use additional networks to reduce the dimensionality of these
      features, which often hampers inference speed. We can seek more efficient feature embedding meth-
070
      ods to overcome this limitation and improve query performance.
071
072   Based on these insights, we present the first feed-forward method for constructing semantic fields
073   from sparse viewpoints. Our model takes sparse-view RGB images as input and utilizes two
074   branches: one for predicting base Gaussian parameters and another for semantic parameters, en-
075
      abling fast construction of 3DGS-based semantic fields. For base Gaussian parameters, we build on
      existing 3DGS feed-forward methods like (Charatan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024), while a ded-
076
      icated module handles feature extraction and mapping for semantic predictions. After predicting
077
      both sets of parameters, we render 3D semantic features using the splatting technique. Then we in-
078   troduce a multi-view language memory bank with low-dimensional semantic label IDs, which also
079   facilitates efficient matching between rendered semantic features and query prompts. Additionally,
080   we address inconsistent multi-view segmentation from the SAM model by applying video object
081   tracking techniques. In sparse-view experiments on the LERF and 3D-OVS datasets, our method
082   achieves sharp 3D object segmentation boundaries and outperforms existing approaches in chosen
083   IoU, Localization Accuracy, and mIoU. At a resolution of 416 × 576, our method delivers scene
084   inference in around 25 seconds and query response times of 0.011 seconds, significantly faster than
085   current methods.
086   In summary, our contributions are:
087
088          • To our knowledge, we are the first to propose a feed-forward method for constructing 3D
089            semantic fields from sparse viewpoints. Our approach generates 3D semantic fields from
090            only two-view images, enabling fast inference with strong generalization capabilities.
091          • We introduce a multi-view language memory bank that links semantic masks to natural lan-
092            guage information, addressing the limitations of existing methods in sparse-view scenarios
093            and significantly accelerating open-vocabulary querying.
094          • Our method surpasses state-of-the-art approaches in sparse-view 3D object localization and
095            segmentation tasks, achieving notable improvements in chosen IoU, Localization Accu-
096            racy, and mIoU, while providing 10× faster scene inference and 7× faster open-vocabulary
097            querying than LangSplat.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
100
101
      NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS). In recent years, Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)
102   (Mildenhall et al., 2021) and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) have made signifi-
103   cant advancements in 3D scene modeling and rendering. NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) synthesizes
104   photorealistic scenes through volumetric rendering and multi-view consistency backpropagation,
105   but its reliance on ray marching results in slow rendering speeds. Subsequent research, such as mip-
106   NeRF (Barron et al., 2021) and TensoRF (Chen et al., 2022), improved its rendering efficiency and
107   multi-resolution representation. Unlike NeRF, 3DGS employs explicit 3D Gaussian distributions
      instead of MLPs, achieving real-time and efficient rendering through adaptive density control and


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tile-based rasterization. Tang et al. (2023), Liu et al. (2024) demonstrated the potential of 3DGS
109   in generative tasks. Methods like (Yang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
110   2023; He et al., 2024) enhanced Gaussians’ flexibility in handling dynamic and complex scenes by
111   learning 3D Gaussians in canonical space combined with deformation fields. Zhang et al. (2024)
112   introduces separated intrinsic and dynamic appearance feature to capture the unchanged scene ap-
113   pearance along with dynamic variation like illumination and weather. Additionally, Qin et al. (2024);
114   Wu et al. (2024b); Shi et al. (2024) incorporate language information into 3DGS scene representa-
115   tion, enabling high-quality open-vocabulary 3D scene queries.
116   Sparse View Reconstruction. Although techniques of NeRF and 3DGS perform well in dense-
117   view scenarios, they face challenges in sparse-view settings, particularly in maintaining high-quality
118   scene generation under sparse data conditions, necessitating further optimizations to improve gen-
119   eration quality and efficiency. Methods like (Yu et al., 2021; Charatan et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
120   2024) use a feed-forward manner to learn the 3D scene, which take images as input and predict
121   the parameters of the 3D representation.Specifically, PixelNeRF (Yu et al., 2021) uses convolutional
122   neural networks to extract features from input contexts, addressing the challenges of low-quality
123
      reconstructions caused by sparse views. PixelSplat leverages epipolar transformers to extract scene
      features, while MVSplat uses cost volumes to improve scene feature extraction. FreeNeRF further
124
      improves reconstruction by employing frequency and density regularization strategies to reduce ar-
125
      tifacts from insufficient inputs, without additional computational costs. To mitigate overfitting in
126   3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) models under sparse views, approaches like FSGS (Zhu et al., 2023) and
127   SparseGS (Xiong et al., 2023) introduce depth estimators to regularize the optimization process.
128   FreeSplat (Wang et al., 2024) builds on this by incorporating not only cost volume construction
129   but also multi-scale feature aggregation, allowing accurate 3D Gaussian distribution localization
130   and supporting free-viewpoint synthesis. These advancements demonstrate the growing potential
131   of sparse-view reconstruction technologies. Learning from the 3DGS-based generative frameworks
132   (Charatan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), SparseLGS adopts a similar approach
133   to infer high-quality 3DGS semantic scenes in sparse-view semantic learning tasks.
134   3D Semantic Field. Integrating semantic information into 3D scenes is crucial for downstream
135   fields such as robotics, AR, and VR. Zhi et al. (2021) first introduced semantic layers in NeRF, lay-
136   ing the foundation for deeper scene understanding. Subsequent studies, such as LERF (Kerr et al.,
137   2023), ISRF (Goel et al., 2023), and DFF (Yen-Chen et al., 2022), have incorporated more refined
138   semantic segmentation techniques, significantly improving the level of detail in 3D visual repre-
139   sentations. Furthermore, 3DGS technology has also made breakthroughs in semantic tasks. The
140
      foundational work of Kerbl et al. (2023) and the subsequent developments of EgoLifter (Gu et al.,
      2024), SAGA (Cen et al., 2023), Gaussian Grouping (Ye et al., 2023), and Gaga (Lyu et al., 2024)
141
      have greatly optimized the application of 3DGS in semantic segmentation. LangSplat, LeGaussian,
142
      OpenGaussian and FastLGS (Ji et al., 2024) further explored the integration of language informa-
143   tion into 3DGS, deepening semantic interpretation in 3D scene modeling. However, these advanced
144   techniques often exhibit limitations in handling sparse view inputs, especially when viewpoints are
145   highly limited, making it difficult to effectively manage semantic consistency between views. The
146   lack of sufficient RGB information leads to suboptimal reconstruction quality of both 3D scenes and
147   their semantic content, highlighting the urgent need for efficient and accurate 3D semantic learning
148   under sparse views.

      Mask Association. Directly using the multi-view segmentation results inferred by the SAM model
239
      as input for our model can lead to confusion, as the inconsistent 2D segmentations across views
240   interfere with the spatial mapping of semantic labels, as shown in the example from the appendix
241   (Figure 10). Existing methods mitigate this issue by using dense viewpoint information and multi-
242   scale segmentation of SAM results. However, when reduced to the extreme sparse scenario, espe-
243   cially when only two views are available, this multi-scale approach to SAM segmentation becomes
244   ineffective. Research on Gaussian scene segmentation (Ye et al., 2023; Dou et al., 2024) treats multi-
245   view images as consecutive frames in a video and applies a video object tracking technique (Cheng
246   et al., 2023) to unify the multi-view 2D segmentation masks inferred by SAM, achieving relatively
247   good results. We adopted this method to enforce multi-view consistency on SAM’s inference results.
248
      However, when only two sparse-view images are directly processed by the video tracking model,
      the lack of sufficient reference views often result in poor segmentation consistency. To ensure more
249
      stable processing results for sparse-view image inputs, we replicated the input images five times to
250
      create a 10-frame sequence and processed it using the model, resulting in a set of 10 segmentation
251   images with matched indices: {T11 , T12 , . . . , T15 , T21 , T22 , . . . , T25 }, where Tij represents the j-th
252   frame segmentation corresponding to the i-th view. Each pixel in the segmentation images is la-
253   beled with the corresponding object index. For each pixel v in the i-th view, we choose the category
254   through a voting algorithm:

258   and W represent the height and width of the segmentation image, respectively.

260   Construction of Language Memory Bank. Based on the multi-view consistent segmentation ob-
261
      tained, we use CLIP to encode the corresponding regions of the RGB images, resulting in high-
      dimensional language features Li1 and Li2 (512 dimensions in our experiments) for the two views.
262
      If the object does not exist in a particular view, the corresponding language feature is represented by
263
      a zero vector. Directly applying these high-dimensional language features to 3DGS would lead to a
264   memory overflow in 3DGS. Qin et al. (2024) addresses this issue by using a per-scene autoencoder
265   to compress the high-dimensional features, which are later decoded during querying. However, this
266   approach is not suitable for our method, as our goal is to infer the semantic field for new scenes
267   without retraining. A simple autoencoder cannot effectively fit the wide range of language infor-
268   mation in the real world. Additionally, the differences in language information between the two
269   views might cause the inferred language features to neutralize, making it impossible to recover the
      corresponding semantics. Therefore, this dimensionality reduction method is not applicable.

319   mask identifiers during training.

321   further retraining on datasets such as LERF (Kerr et al., 2023) and 3D-OVS (Liu et al., 2023).
322   Instead, we directly input two images and their camera poses from these datasets, using our model

467   4.5      A BLATION S TUDY
468
469   Ablation study on LERF dataset. The results of the ablation study on the LERF dataset (Table
470   5) show a significant improvement in Chosen IoU (object region accuracy) and Localization Accu-
471
      racy as model components are gradually introduced. Without the feed-forward Gaussian inference
      model, mask association (MA), or Multi-View Language Memory Bank (MV-LMB)—essentially
472
      replicating LangSplat—the model achieves 27.09% Chosen IoU and 51.87% Localization Accu-
473
      racy. However, when only the feed-forward model is introduced, both metrics drop sharply to
474   5.06% and 9.11%, indicating the detrimental effect of inconsistent 2D semantic feature supervi-
475   sion across views. Adding mask association (MA) further decreases performance (3.06% Chosen
476   IoU and 5.05% Localization Accuracy), showing that mask association alone is insufficient under
477   sparse views. Finally, introducing the MV-LMB boosts Chosen IoU to 41.50% and Localization
478   Accuracy to 70.80%, demonstrating the significant role of consistent multi-view language features
479   in improving inference and query performance.
480   Comparison between our method and the 2D query method Our approach renders language-
481   encoded feature maps from novel viewpoints and compares them with the Multi-View Language
482   Memory Bank, while the 2D method renders RGB images, segments them with SAM, and encodes
483   regions using CLIP for comparison with query text. Table 6 shows the LERF dataset results, com-
484   paring Chosen IoU, Localization Accuracy, and Query Speed. Both methods perform similarly in
485   terms of IoU and accuracy, but our method stands out with near real-time querying, requiring only
      0.011 seconds per query, whereas the 2D method takes up to 18 seconds for a single query.

      5     L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
518
519
      Conclusion. We present SparseLGS, a feed-forward method for constructing 3D semantic fields
      from sparse viewpoints. By leveraging a feed-forward framework for base Gaussians and semantic
520
      parameters prediction, along with a multi-view language memory bank, our approach significantly
521
      enhances the reconstruction quality and inference speed in sparse-view scenarios.
522
523   Limitations and Prospects. Our model relies on pre-trained CLIP and SAM models, which limits
524   real-time performance. Moving forward, two key directions are: improving the inference speed
525
      of these pre-trained models and developing an integrated approach to directly infer 3D semantic
      fields from multi-view RGB images, eliminating the need for external models and streamlining the
526
      process.
