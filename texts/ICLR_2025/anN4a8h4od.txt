009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            How can we retrieve search results that are both semantically relevant and satisfy
012            certain filter criteria? Modern day semantic search engines are increasingly reliant
013
               on vector-based search, yet the ability to restrict vector search to a fixed set of filter
               criteria remains an interesting problem with no known satisfactory solution. In this
014
               note, we leverage the rich emergent structure of vector embeddings of pre-trained
015
               search transformers to offer a simple solution. Our method involves learning, for
016            each filter, a vector direction in the space of vector embeddings, and adding it to the
017            query vector at run-time to perform a search constrained by that filter criteria. Our
018            technique is broadly applicable to any finite set of semantically meaningful filters,
019            compute-efficient in that it does not require modifying or rebuilding an existing k-
020            NN index over document vector embeddings, lightweight in that it adds negligible
021            latency, and widely compatible in that it can be utilized with any transformer
022            model and k-NN algorithm. We also establish, subject to mild assumptions, an
023            upper bound on the probability that our method errantly retrieves irrelevant results,
024
               and reveal new empirical insights about the geometry of transformer embeddings.
               In experiments, we find that our method, on average, yields more than a 21%
025
               boost over the baseline (measured in terms of nDCG@10) across three different
026
               transformer models and datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Search is an age-old problem in the field of information retrieval. It has been revolutionized by
031   modern machine learning systems, particularly by pre-trained transformer models Nogueira & Cho
032   (2020); Devlin et al. (2018). When these models are fine-tuned for search—hereafter referred to
033
      as search transformers—they demonstrate state-of-the-art performance across a wide variety of
      information retrieval tasks, domains, and data modalities Yates et al. (2021). The natural language
034
      understanding capabilities of these search transformers have enabled users to pose queries in natural
035
      language and retrieve semantically relevant results.
036
037   A query for shoes in an e-commerce corpus retrieves not only product articles about shoes but also
038   those on boots, loafers, sneakers, etc. In many situations, this is the intended behavior. However,
039
      semantic relevance is not the sole criterion for obtaining relevant results. Often, users seek results
      that are semantically relevant and meet specific keyword criteria. For example, a user might want to
040
      find shoes from a specific brand, such as Nike. In this case, the ideal results would be semantically
041
      relevant to shoes, such as shoe and sneakers, while exactly matching the brand Nike. In this paper,
042   we study how one can retrieve results that simultaneously exhibit semantic relevance while satisfying
043   certain filter criteria.
044
045
      The fundamental principle of search transformers is to generate a vector embedding for every item
      in the corpus, henceforth referred to as documents, in a way that ensures that vector embeddings
046
      of relevant documents are positioned closer to each other in the vector space and vice versa. The
047
      resulting collection of document vectors forms the universe over which searches are conducted. At
048   the time of a query, the query q is converted into a vector, and the nearest neighbors of the query
049   vector ⃗vq are retrieved as the relevant results. The nearest neighbor search is executed either using
050   an exact search or, more commonly, an approximate k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN) method such as
051   Hierarchical Navigable Small Worlds (HNSW) Malkov & Yashunin (2020) or Product Quantization
052   (PQ) Jégou et al. (2011). Both approximate methods involve a computationally intensive step of
053   building an index, which, while substantially increasing the initial setup time, significantly reduces
      downstream latency during query retrieval.

      Our method relies on the intuition that there exist emergent directions in the space of vector embed-
      dings that correspond to different filters such as Nike or Sony, and that these directions can be found
073
      using a simple algorithm that employs linear probes with a limited amount of un-supervised data. At
074
      run-time, a query q with a filter f is vectorized into a simple linear combination, such as ⃗vq+f + λ⃗νf ,
075   before being submitted to k-NN search. Here λ is a scalar, ⃗vq+f refers to the model embedding of the
076   concatenated query and filter string, and ⃗νf corresponds to the learned filter direction. Experimentally,
077   this simple protocol achieves an improvement over the baseline of almost 21% across different
078   datasets and models, as measured in terms of nDCG@10 and Recall@10.
079
080   We discover that other linear combinations also yield comparable performance. For illustration and
081   theoretical analysis, we work with ⃗vq + λ⃗νf , while for our experiments, we use ⃗vq+f + λ⃗νf . The
082
      former greatly simplifies the illustration and theoretical analysis, while the latter allows for a more
      intuitive comparison with the experimental baseline. As shown in Figure 1, our method leverages the
083
      continuum of semantically meaningful linear combinations ⃗vq + λ⃗νf within transformer embeddings
084
      as we vary λ. Specifically, using image embeddings from clip with Tiny Imagenet Le & Yang
085   (2015), we find that increasing λ spans concepts from an image of a sweater (Poncho) to that of a red
086   object (red sports car). For an appropriate value of λ, our method retrieves the relevant results. Thus,
087   λ serves as a hyperparameter in our approach, which we fix for a given dataset and model.
088
089   We also derive some theoretical results about our method and establish bounds on the probability
090   of retrieving irrelevant results subject to a particular generative model with mild assumptions. We
091
      motivate these underlying assumptions with new insights into the geometry of search transformers,
      specifically regarding the isotropy of their output representations. Contrary to previous findings,
092
      our research indicates that search transformers do not exhibit the kind of anisotropy often assumed
093
      to indicate degenerate representations. For example, our findings suggest that for most search
094   transformers, vector embeddings of random, unrelated documents are nearly orthogonal to one another,
095   i.e., are not anisotropic, and they do not possess “rogue” dimensions that could disproportionately
096   influence the nearest neighbor distance calculation.
097
098   Query latency is a critical metric in modern search applications; thus, the proposed method should
099   not be computationally heavy and must not add considerable latency during runtime. Additionally, a
100
      solution that necessitates rebuilding of the k-nearest neighbors index is impractical due to the vast size
      of contemporary search corpora. Our method is computationally efficient, adds negligible latency,
101
      and does not require the index to be rebuilt. It can be seamlessly integrated with any existing search
102
      transformer without the need for fine-tuning. However, since our approach relies on the presence of
103   emergent directions leverageable by search transformers, it is effective only for filters with semantic
104   relevance. Concretely, the filters cannot be arbitrary alphanumeric strings, such as serial number, size
105   or price. To summarize, our contributions include
106
107         1. A simple, lightweight, and low latency algorithm for semantic search with filters, indepen-
               dent of both the transformer model and the underlying k-NN search algorithm.

      2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED WORK
115   One of the oldest and most widely used techniques for information retrieval is BM25 Robertson
116   & Zaragoza (2009). It is based on keyword matching and frequency statistics, providing a robust,
117   generalizable search solution for many use cases. However, keyword-based search lacks the semantic
118   understanding of a pre-trained search transformer, and thus it cannot be used to retrieve semantically
119   relevant results with or without a filter.
120   Another common solution involves post-filtering the results using a two-stage approach. The first
121   stage involves retrieval with a large k using an approximate k-NN algorithm such as HNSW, while
122   the second stage subselects those results that satisfy the filter criteria using a pre-built index or
123   BM25. These two-stage retrieval systems are limited by the Recall@k of the first stage retriever. We
124   demonstrate that our method significantly improves recall during the first stage retrieval of HNSW
125   for semantic search with filters. Other k-NN algorithms, such as Filtered-DiskANN Gollapudi et al.
126
      (2023) and FAISS filtering Johnson et al. (2019), either necessitate rebuilding the document index,
      require constructing new indices that independently encapsulate filter information, or increase query
127
      latency by dynamically checking filter criteria while searching through the vectors. That being said,
128
      our method can still be combined with these algorithms, potentially serving as a more efficient entry
129   point in the search index.
130
131   To our knowledge, the work most closely aligning with our research has been independently explored
132
      within the computer vision community under the concept of compositional learning Neculai et al.
      (2022); Nagarajan & Grauman (2018); Vo et al. (2018); Misra et al. (2017). The task in this domain
133
      involves retrieving images with specific attributes, such as dogs that are also large or brown. However,
134
      as far as we are aware, these methods are not model-agnostic and/or necessitate joint pre-training.
135
136   On the other hand, the emergence of semantically meaningful directions in deep neural networks has
137   been extensively studied, including the seminal work on word vectors by Mikolov et al. (2013). Recent
138
      advancements in mechanistic interpretability have further uncovered deep connections between feature
      representations (i.e., filters) and neuron activations (i.e., vector directions) Bricken et al. (2023);
139
      Cammarata et al. (2020). Previous studies have employed linear probes to uncover syntax trees and
140
      linguistic features within transformer models Hewitt & Manning (2019); Coenen et al. (2019). Our
141   method is inspired by these investigations, aiming to leverage the geometry of these models using
142   linear probes for the downstream task of semantic search with filters.

144   3    M ETHOD
145
      Problem The problem we study is as follows. We are given a corpus of n documents D, their
146
      corresponding filters, and corresponding embedding vectors VD as generated by a transformer model.
147
      For a given corpus, the collection of filter sets {F1 , F2 , · · · } is fixed and we denote it as F. For each
148   filter set Fa ∈ F, every document di ∈ D possesses some filter fai ∈ Fa . A document is said to
149   possess a filter if it satisfies that particular filter criteria. The task, for a given query q with filter f in
150   some Fa , is to retrieve k documents that are relevant to q and satisfy f .
151
      For example, if the corpus D represents a shoe catalog, the collection F of filter sets could be
152
      {brand, color, size, ...}, where the filter set F1 for brand may include {Nike, Sony, Apple, ...}. Given
153
      a query, q = athletic shoe with filter f = Nike, we wish to find k items from the catalog semantically
154   matching the query athletic shoe and exactly matching the filter Nike.
155
156   We assume that |Fa | ≪ |D| for all a, e.g., the number of distinct brands is much smaller than the size
157
      of the corpus. Additionally, we assume that each document possesses a unique filter for a given filter
      set, i.e., a document corresponding to an athletic shoe cannot possess both Nike and Puma as filters
158
      (but can possess Nike and purple, since they belong to different filter sets).
159
160   Intuition To the best of our knowledge, efficiently filtering a k-NN vector search remains an open
161   combinatorial geometry problem, even for the special case of retrieving documents containing a
      specific string or keyword. One natural approach toward filtered k-NN search would be to limit


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      the search solely to the subregion of vectors meeting the filter criteria. For an arbitrary distribution
163   of vectors, this region could be highly non-linear, disconnected, and challenging to find. The core
164   premise of our method is the observation that vector embeddings generated by transformers possess
165   considerable linear structure, for reasons not yet fully understood, allowing for the identification of
166   semantically meaningful directions.
167
      To be precise, we propose that every semantically meaningful filter can be associated with a cor-
168
      responding direction, and that the documents matching these filters are those whose embeddings
169
      have a high dot product with this direction. We hypothesize that these directions exist either be-
170   cause a sufficiently powerful language model can implicitly infer a connection between documents
171   and their filters (for example, drawing a connection between Air Jordans and the filter Nike), or
172   because the document explicitly mentions the filter in its description (Volkswagen Beetle and the filter
173   Volkswagen), and the model has contextualized it within the document’s context.
174
175

199   2022). See Appendix E for more discussion.

250   Algorithm We now describe a pair of algorithms. Algorithm 1, run independently for each filter
251   set Fa ∈ F, attempts to learn a collection of linear probes (i.e. a collection of directions in the space)
252   that best predicts which filter f in Fa , if any, applies to a given document. To turn this classification
253   problem into a geometric one, we use a one-hot encoding to identify each filter with a basis direction
254   in R|Fa | , and learn a linear mapping from these one-hot encoded filter vectors into the space of
255   document vectors. This mapping is constructed to maximize the inner product between the learned
256   filter directions and the embedded documents matching the corresponding filter. The mapping of
257
      these vectors, which define the columns of the learned matrix RFa , will form the set of linear probes
      we use throughout the remainder of the paper.

      The quality of the results coming out of our k-NN search depends on the number of irrelevant versus
      relevant search results we return among the top k nearest vectors. Intuitively, irrelevant results are
302
      those items that have little to do with the query (“D-cell battery” as a response to “sweater, red”), and
303
      therefore the process generating their corresponding vector is independent of either the choice of the
304   query vector ⃗vq or the filter vector ⃗νf . In contrast, relevant search results are those that directly have
305   to do with the query (“Striped Christmas sweater” as a reply to the same query), and the generative
306   process is consequently a function of some combination of ⃗vq and ⃗νf .
307
308   Specifically, with respect to fixed i.i.d N (⃗0, Id /d) samples of a query vector ⃗vq and filter vector ⃗νf (a
309
      standard model for random unit vectors of near-unit length in high-dimensional Euclidean space), we
      assume the data is generated via one of two generative processes:
310
311          • Irrelevant vectors are sampled from some spherical Gaussian of mean ⃗0 and covariance d1 Id ,
312            independent of ⃗vq and ⃗νf .
313                                                                                                  2
             • Relevant vectors are sampled from some spherical Gaussian of covariance σd Id centered
314            around ⃗vq + γ⃗νf for some unknown γ, σ ∈ [0, 4/5].

      In the analysis, we analyze two different distributions: that of squared distances between the query
379   vector ⃗v∗ = ⃗vq + λ⃗νf and irrelevant vectors (Dr ), and that of the squared distances between ⃗v∗ and
380   relevant vectors (Di ). We show that these are both scaled mostly-independent χ2 (d) distributions,
381   and that the probability that ⃗v∗ is closer to a vector drawn from Dr than one from Di matches the
382   probability that the difference of two other closely related (but now truly independent) χ2 distributions
383   is negative. By analyzing the moment generating function of this difference (Lemma 2), we show
384   that it only takes on negative values an exponentially small fraction of the time.
385
      Lemma 2. Let X1 and X2 be independent random variables sampled as X1 ∼ c1 χ2 (k) and
386                                                                  p   k
387   X2 ∼ c2 χ2 (k), respectively. If c1 > 2c2 , then Pr[X2 ≥ X1 ] ≤ 8/9 ≈ 0.943k .
388   Given Lemma 2 (whose proof is deferred to Appendix A), one can derive Theorem 1 as follows.
389
390   Proof of Theorem 1. Let ⃗vr be the embedding of a vector relevant to both q and f , and let ⃗vi be the
391   embedding of a vector irrelevant to both. Define Di as the distribution over ||⃗v∗ − N (⃗0, Id )||22 , i.e.
392   the prior distribution of the squared distance from some fixed ⃗v∗ to an irrelevant ⃗vi . Define Dr as the
393   distribution over ||⃗v∗ − (⃗vq + γ⃗νf + N (⃗0, σ 2 Id ))||22 , i.e. the prior distribution of the squared distance
394   from a fixed ⃗v∗ to a relevant ⃗vr .

397         Dr = ||⃗v∗ − (⃗vq + γ⃗νf + N (⃗0, σ 2 Id ))||22 = ||⃗vq + λ⃗νf − (⃗vq + γ⃗νf + N (⃗0, σ 2 Id ))||22
398
               = ||N (⃗0, σ 2 Id ) + (λ − γ)N (⃗0, Id )||22 = (σ 2 + (λ − γ)2 )χ2 (d),

401          Di = ||⃗v∗ − N (⃗0, Id )||22 = ||(N (⃗0, Id ) + λN (⃗0, Id )) − N (⃗0, Id )||22

404   Thus, both distributions are scaled χ2 distributions with the same parameter d. However, having
405   fixed a value for ⃗v∗ , the squared distances in Dr and Di both depend on our choice of ⃗v∗ , so
406   jointly sampling x1 ∼ Dr and x2 ∼ Di for the same ⃗v∗ yields random values that are not entirely
407   independent. However, rotating the sampled vectors so that ⃗v∗ lies on a basis direction shows that
408   the samples are still independent among the remaining d − 1 dimensions, and hence x1 − x2 can be
409   expressed as the difference of two χ2 (d − 1) distributions with scales as above.
410
                               D = (2 + λ2 )χ2 (d − 1) − (σ 2 + (λ − γ)2 )χ2 (d − 1)
411
412   It can be verified that the ratio (σ 2 + (λ − γ)2 )/(2 + λ2 ) is at most 1/2. Therefore, plugging this
413   difference of distributions into Lemma 2, we conclude that the probability it is negative (and hence
414   the probability that a sample from Dr exceeds that from Di ) is at most 0.943d−1 .
415   The above statement is true for a single sample from Di and Dr . With k samples from Dr , the
416   probability of a single sample from Di exceeding any of the k of them (and thus appearing among
417   the k nearest neighbors) is at most 0.943d−1 k. By a second union bound, the probability that of the n
418   samples in D is one of the k-nearest neighbors is at most 0.943d−1 nk.
419
420   For a 384-dimensional dataset (such as minilm) supported on 1B points, this calculation estimates
421   that at most 1.73 irrelevant vectors will show up in the top 10 nearest neighbors, or that 80+% of
422   results will be relevant. For higher dimensional models such as the 768-dimensional mpnet, the
423   dataset will need to be more than a billion times larger before we expect a single irrelevant result.

431   such as product description, brand, color, etc. We sub-sample the dataset to only contain products

      E.2 C OMPARISON WITH PRIOR WORK ON ANISOTROPY
950
951
      It is widely recognized that contextualized word vectors exhibit high cosine similarity across different
      contexts. For example, the cosine similarity for the word ground, whether in the context of ground
952
      truth or Earth, is notably high Ethayarajh (2019). Furthermore, many transformer models feature
953
      rogue dimensions—specific dimensions that dominate over others in the computation of cosine simi-
954   larities Timkey & van Schijndel (2021). Such phenomena, characterized by high cosine similarity and
955   the presence of rogue dimensions, have been interpreted as indications of degenerate representations
956   and a lack of expressivity. As a result, numerous studies have attempted to mitigate this anisotropy to
957   enhance model performance Jung et al. (2022); Rajaee & Pilehvar (2021); Gao et al. (2019); Liang
958   et al. (2021). However, the outcomes have been relatively inconsistent, with no isotropization strategy
959   proving to consistently benefit performance Ding et al. (2022).
960   We delve into these claims from the perspective of our downstream task: search. We posit that the
961   utility of a model’s isotropy should be assessed based on its impact on the downstream task. For
962   search applications, anisotropy likely undermines model performance, as the geometry of the vector
963   embeddings directly influences the output. Given that anisotropic embeddings limit the model’s
964   capacity to explore the full available space, it is plausible that this restriction could detrimentally
965   affect performance. In essence, geometry has a tangible effect on search performance.
966   This argument, however, may not extend to models designed for text generation or masked language
967   modeling, where the relationship between geometry and performance is more indirect. Without a
968   thorough mechanistic understanding of how large neural networks function, making definitive claims
969   about these models remains challenging. Either-way we focus on search transformers and find two
970   major themes. First, vector embeddings for search transformers are quite isotropic, and that there
971   do not exist rogue dimensions in the geometry of search transformers that disproportionately affect
      performance.
