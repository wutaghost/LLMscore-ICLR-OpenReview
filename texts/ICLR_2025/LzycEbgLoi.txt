                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
               Automatically generating radiology reports from three-dimensional medical
015
               images, such as 3D CT scans, plays a crucial role in modern diagnostics.
016
               Current approaches for generating 3D reports often adopt video processing
017            methods, which struggle to effectively capture the relationships along the Z-axis.
018            Additionally, multimodal large language model-based methods for generating
019            3D image reports face significant limitations, particularly in terms of the image
020            encoder’s ability to represent 3D structures and the hallucinations that arise in
021            generated content. To address these challenges, we propose the 3D-CT-GPT++
022            model. This model integrates the optimized 3D image encoder CTViT-V,
023            specifically designed for chest CT scans, and builds upon the LLaVA-1.5
024            architecture. Furthermore, we introduce Direct Preference Optimization (DPO),
025
               where GPT-4 is used to score the outputs of our fully fine-tuned (SFT) model,
               creating a preference dataset for subsequent DPO training. DPO significantly
026
               reduces hallucinations in the report generation process, ensuring the generated
027
               reports are more aligned with clinical needs. We fine-tuned the model on both
028            high-quality private and public datasets to ensure clinical relevance. Extensive
029            experiments were conducted using standard natural language generation (NLG)
030            evaluation metrics, including BLEU, METEOR, ROUGE-L, and GREEN, to
031            assess the report generation performance. Experimental results demonstrate
032            that 3D-CT-GPT++ significantly outperforms existing methods in terms of
033            accuracy, fluency, clinical factual consistency, and clinical relevance, advancing
034            the automation of 3D medical report generation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Medical imaging plays a critical role in modern diagnostics, providing clinicians with precise
      anatomical information for accurate medical decisions (Liu et al., 2024). Three-dimensional
040
      computed tomography (3D CT), in particular, offers richer spatial information compared to
041
      two-dimensional (2D) images, aiding in the detection of pathological details that traditional
042   techniques may miss. However, current CT image interpretation relies heavily on manual analysis
043   by radiologists, which is time-consuming, error-prone, and adds to the clinical workload (Farahani
044   et al., 2017). While advances have been made in generating 2D image reports (Chen et al., 2022;
045   2020; Qin & Song, 2022), processing 3D images, such as 3D CT scans, remains challenging due
046   to the complexity of spatial feature extraction and high computational costs (Li et al., 2023b).
047   Maintaining slice consistency across multiple slices is a key issue, as it is crucial for accurate
048   diagnosis. These challenges drive the need for models that can efficiently process 3D data while
049   preserving spatial coherence and improving diagnostic accuracy.
050   Despite advancements in generating reports from 3D CT images, existing approaches still face
051   significant challenges. As shown in Figure 1, models like RadMD (Wu et al., 2023) and M3D
052   (Bai et al., 2024) employ 3D Vision Transformers (3DViT) for feature extraction from 3D CT
053   scans. However, processing high-dimensional 3D images with 3DViT often requires compressing
      high-resolution data, leading to the potential loss of critical medical details that affect diagnostic

083   Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Li et al., 2023d), which relies on human-annotated data
084   to guide the model’s outputs. However, RLHF can be resource-intensive and challenging to scale
085   due to the high costs and time associated with collecting human feedback.

112   2     R ELATED W ORK
113
114   Multimodal Medical Large Models (MMLMs). In recent years, the development of Multimodal
115   Medical Large Models (MMLMs) has greatly advanced the automatic generation of medical imaging
116   reports. Current methods for generating medical reports mainly include model aggregation, joint
117   vision-language model generation, and end-to-end fine-tuning approaches. Model aggregation
118   methods combine outputs from multiple models and use specially designed prompts to generate
119
      complete reports, as seen in ChatCAD (Wang et al., 2023b) and ChatCAD+ (Zhao et al., 2024).
      Joint vision-language model generation methods, such as XrayGPT (Thawkar et al., 2023) and
120
      XrayPULSE, extract image features through vision encoders and integrate them with language
121
      models to achieve effective multimodal fusion. End-to-end fine-tuning methods, such as Med-PaLM
122   (Tu et al., 2023) and XrayGLM (Wang et al., 2023a), perform joint training on image and text
123   data, significantly improving the model’s understanding and generation capabilities. Some of the
124   leading models, such as LLaVA-Med (Li et al., 2023a), Med-PaLM2 (Singhal et al., 2023), and
125   MedFlamingo Moor et al. (2023), have demonstrated strong performance in 2D image analysis by
126   leveraging large-scale medical image datasets and language models. However, these models still
127   face challenges when processing 3D images, such as CT and MRI scans, due to the complexity of
128   spatial feature extraction and high computational costs. Although RadFM (Wu et al., 2023) and
129   M3D-LaMed (Bai et al., 2024) have explored 3D image analysis, the generated reports still lack
130
      coherence and accuracy. Additionally, the 3D-CT-GPT (Chen et al., 2024a) model also exhibits
      limitations in maintaining global dependencies between slices.
131
132
133   Preference Optimization and Reinforcement Learning. Reinforcement Learning from Human
134
      Feedback (RLHF) (Li et al., 2023d) has been widely applied to improve the output quality of
      Large Language Models (LLMs). By incorporating human preference data, RLHF allows models
135
      to gradually learn to generate more reliable and useful outputs, especially in multimodal tasks.
136
      However, RLHF faces several challenges when applied to multimodal tasks, particularly in aligning
137   different modalities, such as text, images, and videos. Designing an effective reward system is
138   crucial, as poor reward design can lead to models generating inaccurate or irrelevant content.
139   Additionally, scaling RLHF is costly, especially when collecting large-scale preference data. For
140   instance, Sun et al. (2023) reported that collecting 10,000 human-labeled preference data points
141   for LLaVA-RLHF cost approximately $3,000. Li et al. (2023c) also encountered scalability issues
142   when applying GPT-4V to preference modeling, particularly when handling video inputs. Ahn
143   et al. (2024) proposed using Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) models for self-evaluation, although
144   this approach has not yet been fully validated for complex video-related tasks.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
147
148
      In this section, we introduce the 3D-CT-GPT++ model, designed to enhance the automatic
149
      generation of radiology reports from 3D medical images, specifically chest CT scans. As shown
150   in Figure 2, the model leverages the optimized 3D image encoder, CTViT-V, to efficiently process
151   complex volumetric data while a pre-trained large language model (LLM) generates coherent and
152   contextually accurate reports. Additionally, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al.,
153   2023) is integrated to ensure that the generated reports align with clinicians’ diagnostic preferences,
154   and feedback from ChatGPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2023) is incorporated to further refine the reports’
155   accuracy and coherence.
156

158

160   enhanced encoder architecture called CTViT-V, building upon the original CTViT model (Hamamci
161   et al., 2023). Figure 2(a) illustrates the architecture of the proposed CTViT-V model. Our key
      improvements are:

187           interactions to adjacent slices, our approach allows for bidirectional information flow,
188           enhancing the model’s ability to understand complex spatial relationships.
189
            • Relative Position Encoding: To better model the positional relationships between slices,
190           we incorporate relative position encoding (Shaw et al., 2018) within the slice Transformer.
191           This allows the model to recognize the relative distances and positions of slices, improving
192           spatial coherence and the accuracy of feature extraction.
193
            • Computational Efficiency: By optimizing the attention mechanisms and incorporating
194           efficient architectural designs inspired by ViViT (Arnab et al., 2021) and C-ViViT
195           (Villegas et al., 2022), we reduce the computational overhead associated with processing
196           high-resolution 3D volumes. This makes it feasible to process multi-slice CT scans without
197           significant loss of detail.

      3.2     V ISION -L ANGUAGE I NTEGRATION
221
222   After obtaining the encoded visual features Zv′ from the CTViT-V encoder, we integrate these
223   features with a pre-trained large language model (LLM) to generate radiology reports. The
224   overall architecture follows the LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a) framework, with modifications to
225   accommodate the 3D visual features. As shown in Figure 2(b), we combine the pre-trained
226   Vicuna-1.5 (7B) (Chiang et al., 2023) language model with the CTViT-V encoder. The visual
227   features are transformed into language embedding tokens Hv using a lightweight 2-layer MLP
228
      projection matrix W , matching the dimensionality of the word embeddings in the LLM:
229
230                                              Hv = W · Zv′ .                                          (4)
231
232   During the report generation process, the prompt text (e.g., a clinician’s question or instruction)
233   is combined with an image placeholder to form the input prompt. This prompt is tokenized by
234   the LLM’s tokenizer, generating text tokens Mq . These tokens are split into Mq1 and Mq2 around
235   the image placeholder. The visual tokens Hv are concatenated with these text tokens to form the
236
      complete input:
237
238                                      M = concat([Mq1 , Hv , Mq2 ]).                                  (5)
239
240   The LLM processes this input to generate the output tokens, which are decoded into the final
241   radiology report Xa :
242
243                                             Xa = LLM(M ).                                            (6)
244
245   For a detailed description of the vision-language integration and further steps involved, please refer
246   to Appendix A.2.

248   3.3     D IRECT P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION (DPO)
249
250   After training, we have developed a model capable of generating reports from 3D CT lung
251   medical images. However, like other generative models, ours still encounters challenges such as
252   hallucination, where the generated reports may include information inconsistent with real clinical
253   scenarios. To address this, we introduce Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al.,
254
      2023) into 3D CT medical image report generation, drawing inspiration from LLaVA-Hound-DPO
      (Zhang et al., 2024). Our approach utilizes ChatGPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2023) to score the outputs
255
      of our supervised fine-tuned (SFT) model, creating a preference dataset that guides DPO training,
256
      ensuring the generated reports align more closely with clinicians’ diagnostic preferences.
257
258

259
260   To construct the preference dataset, we first use the trained SFT model to generate a large
261   number of medical reports from preprocessed public and private datasets. Then, these reports are
262   evaluated using the ChatGPT-4 API. The scoring process involves inputting the ground truth reports,
263   model-generated reports, and detailed descriptions as supporting evidence, as illustrated in Figure 3.
264   As shown in Figure 3(A), we sample multiple outputs from the 3D-CT-GPT++(SFT) model using

267   Figure 3(B), GPT-4 evaluates the outputs based on the evaluation prompt (eval prompt), which
268   includes both the ground truth and model-generated reports, providing feedback in the form of
269   language-based explanations and numerical scores. The detailed evaluation prompt used for scoring

      3.4   DATASET
325
326   Data Collection For this study, we utilized a subset of the publicly available CT-RATE dataset
327   (Hamamci et al., 2024), which includes 25,692 non-contrast chest CT volumes. After various
328   reconstruction techniques (Willemink & Noël, 2019), this dataset was expanded to 50,188 volumes
329   representing 21,304 unique patients, paired with corresponding radiology reports, abnormality
330   labels, and metadata. We directly selected the entire set of 21,304 cases from the CT-RATE
331   dataset, with 20,000 cases allocated to the training set and 1,304 cases reserved for the testing set.
332
      Additionally, we collected 2,000 3D chest CT scans and their corresponding radiology reports from
      a well-known international hospital, which we refer to as Dataset-XY. These scans cover a wide age
333
      range (20 to 88 years) and have a mean axial resolution of 512x512 pixels, with slices per volume
334
      ranging from 100 to 600. Both datasets have been anonymized and de-identified prior to use.
335
336
      Data Preprocessing We performed extensive preprocessing to ensure the quality and consistency
337
      of the image and report data. For Dataset-XY, we applied standard de-identification protocols,
338   removed duplicates, and filtered out irrelevant text in the reports. For the CT images, we excluded
339   low-resolution and redundant scans, followed by manual review to further ensure consistency and
340   uniformity. Both datasets were converted to Hounsfield Units (HU) using metadata slope and
341   intercept values, and cropped to the range [-1000 HU, +200 HU] to reflect the diagnostic limits.
342   Volumes were resampled to uniform spacing of 0.75 mm along the x and y axes and 1.5 mm along
343   the z axis, and resized to a consistent resolution of 240x480x480. From the CT-RATE dataset,
344   20,000 cases were used for training purposes, while 1,304 cases were designated for testing to
345   evaluate the model’s performance. For a summary of dataset statistics, including the number of
346
      cases and average report length, see Table 3 in Appendix A.4.
347
348   3.5   T RAINING P ROCESS
349
350
      We divided the training process into four main stages.
351
352   Stage 1: Image Encoder Training In this stage, we trained the TViT-V encoder on a large-scale
353
      3D CT scan dataset using the CT-CLIP framework. CT-CLIP, a 3D adaptation of the CLIP
      architecture, was employed for self-supervised contrastive learning, aligning image and text
354
      embeddings in a shared latent space,as illustrated in Figure 2(c).
355
356   Self-Supervised Contrastive Learning: We utilized contrastive learning to maximize the similarity
357   between matching 3D CT scans and corresponding radiology reports (positive pairs) while
358   minimizing similarity between non-matching pairs (negative pairs). This method enables the model
359
      to learn effective, discriminative representations of the 3D data.
360   Image Encoder Implementation: CTViT-V extracts low-dimensional CT tokens from volumetric
361   images, which are projected into a shared 512-dimensional space. This enables contrastive learning
362   with the text encoder, ensuring robust image representation.
363   Text Encoder Integration: For the text encoder, we used a pre-trained CXR-BERT, which encodes
364   radiology reports into the same 512-dimensional space for contrastive alignment.
365
366
      Training Objective: The primary training objective is to align image and text embeddings by
      maximizing the cosine similarity between positive pairs and minimizing the similarity between
367
      negative pairs. This optimizes the CTViT-V encoder for extracting features from 3D CT images,
368
      ensuring effective medical report generation.
369
370
      Stage 2: Pre-training In this stage, the model was trained to understand the relationship
371
      between 3D CT image features and their corresponding reports by analyzing a large set of 3D
372   CT image-report pairs. During this process, both the image encoder and language model were
373   frozen, and we focused on training the projection layer. The training was conducted using a
374   custom-built dataset, which comprises both public and private datasets. Due to the scarcity of paired
375   3D CT images and reports, we were unable to perform the large-scale alignment training typical of
376   multimodal models. Instead, we adopted a pre-training approach by training separately on the public
377   dataset and the private dataset, and comparing the effectiveness of different methods to address this
      challenge.


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Stage 3: Fine-tuning In this stage, we further refined the model to align 3D CT image features
379   with specific radiology reports. We employed two fine-tuning strategies ,where the fine-tuning
380   approach resembles that described in LLM-CXR (Lee et al., 2024).
381
382
      LoRA-based Lightweight Fine-tuning During this phase, we employed LoRA (Low-Rank
      Adaptation) (Hu et al., 2021) for lightweight fine-tuning. The image encoder remained frozen,
383
      while parts of the language model and projection layers were fine-tuned. This efficient approach
384
      helped avoid overfitting.
385
386   Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT) Additionally, during this phase, we applied supervised fine-tuning
387   (SFT) (Brown et al., 2020), optimizing all parameters of the language model using labeled data. This
388
      contrasts with parameter-efficient methods like LoRA, which adjust only a subset of layers. The
      number of training epochs was carefully selected through cross-validation to prevent overfitting.
389
390
391
      Stage 4: Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) After fine-tuning, the model underwent
      Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) to ensure that generated reports aligned more closely with
392
      clinicians’ diagnostic preferences.
393
394   GPT-4 Scoring and Preference Dataset GPT-4 was used to score the generated reports based
395   on factual consistency and coherence, producing a preference dataset. Reports with scores above
396   a threshold were used as positive examples, and those below the threshold served as negative
397
      examples, as described in Section 3.3.1.
398   DPO Training Using this preference dataset, the model was trained to optimize report generation
399   by adjusting the likelihood of producing higher-scoring reports, following the DPO objective. This
400   helped the model generate more clinically relevant and accurate reports,as described in Section 3.3.2.

438   coherence in generated reports. Similarly, ROUGE-L increases from 0.3306 and 0.3199 to 0.3692,
439   suggesting that our model produces reports with structures more closely aligned with reference texts.
440   In particular, the GREEN score for 3D-CT-GPT++ (SFT+DPO) reaches the highest value of 0.3527,
441   indicating superior overall performance in terms of both content and coherence. The improvements
442   across all metrics reflect the effectiveness of our approach, integrating SFT with DPO for enhanced

448   As shown in Section (B) of Table 2, our model 3D-CT-GPT++ significantly outperforms existing

453   effectiveness of our approach, attributed to the advanced encoder architecture and the incorporation

457   4.2.3   I MPACT OF T EMPERATURE ON P ERFORMANCE
458
459   Section (C) of Table 2 explores the effect of varying the temperature parameter during inference
460   on the performance of 3D-CT-GPT++. A temperature of 0.4 yields the highest scores for most
461   metrics, including BLEU-1, BLEU-4, ROUGE-1, and METEOR. Specifically, at temperature 0.4,
462   the model achieves a BLEU-4 score of 14.47 and a ROUGE-L score of 0.3807. However, higher
463   temperatures (e.g., 0.8 and 0.9) lead to a slight decline in performance metrics. This is expected, as
464   higher temperatures introduce more randomness, increasing the diversity of the generated text but
465   potentially reducing the overlap with reference texts, as measured by BLEU and ROUGE scores.
466
      Based on these observations, a temperature of 0.4 appears to provide the best balance between
      diversity and accuracy for our task. However, depending on the specific requirements of report
467
      generation—such as the need for more deterministic outputs—a temperature of 0.7 may still be
468
      preferable.

472   4.2.4   I MPACT OF DATA Q UANTITY ON DPO P ERFORMANCE
473
474   Section (D) of Table 2 compares the supervised fine-tuned model 3D-CT-GPT++ (SFT), the
475   DPO-trained model using the initial data selection method 3D-CT-GPT++ (1), and the DPO-trained
476   model with a refined data selection strategy 3D-CT-GPT++ (2). The refined strategy in
477
      3D-CT-GPT++ (2) aimed to increase contrast by selecting the highest-scoring candidate as the
      positive example and the lowest-scoring as the negative; if no candidate scored ≥ 3, we used the
478
      real report as the positive example. However, this refined strategy resulted in decreased performance
479
      compared to 3D-CT-GPT++ (1); for example, BLEU-4 decreased from 13.32 to 12.41. Similarly,
480   Chen et al. (2024b) introduced a self-play method for DPO training, preferring ground-truth cases
481   over model-generated responses. Zhang et al. (2024) found that their LLaVA-Hound-DPO (Zhang
482   et al., 2024) model showed a 3% accuracy decline compared to SFT models when using self-play
483   (Chen et al., 2024b). These findings suggest that while reward incorporation benefits complex tasks,
484   extreme contrasts between examples may hinder performance. Our results support this, indicating
485   that in DPO training, the quality and representativeness of preference data are more important than
      the quantity or extremity of examples.

519   5     C ONCLUSION
520
521   We have introduced 3D-CT-GPT++, a novel model for radiology report generation that
522   leverages advanced encoder architectures and Direct Preference Optimization to achieve superior
523   performance. Through comprehensive experiments and ablation studies, we demonstrate the
524   model’s effectiveness and potential for clinical applications.
525
526   5.1    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528   While 3D-CT-GPT++ has demonstrated significant advancements in radiology report generation,
529   several limitations remain. Improving the clinical relevance of generated reports requires integrating
530   patient-specific information, such as medical history and symptoms. Future work will explore
531
      combining clinical background data with imaging features to produce reports better aligned with
      real-world needs. We also plan to expand our dataset to include diverse medical imaging types, such
532
      as X-rays and MRIs, to enhance generalization. Collaborating with medical institutions, we aim to
533
      conduct large-scale clinical trials to gather clinician feedback and optimize model performance in
534   real-world settings. Additionally, we will develop evaluation metrics focused on clinical relevance
535   and improve model interpretability to ensure accuracy and clarity for clinicians. To address
536   computational challenges, we will explore more efficient model architectures and optimization
537   techniques to reduce training and inference costs, facilitating deployment in resource-constrained
538   healthcare environments. By addressing these limitations and pursuing these directions, we aim
539   to enhance the performance and practicality of 3D-CT-GPT++, promoting its adoption in clinical
      radiology.

957   B.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
958
959   To evaluate our radiology report generation model, we use standard NLG metrics: BLEU (Papineni
960   et al., 2002), METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), and ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L
961   (Lin, 2004), computed using the sacrebleu (Post, 2018), nltk, and rouge score libraries. BLEU
962   measures n-gram overlap up to four in length, focusing on precision, with scores ranging from
963
      0 to 100. In contrast, METEOR and ROUGE scores typically range from 0 to 1. METEOR
      accounts for synonyms, stemming, and paraphrasing, balancing precision and recall to capture both
964
      accuracy and completeness. ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L measure unigram, bigram, and
965
      longest common subsequence overlap, respectively, assessing fluency and coherence. These metrics
966   collectively provide a comprehensive evaluation of text quality, focusing on precision, recall, and
967   structural similarity. Additionally, to assess clinical accuracy and factual correctness, we employ
968   the GREEN metric (Ostmeier et al., 2024), which is specifically designed for evaluating radiology
969   reports by detecting factual errors and hallucinations. By combining traditional NLG metrics with
970   the GREEN metric, we ensure a comprehensive evaluation of both linguistic quality and clinical
971   relevance of the generated reports. While they don’t directly measure clinical accuracy, they are
      widely accepted for assessing the linguistic quality of generated reports in medical applications.
