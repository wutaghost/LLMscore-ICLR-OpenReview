009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              Traditional recommender systems usually take the user-platform paradigm, where
012              users are directly exposed under the control of the platform’s recommendation
013              algorithms. However, the defect of recommendation algorithms may put users in
014              very vulnerable positions under this paradigm. First, many sophisticated models
015              are often designed with commercial objectives in mind, focusing on the platform’s
016              benefits, which may hinder their ability to protect and capture users’ true interests.
017
                 Second, these models are typically optimized using data from all users, which
                 may overlook individual user’s preferences. Due to these shortcomings, users
018
                 may experience several disadvantages under the traditional user-platform direct
019
                 exposure paradigm, such as lack of control over the recommender system, potential
020              manipulation by the platform, echo chamber effects, or lack of personalization
021              for less active users due to the dominance of active users during collaborative
022              learning. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop a new paradigm to protect
023              user interests and alleviate these issues. Recently, some researchers have introduced
024              LLM agents to simulate user behaviors, these approaches primarily aim to optimize
025              platform-side performance, leaving core issues in recommender systems unresolved.
026              To address these limitations, we propose a new user-agent-platform paradigm,
027              where agent serves as the protective shield between user and recommender system
028
                 that enables indirect exposure. To this end, we first construct four recommendation
                 datasets, denoted as I NSTRUCT R EC, along with user instructions for each record.
029
                 To understand user’s intention, we design an Instruction-aware Agent (iAgent)
030
                 capable of using tools to acquire knowledge from external environments. Moreover,
031              we introduce an Individual Instruction-aware Agent (i2 Agent), which incorporates
032              a dynamic memory mechanism to optimize from individual feedback. Results
033              on four I NSTRUCT R EC datasets demonstrate that i2 Agent consistently achieves
034              an average improvement of 16.6% over SOTA baselines across ranking metrics.
035              Moreover, i2 Agent mitigates echo chamber effects and effectively alleviates the
036              model bias in disadvantaged users (less-active), serving as a shield between user
037              and recommender systems. Datasets and code are publicly available at the URL1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
041
042
      Over the past decades, recommender systems have been extensively applied across various platforms
      to provide personalized services to users. In the traditional ecosystem of recommender systems, the
043
      recommendation models are predominantly delivered through a user-platform paradigm, where users
044
      are directly subject to the platform’s algorithms. This paradigm places users in a vulnerable position,
045   such as lack of control over their recommendation results, potentially being manipulated by the
046   platform’s recommendation algorithms, being trapped in echo chambers, or lack of personalization
047   for those less active users due to the active users’ dominance of the recommendation algorithm.
048
      Firstly, the majority of recommendation models (Cheng et al., 2016; Kang & McAuley, 2018; Hidasi
049
      et al., 2015) are designed to optimize the commercial objectives of the platforms, such as increasing
050
      user clicks or conversion rates in e-commerce. This often results in users losing sight of their actual
051   needs due to the algorithmic manipulation (Aguirre et al., 2015; Edizel et al., 2020; Grisse, 2023).
052   Secondly, although recommendation models aims at offering personalized services, they are primarily

081   To tackle these issues, researchers have approached the problem from various perspectives. On one
082   hand, efforts are made to better understand user interests, such as using user’s explicit feedback to
083   improve the model performance and explanation (Zhang et al., 2014; Xie et al., 2021) or allowing

      interests from various dimensions, such as capturing user’s diverse interests based on multi-behavior
      and multi-interest modeling (Zhou et al., 2018; 2019; Li et al., 2019). Most recently, language-based
087
      agents are utilized to mock the user behaviors and explore the user interests (Zhang et al., 2024b;a).
088
089   However, the two challenges remain insufficiently addressed due to the reliance on modeling user in-
090   terests across all users’ data and the focus on platform-side optimization. To address these limitations,
091   we propose a new user-agent-platform paradigm, where agent serves as the protective shield between
092
      user and recommender system that enables indirect exposure. Our contributions are three-fold:
093   ◦ New Datasets and Problem: To provide benchmarks for the new user-agent-platform paradigm, we
094   construct four recommendation datasets with user-driven instructions, referred to as I NSTRUCT R EC,
095   constructed from existing datasets such as Amazon, Goodreads, and Yelp. Building on this, we
096   propose an Instruction-aware Agent (iAgent), designed to learn user interests from the provided free-
097   text instructions while leveraging external knowledge to act as a domain-specific expert. Unlike the
098
      instructions in CRS (Sun & Zhang, 2018) and Webshop (Yao et al., 2022), the free-text instructions
      in I NSTRUCT R EC allow users to flexibly express their requirements beyond just product attributes.
099
100   ◦ Agent Learning from Individual Feedback: We design Individual Instruction-aware Agent (i2 Agent),
101   incorporating a dynamic memory mechanism with a profile generator and dynamic extractor to further
102   explore user interests and learn from user’s individual feedback. The profile generator constructs and
103   maintains a user-specific profile by leveraging historical information and feedback. The dynamic
104
      extractor captures evolving profiles and interests based on the user’s real-time instructions. Different
      from existing recommendation models, i2 Agent is optimized specifically for individual users and is
105
      not influenced by the interests or behaviors of other users, protecting the interests of less-active users.
106
107   ◦ Empirical Results: Empirical experiments on four datasets demonstrate that our i2 Agent consis-
      tently outperforms state-of-the-art approaches, achieving an improvement of up to 16.6% on average

      2       TASK D EFINITIONS AND C OMPARISIONS
122
      Sequential Recommendation. Consider a set of users U and a set of items I. Each user’s historical
123
      interactions are represented by a sequence Su = [s1 , . . . , si , . . . , sT ], where si ∈ I and T is the
124   length of the sequence. The goal of sequential recommendation is to predict the next item sT +1 that
125   the user u is likely to interact with, based on their past interactions Su (Hidasi et al., 2015; Kang &
126   McAuley, 2018; Sun et al., 2019; Geng et al., 2022). Formally, this involves estimating the probability
127   distribution over the items for the next interaction:
128                                          î = arg max P (sT +1 = i | Su ; ψ).                                     (1)
129                                                       i∈I
130   where ψ is the model’s parameters. Recent work on recommendation agents (Zhang et al., 2024b;
131   Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a) has leveraged large language models (LLMs) to simulate
132   user behavior by prompting them with plain text descriptions of user history and learn from the
133   external knowledge via tool usage. Despite the shift to a language-based framework, it shares the
134
      same optimization objective as the traditional sequential recommendation.
135   Conversational Recommendation. Traditional conversational recommendation system (Sun &
136   Zhang, 2018; Zhang et al., 2018) analyzes the user’s intention via the multiple turn dialogue and
137   consider historical information to achieve personalized recommendation. Mathematically, the recom-
138   mendation model part2 can be summarized as:
139                                       î = arg max P (sT +1 = i | Su , Hu ; ψ).                                   (2)
140                                                   i∈I

141   where Hu = [h1 , ..., hR ] represents multiple historical dialogues of a user, R represents the number
142   of dialogues and ψ is the model’s parameters.
143   Our Task. Unlike sequential and conversational recommendation, our task focuses on learning from
144   user’s instructions to build an agentic shield between user and recommender system and meanwhile
145   provide personalized recommendations for users. Mathematically, this can be summarized as follows:
146
                                        î = arg max P (sT +1 = i | Su , Ωu , E; ψu ).                                (3)
147                                                 i∈I
148   where Ωu represents the user’s instructions, and ψu denotes the user-specific model parameters. E
149   represents the external environment, which can supply real-time information to the agent.
150
      In Table 1, we highlight the key differences between previous recommendation models and our
151
      proposed model. Unlike existing recommendation models, our approach conducts an in-depth
152   analysis of users’ instructions and learns from individual feedback. Additionally, leveraging the
153   power of LLMs, our model supports a highly flexible range of instructions and dialogues.

      3.1     I AGENT
188
189   Parser. The user’s instructions encompass both direct lower-level demands and hidden higher-
190   order preferences. Addressing these higher-order preferences requires agents to be equipped with
191   relevant knowledge, transforming them into domain-specific experts that serve the user. Domain-
192   specific experts use their professional knowledge to recognize differences between products, such as
193   parameterized variations, and connect these distinctions to the user’s expressed needs. The parser
194   model is built upon a large language model (LLM), represented by Mp , which is specifically prompted
195   to generate internal knowledge and decide whether to use external tools to extract knowledge from
196   the open world based on the given instruction. In the first step, we concatenate the instruction XI
197
      with the parser’s prompt template Ptp and prompt the LLM to output the related internal knowledge
      XIK about the instruction. This step also involves deciding whether to use external tools OT and
198
      generating the instruction keywords XKW . For example, in the book domain, this may include
199
      understanding each book’s theme, types of storylines, and other related aspects. Next, if the parser
200   Mp decides to use external tools, the instruction keywords XKW and the potential tool options OT
201   are utilized to explore the external knowledge XEK .
202
                                     OT , XKW , XIK ← Mp (XI ∥ Ptp ); XEK ← Mp (OT ∥ XKW )                                                   (4)
203
204
      Reranker. After obtaining the instruction-related knowledge, the reranker, denoted by the LLM-
205   based model Mr , reranks the initial ranking list R from the recommender platform. In addition to
206   the generated knowledge XIK and XEK , we incorporate the user’s historical sequential information
207   XSU , which serves as a static memory of the user. Similarly, the textual information XItem of the
208   items in the ranking list is also provided. Overall, the instruction-related knowledge, the textual
209   information XSU and XItem , along with the reranker’s prompt template Ptr , are fed into the reranker
210   Mr . Formally, this process can be written as follows:
211                                              R∗ ← Mr (XIK ∥XEK ∥XSU ∥ XItem ∥ Ptr )                                                      (5)

      where R is the reranked item lists and XItem includes the textual information (such as title and
213
      description) of the candidate items and item index from the initial ranking list R.

215   Self-reflection Mechanism. Large language models output content in a generative manner, which can
      lead to hallucination problems (Huang et al., 2023a). To address this, we designed a self-reflection

222   3.2   I 2 AGENT
223
224   Although our basic framework iAgent can explore knowledge based on the user’s instructions, it
225   fails to effectively model the dynamic interests within the instructions and cannot learn from user
226   feedback.. To address this, we design a profile generator to build user’s personal profile that learns
227
      from the user feedback and a dynamic extractor to extract dynamic interest and build dynamic profile
      according to the instruction. Unlike existing recommendation models, i2 Agent is uniquely optimized
228
      for individual users, remaining unaffected by the behaviors of other users.
229

231   first feeding training data pairs into the generator, followed by presenting the ground truth interacted

233
      recent interacted item is selected as the positive sample, and a negative item is randomly selected from
      the non-interacted items. The sampled pair, along with their corresponding textual information, are
234
      combined and fed into the generator Mge , which selects one item from the two as the recommended
235
      item for the user. Moreover, the user’s static memory XSU and the rank prompt template Ppr1 are
236   also input into the model. Formally, this process can be expressed as:

      Dataset. Given the absence of a recommendation dataset that includes proactive user instructions in
290
      the user-agent-platform paradigm, we construct I NSTRUCT R EC datasets using existing recommenda-
291
      tion datasets, including Amazon (Ni et al., 2019), Yelp3 , and Goodreads (Wan et al., 2019). These
292   datasets provide textual information such as item titles, descriptions, and reviews. We eliminate
293   users and items that have fewer than 5 associated actions to ensure sufficient data density. For each
294   interaction, we generate the instruction for this interaction based on the corresponding user review and
295   filter through a post-processing verification mechanism. To further enhance the linguistic diversity of
296   the instructions, we assign a persona to each user. More details are in the following.
297
      Instruction Generator: Initially, we manually annotate several instruction-review pairs, providing
298   few-shot examples for LLMs to facilitate in-context learning. These few-shot examples, along with
299   reviews paired with a random persona from Persona Hub (Chan et al., 2024), are then fed into the
300   LLM4 to generate instructions. To ensure that the few-shot examples remain dynamic, we create
301   a list to store the instruction-review pairs and allow the LLM to decide whether a newly generated
302   instruction should be included as an example. Examples of the annotated instruction-review pairs,
303   generated instructions, and the data construction processes can be found in Appendix B.3.
304   Instruction Cleaner: To prevent data leakage from the reviews, we test if or not the LLM can recover
305   the item from the generated instruction. More specifically, given the instruction, we employ the LLM
306   to choose between the ground-truth item and a randomly selected negative item. The LLM generates
307   a certainty score based on the instruction and the item’s textual information. Based on the result, we
308   retain all of those instructions for which the LLM cannot infer the ground-truth item, and also keep
309   an equal number of correctly inferred instructions that has low certainty scores. Statistical analysis of
310   I NSTRUCT R EC dataset is in Table 2. For the filtered instructions and the retained instructions, we
311   show some examples in Appendix B.3.2.

      5.1                             R ECOMMENDER S YSTEM
469
470   Sequential recommendation models (Hidasi et al., 2015; Sun et al., 2019; Kang & McAuley, 2018) pri-
471   marily focus on developing temporal encoders to capture both short- and long-term user interests. For
472   instance, SASRec (Kang & McAuley, 2018) leverages an attention mechanism to capture long-term
473   semantics, while BERT4Rec (Sun et al., 2019) uses a bidirectional encoder with a masked item train-
474   ing objective. In the context of embracing large language models, generative recommenders (Geng
475   et al., 2022; Zhai et al., 2024) treat item indices as tokens and predict them in a generative manner.
476
      Meanwhile, LLMs (Li et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024) are utilized to play as a sequential embedding
      extractor to improve the recommendation performance. In our framework design, all recommendation
477
      models can be considered as components of the tools.
478
479   Before large language model become popular, conversational recommendation system (CRS) (Sun &
480   Zhang, 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Qu et al., 2019) aims at designing better dialogue understanding
481   models or incorporating reinforcement learning for multiple dialogues answering. Due to the capacity
482
      of the conventional language model, it lose the flexibility of the dialogue including the dialogue
      format and number of turns. To resolve this problem, some researchers (Friedman et al., 2023; Feng
483
      et al., 2023) leverage the power of LLM to better understand the intention of user.
484
485   The echo chamber effect occurs when individuals are exposed only to information and opinions that
      reinforce their existing beliefs within their social networks (Bakshy et al., 2015; Chitra & Musco, 2020;


                                                                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Garimella et al., 2018), leading to a lack of diverse perspectives and increased polarization (Aslay
487   et al., 2018; Kaminskas & Bridge, 2016; Kunaver & Požrl, 2017). In the context of recommender
488   systems, researchers have begun to study echo chambers and feedback loops (Ge et al., 2020; Xu
489   et al., 2022; Chaney et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2019; Möller et al., 2020; Kalimeris et al., 2021).
490   Kalimeris et al. (Kalimeris et al., 2021) propose a matrix factorization-based recommender system
491   with a theoretical framework for modeling dynamic user interests, while ∂CCF (Chitra & Musco,
492   2020) employs counterfactual reasoning to mitigate echo chambers.
493
494   5.2   P ERSONAL L ANGUAGE - BASED AGENT
495
496   In the early stages, some researchers (Zhang, 2018; Park et al., 2023; Shanahan et al., 2023) in
497   the NLP field developed dialogue agents with personas to enhance dialogue quality. Language
498   models (Park et al., 2023) are prompted with role descriptions to simulate realistic interactions by
499   storing experiences, synthesizing memories, and dynamically planning actions, resulting in believable
500   individual and social behaviors within interactive environments. WebShop (Yao et al., 2022) attempts
501   to understand product attributes from human-provided text instructions using reinforcement learning
502
      and imitation learning. Similar to traditional conversational recommender systems (CRS) (Zhang
      et al., 2018), it is impractical for users to describe each product attribute every time. With the
503
      advancement of large language models (such as GPTs (Achiam et al., 2023)), many researchers (Gur
504
      et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024) have begun designing domain-specific agents that
505   integrate various tool learning and memory mechanisms.
506
507   More recently, recommendation agents (RecAgent) (Zhao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
508
      2024a;b; Wang et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023b) have been developed to simulate user behaviors
      and predict user-item interactions. A common design feature among these agents is the use of
509
      historical interaction information as user memory (Zhao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
510
      2023b), with LLMs utilized to generate the ranking results. Unlike platform-side RecAgents, iAgent
511   and i2 Agent are the first to operate on the user side, generating re-ranking results based on user
512   instructions and individual memory, unaffected by the influence of advantaged users.
513
514
515   6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTION
516
517   In this work, we first establish an instruction-aware recommendation benchmark and design a
518   straightforward instruction-aware agent (iAgent) to analyze user instructions and integrate relevant
519   and comprehensive knowledge. Moreover, to enhance the agent’s personalized abilities, we propose
520   individual instruction-aware agent (i2 Agent), which incorporates a dynamic memory mechanism to
521   learn from user’s personal feedback and extracts the dynamic interests. In addition to these technical
522   contributions, our work also presents unique and complementary avenues for future research.
523   More Effective Reranker. In this version of iAgent and i2 Agent, we construct a zero-shot reranker
524   based on LLMs, such as GPT4-o-mini. Recently, several open-source LLMs (Gunter et al., 2024;
525   Abdin et al., 2024; Team et al., 2024), typically containing fewer model parameters (2-3 billion),
526   have demonstrated strong performance. It is feasible to fine-tune smaller LLMs to build a more
527   effective reranker on our I NSTRUCT R EC dataset. Furthermore, existing advanced recommendation
528   models (Zhai et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024) can serve as tools for the agent to retrieve candidate items.
529   Multi-step Feedback. Although we have constructed various datasets rich in abundant instructions,
530   the feedback for re-ranking results is limited to a single ground-truth item, lacking continuous, multi-
531   step feedback on interactions between users and agents. Additionally, the feedback explanations from
532   users are insufficient. If i2 Agent were deployed in a real-world environment, more comprehensive
533   feedback could be collected, enabling the development of more interpretable agents for users.
534   Mutual Learning. This work builds an agent for users that makes decisions for users and collect
535   feedback from users. The platform-side recommendation models can improve their performance by
536   leveraging the feedback and explanations provided by agents on behalf of their users. Furthermore,
537   recommendation agents (Zhao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
538   2024a) can autonomously and iteratively improve through mutual learning with i2 Agent. Moreover,
539   i2 Agent can serve as a reward function for RL-based recommendation models (Afsar et al., 2022;
      Zheng et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020; Ge et al., 2022b), enhancing their performance.

      18-year span from May 1996 to July 2014. For our experiments, we leverage two distinct subsets:
      ”Books” and ”Movies and TV.” Each dataset includes anonymized user and item identifiers, along
875
      with user-provided ratings on a 1-5 scale and corresponding textual reviews. Furthermore, rich
876
      product metadata is incorporated, such as detailed descriptions, categorical classifications, pricing
877   information, and brand data. This multifaceted dataset provides a fertile ground for both collaborative
878   filtering and content-based recommendation approaches, where the interplay between user behavior,
879   product attributes, and textual feedback can be modeled to advance the state of recommendation
880   systems.
881
      Goodreads. 6 (Wan et al., 2019) The Goodreads dataset is derived from one of the largest online
882
      platforms dedicated to book reviews, offering user-generated ratings, reviews, and a variety of
883   associated metadata. Each user in the dataset is represented by an anonymized identifier, with
884   interactions including rating and reviewing a broad selection of books. The books are identified
885   through International Standard Book Numbers (ISBNs) and accompanied by an extensive set of
886   metadata, including title, author, publication year, and genre classifications. This data is especially
887   valuable for the development of content-aware recommendation models, where leveraging the
888   contextual features of both user interactions and book attributes can enhance predictive accuracy. The
889   textual reviews, in particular, provide a rich source of natural language data, capturing nuanced user
890   feedback that can be further utilized in sentiment analysis, opinion mining, and advanced NLP tasks.
891
      Ratings, similarly to the Amazon dataset, are presented on a 1-5 scale, providing a consistent metric
      for comparative analysis across different datasets.
892
893   Yelp. 7 The Yelp dataset contains over 67,000 reviews focused on businesses, particularly restaurants,
894   from three major English-speaking cities, sourced from the popular Yelp platform. The dataset
895   includes detailed metadata on both businesses and user interactions. Each business is uniquely
896   identified and linked to comprehensive metadata, including its name, geographic location, category
897
      (e.g., restaurant, bar, or retail establishment), and additional attributes such as parking availability
      and reservation policies. This data is invaluable for context-aware recommendation systems, where
898
      business features and user feedback intersect to inform personalized recommendations. Anonymized
899
      user IDs track user interactions, with additional features such as the number of reviews written,
900   average rating, and social features (e.g., ”friends,” ”useful votes”). Yelp’s textual reviews provide a
901   rich dataset for natural language processing, where the diverse nature of user opinions, combined
902   with structured metadata, offers a robust framework for evaluating and improving context-aware
903   recommendation models.

932   A.2.2    I NSTRUCTION - AWARE METHODS
933
934   We treat the concatenated text of the instruction as the query, while each candidate item is represented
935   by its various metadata (e.g., title, description), transformed into textual format. These textual
936   representations of candidate items are treated as individual ’documents,’ forming the document
937   corpus that instruction-aware methods rank based on relevance to the query. By leveraging the
938
      semantic richness of both the query and item metadata, this approach enables a context-aware ranking
      system, prioritizing items according to their alignment with the user’s intent and preferences as
939
      conveyed through the instruction.
940
941   BM25. (Robertson et al., 2009) BM25, a probabilistic ranking function, is a foundational method in
942   information retrieval, widely used to rank documents based on their relevance to a given query. The
943   core concept of BM25 is to measure the similarity between a query and a document by considering
944
      both the frequency of query terms within the document and the distribution of those terms across the
      entire document corpus. BM25 balances two key factors: term frequency, which reflects how often a
945
      query term appears in a document (assuming that higher frequency indicates greater relevance), and
946
      inverse document frequency, which assigns more weight to rarer terms in the dataset, as they carry
947   greater informational value. The PyTorch implementation of BM25 is available at the URL11 .
948
949   BGE-Rerank. (Xiao et al., 2023) The BGE-Rerank model utilizes a cross-encoder architecture,
950
      where both the query and document are processed together as a single input to directly generate
      a relevance score. Unlike bi-encoder models, which create independent embeddings for the query
951
      and document before computing their similarity, the cross-encoder applies full attention over the
952
      entire input pair, capturing more fine-grained interactions. This approach leads to higher accuracy in
953   estimating relevance. In our implementation, we use the BGE-Rerank model to reorder candidate
954   documents based on the relevance score for each query-document pair. The PyTorch implementation
955   of BGE-Rerank is available at the URL12 .
956
      EasyRec. EasyRec (Ren & Huang, 2024) is a lightweight, highly efficient recommendation system
957
      based on large language models, shown through extensive evaluations to outperform many LLM-
958
      based methods in terms of accuracy. Central to its success is the use of contrastive learning, which
959   effectively aligns semantic representations from textual data with collaborative filtering signals. This
960   approach enables EasyRec to generalize robustly and adapt to new, unseen recommendation data.
961   The model employs a bi-encoder architecture, where text embeddings for queries and documents are
962   pre-computed independently. These embeddings are then used to calculate similarity scores, allowing
963   for the reordering of candidate items based on relevance. The PyTorch implementation of EasyRec is
964   available at the URL13 .

982    A.2.3    R ECOMMENDATION AGENTS
983
984    ToolRec. (Zhao et al., 2024) uses large language models (LLMs) to enhance recommendation
985    systems by leveraging external tools. The methodology involves treating LLMs as surrogate users,
986    who simulate user decision-making based on preferences and utilize attribute-oriented tools (such as
987
       rank and retrieval tools) to explore and refine item recommendations. This iterative process allows
       for a more fine-grained recommendation that aligns with users’ preferences.
988
989    AgentCF. (Zhang et al., 2024b) AgentCF is an innovative approach that constructs both user and item
990    agents, powered by LLMs, to simulate user-item interactions in recommender systems. These agents
991    are equipped with memory modules designed to capture their intrinsic preferences and behavioral
992    data. At its core, AgentCF facilitates autonomous interactions between user and item agents, enabling
993
       them to make decisions based on simulated preferences. A key feature of this framework is the
       collaborative reflection mechanism, through which agents continuously update their memory, thereby
994
       improving their capacity to model real-world user-item relationships over time.
995
996    To ensure a fair comparison and optimize computational efficiency, the number of memory-building
997    rounds in AgentCF is set to 1, matching that of our i2 Agent. In AgentCF’s experiments, the dataset
998    size is 100, which represents only around 0.1% of the size of our dataset. Moreover, to ensure the
999
       generated reranking list without hallucination, we also equipped ToolRec and AgentCF with our
       self-reflection mechanism.

       A.3.1    E CHO C HAMBER E FFECT
1005
       We also report the experimental results evaluating the echo chamber effect in Table 7, Table 8
1006   and Table 9. Ads items are randomly inserted into the candidate ranking list from other domains
1007   to simulate advertising scenarios that users may have encountered. To mitigate position bias in
1008   LLMs (Liu et al., 2024b), Ads items are added randomly within the candidate list positions. i2 Agent
1009   accurately identifies users’ instructions and extracts knowledge about their underlying needs, thereby
1010   effectively removing undesired Ads. Benefitting from not being trained in a purely data-driven
1011   manner and constructing user profiles based on their feedback, our i2 Agent also recommends more
1012   diverse items to users (both active and less-active items), instead of focusing solely on popular items,
1013   and meanwhile improves the overall recommendation performance. Drawing from these experimental
1014
       results, we conclude that our i2 Agent can mitigate the echo chamber effect and act as a protective
       shield for users.

1018
       We define the top 20% of users as active, with the remaining 80% classified as less-active (Li
1019
       et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023). Since our data is sampled and filtered using a 10-core process, most
1020   users exhibit rich behavioral patterns. Consequently, active users tend to show poorer performance
1021   compared to less-active users, largely due to the decline in LLM performance with longer texts (Liu
1022   et al., 2024a). As illustrated in Table 10, Table 11 and Table 12, our i2 Agent enhances the performance
1023   for both active and less-active users. For less-active users, we construct individual profiles based on
1024   their feedback, ensuring that these profiles are not influenced by other users. The experimental results
1025   demonstrate that our dynamic memory mechanism offers personalized services tailored to each user
       individually.
