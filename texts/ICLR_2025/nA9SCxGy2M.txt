                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Supervised fine-tuning is a prevalent technique for boosting model performance.
010
               However, it heavily depends on extensive training over labeled data. This paper in-
011            troduces a novel model-driven fine-tuning method that operates independently of
012            supervised training and labeled data. By harnessing the collective intelligence of a
013            diverse model pool, our method enhances individual model performance through
014            a two-phase process. Initially, we consolidate the expertise of the models within
015            the pool to create a general meta-model. This meta-model then serves as a guide
016            for iteratively fine-tuning the original models in a few shots, promoting a syn-
017            ergistic improvement in performance. Our experimental results show that this
018            model-driven approach not only surpasses the performance of full-parameter fine-
019
               tuning models but also does so without the need for supervised training. This
               breakthrough offers a cost-effective and scalable alternative to traditional super-
020
               vised fine-tuning, addressing the challenge of data scarcity and paving the way for
021
               future research in unsupervised model enhancement. Our work represents a sig-
022            nificant step towards making fine-tuning techniques more accessible and practical
023            in environments where labeled data is limited or even unavailable.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Foundation models in computer vision (CV) and natural language processing (NLP) have seen un-
029   precedented development, driven by the flourishing growth of data and computational power. The
030   surge of foundation models can be largely attributed to their ability to capture intricate patterns and
031   relationships within the ever-expanding data. Notable examples include BERT Devlin et al. (2018)
032   in the field of NLP and Vision Transformer Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) in CV. The future trajectory of
033
      foundation models, spanning from vision Zhai et al. (2022) and language Hoffmann et al. (2022) to
      multi-modal Aghajanyan et al. (2023) contexts, is likely to inexorably involve the continual scaling
034
      of model sizes and the innovation of network architectures. A primary obstacle in developing foun-
035
      dational models is the high cost of training, hindered by time-consuming processes and the demand
036   for high-performance computing resources. The sheer scale of these models necessitates the use of
037   advanced hardware, such as GPUs and TPUs. Then, the extensive need for large volumes of labeled
038   data further compounds the challenges in model development. Collecting, curating, and annotating
039   vast amounts of data is a labor-intensive and resource-intensive task.
040
      Fine-tuning has become a prevalent strategy to address the prohibitive expenses of foundational
041
      model training. This approach leverages pre-existing models and adjusts them to better suit specific
042   tasks, reducing the need for extensive training and computational resources. Full fine-tuning (FFT)
043   involves updating all the parameters of a pre-trained model, allowing the model to fully adapt to
044   the new task, potentially achieving high performance. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) has
045   been introduced to further reduce computational expenses. PEFT concentrates on updating a limited
046   subset of parameters or incorporating additional lightweight modules, thereby achieving efficiency
047   gains. Notably, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2021) stands out as a prevailing approach
048   that fine-tunes large language models by introducing low-rank matrices to the existing weight matri-
049   ces, allowing for significant reductions in the number of trainable parameters. This method not only
050
      reduces the computational burden but also minimizes the risk of overfitting.
051   Although fine-tuning provides substantial advantages, it still demands costly training. Whether
052   executed through FFT or PEFT, the fine-tuning process inherently incurs computational overhead.
053   This is due to the need to update and optimize model parameters for tailored tasks. Moreover, even
      with PEFT methods like LoRA, which aims to reduce the number of trainable parameters, the need

054
      for labeled data remains a critical bottleneck. Labeled data is essential for fine-tuning models, as it
055   provides the necessary supervision to guide the learning process. In domains like medicine Kebaili
056   et al. (2023), low-resource language Magueresse et al. (2020); Ranathunga et al. (2023), and rare
057   objection detection Wang et al. (2020b); Minderer et al. (2024), high-quality labeled data is scarce,
058   further complicating the fine-tuning process and limiting the applicability of these techniques.
059
      Unsupervised fine-tuning refers to fine-tuning using unlabeled data. This learning approach lever-
060
      ages the intrinsic structure of the data to infer patterns, relationships, and representations without
061   the guidance of explicit annotations Jaiswal et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021). The primary challenge
062   of unsupervised fine-tuning lies in the ambiguity of the optimization direction and the design of the
063   loss function Fang et al. (2024). It is difficult to evaluate the performance of unsupervised models
064   due to the absence of ground truth labels. This makes it hard to assess the quality and applica-
065   bility of the learned representations. Additionally, unsupervised models may struggle to capture
066   relevant features for specific tasks, resulting in suboptimal performance compared to the supervised
067   schemes Heckler et al. (2023).
068   In this paper, we propose a novel unsupervised fine-tuning method known as model-driven fine-
069   tuning, which harnesses existing models without needing labeled data. Two stages are included in
070   our proposed method. First, we propose a novel method for meta-model construction leveraging pre-
071   existing fine-tuned models within the model pool using an unsupervised approach. Specifically, we
072   propose an effective unsupervised learning method based on information entropy, which addresses
073   the issues of entropy minimization’s error accumulation and sensitivity to harmful samples. This
074   method successfully synthesizes a general meta-model, achieving efficient versatility. Secondly, we
075
      refine the meta-model to enhance its specialization. This is achieved by conducting an unsupervised
      adaptation to minimize the disparity in representations between the meta-model and the original fine-
076
      tuned model. Experimental results demonstrate that the meta-model can be effectively fine-tuned
077
      with just several dozen steps of representation alignment, surpassing the performance of models
078   fine-tuned with full parameters, all without the involvement of any labeled data.
079
080   Contributions. (1) We introduce a pioneering unsupervised fine-tuning method termed model-
081
      driven fine-tuning. This framework innovatively utilizes pre-existing trained models to perform
      fine-tuning tasks without relying on labeled data, thereby overcoming the limitations of traditional
082
      supervised learning methods. (2) We propose a novel loss function designed specifically for un-
083
      supervised fine-tuning. This loss function is grounded in information entropy principles, adeptly
084   addressing challenges of error accumulation in entropy minimization. It can be used to build a gen-
085   eral model in an unsupervised way. (3) Experimental results demonstrate that our method matches
086   and even exceeds the performance of full-parameter fine-tuning without labeled data. Furthermore,
087   in a multi-task learning context, our model-driven approach has been shown to outperform the state-
088   of-the-art models, underscoring its superiority in handling complex, real-world scenarios without
089   the need for labeled data.

092   2    R ELATED W ORKS
093
094
095   Supervised Fine-tuning. Supervised Fine-tuning has emerged as a pivotal technique for adapting
096   pre-trained language models to specific tasks or domains. This allows the model to specialize and
097   enhance its performance on that task while retaining the broad knowledge it gained during pre-
098   training. Full Parameter Fine-tuning (FFT) involves updating all the parameters of the pre-trained
099
      model. This approach can lead to significant performance improvements as it allows the model to
      fully adapt to the new task. However, it is computationally expensive and requires much labeled
100
      data. FFT is beneficial when there are sufficient data and computational resources. Still, it may not
101
      be practical for all the applications due to its high cost and the risk of overfitting when limited data is
102   available. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, on the other hand, fine-tune only a small subset
103   of the model’s parameters. This approach is designed to be more resource-efficient and to reduce
104   the risk of catastrophic forgetting, where the model loses previously learned knowledge. PEFT
105   can be classified into four main categories: additive fine-tuning, partial fine-tuning, reparameterized
106   fine-tuning, and hybrid fine-tuning, as detailed in Xu et al. (2023). Within these categories, classic
107   methods include Adapter Houlsby et al. (2019), Prompt-tuning Lester et al. (2021), BitFit Zaken
      et al. (2021), and FISH MASK Sung et al. (2021) for additive fine-tuning; LoRA Hu et al. (2021)


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and QLoRA Dettmers et al. (2024) for reparameterized fine-tuning; and UniPELT Mao et al. (2021)
109   and AutoPEFT Zhou et al. (2024) for hybrid fine-tuning.
110
111   Unsupervised Fine-tuning. Unsupervised fine-tuning Huang et al. (2020); Tanwisuth et al. (2023)
112
      is a distinctive technique that enhances model performance by integrating both labeled and unla-
      beled data. Self-supervised learning (SSL) Hinton & Salakhutdinov (2006) trains models to predict
113
      aspects of the input data itself, without relying on external labels. While it effectively utilizes redun-
114
      dant unlabeled data, SSL faces challenges in learning imperfect representations and necessitates the
115   meticulous design of pretext tasks Gidaris et al. (2018). The entropy-minimization method adjusts
116   the model based on the current information entropy, offering simplicity but suffering from error ac-
117   cumulation and potential degradation after numerous optimization steps. Contrastive learning Wang
118   & Qi (2022); Jaiswal et al. (2020), a prevalent SSL method, focuses on learning representations
119   where similar examples are closely grouped and dissimilar examples are widely separated in the
120   embedding space. This approach yields robust features but is sensitive to data augmentation choices
121   and demands substantial computational resources Wu et al. (2024). Cluster-based methods Zhou
122   et al. (2003) group unlabeled data into clusters based on data distribution, using these groupings as
123
      pseudo-labels to guide the learning process. Although naive and capable of revealing inherent data
      structures, these methods are sensitive to the choice of distance metrics Yang et al. (2016). None
124
      of the aforementioned unsupervised fine-tuning methods are capable of fine-tuning and enhancing
125
      model performance with limited unlabeled data.

      task-specific or layer-specific. Subsequently, we introduce an innovative unsupervised optimization

      indices, having assimilated the versatile capability from the constituent models. In the second stage,
153
      we undertake the model-driven fine-tuning facilitating specialization from the general meta-model.
154   Precisely, we fine-tune the meta-model by aligning its representations with those of a pre-existing
155   fine-tuned model, thereby enabling a tailored specialization.

      by only 0.2% and 0.3%, respectively, as these are simple tasks that have been extensively trained.

480   5     C ONCLUSION
481
482   Supervised fine-tuning is computationally expensive and often necessitates much labeled data,
483   whereas unsupervised fine-tuning faces challenges in providing effective guidance and designing
484   appropriate supervision. In this paper, we introduce a novel model-driven labeled free fine-tuning
485   method, which allows fine-tuning and boosting models without labeled data and extensive train-
      ing. Leveraging the collective intelligence of diverse models, our two-phase approach significantly


                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      enhances individual model performance by creating a general meta-model and subsequently fine-
487   tuning the original model in a few shots. Our method, which operates independently of any super-
488   vised data, surpasses the performance of supervised full-parameter fine-tuning. Our experimental
489   results demonstrate that this model-driven approach offers a cost-effective and scalable alternative
490   to traditional supervised fine-tuning. It effectively addresses the challenge of data scarcity, making
491   fine-tuning techniques more accessible and practical in environments where labeled data is limited
492   or inaccessible. This breakthrough paves the way for future research in unsupervised fine-tuning.
