011                                                           A BSTRACT
012
013            Point cloud detection is crucial in applications such as autonomous driving systems and robotics.
014
               These systems utilize onboard LiDAR sensors to capture input point clouds, consisting of numerous
               three-dimensional coordinates and their corresponding intensity of laser reflection. Recent studies have
015
               proposed various adversarial schemes to highlight the vulnerability of point cloud detectors. However,
016
               these studies primarily focused on generating or perturbing the coordinate positions of input points and
017            are hard to attack in the physical world, while largely overlooking the significance of their intensity.
018            Through our exploration, we found that perturbing point cloud intensity poses significant security
019            risks for point cloud object detectors. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to attack on point
020            cloud intensity and we propose an effective adversarial attack scheme, named I-ADV. Our method
021            employs a voxel partition scheme to enhance physical implementation. To boost attack performance,
022            we incorporate a gradient enhancement technique using 3D angle and distance features, along with an
023            extremum-based gradient fusion strategy. Extensive experimental results demonstrate that by altering
024            only point cloud intensity, our approach achieves state-of-the-art performance across detectors with
025
               various input representations, attaining attack success rates between 83.9% and 99.1%. Comprehensive
               ablation studies confirm the effectiveness and generality of the method’s components. Additionally,
026
               comparing different attack schemes underscores the advantages of our point cloud intensity attack
027
               method in both performance and real-world applicability.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The LiDAR-based point cloud object detectors are extensively used in applications such as autonomous driving systems
033   and robotics (Guo et al. (2021)). They rely on onboard LiDAR sensors to capture precise point clouds as inputs, which
034   typically consist of three-dimensional coordinates and their corresponding point cloud intensity of the laser reflections.
035   The point cloud intensity indicates the strength of reflectivity of the objects’ surfaces (Sun et al. (2020)), and it has become
036
      essential information for various tasks like road detection (Caltagirone et al. (2017)), segmentation (Tatoglu & Pochiraju
      (2012)), localization (Aldibaja et al. (2017)), and so on.
037
038   Recent studies (Szegedy et al. (2014); Goodfellow et al. (2015)) have shown that deep learning models are vulnerable
039   to adversarial attacks. Attackers can induce errors in the system by adding customized perturbations to the input data,
040   posing significant security risks in scenarios such as autonomous driving. Research on the adversarial vulnerability of
041   point cloud detection can be broadly categorized into two types. The first type focuses on sensor-level attacks (Cao
042
      et al. (2019a; 2023); Jin et al. (2023)), where attackers exploit the working principles of LiDAR sensors by forging
      laser echo signals to create or hiding point clouds. The second type involves algorithm-level attacks, where attackers
043
      optimize corresponding adversarial samples targeting the detection algorithms. They obtain the coordinate perturbations
044
      by designing an IOU (Intersection over Union) loss (Cai et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2021)), maximizing LiDAR detection
045   runtime (Liu et al. (2023)) and searching for the critical adversarial locations (Zhu et al. (2021)). Additionally, some
046   studies utilize a differentiable LiDAR rendering algorithm (Möller & Trumbore (2005)) to optimize a universal adversarial
047   object, aiming to hide target cars (Tu et al. (2020)), obscure obstacles (Cao et al. (2019b)), or create fake targets (Yang
048   et al. (2021)).
049
      However, to the best of our knowledge, existing research on adversarial vulnerabilities has primarily focused on
050
      manipulating the position coordinates of point cloud data, overlooking the widely used reflectivity intensity. First, our
051   exploration indicates that replacing the original point cloud intensity of vehicles with random noise leads to an average of
052   34.5% of objects becoming undetectable across different detectors (see Table 1). This reveals that point cloud intensity
053   is crucial for detection performance, and even random interference can significantly hinder recognition. Furthermore,
054   unlike traditional adversarial attacks that disrupt the geometric shape of objects by modifying point cloud coordinates,
055   developing an efficient attack method that relies solely on altering point cloud intensity presents a novel challenge. Lastly,
      point cloud intensity adversarial attacks possess a distinct advantage in terms of physical realizability, as attackers can

078   In this paper, we introduce the first adversarial attack scheme targeting point cloud intensity, namely I-ADV. To enhance

080   incorporate 3D angle features and distance features to enhance the gradient of each point cloud. Additionally, we design
081   an extremum-based gradient fusion strategy to update the point cloud intensity within each voxel. Extensive experimental
082   comparisons show that I-ADV achieves state-of-the-art attack performance against various baseline algorithms across
083   different detector input representations, with an impressive attack success rate of up to 99.1%. This highlights the
084
      significant security threats posed by point cloud intensity attacks in real-world scenarios. In summary, we make the
      following three contributions:
085
086             • Unlike previous works that modify point cloud coordinates, we are the first to demonstrate that altering point
087               cloud intensity can significantly impact the detection capabilities of point cloud object detectors, highlighting
088               the importance of addressing adversarial robustness concerning point cloud intensity.
089
                • We introduce I-ADV, the first attack scheme targeting point cloud intensity. This approach ensures physical real-
090               izability through a voxel partitioning-based scheme, along with novel gradient enhancement techniques utilizing
091               3D angle and distance features, and an extremum-based gradient fusion strategy to boost the performance.
092
                • Extensive experiments validate our algorithm’s superior attack performance across various input representatives
093               of detectors. Comprehensive ablation studies confirm the effectiveness of our method from multiple perspectives,
094               e.g., component contributions and transferability. Additionally, discussions of different types of attack algorithms
095               underscore the advantages of point cloud intensity attacks in terms of performance and real-world applicability.

104   2.1      ATTACKS ON I MAGES
105
106   Adversarial attacks on image-based deep learning models exploit vulnerabilities through small, often imperceptible
107
      perturbations. One of the earliest attacks is the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) (Goodfellow et al. (2015)), which
      perturbs images along the gradient of the loss function concerning the input image. Iterative Fast Gradient Method
108
      (I-FGSM) (Kurakin et al. (2018)) further improves FGSM by using multiple iterations, while MI-FGSM (Dong et al.
109
      (2018)) adds momentum to stabilize updates and escape local maxima. The Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) (Madry
110   et al. (2018)) enhances the iterative attacks by introducing random initialization of perturbation. Adversarial attacks

      are categorized into white-box attacks (Li et al. (2021a)) and black-box attacks (Chen et al. (2017)), based on the level
113   of access the attacker has to the target model. Of these two classifications, white-box attacks enable the generation
114   of highly effective adversarial examples by directly manipulating the model’s gradients to maximize prediction errors,

119   2.2   ATTACKS ON POINT CLOUD DETECTORS
120
121   Sensor-level attacks. Cao et al. (2019a) formulates the control strategy of spoofed points as an optimization problem
122   to deceive the machine learning model through sensor attacks. In another work, Cao et al. (2023) makes the obstacles
123
      undetectable by leveraging the inherent automatic transformation and filtering processes of LiDAR sensors. Jin et al.
      (2023) explores the possibility of physically deceiving LiDAR-based detectors by injecting recorded or optimized point
124
      clouds using lasers.
125
126   Algorithm-level attacks. Some studies achieve the attack by focusing on the modification of point cloud coordinates.
127   For instance, Wang et al. (2021) achieves coordinate perturbation for the car category by using an IOU (Intersection over
128   Union) loss and imperceptible loss, significantly degrading the performance of the detection models. Liu et al. (2023)
129   addresses the non-differentiable issue for the detectors and designs a novel loss to minimize the modifications while
130   maximizing the runtime of the detection pipeline. Wang et al. (2023) spoofs fake obstacles at arbitrary locations by
131   perturbing point cloud coordinates along each direction of the laser ray. Zhu et al. (2021) generates critical adversarial
132   locations to fool the LiDAR perception system by designing heuristic location probing and location selection algorithms.
133
      Moreover, other studies insert a mesh object by a differentiable LiDAR rendering algorithm (Möller & Trumbore (2005))
      and optimize the object’s shape. For example, Cao et al. (2019b) explores the vulnerabilities of an industrial-level LiDAR-
134
      based autonomous driving system by proposing an optimization-based approach to generate 3D printable adversarial
135
      objects. Tu et al. (2020) generates a universal adversarial object that is equipped on the rooftop of target vehicles and
136   makes them undetectable. Yang et al. (2021) optimizes a roadside adversarial object that will be recognized as vehicles
137   invading the lane. However, there is currently no dedicated attack scheme targeting point cloud intensity. Due to the
138   distinct nature of modifying intensity as the attack target, developing an effective new method is urgently needed. This
139   paper addresses that gap.

151                                   X∗ = X + δ
152                                                                                                                           (2)
                                         = {Pn |Pn = (pcoor , pi + δi )n , n = 1, ..., N }.

155   formulate the purpose of the intensity attacks as the following optimization problem:
156                                        F(X) ̸= F(X ∗ ), s.t. D(X − X ∗ ) < η,                                             (3)

251
252   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
253
254   Datasets and Target Models. The KITTI dataset (Geiger et al. (2012)) is a widely-used benchmark for autonomous
255   driving research, offering 3712 training samples, 3769 validation samples, and 7518 test samples. It includes LiDAR
256   point clouds along with 3D bounding box annotations for various objects at three difficulty levels: Easy, Moderate, and
257   Hard. To ensure comprehensive coverage, we selected three types of both classic and recent point cloud object detectors
258
      as our target networks: (1) point-based detectors, including PointRCNN (Shi et al. (2019)) and IA-SSD (Zhang et al.
      (2022)); (2) voxel-based detectors, such as PointPillar (Lang et al. (2019)) and Voxel R-CNN (Deng et al. (2021)); and (3)
259
      point-voxel-based detectors, including PV-RCNN (Shi et al. (2020)) and PDV (Hu et al. (2022)).
260
261
      Evaluation Metrics. Our evaluation metric is attack success rate (ASR), which measures the percentage of instances
262   where a target object is initially detected but fails to be detected after the attack. In our experiments, we specifically target
263   the "Car" class and treat each object within a scene as an individual sample. A car is considered successfully detected if
264   the resulting 3D IoU (Intersection over Union) exceeds 0.7.
265
266   Comparison Baselines. We primarily consider two types of iterative attacks as comparison methods. The first category
267   is point-wise modification attack, including uniform random noise, PGD (Madry et al. (2018)), MI-FGSM (Dong et al.
268   (2018)), and NI-FGSM (Lin et al. (2020)). The second category focuses on voxel-wise modifications, where the voxel
269   serves as the smallest modification unit, and the modification direction is determined through a gradient fusion strategy.
270   These voxel-based approaches are further divided according to how the gradient fusion result is obtained, including (1)
271
      the Voxel-Random method, where the gradient of a randomly chosen point within the voxel is used as the fused gradient;
      (2) Voxel-Average method, which uses the average of all gradients within the voxel; and (3) Voxel-Voting method, where
272
      the update direction is decided by majority rule, based on counting the number of positive and negative values of all
273
      gradients within the voxel.
274
275   Implementation Details. In our experiments, to ensure a fair comparison, all attack methods were executed for 10
276   iterations. Note that the point cloud intensity ranges from [0, 1]. The intensity is updated by 0.2 per iteration, with a
277   maximum perturbation limit of 1, as any intensity magnitudes within this range are commonly seen and achievable in the
278   real world (refer to Figure 1). In our approach, the voxel size Vsize for the voxel partition is set to be a cube with an edge
279   length of 0.1 meters, the decay factor µ is set to 1.0, the hyper-parameter λ is set to 1000, and the maximum detection
      range dM AX is 75 meters.

308
309   The ASR (Attack Success Rate) evaluation results for different detection models under various attack algorithms are
310   presented in Table 1. Based on these results, we make the following key observations: (1) Random noise method:
311   Optimization-based methods significantly outperform the random noise approach, demonstrating that learning adversarial
312
      intensity distributions through algorithmic optimization provides a clear advantage. (2) Different detector architectures:
      The vulnerability of voxel-based and point-voxel-based detectors is relatively similar, but point-based detectors show
313
      polarized performance. Specifically, PointRCNN proves highly resilient to attacks, while IA-SSD is the most vulnerable,
314
      indicating that despite IA-SSD’s efficiency in detection, it sacrifices robustness. (3) Point-wise vs. voxel-wise optimization:
315   There is no clear winner between point-wise and voxel-wise methods. However, the voting-based method consistently
316   outperforms other baselines, suggesting that intelligently fusing gradients enhances attack success. (4) Our method:
317   Our approach stands out across all detection networks, achieving ASR between 87.3% and 99.1%, highlighting the
318   effectiveness of our gradient enhancement and fusion strategy.

320   4.3   A BLATION S TUDY
321
322   Components of our method. In our method, gradient enhancement techniques, including the utilization of 3D angle
323   features and distance features, and the extremum-based gradient fusion strategy are the key components. Here, we
324   separately validate these components as shown in Table 2. From Table 2, it is evident that using either 3D angle features
325   or distance features alone results in an ASR improvement of 1.3–2.0% and 1.2–1.9%, respectively. When both features
326   are combined, the improvement increases to 1.7–2.8%. Notably, the extremum-based gradient fusion strategy alone yields
327
      a substantial boost of 39.9–47.1%. When this strategy is combined with gradient enhancement, the ASR further increases
      to 40.2–48.1%. It indicates that the proposed fusion strategy is essential for achieving successful attacks, while gradient
328
      enhancement provides an additional performance boost.
329
330
      Voxel size and perturbation range. In addition to gradient enhancement and fusion, we found that voxel size during
331   partition and the maximum perturbation limit significantly impact attack performance. The ablation studies for these
332   factors are shown in Figure 3. As demonstrated in Figure 3(a), smaller voxel sizes consistently lead to better ASR
333   performance, suggesting that finer voxel granularity enhances optimization in our method. However, as voxel size
334   increases (e.g., to 0.5 m), the ASR starts to plateau. Moreover, Figure 3(b) also shows that larger perturbation limits result
335   in higher ASR performance, highlighting that perturbation amplitude is one of the key factors for adversarial attacks on
      point cloud intensity.

476   From the table, two key observations can be made. First, in terms of performance, the method involving the insertion of a
477   mesh object is the least effective, which we attribute to its partial augmentation of the target object’s geometric profile,
478   resulting in limited attack capability. On the other hand, attack methods based on modifying point cloud coordinates
479
      achieve better results, as they can fully disrupt the geometric shape of the target object, making it more difficult for the
      detector to recognize. Our method, by modifying point cloud intensity, demonstrates the strongest attack performance,
480
      which we argue is due to the detector’s strong reliance on the distribution characteristics of point cloud intensity, making
481
      intensity modifications particularly impactful. Second, regarding physical applicability, the insertion of mesh objects
482   requires the 3D printing of large adversarial objects, which is costly. Modifying point cloud coordinates, while effective,
483   is difficult to achieve in a physical context. In contrast, our method can be realized by applying stickers with varying
484   reflective intensity to the surface of objects, offering both physical feasibility and low cost.
485
486   Limitations and future work. Despite the innovation of our method, there are some unresolved issues as follows. (1)
487   Physical exploration: We modeled the point cloud intensity attacks using physically feasible methods. While the intensity
488   distribution generated by the attacks is broad, determining which materials can adjust the intensity by specific magnitudes
489   in the real world requires further engineering exploration. (2) Limited attack variability: The performance of adversarial
490   attacks under combined attacks targeting both intensity and coordinates, remains unexplored due to the lack of unified
491   standards for perturbation magnitudes. (3) Focus on LiDAR-only systems: Multi-sensor fusion allows the system to
492   leverage both the rich visual information from cameras and the precise spatial data from LiDAR. The potential for jointly
493
      optimizing adversarial perturbations across both image texture patches and point cloud intensity remains unexplored.
494
495   6   C ONCLUSION
496
497
      In this paper, we have highlighted the critical role of point cloud intensity in the adversarial attack on LiDAR-based
498
      object detectors, addressing a significant gap in the existing literature on adversarial vulnerabilities. By introducing
499   I-ADV, the first attack scheme dedicated to modifying point cloud intensity, we have demonstrated the outstanding
500   impact these alterations can have on detection capabilities. Our innovative optimization framework, which incorporates
501   voxel partition, enhanced gradient techniques and gradient fusion strategy, achieves state-of-the-art attack performance.
502   Extensive comparisons validate the effectiveness, generality, and real-world feasibility of our approach. This research
503   emphasizes the urgent security threats posed by intensity-based adversarial attacks in real-world applications such as
      autonomous driving.
