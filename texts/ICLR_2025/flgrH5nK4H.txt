011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Isolated Sign Language Recognition (ISLR) is crucial for scalable sign language
014            technology, yet language-specific approaches limit current models. To address
015            this, we propose a one-shot learning approach that generalises across languages
016
               and evolving vocabularies. Our method involves pretraining a model to embed
               signs based on essential features and using a dense vector search for rapid, ac-
017
               curate recognition of unseen signs. We achieve state-of-the-art results, including
018
               50.8% one-shot MRR on a large dictionary containing 10,235 unique signs from
019            a different language than the training set. Our approach is robust across languages
020            and support sets, offering a scalable, adaptable solution for ISLR. Co-created with
021            the Deaf and Hard of Hearing (DHH) community, this method aligns with real-
022            world needs, and advances scalable sign language recognition.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Isolated sign language recognition (ISLR) is a crucial first step toward achieving full sign language
028   translation (SLT). Despite notable progress, most advances in ISLR have been confined to specific
029   languages and respective datasets, limiting the range of vocabularies that can be recognised in real-
030   world applications. In addition, with sign languages constantly evolving, this limitation becomes
031   increasingly problematic. More flexible approaches are required – ones that allow for the expansion
032
      of vocabularies and are robust enough to handle large-scale dictionaries for SLT.
033   To overcome these challenges, it is essential to shift the focus away from predefined vocabularies and
034   instead emphasise the intrinsic features of signs. Once an effective representation of a specific sign
035   is established, the surrounding context can be integrated afterwards. An effective sign representation
036   requires two key elements: (1) each sign is uniquely identifiable, and (2) only the essential features,
037   i.e. sign phonemes, are captured to ensure reliable discrimination and adaptability across varied
038
      signing conditions.
039   Following this idea, we propose leveraging one-shot learning for sign language recognition. One-
040   shot learning is particularly well-suited for sign language recognition since it allows the model to
041   generalise from limited examples, making it possible to recognise new signs without extensive re-
042   training or data collection. By focusing on embedding signs efficiently, this method enables accurate,
043   context- and even language-independent isolated sign recognition.
044   Our approach consists of two steps: (1) pretraining a model that can reliably embed signs, and (2)
045   using this frozen pretrained model for a dense vector search. In the first step, we train the model on a
046   diverse set of signs to capture their essential features. This creates embeddings representing signs as
047   points in a high-dimensional space. In the second step, a dense vector search matches new, unseen
048   variations of signs to the closest embedding in this space, enabling rapid and accurate recognition
049   without requiring further retraining.
050   We evaluate both the pretraining and one-shot classification, achieving state-of-the-art results. No-
051   tably, we attain a 50.8% one-shot MRR on an extremely large dictionary from a language entirely
052   different from the pretraining data. Our method also demonstrates strong robustness across mul-
053   tiple languages, even when introducing variations in the set of exemplary signs. These findings
      underscore that ISLR systems can be generalised across languages, independent of the pretraining

      data. This enables recognition of vast vocabularies while adapting to the evolving nature of sign
055   languages. This adaptability positions our approach as a progressive solution for enhancing the
056   scalability and effectiveness of sign language technology in diverse contexts.

074   2.1       I SOLATED S IGN L ANGUAGE R ECOGNITION
075
      ISLR is a classification problem. Traditionally, a system analyses a video depicting an isolated
076
      sign and aims to predict the corresponding label or gloss. These input videos can be processed
077   in several ways. Recent advancements (Papadimitriou & Potamianos, 2023; Chen et al., 2022)
078   have demonstrated a positive impact using pose estimation models, such as MediaPipe Holistic
079   (Grishchenko & Bazarevsky, 2020) (henceforth MediaPipe) and OpenPose (Cao et al., 2017). These
080   models transform input videos into sequences of skeletal representations, capturing “keypoints” or
081   “landmarks” of the human pose in 2D or 3D Cartesian coordinates. By removing all information
082   about a person’s appearance, these tools enhance the generalisability to downstream tasks. This
083   transformation allows ISLR models to focus solely on the structural aspect of sign videos: how
084   people move their arms, hands, and face.
085   MediaPipe has significantly advanced the current state-of-the-art of ISLR, but there is still consider-
086   able room for improvement. Moryossef et al. (2021) argued that this tool is not directly applicable to
087   fine-grained tasks like sign language recognition. Although the keypoint estimator is generally ac-
088   curate, MediaPipe struggles when two body parts interact. Since this interaction is elemental to sign
089   language, crucial information is lost. However, recent Kaggle competitions (Chow et al., 2023b;a)
090   based on keypoint estimation using MediaPipe present a different perspective, showing promising
091
      results in SLR using keypoint estimation. A key component appears to be the addition of a frame
      embedding that is not present in the work by Moryossef et al., but present in all top Kaggle competi-
092
      tion entries. This frame embedding allows the network to learn the non-linear relationships between
093
      keypoints (De Coster et al., 2023).
094
095   Not only the preprocessing of sign language videos is essential. The architecture of the models
096   also has a great impact. Until 2020, deep learning approaches to ISLR primarily used variations of
097
      Recurrent Neural Networks (Koller et al., 2016; 2017; Ye et al., 2018). The common factor of these
      models is that they are proficient at handling sequential data and dealing with temporal dependencies
098
      between different poses. The introduction of transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) in 2017 initiated a
099
      paradigm shift. The combination of keypoints and attention leads to powerful models for ISLR: the
100   top scoring on the Kaggle ASL ISLR competition (Chow et al., 2023b) method achieved 89.3% test
101   set accuracy on 250 sign categories using keypoint data.

      2.2       F EW-S HOT S IGN L ANGUAGE R ECOGNITION
104
105   The evolving nature of sign languages motivates the need for flexible ISLR techniques. The tradi-
106   tional sign language classification models described in the previous section lack this flexibility, as

      they are limited to the glossary provided in the training set and require a large number of examples
109   for every sign. Fei-Fei et al. (2006) argued that “one can take advantage of knowledge coming from
110   previously learned categories, no matter how different these categories might be.” This insight led
111   to the introduction of few-shot learning, where fewer examples per category are required.
112
      There are various gradations to few-shot learning, including zero-shot learning. In zero-shot learn-
113
      ing, the model has never seen an example for a given category and relies on its prior knowledge and
114
      some form of description of the category. Zero-shot learning was first introduced to SLR by Bilge
115   et al. (2019). Their method involved matching textual descriptions to video inputs, utilising BERT
116   text embeddings (Devlin et al., 2018) and 3D CNNs alongside bidirectional LSTMs for video pro-
117   cessing. More recently, Rastgoo et al. (2021) approached zero-shot ISLR with a similar technique.
118   This method combined BERT text embeddings with pose estimators and vision transformers, fol-
119   lowed by LSTMs for temporal modelling. These zero-shot methods rely on the presence of a textual
120   description of a sign, which is not always available.
121   More often, there are one or multiple example videos available for one sign, which can be used
122   for few-shot learning. If none are available for a given sign, recording one example is easier than
123   accurately describing the sign through text. Wang et al. (2021), for example, leverage multiple
124   examples (i.e., they perform few-shot learning) of one sign to perform K-means clustering and a
125   custom matching algorithm. For some sign languages, a dictionary is available. In the case of VGT,
126   the dictionary contains exactly one example per unique sign, which is ideal for one-shot learning.
127   De Coster & Dambre (2023) in essence performed one-shot learning to recognise signs in the VGT
128   dictionary (Van Herreweghe et al., 2004). In their work, a pretrained model outputs embeddings,
129
      which are used in a Euclidean distance-based vector search. However, despite being pretrained on
      VGT data, the model’s performance on dictionary lookup was suboptimal. This shortfall can be
130
      attributed to the Zipfian class distribution in the dataset and the fact that the examples come from
131
      continuous signing rather than isolated signs.

      3.1   O NE -S HOT S IGN L ANGUAGE R ECOGNITION
137
138   Our one-shot inference exists out of two steps, namely: initialisation and inference. These steps
139   are illustrated in fig. 1. In the initialisation step – illustrated with solid lines – the frozen model
140   converts all dictionary videos to embeddings. This collection of embeddings is known as the support
141   set, which serves as a reference for subsequent comparisons during the inference phase. During the
142   inference step – depicted with dashed lines – the model processes an input query video and generates
143   its corresponding embedding. This embedding is then compared against the support set to determine
144   the most similar entry. The comparison utilises the attention mechanism as described by (Bahdanau
145   et al., 2014), which effectively identifies the closest match from the support set solely based on the
146
      embeddings.
147   In essence, this method performs search as described by Mittal et al. (2021), who formulated it as
148   the “selection of a relevant entity from a set via query-key interactions”. In our case, the queries
149   and keys are the query videos and the support set respectively. Furthermore, this approach applies
150   the softmax function, and hence the output can be interpreted as probabilities. These probabilities
151   provide a measure of confidence in the match, which in turn allows for more nuanced decision-
152
      making, ultimately improving the system’s interpretability and reliability.
153   We evaluate the one-shot classification method using two approaches. The first evaluation involves a
154   limited query set with a large support set, assessing the model’s ability to handle applications requir-
155   ing extensive vocabularies, such as SLT. The second experiment introduces variability by randomly
156   selecting different instances from a sign language dataset multiple times, effectively reconstructing
157   the same classes within the support set. By measuring the spread of evaluation metrics across these
158
      variations, we investigate how the variability within sign categories and the selection of support set
      instances influence our approach.
159
160   Although this one-shot technique is remarkably elegant, the performance heavily relies on how
161   the manual sign features are represented within the embedding. Two main factors independently
      impact this objective: the pretraining and the utilised datasets. We employ isolated sign language

192   3.2     DATASETS
193
194   Several kinds of datasets are required to perform our experiments. First, we need one or more
195   pretraining datasets, on which we can train a model for ISLR. Second, datasets for the one-shot
196   ISLR evaluation are needed. These datasets include both real-world sign language dictionaries and
197   existing isolated sign language datasets that we have adapted for the one-shot task.
198
199   3.2.1    P RETRAINING DATASETS
200
201   One primary example of one-shot ISLR is dictionary retrieval, like the study by De Coster & Dambre
202   (2023). To facilitate comparisons with this work, the same pretraining dataset is utilised. This
203   dataset is derived from the VGT corpus (Van Herreweghe et al., 2015). However, the limited num-
204   ber of classes and the severe class imbalance in this dataset may have contributed to suboptimal
205   performance. Therefore, we look for a larger and more varied dataset for ISLR: we choose ASL
206
      Citizen (Desai et al., 2024) for its sizeable vocabulary and because DHH signers recorded the signs.
207   The VGT dataset consists of 24,967 examples for 292 signs, but these examples are not uniformly
208   distributed (fig. 2a). Since the dataset is derived from a corpus that contains spontaneous language
209   use, the distribution is rather Zipfian. The majority of examples represent only a minority of the
210   signs. The examples are cut from continuous signing, which means that every sign execution is
211   influenced by preceding and subsequent signs, a phenomenon referred to as co-articulation.
212   The ASL Citizen dataset is richer, containing 83,399 examples for 2,731 signs. These examples are
213   more uniformly distributed, with an average of 30 instances per sign (fig. 2b). Since the dataset
214   entries were recorded in isolation, no co-articulation occurred. This more closely matches the task
215   of dictionary search. In fact, the ASL Citizen dataset was envisioned as a dataset of “in-the-wild
      dictionary queries” (Desai et al., 2024).

247   3.2.2              E VALUATION DATASETS
248
      One application of our work is enabling native and non-native signers to search through sign lan-
249
      guage dictionaries by recording or uploading a video of a sign. To evaluate the performance of a
250   dictionary search system (one-shot classifier) we always require two sets: dictionary entries, referred
251   to as the support set, and dictionary queries, which serve as test examples. Both sets consist of short
252   videos containing individual signs that mirror the entries found in the dictionary. Ideally, these signs
253   are performed in citation form, meaning that each sign starts and ends in a resting position.
254
      Due to sign language dialects and synonyms, multiple signs may correspond to a single word or
255
      concept. However, we do not consider this as the same sign and instead label signs by unique IDs.
256   In this work, we focus on retrieving the exact sign matching the query video, rather than its meaning
257   in spoken language.
258
259

      gories (fig. 3)2 . The 10 categories used in prior dictionary retrieval research (De Coster & Dambre,

261

262   10,235 unique signs at the time of writing. Like many other sign language dictionaries, every sign
263   has exactly one example, which is why we opt for one-shot ISLR.

      et al., 2020), which consist of Turkish and American Sign Language respectively. The WLASL

277   3.3   P RETRAINING : K EYPOINT- BASED S IGN L ANGUAGE R ECOGNITION
278
279   To perform one-shot sign language recognition, we first need to pretrain a sign language recognition
280   model. We choose to use a keypoint-based model. By performing human keypoint estimation on the
281   sign language videos, the sign language recognition task is facilitated. Moreover, we hypothesise
282   that using a keypoint-based model is beneficial to the one-shot classification task, because it reduces
283
      the impact of the visual properties (background, lighting, clothing, etc.) of the query and the dictio-
      nary videos, and it better aligns the input distributions of the data used in the pretraining step and
284
      the querying step. Another advantage of using keypoints is the ability to integrate publicly available
285
      estimators, such as MediaPipe, into client-side applications, ensuring both privacy and low-latency
286   communication.
287
288   More specifically, we choose to employ MediaPipe (Grishchenko & Bazarevsky, 2020) for the rea-
289
      sons listed in section 2.1. MediaPipe predicts the pose, hands and face keypoints. We only utilise the
      pose and hand information. Thus, our approach solely focuses on the manual components of signs:
290
      handshape, movement, place of articulation, and orientation. These manual features are transferable
291
      across sign languages. We also trained, optimised and tested embedding models that use mouth
292   keypoints, but the results for one-shot recovery were worse. This decrease can be attributed to the
293   fact that mouthings are more language-specific (Bank et al., 2015). Therefore, we do not deem them
294   essential to this work, but acknowledge their importance in broader sign language processing.
295
      We build on the architectural style of the SignON research project (Holmes et al., 2023), making
296
      slight modifications to the number of layers and blocks to better fit the requirements of our task. A
297
      schematic overview of this so-called PoseFormer network is shown in fig. 4. The network integrates
298   dense and convolutional blocks, followed by a multi-head attention mechanism.

339   3.4   E VALUATION M ETRICS
340
341   Several evaluation metrics are used throughout the entire pipeline to ensure a robust evaluation of
342   the model. First, we report the mean Recall@K for K ∈ [1, 5, 10]. Furthermore, two ranking
343   metrics are employed: mean reciprocal rank (MRR) and normalised discounted cumulative gain
344   (nDCG). Given that there is only one relevant item per prediction (its label), the interpretations of
345
      MRR and nDCG are similar in this scenario. The key difference is that the inverse of the MRR is the
      harmonic mean of the ranks of all predictions, which provides an alternative view of the results. For
346
      all three metrics, higher is better and a value of one indicates optimal performance. All three metrics
347
      – Recall@K, MRR, and nDCG – are used to evaluate the pretraining, to allow for comparison with
348   a baseline ISLR model. For the evaluation of the one-shot methods, reporting only the Recall@K
349   and the MRR is deemed sufficient, since the interpretation of MRR and nDCG is very similar.

455   5    D ISCUSSION
456
457   The results reveal four key insights. First, we confirm that keypoint-based models can achieve state-
458   of-the-art results on the challenging isolated sign language recognition task. By utilising keypoints,
459
      the one-shot classification task becomes more feasible, as the pose estimator eliminates visual differ-
      ences between datasets. Second, we demonstrate that the size, vocabulary, and class distribution of
460
      the dataset are critical for the pretraining phase, significantly influencing downstream performance
461
      in one-shot classification. Next, we find that alignment between the pretraining and downstream
462   languages is less important than these dataset characteristics. Finally, our results highlight that rep-
463   resenting individual signs, rather than relying on translations, is feasible and also crucial for creating
464   future-proof sign language technologies. We detail these insights below.
465
      Indeed, the keypoint-based PoseFormer achieves state-of-the-art results on the challenging large-
466
      vocabulary ASL Citizen dataset. It outperforms the I3D baseline by 0.120 Recall@1 (a 19% in-
467
      crease). Moreover, the model also transfers seamlessly to downstream tasks on different languages,
468   such as vector-based dictionary search for VGT, as our results illustrate.
469
470   The model’s performance in the one-shot setting depends on the richness of the pretraining data. Two
471
      specific traits of the ASL Citizen dataset enable the high performance of our one-shot classification
      approach. First, it encompasses a broad vocabulary of 2,731 unique glosses. This large and varied
472
      gloss set ensures exposure to a diverse range of signs, which is critical for robust model performance.
473
      Second, the ASL Citizen dataset maintains a uniform class distribution, offering sufficient examples
474   across different handshapes and movements. In contrast, the VGT Corpus (Van Herreweghe et al.,
475   2015), used in prior one-shot research (De Coster & Dambre, 2023), follows a Zipfian distribution,
476   where most samples feature the simple pointing handshape. As a result, models trained on VGT
477   struggle to generalise to unseen signs that involve more complex handshapes, as the dataset lacks the
478   necessary variety. This difference in data diversity explains the significantly higher performance of
479   our PF-ASL model compared to PF-VGT. The PoseFormer model, trained on ASL Citizen, benefits
480   from encountering a wide array of handshapes, enabling it to learn more transferable representations
481   for unknown signs.
482   The variety of the ASL Citizen dataset plays a crucial role in the success of our model in one-shot
483   classification. By ensuring exposure to a wide range of signs, the model minimises the chance of
484   encountering unfamiliar signs, leading to better generalisation. Importantly, our results demonstrate
485   that the diversity within a dataset is more critical than consistency in the language used during pre-
      training. Even though WLASL (Li et al., 2020) and ASL Citizen (Desai et al., 2024) both represent


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      ASL, their partially shared, but differing vocabularies yield markedly different results in pretraining
487   and one-shot classification. This suggests that limiting pretraining to a single dataset or language
488   may not capture the full complexity of an entire sign language, and future models should prioritise
489   dataset variety to enhance robustness in real-world applications.
490
      Sign representation plays a pivotal role in achieving robust performance in one-shot sign language
491
      classification. By focusing on pose-based embeddings, our approach abstracts away from surface-
492
      level visual differences, emphasising core elements of sign structure like handshapes, movement,
493   and orientation. This abstraction enables models to generalise across different languages and
494   datasets, which addresses the challenges posed by variations in signer appearance, environment,
495   and video quality. As sign languages evolve and new signs emerge, systems that rely on such repre-
496   sentations rather than static translations are better equipped to adapt and remain relevant, paving the
497   way for scalable and inclusive sign language technologies.

500   6   F UTURE WORK
501
502   Despite our considerable gains in ISLR performance and the first results of one-shot ISLR, there are
503   several promising directions for future research. One such direction involves leveraging the lexical
504   information from the ASL-LEX database (Caselli et al., 2017) when using the ASL-citizen dataset.
505   Additionally, extending the existing data with resources like the Sem-Lex benchmark dataset (Kezar
506   et al., 2023) could further enhance recognition performance. However, we argue that the key to
507
      achieving more robust sign recognition lies not just in more data but in the diversity of signs it
      contains. Therefore, multilingual training, where a shared sign representation is used to recognise
508
      signs across different languages, may be a more effective approach. Such an approach could vastly
509
      expand the potentially recognisable glossary of signs, contributing to more versatile and scalable
510   models.
511
512   Finally, the proposed method not only enables highly accurate dictionary search applications but
513
      also creates opportunities for other downstream tasks. As mentioned in section 3.1, Mittal et al.
      (2021) formally described search, but also introduced retrieval as the extraction of relevant features.
514
      We envision the combination of the search technique proposed in this paper for the retrieval of
515
      token embeddings for large language models. The integration into LLMs could facilitate a sign
516   language translation tool or even a sign language-enabled virtual assistant. In summary, due to its
517   effectiveness and lower data requirements, this technique has the potential to significantly advance
518   sign language research, which is currently constrained by data availability. This could lead to the
519   development of more practical tools for the DHH community in the near future.
520
521
522   7   C ONCLUSION
523
524   Sign language recognition models based on keypoints and self-attention, trained on large-vocabulary
525   datasets, can classify unknown signs in a different language with just one training example. We
526   leverage the proven PoseFormer model, pretrain it on the ASL Citizen dataset, and use it in a one-
527   shot classification setting by leveraging the attention mechanism in the embedding space of the
528   internal representations learnt by the PoseFormer. The PoseFormer achieves state-of-the-art sign
529   language recognition on the ASL Citizen dataset (a 19% increase in Recall@1 compared to previ-
530
      ous work) and on one-shot sign classification (0.508 MRR and 0.374 Recall@1 on 10,235 signs).
      For the first time, large vocabulary ISLR is enabled thanks to the one-shot classification approach.
531
      Furthermore, we prove that the method generalises to different languages and is independent of the
532
      used sign-variations inside the support set. Despite the multi-lingual evaluation, we leave the multi-
533   lingual pretraining for one-shot ISLR for future research. Finally, the results of this paper led to the
534   development of a publicly available dictionary look-up application for the DHH community.
