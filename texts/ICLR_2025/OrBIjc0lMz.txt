009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Eye gaze, encompassing fixations and saccades, offers valuable insights into hu-
012
               man intentions and future actions. This study presents a novel approach to enhanc-
013
               ing Vision Language Models (VLMs) for human action prediction by integrating
014            eye gaze data into egocentric video analysis. Existing methods for action pre-
015            diction in egocentric videos often rely solely on visual data, potentially missing
016            critical information provided by eye gaze. To address this limitation, we propose a
017            unique gaze-augmented framework that integrates eye gaze directly into the VLM
018            architecture and training process. By generating gaze heatmaps from eye gaze co-
019            ordinates, our model dynamically focuses on regions highlighted by gaze patterns.
020            Additionally, a gaze-regularization mechanism ensures the model maintains atten-
021            tion on gaze-allocated areas, thereby improving prediction accuracy and robust-
022
               ness. Our approach significantly enhances the model’s ability to generate precise
               and detailed predictions of future actions. Compared to baseline models with-
023
               out leveraging gaze data, our method achieves a nearly 13% improvement in the
024
               semantic score of predictions. This substantial improvement underscores the ef-
025            fectiveness and novelty of integrating eye gaze with a gaze-regularized attention
026            mechanism in VLMs for action prediction. Moreover, our work demonstrates that
027            incorporating eye gaze through this gaze-augmented framework can significantly
028            boost the predictive capabilities of VLMs, enhancing their potential in applica-
029            tions that require accurate human action prediction.

      1   INTRODUCTION
034
035   VLMs are foundation models that combine computer vision and natural language processing to
036   understand and generate both visual and textual information. Examples of such models include
037   ViLBERT, LXMERT, and CLIP (Lu et al., 2019a; Tan & Bansal, 2019; Radford et al., 2021). These
038   models can describe images, answer questions about visual content, and even generate images from
039
      textual descriptions. When oriented to predict future events and actions, VLMs have the potential
      to facilitate human-machine interaction in various applications like assistive robots (Li et al., 2024),
040
      accessibility for visually impaired individuals (Zhao et al., 2024b), and autonomous driving (Zhou
041
      et al., 2024), contributing to safer and more inclusive environments.
042
043   To achieve the full potential, we propose that the key is to endow VLMs with the capability for fine-
044   grained action prediction, which provides more actionable information to facilitate human-machine
045
      interaction. While coarse predictions might identify general activities like “brewing coffee,” fine-
      grained predictions specify detailed steps such as “reaching for the coffee capsule in the top-right
046
      cabinet, then filling the tank with the cup on the rack.” These detailed predictions provide useful
047
      insights that allow machines to assist more effectively (Goyal & Durrett, 2021). However, achiev-
048   ing accurate fine-grained predictions requires understanding the human agent’s short-term goals,
049   which, we propose, can be inferred from eye gaze patterns. On one hand, eye gaze reveals which
050   objects the human is focusing on or intends to interact with, making it a valuable component for
051   enhancing action prediction (Frischen et al., 2007),(Tipper, 2010). Moreover, humans often scan
052   their surroundings to collect information necessary to accomplish short-term goals before executing
053   an action. Thus, incorporating eye gaze into VLMs for action prediction provides insights into a
      person’s intentions, and shall lead to more accurate and reliable predictions of human activities.

079   In this study, we propose a novel gaze-augmented framework that significantly enhances fine-grained
080   action prediction by integrating eye gaze information into the VLM architecture and training pro-
081   cess. Our approach involves generating gaze heatmaps from eye gaze coordinates, which dynami-
082   cally guides the model’s focus to regions of interest highlighted by gaze patterns. Specifically, the
083   gaze-augmented framework employs a sophisticated attention mechanism where features derived
084
      from gaze-overlay images serve as queries in a Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021),
      while RGB image features act as keys and values. This method ensures that attention is calculated
085
      specifically to emphasize gaze-highlighted regions, which enhances the relevance of the extracted
086
      features. Furthermore, we introduce an explicit gaze-regularization mechanism that enforces the
087   model to consistently allocate attention to gaze-concentrated areas, thereby promoting the predic-
088   tive capability of the model.
089
090
      Our experimental results show that augmenting the VLM architecture with gaze information greatly
      improves its performance, with nearly a 13% increase in the semantic score of predictions compared
091
      to baseline models without gaze data. This improvement highlights the effectiveness of using eye
092
      gaze with a regularized attention mechanism for detailed action prediction. By experimenting with
093   both singular and aggregated gaze-augmented models, we validate the benefits of discounting noisy
094   and irrelevant eye gaze points, which enhances robustness in highly dynamic environments. Fur-
095   ther experiments with more detailed annotations show significant improvement in the performance
096   of gaze-augmented models (by nearly 12%), whereas the base model’s performance remains un-
097   changed. This indicates that finer-grained annotations can help strengthen and unleash the proposed
098   model’s potential to leverage gaze data for better action prediction. In summary, our contributions
099   are 1) A novel gaze-augmented VLM for fine-grained human action prediction; 2) A unique gaze-
100   regularized attention mechanism that guarantees effective training of the gaze-augmented VLMs; 3)
101
      An efficient gaze sanity check to ensure the relevance of the gaze information; and 4) An extensive
      study that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed framework and validates the significance
102
      of gaze information for human action prediction.

106
107   Action prediction and activity forecasting tasks Action prediction and activity forecasting tasks
      both aim to predict future human actions. While action prediction tasks focus on predicting immedi-


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ate actions given an input image sequence or video clip, activity forecasting emphasizes predicting
109   broader human activities over a longer time frame (Gao et al., 2017). However, both the tasks
110   share the same goal of understanding human behaviour and making reasonable predictions. These
111   tasks have been extensively studied, particularly through the challenges established by the Ego4D
112   benchmark (Grauman et al., 2022). Previous approaches have leveraged Long Short-Term Memory
113   (LSTM) networks and modality attention mechanisms (Furnari & Farinella, 2019) to predict future
114   actions. Other studies have combined bottom-up and top-down approaches to infer latent goals and
115   predict actions by modeling temporal dynamics (Zhao et al., 2024a). Mascaro et al. (2024) de-
116   composed the action prediction problem into predicting low-level actions derived from high-level
117
      intentions, which showed an improvement in prediction performance. Similarly,Ashutosh et al.
      (2023) presented a hierarchical approach for action anticipation by modeling both short-term low-
118
      level actions (“what the person is doing right now”) and high-level long-term intentions (“what the
119
      person wants to do”) using contrastive learning. Cho et al. (2024) highlighted the significance of
120   interactions between the next active object and human hands for short-term anticipation, predicting
121   the next active object first to model future interactions. Transformers and their variants have also
122   been employed to model interactions between objects and hands, enhancing the prediction of fu-
123   ture actions (Roy et al., 2024). In our work, we aim to predict future human actions in the form
124   of fine-grained and descriptive annotations by incorporating eye gaze as an additional modality and
125   employing a gaze-regularized attention mechanism.
126
127   Attention and gaze-augmented models Attention-based models facilitate the discovery of im-
128   portant features and enhance performance on downstream tasks, and have been applied to various
129   domains, including autonomous driving (Braunagel et al., 2017) , action prediction and human-
130
      computer interaction (Weber et al., 2020; Shafti et al., 2019; Aronson et al., 2018). For example -
      class activation maps have been used to leverage pooling layers in deep learning networks to generate
131
      class saliency maps (Sudhakaran & Lanz, 2018), allowing the model to focus on regions contain-
132
      ing objects that correlate with the activity being considered for action anticipation and prediction.
133   Guided-attention mechanisms have also been employed to model the next active object and predict
134   future interactions (Thakur et al., 2023). The Spatiotemporal Attention Module (STAM) (Lu et al.,
135   2019b) uses eye gaze information as supervision to train a network that predicts an attention map
136   for activity recognition. Other studies similarly also use gaze as supervision to form attention maps
137   for activity recognition tasks (Min & Corso, 2020; Awale & Sarikaya, 2022).
138
      A lot of the previous work has utilized eye gaze data as ground truth to train attention prediction
139   networks and to predict eye gaze. However, in our study, we use eye gaze data as a direct signal for
140   human action prediction, building a gaze-regularized attention mechanism. With advancements in
141   eye-tracking methods and devices, and the abundance of egocentric videos, eye gaze can be easily
142   obtained and should be used as a direct signal. Our model conditions predictions on eye gaze data
143   as an input signal to output fine-grained text annotations of human actions.

      3.3.3   S INGULAR AND AGGREGATED GAZE AUGMENTED MODELS
312
313   In eye-tracking literature, eye movements are primarily categorized into fixations and saccades.
314   During a fixation, the eyes pause and focus on a specific area, gathering detailed visual information.
315   Most visual data is acquired during fixations, which typically last around 200 ms (Rayner, 2009).
316   Although small involuntary eye movements may occur during fixations, they still concentrate on a
317   specific region of interest. Saccades, on the other hand, are rapid eye movements that shift focus
318   from one point of interest to another, causing significant changes in the visual scene. Saccades are
319   crucial for visual search, and two consecutive saccades can cover considerable distances. Given the
320
      nature of saccades and fixations, as well as their average durations, detailed or relevant information
      is typically collected within a temporal window of δ = 200 milliseconds.
321
322   Correspondingly, we provide both a singular gaze-augmented model and an aggregated gaze-
323   augmented model. For each image frame It , we generate a heatmap image Ht and a gaze-overlaid
      image Gt using gaze coordinates available at time t. Gaze data from the Ego4D dataset (Grauman

      4.1   E VALUATION METRICS
404
405   For evaluation, we propose using a semantic transformer (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) to provide
406   a quantifiable score that compares the generated output with the ground-truth action text. This
407   scoring system is designed to ensure that the model does not penalize variations of sentences that
408   are semantically similar. Additionally, we aim for the model to penalize sentences where the word
409   order is nonsensical or incomprehensible to humans. Alongside the semantic score, we also utilize
410   the ROUGE-L score and METEOR score, which are widely employed to assess text similarity.
411
412   4.2   C OMPARISON BETWEEN BASE MODEL AND GAZE - AUGMENTED MODELS
413
414
      The objective of this experiment is to quantify the impact of eye gaze data on human action pre-
      diction by comparing model performance using measurable metrics. The base model receives only
415
      RGB image frames as input, while the gaze-augmented models incorporate multiple variations of
416
      gaze information. All gaze-augmented models share the same architecture but differ in the way gaze
417   data is utilized. In the Singular Gaze-Augmented Model, for each image frame It at time t, the
418   corresponding heatmap Ht is generated using a single gaze point gt , obtained from the timestamp
419   closest to t. In the Aggregated Gaze-Augmented Model, for each image frame It at time t, the cor-

      than those from our initial setup. While the base model’s performance remained similar with both

      sets of annotations, the gaze-augmented model showed a nearly 12 percent improvement when using
513
      the more fine-grained annotations, as shown in Table 1. This improvement can likely be attributed
514   to the model’s ability to leverage areas of concentrated gaze when processing more descriptive cap-
515   tions, allowing for better prediction of detailed actions. From the experiment, we conclude that the
516   importance of eye gaze increases even further when the granularity of the annotations is higher.

      5     CONCLUSION
520
      Our work demonstrates that incorporating gaze data significantly enhances the predictive abilities of
521
      VLMs, proving valuable for applications requiring precise human action prediction, such as assistive
522
      robotics and human-machine collaboration. By integrating eye gaze data, our approach improves the
523   model’s focus on critical image features, leading to enhanced prediction accuracy and reliability.
524
525   Our gaze-augmented model is trained with a gaze-regularized attention strategy, which simulates the
526
      natural attention mechanism driven by human gaze. This integration results in improved semantic
      and ROUGE scores, underscoring the effectiveness of gaze-regularized attention in boosting model
527
      performance. Through additional experiments, we highlight the crucial role of employing a gaze
528
      regularizer and gaze-augmented attention blocks, which provide insight into how to maximize the
529   utilization of gaze information.
530
531   Looking ahead, we aim to further refine our model by enhancing the integration of gaze data and
532
      exploring other training techniques. We also plan to develop a dedicated large-scale dataset to
      support other researchers in running their models and advancing this methodology. To facilitate
533
      further research and development, we will make our code and dataset publicly available, allowing
534
      others to build upon our architecture and enhance its capabilities.

      formation on the dataset creation process and the design of the prompts used in our experiments. We

764
      8.1     DATASET
765
766   The Ego4D and Epic-Kitchens datasets consist of egocentric videos capturing camera-wearers per-
767   forming daily activities in controlled settings (Grauman et al., 2022; Damen et al., 2022). Other
768   relevant datasets include the EGTEA+ Gaze dataset, which offers 28 hours of content focused on
769   cooking and kitchen activities (Li et al., 2020), and the more recent Visual Data Experience (VDE)
770   dataset, which contains around 240 hours of clips documenting day-to-day activities coupled with
771   gaze and head tracking (Greene et al., 2024).
772   These datasets are supplemented with text annotations in the form of (action, verb) tuples as well as
773   coarse-grained narrations, which are used for action anticipation and activity forecasting challenges.
774   For our study, we collected visual eye gaze data from the Ego4D dataset and modified the text
775   annotations from coarse-grained descriptions to more descriptive fine-grained descriptions. In the
776   future, we plan to incorporate the more recent VDE dataset into our study as well.
777   In this section, we provide details about the dataset creation process used for model training and
778   testing, the prompt design, and alternative sources of annotations in the form of VLMs.
779
780
      8.1.1     DATASET CONSTRUCTION AND PROMPT DESIGN
781
782   The Ego4D dataset comprises egocentric video clips along with supplementary data such as audio,
783   text annotations, eye gaze data, and additional metadata (Grauman et al., 2022). For our project, we
784   focused on the subset of video clips that include eye gaze data, containing approximately 33.3 hours
785   of egocentric videos recorded from 80 participants. The eye gaze data is provided in numerical
786   form, containing canonical timestamps and the pixel coordinates of gaze points. We transform gaze
787
      points to images to represent important visual regions, aligning with how humans perceive spatial
      information (Laeng et al., 2014). Due to the minute differences between consecutive images in
788
      the original videos, we perform downsampling to one image per second to reduce computational
789
      requirements while remaining effective.
790
791   Since our focus is fine-grained action prediction from ego-centric videos, we augment existing anno-
792   tations with detailed textual descriptions to enhance human-machine interaction. We leverage GPT-
793
      4V to generate annotations for video frames by processing a sequence of images and prompting it to
      describe each frame. This method ensures contextual coherence across frames. After obtaining ini-
794

       8.2.2   G AZE - GUIDED ATTENTION BLOCK
999    In our study, we propose the inclusion of a gaze-regularized attention mechanism, which features
1000   a gaze-guided attention block that operates in conjunction with the gaze regularizer. In the gaze-
1001   guided attention block, gaze-overlaid images are processed through the ViT to extract features that
1002   serve as queries for the attention module. These queries contain information about both the scene
1003   and the regions occupied by gaze, leading us to refer to this block as the gaze-guided attention block.
1004   Once the gaze-based features are obtained, they pass through a learnable linear layer to produce the
1005   queries involved in the attention mechanism. The visual features obtained from the RGB image
1006   frames, after passing through the ViT, are used as keys and values within the gaze-guided attention
1007
       block. Similar to the gaze-based features , the features obtained from the RGB image frames pass
       through a linear layer to form the keys and values for the attention mechanism. The final output,
1008
       consists of gaze-enhanced features that are informed by the gaze-based queries.
1009
1010   Additionally, the attention distribution is calculated, reflecting how attention is allocated across all
1011   tokenized patches. Initially, this attention distribution may not align with the gaze distribution.
1012   Therefore, through regularization, we aim to progressively align the attention distribution with the
1013
       gaze distribution over time. A closer look at the gaze-guided attention block can be found in Fig-
       ure 9.

       8.5   T RAINING AND EVALUATION DETAILS
1263
       Both the base model and the gaze-augmented model were trained using two NVIDIA A800 40GB
1264   GPU cards. The base model required approximately 36-38 hours for training, while the gaze-
1265   augmented model took around 50 hours. The training utilized a batch size of 32 and a learning
1266   rate of 7e − 5. The vision encoder is pre-trained by OpenAI. Additionally, the tokenizer for the text
1267   annotations used was the OPT (Open Pre-trained Transformer) language model developed by Meta.
1268   To accelerate the training process, we employed Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP), a technique
1269   that efficiently distributes model parameters and gradients across multiple GPUs, reducing memory
1270   usage and improving training speed. Data loading was managed using the WebDataset loader, and
1271   the dataset was converted to .tar files to align with the format required for integration with both the
1272
       WebDataset loader and FSDP.
1273   In terms of model complexity, the base model has approximately 944 million parameters. The gaze-
1274   augmented model with 5 layers contains about 996 million parameters while the gaze-augmented
1275   model with 1 layer has around 955 million parameters. Lastly, the model with 2 layers, which is the
1276   best performing model, has approximately 966 million parameters.
1277   For evaluation, both models were assessed using the semantic transformer (SBERT) developed by
1278   Reimers & Gurevych (2019), along with ROUGE-L and Meteor scores. The inference time on the
1279   test set for the base model was approximately one hour, while the gaze-augmented model had a
1280   slightly longer inference time but remained comparable to that of the base model.
