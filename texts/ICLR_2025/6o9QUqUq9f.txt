011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013             Understanding how large language models (LLMs) generate tokens is crucial for
014             enhancing their performance and interpretability. We hypothesize that cause-
015             effect relationships exist among candidate output tokens during next token pre-
016             diction in LLMs. Specifically, we propose that certain candidate output tokens—
017
                termed “effect tokens”—are causally influenced by other candidate tokens acti-
                vated in earlier layers, referred to as “cause tokens”. To test this hypothesis, we
018
                develop a causal analysis methodology that uncovers these relationships within
019
                open-source LLMs. We find that while cause tokens are essential for generating
020             effect tokens, including them in the final output can degrade model performance.
021
                Building on these findings, we introduce a decoding algorithm that employs two
022             heuristics: Critical Layer Ablation (CLA), which approximates causal relation-
023             ships by selectively removing transformer layers and observing their impact on
024             token generation, and Causally-Informed Decoding (CID), which uses the rela-
025             tionships identified by CLA to adjust token probabilities. Specifically, CID in-
026             creases the probability of selecting effect tokens while decreasing that of cause
027             tokens during generation. Our method achieves measurable accuracy improve-
028             ments across various benchmark datasets, demonstrating its potential to enhance
029             both the controllability and performance of LLM-generated text.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved impressive performance in natural language pro-
035
      cessing tasks, attracting significant attention from academia and industry (OpenAI, 2022; Dubey
      et al., 2024). Researchers are increasingly interested in understanding the activation dynamics within
036
      LLMs and how it influences the generation process (Edunov et al., 2019; Li et al., 2024). However,
037
      the internal mechanisms of LLMs remain largely opaque, posing challenges for interpretability and
038   trustworthiness.
039
040   Previous work has investigated the dynamics of LLMs across different layers. Tenney et al. (2019)
041
      found that different layers in an LLM serve distinct functions–early layers handle basic linguistic
      information such as grammar, while later layers capture broader contextual relationships and are
042
      thus better at reasoning. Therefore, it is possible that tokens activated1 in earlier layers differ from
043
      those activated in later layers. Furthermore, modern LLMs consist of multiple transformer lay-
044   ers (Vaswani, 2017) with residual connections, which were introduced to stabilize training in deep
045   models (Liu et al., 2020). Each layer transforms its input into a residual “correction” that is then
046   added to the input. This means that tokens activated in early layers—even if they are incorrect or
047   suboptimal—can persist and influence the final output, possibly appearing with high probability.
048
      This creates a fascinating paradox: tokens that appear incorrect in earlier layers may not only be
049
      crucial stepping stones for generating accurate tokens in later layers but also persist in the final
050   output due to residual connections. Fig. 1 illustrates this phenomenon, where the initial token “Yes”,

      With these insights, we hypothesize that during token generation, certain candidate output tokens
082
      causally result from the activation of others. We term the anticedent tokens “cause tokens” and the
083
      subsequent ones “effect tokens.” In the example of Fig. 1, “Yes” is a cause token and “While” is
084   an effect token. The logit values for both tokens are tracked across layers, highlighting how early
085   activations can influence later outputs. This example underscores the complex causal dynamics
086   within LLMs, where cause tokens can trigger the activation of more contextually appropriate effect
087   tokens in deeper layers.
088
      We propose a novel approach to test the hypothesis that cause-effect relationships exist among can-
089
      didate output tokens during inference. Recognizing that, due to residual connections, the trans-
090   formations from all layers are ultimately stacked into the final layer’s output, we simplify causal
091   analysis by focusing directly on this final output and constructing the causal graph based on the can-
092   didate output tokens themselves. This direct construction of causal graph, as elaborated in Section 3,
093   enables a clear and intuitive understanding of the relationships among these tokens. In contrast, pre-
094   vious works have often tackled the more intricate task of analyzing the causal graph across the entire
095   neural network.
096   As a second major contribution, inspired by our understanding of the causal relationships among
097   candidate output tokens, we propose to leverage this knowledge to enhance the decoding process.
098   Our aim is to guide the LLM towards selecting tokens that better align with the discovered causal
099   dependencies. Specifically, we propose to increase the probabilities of choosing effect tokens, while
100   decreasing the probabilities of choosing their corresponding cause tokens. However, directly inte-
101   grating full-scale causal analysis into decoding is computationally prohibitive. To address this chal-
102   lenge, we introduce the Critical Layer Ablation (CLA) heuristic, a method designed to efficiently
103   approximate causal relationships in real-time text generation scenarios.
104   Building upon the causal discoveries by CLA, we introduce a novel decoding algorithm, Causally-
105   Informed Decoding (CID). CID dynamically modifies logit values prior to sampling the output
106   token, incorporating causal considerations to enhance the generation process. CID significantly
107   improves the models’ reasoning performance under the zero-shot and zero-shot chain-of-thought
      settings across multiple arithmetic benchmarks.

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110
      2.1   C AUSAL ANALYSIS FOR LLM INTERPRETABILITY
111
112   The investigation of reasoning mechanism within (large) language models has garnered increasing
113   attention. The most pertinent series of work to ours adopted a causal analysis tool, called causal
114   mediation analysis (PEARL, 2001) or activation patching (Zhang & Nanda, 2024) in some context.
115   Causal mediation analysis detects which part of a neural network is responsible for the correct rea-
116   soning over a specific input sample through intervention, which corrupts the prediction by perturbing
117   the input and restores it by patching the activations from the clean run. This methodology was first
118   introduced to language modeling by Vig et al. (2020) for gender bias investigation. Following work
119   has then focused on its application to different tasks, including subject-verb agreement (Finlayson
120
      et al., 2021), natural language inference (Geiger et al., 2021), indirect object identification (Wang
      et al., 2022a), multiple-choice question answering (Wiegreffe et al., 2024) and syllogistic reason-
121
      ing (Kim et al., 2024).
122
123   These prior works differ from ours in their focus on internal model representations. We, however,
124   shift the focus to the causal dynamics among the output tokens themselves. We acknowledge that
125   while analyzing activations within internal layers is intuitive, residual connections in LLMs allow
126
      earlier activations’ causal influence to persist, impacting the final output. Therefore, we aim to
      uncover cause-effect relationships directly among candidate tokens, offering a unique perspective.
127
128
129   2.2   L OGIT- AND DECODING - LEVEL REASONING
130
131   In addition to causal analysis, research has explored reasoning at the token and decoding levels in
132
      LLMs. Wang et al. (2022b) proposed self-consistency checking during Chain-of-Thought decoding
      to enhance coherence. Chuang et al. (2023) introduced DoLa, modifying logits by differentiating
133
      between layers to improve text quality and diversity. These techniques, while valuable, do not
134
      explicitly consider underlying causal relationships among tokens. Our work complements these
135   efforts by introducing a causally-informed decoding algorithm that leverages identified cause-effect
136   relationships to further enhance accuracy and faithfulness to the model’s internal reasoning. By
137   integrating causal analysis with token-level reasoning, we aim to bridge the gap between theoretical
138   understanding and practical control of LLMs, paving the way for more controllable, reliable, and
139   causally-grounded text generation.
140
141   2.3   A DDITIONAL RELEVANT WORKS
142
143   Rajani et al. (2019) presented an early yet impactful work on causal reasoning in language models,
144   emphasizing the importance of understanding cause-effect relations. Feder et al. (2021) explored
145   the integration of causal graphs into language models, fine-tuning deep contextualized embedding
146   models with auxiliary adversarial tasks. Jin et al. (2023) introduced a novel task of causal inference
147   in natural language, accompanied by a dataset comprising causal graphs and queries. Chen et al.
148
      (2024) proposed a new causal evaluation framework, offering a systematic approach to assess causal
      reasoning abilities.

305
306   4.1   T HE CLA H EURISTIC
307
308   The CLA heuristic efficiently approximates cause-effect relationships among candidate tokens, sig-
309   nificantly faster than the causal discovery algorithms used in Section 3.1. Inspired by intervention
310   techniques, CLA simulates variable manipulation by systematically “ablating” transformer layers
311   and observing the impact on token generation. The core idea is to identify the layer whose transfor-
312
      mation has the most significant impact on the logit value of a given token. This layer is considered

313

      4.3   T HE CID A LGORITHM
379
380
      The CID algorithm leverages causal relationships identified by CLA to modify logits during de-
381   coding. CID reduces the probability of sampling cause tokens and increasing the probability of

406   Models, Datasets We utilize two tiny and four small state-of-the-art and representative LLMs
407   to empirically test the performance of the CID algorithm, namely Gemma-2-2B-Instruct (Team
408   et al., 2024), Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct (Touvron et al., 2023), Gemma-2-9B-Instruct (Team et al.,

412
413   Settings We adopt two types of prompts to the LLMs, ‘Raw’ and ‘CoT’, where ‘Raw’ refers to zero-
414   shot question answering without additional prompts, ‘CoT’ refers to zero-shot prompt with ‘Let’s
415   think step by step’ (Kojima et al., 2022), which is shown to significantly increase models’ reasoning
416   capability. We apply two sets of hyper-parameters for the proposed CID algorithm, CID with mild
417
      hyper-parameter ((d, h) = (2,5)) and CID+ with aggressive hyper-parameter ((d, h) = (5,10)).
418   Observations: Table 2 presents the accuracy results of our Causally-Informed Decoding (CID) al-
419   gorithm compared to the original decoding method across various language models and arithmetic
420   reasoning datasets. We observe substantial gains in accuracy when applying CID to the Yi-1.5-
421   9B-Chat, Mistral-Nemo-Instruct and Gemma-2-2b-Instruct models. For instance, on the GSM8K
422   dataset, accuracies are dramatically improved from 24.30% to 41.30% for Yi-1.5B-Chat and from
423
      12.80% to 43.30% for Mistral-Nemo-Instruct. This suggests that CID is particularly effective in en-
      hancing the reasoning capabilities of these models. While the improvements are less pronounced for
424
      Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, we still see consistent gains across all datasets.
425
      As to Gemma-2-9B-Instruct, CID can at most maintain the performance of this model itself. Note
426   that the CE token prediction performance with CLA on this model is also the worst among all
427   models (see Fig. 3), which might explain why the effect of CID on this model is not particularly sig-
428   nificant. These empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of our Causally-Informed Decoding
429   (CID) algorithm in enhancing the reasoning capabilities of diverse language models across various
430   benchmark datasets. By leveraging causal relationships extracted through the CLA heuristic, CID
431   guides the decoding process towards generating more logically coherent and accurate responses,
      particularly in challenging reasoning scenarios.

479   5    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
480
481
482   In this paper, we first hypothesize and verify the CE relationship between candidate output tokens.
483   Inspired by the CE relationship, we designed the CLA method to more efficiently explore the CE
484   connections between tokens and proposed the CID decoding algorithm to enhance the reasoning
485   capabilities of LLMs. Across multiple arithmetic datasets, CID significantly improved the perfor-
      mance of various LLMs.

489   performance across different model architectures and dataset characteristics.

      A    D ETAILS OF P ETER -C LARK ALGORITHM
649
650
      Here, we provide more details of the Peter-Clark (PC) algorithm (Spirtes et al., 2001) and how we
651   apply it to our scenarios. Given the input as a tensor T ∈ Rn×k , which contains the logit value of
652   n candidate tokens repeatedly sampled k times. PC algorithms output a causal graph among each
653   candidate variable via independent tests as follows:
654
655        1. We repeatedly perturb the LLM to generate k = 1000 samples of logit values for the
656           candidate tokens, {s1 , s2 , . . . , sk }. Each time,the LLM is perturbed by applying Bernoulli
657           random scalars with success probability 0.95 for the layers as described in Section 3.1,
658
              effectively removing some of the transformer layers.
659        2. We then apply the PC algorithm to the generated samples {s1 , s2 , . . . , sk } using Fisher’s z-
660           test for independence with a significance level of 10−4 . This is done using the causal-learn
661           package (Zheng et al., 2024) as footnoted on page 5. The PC algorithm outputs a causal
662
              graph between the tokens.
663        3. Finally we convert the source and destination tokens of each directed edge of the causal
664           graph to a cause-effect pair.
