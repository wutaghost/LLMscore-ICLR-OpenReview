009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Optimal trajectory design is computationally expensive for nonlinear and high-
012            dimensional dynamical systems. The challenge arises from the non-convex na-
013            ture of the optimization problem with multiple local optima, which usually re-
014            quires a global search. Traditional numerical solvers struggle to find diverse
015            solutions efficiently without appropriate initial guesses. In this paper, we introduce
016            DiffuSolve, a general diffusion model-based solver for non-convex trajectory
017            optimization. An expressive diffusion model is trained on pre-collected locally
018
               optimal solutions and efficiently samples initial guesses, which then warm-starts
               numerical solvers to fine-tune the feasibility and optimality. We also present
019
               DiffuSolve+, a novel constrained diffusion model with an additional loss in
020
               training that further reduces the problem constraint violations of diffusion samples.
021            Experimental evaluations on three tasks verify the improved robustness, diversity,
022            and a 2ˆ to 11ˆ increase in computational efficiency with our proposed method,
023            which generalizes well to trajectory optimization problems of varying challenges.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Optimal trajectory design is fundamental in decision-making for autonomous agents. In the open-
029   loop case, with a predetermined initial configuration, the goal is to plan an optimal path and control
030   sequence for an agent to reach a target while satisfying the dynamics and safety constraints. In
031   the case of complex nonlinear system dynamics and environmental constraints, these problems
032   often have non-convex structures. For example, within a chaotic dynamical system like the Circular
033
      Restricted Three-Body Problem (CR3BP) (Koon et al., 2000), the trajectory optimization problem
      for the spacecraft has multiple local optima of different qualitative behaviors with various tradeoffs
034
      (Hartmann et al., 1998; Russell, 2007). Identifying these diverse, locally optimal, and feasible
035
      solutions efficiently remains a significant challenge in the field.
036
037   Control transcription of the open-loop trajectory optimization problem involving nonlinear functions
038   results in a nonlinear program (NLP), where an initial guess is required to get the solution (Betts,
039
      1998). When one is interested in solving for several solutions, a two-step global search is needed:
      (i) sampling a distribution on the control space, and (ii) using this sample as an initial guess to the
040
      NLP solver that hopefully generates a solution. For each solution from the NLP, optimality (and also
041
      feasibility) is often verified by the solver by checking whether the first-order necessary conditions,
042   the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions, are satisfied (Kuhn & Tucker, 2013; Karush, 1939).
043   However, the high dimensionality and non-convex nature of the problem often make the two-step
044   global search process time-consuming such that the efficiency demands of autonomous systems are
045   not met.
046
      Machine learning has also gained popularity in trajectory optimization, but primarily focuses
047
      on solving the convexified problem rather than learning the solution distribution to the original
048   non-convex problem. For example, investigations have been made on warm-starting Quadratic
049   Programs (QP) using neural networks (Chen et al., 2022; Cauligi et al., 2021; Sambharya et al.,
050   2023). Recently, generative models, particularly diffusion models, have shown good performance in
051   generating trajectories or controls from a distribution (Janner et al., 2022; Ajay et al., 2022; Chi et al.,
052   2023; Carvalho et al., 2023; Sridhar et al., 2023). When conditioned on the environmental parameters,
053   diffusion models can efficiently generate appropriate and diverse trajectories even in an a priori
      unknown environment. However, the diffusion model alone without the NLP solver may output trajec-

073   In this paper, we propose DiffuSolve: a diffusion model-based solver for non-convex trajectory
074   optimization with improved efficiency, diversity, and robustness. Our contribution is two-fold: First,
075   we train a conditional diffusion model to learn the complex solution distribution of non-convex
076   optimization problems, and efficiently sample a set of diverse and high-quality trajectory predictions.
077
      These samples are then used as warm starts for an NLP solver to derive locally optimal and feasible
      solutions, ensuring the robustness of DiffuSolve. Second, to mitigate the effect of diffusion
078
      samples that violate the problem constraints, we developed DiffuSolve+ with a novel constrained
079
      diffusion model that incorporates an additional constraint violation loss in the training process.
080
081   DiffuSolve has the following appealing properties: (a). Computational Efficiency. The diffusion
082   model samples high-quality solutions that are close to local optima as warm starts, from which the
083
      NLP solver can quickly converge. (b). Robustness and Diversity. A constrained diffusion model
      produces diverse samples with minimal constraint violations, further refined by an NLP solver
084
      for enhanced feasibility and optimality. (c). Automatic Data Generation. With an NLP solver,
085
      we can generate diverse problems and collect corresponding solutions ourselves, allowing for on-
086   demand data augmentation whenever required. (d). Generalization. Our framework is suitable for
087   trajectory optimization across various system dynamics and environment settings, with the potential
088   for optimization problems in other areas like finance (Sambharya et al., 2023), etc.
089
      We demonstrate the efficacy of our method on three non-convex trajectory optimization problems:
090
      two-car reach-avoid, quadrotor navigation, and cislunar low-thrust transfer for a spacecraft.

093   2    R ELATED W ORK
094
095   Machine Learning for Optimization. Machine learning methods have been widely used to solve a
096   variety of optimization problems with different techniques, including convex Quadratic Programming
097   (QP) (Zeilinger et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2022; Sambharya et al., 2023), mixed-
098   integer programming (Cauligi et al., 2021), combinatorial optimization (Sun & Yang, 2023; Huang
099
      et al., 2023) and black-box optimization (Kumar & Levine, 2020; Trabucco et al., 2022; Krishnamoor-
      thy et al., 2023), etc. For the Combinatorial Optimization problem, graph-based diffusion models
100
      are used to directly generate solutions (Sun & Yang, 2023; Huang et al., 2023). To improve the
101
      constraint satisfaction in optimization problems, neural networks have been incorporated with fully
102   differentiable constraint function (Donti et al., 2021). Conditional variational autoencoder (CVAE)
103   and long short-term memory (LSTM) are proposed to warm-start the general nonlinear trajectory
104   optimization problems (Li et al., 2023). Another branch of work leverages machine learning methods
105   and pre-collected solution data points for solving partial differential equations (PDEs) (Cai et al.,
106   2021). One of the most pupular work is physics-informed neural networks (PINN) (Raissi et al.,
107   2019), which introduces the solution prediction model with a hybrid training loss for labeled solution
      prediction and violation of the original PDEs.

108
      Diffusion Models. The diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2020b) is able to
109   sample the multi-modal distribution with a stochastic differential equation (SDE). The models are
110   further developed into denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020), denoising
111   diffusion implicit models (DDIM) (Song et al., 2020a), etc., for application of image generation. Both
112   classifier-guided diffusion (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) and classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans,
113   2022) are proposed to achieve conditional generation with diffusion models.
114
115   Diffusion-based Trajectory Generation with Constraints. Diffusion models are becoming pop-
116   ular in robotics for generating controls or trajectories (Ajay et al., 2022; Janner et al., 2022; Liang
117   et al., 2023; Sridhar et al., 2023; Chi et al., 2023; Mishra et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2024). Many
118
      efforts have been made to improve the diffusion model for creating safe trajectories for safety-critical
      systems. Control barrier functions (Xiao et al., 2023; Botteghi et al., 2023) and collision-avoidance
119
      kernels (Chang et al., 2023) are employed to guide diffusion models to sample feasible trajectory
120
      solutions. To adapt to new constraints in testing, prior work investigated composing diffusion models
121   to improve the generalizability (Power et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023).
122
123   Different from existing work, our paper focuses on high-dimensional, highly nonlinear trajectory op-
124
      timization problems with many local optima, and aims to find diverse solutions efficiently. Instead of
      incorporating constraints in the sampling process, our constrained diffusion model in DiffuSolve+
125
      includes a novel loss in training, which can be combined with the current work to further reduce
126
      the violation. Moreover, DiffuSolve and DiffuSolve+ are equipped with an NLP solver that
127   finetunes the diffusion samples until feasibility and optimality are obtained at its best.

185   We first propose DiffuSolve, a general framework for efficient and robust non-convex trajectory
186   optimization using diffusion models and NLP solvers. Then we introduce a novel constrained
187   diffusion model specifically designed to guide the diffusion models to generate feasible solutions,

      It has been observed that for similar trajectory optimization problems Py in Eq. (1), their solutions
210   x˚ often exhibit similar structures (Amos et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). Similar problems are those Py
211   with the same objective J and constraints g, h, but with different parameters y. Thus, the key insight
212   of DiffuSolve is to use a diffusion model to learn ppx˚ |yq, the conditional probability distribution
213   of locally optimal solutions based on pre-solved similar problems. Then the diffusion model can
214   generalize and predict diverse solutions x̃˚new to new scenarios where the condition ynew value is
215   unseen. However, diffusion models inevitably make prediction errors, which can result in constraint
      violations. To address this, we use diffusion samples x̃˚new as initial guesses for the NLP solver, which


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      fine-tunes them to derive the final solutions x˚new , increasing both feasibility and optimality. This
217   approach acts like a safety net, enhancing the robustness of the solutions.
218
219   We illustrate our proposed DiffuSolve framework in Fig. 2. In the offline data generation process,
220
      we use an NLP solver π to collect locally optimal and feasible solutions x˚ for various problem
      instances Py with uniformly sampled initial guesses. The solutions with sub-optimal objective values
221
      are filtered out manually and the remaining dataset is used as the training data. For DiffuSolve
222
      training, we use a conditional diffusion model to learn ppx˚ |yq with classifier-free guidance (Ho &
223   Salimans, 2022), with the loss function in Eq. 5. In the online testing process when a new problem
224   Pynew is presented with a priori unknown value ynew , we first use the diffusion model to predict diverse
225   solution candidates x̃˚new as initial guesses. Then the NLP solver π only requires minor adjustments to
226   derive the final solutions x˚new with improved feasibility and optimality. This warm-starting approach
227   is fully parallelizable for both diffusion sampling and the solving process of π.

      In this section, we first evaluate DiffuSolve with a vanilla diffusion model (DM) on three
290   high-dimensional and highly nonlinear trajectory optimization tasks. Secondly, we present the
291   results of DiffuSolve+ with our novel constrained diffusion model (CDM). As a standalone
292   method, the CDM reduces problem constraint violation of the samples compared to DM. Within
293   DiffuSolve+, CDM helps further improve the efficiency of obtaining locally optimal and feasible
294   solutions compared to DiffuSolve.
295
      The experiments mainly involve three stages: 1. Use an NLP solver to collect solutions on each task
296   domain; 2. Train the data-driven models to fit the solution dataset; 3. For unseen new tasks within
297   each domain, sample the initial guesses from each model that warm-start the solver to derive final
298   solutions. The details of data collection and model training refer to Appendix A.

524   6   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
525
526   This paper presents DiffuSolve: a general diffusion model-based solver for non-convex trajectory
527   optimization. It can generate locally optimal, feasible, and diverse solutions with high computational
528   efficiency. We also propose DiffuSolve+ which includes a novel constrained diffusion model.
529
      It has fewer constraint violations as a standalone method and further improves the computational
      efficiency when integrated with DiffuSolve+. It is also a general acceleration framework that has
530
      the potential to solve optimization problems in other areas beyond trajectory optimization such as
531
      finance, computational chemistry, etc.
532
533   Limitations also exist for the current paper. We don’t spend extra effort to optimize the diffusion
534   model’s sampling speed. As a result, the current DiffusSolve and CDM may not be able
535
      to achieve real-time optimization for high-dimensional systems or highly dynamic environments.
      This can be improved with existing acceleration techniques such as stride sampling, DDIM (Song
536
      et al., 2020a), etc. In addition, although most constraint functions in trajectory optimization are
537
      differentiable (almost everywhere), the current CDM does not work for non-differentiable violation
538   functions. For non-differentiable constraints, transforming them into differentiable functions or
539   applying approximation with appropriate bump functions or neural networks is possible.
