009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Local learning offers an alternative to traditional end-to-end back-propagation in
012            deep neural networks, significantly reducing GPU memory usage. While local
013            learning has shown promise in image classification tasks, its application to other
014            visual tasks remains limited. This limitation arises primarily from two factors:
015            1) architectures tailored for classification are often not transferable to other tasks,
016            leading to a lack of reusability of task-specific knowledge; 2) the absence of cross-
017            scale feature communication results in degraded performance in tasks such as ob-
018
               ject detection and super-resolution. To address these challenges, we propose the
               Memory-augmented Auxiliary Network (MAN), which introduces a simplified
019
               design principle and incorporates a feature bank to enhance cross-task adaptabil-
020
               ity and communication. This work represents the first successful application of
021            local learning methods beyond classification, demonstrating that MAN not only
022            conserves GPU memory but also achieves performance on par with end-to-end
023            approaches across multiple datasets for various visual tasks.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Back-propagation (BP) remains the cornerstone of deep learning optimization, but as models scale
029   to larger sizes Bengio et al. (2006); Krizhevsky et al. (2017), End-to-End (E2E) methods expose sev-
030   eral limitations Hinton et al. (2006); Guo et al. (2020). BP relies on the propagation of error signals
031   across multiple layers, a process that contrasts with biological neural transmission systems Crick
032   (1989) and introduces challenges, such as error accumulation in deep networks. This can degrade
033
      the learning effectiveness of shallow neurons Qu et al. (1997). Moreover, updating hidden layers
      in the deep network requires the completion of forward and backward passes, which hinders paral-
034
      lel computation and significantly increases memory consumption on GPUs Jaderberg et al. (2017);
035
      Belilovsky et al. (2020). As an alternative to E2E methods, supervised local learning enhances mem-
036   ory efficiency and parallelism by splitting the network into gradient-isolated blocks, each updated
037   independently via its own auxiliary network Belilovsky et al. (2020); Nøkland & Eidnes (2019).
038
039
      However, current applications of local learning have largely been confined to image classification
      tasks, where they have demonstrated competitive performance Ma et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2021)
040
      compared to E2E methods by tailor-made auxiliary network. Despite this, the focus on auxiliary
041
      networks architecture for classification has constrained their general applicability. When extend-
042   ing these architectures to more complex tasks like object detection or super-resolution, they often
043   fall short due to their lack of cross-task adaptability and the widely recognized ”short-sightedness”
044   problem Su et al. (2024b). Although the work Su et al. (2024a) mitigates short-sightedness by using
045   exponential moving averages to enhance single-scale communication, it fails to address the deeper
046   limitations posed by cross-task adaptability challenges, especially where multi-scale information
047   is essential. For example, object detection requires multi-scale information, and the classification-
048   oriented architecture’s lack of it exacerbates the short-sightedness issue. Consequently, these issues
049   limit the potential of traditional local learning methods, hindering their generalization and portability
050
      across diverse visual tasks.
051   To this end, we present the Memory-Augmented Network (MAN), a novel framework designed to
052   address the challenges of scaling local learning methods across diverse tasks. This streamlined ap-
053   proach alleviates the above short-sightedness issue between local modules at different scales and
      enables performance that closely matches end-to-end training. Specifically, MAN operates as an

102   plesCrick (1989), aimed at utilizing memory more effectively for deep learning. Its key advantage
103   lies in addressing the limitations of global end-to-end (E2E) trainingHinton et al. (2006), thereby
104   fostering the development of alternative supervised local learning methods. In supervised local
105   learning, the progression of training primarily depends on using reasonable supervised local loss
106   functions or constructing efficient manual auxiliary networks. Previous research in differentiable
107   search algorithms utilizes self-supervised contrastive loss functions under local learning rulesIlling
      et al. (2021); Xiong et al. (2020); Nøkland & Eidnes (2019); Wang et al. (2021), enabling local

164   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
165
166   To establish the foundation of our work, we begin with a brief overview of traditional end-to-end
167   supervised learning and backpropagation mechanisms. Let x denote a data sample and y its corre-
168   sponding ground-truth label. We partition the entire deep network into several local blocks. During
169   forward propagation, the output of the j-th block becomes the input to the (j + 1)-th block, which
170   can be expressed as xj+1 = fθj (xj ). Here, θj represents the parameters of the j-th local block,
171
      and f (·) denotes the forward computation performed by the block. We compute the loss function
      L(ŷ, y) between the output ŷ of the final block and the ground-truth label y, and then iteratively
172
      propagate this loss backward through the preceding blocks.
173
174   Supervised local learning strategies Nøkland & Eidnes (2019); Wang et al. (2021); Belilovsky et al.
175   (2020) incorporate auxiliary networks to provide local supervision. In this approach, an auxiliary
176   network is attached to each local block. The output of each local block is fed into its corresponding
177
      auxiliary network, generating a local supervisory signal expressed as yˆj = gγj (xj+1 ). Here, γj
      denotes the parameters of the j-th auxiliary network.
178
179   In this setup, we update the parameters of the j-th auxiliary network and local block, γj , θj , as
180   follows:
181
182                                       γj ← γj − ηa × ∇γj L(yˆj , y)                                   (1)
183
184
185
                                          θj ← θj − ηl × ∇θj L(yˆj , y)                                   (2)
186   Here, ηa and ηl denote the learning rates of the auxiliary networks and the local blocks, respectively.
187   By attaching auxiliary networks, each local block becomes gradient-isolated and can be updated
188   using local supervision instead of global backpropagation.
189
      However, when attempting to transfer existing local learning architectures to other tasks, signifi-
190
      cant performance gaps arise for two main reasons. First, traditional local learning methods rely on
191   meticulously designed auxiliary network structures. While these structures enhance performance
192   in classification tasks, they constrain the network design to specific tasks, hindering adaptability
193   during transfer to other tasks. Second, Su et al. (2024b;a) highlight that a key limitation of local
194   learning architectures is the short-sightedness problem in addressing local modules. Unlike classi-
195   fication tasks, other tasks often require features at different scales, which exacerbates the effects of
196   short-sightedness and complicates task transfer.

198   3.2   M EMORY-AUGMENTED N ETWORK
199
200
      We propose the Memory-Augmented Network (MAN) architecture to extend local learning to dif-
      ferent tasks. In this Chapter, we will briefly explain the MAN framework, and introduce how MAN
201
      can transfer local-learning to different tasks in detail in Chapter 4.
202
203   As shown in the Figure 2, MAN consists of Simple Local Modules (SLM) and a Feature Bank, each
204   addressing two key challenges in task transfer. SLM provides a simple auxiliary network adaptable
205   to various tasks, overcoming the challenge of task-specific designs. The feature bank stores multi-
206
      scale features and applies them in the auxiliary network, which alleviates the myopic problem of
      local modules. Together, these components simplify the transfer of local learning techniques across
207
      tasks. We will introduce these two parts separately
208
209   SLM: In networks designed for different tasks, the backbone is often carefully crafted based on
210   the specific task, leading to significant differences in the feature extraction processes of different
211   network architectures. By simply downsizing the backbone network, we retain its feature extraction
212
      capabilities and ensure that the auxiliary network aligns with the objectives of the backbone. This
      allows SLM to be easily adapted to different task requirements.
213
214   Through the design of SLM, local-learning can be easily extended to various tasks. However, this
215   can not solve the short-sighted problem of local modules inherent in local-learning, resulting in the
      loss of task performance. We propose Feature bank to make up for this deficiency.

256   4   A PPLICATIONS
257
258   In different tasks, models are typically divided into a backbone network for feature extraction and
259   a head network for task execution. This structure allows for easy transfer of an end-to-end (E2E)
260   framework to other tasks by simply replacing the task-specific head. However, in local learning, the
261   incomplete network structure complicates the straightforward transfer to other tasks. For instance, in
262   object detection, predictions require features from different scales; however, gradient-independent
263   local networks often miss other essential features, making accurate predictions challenging.
264   Next, we will elaborate on how the MAN framework is applied to different tasks. We have selected
265   three classic tasks: classification, object detection, and super-resolution. They respectively represent
266   traditional application scenarios of local learning, challenging tasks in high-level tasks, and the most
267   representative tasks in low-level tasks.Due to the characteristics of classification networks, we will
268   focus on the construction of the backbone network in classification tasks, as well as how to apply
269   MAN to these tasks. In subsequent sections on detection and super-resolution, we will reuse the
      backbone network architecture and emphasize how to leverage MAN for task transfer.

528   6     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we introduce Memory-Augmented Network (MAN), a novel design of auxiliary net-
531   works that for the first time extends the application of local learning to different tasks. The design
532   of MAN eliminates the need for tedious manual configuration and instead makes full use of the
533   structure of the backbone network as well as the features at different levels. By augment the fea-
534   ture memory and increasing the utilization of cross-scale information by local modules, we apply
535   MAN to different tasks, such as object detection and image super-resolution, and show that MAN
536
      significantly reduces GPU memory usage while maintaining comparable performance to BP.
537   Limitations and Future Work: Although the proposed Memory Augmented Network (MAN) per-
538   forms well in terms of performance and adaptability for various tasks, it uses an explicit Feature
539   bank, which brings additional memory overhead. Our future work will explore how to transfer
      information across scales implicitly.
