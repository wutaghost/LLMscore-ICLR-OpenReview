011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Matrix preconditioning is a crucial modern technique for accelerating the solv-
014            ing of linear systems. Its effectiveness heavily depends on the choice of precon-
015            ditioning parameters. Traditional methods often depend on domain expertise to
016
               define a set of fixed constants for specific scenarios. However, the characteristics
               of each problem instance also affect the selection of optimal parameters, while
017
               fixed constants do not account for specific instance characteristics and may lead
018
               to performance loss. In this paper, we propose Symbolic Matrix Preconditioning
019            (SymMaP), a novel framework based on Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)
020            for automatically generating symbolic expressions to compute efficient precon-
021            ditioning parameters. Our method begins with a grid search to identify optimal
022            parameters according to task-specific performance metrics. SymMaP then per-
023            forms a risk-seeking search over the high-dimensional discrete space of symbolic
024            expressions, using the best-found expression as the evaluation criterion. The re-
025            sulting symbolic expressions are seamlessly integrated into modern linear sys-
026            tem solvers to improve computational efficiency. Experimental results demon-
027
               strate that SymMaP consistently outperforms traditional algorithms across various
               benchmarks. The learned symbolic expressions can be easily embedded into exist-
028
               ing specialized solvers with negligible computational overhead. Furthermore, the
029
               high interpretability of these concise mathematical expressions facilitates deeper
030            understanding and further optimization of matrix preconditioning strategies.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Linear system problems are widely used in machine learning, physics, engineering, and other sci-
036   entific disciplines (Leon et al., 2006; LeVeque, 2007). These problems often cannot be solved
037   through analytical or closed-form solutions, rendering the development of efficient numerical al-
038   gorithms (Demmel, 1997). Among these techniques, matrix preconditioning is one of the most pop-
039   ular approaches to enhance the efficiency of solving linear systems (Trefethen & Bau, 2022; Chen,
040   2005). During the preconditioning process, the selection of preconditioning parameters—such as
041   the over-relaxation factor in Successive Over-Relaxation (SOR) (Golub & Van Loan, 2013)—plays
042   a crucial role.
043   In traditional numerical computation, fixed constants for preconditioning parameters are often tai-
044   lored for specific scenarios based on domain expertise (Chen, 2005). However, apart from problem-
045   specific scenarios, the characteristics of the problem also influence the selection of optimal precon-
046   ditioning parameters. Choosing a fixed constant as the parameter (Benzi, 2002), on the other hand,
047   would overlook the inherent characteristics of the problem, thereby leading to suboptimal perfor-
048   mance. Existing heuristic formulas for predicting parameters, such as Young’s formula (Golub &
049   Van Loan, 2013), impose many restrictions on both the input and the problem structure and thus
050
      are often not suitable for more generalized scenarios. The above all highlight the urgent need for a
      more flexible approach that not only accommodates a wider range of scenarios but also dynamically
051
      adjusts to the evolving characteristics of the problems.
052
053   A good algorithm for discovering the optimal preconditioning parameters must overcome several
      significant challenges: (C1) Strong Generalization Capability. Different application scenarios and


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      preconditioning goals demand that the algorithm possesses robust generalization capabilities to
055   adapt effectively across various contexts (Golub & Van Loan, 2013).
056
057   (C2) Computational Efficiency. Given the frequent invocation of linear system solvers in scientific
058
      computing, any parameter prediction algorithm must incur minimal computational overhead and
      seamlessly integrate with existing specialized solver libraries (Chen, 2005).
059
060   (C3) Algorithmic Transparency. The often opaque nature of prediction algorithms hinders re-
061   searchers’ ability to understand and trust their outcomes, making it difficult to validate and refine
062   the models effectively. Conversely, transparent algorithms allow practitioners to validate and verify
063   the underlying mechanisms, which helps ensure that the prediction results are derived in a logically
064
      coherent and justifiable manner (Lipton, 2016).
065   To address these challenges, we propose Symbolic Matrix Preconditioning (SymMaP), a novel
066   Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)-based symbolic discovery framework that searches for symbolic
067   expressions of efficient preconditioning parameters. We begin by applying grid search to iden-
068   tify the optimal preconditioning parameters based on task-specific performance metrics. Next, the
069   framework conducts a risk-seeking search within the high-dimensional discrete space of symbolic
070
      expressions, with the risk-seeking strategy evaluating the best-found symbolic expression. Finally,
      these symbolic expressions are integrated into modern solvers for linear systems to enhance their
071
      computational efficiency.
072
073   The experimental results highlight several advantages of our algorithm:
074
075          • Superior Performance: Our novel matrix preconditioning approach, based on symbolic
076            regression, consistently outperforms traditional algorithms across various benchmarks.
077          • Robust Generalization Capability: It is widely applicable to predicting diverse matrix
078            preconditioning parameters and optimizing for various objectives.
079          • Easy Deployment: The learned symbolic expressions can be easily embedded into existing
080            specialized linear system solvers with negligible additional computational time, which is
081            friendly to the pure CPU environments.
082
             • High Interpretability: The learned strategies are concise, one-line mathematical expres-
083
               sions, facilitating further understanding and optimization of matrix preconditioning algo-
084            rithms by researchers.

089   2.1   M ATRIX P RECONDITIONING T ECHNIQUE
090
091
      Matrix preconditioning is a technique employed to accelerate the convergence of iterative solvers
      and enhance the stability of algorithms. It is generally employed in solving linear systems (Chen,
092
      2005; Golub & Van Loan, 2013). These systems are typically expressed in the form:
093
094                                                 Ax = b.                                              (1)
095
096   The fundamental idea of preconditioning is to transform the original problem into an equivalent one
097   with better numerical properties. The objectives are twofold: 1. to accelerate the convergence of
098   iterations by altering the spectral distribution of the matrix A. 2. to reduce the condition number of
099   the matrix A, thereby lessening its ill-conditioning and enhancing the stability of iteration. Specifi-
100   cally, this technique involves finding a preconditioner M that approximates either the inverse of A
101   or some form conducive to iterative solutions (Chen, 2005). Consequently, the original equation 1
102
      is transformed into
103                                                M Ax = M b.                                           (2)
104   The preconditioned matrix M A should have a lower condition number than the original matrix
105   A, allowing iterative methods such as Generalized Minimal Residual Method (GMRES) (Saad &
106   Schultz, 1986) to converge more rapidly. Some common preconditioning techniques include the
107   Jacobi, Gauss-Seidel, SOR (Young, 1954) , Algebraic Multigrid (AMG) (Trottenberg et al., 2000),
      etc.

126    2.2               P REFIX N OTATION AND G ENETIC P ROGRAMMING
127
128    Prefix notation is a mathematical format where every operator precedes its operands, eliminating the
129    need for parentheses required in conventional infix notation and simplifying symbolic manipulation.
130    This representation is particularly advantageous in symbolic regression, as it allows mathematical
131    expressions to be expressed as sequences of tokens that can be easily processed by neural networks.
132    In this notation, operators can be unary (e.g., sin, cos) or binary (e.g., +, −, ×, ÷), while operands
133    can be constants or variables (Landajuela et al., 2021). Each prefix expression uniquely corresponds
134    to a symbolic tree structure, facilitating the conversion back to the original mathematical expres-
135    sion (Lample & Charton, 2019).
136    Genetic Programming (GP) is a well-known algorithm that builds on this notation by evolving math-
137    ematical expressions that model relationships within data. As an evolutionary computation tech-
138    nique, GP generates solutions without requiring prior knowledge of their structure (Espejo et al.,
139    2009). The expressions are represented as tree structures, with internal nodes corresponding to op-
140    erators and leaf nodes representing variables or constants (Banzhaf et al., 1998). This representation
141    aligns well with prefix notation, allowing for effective manipulation and optimization.
142    GP begins with an initial population of randomly generated expression trees and iteratively applies
143    genetic operations—selection, crossover, and mutation—to improve their fitness. Selection identi-
144    fies the fittest expressions based on a predefined metric (Eiben & Smith, 2015). Crossover combines
145    parts of two parent expressions, while mutation introduces random changes, facilitating the explo-
146    ration of new potential solutions. Through these iterative processes, GP refines the population of
147    expressions, converging towards optimal solutions that accurately capture the underlying relation-
148    ships in the data (Espejo et al., 2009; Langdon & Poli, 2013).

176   3.2   C HALLENGES IN P REDICTING E FFICIENT P RECONDITIONING PARAMETERS
177
178   The choice of preconditioning parameters directly influences the efficiency of solving linear systems.
179   We aim to develop a universal framework for predicting efficient parameters. However, the context
180
      of solving linear systems imposes specific challenges on algorithms that predict preconditioning
      parameters:
181
182   (C1) Strong Generalization Capability: Real-world scientific computing scenarios vary signifi-
183   cantly. For instance, the grid format and feature selection of PDEs in different physical environments
184   can lead to significant variations in matrix structure (Johnson, 2009), resulting in distinct optimal
185   parameters. Moreover, preconditioning faces various tasks such as reducing computational costs and
186
      lowering condition numbers (Chen, 2005). This necessitates that parameter prediction algorithms
      possess robust generalization capabilities: they should take problem scenarios and characteristics as
187
      inputs while being applicable to different preconditioning methods and optimization goals.
188
189   (C2) Computational Efficiency: Solving linear systems typically relies on Krylov subspace meth-
190   ods implemented in low-level libraries optimized for CPU architectures, such as PETSc (Balay et al.,
191   2024), LAPACK (Anderson et al., 1999). Algorithms like GMRES (Saad & Schultz, 1986) and Con-
192
      jugate Gradient (CG) (Greenbaum, 1997) iteratively compute the matrix’s invariant subspace, favor-
      ing single-threaded or limited multi-threaded execution modes. Preconditioning techniques aim to
193
      accelerate these solvers without significant additional computational overhead, often adopting im-
194
      plicit iterative formats (e.g., SOR (Chen, 2005)) or utilizing low-cost matrix decompositions (e.g.,
195   AMG (Trottenberg et al., 2000)). Therefore, any parameter prediction algorithm must be compatible
196   with CPU environments and seamlessly integrate into existing algorithm libraries while maintaining
197   low computational overhead to preserve the performance gains of preconditioning.
198
      (C3) Algorithmic Transparency: Algorithms in scientific computing often require rigorous anal-
199
      ysis under mathematical theories. Opaque prediction algorithms could confuse researchers. For
200
      instance, the relaxation factor ω in SOR needs to avoid being too close to 0 or 2 in some scenar-
201   ios (Agarwal, 2000). This is an issue that opaque algorithms cannot avoid in advance. Moreover,
202   interpretable algorithms can guide researchers to conduct further studies and reveal the underly-
203   ing mathematical structures of problems. Therefore, these pose challenges to the transparency and
204   interpretability of the parameter prediction algorithms.
205

      In this study, we introduce a novel framework, SymMaP, for symbolic discovery in matrix precondi-
253
      tioning by utilizing RNN. As shown in Figure 2, we first obtain the optimal preconditioning param-
254   eters for the given scenario through a grid search to construct a training dataset. Then we employ an
255   RNN to generate symbolic expressions in prefix notation, which are then evaluated for their fitness.
256   The RNN is trained using a reward function based on the performance of the generated expressions.
257   By optimizing the RNN parameters to maximize this reward function, we generate symbolic expres-
258   sions that approximate the desired mathematical functions. Finally, we deploy the learned symbolic
259   expressions into linear system solvers. The detailed steps are as follows and pseudocode is provided
260   in the Appendix C.

      use these parameters as input features for symbolic learning in SymMaP.

281   Token Library. For SymMaP, we define the library of mathematical operators and operands as
282   {+, −, ×, ÷, sqrt, exp, log, pow, 1.0}. Although other operators such as poly, sin and cos are fre-
283   quently used (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020), we decided to exclude them because they offer limited
284   explanatory power in matrix preconditioning and significantly increase the time and memory con-
285   sumption during training.
286   After converting the mathematical expressions into prefix notation, we leverage this tokenized rep-
287   resentation as a pre-order traversal of the expression tree (Zaremba & Sutskever, 2014). In each
288   iteration, the RNN receives a pair consisting of a parent node and a sibling node as inputs and
289   outputs a categorical distribution over all possible next tokens. The parent node refers to the last
290   incomplete operator that requires additional operands to form a complete expression. The sibling
291   node, in the context of a binary operator, represents the operand that has already been processed and
292   incorporated into the expression. In cases where no parent or sibling node is applicable, they are
293
      designated as empty nodes. This structured input method enables the RNN to maintain contextual
      awareness and effectively predict the sequence of tokens that form valid mathematical expressions.

      A     R ELATED WORK

      A.1   M ACHINE L EARNING FOR A LGORITHM D ISCOVERY
705
706   Machine learning has the potential to uncover implicit rules beyond human intuition from training
707   data, enabling the construction of algorithms that outperform handcrafted programs. Approaches to
708   algorithm discovery in machine learning encompass symbolic discovery, program search, and more.
709   Specifically, program search focuses on optimizing the computational processes of algorithms. For
710   example, Mankowitz et al. (2023) explores the discovery of faster sorting algorithms, while Chen
711   et al. (2024) investigate efficient optimization algorithms.
712   In contrast, symbolic discovery aims to search within the space of small mathematical expressions
713   rather than computational streams (Petersen et al., 2019; Landajuela et al., 2021). This approach
714   is analogous to an extreme form of model distillation, where knowledge extracted from black-box
715   neural networks is distilled into explicit mathematical expressions. Traditional methods for sym-
716   bolic discovery have relied on evolutionary algorithms, including genetic programming (Poli et al.,
717   2008). Recently, deep learning has emerged as a powerful tool in this domain, offering enhanced
718   representational capacity and new avenues for solving symbolic discovery problems (Schmidt &
719   Lipson, 2009; Cranmer et al., 2020).
720
721   A.2   N EURAL N ETWORKS FOR M ATRIX P RECONDITIONING
722
723   Recent studies have explored the use of neural networks to improve matrix preconditioning tech-
724   niques. Greenfeld et al. (2019); Luz et al. (2020); Taghibakhshi et al. (2021) demonstrate the ef-
725   fectiveness of neural networks in refining multigrid preconditioning algorithms, thus streamlining
726
      the computational process. Götz & Anzt (2018) utilized Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
      for the optimization of block Jacobi preconditioning algorithms, while Stanaityte (2020) developed
727
      corresponding Incomplete Lower-Upper Decomposition (ILU) preconditioning algorithms leverag-
728
      ing machine learning insights.Although these algorithms achieved impressive results, they still face
729   challenges such as limited interpretability and reduced computational efficiency when deployed in
730   pure CPU environments. This paper attempts to address these issues by incorporating symbolic
731   learning into the framework.
732
733
734   B     D ETAILED INTRODUCTION OF MATRIX PRECONDITIONING
735
736   B.1   OVERVIEW OF M ATRIX P RECONDITIONING M ETHODS
737          • Jacobi Method: The Jacobi preconditioner utilizes only the diagonal elements of a ma-
738            trix to precondition a linear system. By approximating the inverse of the diagonal matrix,
739            this method is computationally simple and effective for systems with strong diagonal dom-
740            inance. However, its convergence rate can be slow, and its performance diminishes for
741            poorly conditioned or weakly diagonally dominant matrices. The Jacobi method is typ-
742            ically used as a baseline for comparison with more sophisticated preconditioners (Saad,
743            2003).
744          • Gauss-Seidel (GS) Method: The Gauss-Seidel preconditioner improves upon the Jacobi
745            method by considering both the lower triangular and diagonal parts of the matrix in a se-
746            quential manner. Unlike the Jacobi method, which updates all variables simultaneously, the
747            GS method updates each variable in sequence using the most recent values. This leads to
748            faster convergence, especially for diagonally dominant matrices. However, the GS method
749            can still struggle with poorly conditioned systems, and its forward-only approach can limit
750            performance in some applications (Saad, 2003).
751          • Successive Over-Relaxation (SOR): The SOR method builds on the Gauss-Seidel method
752            by introducing a relaxation factor ω to accelerate convergence. This factor allows for over-
753            relaxation (ω > 1) or under-relaxation (ω < 1), tuning the method for faster performance
754            on certain types of problems. SOR can significantly reduce the number of iterations needed
755            for convergence compared to both the Jacobi and GS methods, but choosing the optimal
               relaxation factor is problem-dependent (Young, 1954).


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
              • Symmetric Successive Over-Relaxation (SSOR): SSOR is a symmetric version of the
757             SOR method, where relaxation is applied in both forward and backward sweeps of the
758             matrix. This bidirectional process improves stability and is well-suited for use with itera-
759             tive solvers like the conjugate gradient method, which requires symmetric preconditioners.
760             SSOR’s symmetry ensures that the preconditioner maintains the properties needed for ef-
761             ficient and stable convergence, making it a popular choice for symmetric positive-definite
762             systems (Golub & Van Loan, 2013).
763           • Algebraic Multigrid (AMG): AMG is an advanced preconditioning technique designed
764             to handle large, sparse systems of linear equations, especially those arising from the dis-
765             cretization of partial differential equations. Unlike traditional methods, AMG operates on
766             multiple levels of the matrix structure, coarsening the matrix to form a hierarchy of smaller
767             systems that are easier to solve. Solutions on the coarser grids are then interpolated back to
768             the finer grids. This multilevel approach makes AMG highly efficient for large-scale prob-
769             lems, as it can dramatically reduce the number of iterations needed to achieve convergence.
770             AMG is often used in combination with methods like SSOR or Gauss-Seidel as a smoother
771
                on each grid level, and it is particularly effective in cases where the problem exhibits a
                multiscale nature (Ruge & Stüben, 1987).
772
773
      Relationship Among Jacobi, GS, and SOR Methods: The Jacobi method is the simplest of the
774
      three, using only diagonal information. The GS method improves upon the Jacobi method by using
775   both diagonal and lower triangular matrix elements to achieve faster convergence. SOR further
776   refines the GS method by introducing a relaxation factor to optimize the update process. Both
777   the GS and SOR methods can be seen as iterative improvements on the Jacobi method, with SOR
778   offering a more flexible and potentially faster alternative by adjusting the relaxation factor. SSOR
779   extends SOR symmetrically, making it suitable for use in more advanced iterative solvers like the
780   conjugate gradient method (Saad, 2003; Golub & Van Loan, 2013).
781
782   B.2     PARAMETERS IN M ATRIX P RECONDITIONING
783
784   The choice of preconditioning parameters significantly influences the effectiveness of the precondi-
785   tioning process, especially in the iterative solving of linear systems (Chen, 2005). Below, we discuss
786   three specific preconditioning techniques—SOR, SSOR, and AMG—focusing particularly on how
787
      their key parameters affect the preconditioning results.
788
789   B.2.1    R ELAXATION FACTOR ω IN SOR AND SSOR M ETHODS
790
      In the SOR preconditioning method, the relaxation factor ω is a critical parameter that determines
791   the acceleration of iteration. SOR evolves from the Gauss-Seidel method by introducing ω to speed
792   up convergence. The SOR iteration formula is given by:
793
794                                           h                           i
795                        x(k+1) = (D + ωL)−1 (1 − ω)Dx(k) + ωb − ωU x(k) ,                             (13)
796
797   where D, L, and U are the diagonal, strictly lower triangular, and strictly upper triangular parts of
798   the matrix A, respectively (Golub & Van Loan, 2013).
799
      The SSOR preconditioning method can be represented by the following formula:
800
801
                                               1
802                              MSSOR =             (D − ωU )D −1 (D − ωL),                             (14)
803                                         ω(2 − ω)
804
805   where MSSOR constitutes the preconditioner, and D, L, U , and ω are defined similarly to their
806
      roles in the SOR method. This symmetrical formulation enhances the stability and effectiveness of
      the preconditioning, particularly benefiting symmetric positive-definite matrices by optimizing the
807
      convergence properties of the iterative solver (Golub & Van Loan, 2013).
808
809   The choice of ω directly impacts the speed of convergence and the condition number of the ma-
      trix. Different problems and scenarios often require different choices of ω, which typically need to


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      be determined based on the specific properties of the problem and through numerical experimenta-
811   tion (Golub & Van Loan, 2013). In the PETSc library, the default relaxation factor ω for both SOR
812   and SSOR is set to 1, at which point SOR degenerates to GS preconditioning.
813
814   B.2.2    T HRESHOLD PARAMETERS θT IN AMG
815
816   In the AMG method, the threshold parameter θT determines whether the non-zero elements of the
817   matrix are ”strong” enough to be considered in the construction of a coarse grid during the multigrid
818
      process. This parameter is crucial for establishing the connectivity between coarse and fine grids in
      the hierarchical multilevel structure (Ruge & Stüben, 1987).
819
820   The AMG method solves the equation system through multiple levels of grids, each corresponding
821   to a coarser version of the original problem. During this process, the threshold parameter is used to
822   determine whether a given non-zero matrix element is strong enough to keep the corresponding grid
823   points connected during coarsening.
824
              • A lower threshold often leads to more elements being considered as strong connections,
825
                which might increase the complexity of the coarse grid but can help preserve the essential
826
                characteristics of the original problem, thus improving the efficiency and convergence of
827             the multigrid method.
828
              • A higher threshold might result in fewer strong connections, thereby reducing the com-
829
                plexity of the coarse grid. However, this can weaken the effectiveness of the AMG method,
830
                especially in maintaining the characteristics of the original problem.
831
832   Different values of θT directly influence the condition number of the preconditioned matrix. Se-
833   lecting the appropriate threshold parameter typically involves considering the specific structure
834   and characteristics of the problem, and adjustments are made through experimental fine-tuning to
835   achieve the optimal balance (Trottenberg et al., 2000). In the PETSc library, the default threshold
836   parameter θT is set to 0.

      D.1    DATASETS
921
922   1. Darcy Flow Problem
923
      We consider two-dimensional Darcy flows, which can be described by the following equation (Li
924
      et al., 2020; Rahman et al., 2022; Kovachki et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2022):
925
926                                      −∇ · (K(x, y)∇h(x, y)) = f,
927
      where K is the permeability field, h is the pressure, and f is a source term which can be either a
928
      constant or a space-dependent function.
929
930   In our experiment, K(x, y) is generated using truncated Chebyshev polynomials. We convert the
931   darcy flow problem into a system of linear equations using the central difference scheme of Finite
932
      Difference Methods (FDM) (LeVeque, 2007). The coefficients of the Chebyshev polynomials serve
      as input features for our symbolic learning algorithm.
933
934   2. Second-order Elliptic Partial Differential Equation
935
      We consider general two-dimensional second-order elliptic partial differential equations, which are
936   frequently described by the following generic form (Evans, 2022; Bers et al., 1964):
937
938                      Lu ≡ a11 uxx + a12 uxy + a22 uyy + a1 ux + a2 uy + a0 u = f,
939   where a0 , a1 , a2 , a11 , a12 , a22 are constants, and f represents the source term, depending on x, y.
940   The variables u, ux , uy are the dependent variable and its partial derivatives. The equation is classi-
941   fied as elliptic if 4a11 a22 > a212 .
942
943
      In our experiments, a11 , a22 , a1 , a2 , a0 are uniformly sampled within the range (−1, 1), while the
      coupling term a12 is sampled within (−0.01, 0.01). We then select equations that satisfy the elliptic
944
      condition to form our dataset. Similar to the approach with the darcy flow problem, we convert the
945
      PDE into a system of linear equations using the central difference scheme of FDM. The coefficients
946   a0 , a1 , a2 , a11 , a12 , a22 serve as input features for our symbolic learning algorithm.
947
948   3. Biharmonic Equation
949   We consider the biharmonic equation, a fourth-order elliptic equation, defined on a domain Ω ⊂
950   R2 . The equation is expressed as follows (Ciarlet & Raviart, 1974; Glowinski & Pironneau, 1979;
951   Barrata et al., 2023):
952                                           ∇4 u = f in Ω,
953   where ∇4 ≡ ∇2 ∇2 represents the biharmonic operator and f = 4.0π 4 sin(πx) sin(πy) is the
954   prescribed source term.
955
      In our experiments, we construct the dataset by varying the solution domain Ω. We utilize the
956
      discontinuous Galerkin finite element method from the FEniCS library to transform this problem
957   into a system of linear equations (Barrata et al., 2023). The parameters of the domain serve as input
958   features for our symbolic learning algorithm.

       D.3   T RAINING DATA G ENERATION
979
980    We employed an adaptive grid search to generate the training dataset. Initially, we traversed a coarse
981    grid, sampling every 0.05, and from this dataset, we selected the three points with the smallest val-
982    ues. Subsequently, we conducted a finer grid search around these points, sampling every 0.001, to
983    identify the point with the minimum value, which we designated as our optimal parameter. Partic-
984    ularly, after experimental validation confirmed the dataset’s convexity, we utilized a binary search
985    sampling method for a dataset derived from the second-order elliptic equation’s SOR precondition-
986    ing. Starting with points at 0.0, 1.0, and 2.0, we compared these values. If the value at 0.0 was
987    lowest, we computed at 0.5; if at 2.0, then at 1.5; and if at 1.0, then at both 0.5 and 1.5. This process
988
       was repeated until achieving a minimum point with a precision of 0.001.
989    For SOR preconditioning, we evaluated second-order elliptic equations, Darcy flow equations,
990    and biharmonic equations, with solution time as the metric for optimal preprocessing parameters,
991    achieved by minimizing solution time using the previously described grid method. In SSOR precon-
992    ditioning, applied to second-order elliptic and Darcy flow equations, we utilized a hybrid metric that
993    combined normalized computation time and iteration counts, aiming to simultaneously optimize
994
       both iteration counts and solution times. For AMG preconditioning, also examined with second-
       order elliptic and Darcy flow equations, we used the condition number of the preconditioned matrix
995
       as the metric, where a lower value indicates better performance.
