009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) sheds light on the decision-making
012            ground of black-box models by offering explanations. These explanations need
013            to be robust for trustworthy time series regression applications in high-stake ar-
014            eas like medicine or finance, which yet remains largely unexplored. Furthermore,
015            most adversarial attack methods currently rely on white-box strategies, which re-
016
               quire access to gradient information from both the model and the XAI method.
               In real-world scenarios, such information is often difficult or impossible to ob-
017
               tain. To address these challenges, we propose a novel gradient-free adversarial
018
               attack method specifically designed for time series explanations, targeting non-
019            differentiable XAI techniques. To enhance the effectiveness of our method for
020            time series data, we introduce an attack objective function based on Dynamic
021            Time Warping (DTW). Additionally, we implement an explanation-based local
022            attack strategy, which ensures that the adversarial perturbations remain imper-
023            ceptible within the time series data. In our experiments, we generate adversarial
024            examples to attack four different XAI methods across three black-box models,
025            using two time series datasets. The results reveal the vulnerability of current non-
026            differentiable XAI methods. Furthermore, by comparing our approach with ex-
027
               isting attack methods, we demonstrate the superiority of our proposed objective
               function and local attack strategy.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The ”black box” nature of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has raised public concerns about its lacking
033   of transparency and explainability in decision-making process. To address this issue, Explainable
034   AI (XAI) has emerged, which aims to provide clear and easy-to-understand explanations of AI’s
035   decision making in order to eliminate the public’s concerns about AI, enhance users’ trust, and fur-
036   ther promote the application of AI in critical fields such as military, healthcare, and finance (Ali
037   et al., 2023). Many XAI methods are developed and applied nowadays, such as attribution-based
038   methods or example-based methods, which, however, generally lack of the robustness of the expla-
039
      nation. Taking the medical field as an example, doctors can diagnose or make decisions based on
      XAI-generated explanations, and if a small perturbation (e.g., adversarial attack) is made to the input
040
      sample can generate incorrect explanations. Making decisions based on such compromised expla-
041
      nations could have serious consequences, potentially endangering a patient’s life (van der Velden
042   et al., 2022). This vulnerability raises questions about the validity and reliability of XAI-generated
043   explanations when faced with adversarial attacks, emphasizing the need for more robust XAI meth-
044   ods.
045
      Major research on XAI robustness have focused on adversarial attacks (including adversarial ex-
046
      ample generation, adversarial model generation, or data poisoning) and the corresponding defense
047
      methods (Baniecki and Biecek, 2024). The most widely studied attack approach in this area is adver-
048   sarial example generation, where the core idea is to introduce perturbations to the input data that alter
049   the model’s explanations while leaving its classification results unchanged.(Ghorbani et al., 2019).
050   Corresponding defense methods seek to make the explanations as stable as possible under such situ-
051   ations. However, most methods for generating adversarial examples rely on gradient descent, which
052   is a white-box attack technique that requires access to the model’s loss function and internal gradient
053   information of both the model and the XAI method(Huang et al., 2023). This requirement for prior
      knowledge of gradients is often difficult to fulfill in practical applications (Akhtar and Mian, 2018).

      tion (PSO), which searches for adversarial examples without needing gradient information, making
      it more effective in real-world scenarios and with non-differentiable XAI techniques.

076   On the other hand, most current research on the robustness of XAI has been concentrated in the
077   image domain, with very limited attention given to time series data. Time series data present a
078   unique challenge for adversarial attacks due to the way perturbations are perceived by humans. In
079   image data, slight changes in pixel colors may go unnoticed by the human eye, but humans can
080
      easily detect subtle perturbations in the patterns or lines of a time series. For instance, as shown in
      Fig.??, while small variations in image pixel colors are hard to perceive, humans are more sensitive
081
      to perturbations in time series lines (Yang et al., 2022). If the common strategy of perturbing the
082
      entire sample (which is widely applied in current XAI attacks) is used for time series data, the
083   generated adversarial perturbations are more likely to be detected, reducing the effectiveness of
084   the attack. Therefore, we further design a local attack strategy based on explanation, where only
085   some time points in the time series are selected to be perturbed according to the results of XAI.
086   This strategy not only minimizes the magnitude of perturbations, making them harder to detect,
087   but also increases the efficiency of the attack. Additionally, another challenge arises from the fact
088   that existing objective functions used in adversarial attacks are often unsuitable for time series data.
089   To tackle this, we leverage Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) to design a more appropriate objective
090   function, ensuring that the attack is more effective for time series data.
091   In summary, the research goal of this paper is to design a new attack method against XAI on time
092   series regression problems, demonstrating the vulnerability of XAI methods. The contributions of
093   this paper are summarized as follows:
094
095          • We propose a gradient-free adversarial attack method to test the robustness of XAI
096
               explanations. By introducing the PSO search method, we can attack non-differentiable
               XAI methods without requiring gradient computation. Additionally, we design a DTW-
097
               based objective function and a local attack strategy aimed at enhancing the effectiveness of
098
               the attack in time series regression problems.
099
100
             • Our experimental study demonstrates the effectiveness of our proposed attack. The
               proposed method successfully attacks non-differentiable XAI explanations, validating its
101
               capability to test XAI robustness. Furthermore, the explanation-based local attack strategy
102
               not only reduces computational overhead but also makes the attack more stealthy while
103            achieving results comparable to those of global attack strategies.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Adversarial attacks on XAI explanations can be categorized into three main approaches: generat-
111   ing adversarial examples, generating adversarial models, and data poisoning (Baniecki and Biecek,
112   2024). We focus on adversarial example generation because it was first proposed as a method to
113   demonstrate the fragility of XAI explanations (Ghorbani et al., 2019), and it remains the most ex-
114   tensively researched form of adversarial attack. The work designing an iterative attack based on
115   gradient descent against simple gradient, integrated gradient and deeplift, with analysis of Hessian
116   matrix to reveal robustness is a common challenge in current XAI methods (Ghorbani et al., 2019).
117   These attacks are effective because the nonlinear and non-smooth properties of neural networks
118   cause the gradient to vary significantly over small input perturbations (Ghorbani et al., 2019; Dom-
119
      browski et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020). In response, various defense strategies have been developed
      to counter these attacks, including smoothing activation functions (Dombrowski et al., 2019), adding
120
      regularization terms (Wang et al., 2020; Joo et al., 2023), applying weight decay (Dombrowski et al.,
121
      2022), and minimizing the network’s Hessian matrix (Dombrowski et al., 2022).
122
123   However, a significant limitation of gradient-based attacks is the requirement for knowledge of the
124   model’s loss function and gradient, which is often inaccessible in practical applications (Akhtar and
125
      Mian, 2018). Moreover, when the XAI method is non-differentiable (as is the case with perturbation-
      based methods), these gradient-based attacks become ineffective because the explanation’s gradient
126
      with respect to the input cannot be calculated. In such cases, researchers have resorted to adver-
127
      sarial model generation (Slack et al., 2020) or data poisoning attacks (Baniecki et al., 2022). Re-
128   cently, adversarial example generation methods for non-differentiable XAI have emerged, utilizing
129   gradient-free techniques such as Genetic Algorithms (GA) to overcome the limitations of traditional
130   approaches (Huang et al., 2023; Baniecki and Biecek, 2022). However, these gradient-free methods
131   have so far been applied to image and tabular data (Huang et al., 2023; Baniecki and Biecek, 2022),
132   with no work addressing time series data. Therefore, our objective is to propose a gradient-free
133   adversarial example generation method specifically tailored for time series XAI tasks.

137   3.1   ATTRIBUTION BASED XAI
138
139   There has been extensive research on obtaining explanations from black-box models, with
140   attribution-based methods being the focus of this paper. Attribution-based methods aim to iden-
141   tify which features have the greatest impact on a model’s decisions and attempt to quantify the
142   contribution of each feature to the model’s predictions. These methods can be divided into two
143
      main categories: gradient-based methods and perturbation-based methods. Gradient-based methods
      assess the importance of features by using the model’s gradient with respect to input features. In
144
      contrast, perturbation-based methods determine feature importance by masking or modifying fea-
145
      tures and observing the resulting changes in the model’s predictions. While gradient-based methods
146   are computationally more efficient, their results are often not directly aligned with changes in the
147   model’s output (Ancona et al., 2018). Furthermore, gradient-based methods are only applicable to
148   differentiable models, limiting their versatility compared to perturbation-based methods.
149
      In this paper, we propose adversarial attacks on perturbation-based XAI methods, with a focus on
150
      two widely-used techniques: Locally Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explainer (LIME) and SHapley
151
      Additive exPlanation (SHAP). Additionally, to compare the effectiveness of our attacks on gradient-
152   based methods, we include saliency maps and SmoothGrad in the analysis.

      4     PSO BASED LOCALLY ATTACK ON TIME SERIES
256
257
      Our primary goal is to design an adversarial example generation method that does not require gra-
258   dient computation. To achieve this, we consider employing an evolutionary algorithm based on a
259   search strategy to solve the optimization problem in Eq.5. XAI methods, particularly those relying
260   on perturbations, are often time-consuming and computationally expensive. In generating adversar-
261   ial examples, it is necessary to repeatedly obtain explanations for the perturbed samples, significantly
262   increasing the computational burden. Thus, selecting a more efficient optimization algorithm is cru-
263   cial. Among various evolutionary algorithms, PSO offers several advantages over alternatives such
264   as genetic algorithms and differential evolutionary algorithms. It has fewer parameters, reducing the
265   time spent on parameter tuning. Additionally, PSO strikes a better balance between global and local
266
      search while converging faster than genetic algorithms. Moreover, PSO’s particle update mecha-
      nism is simpler to parallelize, which can further reduce computational time. For these reasons, we
267
      ultimately select PSO (Kennedy and Eberhart, 1995) to address this optimization problem.
268
269   PSO involves five key steps: initialization, evaluating fitness, updating individual and global optima,
      updating particle velocity and position, and checking termination criteria. The middle three steps

273   Initialization: In this step, we initialize the particle swarm’s position, velocity, inertia factor, accel-
274
      eration constants, and the maximum number of iterations. While traditional PSO typically initializes
      particle positions randomly within the search space, we modify this by setting the initial position
275
      of each particle to match the original sample. This approach increases the likelihood of generating
276
      adversarial examples that closely resemble the original sample. Additionally, we implement a local
277   attack strategy to make the attack less detectable in time series data. Instead of perturbing all time
278   points, we select only a certain percentage of time points to perturb. These time points are chosen
279   based on their significance in the XAI-generated explanation, focusing on reducing the importance
280   of these critical time points.
281
      Evaluating fitness: The core of the PSO algorithm lies in the design of the fitness function, which
282
      directs the search path for the particles. The fitness function typically measures the difference be-
283   tween the original and adversarial explanations, with particles searching for solutions that maximize
284   this difference. According to Eq.5, the fitness function is generally designed as the difference be-
285   tween two explanations, and the particle will search in the direction where the difference is greater.
286   In Sec.3.3, we discuss two commonly used fitness functions, top-k and center of mass, but they may
287   not be ideal for time series data. For time series data, XAI explanations indicate the importance
288   of individual time points, which can be interpreted as a time series themselves. Thus, we employ
289   Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) (Berndt and Clifford, 1994), a method for measuring the similarity
290   between time series, to evaluate the difference between the original and adversarial explanations.

368

374   and Spearman’s correlation metrics have a range of [0,1], where smaller values indicate a more suc-
375   cessful attack. Finally, L2 distance is a direct measure of the normalized distance between the two
376   explanations, with larger values indicating a more successful attack. These metrics provide a com-

401   5.3   R ESULTS ON D IFFERENT M ODELS AND XAI M ETHODS
402
403   We begin by attacking the explanations generated by the combination of the three models and the
404   four XAI methods across two datasets using the Ddtw metric. The robustness of the explanations
405   is assessed and summarized in Table 1. The attack strategy perturbs the 20% most important time
406
      points as determined by the explanations. In the table, the explanations with the highest robustness
      are highlighted in bold, while the least robust explanations are italicized.
407
408   It is evident that LSTM with SG generates the most robust explanations. The Top-K Intersection
409   (TKI) and Spearman’s Rank-Order Correlation (SRC) metrics close to 1 indicate that the explana-
410   tion remains almost unchanged after slight perturbations to the input sample. This robustness can
411   be attributed to SG, which reduces the uncertainty in gradient explanations by calculating multiple
412
      gradients and averaging them. Additionally, LSTM’s gating mechanism and memory cell effec-
      tively prevent the gradient vanishing and exploding problems, contributing to more stable explana-
413
      tions. Consequently, gradient-based explanations like SM and SG are more robust when paired with
414
      LSTM. In contrast, TCN and Transformer models are less effective at handling long time sequences.
415   The convolutional kernel in TCN and the attention mechanism in Transformer can introduce spar-
416   sity in information, which hinders gradient propagation through the deep network and reduces the
417   stability of SM and SG explanations.
418
      On the other hand, the perturbation-based methods, LIME and SHAP, are model-agnostic and focus
419
      on feature interactions and importance. As a result, their explanations show similar robustness across
420
      the three AI models. However, the results also demonstrate that both LIME and SHAP explanations
421   are relatively unstable, underscoring the effectiveness of our proposed attack. Specifically, SHAP-
422   generated explanations diverge significantly from the original explanations when facing adversarial
423   samples. Overall, our attack is successful not only against non-differentiable methods like LIME
424   and SHAP but also against gradient-based methods (except for LSTM+SG). This highlights the
425   robustness weaknesses in existing XAI techniques and offers insights for improving future XAI
426   methods.

517   6    C ONCLUSIONS
518
519   In this paper, we propose a gradient-free adversarial attack method targeting explanations gener-
520   ated by explainable artificial intelligence (XAI). We experimentally demonstrate that our attack can
521   significantly alter the explanations produced by XAI methods with only minor modifications to the
522   input samples. This approach not only enhances the stealthiness of the attack but also improves its
523   efficiency and applicability. Our experiments reveal the non-robustness of time series explanations,
524   highlighting vulnerabilities that can be exploited. These findings lay the groundwork for future
525   research aimed at developing defenses against such adversarial attacks on time series explanations.
