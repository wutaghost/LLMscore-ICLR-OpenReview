009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In two-sided markets, the accurate estimation of treatment effects is crucial yet
012            challenging due to the inherent interference between market participants, which
013            violates the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA). This paper in-
014            troduces a novel framework that leverages random graph asymptotics to model
015            and estimate treatment effects under network interference in two-sided markets.
016            By incorporating a random graph model, we handle two-sided randomization by
017            modeling customer interference within the potential outcome function as a func-
018            tion of graph topology and equilibrium dynamics, while capturing listing interfer-
019
               ence through the random graph structure. Our new estimation process provides
               asymptotically normal estimators with robust theoretical properties, suitable for
020
               large-scale market scenarios. Our theoretical findings are supported by extensive
021
               numerical simulations, demonstrating the effectiveness and practical applicabil-
022            ity of our approach in estimating direct and indirect causal effects within these
023            complex market structures.

026   1     I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Two-sided markets have been a fundamental focus in microeconomics for several decades, playing a
029   crucial role in various economic and digital platforms. In a two-sided market, two distinct groups of
030   participants interact, with each group’s success dependent on the presence and activity of the other.
031   In the context of two-sided markets, such as online platforms or rental services, the two primary
032   groups are typically customers and listings (e.g., products, services, or properties). Customers arrive
033   sequentially and make choices from the listings that are available at the time of their arrival. Once a
034   customer books a listing, that listing becomes unavailable for a period while it is being replenished.
035
      This temporary unavailability creates dynamic competition among customers as they vie for listings,
      and it also introduces a time-sensitive element to decision-making in the market. This structure
036
      creates complex interdependencies between the two sides, which present significant challenges in
037
      accurately estimating causal effects within two-sided markets.
038
039   Estimating causal effects within two-sided markets is a critical challenge across various fields. In
040   these markets, interventions affect interactions between participants in the market, and decisions on
041
      whether to launch the intervention across the entire market depend heavily on the accuracy of ex-
      perimental estimates. However, these estimates are often compromised by biases and high variances
042
      due to interference between market participants. As participants interact and compete, the treatment
043
      assigned to one individual can influence the behavior of others, thereby violating the Stable Unit
044   Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA) (Imbens & Rubin, 2015). Previous studies have shown that
045   interference in markets can lead to substantial biases (Johari et al., 2022) and potentially infinite
046   variances as market size increases (Li & Wager, 2022).
047
048   1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
049
050   Estimating treatment effects in two-sided markets has been a challenging area of research due to
051   the interference between participants, which violates the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption
052   (SUTVA). Several studies have attempted to address these challenges by developing models that ac-
053   count for this interference. One common approach is the bipartite experimental framework, where
      treatments are assigned to one set of market participants (e.g., sellers) and outcomes are observed


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      on another set (e.g., buyers) (Pouget-Abadie et al., 2019)(Zigler & Papadogeorgou, 2018). This
055   framework is often represented as a bipartite graph connecting the two groups, which enables the
056   modeling of market dynamics and interference (Harshaw et al., 2022). These studies laid the ground-
057   work for designing experiments in two-sided markets but faced limitations when not only one side
058   of the market receives treatment.
059
      Recent advancements have focused on two-sided randomization designs, where both sides of the
060
      market receive treatment and control (Masoero et al., 2024). The predominant approach involves
061   using a continuous Markov chain model combined with a mean field limit analysis (Johari et al.,
062   2022). Wager & Xu (2021) and Li et al. (2022) leverage domain-specific structures within two-sided
063   marketplaces and network routing to assess the bias of difference-in-means estimators across differ-
064   ent experimental designs. Their model captures the dynamics of two-sided markets by analyzing
065   the limiting case, where both the number of listings and the customer arrival rates approach infinity.
066   Although this framework provides a solid foundation for analysis and introduces novel estimators,
067   these estimators are particularly sensitive to the relative volumes of supply and demand. Biases can
068   emerge due to the interconnected nature of participants, potentially leading to network effects and
069
      interference (Blake & Coey, 2014)(Cortez-Rodriguez et al., 2023)(Fradkin, 2019). Moreover, these
      estimators lack desirable asymptotic properties, underscoring the need for new approaches that can
070
      more effectively account for interference in two-sided markets.
071
072   In response to the limitations of traditional randomization techniques, a growing body of work has
073   explored network-based interference models using exposure graphs. For example, Doudchenko et al.
074   (2020) puts forward a framework that considers network structures in which edges probabilistically
075
      form between participants in bipartite structures, connecting treatments and outcomes through ran-
      dom formations. Erdos-Rényi random graphs are utilized for experimentation purposes to simulate
076
      network structures with interference. Cai et al. (2023) proposes an independent set design, which
077
      partitions a network into two sets: the independent set (non-interfering units) and the auxiliary set
078   (interfering units), helping to control bias and variance in causal estimators by separating treatment
079   and interference effects. Zheleva & Arbour (2021) further discusses techniques to mitigate inter-
080   ference in network-based randomized controlled trials, including block designs, chain graphs, and
081   abstract ground graphs, which model the relationships between entities in the networks.
082
      Utilizing random graphs to estimate causal effects is particularly valuable in networks with interfer-
083
      ence, such as two-sided markets, where traditional randomized experiments often fail to accurately
084   estimate causal effects due to spillovers between connected units. Our research focuses on a model
085   in which units are represented as vertices on a graph, with edges connecting any two units if the
086   treatment of one may influence the potential outcome of the other (Li & Wager, 2022). This method
087   has gained traction for its ability to model network interference, particularly in settings where the
088   interference structure is complex. Appropriate random graph assumptions can facilitate more man-
089   ageable analyses of treatment effects under network interference, leading to methodological ad-
090   vancements.

093
094
      By addressing the limitations of existing approaches, our contributions offer a significant advance-
      ment in the understanding and practical application of treatment effect estimation in two-sided mar-
095
      kets. We introduce a new framework for analyzing experiments conducted in two-sided markets,
096
      and we propose a novel estimation process designed to achieve estimators with robust asymptotic
097   properties. Our key contributions are as follows:
098
099          • We incorporate the random graph model proposed in Li & Wager (2022) to the two-sided
100            markets, where the graph structure reflects the inherent competition and interference among
101            market participants. To deal with two-sided randomization, we model the interference be-
102            tween customers within the potential outcome function as a function of both graph topology
103            and equilibrium dynamics, and capture the interference between listings through the ran-
104            dom graph model.
105          • We use the steady-state mass of listings to determine the graphon value, which represents
106            the probability of two listings being connected, accounting for the interference between
107            them. This allows for a realistic and analytically tractable analysis of treatment effects in
               two-sided markets.

188   3    R ANDOM G RAPH M ODEL
189
190   Investigating treatment effect estimation under random graph asymptotics has proven effective (Li &
191   Wager, 2022). However, existing methods are limited to single-sided randomization, where only one
192   set of units receives treatment. To extend this framework to two-sided randomization, we account for
193   customer-side interference in the potential outcome function by incorporating both graph topology
194   and equilibrium dynamics. Meanwhile, listing-side interference is modeled using a random graph
195   approach. Specifically, we model the listings as the vertices of a random graph, with the interference
196
      graph represented as a random draw from a graphon. Given a set of regularity assumptions detailed
      below, we can estimate causal effects within a two-sided market framework in our setting. For
197
      direct effect estimation, standard estimators from the literature are both unbiased and asymptotically
198
      Gaussian. For indirect effects, our use of the PC balancing estimator demonstrates significant power.
199

      Eij = 1. We consider the following assumptions and demonstrate their validity in our setting of the

527   This study advances the understanding of treatment effect estimation in two-sided markets by ad-
528   dressing the limitations of existing methodologies, particularly those affected by interference. By
529
      introducing a random graph model and novel estimation techniques, we provide a robust framework
      capable of handling the complexities of two-sided markets. Our simulations confirm the reliability
530
      of the proposed estimators, showing their potential for practical application in real-world scenarios.
531

532   A/B testing scenarios. These contributions open new avenues for designing and analyzing experi-
533   ments in two-sided markets, with implications for both theoretical research and applied economic
534   strategies.
