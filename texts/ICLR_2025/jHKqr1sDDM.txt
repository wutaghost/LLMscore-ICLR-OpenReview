                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Large language models (LLMs) trained on antibody sequences have shown signifi-
               cant potential in the rapidly advancing field of machine learning-assisted antibody
013
               engineering and drug discovery. However, current state-of-the-art antibody LLMs
014
               often overlook structural information, which could enable the model to more ef-
015            fectively learn the functional properties of antibodies by providing richer, more
016            informative data. In response to this limitation, we introduce IgBlend, which
017            integrates both the 3D coordinates of backbone atoms (C-alpha, N, and C) and
018            antibody sequences. Our model is trained on a diverse dataset containing over 4
019            million unique structures and more than 200 million unique sequences, including
020            heavy and light chains as well as nanobodies. We rigorously evaluate IgBlend using
021            established benchmarks such as sequence recovery, complementarity-determining
022            region (CDR) editing and inverse folding and demonstrate that IgBlend consistently
023
               outperforms current state-of-the-art models across all benchmarks. Furthermore,
               experimental validation shows that the model’s log probabilities correlate well with
024
               measured binding affinities.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Antibodies are key components of the adaptive immune system, capable of recognizing and neutraliz-
031   ing a wide range of pathogens, including viruses, bacteria, and other foreign invaders. Their ability
032   to bind specific targets with high affinity makes them essential tools in therapeutic development.
033   Recent advancements in natural language processing (NLP) have led to the creation of foundational
034   language models that can learn from and modify antibody sequences (Olsen et al., 2022b; 2024;
035   Prihoda et al., 2022). Moreover, the three-dimensional (3D) structure of an antibody is closely linked
036
      to its specificity, affinity, and interaction with antigens. Therefore, capturing the relationship between
      sequence and structure is crucial for tasks such as affinity maturation, de novo antibody design, and
037
      optimizing antibody-antigen interactions for therapeutic applications. While current language models
038
      excel at either sequence-to-sequence or structure-to-sequence (inverse folding) tasks, relying on only
039   one of these modalities at the input limits their capability and flexibility in more complex antibody
040   engineering tasks (Olsen et al., 2022b; 2024; Prihoda et al., 2022; Høie et al., 2023). In this paper,
041   we introduce IgBlend, a multi-modal model designed to incorporate both sequence and structural
042   information for antibody engineering. Our approach can utilize either sequence, structure, or both,
043   enabling the model to not only sample sequences that can fold to the same parental backbone but
044   also generate more diverse sequences, providing greater flexibility in designing antibody sequences.
045   Moreover, by utilizing both experimentally resolved structures (Dunbar et al., 2014) and synthetic
046   data generated through structure prediction models (Abanades et al., 2023b; Ruffolo et al., 2023),
047
      we aim to improve model performance on key antibody engineering tasks. Our contributions can be
      summarized as follows:
048
049
             • We introduce IgBlend, a model that learns antibody representations from either sequence,
050
               structure or sequence-structure pairs when structural data is available.
051
052          • We present a pre-training strategy with multiple sub-objectives as well as a procedure for
053            training and dataset processing, all of which can broadly be applied to other multi-modal
               training settings.

              • We empirically demonstrate that integrating structural information, even when synthetically
055             generated, significantly improves the performance of large models across a wide range of
056             benchmarks.

059   The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. First, we review related works and introduce the
060   notations. Section 2 details the architecture of IgBlend, datasets, and training procedures. Then, we
061
      compare the performance of IgBlend against existing models in Section 3. Finally, we point out that
      a more detailed background on antibodies can be found in Appendix A.
062
063   Related work. In recent years, significant progress has been made in developing protein and antibody
064   foundation models, drawing from advances in natural language processing (NLP). These models can
065   be broadly categorized based on their focus—either on general protein design or antibody-specific
066   tasks—and the way they approach the problem, such as sequence-to-structure prediction, structure-
067
      to-sequence generation, or sequence-structure co-design. For general protein design, sequence-to-
      structure models, including AlphaFold (Jumper et al., 2021) and RoseTTAFold (Baek et al., 2021),
068
      have significantly improved the prediction of protein structures from amino acid sequences. In
069
      the structure-to-sequence domain (inverse folding), ESM-IF (Hsu et al., 2022) predicts amino acid
070   sequences that fold into a given structure. Meanwhile, sequence-to-sequence models, such as ESM
071   (Rives et al., 2021) and its variants, excel at identifying patterns within sequences for tasks such as
072   sequence recovery and mutation prediction. Moreover, some recent works have focused on sequence-
073   structure co-design, with models such as ESM3 (Hayes et al., 2024) incorporating both sequence
074   and structure as well as function to improve protein design. There is also hybrid approaches such
075   as LM-Design (Zheng et al., 2023) that leverage both sequence and structural inputs to design new
076   protein sequences, aligning with the approach used in this paper.
077   In the context of antibodies, several models have emerged with a similar framework but tailored for
078   immunoglobulins. For sequence-to-structure prediction, antibody-specific models such as ImmuneB-
079   uilder (Abanades et al., 2023b) and IgFold (Ruffolo et al., 2023) predict 3D antibody structures
080   from sequence data. In the structure-to-sequence domain, AntiFold (Høie et al., 2023) addresses
081   the inverse folding problem, predicting antibody sequences that correspond to a given backbone
082   structure. For sequence-to-sequence tasks, models such as AbLang (Olsen et al., 2022b), AbLang-2
083   (Olsen et al., 2024), AntiBERTy (Ruffolo et al., 2021), and Sapiens (Prihoda et al., 2022), which are
084
      predominantly based on the BERT architecture (Devlin et al., 2018), are specifically designed for
      antibody sequences, helping to improve performance on tasks such as residue restoration and paratope
085
      identification. To the best of our knowledge, although these models focus on either sequence or
086
      structure, no existing antibody-specific LLM effectively integrates both modalities. In this paper, we
087   address this gap by introducing a sequence-structure-to-sequence framework, similar to LM-Design
088   (Zheng et al., 2023) for proteins, to learn a richer and more informative representation. IgBlend learns
089   joint representations of structure and sequence during pre-training, improving upon models that rely
090   on a single modality. Finally, we leave out diffusion, flow matching, and graph-based approaches to
091   antibody design to maintain our focus on language models.

133   2.1    M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
134
      The proposed architecture, IgBlend, is illustrated in Fig 2 and consists of three primary components:
135
      a structure encoder that handles the backbone coordinates of the antibody, a sequence encoder that
136   processes the amino acid sequence, and a multi-modal trunk that processes both structural and
137   sequential representations.

170   representation (i.e., embedding of size 512) for each amino acid si ∈ A in the full sequence
171   s = (s1 , . . . , sn ). The architecture includes a one-hot encoded input followed by two blocks of a
172   standard Transformer model (Vaswani et al., 2017). This architecture has already been shown to learn
173   relevant information from antibody sequences (Olsen et al., 2022b; 2024). Specifically, the module
174   utilizes sinusoidal positional embeddings, a SwiGLU activation function (Shazeer, 2020), and has
175   an embedding dimension of 512. Additionally, any amino acid within the sequences can be masked
176   using the masked token ∗.
177   Multi-modality encoder. The fusion layer processes both modalities in two steps. First, it combines
178   the abstract representations from the sequence and structure encoders by concatenating them along
179   the embedding dimension, forming a single vector of size 1024 for each residue. It then processes the
180   concatenated modalities through a series of four Transformer blocks with SwiGLU activations.
181   Classification head. Finally, the classification head consists of a multi-layer perceptron (MLP)
182   followed by a softmax function and processes the multi-modal representation to generate a probability
183   distribution over amino acid types at each position. Further details can be found in Appendix B.

275   3     E MPIRICAL RESULTS
276
277
278
      In this section, we evaluate the impact of incorporating structural information into the pre-training
      of antibody LLMs. Our evaluation focuses on three tasks: (i) sequence recovery of the variable
279
      region, (ii) editing of the CDR regions, and (iii) inverse folding. We compare the performance of
280
      IgBlend with five existing open-source antibody and nanobody language models, including AbLang
281   (Olsen et al., 2022b), AbLang2 (Olsen et al., 2024), AntiBERTy (Ruffolo et al., 2021), Sapiens
282   (Prihoda et al., 2022) and Nanobert (Hadsund et al., 2024) as well as two inverse folding models,
283   including AntiFold (Høie et al., 2023) and ESM-IF (Hsu et al., 2022).
284
285
286   3.1   S EQUENCE RECOVERY
287
288   First, we evaluated the task of recovering missing residues in the variable region of an antibody.
289   This task is particularly relevant for various applications where the goal is either to recover, edit,
290
      or mutate specific amino acids within a sequence. Following the benchmark established in (Olsen
      et al., 2022b; 2024), we proceeded as follows. First, we sampled 1,000 sequences/structures pairs,
291
      (s, x), per chain type from the test distribution, using Equation (1). Then, for each pair (s, x), we
292
      randomly sample a sequential mask Ms that contains between 10% and 40% of the residue indices
293   from the full sequence [1, . . . , |s|]. To evaluate the benefits of incorporating structural information,
294   we compared the performance of IgBlend using different input types. Specifically, we assessed each
295   model’s ability to recover the full sequence under various conditions: for sequence-only models,
296   we used sb = Model(s/Ms ); for structure-guided sequential models, we used sb = Model(s/Ms , x);
297   for sequential models that use masked structural information, we used sb = Model(s/Ms , x/Ms );
298   and for inverse folding models, we used sb = Model(x). For each chain type and each region
299   reg∈[FW1, CDR1, FW2, CDR2, FW3, CDR3, FW4], where FW and CDR refer to framework
300   and CDR regions of the chain respectively, we recorded the empirical accuracy of the models by
301   computing the Levenshtein({si , i ∈ Ms ∩ reg}, {b      si , i ∈ Ms ∩ reg}) distance over each region and
302   averaged the results over 1,000 sequences. Results are reported in Table 2 for the CDR3 regions and
303   the remaining regions can be found in Table 4 of the Appendix. A few remarks are of order:
304
305          • First, sequence-only models (AbLang, AbLang2, AntiBERTy, Sapiens, IgBlend) performed
306
               similarly across all regions, with at most a 3% difference in accuracy between the models in
               most regions. In this sense, it has to be noted that IgBlend, trained using a combination of
307
               three different objectives shown in Equation (2), performed on par with models trained solely
308
               on the seq2seq task, indicating that multi-modal training does not compromise performance
309            on individual tasks.
310
311          • Secondly, it is important to note that the performance of IgBlend consistently improves
312            with the addition of more input modalities across all chain types. Specifically, for each
313            chain type, IgBlend shows the same ranking in recovery rate: IgBlend(seq+struct guided)
314            > IgBlend(seq+masked struct) > IgBlend(seq-only) and IgBlend(seq+struct guided) >
315            IgBlend(inverse folding). Hence, adding information helps the model to be more precise and
316            we deduce that proposed training procedure allows us to merge both modalities successfully.
317
             • Most notably, by incorporating structural information alongside the masked sequence
318
               (IgBlend(seq+struct guidance)), we achieved consistently better results than sequence-only
319            models across all regions and for all modalities. This improvement was particularly notable
320            in the CDR3 region of the nanobody (N-CDR3), where IgBlend(seq+struct) outperformed
321            the second-best model by 11.8% in accuracy. Similar improvements were observed in the
322            CDR3 regions of the light chain (L-CDR3) with a 6.6% increase, and the heavy chain
323            (H-CDR3) with a 7.7% increase.

      3.2   C OMPLEMENTARITY- DETERMINING REGION (CDR) EDITING
339
340   Second, we focused on the task of editing/recovering the CDR regions of a single chain, which is
341   of particular importance in the process of optimizing antibodies for affinity. In this task, one of the
342   CDR regions is randomly selected and fully masked, i.e., Ms ∈ {CDR1, CDR2, CDR3}, and the
343   models are asked to predict the residues within the selected fully masked CDR. Using the same
344   experimental setup with the masked CDR Ms , we evaluated the models—seq-only, structure-guided,
345   and inverse folding—on 1,000 sequences sampled from the test set as described in Equation (1).
346   These sequences were not seen during the training of IgBlend, and we recorded the percentage of
347
      successfully recovered residues for each chain type. The results can be found in the table shown
      in Figure 3 with all models being evaluated on the same masked sequences. To further evaluate
348
      how well models using structural information adhere to structural instructions, we assessed the
349
      structural similarities between the generated sequences and the input structure x. We compared
350   the top models in each category: AbLang2 for heavy/light chains, NanoBert for nanobodies, and
351   AntiFold for inverse folding. We sampled 500 sequences per chain type from the test distribution and
352   tasked each model with recovering a missing CDR. For each recovered sequence sb, we computed
353   its structural approximation x   b = IgFold(b s) using IgFold with PyRosetta refinement. We then
354   measured the Levenshtein({si , i ∈ Ms }, {b    si , i ∈ Ms }) distance in the masked region and the
355   RMSD({xi , i ∈ Ms }, {b    xi , i ∈ Ms }) between the original and recovered structures to assess
356   structural similarity. Results are shown in Figure 3, with extended findings available in Appendix E.2.
357   From both evaluations, key observations include:
358
              • First, as in previous experiments, the top-performing sequence-only models (AbLang,
359
                AbLang2, AntiBERTy, IgBlend) showed similar performance across different CDR re-
360
                gions. However, IgBlend outperformed the best sequence-only model by over 9% in
361             accuracy for nanobodies. Notably, incorporating additional information consistently im-
362             proved IgBlend’s performance across all chain types (i.e., IgBlend (Seq+Struct Guided)
363             > IgBlend (Seq+Masked Struct) > IgBlend (Seq-only)). Specifically, adding structural
364             information to the masked sequence (IgBlend (seq+struct guidance)) significantly enhanced
365             performance compared to the best sequence-only models, with improvements of 11.8% in
366             H-CDR3, 6.74% in L-CDR3, and 15.43% in N-CDR3.
367           • Second, unlike the previous experiments, IgBlend (seq+struct guidance), which uses both
368             (sMs , xMx ), shows performance closer to IgBlend (inverse folding), which relies solely on
369             the structure x, than to IgBlend (seq-only), which depends only on the sequential information
370             sMs . This suggests that, in the task of re-editing complete CDR regions, IgBlend relies
371             more on structural information than on sequential data.

415   set, which was not seen during IgBlend’s training. Each inverse folding model was then asked to
416   predict the sequence bs = Model(x) based solely on the structure x. To evaluate model performance,
417   we tested different temperatures: T = 1e − 4 for the highest probability sequence, T = 1 for

463
      Finally, we assessed the ability of our models to score sequences for H-CDR3 design using experimen-
464   tal data. We followed the approach outlined by Shanehsazzadeh et al. (2023), where deep learning
465   models were used to re-design H-CDR3 sequences targeting HER2. In particular, they selected the
466   therapeutic antibody trastuzumab, which targets HER2, as a template and re-designed the heavy chain
467   CDR3 sequences s, conditioned on the HER2 antigen backbone structure from PDB 1N8Z and the
468   trastuzumab framework sequences. The KD values of the generated sequences were then measured
469   using a Fluorescence-activated Cell Sorting (FACS)-based ACE assay. To evaluate how well our
470   models could pre-screen sequences likely to exhibit strong binding, we scored the sequences they
471   generated (keeping the same H-CDR3 length as trastuzumab) by computing their log-likelihood using
472
      both AbLang2 and IgBlend (seq-only). To assess whether structural information improves scoring,
      we also scored the sequences using IgBlend (inverse folding), where the backbone structure x of
473
      trastuzumab was provided as input, steering the model to favor sequences with a structure similar to
474
      trastuzumab and AntiFold. The − log(KD ) values against the likelihood scores from each model are
475   displayed in Figure 5 as well as the Spearman correlation and Kendall tau.
476
477                  • First, it is evident that sequence-only models, such as AbLang2 and IgBlend (seq-only),
478                    offer limited utility in predicting which sequences are likely to have strong binding affinities.

480                    actual binding affinities. This can be attributed to the fact that these models are trained
481                    on sequence data alone, with no information about conformational states that would favor
482                    binding to HER2. As a result, they tend to generate biologically plausible sequences without
483
                       prioritizing binding affinity (since much of the training set is derived from the OAS dataset,

508    4            C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
509
510    In this study, we investigated how incorporating structural information into antibody LLMs enhances
511    performance. We introduced a model that integrates both structural and sequential data, showing that
512    this combination consistently improves performance across all benchmarks compared to sequence-
513
       based and inverse-folding models. However, while effective, our approach sometimes sacrifices
       sequence diversity for accuracy. Future work will focus on including side-chain information and
514
       expanding structural datasets.
