011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Text-conditioned generation models are commonly evaluated based on the qual-
014            ity of the generated data and its alignment with the input text prompt. On the
015            other hand, several applications of prompt-based generative models require suffi-
016
               cient diversity in the generated data to ensure the models’ capability of generating
               image and video samples possessing a variety of features. However, the exist-
017
               ing diversity metrics are designed for unconditional generative models, and thus
018
               cannot distinguish the diversity arising from variations in text prompts and that
019            contributed by the generative model itself. In this work, our goal is to quantify the
020            prompt-induced and model-induced diversity in samples generated by prompt-
021            based models. Specifically, we propose the application of matrix-based informa-
022            tion measures to address this task, decomposing the kernel-based entropy H(X)
023            of generated data X into the sum of conditional entropy H(X|T ), given text vari-
024            able T , and mutual information I(X; T ). We show that this information-theoretic
025            approach decomposes the existing Vendi diversity score defined based on H(X)
026            into the product of the following two terms: 1) Conditional-Vendi score based on
027
               H(X|T ) to quantify the model-induced diversity, and 2) Information-Vendi score
               based on I(X; T ) to measure the statistical relevance between X and prompt T .
028
               Our theoretical results provide an interpretation for this diversity quantification
029
               and show that the Conditional-Vendi score aggregates the Vendi scores within
030            the modes of a mixture prompt distribution. We conduct several numerical ex-
031            periments to show the correlation between the Conditional-Vendi score and the
032            internal diversity of text-conditioned generative models.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Prompt-based generative models, including text-to-image and text-to-video generation schemes, are
038   widely used in various artificial intelligence (AI) applications. In prompt-based generative AI, the
039   sample creation process begins with a text input and produces a random output aligned with that
040   text. The conditional nature of this sample generation distinguishes prompt-based generative models
041   from standard unconditional generative models where the objective is to produce samples distributed
042   similarly to real data without any guiding input prompt. Since most evaluation metrics for generative
043   models had been developed for unconditional models in the previous decade, the recent literature
044
      has sought to create scores tailored specifically for text-conditioned generative models.
045   The existing evaluation metrics for prompt-based generative models typically focus on fidelity and
046   relevance in sample generation, i.e., they assess the visual quality of the produced samples and
047   their alignment with the input prompt. Relevance is often measured by calculating a similarity
048   score between a shared embedding of the text and image samples, e.g. in ClipScore (Hessel et al.,
049   2021) which utilizes the CLIP embeddings of text and image data. Such shared embedding-based
050
      evaluation mechanisms have been further adapted to quantify the aesthetics, semantic consistency,
      and compositional accuracy of the generated data based on the input text prompt.
051
052   On the other hand, the diversity performance of prompt-based generative models has not been exclu-
053   sively studied in the literature. The diversity scores proposed for unconditional generative models,
      such as Recall (Sajjadi et al., 2018; Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019), Coverage (Naeem et al., 2020),

077   applied to quantify the variety of generated samples. However, in text-based generative models, the
078   generated samples are typically produced in response to different input prompts, where the variation
079   in input texts can significantly contribute to the diversity of the generated image or video samples.
080   Thus, the diversity of data produced by prompt-based models is influenced by two main factors: 1)
081   the variety of input prompts, and 2) the internal diversity of the model introducing randomness into
082   the output samples. Figure 1 illustrates examples of prompt-induced diversity, where the variety of
083
      generated images is mostly due to the different prompts and the generated data has little variety in
      other details (images of similar people with similar poses), and model-induced diversity where the
084
      details not specified by the prompts vary significantly between generated images. This decompo-
085
      sition of diversity in text-based generative models has not been studied in the existing literature on
086   conditional generative models including text-based image and video generation.
087
088   In this work, we focus on quantifying the two diversity components mentioned for prompt-based
089
      generative models. To this end, we propose an information-theoretic decomposition of the diversity
      of the model’s output data, X. The proposed decomposition is based on a classical identity in infor-
090
      mation theory, which shows that for variables X and T , the Shannon entropy H(X), representing
091
      the uncertainty of X, can be decomposed into two terms as follows:
092
093                                    H(X) = H X|T + I X; T
094
      Here, H(X|T ) denotes the conditional entropy of data X given the text variable T , which we
095
      interpret as the internal diversity of the text-based generative model not caused by variation in the
096   input text T . Furthermore, the mutual information term I(X; T ) can be viewed as a measure of
097   statistical relevance between the text T and the generated data X, quantifying how much information
098   the model’s output conveys about the input text.
099
      To mathematically define the entropy-based scores, we follow the kernel-matrix-based entropy def-
100
      inition, which has been applied by (Friedman & Dieng, 2023; Jalali et al., 2023; Pasarkar & Dieng,
101
      2024) to unconditional generative models. These references apply the matrix-based entropy in quan-
102   tum information theory, that is the entropy of the eigenvalues of the kernel matrix of generated data
103   X, to measure the diversity of an unconditional model’s generated data. To extend the framework to
104   conditional prompt-based generative models, we utilize the definition of matrix-based conditional
105   entropy proposed by Giraldo et al. (2014). This work provides a definition for the conditional en-
106   tropy of two general positive semi-definite matrices, which we select to be the kernel matrices of
107   generated data X and text T . Following these definitions, our work extends the entropy-based
      approach in (Friedman & Dieng, 2023; Jalali et al., 2023) to conditional generative models. We de-

109   score to model-induced and prompt-induced diversity measures.

111   To statistically interpret the defined scores, we derive the statistic estimated by the proposed scores
112
      from empirical generated samples. We show how the target statistic can be formulated in terms of
      the kernel covariance matrix of the Kronecker product of text T and data X vectors. Importantly,
113
      we prove a theorem to interpret and provide an operational meaning for the proposed entropy-based
114
      scores. Note that the conditional entropy measures in (Giraldo et al., 2014) do not follow standard
115   information theory, and hence the defining equation H(X, T ) − H(T ) = Et∼PT [H(X|T = t)],
116   well-known for Shannon conditional entropy, does not hold for the conditional entropy measure
117   in (Giraldo et al., 2014). To address this gap and provide a practical interpretation for the defined
118   conditional entropy H(X|T ), we prove that under a mixture distribution PT for text data with a
119   hidden group variable G, the defined conditional entropy indeed aggregates H(X|G = g) given
120   prompt type g ∼ PG . This result shows the Conditional-Vendi is an aggregation of Vendi scores.
121   Specifically, our theoretical analysis on the connection between the Conditional VENDI score and
122   average of unconditional entropy scores indicate a spectral approach to interpret the diversity evalua-
123   tion of the proposed score. Following the spectral identification of modes in the eigendecomposition
124   of matrix-based entropy function, we show that the modes used by the Conditional-VENDI score
125   follow from the eigenvectors of the kernel covariance matrix of the text prompt distributions. There-
126   fore, we visualize the diversity contributed by the prompt-based generative model using the spectral
127   clustering of text samples according to the kernel function used in the definition of the diversity
128   score. After computing the eigendirections of the text kernel matrix, we analyze the Hadamard
129
      product of each eigendirection-based rank-1 matrix and the joint (prompt,data) kernel matrix. This
      analysis reveals the variant clusters of images generated for each group of input text samples.
130
131   We numerically evaluate the proposed diversity scores for standard text-to-image, text-to-video, and
132   image-captioning generative models. In our experiments, we simulate text-based generative models
133   for which the ground-truth rankings of internal diversity and relevance are known. Our experimental
134   results validate the consistency of our proposed information-theoretic scores and the ground-truth
135
      ranking of the models. We further decompose the Conditional-Vendi score across different modes
      of input text data, evaluating the models’ internal diversity across different types of input text. The
136
      following is a summary of the contributions of this work:
137
138   • Highlighting the diversity evaluation task in the context of conditional generative models,
139
      • Proposing an information-theoretic framework for decomposing the diversity of generated data
140     into prompt-induced and model-induces components to evaluate the internal diversity of prompt-
141     based generative models
142
      • Providing an operational meaning for the defined scores and interpreting them as the average of
143
        entropy scores over the modes of a multi-modal text distribution
144
      • Presenting numerical results on the consistency between the conditional entropy score and the
145
        model-induced diversity of text-to-image and text-to-video generative models.

148   2   R ELATED W ORK
149
150   Evaluation of deep generative models: The existing metrics for the evaluation of generative mod-
151   els can be divided into reference-dependent and reference-free categories, as discussed in (Borji,
152   2022). As one type of reference-dependent metrics, a distance between generated and reference
153
      distributions is measured using metrics such as FID (Heusel et al., 2017) and KID (Bińkowski et al.,
      2018). Other reference-based metrics such as the Inception Score (Salimans et al., 2016), GAN-
154
      train/GAN-test (Shmelkov et al., 2018), Precision/Recall (Sajjadi et al., 2018; Kynkäänniemi et al.,
155
      2019), and Density/Coverage (Naeem et al., 2020) are defined to quantify the diversity and quality
156   of generated data in comparison to the samples in the real dataset. In addition, assessing memoriza-
157   tion and novelty has been studied in several references, including the authenticity score (Alaa et al.,
158   2022) and Feature Likelihood Divergence (Jiralerspong et al., 2023) to assess generalizability, and
159   the rarity score (Han et al., 2023), KEN (Zhang et al., 2024) proposed to assess novelty. Note that the
160   memorization metrics are inherently reference-based. In contrast, reference-free evaluations mea-
161   sure diversity and quality based only based on the generated data. Specifically, the Vendi (Friedman
      & Dieng, 2023; Pasarkar & Dieng, 2024) and RKE scores (Jalali et al., 2023) fall into this category.

162
      Evaluation of conditional generative models: The evaluation of prompt-based generative models,
163   including text-to-image and text-to-video models, has been studied in several related works. Most
164   of the existing evaluation metrics attempt to measure the correlation between the prompt and the
165   output. A standard metric for measuring the alignment of prompt and image is CLIPScore (Hessel
166   et al., 2021), which measures the cosine similarity of the paired data using CLIP embedding. Some
167   other works introduce different benchmarks and sets of prompts to evaluate different aspects. HEIM
168   (Lee et al., 2023) assesses twelve aspects of the sample generation, including text-image alignment,
169   image quality and bias. As noted by Astolfi et al. (2024), standard metrics focusing on style, aes-
170   thetics, and image quality, may overlook the diversity of images given a particular prompt. In their
171
      work, they measure diversity separately for each prompt using a similarity function and average the
      scores for the prompts. Kannen et al. (2024) follow a similar approach with the Vendi score. We
172
      note that both these methods require generating multiple images per prompt with different seeds to
173
      measure the score. On the other hand, our proposed Conditional-Vendi score does not require mul-
174   tiple sample generations per prompt and instead analyzes the types of text prompt in the assessment.
175   Our theoretical results interpret Conditional-Vendi as an aggregation of the scores over text types.
176
177
      Information measures for evaluating conditional generative models: Kim et al. (2022) utilize
      the mutual information (MI) between continuous text and image variables, and propose the Mutual
178
      Information Divergence (MID) score. This work fits a multivariate Gaussian distribution to the
179
      text and image data and then estimates their mutual information to quantify a relevance metric for
180   conditional generative models. We note that our proposed Information-Vendi score is based on
181   the matrix-based entropy score by Giraldo et al. (2014) which is different from the MI between
182   Gaussian vectors fitted to the text and image data used in the MID score. Different from MID,
183   Information-Vendi relies on kernel similarity values to identify a cluster variable for MI calculation.

      Throughout the work, we focus on a conditional generative model that produces a data vector X ∈
187   X given an input text prompt T ∈ T according to a conditional distribution PX|T , i.e., for text
188   prompt T = t the model outputs a random sample following PX|T =t . We consider n sample pairs

191   internal diversity of the prompt-based generative model, influencing the variety of data generated
192   x1 , . . . , xn independently of the diversity of input texts t1 , . . . , tn .

351   We empirically evaluated the Conditional-Vendi and Information-Vendi scores for three types
352   of conditional generative models: 1) text-to-image, 2) text-to-video generation, and 3) image-
353   captioning models. For text-to-image models, we tested Flux (Lab, 2024), Stable Diffusion 2.1
354   (Rombach et al., 2022), Stable Diffusion XL (Podell et al., 2024), GigaGAN (Kang et al., 2023),
355   Kandinsky (Razzhigaev et al., 2023), and PixArt (Chen et al., 2023b; 2024b). For prompt-based
356   video generative models, we considered VideoCrafter1 (Chen et al., 2023a), Show-1 (Zhang et al.,
357
      2023), and Open-Sora (Zheng et al., 2024). For image-captioning models, we experimented with
      BLIP (Li et al., 2022), GIT (Wang et al., 2022), and GPT4o-mini (OpenAI, 2024).
358
359   Embeddings used in the evaluation of generative models. Unlike standard embedding-based
360   scores for text-to-image models such as CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2021), which require the same
361   embedding model for the text and generated image, the definitions of Conditional-Vendi and
362   Information-Vendi allow different feature extractors for text and generated sample. In our exper-
363
      iments, we followed (Stein et al., 2023; Kynkäänniemi et al., 2023), to use the DINOv2 (Oquab
      et al., 2023) embedding for image data. For text data, we used Gemini (Team, 2024) and CLIP
364
      (Radford et al., 2021), and for video samples, following the video evaluation literature (Kim et al.,
365
      2024; Saito et al., 2020; Unterthiner et al., 2019), we used I3D (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017). To
366   select the bandwidth parameter σ, similar to (Jalali et al., 2023), we chose the Gaussian kernel band-
367   width for each type of data as the smallest σ that ensures a variance below 0.01 in the evaluated
368   score over independent evaluations. We observed that for image data, σ ∈ [20, 30]; for text data,
369   σ ∈ [0.1, 0.8]; and for video data, σ ∈ [10, 20] can satisfy this requirement.

      7     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we proposed an evaluation score to measure the internal diversity of prompt-based
531
      generative models, isolating diversity that is not induced by variations in text prompts. The pro-
      posed method is based on a decomposition of unconditional matrix-based entropy scores, Vendi and
532
      RKE, into Conditional-Vendi and Information-Vendi components. From a theoretical perspective,
533
      we derived the kernel-based statistics estimated by these scores and demonstrated their connection
534   to the expectation of unconditional entropy values given a fixed text prompt. In our experiments, we
535   evaluated the proposed scores in multiple settings where the ground-truth ranking of model diversity
536   and relevance was known, showing that the scores correlate well with the ground-truth rankings. A
537   future direction is to apply the proposed scores to quantify biases in existing models regarding sam-
538   ple generation across different human ethnicities and genders. Additionally, using these scores as
539   a regularization penalty to train more diverse prompt-based models is another interesting area for
      further exploration.

944   require the same embedding for both text and image, our method places no such restriction. This
945   allows for the use of different embeddings for text and image. Furthermore, our approach can be
946   easily generalized to other conditional models, such as text-to-text or text-to-video generation.

       To validate the theoretical connection between the Vendi and Conditional-Vendi scores, we per-
1053   formed an experiment and evaluated a baseline metric called GroundTruth-Cluster-Vendi score. To
1054   measure the GroundTruth-Cluster-Vendi score, we utilize the side knowledge of the ground-truth
1055   clusters of the input prompts and then compute and average the regular Vendi scores for the data
1056   generated within each cluster. Mathematically, given t sample cluster sets in S = {S1 , . . . , St },
1057   which partition the input text indices {1, . . . , n}, we define the Cluster-Vendi score as follows,
1058   where |Sj | denotes the cardinality of subset Sj :

1062
1063   Note that the above definition requires the knowledge of the clusters, which could be given by an
1064   oracle in the case of the GroundTruth-Cluster-Vendi score, or computed by a clustering algorithm
1065   such as K-Means to obtain the KMeans-Cluster-Vendi score. Observe that given the knowledge of
1066   the clusters revealed by an oracle, the GroundTruth-Cluster-Vendi score is a sensible definition of
1067   internal model diversity, which, as shown in Theorem 1, is expected to correlate with our defined
1068   Conditional-Vendi score.
1069   In the numerical settings of the previous section, where we know the ground-truth clusters based
1070   on the type of animal, fruit, or object in the texts, we computed the value of the GroundTruth-
1071   Cluster-Vendi score and compared it with the evaluated Conditional-Vendi score. As demonstrated
1072   in Figures 22, the two diversity scores, Conditional-Vendi and GroundTruth-Cluster-Vendi, highly
1073   correlate for the ten simulated generative models in the experiments.
1074   However, note that in a real-world scenario, we do not have access to the ground-truth clusters.
1075   To estimate the score, we should use a clustering algorithm such as K-Means to find the clusters
1076   and compute the Cluster-Vendi score. We note that the optimization problem addressed by stan-
1077   dard clustering algorithms represents a challenging non-convex optimization, which, depending on
1078   the algorithm’s initial point, could converge to different solutions. Our numerical results with the
1079   K-Means clustering algorithm in Figure 23 also demonstrated these clustering challenges and, in
       several cases, failed to find the ground-truth clusters with high accuracy.
