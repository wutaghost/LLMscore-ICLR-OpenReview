009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge transfer aims to apply existing knowledge to different tasks or new data,
012            and it has extensive applications in multi-domain and multi-task learning. The key
013            to this task is quickly identifying a fine-grained object for knowledge sharing and
014            efficiently transferring knowledge. Current methods, such as fine-tuning, layer-wise
015            parameter sharing, and task-specific adapters, only offer coarse-grained sharing
016            solutions and struggle to effectively search for shared parameters, thus hindering
017            the performance and efficiency of knowledge transfer. To address these issues, we
018
               propose Channel-Wise Parameter Sharing (CWPS), a novel fine-grained parameter-
               sharing method for Knowledge Transfer, which is efficient for parameter sharing,
019
               comprehensive, and plug-and-play. For the coarse-grained problem, we first achieve
020
               fine-grained parameter sharing by refining the granularity of shared parameters
021            from the level of layers to the level of neurons. The knowledge learned from
022            previous tasks can be utilized through the explicit composition of the model neurons.
023            Besides, we promote an effective search strategy to minimize computational costs,
024            simplifying the process of determining shared weights. In addition, our CWPS
025            has strong composability and generalization ability, which theoretically can be
026            applied to any network consisting of linear and convolution layers. We introduce
027            several datasets in both incremental learning and multi-task learning scenarios. Our
028            method has achieved state-of-the-art precision-to-parameter ratio performance with
029
               various backbones, demonstrating its efficiency and versatility.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Knowledge transfer is a crucial task that involves extracting valuable information from existing tasks
034
      to enhance performance in new tasks or data. Existing knowledge can be repurposed and applied to
035
      new problems, offering a wide range of potential applications. For example, adapting a supernet to
036   a new domain can be efficiently achieved through knowledge transfer (Zhuang et al., 2020). Two
037   common and correlated task settings among these applications are Multi-Domain Learning (MDL)
038   and Multi-Task Learning (MTL). MDL transfers knowledge from one domain to the target domain,
039   while MTL transfers knowledge between several target tasks. Both involve knowledge transfer during
040   training, which requires an efficient method.
041
      However, it is not easy to design an algorithm that creates an appropriate model, shares knowledge
042   between tasks without introducing too many parameters, and has high universality (Gesmundo &
043   Dean, 2022). The algorithm needs to identify the level of granularity of parameter sharing, measure
044   the relations of tasks, and operate at the unified level of neural networks, which can be challenging
045   to balance. In addition, the consumption of extra training for knowledge transfer is also of concern,
046   which puts more stringent demands on our algorithm. Therefore, we deduce that the key point is
047   identifying a suitable shared object and efficiently measuring the relations between various tasks.
048   The existing coarse and parameter-efficient method fixes the parameters of the base model and then
049   adds some trainable task-specific output layers (Vandenhende et al., 2020) or adapter layers (Rebuffi
050   et al., 2017), sharing the entire backbone. Some other works explore ways to optimize parameter
051   usage by investigating finer layer-wise parameter sharing (Gesmundo & Dean, 2022; Wallingford
052   et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2017) and finest single-weight-wise parameter sharing (Mallya et al., 2018).
053   However, neurons are the smallest computational units in neural networks and are also considered the
      smallest memory units of knowledge. While layer-wise parameter sharing provides a coarse-grained

068   at the neuron level. Finest-grained methods split the weight parameters of each neuron in the network

      to share knowledge (Mallya et al., 2018). Still, neglecting the "atomic" nature of the neuron as the

072   Further, heuristic algorithms (Gesmundo & Dean, 2022) are used to search for parent models to solve
073   the enormous search space of shared objects(layers or weights), while Wallingford et al. (2022);
074   Mallya et al. (2018) simplify the search process using learnable parameters. However, these methods
075
      to model the relations between tasks are costly and arbitrarily assume the most related base model,
      leading to inefficient and weak knowledge exchange between tasks.
076
077   To conclude, the decision to select the right level of granularity and solve task relations’ search
078   space is a dilemma: a coarser level of granularity reduces the search space but is less efficient, and a
079   finer level of granularity provides more precise control but increases the search space. So, a natural
080   question arises: can we seek a solution that efficiently determines the fine-grained sharing of objects?
081   In this paper, we propose a Channel-Wise Parameter Sharing (CWPS) method that avoids the dilemma
082   of granularity and search space. In theory, our method is defined by the finest parameter-sharing unit,
083   which is a single neuron. The neuron, also known as the channel, serves as the minimal concept
084   of the neural network and has not been carefully explored by other parameter-sharing methods. As
085   shown in Figure 1, incorporating channel-wise parameter-sharing mechanisms allows fine-grained
086   control of task-specific parameters without compromising neuron consistency, resulting in more
087
      efficient parameter sharing. Besides, we propose a searching method called Composite Parent
      Model Searching(CPMS), which constructs a composite parent model for every search process by
088
      measuring parent and child weights. This search method greatly reduces the search space fine-grained
089
      control brought and naturally measures the relations between tasks. Further, CWPS operates at
090   the fundamental network level, making it applicable in a wide range of scenarios. As long as the
091   backbone comprises linear and convolution layers, CWPS can be utilized. Its universal nature enables
092   a seamless transition from various task settings through iterations.
093
      We measure our method in ImageNet-to-Sketch incremental learning benchmark (Berriel et al., 2019)
094
      and DomainNet multi-task learning benchmark (Peng et al., 2019). CWPS can transfer from one
095
      domain to another and reach state-of-the-art performance in incremental and multi-task learning
096   scenarios. The contributions of our work are as follows:
097
098
099         1. We introduce CWPS, a fine-grained method for knowledge transfer across tasks. Compared
100            to other parameter-sharing methods, it offers a more natural perspective and allows for the
101            optimal and efficient utilization of neurons across various tasks.
102
103
            2. We have developed a rapid architecture search algorithm called CPMS to determine shared
104            parameters. This algorithm simultaneously models the relationships between different tasks.
105
106
107         3. CWPS is highly scalable, allowing simultaneous use in various knowledge transfer tasks to
               achieve state-of-art performance, and is adaptable to multiple network structures.

      2.1   M ULTI - TASK L EARNING
111
112   Multi-task Learning (MTL) is a transfer learning strategy that aims to enhance overall performance
113   by concurrently addressing and learning from multiple correlated tasks within the same model.
114   The main classification of multi-task frameworks is based on whether they are encoder-based or
115   decoder-based. As exemplified by Cross-stitch Networks (Misra et al., 2016), encoder-based designs
116   Misra et al. (2016); Liu et al. (2019); Gao et al. (2019); Lu et al. (2017) involve the sharing of
117   task-specific features during the encoding phase. Subsequently, each task possesses an independent
118   task head for processing the shared encoder features during decoding. Conversely, decoder-based
119
      multi-task frameworks Vandenhende et al. (2020); Xu et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2018); Neseem et al.
      (2023) address all tasks directly within the same stage, acquiring outputs from all tasks in parallel
120
      or sequentially. Moreover, studies (Bhattacharjee et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2020) demonstrate the
121
      sharing of feature information in both the encoding and decoding stages simultaneously. These three
122   frameworks are efficient in their respective task settings, but their specificity limits their universal
123   applicability.
124
125
      2.2   M ULTI -D OMAIN L EARNING AND I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
126
127   Multi-domain learning focuses on overcoming data disparities between domains to enhance model
128   adaptability. It aims to address knowledge transfer between domains, resolving the challenge of
129   using multiple datasets with differing statistical properties for the same task. Rebuffi et al. (2017)
130   proposed the residual adapter method, incorporating the adapter into data representation from multiple
131   domains to identify shared features in less similar domains. Subsequent algorithms Rebuffi et al.
132   (2018); Rosenfeld & Tsotsos (2018), building upon the adapter concept, further refined it to achieve
133
      parameter efficiency while maintaining performance in the original domain. Other methods (Mallya
      et al., 2018; Mancini et al., 2018) utilize binary masks for simpler and more efficient MDL, but
134
      they balance reducing the parameter count and performance. Task Adaptive Parameter Sharing
135
      (TAPS)(Wallingford et al., 2022) tries to select the minimal subset of existing layers and retrain them.
136   However, the layer-level parameter sharing still falls short of the idealized small parameter count.
137
138   Meanwhile, Multi-Domain Learning aims to transfer knowledge without forgetting the previous
139
      knowledge, which remains the same as common incremental learning. Consequently, the primary
      challenge MDL and incremental learning must address is mitigating catastrophic forgetting. Various
140
      methods have been proposed to address such issues, including functional and parameter regularization
141
      (Aljundi et al., 2018; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Schwarz et al., 2018), developing incrementally updated
142   components (Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2020), improving representation learning with additional
143   inductive biases (Cha et al., 2021; Ni et al., 2023; 2021), they are suggestive to MDL.
144
145
      2.3   M ULTI -D OMAIN L EARNING VS . M ULTI - TASK L EARNING
146
147   To conclude, we could summarize that MDL is MTL in very restricted situations. Multi-task learning
148   learns different tasks without strict task constraints, while MDL learns one by one without revisiting
149   prior data. Our objective for MDL aligns with the approach described in Wallingford et al. (2022),
150   which involves starting from a pre-trained base model and then learning new tasks or domains. On
151   the other hand, the aim of MTL is similar to the method outlined in Gesmundo & Dean (2022), where
152   the final models are obtained iteratively through multiple datasets.
153
154   3     M ETHODOLOGY
155
156   Based on the conclusion in Section 2.3, our method begins with the restricted situation (MDL) and is
157   then extended to a broader range of scenarios to facilitate knowledge transfer. For an incremental
158   task, we first use CPMS to efficiently search and calculate the relations between the knowledge
159   contained in the model of the previous task and the new task, and determine the relations between
160   the neurons of the new task and each neuron of the previous task. Then, we use our fine-grained
161   parameter sharing method, CWPS, to obtain the knowledge of the previous task, and fine-tune the
      model for a few training epochs. We iterate this process for each new task, thus completing the MDL.

345   3.3     I TERATIVE J OINT L EARNING
346
      The difference between incremental learning and common multi-task learning is that the tasks come
347
      incrementally, and all data is available at once in incremental learning, while multi-task learning is
348
      not. So, to fully use the training data, many methods (Gesmundo & Dean, 2022; Lin et al., 2019)
349   train the models iteratively, which means more opportunities to search for child model structures.
350
351   CWPS can be easily extended to any multi-task learning method from Multi-Domain learning for
352
      its flexibility. As shown in Algorithm 1, when changing CWPS into multi-task learning mode, the
      training iteration won’t terminate after finishing training the model of TK . Instead, T1 is the next task
353
      to be dealt with, and we will then go through τ one or more times. During the traversal, we only save
354
      the model with the best performance (highest validation accuracy) for one task, and we replace the
355   weights from the worst model in Wtrained with those from the better model. Thus, every iteration
356   of the traversal will gain better models and the number of weights in Wtrained will be kept within
357   specified bounds.

498   5    D ISCUSSIONS
499
500   Although our approach focuses on parameter efficiency in incremental and multi-task learning,
501   other methods, such as Mixture of Experts (MoE), model merging, pruning-based techniques, and
502   prompt-based techniques, are intuitive. In this section, we will compare these methods with CWPS.
503   Some MoE-based methods, such as AdaMV-MoE (Chen et al., 2023) and M3ViT (Fan et al., 2022),
504   address the issues of gradient interference in multi-task learning and the impact of task quantity on
505   model inference speed to some extent. The MoE method involves simultaneous training of expert
506   selection and multiple expert models, resulting in a dynamic model structure. However, while their
507   inference structures are flexible, they often focus on a single transformer architecture and are not
508   universally applicable across other structures compared to CWPS. Additionally, they only share
509   parameters between layers and require multiple models to be pre-loaded during inference, leading to
510
      certain parameter waste.
511   Model merging (Matena & Raffel, 2022; Ilharco et al., 2022; Yadav et al., 2024) and our approach
512   involve parameter sharing among multiple pre-trained models to adapt to different tasks. The method
513   based on task vectors (Zhang et al., 2024) was very inspiring for our design approach. Our method
514   essentially seeks to identify a finer-grained task vector that recognizes the underlying common
515   information between tasks. Similar to MoE, the granularity and proportion of parameter sharing in
516
      model merging are relatively low, potentially leading to higher computational resource consumption
      during inference.
517
518   On the other hand, pruning-based methods, such as CPG (Hung et al., 2019) and BA2 (Santos
519   et al., 2022) subsequent approach, aim to reduce the number of parameters in multi-task settings,
520   thereby improving inference speed. However, the lack of direct parameter sharing results in lower
521   utilization of model parameters compared to parameter-sharing methods like ours, and the reduced
522
      computational load during inference can lead to decreased model performance.
523   Methods based on prompts (Wang et al., 2022) attempt to establish a prompt for each task and
524   category to measure their correlations, quantifying the relationships between tasks. However, due to
525   the limitations of the transformer architecture, this approach still has certain application constraints
526   on other backbones, such as convolutional networks.
527   These methods aim to improve each task’s performance by incorporating information from multiple
528   domains, while our method prioritizes achieving a balance between parameter count and performance
529   across multiple tasks.

532   6    C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper demonstrated Channel-Wise Parameter Sharing, a plug-and-play incremental and multi-
535   task learning method. By fine-grained control and composite parent model, our method exhibits
536   superior knowledge transfer capabilities, requiring fewer iterations and fewer parameters while
537   maintaining high precision. Our experiments confirm these capabilities and demonstrate that CWPS
538   can automatically adapt pre-trained models to various tasks and uncover relationships between tasks.
539   Our methods have limitations, including increased video memory usage during training and requiring
      more steps to save models. Further research is needed.

855   The scalability of CWPS. As shown in Table 5, CWPS can be easily applied to the transformer-
856   based backbone without changing the structure. Our method utilized only 2.6× parameters to get the
857   same performance as TAPS(its parameter count is not reported in detail). The basic structure of the
858   transformer layer is the linear layer, which serves as the projection function to get q, k, and v. We
859   deduce that the transformer (linear-based) layers are denser than the convolution layers, making it
860   easier for our method to identify fine-grained sharing candidates. As a result, more parameters in
861   transformer layers can be shared.

871   The computational complexity analysis of CPMS Our computational efficiency derives from
872   more universal parent model searching. The efficiency of our parameter search method is primarily
873   reflected in more effective information exchange across domains. Suppose we have N tasks where
874   each model can obtain the state that information from all other datasets is available to the model. This
875
      state can be referred to as the complete information exchange state. The faster we reach this state,
      the higher the efficiency of the information exchange. The parent model parameters in our method
876
      come from all the trained models, thus requiring at least two passes through all tasks, which amounts
877
      to 2N model training to achieve the state. In contrast, methods like MuNet and TAPS, which also
878   require parameter search, derive the parent model parameters from only one trained model directly at
879
      a time. They need N + (N − 1) + ... + 1 = N (N2+1) times training to reach the state. Therefore, we
880   have reduced the complete information exchange from polynomial to constant complexity.
