009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Several companies–such as Google, Microsoft, and OpenAI–have deployed tech-
012            niques to watermark AI-generated images to enable proactive detection. However,
013            existing literature mainly focuses on user-agnostic detection. Attribution aims to
014            further trace back the user who generated a detected AI-generated image. Despite
015            its growing importance, attribution is largely unexplored. In this work, we aim
016            to bridge this gap by providing the first systematic study on watermark-based,
017
               user-aware detection and attribution of AI-generated images. Specifically, we theo-
               retically study the detection and attribution performance via rigorous probabilistic
018
               analysis. Moreover, we develop an efficient algorithm to select watermarks for
019
               the users to enhance attribution performance. Both our theoretical and empirical
020            results show that watermark-based detection and attribution inherit the accuracy
021            and (non-)robustness properties of the watermarking method.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Generative AI (GenAI) can synthesize very realistic-looking images. Beyond its societal bene-
027   fits, GenAI also raises ethical concerns. For instance, they can be misused to generate harmful
028   images (Yang et al., 2024); they can be used to aid disinformation and propaganda campaigns by gen-
029   erating realistic-looking images (Dhaliwal, 2023); and people can falsely claim copyright ownership
030   of images generated by them (Escalante-De Mattei, 2023).
031   Watermark-based detection and attribution of AI-generated images is a promising technique to miti-
032   gate these ethical concerns. For instance, several companies–such as Google, OpenAI, Stability AI,
033   and Microsoft–have deployed such techniques to watermark their AI-generated images. Specifically,
034   OpenAI inserts a visible watermark into the images generated by its DALL-E 2 (Ramesh et al., 2022);
035   Google’s SynthID (Gowal & Kohli, 2023) inserts an invisible watermark into images generated by its
036   Imagen; Stability AI deploys a watermarking method in its Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022);
037
      and Microsoft watermarks all AI-generated images in Bing (Mehdi, 2023).
038   However, existing literature mainly focuses on user-agnostic detection of AI-generated images. In
039   particular, the same watermark is inserted into all the images generated by a GenAI service; and an
040   image is detected as generated by the GenAI service if a similar watermark can be decoded from
041   it. Attribution aims to further trace back the registered user of the GenAI service who generated a
042   given image.1 Such attribution can aid the GenAI service provider or law enforcement in forensic
043
      analysis of cyber-crimes, such as disinformation and propaganda campaigns, that involve a given
      AI-generated image. Despite the growing importance of attribution, it is largely unexplored.
044
045   In this work, we bridge this gap by conducting the first systematic study on the theory, algorithm, and
046   evaluation of watermark-based detection and attribution of AI-generated images. Our work assumes
047   an image watermarking method has been designed. Our contribution is to study the theory and
048   algorithm of leveraging this watermarking method for AI-generated image detection and attribution
049
      (illustrated in Figure 1). When a user registers in a GenAI service, a watermark (i.e., a bitstring) is
      selected for him/her and stored in a watermark database. When a user generates an image using the
050
      GenAI service, the user’s watermark is embedded into the image using the watermark encoder. An

067
      We theoretically analyze the performance of watermark-based detection and attribution. Specifically,
      we define three key evaluation metrics: true detection rate (TDR), false detection rate (FDR), and
068
      true attribution rate (TAR). We show that other relevant evaluation metrics can be derived from these
069
      three. Based on a formal quantification of a watermarking method’s behavior, we derive lower bounds
070   of TDR and TAR, and an upper bound of FDR no matter how the users’ watermarks are selected.
071
072   Selecting watermarks for the users is a key component. We formulate a watermark selection problem,
073
      which aims to select a watermark for a new registered user via minimizing the maximum similarity
      between the selected watermark and the existing users’ watermarks. We find that our watermark
074
      selection problem is equivalent to the well-known farthest string problem (Lanctot et al., 2003),
075
      which has been studied extensively in theoretical computer science. Thus, we adapt the bounded
076   search tree algorithm (Gramm et al., 2003), a state-of-the-art solution to the farthest string problem,
077   to solve our watermark selection problem.
078
079
      We empirically evaluate our method for AI-generated images on three GenAI models, i.e., Stable
      Diffusion, Midjourney, and DALL-E 2. We use HiDDeN (Zhu et al., 2018), a deep-learning-based
080
      image watermarking method that is the basis for modern image watermarks. Our results show
081
      that detection and attribution are very accurate, i.e., TDR/TAR is close to 1 and FDR is close to 0,
082   when AI-generated images are not post-processed; detection and attribution are still accurate when
083   common post-processing, such as JPEG compression, Gaussian blur, and Brightness/Contrast, is
084   applied to AI-generated images; and adversarial post-processing (Jiang et al., 2023) with a small
085   number of queries to the detection API degrades the image quality substantially in order to evade
086   detection/attribution. Moreover, we show our watermark selection algorithm outperforms baselines.

      2      R ELATED W ORK
090
091   An image watermarking method typically consists of three components: watermark, encoder, and
092   decoder. We consider a watermark w to be a bitstring with n bits. E(C, w) means that encoder
093
      E embeds w into an image C, while D(C ′ ) is the watermark decoded from a (watermarked or
      unwatermarked) image C ′ by decoder D. Note that E and w can also be embedded into the
094
      parameters of a GenAI model such that its generated images are inherently watermarked with
095
      w (Fernandez et al., 2023).
096
097   Non-learning-based vs. learning-based: Watermarking methods can be categorized into two
098   groups based on the design of E and D: non-learning-based and learning-based. Non-learning-based
099
      methods (Pereira & Pun, 2000; Bi et al., 2007; Wang, 2021; Wen et al., 2023) design E and D based
      on some hand-crafted heuristics, while learning-based methods (Zhu et al., 2018; Abdelnabi & Fritz,
100
      2021; Luo et al., 2020; Wen & Aydore, 2019; Tancik et al., 2020; Fernandez et al., 2023) use neural
101
      networks as E/D and automatically learn them using an image dataset. For instance, Tree-Ring (Wen
102   et al., 2023) is a non-learning-based watermarking method, while HiDDeN (Zhu et al., 2018) is a
103   learning-based method. Our theory and algorithm are applicable to both categories of watermarking
104   methods as long as they use bitstring-based watermarks such as HiDDeN (Zhu et al., 2018), Stable
105   Signature (Fernandez et al., 2023), StegaStamp (Tancik et al., 2020), and Smoothed HiDDeN (Jiang
106   et al., 2024). We note that our results are not applicable to Tree-Ring, which employs a non-bitstring
107   watermark. Since learning-based methods are more robust due to adversarial training (Zhu et al.,
      2018), we adopt a learning-based method in our experiments.


                                                                              2

108
      Standard vs. adversarial training: In learning-based methods, E and D are automatically learnt.
109   Specifically, given an image C and a random watermark w, the decoded watermark D(E(C, w)) for
110   the watermarked image E(C, w) should be similar to w, i.e., D(E(C, w)) ≈ w. Standard training
111   aims to jointly learn E and D such that D(E(C, w)) is similar to w for an image dataset (Kandi
112   et al., 2017). A watermarked image E(C, w) may be post-processed, e.g., a watermarked image
113   may be post-processed by JPEG compression during transmission on the Internet. Zhu et al. (2018)
114   extended adversarial training (Goodfellow et al., 2015; Madry et al., 2018), a technique to train robust
115   classifiers, to train watermarking encoder and decoder that are more robust against post-processing.
116   Specifically, adversarial training aims to learn E and D such that D(P (E(C, w))) is similar to w,
117
      where P stands for a post-processing operation and P (E(C, w)) is a post-processed watermarked
      image. In each epoch of adversarial training, a P is randomly sampled from a given set of them for
118
      each image in the image dataset.
119
120   Robustness of watermarking: We stress that building robust watermarking methods is orthogonal
121   to our work and is still an ongoing effort. Non-learning-based methods (Pereira & Pun, 2000; Bi
122   et al., 2007; Wang, 2021; Wen et al., 2023) are known to be non-robust to common post-processing
123
      such as JPEG compression (Zhu et al., 2018). Learning-based methods (Kandi et al., 2017; Zhu
      et al., 2018; Abdelnabi & Fritz, 2021; Luo et al., 2020; Wen & Aydore, 2019; Fernandez et al., 2023;
124
      Saberi et al., 2024) are more robust to such common post-processing because they can leverage
125
      adversarial training. For instance, common post-processing has to substantially decrease the quality
126   of a watermarked image in order to remove the watermark (Luo et al., 2020; Wen & Aydore, 2019).
127   Adversarial post-processing (Jiang et al., 2023; Lukas et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Saberi et al.,
128   2024) strategically perturbs a watermarked image to remove the watermark. Learning-based image
129   watermarking methods are not yet robust to adversarial post-processing in the white-box setting
130   where an attacker has access to D. However, they have good robustness to adversarial post-processing
131   when an attacker can only query the detection API for a small number of times in the black-box
132   setting or does not have access to the detection API. In particular, adversarial post-processing
133   substantially decreases the quality of a watermarked image in order to remove the watermark in such
134
      scenarios. To defend against adversarial post-processing, Jiang et al. (2024) proposed a framework to
      build certifiably robust image watermarks that cannot be removed when the ℓ2 norm of the added
135
      perturbation is bounded. We acknowledge that our watermark-based detection and attribution inherit
136
      the watermarking method’s (non-)robustness properties discussed above.

      Suppose we are given a generative AI model, which is deployed as a GenAI service. A registered
142   user sends a prompt (i.e., a text) to the GenAI service, which returns an AI-generated image to the
143   user. In this work, we consider detection and attribution of AI-generated image. Detection aims to
144   decide whether a given image was generated by the GenAI service or not; while attribution further
145   traces back the user of the GenAI service who generated an image detected as AI-generated. Such
146   attribution can aid the GenAI service provider or law enforcement in forensic analysis of cyber-crimes,
147   e.g., disinformation or propaganda campaigns, that involve a given AI-generated image. We define
148   the detection and attribution problems as follows:
149   Definition 1 (Detection of AI-generated image). Given an image and a GenAI service, detection
150   aims to infer whether the image was generated by the GenAI service or not.
151
152
      Definition 2 (Attribution of AI-generated image). Given an image, a GenAI service, and s users
      U = {U1 , U2 , · · · , Us } of the GenAI service, attribution aims to further infer which user used the
153
      GenAI service to generate the image after it is detected as AI-generated.
154
155
      We note that the set of s users U in attribution could include all registered users of the GenAI service,
156   in which s may be very large. Alternatively, this set may consist of a smaller number of registered
157   users if the GenAI service provider has some prior knowledge on its registered users. For instance,
158   the GenAI service provider may exclude the registered users, who are verified offline as trusted, from
159   the set U to reduce its size. Moreover, malicious users may be identified by conventional network
160   security solutions, such as IP addresses and behavior patterns (Yuan et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2021).
161   How to construct the set of users U in attribution is out of the scope of this work. Given any set
      U , our method aims to infer which user in U may have generated a given image. We also note that

167
168   Figure 1 illustrates our watermark-based detection and attribution method. When a user registers in
169
      the GenAI service, the service provider selects a unique watermark for the user. We denote by wi the
      watermark selected for user Ui , where i = 1, 2, · · · , s is the user index. During generation, when a
170
      user Ui sends a prompt to the GenAI service to generate an image, the provider uses the watermark
171
      encoder E to embed watermark wi into the image. During detection and attribution, a watermark is
172   decoded from a given image; the given image is detected as generated by the GenAI service if the
173   decoded watermark is similar enough to at least one of the users’ watermarks; and the given image is
174   further attributed to the user whose watermark is the most similar to the decoded watermark after it is
175   detected as AI-generated.
176
177   4.1     D ETECTION
178
179   We use bitwise accuracy to measure similarity between two watermarks. Specifically, given any two
180   watermarks w and w′ , their∑︁bitwise accuracy (denoted as BA(w, w′ )) is the fraction of matched bits
                                     n
181   in them: BA(w, w′ ) = n1 k=1 I(w[k] = w′ [k]), where n is the watermark length, w[k] is the kth
182
      bit of w, and I is the indicator function that has a value 1 if w[k] = w′ [k] and 0 otherwise. Given an
      image C, we use the decoder D to decode a watermark D(C) from it. We detect C as AI-generated
183
      if there exists a user’s watermark that is similar enough to D(C), i.e., if the following is satisfied:
184
      maxi∈{1,2,··· ,s} BA(D(C), wi ) ≥ τ , where τ > 0.5 is the detection threshold.
185
186
      4.2     ATTRIBUTION
187
188   Attribution is applied only after an image C is detected as AI-generated. Intuitively, we attribute the
189   image to the user whose watermark is the most similar to the decoded watermark D(C). Formally,
190   we attribute image C to user Ui∗ , where i∗ is as follows: i∗ = arg maxi∈{1,2,··· ,s} BA(D(C), wi ).
191
192   4.3     WATERMARK S ELECTION
193
194   A key component of watermark-based detection and attribution is how to select watermarks for the
195   users. Next, we first formulate watermark selection as an optimization problem, and then propose a
196   method to approximately solve it.
197
198   4.3.1    WATERMARK S ELECTION P ROBLEM

200   attribution. In fact, our theoretical analysis in Section 5 shows that attribution performance is better if
201   the maximum pairwise bitwise accuracy between the users’ watermarks is smaller. Thus, we propose
202   to select watermarks for the s users to minimize their maximum pairwise bitwise accuracy. Formally,
203   we formulate watermark selection as the following problem:

      4.3.2   S OLVING THE P ROBLEM
217
218   NP-hardness: Our watermark selection problem in Equation 2 turns out to be NP-hard. In particular,
219   we can reduce the well-known NP-hard farthest string problem (Lanctot et al., 2003) to our watermark
220   selection problem. The farthest string problem aims to find a string that is the farthest from a given
221   set of strings. We can view a string as a watermark in our watermark selection problem, the given
222   set of strings as the watermarks of the s − 1 users, and the similarity metric between two strings as
223   our bitwise accuracy. Then, we can reduce the farthest string problem to our watermark selection
224
      problem, which means that our watermark selection problem is also NP-hard. This NP-hardness
      implies that it is very challenging to develop an efficient exact solution for our watermark selection
225
      problem. We note that efficiency is important for watermark selection as a watermark is selected for a
226
      user at the time of registration. Therefore, we aim to develop an efficient algorithm that approximately
227   solves the watermark selection problem.
228
229   Random: The most straightforward method to approximately solve the watermark selection problem
230
      in Equation 2 is to generate a n-bit bitstring uniformly at random as ws . We denote this method as
      Random. The limitation of this method is that the selected watermark ws may be very similar to some
231
      existing watermarks, i.e., maxi∈{1,2,··· ,s−1} BA(wi , ws ) is large, making attribution less accurate,
232
      as shown in our experiments.
233
234   Decision problem: To develop an efficient algorithm to approximately solve our watermark selection
235   problem, we first define its decision problem. Specifically, given the maximum number of matched
236
      bits between ws and the existing s−1 watermarks as m, the decision problem aims to find such a ws if
      there exists one and return NotExist otherwise. Formally, the decision problem is to find any watermark
237
      ws in the following set if the set is nonempty: ws ∈ {w| maxi∈{1,2,··· ,s−1} BA(wi , w) ≤ m/n},
238
      where n is the watermark length. Next, we discuss how to solve the decision problem and then turn
239   the algorithm to solve our watermark selection problem.
240
241   Approximate bounded search tree algorithm (A-BSTA): Our A-BSTA is an adapted version of
242
      the bounded search tree algorithm (BSTA), the state-of-the-art exact algorithm to solve the decision
      problem version of the farthest string problem. The details of BSTA can be found in Appendix A. Our
243
      A-BSTA makes two adaptions of BSTA. First, we constrain the recursion depth d to be a constant
244
      (e.g., 8 in our experiments) instead of m, which makes the algorithm approximate but improves the
245   efficiency substantially. Second, instead of initializing ws as ¬w1 , we initialize ws as an uniformly
246   random watermark. As our experiments in Table 2 in Appendix show, our initialization further
247   improves the performance of A-BSTA. This is because a random initialization is more likely to have
248   small bitwise accuracy with all existing watermarks. Note that A-BSTA returns NotExist if it cannot
249   find a solution ws to the decision problem.
250
      Solving our watermark selection problem: Given an algorithm (e.g., A-BSTA) to solve the
251   decision problem, we turn it as a solution to the watermark selection problem. Our idea is to start
252   from a small m, and then solve the decision problem. If we cannot find a watermark ws for the
253   given m, we increase it by 1 and solve the decision problem again. We repeat this process until
254   finding a watermark ws . Note that we start from m = maxi∈{1,2,··· ,s−2} n · BA(wi , ws−1 ), i.e., the
255   maximum number of matched bits between ws−1 and the other s − 2 watermarks. This is because an
256   m smaller than this value is unlikely to produce a watermark ws as it failed to do so when selecting
257   ws−1 . Algorithm 3 in Appendix shows our method.

301   5.2   F ORMAL Q UANTIFICATION OF WATERMARKING
302
303   Intuitively, to theoretically analyze the detection and attribution performance (i.e., TDRi , FDR, and
304   TARi ), we need a formal quantification of a watermarking method’s behavior at decoding watermarks
305   in AI-generated and non-AI-generated images. Towards this end, we formally define β-accurate
306   watermarking and γ-random watermarking, the details of which are in Appendix C.
307   β-accurate watermarking is used to characterize the accuracy of the watermarking method at en-
308   coding/decoding a watermark in an AI-generated image. In particular, the watermarking method
309   is more accurate when β is closer to 1. γ-random watermarking characterizes the behavior of the
310   watermarking method for non-AI-generated images. In particular, the decoded watermark for a
311   non-AI-generated (i.e., non-watermarked) image is close to a uniformly random watermark, where
312   γ quantifies the difference between them. The watermarking method is more random for non-AI-
313   generated images if γ is closer to 0.
314   User-dependent βi : Since the users’ AI-generated images may have different distributions Pi , the
315   same watermarking method may have different β for different users. To capture this phenomena, we
316   consider the watermarking method is βi -accurate for user Ui ’s AI-generated images embedded with
317   watermark wi . Note that the same γ is used across different users since it is used to characterize the
318   behavior of the watermarking method for non-AI-generated images, which is user-independent.
319   Incorporating post-processing: Our β-accurate and γ-random watermarking can also incorporate
320   post-processing (e.g., JPEG compression) that an attacker may apply to AI-generated or non-AI-
321   generated images. In particular, we can replace D(C) as D(P (C)) in definitions, where P stands for
322   post-processing of the image C. When the AI-generated image is post-processed, the watermarking
323   method may become less accurate, i.e., β may decrease. The parameters β and γ can be estimated
      using a set of AI-generated and non-AI-generated images, as shown in our experiments.

354   Impact of s on the bounds: Intuitively, when there are more users, i.e., s is larger, it is more
355   likely to have at least one user whose watermark has a bitwise accuracy with the decoded watermark
356   D(C) that is no smaller than τ . As a result, both TDRi and FDR may increase as s increases, i.e.,
357   s controls a trade-off between TDRi and FDR. Our theoretical results align with this intuition. On
358   one hand, Theorem 1 shows that the lower bound of TDRi is larger when s is larger. In particular,
359   when s increases, the parameter αi may become smaller. Thus, the second term of the lower bound
360   increases, leading to a larger lower bound of TDRi . On the other hand, the upper bound of FDR in
361
      both Theorem 2 and Theorem 3 increases as s increases. In particular, in Theorem 2, α1 becomes
      larger when s increases, leading to a larger second term of the upper bound.
362
363   User-agnostic vs. user-aware detection: Existing watermark-based detection is user-agnostic, i.e.,
364   it does not distinguish between different users when embedding a watermark into an AI-generated
365   image. The first term of the lower bound in our Theorem 1 is a lower bound of TDR for user-agnostic
366   detection; the first term of the upper bound in our Theorem 2 is an upper bound of FDR for user-
367
      agnostic detection; and the upper bound with s = 1 in our Theorem 3 is an alternative upper bound of
      FDR for user-agnostic detection. Compared to user-agnostic detection, user-aware detection achieves
368
      larger TDR but also larger FDR.

394
395   Datasets: We consider both AI-generated and non-AI-generated images. For AI-generated, we use
396   three public datasets (Wang et al., 2023; Turc & Nemade, 2022; Images, 2023) generated respectively
397   by Stable Diffusion, Midjourney, and DALL-E 2. Following HiDDeN (Zhu et al., 2018), for each
398
      dataset, we sample 10,000 images for training watermark encoders and decoders; and we sample
      1,000 images for testing. For non-AI-generated, we combine the images in COCO (Lin et al., 2014),
399
      ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), and Conceptual Caption (Sharma et al., 2018), and sample 1,000
400
      images from the combined set uniformly at random as our non-AI-generated dataset. We scale the
401   image size in all datasets to be 128 × 128.
402
403   Watermarking method: We use the learning-based method HiDDeN (Zhu et al., 2018) because
404
      it is the basis of modern image watermarks like Stable Signature (Fernandez et al., 2023), StegaS-
      tamp (Tancik et al., 2020), and Smoothed HiDDeN (Jiang et al., 2024). Unless otherwise mentioned,
405
      we use standard training with the default parameter settings in the publicly available code. For each
406
      GenAI model, we train a watermark encoder/decoder using the corresponding AI-generated image
407   training set and evaluate performance on the testing set.
408
409   Evaluation metrics: We use TDR, FDR, and TAR. FDR is the fraction of the 1,000 non-AI-generated
410
      images that are falsely detected as AI-generated. For each user Ui , we embed its watermark into 100
      images randomly sampled from a testing AI-generated image dataset; and then we calculate the TDRi
411
      and TARi for the user. In most of our experiments, we report the average TDR and average TAR,
412
      which respectively are the average TDRi and TARi among the s users. However, average TDR and
413   average TAR cannot reflect the detection/attribution performance for the worst-case users, i.e., some
414   users may have quite small TDRi /TARi , but the average TDR/TAR may still be very large. Therefore,
415   we further consider the 1% users (at least 1 user) with the smallest TDRi (or TARi ) and report their
416   average TDR (or TAR), which we call worst 1% TDR (or worst 1% TAR).
417
      Parameter settings: By default, we set s = 100, 000 (due to limited computation resource), n = 64,
418
      and τ = 0.9. We also explore s = 1, 000, 000. Unless otherwise mentioned, we show results for the
419   Stable Diffusion dataset.

      7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
532
533   We show that watermark can be used for user-aware detection and attribution of AI-generated image.
534   Moreover, via both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation, we find that such detection and
535   attribution inherit the accuracy/(non-)robustness properties of the watermarking method. We also
536
      find that selecting dissimilar watermarks for users enhances attribution performance.
537   Text watermarking: Our theory and algorithm may not be applicable to text watermarking (Kirchen-
538   bauer et al., 2023) that does not use bitstring as watermark, but is applicable to text watermarking (Ab-
539   delnabi & Fritz, 2021) that uses bitstring as watermark (Appendix K shows more details). Interesting
      future work is to extend our work to text or audio watermarking.

      Bounded search tree algorithm (BSTA) (Gramm et al., 2003): Recall that our watermark selection
      problem is equivalent to the farthest string problem. Thus, our decision problem is equivalent to that
730
      of the farthest string problem, which has been studied extensively in the theoretical computer science
731
      community. In particular, BSTA is the state-of-the-art exact algorithm to solve the decision problem
732   version of the farthest string problem. We apply BSTA to solve the decision problem version of our
733   watermark selection problem exactly, which is shown in Algorithm 1 in Appendix. The key idea
734   of BSTA is to initialize ws as ¬w1 (i.e., each bit of w1 flips), and then reduce the decision problem
735   to a simpler problem recursively until it is easily solvable or there does not exist a solution ws . In
736   particular, given an initial ws , BSTA first finds the existing watermark wi∗ that has the largest bitwise
737   accuracy with ws . If BA(wi∗ , ws ) ≤ m/n, then ws is already a solution to the decision problem and
738   thus BSTA returns ws . Otherwise, BSTA chooses any m + 1 bits that ws and wi∗ match. For each of
739   the chosen m + 1 bits, BSTA flips the corresponding bit in ws and recursively solves the decision
740
      problem using the new ws as an initialization. The recursion is applied m times at most, i.e., the
      recursion depth d is set as m when calling Algorithm 1.
741
742   A key limitation of BSTA is that it has an exponential time complexity Gramm et al. (2003). In fact,
743   since the decision problem is NP-hard, all known exact solutions have exponential time complexity.
744   Therefore, to enhance computation efficiency, we resort to approximate solutions. Next, we discuss
745   the state-of-the-art approximate solution that adapts BSTA and a new approximate solution that we
746
      propose.
747   Non Redundant Guess (NRG) (Chen et al., 2016): Like BSTA, this approximate solution also first
748   initializes ws as ¬w1 and finds the existing watermark wi∗ that has the largest bitwise accuracy with
749   ws . If BA(wi∗ , ws ) ≤ m/n, then NRG returns ws . Otherwise, NRG samples n · BA(wi∗ , ws ) − m
750   bits that ws and wi∗ match uniformly at random. Then, NRG flips these bits in ws and recursively
751   solve the decision problem using the new ws as an initialization. Note that NRG stops the recursion
752
      when m bits of the initial ws have been flipped. Algorithm 2 in Appendix shows NRG.

889   Definition 3 (β-accurate watermarking). For a randomly sampled AI-generated image C ∼ P
890   embedded with the watermark w, the bits of the decoded watermark D(C) are independent and each
891   bit matches with that of w with probability β, where β ∈ [0, 1]. Formally, we have P r(D(C)[k] =
892
      w[k]) = β, where C ∼ P, D is the decoder, and [k] represents the kth bit of a watermark. We say a

893
894   Definition 4 (γ-random watermarking). For a randomly sampled non-AI-generated image C ∼ Q
895   without any watermark embedded, the bits of the decoded watermark D(C) are independent and
896   each bit is 1 with probability at least 0.5 − γ and at most 0.5 + γ, where γ ∈ [0, 0.5]. Formally, we
897
      have |P r(D(C)[k] = 1) − 0.5| ≤ γ, where C ∼ Q and [k] represents the kth bit of a watermark.

1100   Our method can also be used for the detection and attribution of AI-generated texts based on text
1101   watermarking that uses bitstring as watermark. For text watermarking, we use a learning-based
1102   method called Adversarial Watermarking Transformer (AWT) (Abdelnabi & Fritz, 2021). Given a
1103
       text, AWT encoder embeds a bitstring watermark into it; and given a (watermarked or unwatermarked)
       text, AWT decoder decodes a watermark from it. Following the original paper (Abdelnabi & Fritz,
1104
       2021), we train AWT on the word-level WikiText-2 dataset, which is derived from Wikipedia
1105
       articles (Merity et al., 2017). We use most of the hyperparameter settings in the publicly available
1106   code of AWT except the weight of the watermark decoding loss. To optimize watermark decoding
1107   accuracy, we increase this weight during training. The detailed hyperparameter settings for training
1108   can be found in Table 4.
1109
       We use A-BSTA to select users’ watermarks. For each user, we sample 10 text segments from the test
1110
       corpus uniformly at random, and perform watermark-based detection and attribution. Moreover, we
1111
       use the unwatermarked test corpus to calculate FDR. Figure 10 shows the detection and attribution
1112   results when there is no post-processing and paraphrasing (Damodaran, 2021) is applied to texts,
1113   where n = 64, τ = 0.85, and s ranges from 10 to 100,000. Due to the fixed-length nature of AWT’s
1114   input, we constrain the output length of the paraphraser to a certain range. When paraphrasing is
1115   used, we extend adversarial training to train AWT. Specifically, we employ T5-based paraphraser
1116   to post-process the watermarked texts generated by AWT. Due to the non-differentiable nature of
1117   the paraphrasing process, we cannot jointly adversarially train the encoder and decoder since the
1118   gradients cannot back-propagate to the encoder. To address the challenge, we first use the standard
1119   training to train AWT encoder and decoder. Then, we use the encoder to generate watermarked texts,
1120
       paraphrase them, and use the paraphrased watermarked texts to fine-tune the decoder. The detail
       parameter settings of fine-tuning are shown in Table 4.
1121
1122   Note that the average TDR/TAR and FDR are all nearly 0 when AWT is trained by standard training
1123   and paraphrasing is applied to texts. The results show that our method is also applicable for AI-
1124   generated texts, and adversarially trained AWT has better robustness to paraphrasing.

1129   relevant attribution problem is to trace back the GenAI service (e.g., Google’s Imagen, OpenAI’s

1132   When GenAI service generates an image, its corresponding watermark is embedded into it. Then, our
1133   method can be applied to detect whether an image is AI-generated and further attribute the GenAI
       service if the image is detected as AI-generated.

1170   In service attribution, selecting watermarks for different GenAI services can be coordinated by a
1171   central authority, who runs our watermark selection algorithm to pick unique watermarks for the
1172   GenAI services. A GenAI service registers to the central authority in order to obtain a unique water-
1173   mark. Such a central authority is similar to the certificate authority in the Public Key Infrastructure
1174   (PKI) that is widely used to secure communications on the Internet. The central authority may also
1175   perform detection and attribution of AI-generated images since it has access to all GenAI services’
1176
       watermarks. However, the central authority may become a bottleneck in such detection and attribution.
       To mitigate this issue, the watermarks of all GenAI services can be shared with each GenAI service,
1177
       so each GenAI service can perform detection and attribution. We note that a central authority is not
1178
       needed in user attribution of a particular GenAI service. This is because the GenAI service can select
1179   watermarks for its users and perform detection/attribution.
1180
1181   Hierarchical attribution: We can perform attribution to GenAI service and user simultaneously.
1182
       Specifically, we can divide the watermark space into multiple subspaces; and each GenAI service
       uses a subspace of watermarks and assigns watermarks in its subspace to its users. In this way, we
1183
       can trace back both the GenAI service and its user that generated a given image.
