008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010
               Designing novel proteins that bind to small molecules is a long-standing challenge
011            in computational biology, with applications in developing catalysts, biosensors, and
012            more. Current computational methods rely on the assumption that the binding pose
013            of the target molecule is known, which is not always feasible, as conformations of
014            novel targets are often unknown and tend to change upon binding. In this work, we
015            formulate proteins and molecules as unified biotokens, and present ATOM F LOW, a
016            novel deep generative model under the flow-matching framework for the design of
017            ligand-binding proteins from the 2D target molecular graph alone. Operating on
018            representative atoms of biotokens, ATOM F LOW captures the flexibility of ligands
019
               and generates ligand conformations and protein backbone structures iteratively.
               We consider the multi-scale nature of biotokens and demonstrate that ATOM F LOW
020
               can be effectively trained on a subset of structures from the Protein Data Bank,
021
               by matching flow vector field using an SE(3) equivariant structure prediction
022            network. Experimental results show that our method can generate high-fidelity
023            ligand-binding proteins and achieve performance comparable to the state-of-the-art
024            model RFDiffusionAA, while not requiring bound ligand structures. As a general
025            framework, ATOM F LOW holds the potential to be applied to various biomolecule
026            generation tasks in the future.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Proteins are indispensable macromolecules that drive the essential processes of living organisms. A
033   crucial mechanism by which they accomplish this is through binding with small molecules (Schreier
034   et al., 2009). Continuous progress has been made to design ligand-binding proteins with various
035   biological functions, such as catalysts and biosensors (Bennett et al., 2023). However, the problem
036
      remains challenging due to the complex interactions between proteins and molecules, as well as the
      inherent flexibility of ligands. The most well-established approaches depend on shape complementar-
037
      ity to dock molecules onto native protein scaffold structures (Bick et al., 2017; Polizzi & DeGrado,
038
      2020), which are computationally expensive.
039
040   Recently, RFDiffusionAA (Krishna et al., 2024), a de novo protein design method based on the
041   all-atom structure prediction model RoseTTAFoldAA (Krishna et al., 2024), has shown remarkable
042
      performance in designing novel ligand-binding proteins for small molecules. This method explicitly
      captures the interactions between proteins and molecules, achieving superior performance compared
043
      to its predecessor RFDiffusion (Watson et al., 2023), which can only model interactions between
044
      amino acid residues. Despite their great potential for ligand-binding protein design, current ap-
045   proaches assume that the bound conformation of the target molecule is known and rigid. However, the
046   binding pose of the target molecule is not always available, especially for molecules that do not bind
047   to any known natural proteins (Bick et al., 2017). While it is possible to mitigate this limitation by
048   sampling a diverse set of conformers and subsequently filtering them using expert knowledge (Krishna
049   et al., 2024), this approach demands potentially prohibitive computational resources. Additionally, the
050   constraint of ligand rigidity is suboptimal, as ligands often undergo significant conformation changes
051   upon binding with proteins (Mobley & Dill, 2009). We illustrate this phenomenon in Figure.1. Some
052   pioneering efforts have been made to account for ligand flexibility (Zhang et al., 2024; Stark et al.,
053
      2024), however, these methods can only design the portions of proteins that directly interact with the
      ligands and require the rest part of the proteins as input.

080   types and has the potential to be applied to various biomolecule generation tasks.

082

083   ATOM F LOW matches the overall performance of RFDiffusionAA and demonstrates advantages in
084   various situations. An ablation study further highlights that when the bound structure is unknown,
085   ATOM F LOW successfully designs protein binders with high binding affinity, whereas RFDiffusionAA
086   can be constrained by its dependence on a fixed, suboptimal ligand structure.

088   2    R ELATED W ORK
089
090   Ligand-binding Protein Design. Traditional approaches to ligand-binding protein design mainly rely
091   on docking molecules onto large sets of shape-complementary protein pockets (Polizzi & DeGrado,
092   2020; Lu et al., 2024). While the screening process can be accelerated with deep learning models (An
093   et al., 2023), conventional methods are computationally expensive and often depend on domain
094   experts (Bick et al., 2017). Recent advances in deep generative models have paved the way for
095   data-driven approaches, and a variety of models have been proposed to design proteins conditioned on
096   binding targets (Shi et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2023; Watson et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). Focusing
097
      on molecule binder design, RFDiffusion (Watson et al., 2023) generates novel proteins from scratch,
      using a heuristic attractive-repulsive potential to measure shape complementarity. The follow-up
098
      work RFDiffusionAA (Krishna et al., 2024) improves the performance by explicitly modeling the
099
      interactions between proteins and molecules with an all-atom formulation. These approaches assume
100   binding poses of ligands are known and impose rigidity constraints on ligand structures. Another
101   line of research focuses on designing binding pockets for small molecules (Stark et al., 2024; Zhang
102   et al., 2024). While taking ligand flexibility into consideration, they can only design the portions of
103   proteins that interact with the ligands and require the rest part of the proteins as input. Our model
104   also accounts for the ligand flexibility, but is able to design full ligand-binding proteins from 2D
105   molecular graph alone.

      Protein Generative Model and Structure Prediction. Recently, various deep generative models
109   for protein generation have emerged (Ingraham et al., 2023; Lin & AlQuraishi, 2023; Yim et al.,
110   2023b;a; Wu et al., 2024; Watson et al., 2023; Krishna et al., 2024). For example, Genie (Lin &
111   AlQuraishi, 2023) introduces a diffusion process defined on Cα coordinates of proteins and allows
112   for the incorporation of motif structures as conditions. FrameDiff (Yim et al., 2023b) takes a step
113   further by generating novel protein backbone structures using an SE(3) diffusion process applied to
114   residue frames. Its successor, FrameFlow (Yim et al., 2023a), accelerates the generation process by
115   leveraging the flow-matching framework. However, these approaches are tailored for single-chain
116   protein generation and fall short in modeling multiple biomolecules. In contrast, we treat multiple
117
      biomolecules, e.g., proteins and molecules, as biotokens and define a novel flow-matching model on
      their representative atoms. This allows us to design ligand-binding proteins based solely on molecular
118
      graphs, effectively capturing the flexibility of biomolecules and the intricate interactions between
119
      them. Our work is also related to approaches that perform protein structure predictions within the
120   all-atom framework, such as RoseTTAFoldAA (Krishna et al., 2024) and AlphaFold 3 (Abramson
121   et al., 2024). These methods tokenize various types of biomolecules into unified tokens, aiming to
122   develop a universal structure prediction model for all molecular types presented in the Protein Data
123   Bank. Our ATOM F LOW adopts the same practice, and we believe this formulation can maximize the
124   information flow between proteins and molecules (Bryant et al., 2024), while our structural modeling
125   on the representative atoms encourages the model to focus on the key patterns of biointeractions.

      Notations. In this work, a protein-ligand complex is represented as a series of N biotokens {ai |
      ai = (si , xi ), i = 1, 2, . . . , N }, where each token ai corresponds to either a protein residue or a
132
      ligand atom, si denotes the token type, and xi ∈ R3 denotes the token position, i.e. the coordinate of
133
      its representative atom. Let Sprotein and Satom be the set of amino acid types and chemical elements,
134   respectively. For protein residues, si ∈ Sprotein , with xi being the position of the C-α carbon. For
135   ligand atoms, si ∈ Satom , with xi being the atomic position. We define the protein token set as
136   P = {ai | si ∈ Sprotein }, with Np = |P| being the number of protein residues, and the ligand token
137   set as M = {ai | si ∈ Satom }, with Nm = |M| representing the number of ligand atoms. In our
138   settings, N = Np + Nm . The biotokens are attributed with token-level features f token ∈ RN ×ct and
139   pair-level features f pair ∈ RN ×N ×cp , where ct and cp denote the feature dimensions.
140
      Problem Formulation. Given a ligand molecule represented as a chemical graph G = (V, E) and a
141   residue count Np for the protein binder to be designed, we aim to generate a protein-ligand complex,
142   where a conformer of G is docked to a protein binder with Np residues. Specifically, by describing
143   the target protein-ligand complex as a series of biotokens, we generate the token positions {xi }, with
144   xm = {xi | ai ∈ M} being a valid conformer for G, and xp = {xi | ai ∈ P} being a protein binder
145   with high binding affinity to xm . Following previous works (Krishna et al., 2024; Yim et al., 2023b),
146   we additionally generate the token frames {Ti = (ri , ti ) | ai ∈ P} for protein tokens as described in
147   Appendix A.1, which can be used to recover full backbone coordinates of residues. The design of
148   residue types {si | ai ∈ P} is delegated to an existing reverse folding model (Dauparas et al., 2023).

      4.2      R EPRESENTATION OF C ONDITIONAL F EATURES
249
250   The generation process of ATOM F LOW is conditioned on the ligand chemical graph G and a designated
251   protein length Np . We model such conditions as an additional condition to the vector field u. As a
252   result, the inputs of the prediction network x̂1 are augmented to accept conditional features. With the
253   biotoken representation, we embed all such features as f token and f pair as illustrated in Figure 2A.
254
      For a ligand chemical graph G, we embed the chemical element, as well as other known chemical
255
      properties as f token of ligand tokens. The chemical bonds E are embedded in f pair as a multi-
256   dimensional adjacency tensor, each dimension representing a bond type. For residue tokens, we
257   embed the relative residue position (Shaw et al., 2018) in f pair , while f token may represent other
258   known conditions. We concatenate the protein and ligand features to form a unified feature tensor,
259   eliminating the need to distinguish different types of tokens when processing the features.

      Training Data. We train the denoising model on two datasets: PDBBind (Liu et al., 2017), a protein-
362   ligand conformer dataset derived from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) (Berman et al., 2000), and
363   SCOPe (Chandonia et al., 2022), a structure categorical dataset for protein. The model is first trained
364   on solely generating the protein structure for 400k steps, and then finetuned on co-generating both
365   the protein and ligand structure for 300k steps.
366
      Baseline and Model Variant. We compare ATOM F LOW with the state-of-the-art binder generation
367
      method RFDiffusionAA (Krishna et al., 2024), which is extensive trained on almost all known data.
368   Since RFDiffusionAA requires a fixed ligand structure at the binding state as input, we extend our
369   method to work under its setting. For ATOM F LOW, besides the original setting (ATOM F LOW-N),
370   we also train a version of our model with the pairwise distance matrix of the bound structure as
371   an auxiliary hint input (ATOM F LOW-H). This version still needs to generate the ligand structure
372   itself, rather than rely on a fixed structure, as other specifications is not modified. We exclude
373   PocketGen (Zhang et al., 2024) and FlowSite (Stark et al., 2024) since they can only refine the pocket
374   residues of a given binder. We discuss them with an additional experiment in the appendix.
375   Evaluation Set. We mainly evaluate all methods on a selected ligand set (evaluation set) from
376   RFDiffusionAA (FAD, SAM, IAI, OQO). The evaluation set comprises ligands from inside and
377   outside the training set, with both long and short lengths. We conduct evaluations on an extended
      ligand set (extended set, see Appendix A.5) to further demonstrate the performance of ATOM F LOW.

494   5.4   D IVERSITY AND N OVELTY
495
496   In this section, we report the diversity and novelty of ATOM F LOW, following common practice in
497   literature (Krishna et al., 2024; Yim et al., 2023b). Diversity refers to the structural divergence of the
498
      designed binders for a certain ligand, while novelty refers to how close a designed protein is to the
      known proteins. For diversity, we generate 100 structures with 200 residues for each ligand, and then
499
      use MaxCluster (Herbert, 2008) to calculate the pairwise structural distance of the outputs and report
500
      the number of clusters using different thresholds of maximum distance within a cluster. For novelty,
501   we generate 4 structures with residue count in [100, 101, · · · , 300] for each ligand, and then calculate
502   the highest TM-score (Zhang, 2005) between a designed structure and any similar structure searched
503   by FoldSeek (Kempen et al., 2024) (pdbTM), as well as the protein scRMSD. The search range of
504   pdbTM is all known protein structures in PDB.

530   6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
531
      In this work, we proposed ATOM F LOW, a de novo protein binder design method for small molecule
532
      ligands considering the flexibility of ligand structure. Unlike previous works, ATOM F LOW no longer
533
      relies on a given bound ligand conformer as input. We represent the protein-ligand complex as
534   unified biotokens, learning the structure distribution of both the proteins and the ligands simulta-
535   neously from the data with an SE(3)-equivariant flow matching model on the representative atoms.
536   During the evaluation, ATOM F LOW shows comparable design quality to the state-of-the-art model
537   RFDiffusionAA, which requires the ligand conformer to be fixed before design. Further evaluation
538   exhibits the advantage of ATOM F LOW in the circumstance when the ligand conformer is not known.
539   A direct future work is to support more precise control of the generated structures, and we’re working
      to migrate ATOM F LOW to all kinds of biomolecules, including DNA, RNA, and metal ions.

      A.1    P ROTEIN F RAMES
759
760   Proteins are composed of amino acid chains linked by peptide bonds, forming a backbone with
761   protruding side chains. Each amino acid’s position and orientation are described by a local coordinate
762   system, or protein frame, centered on three key backbone atoms: the alpha carbon (Cα), the carbonyl
763   carbon (C), and the amide nitrogen (N). These atoms act as reference points for establishing the frame.
764   The alpha carbon (Cα) typically acts as the origin. The vector from Cα to the amide nitrogen (N) is
765   normalized to define one axis of the frame. A second axis is defined by the normalized vector from
766
      Cα to the carbonyl carbon (C). The third axis is formed by the cross product of these two vectors,
      creating an orthogonal, right-handed coordinate system. The residue frame is typically represented
767
      as an SE(3) transformation T = (R, t), which maps a vector from this local system to the global
768
      coordinate system. In this transformation, t corresponds to the position of Cα in the global system,
769   and R represents the rotation needed to align the residue’s structure within the global context.

787   Token Features For ligand atom tokens, the token-level feature set includes: chirality, degree,
788   formal charge, implicit valence, number of H atoms, number of radical electrons, orbital hybridization,
789   aromaticity, and ring size. The pair-level feature is provided as one-hot embedding of the bond type.
790   For residue tokens, no token-level feature is known, while the pair-level feature only contains the
791
      binned distance of residue index between residues. All features are encoded as a one-hot vector and
      concatenated.
792
793
      Token Frames The final loss we adopted LCFM-FAPE requires aligning the predicted structure to the
794
      local frame of every token. The frames of protein residues can be naturally defined as in Section 3.
795
      However, the frames of ligand atoms could not be chosen directly. Since a frame could be calculated
796   from the coordinate of 3 atoms, we need to choose an atom triplet for every atom token.
797
798   We first obtain a canonical rank of every atom that does not depend on the input order (Schneider
799
      et al., 2015). The atoms are then renamed to their rank. For atoms x with a degree greater than or
      equal to 2, we select the lexicographically smallest triplet (u, x, v) to define the frame, where u and
800
      v are neighbors of x. For atoms with a degree of 1, u is the only neighbor of x, and v is chosen as
801
      one of u ’s neighbors. This method ensures that each atom’s frame is defined in a consistent manner,
802   irrespective of its position in the input sequence, thereby facilitating the model to learn a consistent
803   structural target.
804
805   Extending Token Types and Features Though ATOM F LOW only considers the interaction between
806   protein and molecule ligands, the unified biotoken has the potential to extend to all biological entities,
807   including DNA, RNA, etc, by defining the token position, token frame, local and pair features, and
808   the representation of the internal structure. For example, an RNA can be represented as a sequence of
809   nucleotides, with the token position defined as its mass center, and the token frame calculated from
      an atom triplet, such as C2-N1-C6.

817   For all types of tokens, we only consider their token positions to simplify the flow matching process.
818   Thus, the positions of all tokens lie in the Euclidean space RN ×3 . Since a complex could be arbitrarily
819   moved or rotated in the coordinate space without changing its structure, we need an algorithm that
820   treats different position series as the same if they could be aligned with an SE(3) translation. Thus,
821   every data point we consider now lies in the quotient space RN ×3 /SE(3). This quotient space is
822   proved to be a Riemannian manifold (Diepeveen et al., 2024).
823   For a Riemannian manifold, the flow matching process could be defined using a premetric (Chen &
824   Lipman, 2024). A premetric d : M × M → R should satisfy: 1. d(x, y) ≥ 0 for all x, y ∈ M; 2.
825   d(x, y) = 0 iff x = y; 3. ∇d(x, y) ̸= 0 iff x ̸= y.
826
      We define our premetric as the minimum point-wise rooted sum of squared distance (RMSD) among
827   all pairs of possible structures in the original space RN ×3 for two elements in the quotient space
828   d(x, y) = ∥alignx (y) − x∥, which satisfies all three conditions (Proposition 1).

      A.4    D ETAILS ON THE P REDICTION N ETWORK

858   Structure Module Specifications The main components of the structure module are derived from
859   Alphafold 2 (Jumper et al., 2021), while our implementation builds on top of the widely acknowledged
860   reimplementation OpenFold (Ahdritz et al., 2024). The TransformerStack consists of 14 layers of
861   simplified Evoformer block, and the IPAStack consists of 4 layers of Invariant Point Attention (IPA)
862   blocks. The MSA operations in the Evoformer block are simplified by replacing the operations on
863   the MSA feature matrix with the single representation si . The weights of the IPA blocks are shared,
      and the structural loss is calculated on the outputs of each block and averaged.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      Training Details During training, we equally sample data from the SCOPe dataset (v2.08) and the
865   PDBBind dataset (2020). We simply drop the data with more than 512 tokens, and we don’t crop
866   the filtered complexes since the cutoff is large enough and only filters out a relatively small portion
867   of the data. We train our model on 10 NIVIDA RTX 4090 acceleration cards, with a batch size set
868   to 10, which means the batch size on each device is set to 1. We use the Adam Optimizer (Kingma,
869   2014) with a weight-decaying learning rate scheduler, starting from 10−3 and decays the learning
870   rate by 0.95 every 50k steps. We separate the training process into two stages: 1) initial training,
871   α1 = 0.5, α4 = 0.3, α2 = α3 = 0; 2) finetuning, α1 = α2 = α3 = 0.5, α4 = 0.3.
872   Ligand tokens are not given during the first training stage. The first stage trains an unconditional
873   protein generation model, while the second stage turns it to a conditional protein binder and ligand
874   conformer generation model. The FAPE loss is defined as an average of all pairs of tokens in the
875   original paper, so the calculation process first yield a FAPE matrix and then produce the average
876   value of the matrix. The protein-protein, protein-ligand and ligand-ligand loss calculates the average
877   value of the sub-matrixs defined as (row: protein, col: protein), (row: protein, col: ligand), and (row:
878   ligand, col:ligand).
879   Since training a protein design model is significantly time-consuming, the design choices of our
880   training strategy is largely determined by grid searching possible design space and save the training
881   trajectory of the first 30∼50k steps. We compare the training trajectories and select the best configu-
882   ration that meets the following criteria: a) The final distogram loss should close the minimum we get
883   among the configurations (around 2.0). b) The LCFM-FAPE should not decline too fast at the first 10k
884   steps. The first 10k steps is for the transformer stack to learn a relatively steady output, indicated by
885
      the decline of the distogram loss. A decline of LCFM-FAPE at this stage will resulted in an undesired
      local minimum. Then LCFM-FAPE should decline fast right after the distogram loss turns to decline
886
      much smoother. We select the configuration with the lowest LCFM-FAPE at the end of training.
887
888   We decide the end of each training stage when the training converges, with the following criteria: a)
889   the decline rate of every single loss is small. b) the structural violence of sampled structures (counts
890   of CA atom violation) converges.
891   An initial study on directly train the second stage shows unsatisfactory training trajectory. Since the
892   ligand conformer is way easier to generate compared to protein folds, the FAPE loss declines too
893   fast even before the distogram loss, resulted in unstable TransformerStack output, and leading to a
894   diverge of the model after 30k steps. The resulted model with minimum loss is able to predict the
895   ligand structure, with random protein residue position, which is unusable.

949   Additional Binding Affinity Metric We are aware that Vina might not be a perfect proxy for
950   binding affinity. We noticed that AlphaProteo adopts several metrics produced by AlphaFold 3 as in
951   silico filters. At the time we finished our draft, there’s no publicly available AlphaFold 3 for us to run
952   locally. Recently, several AlphaFold 3 replications and the original AlphaFold 3 are released. We
953   developed an alternative in silico metric based on Chai-1, calculating the minimum value across all
954   interchain terms in the PAE matrix (min_ipAE, lower is better). Note that this metric is proved to
955
      be a good indicator for protein-protein binder design, but not validated on small molecule-protein
      binders. The results in Figure 9 show that the AtomFlow-generated binders have lower min_ipAE
956
      than the ones from RFDiffusion-AA, and both models have the ability to generate binders with
957
      similar min_ipAE as natural ones. We’re working to develop better metrics as in silico proxy for
958   ligand-binding protein design based on wet lab verification.
