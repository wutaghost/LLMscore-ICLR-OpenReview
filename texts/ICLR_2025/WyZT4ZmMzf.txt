011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Neuroscience and artificial intelligence (AI) both face the challenge of interpreting
014            high-dimensional neural data, where the comparative analysis of such data is crucial
015            for revealing shared mechanisms and differences between these complex systems.
016
               Despite the widespread use of representational comparisons and the abundance
               classes of comparison methods, a critical question remains: which metrics are most
017
               suitable for these comparisons? While some studies evaluate metrics based on their
018
               ability to differentiate models of different origins or constructions (e.g., various
019            architectures), another approach is to assess how well they distinguish models that
020            exhibit distinct behaviors. To investigate this, we examine the degree of alignment
021            between various representational similarity measures and behavioral outcomes,
022            employing group statistics and a comprehensive suite of behavioral metrics for
023            comparison. In our evaluation of eight commonly used representational similarity
024            metrics in the visual domain—spanning alignment-based, Canonical Correlation
025            Analysis (CCA)-based, inner product kernel-based, and nearest-neighbor meth-
026            ods—we found that metrics like linear Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) and
027
               Procrustes distance, which emphasize the overall geometric structure or shape
               of representations, excelled in differentiating trained from untrained models and
028
               aligning with behavioral measures, whereas metrics such as linear predictivity,
029
               commonly used in neuroscience, demonstrated only moderate alignment with be-
030            havior. These insights are crucial for selecting metrics that emphasize behaviorally
031            meaningful comparisons in NeuroAI research.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Both neuroscience and artificial intelligence (AI) confront the challenge of high-dimensional neural
037   data, whether from neurobiological firing rates, voxel responses, or hidden layer activations in artificial
038   networks. Comparing such high-dimensional neural data is critical for both fields, as it facilitates
039   understanding of complex systems by revealing their underlying similarities and differences.
040
      In neuroscience, one of the main goals is to uncover how neural activity drives behavior and to
041
      understand neural computations at an algorithmic level. Comparisons across species and between
042   brain and model representations, particularly those of deep neural networks, have been instrumental
043   in advancing this understanding (Yamins et al. (2014); Eickenberg et al. (2017); Güçlü & Van Gerven
044   (2015); Cichy et al. (2016); Khaligh-Razavi & Kriegeskorte (2014); Schrimpf et al. (2018; 2020);
045   Storrs et al. (2021); Kriegeskorte et al. (2008)). A growing interest lies in systematically altering
046   model parameters—such as architecture, learning objectives, and training data—and comparing the
047   resulting internal representations with neural data (Yamins & DiCarlo (2016); Doerig et al. (2023);
048   Schrimpf et al. (2018; 2020)).
049   Similarly, in AI, researchers are increasingly focused on reverse-engineering neural networks by
050   tweaking architectural components, training objectives, and data inputs to examine how these
051   modifications impact the resulting representations. However, studying neural networks in isolation
052   can be limiting, as interactions between the learning algorithms and structured data shape these
053   systems in ways we do not yet fully understand. Comparative analysis of model representations offers
      a powerful tool to probe these networks more deeply. This endeavor is rooted in the universality

054
      hypothesis that similar phenomena can arise across different networks. Indeed, a large number
055   of studies have provided empirical evidence licensing these universal theories (Huh et al. (2024);
056   Kornblith et al. (2019); Bansal et al. (2021); Li et al. (2015); Roeder et al. (2021); Lenc & Vedaldi
057   (2015)) but the extent to which diverse neural networks converge to similar representations is not
058   well understood.
059
      Given the growing interest in comparative analyses across neuroscience and AI, a key question
060
      arises: what are the best tools for conducting such analyses? Over the past decade, a wide variety of
061   approaches have emerged for quantifying the representational similarity across artificial and biological
062   neural representations (Sucholutsky et al. (2023); Klabunde et al. (2023); Williams et al. (2021)).
063   Most of these approaches can be classified as belonging to one of four categories: representational
064   similarity based measures, alignment-based measures, nearest-neighbor based measures and canonical
065   correlation analysis-based measures (Klabunde et al. (2023)). With the wide range of available
066   approaches for representational comparisons, researchers are tasked with selecting a suitable metric.
067   The choice of a specific metric implicitly prioritizes certain properties of the system, as different
068   approaches emphasize distinct invariances and are sensitive to varying aspects of the representations.
069
      This complexity ties into broader issues in the concept and assessment of similarity, which, as
      emphasized in psychology, is highly context-dependent (Tversky (1977)).
070
071   What, then, are the key desiderata for network comparison metrics? Networks may exhibit similarities
072   in some dimensions and differences in others, but the critical question is whether these differences are
073   functionally relevant or merely reflect differences in origin or construction. This consideration leads
074   to a central criterion for effective metrics: behavioral differences should correspond to differences
075
      in internal representational similarity (Cao (2022)). However, identifying which measures reliably
      capture behaviorally meaningful differences remains an open question.
076
077   Our study aims to address the above challenge. Here, we make the following key contributions:
078
079
080          • We conduct an extensive analysis of common representational comparison measures (in-
081
               cluding alignment-based, representational similarity matrix-based, CCA-based, and nearest-
               neighbor-based methods) and show that these measures differ in their capacity to distinguish
082
               between models. While some measures excel at distinguishing between models from
083
               different architectural families, others are better at separating trained from untrained models.
084          • To assess which of these distinctions reflects differences in model behaviors, we perform
085            complementary behavioral comparisons using a comprehensive set of behavioral metrics
086            (both hard and soft prediction-based). We find that behavioral metrics are generally more
087            consistent with each other than representational similarity measures.
088          • Finally, we cross-compare representational and behavioral similarity measures, revealing
089            that linear CKA and Procrustes distance align most closely with behavioral evaluations,
090            whereas metrics like linear predictivity, widely used in neuroscience, show only modest
091            alignment. This finding offers important guidance for metric selection in neuroAI, where
092
               the functional relevance of representational comparisons is paramount.
093

096   Related Work Although few studies directly compare representational similarity measures based
097   on their discriminative power, most efforts in this area focus on identifying metrics that distinguish
098   between models by their construction. These efforts typically involve assessing measures based on
099
      their ability to match corresponding layers across models with varying seeds (Kornblith et al., 2019)
      or identical architectures with different initializations (Han et al., 2023; Rahamim & Belinkov, 2024).
100
      The closest to our work are studies by Ding et al. (Ding et al., 2021) and Cloos et al. (Cloos et al.,
101
      2024). Cloos et al. (Cloos et al., 2024) optimized synthetic datasets to resemble brain activity under
102   various measures, demonstrating that metrics like linear predictivity and CKA can yield high scores
103   even when task-relevant variables are not encoded. Ding et al. ((Ding et al., 2021)) examined the
104   sensitivity of representational similarity measures—CCA, CKA, and Procrustes—in BERT models
105   (NLP) and ResNet models (CIFAR-10) to factors that either preserve functional behavior (e.g.,
106   random seed variations) or alter it (e.g., principal component deletion). However, these studies
107   examine a limited set of similarity measures and primarily assess functional similarity based on task
      performance alone, without evaluating the finer-grained alignment of predictions across models.

334   2.1   D IFFERENT R EPRESENTATIONAL S IMILARITY M EASURES HAVE D ISTINCT C APACITIES
335         FOR M ODEL S EPARATION
336
337
      To characterize how different representational similarity measures discriminate models, we first
      visualize the model-by-model similarity matrices for each measure. We observed that while some
338
      measures like the soft-matching distance were effective at differentiating architectural families (Fig.
339
      2, right), others like the Procrustes distance were more sensitive to the effects of training (Fig. 2, left),
340   clearly separating trained from untrained models. Other measures, like linear predictivity, which allow
341   greater flexibility in aligning the two representations, showed limited ability in distinguishing between
342   models trained with different architectures or trained from untrained models (see Appendix A.4 for
343   additional similarity matrices). To quantify these distinctions, we computed d′ scores (Appendix A.2)
344   to assess each measure’s ability to differentiate two categories of models: (a) those from different
345   architectural families, and (b) those with varying levels of training (trained vs. untrained). Significant
346   differences in d′ scores emerged across measures (Fig. 3). For instance, Procrustes achieved d′
347   scores with a mean of 3.70 when separating trained from untrained models across all datasets,
348
      while commonly used measures like CCA and linear predictivity produced much lower scores
      with means of 0.53 and 0.87, respectively. Similarly, some measures were better at discriminating
349
      architectural differences, with the soft-matching distance demonstrating the highest discriminability
350
      (mean of d′ scores = 1.6). Previous studies have also demonstrated that different measures vary
351   in their effectiveness at establishing layer-wise correspondence across networks with the same
352   architecture (Kornblith et al., 2019; Thobani et al.). Considering these differences in how measures
353   distinguish between models, a key question emerges: Which distinctions should we prioritize?

      We next examined the consistency across different representational similarity measures and across
413
      different behavioral measures by computing correlations between the model-by-model similarity
414   matrices generated by each measure. As shown in Fig. 4 (Top),we find that behavioral metrics (mean
415   r: 0.85 ± 0.01) are more correlated on average than representational metrics (mean r: 0.75 ± 0.007),
416   with a significant difference (z = −7.10, p = 5 × 10−8 < 0.0001).
417
      To further understand the relationships between different representational similarity measures, we ana-
418
      lyzed the MDS plot (Fig. 4 (Bottom)). This visualization revealed distinct clusters of measures based
419
      on their theoretical properties. Measures that rely on inner product kernels (stimulus-by-stimulus
420   dissimilarities) tend to group together, indicating they capture similar aspects of representational
421   structure. On the other hand, measures that use explicit, direct mappings between individual neu-
422   rons—such as Linear Predictivity and Semi-Matching—form a separate cluster. Notably, Procrustes
423   Distance and CCA also involve alignment, similar to Linear Predictivity and Semi-Matching; however,
424   this alignment is achieved collectively across all units or neurons rather than through independently
425   determined mappings for each neuron. Procrustes aligns the entire configuration of points, while
426   CCA projects the two representations onto common subspaces to maximize correlation, further
427   distinguishing them from other representational similarity approaches.

429   in neuroscience and AI revolve around understanding computations and how those computations relate
430   to behavior; behaviorally grounded comparisons of model representations are key to this endeavor.
431   We find that behavioral metrics distinguish between models in a consistent manner across different
      datasets, reinforcing the robustness of the model relationships they uncover (Appendix A.3). The

      3   D ISCUSSION
509
      In this study, we compared 8 neural representational similarity metrics and 9 behavioral measures
510
      across 17 datasets.Based on the premise that behavioral differences should be mirrored in the
511
      representational structure of neural networks, we examined practical distinctions in their alignment
512   with behavior. Metrics like RSA, CKA, and Procrustes distance, which preserve the overall geometry
513   of neural representations, tend to align closely with behavioral measures. In contrast, methods
514   like linear predictivity, which align dimensions without preserving global geometry, show weaker
515   alignment. This divergence likely arises because linear predictivity has the capacity of mapping
516   complex, distributed geometric structures to simpler, compressed ones while maintaining prediction
517   accuracy. For instance, trained networks were observed to predict untrained network activation
518   patterns well, yielding high symmetrized scores.
519   Moreover, while different behavioral measures generally show consistency, neural representational
520   similarity metrics do not, underscoring the need for a deeper understanding of how these representa-
521   tional metrics discriminate between models in practical applications. Our analysis sets a new standard
522   for representational similarity measures in neuroscience and AI, using downstream behavioral ro-
523   bustness as a guide for selecting the most suitable metric. This framework is especially crucial in
524   model-brain comparisons, where representational analyses are frequently applied to assess if artificial
525   neural networks and biological systems are serving comparable functional roles in terms of perceptual
526
      and cognitive processes.
527   Our framework for representational metric selection, though robust, makes some key assumptions.
528   It assumes a specific mechanism for how behavior is ‘reading out’ from neural representations,
529   and different readout mechanisms could reveal qualitatively different relationships between models.
530   For example, applying biologically-inspired constraints, such as sparsity, could reveal divergent
531   relationships, especially if some models encode behaviorally relevant information in a sparse manner
532
      that others do not. In such cases, the precise representation structure at the unit-level becomes
      critical. Additionally, we defined "behavior" within the scope of object classification across multiple
533
      out-of-distribution (OOD) image datasets. Extending evaluations to include fine-grained visual
534
      discrimination or broader tasks beyond categorization would better capture the full range of visual
535   processing. Lastly, a stronger theoretical framework explaining why certain similarity measures align
536   more closely with behavior than others is currently lacking in our work, but this remains an exciting
537   direction for future research.

      similarity scores between every pair of trained models. B represents the set of similarity scores
766
      from inter-group comparisons, specifically the similarity scores between each pair of trained and
767
      untrained models. Equivalent to the set of entries located at the intersection of trained model rows
768   and untrained model columns in the model-by-model similarity matrix of the metrics.
