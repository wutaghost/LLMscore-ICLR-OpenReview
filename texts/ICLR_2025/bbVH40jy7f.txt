009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems enhance large language models
012            (LLMs) by integrating external knowledge sources, enabling more accurate and
013            contextually relevant responses tailored to user needs. However, existing RAG sys-
014            tems have significant limitations, including reliance on flat data representations and
015            inadequate contextual awareness, which can lead to fragmented answers that fail
016            to capture complex inter-dependencies. To address these challenges, we propose
017            LightRAG, which incorporates graph structures into text indexing and retrieval
018
               processes. This innovative framework employs a dual-level retrieval system that
               enhances comprehensive information retrieval from both low-level and high-level
019
               knowledge discovery. Additionally, the integration of graph structures with vector
020
               representations facilitates efficient retrieval of related entities and their relationships,
021            significantly improving response times while maintaining contextual relevance.
022            This capability is further enhanced by an incremental update algorithm that ensures
023            the timely integration of new data, allowing the system to remain effective in dy-
024            namic environments. Extensive experimental validation demonstrates considerable
025            improvements in retrieval accuracy and efficiency compared to existing approaches.
026            For the reproducibility of our results, we have made our LightRAG available
027            anonymously at: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/LightRAG-2BEE.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems have been developed to enhance large language
033
      models (LLMs) by integrating external knowledge sources Sudhi et al. (2024); Es et al. (2024);
      Salemi & Zamani (2024). This innovative integration allows LLMs to generate more accurate and
034
      contextually relevant responses, significantly improving their utility in real-world applications. By
035
      adapting to specific domain knowledge Tu et al. (2024), RAG systems ensure that the information
036   provided is not only pertinent but also tailored to the user’s needs. Furthermore, they offer access to
037   up-to-date information Zhao et al. (2024), which is crucial in rapidly evolving fields. Chunking plays
038   a vital role in facilitating the retrieval-augmented generation process Lyu et al. (2024). By breaking
039   down a large external text corpus into smaller, more manageable segments, chunking significantly
040   enhances the accuracy of information retrieval. This approach allows for more targeted similarity
041   searches, ensuring that the retrieved content is directly relevant to user queries.
042   However, existing RAG systems have key limitations that hinder their performance. First, many
043   methods rely on flat data representations, restricting their ability to understand and retrieve information
044   based on intricate relationships between entities. Second, these systems often lack the contextual
045   awareness needed to maintain coherence across various entities and their interrelations, resulting
046   in responses that may not fully address user queries. For example, consider a user asking, “How
047   does the rise of electric vehicles influence urban air quality and public transportation infrastructure?”
048   Existing RAG methods might retrieve separate documents on electric vehicles, air pollution, and
049   public transportation challenges but struggle to synthesize this information into a cohesive response.
050
      They may fail to explain how the adoption of electric vehicles can improve air quality, which in turn
      could affect public transportation planning. As a result, the user may receive a fragmented answer
051
      that does not adequately capture the complex inter-dependencies among these topics.
052
053   To address these limitations, we propose incorporating graph structures into text indexing and
      relevant information retrieval. Graphs are particularly effective at representing the interdependencies

      among different entities Rampášek et al. (2022), which enables a more nuanced understanding
055   of relationships. The integration of graph-based knowledge structures facilitates the synthesis of
056   information from multiple sources into coherent and contextually rich responses. Despite these
057   advantages, developing a fast and scalable graph-empowered RAG system that efficiently handles
058   varying query volumes is crucial. In this work, we achieve an effective and efficient RAG system by

067   more coherent and contextually rich responses. LightRAG employs efficient dual-level retrieval

      relationships, and high-level retrieval, which encompasses broader topics and themes. By combining

071
      Additionally, by integrating graph structures with vector representations, our framework facilitates

088

094   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) integrates user queries with a collection of pertinent doc-
095   uments sourced from an external knowledge database, incorporating two essential elements: the
096

      for fetching relevant documents or information from the external knowledge database. It identifies and
097
      retrieves the most pertinent data based on the input query. 2) After the retrieval process, the generation
098
      component takes the retrieved information and generates coherent, contextually relevant responses. It
099   leverages the capabilities of the language model to produce meaningful outputs. Formally, this RAG
100   framework, denoted as M, can be defined as follows:
101

106   includes two key functionalities: i) Data Indexer φ(·): which involves building a specific data
107   structure D̂ based on the external database D. ii) Data Retriever ψ(·): The relevant documents are
      obtained by comparing the query against the indexed data, also denoted as “relevant documents”. By

      3.1           G RAPH - BASED T EXT I NDEXING
133
134   Graph-Enhanced Entity and Relationship Extraction. Our LightRAG enhances the retrieval
135   system by segmenting documents into smaller, more manageable pieces. This strategy allows for
136   quick identification and access to relevant information without analyzing entire documents. Next,
137   we leverage LLMs to identify and extract various entities (e.g., names, dates, locations, and events)
138   along with the relationships between them. The information collected through this process will be
139   used to create a comprehensive knowledge graph that highlights the connections and insights across
140
      the entire collection of documents. We formally represent this graph generation module as follows:
141                                               D̂ = (V̂, Ê) = Dedupe ◦ Prof(V, E), V, E = ∪Di ∈D Recog(Di )                                                                                                          (2)
142
143   where D̂ represents the resulting knowledge graphs. To generate this data, we apply three main
144
      processing steps to the raw text documents Di . These steps utilize a LLM for text analysis and
      processing. Details about the prompt templates and specific settings for this part can be found in
145
      Appendix 7.3.2. The functions used in our graph-based text indexing paradigm are described as
146
      follows:
147
148   • Extracting Entities and Relationships. R(·): This function prompts a LLM to identify entities
149     (nodes) and their relationships (edges) within the text data. For instance, it can extract entities
150     like "Cardiologists" and "Heart Disease," and relationships such as "Cardiologists diagnose Heart
151     Disease" from the text: "Cardiologists assess symptoms to identify potential heart issues." To
152     improve efficiency, the raw text D is segmented into multiple chunks Di .
153   • LLM Profiling for Key-Value Pair Generation. P(·): We employ a LLM-empowered profiling
154     function, P(·), to generate a text key-value pair (K, V ) for each entity node in V and relation
155     edge in E. Each index key is a word or short phrase that enables efficient retrieval, while the
156     corresponding value is a text paragraph summarizing relevant snippets from external data to aid in
157     text generation. Entities use their names as the sole index key, whereas relations may have multiple
158
        index keys derived from LLM enhancements that include global themes from connected entities.
159   • Deduplication to Optimize Graph Operations. D(·): Finally, we implement a deduplication
160     function, D(·), that identifies and merges identical entities and relations from different segments of
161     the raw text Di . This process effectively reduces the overhead associated with graph operations on
        D̂ by minimizing the graph’s size, leading to more efficient data processing.

      Our LightRAG offers two advantages through its graph-based text indexing paradigm. First, Com-
163   prehensive Information Understanding. The constructed graph structures enable the extraction
164   of global information from multi-hop subgraphs, greatly enhancing LightRAG’s ability to handle
165   complex queries that span multiple document chunks. Second, Enhanced Retrieval Performance.
166   the key-value data structures derived from the graph are optimized for rapid and precise retrieval.
167   This provides a superior alternative to less accurate embedding matching methods (Gao et al., 2023)
168   and inefficient chunk traversal techniques (Edge et al., 2024) commonly used in existing approaches.
169   Fast Adaptation to Incremental Knowledge Base. To efficiently adapt to evolving data changes
170   while ensuring accurate and relevant responses, our LightRAG incrementally updates the knowledge
171   base without the need for complete reprocessing of the entire external database. For a new document
172   D′ , the incremental update algorithm processes it using the same graph-based indexing steps φ as
173   before, resulting in D̂′ = (V̂ ′ , Ê ′ ). Subsequently, LightRAGcombines the new graph data with the
174   original by taking the union of the node sets V̂ and V̂ ′ , as well as the edge sets Ê and Ê ′ .
175
176
      Two key objectives guide our approach to fast adaptation for the incremental knowledge base: Seam-
      less Integration of New Data. By applying a consistent methodology to new information, the
177
      incremental update module allows the LightRAG to integrate new external databases without disrupt-
178
      ing the existing graph structure. This approach preserves the integrity of established connections,
179   ensuring that historical data remains accessible while enriching the graph without conflicts or redun-
180   dancies. Reducing Computational Overhead . By eliminating the need to rebuild the entire index
181   graph, this method reduces computational overhead and facilitates the rapid assimilation of new data.
182   Consequently, LightRAG maintains system accuracy, provides current information, and conserves
183   resources, ensuring users receive timely updates and enhancing the overall RAG effectiveness.

185   3.2   D UAL - LEVEL R ETRIEVAL PARADIGM
186
187   To retrieve relevant information from both specific document chunks and their complex inter-
188   dependencies, our LightRAG proposes generating query keys at both detailed and abstract levels.
189   • Specific Queries. These queries are detail-oriented and typically reference specific entities within
190     the graph, requiring precise retrieval of information associated with particular nodes or edges. For
191     example, a specific query might be, “Who wrote ’Pride and Prejudice’?”
192   • Abstract Queries. In contrast, abstract queries are more conceptual, encompassing broader topics,
193     summaries, or overarching themes that are not directly tied to specific entities. An example of an
194     abstract query is, “How does artificial intelligence influence modern education?”
195
196   To accommodate diverse query types, the LightRAG employs two distinct retrieval strategies within
197   the dual-level retrieval paradigm. This ensures that both specific and abstract inquiries are addressed
198
      effectively, allowing the system to deliver relevant responses tailored to user needs.
199
      • Low-Level Retrieval. This level is primarily focused on retrieving specific entities along with their
200     associated attributes or relationships. Queries at this level are detail-oriented and aim to extract
201     precise information about particular nodes or edges within the graph.
202
      • High-Level Retrieval. This level addresses broader topics and overarching themes. Queries at this
203
        level aggregate information across multiple related entities and relationships, providing insights
204
        into higher-level concepts and summaries rather than specific details.
205
206   Integrating Graph and Vectors for Efficient Retrieval. By combining graph structures with
207   vector representations, the model gains a deeper insight into the interrelationships among entities.
208   This synergy enables the retrieval algorithm to effectively utilize both local and global keywords,
209   streamlining the search process and improving the relevance of results.
210
      • (i) Query Keyword Extraction. For a given query q, the retrieval algorithm of LightRAG begins
211
        by extracting both local query keywords k (l) and global query keywords k (g) .
212
213
      • (ii) Keyword Matching. The algorithm uses an efficient vector database to match local query
        keywords with candidate entities and global query keywords with relations linked to global keys.
214
215   • (iii) Incorporating High-Order Relatedness. To enhance the query with higher-order relatedness,
        LightRAGfurther gathers neighboring nodes within the local subgraphs of the retrieved graph

          elements. This process involves the set {vi |vi ∈ V ∧ (vi ∈ Nv ∨ vi ∈ Ne )}, where Nv and Ne
217       represent the one-hop neighboring nodes of the retrieved nodes v and edges e, respectively.
218
219   This dual-level retrieval paradigm not only facilitates efficient retrieval of related entities and relations
220   through keyword matching, but also enhances the comprehensiveness of results by integrating relevant
221   structural information from the constructed knowledge graph.

225   Utilization of Retrieved Information. Utilizing the retrieved information ψ(q; D̂), our LightRAG
226   employs a general-purpose LLM to generate answers based on the collected data. This data comprises
227
      concatenated values V from relevant entities and relations, produced by the profiling function P(·). It
      includes names, descriptions of entities and relations, and excerpts from the original text.
228
229   Context Integration and Answer Generation. By unifying the query with this multi-source text,
230   the LLM generates informative answers tailored to the user’s needs, ensuring alignment with the
231   query’s intent. This approach streamlines the answer generation process by integrating both context
232   and query into the LLM model, as illustrated in detailed examples (Appendix 7.2).

      In this section, we analyze the complexity of our proposed LightRAG framework. The complexity is
      primarily divided into two parts. The first part is the graph-based Index phase. During this phase, we
237
      utilize the LLM to extract entities and relationships from each chunk of text. Thus, the LLM needs
238
      to be called total tokens
                    chunk size times. Apart from this, there is no additional overhead, making this approach
239   highly efficient in handling new text updates.
240
241   The second part is the graph-based retrieval phase. For each query, we first invoke the LLM to
242
      generate the relevant keywords. Similar to traditional RAG systems Gao et al. (2023; 2022); Chan
      et al. (2024), the retrieval process relies on vector-based search. However, instead of retrieving
243
      chunks as in traditional RAG, we focus on retrieving entities and relationships. Compared to the
244
      community-based traversal retrieval method used in GraphRAG, our approach significantly reduces
245   the retrieval overhead.

257   Evaluation Datasets. To conduct a comprehensive analysis of LightRAG, we selected four datasets
258   from the UltraDomain benchmark (Qian et al., 2024). The UltraDomain data is sourced from 428
259   college textbooks, covering 18 distinct domains, including agriculture, social sciences, and humanities.
260   From these domains, we chose the Agriculture, CS, Legal, and Mix datasets. The total number
261   of tokens in each dataset ranges from 600,000 to 5,000,000, with detailed information provided in
262   Table 4. Below is a specific introduction to the four domains used in our experiments:
263
      • Agriculture: This domain focuses on agricultural practice, covering diverse topics including
264
        beekeeping, hive management, crop production, disease prevention, and so on.
265
266   • CS: This is the computer science domain. It encompasses key areas of data science and soft-
267
        ware engineering, particularly machine learning and big data processing, featuring content on
        recommendation systems, classification algorithms, and real-time analytics using Spark.
268
269   • Legal: It centers around corporate legal practices, including materials on corporate restructuring,
        legal agreements, regulatory compliance, and governance, targeting the legal and financial sectors.

270
      • Mixed: This domain offers a diverse range of literary, biographical, and philosophical texts. It
271     covers a wide spectrum of disciplines such as cultural, historical, and philosophical studies.
272
273   Question Generation. To evaluate the effectiveness of RAG systems for more high-level sensemaking
274   tasks, we merge all the text content from each dataset as context and follow the generation method
275   used in Edge et al. (2024). Specifically, we task an LLM with generating 5 RAG users and 5 tasks
276   for each user. Each generated user is associated with a textual description for his/her expertise and
277   traits that motivates his/her activities in raising questions. Each task of users is also described with
278   texts, highlighting one of the user’s potential intentions when interacting with RAG systems. For
279
      each user-task combination, the LLM is then required to generate 5 questions that necessitate an
      understanding of the entire corpus. In total, 125 questions are generated for each dataset.
280
281   Baselines. LightRAG is compared to the following four baseline methods across all datasets:
282
283   • Naive RAG (Gao et al., 2023): This model is a standard baseline in simple RAG systems. It
284
        segments raw texts into chunks and stores them in a vector database with text embeddings. For
        queries, Naive RAG creates vectorized representations to directly retrieve text chunks based on the
285
        highest similarity in their vector representations, ensuring efficient and straightforward matching.
286
287   • RQ-RAG (Chan et al., 2024): This approach utilizes the LLM to decompose the input query into
288     several sub-queries. These sub-queries are designed to enhance search accuracy through explicit
289
        query rewriting, decomposition, and disambiguation.
290   • HyDE (Gao et al., 2022): This method employs the LLM to generate a hypothetical document
291     based on the input query. The hypothetical document is then used to retrieve relevant text chunks,
292     which are subsequently used to formulate the final answer.
293   • GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024): This is a graph-enhanced RAG system which leverages an LLM
294     to extract entities and relationships as nodes and edges from the text and generates corresponding
295     descriptions for them. It aggregates nodes into communities and generates a community report
296     to capture global information. When faced with high-level queries, GraphRAG retrieves more
297     comprehensive information by traversing the communities.
298
      Implementation and Evaluation Details. In our experiments, we use the nano vector database for
299
      vector data management and access. By default, GPT-4o-mini is used for all LLM-based operations
300
      in LightRAG. The chunk size across all datasets is set to 1200, and the gleaning parameter is fixed at
301   1 for both GraphRAG and our LightRAG.
302
303   As it is hard to define ground truth for many RAG queries, especially for complex queries involving
304
      high-level semantics, we follow existing work (Edge et al., 2024) and adopt the LLM-based multi-
      dimensional comparison method. A strong LLM, specifically GPT-4o-mini, is used to rank between
305
      each baseline and our LightRAG. The prompt we used for evaluation is elaborated in Appendix 7.3.4.
306
      Totally four evaluation dimensions are utilized, including:
307
308   i) Comprehensiveness: How much detail does the answer provide to cover all aspects and details of
309   the question? ii) Diversity: How varied and rich is the answer in providing different perspectives and
310
      insights on the question? iii) Empowerment: How well does the answer help the reader understand
      and make informed judgments about the topic? iv) Overall: Considers the cumulative performance
311
      across the other three dimensions to determine the best overall answer.
312
313   The LLM directly compare two answers for each dimension and select the superior answer for each
314   dimension. After determining the winning answer for each of the three dimensions, the LLM then
315   combine the results across all dimensions to identify the overall better answer. To avoid any bias
316
      caused by the order of answers in the prompt during evaluation, we alternate the placement of each
      answer in the prompt and calculate win rates accordingly to obtain the final result.

498   5.1   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION WITH LLM S
499
500   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems enhance LLM inputs by retrieving relevant infor-
501   mation from external sources, grounding responses in factual, domain-specific knowledge Ram et al.
502
      (2023); Fan et al. (2024). Current RAG approaches Gao et al. (2022; 2023); Chan et al. (2024); Yu
      et al. (2024) typically embed queries in a vector space to find the nearest context vectors. However,
503
      many of these methods rely on fragmented text chunks and only retrieve the top-k contexts, limiting
504
      their ability to capture comprehensive global information needed for effective responses.
505
506   Although recent studies Edge et al. (2024) have explored using graph structures for knowledge
507   representation, two key limitations persist. First, these approaches often lack the capability for
508
      dynamic updates and expansions of the knowledge graph, making it difficult to incorporate new
      information effectively. In contrast, our proposed model, LightRAG, addresses these challenges
509
      by enabling the RAG system to quickly adapt to new information, ensuring the model’s timeliness
510
      and accuracy. Additionally, existing methods often rely on brute-force searches for each generated
511   community, which are inefficient for large-scale queries. Our LightRAG framework overcomes this
512   limitation by facilitating rapid retrieval of relevant information from the graph through our proposed
513   dual-level retrieval paradigm, significantly enhancing both retrieval efficiency and response speed.
514
515   5.2   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL FOR G RAPHS
516
517   Graphs are a powerful framework for representing complex relationships and find applications
518   in numerous fields. As Large Language Models (LLMs) continue to evolve, researchers have
519   increasingly focused on enhancing their capability to interpret graph-structured data. This body of
520   work can be divided into three primary categories: i) GNNs as Prefix where Graph Neural Networks
521
      (GNNs) are utilized as the initial processing layer for graph data, generating structure-aware tokens
      that LLMs can use during inference. Notable examples include GraphGPT Tang et al. (2024) and
522
      LLaGA Chen et al. (2024). ii) LLMs as Prefix involves LLMs processing graph data enriched with
523
      textual information to produce node embeddings or labels, ultimately refining the training process
524   for GNNs, as demonstrated in systems like GALM Xie et al. (2023) and OFA Liu et al. (2024). iii)
525   LLMs-Graphs Integration focuses on achieving a seamless interaction between LLMs and graph
526   data, employing techniques such as fusion training and GNN alignment, and developing LLM-based
527   agents capable of engaging with graph information directly Li et al. (2023); Brannon et al. (2023).
