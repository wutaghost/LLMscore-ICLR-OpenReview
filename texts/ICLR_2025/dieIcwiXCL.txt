009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We address an important problem in ecology called Species Distribution Model-
012            ing (SDM), whose goal is to predict whether a species exists at a certain posi-
013            tion on Earth. In particular, we tackle a challenging version of this task, where
014            we learn from presence-only data in a community-sourced dataset, model a large
015            number of species simultaneously, and do not use any additional environmental
016            information. Previous work has used neural implicit representations to construct
017            models that achieve promising results. However, implicit representations often
018
               generate predictions of limited spatial precision. We attribute this limitation to
               their inherently global formulation and inability to effectively capture local fea-
019
               ture variations. This issue is especially pronounced with presence-only data and a
020
               large number of species. To address this, we propose a hybrid embedding scheme
021            that combines both implicit and explicit embeddings. Specifically, the explicit em-
022            bedding is implemented with a multiresolution hashgrid, enabling our models to
023            better capture local information. Experiments demonstrate that our results exceed
024            other works by a large margin on various standard benchmarks, and that the hy-
025            brid representation is better than both purely implicit and explicit ones. Qualitative
026            visualizations and comprehensive ablation studies reveal that our hybrid represen-
027            tation successfully addresses the two main challenges. Our code is open-sourced
028            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/HSR-SDM-7360.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Understanding species distribution ranges is a key issue in ecological research, and it has become
      increasingly important in the context of the current global climate crisis and biodiversity decline.
034
      Conventionally, species distribution data has been collected through field studies by human experts
035
      and explorers, who must gather and assess large amounts of information to determine whether a
036   species is present in a given region. These processes are typically slow and labor-intensive, and by
037   the time the models are completed, they may already be outdated or irrelevant.
038
039
      Species Distribution Modeling (SDM) is a method that aims to use collected data to directly predict
      the distribution range of species, thus making related ecological research easier (Elith & Leathwick,
040
      2009; Elith et al., 2010; Miller, 2010). SDMs have many crucial applications in fields such as cli-
041
      mate change assessment (Santini et al., 2021), invasive species management (Srivastava et al., 2019),
042   and extinction risk mapping (Ramirez-Reyes et al., 2021). Whilst such models have achieved some
043   success over the past two decades, most SDMs remain poor indicators of important ecological pa-
044   rameters (Lee-Yaw et al., 2022). Consequently, new SDM methodologies employing more advanced
045   modeling techniques have continued to emerge (Beery et al., 2021).
046
      One challenge in constructing SDMs is the collection of sufficient data for both training and test-
047
      ing (Feeley & Silman, 2011; Vaughan & Ormerod, 2005). The large volume of data required for
048   constructing accurate models, coupled with the difficulty of obtaining it, has consistently been a
049   major obstacle in the development of SDMs. With the emergence of community-sourced data plat-
050   forms such as iNaturalist, eBird, and PlantNet, difficulties in data collection have been somewhat
051   mitigated, but new challenges have arisen regarding data quality and the model’s ability to process
052   large volumes of data (Hartig et al., 2024). For example, due to the nature of the data collection
053   process, most large species distribution datasets are highly susceptible to sampling bias, class im-
      balance, and noise (Benkendorf et al., 2023; Dubos et al., 2022; Kramer-Schadt et al., 2013).

072   Recent advances Cole et al. (2023) in using deep learning for SDMs has reduced the demand for
073   large amounts of high-quality data. In particular, some recent methods applying implicit neural
074   representations achieved considerable accuracy, and no longer required training signals besides
075   presence-only data. However, in practice, predictions from those models are often of limited spatial
076   precision due to the implicit nature of their representational schemes: neural networks inherently
077   produce global embeddings that are not grounded in local features.
078   In this paper, we explore a challenging task that highlights the limitations of implicit representations.
079   First, we use presence-only data instead of presence-absence data. Since confirming a species’
080   presence is generally easier than confirming its absence, many previous studies have constructed
081   SDMs using presence-only data (Barbet-Massin et al., 2012; Mac Aodha et al., 2019; Cole et al.,
082   2023), making this a more difficult but valuable task. Second, we use no additional environmental
083   information. Although conventional SDMs usually use a lot of environmental inputs, and satellite
084
      images are also a common source of information (Dollinger et al., 2024; Gillespie et al., 2024;
      He et al., 2015; Klemmer et al., 2023), we will focus on exploring the locational embedding and
085
      thus not use those information. Third, similar to most previous deep learning-based methods, we
086
      use iNaturalist, a community-sourced dataset, which, as previously discussed, presents various
087   difficulties. Finally, we construct a single model for a large number of species simultaneously.
088
089   To address these issues, inspired by advances in explicit and hybrid representations, we propose a
090
      Hybrid Spatial Representation for Species Distribution Modeling. Specifically, our representation
      combines an implicit component based on FCNet (Mac Aodha et al., 2019) with an explicit compo-
091
      nent based on multiresolution hashgrids (Müller et al., 2022), forming a hybrid model well-suited
092
      for the SDM task. An intuitive view of these embeddings is shown in Figure 1.
093
094   Experiments show that our method achieves the best of both worlds, producing state-of-the-art re-
095   sults on this challenging task, outperforming both previously proposed methods and purely implicit
096
      or explicit versions of our model by significant margins. We also investigate the mechanisms be-
      hind the effectiveness of hybrid representations and characterize our model across a wide range of
097
      settings and evaluation methods for additional insights. Specifically, we show that hybrid represen-
098
      tations are well-suited for learning from presence-only observations and modeling a large number
099   of species simultaneously.

      2.2     I MPLICIT, E XPLICIT, AND H YBRID R EPRESENTATIONS
139
140   In fields such as signal processing, 3D vision, and computer graphics, Neural Implicit Representa-
141   tions (NIR) have achieved great results (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Mildenhall et al., 2021). A common
142   pattern in those fields is that the success of implicit representations was often followed by the ap-
143   pearance of explicit ones which offer advantages such as increased accuracy and efficiency (Chen
144   et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021). In practice, hybrid representations that combine both
145   implicit and explicit schemes often achieve superior performance by capturing the strengths of of
146
      both approaches.
147   While some previous works in the context of global spatial encoding have used hybrid or explicit
148   representations (Kim et al., 2024; Mai et al., 2020; Rußwurm et al., 2023), to the best of our knowl-
149   edge, no works on SDM have done this. In this work, inspired by Müller et al. (2022), we design
150   an innovative two-dimensional multiresolution hashgrid as an explicit representation, specifically
151   suited to the task of SDM construction. This model generates explicit embeddings, which are ag-
152
      gregated with conventional implicit embeddings from an FCNet-inspired portion. The aggregated
      embeddings are then put through a regular neural network as results. We demonstrate the merits
153
      of explicit representations, and show that our hybrid representation outperforms both implicit and
154
      explicit schemes by combining their advantages.

159   Let P(·) : [−1, 1]n×2 → {0, 1}n×S be the ground-truth presence function, where S is the number
160   of species in the model, and for the coordinates (lat, lon) (regularized between -1 and 1), we have
161   P([lat, lon])i = 1 iff the species i is present at (lat, lon). Let X ∈ [−1, 1]N ×2 be a matrix of
      coordinates where observations have been performed, where N is the number of observation entries.

162
      Corresponding to the observations are species indices s ∈ [1, S]N , where P(xn )i = 1 if i = sn .
163   Here xn represents the nth row of X. While it is possible in practice that false presences occur
164   through misidentification or aberrant migration, we treat this as regular noise in the data rather
165   than a part of the problem formulation. Hence we need to construct a model f (·) : [−1, 1]n×2 →
166   {0, 1}n×S such that f (X) approximates P(X).
167
      This can be seen as a multiclass version of the positive-unlabeled learning problem, since observa-
168
      tions can be seen as presences for one species and unlabeled data points for all others. Du Plessis
169   et al. (2014) show that this is equivalent to a weighted positive-absence problem with the unlabeled
170   points as absences, which coincides with works in SDM that consider “pseudoabsences” (Barbet-
171   Massin et al., 2012). Works like Phillips et al. (2009) have used additional randomly sampled points
172   as unlabeled points for all species.
173
      Given random all-unlabeled pseudoabsences Z ∈ [−1, 1]N ×2 , the Assume Negative Full Loss as in
174
      Cole et al. (2023) can be written as follows:

      Usually h(·) has a simple structure, such as being a single linear layer, while g(X) is a more sophis-
188   ticated model. Hence the significance of the embedding g lies in encoding the coordinates in such
189   a manner that it would be easy for h to conduct the final mapping step. We call the architecture of
190   g the representation scheme. The current state-of-the-art is FCNet (Mac Aodha et al., 2019), which
191   makes use of a Linear-ReLU layer followed by four repetitions of Linear-ReLU-Dropout-Linear-
192   ReLU residual blocks.
193
      At the beginning of g there is also often a quick positional encoding of the coordinates. This is
194   because, per Sitzmann et al. (2020), implicit representations perform better when the inputs are of
195   high frequency. FCNet uses the wrap encoding (sin(πlon), cos(πlon), sin(πlat), cos(πlat)).

199   4     M ETHODS
200
201   4.1   M OTIVATION
202
203   We notice two insufficiencies in the implicit formulation above.
204
205
      Global Parameterization Since g is a neural network, in the back-propagation process, most pa-
      rameters have nonzero gradient steps. More intuitively, one can see the implication that each param-
206
      eter is equally capable of being associated with the Amazon Rainforest as with the Saharan Desert.
207
      In the process of training parameters gradually get implicitly mapped to different features, but there
208   is still no guarantee that the parameters can reliably describe local environmental information.
209
210   Low Signal Frequency In addition, since MLPs follow the Lipschitz constraint, intuitively main-
211   taining a degree of “smoothness,” they often struggle to describe high-frequency patterns. Indeed, a
212   lot of previous work in NIRs has focused on encoding the data to facilitate modeling. In our task,
213   this means that embeddings for nearby locations tend to be similar regardless of their characteris-
214   tics, which indicates an inability to describe local details. In practice this is very undesirable, since
215   many ecological boundaries (e.g., mountain ridges, wide rivers, and artificial constructs) cause sharp
      distinctions between ecosystems in physical proximity of each other.

      Principle for Explicit Embedding We propose a guiding principle which could simultaneously
232
      solve to problems above. We introduce explainable parameters which each correspond to only a spe-
233
      cific region on Earth. If a data point is not contained within the region associated with a parameter,

236   of associated parameters, the Lipschitz constraint no longer applies, and thus the output can have
237   arbitrarily high signal frequency.
238

239
      eters and geographical regions, which currently no one has considered. Hence, we introduce a new
240   explicit embedding scheme which suits our purposes.

      4.2   M ULTIRESOLUTION H ASHGRIDS FOR SDM S
243
244   We propose using a multiresolution hashgrid encoding scheme as an explicit representation for SDM
245   modeling. Specifically, we divide the Earth’s surface into multiple grids with different resolution
246   levels, and store trainable feature parameters associated with lattices of the grid in a hashtable.
247   Embedded features on any given point for each resolution level are then calculated via bilinear
248   interpolation. Finally, the output embedding is given by concatenating all hashed features from the
249   different layers. An intuitive depiction is shown in Figure 3.
250   The resolution of each layer follows a geometric sequence from a maximum resolution to a mini-
251   mum resolution (both of which are hyper-parameters). Specifically, given maximum and minimum
252   resolutions Rmax and Rmin , and a total of L layers, the resolution of layer l is calculated as follows:
253
                                                       l
254                                Rl = Rmin exp(         (log Rmax − log Rmin ))                                        (3)
                                                      L−1
255
256   The grids allow for our model to explicitly capture information regarding the local environment by
257   using the features as representation. Furthermore, the different resolutions allow for the description
258   of explicit interactions in between smaller grids, based on their mutual intersection with larger grids.
259   The hashgrid, meanwhile, ensures that the number of parameters created is not of overly large size.

266
      global information is still valuable, while this ability is weakened for an explicit embedding. Hence,
      we propose a method to combine the two, and further, to make the model tunable between purely
267
      explicit and implicit encoding schemes.

303   Our codebase was altered from Cole et al. (2023), and we use their version of the FCNet encoding

308   All experiments were conducted on a single GPU: either an RTX A4000 (16GB) or a GeForce RTX
309   3090 (24GB). We run our models for 10 epochs each across learning rates 0.01, 0.003, 0.001, 0.0003,
310   and 0.0001, and report the best results here. For most of the experiments below we also ran models
311   across 9 implicitness settings from 0.0 to 1.0 with a step size of 0.125 but may have only reported
312   some of the results here for clarity of presentation –– for the full results, results under suboptimal
313   learning rates, and raw data from graphs, please refer to Appendix A. For models with implicitness
314
      smaller than 0.5 we use M = 16, while for models with implicitness larger than 0.5 we use M = 8.
315   The dataset used was iNaturalist, which is a popular choice for benchmarking SDMs and has been
316   used in multiple previous works (Mac Aodha et al., 2019; Cole et al., 2023; Rußwurm et al., 2023).
317   We use standardized settings introduced by Cole et al. (2023) in order to ensure fair comparison,
318   and the specific version of the iNaturalist dataset we used was the same as theirs as well. To deal
319   with class imbalance, as well as to condition on the amount of data provided, we sample 10, 100, or

      1000 observations per species from the dataset, referred to below as the “Observation Cap” or “Obs.
      Cap” for short. The data, and thus the model, covers a total of 47375 species.
321
322   It should be noted that the purely implicit version of our model, which uses FCNet (Mac Aodha et al.,
323   2019), is architecturally equivalent to SINR (Cole et al., 2023), and differences between results from

407   ference under the same settings on the S&T benchmark. We found that all standard deviations for
408   the training time are within 2% times the mean, suggesting that the results have high statistical sig-
409   nificance, so we omit reporting them and just report the means here. Results are shown in Figures 5
410   and 6.
411   As shown, we see that when implicitness is less than 0.5 (the model leans explicit), there seems to
412   be a training overhead of up to around 47% times the implicit model, presumably due to the larger
413   number of features per level. However, for models with implicitless greater than or equal to 0.5 (the
414   model leans implicit), there is no overhead compared to the implicit model. Hence, using our hybrid
415   model for better results does not require incurring sacrifices in speed. Meanwhile, no statistically
416   significant difference between the models was observed for inference time.
417
418   Comparison with Larger Implicit Networks In all of the experiments above, the dimension of
419   the location embedding is 256 as in SINR. One may wonder whether simply increasing the di-
420   mension of this feature embedding would allow the resulting “fat” implicit model to achieve better
421   results than our explicit representation. We conduct experiments to show that this is not the case:
422   while marginal performance gains can be achieved, they come at a very heavy cost for training and
423
      inference speed, and still cannot exceed results of our hybrid model. Results are reported in Table 3.

512   6   C ONCLUSION
513
514
      Our work explores the application of explicit and hybrid representations to the task of SDM con-
515   struction. We introduce an innovative explicit representation scheme, and use it in conjunction with
516   conventional implicit methods to form a hybrid representation. Experiments show that our hybrid
517   models consistently achieve state-of-the-art accuracy on multiple standard benchmarks, outperform-
518   ing both implicit and explicit models, and that our explicit representations are good at representing
519   local environmental information. We also conducted extensive experiments to characterize our mod-
520   els and investigate their properties.
521   As supported by our experiments, we conclude that the hybrid representation is well- suited to our
522   problem formulation because:
523
524         • Presence-Only Data: The hybrid model presents informative embeddings that have both
525           low noise and high signal frequency, thus allowing accurate and precise inference based on
526           presence-only data.
527
            • No Additional Environmental Information: The explicit portion of the model can boot-
528           strap environmental information from the species presences, thus providing a proxy for
529           environmental data.
530
531
            • Community-Sourced Dataset: Keeping the implicit portion allows for reduction of noise
              and bias from the dataset, preventing the explicit portion from overfitting to inaccuracies.
532
533         • Large Number of Species: As the number of species increases, the hybrid representa-
534           tion consistently gains performance, while the explicit representation (although prone to
535           overfitting) becomes better at inferring environmental information.
536
537   Limitations Since our work concentrates on effective representations for the locational embedding
538   portion of SDMs, we did not incorporate extra components such as remote sensing data, presence-
539   absence data, or environmental data. We consider those extensions to our model interesting direc-
      tions for future research.
