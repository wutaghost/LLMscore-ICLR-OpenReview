009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We address the challenge of offline reinforcement learning using realistic data,
012            specifically non-expert data collected through sub-optimal behavior policies. A
013            primary concern is that the learned policy must be conservative enough to manage
014            distribution shift while maintaining sufficient flexibility for generalization. To
015            tackle this issue, we introduce a novel method called Outcome-Driven Action Flex-
016
               ibility (ODAF), which seeks to reduce reliance on the empirical action distribution
               of the behavior policy. Specifically, we develop a new reward mechanism that
017
               evaluates whether the subsequent states, following the current policy, meet speci-
018
               fied performance requirements (e.g., safety—remaining within the state support
019            area), rather than solely depending on the characteristics of the actions taken (e.g.,
020            whether the action imitates the behavior policy). Besides theoretical justification,
021            we provide empirical evidence on widely used D4RL benchmarks, demonstrating
022            that our ODAF method, implemented using uncertainty quantification techniques,
023            effectively tolerates unseen transitions for improved "trajectory stitching," while
024            enhancing the agent’s ability to learn from realistic non-expert data.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Offline reinforcement learning (RL) aims to learn a high-capacity policy from an offline dataset
029
      previously collected via a behavior policy (Zhang & Tan, 2024), which has yielded significant
030   improvements in various fields, including robotics tasks (Mnih et al., 2015; Peng et al., 2017), game
031   playing (Silver et al., 2017), and large language models (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023).
032   However, prior studies (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2020) have indicated that offline RL
033   algorithms suffered from the distributional shift (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Jin et al., 2021b) problem,
034   where the divergence between the new and behavior policies makes the agent encounter with some
035   unseen actions or states, which are challenging for generalization.
036   In practice, obtaining ideal expert data is often challenging, and the most realistic data used for
037   training is generated through sub-optimal behavior policies. The difficulty of addressing distributional
038   shift becomes more pronounced when learning from realistic non-expert data, as blindly cloning these
039   potentially highly sub-optimal behaviors can be dangerous. Unfortunately, many previous works,
040   such as Behavior Regularized Actor-Critic (BRAC) (Wu et al., 2019b), Conservative Q-Learning
041   (CQL) (Kumar et al., 2020), and TD3+BC (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021), focus on cloning expert behaviors
042   and may be adversely affected by the sub-optimal behaviors present in the dataset (Bai et al., 2022).
043   While more recent action-based support set approaches, such as Bootstrapping Error Accumulation
044   Reduction (BEAR) (Wu et al., 2019a) and Supported Policy Optimization (SPOT) (Wu et al., 2022),
045   attempt to relax cloning conditions through supported regularization, they still face the challenge of
046   being overly restrictive when learning from non-expert offline data. Specifically, they may reject all
047   actions not taken by the behavior policy (i.e., Out of Distribution, OOD actions), including those that,
048   while potentially unsafe, could lead to in-distribution safe states.
049   In this paper, we proposed a new method to address the above issues. Our key idea is to design
050   a reward mechanism based on whether the subsequent states by following the current policy are
051   beneficial to improve the performance (e.g., being safe - falling within the state support area), instead
052   of explicitly restricting the range of action space for each state. In other words, in our method, the
053   OOD actions are allowed as long as they are safe and are beneficial to performance improvement.
      In this way, our approach is not only more conducive to shaping the desired behavior but also

091   effectiveness of both methods under various settings. Finally, the paper concludes with a summary.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
095
096
      Action-supported offline RL. Action-supported regularization plays a pivotal role in offline RL,
      striking a balance between conservatism and the ability of the learned policy to stitch trajectories.
097
      The Bootstrapping Error Accumulation Reduction (BEAR) (Wu et al., 2019a) method pre-trains an
098
      empirical behavior policy and regulates the divergence within a relaxation factor of the new policy.
099   Supported Policy Optimization (SPOT) (Wu et al., 2022) takes a different approach by explicitly
100   estimating the behavior policy’s density using a high-capacity Conditional VAE (CVAE) (Kingma
101   & Welling, 2014) architecture. The most recent advancement in this field is Supported Value
102   Regularization (SVR) (Mao et al., 2023), which simplifies action-supported regularization by only
103   requiring an estimation of the behavior policy’s action visitation frequency, significantly reducing
104   estimation errors and enhancing robustness. However, action-supported regularization would be too
105   restrictive in avoiding all unseen actions, even those with safe consequences and are worth exploring.
106
107   State recovery-based offline RL. As the relaxation of action-constraint, state recovery-based
      methods like State Deviation Correction (SDC) (Zhang et al., 2022) align the transitioned distributions


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of the new policy and the behavior policy, forming a robust transition to avoid the OOD consequences.
109   To further avoid the explicit estimation of consequences in high-dimensional state space, Out-
110   of-sample Situation Recovery (OSR) (Jiang et al., 2023) introduces an inverse dynamics model
111   (IDM) (Allen et al., 2021) to consider the consequential knowledge in an implicit way when decision
112   making. In this paper, we also consider them as the consequence-driven methods that implicitly avoid
113   the state distributional shift problem via aligning the transitioned distribution of the new policy with
114   that of the behavior policy. But such methods would hinder their ability of trajectory stitching and
115   generalization on non-expert data.
116
117   Trajectory stitching in offline RL. Recently, Model-based Return-conditioned Supervised Learn-
118   ing (MBRCSL) (Zhou et al., 2023) is proposed to equip the agent with trajectory stitching ability.
119
      Although this method has achieved great improvement in certain scenarios, demonstrating the impor-
      tance of trajectory stitching, it needs a large number of rollouts with the pre-trained model to correct
120
      the sub-optimal data distribution of the dataset, accumulating the model error. This motivates us to
121
      propose the ODAF method to achieve the trajectory stitching ability via only policy constraint.

497   approximations U π (s′ ). Both components are integrated into the ODAF term in Eq. (11), which
498   evaluates the safety of the outcome resulting from a given action. To assess the effectiveness of the

503   either the true dynamics model (TDM) or our pre-trained dynamics model (PDM) to predict the next
504   states of these actions and assess their safety as score(s, a) = EP̂ (s′ |s,a) U π (s′ ).

522   6     C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we propose a novel method called Outcome-Driven Action Flexibility (ODAF) to
525   trade-off the conservatism and generalization when learning from non-expert data in offline RL.
526   In particular, ODAF liberates the agent from the shackles of non-expert data in a consequence-
527   driven manner - it implicitly avoids the agent suffering from distributional shift via controlling its
528   consequences within in-distributional (safe) regions, while preserving its ability of trajectory stitching,
529
      which is critical for achieving superior performance from non-expert demonstrations. We provide two
      ways to implement the proposed idea. Theoretical and experimental results validate the effectiveness
530
      and feasibility of ODAF. We believe that the problem addressed in this work and the proposed method
531
      hold promise for practical applications of offline RL.

1090
1091   Just like the methods based on the traditional state recovery principle, the proposed ODAF is also
1092   unable to generalize to those states that are quite far away from the offline dataset, where any action
1093   executed would not lead to any low-uncertainty state. In this situation, the ODAF term would not
1094
       embed any useful information for the new policy, because all the forward consequences have high
       uncertainty, which make such guidance degrade to a random-walk. Exploring the performance
1095
       boundary of ODAF is also a major direction for our future work.
