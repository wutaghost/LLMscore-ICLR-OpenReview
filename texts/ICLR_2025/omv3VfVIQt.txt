011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite their extensive applications, large language models trained on vast histor-
014            ical datasets still struggle with hallucination issues, particularly when address-
015            ing open-ended, factual, and commonsense questions. In contrast, Retrieval-
016
               Augmented Generation (RAG) methods have proven effective in enhancing large
               language models’ responses to such inquiries, making them a focal point of re-
017
               search. However, previous RAG approaches overlook the lexical diversity of
018
               queries, hindering their ability to achieve a granular relevance assessment between
019            queries and retrieved documents, resulting in suboptimal performance. In this pa-
020            per, we introduce a Lexical Diversity-aware RAG (DRAG) model, comprising a
021            Diversity-sensitive Relevance Analyzer (DRA) and a Contrastive Relevance Cal-
022            ibration Module (CRC). Specifically, DRA decouples and assesses the relevance
023            of different query components (words, phrases) based on their levels of lexical
024            diversity, ensuring precise and comprehensive document retrieval. According to
025            the DRA assessment, CRC further emphasizes the pertinent knowledge of the re-
026            trieved relevant documents through contrastively eliminating the adverse effects
027
               of irrelevant contents. By integrating DRA and CRC, the proposed method effec-
               tively retrieves relevant documents and leverages their pertinent knowledge to re-
028
               fine the original results and generate meaningful outcomes. Extensive experiments
029
               on widely used benchmarks demonstrate the efficacy of our approach, yielding a
030            12.4% accuracy improvement on HotpotQA.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The rapid development of large language models (LLMs) has led to widespread deployment across
036   various fields, including conversational assistants (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Dubey
037   et al., 2024), medical diagnosis (Yang et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024), and code generation (Wei et al.,
038   2023; Luo et al., 2023). However, LLMs rely solely on their training parametric knowledge for
039   inference, which frequently results in issues such as factual hallucinations, outdated information,
040   and low interpretability (Mallen et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023), particularly in tasks
041   requiring open-domain knowledge or real-time information (Shuster et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023).
042   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020) has demonstrated significant effec-
043   tiveness in improving factual accuracy by integrating external retrieval knowledge to enhance LLM
044   generation. Typically, RAG methods first utilize a retriever to acquire potentially relevant documents
045   based on an input query in the retrieval stage, and subsequently extract key information from these
046   documents to augment LLMs in the generation stage. Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2023) trains LLM to
047   assess whether the retrieved documents are related to the input query to improve the retrieval valid-
048   ity. RA-DIT (Lin et al., 2023) fine-tunes both the LLM and the retriever, concurrently enhancing
049   their retrieval and generation performance. Methods such as RECOMP (Xu et al., 2024) and SuRe
050
      (Kim et al., 2024) summarize retrieved documents to extract key content to mitigate the impact of
      irrelevant text on the model during the generation stage.
051
052   However, previous RAG methods struggle to establish a granular relevance assessment between
053   queries and retrieval documents, leading to an under-utilization of external relevant knowledge. As
      shown in Figure 1, in the retrieval stage, existing RAG approaches assess the relevance of retrieved

083
      In this paper, we propose a Lexical Diversity-aware RAG (DRAG) deployed with a Diversity-
      sensitive Relevance Analyzer (DRA) and a Contrastive Relevance Calibration Module (CRC), ef-
084
      fectively harnessing the relevant external knowledge. To handle the lexical diversity, DRA intro-
085
      duces distinct relevance assess criteria for different query components, enhancing granular query-
086   document matching. We investigate the varied lexical diversity attributes and assessment mecha-
087   nism, and prompt the analyzer to achieve query decomposition and relevance evaluation. As the
088   degree of lexical diversity increases, the evaluation criteria become more flexible and detailed, en-
089   suring the accuracy and adequacy of the returned documents. To strengthen the granular relevant
090   knowledge, CRC minimizes the impact of irrelevant information contained in the retrieved docu-
091   ments through decoding adjustment. It first approximates the irrelevant information through con-
092   structing a noisy reference document based on the relevant assessment of DRA. The noisy reference
093   document that strongly interferes with the generation process, is then fed into the generation model,
094
      where the original generation result is further refined by eliminating the noisy reference decoding
      distribution. This contrastive calibration strategy could effectively avoid information omission from
095
      unsuitable reduction and the resource consumption associated with retraining. Thus, the proposed
096
      method could retrieve comprehensive relevant documents through the granular assessment criteria
097   and promote relevant knowledge aggregation for LLM generation.
098
099   To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed method, we perform extensive experiments on com-
100
      monly used open-domain question answering benchmarks. The experimental results demonstrate
      that our approach efficiently extracts retrieval content that is semantically aligned with the query,
101
      leading to significant improvements in the factual accuracy of RAG. Specifically, on HotpotQA, our
102
      method performs a 12.4% accuracy boost over the second-best method.

142   3      M ETHODOLOGY
143
144   We propose a Lexical Diversity-aware RAG (DRAG) framework, which incorporates a fine-grained
145   relevance evaluation mechanism across both the retrieval and generation stages to sufficiently lever-
146   age the relevant knowledge. DRAG consists of a Diversity-sensitive Relevance Analyzer (DRA)
147   and a Contrastive Relevance Calibration Module (CRC), designed to operate with minimal training
148
      resources. In this section, we first present the problem formalization and an overview of DRAG in
      3.1, followed by a detailed introduction to the DRA module in 3.2 and the CRC module in 3.3.
149

152

153   Domain Question Answering task. In the retrieval stage, given an input query x, the retriever returns
154   the top k retrieved documents D = {d1 , d2 , . . . , dk } based on embedding similarity. Advanced

156   relevant documents set Drel feeding into the generation model M. In the generation stage, the model
157   M is tasked to predict an answer y to the given query x based on Drel and its parametric knowledge.

185   3.2   D IVERSITY-S ENSITIVE R ELEVANCE A NALYZER
186
187
      Existing approaches, which apply a single relevance criterion, fail to capture the granular relevance
      between queries and retrieved documents, disregarding the effects of lexical diversity. Therefore,
188
      we propose the Diversity-sensitive Relevance Analyzer (DRA), which decouples the relevance as-
189
      sessment process to accommodate varying degrees of lexical diversity among query components.
190   DRA decomposes the query into distinct components and diversely evaluates the intrinsic relevance
191   between each component and the retrieved documents, employing tailored criteria according to the
192   extent of diversity. This enables a more accurate and fine-grained relevance assessment between the
193   retrieved documents and the full query.
194
195   Lexical Diversity-Driven Query Decoupling. To perform granular relevance assessment, We first
196   explore the different attributes of lexical diversity and the corresponding assessment mechanisms,
197   guiding the analyzer to perform query decomposition. Specifically, we categorize the query compo-
198   nents into three attributes: A = {< Invariant >, < V ariant >, < Supplementary >}, taking
199   the query “What is Portland the capital of?” as an example:
200
201
             • < Invariant >: Components without lexical diversity that are directly extracted from
               the query. The invariant component of the example query is “Portland”, whose expression
202
               cannot be altered.
203
204          • < V ariant >: Components with lexical diversity that are directly extracted from the
205
               query. The variant component of the example query is “Capital”, which could be expressed
               with synonyms such as “Administrative center” or “Seat of government”.
206
207          • < Supplementary >: Components not explicitly mentioned in the query but can be rea-
208            sonably inferred to supplement and enhance relevance assessment, which is not mandatory
209            and demonstrates a significant degree of lexical diversity. A possible supplementary com-
210
               ponent of the example query is “State or country”, which can be inferred from the query
               and is beneficial for relevance assessment.
211
212

213   ponents c = {< c1 , a1 >, < c2 , a2 >, . . . , < cn , an >} and assign the attribute aj ∈ A to each
214   component cj . n represents the total number of elements, which is determined by R:

248   3.3   C ONTRASTIVE R ELEVANCE C ALIBRATION
249
250   Existing methods attempt to eliminate irrelevant text from retrieved documents through reductive
251   summarization, but they typically lead to the omission of critical information due to imprecise reduc-
252
      tion. Therefore, we propose a Contrastive Relevance Calibration (CRC). Inspired by the inference-
      time augmentation method contrastive decoding (Li et al., 2022), We calibrate the model’s decoding
253
      distribution by comparing it against a noise reference, rather than performing reduction on the input.
254
      This approach effectively minimizes the influence of granular noise while avoiding the omission of
255   important information.
256
257   Motivation. Usually the retrieved document di contains the relevant knowledge t+ and a signif-
258   icant amount of irrelevant noise text t− . Previous reduction-based RAG methods normally lower
259   the impact of the irrelevant noise and generate the final results through the reductive compression
260   reduction( ) as follows:
261

266   However, reductive compression often leads to the omission of critical information when granu-
267   lar relevance assessment is lacking, adversely affecting the accuracy of the output. Additionally,
268   training-based generation methods aim to introduce more relevant data for updating the parameter
269   θM , which can result in excessive resource consumption. Therefore, we come up with an efficient
      generation method inspired by constrastive decoding (Li et al., 2022).

528   6        C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we introduced Lexical Diversity-aware RAG, a retrieval-augmented generation frame-
531
      work designed to address the limitations of existing RAG methods by incorporating granular rele-
      vance assessment. The Diversity-sensitive Relevance Analyzer enhances the precision of retrieval
532
      by applying tailored criteria based on lexical diversity, while the Contrastive Relevance Calibration
533
      Module refines the generation process by mitigating the impact of irrelevant information through
534   contrastive calibration. Our approach effectively improves the retrieval of semantically aligned
535   documents and promotes the aggregation of relevant knowledge, leading to significant advance-
536   ments in LLM generation. The results from extensive evaluations on several open-domain question-
537   answering benchmarks validate the superiority of our method, demonstrating its potential to substan-
538   tially enhance factual accuracy in a wide range of applications. A potential limitation of our method
539   is its generalization to more complex generation tasks, such as multi-turn dialogue. In the future, we
      will further explore the application of Lexical Diversity-aware RAG across different domains.

      Contrastive Decoding. Contrastive decoding (Li et al., 2022) is a technique for enhancing open-
705
      ended text generation without requiring additional training, achieved by maximizing the difference
706   in log probabilities between an expert LLM and an amateur LLM. This method has demonstrated
707   strong performance across various domains, including reasoning (O’Brien & Lewis, 2023) and neu-
708   ral machine translation (Waldendorf et al., 2024). CAD (Shi et al., 2023a) employs pointwise mutual
709   information to adjust the output probability distribution, addressing conflicts between internal and
710   external knowledge within the model. Additionally, Chuang et al. (2023) propose a decoding strat-
711   egy that contrasts different layers of the same LLM to more effectively highlight factual knowledge
712   acquired during pre-training. In contrast to these contrastive decoding approaches, our Contrastive
713   Relevance Calibration refines the model’s generation by calibrating it against a constructed noise
714
      reference, reducing the impact of granular irrelevant text and enhancing the model’s ability to ag-
      gregate and utilize relevant knowledge.

718
719   Inference of DRA. The DRA module performs a granular relevance assessment on the documents
720   returned by the retriever during the retrieval to accurately identify the documents truly relevant to
721   the query. Specifically, in the retrieval stage, given a query x and several retrieved documents D
722   return by embedding-based retriever, we train the DRA module R based on a lightweight language
723   model to conduct a further assessment of the intrinsic relevance between each document di ∈ D and
724
      x. We first prompt the DRA module R with the instruction Idec to decouple the query x into various
      components C = {< c1 , a1 >, < c2 , a2 >, . . . , < cn , an >}. Each component cj is attributed
725
      as aj based on its lexical diversity and extraction strategy during decoupling. We predefined three
726
      different component attributes and established progressive assessment criteria based on their lexical
727   diversity. Then we prompt the DRA module with instruction Isco to analyze the relevance of the
728   retrieval document di ∈ D and each component cj and assign a relevance score sij . All component
729   scores are integrated to accurately characterize the overall relevance of the document di to the entire
730   query as si . We then sort documents and sent the top r retrieved documents Drel = {d’1 , d’2 , . . . , d’r }
731   according to their relevance to model M for enhancing generation.
732
733   Evaluation Criteria. For the evaluation criteria σ1 , a score of 1 is assigned if the document ex-
734   plicitly matches the extracted invariant component; otherwise, it is set to 0. As for the evaluation
735   criteria σ2 , the score is a continuous value between [0,1], reflecting the document’s relevance to both
736   the variant and supplementary components of the query. A higher score indicates stronger relevance
737   to the variant component, while the supplementary component is evaluated more leniently, requiring
738
      only partial relevance to achieve a score within the same range.
739
740   Data Collection for DRA. The instance (i, o) of the DRA training data consists of two different
741
      types: (1) Query decomposition data. The input of DRA i is the query x and the instruction Idec
      (“You are an assistant in extracting key components from a given question.”), the output o is the
742
      decoupled components set C extracted based on x; (2) relevance assessment data. The input i is
743
      a combination of the query x, the retrieved document d, the decoupled components set C, and the
744   output o is an analysis of the relevance between the retrieved document d and each component in
745   C, including both match scores and explanations. Following the approach of works (Asai et al.,
746   2023), we utilize the state-of-the-art LLM GPT-4 to generate training data for both the components
747   set construction and the relevance assessment processes. Specifically, we prompt GPT-4 with type-
748   specific instructions followed by few-shot demonstrations of the original task input x to generate
749   the decoupling components set C. Next, we prompt GPT-4 with instructions followed by few-shot
750   demonstrations of the original task input x, the generated components set C, and the retrieved doc-
751   uments D to predict the semantic matching analysis. Manual evaluation indicates that GPT-4’s pre-
752
      dictions align well with human assessments. We collected a total of 1200 instances for decoupling
      components set construction data and 5543 instances for progressive relevance assessment data to
753
      form the supervised training dataset for the analyzer.
