009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Data acquisition in array signal processing (ASP) is costly because achieving high
012            angular and range resolutions necessitates large antenna apertures and wide fre-
013            quency bandwidths, respectively. The data requirements for ASP problems grow
014            multiplicatively with the number of viewpoints and frequencies, significantly in-
015            creasing the burden of data collection, even for simulation. Implicit Neural Rep-
016
               resentations (INRs) — neural network-based models of 3D objects and scenes
               — offer compact and continuous representations with minimal radar data. They
017
               can interpolate to unseen viewpoints and potentially address the sampling cost
018
               in ASP problems. In this work, we select Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) as a
019            case from ASP and propose the Radon Implicit Field Transform (RIFT). RIFT
020            consists of two components: a classical forward model for radar (Generalized
021            Radon Transform, GRT), and an INR based scene representation learned from
022            radar signals. This method can be extended to other ASP problems by replacing
023            the GRT with appropriate algorithms corresponding to different data modalities.
024            In our experiments, we first synthesize radar data using the GRT. We then train
025            the INR model on this synthetic data by minimizing the reconstruction error of
026            the radar signal. After training, we render the scene using the trained INR and
027
               evaluate our scene representation against the ground truth scene. Due to the lack
               of existing benchmarks, we introduce two main new error metrics: phase-Root
028
               Mean Square Error (p-RMSE) for radar signal interpolation, and magnitude-
029
               Structural Similarity Index Measure (m-SSIM) for scene reconstruction. These
030            metrics adapt traditional error measures to account for the complex nature of radar
031            signals. Compared to traditional scene models in radar signal processing, with
032            only 10% data footprint, our RIFT model achieves up to 188% improvement in
033            scene reconstruction. Using the same amount of data, RIFT is up to 3× better at
034            reconstruction and shows a 10% improvement generalizing to unseen viewpoints.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Array signal processing (ASP) is a subdomain of digital signal processing (DSP) which involves
      multiple spatially distributed sensors (Swindlehurst et al., 2014). For some ASP problems – in
040
      particular, for imaging and detection problems – the cost of data acquisition is often high because of
041
      the relationship between resolution requirements, aperture size, and signal bandwidth. The angular
042   resolution achieved by an array is a direct consequence of the aperture size, and the range resolution
043   depends on the total bandwidth of the received signal (Richards, 2022; Moccia & Renga, 2011; Liu
044   et al., 2021). The data acquisition cost grows linearly with each the number of samples, the aperture
045   size (assuming antennas are Nyquist-spaced), and the number of frequency bins. In this work, we
046   use radar imaging as a representative of ASP problems and address the cost of data acquisition with
047   a deep learning model. Such combinations of machine learning and radar signal processing have
048   been used in autonomous vehicles (Bilik et al., 2019), robotics (Ali et al., 2014), and geographic
049   information systems (Javali et al., 2021).
050   Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) is a specialized radar imaging technique that synthesizes a large
051   virtual antenna aperture by moving the sensor relative to the scene (Moreira et al., 2013). This pro-
052   cess involves the coherent processing of successive radar echoes received at multiple points along
053   the sensor path to reconstruct a high-resolution image. Most often, the radar samples are uniformly
      spaced throughout the synthetic aperture, so the size of the aperture determines the amount of view-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      point samples radar takes. In order to give a concrete example of the amount of data needed in SAR
055   imaging, according to NASA, for a satellite with C-band radar, to get a spatial resolution of 10 m,
056   the synthetic radar aperture size needs to be of the size of 47 soccer fields (∼ 5km) (NASA, 2023).
057   The resulting amount of data can be on the order of terabytes.
058
      A potential remedy to the cost of data acquisition for SAR imaging is learning based reconstruction
059
      of objects and scenes. One example is Implicit Neural Representations (INR), which involves a
060
      neural network learning scene properties1 (colors, opacity, and so on) through measurement signals
061   like pictures. The prospect of INR interpolating between different view points, particularly for
062   visual data, is accomplished with different scene representation mechanisms, e.g., voxels (Choy
063   et al., 2016), point clouds (Achlioptas et al., 2018), meshes (Kanazawa et al., 2018) and especially
064   the occupancy network by Mescheder et al. (2019). More recently, Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)
065   by Mildenhall et al. (2020) integrates a physical process called light field rendering from Levoy &
066   Hanrahan (1996) to improve model performance. NeRF sparked works illustrate that the integration
067   of underlying physical mechanisms enables better learning and scene representations.
068   In this study, we integrate deep learning methods with a traditional forward model for radar sig-
069   nals called the Generalized Radon Transform (GRT) (Nolan & Cheney, 2002; Monga et al., 2018).
070   Analogous to the light marching used in NeRF, the GRT is the physical mechanism integrated in the
071   rendering process for radar, so the model can learn scene reconstruction directly from the observed
072   radar signals. We denote our architecture as Radon Implicit Field Transform (RIFT).
073   The main contributions of this work are as follows:
074
075              • We present the first method to learn implicit scene representations directly from radar
076                signals.
077
                 • Using our method, we achieve better scene reconstruction and viewpoint interpolation with
078                fewer measurements than traditional algorithms.
079
080
                 • We formulate the first joint benchmark for both radar scene reconstruction and signal inter-
                   polation which aligns with perceived quality.

085   2.1       A RRAY S IGNAL P ROCESSING AND (S YNTHETIC A PERTURE ) R ADAR
086
087   Array signal processing (ASP) generally refers to the use of two or more antennas for coherent
088
      processing. ASP is a fundamental technique and has diverse applications in radar, sonar, and com-
      munications. The use of multiple transmitters or receivers enhances overall system performance by
089
      increasing gain, enabling beamforming, providing spatial filtering, and increasing signal-to-noise ra-
090
      tio (SNR) (Van Veen & Buckley, 1988). As wireless spectrum becomes more crowded and systems
091   evolve to utilize higher frequency bands (Berger, 2014) – e.g., in 5G networks – the use of larger
092   and more sophisticated antenna arrays has become crucial for achieving the precise beamforming
093   necessary for efficient communication.
094
      The basic principle of ASP in the narrowband setting involves adjusting the phase and amplitude of
095
      signals received by (or transmitted from) each element in the array. For signals that originate far from
096
      the antenna array, the spherical wavefront impinging on the antenna array appears locally as a plane
097   wave. Coherently summing signals from any given direction can be accomplished by weighting the
098   signal received at each element and adding up the signals over the array. The weights are simple
099   phase shifts which depend on the array geometry (e.g., linear, planar, circular, etc.) and the direction-
100   of-arrival (DoA) of the incoming signal. The phase adjustment allows for constructive interference
101   in desired directions and destructive interference in others, effectively shaping the radiation pattern
102   of the array. DoA estimation considers the signal angle as an unknown and tries to find the angle
103   which best explains an observed signal.
104   Synthetic aperture radar (SAR) is related to DoA estimation in that goal is to sense an unknown
105   environment. There are two important modifications, however. First, DoA estimation assumes that
106   the signals exist in space and are being passively observed by the array. On the other hand, SAR
107

      techniques use a transmit antenna to excite the scene and observe reflections. A second modification
109   that distinguishes SAR from conventional radar is the motion of the antenna array relative to the
110   scene. As the array is moving, pulses are repeatedly transmitted, and reflections are stored at a va-
111   riety of viewpoints. SAR processing takes these measurements and uses array position information
112   to form a large “synthetic” aperture. Even with a small antenna array, the path traced by the antenna
113   can be orders of magnitude larger, leading to greatly improved imaging capabilities (Moreira et al.,
114   2013).

128   nition. We define the GRT operator F such that the perceived radar signal d(ω, s) can be expressed

142   2.3   I MPLICIT N EURAL R EPRESENTATION
143
144
      INR is a class of methods that learn a scene or an object through parameterized signals, providing
      a continuous interpolation that maps the signal to its domain. Compared to traditional grid-based
145
      representations, INR is more compact, as the spatial resolution in grid-based methods is inherently
146
      tied to the grid’s granularity.
147
148   Following the integration of light marching by NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020), there is a resurgence
149   of research into INR trained on visual data to demonstrate concrete improvement of speed in training
150
      (Garbin et al., 2021), accuracy (Barron et al., 2021), and generalizability across viewpoints (Barron
      et al., 2022). The above works demonstrated data efficiency, training speed and reconstruction
151
      accuracy and set cornerstones to our work.

154   3     M ETHODS AND E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
155
156   In this section, we present the relevant details of the GRT, the design of RIFT, and the error metrics
157   we customize for radar data modality. The INR from RIFT takes as input the location in the scene
158   and returns the complex reflectivity of the scene at that point. Upon receiving the reflectivity estimate
159   from the INR, the GRT directly produces the radar signal at different viewpoints. The predicted
160   signal is compared to the signal corresponding to the ground truth scene (referred to as ”true signal”
161   in this work), and automatic differentiation with gradient descent accumulates gradients to update
      the estimation of the scene.

163
164   Without loss of generality, in this research, we normalize all the magnitude of radar signal S to [0, 1].
165   To accurately simulate real-world scenarios, we use the bistatic radar configuration mentioned in
166   Section 2.2 In the case, the transmitter (TX) and receiver (RX) are spatially separated at each time
167   step. Further details of the radar setup are provided in Appendix B.1.
168   The combination of the INR learning ρ(x) and the GRT generating the radar signal constitutes the
169   inverse problem relative to the forward signal generation. We define the granularity as the distance
170   between neighboring voxels along the coordinate axes. The granularity is only a parameter for
171   scene discretization. Unless otherwise noted, the scene extends a 3D cubical space with edges of
172   10m. The granularity of the forward problem to 0.2m, and that of the inverse problem to 0.4m. This
173   twofold difference in granularity between the forward and inverse problems is make the RIFT more
174   compact.
175   It is important to note that the dependence of the matrix A on x makes our assumption of no loss of
176   generality nontrivial. To address this x dependence, we consider two distinct cases: the near-field
177   approximation and the far-field approximation. However, since this pertains to an ASP problem
178   and an extensive discussion would be tangential to the primary objective of developing a neural
179   radar reconstruction algorithm, we defer the justification of the normalization and the difference in
180   approaches for near and far-field approximations to Appendix B.3.

249   up for significant variance in the magnitude of radar signal causing the model to learn only a small

266   acquisition costs in SAR systems. RIFT offers two primary solutions. The first is that by recon-
267   structing the scene using fewer radar measurements, RIFT lowers the demand for extensive data
268   collection. This is particularly beneficial in scenarios where data acquisition is time-consuming
269   or resource-intensive. The effectiveness of scene reconstruction is evaluated using error metrics
      detailed in Section 3.3, which compare the ground truth scene reflectivity with the reconstructed

415   of each bar are 2.4m in length, 0.72m in width, and 0.48 m in height. In order to mimic the practical
416
      scenarios, instead of generating signal from a hypothetical sphere surrounding the scene, we limit
      the azimuth and elevation angle samples to 41 and 21 samples on [0.1π, 0.3π], respectively.
417
418   The bar on the negative x-side is assigned a reflectivity of 1.0, while the bar on the positive x-side is
419   assigned reflectivity of 1.0, 0.5, 0.333, and 0.25 in four separate experiments. Both the RIFT model
420   and the least squares model use 500 input data points. All other training setups are identical to those
421   in the experiments described in Section 4.1. Figure 5 presents the resulting reconstructions of the
422
      scene.
423   From Figure 5(a)–(d), we observe that in far-field simulations, the least squares baseline models
424   are unable to resolve the two reflectors, regardless of differences in their reflectivities. This out-
425   come corresponds to the Weak Target Detection (WTD) problem, where radar systems encountering
426   such scenarios can only identify a general area of reflectivity or may even ignore the weaker object
427   entirely.
428   In contrast, the RIFT model provides sufficient expressiveness to resolve the two reflectors, even
429   when there is a fourfold difference in reflectivity between them. Although the reconstructed re-
430   flectivity of the weaker object is diminished, its accurate localization demonstrates the value of
431   integrating the physical process into the Implicit Neural Representation (INR) in different modali-
      ties.

490   5   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
491
492   In this paper, we introduced the Radon Implicit Field Transform (RIFT) workflow, which integrates
493
      an INR with a traditional forward model for radar signals to reconstruct scenes only from radar data
      with no exposure to the real scene structure (as compared to (Borts et al., 2024)). Compared to
494
      traditional inverse models, RIFT achieves superior scene reconstruction across all experiments and
495
      enhances interpolation of unseen viewpoints in certain cases, all while utilizing significantly less
496   data. These results indicate that RIFT effectively addresses the high cost of data acquisition in SAR
497   problems by reconstructing scenes with reduced data requirements.
498
499
      To assess the performance of RIFT-type models, we introduced customized error metrics for recon-
      struction and unseen viewpoint interpolation. The m-SSIM empirically aligns with our visual eval-
500
      uations. However, since RIFT employs a neural network to model scene properties—in contrast to
501
      the Kaczmarz-based least square inversion of the forward radar model with well-established conver-
502   gence properties—it may experience numerical stability issues. Consequently, there is one instance
503   where the RIFT model underperforms in unseen viewpoint interpolation. As illustrated in Figure
504   7(g), the RIFT model occasionally fails due to convergence to local minima during optimization
505   or vanishing gradients, highlighting the need for further investigation and customized optimization
506   methods. Additionally, data preprocessing techniques like normalization and standardization are not
507   adequate for radar signals because the magnitude after normalization of the 2m cube can differ by
508   orders of magnitude thus undermines the numerical stability.
509   To fully realize the potential of RIFT, we require datasets of real-world scenes and corresponding
510   radar signals. We reproduce occupancy of simple objects from (Ans, 2024), but further investigation
511   of reconstruction from complicated scene is necessary. We acknowledge the necessity of contin-
512   ued research into RIFT models to bridge them with real-world radar sensing applications, such as
513   compact high-resolution mapping for autonomous vehicles or robotic navigation.

      B.4   M ODEL S TRUCTURE
952
953   The two configurations of the RIFT models share the same structure shown in Figure 10. Both
954   models have 3 dimensional input of the radar array center position γradar . The output of the models
955   are 2 dimensional, which are real and imaginary part of learned scene reflectivity ρ̂(x). The hidden
956   size of all hidden layers are 64.
957
      The primary difference between the RIFT(N) and RIFT(S) models arises from the definitions of their
958   units and nonlinearities. In the RIFT(N) models, each unit consists of a Linear layer, followed by a
959   nonlinearity σ, and then a LayerNorm layer. The nonlinearities σ and τ are the LeakyReLU func-
960   tion (Maas et al., 2013) and the hyperbolic tangent function, respectively. In contrast, the RIFT(S)
961   models have a simpler structure: each unit is a single Linear layer, and all nonlinearities σ and τ are
962   sine functions.
963
      As previously mentioned, the optimization process faces challenges due to the dynamic range of
964   radar signals. Among all the different multilayer perceptron (MLP) structures we experimented
965   with, the INR architectures used in RIFT(N) and RIFT(S) models were empirically found to perform
966   the best. These structures optimize effectively despite frequent vanishing gradients and convergence
967   to local minima.

1008

1009   cease after 500 epochs, with learning rate annealing (You et al., 2023) by halving every 100 epochs.

       B.6    F URTHER D ISCUSSION ON M ETRICS
1020
1021   The mathematical intuition behind separating SSIM for magnitude and phase stems from the for-
1022   mulation of the INR (see Section 3.2) and forward radar signal synthesis. In radar signal synthesis,
1023   we assign a reflectivity function ρ(x) to the scene, as discussed in Section 2.2. For simplicity, when
1024   the scene is not occupied by a particular object, the reflectivity is set to zero. The subset x0 , where
1025   ρ(x0 ) = 0, corresponds to regions of the scene without objects, while the subset x \ x0 represents
       the parts of the scene occupied by objects.


                                                                    19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       By comparing the geometry of the occupied regions x \ x0 with the ground truth geometry, we can
1027   assess how well the INR learns the scene, even at angles where reflections from the scene are weak.
1028   In all the experiments presented in this work, we select 100 unseen viewpoints and calculate the
1029   p-RMSE of all radar signals across these viewpoints.
1030
       The threshold Intersection-over-Union (tIoU) metric is inspired by the constant false alarm rate
1031
       (CFAR) used in radar signal processing. In radar imaging, persistent background noise is present
1032
       in radar signals, and various methods have been developed to reduce the influence of this constant
1033   false alarm (Schou et al., 2003; Hou et al., 2015). In this work, to address CFAR, we introduce a
1034   threshold to the magnitude of the learned reflectivity ρ̂(x) by assuming an apparent SNR, and discard
1035   all values below this threshold. We then calculate the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) to assess how
1036   much of the scene has been learned, especially in the reconstruction of a single object.
1037
       Due to the unavoidable noise level, tIoU values are often low because the presumed SNR cutoff
1038
       must be held constant when comparing reconstructions from different methods, and it cannot be
1039   optimal for all methods simultaneously. However, tIoU can be considered a robustness measure for
1040   the model, since under the same presumed threshold, a higher tIoU value indicates better alignment
1041   between the ground truth scene and the reconstructed scene. The less noise present in the model’s
1042   inference, the more of the inference surpasses the presumed SNR threshold, resulting in a higher
1043   tIoU.
1044
       For the calculation of tIoU and figure generation, we apply the thresholding of the presumed SNR at
1045   a fixed value for a fixed number of viewpoints. The thresholding only affects the results of tIoU and
1046   visualization. The m-SSIM calculations are conducted by slicing the 3D scene on a fixed 2D plane,
1047   and the result for a scene is the average m-SSIM of all slices.
1048
       For the unseen viewpoint interpolation, we measure the Mean Squared Error (MSE) of the phase of
1049
       the radar signal, which we denote as p-RMSE. We discard the magnitude in the MSE calculation
1050
       due to the nature of radar signals. Given a scene, reflections from certain viewpoints can differ by
1051   orders of magnitude if the reflectivity ρ(x) is anisotropic. To test the generalizability of the model,
1052   we aim to reduce the impact of the signal magnitude, focusing instead on the phase information,
1053   which is necessary for the correct coherent addition of radar signals in SAR imaging.
1054
