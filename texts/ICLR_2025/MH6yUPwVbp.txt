009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Several optimization problems seek a path of predetermined length in a state
012            space that minimizes a cost function. Conventionally, such problems are tackled
013            by dynamic programming (DP) applying a Bellman-type equation. A prominent
014            example is Viterbi decoding, which returns the path in a Hidden Markov Model
015            that best explains a series of observations, with applications from bioinformatics
016            to communication systems and speech recognition. However, DP-based solutions
017            (i) exhaustively explore a search space linear in both state space size and path length
018
               in time quadratic in state space size, without exploiting data characteristics, and
               (ii) require memory commensurate with that search space to reconstruct the optimal
019
               path. In this paper, we propose Isabella (Dijkstra-Bellman), a novel framework that
020
               finds optimal paths of predetermined length in time- and space-efficient fashion
021            by a combination of best-first-search, depth-first-search, and divide-and-conquer
022            strategies. The best-first-search component avoids the exhaustive exploration of
023            the search space using a priority queue; the depth-first-search component keeps the
024            size of that queue in check; and the divide-and-conquer component constructs the
025            optimal path recursively with low space complexity after determining its cost. We
026            apply Isabella to Viterbi decoding, introducing algorithms that prioritize the most
027            promising pathways and control memory consumption. To emphasize the generality
028            of Isabella, we also instantiate it with an algorithm for histogram construction.
029
               To our knowledge, no previous work addresses such problems with this novel
               combination of strategies. Our experimental evaluation shows our solutions to be
030
               highly time- and space-efficient compared to standard dynamic programming.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Several problems that call to find an optimal sequence of given length L over a state space of size n
036   are conventionally solved by dynamic programming (DP). Two prominent examples are Viterbi
037   decoding, i.e., finding a sequence of hidden states—a Viterbi path—in a Hidden Markov Model
038   (HMM) that best explains an observed event sequence (Viterbi, 1967; 2006) and finding a sequence
039   of bucket boundaries—a V-optimal histogram—over a value sequence that minimizes the aggregate
040   Euclidean error when assigning one representative to each bucket (Bellman, 1961; Jagadish et al.,
041   1998). As we show, the underlying formulation of the solution is the same.
042   Such DP algorithms use the solutions to all length-k subproblems to solve all length-k+1 subprob-
043   lems; to build the optimal sequence after reaching the target length L, one may follow one of two
044   strategies: (i) the memoization strategy retains and backtracks over processed subproblem solutions
045   per step, requiring Θ(nL) space and O(n2 L) time; (ii) the in-place strategy discards processed sub-
046   problem solutions and reruns the algorithm from scratch and on-demand backwards per step, hence
047   requires O(n) space but O(n2 L2 ) time. An algorithm recently proposed by Ciaperoni et al. (2022;
048   2024) constrains space to O(n) and runs in O(n2 L log L) time, but still evaluates all subproblems
049   exhaustively and indiscriminately, constraining its scalability to large problem instances. Still, in
050
      practice only a few subproblems aid the solution, while most are subpar building blocks; despite their
      long history, state-of-the-art DP algorithms fail to utilize this fact.
051
052   In this paper, we introduce Isabella (Dijkstra-Bellman), an algorithm design framework for the
053   space- and time-efficient optimization of predetermined-length sequences, such as Viterbi paths and
      V-optimal histograms. Isabella combines best-first search, depth-first search, and divide-and-conquer

      components. The best-first-search (BestFS) component prioritizes the most promising subproblems
055   to avoid exhaustively exploring the subproblem space and enhance time efficiency. We also use
056   bidirectional search and a bounding scheme for more effective subproblem prioritization. Of course
057   the applicability of BestFS to find an optimal-cost path of arbitrary length is straigthforward (Russell
058   & Norvig, 2010); however, its application to find an optimal-cost path of a predetermined length
059   raises additional space requirements for (i) tabulating the optimal cost per state and step to enable
060   backtracking of the optimal sequence and (ii) maintaining a correspondingly larger priority queue. To
061   ameliorate these memory requirements, we amend BestFS with a depth-first search (DFS) strategy that
062   prevents the priority queue from overextending and a divide-and-conquer (D&C) provision that omits
063
      tabulating all subproblem solutions. These innovations reduce both time and space requirements,
      while allowing one to control the ensuing tradeoff between time and memory demands via a tunable
064
      space budget parameter. To our best knowledge, no previous work tackles problems of this type as
065
      we do. We apply Isabella to Viterbi decoding (Viterbi, 1967) and V-optimal segmentation (Jagadish
066   et al., 1998) and evaluate our solutions over real and synthetic data, showing that they gain in both
067   runtime and memory vs. DP algorithms, especially under skewed distributions of path costs.

      2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
071
072
      Dynamic programming (DP) (Bellman, 1966) recursively decomposes a problem into subproblems,
      exploiting an optimal substructure property, by which a globally optimal solution combines locally
073
      optimal solutions. Nevertheless, its application is constrained by its computational requirements.
074
075   Best-First search (BestFS) (Pearl, 1984) repetitively expands a most promising partial solution to a
076   problem. Dijkstra’s algorithm (1959) finds a minimum-cost path of arbitrary length from a start to
077   an end node; in each iteration it explores, i.e., visits the neighbors of, the nearest unexplored visited
078   node, until it reaches the end node. This algorithm resembles a DP algorithm (Sniedovich, 2006), as
079   it exploits optimal substructures to expand its solution. Thus, DP and BestFS are closely intertwined.
080   We build upon this connection, applying BestFS to find an optimal-cost path of fixed length. We
081
      stress that, contrariwise to the Dijkstra algorithm, our task requires monitoring both path length and
      cost. A∗ (Foead et al., 2021) augments Dijkstra with a heuristic that prioritizes paths appearing closer
082
      to the end, while the search may proceed from both start and end by bidirectional search (Pohl, 1969).
083
084   Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) explain observation sequences. An HMM comprises a set of K
085   hidden states, each with probabilities to be an initial state, transition to other states, and emit an
086   observation. Decoding seeks a sequence of states most likely to generate a sequence of observations:
087   Problem 1 (Decoding). Given an HMM and a sequence of T observations Y = {y1 , y2 , . . . , yT },
088   find the sequence of hidden states Q = {s∗1 , s∗2 , . . . , s∗T } that maximizes the likelihood P (Q, Y ).
089
      The Viterbi algorithm (Viterbi, 1967; 2006) solves Problem 1 optimally by DP; it finds application
090
      from networking and telecommunications (Viterbi, 2006) to speech recognition (Gales & Young,
091   2007; Braun et al., 2020), where it serves to find the most probable transcription for an input acoustic
092   signal, or for forced alignment, the task of aligning orthographic transcriptions to audio recordings.
093   In modern speech-recognition systems, the Viterbi algorithm runs on the composition of several
094   small HMMs in which states represent words and their phonemes, to find the best transcription
095   of a spoken utterance. However, this algorithm raises high memory and runtime requirements.
096   A recent work (Jihyuk Jo, 2019) on HMM-based isolated word recognition employed a search
097   heuristic, without proving its correctness. Another recent work (Ciaperoni et al., 2022) enhances the
098   space efficiency of decoding at the cost of a runtime overhead. Other works reduce the state space
099
      representation for particular classes of HMMs (Siddiqi & Moore, 2005; Felzenszwalb et al., 2003).
      Still, the time complexity of the algorithm remains prohibitive for problem instances with large state
100
      space and long observation sequences. We aim to improve upon both the time and space efficiency
101
      of Viterbi decoding by provably correct best-first search, pruning, and bounding policies. This is a
102   difficult undertaking, as indicated by derived lower bounds (Backurs & Tzamos, 2017).
103

      associated bucket representatives x̂b that minimizes error function EI (HB ).

125   Isabella solves problems seeking a given-length sequence that optimizes a cost measure, which are
126   typically solved by dynamic programming, visiting subproblems in a fixed order, oblivious of the

128
      subproblems in a best-first fashion until a stopping condition is met. Figure 1 shows an example.

162
      The entailed solution may be implemented by either DP or token passing (Young et al., 1989); both
163   calculate all solutions of length ℓ, Opt(·, ℓ), before those of length ℓ+1, Opt(·, ℓ+1), by breadth-first
164   search. DP draws from solutions of length ℓ to build each solution of length ℓ + 1 by a pull approach.
165   Token passing broadcasts a token for each solution of length ℓ to its continuations of length ℓ + 1 by
166   a push approach. In both cases, solutions solidify at length ℓ before moving to length ℓ + 1.
167
      Isabella abolishes this breadth-first orientation in favor of a best-first one; it organizes sub-problem
168
      solutions (represented by tokens) in a priority queue Q in which it initially inserts all tokens (·, 1).
169   Thereafter, in each step, it selects the most promising token (j, ℓ) from Q and, for each eligible
170   successor (i, ℓ + 1) not already extracted from Q, it computes Opt(j, ℓ) + G(j, i) and updates the
171   priority of (i, ℓ + 1) in Q accordingly. Isabella resembles Dijkstra’s shortest-path algorithm (1959);
172   however, whereas Dijkstra minimizes a cost objective regardless of length (i.e., number of steps),
173   Isabella optimizes the objective under a fixed-length constraint. In the worst case, it examines all
174   sequence continuations for each length, hence takes O(nL(n + log nL)) time, where the log nL
175   term expresses the overhead of maintaining the priority queue, more compactly O(nL(n + log L)).
176   However, in practice, it gains performance as it quickly derives tokens corresponding to DP table
177
      cells without considering all possible paths and does not produce some tokens at all. As we will see
      in Section 4, this pruning capacity results in significant runtime savings, particularly in real problem
178
      instances. In the best case, Isabella may produce only L tokens, each in constant time.
179
180   Nevertheless, the priority queue Q may reach size Θ(nL), holding one token for each state-length
181   pair, while we need to keep all tokens in memory after popping them from Q to facilitate backtracking
182   the solution sequence. To contain this space demand, we eschew backtracking and instead reconstruct
183
      the solution by a divide-and-conquer strategy as in (Ciaperoni et al., 2022; 2024) that achieves O(n)
      space and O(n2 L log L) time; we keep the size of Q in O(n) by a depth-first-search strategy.
184
185   In our Isabella variants, we anticipate, by bounding, the values a sequence may achieve as it expands
186   and prioritize tokens according to those anticipatory values. We also derive bidirectional-search
187   variants that produce both prefixes and suffixes of sequences until they reach the target length.

222

223      yet Dijkstra finds a shortest path without a constraint, while a Viterbi path optimizes path likelihood

327   3.3   L INEAR - SPACE MINT (MINT-LS)
328
329
      The backtracking discussed in Section 3.2 keeps all tokens in memory even after they are popped
      from Q, incurring a O(KT ) space complexity. Instead, we reconstruct the solution by a divide-and-
330
      conquer strategy and keep the size of Q in O(K) by a depth-first-search strategy.
331
332   To avoid materializing paths, we adapt the logic of SIEVE-Middlepath (Ciaperoni et al., 2022)
333   to MINT. SIEVE-Middlepath reformulates the Viterbi algorithm by a divide-and-conquer strategy.
334   Instead of tabulating all subproblem solutions, it only maintains those for the most recent frame, which
335
      it uses to solve subproblems in the next frame; it records, with each solution, the edge at the middle
      frame of the solution path (the middle pair), which it identifies upon reaching that middle frame. After
336
      termination, it recursively reruns on the L/2-hop predecessors and successors of the solution middle
337
      pair to reconstruct the full path. Likewise, MINT-LS identifies a middle pair upon reaching a middle
338   frame and stores it in the solution token. After establishing the best token at the last frame, it reruns
339   among the predecessors and successors of that token middle pair to reconstruct the entire Viterbi path.
340   Middle pairs are retrieved in orderly fashion, as in an in-order tree traversal (Ciaperoni et al., 2022).
341   Eschewing path materialization requires O(K 2 T log T ) time, yet does not guarantee O(K) space, as
342   the size of Q may still grow to Θ(KT ). Next, we address the queue size.
343
      We keep the size of Q in check via a depth-first-search (DFS) strategy which is novel in this context.
344   When Q exceeds a predefined size threshold θ after we insert a token (sj , t), we pick the lowest-cost
345   token among the set of tokens for sj , S j = {(sj , ·)}, say (sj , t∗ ), and produce all its derivatives on
346   demand via a DFS traversal of the HMM graph G from sj . Each branch of DFS terminates when
347   it either reaches the last frame or injects a token into the token set of a state sj ′ that substitutes a
348   pre-existing token without memory increase. The paths DFS explores identify and pass on middle
349   pairs as usual. By virtue of this DFS operation, we maintain a priority queue of size O(K). In
350   practice, we let the priority queue size threshold θ grow slightly with T , as an increase of T without a
351   commensurate alteration of θ triggers more DFS calls and hence burdens runtime.
352   Algorithm 4 in Appendix E provides the pseudocode of MINT-LS, which invokes the DFS subroutine
353   of Algorithm 5 therein. MINT-LS propagates tokens like MINT does, yet it stores in each token (si , t)
354   its predecessor state and the currently valid middle pair for the path this token belongs to. These details
355   help identify the subproblems to be solved recursively. Upon reaching the final frame (and final state,
356   if given), it extracts the middle pair associated with the solution path and reruns recursively among Np -
357   hop predecessors of the middle pair in the Np frames preceding it and among Ns -hop successors
358   of the middle pair in the Ns frames following it, found by breadth-first search (Lines 15 and 22).
359
      Besides, MINT-LS eschews storing new tokens that would enlarge the queue beyond a threshold θ by
      invoking the DFS subroutine that only stores tokens replacing others, or at the last frame.

      Other TECH variants work by analogy to MINT variants. Bidirectional TECH applies forward and
433   backward BestFS in alternation. When we pop a pair (i, b) from Qf , we consider entries (j, b + 1)

533          5             C ONCLUSION
534         We introduced Isabella, a framework that efficiently solves problems of fixed-length path optimization
535         by best-first search, while delimiting its space complexity by depth-first search and a divide-and-
536         conquer strategy. We designed an Isabella-based algorithm for Viterbi decoding and one for histogram
537         construction, with bidirectional and bounding variants. Our experiments evince that Isabella gains up
538         to four orders of magnitude in time and space efficiency, the advantage being more pronounced on
539         real data with nonuniform path cost distributions. In Appendix D, we apply Isabella to temporal-graph
            community search.

      Proposition 1. Standard MINT is correct.
651
652
653   Proof. Let T be the set of all tokens (i.e., state-frame pairs) and V the set of visited tokens, for which
654
      optimal cost has been computed. Initially V includes the source s with optimal cost ps = − log Bs,y1 ,
      and −ps is its log-likelihood. In each iteration, we add to V a token (si , t) from T \ V with cost pi .
655
      To complete the induction, we must show that pi is optimal for its length t. First, if the true t-
656
      length-optimal path goes only through tokens in V, for which, by the inductive hypothesis, the
657   optimal cost is known, then pi must be t-length-optimal. Assume that the t-length-optimal path goes
658   through a token (sj , t′ ) not in V; this token necessarily has cost pj ≥ pi , hence any path through it is
659   suboptimal. Therefore, −pi is the maximum Viterbi path log-likelihood maxsi T[si , t].
660
661   Proposition 2. MINT Bound is correct.
662
663   Proof. We prove the statement by contradiction. Assume the algorithm returns a token (s∗ , T ) with
664   cost c∗ > maxsi T[si , T ]. Then there must be an unvisited token (s′ , t′ ), which, if propagated to
665   the last frame, produces a path of likelihood maxsi T[si , T ]. Such a token would have priority c′ +
666   (T − t′ ) · ĉ1 where c′ is the real cost of arriving to s′ in t′ frames. Since ĉ1 lower-bounds the cost
667   of moving from frame to frame, it follows that c′ + (T − t′ ) · ĉ1 ≤ maxsi T[si , T ] < c∗ at any
668   time t′ ≤ T . Therefore (s∗ , T ) cannot be dequeued before (s′ , t′ ), ergo the proof is completed.
669
      Proposition 3. Bidirectional MINT is correct.
670
671
672   Proof. The algorithm alternates between a forward and backward step and maintains the best-so-far
673   path of T steps. Correctness rests on the stopping condition. The algorithm terminates when either (i)
674   both queues are empty or (ii) the elements (sfi , tf ) and (sbi , tb ) popped from the queues have joint
675   cost df [(sfi , tf )] + db [(sbi , tb )] that is larger than the current best path cost µ. Regarding condition
676   (i), when both queues are empty, all possible paths have been generated, so the algorithm returns the
677   optimal. Regarding condition (ii), assume that the optimal path is not yet found when the algorithm
678
      terminates returning −µ. Then there must be a path Q∗ of cost µ∗ < µ containing at least one not
679   yet visited token in Vf ∪ Vb . Such a token would have cost at least df [(sfi , tf )] on the forward side
680   and db [(sbi , tb )] on the backward side, hence path Q∗ would have cost µ∗ ≥ µ, a contradiction.

      We conduct a case study that applies Isabella on an emerging real-world problem of temporal
857
      community search (Galimberti et al., 2020). Given a temporal graph GT defined over a temporal
858   domain T = [0, 1, . . . tmax ], an integer h, and a set of query nodes q, the problem seeks a partition-
859   ing P of the temporal domain into h segments and a subgraph Gh containing the query nodes q
860   within each bucket, which yield the maximum sum of minimum degrees of subgraphs in P . This
861   problem is pertinent nowadays as the growing availability of timestamped data generates interest in
862   temporal graph management. Further, large real-world temporal graphs typically align themselves in
863   evolving communities. To study such communities, it often suffices to focus on a restricted set of
      query nodes, rather than partitioning the entire temporal graph.


                                                                                           16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      The problem is solved by the dynamic-programming recursion:
865
866                                p∗ (i, b) =          max      p∗ (j, b − 1) + vq∗ (j + 1, i),              (5)
                                                      0≤j<tmax

868   where p∗ (i, b) denotes the optimal objective value for a partition of the first i timestamps in b segments
869   and vq∗ (j+1, i) is the maximum minimum degree of a subgraph containing query nodes q and enduring
870   from the (j + 1)th to the ith timestamp. A cross-examination of Equation 5 and Equation 4 reveals
871   their analogy, with the main difference lying in the value associated with each segment, i.e., in the
872   terminology of Section 3, the gap function. Thus, the dynamic-programming algorithm for histogram
873   construction also solves temporal community search with the necessary modifications.
874   Nevertheless, to compute the gap function vq∗ (j, i) we need to identify a subgraph containing the
