032                                                 A BSTRACT
033
                The practical use of text-to-image generation has evolved from simple, monolithic
034             models to complex workflows that combine multiple specialized components.
035             While workflow-based approaches can lead to improved image quality, crafting
036             effective workflows requires significant expertise, owing to the large number of
037             available components, their complex inter-dependence, and their dependence on
038             the generation prompt. Here, we introduce the novel task of prompt-adaptive
039             workflow generation, where the goal is to automatically tailor a workflow to each
040             user prompt. We propose two LLM-based approaches to tackle this task: a tuning-
041             based method that learns from user-preference data, and a training-free method
042
                that uses the LLM to select existing flows. Both approaches lead to improved im-
                age quality when compared to monolithic models or generic, prompt-independent
043
                workflows. Our work shows that prompt-dependent flow prediction offers a new
044
                pathway to improving text-to-image generation quality, complementing existing
045             research directions in the field.

048   1     I NTRODUCTION
049
050   As the field of text-to-image generation (Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2021) matures, re-
051   searchers and practitioners shift from simple, monolithic workflows to more complex ones. Instead
052   of relying on a single model to produce an image, those advanced workflows combine a variety of
053
      components, or blocks, designed to enhance the quality of the generated image (AUTOMATIC1111,
      2022; Zhang, 2023; comfyanonymous, 2023). These components may include fine-tuned versions


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      of the generative model, large language models (LLMs) for refining the input prompt, LoRAs (Luo
055   et al., 2023; Ryu, 2023) trained to correct poorly generated hands or to introduce specific artistic
056   styles, improved latent decoders for creating finer details, super resolution blocks, and more.
057
058
      Importantly, effective workflows are prompt-dependent. The choice of blocks often depending on
      the text prompt and the content of the image being created. For example, a workflow aimed at
059
      emulating nature photographs may elect to use a model fine-tuned for photorealism, while workflows
060
      focused on generating human images often contain the term “bad anatomy” as a negative prompt
061   or leverage specific super-resolution models that also correct distorted facial features, such as the
062   eyes. Due to the richness of available blocks and complexity of workflows, building a well-designed
063   workflow often requires considerable expertise.
064
      In this work, we propose to learn how to build text-to-image generation workflows, conditioned on
065
      a user prompt. Specifically, we propose to leverage an LLM to take as input a prompt describing
066
      an image, and output a workflow that is specifically tailored to that prompt. Below, we outline two
067   approaches to achieving this goal. The prompt-specific workflow can then be used to synthesize im-
068   ages for that prompt, resulting in improved quality compared to using fixed base models or popular
069   human-crafted workflows. Importantly, using an LLM enables the model to leverage its extensive
070   prior knowledge to parse the prompt and match its content to the most appropriate blocks.
071
      To represent flows, we build on ComfyUI (comfyanonymous, 2023), a widely used tool that stores
072
      workflows as JSON files, which can be easily parsed by recent LLMs. The popularity of ComfyUI
073   also provides access to multiple human-created workflows, which we then augment to create a more
074   diverse training set. To teach the LLM the link between flow components and image quality, we
075   collect 500 diverse prompts from human users.1 These prompts are used for generating images
076   using each workflow in our training set, and the results are scored by an ensemble of aesthetic
077   predictors and human preference estimators (Kirstain et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023b).
078   This process effectively creates a training set composed of triplets of (prompt, flow, score).
079   We then consider two approaches for matching flows to novel prompts. In the first, we leverage
080   a closed-source LLM, and provide it with a table of flows and their scores across a closed-set of
081   categories automatically derived from our training prompts. This table serves as a context for a
082   followup request, where we ask the LLM to select the flow that is most suitable for a novel prompt.
083   In the second approach, we fine-tune an open LLM (Dubey et al., 2024) so that, given a prompt
084   and an ensemble score, it predicts the flow that achieved that score. During inference, we provide
085   the LLM with an unseen prompt and a target score and ask it to provide us with an appropriate
086   workflow. We name these approaches ComfyGen-IC and ComfyGen-FT respectively. The design
087
      choices behind each approach and their motivations are discussed below.
088   We compare our prompt-adaptive approach against several baselines, including: (1) single-model
089   approaches (the baseline SDXL model (Podell et al., 2024), popular fine-tunes, and a DPO-
090   optimized version (Rafailov et al., 2024; Wallace et al., 2024)), and (2) prompt-independent, popular
091   workflows. ComfyGen outperforms all baselines on both human-preference and prompt-alignment
092   benchmarks, highlighting the benefit of prompt-dependent flows.
093   Finally, we analyze the workflows selected by our method, demonstrate their relation to the domains
094   represented in the input prompts, and investigate the scaling behaviors of our model.

096   2       R ELATED WORK
097
098   Improving Text-to-image generation quality. With the growing popularity of text-to-image dif-
099   fusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022), a range of works
100   sought to improve the visual quality of their outputs, and their alignment to human preferences.
101   One approach is to fine-tune pretrained models using curated, high quality datasets or improved
102   captioning techniques (Dai et al., 2023; Betker et al., 2023; Segalis et al., 2023). Instead of col-
103   lecting data, a range of works use reward models (Kirstain et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023b; Xu et al.,
104   2024; Lee et al., 2023) to guide text-to-image generation. This can be done using reinforcement-
105   learning (Black et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). However, these
106   methods can be computationally expensive and struggle to generalize effectively. As an alterna-

114
      Our work proposes a new, orthogonal path to improving image quality. Instead of modifying the dif-
115   fusion model or intervening in its sampling process, we use reward models to better match workflow
116   components to a given prompt, aligning the entire pipeline towards human preferences.
117
118   LLM-based tool selection and Agents Recent advancements in large language models have
119   demonstrated significant improvements in reasoning abilities and their capacity to adapt to novel
120   content and tasks. This adaptability can be achieved through efficient fine-tuning methods, but more
121   commonly simply through zero-shot prompting or in-context learning.
122
      Building on these capabilities, a range of works proposed to leverage LLMs for tasks beyond text
123   generation. A common line of work aims to equip the LLM with external tools (Schick et al., 2024),
124   either through appropriate API tags within the generated text (Schick et al., 2024), by providing
125   in-context API documentations (Wang et al., 2024; Surı́s et al., 2023), model descriptions (Shen
126   et al., 2024) and code samples (Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023), or by retrieving models from a pre-
127   defined collection. (Wu et al., 2023a). Such tools are often referred to as LLM agents, and their
128   latest variants are often equipped with components such as memory mechanisms, retrieval modules
129   or self-reflection and reasoning steps, all aimed at improving their overall performance.
130   Our work can similarly be viewed as an agent, as it employs an LLM to directly select and connect
131   external tools. Here, we focus on the novel task of prompt-adaptive pipeline creation, and on tapping
132   this under-explored path to improving the quality of downstream generations.
133
134   Worfklow generation An emerging trend in machine learning research is the use of compound
135   systems, where multiple models are used in collaboration to achieve state-of-the-art results. These
136   systems have been successfully used across various domains, ranging from coding competitions (Al-
137   phaCode Team, 2024) to olympiad-level problem solving (Trinh et al., 2024), medical reason-
138   ing (Nori et al., 2023) and video generation (Yuan et al., 2024). Crafting such compound systems
139
      can be a daunting task, as the components must be carefully selected and their parameters tuned to
      perform well in tandem, rather than optimized on each individual step of the task (). To address this,
140
      recent approaches have proposed optimization-based frameworks that tune pipeline parameters for
141
      improved end-to-end performance (Khattab et al., 2023), or even optimize the connections within a
142   graph representing the components of a complex system (Zhuge et al., 2024).
143
144   Our work similarly tackles the task of pipeline generation. Here, we focus on text-to-image models,
145
      and demonstrate that their performance can be enhanced by designing compound pipelines that
      depend on the user’s prompt.

148   3     M ETHOD
149
      Given an input prompt describing an image, our goal is to match it with an appropriate text-to-image
150   workflow, leading to improved visual quality and prompt alignment. We hypothesize that effective
151   workflows will depend on the specific topics present in the prompt. Therefore, we propose to tackle
152   this task by leveraging an LLM that can concurrently reason over the prompt and identify these
153   topics, while also serving as a means to directly select or synthesize the new flow.
154
      In the following section, we provide a detailed description of ComfyUI and our method, focusing
155
      on our training data, as well as our retrieval-based and score-based tuning approaches.

197   3.2   T RAINING DATA
198   As a starting point, we collect a set of approximately 500 human-generated ComfyUI workflows
199   from popular generative-resource-sharing websites such as Civitai.com. We limit ourselves to text-
200   to-image workflows, flitering out video generation flows, and flows that take a control image as an
201   input. We further discard highly complex flows, whose JSON representations often span tens of
202   thousands of lines. Finally, we discard flows that use community-written blocks appearing in fewer
203   than three flows. This leaves us with a small set of 33 flows, which we augment by randomly switch-
204   ing diffusion models (see supplementary for list), LoRAs and samplers, or changing parameters like
205   the guidance scale and number of steps. In total, this process resulted in 310 distinct workflows.
206   Recall that our goal is to predict effective flows for a given prompt, which will enhance the quality
207   of the generated output. To achieve this, we need a way to assess workflow performance. To
208   do so, we begin by collecting a set of 500 popular prompts from Civitai.com and using them to
209   synthesize images with each flow in our dataset. These images are then scored using an ensemble
210   of quality prediction models (LAION Aesthetic Score (Schuhmann et al., 2022), ImageReward (Xu
211   et al., 2024), HPS v2.1 (Wu et al., 2023b), and Pickscore (Kirstain et al., 2023)). We standardize
212
      the outputs of these models so that they are of approximately the same scale, and sum them up,
      assigning higher weights to models that better align with human preferences. This process yields
213
      a single scalar score for each prompt and flow pair, where higher scores typically correlate with
214
      better image quality. Figure 2b,c shows the distribution of scores in our data set, along with visual
215   examples of images created with the same prompt, across a range of scores.

      Our final dataset consists of triplets of prompt, workflow, and score. We use these to implement both

219   3.3   C OMFY G EN -IC
220
221
      As a first approach to providing prompt-dependent flows, we look to in-context based solutions
      that leverage a powerful, closed-source LLM. To do so, we first need to provide the LLM with
222
      some knowledge about the quality of results produced by each flow. We thus start by asking the
223
      LLM to come up with a list of 20 labels which will best fit our 500 training prompts. These include
224   object-categories (“People”, “Wildlife”), scene categories (“Urban”, “Nature”) and styles (“Anime”,
225   “Photo-realistic”). A complete list of the labels is provided in the supplementary. With these labels
226   in hand, we can now calculate the average quality score of images produced by each flow across all
227   prompts belonging to a specific label. Repeating this for all flows and all labels gives us a table of
228   flows and a measure of their performance across all 20 categories.
229
      Ideally, we would have liked to provide the LLM with the full JSON description of the flows, allow-
230   ing it to learn the relationships between flow components and downstream performance on specific
231   categories. Unfortunately, the flows are too lengthy to fit more than a handful into the context win-
232   dow of most LLMs. Hence, our table contains only flow identities, and we simply ask the LLM to
233   choose the flow that it believes will perform best on a given, unseen prompt.
234
      All in all, this approach provides us with a classifier capable of parsing new prompts, breaking them
235
      down into relevant categories, and selecting the flow that best matches these categories. We name
236   this variation ComfyGen-IC.
237
238   3.4   C OMFY G EN -FT
239
      As an alternative approach, we can fine-tune an LLM to predict high-quality workflows for given
240
      prompts. One way to approach this problem could be to simply fine-tune the LLM so that, given
241
      an input prompt provided in-context, it would need to predict the flow that achieved the highest
242   score for that prompt. However, this approach has several drawbacks: it significantly reduces the
243   number of training tokens, using only one flow per prompt instead of all 310; it’s more sensitive to
244   randomness in our data creation process, such as the seed chosen for each generated image; and it
245   doesn’t allow learning from negative examples, which could help the model identify ineffective flow
246   components or combinations.
247
      Instead, we propose an alternative fine-tuning formulation where the LLM takes as its context both
248   the prompt and a score. The model is then tasked with predicting the specific flow that achieved the
249   given score for the corresponding prompt. This approach tackles the drawbacks of the best-scoring-
250   flow method. First, it greatly increases the number of tokens available for training by utilizing all
251   available data points, not just the highest-scoring ones. Second, it reduces the impact of random
252   fluctuations by considering a wider range of scores and their associated flows. Third, it allows the
253   model to learn from negative examples, i.e., flows that achieved low scores for a given prompt. At
254   inference time, we can simply provide the LLM with a prompt and a high score and have it predict
255   an effective flow for our prompt. We name this variation ComfyGen-FT.
256
      3.5   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
257
258   We implement ComfyGen-IC using Claude Sonnet 3.5, and ComfyGen-FT on top of pre-trained
259   Meta Llama3.1 8B and 70B checkpoints (Dubey et al., 2024). Unless otherwise noted, all
260   ComfyGen-FT results in the paper use the 70B model with a target score of 0.725. In all cases,
261   we finetune for a single epoch using a LoRA of rank 16 and a learning rate of 2e − 4. Additional
262   details are in the supplementary.

      6   L IMITATIONS
511
      While ComfyGen’s prompt-dependent workflow approach demonstrates improvements over mono-
512   lithic models and constant flows, it is not free of limitations. Our current model is limited to text-to-
513   image workflows, and cannot address more complex editing or control-based tasks. However, this
514   could potentially be resolved in the future through the use of vision-language models (VLMs).
515
      Similarly, both of our approaches require us to generate images using a large number of flows. With
516
      typical generations taking an order of 15 seconds, even a modest set of 500 prompts and 300 flows
517
      requires a month of GPU time to create. Therefore, scaling up the approach would likely require
518   significant computational resources or more efficient ways (e.g., Reinforcement Learning) to explore
519   the flow parameter space.
520
521
      Finally, each of our two methods has its own unique drawbacks. The fine-tuning approach cannot
      easily generalize to new blocks as they become available, requiring retraining with new flows that
522
      include these blocks. On the other hand, the in-context approach can be easily expanded by including
523
      the new flows in the score table provided to the LLM. However, this increases the number of input
524   tokens used, making it more expensive to run and eventually saturating the maximum context length.
525   We hope that these limitations can be addressed through more advanced retrieval-based approaches
526   or through the use of collaborative agents.
527
528   7   C ONCLUSIONS
529
530   We introduced the task of prompt-adaptive workflow generation, and presented ComfyGen - a set
531   of two approaches that tackle this task. Our experiments demonstrate that such prompt-dependent
532   flows can outperform monolithic models or fixed, user created flows, in a sense providing us with a
533   new path to improving downstream image quality.
534   In the future, we hope to further explore prompt-dependent workflow creation methods, increasing
535   their originality and expanding their scope to image-to-image or even video-related tasks. Perhaps in
536   the future we could collaborate with the language model on the creation of such flows, providing it
537   feedback through additional instructions or examples of outputs, thereby enabling non-expert users
538   to further push the boundary of content creation.
