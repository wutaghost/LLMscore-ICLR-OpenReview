009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite the impressive performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) across
012            numerous tasks and widespread application in real-world scenarios, LLMs still
013            struggle to guarantee their responses to be accurate and aligned with objective
014            facts. This leads to factual hallucination of LLMs, which can be difficult to de-
015            tect and mislead users lacking relevant knowledge. Post-training techniques have
016            been employed to mitigate this issue, yet they are usually followed by a trade-
017            off between honesty and helpfulness, along with a lack of generalized improve-
018
               ments. In this paper, we propose to address it by augmenting LLM’s fundamental
               capacity of leveraging its internal memory, that is, the knowledge derived from
019
               pre-training data. We introduce FactualBench, a comprehensive and precise fac-
020
               tual QA dataset consisting of nearly 200k Chinese generative QA data spanning
021            21 domains for both evaluation and training purposes. Furthermore, we propose
022            self-alignment with memory, i.e., fine-tuning the model via preference learning
023            on self-generated pairwise data from FactualBench. Extensive experiments show
024            that our method significantly enhances LLM’s performance on FactualBench, with
025            consistent improvements across various benchmarks concerning factuality, help-
026            fulness and multiple skills. Additionally, different post-training techniques and
027            tuning data sources are discussed to further understand their effectiveness.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Factual hallucination occurs when large language models (LLMs) generate inaccurate or entirely
033
      fabricated information in response to user queries (Zhang et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023). Detect-
      ing and mitigating factual hallucinations is crucial, because such errors can undermine trust in these
034
      models and potentially cause significant harm to users, especially when they are used in high-stakes
035
      applications (Ji et al., 2023; Mello & Guha, 2023; Kornblith et al., 2022). However, identifying these
036   hallucinations poses a unique challenge, as the fabricated content is often presented in a plausible
037   and convincing manner, making it difficult for users and models to recognize inaccuracies (Kaddour
038   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b). This complexity underscores the necessity of addressing factual
039   hallucination as a critical focus for enhancing the reliability of LLMs (Tian et al., 2023).
040
      Previous studies have explored mitigating hallucinations through post-training techniques such as
041
      Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT) (Elaraby et al., 2023; Moiseev et al., 2022; Santos et al., 2022) and
042   Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Tian et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Ouyang et al., 2022). However,
043   the implementation of these methods can inadvertently introduce more hallucinations (Gudibande
044   et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b) if training on novel knowledge that model has not
045   previously encountered (Gekhman et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023), and create a trade-off between
046   generating responses that are truthful and those considered helpful (Lin et al., 2024) when training
047   under inappropriate signals, for example, balancing response length and factuality in long-form tasks
048   or implicitly favoring specific response modes (Singhal et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2024; Torabi et al.,
049   2018; Kumar et al., 2024). These limitations restrict model’s ability to generalize effectively to new
050
      tasks and domains (Zhang et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023).
051   These challenges motivate us to enhance model factuality and facilitate generalized improvements
052   by more effectively leveraging existing memory (i.e. pre-trained knowledge), a fundamental capabil-
053   ity of LLMs (Zhao et al., 2023), rather than injecting new information. Specifically, we concentrate
      on precise closed-book question-answering (QA) task, which serves as a metric for assessing a

054
      model’s ability to accurately utilize its stored knowledge (Roberts et al., 2020) and has a golden cri-
055   terion: the correctness of the provided answer. However, current QA datasets are often insufficient
056   and inaccurate for both comprehensive evaluation and enhancement of model performance.
057
058
      To address the limitation, we propose FactualBench, a large-scale, multi-domain Chinese genera-
      tive QA dataset designed to evaluate and improve knowledge utilization ability of LLMs. Factual-

      Bench is constructed from high-quality, publicly available encyclopedia entries that are commonly
060

061   model’s existing knowledge. It comprises 181,176 benign samples across 21 domains, representing
062   a diverse set of important topics filtered by view counts. Preliminary evaluations with this dataset
063   reveal that the task is challenging for most LLMs, despite not requiring advanced skills or long-
064   tailed knowledge. Interestingly, we observe that models frequently generate correct answers under
065   high-temperature configuration (Figure 1(a)), suggesting that the knowledge is internalized but not
066   effectively utilized. This highlights a potential for improvement in factuality.

094
      Preference Optimization (Rafailov et al., 2024)) to deliver a more precise bi-directional signal.

102   how different post-training algorithms and sources of tuning data influence the training outcomes.

105   2         R ELATED WORKS
106
107   Hallucination is defined as generated content that is nonsensical or unfaithful to the provided source
      content (Ji et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023). It presents a mutifaceted challenge Li et al. (2024b)


                                                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      across a wide range of tasks (Ji et al., 2023). Hallucination can be categorized into intrinsic and
109   extrinsic parts, depending on whether the response conflicts with context itself or with the fact
110   beyond the context (Ji et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023), where the latter type tends to have more side
111   effects (Zhang et al., 2023b) and is mainly focused in our work.
112
      To mitigate hallucinations, several studies (Gardent et al., 2017; Wang, 2019) find that enhancing
113
      the quality of pre-training data can be effective. But processing vast scale training data could be
114
      time-consuming and is not be applicable to models that complete pre-training. Other approaches
115   focus on improving model’s factuality through decoding strategies (Zhang et al., 2023a; Li et al.,
116   2024b; Lee et al., 2022; Chuang et al., 2023), yet these strategies often increase inference complex-
117   ity and have more difficulty generating fluent or diverse text (Ji et al., 2023). Additionally, some
118   methods utilize retrieval-augmented generation techniques (Nakano et al., 2021; Gou et al., 2023),
119   but introduce significant system complexity (Tian et al., 2023) and rely on external resources. Con-
120   sequently, we emphasize enhancing model factuality through post-training by directly optimizing
121   model’s inherent parameters. Post-training algorithms, such as SFT and RL are frequently used to
122   improve model’s instruct following ability and align model with human preferences (Xu et al., 2024;
123
      Lin et al., 2024). However, SFT is considered to be sub-optimal for mitigating hallucinations due
      to its limited generalization capabilities in out-of-distribution cases (Zhang et al., 2023b). Recent
124
      RL studies improve factuality within a single task (Kang et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2023), or face
125
      a trade-off between factulity and helpfulness (Lin et al., 2024). In contrast, our method achieves
126   generalized improvements across factuality, helpfulness, and comprehensive abilities.
127
128   The principle underlying our method is to enhance model factuality by better utilizing existing mem-
129
      ory. However, current datasets are often insufficient and inaccurate for evaluating and improving this
      capability. Tasks that require long-form answers and multi-sentence reasoning (Wei et al., 2024; Min
130
      et al., 2023; Joshi et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2018) are often imprecise in measuring a model’s funda-
131
      mental knowledge utilization, as their performance is heavily influenced by instruct following and
132   complex reasoning abilities. Similarly, multiple-choice and detection tasks that rely on rule-based
133   automatic metrics (Li et al., 2023a;b; Liu et al., 2022; Mishra et al., 2024; Thorne et al., 2018) intro-
134   duce significant biases into model evaluations (Lou et al., 2024). Datasets designed with adversarial
135   intent, such as TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022) and others (Cheng et al., 2023), effectively stimulate
136   factual hallucinations but tend to focus on specific scenarios, thereby limiting their capacity to re-
137   flect model accuracy on general, everyday questions. While datasets focused on precise generative
138   QA (Yang et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024a; Yin et al., 2023; Berant et al., 2013;
139   Kwiatkowski et al., 2019) exist, they are generally constrained by small sample sizes or lack of
140
      domain classification, rendering them inadequate for a comprehensive evaluation of modern LLMs.
      These gaps in existing datasets motivate the need for more robust and large-scale resources like
141
      FactualBench, which we propose to address these shortcomings.

149
      tal capability of leveraging existing memory from pre-trained corpus. The optimization goal can be

      3.1       FACTUAL B ENCH
166
167   A large-scale and comprehensive dataset containing benign and precise questions is needed for ac-
168   curately evaluating and enhancing the knowledge utilization ability of models. We choose publicly
169   available Internet encyclopedias as a reliable knowledge base as they are widely used as training
170   corpora for LLMs (Liu et al., 2024b; Ando et al., 2024) and contain a wide range of topics across
171   various domains (Bai et al., 2024). A model-based approach is adopted to generate large amounts of
172   data costlessly and quickly. To avoid generating low-quality data (more details of such low quality
173
      data can be found in Appendix A), we utilize few-shot prompts (Brown, 2020), chain-of-thought
      (Wei et al., 2022) technologies, and apply several filtering strategies. GPT4 (Achiam et al., 2023)
174
      and Baichuan model1 are adopted in construction for their strong command-following capabilities.
175
176
      3.1.1         C ONSTRUCTION AND COMPOSTION
177
178   The construction pipeline for FactualBench is organized into 5 steps: 1) Entry filtering. We ini-
179   tially sampled millions of entries from encyclopedia data, covering a broad spectrum of subjects and
180   domains. To avoid testing on long-tailed knowledge, we set a view-counts threshold, and 89,658
181   encyclopedia entries remained. 2) Description filtering. Model’s performance tends to decline as
182   context length increases (Liu et al., 2024a; Sun et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024c). Excessively lengthy
183
      description may lead to low quality responses. Conversely, a overly brief description lacks suffi-
      cient factual information. To balence this, we filter out descriptions shorter than 100 characters and
184
      truncated those exceeding 800 characters. 64315 entries remained after this process. 3) Question
185
      generation. We instruct GPT4 to generate at most 3 precise questions based on each truncated de-
186   scription. For each question, GPT4 is also required to provide 1 standard answer and 3 misleading
187   incorrect answers. To ensure adherence to our instructions, we included two examples in the prompt
188   as few shots. Totally 192,927 QA data are generated. 4) Question classification. Following ques-
189   tion generation, a domain classifier, fine-tuned on Baichuan model, is employed to categorize all
190   questions into 20 distinct domains. Questions that don’t fit into any domain are uniformly classi-
191   fied as others. 5) Question filtering. We query GPT4 once more to filter out low-quality questions.
192   Each question is assessed without corresponding encyclopedia description and GPT4 is instructed to
193   judge whether question is low-quality through a step-by-step reasoning. Finally 181,176 questions
194
      are reserved, among which high-quality data accounts for 90% under human assessment. The com-
      plete prompts utilized throughout the generation pipeline can be found in Appendix B.1.1. Table 3
195
      presents a sample from our dataset, while additional examples are provided in Appendix B.2.

232   3.1.2      E VALUATION
233
234   Similar to previous works (Liu et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024), a robust model-based approach
235
      is employed to expedite the assessment process. Given that rule-based automatic metrics such as
      ROUGE (Lin, 2004) and BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) exhibit significant biases in evaluation (Lou
236
      et al., 2024), we assess the correctness of answer at semantic-level. The question, model answer,
237
      and standard answer are provided for judgement and evaluator is supposed to focus solely on the
238   content that directly addressed the question and determine whether the model’s answer aligns with
239   the standard answer.
240
241
      In our present work, the evaluator judges an answer as correct (i.e. J (x, y) = 1 in equation 1) only
      when model answers the question and the answer matches the standard answer. It is reasonable since
242
      model is expected to have been trained on relevant data and therefore should possess the knowledge.
243
      Moreover, this knowledge is not long-tailed, but rather high frequency viewed. Additionally, we an-
244   alyze the responses from different tested models and find that the proportion of evasive answers (e.g.
245   ”I don’t know”) is only approximately 1%. To enhance judgment accuracy, we instruct the evaluator
246   to provide analysis and include several examples in the prompt. GPT4-0125-preview is chosen as
247   the evaluator, achieving a 96% consistency rate with human, thus validating the effectiveness and
248   accuracy of our evaluation process. The prompt used for evaluation is detailed in Appendix B.1.2.
249
      We evaluate 14 popular open and closed source LLMs on FactualBench, including Baichuan series
250   (Yang et al., 2023), Qwen series (Yang et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023), Llama-3 series (AI@Meta,
251   2024), Yi (AI et al., 2024), Command-R series (Gomez, 2024a;b), DeepSeek (DeepSeek-AI, 2024),
252   and GPT4 (Achiam et al., 2023). We prioritize the chat/instruct versions of these models. Detailed
253   settings are shown in Appendix C.1 and the results are listed in Table 2. The accuracy of LLMs on
254   our benchmark ranges from 39.11% to 75.62%, indicating that most models still have lacks even in
255   basic factual QA task. Detailed results for each domain and analysis are shown in Appendix D.

258
      For questions where model response incorrectly, we observe that it can still generate correct answers
259
      when allowed greater diversity in its outputs. Taking Baichuan1 as an example, we encourage the
260
      model to generate varied answers by increasing the generation temperature. By sampling model’s
261   responses 8 times under this condition (which we refer as high temperature BO8), as opposed to
262   the standard inference condition (termed low temperature BO1). We consider the model to possess
263   relevant information and the ability to utilize it if at least one of the generated answers is correct. As
264   illustrated in Figure 1(a), comparing the accuracy of BO8 and BO1, we find a significant portion of
265   the model’s capability have not been fully stimulated, i.e., potential.

      4      E XPERIMENTS AND ANALYSIS
308
      In this section, we present the training result obtained using our method. Comparing the effec-
309
      tiveness of SFT and DPO, and tuning data from different sources, we observe that: 1) Training on
310   precise factual QA task that related to pre-training knowledge can lead to generalized improvements
311   on other tasks; 2) Training on pairwise data has an advantage over pointwise data in the task; 3)
312   Training effect is enhanced when tuning on self-generated data. For pairwise data, it is crucial that
313   labels from both directions adhere to the same distribution to avoid being hacked easily.

      4.3       A NALYSIS
419
420   In transformer architecture LLMs, Attention layers and Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) layers extract
421   useful features from input (Jiang et al., 2024), in which MLP layers are regarded to implement a
422   lookup table for factual recall to output multi-token embedding with related information (Nanda
423   et al., 2023) and provide factual knowledge (Dai et al., 2022). Inspired by the skill graph (Arora &
424   Goyal, 2023), we summarize the role of MLP as a memory graph, with edge connecting different
425   input contexts and internalized knowledge. A more precise memory graph leads to better knowledge
426   utilization, and therefore results in accurate answer distribution. The task we choose is highly related
427   to knowledge utilization, providing a direct optimization signal for the ability, a fundamental ability
428
      that could affect diverse tasks.
429   In contrast to the single signal provided in SFT, preference learning like DPO provides a bi-
430   directional signal, which can weaken useless or wrong edge as well as connect or strengthen an
431   needed edge, a more precise control than SFT. Besides, there could be diverse data samples on the
      same input, making training process more robust. Compared to external label, a self-generate label


                                                                  8
        Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
        has the same answer mode as model itself, avoiding overoptimizing on new answer pattern. And we
433     could also explain why a model fine-tuned on new knowledge it doesn’t possess will cause more hal-
434     lucinations: Since there is no corresponding knowledge in memory graph, model cannot learn how
435     to obtain the correct distribution but an overfitting on the label through building incorrect edges.
436
        A better knowledge utilization leads to better representation. Recent work (Huh et al., 2024) shows
437
        that representation alignment increases with LLM’s scale and performance. We choose Qwen2-
438
        72B-Instruct (Bai et al., 2023) as a good representation function, and the last hidden state value as
439     representation. Our experiments prove that after DPO training, Baichuan1 has further aligned with
440     Qwen2-72B, indicating a better representation ability is achieved. We use mutual nearest-neighbor
441     metrics (Huh et al., 2024) (we explain the calculation process of the metric in the Appendix E)
442     to evaluate alignment between two models, set k = 10, and randomly select 200 data points to
443     calculate.
444
        We show the alignment change before and after training based on FactualBench in Figure 6, and
445     alignment changes based on another four benchmarks in Figure 7. Our method achieves higher align-
446     ment with Qwen2-72B on four benchmarks and most domains in FactualBench, indicates model has
447     gained stronger representation ability, and result in a better response accuracy. Since different tasks
448     have different difficulties and features, the alignment cannot accurately predict the performance of
449     the model. However, it still works as a signal, reflecting the trend of the model performance chang-
450     ing. Comparing with SFT, DPO has higher alignment degree on average, proving that bi-directional
451     signal of DPO is better for model to achieve generalized improvements.

      5       C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this article, we aim to mitigate factual hallucination and achieve generalized improvement in
      model performance. We select factual QA as our training task to enhance model’s ability to utilize
532
      its memory, i.e. existing knowledge derived from pre-training data. We first extract knowledge from
533
      encyclopedia to construct a large-scale, multi-domain Chinese factual QA dataset FactualBench.
534   Based on FactualBench, we observe that model still possesses significant potential for knowledge
535   utilization. Consequently we propose self-alignment with memory: construct self-generated tuning
536   data on FactualBench and train the model using DPO loss. Our method significantly improves
537   model’s performance on our benchmark as well as multiple other open-source benchmarks that
538   evaluate factuality, helpfulness, and comprehensive skills. We attribute the effectiveness of our
539   method is originated from better representation ability. Finally, we establish the necessity of bi-
      directional signals and self-generated data through a series of ablation experiments.

870   In pre-generation experiment, we randomly sampled 3,000 encyclopedia entries and directly used
871   simple prompts to generate approximately 9,000 questions. After manually evaluating the questions
872   and answers, we found four types of low-quality data: 1) The related knowledge is too long-tailed;
873   2) The answer to the question is not unique; 3) The answer generated by GPT4 is incorrect; 4) The
874   question is not self-contained.
875   In this section, we will provide a detailed introduction to these typical low-quality data. In the
876   subsequent generation process, we focused on how to avoid the generation of such low-quality data
877   and formulated a better pipeline. The basic introductions and solutions of four typical types of
878   low-quality data are shown in table 7.

899   The related knowledge is too long-tailed Some entries’ subjects are relatively unknown, and the
900   related knowledge appears less on the internet since it is not important for most of people. Questions
901   on those subjects have little value, and are too hard to be answered correctly.
902   The correct answer to the question is not unique The answers to some questions are not unique
903   and may extend beyond the knowledge provided in encyclopedia entries’ descriptions. Additionally,
904   some terms in questions involve subjective judgments hence questions will have more possible and
905   reasonable answers.
906   The answer generated by GPT4 is incorrect GPT4 is applied to output both question and stan-
907   dard answer based on the description of encyclopedia entry. As the description’s length increases, it
908   will challenge model’s ability to follow instruction and catch the precise knowledge of the descrip-
909   tion. Sometimes the description itself is difficult to understand, such as poetry and classical Chinese
910   article, and GPT4 could provide incorrect answers or even semantically incoherent questions facing
911   them. Another situation is that some encyclopedia entries do not emphasize the time limitation of
912   information, and for some rapidly changing data, the knowledge could be outdated.
913   The question is not self-contained During benchmarking, only the question is provided for the
914   model without encyclopedia content. Therefore, the question should be understandable, complete,
915   and cannot contain vague pronouns or nouns with multiple interpretations such as abbreviations
916   and names without clear context, unless in the vast majority of cases, the word has the same ref-
917   erence or meaning. Sometimes low-quality encyclopedia entries’ descriptions themselves are not
      self-contained.
