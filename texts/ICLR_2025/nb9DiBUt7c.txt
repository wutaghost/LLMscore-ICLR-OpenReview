009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Foundation models have achieved tremendous success in different domains. How-
012            ever, their huge computation and storage complexity make these models difficult
013            to fine-tune and also less applicable in practice. Recent study shows training in
014            Fourier domain can be an effective fine-tuning method in terms of both model
015            performance and number of training parameters. In this work, we propose to fur-
016            ther reduce the complexity by the factorization through the product of interleaved
017            circulant and diagonal matrices. Our method avoids the construction of weight
018            change matrix and utilizes 1D fast Fourier transform (FFT) instead of 2D FFT.
019            Experimental results show that our method achieves similar or better performance
020
               across various tasks with much less floating-point operations (FLOPs) and the
               number of trainable parameters. Compared with other Fourier domain based fine-
021
               tuning methods, the FLOPs of the RoBERTa base model achieves 33.1Г— reduc-
022
               tion, while the ViT base model achieves 7.76Г— reduction. For number of trainable
023            parameters of the RoBERTa base model, our method is 5.33Г— smaller than LoRA,
024            and for ViT base model ours can be 10.7Г— smaller.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large foundation models (LFMs) are widely utilized in various fields, including natural language
031   processing (PaaГҹ & Giesselbach (2023)), image recognition and generation (Li et al. (2024a)), med-
032   ical diagnosis (Li et al. (2024b)), and autonomous driving (Chen et al. (2024)). Devlin (2018) have
033
      proposed the bidirectional transformer architecture that understands input data from left to right
      and right to left. It is trained to predict missing words given input context, and it has served as
034
      a foundation model that can be fine-tuned for many downstream tasks. Following the transformer
035
      architecture, generative pre-trained transformer (GPT) model by Radford & Narasimhan (2018) han-
036   dles input data from left to right following a sequential prediction order. This mechanism turns out
037   successful in many generation tasks such as text summary, question answering, etc.
038
039
      Although LFMs learn extensive general knowledge during the pre-training phase, they still require
      extra adjustments in downstream applications to effectively fullfill the task. Fine-tuning is a typ-
040
      ical approach to continue learning on given downstream data and update from pre-trained model
041
      parameters. While fine-tuning significantly reduces computational costs compared to training from
042   scratch, existing fine-tuning methods still suffer from the huge complexity of LFMs. As the original
043   model parameters are still kept and maintained during fine-tuning stage, this leaves limited space for
044   the development of fine-tuning methods.
045
      To address the challenge of fine-tuning LFMs, Hu et al. (2021) have proposed low-rank adaptation
046
      (LoRA). This method is an efficient fine-tuning approach designed for LFMs, reducing the number
047
      of parameters required during fine-tuning by introducing low-rank matrices. The essential idea is
048   assuming the weight change matrix with low rank structure, expressing it as the product of two
049   low rank matrices and only training these two smaller matrices while keeping the original weights
050   frozen. FourierFT proposed by Gao et al. (2024) assumes a sparse structure in fourier domain of the
051   weight matrix updates вҲҶW. Although FourierFT reduces the number of training parameters, the
052   computational and storage requirements of the model remain very high, particularly when dealing
053   with LFMs. The two-dimensional Fourier transform used to restore вҲҶW contributes to most of
      its computation and storage complexity. As a result, the fine-tuning model continues to necessitate

054
      high-performance hardware support, including substantial GPU resources and memory, which may
055   be challenging to achieve in practical applications.
056
057   Huhtanen & PeraМҲmaМҲki (2015) has demonstrated that a general complex matrix X вҲҲ CnГ—n can be
058
      factorized into the product of multiple circulant matrices and diagonal matrices, with total number
      of factors not exceeding 2n вҲ’ 1. This decomposition method offers several advantages, particularly
059
      in terms of computational efficiency and storage optimization. The computation and storage of diag-
060
      onal matrices can be efficiently managed using vector representations. Moreover, circulant matrices
061   possess a unique structure that allows them to be diagonalized using the fast Fourier transform (FFT),
062   significantly reducing the complexity of matrix operations and accelerating computation speed.
063
064
      Inspired by previous works, we propose circulant and diagonal vector based fine-tuning (CDVFT),
      which is also a Fourier domain based method. Our method represents the weight change matrix
065
      вҲҶW with the product of interleaved circulant and diagonal matrices. This factorization simpli-
066
      fies the matrix calculation process and reduces storage requirements. Due to the unique properties
067   of matrix product for circulant and diagonal matrices, the quadratic computation complexity now
068   becomes loglinear. Different from FourierFT based on 2D FFT, our fine-tuning process avoids the
069   restoration of the weight change вҲҶW and only takes 1D FFT operations. As a result, CDVFT can
070   achieve efficient storage and computation at the same time. We summarize our main contributions
071   as following:
072
073          вҖў We introduce CDVFT method that represents вҲҶW using the product of interleaved circu-
074            lant and diagonal matrices. These matrices have linear storage complexity as each of them
075            can be determined by a single weight vector. In practice, we find only using a few circulant
076
               and diagonal matrix is sufficient to perform fine-tuning.
077          вҖў CDVFT avoids the restoration of weight change matrix and has loglinear computation com-
078            plexity. The circulant matrix vector product can be transformed into 1D FFT, and diagonal
079            matrix vector product is linear in nature. Thus, the overall computation complexity be-
080            comes loglinear.
081          вҖў We evaluate our method on natural language understanding, instruction adjustment, and im-
082            age classification. Experimental results show that our method achieves similar or even bet-
083            ter results in terms of moder performance, number of training parameters and FLOPs. For
084            example, for the ViT base model, our method results in 7.76Г— FLOPs reduction compared
085            to FourierFT and 10.7Г— trainable parameters saving compared to LoRA, while resulting in
086            similar or even better accuracy.

088   2   R ELATED W ORKS
089
090   Fine-tuning LFMs is a challenging problem due to the large model size and computation require-
091   ment. Although training LFMs from scratch is performed on cloud platforms like LLaMA model
092   by Touvron et al. (2023), fine-tuning is often limited to a specific task and a low-cost computing
093   environment. Besides, fine-tuning runs on a much smaller dataset than the pre-training dataset for
094   LFMs. Thus, fine-tuning process is expected to be cost-effective. The overall complexity should be
095   small and affordable in practice.
096   Full fine-tuning is a classical approach training and updating all model parameters at the same time.
097   However, it is difficult to perform full fine-tuning on LFMs given the huge computation and stor-
098   age requirement. Brown et al. (2020) find LFMs are able to generalize to new tasks with few-shot
099   demonstrations as prompt, thereby saving the effort of training on parameters. Li & Liang (2021) ar-
100   gue that adding few-shot demonstrations is bounded by the input length constraint of current LFMs.
101   Instead, they propose the prefix tuning method to train a parameter vector and prepend to input,
102   which is expected to work as prompt in unlimited length.
103   Updating all model parameters is not desirable in practice, since each task needs to maintain a model.
104   Houlsby et al. (2019) propose the adapter method, where task dependent parameters are inserted to
105   LFMs. Fine-tuning process only updates those new parameters, thereby each task effectively sharing
106   pre-trained LFM parameters. Mahabadi et al. (2021) further reduce the task dependent parameters
107   amount by grouping adapters into a hyper network model such that the network can produce task
      specific parameters on the fly. Sung et al. (2022) discover backpropagation process through LFMs

      A    A PPENDIX
595
596
      We show the forward process of CDVFT in algorithm 1. First, m is used to determine the number of
597   diagonal and circulant matrices, and then the size of the diagonal and circulant vectors is determined
598   by dimension d. Then, вҲҶh = A2mвҲ’1 Г— . . . Г— C2j Г— A2jвҲ’1 Г— . . . Г— A3 Г— C2 Г— A1 Г— x is completed
599   through element wise multiplication and one-dimensional Fourier transform, and finally the final
600   output change is obtained, which is combined with the original output to obtain the final output of
601   the fine-tuning layer.
602
603   Algorithm 1 PyTorch-style pseudocode for CDVFT
604
      class CDVFT(nn.Module):
605
          def __init__(
606           self,
607           m: int = 2,
608           alpha: float = 1e-4, # scaling
609           d: int = 4096,
              base_layer: nn.Module # pre-trained layer
610
          ):
611           # definitions
612           self.m = m
613           self.d = d
614           self.scale = alpha
              self.base_layer = base_layer
615
              # diagonal matrices and circulant matrix initialization
616           self.diags = nn.ParameterList([nn.Parameter(torch.randn(1, self.n
617       )) for _ in range(i)])
618           self.circs = nn.ParameterList([nn.Parameter(torch.randn(1, self.n
619       )) for _ in range(i-1)])
620
           def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor):
621            for i in range(len(self.diags)):
622                # compute diagonal matrix multiplication (Eq.2)
623                x = x * self.diags[i].unsqueeze(0)
624                # compute circulant matrix multiplication (Eq.3)
                   if i < len(circs):
625
                       fd = torch.fft.fft(x,dim=2)
626                    fc = torch.fft.fft(self.circs[i],dim=1)
627                    fdc = fd * fc.unsqueeze(0)
628                    x = torch.fft.ifft(fdc,dim=2)
629            #compute delta output and merge (Eq.4, 5)
               x = x.real * self.scale
630
               result = self.base_layer(x)
631            result += x
632            return result
