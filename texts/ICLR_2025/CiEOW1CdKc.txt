009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Imitation Learning (IL) enables agents to mimic expert behavior by learning
012            from demonstrations. However, traditional IL methods require large amounts of
013            medium-to-high-quality demonstrations as well as actions of expert demonstra-
014            tions, both of which are often unavailable. To address these limitations, we pro-
015            pose LWAIL (Latent Wasserstein Adversarial Imitation Learning), a novel adver-
016            sarial imitation learning framework that focuses on state-only distribution match-
017            ing by leveraging the Wasserstein distance computed in a latent space. To obtain
018            a meaningful latent space, our approach includes a pre-training stage, where we
019
               employ the Intention Conditioned Value Function (ICVF) model to capture the
               underlying structure of the state space using randomly generated state-only data.
020
               This enhances the policy’s understanding of state transitions, enabling the learn-
021
               ing process to use only one or a few state-only expert episodes to achieve expert-
022            level performance. Through experiments on multiple MuJoCo environments, we
023            demonstrate that our method outperforms prior Wasserstein-based IL methods and
024            prior adversarial IL methods, achieving better sample efficiency and policy robust-
025            ness across various tasks.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As a powerful tool for solving sequential decision-making problems, Reinforcement Learning (RL)
031   has achieved remarkable success in recent years across various fields, such as gaming (Silver et al.,
032   2016) and training of large language models from human feedback (Ramamurthy et al., 2023).
033   However, RL relies heavily on well-defined reward signals (Li et al., 2021), which can be difficult to
034   obtain in real-world settings (e.g., robot control (Ibarz et al., 2021) with varied target tasks) or may
035   require careful, environment-specific considerations (Yu et al., 2020).
036   Imitation Learning (IL) provides a way to avoid the use of rewards, and can generally be divided
037   into two types: Behavioral Cloning (BC) (Ross et al., 2011; Torabi et al., 2018a) and Inverse Re-
038   inforcement Learning (IRL) (Ho & Ermon, 2016; Fu et al., 2018; Bobrin et al., 2024). Compared
039   to BC, which directly learns to map states to actions by imitating an expert, inverse reinforcement
040   learning is more flexible and robust as it recovers a (usually dense) reward function from existing
041   demonstrations (Arora & Doshi, 2021) that exhibit the essence of the target policy.
042   However, similar to well-defined reward signals, expert demonstrations are often sparse in real-life
043   applications too, as human effort is usually involved (e.g., teleoperating robotic arms) whenever a
044   new task is considered. In this paper, we focus on one type of expert data shortage that has been
045   widely studied by the community: state-only expert demonstrations, which is also referred to as
046   ‘Imitation Learning from Observations’ (LfO). Classic IRL-based methods, such as GAIL (Ho &
047   Ermon, 2016) and AIRL (Fu et al., 2018), require access to expert actions, which are unavailable
048   in LfO. Existing methods for LfO (Torabi et al., 2018b; Zhu et al., 2020), similar to GAIL, train
049   a discriminator to distinguish an expert’s behavior observed via expert demonstrations from the
050
      agent’s behavior encoded in the learned policy.
051   There are also recent LfO methods minimizing state (Ma et al., 2022) or adjacent state-pair (Liu
052   et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2022a) occupancy divergence. They often require large amounts of medium-
053   to-high-quality offline data to perform well. In contrast, random data are much easier to obtain from
      suboptimal agents, exploratory behaviors, or even failed attempts at completing a task. Thus, we

078
         To answer this question, we propose an adversarial imitation learning approach for state-only dis-
         tribution matching using the Wasserstein distance. Different from prior work, we improve upon the
079
         classic and default Euclidean distance metric in the Wasserstein formulation by incorporating an
080
         embedding model which is trained prior to policy optimization with a large amount of random data
081      via the Intention Conditioned Value Function (ICVF) (Ghosh et al., 2023). Using it, we acquire a
082      distance metric which captures the dynamics and hidden relationships between states. Compared to
083      prior adversarial imitation learning methods such as GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016) and AIRL (Fu et al.,
084      2018) which often use state-action pairs to learn, our proposed method needs much less data; com-
085      pared to prior Wasserstein-based imitation learning methods such as WDAIL (Zhang et al., 2020b)
086      and IQlearn (Garg et al., 2021), our learned distance metric improves upon the classic Euclidean
087      distance inherently applied by the popular Kantorovich-Rubinstein (KR) duality (see Sec. 3.2 for
088      details) without using surrogates such as the one in PWIL (Dadashi et al., 2021).
089      To validate our approach, we conduct experiments in two settings. First, we validate our method
090      on Maze2D environments from the D4RL benchmark (Fu et al., 2020). This setting provides the
091      added advantage of compelling visualizations, enabling a clear validation of our idea. Further, we
092      extend our experiments to challenging locomotion tasks in the MuJoCo environment. We train our
093      ICVF distance metric model via random data, both from the existing D4RL dataset and data we
094      collected through random actions. We find that the ICVF-learned metric grasps the reachability
095
         relations within a trajectory much better than the Euclidean distance. In terms of imitation learning
         we achieve strong results using only a single trajectory of state-based expert data.
096
097      Our contributions can be summarized as follows: 1) We conceptually and empirically show that the
098      latent space from ICVF provides a good metric for state-based Wasserstein occupancy matching,
099      circumventing an inherent problem of the KR duality; 2) We propose a simple but effective method
100      that can achieve expert level performance with a single state-only expert trajectory; 3) We empiri-
101
         cally show that our method outperforms a variety of baselines on multiple testbeds, thus proving a
         better distance metric can greatly benefit Wasserstein-based adversarial imitation learning.

106      Markov Decision Process (MDP). A Markov Decision Process (MDP) is a mathematical frame-
107      work which describes the interactions of an agent with an environment at discrete time steps. It is
         defined by the tuple (S, A, P, R, γ), where S represents the state space, and A denotes the action

457   5   R ELATED W ORK
458
459   Imitation by occupancy matching / adversarial training. GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016) is one
460   of the first works to study adversarial imitation learning. A bi-level optimization of “RL over in-
461   verse RL” is considered, which corresponds to state-action occupancy matching between an expert
462   and the learner’s policy. Followup works (Fu et al., 2018; Kostrikov et al., 2019; Torabi et al.,
463   2018b; Zhu et al., 2020) have further explored the adversarial training paradigm, jointly training
464   1) a discriminator to distinguish policy occupancy from expert occupancy; and 2) the policy fitting
465
      expert demonstrations. While some occupancy matching works like DIstribution Corrected Estima-
      tion (DICE) (Ma et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022a; Yan et al., 2024) are trained using a single-level
466
      optimization instead of an adversarial setup, they essentially derive a closed-form solution for the
467
      policy given the discriminator. Most works in this field, however, focuses on f -divergence (espe-
468   cially KL (Zhu et al., 2020) or χ2 (Ma et al., 2022)) minimization. In contrast, our work studies the
469   Wasserstein distance and overcomes initial limitations.
470
471
      Wasserstein-based imitation learning. The Wasserstein distance (Kantorovich, 1939), is widely
      adopted in IL/RL (Xiao et al., 2019; Agarwal et al., 2021; Fickinger et al., 2022). It provides a
472
      geometry-aware measure between policy occupancies. Among different forms of the Wasserstein
473
      distance, the primal form (Dadashi et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024; Bobrin et al.,
474   2024) and the Rubinstein-Kantorovich dual (Kantorovich & Rubinstein, 1958; Zhang et al., 2020b;
475   Garg et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2021) are most prominent. The former allows for larger freedom in
476   its underlying metric, but requires a regularizer (Yan et al., 2024), surrogates (Dadashi et al., 2021),
477   or a direct match between trajectories (Luo et al., 2023; Bobrin et al., 2024). The latter is easier
478   to optimize with gradient descent, but the underlying metric is limited to Euclidean, which is often
479   suboptimal (Stanczuk et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2024). Our work chooses the latter but overcomes its
480   shortcomings.
481   Among all these works, IQ-learn (Garg et al., 2021) is most similar to ours. Our objective in Eq. (4)
482   is a special case of IQ-learn with Wasserstein distance. However, three key differences exist: 1)
483   IQ-learn uses SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018) instead of TD3; 2) IQ-learn focuses on χ2 -divergence
484   in the online setting, which was found to be less effective in several prior works (Ma et al., 2022;
485   Yan et al., 2024); 3) We point out and overcome the underlying metric limitation by adopting ICVF,
      which is not considered in IQ-learn.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Imitation from observation. Imitation (Learning) from Observation (LfO) aims to retrieve an ex-
487   pert policy without labeled actions. This is particularly interesting in robotics, where the expert
488   action can be either inapplicable during cross-embodiment imitation (Sermanet et al., 2017) or un-
489   available when imitating from videos (Pari et al., 2022). The three primary strategies of LfO can
490   be categorized as follows: 1) minimizing an occupancy divergence through DICE methods (Zhu
491   et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022a;b; Yan et al., 2024) or iterative
492   inverse-RL updates (Torabi et al., 2018b; Xu & Denil, 2019; Zolna et al., 2020); 2) predicting miss-
493   ing actions through inverse dynamics modeling (Torabi et al., 2018a; Kumar et al., 2019); and 3)
494   similarity-based reward assignment (Sermanet et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2019). Our
495
      work belongs to the second category, and adopts the Wasserstein distance as the measure between
      occupancies, which improves results over prior works.
496
497   Offline-to-online IL. While offline IL (Zolna et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022a) and
498   online IL (Ho & Ermon, 2016; Fu et al., 2018) are both well-studied areas, offline-to-online IL
499   is relatively under-explored, especially when compared with offline-to-online RL (Schmitt et al.,
500   2018; Kostrikov et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) which combines the best of offline RL (high data
501
      efficiency) and online RL (active data collection). While there are some works that use offline data
      to aid online imitation (Watson et al., 2024) by building dynamic models (Chang et al., 2021; Yue
502
      et al., 2023) or aligning discriminator and policy (Yue et al., 2024), they have two shortcomings
503
      compare to our proposed method: 1) their solution requires medium-to-high quality offline data
504   and does not work well with random offline data, while our ICVF-learned metric works well with
505   random offline data; 2) they require state-action pairs for expert demonstrations, while our method
506   only requires expert states.
507
      State embedding. Many works have explored the possibility of learning a good state space em-
508
      bedding that better captures the dynamics of the environment and boosts RL performance (Zhang
509
      et al., 2020a; Ghosh et al., 2023; Modi et al., 2024). These works can be roughly categorized into
510   two groups: 1) the ‘theoretical group’, which focuses on state equivalence (also known as “bisim-
511   ulation”) (Zhang et al., 2020a; Kemertas & Aumentado-Armstrong, 2021; Le Lan et al., 2021) and
512   the low-rank property of the MDP (Agarwal et al., 2020; Uehara et al., 2022; Modi et al., 2024); and
513   2) the ‘empirical group’ often tested on visual RL with high-dimensional input (Anand et al., 2019;
514   Laskin et al., 2020; Yarats et al., 2022)), which focuses on representation learning (Ha & Schmidhu-
515   ber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2023; Bruce et al., 2024), autoencoder methods (Senthilnath et al., 2024),
516   and contrastive learning (Sermanet et al., 2017; Anand et al., 2019; Laskin et al., 2020). The recently
517   proposed ICVF (Ghosh et al., 2023) studies an empirical, intention-based method for state embed-
518
      ding computation. It was shown to be effective in downstream tasks (Ghosh et al., 2023; Bobrin
      et al., 2024). Our work is the first to leverage ICVF state embeddings to successfully overcome the
519
      metric limitation of the KR duality of the Wasserstein distance.

      6   C ONCLUSION
524
525
526
      We propose a novel adversarial imitation learning approach for state-only distribution matching us-
527
      ing the Wasserstein distance. Unlike prior methods that rely on Euclidean distance metrics, we op-
528   timize this distance metric by leveraging an embedding learned by the Intention Conditioned Value
529   Function (ICVF), which captures environmental dynamics. This allows us to better align the state
530   distributions between the expert and the agent, even when only sparse state-only demonstrations are
531   available. Through multiple experiments on the Maze2D and MuJoCo environments, we demon-
532   strate that the ICVF-learned distance metric outperforms several baselines, enabling more efficient
533   and accurate imitation from limited expert data with only one expert trajectory. We believe our
534   work provides a new direction for improving state-only imitation learning by using the Wasserstein
535   distance while addressing the limitations of traditional distance metrics.
536   Limitations. Similar to other prior adversarial imitation learning methods such as WDAIL (Zhang
537   et al., 2020b), our pipeline requires an iterative update of the actor-critic agent and the discriminator
538   during online training. The update frequency needs to be balanced during training. Also, testing our
539   method on more complicated environments, such as image-based ones, is interesting future research.

766
767   Intention Conditioned Value Function (ICVF). Intuitively, V (s, s+ , z) is designed to evaluate the
768   likelihood of the following question: How likely am I to see s+ if I act to perform z from state s?
769   The learning of ICVF is similar to other value-learning algorithms. ICVF satisfies the following
770   Bellman equation:
771
772                     V (s, s+ , z) = Ea∼πz∗ I(s = s+ ) + γEs′ ∼Pz (·|st ) [V (s′ , s+ , z)] ,

773                                                                                                        (10)
                          where πz∗ = arg max rz (s) + γEs′ [V (s′ , z, z)] .
774                                           a
775
776
      Here, (s, s′ ) is a transition and Pz (st+1 |s) is the transition probability from st to st+1 when acting
      according to intent z. Further, rz defines the agent’s objective for a particular intention z. Note,
777
      rz (s) is not the ground truth reward signal. Instead, it describes whether a state s is desirable by
778
      intent z and thus depends on data; in other words, the agent aims to maximize the reward specified
779   by rz when pursuing intention z. The original reward is not needed in ICVF training.
780
781   The original paper adopts implicit Q-learning (IQL) for ICVF learning. In one update batch,
782
      we sample transition (s, s′ ), potential future outcome s+ , and intent z. Similar to the original
      IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2022), we update the critic with asymmetric critic losses to avoid out-of-
783
      distribution overestimation. To do this, we apply different weights on critic loss with respect to the
784
      positivity of advantage. Note, as we care about whether the transition (s, s′ ) corresponds to acting
785   with intention z, our goal s+ is equal to z. Thus, the advantage A is defined as:
786
787                                  A = rz (s) + γVθ (s′ , z, z) − Vθ (s, z, z).                          (11)

      Following that, the critic loss is defined as:
