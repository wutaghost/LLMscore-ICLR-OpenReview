009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences is critical for their
012                deployment. Recently, decoding-time alignment has emerged as an effective plug-
013                and-play technique that requires no fine-tuning of model parameters. However,
014                generating text that achieves both high reward and high likelihood remains a
015                significant challenge. Existing methods often fail to generate high-reward text
016                or incur substantial computational costs. In this paper, we propose CAscade
017                RewarD Sampling (CARDS) to address both issues, guaranteeing the generation
018
                   of high-reward and high-likelihood text with significantly low costs. Based on
                   our analysis of reward models (RMs) on incomplete text and our observation
019
                   that high-reward prefixes induce high-reward complete text, we use rejection
020
                   sampling to iteratively generate small semantic segments to form such prefixes.
021                The segment length is dynamically determined by the predictive uncertainty of
022                LLMs. This strategy guarantees desirable prefixes for subsequent generations
023                and significantly reduces wasteful token re-generations and the number of reward
024                model scoring. Our experiments demonstrate substantial gains in both generation
025                efficiency and alignment ratings compared to the baselines, achieving five times
026                faster text generation and 99% win-ties in GPT-4/Claude-3 helpfulness evaluation.1

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable performance across a wide variety of
032   tasks (Wei et al., 2022; Bubeck et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Kaddour et al., 2023). Despite
033   their impressive capabilities, there are growing concerns regarding their safety and reliability (Bai
034
      et al., 2022a; Deshpande et al., 2023; Weidinger et al., 2022; Gehman et al., 2020). The field of
      LLM alignment aims to address these issues by ensuring that LLMs adhere to human preferences and
035
      ethical standards. However, one critical challenge is that the generated text must satisfy constraints,
036
      including helpfulness and ethical considerations, while simultaneously maintaining fluency.
037
038   Various alignment strategies have been developed, such as reinforcement learning with human
039   feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Bai et al., 2022b; Ouyang et al., 2022) and supervised
040
      fine-tuning methods (Liu et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024). Recently,
      decoding-time alignment, which only modifies the decoding procedure to generate aligned text, has
041
      gained increasing attention due to its simplicity and flexibility (Deng & Raffel, 2023; Khanov et al.,
042
      2024). This approach does not require fine-tuning of LLM parameters, allowing for the plug-and-play
043   adaptation for any unaligned LLM. Decoding-time alignment naturally supports frequently changing
044   LLMs and reward models (RMs), potentially enabling some complicated tasks like multi-objective
045   alignment (Vamplew et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024). However, while some of
046   the existing decoding-time alignment methods still struggle with the trade-off between alignment
047   and fluency, they all encounter significant efficiency challenges due to auxiliary steps added to
048   their decoding process. For example, the reward-guided search paradigm (Deng & Raffel, 2023;
049   Khanov et al., 2024) introduces considerable overhead of RM scoring, significantly slowing down the
050   generation.
051   In this paper, we propose CAscade RewarD Sampling (CARDS), a novel decoding-time alignment
052   method that guarantees high-reward and high-likelihood responses, while substantially reducing

105   Fine-tuning-based alignment. The goal of fine-tuning-based alignment methods is to minimize
106   the inference cost after deployment (Frantar et al., 2023). These methods typically assume that both
107   LLMs and human preferences remain fixed. Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)
      is a direct approach (Christiano et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022), utilizing RMs


                                                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      as proxies for human preferences and refining LLMs within the RL framework. The supervised
109   fine-tuning (SFT) approach (Liu et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024) addresses
110   the instability inherent in RL training while further improving the efficiency of alignment training.
111   The proposed CARDS framework is different from fine-tuning-based methods in that CARDS directly
112   samples from the optimal policy, eliminating the need to modify LLMs.
113
114   Decoding-time alignment. Aligning language models during decoding can adaptively fit any
115   preference via different RMs (Huang et al., 2024), which introduce auxiliary steps into the generation
116   process but no longer need parameter fine-tuning. Reward-guided search (Deng & Raffel, 2023;
117   Khanov et al., 2024) uses reward scores to rank the next token, similar to conditional text gener-
118   ation (Yang & Klein, 2021). They are token-level best-of-N searching algorithms (Nakano et al.,
119
      2021; Touvron et al., 2023), where N candidates are drawn and the one with the highest reward
      is selected. In contrast, CARDS uses segment-level rejection sampling and eliminates the need to
120
      exhaustively search the top-N token space. In-context learning (Lin et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) is
121
      also an efficient decoding-time alignment method, which prompts the base LLMs to align themselves.
122   Chakraborty et al. (2024) transfer the preference from a fine-tuned baseline LLM to a new LLM
123   without further fine-tuning. Mudgal et al. (2024) train a value-function module to conduct token-level
124   scoring, and Chen et al. (2024) train a generative token-level RM. They both address the low accuracy
125   of instance-level RMs in reward-guided search, but introduce significant computational overhead in
126   obtaining such token-level scorers. In contrast, our method adopts segment-level reward evaluation,
127   which makes the existing instance-level RMs accurate in scoring semantically complete prefixes.
128
129   Rejection sampling for language model alignment. Rejection sampling enables sampling from
130   intractable target distributions. Khaki et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024); Xiong et al. (2023) use rejection
131   sampling to generate preference data for tuning language models. Eikema et al. (2021) directly
132   samples responses using rejection sampling, but this does not apply to LLMs due to efficiency
133
      issues. The cascade sampling strategy in our method addresses the efficiency issue by sampling small
      semantic segments iteratively to reduce the search space.
134
135
      Segment-based text generation. Splitting text into segments is a well-studied technique (Pak &
136
      Teh, 2018). In LLM alignment, ToT (Yao et al., 2023) first introduced similar ideas, in which the
137
      starting and ending points of segment candidates are task-related, but they have fixed lengths. In
138   RAIN (Li et al., 2024), the length of each segment candidate is still a fixed hyper-parameter. In
139   contrast, we adopt a flexible approach where semantic segments can vary in length. We allow the
140   LLM to determine the number of tokens within a semantic segment based on its dynamic and adaptive
141   predictive uncertainty, which can vary for different texts. Similar ideas involving dynamic segment
142   length can be found in tasks outside of alignment, such as speculative decoding (Xia et al., 2024),
143   where the length of the candidate sequence is determined by the LLMs’ self-verification.

      4     M ETHODOLOGY: C ASCADE R EWARD S AMPLING
181
182
      Generating high-reward responses efficiently is the primary challenge in decoding-time alignment.
      The efficiency issue involves a trade-off between token re-generations and reward model (RM) scoring.
183
      Naive rejection sampling introduced in Section 3 will induce excessive token re-generation due to the
184
      large search space; on the other hand, reward-guided search (Deng & Raffel, 2023; Khanov et al.,
185   2024) deterministically evaluates the Top-k candidate tokens in every decoding step, leading to too
186   many RM calls. Our method (CARDS) addresses this efficiency challenge by iteratively generating
187   full responses in smaller segments to compress the search space at each step, and applying rejection
188   sampling rather than deterministic search to limit the number of RM calls.
189
      In this section, we first discuss the correctness of our cascade sampling strategy for efficiently gener-
190
      ating high-reward text (Section 4.1), followed by a detailed explanation of our method (Section 4.2).
191
192
      4.1     R EWARD M ODELS ON I NCOMPLETE T EXT
193
194   Generating high-reward complete responses in smaller segments (in a “cascade” fashion) requires
195   that: i) RMs are aligned with human judgments on incomplete responses; and ii) conditioned on high-
196   reward prefixes, the complete responses are more likely to get high rewards. The first requirement
197   ensures that the reward scores for prefixes serve as informative alignment metrics. The second
198   requirement ensures that generating smaller segments is an efficient search method for high rewards.
199   We discuss and validate each requirement in the following sections.
200
201   4.1.1    R EWARD S CORES OF S EMANTICALLY C OMPLETE P REFIXES
202   Reward models are trained to evaluate how responses are aligned with human preference. One of the
203   dominant RM training objectives is pairwise comparison (Stiennon et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2023;
204   Xiong et al., 2023) (also known as the Bradley–Terry models (Bradley & Terry, 1952)):
205
                            L(x, y + , y − ; θRM ) = log σ rθRM (x, y − ) − rθRM (x, y + ) ,

                                                                                                    (3)
206
207   where σ(·) is the sigmoid function, x is the input text, and y + /y − is the chosen/rejected response.
208   We hypothesize that reward scores for prefixes (incomplete responses) are more accurate if the
209   prefixes are semantically complete, as RMs are typically trained on complete responses. Semantically
210   complete prefixes are closer to the data that RMs have seen during training. We empirically verify
211   this hypothesis in Fig. 2c, where we compute the averaged reward of all prefixes obtained by
212
      segmentation. Fig. 2c shows that the semantically-segmented prefixes (see Section 4.2.1 for details)
      are more aligned with the full-length responses than static segmentation (not semantically complete),
213
      as semantically-segmented prefixes have much lower reward scores on rejected responses.
214
215   The ability of RMs to evaluate both complete responses and their prefixes also implies that RMs are
      similar to the value function in reinforcement learning (Bellman, 1966; Ouyang et al., 2022). The

      4.1.2    F ULL - RESPONSE REWARD IS APPROXIMATELY MONOTONIC TO PREFIX REWARD
232
233   Generating responses in smaller segments can reduce the search space. However, it is important to
234   ensure that the full-response reward will be high given a high-reward prefix. Mathematically, we can
235   represent this relationship as follows. We assume that given response prefix y<t , the full-response
236   reward r(x, y) follows a distribution (for simplicity, we use a Gaussian distribution), with the mean
237
      controlled by the prefix reward r(x, y<t ):
238                                       r(x, y) ∼ N (r(x, y<t ) + ϵt , σt2 ),                                (5)
239
240   where ϵt > 0 is a positive mean shift, indicating that full responses tend to have higher rewards than
241   their prefixes. This is based on the observation that longer responses tend to have higher rewards
242
      (Appexidx C.4). We visualize this assumption in Fig. 2a, where higher prefix rewards make it more
      likely to get high full-response rewards.
243
244   To empirically verify the above assumption, we test Llama RM (Khanov et al., 2024) and Mistral
245   RM (Xiong et al., 2023) on HH-RLHF2 in Fig. 2, which shows that prefix’s rewards have monotonic
246   relationship with full-response rewards.3 Additionally, we show that the variance term σt2 in Eq. (5)
247   is related to the length difference between full response and prefix, and longer prefixes (larger t)
248
      typically induce smaller σt2 (Appendix C.3), which means that the reward of response is highly
      predictable if only the last few tokens remain unknown. Therefore, as we concatenate semantic
249
      segments into a longer and higher-reward prefix, generating a high-reward full response will be easier.
250
251   In summary, we validate the cascade generation strategy in CARDS, and show that it is an efficient
252   approach to obtain high-reward responses. At each step of segment generation, the new prefix (formed
253   by adding the new semantic segment to the current prefix) will, on average, have a higher reward
254
      than the prefix from the previous step.

      5.1     E FFICIENCY E VALUATION
398
399   The computational cost in an LLM-RM architecture mainly arises from the number of LLM/RM
400   calls. RMs are typically fine-tuned from unaligned LLMs (Deng & Raffel, 2023; Khanov et al.,
401   2024), and thus the cost for one forward pass of RMs is the same as LLMs. We show the efficiency
402   evaluation results in Table 1. The number of tokens RMs evaluated at a time is an important metric
403   for understanding the efficiency of decoding-time alignment. If evaluating one token each time
404   (e.g., in RAD/ARGS), LLM token re-generations can be saved but RM calls will be too expensive.
405   Conversely, if evaluating the entire response at once (e.g., naive rejection sampling), only a few
406
      RM calls are needed but the LLM token re-generations will be too expensive. Our method strikes a
      balance between LLM and RM calls by using the RM to evaluate a partial response at a time. Our
407
      approach results in the lowest number of total calls (LLM + RM calls) and the smallest inference
408
      time. Compared to existing decoding-time alignment methods RAD/ARGS, our method reduces the
409   number of total calls by 9x and decreases inference time by 5x. The results on UltraFeedback (Cui
410   et al., 2023) are in Appendix C.8.

526   6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
527
528   In this paper, we proposed the CAscade RewarD Sampling (CARDS) for efficient decoding-time
529   alignment. We first empirically analyze the properties of reward models (RMs) and show the
530   relationship between full-response reward and prefix reward. Then we leverage rejection sampling to
531   iteratively generate small semantic segments of high reward, where the predictive uncertainty of LLMs
532   dynamically determines the segment length. Our method substantially reduces the computational
533   cost compared to existing decoding-time alignment methods. In our experiments, we evaluate the
534   efficiency, alignment rating, and fluency of the generated responses. Our method achieves excellent
535   results under all metrics.
536   Despite the superiority of our method, some technical limitations still exist and prevent our method
537   from being more effective. For example, dynamic segmentation is hard to parallelize to batched
538   inference without compromising accuracy (Appendix B.5). Additionally, accuracy of the reward
539   models itself is a critical bottleneck for alignment rating (Appendix B.6). We aim to address these
      issues in future work.

861   B.4    P ROMPT L ENGTH A LIGNMENT FOR E FFICIENT BATCHED D ECODING
862
863   In batched inference, shorter prompts will be padded to be aligned with the longest prompt. The padding length
      is a source of extra computational cost. We address this problem by choosing prompts of similar lengths into one


                                                             16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      batch. Specifically, we sort the test set and form batches iteratively. This way the prompts within one batch are
865   always of similar length.
866
867   B.5      D ISCUSSION ON PARALLELIZATION
868
869   The dynamic segmentation proposed in this paper presents inherent challenges for parallelization, as the re-
870   generation of segments can cause sentences within a batch to be no longer aligned and introduce significant
      padding cost. To address this, we have implemented a naive parallelization scheme in our codebase:
871
872             • For each of the sentences within a batch, the predictive uncertainty is computed in parallel.
873             • The end of the current segments is identical for all sentences and is determined by the average
874               predictive uncertainty of the batch.
875
876   As shown in Table 7, this naive parallelization compromises the accuracy of uncertainty-based segmentation in
      favor of faster text generation. However, it still achieves relatively promising results, indicating that CARDS has
877
      the potential to scale up for workload-intensive applications.
878
879   Table 7: Ablation study on the batch sizes of CARDS, evaluated by Mistral 7B (Jiang et al., 2023) on
880   HH-RLHF test set. Batch sizes greater than 1 slightly compromise the segmentation accuracy for
881   parallelization.

      Ultimately, the parallelization problem may be addressed by the iteration-level batching (Yu et al., 2022).
889   This technique eliminates the need for re-padding when the length of one response within a batch changes.
890   Specifically, the batch size dynamically adjusts: if one response within a batch is completed, that response
891   is excluded from the batch. While iteration-level batching can significantly reduce padding overhead, it may
892   introduce instability in GPU memory usage. We plan to continue integrating this technique into the CARDS
      framework in future work.

      B.6      D ISCUSSION ON R EWARD M ODEL ACCURACY
896   The effectiveness of CARDS is intrinsically linked to the accuracy of the reward models (RMs), as it aligns
897   the LLM to favor outputs that RMs rate highly. This dependency on RMs is a common limitation across many
898   alignment methods, including PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) and ARGS (Khanov et al., 2024). However, CARDS
899   offers a notable advantage in mitigating this limitation through its flexibility in selecting different reward models.
      The proposed framework is designed to seamlessly adapt to more powerful scoring models without necessitating
900
      fine-tuning. Furthermore, extensive experiments involving diverse reward models (Appendix C.7) demonstrate
901   that CARDS can achieve promising results when utilizing different or even less robust reward models.
902
903   B.7      D ISCUSSION ON W EAK - TO - STRONG A LIGNMENT
904
905   There are increasing interests in the field of weak-to-strong generalization (Burns et al., 2024), which focuses on
906
      the problem of aligning large and powerful base models with small and restricted supervision. In the context of
      LLM alignment, this problem is equivalent to using a small RM to align a large LLM. We provide additional
907   experiments on this problem, using a small 3B RM7 to align a large 7B LLM. The results are shown in Table 8.
908   CARDS outperforms the compared baseline in both alignment ratings and efficiency, strongly supporting that
909   CARDS is adaptive to smaller RMs and has the potential for weak-to-strong alignment.
