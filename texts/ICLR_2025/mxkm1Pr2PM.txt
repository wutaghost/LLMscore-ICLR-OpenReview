007                                              A BSTRACT
008
009            In current graph neural networks (GNNs), it is a common practice to apply a
010            pre-defined message passing heuristics to all graph data, even though the stereo-
011            typical relational inductive bias (e.g., graph heat diffusion) might not fit the un-
012            seen graph topology. Such gross simplification might be responsible for the lack
013            of an in-depth understanding of graph learning principles, which challenges us to
014            push the boundary from crafting application-specific GNNs to embracing a “meta-
015            learning” paradigm. In this work, we ratchet the gear of GNN another notch for-
016
               ward by formulating GNN as a mean field game, that is, the best learning outcome
               occurs at the Nash-equilibrium when the learned graph inference rationale allows
017
               each graph node to find what the best feature representations is for not only the
018
               individual node but also the entire graph.
019
               We formulate the search for novel GNN mechanism into a variational framework
020
               of mean-field control (MFC) problem, where the optimal relational inductive bias
021            is essentially a graph mapping of control patterns associated with mean-field in-
022            formation dynamics. Specifically, we seek for the topology-adaptive control func-
023            tion of transportation mobility (controlling information exchange throughout the
024            graph) and reaction mobility (controlling feature representation learning on each
025            node), on the fly, which allows us to uncover the most suitable learning mechanism
026            for a GNN instance by solving an MFC variational problem through the lens of
027            Hamiltonian flows. In this context, our variational framework brings together ex-
028            isting GNN models into various mean-field games with distinct equilibrium states,
029
               each characterized by the learned control patterns. Furthermore, we present an ag-
               nostic end-to-end deep model, coined Nash-GNN (in honor of Nobel laureate Dr.
030
               John Nash), to jointly identify the inductive bias and fine-tune the GNN hyper-
031
               parameters on top of the elucidated learning mechanism. Nash-GNN has achieved
032            SOTA performance on diverse graph data including popular benchmark datasets
033            and human connectomes. More importantly, the mathematical insight of MFC
034            framework provides a new window to understand the foundational principles of
035            graph learning as an interactive dynamical system, which allows us to reshape the
036            idea of designing next-generation GNN models.

      We live in a world of complex systems where individual objects are intricately connected (Zeng
042   et al., 2017). Since graph is a powerful object to model the object-to-object relationship in the
043   complex system (Smith & Johnson, 2020), graph neural networks (GNNs) that perform machine
044   learning on graph data have been successfully deployed in various fields of science and engineering,
045   including social network analysis (Hamilton et al., 2017a), recommendation systems (Ying et al.,
046   2018), biochemical engineering (Kearnes et al., 2016), knowledge graph (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018),
047   traffic flow prediction (Ma et al., 2020), drug repurposing (Goh et al., 2017), etc.
048   Despite various GNN instances, their machine learning backbones share many common compo-
049   nents. For example, the learning process typically consists of (1) a feature representation module for
050   individual graph nodes and (2) a message passing mechanism to disseminate these feature represen-
051   tations across the graph (Wu et al., 2020). In addition, the inductive biases – In the realm of GNN,
052   relational inductive bias refers to the inherent assumptions and biases (e.g., permutation invariance
053   and message passing) in the model design that leverages the structure and properties of graphs to
      improve learning and generalization. – are critical in all GNNs which are closely associated with

055   plicit relationships between graph nodes (encoded in an adjacency matrix), regardless of using graph
056   convolution network (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2017) or Transformer backbone (Ma et al., 2023).

063   adaptive channel mixing (Luan et al., 2022) and directly measuring the degree of graph heterophily
064   (Zhu et al., 2021). Another well-known challenge in GNNs is the over-smoothing issue, due to ex-

      (Wang et al., 2022; Rusch et al., 2022; Choi et al., 2023; Thorpe et al., 2022; Brandstetter et al.,
070
      2021; Eliasof et al., 2021), which are essentially derived from discretizations of different PDEs.

072   issue. For example, ResNet-based machine learning backbones have been integrated into GNNs (Li
073   et al., 2018; 2019), alongside the adoption of Transformer backbone to efficiently capture global fea-
074   ture representations without the restriction of step-by-step traversal on the graph (Kim et al., 2022;
075   Yun et al., 2019).

102   To establish the physics-informed framework generalizing GNNs instances, we conceptualize the
103   learning process in GNNs as a dynamical system of a large number of particles (i.e., graph nodes),
104   where each particle solves its own feature representation learning problem by taking other particles’
105   learning results into account. This conceptualization is supported by recent works that frame the
106   feature representation learning as a dynamic process of graph heat diffusion (Chamberlain et al.,
107   2021; Dan et al., 2023). By regarding the feature vector on each node as the potential energy, we
      further hypothesize that the system dynamics follows the second law of thermodynamics (Onsager,

      1949): energy tends to evolve in the direction where the negative entropy functional dissipates most
109   rapidly. This dissipative nature is given by Onsager’s reciprocal relations (Onsager, 1949), simply
110   stating that “Heat does not spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter body (Cengel & Boles,
111   2010)”.
112
      Following this notion, we propose to characterize the collective behaviors of simultaneous message
113
      passing and feature representation learning within GNNs based on the second law of thermody-
114
      namics, where the energy dissipation pattern is determined by a selected (learnable) “free energy
115   functional”. Inspired by the mean-field theory in physics (Lasry & Lions, 2007), we further con-
116   ceptualize that the dynamic process of graph feature representation learning forms a gradient flow
117   controlled by the graph-adaptive “free energy functional”. In this context, the most promising learn-
118   ing mechanism (aka. relational inductive bias in GNNs) for the specific graph data is characterized
119   by an optimal transport from the initial feature representations to an Nash-equilibrium state (depend-
120   ing on the down-stream learning tasks), where each graph node finds the best feature presentation
121   for itself and the entire graph.
122   Taken together, we present a variational framework of mean-field control (MFC) problem to achieve
123   the physics-informed learning paradigm of designing novel GNN mechanisms, where the optimal re-
124   lational inductive bias is essentially the mean-field control pattern associated with underlying graph
125
      data. Specifically, the MFC control pattern consists of two functions of kinetic dynamics: (1) a func-
      tion of transportation mobility for controlling node-to-node information exchange and (2) a function
126
      of reaction mobility for learning the feature representation on each node. These two mobility func-
127
      tions determine the characteristic control pattern that yields relation inductive bias (message passing
128   and representation learning) within the specific GNN instance, by shaping the kinetic dynamics of
129   underlying “free energy functional”. Since different graph data forms a unique dynamical system
130   with distinct transportation and reaction functions, our proposed physics-informed learning frame-
131   work for GNN, coined as Nash-GNN (name after Nash equilibrium), integrates two hierarchical
132   machine learning modules at both the mechanism and model instance levels. At the mechanism
133   level, we seek the most suitable control patterns that shape the gradient flow in the MFC variational
134   problem. Furthermore, we derive Hamiltonian flows for the governing equation of the underlying
135   MFC problem. As multiple lines of work have demonstrated that GNN is equivalent to an under-
136
      lying PDE (Zhao et al., 2024; Dan et al., 2023; Chamberlain et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022), the
      Hamiltonian flow becomes a stepping stone, which allows us to link the abstract learning of GNN
137
      mechanism in the variational framework and the fine-tuning hyper-parameters at the GNN model
138
      instance level. The outcome of our work is an end-to-end deep model that jointly identifies the most
139   suitable relational inductive bias and refines hyper-parameters for the corresponding GNN instance.
140
141   The major technical contributions are three-fold. First, we present a physics-informed learning
142   framework for GNN that crafts the most suitable GNN model while performing machine learning
143   on graph data. Second, we integrate the theory of mean-field control into graph neural networks
144   which not only offers an in-depth understanding of GNNs but also provides a general guideline for
145   developing deep models for unseen graph data. Third, we present a practical end-to-end solution,
146   based on Hamiltonian flows, to customize the best GNN model for the underlying graph data. In
147   addition to the comprehensive evaluation on benchmark datasets, we explore the foundational prin-
148
      ciples of graph learning as an interactive dynamical system, which is valuable for the conceptual
      framework of developing future GNN models.

532   4    C ONCLUSIONS
533
      In this work, we embarked on a new abstract learning framework for GNN to customize graph neu-
534   ral networks for various graph-based machine learning tasks. We integrate the theory of mean-field
535   control into GNNs to enhance our understanding and guide the development of deep models for
536   new graph datasets. We also provide an end-to-end solution using Hamiltonian flows to jointly learn
537   suitable inductive bias for GNN model and fit the customized GNN model to the underlying graph
538   data. Our approach is thoroughly evaluated on standard benchmark datasets, and we explore fun-
539   damental principles of graph learning as an interactive dynamical system, which not only advances
      GNN understanding but also contributes to the broader field of graph-based machine learning.

      Setup of mean-field game for GNN. Following the notion of mean-field game, each graph node is
925   acting as an agent. The game is to find the best feature presentations for all graph nodes that mini-
926   mize the loss function in GNN. In a mean field game, each agent (aka. graph node) makes decisions
927   based on both their individual state and the aggregate effect of the states and actions of all other
928   agents, often referred to as the “mean field.” The primary goal is to find a Nash equilibrium, which
929   is a strategic decision-making in very large populations of interacting agents such that no agent can
930   benefit by changing their strategy while others keep theirs unchanged. Mathematically, mean field
931   games often involve solving coupled partial differential equations such as Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman
932   (HJB) equation which describes the optimal control problem for the evolution of the distribution of
933
      agents’ states over time. In our work, we introduce mean-field game and mean-field control (MFC)
      into GNN, as described below.
934
935   A MFC framework for designing a novel GNN model. First, we extend the heat equation to a graph-
936   based reaction-diffusion model, where the system behavior is determined by a mobility functional
937   (Θ1 ) and reaction functional (Θ2 ). Second, we follow the recent work of MFC (Li et al., 2022) to
938   define the main variational problem, consisting of a metric space (Eq. 6) and gradient flow (Eq.
939
      7). After that, we study the critical point of the variational problem, yielding a Hamiltonian flow in
      MFC problem (Proposition 2).
940
941   Takeaway. We formulate the dynamic process of graph feature representation as a mean-field game
942   where the game policy is defined in a mean-field control perspective. In the real application, our
943   model simultaneously (1) crafts GNN model instance by identifying the most appropriate game pol-
944   icy (i.e., derive the instance of mobility functional Θ1 and reaction functional Θ2 ), and (2) optimizes
945
      GNN instance using Hamiltonian flow.
