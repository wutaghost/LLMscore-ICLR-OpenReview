011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The rising interest in decentralized data and privacy protection has led to the emer-
014            gence of Federated Learning. Many studies have compared federated training with
015
               classical training approaches using centralized data and found from experiments
               that models trained in a federated setup with equal resources perform poorly on
016
               tasks. However, these studies have generally been empirical and have not explored
017
               the performance gap further from a theoretical perspective. The lack of theoretical
018            understanding prevents figuring out whether federated algorithms are necessarily
019            inferior to centralized algorithms in performance and how large this gap is accord-
020            ing to the training settings. Also, it hinders identifying valid ways to close this
021            performance distance. This paper fills this theoretical gap by formulating feder-
022            ated training as an SGD (Stochastic Gradient Descent) optimization problem over
023            decentralized data and defining the performance gap within the PAC-Bayes (Prob-
024            ably Approximately Correct Bayesian) framework. Through theoretical analysis,
025            we derive non-vacuous bounds on this performance gap, revealing that the dif-
026
               ference in generalization performance necessarily exists when training resources
               are equal for both training setups and that variations in the training parameters
027
               affect the gap. Moreover, we also prove that the complete elimination of the per-
028
               formance gap is only possible by introducing new clients or adding new data to
029            existing clients. Advantages in other training resources are not feasible for closing
030            the gap, such as giving larger models or more communication rounds to federated
031            scenarios. Our theoretical findings are validated by extensive experimental results
032            from different model architectures and datasets.

035   1   INTRODUCTION
036
037   Classical deep learning algorithms are typically performed in centralized settings (LeCun et al.,
038   1998; He et al., 2016; Vaswani et al., 2017). Specifically, deep neural networks are trained with
039
      massive amounts of data on servers equipped with strong computation power. Enormous research
      and projects have proven this training setup to work well. For example, Large Language Models
040
      (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Lieber et al., 2021; Black et al., 2022; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Thoppilan
041
      et al., 2022), which have recently received significant attention due to their impressive performance
042   on various tasks, are generally trained with the centralized setup. However, an inherent limitation
043   of this approach is the imperative centralization of training data (Chen et al., 2023). In reality,
044   the majority of data is generated and stored in a distributed manner. If data containing sensitive
045   information is centralized, the privacy of participating parties will likely be compromised. The
046   challenge of expanding data size while protecting data privacy has led to the emergence of a new
047   type of learning methods that exploit training with distributed data. One such popular method is
048   called Federated Learning (McMahan et al., 2017; Zhuang et al., 2021; Karimireddy et al., 2020;
049   2021; Tang et al., 2022). In the training scenario of Federated learning, the training data is preserved
050
      on participating clients, and multiple clients collaborate with a central server to train a model without
      sharing data (Li et al., 2021).
051
052   The introduction of federated training effectively alleviates the privacy problem, but there is no
053   perfect solution (AbdulRahman et al., 2020). By comparing the two types of training setups, many
      studies have found that under equal training resources, the models trained in a federated scenario


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      do not perform as well as models trained in a centralized scenario in test datasets or downstream
055   tasks (Elnakib et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2018), which drastically hinders the broad application of
056   federated learning. Notably, this conclusion was established through empirical evidence, and the
057   theoretical aspect has yet to be fully explored (Garst et al., 2023; Mar’i et al., 2023). The lack of
058   theoretical understanding has resulted in long-term arguments on the existence of the performance
059   gap (Drainakis et al., 2023). Also, it prevents the identification of appropriate ways to close this gap.
060   Significant resources have been wasted on repeated experiments in search of promising directions.
061   In this paper, we re-visit the question: Given the same model, training data, and total training
062   compute, can federated learning catch up with or surpass centralized learning in terms of gener-
063   alization performance? To advance the theoretical underpinnings, we model two types of learn-
064   ing as Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) (eon Bottou, 1998; Sutskever et al., 2013) optimization
065   problems on centralized and decentralized data, respectively, and establish PAC-Bayes (Probably
066   Approximately Correct Bayesian) bounds (McAllester, 1998; 1999) on the generalization error of
067   the models trained in each training setup. Since the generalization bound is usually considered an
068   essential index of the generalization ability of the learning algorithm, the performance gap is for-
069
      mulated as the distance between two generalization bounds. By analyzing this distance equation,
      we find that the number of clients positively correlates with the performance gap and derive non-
070
      vacuous lower and upper bounds on the performance gap. These bounds theoretically show that the
071
      performance gap will necessarily exist under equivalent training conditions and is affected by the
072   training settings. Therefore, completely bridging the performance gap requires federated scenarios
073   to be provided with more training resources. Following this idea, we theoretically prove that the
074   complete close of the performance gap is only possible by incorporating new clients or adding data
075   to existing clients. In addition to theoretical analyses, we also empirically explore the performance
076   gap by conducting extensive experiments. To ensure our theoretical findings can be generalized to
077   different models and data, we chose two popular architectures, ResNet (He et al., 2016) and Vision
078   Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), and collected their training and testing results on two stan-
079   dard datasets, CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and Mini-ImageNet (Vinyals et al., 2016; Deng
080
      et al., 2009). The experimental data is found to be closely aligned with our theoretical conclusions.
081   In summary, the key contributions of our paper are shown below:
082
083         1. We introduce a novel theoretical perspective to understand the performance gap between
084            centralized and federated training, defining this gap as the distance between the PAC-Bayes
085            generalization bounds of two scenarios.
086         2. We prove that the performance gap monotonically increases with the number of clients
087            and establish non-vacuous lower and upper bounds on this gap, demonstrating that the
088            gap inevitably exists when two training scenarios are provided with equivalent training
089            resources. Our analysis also reveals the influence of training settings on this gap.
090         3. We derive that only introducing new clients or adding data to existing clients are possible to
091            completely bridge the performance gap. Other approaches, such as scaling up model size
092            or increasing communication rounds, cannot fully close this gap.
093         4. Extensive experiments on different model architectures and datasets validate the correct-
094            ness of our theoretical results.
095

098   mance gap in Section 4, followed by the empirical validation of our theoretical findings in Section 5.
099   Finally, we give a conclusion of the paper in Section 6. The Appendix presents the details omitted

      from the main manuscript.

104   2.1   F EDERATED L EARNING
105
106   Federated learning is a class of distributed learning methods proposed for collaborative model train-
107   ing without compromising privacy (AbdulRahman et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021). The benchmark
      algorithm for federated learning is Federated Averaging (FedAvg) (McMahan et al., 2017). This


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      algorithm first introduces the scenario of federated learning, consisting of massive decentralized
109   clients and a central server that establishes communications with all clients. During training, mul-
110   tiple clients train models received from the server using local training data, and then the server
111   aggregates the training updates received from these clients to update the model. Client privacy is
112   protected as no local data is shared during training. In recent years, as people have become aware
113   of the importance of data privacy for security, many research works related to federated learning
114   have emerged (Zhuang et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2018; Tran et al., 2019). These
115   works generally hold the impression that centralized learning must perform better than federated
116   learning, and many of them focus on proposing advanced federated algorithms to catch up with the
117
      centralized baseline (Karimireddy et al., 2021; Zhuang et al., 2021). However, the correctness of
      this impression has not been fully explored from a theoretical aspect. Our work fills this gap and
118
      identifies generic strategies that can bridge the gap between the two training setups.
119
120
121
      2.2   C OMPARE F EDERATED LEARNING WITH C ENTRALIZED L EARNING
122
      Since federated learning was proposed, there have been studies focusing on the comparison between
123   federated and centralized training. Some works aim to compare the performance of the models
124   trained in each training scenario. These comparative evaluations report that models trained in a
125   centralized setup generally outperform models trained in a federated setup across a variety of tasks
126   and datasets, such as MNIST (Peng et al., 2022; Mar’i et al., 2023), CIFAR-10 (Zhao et al., 2018),
127   and CICIDS2017 (Elnakib et al., 2023). Similar experimental results are also found in the federated
128   studies that adopt the centralized training results as one of the baselines (Zhuang et al., 2021). In
129   addition to performance comparison, there are comparisons on the training convergence rate. Unlike
130   the above studies, these studies show that federated algorithms can attain the same order or faster
131
      convergence rate than centralized algorithms (Karimireddy et al., 2020; 2021; Asad et al., 2021).
      Furthermore, a recent study by Drainakis et al. explores the differences between federated and
132
      centralized training from the perspectives of energy cost and bandwidth cost (Drainakis et al., 2023).
133
      However, most of these works have primarily offered observational insights based on empirical
134   evidence, especially those targeting the performance gap. The lack of theoretical underpinnings
135   has prevented researchers from explaining how the performance gap develops and proving to others
136   whether it necessarily exists. To address this shortcoming, we quantify the performance gap as a
137   bounded analytic solution and theoretically analyze it in this paper.
138
139   2.3   G ENERALIZATION B OUND FOR S TOCHASTIC A LGORITHMS
140
141   Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) (eon Bottou, 1998; Sutskever et al., 2013) is a foundational op-
142   timization method in machine learning (LeCun et al., 1998; Hinton & Salakhutdinov, 2006; Good-
143   fellow et al., 2014; McMahan et al., 2017; Tang et al., 2022). Extensive research has quantified the
144
      generalization abilities of stochastic algorithms through PAC-Bayes upper bounds (He et al., 2019;
      Mou et al., 2018; London, 2017; Pensia et al., 2018) and utilized these bounds to study different as-
145
      pects, including algorithm convergence (Mou et al., 2018; Pensia et al., 2018), training stability (Zhu
146
      et al., 2024), and hyper-parameter tuning strategies (He et al., 2019). The generalization bound also
147   plays an important role in research works related to federated learning (Yuan et al., 2021). Several
148   studies propose new training frameworks to tackle problems such as non-IID data distribution (Zhao
149   et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024b) and model personalization (Boroujeni et al., 2024; Achituve et al.,
150   2021; Vedadi et al., 2024) based on this bound. Moreover, this bound has been used to understand
151   the impact of the parameters (Sefidgaran et al., 2024) or the network structure (Sun et al., 2024a)
152   on generalization. However, existing works focus on using the generalization bound to analyze a
153   single learning regime, unconcerned about the difference between centralized and federated training
154   in generalization. We establish a theoretical expression for the generalization gap according to the
155
      distance between the generalization bounds of stochastic algorithms in both settings.

      In this section, we develop theoretical foundations for the performance gap between federated and

396

397   as long as equal training resources are provided for two scenarios. Therefore, if we still look forward
398   to federated training catching up with centralized training, the federated scenario has to be allowed
399   with an advantage in some training resources. Generally, increasing the data size and model size can
400   result in an improvement in model performance. For example, researchers have concluded scaling

      the related parameters n, m, d, and T in federated settings with a reasonable assumption to identify

      6   C ONCLUSION
531
      This paper re-studies the problem that models trained in federated setups do not perform as well as
532
      models trained in centralized setups, focusing on the theoretical exploration of this generalization
533
      gap and valid strategies to bridge it. By formulating the gap as the distance between the PAC-Bayes
534   generalization bounds of two scenarios, we derive non-vacuous bounds on this gap and find that
535   it is affected by the training settings and necessarily exists when both scenarios are allocated with
536   equivalent training resources. Therefore, we further consider the case that the federated scenario
537   holds an advantage in training resources and prove that the gap can be closed by introducing new
538   clients or adding data to existing clients, while strategies like increasing model size or communica-
539   tion rounds are not feasible. In addition, extensive experiments are conducted to empirically analyze
      the performance gap. The experimental results are fully aligned with our theoretical findings.
