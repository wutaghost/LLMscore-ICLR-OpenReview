009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) have demonstrated impressive performance
012            in short video understanding tasks but face great challenges when applied to long
013            video understanding. In contrast, Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit out-
014            standing capabilities in modeling long texts. Existing work attempts to address
015            this issue by introducing long video-text pairs during training. However, these ap-
016            proaches require substantial computational and data resources. In this paper, we
017            tackle the challenge of long video understanding from the perspective of context
018
               windows, aiming to apply LMMs to long video tasks without retraining on long
               video datasets. We first conduct an in-depth analysis of why pretrained LMMs
019
               struggle to understand lengthy video content, identifying that discrepancies be-
020
               tween visual and language modalities lead to different context windows for visual
021            and language tokens, making it difficult to directly extend the visual tokens to
022            match the language context window. Based on this, we propose to adapt LMMs
023            for long video understanding tasks by extending the visual context window, elim-
024            inating the need for retraining on large-scale long video datasets. To further miti-
025            gate the significant memory consumption caused by long sequences, we introduce
026            a progressive pooling inference strategy that selectively adjusts the spatial resolu-
027            tion of frame embeddings, reducing the number of visual tokens while retaining
028            important spatial information. Across multiple long video understanding bench-
029
               marks, our method consistently improves the performance as the number of video
               frames increases. On the MLVU benchmark, our method outperforms GPT-4o,
030
               even though our model size is only 7B. Additionally, in the 256-frame setting, our
031
               method reduces memory usage by approximately 45% compared to the baseline,
032            without introducing any performance loss.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large Multimodal Models (LMMs), built on pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) and
038   trained on massive image-text pairs, have shown remarkable capabilities in image understanding (Li
039   et al., 2023b; Gao et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Liu
040   et al., 2023a). Recently, by segmenting high-resolution images into multiple sub-images for input,
041   LMMs have not only improved in fine-grained image understanding but also demonstrated zero-shot
042   video understanding capabilities (Liu et al., 2024b; Yao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a). Despite these
043   advancements, current LMMs are still limited to short video understanding tasks and face difficulties
044
      when applied to long videos due to the excessive sequence lengths involved.
045   Several approaches (Li et al., 2023d; Jin et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024) have explored using vi-
046   sual resamplers to reduce the number of visual tokens, allowing the models to process more video
047   frames. However, this token reduction inevitably leads to a loss of critical information, negatively
048   affecting performance. Recent efforts (Xue et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c) have tackled this issue by
049   incorporating long video-text pair datasets during pre-training. However, this approach faces signif-
050
      icant challenges due to the high computational cost associated with the quadratic complexity of the
      attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017) and the scarcity of high-quality long video-text data.
051
052   To alleviate the high computational costs and data collection challenges associated with long video
053   understanding, we approach the problem from the perspective of the context window. First, we
      observe that in recent open-source LMMs, language decoders generally support longer language

088   Building on this observation, we propose to extend the commonly used language context window
089   extension method, YaRN (Peng et al., 2024), to LMMs for long video understanding. Specifically,
090   we redefine the scaling factor of the base frequency in positional embeddings as the ratio of the
091   visual context window to the target context window. By modulating the rotational frequency of the
092
      positional embeddings, we expand the effective range of the visual context window, enabling LMMs
      to handle longer video sequences. It’s important to note that extending the visual context window
093
      does not directly narrow the modality gap between visual and language embeddings.
094
095   Additionally, to alleviate the rapid memory consumption caused by long sequences, we propose a
096   progressive pooling strategy to handle video frame embeddings. Specifically, considering the re-
097   dundancy between consecutive frames in the same event, such as a static background, we uniformly
098
      sample the video frames into multiple groups. We assume that each group represents an event, and
      we control the group size through hyperparameters. In each group, the first frame’s embedding re-
099
      tains a higher spatial resolution, while the subsequent frames are pooled with a larger stride to lower
100
      resolutions. We believe the first frame preserves rich spatial, fine-grained information compared to
101   the other frames within the group, while the remaining frames reduce intra-group redundancy. This
102   approach minimizes the loss of spatial information while reducing the number of visual tokens.
103
104
      Across multiple long video understanding benchmarks, our method consistently improves perfor-
      mance as the number of video frames increases. Notably, on the MLVU benchmark (Zhou et al.,
105
      2024), our method outperforms GPT-4o. Most importantly, our approach does not require retraining,
106
      allowing it to benefit from continuous advancements in open-source LMMs.

147   2.2   R ELATED WORK
148
      Large Multimodal Models LMMs typically consist of a visual encoder, a pre-trained LLM, and a
149
      modality projection module that converts visual content into token sequences for the LLM. Leverag-
150   ing large amounts of high-quality image-text paired data, LMMs have shown strong capabilities in
151   image understanding (Li et al., 2023b; Gao et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Ye et al.,
152   2023; Li et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023a; 2024b; Yao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a). By sampling
153   videos into multiple frames, LMMs can extend to video understanding tasks (Xu et al., 2024; Chen
154   et al., 2023a; Maaz et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023c; 2024b). Examples include Video-
155   ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024), VideoChat2 (Li et al., 2024b), and PLLaVA (Xu et al., 2024), which
156   enhance LMMs’ video understanding through high-quality data and fine-tuning methods. However,
157   these methods face challenges with long videos due to the large number of visual tokens generated
158
      per frame.
159   To address this, visual token compression methods have been proposed (Li et al., 2023d; Jin et al.,
160   2023; Song et al., 2024). For instance, LLaMA-VID (Li et al., 2023d) uses only two tokens per
161   frame, and MovieChat (Song et al., 2024) introduces a memory mechanism to compress long video
      tokens into a fixed size. These methods, however, often result in information loss.

182   In recent work, LongVILA (Xue et al., 2024) attempted to introduce long video-text pairs into the

      training of LMMs to expand the context window size. LongVA (Zhang et al., 2024) expands the

184

186
187   Context Window Extension for LLMs The fixed context length during pre-training limits the infer-
188   ence performance of language models in scenarios involving long sequence inputs. To address this
189
      issue, researchers have proposed a series of RoPE-based language positional embedding extension
      methods, such as Position Interpolation (PI) (Chen et al., 2023b; kaiokendev, 2023), NTK Interpola-
190
      tion (bloc97, 2023), and YaRN (Peng et al., 2024). Specifically, PI scales the positions of long texts
191
      that exceed the context window down to the original window size. However, it compresses distances
192   between nearby tokens, which can degrade performance. NTK interpolation extends the context
193   window by adjusting the rotational speed of RoPE through reducing the base frequency. Building
194   upon NTK interpolation, YaRN further distinguishes between high-frequency and low-frequency
195   information to accommodate different RoPE embeddings.

      3.2   P ROGRESSIVE P OOLING
249   In this section, we discuss another factor that limits the performance of long video understanding:
250   memory constraints. Taking LLaVA-OneVision as an example, given a video V uniformly sampled
251   into N video frames, the visual encoder and multimodal projection module process these frames to
252   obtain the video sequence embeddings Fv ∈ RN ×729×d . To reduce the number of visual tokens,
253   LLaVA-OneVision performs bilinear pooling with a stride of 2 on each video frame embedding,
254   which then serves as the input to the LLM decoder. However, even after bilinear pooling, a video
255   sequence of 256 frames generates 50,176 tokens.
256   Long sequences contribute to high memory consumption. Inference in LMMs can be divided into
257   two stages: prefill and decoding. During the prefill stage, all visual tokens are projected into a
258   high-dimensional space and stored as KVCache for efficient decoding later. This incurs substantial
259   memory costs. Even with bilinear pooling, processing 256 frames generates 50,176 tokens, requiring
260   approximately 73 GB of GPU memory. This greatly limits the deployment of LMMs for long video
261   understanding.
262   To alleviate excessive memory consumption, we propose a progressive pooling strategy. As shown
263   in Figure 3, we first uniformly divide the video sequence embeddings Fv into multiple groups, with
264   a division stride defined as K. We assume that each group represents an event. Considering the
265   redundancy between consecutive frames in the same event, such as a static background, we retain
266   only the first frame of each group at a higher spatial resolution. The remaining frames within each
267   group are stored at a lower spatial resolution using a larger pooling stride. Specifically, the video
268   sequence embeddings Fv are divided into multiple groups, each containing K frames, resulting in a

302   4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETTING
303
304   We evaluate the long video understanding capabilities of our method on three key benchmarks:
305   VideoMME (Fu et al., 2024), MLVU (Zhou et al., 2024), and LongVideoBench (Wu et al., 2024).
306   VideoMME is a widely used benchmark for assessing the ability of LMMs to handle long videos
307   in real-world scenarios. It divides the test set into three subsets based on video length: short videos
308   (< 2 minutes), medium-length videos (4 to 15 minutes), and long videos (30 to 60 minutes), with
309   durations ranging from 11 seconds to 1 hour.
310   MLVU offers a diverse collection of video lengths, types, and evaluation tasks. It includes long
311   video understanding tasks (TR: Topic Reasoning, AR: Anomaly Recognition), single-detail long
312   video understanding tasks (NQA: Needle QA, ER: Ego Reasoning, PQA: Plot QA), and multi-detail
313   long video understanding tasks (AO: Action Order, AC: Action Count). The benchmark includes
314   videos of various types, such as movies, surveillance footage, egocentric videos, cartoons, and game
315   videos, with lengths ranging from 3 minutes to over 2 hours.
316   LongVideoBench focuses on long-span understanding, particularly on referential reasoning prob-
317   lems that depend on long-frame inputs and cannot be resolved using only a single or sparse frames. It
318   evaluates videos of varying lengths, including (8s, 15s], (15s, 60s], (180s, 600s], and (900s, 3600s].

      In this paper, we address the long video understanding issue from the perspective of context win-
479
      dows, effectively avoiding the resource consumption associated with training from scratch. By
480   redefining the effective context window of LMMs into visual and language context windows, we
481   propose the visual context window extension. This approach allows LMMs trained on short videos
482   to be applied to long video understanding tasks without fine-tuning. Additionally, we introduce a
483   progressive pooling strategy to mitigate memory consumption issues caused by long sequences. In
484   a 256-frame setting, this strategy reduces memory usage by approximately 45% without introducing
485   any performance loss. We hope this work will advance research in long video understanding and
      provide insights for the design of future long video understanding models.
