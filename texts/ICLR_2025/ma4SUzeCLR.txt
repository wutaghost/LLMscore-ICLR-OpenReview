009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            When designing math word problems, teachers must ensure the clarity and preci-
012            sion of the question to avoid multiple interpretations and unanswerable situations,
013            thereby maintaining consistent grading standards and effectiveness. We address
014            these issues to provide comprehensive support to teachers in creating clear, solv-
015            able, and formal math word problems. In this paper, we present MathError, a
016            dataset of real-world math word problems annotated with error types to inves-
017            tigate the need for question correction. Our work explores how large language
018            models (LLMs) can assist teachers in detecting problematic questions to support
019
               math word problem design in scenarios with limited data, simulating real-world
               conditions with minimal training samples. Preliminary results demonstrate the
020
               models’ capabilities in detecting problematic questions and identify areas for fur-
021
               ther research and development in educational applications.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      When coming up with exam questions, teachers must ensure that the questions are clear and pre-
027
      cise. This prevents students from misunderstanding the questions, which could lead to inconsistent
028
      grading standards and render the questions ineffective. This is particularly important for math word
029   problems where there should usually be only one correct answer. However, when questions are for-
030   mulated, there may be blind spots or minor oversights that lead to misinterpretations by students,
031   or a lack of crucial details, making immediate comprehension difficult. For example, “The original
032   price of an apple is 2 dollars. It has been discounted twice: the first discount is 10%, and the second
033   discount is 5%. What is the current price of the apple?” It is unclear whether the second discount
034   is to be applied to the original price or to the price after the first discount. Consequently, this de-
035   scription may cause confusion and uncertainty for students. It is therefore essential to construct a
036   system that assists teachers in ensuring that the questions do not have multiple interpretations or are
037
      unanswerable.
038   Several studies have investigated situations where questions are unanswerable. Questions can
039   be unanswerable in the following scenarios: (1) The knowledge sources are incomplete, failing
040   to cover all the necessary facts required to answer the question (Patidar et al., 2023); (2) User-
041   generated questions are poorly formatted, are missing entities or predicates, or contain ungrammati-
042   cal phrases (Faustini et al., 2023); (3) The question is ambiguous and thus allows for more than one
043
      interpretation (Min et al., 2020); (4) Details in the question are inconsistent with the facts (Yen et al.,
      2021). Unlike previous studies that focus on knowledge base question answering or open-domain
044
      questions, we address multiple interpretations and unanswerable issues in math word problems.
045
      Specifically, we seek to detect the following conditions to support teachers in designing math word
046   problems.
047
048          • Questions may result in multiple or unintended solutions due to imprecise descriptions,
049
               missing conditions or constraints, or unclear relationships between multiple values.
             • Questions may be unanswerable as they contain unclear terms or noticeable omissions.
050
051   This issue could be influenced by language, as imprecise descriptions, missing conditions, and un-
052   clear relationships between values may manifest differently across languages. Additionally, the
053   complexity of error types can vary depending on the difficulty level of the math problems. In this
      pilot study, we focus on elementary-level math word problems presented in Chinese.

054
      Sun et al. (2024) have explored similar issues, emphasizing the importance of detecting problem-
055   atic math word problems by constructing a dataset of unanswerable questions with predefined error
056   types. This highlights the growing attention to the task of identifying and addressing challenges in
057   math word problem design. However, while their study focuses on error types specifically created
058   for their experiment, our work shifts towards detecting naturally occurring errors in real math word
059   problems. In addition, we investigate more nuanced issues, such as multiple interpretations or un-
060   intended solutions, which can be challenging for models to identify. Hence, we extend the existing
061   Chinese math word problem dataset—Math23K (Wang et al., 2017)—with error type annotations.
062   Math23K questions provide rich textual descriptions that naturally meet our requirements.
063   Given the remarkable capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in language understanding and
064   generation, recent studies use LLMs to generate test questions for student practice (Gonzalez et al.,
065   2023; Feng et al., 2024; Song et al., 2023). The pedagogical ability of LLMs in mathematics educa-
066   tion has also been studied (Yen & Hsu, 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Daheim et al., 2024). Some works
067   have investigated the role of LLMs in assisting teachers with tasks such as distractor generation for
068   math multiple-choice questions (Feng et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; Hunter McNichols et al., 2024).
069
      Liu et al. (2023) have shown that modeling ambiguity remains a significant challenge for LLMs, re-
      inforcing the importance of developing methods to detect and address these problems. However, the
070
      capability of LLMs to recognize errors in math word problems and disentangle potential meanings
071
      is rarely explored. Thus, this work explores the capability of LLMs in identifying problematic math
072   word problems. We further investigate a self-optimizing approach that allows the model to learn
073   from its mistakes. By iteratively reflecting on the wrong predictions, the model refines instructions
074   and demonstrations within the prompt, improving performance in detecting error types.
075
      To sum up, the contributions of our work are threefold: (1) We assist teachers in ensuring the clarity
076
      of math word problems by detecting errors in question statements that can lead to several interpreta-
077
      tions or render the problems unanswerable. (2) We present the MathError dataset,1 which is designed
078   for detecting errors in the statements of math word problems, to facilitate the investigation of the
079   need for correcting problematic questions. (3) We explore a self-optimizing framework where the
080   model iteratively refines its instructions and demonstrations through a reflection mechanism. This
081   approach simulates real-world scenarios where data is scarce by utilizing only a few examples, offer-
082   ing a preliminary solution to the challenge of error detection in math word problems. Experimental
083   results show that the prompts refined by our reflection mechanism yield better performance.

085   2       R ELATED W ORK
086
087   Ambiguous and Unanswerable Questions: There are several types of ambiguity: lexical, syntac-
088   tic, semantic, pragmatic, and anaphoric (Li et al., 2024). Numerous works address disambiguation
089   using methods such as syntactic and semantic parsing (Tanaka et al., 2007; Koller et al., 2008) or
090   coreference resolution (Kocijan et al., 2019). In question-answering applications, ambiguous user
091   queries lead to unanswerable queries. Methods have been developed to identify question answer-
092   ability (Zhang et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2019) and generate clarification questions (Zamani et al.,
093   2020; Krasheninnikov et al., 2022) or correct unanswerable questions (Yen et al., 2021). There has
094   also been growing interest in addressing ambiguity in math word problems. Sun et al. (2024) de-
095
      fine five different categories of unanswerable questions. Curated annotators modified answerable
      questions into unanswerable ones based on the categories. By contrast, we construct a dataset by
096
      annotating error types of real-world questions rather than modifying questions into unanswerable
097
      forms based on specific categories, which may make it difficult for models to identify specific pat-
098   terns to determine whether a question contains errors. Consequently, our dataset comprises not only
099   unanswerable questions but also questions with multiple possible solutions.
100
101
      Self-Optimization with LLMs: LLMs have made significant advancements in producing coherent
      text and following given instructions (Wei et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022). Recently, methods
102
      have been investigated that elicit feedback from LLMs on self-generated solutions, enabling iterative
103
      improvement of outputs based on the feedback. Madaan et al. (2024) propose a framework that
104   iteratively refines the generated output via self-evaluation. Several studies explore the use of LLMs
105   for optimizing prompts. Zhou et al. (2022) employ the LLM to create instructions, select the proper
106   instructions based on accuracy, and instruct the LLM to generate a semantically similar variant.

110   feedback generated by LLMs to refine the model’s output (Chen et al., 2023; Ganguli et al., 2023;
111   Shinn et al., 2023). Inspired by these studies, we introduce a reflection mechanism to our framework

      The two conditions outlined in Section 1 lead to errors that could significantly impact the clarity and
117   accuracy of math word problems:
118
119             1. Multiple Interpretations (INTPN): The question allows for multiple possible interpreta-
120
                   tions, leading to more than one possible solution.
                2. Informal Wording (Informal): The wording of the question is not formal or is incomplete,
121
                   such as including unnecessary words or symbols, having noticeable omissions, or contain-
122
                   ing typographical errors, making the problem statement difficult to understand.
123             3. Unitless (Unit): The question does not specify the required unit, which may lead to confu-
124                sion about what measurement is expected.
125             4. Unclear Relationship (Rel): The description fails to clearly indicate the relationship be-
126                tween the values, leading to misunderstandings about the question’s meaning.
127             5. Calculation Error (Calc): The problem uses imprecise words to describe a mathematical
128                expression, for instance, making it unclear whether to perform multiplication or division
129                before addition or subtraction; this can cause students to calculate in the wrong order.
130   If a math word problem exhibits none of the issues mentioned above, it belongs to the None type.
131   Note that we focus extends beyond detecting ambiguities in math word problems. We aim to address
132   a challenge: ensuring that problem descriptions are formal and complete for use in official exami-
133   nations. Our goal is to support teachers in refining the clarity and precision of problem statements,
134   helping to eliminate informal language and incomplete details that could lead to misinterpretations.
135   As math word problem error types can be highly diverse, it is difficult to immediately identify all
136   possible types. The error type definitions and dataset construction are in Section 4.

140   Error Type Definition. Math23K comprises a total of 23,162 Chinese math word problems. To
141   establish an initial set of error types, we randomly sampled 200 questions, referred to as the initial
142   set, and categorized the errors present in these questions. We conducted a preliminary annotation
143   of problematic descriptions, after which we consolidated these initial error types by merging similar
144   ones. This process resulted in the identification and definition of five distinct error types. Yet, we
145
      are unsure whether the five error types are sufficient and whether they cover all possible errors.
      Additionally, we cannot guarantee the completeness of these error definitions. Thus, we established
146
      an iterative refinement annotation process to ensure the quality of the dataset annotation.
147
148   Iterative Refinement Annotation. We invited three annotators and split the entire dataset into four
149   parts, with each person responsible for the labeling of 5,815 samples.2 We labeled one of these parts
150   by ourselves. To ensure consistency and quality across the dataset, we conducted a quantitative
151
      evaluation of the annotators’ labeling accuracy using our pre-annotated set of 100 samples, which
      we refer to as the golden set. Of these, 23 samples contained math word problems with error state-
152
      ments, whereas 77 samples had no errors. To evaluate the correctness of the annotators’ labels, we
153
      divided the 100 samples from the golden set into five subsets, each containing 20 samples, ensuring
154   a balanced representation of error types. These golden sets were inserted into the subsets that the
155   annotators were to label.
156
157
      The annotation process was as follows. The annotators first labeled the initial subset of 20 samples
      from the golden set. These 20 samples were used to verify the annotators’ labeling correctness.
158
      Since these samples had gold labels, we assessed the macro F-score of each annotator’s results.
159
      Based on these results, we then provided further clarification and discussion on unclear aspects. An-

      5   P ROMPT R EFINEMENT THROUGH S ELF -O PTIMIZATION

248   Various prompting methods have been proposed to enhance LLM performance. With zero-shot or
249   few-shot learning (Brown et al., 2020), LLMs can tackle a range of tasks by providing a few ex-
250
      amples about the task. Chain-of-thought prompting (CoT) techniques (Wei et al., 2022b; Yasunaga
      et al., 2024) successfully guide models to solve complex problems through step-by-step reasoning.
251
      Thus, we utilize LLMs with appropriate prompts for error type detection. Most research typically
252
      involves humans carefully designing prompts to instruct LLMs in performing tasks. We pose the
253   question of whether allowing the model to independently understand the task objectives and reflect
254   on its execution results to adjust its instructions and few-shot examples can enhance the performance
255   of LLMs on this task. A mechanism may be needed to generate definitions that the model can un-
256   derstand and to provide suitable examples for specific error types that the model struggles with. We
257   propose a method to iteratively reflect on incorrect classification results. By refining error type defi-
258   nitions based on these reflections, the model articulates task details according to its comprehension.
259   Furthermore, the model selects proper examples to strengthen its reasoning abilities. Figure 1 (a)
260   shows an overview of the framework for prompt refinement through self-optimization (PRO).
261   PRO comprises a classifier Mc , a definition reflector Ms , an example reflector Me , an evaluator
262   fe , and a judge fj . Mc , Ms , and Me are the same LLM but with different prompts. All the prompt
263   templates used in this work are presented in Appendix B. For the self-optimization process, we
                                                    ′
264   partition our training set into sets D and D for demonstration and reflection, respectively. In PRO,

      After obtaining the initial classification results, we proceed to the self-optimization phase detailed
271   in Figure 1 (b). We set a maximum number of trials to iteratively refine S and update d through Ms
272   and Me , respectively. The evaluator fe measures the classification results using the macro-averaged
273   F-score, allowing us to judge the model’s performance in each round. For each t, incorrectly pre-
274   dicted questions are extracted for self-reflection. Formally, if the predicted error type yit does not
275   match the ground truth ŷit , the question qit is appended to reflection set R. Subsequently, the refined
276   definition s̃t of t is obtained as s̃t = Ms (st , R). Additionally, the demonstrations are updated by
277   extracting questions in R. Given R and the current demonstrations dt for t, we obtain the updated
278   demonstrations d˜t = Md (dt , R). s̃t and d˜t are used in the next round as st and dt , respectively.
279   Finally, to determine whether the updates to st and dt have converged, judge fj compares the dif-
280   ference in the reflection results between the previous and current rounds. We measure the difference
281   between the concatenation of st ⊕ dt and s̃t ⊕ d˜t using ROUGE-1 (Lin, 2004). If the ROUGE-1
282   score r = fj (st ⊕ dt , s̃t ⊕ d˜t ) is greater than the threshold T , we view it as convergence. In this
283   case, st and dt are not refined in the next round. If S and D converge, self-optimization ceases. To
284   determine which definitions and examples to use, we measure the performance of S and d in each
285   round using z = fe (ŷ, y): S and d from the highest-scoring round are used to instruct Mc to detect
286   error types in the test set. The number of max trials and T are set to 10 and 0.9, respectively.

293
      Since most questions in Math23k were of the None type, we randomly selected a subset of math
      word problems from this type. This resulted in a total of 4,766 math word problems in MathError. To
294
      simulate limited data availability, we identified patterns from sparse data and applied them to specific
295
      tasks. Specifically, we focused on detecting error types in math word problems, despite having few
296   such questions with errors. The data used for demonstration and reflection consisted of 15 and 30
297   math word problems, respectively. The remaining 4,721 math word problems were used for testing.
298   The demonstration set consisted of 3 questions for each error type, providing a foundation for the
299   initial model for inference. The reflection set, designed to refine and validate the model, included
300   2 questions for each error type and an additional 20 questions without errors. In the test set, the
301   number of questions for INTPN, Informal, Unit, Rel, Calc, and None were 131, 1,071, 411, 601, 62,
302   and 2,500, respectively.3 We utilized OpenAI’s API, in particular the gpt-3.5-turbo-0125
303   and gpt-4o models. The temperature was set to 0 to increase reproducibility.

      In this section, we formulate and discuss six research questions which we address by conducting
      corresponding experiments. The results reported below are based on the “directly classify” strategy
400
      for each method. This pilot study seeks to explore how in-context learning can be utilized to detect
401
      problematic questions. Hence, the first research question (RQ1) arises: Which of the commonly
402   used prompting methods—zero-shot, few-shot, or CoT prompting—yields the best performance in
403   detecting errors in math word problems?
404
405
      Impact of Different Prompting Methods: Table 5 presents the results of utilizing GPT-3.5 to detect
      error types using different prompting methods. The results indicate that few-shot prompting based
406
      on human-written definitions and examples outperforms the other prompting methods, although the
407
      difference is not statistically significant. We found that CoT prompting struggles with detecting
408   questions that have multiple interpretations. The analysis of the model’s reasoning output indicates
409   that CoT prompting often leads the model to infer a fixed interpretation of the question, overlooking
410   alternative interpretations. In contrast, GPT-3.5 with few-shot prompting demonstrates promising
411   performance in detecting questions with multiple interpretations, informal wording, and unclear
412   relationships. These error types are critical for teachers when refining problem design, as they
413   significantly influence the clarity and validity of the questions posed to students.
414   In the experiments in Table 3, we set error type detection as multilabel classification. However, the
415   model can also perform binary classification for each error type by querying whether a specific math
416   word problem contains that particular error. This raises the second research question (RQ2): Which
417   method for error type detection is more suitable: multilabel classification or binary classification?
418
      Multilabel Classification vs. Binary Classification: Table 6 shows the results when treating error
419
      type detection as multilabel or binary classification. We used the GPT-3.5 model with zero-shot
420   prompting. The strategies in all settings are “Directly classify”. We find that multilabel classification
421   outperforms binary classification. Additionally, binary classification causes the model to tend to
422   view every math word problem as erroneous. A possible reason is that the prompt for multi-label
423   classification provides definitions of all types, which helps the model better understand the error
424   types compared to binary classification. However, as shown in Table 6, the performance when using
425   the GPT-3.5 definitions is better than that using the human-written definitions. This leads to the
426   third research question (RQ3): Should the definitions and examples in this task be generated by the
427   model itself or provided by humans?
428   Human-Written vs. Model-Generated Prompts: Table 7 presents the results when using GPT-3.5
429   with few-shot prompting with various settings to confirm which definitions and examples are bet-
430   ter. The overall best performance is achieved when human definitions are written and paired with
431   human-written examples. However, as shown in Table 7, the average performance in detecting five
      error types using GPT-3.5-generated definitions is better than that using human-written definitions.

515   8       C ONCLUSION
516
517   Recent years have witnessed a surge of work on ambiguous and unanswerable questions. In contrast
518   to previous studies, this paper focuses on detecting issues in math word problems that can lead to
519   multiple solutions or render them unanswerable. These issues arise from different interpretations
520   and misunderstandings due to imprecise problem descriptions or missing information needed for
521   problem-solving. We present a task on math word problem design support and construct MathError,
522
      the first human-annotated dataset, to explore the method of error type detection. We explore prompt
      refinement through self-optimization (PRO) to instruct an LLM to adapt to the given task. We inves-
523
      tigate whether definitions for error types and corresponding few-shot examples are more effectively
524
      provided by humans or models. The results show that machine-generated definitions of error types,
525   supplemented by human-written examples, enhance effectiveness in error type detection. During
526   the self-optimization process, we modify the definitions based on the classification errors of the data
527   retained from the training set, and allow the LLM to select which examples to add. However, ac-
528   curately identifying the errors in a math word problem is still challenging; more advanced methods
529   are left as future work. We also plan to explore methods for correcting problematic questions. At
530   the current stage, this work has several limitations. We extend the Math23K dataset with error type
531   annotation. While this is the first human-annotated dataset used for detecting errors in math word
532   problems, our research was limited to Chinese math word problems and did not explore other lan-
533
      guages. Additionally, our work currently relies on a single dataset comprising questions collected
      from an online educational platform, predominantly at the elementary school level, which may not
534
      offer sufficient diversity in problem types. Moreover, we define only five error types within this
535
      dataset: these may not encompass all possible errors in math word problems. At this stage, our
536   primary goal is to provide teachers with identified error types in math word problems, though con-
537   sidering factors like students’ prior knowledge and cognitive barriers remains important for future
538   research.
