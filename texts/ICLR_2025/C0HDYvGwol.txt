011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013              Multi-view image diffusion models have significantly advanced open-domain 3D
014              object generation. However, most existing models rely on 2D network architec-
015              tures that lack inherent 3D biases, resulting in compromised geometric consis-
016
                 tency. To address this challenge, we introduce 3D-Adapter, a plug-in module de-
                 signed to infuse 3D geometry awareness into pretrained image diffusion models.
017
                 Central to our approach is the idea of 3D feedback augmentation: for each denois-
018
                 ing step in the sampling loop, 3D-Adapter decodes intermediate multi-view fea-
019              tures into a coherent 3D representation, then re-encodes the rendered RGBD views
020              to augment the pretrained base model through feature addition. We study two vari-
021              ants of 3D-Adapter: a fast feed-forward version based on Gaussian splatting and
022              a versatile training-free version utilizing neural fields and meshes. Our extensive
023              experiments demonstrate that 3D-Adapter not only greatly enhances the geome-
024              try quality of text-to-multi-view models such as Instant3D and Zero123++, but
025              also enables high-quality 3D generation using the plain text-to-image Stable Dif-
026              fusion. Furthermore, we showcase the broad application potential of 3D-Adapter
027
                 by presenting high quality results in text-to-3D, image-to-3D, text-to-texture, and
                 text-to-avatar tasks.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021) have recently made significant strides in visual
      synthesis, achieving production-quality results in image generation (Rombach et al., 2022). How-
034
      ever, the success of 2D diffusion does not easily translate to the 3D domain due to the scarcity
035
      of large-scale datasets and the lack of a unified, neural-network-friendly representation (Po et al.,
036   2024). To bridge the gap between 2D and 3D generation, novel-view or multi-view diffusion mod-
037   els (Liu et al., 2023b; Long et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Voleti
038   et al., 2024) have been finetuned from pretrained image or video models, facilitating 3D generation
039   via a 2-stage paradigm involving multi-view generation followed by 3D reconstruction (Liu et al.,
040   2023a; 2024a; Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024b). While these models generally
041   exhibit good global semantic consistency across different view angles, a pivotal challenge lies in
042   achieving local geometry consistency. This entails ensuring precise 2D–3D alignment of local fea-
043   tures and maintaining geometric plausibility. Consequently, these two-stage methods often suffer
044
      from floating artifacts or produce blurry, less detailed 3D outputs (Fig. 1 (c)).
045   To enhance local geometry consistency, previous works have explored inserting 3D representations
046   and rendering operations into the denoising sampling loop, synchronizing either the denoised out-
047   puts (Gu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024c; Zuo et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Tang et al., 2024c) or
048   the noisy inputs (Liu et al., 2023c; Gao et al., 2024) of the network, a process we refer to as I/O
049   sync. However, we observe that I/O sync generally leads to less detailed, overly smoothed textures
050
      and geometry (Fig. 1 (b)). This phenomenon can be attributed to two factors:
051       • Diffusion model sampling is sensitive to error accumulations (Li & van der Schaar, 2024). I/O
052         sync methods insert 3D reconstruction and rendering operations into the denoiser in a way that
053         disrupts the original model topology and introduces errors during each denoising step (unless
            reconstruction and rendering are perfect).

079    • For texture generation methods in Liu et al. (2023c); Gao et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024a), I/O
080      sync is equivalent to multi-view score averaging, which theoretically leads to mode collapse,
081      causing the loss of fine details in the generated outputs (analyzed in Appendix A.1).
082
      To overcome the limitations of I/O sync, we propose a novel approach termed 3D feedback augmen-
083   tation, which attaches a 3D-aware parallel branch to the base model, while preserving the original
084   network topology and avoiding score averaging. Essentially, this branch decodes intermediate fea-
085   tures from the base model to reconstruct an intermediate 3D representation, which is then rendered,
086   encoded, and fed back into the base model through feature addition, thus augmenting 3D aware-
087   ness. Specifically, when using a denoising U-Net as the base model, we implement 3D feedback
088   augmentation as 3D-Adapter, which reuses a copy of the original U-Net with an additional 3D re-
089   construction module to build the parallel branch. Thanks to its ControlNet-like (Zhang et al., 2023)
090   model reuse, 3D-Adapter requires minimal or, in cases where suitable off-the-shelf ControlNets are
091
      available, zero training.
092   To thoroughly evaluate its performance and demonstrate its flexibility, we have tested multiple vari-
093   ants of 3D-Adapter using various base models and reconstruction methods. The base models include
094   Instant3D (Li et al., 2024), Zero123++ (Shi et al., 2023), Stable Diffusion(Rombach et al., 2022)
095   v1.5 and its customizations. The reconstruction methods include GRM (Xu et al., 2024b), texture
096   backprojection, Instant-NGP neural radiance field (NeRF) (Müller et al., 2022; Mildenhall et al.,
097
      2020) and DMTet mesh(Shen et al., 2021) optimization. This wide range of possible combinations
      makes 3D-Adapter models capable of many applications, as shown in Fig. 1. Extensive evaluations
098
      show that 3D-Adapter improves geometry consistency compared to the two-stage methods, without
099
      suffering from the quality degradation observed with I/O sync.
100
101   We summarize the main contributions of this paper as follows:
102    • We propose 3D-Adapter, which enables high-quality 3D generation with enhanced multi-view
103      geometry consistency by integrating a 3D feedback module into a base image diffusion model.
104    • We demonstrate that 3D-Adapter is compatible with various base models and reconstruction
105      methods, making it highly adaptable to a range of tasks.
106    • We conduct extensive experiments to show that 3D-Adapter improves geometry consistency
107      while preserving visual quality, outperforming previous methods on text-to-3D, image-to-3D,
         and text-to-texture tasks.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      3D-native diffusion models. We define 3D-native diffusion models as injecting noise directly into
111
      the 3D representations (or their latents) during the diffusion process. Early works (Bautista et al.,
112
      2022; Dupont et al., 2022) have explored training diffusion models on low-dimensional latent vec-
113   tors of 3D representations, but are highly limited in model capacity. A more expressive approach
114   is training diffusion models on triplane representations (Chan et al., 2022), which works reason-
115   ably well on closed-domain data (Chen et al., 2023b; Shue et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2023; Wang
116   et al., 2023). Directly working on 3D grid representations is more challenging due to the cubic
117   computation cost (Müller et al., 2023), so an improved multi-stage sparse volume diffusion model is
118   proposed in Zheng et al. (2023). In general, 3D-native diffusion models face the challenge of limited
119   data, and sometimes the extra cost of preprocessing the training data into 3D representations (e.g.,
120   NeRF), which limit their scalability.
121   Novel-/multi-view diffusion models. Trained on multi-view images of 3D scenes, view diffusion
122   models inject noise into the images (or their latents) and thus benefit from existing 2D diffusion re-
123   search. Watson et al. (2023) have demonstrated the feasibility of training a conditioned novel view
124   generative model using purely 2D architectures. Subsequent works (Shi et al., 2024; 2023; Liu et al.,
125   2023b; Long et al., 2024; Zheng & Vedaldi, 2024) achieve open-domain novel-/multi-view genera-
126   tion by fine-tuning the pre-trained 2D Stable Diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022). However, 3D
127
      consistency in these models is generally limited to global semantic consistency because it is learned
      solely from data, without any inherent architectural bias to support detailed local alignment. To this
128
      end, Huang et al. (2024b); Kant et al. (2024) have introduced epipolar attention, and Xie et al. (2024)
129
      propose to finetune the multi-view model using reinforcement learning.
130
131   Two-stage 3D generation. Two-stage methods (Fig. 2 (a)) link view diffusion with multi-view
132   3D reconstruction models, offering a significant speed advantage over score distillation sampling
133
      (SDS) (Poole et al., 2023). Liu et al. (2023a) initially combine Zero-1-to-3 (Liu et al., 2023b)
      with SparseNeuS (Long et al., 2022), and subsequent works (Liu et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024b;
134
      Long et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024a; Hong et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024; Wang
135
      et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024) have further explored more effective multi-view diffusion models
136   and enhanced reconstruction methods. A common issue with two-stage approaches is that exist-
137   ing reconstruction methods, often designed for or trained under conditions of perfect consistency,
138   lack robustness to local geometric inconsistencies. This may result in floaters and texture seams.
139   To enhance 3D consistency, IM3D (Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2024) applies repeated SDEdit-like re-
140   finements (Meng et al., 2022) to the rendered views, which is an orthogonal contribution to our
141   3D-Adapter.
142   View diffusion with 3D representation. To introduce 3D representation in single-image diffusion
143   models, Anciukevicius et al. (2023); Tewari et al. (2023) elevate image features into 3D NeRFs to
144   render denoised views. Xu et al. (2024c); Tang et al. (2024c); Zuo et al. (2024) further extend this
145   concept to multi-view diffusion. However, these methods often produce slightly blurry outputs due
146   to error accumulation. Liu et al. (2024b) attempt to preserve the original model topology through
147   attention-based feature fusion, yet it lacks a robust architecture, leading to subpar quality as noted in
148   Liu et al. (2023a). On the other hand, optimization-based I/O sync methods in Gu et al. (2023); Liu
149   et al. (2023c); Gao et al. (2024) either require strong local conditioning or suffer from the pitfalls
150
      of score averaging, resulting in overly smoothed textures. Ouroboros3D (Wen et al., 2024) is a
      concurrent work proposing a similar 3D feedback mechanism to ours. The high-level difference is
151
      that Ouroboros3D feeds the rendering to the next denoising timestep, while ours operates within the
152
      current timestep.

      4      3D-A DAPTER
200
201
      To overcome the limitations of I/O sync, our key idea is the 3D feedback augmentation architec-
202   ture, which involves reconstructing a 3D representation midway through the denoising network and
203   feeding the rendered views back into the network using ControlNet-like feature addition. This ar-
204   chitecture preserves the original flow of the base model while effectively leveraging its inherent
205   priors.
206
      Based on this idea, we propose the 3D-Adapter, as illustrated in Fig. 2 (c). For each denoising step,
207
      after passing the input noisy views xt through the base U-Net encoder, we use a copy of the base
208   U-Net decoder to first output intermediate denoised views x̂′t . A 3D reconstruction model then lifts
209   these intermediate views to a coherent 3D representation, from which consistent RGBD views x̃t
210   are rendered and fed back into the network through a ControlNet encoder. The output features from
211   this encoder are added to the base encoder features, which are then processed again by the base
212   decoder to produce the final denoised output x̂t . The full denoising step can be written as:

265   4.2     3D-A DAPTER U SING 3D O PTIMIZATION /T EXTURE BACKPROJECTION
266
267   Feed-forward 3D reconstruction methods, like GRM, are typically constrained by specific camera
268   layouts. In contrast, more flexible reconstruction approaches, such as optimizing a NeRF (Milden-
269   hall et al., 2020) or mesh, can accommodate diverse camera configurations and achieve higher-
      quality results with denser cameras, although they require longer optimization times.

270
      To demonstrate the compatibility with optimization-based reconstruction methods, we explore
271   a new variation of 3D-Adapter (Fig. 9), using Instant-NGP NeRF (Müller et al., 2022) and
272   DMTet mesh (Shen et al., 2021) optimization as the reconstruction module, with Stable Diffusion
273   v1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022) being the base model. For feedback augmentation, Stable Diffusion
274   comes with off-the-shelf ControlNets (Zhang et al., 2023), which empirically work very well as the
275   feedback encoder. Specifically, we simultaneously use the “tile” ControlNet (originally trained for
276   superresolution) for RGB feedback, and the depth ControlNet for depth feedback. Dense cameras
277   are randomly generated around the object for multi-view diffusion. Since Stable Diffusion is a single
278   image model, 3D-Adapter (or I/O sync) is the only module that synchronizes multi-view samples.
279   Alternatively, for texture generation only (Section 5.4), multi-view aggregation can be achieved by
280   backprojecting the views into UV space and blending the results according to visibility.
281
      Details on NeRF/mesh optimization. During the sampling process, the adapter performs NeRF
282
      optimization for the first 60% of the denoising steps. It then converts the color and density fields
283   into a texture field and DMTet mesh, respectively, to complete the remaining 40% denoising steps.
284   All optimizations are incremental, meaning the 3D state from the previous denoising step is retained
285   to initialize the next. As a result, only 96 optimization steps are needed per denoising step. we
286   employ L1 and LPIPS losses on RGB and alpha maps, and total variation (TV) loss on normal
287   maps. Additionally, we enforce stronger geometry regularization using ray entropy loss for NeRF,
288   and Laplacian smoothing loss (Sorkine et al., 2004) plus normal consistency loss for mesh, making
289   the optimization more robust to imperfect intermediate views x̂′t . More details can be found in
290   Appendix C.
291   Limitations. It should be noted that, when using single-image diffusion as the base model, 3D-
292   Adapter alone cannot provide the necessary global semantic consistency for 3D generation. There-
293   fore, it should be complemented with other sources of consistency, such initialization with partial
294   noise like SDEdit (Meng et al., 2022) or extra conditioning from ControlNets. For text-to-avatar
295   generation, we use rendered views of a human template for SDEdit initialization with the initial
296   timestep tinit set to 0.88T , and employ an extra pose ControlNet for conditioning. For text-to-texture
297
      generation, global consistency is usually good due to ground truth depth conditioning.
298
299   4.3     T EXTURE P OST-P ROCESSING
300
301   To further enhance the visual quality of objects generated from text, we implement an optional
302   texture refinement pipeline as a post-processing step. First, when using the GRM-based 3D-Adapter,
303   we convert the generated 3DGS into a textured mesh via TSDF integration. With the initial mesh,
304   we render six surrounding views and apply per-view SDEdit refinement (tinit = 0.5T ) using Stable
305   Diffusion v1.5 with “tile” ControlNet. Finally, the refined views are aggregated into the UV space
306   using texture backprojection. For fair comparisons in the experiments, this refinement step is not
307   used by default unless specified otherwise.

      5.4      T EXT- TO -T EXTURE G ENERATION
468
469   For text-to-texture evaluation, 3D-Adapter employs fast texture backprojection to blend multiple
470   views for intermediate timesteps, and switches to high-quality texture field optimization (similar
471   to NeRF) for the final timestep. A community Stable Diffusion v1.5 variant, DreamShaper 8, is
472   adopted as the base model. During the sampling process, 32 surrounding views are used initially,
473   and this number is gradually reduced to 7 views during the denoising process to reduce computation
474   in later stages. We adopt the EDM Euler ancestral solver with 24 denoising steps. 92 BLIP-captioned
475   objects are sampled from a high-quality subset of Objaverse as our test set.
476   Comparison with baselines. As shown in Table 5 and Fig. 5, the two-stage baseline has good
477   texture details but notably worse CLIP score due to poor consistency. The I/O sync baseline has
478   much better consistency, but it sacrifices details, resulting in the worst aesthetic score. In compar-
479   ison, 3D-Adapter excels in both metrics, producing detailed and consistent textures. Additionally,
480   we demonstrate that 3D-Adapter and I/O sync should not be used simultaneously, as I/O sync con-
481   sistently compromises texture details, as evidenced by the Aesthetic score and qualitative results.
482   Comparison with other competitors. We compare 3D-Adapter with SyncMVD (Liu et al.,

484   et al., 2024a), where SyncMVD and TexPainter are also I/O sync methods. Quantitatively, Table 6

512   5.5   T EXT- TO -AVATAR G ENERATION
513
514   For text-to-avatar generation, the optimization-based 3D-Adapter is adopted with a custom pose
515   ControlNet for Stable Diffusion v1.5, which provides extra conditioning given a human pose tem-
516
      plate. 32 full-body views and 32 upper-body views are selected for denoising, capturing both the
      overall figure and face details. These are later reduced to 12 views during the denoising process. We
517
      use the EDM Euler ancestral solver with 32 denoising steps, with an inference time of approximately
518
      7 minutes per object. Texture editing (using text-to-texture pipeline and SDEdit with tinit = 0.3T )
519   and refinement can be optionally applied to further improve texture details, which costs 1.4 min-
520   utes. For evaluation, we compare 3D-Adapter with baselines using 21 character prompts on the
521   same pose template. As shown in Table 4, 3D-Adapter achieves the highest scores across all three
522   metrics, indicating superior appearance and geometry. Fig. 6 reveals that I/O sync produces overly
523   smoothed texture and geometry due to mode collapse, while the two-stage baseline results in noisy,
524   less coherent texture and geometry. These observations also align with the quantitative results in
525   Table 4.
526
527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we have introduced 3D-Adapter, a plug-in module that effectively enhances the 3D ge-
530   ometry consistency of existing multi-view diffusion models, bridging the gap between high-quality
531   2D and 3D content creation. We have demonstrated two variants of 3D-Adapter: the fast 3D-Adapter
532   using feed-forward Gaussian reconstruction, and the flexible training-free 3D-Adapter using 3D op-
533   timization and pretrained ControlNets. Experiments on text-to-3D, image-to-3D, text-to-texture,
534   and text-to-avatar tasks have substantiated its all-round competence, suggesting great generality and
535   potential in future extension.
536   Limitations. 3D-Adapter introduces substantial computation overhead, primarily due to the VAE
537   decoding process before 3D reconstruction. In addition, we observe that our finetuned ControlNet
538   for 3D feedback augmentation strongly overfits the finetuning data, which may limit its generaliza-
539   tion despite the proposed guidance method. Future work may focus on developing more efficient,
      easy-to-finetune networks for 3D-Adapter.

      C    D ETAILS ON O PTIMIZATION -BASED 3D-A DAPTER
919
920
      The optimization-based 3D-Adapter faces the challenge of potentially inconsistent multi-view in-
921   puts, especially at the early denoising stage. Existing surface optimization approaches, such as
922   NeuS (Wang et al., 2021a), are not designed to address the inconsistency. Therefore, we have devel-
923   oped various techniques for the robust optimization of InstantNGP NeRF (Müller et al., 2022) and
924   DMTet mesh (Shen et al., 2021), using enhanced regularization and progressive resolution.
925
      Rendering. For each NeRF optimization iteration, we randomly sample a 128×128 image patch
926
      from all camera views. Unlike Poole et al. (2023) that computes the normal from NeRF density
927   gradients, we compute patch-wise normal maps from the rendered depth maps, which we find to
928   be faster and more robust. For mesh rendering, we obtain the surface color by querying the same
929   InstantNGP neural field used in NeRF. For both NeRF and mesh, Lambertian shading is applied in
930   the linear color space prior to tonemapping, with random point lights assigned to their respective
931   views.
932
      RGBA losses. For both NeRF and mesh, we employ RGB and alpha rendering losses to optimize
933   the 3D parameters so that the rendered views x̃t match the intermediate denoised views x̂′t . For
934   RGB, we employ a combination of pixel-wise L1 loss and patch-wise LPIPS loss (Zhang et al.,
935   2018). For alpha, we predict the target alpha channel from x̂′t using an off-the-shelf background
936   removal network (Lee et al., 2022) as in Magic123 (Qian et al., 2024). Additionally, we soften the
937   predicted alpha map using Gaussian blur to prevent NeRF from overfitting the initialization.
938
      Normal losses. To avoid bumpy surfaces, we apply an L1.5 total variation (TV) regularization loss
