009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Active learning methods aim to improve sample complexity in machine learning.
012            In this work, we investigate an active learning scheme via a novel gradient-free
013            cutting-plane training method for ReLU networks of arbitrary depth. We demon-
014            strate, for the first time, that cutting-plane algorithms, traditionally used in linear
015            models, can be extended to deep neural networks despite their nonconvexity and
016            nonlinear decision boundaries. Our results demonstrate that these methods pro-
017            vide a promising alternative to the commonly employed gradient-based optimiza-
018
               tion techniques in large-scale neural networks. Moreover, this training method
               induces the first deep active learning scheme known to achieve convergence guar-
019
               antees. We exemplify the effectiveness of our proposed active learning method
020
               against popular deep active learning baselines via both synthetic data experiments
021            and sentimental classification task on real datasets.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Large neural network models are now core to artificial intelligence systems. After years of devel-
027   opment, current large NN training is still dominated by gradient-based methods, which range from
028   basic gradient descent method to more advanced online stochastic methods such as Adam (Kingma
029   & Ba, 2017) and AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019). Recent empirical effort has focused on
030   cutting down storage requirement for such optimizers, see (Griewank & Walther, 2000; Zhao et al.,
031   2024); accelerating convergence by adding momentum, see (Xie et al., 2023); designing better step
032   size search algorithms, see (Defazio & Mishchenko, 2023). Despite its popularity, gradient-based
033   methods suffer from sensitivity to hyperparameters and slow convergence. Therefore, researchers
034
      are persistently seeking for alternative training schemes for large NN models, including involve
      zero-order and second-order algorithms.
035
036   Cutting-plane method is a classic optimization algorithm and is known for its fast convergence rate.
037   Research on cutting-plane type methods dates back to 1950s when Ralph Gomory (Gomory, 1958)
038   first studied it for integer programming and mixed-integer programming problems. Since then, this
039   method has also been heavily investigated for solving nonlinear problems. Different variations of
040
      cutting-plane method emerge, including but not limited to center of gravity cutting-plane method,
      maximum volume ellipsoid cutting-plane method, and analytic center cutting-plane method, which
041
      mainly differ in their center-finding strategy.
042
043   Historically, deep NN training and cutting plane methods have developed independently, each with
044   its own audience. In this work, we bridge them for the first time by providing a viable cutting-plane-
045   based deep NN training and active learning scheme. Our method finds optimal neural network
046
      weights and actively queries additional training points via a gradient-free cutting plane approach.
      We show that an active learning scheme based on our newly proposed cutting-plane-based strategy
047
      naturally inherits classic convergence guarantees of cutting-plane methods. We present synthetic
048
      and real data experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed methods.

070   3    P RELIMINARIES
071
072   Classic cutting-plane method’s usage in different optimization problems has been heavily studied.
073   To better demonstrate the problem and offer a more self-contained background, we start with de-
074
      scribing basic cutting-plane method’s workflow below.
075   Cutting-Plane Method. Consider any minimization problem with an objective function f (θ),
076   where the solution set, denoted as Θ, is a convex set. Cutting-plane method typically assumes
077   the existence of an oracle that, given any input θ0 , either confirms that θ0 ∈ Θ, thereby terminating
078   with θ0 as a satisfactory solution, or returns a pair (x, y) such that xT θ0 ≤ y while xT θ > y for
079   all θ ∈ Θ. If the cut is “good enough,” it allows for the elimination of a large portion of the search
080   space, enabling rapid progress toward the true solution set Θ. The classic convergence results of the
081   cutting-plane method are highly dependent on the quality of the cut in each iteration. For instance, if
082
      the center of gravity of the current volume is removed, it guarantees a volume reduction of approx-
      imately 63%. Similar results hold for the analytic center and the center of the maximum volume
083
      ellipsoid. Figure 1 illustrates a single step of the cutting-plane method, showing how a “good” cut
084
      near the center of the current volume induces a much larger volume reduction compared to a “bad”
085   cut near the edge.
086
087   Cutting-Plane-Based Active Learning (AL) with Linear Models (Louche & Ralaivola, 2015).
088   Cutting-plane method provides a natural active learning framework to localize a set of deep NN
089   classifiers with certain classification margins. Prior work (Louche & Ralaivola, 2015) has studied
090
      the use of cutting-plane method in the context of active learning with linear models for binary clas-
      sification. The setup is the following. One is given a set of unlabeled data {x1 , · · · , xn } ∈ D. The
091
      authors consider a linear binary classifier f (x; θ) := ⟨θ, x⟩ with prediction sign(f (x; θ)) for any in-
092
      put x. Define the set of model parameters that correctly classify our dataset as TD , which is a set of
093   linear inequalities. The size of parameter set reflects the level of uncertainty in the classifier. Starting
094   at an initial set T 0 , the goal is to query additional data points and acquire their labels to reduce the
095   size of T 0 to approach TD , which hopefully would have high test accuracy when the generalization
096   error is small. The cutting-plane-based active learning framework developed by Louche & Ralaivola
097   (2015) (Algorithm 3) starts with a localized convex set T 0 . The algorithm is presented with a set
098   of unlabeled data. At each step t, it performs the following steps: (i) computes the center of the
099   current parameter space θct := center(T t ); (ii) queries the label yt for xt from the unlabeled dataset
100   D which has minimal prediction margin with respect to θct ; (iii) reduces the parameter space via a
101
      cutting-plane in the case of mis-classification: T t+1 = T t ∩ {θ | yt ⟨xt , θ⟩ > 0}. The algorithm ter-
      minates when set T t is small enough or maximum iterations or data budget have been reached. This
102
      active learning scheme has strong convergence result inherited from classic cutting-plane method,
103
      which has been investigated in (Louche & Ralaivola, 2015).
104
105   Disentangling Model Training and Active Learning. Although Louche & Ralaivola (2015) pri-
106   marily focuses on the active learning setting, their method implicitly suggests a cutting-plane-based
107   training workflow for linear binary classifiers. To illustrate this, consider a set of training samples
      {(x1 , y1 ), . . . , (xn , yn )}. Each pair (xi , yi ) induces a cut on the parameter space {θ | yi ⟨θ, xi ⟩ > 0}.


                                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      The final center, i.e., θc = center({θ | yi ⟨θ, xi ⟩ > 0, i ∈ [n]}), accounts for all such cuts while
109   maintaining the desired property sign(f (x; θ)) = y for all training samples. This choice makes θc
110   not only an optimal solution, but also a robust choice, as it remains stable under data perturbations.
111
112
      By disentangling the model training process from the active learning query strategy described in
      (Louche & Ralaivola, 2015), we derive a gradient-free cutting-plane-based workflow for training
113
      linear binary classifiers. However, this approach has several key limitations: (1) it is restricted to
114
      linear models, (2) it requires the data to be linearly separable to ensure an optimal parameter set, and
115   (3) it only supports binary classification tasks. Our current work addresses all three limitations by
116   extending the cutting-plane method to train deep nonlinear neural networks for both classification
117   and regression tasks, without requiring linear separability of the data. Similar to the linear binary
118   classification case, our vanilla training scheme lacks desirable convergence guarantees, as the cuts
119   may occur at the edge of the parameter set. To overcome this, we focus on a cutting-plane-based
120   active learning scheme that enables cuts to be near the center of the parameter set. We provide
121   convergence results for this approach, which, to the best of our knowledge, is the first convergence
122   guarantee for active learning algorithms applied to deep neural networks.
123   Outline. The paper is organized as follows: in Section 4, we adapt the cutting-plane method for
124   nonlinear model training by transforming the nonlinear training process into a linear programming
125   problem. Section 5 introduces the general gradient-free cutting-plane-based training algorithm for
126   deep NNs. In Section 6, we explore the resulting cutting-plane AL framework and prove its con-
127   vergence. Section 7 demonstrates the practical effectiveness of our proposed training and active
128   learning methods through extensive experiments.

133   The cutting-plane AL scheme proposed by Louche & Ralaivola (2015) (summarized in Section 3)
134   is designed for linear models like f (x; θ) = ⟨θ, x⟩. Extending this method to nonlinear models,
135   such as a two-layer ReLU network f (x; θ) = (xT θ1 )+ θ2 , with θ1 ∈ Rd×m and θ2 ∈ Rm , presents
136   additional challenges. Specifically, for a mispredicted data pair (xi , yi ), determining how to cut
137   the parameter space (θ1 , θ2 ) is far more complex, whereas in the linear case, the cut is simply
138   yi ⟨θ, xi ⟩ > 0.
139   To break this bottleneck and extend the cutting-plane-based learning method to nonlinear models,
140   we observe that training a ReLU network for binary classification can be formulated as a linear
141   programming problem. This insight is crucial for extending the learning scheme in (Louche &
142   Ralaivola, 2015) to more complex models. We now develop our core idea of reframing binary
143   classification with ReLU models as linear programs. For clarity, we present our results in two
144   theorems: one for two-layer ReLU networks and another for ReLU networks of arbitrary depth. We
145   focus on the two-layer case in the main paper for detailed discussion, deferring the more abstract
146
      general case to Appendix F.2. Since the general case is an extension of the two-layer model, focusing
      on the two-layer case should provide a clearer understanding of the core concepts.

      Two key functions in our proposed training scheme are the “center” function and the “cut” function,
      which we detail below:
275
276          • Center. The “center” function calculates the center of the convex set T t . There are a couple
277            of notions of centers, such as center of gravity (CG), center of maximum volume ellipsoid
278            (MVE), Chebyshev’s center, and analytic center (Boyd & Vandenberghe (2004)). Among
279            these, the analytic center is the easiest to compute and is empirically known to be effective
280            (Goffin et al. (1997), Atlason et al. (2008)). This is the notion of center that we will adopt
281            to compute the query point in our algorithm. See Appendix H.1 for details.
282          • Cut. The “cut” function determines the cutting planes we get from a specific train-
283                  P (xnt , ynt ). For two-layer model, “cut” function would return the constraint set
               ing data
284            {ynt ( i Dint xTnt (ui − u′i )) ≥ 1, (2Dint − 1)xTnt ui ≥ 0, (2Dint − 1)xTnt u′i ≥ 0}. For
285            deeper NNs, “cut” function would return constraints listed in Problem (6).

      8     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
532
533
      In this work, we introduce a novel cutting-plane-based method for deep neural network training.
534   We also explore an active learning scheme built on our proposed training framework. Despite its
535   novelty, our current implementation has several key limitations that hinder its competitiveness with
536   large-scale models trained using gradient-based methods: (1) our hyperplane subsampling process is
537   not exhaustive; (2) our implementation relies on CPU-based convex program solver and is inefficient
538   for large-scale problems with many variables; (3) our approach has so far been applied only to
539   classification and regression tasks. Due to space constraints, the prior work section is deferred
      to Appendix A and detailed conclusion and limitations section is deferred to Appendix B.

      A    P RIOR W ORK D ISCUSSION
865
866
      We emphasize the novelty of our work as the first to introduce both cutting-plane-based training
867
      schemes and cutting-plane-based active learning methods to deep neural network models. To the
868   best of our knowledge, this work also provides the first convergence guarantees among existing
869   active learning methods for nonlinear neural networks. As discussed in Section 3, the most closely
870   related work is (Louche & Ralaivola, 2015), which also integrates the concepts of cutting-plane
871   methods, active learning, and machine learning for binary classification tasks. We are not aware of
872   any other studies that closely align with our approach. However, we review additional works that,
873   while less directly connected, share some overlap with our research in key areas.
874
875   Cutting-Plane Method. Cutting-plane methods are first introduced by Gomory (1958) for linear
876   programming. Though being considered as ineffective after it was first introduced, it has been later
877   shown by Balas et al. (1993) to be empirically useful when combined with branch-and-bound meth-
878   ods. Cutting-plane method is now heavily used in different commercial MILP solvers. Commonly
879   employed cutting planes for solving convex programs are tightly connected to gradient informa-
880
      tion, which is also the underline logic for well-known Kelley’s cutting-plane method (Kelley, 1960).
      Specifically, consider any minimization problem with objective f (θ) whose solution set, we denote
881
      as Θ, is a convex set. Gradient-based cutting-plane method usually assumes the existence of an
882
      oracle such that given any input θ0 , it either accepts θ0 ∈ Θ - and thus terminates with a satisfactory
883   solution θ0 being found - or it returns a pair (x, y) such that xT θ0 ≤ y while xT θ > y for any θ ∈ Θ,
884   i.e., we receive a cutting plane that cuts between the current input θ0 and the desired solution set
885   Θ. Such cut is given by subgradient of f at query point θ0 . Consider any subgradient g ∈ ∇f (θ0 ),
886   we have the inequality f (θ) ≥ f (θ0 ) + g T (θ − θ0 ), which then raises the cut g T (θ − θ0 ) ≤ 0
887   for minimization problem. Recent development of cutting-plane methods involves those designed
888   for convex-concave games (Jiang et al., 2020), combinatorial optimization (Lee et al., 2015), and
889   also application to traditional machine learning tasks such as regularized risk minimization, multiple
890   kernel learning, and MAP inference in graphical models (Franc et al., 2011).
891
892   Active Learning. With the fast growth of current deep learning model sizes, more and more data
893   is in need for training an effective deep NN model. Compared to just feeding the model with a set
894
      of randomly-selected training samples, how to select the most informative data for each training
      iteration becomes a valuable question. Efficient data sampling algorithm is thus increasingly impor-
895
      tant, especially for current RL-based language model training scheme. With the increasing power
896
      of current pretrained models, researchers found that since most of daily questions can be addressed
897   correctly by the model already, these questions are valueless for further boosting the language mod-
898   els’ capacity. Therefore, actively selecting questions that LLM cannot address correctly is key to
899   current LLM training (Lightman et al., 2023). Based on different learning settings, active learning
900   strategies can be divided into: stream-based selective sampling used when the data is generated con-
901   tinuously (Woodward & Finn, 2017); pool-based sampling used when a pool of unlabeled data is
902   presented (Gal et al., 2017b); query synthesis methods used when new samples can be generated
903   for labeling. Based on different information measuring schemes, active learning algorithms can be
904   further divided into uncertainty sampling (Lewis & Gale, 1994b) which selects data samples to re-
905
      duce prediction uncertainty, query-by-committee sampling which involves multiple models for data
      selection, diversity-weighted method which selects the most diverse data sample, and expected error
906
      reduction method (Mussmann et al., 2022) which selects samples to best reduce models’ expected
907
      prediction error.
908
909
      Convex NN. We note that the idea of introducing hyperplane arrangements to derive equivalent
910
      problem formulation for deep NN training task has also been investigated in prior convexification
911
      of neural network research. For example, Ergen & Pilanci (2021b) has exploited this technique
912   to derive a convex program which is equivalent to two-layer ReLU model training task. More
913   developments involving (Ergen & Pilanci, 2021a) which derives convex programs equivalent to
914   training three-layer CNNs and (Zhang & Pilanci, 2024) which derives convex programs for diffusion
915   models. However, those work neither derive linear programs as considered in our case, nor did they
916   connect such reframed problems to active learning platform. On the contrast, they mainly focus on
917   solving NN training problem by solving the equivalent convex program (directly) they have derived
      via convex program solver such as CVXPY (Diamond & Boyd, 2016).

      B     L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
919
920
      In this work, we introduce a novel cutting-plane-based method for deep neural network training,
921
      which, for the first time, enables the application of this approach to nonlinear models. Additionally,
922
      our new training scheme removes previous restrictions on the training data distribution and extends
923   the method beyond binary classification to general regression tasks. We also explore an active learn-
924   ing scheme built on our proposed training framework, which inherits convergence guarantees from
925   classic cutting-plane methods. Through both synthetic and real data experiments, we demonstrate
926   the practicality and effectiveness of our training and active learning methods. In summary, our work
927   introduces a novel, gradient-free approach to neural network training, demonstrating for the first
928   time the feasibility of applying the cutting-plane method to neural networks, while also offering the
929   first deep active learning method with convergence guarantees.
930   Despite its novelty, our current implementation has several key limitations that hinder its competi-
931   tiveness with large-scale models trained using gradient-based methods. First, although we employ
932   subsampling of activation patterns and propose an iterative filtering scheme (see Appendix F.4), the
933   subsampling process is not exhaustive, which impacts model performance, especially with high-
934   dimensional data. Refining the activation pattern sampling strategy could significantly improve
935   results. Second, we rely on analytic center retrieval during training, which we solve using CVXPY.
936   However, this solution is CPU-bound and becomes inefficient for large-scale problems with many
937
      variables. Developing a center-finding algorithm that leverages GPU parallelism is crucial to un-
      locking the full potential of our training method. Finally, while current large language model (LLM)
938
      training often involves cross-entropy loss, our approach has so far been applied only to classifica-
939
      tion and regression tasks. Extending our method to handle more diverse loss functions presents an
940   exciting avenue for future research.

1228   In this section, we discuss two important generalizations of our cutting-plane AL method: (i). the
1229   relaxation in data distribution requirement and (ii). the extension from classification to regression.
1230
1231   E.1   R ELAXED DATA D ISTRIBUTION R EQUIREMENT
1232
1233
       For linear classifier f (x; θ) = xT θ, the training data is expected to be linearly separable for an
       optimal θ⋆ to exist. However, this constraint on training data distribution is too restrictive in real
1234
       scenarios. With ReLU model, due to its uniform approximation capacity, the training data is not
1235
       required to be linearly separable as long as there is a continuous function h such that sign(h(x)) = y
1236   for all (x, y) pairs. Due to the discrete nature of sampled data points, this is always satisfiable, thus
1237   we can totally remove the prerequisite on training data. For sake of completeness, we provide a
1238   version of uniform approximation capacity of ReLU below, with an extended discussion about a
1239   sample compression perspective of our cutting-plane based model training scheme thereafter. Given
1240   the fact that two-layer model has weaker approximation capacity compared to deeper models, we
1241   here consider only two-layer model without loss of generality. Uniform approximation capacity of
       single hidden layer NN has been heavily studied (Chen & Chen, 1993; Chui & Li, 1992; Costarelli


                                                            23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       et al., 2013; Cotter, 1990; Cybenko, 1989; ichi Funahashi, 1989; Gallant & White, 1988; Hornik,
1243   1991; Mhaskar & Micchelli, 1992; Leshno et al., 1993; Pinkus, 1999), here we present the version
1244   of ReLU network which we find most explicit and close to our setting.
1245
       Theorem E.1. (Theorem 1.1 in (Shen et al., 2022)) For any continuous function f ∈ C([0, 1]d ),
1246   there exists a two-layer ReLU network ϕ = (xW1 + b1 )+ W2 + b2 such that
1247                                                     √
1248                            ∥f − ϕ∥Lp ([0,1]d ) ∈ O    dωf (m2 log m)−1/d
1249
       where ωf (·) is the modulus of continuity of f .
1250
1251   The above result can be extended to any f ∈ C([−R, R]d ), see Theorem 2.5 in Shen et al. (2022) for
1252   discussion. Therefore, for any fixed dimension d, as we increase number of hidden neurons, we are
1253   guaranteed to approximate f a.e. (under proper condition on ωf ). Thus the assumption on training
1254   data can be relaxed to existence of some {W1 , b1 , W2 , b2 } such that sign(ϕ(x)) = y, which happens
1255   almost surely. A minor discrepancy here is that the ϕ being considered in above Theorem incorpo-
1256   rates the bias terms b1 , b2 while our Theorem 4.1 considers two-layer NN of form (xW1 )+ W2 . We
1257
       note here that (xW1 )+ W2 is essentially the same as the one with layer-wise bias. To see this, we can
       append the data x by a 1-value entry to accommodate for b1 . After this modification, we can always
1258
       separate a neuron of form (XW1i )+ W2i , set W1(d+1) = 1 and zeros elsewhere, and W2i would
1259
       then accommodate for bias b2 . Therefore, our relaxed requirement on training data still persists. For
1260   deeper ReLU NNs, similar uniform approximation capability has been established priorly and fur-
1261   thermore the general NN form we considered in Theorem 4.2 incorporates the form with layer-wise
1262   bias and thus we still have such relaxation on training data distribution, see Appendix F.3 for more
1263   explanation.

1265   E.2    F ROM C LASSIFICATION TO R EGRESSION
1266
1267   Algorithm 2 shows how we train deep NN for binary classification task with cutting-plane method.
1268   Nevertheless, it can be easily extended to multi-class using, for instance, the so-called “one-versus-
1269
       all” classification strategy. To illustrate, to extend the binary classifier to handle K classes under
       this approach, we decompose the multi-class problem into K binary subproblems. Specifically, for
1270
       each class Ck , we define a binary classification task as:
1271                                                                  [
1272                                       Classify between Ck and       Ci ,
1273                                                                i̸=k
1274   where we classify Ck against all other classes combined as a single class. This creates K binary
1275   classification problems, each corresponding to distinguishing one class from the rest.
1276
       In fact, our cutting-plane AL can be even applied to the case of regression, where the number of
1277   classes K → ∞. The core intuition behind this is still the uniform approximation capability of
1278   nonlinear ReLU model. Given any training data x with its label y, we want a model f (x; θ) to be
1279   able to predict y exactly. Here the data label is no longer limited to plus or minus one and can be
1280   any continuous real number. For linear model f (x; θ) = xT θ considered in (Louche & Ralaivola,
1281   2015), train such a predictor is almost impossible since it is highly unlikely there exists such a θ for
1282   real dataset. However, with our ReLU model, we are guaranteed there is a set of NN weights that
1283   would serve as a desired predictor due to its uniform approximation capacity.
1284   Therefore, for regression task, the training algorithm will be exactly the same as Algorithm 3 instead
1285   that the original classification cut ynt f (xnt ; θ) ≥ 1 will be replaced with f (xnt ; θ) = ynt . All
1286   other activation pattern constraints are leaved unchanged. A minor discrepancy here is that though
1287   theoretically sounding, the strict inequality f (xnt ; θ) = ynt may raise numerical issues in practical
1288   implementation. Thus, we always include a trust region as ynt − ϵ ≤ f (xnt ; θ) ≤ ynt + ϵ with some
1289   small ϵ for our experiments. See Algorithm 6 for our implementation details.

2330         • Entropy Sampling (Settles, 2009). This technique selects samples for labeling based on the
2331           entropy of the predicted class probabilities. Recall that the entropy for a sample x with
2332           predicted class probabilities p(y|x) is given by:
2333                                                 X
2334                                   H(y|x) = −         p(y = c|x) log p(y = c|x)
2335                                                  c

2336           Higher entropy indicates greater uncertainty, making such samples good candidates for
2337           active learning.

             • Bayesian Active Learning Disagreement (BALD) with Dropout (Gal et al., 2017a). BALD
2339           aims to choose samples that maximize the mutual information I[y, θ|x, Dtrain ] between
2340           predictions y and model parameters θ, given a sample x and training data Dtrain . Mathe-
2341           matically, this is expressed as:
2342
2343                          I[y, θ|x, Dtrain ] = H[y|x, Dtrain ] − Ep(θ|Dtrain ) [H[y|x, θ]]
2344           BALD with dropout extends this approach by using dropout during inference to approxi-
2345           mate Bayesian uncertainty, allowing for efficient estimation of uncertainty in deep learning
2346           models through Monte Carlo dropout.

2348

2358           an ensemble of estimators (a “committee”) to identify samples on which there is disagree-
2359           ment. The committee members vote on the label of each sample, and the sample with the
2360           highest disagreement is selected for labeling. This disagreement is often quantified using
2361           measures like vote entropy:

2368         • Greedy Sampling on the Feature Space (GreedyX) (Wu et al., 2019). GreedyX implements
2369           greedy sampling on the feature space, aiming to select samples that increase the diversity
2370           of the feature space the most. The method iteratively selects samples that maximize the
2371           distance between the newly selected sample and the previously chosen ones, ensuring that
2372           the selected subset of samples represents diverse regions of the feature space. This is often
2373           formulated as:
