009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               We study building agents capable of solving long-horizon tasks using offline
012            model-based reinforcement learning (RL). Existing RL methods effectively learn
013            individual skills. However, seamlessly combining these skills to tackle long-
014            horizon tasks presents a significant challenge, as the termination state of one skill
015            may be unsuitable for initiating the next skill, leading to cumulative distribution
016            shifts. Previous works have studied skill stitching through online RL, which is
017            time-consuming and raises safety concerns when learning in the real world. In
018            this work, we propose a fully offline approach to learn skill stitching. Given that
019            the aggregated datasets from all skills provide diverse and exploratory data, which
020
               likely includes the necessary transitions for stitching skills, we train a dynamics
               model designed to generalize across skills to facilitate this process. Our method
021
               employs model predictive control (MPC) to stitch adjacent skills, using an ensem-
022
               ble of offline dynamics models and value functions. To mitigate overestimation
023            issues inherent in models learned offline, we introduce a conservative approach
024            that penalizes the uncertainty in model and value predictions. Our experimental
025            results across various benchmarks validate the effectiveness of our approach in
026            comparison to baseline methods under offline settings.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   In the context of sequential decision making, an essential capability for intelligent agents is to solve
033
      long-horizon tasks composed of multiple stages. A long-horizon task can be viewed as an ordered
      combination of several “sub-tasks” or “skills”. For example, consider a scenario where an intelligent
034
      robot has independently learned two skills: boiling water (skill-1) and pouring water into a glass
035
      (skill-2). To complete the task of “giving me a cup of boiled water”, directly executing these skills
036   in sequence could lead to significant failure, as the robot may struggle to pour water correctly after
037   executing the boiling water skill. The termination state of skill-1 may be unsuitable to execute
038   skill-2, especially when there are large discrepancies in the data used to train the different skills.
039   Therefore, it is crucial to introduce a policy for stitching adjacent skills together when dealing with
040   long-horizon tasks.
041
      Reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a dominant paradigm for solving sequential decision
042   making tasks, demonstrating remarkable capabilities in learning diverse real-world skills (Margolis
043   & Agrawal, 2023; Yuan et al., 2023). While RL achieves excels in simpler tasks with relatively
044   fixed goals, managing long-horizon tasks that require multiple sequential skills remains an unsolved
045   challenge. Long-horizon tasks involve more transitions and require higher-level multi-step reason-
046   ing, making it difficult for RL to learn efficiently with a single policy. Additionally, if we learn
047   each skill individually using RL, the gap between the transitions observed for different skills can be
048   significantly large, complicating the direct chaining of these skills. To address these challenges, we
049   propose to learn skill stitching to facilitate the integration of RL skills.
050   Previous studies have explored learning and stitching skills using online RL (Konidaris & Barto,
051   2009; Bagaria & Konidaris, 2019; Gu et al., 2023), which can be time-consuming and sample-
052   inefficient. Other works (Lee et al., 2019; 2021; Kang & Oh, 2022; Chen et al., 2023) have managed
053   to pre-train skills from offline datasets but still rely on online RL for stitching. This online learning
      stage introduces inefficiencies and safety concerns, especially in real-world settings. For example,

081
      In this paper, we propose a fully offline method to tackle long-horizon tasks through skill stitch-
      ing. We assume access to various skills and their associated datasets, recognizing that high-quality
082
      datasets for long-horizon tasks can be challenging to obtain. A proper combination of these skills
083
      can effectively accomplish a range of long-horizon tasks. Our primary motivation is that the ag-
084   gregated datasets of all skills – whether from training datasets or RL replay buffers – offer rich
085   coverage of environment transitions, which can be leveraged to learn skill stitching. We propose to
086   train a dynamics model on these aggregated datasets, capturing environment transitions independent
087   of specific skills, thereby demonstrating promising generalization for predicting transitions in states
088   not observed by a specific skill. Additionally, we train each individual skill using offline RL with
089   the skill datasets. With the offline-trained skills and dynamics models, we can perform model-based
090   planning to facilitate the stitching process, which is integrated between two adjacent skills during
091   the execution of long-horizon tasks. The planning process of model-based stitching is guided by the
092
      value function of the next skill, encouraging the agent to visit states that are suitable for executing
      that skill. For example, in a maze environment, as illustrated in Figure 1, the right figure demon-
093
      strates how the agent can accomplish the long-horizon task through skill stitching, while the middle
094
      figure shows that chaining skills directly without stitching leads to failure.
095
096   Since dynamics models and skill value functions are trained offline, prediction errors can increase
097   when encountering states outside the training distribution. This results in significant cumulative
098
      error in model-based planning, as the planning algorithm utilizes the overestimated values of inac-
      curately predicted states to maximize its objective. To address this issue, we introduce a conservative
099
      approach for training offline models and conducting model-based planning. We train an ensemble
100
      of value functions for each skill and an ensemble of dynamics models. During the stitching process,
101   we incorporate the variance of both model predictions and value predictions into the optimization
102   objective, discouraging the agent from visiting risky out-of-distribution states.
103
104
      In our experiments, we evaluate our method across three distinct benchmarks, including a maze en-
      vironment and two robotic manipulation benchmarks – Kitchen (Fu et al., 2020) and Robosuite (Zhu
105
      et al., 2020). The results demonstrate that our method effectively manages diverse combinations of
106
      skills in each environment, outperforming various baselines in fully offline settings.
107
      To summarize, our main contributions are as follows:

      2.2   O FFLINE M ODEL -BASED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
144
145   Offline reinforcement learning (Levine et al., 2020) is a branch of RL algorithms where agents have
146   no access to interacting with the environment to collect trajectories, experiences and data. Instead, a
147   static dataset of transitions D = {(st , at , rt , st+1 )} is provided for training. The core issue is to get
148   a sufficient understanding of the underlying MDP through limited data, so as to learn a good pol-
149   icy. Typical algorithms in offline RL include batch-constrained Q-learning (BCQ) (Fujimoto et al.,
150   2019), conservative Q-learning (CQL) (Kumar et al., 2020), implicit Q-learning (IQL) (Kostrikov
151   et al., 2022), etc.
152   In offline RL, a main problem is the overestimation in value of the out-of-distribution states/actions
153   (unseen in the dataset) due to the distributional shift between the dataset and the learned policy (Ku-
154   mar et al., 2020). To tackle this problem, CQL learns a conservative Q-function by producing a lower
155   bound on the value of the current policy, while IQL introduces expectile regression to lower the esti-
156   mation. Besides, value ensemble (An et al., 2021) is also widely used to reduce the overestimation,
157   based on the extent of uncertainty during estimation.
158   Model-based RL (Janner et al., 2019) is put forward with the idea of modeling the transition (dy-
159   namics) of the environment directly. Specifically, the sampled experiences or the collected datasets
160   are used to fit a dynamics model approximately, which can become a substitution for the real en-
161   vironment to generate “virtual” transition data. These synthesized data can reduce the need of real
      data from the environment, which is much more sample-efficient than model-free methods.

243   We evaluate the quality of the new state by the value function of the IQL policy of the next skill,
244   meaning that a state with a higher value (from the perspective of the next skill) tends to be a better
245   state for executing the next skill. The planning stage loops as long as the predicted value of the
246   new state is higher than the previous one, forming a monotonically increasing sequence. When the
247   value stops increasing or the number of stitching steps exceeds the maximum value, stitching is
248   terminated.
249   To reduce the problem of overestimation in value prediction, we adopt the trick of ensemble and
250   train multiple models of the value function of IQL and the dynamics models. Since we predict the
251   new state given sampled actions via dynamics models and predict the value of the new state using
252   the value functions of IQL, the inaccuracy comes from both the value functions and the dynamics
253   models. In practice, we calculate the ensemble value vensemble following Equation 2:
254                vensemble (st+1 ) = E [v(st+1 )] − αVar(v(st+1 )) − βVar(Tϕ (st+1 | st , at )),       (2)

      Tϕ (· | st , at ) represents the predicted next state via the dynamics model. We use the average of
257
      multiple models to fit the expectation approximately, and use their standard deviation to approximate
258   the variance.

      Robosuite. This benchmark (Zhu et al., 2020) provides a variety of manipulation tasks in robotics,

287           • Door → CloseDoor (open the door first and close it afterwards);
288           • PickPlaceCan → PickPlaceCan (pick and place the can twice).

291   microwave → kettle, the agent must open the microwave first and move the kettle afterwards.

295   4.1.2    BASELINES
296   As discussed before, the most natural idea can be directly train a policy from the dataset of long-
297   horizon tasks, while in most cases such long datasets are unavailable. Additionally, the policies
298   directly derived from long-horizon datasets can only generally accomplish the tasks with the same
299   order of skills. We provide experiments of this as an ablation study in Section 4.3.1.
300
      We denote our method as MB-Stitching, since we add model-based planning as the stitching part be-
301
      tween two adjacent skills with IQL policies. Specifically, we utilize random shooting method (Press,
302   2007; Bonnans, 2013) as MPC planning in MazeRunner, by generating random actions from a uni-
303   form distribution. For the benchmarks with continuous states and actions, we adopt cross entropy
304   method (CEM) (Rubinstein, 1999; De Boer et al., 2005), sampling the actions from a Gaussian dis-
305   tribution and optimize the mean and standard until convergence. For comparison, we consider the
306   following baselines in the domain of fully offline RL:
307
308   No-Stitching. We adopt implicit Q-learning (IQL) itself as the most basic baseline. The poli-
309   cies of skills are executed, without any stitching. In implementation, we use one multi-task pol-
310   icy (i.e., the goal-conditioned policy) instead of multiple single-task policies for skill execution in
311
      MazeRunner and Kitchen. In the Maze environment, a single policy can naturally handle four dif-
      ferent skills, since the goal information is naturally contained within the observations of the Maze
312
      environment. In terms of the Kitchen environment, following the tradition of vector-based RL, we
313
      use a 7-dimensional one-hot vector (since there are 7 tasks in Kitchen) to denote which skill is being
314   executed, and the one-hot vector is concatenated with the observation vector before taken by the
315   policy network as input.
316
317   Random-Stitching. Randomly generated actions are used for the stitching part. The terminate
318   function still holds the same as MB-Stitching, i.e., either the (predicted) value of the new state
319   stops to increase monotonically or the number of stitching steps exceeds a maximum threshold.
320
321   MF-Stitching. This is a model-free baseline in the domain of offline RL. An extra policy is trained
322   to serve as the stitching part, which is executed between two adjacent skills. This extra policy is
323   offline trained from a “mixed” dataset, in which the second half of the trajectories of the previous
      skill and the first half of the trajectories of the next skill are directly mixed together.

445
446   In this section, we explore the effect of stitching related to the quality of the dataset. We test the per-
447   formances of skill stitching using different versions of the dataset, each with a different level of qual-
448   ity (from non-expert trajectories to expert ones). We adopt the dataset of Door and CloseDoor in
449   the Robosuite benchmark with four different levels, in which level-1 denotes the non-expert (which
450   is sub-optimal) dataset, and level-4 is the most expert one.
451   The results of different levels of the dataset are listed in Table 5. A dataset of lower level (represent-
452   ing the non-expert dataset) yields policies with worse performance, but brings higher positive effect
453   of stitching. On the contrary, a dataset of higher level, which stands for a more expert dataset, yields
454   policies with better performance, but with relatively lower effect of stitching and even negative op-
455   timization.
456   Analyzing this phenomenon, the more optimal the dataset we use, the better performance the policy
457   will have, thus it can handle the next skill better even without the process of stitching. On the other
458   hand, training with an expert dataset has more trend of “overfitting” - the policy can actually handle
459   a smaller range of states since the variety of states in the expert dataset tends to be poor. Hence, the
460   “overfitted” policy tends to result in a negative effect with the stitching process - the new state after
461   stitching will probably become a state unseen in the expert dataset. In this aspect, policies trained
462   from those non-expert datasets tend to be more capable in terms of “generalization”.
463
464   5    R ELATED W ORK
465
466   Online skill learning. This refers to the process of skill acquisition and skill stitching both taking
467   place online. Konidaris & Barto (2009) was the first to introduce skill stitching as a method of
468   skill discovery, such that an agent in an environment with continuous state space can construct a
469   sequence of options to optimize the behaviors in the target MDP. However, it can only tackle simple
470   environments with relatively low-dimensional states and discrete actions. Based on this, Deep Skill
471   Chaining (DSC) (Bagaria & Konidaris, 2019) extends skill stitching to high-dimensional cases with
472   continuous state and action spaces by leveraging hierarchical reinforcement learning. Inspired by
473   the intuition that it is easier to solve a long-horizon task from the states in the local neighborhood of a
474
      goal (Bagaria & Konidaris, 2019), DSC defines the initial states of the current options as the terminal
      states for later options and learns the current options recursively. For instance, the initial state of the
475
      first option is placed near the goal, and the terminal state of the first option is the goal itself. Some
476
      other work, including Multi-Skill Mobile Manipulation (M3) (Gu et al., 2023), focused on the errors
477   in skill stitching. For example, a stationary manipulation skill may perform poorly when applied
478   in an inappropriate location. To overcome such issues, M3 incorporates navigation skills that are
479   trained with region-based goals rather than point-specific ones, as well as manipulation skills that
480   can offer enhanced mobility and flexibility.
481
482   Offline skill acquisition with online stitching. Offline skills acquisition with online stitching
483   refers to a framework where skills are pretrained or drawn from offline skill datasets, while the
484   stitching policy is learned through online RL. In this context, the primary focus is on defining the
485   initial and terminal state sets for these skills. Some work (Lee et al., 2019; Kang & Oh, 2022)
      specifically examined the design of the reward function for the stitching policy. In Lee et al. (2019),


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the reward function incorporates a learned distance to the initial state of the next skill as a part of the
487   reward signal. Similarly, in Kang & Oh (2022), the reward function is based on the learned distance
488   to a certain cluster center of the next skill. To prevent the uncontrollable expansion of the initial state
489   set, T-STAR (Lee et al., 2021) introduced Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning (GAIL) (Ho
490   & Ermon, 2016) rewards, encouraging the agent to retain near-expert trajectories. Additionally, an
491   adversarial framework is devised to regularize the terminal state distribution to ensure its remaining
492   close to the initial set of the subsequent policy. Closely related to our work, Chen et al. (2023)
493   proposed the Transition Feasibility Function, which measures the ability of the skills to succeed in
494   the end when starting from a given state, and this function is learned using online reward signals.
495
      However, our research diverges by focusing on fully offline skill stitching, with no online learning
      or adaptation employed. Instead, we rely on the offline value function of the next skill to guide the
496
      process of stitching.
497
498
      Offline skill stitching. To the best of our knowledge, there has been little work on fully offline skill
499
      stitching, where both skill learning and skill stitching are conducted offline. The most recent relevant
500   work in this domain is STAP (Agia et al., 2023), which evaluates the feasibility of skills using Q-
501   values learned from offline datasets, and generates action sequences via the cross entropy method
502   (CEM). GSC (Mishra et al., 2023) introduces a generative framework for producing sequences of
503   states and skill parameters using chained and skill-level diffusion models. Among these approaches,
504   the skills are predefined with specific parameters, and CEM is used solely to search for the suitable
505   skill parameters. By contrast, in our work, skills are represented as policies within MDPs, and we
506   employ CEM directly to search in the action space for skill stitching. This distinction allows for a
507   more flexible and direct integration of the skills in the offline setting.
508
509   6    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
510
511   In this paper, we explore the problem of offline skill stitching within the context of reinforcement
512   learning. By formulating the problem and defining proper objectives, we propose a model-based ap-
513   proach that employs an ensemble of offline-trained dynamics models and a planning algorithm with
514   conservative objectives for effective stitching execution. Our method outperforms various baselines,
515   demonstrating improved performance across diverse combinations of long-horizon tasks in different
516
      benchmarks. These results highlight the promising potential of offline model-based algorithms in
      the field of skill stitching for addressing long-horizon tasks.
517
518   Our method has some limitations that could be addressed in future work. As discussed in Sec-
519   tion 4.3.2, it can be challenging for our method to yield positive effects when the quality of the
520   datasets is excessively high, meaning the skills are sufficient to handle long-horizon tasks without
521   the need for stitching. Additionally, our approach may struggle to adapt to environments with par-
522
      tial observations. To address this issue, methods for training dynamics models in partial observable
      settings (Hafner et al., 2023) could be applied.

      A.1   M AZE RUNNER
653
654   Maze runner is 9 × 9 a maze-like environment for navigation, which is slightly modified from
655   AMAGO (Grigsby et al., 2024). The initial location is always at the bottom center of the map. An
656   agent navigates in the maze from the initial location with a discrete action space (up, down, left,
657   right), and each action yields taking one step (grid) in the corresponding direction. The observation
658   space is continuous, including the normalized absolute coordinates of both the current place and the
659   location of the goal, as well as the normalized distance to the walls in the four directions. There
660
      are four fixed goals (denoted as g0 , g1 , g2 , g3 in Figure 1) set in the maze environment in total,
      corresponding to the four skills to be accomplished.
661
662   Correspondingly, the four datasets contain the trajectories of accomplishing the four skills respec-
663   tively. In the maze runner environment, a long-horizon task contains multiple ordered goals (e.g.,
664   g0 → g1 → g3 → g2 ), and the agent ought to reach each goal in the same order by navigation.
665   Arriving at a certain goal without reaching the previous goals will be invalid, and is not recorded as
666
      skill accomplishment.

668   A.2   K ITCHEN
669
      Kitchen implements a 9-DoF Franka Panda robot placed in a simulated kitchen environment to ma-
670   nipulate several household items (Fu et al., 2020). There are seven skills in this environment, each
671   corresponding to interacting with an item to reach a target state. By default, the robot always starts
672   from a fixed posture. The 59-dimensional observation space of Kitchen includes the proprioception
673   of the robot arm and the states of the seven objects, while the 9-dimensional action space is con-
674   structed with the control of the velocity of the joints and the gripper fingers. The seven skills (Fu
675   et al., 2020) are defined as:
676
677
            1. Bottom burner: twist the bottom control knob to activate the bottom burner of the
               stove.
678
679         2. Top burner: twist the top control knob to activate the top burner of the stove.
680         3. Light switch: twist the switch to turn on the light.
681         4. Slide cabinet: slide the door of the cabinet to the right to open it.
682
            5. Hinge cabinet: open the door of a hinge cabinet by rotating it.
683
684
            6. Microwave: open the door of the microwave.
685         7. Kettle: move the kettle from the bottom burner onto the top burner.
686
      In this paper, we assume access to seven datasets, each containing the trajectories of a single skill. In
687
      the Kitchen benchmark, the long-horizon tasks are the combinations of several skills, e.g., bottom
688
      burner → kettle → microwave is a long-horizon task with three skills.

750   by estimating the value of the new states using the value function of the IQL policy of the next skill,
751   and execute this particular individual (5 steps of actions) as the stitching part.
752   For continuous environments including Kitchen and Robosuite, we adopt cross entropy method
753   (CEM) (Rubinstein, 1999; De Boer et al., 2005), sampling the actions from a Gaussian distribution
754   and optimize the mean and standard until convergence. Specifically, a population size of action se-
755   quences are sampled from the initial Gaussian distribution N (0, 1), and each sequence has a length
      of “horizon” (number of steps). The dynamics model predicts the new states after executing these

759   ing a max number of iterations of update, the CEM process terminates, and one step of action is
760   finally executed in the environment according to the mean of the current Gaussian distribution. The
