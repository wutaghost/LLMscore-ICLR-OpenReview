009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Model extraction attacks (MEAs) on large language models (LLMs) have re-
012            ceived increasing attention in recent research. However, existing attack meth-
013            ods typically adapt the extraction strategies originally developed for deep neu-
014
               ral networks (DNNs). They neglect the underlying inconsistency between the
               training tasks of MEA and LLM alignment, leading to suboptimal attack perfor-
015            mance. To tackle this issue, we propose Locality Reinforced Distillation (LoRD),
016            a novel model extraction algorithm specifically designed for LLMs. In particu-
017            lar, LoRD employs a newly defined policy-gradient-style training task that uti-
018            lizes the responses of victim model as the signal to guide the crafting of prefer-
019
               ence for the local model. Theoretical analyses demonstrate that I) The conver-
               gence procedure of LoRD in model extraction is consistent with the alignment
020            procedure of LLMs, and II) LoRD can reduce query complexity while mitigat-
021            ing watermark protection through exploration-based stealing. Extensive experi-
022            ments on domain-specific extractions validate the superiority of our method in
023            extracting various state-of-the-art commercial LLMs. Our code is available at:
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/LoRD-MEA-1EF2/.

061   In this paper, we challenge the effectiveness of MLE in stealing a reinforcement-learning-aligned

063
064
      Low query efficiency. Current LLM-oriented MEAs suffer from unacceptably significant query
      times because they must collect enough generated responses, which entails exponential complexity in
065
      terms of generated tokens, resulting in low query efficiency.
066
067   Vulnerability against defenses. Directly learning from the responses of victim models can cause
068   local models to inadvertently incorporate those watermarks (Cong et al., 2022; He et al., 2022;
069   Zhao et al., 2022; He et al., 2021) embedded in the output of victim models. The residue of such
070
      watermarks makes the extraction process less stealthy and even serves as provenance evidence of
      model theft.
071
072   Motivated by these limitations, we propose Locality Rein-forced Distillation (LoRD), a query-
073   efficient and watermark-resistant model extraction attack under a training paradigm with LLM‚Äôs
074   alignments. Stealing LLMs via reinforcement learning paradigms is challenging. The main reason is
075   that the key component in the alignment procedure of LLMs, reinforcement learning with human
076
      feedback (RLHF) (Bai et al., 2022a;b; Perez et al., 2023), heavily relies on the feedback signal of
      human annotators, which is difficult to reproduce directly in the context of MEAs. To tackle this
077
      challenge, we develop a policy-gradient-style extraction procedure. This approach regards the locality
078
      direction between the generations of local models and victim models as the implicit reward signal. It
079   can thus achieve a human-feedback-free reinforcement learning for our extraction attack. From the
080   theoretical perspective, we show why those existing MEAs using MLE and knowledge distillation
081   (KD) are inconsistent with the optimization procedure in LLMs‚Äô alignments. Along this way, we also
082   demonstrate why LoRD can achieve stronger watermark resistance and higher query efficiency.
083

084
      to steal a commercial LLM with 175 billion parameters by a pre-trained local model with only 8
085   billion parameters under a given domain. The resulting local model performs statistically similar
086   to the victim model for tasks not requiring extra knowledge (e.g., data-to-text), and only 0 ‚ąľ 3
087   percentage lower for tasks requiring it (e.g., translation and QAs). This result poses an immediate
088   threat of task-specific extraction on commercial LLMs. To further draw the capability boundary of
089   such a threat, we also illustrate the ‚Äúspectrum‚ÄĚ in difficulties and upper bounds for extracting LLMs.
090
      To summarize, the contributions of our paper are as follows:
091
092   New Perspective of LLM Alignment for MEAs. We present LoRD, a novel model extraction attack
093   algorithm for LLMs. To our best knowledge, it is the first effective and realistic extraction algorithm

095   Theoretical Guarantee. We theoretically prove that the convergence procedure of LoRD in MEAs

097   query complexity while mitigating watermark protection through exploration-based stealing.
098   Systematical Evaluation. Extensive experiments on domain-specific extractions demonstrate that
099   our method outperforms current extraction strategies across different downstream NLP tasks.

309   4.2   C OMPARATIVE A NALYSIS ON M ODEL S TEALING
310
      Query Efficiency. Let NQ and NR denote the sequence lengths of the query text and the response
311
      text, respectively. For MLE, the ideal query numbers to populate the entire text space are given by
312   O(V NQ ¬∑ V NR ), where V represents the size of the vocabulary. In contrast, LoRD possesses the
313   capability to automatically explore the generation token space, thereby significantly reducing the
314   query requirements about generation candidates to a constant level. Specifically, the complexity of
315   LoRD‚Äôs query requirements is O(V NQ ¬∑ C), where C is a constant that correlates with the capability
316   of local models.
317
      Based on the above analysis, a straightforward concern with employing MLE in LLMs‚Äô extraction is
318   that, given the limited query times in real-world practices, it may suffer from incomplete learning,
319   especially for text generation tasks. Consequently, the local model may tend to memorize some
320   specific responses instead of achieving a broad understanding and generation. We call such a
321   phenomenon preference overfitting (PO), which indicates that the local model is only effective on
322   a limited set of explored samples, and yet does not generalize well to unseen scenarios. In such
323   cases, the local model usually exhibits a more ‚Äúrugged‚ÄĚ decision surface, which appears to overfit the
      preference sentences in Dtr , as shown in Figure 11 (b). Figure 10 provides a visualization of it.

      7   E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
541
542
      As discussed in Section 1, MEAs are becoming increasingly prevalent in industrial settings and have
543   already been executed, yet there remains a critical gap in understanding which specific tasks are
544   more susceptible and what capabilities are necessary for effective executions. This lack of knowledge
545   exacerbates the challenges faced by LLM maintainers in safeguarding their systems. Our research can
546   contribute to that. Besides, the theoretical problem we address (as shown in Section 4) offers a novel
547   and insightful perspective on the nature of this threat. Based on these two points, we believe the
548   benefits of our paper outweigh potential harms, which aligns with the principles of the Menlo
549   Report (Bailey et al., 2012) on ethics. Additionally, we have submitted an anonymous version of
550   the paper to the maintainers of the victim models used in our study to assist in improving their model
551
      security.
552   It is important to acknowledge, however, that the algorithms we propose could inadvertently enhance
553   the efficiency of illicit extraction efforts by adversaries. To mitigate this risk, we have introduced
554   and analyzed two defensive strategies in Appendix 8, assessing both their effectiveness and potential
555   vulnerabilities under adaptive attack scenarios. This ensures a comprehensive approach to bolstering
556   the security of LLMs.
557
558   8   P OTENTIAL D EFENSES
559
560   Query Detection. One approach to effectively prevent the attack of LoRD is by detecting the
561   distribution of query texts. This is because LoRD, similar to current MEA algorithms, makes
562   no improvements to query samples, indicating that it can be detected by analyzing the statistical
563   information of the adversary‚Äôs queries, such as the number of queries, distribution of query contents,
564   and so on. However, this defense is usually resource-consuming, as it requires the LLM provider to
565   store all query texts of each user. Besides, the potential for false positives could adversely affect the
566
      user experience.
567   More Powerful Watermarks. While we highlight the watermark resistance of LoRD, watermarking
568   remains one of the most effective solutions to mitigate MEAs. For example, some model-level
569   watermarks, such as backdoor-based watermarking (Jia et al., 2021; Lv et al., 2024), can effectively
570   certify the theft of DNNs. While model-level (e.g. backdoor-based) watermarks on pre-trained
571   models raised increasing concerns recently (Peng et al., 2023a; Gu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a),
572
      model-level watermarking on LLMs remains preliminary. Besides, this technique might not work
      when the adversary only steals a subset of knowledge in which no backdoor is embedded.

1272   C.2    L ANGUAGE M ODELS E XTRACTION
1273
1274   Studies to steal language models originated from the natural language understanding (NLU) models,
1275
       such as BERT(Devlin et al., 2019), and then evolved to generative language models, especially large
       language models recently.
1276
1277   Krishna et al. (2020) highlights early recognition of model extraction threats in language models. By
1278   constructing text inputs with randomly vocabulary sampling, they successfully extract the weights
1279   from BERT-based APIs. Besides, Rafi et al. (2022) investigate the feasibility of side-channel model
1280   extraction attacks, revealing that by analyzing extra signals from GPU kernels, one could accurately
1281
       steal the model architecture and its parameters. Subsequent research (Xu et al., 2022) has thoroughly
       investigated the strategy of ensembling victim models to train a competitor model that surpasses its
1282
       teachers.
1283
1284   The exploration of generative language model extraction is still in its infant stage, with only a handful
1285   of studies thus far. Wallace et al. (2020) investigate imitation attacks on natural language models. By
1286   designing monolingual query texts and collecting responses, they successfully extract the knowledge
1287
       from a simulated machine translation model under the black-box settings. This research exhibits
       that slight architectural differences will not influence the extraction between language models. Li
1288
       et al. (2023b) also explores the potential risks of stealing the code-generation abilities of LLMs into
1289
       smaller downstream models. Unlike previous research (Wallace et al., 2020), this is the first study
1290   that selects LLMs as targets. By collecting large-scale domain-specific samples, they fine-tune a
1291   7-billion local pre-trained model with them and show the similarity between the victim and local
1292   models in both performances and adversarial samples. However, these two studies employ the MLE
1293   loss (Equation 3) as the MEA method, neither considering whether MLE is compatible with LLMs‚Äôs
1294   training, especially the alignment procedure shown in Section 2.2, nor addressing optimizations
1295   related to query efficiency and the watermark resistance. Besides, the scope of these studies is limited
       to stealing specific knowledge in a few downstream domains. At the same time, most of the critical

1314   C.3   T EXT WATERMARKS
1315
       In contrast to stealing LLMs, IP protection methods have received considerable attention recently.
1316
       By sampling a stealthy but representative ‚Äúgreed word set‚ÄĚ on the vocabulary distribution, these
1317   methods (Cong et al., 2022; He et al., 2022; 2021; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023) can remap the generated
1318   words into their synonyms or add the ‚Äúwatermarked‚ÄĚ token automatically, and thus effectively certify
1319   the output. Besides, strategies such as integrating embeddings into the representation as the back-
1320   door (Peng et al., 2023b) or manipulating the probabilities with crafted sinusoidal noises (Zhao et al.,
1321   2022; 2023) are also proposed. However, these approaches often presume more stringent conditions
1322   regarding the victim and the suspected models. This paper will further assess the effectiveness of
1323   LoRD and current MEAs in evading these black-box watermarking strategies.

1325   D     A D ETAILED T HREAT M ODEL
1326
1327   Adversary‚Äôs Objective. The adversary‚Äôs objective is to steal the targeted knowledge from LLMs.
1328   Specifically, we select machine translation, reasoning, data-to-text, structured text generation, and
1329   summarization as the downstream domain-specific tasks. The adversary aims to develop a query-
1330   efficient MEA algorithm, since the amount of input and generated tokens will be counted as the costs.
1331   Additionally, the MEA methods are expected to be watermark-resistant, i.e., they are highly desired
1332   to reduce the risks of exposure to unauthorized stealing.
1333   Targeted Models. We select Llama3-70B, GPT-3.5-turbo, and GPT-4o as the victim models in this
1334   paper. Unlike previous works that only deployed simulated local victim models (e.g., OPT (Zhang
1335   et al., 2022)), our selections aim to expose the stealing threat on realistic AI services. Besides, our
1336   target models are specifically constrained to LLMs fine-tuned with alignment methods (e.g., RLHF)
1337   since they are not only state-of-the-art solutions now but also more valuable due to their human-based
1338   alignments.
1339   Adversary‚Äôs Capabilities. In accordance with the LLM-based AI service APIs, we identify two
1340   attack scenarios: black-box and grey-box attacks. In the black-box scenario, only textual responses
1341   the adversary is allowed to obtain. At the same time, all other information, such as the temperature,
1342   sampling strategies, and the hidden states of LLMs, are unseen and inaccessible. On the contrary,
1343   a grey-box attack allows the adversary to access the generation probabilities distribution of tokens.
1344   Notice that both MLE and our LoRD method are under black-box settings, and we only adopt
1345   grey-box settings on some particular stealing methods, such as knowledge distillation.
1346   Besides, this paper posits that the adversary usually has worse training conditions than the victims.
1347   Specifically, query times and the scale of the local model available to the adversary are much smaller
1348   than the victims‚Äô training datasets and model parameters. This setting has been adopted in previous
1349   LLMs‚Äô extraction (Li et al., 2023b). We call it a LaViSH (Large-Victim-Small-Heist) framework,
       which allows us to estimate the upper bound of MEA risks empirically. For adversaries with more

1364   E    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
1365
1366   MEAs on Multi-modal Models. While this paper delves into MEAs for large language models, it
1367   acknowledges the oversight of the multi-modal attribution of current commercial models (Anil et al.,
1368   2024; Achiam et al., 2024) that integrate various forms of data such as text, images, voice, and so on.
1369   The challenge of extending MEA algorithms to accommodate these models, which requires extra
1370   considerations on the unified representation of concepts, remains unexplored. Future work could
1371   focus on developing MEA methodologies sensitive to multi-modal data nuances.
1372   Capacities beyond LaViSH Settings. We utilize the LaViSH setting to describe the model capacity
1373   of adversaries in our threat model (see Appendix D). However, sometimes, the adversary might
1374   possess comparable or superior training resources to the victims. Though this paper posits that our
1375   MEA algorithms and theoretical analysis are still compatible with such conditions, we concede that
1376   concrete experimental validation and results beyond LaViSH settings are not presented here.
1377
       Lower-level Extractions. This study evaluates MEAs at the performance level, i.e., it measures
1378   the extraction effectiveness simply through task performance metrics, or the similarity of learned
1379   distributions to the victim model. This setting is justified, as performance metrics are essential for
1380   evaluating task-related knowledge and the practical application of LLMs. However, it does not
1381   consider the lower-level similarities between the victim and local models. Can we achieve neuron-
1382   level alignments in LLM‚Äôs MEAs? How does a LaViSH setting hurt LLM‚Äôs MEAs? Is it compatible
1383   to extract a MoE (Mix-of-the-Expert) (Shazeer et al., 2017) victim model with a dense local model?
1384   These questions are not addressed in this research.
