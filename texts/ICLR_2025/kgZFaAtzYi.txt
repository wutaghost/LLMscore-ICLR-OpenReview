009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated significant capabilities across
012            various domains such as healthcare, weather forecasting, finance, and law. These
013            works have showcased the powerful abilities of individual LLMs. Recently, nu-
014            merous studies have shown that coordinated multi-agent systems exhibit enhanced
015            decision-making and reasoning capabilities through collaboration. However, since
016            individual LLMs are susceptible to various adversarial attacks, a key vulnerabil-
017            ity arises: Can an attacker manipulate the collective decision of such systems by
018
               accessing a single agent? This is similar to the Byzantine Fault in distributed
               systems. To address this issue, we formulate it as a game with incomplete in-
019
               formation, where agents lack full knowledge of adversarial strategies. We then
020
               propose a framework, M-Spoiler, which simulates a stubborn adversary in multi-
021            agent debates during the training phase to tackle this problem. Through extensive
022            experiments across various tasks, our findings confirm the risk of manipulation
023            in multi-agent systems and demonstrate the effectiveness of our attack strategies.
024            Additionally, we explore several defense mechanisms, revealing that our proposed
025            attack method remains more potent than existing baselines, underscoring the need
026            for further research on defensive strategies.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional performance and potential. To
032   address domain-specific challenges, numerous applications leveraging LLMs have been proposed,
033   including those for medical purposes (Xu, 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Bao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023b),
034   weather forecasting (Chen et al., 2023a), finance (Chen et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
035   2023b), law Yue et al. (2023), and more. These applications showcase the powerful capabilities of
036   individual LLMs. However, for more complex tasks, the collaboration of different models can lead
037   to superior solutions. For instance, in Du et al. (2023), agents engage in inter-agent communication
038   and debate, which enhances decision-making capabilities, allowing them to solve math problems
039
      that may be challenging for a single agent. Recently, many studies (Du et al., 2023; Liang et al.,
      2023; Chan et al., 2023) have demonstrated that when multiple agents work together in a coordinated
040
      manner, they exhibit improved reasoning, broader perspectives, and stronger overall performance.
041
      Furthermore, building on this cooperative approach, even more complex frameworks (Wu et al.,
042   2023a; Chen et al., 2023c; Li et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024) can be developed to further enhance the
043   performance of multi-agent systems by integrating advanced tools such as task-specific fine-tuning,
044   memory, role-playing, and more.
045
      However, since individual LLMs are vulnerable to adversarial attacks, an important question arises:
046
      Can the collective decision of a multi-agent system be manipulated if one of the agents in the system
047
      is accessible? This type of vulnerability is akin to the Byzantine Fault in distributed systems, where
048   a single compromised or malicious agent can disrupt the overall consensus. Specifically, consider
049   a multi-agent system as a group of mutually trusted experts working together to reach a specific
050   decision. Typically, these experts collaborate, each contributing their insights to arrive at the best
051   solution. But if attackers are able to communicate with one of these experts, could they ’turn’ that
052   expert and influence the group’s decision in the wrong direction? This scenario highlights a potential
053   vulnerability, where external manipulation of a single agent could compromise the integrity of the
      entire system’s decision-making process.

054
      In this scenario, we can only access one agent of a multi-agent system and have no information
055   about the other agents in the system. Each agent lacks full knowledge of the intentions, strategies,
056   or information available to other agents or potential adversaries. This uncertainty complicates the
057   process of learning effective adversarial suffixes, making potential manipulations more challenging.
058   To address this problem, we first formulate the task as a game with incomplete information, which
059   refers to a situation in which an agent lacks full knowledge about the actions of other agents. We then
060   propose a framework called M-Spoiler (Multi-agent System Spoiler), which optimizes adversarial
061   suffixes by simulating a debate with a stubborn adversary within a multi-agent system. The system
062   consists of two agents from the same model but with different characteristics.
063   We conduct experiments on three different tasks based on the AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023), SST-
064   2 (Socher et al., 2013), and CoLA (Warstadt, 2019) datasets. These tasks include determining
065   whether a given prompt is harmful, predicting whether a given review sentence has a positive sen-
066   timent, and evaluating whether a given sentence is grammatically correct. We test the adaptability
067   of our framework on different models, such as Llama2 (Touvron et al., 2023) and Mistral (Jiang
068   et al., 2023), as well as algorithmic backbones, such as GCG (Zou et al., 2023) and I-GCG (Jia
069
      et al., 2024). Additionally, we evaluate the performance of our framework on multi-agent systems
      composed of varying numbers of agents. Our experiments reveal that the risk of manipulation is
070
      significant. Furthermore, we explore several defense methods for multi-agent systems. Under vari-
071
      ous defense strategies, such as the self-perplexity filter (Jain et al., 2023), we find that the proposed
072   framework remains more effective than the corresponding baseline methods. However, additional
073   defense strategies still require further exploration.
074
075
      Our contributions in this work can be summarized as follows:
076
077
            1. We propose a research question on the safety of multi-agent systems: If one agent is acces-
               sible to attackers, can the decision of the entire multi-agent system be manipulated?
078
079
            2. We address this problem by first formulating it as a game with incomplete information and
080            then proposing a framework called M-Spoiler (Multi-agent System Spoiler) to solve it.
081
082         3. We conduct extensive experiments on different tasks, models, and algorithmic backbones
083            to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework and provide insights into miti-
084            gating such risks.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
087
088
089   Adversarial Attacks on LLMs. LLMs are vulnerable to adversarial attacks (Shayegani et al., 2023).
090   These attacks can be either targeted (Di Noia et al., 2020) or untargeted (Wu et al., 2019). Targeted
091   attacks, such as those in Wang et al. (2022), attempt to shift the output toward an attacker’s chosen
092   value by using the loss gradient in the direction of the target class. Untargeted attacks aim to cause a
093
      misprediction, where the result of a successful attack is any erroneous output. For example, Zhu et al.
      (2023a) and Wang et al. (2023) demonstrate that carefully crafted adversarial prompts can skew in-
094
      dividual LLMs’ outcomes. In addition to perceptible attacks, there are imperceptible attacks, known
095
      as semantic attacks (Wang et al., 2022; Zhuo et al., 2023), where the given prompts preserve seman-
096   tic integrity—ensuring they remain acceptable and imperceptible to human understanding—yet still
097   mislead LLMs. Furthermore, jailbreak attacks (Guo et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023b;
098   Zou et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2024) can manipulate LLMs into producing outputs that are misaligned
099   with human values or performing unintended actions.
100
      Risks of Multi-agent systems. The widespread applications of LLMs and their powerful function-
101
      ality have led to numerous studies exploring the underlying risks and trustworthiness associated with
102   individual agents (Liu et al., 2023c; Sun et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023). A finding from Sun et al.
103   (2024) shows that, for LLMs, there is a positive correlation between their general trustworthiness
104   and utility. However, despite the recent studies (Du et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2023;
105   Wu et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023c; Li et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024) demonstrating that multi-
106   agent systems typically achieve better performance, there remain potential risks in such systems. For
107   instance, Zhang et al. (2024) highlights that the dark psychological states of agents pose significant
      safety threats, while Gu et al. (2024) reveals that attacks can propagate within the system. These

139   3.1   SIMULATION WITH ADVERSARY
140
141   Since the task involves incomplete information, we propose a framework called M-Spoiler, which
142   simulates a multi-chat scenario (Fig. 1) where one agent debates with a stubborn version of itself.
143   Assuming the model is Llama2, we create a normal Llama2 and a stubborn Llama2, controlled
144
      by predetermined prompts that express fixed opinions. Suppose the target output for the normal
      Llama2 is “Harmless.” If the normal Llama2 outputs “Harmless,” the stubborn Llama2 will insist on
145
      “Harmful.” Conversely, if the normal Llama2 outputs “Harmful,” the stubborn Llama2 will agree.
146
147   During training, the two models engage in multiple rounds of conversation, maintaining a chat
148   history. For each turn of the debate, we obtain the gradient from the Normal Agent and weigh the
149   gradient according to an exponential decay function based on the turn order. The weighted gradient
150
      is then used to sample suitable candidates. Since the first round of interaction is typically the most
      critical in influencing the system’s output, and its importance naturally diminishes in subsequent
151
      rounds, we decided to decrease the weight of the gradient as the number of rounds increases. In this
152
      case, an exponential decay function is used, which is formulated as: f (λ) = αλ/t where λ is the
153
      order number of the debate, α is a constant that represents the proportion of decay in each half-life,
154   and t is the number of steps needed to decrease to half. In this paper, t is set to 1. For example, if
155   they have 3 turns of debate, the weight of the first turn will be f (0), the weight of the second turn
156   will be f (1), and the weight of the third turn will be f (2). Therefore, the weighted gradient ω∇L

456   (Llama3 and Guanaco). In the incomplete information case, the adversarial suffixes are still op-

459   ing the role of a stubborn agent, and then tested on (Llama2 and Vicuna), or optimized on Llama2
460
      only and tested on (Llama2 and Llama2).
461   According to the results shown in Table 7, as the amount of information available during the training
462   process increases, the performance of the optimized adversarial suffixes improves. Our algorithm
463   outperforms the baseline in all conditions except for the zero information condition. We believe
464   this is because the adversarial suffixes optimized by our framework fit Llama2 more closely and
465   effectively than those optimized by the baseline, which results in lower performance when Llama2

      is absent from the multi-agent system.

490   5   C ONCLUSION
491
492   This study uncovers a critical vulnerability in coordinated multi-agent systems. We demonstrate
493   that an attacker can manipulate the collective decision-making of such systems by accessing just
494   a single agent, similar to the Byzantine Fault in distributed systems. We formulate the task as a
495   game with incomplete information, where agents lack full knowledge of adversarial strategies. We
496   propose a framework called M-Spoiler, which simulates a stubborn adversary in multi-agent debates
497   during the training phase. Through extensive experiments across various tasks, we confirm the risk
498   of manipulation and demonstrate the effectiveness of our attack strategy. Furthermore, this work
499   highlights that existing defense mechanisms are inadequate against these attacks, emphasizing the
500
      urgent need for developing more robust defensive strategies in multi-agent systems.
