                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009            We investigate the phenomenon of generalization through the lens of compression.
010            In particular, we study the complexity dynamics of neural networks to explain
011            grokking, where networks suddenly transition from memorizing to generalizing
012            solutions long after over-fitting the training data. To this end we introduce a
013            new measure of intrinsic complexity for neural networks based on the theory
014            of Kolmogorov complexity. Tracking this metric throughout network training,
015            we find a consistent pattern in training dynamics, consisting of a rise and fall in
016            complexity. We demonstrate that this corresponds to memorization followed by
017
               generalization. Based on insights from rate–distortion theory and the minimum
               description length principle, we lay out a principled approach to lossy compression
018
               of neural networks, and connect our complexity measure to explicit generalization
019
               bounds. Based on a careful analysis of information capacity in neural networks, we
020            propose a new regularization method which encourages networks towards low-rank
021            representations by penalizing their spectral entropy, and find that our regularizer
022            outperforms baselines in total compression of the dataset.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      A central aim of machine learning is to learn a model which explains a dataset. A good explanation—
      and therefore a good model—is one which generalizes to unseen data. How do we choose a good
028
      explanation? Occam’s Razor says that all else being equal, less complex (i.e. simpler) explanations
029
      are expected to generalize better. This leads us to a fundamental open question in machine learning:
030   how do we measure the complexity of a model that has been learned to explain a dataset?
031
032   In this paper, we make this question sharp, then answer it using the tools of information theory,
033
      compression, and the Minimum Description Length principle (MDL), which we apply to neural
      networks to explain grokking. The MDL principle (Rissanen, 1978) is based on the following
034
      observation: any regularity in a dataset can be used to compress that dataset. By Shannon’s source
035
      coding theorem, the expected optimal coding rate for samples drawn from a distribution is equal to
036   the entropy of the distribution. To improve the encoding, we decrease the apparent entropy of the
037   data by finding patterns in it, which is the purpose of a model.
038
039
      However, the specification of the model also requires information, so the total information is the sum
      of information used to specify the model, plus the remaining entropy of the dataset under the model.
040
      We have successfully compressed the data only if the total description of the model and the remaining
041
      entropy of the data is less than the naïve entropy under a random predictor. MDL tells us to minimize
042   the sum of model complexity, denoted C(M ), and data entropy under the model, H(D | M ):
043
                                              min H(D | M ) + C(M )                                       (1)
044                                          M
045   Note that if the model M fully explains some subset DM ⊆ D then the prediction of that data
046   becomes deterministic and H(DM ) = 0. Consider a trivial model which simply memorizes the data
047
      in a lookup table. Whilst this reduces the entropy of the data to zero, the model complexity is exactly
      equal to the original entropy. Therefore total description length has not changed, and no compression
048
      has been achieved by the model. The intuition that memorizing models are not expected to generalize
049
      is made formal via generalization bounds, which we discuss and relate to compression in section 3.
050
051   To avoid learning trivial memorizing models, practitioners introduce regularizers to the model training
052   process – typically modified objective functions which encourage the model towards lower complexity
053
      solutions. Across a range of fields of statistical machine learning, generalization bounds of the form

078   To apply the MDL principle we require a computable measure of model complexity, C(M ). A
079   widespread assumption in machine learning is that a model’s capacity is a good approximation of its
080   complexity. However, capacity is the maximum upper-bound on model complexity. A lower bound
081   on complexity is given by the Kolmogorov complexity. Formally, the Kolmogorov complexity K of
082
      a string s is equal to the length of the shortest program p that prints the string:
083                                  K(s) = min{len(p) : exec(p) = s}                                      (3)
                                                p
084
085   To link this back to the memorising model above, consider the string “11111..." composed of N “1"s.
086   A program which prints this string is: print 1 N times. The length of this program compared
087   to the string’s length is asymptotically small, hence the Kolmogorov complexity of the string is
088   small. Next consider a random string of length N : “10110...", the shortest program which produces
089   it hard-codes the string: print “10110...". The length of this program is approximately equal to
090   the length of the string. In fact, this is the definition of random in algorithmic information theory
091   (AIT): a random string is one which has no description shorter than itself. Equivalently, random data
092
      is incompressible, since the shortest description of a random string is at least as long as that string.
093   While Kolmogorov complexity is uncomputable, compression provides an upper bound: the com-
094   pressed data plus its decompressor form a program that generates the original data. We exploit this
095   fact to produce complexity estimates via compression bounds, and outperform naïve compressors
096   like bzip2 by over an order of magnitude. The sensitivity to the underlying complexity dynamics
097   we achieve is crucial, and enables us to distinguish different complexity phases of the grokking
098
      phenomenon, as shown in Fig. 1. Plotting naïve compression of networks in Fig 1 would show a
      flat line at an order of magnitude larger complexity, and would fail to meaningfully track complexity
099
      dynamics in the network. Our method achieves compression ratios of 30–40× over bzip2.
100
101   Our central theoretical contribution is a principled approach to lossy compression of neural networks,
102   which is best understood as a formalization of the “apparent complexity” measure introduced by
103   Aaronson et al. (2014) using the language of algorithmic information and rate–distortion theory.
104
      We apply the resulting compression-based complexity bound to track the complexity dynamics of
      neural networks as they transition from memorization to generalization. The most striking example
105
      of this transition is the recently observed grokking phenomenon (Power et al., 2022), where networks
106
      suddenly transition from memorization to generalization long after over-fitting the training data.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
123
124   Grokking        The grokking phenomenon (Power et al., 2022) occurs when networks suddenly
125   transition from memorizing to generalizing solutions long after over-fitting the training data. Liu
126   et al. (2022a) characterize conditions under which grokking occurs, and categorize learning into
127   four distinct regimes, comprehension, grokking, memorization, and confusion by examining phase
128   diagrams of learning performance across different hyperparameters. Liu et al. (2022b) demonstrate
129   grokking on non-algorithmic datasets, and propose a link between a network’s parameter norm and
130   grokking. This link is also explored by Varma et al. (2023), who explain grokking in terms of the
131   “efficiency" of a network, as measured by the ratio of the logit to parameter norms. Nanda et al.
132
      (2023) propose task-specific loss-based “progress measures" to detect grokking, but note that we
      “lack a general notion of criticality that would allow us to predict when the phase transition" occurs.
133
      In this work, we suggest that complexity is the key dynamical metric to understand grokking (and
134
      generalization more broadly), and discuss why proxy measures such as the L2 norm are insufficient.
135
136   Generalization       The connection between model capacity and complexity is sometimes misun-
137   derstood amongst machine learning practitioners: the contemporary view in deep learning is that
138
      “larger models are better" (Nakkiran et al., 2019), with empirically observed scaling laws across
      a range of modalities and architectures (Kaplan et al., 2020; Henighan et al., 2020) which show
139
      monotonic improvement of performance with increasing model scale. This view contrasts with the
140
      classical bias-variance tradeoff, which says that “once we pass a certain threshold, larger models
141   are worse" (Nakkiran et al., 2019). The latter view is only true assuming model capacity is equal
142   to model complexity, and would follow from bounds of the form of Equation 2. However, this
143   appears not to be the case. Lotfi et al. (2024) recently established the first non-vacuous generalization
144   bounds for large language models by producing highly compressible models trained in a non-linear
145   low-rank subspace, and bounding their generalization performance under a smoothed entropy loss
146   by the Kolmogorov complexity. While they focus on explicit generalization bounds, we focus on
147   the complexity dynamics of the transition from memorization to generalization to explain grokking.
148   Goldblum et al. (2023) argue for generalization to be understood using Kolmogorov complexity, and
149   apply the Solomonoff prior, P (h) = 2−K(h) for hypotheses h to a finite hypothesis bound (Langford
150
      & Seeger, 2001) to produce a generic generalization bound based on the Kolmogorov complexity.
151   Coarse-graining       Aaronson et al. (2014) study the complexity dynamics of a cellular automaton
152   which simulates the mixing of two liquids. They observe the rise and fall of complexity as entropy
153   increases, and conjecture that this phenomenon is generic. To approximate the complexity of their
154   automaton, they propose an “apparent complexity" measure which is the compressed file size of a
155   coarse-grained description of the state. They informally assert that coarse-graining removes random
156
      information from the state, leaving only “interesting" information behind. We formalize their intuition
      using rate–distortion theory, and use the resultant complexity measure to explain grokking.

161   Understanding generalization is of central importance in machine learning. To what extent can
      we expect our models to generalize, and can we predict their performance ahead of time? Most

163   understanding generalization. Lotfi et al. (2024) provide one such bound, which quantifies the
164   expected risk R(h) for a given hypothesis h, with probability 1 − δ:

      Where K(·) is the Kolmogorov complexity, R̂(h) is the empirical risk, and n the number of samples.
169
      As mentioned in the introduction, the Kolmogorov complexity is not computable, but can be upper-
170
      bounded by compression. Therefore, we can bound our generalization error by compressing models,
171   with better bounds following from tighter compression. It is important to keep in mind that some
172   approaches we might take to compress models, such as reducing the precision of their parameters,
173   can also result in loss of performance. Hence, while we might decrease the complexity term by
174   compressing models, this may result in the empirical risk R̂(h) increasing. To produce strong
175   generalization guarantees, we must jointly minimize the model complexity and the empirical risk.
176
      In fact, this can also be understood as a restatement of the MDL principle. If the empirical risk is
177
      understood as the entropy of the training data under the model, as is typically the case, then the joint
178   minimization of the empirical risk and model complexity is exactly the MDL principle. I.e. the model
179   which generalizes best is the one which compresses the data best, and is itself most compressible.
180
181
      Unfortunately, naïvely compressing a model’s parameters produces poor complexity bounds, since
      the network contains random information left over from initialization, and from the stochastic training
182
      procedure. If we could remove the random information from a network, compressing the resulting
183
      parameters would give much tighter bounds on the network complexity. However, it is undecidable
184   whether information is random (Li & Vitányi, 1993), so there is no generic method to remove it.
185
186   Aaronson et al. (2014) remove random information by coarse-graining their automaton’s state. They
187
      coarse-grain by smoothing and quantizing the state. The coarse-grained state is then compressed with
      off-the-shelf programs such as bzip2, with the compressed file size providing improved complexity
188
      estimates. They dub this metric the “apparent complexity", since it is not actually a bound on the
189
      original state’s complexity, and is arbitrary in the choice and degree of coarse-graining. We adapt
190   their method and apply it to neural networks based on a central insight: the loss function induces a
191   coarse-graining, which can be understood as principled lossy compression of the model.
192
193   3.1   L OSSY C OMPRESSION
194
195   Shannon’s theorem provides a lower-bound on coding rates which can losslessly compress signals, but
196   more efficient coding rates are possible if some distortion is allowed. For example, JPEG compression
197   removes high-frequency details from images which are perceptually irrelevant to the human eye,
198   incurring some distortion. Rate–distortion theory formalizes the trade-off between coding rate,
199   induced distortion, and perceptual quality (Blau & Michaeli, 2019), though it is typically defined
200
      over random variables. Vereshchagin & Vitányi (2004) generalize the theory of rate distortion to the
      algorithmic information setting. They define the algorithmic rate–distortion function rx (y), which is
201
      the minimum number of bits needed to specify y with distortion relative to x no more than ϵ:
202
203                                      rx (y) = min{K(y) : d(x, y) < ϵ}                                       (5)
204
      Where d is some distortion function. We will take the “input string" x to be the model parameter
205
      vector θ, and the “output" y to be the coarse-grained parameter vector θ̂. The choice of distortion
206
      function d is critical. In particular, we are not interested in the distortion in parameter space, |θ − θ̂|2 .
207
      Instead, we are interested in the distortion under the loss function. Hence, we set:
208
209                                        d(θ, θ̂) = L(θ, D) − L(θ̂, D)                                        (6)
210
211   Substituting this distortion function into Eqn 5, we can see that the optimum can be interpreted as the
212   least complex set of model weights θ̂ which achieve approximately the same performance under the
213   loss function. We control the rate–distortion trade-off through the parameter ϵ, which leads to the
214   following distortion criterion to accept a coarse-grained approximation:
215
                                               L(θ, D) − L(θ̂, D) < ϵ                                           (7)

      4     C APACITY AND R EGULARIZATION
234
      To control model complexity, practitioners introduce regularizers to the network training process.
235
      Most regularizers work by limiting a model’s effective capacity, which may limit its complexity. To
236
      see this, we consider a simplified representation of a model parameter. The information capacity I of
237   a single positive parameter with fixed precision δ, and max size λ is given by:
238
                                                              λ
239                                                  I = log                                             (9)
240
                                                              δ
241
      Under this simplified model, it is clear that we can limit the information capacity of the parameters
      either by controlling their range through λ, or by reducing their precision through δ, which can
242
      be thought of as an effective quantization level. Regularizers such as weight decay control the
243
      information capacity by limiting λ, or proxies of it such as an Lp norm. In particular, weight decay
244   adds an independent L2 penalty for each parameter to the loss function. Methods which control
245   information capacity through δ include training in reduced precision (Micikevicius et al., 2017), and
246   quantization-aware training (Ma et al., 2024).
247
      However, training with regularization does not guarantee that the resultant model will be of low
248
      complexity. For example, while weight decay ensures parameter norms are smaller than they
249
      otherwise would have been, the network can still store detail in high-frequency information. Our
250   experiments demonstrate that this indeed occurs in practice. A key requirement for complexity
251   reduction therefore is a regularization scheme which explicitly alleviates this issue.
252
253
      A further complication is information which is spread across multiple parameters. Networks are
      typically parameterized by matrices which represent linear transformations, such that relevant infor-
254
      mation exists in their spectrum. Ultimately such information is accounted for by the total sum of
255
      parameter capacities, but it is helpful to think of information in the spectral representation.
256
257   We now introduce a regularization scheme which addresses both of these challenges.

      While coarse-graining by quantization is simple and effective, it can only address high-frequency
284
      noise. In this section we introduce another coarse-graining procedure which takes advantage of
285   low-rank approximation to remove random information. We use this low-rank approximation both
286   as a regularization method to limit model complexity, and as a coarse-graining method during the
287   compression stage, to produce tighter complexity estimates throughout training.
288
289   Low rank approximation is the problem of finding an approximating matrix M̃ for a matrix M subject
290   to the constraint that rank(M̃ ) ≤ r for some r, typically r ≪ rank(M ). Low-rank approximation of
291
      neural network weight matrices has recently been used to achieve compute– and parameter–efficient
      finetuning of LLMs (Hu et al., 2021; Dettmers et al., 2023), building on the insight that both the
292
      weights and update gradients live on a low-dimension subspace of the total weight space (Li et al.,
293
      2018; Aghajanyan et al., 2020).
294
295   The mismatch between the apparent and effective ranks of the largest models is another manifestation
296   of the difference between the capacity and complexity of networks. While large capacity may be
297
      needed for training dynamics to find optimal solutions, those learned solutions which generalize best
      tend to be of low effective rank, and therefore low complexity.

      5   E XPERIMENTS
379
380
381   To study the complexity dynamics of networks transitioning from memorizing to generalizing
382   solutions, we adopt the “grokking" tasks first reported in Power et al. (2022). These are a set of
383   modular arithmetic tasks which networks easily over-fit, quickly achieving zero training error while
384
      test error remains large, suggesting memorization of the training examples. After many further
      steps of gradient descent after over-fitting, networks suddenly generalize and achieve perfect test
385
      accuracy. Although grokking is a sign of improperly regularized training, the extended delay between
386
      memorization and generalization is convenient for understanding complexity dynamics, and their
387   relationship with generalization.
388
389   We demonstrate a regularizer, Lreg on the grokking tasks and show that 1) it causes grokking 2) it
390
      results in lower complexity networks than weight decay alone, the most widely-used regularization
      method, as measured by our complexity metric.
391
392   We reproduce a subset of the grokking experiments first reported in Power et al. (2022). Our training
393   tasks all consist of learning a binary operation mod a prime number p = 113. For each binary
394   operation we construct a dataset of equations of the form ⟨x⟩⟨op⟩⟨y⟩⟨=⟩⟨x ◦ y⟩, where ⟨a⟩ stands for
395   the token corresponding to element a. We train standard decoder-only transformers with a single layer
396
      for mod addition and multiplication, and two layers for mod subtraction and division. Further training
      details can be found in the appendix. We use Algorithm 1 to compute complexity upper-bounds
397
      on the coarse-grained models. The final compression step is performed by bzip2, an off-the-shelf
398
      compression utility.
399
400   Figure 10 in the appendix is a sanity check to ensure that grokking occurs: indeed, the regularized
401   models generalize, while the unregularized models never achieve generalization. Fig 2 plots accuracy
402
      and complexity together for our regularized models, and indicates that as we expected from Occam’s
      Razor, complexity is maximized during memorization, and falls during generalization. Next, we plot
403
      algorithmic rate–distortion curves of best fit in Fig 3. We fit curves of the form y = a log(x) + b, and
404
      display R2 values in the legend. These curves show how lossy model compression trades off with
405   lossless data compression for the converged models at varying distortion bounds.
406
407   We compare the total description length of all models in Fig 4, and find that the models produced
408
      by our regularizer achieve the best explanation of the dataset amongst the methods. That is, they
      achieve a smaller sum of model complexity and data entropy vs the other methods, hence achieve
409
      the greatest compression of the data in this model class. Plotting the generalization bound from Eqn.
410
      4, we see in Fig 5 that again, the models produced by our regularizer come closest to a non-trivial
411   generalization bound. The goal of this work is to explain grokking, clarify the nature of complexity
412   and its relationship to generalization, and provide a practical method of complexity approximation
413   based on insights from lossy compression. In particular, we explained how lossy compression
414   provides a principled approach to noise reduction and coarse-graining in networks. In particular, we
415   did not attempt to achieve the tightest possible complexity bounds. Nevertheless, it is surprising that
416   we get as close as we do to non-trivial generalization bounds, since the models we use are large, and
417   the datasets small: |D| = 12769 vs. |D| ≈ 1.5 × 1013 for contemporary LLMs (Dubey et al., 2024).

420   6   C ONCLUSION
421
422
423
      This work examines the complexity dynamics of neural networks, and proposes an explanation of the
      grokking phenomenon—the sudden transition from memorization to generalization long after the
424
      training data is over-fit. We do this by introducing a novel measure of intrinsic complexity based on
425
      Kolmogorov complexity and rate-distortion theory. We observe a consistent pattern of the rise and
426   fall of complexity during training, corresponding to memorization followed by generalization. Our
427   principled approach to lossy compression of neural networks formalizes the concept of "apparent
428   complexity" and connects it to explicit generalization bounds. Our proposed spectral entropy
429   regularization method encourages low-rank representations by penalizing a differentiable measure of
430   the model’s effective dimension, which helps limit model complexity and enables tight compression
431   bounds. We hope that a deeper view of complexity beyond mere parameter count and capacity
      measures will lead to greater insight into the nature of generalization in learning systems.
