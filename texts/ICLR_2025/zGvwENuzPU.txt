009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            While chain-of-thought prompting (CoT) has the potential to improve the explain-
012            ability of language model reasoning, it can systematically misrepresent the factors
013            influencing models’ behavior—for example, rationalizing answers in line with a
014            user’s opinion without mentioning this bias. To mitigate this biased reasoning
015            problem, we introduce bias-augmented consistency training (BCT), an unsuper-
016            vised fine-tuning scheme that trains models to give consistent reasoning across
017            prompts with and without biasing features. We construct a suite testing nine forms
018
               of biased reasoning on seven question-answering tasks, and find that applying BCT
               to GPT-3.5-Turbo with one bias reduces the rate of biased reasoning by 86% on
019
               held-out tasks. Moreover, this model generalizes to other forms of bias, reducing
020
               biased reasoning on held-out biases by an average of 37%. As BCT generalizes to
021            held-out biases and does not require gold labels, this method may hold promise
022            for reducing biased reasoning from as-of-yet unknown biases and on tasks where
023            ground truth reasoning is unavailable.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Prompting large language models (LLMs) to produce step-by-step reasoning before giving a final
029   output, termed chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting, improves their performance on many tasks (Nye
030   et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2024). If CoT reasoning is faithful (Jacovi & Goldberg,
031   2020)—that is, it accurately describes the process models use to arrive at predictions—we can
032   improve safety and fairness by checking for flawed or undesirable reasoning (Lightman et al., 2024).
033
      However, a challenge with guaranteeing the faithfulness of CoT is biased reasoning. Turpin et al.
      (2023) found that, for example, using a few-shot prompt where the multiple-choice answers are
034
      always “(A)” leads models to generate CoT reasoning that justifies the answer being “(A)” on a new
035
      question. These results suggest that models do not verbalize all features that influence their reasoning
036   and final predictions, limiting our ability to understand and anticipate model behavior.
037
038   We introduce bias-augmented consistency training (BCT; Figure 2), a simple and scalable unsuper-
039
      vised fine-tuning scheme for reducing biased reasoning. In BCT, we first get a model to generate
      unbiased CoT reasoning (i.e., none of our biasing features are included in the prompt) for a question.
040
      Then, we create a biased prompt by augmenting the original question with a bias toward a random
041
      answer choice. Finally, we perform supervised fine-tuning on the model with this dataset of bias-
042   augmented prompts and unbiased CoT reasoning. Training for consistent reasoning across these
043   prompts reduces susceptibility to influential biasing features that are unverbalized in model explana-
044   tions, thereby reducing biased reasoning. Our approach frames biased reasoning (and explanation
045   faithfulness more broadly) as ultimately a problem of consistency between a model’s explanations
046   and its behavior across inputs (§2.1). This framing allows us to exploit the unsupervised nature of
047   consistency training objectives, avoiding the need for ground truth reasoning.
048   To evaluate BCT, we construct a suite comprising nine biases (Figure 1)—e.g., spurious few-shot
049   patterns, post hoc rationalization, sycophancy (Perez et al., 2022), distractor text—and seven factual
050   question-answering and reasoning tasks. In §4, we perform BCT on GPT-3.5-Turbo (GPT-3.5T;
051   OpenAI, 2022) with a simple form of sycophancy, where the user explicitly suggests which answer
052   they think is correct, and find that this reduces biased reasoning from sycophancy by 86% on held-
053   out tasks. Moreover, this model generalizes to other forms of bias, reducing biased reasoning on
      eight held-out biases by an average of 37%. We find the same results when conducting the same

129   2.1     B IASED R EASONING AS A C ONSISTENCY P ROBLEM
130
131   Turpin et al. (2023) finds that CoT reasoning can be steered towards incorrect answers due to features
132   in the prompt—e.g., by having a user suggest that a specific answer choice is correct—which we
133   refer to as biases. Models do not mention the influence of these features, and instead change their
134   reasoning to rationalize giving biased answers, in comparison to inputs without these features. We
135   refer to such unfaithful rationalizations induced by biases in the prompt as biased reasoning. Turpin
136
      et al. (2023) only considers three types of biases, while in this paper we expand this to nine (§3.4).
137   CoT reasoning can be viewed as an explanation for the model’s final prediction, but because explana-
138   tions do not mention the influence of biases, they are unfaithful—they do not accurately describe the
139   process models are using to make a prediction (Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020). In general, a model’s ex-
140   planation is deemed faithful if, upon reading the explanation, it allows humans to correctly anticipate
141   the model’s behavior (either final predictions or intermediate reasoning steps) across a diverse range
142
      of relevant inputs—this is the simulatability framework of faithfulness (Doshi-Velez & Kim, 2017;
      Hase & Bansal, 2020; Chen et al., 2023). Under this definition, the explanation faithfulness problem
143
      can be viewed as an explanation-consistency problem: Unfaithfulness is an inconsistency between a
144
      model’s explanation for its behavior and its observed behavior on other inputs. Conversely, if we can
145   improve the consistency of model reasoning across similar inputs, we can improve humans’ ability to
146   simulate model behavior (i.e., faithfulness).
147
148
      For example, in Figure 2, when given an unbiased prompt, GPT-3.5T reasons that oil spills typically
      reduce nutrient availability, supporting option A. When given a prompt biased toward B, the model
149
      contradicts its unbiased reasoning, arguing that oil can break down, increasing nutrient availability
150
      for plants on net. This example highlights how inconsistency in reasoning on different inputs allows
151   the model to give coherent reasoning supporting different answers, without verbalizing that the model
152   was sensitive to the bias.

      2.2     M ETHOD
155
156   In this paper, our training scheme reduces sensitivity to influential biasing features that are unverbal-
157   ized in model explanations, which makes the model’s behavior on biased prompts more consistent
158   with its explanations on prompts without our biasing features. We do this by performing supervised
159   fine-tuning on prompts with biasing text paired with CoT responses generated from prompts without
160   the biasing text (Figure 2). This method can also be used with unbiased non-CoT responses in
161   order to debias models’ non-CoT behavior (§5.1). This method does not require ground truth labels
      or reasoning—we do not need to assess the truth value of model reasoning to train them to give


                                                                              3

162
      consistent reasoning in two contexts. This is particularly useful for reducing biased reasoning in
163   cases where the biased reasoning is coherent and might be hard to spot by supervision methods based
164   on human feedback (Christiano et al., 2017), as we show in §5.2.

170
      We apply BCT to gpt-3.5-turbo-0613 (GPT-3.5T; Ouyang et al., 2022) using the OpenAI fine-

172

173   (Kojima et al., 2022); see Appendix M for the full prompts. For the biased prompt, we randomly
174   select which answer choice we bias models towards, so sometimes this bias lines up with the correct
175   response—we want reasoning to be uncorrelated from the bias, not anti-correlated. To reduce variance,
176   we average results across eight fine-tuning runs with the same training data and use several slightly
177   varying prompt formats during training (Appendix C). In Appendix D, we show that BCT is equally
178   effective on open-source models, specifically LLaMA-3 8B Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024).
179   Bias-Augmented Consistency Training (BCT). We perform supervised fine-tuning on biased
180   prompts with unbiased CoT completions. We use 10k prompt-response pairs and use a 50/50 mix of
181   CoT/non-CoT prompt-response pairs. Including non-CoT prompts and responses helps us maintain
182   consistent CoT and non-CoT performance before and after training; eliciting non-CoT responses is
183   needed for evaluating the Are You Sure bias (§3.4).
184
      Self-Training (Control). We train on unbiased prompts with unbiased completions. This baseline
185   allows us to control for the effects of doing further fine-tuning on the model’s outputs. This dataset has
186   the same non-CoT/CoT data mixture and the same number of tokens (inputs and outputs combined)
187   as above.
188
      To maintain instruction-following performance, we add 10k samples (50% of total) of instruction-
189
      following data to both the BCT and control data. To ensure our method is fully unsupervised, we
190
      generate temperature 1.0 completions from the same model (GPT-3.5T) using prompts from the
191   Cleaned Alpaca dataset (Ruebsamen, 2023; Taori et al., 2023).

      3.2   M EASURING B IASED R EASONING
194
195   Following Turpin et al. (2023), we explore biased reasoning in a multiple-choice task setting. We
196   measure biased reasoning by measuring how much more often models choose a particular incorrect
197   answer when guided to do so by the prompt bias, in comparison to how often the models choose that
198   incorrect answer when given a prompt with no injected bias. This difference is termed the biased
199   reasoning rate (BRR).
200   In our evaluation, we always bias towards incorrect answers.2 This difference measures the degree
201   of biased reasoning—and, in turn, explanation unfaithfulness—because models generally do not
202   verbalize the influence of biasing features: We manually check 1040 instances where models answer
203   in line with biases across every combination of models, biases, and evaluation datasets and for most
204   biases they are never verbalized. The only instances of verbalization (GPT-3.5T, control, BCT) are for
205   Suggested Answer (4%, 4%, 0%) and Argument (4%, 14%, 6%). These differences in verbalization
206   between the BCT model and the others are not large enough to account for the differences in BRR.
207   Positional Bias and Are You Sure are measured in a slightly different way from the other biases
208
      (§3.4).
209   We assess the effectiveness of fine-tuning by computing the ratio of the BRR of the model after
210   fine-tuning (either BCT or control) against the BRR of the model before fine-tuning (i.e. GPT-3.5T),
211   termed the BRR ratio. Lower is better.
212   Measuring generalization of bias reduction to new biases and new tasks. It is difficult to predict
213   the biases models will encounter when a model is deployed. So, we would like a training method that

234   3.4   B IASES
235
      We use the following biases throughout our experiments (further details in Appendix C, full prompts
236
      in Appendix L). The Wrong Few-Shot, Post Hoc, and Argument biases contain clarifying text to
237
      make it unambiguous that the biased answer is not necessarily correct.
238
239   Sycophancy: Suggested Answer (Training). Models demonstrate sycophancy, which is the tendency
240   to generate reasoning and answers that align with a user’s view (Perez et al., 2022). We use variants
241
      of the Suggested Answer bias from Turpin et al. (2023) for training, in which the user suggests an
      answer could be correct. To increase diversity, we use GPT-4T to generate 64 slight paraphrases of
242
      the biasing text, add a negated version, and vary where this bias is inserted into the prompt.
243
244   Sycophancy: Are You Sure? Sharma et al. (2023) find that assistants often change their answers
245   when users respond with “Are you sure?” We generate a response across three rounds: (1) generate a
246   non-CoT response from the model and filter to correct responses, (2) ask the model “Are you sure?”,
247
      and then finally (3) ask it to generate CoT reasoning for this second response. Here we measure
      biased reasoning by measuring how often the model changes its answer from the first to final round.
248
      The unbiased baseline is assumed to be 0%.
249
250   Post Hoc Rationalization. If models answer a question incorrectly and then explain, they tend
251   to give incorrect reasoning, even if they can give the correct reasoning when prompted to give an
252   explanation before answering. We explicitly insert an incorrect non-CoT answer into the model’s
253
      side of the chat and prompt the model to explain its reasoning.
254   Wrong Few-Shot. Models can be biased if a user mistakenly uses a few-shot prompt with incorrect
255   labels. We bias models by adding a few-shot example with an incorrect label to the few-shot prompt
256   and then ask the model the same question again.
257   Wrong Argument. Models may erroneously copy over reasoning from arguments in the context. We
258   insert reasoning that supports a wrong answer choice. To differentiate from sycophancy, the user text
259   clarifies that they do not know if this argument is correct.
260
      Spurious Few-Shot: Squares. Language models are sensitive to repeated patterns in prompts (Brown
261
      et al., 2020; McKenzie et al., 2023). We append a black square (■) next to the correct answers in the
262
      few-shot prompt and to an incorrect answer on the final question.
263
264   Spurious Few-Shot: Hindsight. In this task from McKenzie et al. (2023), models are prompted to
265   assess if a bet was worthwhile based on the expected value. Models are biased to give the wrong
266
      answer through a few-shot prompt, where the outcomes of the bets match the expected value, but the
      outcome in the final question does not. The unbiased baseline is a few-shot prompt with examples
267
      where the label does not match the outcome.
268
269   Distractor Fact. Language model reasoning is sensitive to irrelevant information in the context (Shi
      et al., 2023). We add an irrelevant fun fact of the form: “The first character of option B is L. L is


                                                       5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      letter number 12 of the English alphabet” to bias the model towards B. The prompt clarifies that the
271   fact may be irrelevant.
272
273   Positional Bias. Models are sensitive to the order in which answer choices are presented (Zheng
274
      et al., 2023). We ask models to judge which of two model completions (GPT-3.5T and GPT-4T) are
      of higher quality. We measure the rate at which models change their answers when the answer order
275
      is swapped. The unbiased baseline is assumed to be 0%.

      is plausibly a different biasing mechanism than sycophancy-type text that claims an answer is correct.
322
323   These results show that BCT can generalize to biases held out from training, suggesting that this
      method may hold promise for reducing biased reasoning from unknown biases.

      Failure cases highlight the limits of generalization. For Argument, while the results are significant,
325   the bias only sees a BRR ratio of .89. We hypothesize that this reduced effect could be because
326   this bias encourages the model to erroneously copy flawed reasoning steps in the prompt, unlike
327   the other biases, which plausibly work by anchoring the model onto a particular answer choice.
328   Positional Bias only sees a very slight change, with a BRR ratio of .95, which is also achieved by
329   the self-training control. This result is not surprising given that Positional Bias is completely a
330   different type of bias from Suggested Answer. We hypothesize that consistency for this bias may be
331   harder to achieve because there is no explicit biasing text in the prompt that the model can learn to
332   ignore.

336   5.1   BCT WITHOUT C OT GENERALIZES TO REDUCE BIASED REASONING IN C OT
337
338   We perform BCT with non-CoT examples and evaluate biased reasoning when given CoT prompts.
339
      We use the same training/evaluation split of tasks and biases as the main experiments. Table 4 shows
      the results. BCT with non-CoT generalizes well to reduce biased reasoning on held-out biases in a
340
      CoT setting, with a BRR ratio of .70 (BRR = 29.9%). However, doing BCT with CoT is important
341
      for maximum performance, with an overall BRR ratio of .62 (BRR = 26.6%). This 3.3% difference
342   in BRR is statistically significant with a confidence interval of ±1.0% (p < 1e − 4) by paired
343   t-test. Across each bias individually, BCT with CoT outperforms BCT with non-CoT, suggesting
344   this trend is not specific to the biases we test in this paper. This generalization suggests similar
345   mechanisms underlying why models give biased answers in non-CoT contexts and why models give
346   biased reasoning in CoT. For full details, see Appendix G.

386   5.3   BCT MINIMALLY AFFECTS MODEL PERFORMANCE
387
388   We find that BCT minimally degrades model performance. See Appendix I for details. Table 10
389
      shows that when given unbiased prompts on our evaluation datasets, there is no significant difference
      in zero-shot CoT accuracy between the self-training control (61.1%) and BCT model (61.5%), but
390
      we see a slight decrease compared to GPT-3.5T (62.9%). We evaluate few-shot performance on
391
      TruthfulQA, which is the only evaluation dataset where GPT-3.5T benefits from few-shot examples
392   given an unbiased prompt. Figure 9 shows no difference between the control and BCT model in
393   few-shot performance (71%), but we see a slight decrease compared with GPT-3.5T (74.0%).

395
      A particular failure mode that we can imagine resulting from BCT with the Suggested Answer
      bias is teaching models to ignore instructions. To test if this happens, we use MT-Bench to evaluate
396
      instruction-following (Zheng et al., 2023). We find that the model from our main experiments, which
397
      includes instruction-tuning data, gets a score of 8.41, on par with GPT-3.5T’s 8.35 (Table 11). If we
398   remove instruction-tuning data altogether, we find a slight degradation in performance, decreasing to
399   8.25. We find degraded performance on adversarial tasks from McKenzie et al. (2023) that require
400   models to repeat mistakes made by users, with accuracy decreasing from 52.4% to 45.0% (Table 12).
401
      Our bias reduction results are not very sensitive to the proportion of BCT data vs. instruction-tuning
402
      data used (Figure 10). In our main experiments, we use 50% BCT data and 50% instruction-tuning
403

404   examples absolute), we still observe a significant effect on held-out biases, with a BRR ratio of .66,
405   compared with .62 in the main experiments (Table 4).

407   5.4   BCT WITH SYCOPHANCY DOES NOT GENERALIZE TO REDUCE INCONSISTENCY FROM
408         QUESTION PARAPHRASING ON UNBIASED PROMPTS
409
410   Models are known to be highly sensitive to prompt formatting choices (Lu et al., 2022; Sclar et al.,
411   2024). We investigate if the model from §4, which has been trained to reduce bias, generalizes to
412   reduce prompt sensitivity. If prompt sensitivity is due to hidden systematic biases, we might expect
413   that improving reasoning consistency w.r.t. biased prompts through BCT training could improve the
414
      consistency of CoT reasoning across paraphrases of unbiased prompts. In the previous experiments,
      we see that reducing bias across paraphrases of biased prompts works well (given that multiple biases
415
      uses paraphrases); here we see that generalization to reducing prompt sensitivity across unbiased
416
      prompts does not. Using GPT-4T, we generate 10 paraphrased variants per question and manually
417   verify that the paraphrases do not change the ground truth answer. We measure the entropy of the
418   distribution of greedily decoded CoT answers across paraphrase versions, i.e., with one CoT per
419   paraphrased question. We use the same evaluation datasets as described previously (§3.3). We use
420   200 questions per dataset for a total of 600 unique questions (6000 paraphrased questions). We find
421   that GPT-3.5T gives inconsistent CoTs on different paraphrases of the same question, with an entropy
422   of 1.01 bits. Reducing the entropy should be possible (GPT-4T achieves an entropy of 0.76 bits), but
423   we find that our method does not improve it, obtaining 1.10 bits for the BCT model and 1.10 bits
424   for the control model. These results suggest that training for consistency w.r.t. to sycophancy is not
425
      sufficient to get the model to generalize to reduce sensitivity to all types of irrelevant features in the
      inputs. Other modifications to our consistency training scheme could be adapted to this setting (§7).
426
      See Appendix J for further experimental details.

429   6     R ELATED WORK
430
431   Consistency training. Consistency-based methods have been used as an evaluation method (Fluri
      et al., 2023), and as an unsupervised training signal to improve model performance (Xie et al., 2020;


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Elazar et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Akyürek et al., 2024) and adversarial robustness (Uesato et al.,
433   2019). In contrast to these works, we propose using a consistency training objective w.r.t. model
434   explanations as a novel approach to improve the faithfulness of language model explanations. Model
435   explanations help us derive a more diverse range of counterfactuals over which to enforce consistent
436   behavior. Other works improve explanation-consistency by performing inference-time consistency
437   checks on reasoning premises (Kassner et al., 2023). Concurrently to this work, Chen et al. (2024)
438   propose an explanation-consistency training scheme. They generate training data by prompting
439   models to give reasoning on counterfactuals that is consistent with model reasoning on a previous
440   in-context input. In contrast, we use consistency training w.r.t. biasing features to reduce biased
441
      reasoning, an important source of systematic unfaithfulness.
442   Improving faithfulness. Other approaches improve CoT faithfulness by improving consistency in
443   more indirect ways. Task decomposition-based methods (Perez et al., 2020; Creswell & Shanahan,
444   2022; Radhakrishnan et al., 2023) break up tasks into atomic subtasks which can be solved in separate
445   model calls, eliminating the risk of additional context biasing subtask answers. Process-based
446   supervision methods (Stuhlmüller & Byun, 2022) that target the correctness of model reasoning
447
      steps (Uesato et al., 2022; Lightman et al., 2024) can improve consistency—if there is only one
      correct reasoning process, this entails consistency. In contrast, our approach of consistency training
448
      w.r.t. biases requires neither gold demonstrations nor the ability to supervise the correctness of
449
      individual reasoning steps. Other works improve the consistency between a model’s reasoning and
450   final prediction on a single input (Lyu et al., 2023), whereas we focus on the consistency in reasoning
451   across inputs.
452
453
      Measuring faithfulness. Some works conduct simulatability studies where humans (or a model
      simulating a human) are asked to predict what models would respond to a new question after reading
454
      a model’s explanation on another input (Chen et al., 2023; Mills et al., 2023). Atanasova et al. (2023)
455
      find input modifications that change model predictions and see if these are reflected by explanations.
456   Lanham et al. (2023) evaluate faithfulness by measuring if models are sensitive to edits made to their
457   reasoning.
458
459
      Reducing sensitivity to biases. Other works reduce sensitivity to biases like sycophancy using
      supervised fine-tuning with synthetic data (Wei et al., 2024), prompting (Ganguli et al., 2023; Li et al.,
460
      2024a), causal methods (Wu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024), filtering out irrelevant
461
      information in prompts (Weston & Sukhbaatar, 2023), or steering models with perturbations to hidden
462   states (Zou et al., 2023; Rimsky et al., 2023).

465   7    C ONCLUSION
466
467
      We introduce bias-augmented consistency training and demonstrate its promise for improving the
468   faithfulness of externalized model reasoning. We construct a suite of nine widely-applicable biases and
469   show that performing BCT with one bias generalizes to reduce biased reasoning across eight held-out
470   biases. We conduct analysis to ensure that this method is practically applicable: model performance
471   is minimally affected, it works well with little data, and it is insensitive to hyperparameters. We find
472   that BCT reduces instances of coherent biased reasoning without labels, highlighting the utility of the
473   unsupervised nature of consistency training methods. As BCT generalizes to held-out biases and does
474   not require gold labels, this method may hold promise for reducing biased reasoning from as-of-yet
475   unknown biases and on tasks where supervision for ground truth reasoning is unavailable.
476   Future work should consider: (1) Improving reasoning consistency across a more diverse range of
477   counterfactual inputs than just the presence vs. absence of biasing augmentations. For example,
478   models should give consistent reasoning across questions that depend on the same fact or should apply
479   the same assumptions to similar instances of tasks that feature complex reasoning and ambiguity
480   (e.g., medical diagnosis). (2) Digging deeper into understanding why this method generalizes to new
481   biases and improving generalization by increasing the diversity of tasks and biases in training and
482
      evaluation. (3) Instead of learning to ignore biases, we can also attempt to teach models to verbalize
      them, which may be promising for biases that can sometimes be informative, such as user opinions.
483
484   Limitations. BCT requires that we have a prompt where the bias is not present to reduce susceptibility
485   to that bias specifically. Doing this is not obvious for some biases; for example, for Positional
      Bias we cannot construct prompts with no ordering to answer choices.

758   We use the OpenAI fine-tuning API with a learning rate multiplier of 1.6x and batch size of 16. We
759   chose a learning rate early on in experiments that led to a reasonable trade-off in terms of training
760   speed, the debiasing effect, and retaining general model capabilities, and used this throughout the
761   project. We do not set the prompt loss weight (i.e. we take gradients w.r.t. the prompt and response,
762   instead of the response only) as this feature was unavailable at the time of training. Table 2 shows the
763   breakdown of datasets that we use for training.
764   To reduce variance in our setup, we average each question (n=600 for most biases; Table 3) across
765   eight fine-tuning runs. For Argument, Hindsight, we also average across multiple prompts to reduce
766   prompt variance. Thus, each question is reduced to the percentage of times across these conditions
767   that the model answers line with biases. We then use the number of unique questions (usually 600)
768   to compute sampling variance. This avoids artificially decreasing confidence intervals by double
769   counting the same question multiple times across different models and prompt formats.
770   Similarly, different biases overlap on the questions used for evaluation (except for Positional
771   Bias and Hindsight which use separate tasks). So when computing the sampling variance for the
772   micro-average across biases, we use 600 as the sample size for the overlapping biases. Thus, we use
773   a total sample size of 600 + 600 (Positional Bias) + 315 (Hindsight) = 1515.

845   models demonstrate sycophancy, which is the tendency to generate answers that align with the users’
846   view (Perez et al., 2022; Sharma et al., 2023). We apply a number of variations to this prompt:
847   We use GPT-4T to generate 64 paraphrases, add a negated version (“I don’t think the answer is

849   Sycophancy: “Are you sure?” Sharma et al. (2023) find that assistants often change their answers
850   when users respond with “I don’t think that’s right, are you sure?” While Sharma et al. (2023)
851   analyze this effect in a non-CoT setting, we investigate this in a CoT setting. We generate a response
852   across three rounds: (1) generate a non-CoT response from the model and filter to question where
853   the model gets the answer correct, (2) ask the model “Are you sure?”, and then finally (3) ask it to
854
      generate CoT reasoning for this second response. Unlike most other biases, there is no bias towards
      one particular answer, so we measure biased reasoning by measuring how often the model changes
855
      its answer after asking “Are you sure?” For this bias, we expect perfectly unbiased models to not
856
      switch answers from correct to incorrect, so the unbiased baseline is 0%.

859   to give incorrect reasoning, even if they can give the correct reasoning when prompted to give an

      of the chat and prompt the model to perform CoT. We clarify in the prompt that the model should not
861

      Spurious Few-Shot: Squares. Language models are sensitive to repeated patterns in prompts

879   Spurious Few-Shot: Hindsight. We use a task from McKenzie et al. (2023) where models are
880
      prompted to assess if a bet is worth taking. Models are given the odds of the bet as well as the
      outcome. In this context, the desired behavior is that the model makes the decision based on the
881
      expected value of the bet and is not affected by the outcome of the bet and this is how the labels are
882
      assigned for the task. Models are biased to give the wrong answer through a few-shot prompt, where
883   the outcomes of the bets match the expected value, but the outcome in the final question does not.
884   The few-shot prompt contains only labels—no CoT demonstrations.
885
886
      To calculate an unbiased baseline, we measure the rate at which a model answers incorrectly when
      prompted with a few-shot prompt where the outcomes are uncorrelated with the labels. This non-
887
      spurious prompt has an equal proportion of examples where the outcomes of the bets do match
888
      the expected value and the outcomes do not match the expected value. We observe sensitivity to
889   prompting on this task and so use 4 different prompt variations and average across them.

      information in the context (Shi et al., 2023). The evaluation used by Shi et al. (2023) does not bias

893

907   To demonstrate the generalizability of Bias-Augmented Consistency Training (BCT) beyond propri-
908   etary models, we replicate our primary experimental setup (Section 3) using LLaMA-3 8b Instruct,
909   an open-source language model. The experiment closely follows the approach used for GPT-3.5, with
910   a minor modification to the prompt format. Specifically, we prepend the phrase “Let’s think step by
911   step:” to the assistant’s responses to improve compliance with the specified format.
912   We finetune LLaMA-3 8B Instruct for BCT using low-rank adaptation (LoRA Hu et al. (2021)) via
913   the Fireworks AI API with the default settings. In lines with the GPT-3.5 experiments, we finetune
914   LLaMA-3 8B Instruct with BCT using the Suggest Answer bias, and assess its generalization to other
915   held-out biases and tasks. We excluded Positional Bias and Hindsight Neglect from our analysis due
916   to issues with getting this model to comply with the necessary output formats on these tasks. Based
917   on previous results showing minimal impact on Positional Bias, we anticipate that including these
      would slightly reduce the observed BCT improvement.

1112   Since it is common to debias models in a non-CoT setting, we want to know if doing so is effective at
1113   reducing biasing reasoning in CoT. In our main experiments (§4), we measure the bias generalization
1114   effects (i.e. evaluating biased reasoning on held-out biases) that result from doing BCT with CoT and
1115   evaluate with CoT. In this section we measure CoT generalization effects: we do BCT with non-CoT
1116   and evaluate with CoT.
1117   We mix in 5% of unbiased prompt CoT examples in order to retain the ability to elicit CoT responses
1118   after fine-tuning. Bias augmentations are only included on the non-CoT examples in order to ensure
1119   that bias reduction effects come from the non-CoT examples. Non-CoT examples are shorter than
1120   CoT examples, so to hold tokens constant we use more examples—17k vs. 10k previously in §3.1.
1121   We use the same training and evaluation split of tasks and biases from the main experiments. Thus,
1122   we are testing bias, task, and CoT generalization simultaneously.
1123   We perform BCT with non-CoT examples and evaluate biased reasoning when given CoT prompts.
1124   We use the same training/evaluation split of tasks and biases as the main experiments. Table 4 shows
1125   the results. BCT with non-CoT generalizes well to reduce biased reasoning on held-out biases in a
1126   CoT setting, with a BRR ratio of .70 (BRR = 29.9%). However, doing BCT with CoT is important
1127   for maximum performance, with an overall BRR ratio of .62 (BRR = 26.6%). A paired t-test reveals
1128   that this difference in BRR of 3.3% is statistically significant with a confidence interval of ±1.0%
1129   (p < 1e − 4). Across each bias individually, BCT with CoT outperforms BCT with non-CoT,
1130
       suggesting this trend is not specific to the biases we test in this paper.
1131   The generalization is not perfect, suggesting some differences in why models give biased responses
1132   in either setting. This is also compatible with existing works that find that these do not necessarily
1133   transfer to one another: Turpin et al. (2023) find that in some settings CoT can steer models toward
       rationalizing biased answers even when models would have given an unbiased answer without CoT.
