                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large language models (LLMs) have mastered a wide range of reasoning tasks,
012            with an underlying assumption that the tasks are well-specified for LLMs to reach
013            solutions. In reality, queries and instructions to LLMs often contain incomplete
014            or underspecified information. Therefore, LLMs need to be able to actively ac-
015            quire missing information, e.g., by asking clarifying questions, ideally seeking the
016            minimally sufficient piece of information. To assess whether LLMs possess this
017            ability, we construct Q UEST B ENCH, a set of underspecified reasoning tasks that
018            can be solved by asking at most a single question. We frame the tasks as constraint
019            satisfaction problems with missing variable assignments, where the exact model
020
               response cannot be determined unless certain variables’ values are acquired. This
               framework specifically targets tasks where uncertainty stems from missing infor-
021
               mation, rather than semantic ambiguity in language. Q UEST B ENCH includes (1)
022
               Logic-Q: Logical reasoning tasks where one proposition is missing, (2) Planning-
023            Q: PDDL planning problems where the initial state is partially observed, and (3)
024            GSM-Q: Grade school math problems where one variable assignment is missing.
025            Each task presents multiple choices of possible questions, only one of which is
026            correct. We evaluate Gemini and GPT-4o models and find that they achieve 20 –
027            30% accuracy in both zero-shot and few-shot settings. When evaluating GPT-4-o1
028            on a subset of our data, we find that it is only 41 – 44% accurate, despite using
029            state-of-the-art inference-time reasoning techniques. When investigating charac-
030            teristics of QuestBench, we find that LLMs struggle with tasks that are computa-
031
               tionally expensive for traditional search-based CSP solvers. Our analyses reveal a
               negative correlation between LLM accuracy and solver runtime complexity, sug-
032
               gesting that LLMs may share similar limitations to CSP solvers.

140   2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORKS
141
142   Ambiguity in User Requests Natural language queries posed by human users can be ambiguous
143   for many different reasons. Prior work has examined ambiguity in the context of semantic uncer-
144   tainty (Kuhn et al., 2023b), factual question-answering (Min et al., 2020), intents in task-oriented
145   dialogues (Rastogi et al., 2020; Budzianowski et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2024b), personalized human
146   preferences and tasks (Li et al., 2023; Handa et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a). Zhang et al. (2024a)
147   introduces a taxonomy of ambiguity including categories like unfamiliarity (lack of knowledge),
148
      semantics and possible question types such as who, when, what.
149   In this paper, we focus on underspecification, where the user has not provided enough information
150   for the LM to fulfill the request. This may be because the user is unaware of what information is
151   necessary to complete a task, or is unaware of what information the LM does not know.
152   Importance of Information Gathering For humans, Chouinard et al. (2007) finds that actively
153   gathering information (e.g., by asking questions) is an indispensable skill for solving problems under
154   uncertainty, and may even play a crucial role in cognitive development of children.
155
      For autonomous systems, information gathering abilities are especially crucial since the system can
156   often be uncertain. In the literature of AI/ML, researchers have extensively explored strategies to
157   sequentially acquire data for tasks like concept learning (Sammut & Banerji, 1986; Angluin, 1988),
158   active learning (Cohn et al., 1996; Settles, 2009; Houlsby et al., 2011; Gal et al., 2017; Ren et al.,
159   2021), Bayesian optimization (Kushner, 1964; Moc̆kus, 1974; Auer, 2002; Srinivas et al., 2010;
160

      Hennig & Schuler, 2012; Wang & Jegelka, 2017; Garnett, 2023; Wang et al., 2024b), reinforcement
163   learning (Kaelbling et al., 1996; Ghavamzadeh et al., 2015; Sutton, 2018), and robot planning with
164   partially observable states (Kaelbling et al., 1998; Kaelbling & Lozano-Pérez, 2013; Phiquepal &

      Information gathering is also an important skill for LLMs as they often operate under uncertainty.

      Our work proposes basic reasoning tasks to evaluate how well LLMs master such skills.

      Question-asking Problems and Techniques for LMs Prior works have introduced question-
170
      asking methods for subjective or knowledge-based tasks with ambiguous user requests, such as
171
      “What is a good pasta recipe” (Andukuri et al., 2024) or “Who won the US open?” (Zhang & Choi,
172   2023; Pang et al., 2024). Li et al. (2023); Kuhn et al. (2023a) directly prompt a language model
173   to ask questions. Piriyakulkij et al. (2024) augments this process by sampling questions with the
174   highest information gain, while Andukuri et al. (2024) encourages LMs to produce useful questions
175   through self-training. Grand et al. (2024) parses questions into symbolic programs before computing
176   information gain on top of programs. Handa et al. (2024) computes information gain with respect to
177   preferences represented as Bayesian linear models. Hu et al. (2024) introduces a method based on
178   simulating entire conversation trajectories, computing information gain at each turn, and propagat-
179   ing rewards through a conversation. Zhang & Choi (2023) decomposes question-asking into three
180
      stages: (1) determining when clarification is needed, (2) determining what clarifying question to
      ask, and (3) responding accurately with the new information gathered through clarification. Zhang
181
      et al. (2024b) trains agents to ask for clarification in travel planning settings.
182
183   To our best knowledge, these methods either do not apply to our tasks or require significant modifica-
184   tions (such as simulating users) to be applied to the underspecified reasoning tasks in our benchmark.
185   We are not aware of existing methods that solve underspecified CSPs defined in our work.

190   Example 3.1. Please solve the math problem: Janet had a few eggs (variable x0 ) and ate one
191   (variable x1 ). How many eggs does she have now (target variable y)?
192
193   While it is clear that the arithmetic word problem can be parsed to y = x0 − x1 , x1 = 1, the LM
194   cannot compute target variable y without knowing the value of variable x0 . Other examples can
195   be found in Figure 2. In these cases, the desired behavior is for the LM to ask the minimal set of
196   questions that enables it to respond to the user query.

198   3.1       S EMANTIC A MBIGUITY VS . U NDERSPECIFICATION
199
      In this paper, we focus on problems that are semantically equivalent to constraint satisfaction prob-
200
      lems,2 which allow us to formalize underspecification in a well-defined and rigorous manner.
201
202   Solving a problem thus comprises of two stages:
203        1. A natural language query is parsed into a set of variables and constraints, and a target
204            variable corresponding to the desired response. For example, in Example 3.1, there are
205            variables x0 (initial eggs), x1 (eaten eggs), target variable y (current eggs), and a constraint
206            y = x0 − x1 .
207
           2. The model solves for the target variable in the CSP.
208   The model might not know the answer for the target variable due to two possible reasons.
209        1. Semantic ambiguity: There are multiple semantic interpretations of the problem at the
210           parsing stage, inducing a distribution over possible CSPs.
211        2. Underspecification: For a given CSP, some variable assignments or constraints may be
212           missing that makes it impossible to solve for the target variable.
213   Semantic ambiguity has been treated extensively in prior work (Kuhn et al., 2023b), and may vary
214   based on different human’s interpretations due to reasons including mental states or demographics.
215

220
221   We formalize underspecification and the information gathering objective below. This formalism is
222   critical for constructing tasks to evaluate the question asking skills of models. First, we define a CSP
223   as a tuple ⟨X, D, C, A, y⟩ with:
224             • X = {xi }N i=1 is a set of N variables.
225             • D = {Di }N  i=1 is a set of domains for each variable in X.
226             • C = {cj }M j=1 is a set of M constraints. Each constraint is associated with a list of variables
227               in X and specifies the values that can be assigned to those variables.
228             • A = ∧i∈IA (xi = ai ) is a proposition stating that for each i ∈ IA ⊆ [N ], variable xi is
229               assigned a value ai ∈ Di .
230             • y is a target that the user requests to be solved.
231

      We adapt the blocks world problem from PyperPlan (Alkhazraji et al., 2020), a pick-and-place task

316   conjunction of atoms or their negations4 , e.g., for two blocks a and b, a state can be
317
                                       (ontable a), (holding b), ¬(on a b).
318   At each state, there are a set of actions that can potentially be applied to it to transition to another
319
      state. Each action has a precondition specifying atoms the state must satisfy before applying the

      example of an action:

329   Problem Definition The problem can be expressed as a CSP ⟨X, D, C, A, y⟩ as follows:5
330

331

332

333           • C is the set of constraints. For any sequence of actions [qt ]t∈[n] and their corresponding

341   We construct problems such that the initial state is potentially underspecified – we only know the
342   values of certain atoms of the initial state, and must ask the value of at most a single other atom in
343   order to disambiguate the shortest action sequence.
344   Some problems are fully specified CSPs – i.e., the shortest action sequence can be fully determined
345   regardless of uncertainty about the initial state. For example, even if we do not know whether block
346   b is on top of another block or on the table, that information does not affect what the shortest action
347   sequence will be. In these cases, we expect the LLM to output “No questions needed.”
348
      Some problems are 1-sufficient CSPs – the LLM must ask for the value of an atom, which will be
349   enough to fully determine the shortest action sequence, regardless of the value of the atom.

473   6        C ORRELATION BETWEEN S EARCH C OMPLEXITY AND LLM ACCURACY
474
475   The 1-sufficient CSPs can be solved through brute-force (forward) search or backwards search
476   in general. Are LLMs using a similar mechanism or something completely different? In this section,
477   we conduct preliminary investigation by estimating how much model’s accuracy correlate with the
478   human-understandable problem parameters (E.g., depth of the search).
479   We can approximately quantify the difficulty of each problem in these domains based on the runtime
480   complexity of each algorithm. If there is a correlation between the factors that determine algorithmic
481   complexity, and the performance of the LLM, this serves as a high-level signal for the types of
482   mechanisms LLMs may be using to succeed in these tasks.
483

      7   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We introduce a collection of benchmarks for identifying underspecification in problems, which we
      posit is an essential skill necessary for LLMs’ to perform tasks under uncertainty. We found that
532
      SOTA LLMs are relatively good at identifying missing information in simple algebra problems, but
533
      struggle with more complex tasks involving logic and planning. Their performance tends to decline
534   with the increase of the size of solution spaces and search depths, if these problems were to be solved
535   by a search algorithm. We conjecture that LLMs may possess search skills similar to breadth-first
536   search or brute-force approaches, which become less effective as the search space expands. Future
537   work includes understanding the mechanisms of LLMs to determine what information is missing
538   in a given task, and how to enhance their ability to effectively seek that information. One possible
539   method for higher performance is to use LLMs to extract the symbolic CSP for an underspecified
      task and then run search algorithms to find the right variable to clarify.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542   We foresee limited ethical risks with our benchmark, as it focuses on low-risk domains such as log-
543   ical reasoning, blocks-world planning, and math problems. This work will enable progress towards
544   LLMs that can work with humans to ask clarifying questions. We note when or if these techniques
545   are eventually deployed into the real world, they can alleviate unforeseen risks through confirming
546   with users before executing on a task. Furthermore, LLMs that can ask clarifying questions are likely
547
      more easily customizable and personalizable. However, this may also introduce risks if malicious
      actors can more easily use LLMs for malicious purposes; care must be taken to ensure these risks
548
      do not come to fruition, for example, by preventing LLMs from ever displaying certain behavior.

1278
1279   C.1   L OGIC -Q
1280
1281   Brute-force Solution. A problem in Logic-Q can be solved through brute force search as follows.
1282   We have a subroutine infer that allows us to infer the values of all variables consistent with the
1283   current variable assignments. For example, if A = {a, b} and we know that a ∧ b → c, then
1284
       infer(A) gives us {a, b, c}. At a high-level, infer is analogous to breadth-first-search, and thus
       has runtime complexity O(|X| + |C|).
1285
1286   To solve a Logic-Q problem, we first run infer to get values of all variables consistent with the
1287   current assignment A, creating A′ . Next, for all unassigned variables xu ∈ X\A′ , we iterate through
1288   them one at a time and check whether:
1289         1. Including xu in A′ allows us to infer the target variable is either true or false.
1290         2. Include ¬xu in A′ allows us to infer the target variable is the opposite assignment as it was
1291            in case (1).
1292
       The first time 1 and 2 both hold, we have that xu is true.
1293
1294   The runtime of brute-force can thus be computed by the expected number of variables that we
1295   need to iterate through to get to a variable in the sufficient set (denote this EBF ), multiplied by
       the complexity of running the infer algorithm twice. By Markov’s inequality, with probability

       ≥ 1 − δ, the complexity is bounded by
1297
1298                                            O((|X| + |C|)EBF /δ)

1300   Backwards Search Solution. A problem in Logic-Q can be solved through backwards search.
1301
       The procedure is the same as the backwards search used to construct the dataset, described in Ap-
       pendix A.1.1. At each iteration, we keep track of a disjunction of conjunction of variables required
1302
       to prove y.
1303
1304   The complexity of backwards search is given by the search depth d multiplied by the number of
1305   expansions per depth, which is bounded by O(3|X| |C|) (expanding up to |C| rules for conjunction,
1306   for which there are at most 3|X| conjunctions – each variable can be either true/false/missing from
1307   that conjunction) Thus, the overall complexity is O(3|X| |C|d).
1308   Though comprehensive backwards search is EXP-time, we can terminate early as soon as we find
1309   a disjunction consisting of the negation of all initial conditions, the goal variable, and an additional
1310   term, which would mean that asking about the value of the initial term is sufficient to infer the value
1311   of the goal variable.
1312
       If we know this disjunction is at most at depth d, then the runtime is bounded by |X||C|d , where
1313
1314
       |X||C| is the branching factor at each node. The branching factor comes from the cross product of
       at most |X| terms across at most |C| conjunctive constraints. Thus, the total runtime is given by
1315
1316                                                  O(|X||C|d )

1319
1320   Brute-force Solution. Given an underspecified initial state s˜0 , we can generate all physically-
1321   plausible initial states s0 ∈ F (s˜0 ). We can then search all initial states in this set, and partition
1322   the consistent initial states based on their optimal plan. This is analogous to the procedure used to
1323
       construct 1-sufficient partial states for this dataset, described in Appendix A.2.2.
1324         1. Using breadth-first search, we find the shortest path τ̂ from the initial state s0 to the goal
1325            condition.
1326         2. We add s0 to the partition corresponding to τ̂ .
1327   The dataset was constructed so there is at most 2 partitions. At the end of this process, we examine
1328   each partition and extract the attributes, common to the initial states of a partition, that do not appear
1329   in the initial states of the other partition. Knowing the value of these variable allow us to distinguish
1330   which partition we are in, and accordingly which optimal plan takes us to the goal.
1331
       Breadth-first search takes at most O(|S| + |S||A|) time, and we perform Breadth-first search at most
1332
       |S| times, meaning the overall runtime of this solution is O((|S| + |S||A|)|S|). The number of states
1333
       is bounded by |S| = 2|X| , while the number of actions is bounded by |A| = 2b + 2b(b + 1) = O(b2 )
1334   where b is the number of blocks in the domain, so the overall complexity is:
1335
1336                                                  O(22|X| b2 )
1337
1338   Backward Search Solution. We replace the breadth-first-search from each consistent state with
1339
       a single backwards search. Starting from the goal condition, we iterate backwards to find sets of
       initial states that utilize the same path to the goal. This is the same backwards search that was used
1340
       to construct all fully-specified partial states, described in Appendix A.2.1. We expand backwards
1341
       until we arrive at a set of partial initial states that are consistent with the given partially-observed set
1342   s˜0 , and we find the attribute that distinguishes each partial initial state from each other.
1343
1344   Because the number of partial states is bounded by 3|X| (each proposition can take on 3 values:
1345   true/false/unknown), the backwards breadth-first search takes O(3|X| + 3|X| |A|). However, if we
1346   know that the longest path from any initial state consistent with s˜0 to the goal is of length d, meaning
1347
       we only need to search up to depth d, then the runtime is bounded by
1348                                       O(|A|d ) = O(b2d ) or O(3|X|d )
1349
       (Each iteration, expand at most 3|X| states or |A| actions.)
