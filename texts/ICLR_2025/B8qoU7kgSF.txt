011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Neural ordinary differential equations (neural ODEs) represent a widely-used
014            class of deep learning models characterized by continuous depth. Understand-
015            ing the generalization error bound is important to evaluate how well a model is
016
               expected to perform on new, unseen data. Earlier works in this direction involved
               considering the linear case on the dynamics function (a function that models the
017
               evolution of state variables) of Neural ODE Marion (2024). Other related work
018
               is on bound for Neural Controlled ODE Bleistein & Guilloux (2023) that de-
019            pends on the sampling gap. We consider a class of neural ordinary differential
020            equations (ODEs) with a general nonlinear function for time-dependent and time-
021            independent cases which is Lipschitz with respect to state variables. We observed
022            that the solution of the neural ODEs would be of bound variations if we assume
023            that the dynamics function of Neural ODEs is Lipschitz continuous with respect
024            to the hidden state. We derive a generalization bound for the time-dependent and
025            time-independent Neural ODEs. Using the fact that Neural ODEs are limiting
026            cases of time-dependent Neural ODEs we obtained a bound for the residual neu-
027
               ral networks. We showed the effect of overparameterization and domain bound in
               the generalization error bound. This is the first time, the generalization bound for
028
               the Neural ODE with a more general non-linear function has been found.

031   1   INTRODUCTION
032
033   Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (Chen et al. (2018)) are a class of deep learning models
034   where the transformation between layers is treated as a continuous process defined by an ordinary
035   differential equation (ODE). This idea generalizes the concept of residual networks (ResNets), where
036   the evolution of the hidden state z(t) over time is modeled by a differential equation
037
                                  dz(t)
038                                     = f (z(t), t, θ(t))   with z(0) = x,                          (1.1)
039
                                   dt
040   where θ(t) represents the parameters of the model.
041
      Unlike discrete representations from the conventional methods, neural ordinary differential equa-
042   tions (Neural ODEs) directly learn continuous latent representations (or latent states) based on a
043   vector field parameterized by a neural network. Kidger et al. (2020) introduced neural controlled
044   differential equations (Neural CDEs), which are continuous-time analogs of ResNets that use con-
045   trolled paths to represent irregular time series. Neural ODEs are also extended to neural stochastic
046   differential equations (Neural SDEs) with a focus on aspects such as gradient computation, varia-
047   tional inference for latent spaces, and uncertainty quantification. In neural stochastic ODEs (neural
048   SDEs, Oh et al. (2024)), usually a diffusion term is incorporated but a careful design of drift and
049   diffusion term is essential.
050   With neural ODEs, generally, it is difficult to handle irregular time-series data. Neural controlled
051   differential equations (Kidger et al. (2020)) generalize neural ODEs by incorporating a control
052   mechanism, allowing them to model the evolution of hidden states as controlled differential
053   equations. Studying the statistical properties of neural ODEs is not a trivial task. Since standard
      measures of statistical complexity in neural networks, such as those discussed by Bartlett et al.

      (2019), typically increase with depth, it is unclear why models with effectively infinite depth, like
055   neural ODEs, would demonstrate strong generalization capabilities.
056
057   Marion (2024) studied the statistical properties of a class of time-dependent neural ODEs
058   described by the following equation:
059
                                                 dHt
060                                                  = Wt σ(Ht ),
061
                                                  dt
062   where Wt ∈ Rd×d is a weight matrix that depends on the time index t, and σ : R → R is an
063   activation function applied component-wise. The model considered by Marion (2024) does not
064   include the case where there are weights inside the non-linearity since they assume the dynamics at
065   time t to be linear with respect to the parameters.
066   Contribution. For a general class of well-posed neural ODEs, where the neural network involves
067   the non-linear weights within the function, there is no result related to the generalization bound. In
068   this work, we consider a Neural ODE model parameterized by θ(t) of the following form:
069
070
                                    dz(t)
                                          = f (z(t), t, θ(t))   with z(0) = x,                             (1.2)
071                                  dt
072   where f : Rd × Rd → Rd and z(t) : [0, L] → Rd . We provide a generalization bound for the large
073   class of parameterized ODEs instead of a linear class, the bound we provided here will hold for a
074   linear class as well and is stricter than the earlier bounds for the linear class of functions. To the best
075   of our knowledge, this is the first available bound for neural ODEs for this class of functions.
076   Organization. Section 1 is devoted to the introduction, and in Section 2, we discuss the realted
077   works. In section 3, we discuss some of the preliminaries and definitions that are crucial for un-

081   concluding remarks are given in section 8.

083   2    RELATED WORKS
084
085   Hybridizing deep learning and differential equations. The fusion of deep learning with differen-
086   tial equations has recently garnered renewed interest, although the concept has been explored since
087   the 1990s Rico-Martinez et al. (1992; 1994). A notable advancement was presented by Chen et al.
088   (2018), where they introduced a model that learns a representation u ∈ Rn by setting the initial
089   condition z(0) = ϕθ(t) (u) for the following ordinary differential equation (ODE):
090
091
                                              dz(t)
                                                    = f (z(t), t, θ(t))
092                                            dt
093   where both f and ϕθ(t) are neural networks. The solution at the final time t1 , denoted z(t1 ), is then
094   utilized as input to a conventional machine learning model. This approach seamlessly integrates
095   neural networks and ODEs, offering a robust framework for learning complex dynamical systems.
096   Since then, several works have built on this idea, including theoretical advancements and practical
097   applications as seen in Dupont et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2019; 2020); Finlay et al. (2020). For
098
      a more comprehensive overview, readers may refer to the reviews by Massaroli et al. (2020) and
      Kidger (2022), which delve into the intersection of differential equations and deep learning.
099
100   Generalization of Neural Controlled Differential Equations. Bleistein & Guilloux (2023) used a
101   Lipschitz-based argument to obtain a sampling-dependant generalization bound for neural controlled
102   differential equations (NCDEs). The NCDE considered was of the following form:
103
                                               dzt   = Gψ (zt )dx̃t ,
104
105   where zt ∈ Rp , and Gψ : Rp → Rp×d is neural network parametrized by ψ, also x̃t ∈ Rp is
106   continuous path. In this work, it is assumed that (xt ) is Lipschitz which implies that x = (xt )t∈[0,1]
107   is of bounded variation. They also analyzed how approximation and generalization are affected by
      irregular sampling.

      Generalization bounds for neural networks. Bartlett et al. (2017a) derived a margin-based mul-
109   ticlass generalization bound for neural networks that scales with margin-normalized spectral com-
110   plexity, involving the Lipschitz constant (the product of the spectral norms of the weight matrices)
111   and a correction factor. Long & Sedghi (2019) established generalization error bounds for convo-
112   lutional networks based on training loss, parameter count, the Lipschitz constant of the loss, and
113   the distance between current and initial weights, independent of input size and hidden layer dimen-
114   sions. Experiments on CIFAR-10 show these bounds align with observed generalization gaps under
115   varying hyperparameters in deep convolutional networks. Wang & Ma (2022) derive generalization
116   error bounds for deep neural networks trained via SGD by combining control of parameter norms
117
      with Rademacher complexity estimates. These bounds, which apply to various architectures like
      MLPs and CNNs, depend on the training loss and do not require L-smoothness, making them more
118
      broadly applicable than stability-based bounds.

      6    A PPLICATION TO R ESIDUAL N EURAL N ETWORKS
331
      Residual Neural Networks (ResNets) operate on discrete sequences of inputs and produce hidden
332
      states at each time step. The hidden state ht at time step t is updated using the previous hidden state
333
      ht−1 and the current input xt . The update rule for a basic ResNet can be written as:
334
335                                        ht = ht−1 + ∆t · f (ht−1 , θt )
336   where ht is the hidden state at time step t, f (ht−1 , θt ) is the update function (a nonlinear transfor-
337   mation parameterized by weights θt ), and ∆t is the time step size (assumed to be 1). This discrete
338   update rule resembles the Euler’s method for solving ordinary differential equations (ODEs). If we
339   interpret ht as a function of continuous time t, we can write:
340
                                              dh(t)
341                                                 = f (h(t), t, θ(t)).
342
                                               dt
343   This equation describes how the hidden state h(t) evolves continuously over time. The right-hand
344   side has the same update function as in the discrete ResNet, but in this case, it governs continuous
345   evolution.
346   To connect this with Neural ODEs, we take the continuous-time limit of the discrete ResNet. As
347   ∆t → 0, the discrete ResNet update equation becomes a continuous-time differential equation:
348
                                            ht+∆t − ht
349                                     lim            = f (h(t), t, θ(t)).
350
                                       ∆t→0    ∆t
351   This is exactly the form of a Neural ODE:
352
                                              dh(t)
353                                                 = f (h(t), t, θ(t)).
                                               dt
354
355   In this case, h(t) is the continuously evolving hidden state, and f (h(t), t, θ(t)) is the neural network
356
      defining the dynamics. The solution to this differential equation gives the evolution of the hidden
      state over continuous time, instead of discrete steps as in ResNets. Hence, Neural ODEs can be
357
      seen as the limiting case of ResNets when the time step between updates goes to zero, allowing the
358
      hidden state to evolve continuously.

479
480   We obtain the first generalization bounds for time-independent and time-dependent neural ODEs.
481   We proved the generalization bound for the time-dependent neural ODEs of the form dHt =
482   f (Ht , θ(t))dt. Using this result we also obtain the generalization bound for the time-independent
483   neural ODEs. By extending the reasoning for the time-dependent neural ODEs to the discrete case
484   we obtain generalization bound for residual neural networks. We also showed how it depends on
485   the width and depth of the network by considering the term which depends on the depth and width
      of the network as it contains the norm which depends on the width of the network. We also showed


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      how the generalization gap is dependent on the Lipschitz constant of weights for the case of time-
487   dependent Neural ODEs. Since stochastic Neural ODEs have been found to deep limits of a large
488   class of residual neural networks, it will be interesting to extend our result to the more involved case
489   of Neural SDEs.
