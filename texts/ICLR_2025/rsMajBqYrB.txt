009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Missing value is a critical issue in data science, significantly impacting the reliabil-
012                ity of analyses and predictions. Missing value imputation (MVI) is a longstanding
013                problem because it highly relies on domain knowledge. Large language models
014                (LLMs) have emerged as a promising tool for data cleaning, including MVI for
015                tabular data, offering advanced capabilities for understanding and generating con-
016                tent. However, despite their promise, existing LLM techniques such as in-context
017
                   learning and Chain-of-Thought (CoT) often fall short in guiding LLMs to per-
                   form complex reasoning for MVI, particularly when imputing derived missing
018
                   values, which require mathematical formulas by considering data values across
019
                   rows and columns. This gap underscores the need for further advancements in
020                LLM methodologies to enhance their reasoning capabilities for derived missing
021                values. To fill this gap, we propose SketchFill, a novel sketch-based method to
022                guide LLMs in generating accurate formulas to impute missing numerical val-
023                ues. SketchFill first utilizes a general user-provided Meta-Sketch to generate
024                a Domain-Sketch tailored to the context of the input dirty table. Subsequently,
025                it fills this Domain-Sketch with formulas and outputs Python code, effectively
026                bridging the gap between high-level abstractions and executable solutions. Addi-
027                tionally, SketchFill incorporates a Reflector component to verify the generated
028
                   code. This Reflector assesses the accuracy and appropriateness of the outputs
                   and iteratively refines the Domain-Sketch, ensuring that the imputation aligns
029
                   closely with the underlying data patterns and relationships. Our experimental re-
030
                   sults demonstrate that SketchFill significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods,
031                achieving 56.2% higher accuracy than CoT-based methods and 78.8% higher
032                accuracy than MetaGPT. This sets a new standard for automated data cleaning and
033                advances the field of MVI for numerical values.

036   1       I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Missing value imputation (MVI) represents a longstanding data quality challenge, critically impacting
039   the reliability and effectiveness of data-driven industries, such as healthcare (Shetty et al., 2024;
040   Psychogyios et al., 2023), IoT research (Adhikari et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023c), and spatial time-series
041   analysis (Wu et al., 2015; Gong et al., 2023; Tashiro et al., 2021). A notable aspect of this challenge
042
      is the substantial amount of time and resources it demands (Rezig et al., 2019; Rashid & Gupta,
      2020). The State of Data Science 2020 Survey, made by Anaconda1 , revealed that on average 45%
043
      of time is spent getting data ready (19% and 26% for loading and cleaning respectively) before the
044
      data scientists can use it to develop models and visualizations. This not only consumes an excessive
045   amount of human resources but also significantly slows down the analytical processes in data-centric
046   corporations.
047
048
      Given its critical importance, MVI has been extensively explored within the academic and professional
      communities. The literature is rich with a variety of approaches, ranging from traditional statistical
049
      methods to more contemporary machine learning and deep learning-based techniques. Despite the
050
      advancements, the task of MVI continues to pose significant challenges, largely due to the need for
051   substantial domain-specific expertise to accurately handle missing data. In this work, we address a
052   specific subset of this problem, termed Derived Missing Value Imputation (DMVI), which can

154
      Missing Value Imputation (MVI) has been explored with the advent of data science, aiming to look
155   at the patterns and mechanisms that create the missing data, as well as a taxonomy of missing data
156   in datasets (Little & Rubin, 2019). Despite a single imputation method like Hot-Deck (Andridge &
157   Little, 2010; Christopher et al., 2019), which is imputed from a randomly selected similar record, we
158   categorize existing solutions in the context of statistics and machine intelligence for missing value
159   imputation as follows.
160
161   Statistic-based Methods. Initially, the most common strategy for MVI is using a descriptive statistic,
      e.g., mean, median, or most frequent, along each column, or using a constant value. It is widely


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      adopted in existing packages and tools, such as sklearn.SimpleImputer (Pedregosa et al., 2011)
163   and Excel FlashFill (Gulwani, 2011). Besides, curated packages such as MICE (Van Buuren &
164   Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011) can impute incomplete multivariate data by chained equations.
165
166   Machine Learning-based Methods. Tao et al. (2004) improve algorithms in Reverse kNN, allowing
167   to retrieve an arbitrary number of neighbors in multiple dimensions. Sridevi et al. (2011) propose
168   ARLSimpute, an autoregressive model to predict missing values. Stekhoven & Bühlmann (2012)
169   propose an iterative imputation method MissForest that can impute the missing value of mixed-type
170   data. Tsai et al. (2018) propose CCMVI, which calculates the distances between observed data and
171   the class centers to define the threshold for later imputation. Razavi-Far et al. (2020) propose kEMI
172
      and kEMI` for imputing categorical and numerical missing data correspondingly. They both first
      utilize the k-nearest neighbors (KNN) algorithm to search the K-top similar records to a record with
173
      missing values, then invoke the Expectation-Maximization Imputation (EMI) algorithm, which uses
174
      feature correlation among the K-top similar records to impute missing values.
175
176   Deep Learning-based Methods. Gondara & Wang (2018) propose a multiple imputation model
177   based on overcomplete deep denoising autoencoders, which is capable of handling different missing
178   situations in terms of the data types, patterns, proportions, and distributions. With the advent of
179   the diffusion model, the CSDI (Tashiro et al., 2021) acts as a time series imputation method that
180   utilizes score-based diffusion models to exploit correlations on observed values. Later on, Zheng &
181   Charoenphakdee (2022) explore the use of conditional score-based diffusion models for tabular data
182   (TabCSDI) to impute missing values in tabular datasets. Their study evaluates three techniques for
183
      effectively handling categorical variables and numerical variables simultaneously.
184
      LLM-based Methods. With the advance of LLM, especially superb generative models like GPT,
185
      some techniques can be applied to the MVI task. Since LLMs are trained on extensive and diverse
186   corpora, they inherently possess knowledge of a wide array of common entities (Razniewski et al.,
187   2021; Narayan et al., 2022), intuitively, we can directly ask LLM to perform MVI given some
188   dirty data. Besides, CoT prompting (Wei et al., 2023) can significantly improve the ability of
189   complex reasoning. Alternatively, Poldrack et al. (2023) explore the code generation ability utilizing
190   LLM so that a specific script for MVI can be generated. Additionally, LLM-powered agentic tool,
191   MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2023; 2024) reveals its capability on data cleaning tasks. In short, we will
192   seize the above methods and discuss their performance in the later section.

      LLMs have demonstrated their ability to capture the two-dimensional structure of tabular data through
296   techniques such as role-prompting and fine-tuning (Sui et al., 2024; Zha et al., 2023). However, DMVI
297   requires a series of operations, such as locating missing values, building solutions and calculations.
298   Even with step-by-step prompting of CoT reasoning, LLMs still struggle to fully comprehend the
299   formulaic relationship and conduct calculations. To address this challenge, SketchFill includes the
300   Domain-Sketch Generator to construct the imputation solution as one of main contributions that
301   improve DMVI performance (See Phase ①, Figure 3).
302   The Domain-Sketch Generator is tasked with producing the Domain-Sketch, adhering to Meta-Sketch
303   guidance to exploit the LLMs’ embedding knowledge about the specific dataset at hand. The Meta-
304   Sketch, a pseudo-code-like file, contains a series of logical steps and descriptions, encapsulating the
305   formula-based strategy for DMVI. Then, LLMs could generate specific step-by-step Domain-Sketch
306   to guide other agents to output Python code for DMVI. Consider the circumstance that domain experts
307   of dirty data when imputing the missing value. It is common for them to decompose DMVI into the
308   following steps, w.r.t the Meta-Sketch template in Phase ①, Figure 3:
309
                 • S1-S2: locating the missing value and recalling the associated knowledge about the variable
310
                   of missing value, by searching for variables in this dirty table that are related to the missing
311                value,
312
                 • S3-S6: drafting and verifying the formula to calculate the missing value by applying it to
313
                   rows without missing values, and,
314
315

316   In our experiments, we sufficiently illustrate that LLM-powered (See Appendix B.1) Domain-Sketch
317   Generator can carry out a satisfying Domain-Sketch for the specific dataset based on the instruction
318   from Meta-Sketch. To exemplify, given a masked clean subset (Cm ), the Domain-Sketch Generator
319   receives this Cm as input and creates the Domain-Sketch (S) based on the Meta-Sketch.

348
      The motivation for implementing the Summarizer stems from the necessity to conduct the human
349
      review for each imputation of subsets when applying LLMs for DMVI, owing to the token limitation
350
      that LLM can process at a time, particularly when utilizing CoT-based LLM. Meanwhile, LLMs
351   have exhibited better factual consistency and fewer instances of extrinsic hallucinations in generating
352   summary, compared to humans (Zhang et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2023; Pu et al., 2023), as well as
353   promising code understanding (Nam et al., 2023; Richards & Wessel, 2024).
354
      The Summarizer is initialized as an LLM (see Appendix B.4) as part of SketchFill. Given the similar
355
      pattern of P in the DMVI solution within the same column, the Summarizer has the potential to
356
      develop a generalized solution accordingly such that the Summarizer distil the P into a more readable
357   and generalized Python function. By doing so, the Summarizer drastically minimizes the need for
358   human review. As depicted in Phase ③, Figure 3, the Summarizer takes the validated P and abstracts
359   the specifics of the dataset into parameters, creating a flexible function capable of addressing missing
360   values across various subsets derived from the dirty table. Consequently, the Summarizer outputs a
361   finalized Python function (F ). F is then utilized in the Execution module, enabling automatic DMVI
362   across any subset of the original dirty table without additional manual procedures. The Summarizer
363   thus streamlines the imputation process, ensuring consistency and scalability while significantly
364   reducing the manual workload.

370   be automatically imputed. For instance, for the variable (V ) in the tabular data case, the Executor is
371   programmed to apply the DMVI processing with F to impute subsequent missing value in V .

407   appropriate missing rate, similar to settings in other works (Zheng & Charoenphakdee, 2022; Tashiro
408   et al., 2021; Van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011). Consequently, multiple missing values
409   related to the same variable may take place in some testing tuples. Full statistics are shown in Table 1.
410
411   Evaluation Metrics. We assessed the imputation performance using three commonly adopted metrics:
412   (1) Accuracy: This metric evaluates the overall accuracy by comparing the imputed values to the
413   ground truth values. (2) FindAccuracy: This metric measures the accuracy of values that have been
414
      correctly imputed at least once, ignoring variables where all imputations failed. It is particularly
      helpful to evaluate datasets with noisy data, where a formula may not robustly hold for all rows but
415
      correct for the majority. It describes the accuracy of variable detection under the circumstance that
416
      the formula is constructed, even though not entirely correct. (3) RMSE (Root Mean Square Error):
417   This metric calculates the difference between the imputed value and ground truth value. A lower
418   RMSE indicates better imputation accuracy, with a zero value indicating identical imputation.

506   4.3   D ISCUSSION ON OTHER APPROACHES
507
508   The imputation accuracy of KNN approaches is limited across all datasets due to its weakness in
509
      in-context learning on tabular data. MICE achieves good performance in variables that are the linear
      combination of other variables because this approach conducts MVI based on the regression model.
510
      TabCSDI is a deep learning-based approach that leverages diffusion models for DMVI. However, its
511
      generative approach struggles to learn the formulaic relationship of numeric variables in the dataset.
512   MetaGPT has developed a series of data-cleaning strategies (see Appendix D) for handling dirty data.
513   However, these pre-defined strategies lack context awareness, leading to their struggles with DMVI
514   tasks across all datasets.

      5     C ONCLUSION
518
      This paper evaluates the performance of DMVI across several approaches using five datasets from
519
      various domains. The findings highlight the exceptional performance of our proposed approach,
520   SketchFill, particularly in the context of single-step formulas. Notably, SketchFill achieves 74%
521   Accuracy and 100% FindAccuracy on 3 datasets, with fabricated data inside the datasets. Furthermore,
522   SketchFill demonstrates an overall accuracy of 84.2% across these datasets, demonstrating its
523   robustness. And the Accuracy of SketchFill is 56.2% higher than CoT-based approaches, 59% higher
524   than Code Generation approaches and 78.8% higher than MetaGPT. This establishes a new standard
525   for automated data cleaning and points a new direction for missing value imputation. However,
526   SketchFill is not incompatible with rendering non-derived features such as observation, transaction,
527   or trading data (refer to the close price of the Bajaj Finance data from Figure 1). Additionally,
528
      SketchFill encounters difficulties in handling multi-step calculations and noisy data when processing
      Bajaj and LOLChampion datasets respectively. Furthermore, we observe that the performance of
529
      SketchFill relies on the inference capabilities of the backend LLM. As discussed in Appendix C,
530
      the Llama-8B model conducts limited capabilities on complex DMVI tasks. These issues present
531   opportunities for future research on DMVI tasks using LLMs.
