009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Discrete-state denoising diffusion models led to state-of-the-art performance in graph
012            generation, especially in the molecular domain. Recently, they have been transposed
013
               to continuous time, allowing more flexibility in the reverse process and a better
               trade-off between sampling efficiency and quality, though they have not yet been
014
               applied to graphs. Here, to leverage the benefits of both approaches, we propose
015
               C OMETH, a continuous-time discrete-state graph diffusion model, tailored to the
016            specificities of graph data. In addition, we also successfully replaced the set of
017            structural features previously used in discrete graph diffusion models with a single
018            random-walk-based encoding, providing a simple and principled way to boost the
019            model’s expressive power. Empirically, we show that integrating continuous time leads
020            to significant improvements across various metrics over state-of-the-art discrete-state
021            diffusion models on a large set of molecular and non-molecular benchmark datasets.
022            In terms of valid, unique, and novel (VUN) samples, C OMETH obtains a near-perfect
023            performance of 99.5% on the planar graph dataset and outperforms D I G RESS by
024
               12.6% on the large GuacaMol dataset.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Denoising diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) are among the most prominent and
029   successful classes of generative models. Intuitively, these models aim to denoise diffusion trajectories
030   and produce new samples by sampling noise and recursively denoising it, often outperforming competing
031   architectures in tasks such as image and video generation (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2023).
032   Recently, a large set of works, e.g., Chen et al. (2023); Jo et al. (2022; 2024); Vignac et al. (2022), aimed
033
      to leverage diffusion models for graph generation, e.g., the generation of molecular structures. One class
      of such models embeds the graphs into a continuous space and adds Gaussian noise to the node features
034
      and graph adjacency matrix (Jo et al., 2022). However, such noise destroys the graph’s sparsity, resulting
035
      in complete, noisy graphs without meaningful structural information, making it difficult for the denoising
036   network to capture the structural properties of the data. Therefore, discrete-state graph diffusion models
037   such as D I G RESS (Vignac et al., 2022) have been proposed, exhibiting competitive performance against
038   their continuous-state counterparts. These models utilize a categorical corruption process (Austin et al.,
039   2021), making them more suited to the discrete structure of graph data.
040
      In parallel, the above Gaussian-noise-based diffusion models have been extended to continuous time (Song
041
      et al., 2020), i.e., relying on a continuous-time stochastic process (Capasso and Bakstein, 2021), by
042   formulating the forward process as a stochastic differential equation. In addition, discrete-state diffusion
043   models have recently been transposed to continuous time (Campbell et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2022),
044   relying on continuous-time Markov chains (CTMC). Unlike their discrete-time counterparts, which
045   define a fixed time scale during training, they allow training using a continuous-time scale and leave
046   the choice of the time discretization strategy for the sampling stage. Hence, incorporating continuous
047   time enables a more optimal balance between sampling efficiency and quality while providing greater
048   flexibility in designing the reverse process, as various CTMC simulation tools can be utilized Campbell
049   et al. (2022); Sun et al. (2022). However, extending continuous-time discrete-state diffusion models to
050
      graphs is not straightforward. Unlike other discrete data, such as text, where all tokens share the same
      support, nodes and edges in graphs have distinct attributes and must be handled separately. Furthermore,
051
      the noise models used for other data modalities may be suboptimal for graphs (Vignac et al., 2022).
052
053   Present work Hence, to leverage the benefits of both approaches for graph generation, i.e., discrete-state
      and continuous-time, we propose C OMETH, a continuous-time discrete-state graph diffusion model,

      and the flexibility of a continuous-time scale in the design of the sampling algorithm.
075   Related work Diffusion models are a prominent class of generative models successfully applied to many
076   data modalities, such as images, videos, or point clouds (Yang et al., 2023).
077
      Graph generation is a well-studied task applied to various application domains, such as molecule
078   generation, floorplan generation, or abstract syntax tree generation (Shabani et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2019).
079   We can roughly categorize graph generation approaches into auto-regressive models such as Kong et al.
080   (2023); You et al. (2018); Zhao et al. (2024); Jang et al. (2024b) and one-shot models such as diffusion
081   models. The main advantage of one-shot models over auto-regressive ones is that they generate the
082   whole graph in a single step and do not require any complex procedure to select a node ordering. On the
083   other hand, auto-regressive models are more flexible regarding the size of the generated graph, which can
084   remain unknown beforehand, and they do not suffer from the quadratic complexity of one-shot models.
085   While the first diffusion models for graph generation leveraged continuous-state spaces (Niu et al., 2020),
086   they are now largely replaced by discrete-state diffusion models (Haefeli et al., 2022; Vignac et al.,
087   2022), using a discrete-time scale. However, using discrete-time constrains the sampling scheme to
088   a particular form called ancestral sampling, which prevents the exploration of alternative sampling
089   strategies that could optimize sampling time or enhance sampling quality.
090   Another line of research considers lifting the graphs into a continuous-state space and applying Gaussian
091   noise to the node and edge features matrices (Niu et al., 2020; Jo et al., 2022; 2024). Such continuous
092   noise allows the generation of continuous features to be handled smoothly, such as the generation of
093   atomic coordinates in molecular graphs (Jo et al., 2024). The above Gaussian-noise-based diffusion
094   models have many successful applications in computational biology (Corso et al. (2023); Yim et al.
095   (2023)). However, they almost exclusively consider point-cloud generation, focusing on modeling the
096   geometry of the molecules and ignoring structural information. In addition, some hybrid approaches also
097   exist that consider jointly modeling the 2D molecular graphs and their 3D geometry (Hua et al., 2024;
098
      Le et al., 2023; Vignac et al., 2023). These models usually rely on continuous noise for the atomic
      coordinates and discrete noise for the atom and edge types.
099
100   Recent works have tried to scale graph generative models in size (Bergmeister et al., 2023; Luo et al.,
101   2023; Qin et al., 2023). Such frameworks are often built on top of previously proposed approaches, e.g.,
102   S PARSE D IFF (Qin et al., 2023) is based on D I G RESS. Therefore, these scaling methods are likely to
103   apply to our approach.
104   Concurrently with our work, Xu et al. (2024) proposed a continuous-time discrete-state graph diffusion
105   model. While they experiment using an MPNN as the backbone with limited success, we effectively
106   utilize RRWP as a positional encoding. Moreover, our experimental evaluation of C OMETH is
107   more comprehensive, as we successfully implement the predictor-corrector mechanism and include a
      conditional generation experiment.

126   2    BACKGROUND
127
128   In the following, we overview the continuous-time discrete-state diffusion framework on which C OMETH
129   builds. We provide a complete description of this framework in Appendix A.1, providing intuitions and
130   technical details, and refer to Yang et al. (2023) for a general introduction to diffusion models.
131
      Continuous-time discrete diffusion In the discrete diffusion setting, we aim at modeling a discrete
132
      data distribution pdata (z (0) ), where z (0) ∈ Z and Z is a finite set with cardinality S := |Z|. A
133
      continuous-time diffusion model (Campbell et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2022; Lou et al., 2023) is a stochastic
134
      process, running from time t = 0 to t = 1. In the following, we denote the marginal distributions of the
135
      state z (t) ∈ Z at time t by qt (z (t) ), and qt|s (z (t) | z (s) ) denotes the conditional distribution of the state
136
      z (t) given the state z (s) ∈ Z at some time s ∈ [0, 1]. We also denote δz̃,z the Kronecker delta, which
137
      is equal to 1 if z̃ = z and 0 otherwise. We define a forward process which gradually transforms the
138
      marginal distribution q0 (z (0) ) = pdata (z (0) ) into q1 (z (1) ), that is “close” to an easy-to-sample prior
139
      distribution pref (z (1) ), e.g., a uniform categorical distribution.
140
141   We define the forward process as a continuous-time Markov chain (CTMC). The current state z (t)
142   alternates between resting in the current state and transitioning to another state, where a transition rate
143   matrix R(t) ∈ RS×S controls the dynamics of the CTMC. Formally, the forward process is defined
144   through the infinitesimal transition probability from z (t) to another state z̃ ∈ Z, for a infinitesimal time
145   step dt between time t and t + dt,
146

      3.2   N OISE MODEL : EXTENDING MARGINAL TRANSITIONS TO CONTINUOUS TIME
200
201   Noise model Several noise models have been proposed for discrete diffusion models, including uniform
202   transitions, absorbing transitions (Austin et al., 2021), and marginal transitions (Vignac et al., 2022). We
203   propose to use a rate matrix that is analogous to the marginal transition matrix, as it is well adapted for
204   graph data. However, marginal transitions have not yet been utilized in the continuous-time framework.
205   To address this, we extend this concept by constructing the following rate matrices, i.e.,
206
                                   RX = 1m′X − I            and     RE = 1m′E − I,
207
208   where mX and mE are vectors representing the marginal distributions mX and mE of node and edge
209   labels, i.e., they contain the frequency of the different labels in the dataset. With such a rate, the transition
210   rate to a particular state depends on its marginal probability. Specifically, the more common a node or
211
      edge label is in the dataset, the higher the transition rate to that label. Consequently, this approach helps
      preserve sparsity in noisy graphs by favoring transitions to the “no edge” label. Embedding this inductive
212
      bias in the noise model simplifies the model’s task, as it no longer needs to reconstruct this sparsity
213
      during the generative process.
214

      3.4   S IMPLE AND P OWERFUL P OSITIONAL E NCODING WITH RRWP
297
298   In all our experiments, we use the graph transformer proposed by Vignac et al. (2022); see Figure 4 in the
299   appendix. Relying on the fact that discrete diffusion models preserve the sparsity of noisy graphs, they
300   propose a large set of features to compute at each denoising step to boost the expressivity of the model.
301   This set includes Laplacian features, connected components features, and node- and graph-level cycle
302   counts. Even though this set of features has been successfully used in follow works, e.g., Vignac et al.
303   (2023), Qin et al. (2023), Igashov et al. (2023), no theoretical nor experimental study exists to investigate
304   the relevance of those particular features. In addition, a rich literature on encodings in graph learning
305   has been developed, e.g., LapPE (Kreuzer et al., 2021), SignNet (Lim et al., 2023), RWSE (Dwivedi
306
      et al., 2021), SPE (Huang et al., 2023), which led us to believe that powerful encodings developed for
      discriminative models should be transferred to the generative setting.
307
308   Specifically, in our experiments, we leverage the relative random-walk probabilites (RRWP) encoding,
309   introduced in Ma et al. (2023). Denoting A the adjacency matrix of a graph G, D the diagonal
310   degree matrix, and M = D −1 A the degree-normalized adjacency matrix, for each pair of nodes
311   (i, j) ∈ V (G)2 , the RRWP encoding computes

328   cannot detect if a node is contained in a large cycle of a given graph. We say the RRWP encoding counts
329   the number of p-cycles for p ≥ 2 if there do not exist two graphs, one containing at least one p-cycle
330   while the other does not, while the RRWP encodings of the two graphs are equivalent.
331   Proposition 3. For p ≥ 8, the RRWP encoding does not count the number of p-cycles.
332
333   Hence, for p ≥ 8 and K ≥ 0, there exists a graph and two vertex pairs (r, s), (v, w) ∈ V (G)2 such that
                                                        K      K
334   (r, s) is contained in C while (v, w) is not and Pvw = Prs .
335   Equivariance properties Since graphs are invariant to node reordering, it is essential to design methods
336   that capture this fundamental property of the data. Relying on the similarities between C OMETH and
337   D I G RESS, we establish that C OMETH is permutation-equivariant and that our loss is permutation-
338   invariant. We also establish that the τ -leaping sampling scheme and the predictor-corrector yield
339   exchangeable distributions, i.e., the model assigns each graph permutation the same probability. Since
340   those results mainly stem from proofs outlined in Vignac et al. (2022), we moved them to Appendix A.4.

      5    C ONCLUSION
487
488
      Here, to leverage the benefits of continuous-time and discrete-state diffusion model, we proposed
489   C OMETH, a continuous-time discrete-state graph diffusion model, integrating graph data into a continuous
490   diffusion model framework. We introduced a new noise model adapted to graph specificities using
491   different node and edge rates and a tailored marginal distribution and noise schedule. In addition, we
492   successfully replaced the structural features of D I G RESS with a single encoding with provable expressivity
493   guarantees, removing unnecessary features. Empirically, we showed that integrating continuous time
494   leads to significant improvements across various metrics over state-of-the-art discrete-state diffusion
495   models on a large set of molecular and non-molecular benchmark datasets.

985    A.5     C LASSIFIER - FREE GUIDANCE
986
987    In the conditional generation setting, one wants to generate samples satisfying a specific property y, to
988    which we refer as the conditioner. For example, in text-to-image diffusion models, the conditioner
989    consists of a textual description specifying the image the model is intended to generate. The most
990    straightforward way to perform conditional generation for diffusion models is to inject the conditioner
991    into the network—therefore modeling pθ (z (t−1) | z (t) , y)—hoping that the model will take it into
992    account. However, the network might ignore y, and several efficient approaches to conditional generation
993
       for diffusion models were consequently developed.
994    The approach leveraged by Vignac et al. (2022) to perform conditional generation is classifier-guidance.
995    It relies on a trained unconditional diffusion model and a regressor, or classifier, depending on the
996    conditioner, trained to predict the conditioner given noisy inputs. As mentioned in Ho and Salimans
997    (2021), it has the disadvantage of complicating the training pipeline, as a pre-trained classifier cannot be
998    used during inference.
999    To avoid training a classifier to guide the sampling process, classifier-free guidance has been proposed
1000   in Ho and Salimans (2021) and then adapted for discrete data in Tang et al. (2022). A classifier-free
1001   conditional diffusion model jointly trains a conditional and unconditional model through conditional
1002   dropout. That is, the conditioner is randomly dropped with probability puncond during training, in
1003   which the conditioner is set to a null vector. However, Tang et al. (2022) showed that learning the null
1004   conditioner jointly with the model’s parameters is more efficient.
1005   At the sampling stage, the next state is sampled through

1033   B.1    R EVERSE PROCESS : TAU - LEAPING AND PREDICTOR - CORRECTOR
1034
1035   At the sampling stage, we use the τ -leaping algorithm to generate new samples, as proposed by Campbell
1036   et al. (2022). We first sample the graph size and then sample the noisy graph G(t) from the prior
1037   distribution. At each iteration, we keep G(t) and R̂t,θ (G, G̃) constant and simulate the reverse process
1038
       for a time interval of length τ . In practice, we count all the transitions between t and t − τ and apply
                                                                         (t)                       (t)
1039   them simultaneously. The number of transitions between xi and x̃i (respectively eij and ẽij ) is
                                           (t),θ   (t)                     (t),θ   (t)
1040   Poisson distributed with mean τ RX (xi , x̃) (respectively τ R̂E (eij , ẽij )). Since our state space
1041   has no ordinal structure, multiple transitions in one dimension are meaningless, and we reject them.
1042   In addition, we experiment using the predictor-corrector scheme. After each predictor step using
1043   R̂(t),θ (G, G̃), we can also apply several corrector steps using the expression defined in Campbell et al.
1044   (2022), i.e., R̂(t),c = R̂(t),θ + R(t) . This rate admits qt (G(t) ) as its stationary distribution, which
1045   means that applying the corrector steps brings the distribution of noisy graphs at time t closer to the
1046   marginal distribution of the forward process. As τ is fixed only during the sampling stage, its value can
1047   be adjusted to balance sample quality and efficiency, i.e., the number of model evaluations. We perform
1048   such an ablation study in Appendix D.

1411   E    L IMITATIONS
1412
1413   Although our model advances the state-of-the-art across all considered benchmarks, it still faces quadratic
1414   complexity, a common issue in graph diffusion models. This problem could be alleviated by adapting
1415   methods like EDGE (Chen et al., 2023) used to scale D I G RESS for large graph generation. Additionally,
1416
       our approach does not support the generation of continuous features and is restricted to categorical
       attributes. To generate continuous features, it should be combined with a continuous-state diffusion
1417
       model, resulting in an approach similar to Vignac et al. (2023).
