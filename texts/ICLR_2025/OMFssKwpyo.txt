011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The global transition towards sustainable energy sources has positioned solar
014            power as a cornerstone of modern electricity systems, underscoring the critical
015
               need for advanced forecasting techniques in grid management. Accurate net load
               forecasting is crucial for efficient and reliable power grid operations, especially
016
               with the rapid deployment of behind-the-meter (BTM) renewable energy sources
017
               such as rooftop solar. Notably, BTM solar generation is neither controlled nor
018            monitored by utilities and hence only net load data are observed. Different from
019            load forecasting, net load forecasting faces new challenges because BTM solar,
020            a major component of net load, behaves very differently from and is much more
021            variable than loads. To exploit the distinct natures of solar generation and load
022            and unlock their predictive potentials, we propose SONNET, which stands for
023            SOlar-disaggregatioN-based NEt load forecasting with Transformers. It is a novel
024            probabilistic net load forecasting method based on disaggregating net loads into
025            solar generation and loads and feeding both into the predictors. The method further
026
               features a) an enhanced Transformer architecture that integrates both historical and
               future input data, employing a combination of self-attention and cross-attention
027
               mechanisms, and b) a data augmentation method that enhances the robustness of net
028
               load forecasts against weather forecast errors. Extensive experiments are conducted
029            based on the comprehensive real-world data set from a recent net load forecasting
030            competition organized by the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE). It is demonstrated
031            that our proposed method both improves the accuracy and reduces the uncertainty
032            of net load forecasts. Notably, our proposed method significantly outperforms the
033            state-of-the-art. The proposed techniques also have broad applications for energy
034            and/or general forecasting-related problems.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      In an electric grid, the net load is the difference between the electric load and the “behind-the-
      meter” (BTM) power generation, notably BTM solar generation such as rooftop solar on residential,
040
      commercial, and industrial premises. As grid operators typically neither monitor nor control BTM
041
      generation, net load (as opposed to load) is what grid operators need to procure energy supply for
042   at all times. With the rapid growth of renewable energy sources in our power systems, net load
043   forecasting plays an increasingly crucial role for grid operators to efficiently and reliably plan their
044   daily energy procurement. The importance of net load forecasting is no less exemplified by the first
045   net load forecasting competition recently organized by the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) (HeroX,
046   2023), anticipating a very significant penetration of BTM solar generation in the coming years.
047
      Traditionally, with no or little BTM solar generation, the problem of net load forecasting reduces
048   to load forecasting only, for which there have been decades of ongoing innovations and practices
049   (Kuster et al., 2017). As BTM solar penetration increases, the corresponding shift in the composition
050   of net load leads to a fundamental change in the problem nature of net load forecasting: While a)
051   electric load in a future time slot correlates with both historical loads and future weather (among other
052   factors potentially), b) solar generation is predominantly determined by meteorological conditions,
053   particularly solar irradiance (Ahmed et al., 2020). Given these, an important practical limitation
      that makes net load forecasting a particularly challenging and new problem is the following: It is

      2   R ELATED WORK
100
101
      Net Load Forecasting: Net load forecasting has gained increasing attention in recent years due to
102   the growth of BTM solar generation. Algorithms have been developed that take net loads and weather
103   features, including solar irradiance, as input, and forecast net loads as output (see, e.g., (Tziolis
104   et al., 2023; Faustine and Pereira, 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) among others.) These works, however,
105   do not explicitly exploit the different natures of the underlying solar generation and load processes.
106   Other works took the approach of estimating load and solar generation separately. Most of these
107   works require knowledge of the ground truth solar generation and load traces as supervised labels
      for training their respective predictors (see (Zhang et al., 2021; Alipour et al., 2020) among others.)

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      As separately monitored solar generation and load traces are typically not available in practice,
109   disaggregation-based methods have been proposed that only rely on net load traces which are indeed
110   available. (Wang et al., 2018) decomposes net loads into loads, solar generation, and residuals, albeit
111   with a relatively simple disaggregation algorithm and evaluated on synthetic data only. (Jia et al.,
112   2023) decomposes net load into load and solar generation. These works train separate predictors for
113   the respective sub-traces albeit with relatively simple predictor architectures. Notably, while different
114   techniques have been proposed, there has not been a verifiable state-of-the-art due to the lack of a
115   common testing benchmark, until the net load forecasting competition recently organized by the
116   U.S. DOE (HeroX, 2023). In this competition, more than 90 teams have participated including both
117
      researchers and many commercial forecasting companies. The top-ranked results based on a common
      set of real-world data and evaluation metrics demonstrate the first verifiable state-of-the-art for net
118
      load forecasting to the best of the authors’ knowledge.
119
120
      Time Series Forecasting with Transformers: Transformers, introduced in (Vaswani et al., 2017),
121
      are highly effective in sequence modeling benefiting from the attention mechanism. Researchers
122   have successfully applied Transformers to time series forecasting across various fields (Wen et al.,
123   2022; Liang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b), including energy consumption (Nazir et al., 2023),
124   traffic (Zhang et al., 2024), and finance (Mulvey et al., 2022). Models like (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu
125   et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022) modify the underlying attention mechanism to better accommodate
126   long-term series forecasting and reduce computational complexity. Alternatively, another approach
127   avoids altering the attention mechanism itself and instead employs the original Transformer block for
128   time series modeling (Nie et al., 2022; Zhang and Yan, 2022; Liu et al., 2023).

130   3     P ROBLEM FORMULATION
131
132   For an electricity-consuming region of interest, its net load at time t is denoted as Nt . Based on
133   the common practice of power system operations, it is crucial to perform net load forecasting on a
134   day-ahead schedule. Specifically, we adopt the schedule from the DOE competition which broadly
135   embodies real-world power system operation requirements: At 10 am every day, the hourly net
136   loads of the 24 hours of the next day need to be forecasted. Moreover, due to the risk management
137   requirement of reliable power system operations, probabilistic forecasts are needed. In other words,
138
      the goal is to provide a probability distribution of each hourly net load of the next day. To evaluate an
      estimated probability distribution based on the observed ground truths, a commonly used metric (also
139
      adopted in the DOE competition) is the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS):
140

151   4.1   BTM SOLAR DISAGGREGATION
152
153   The net load Nt , load Lt , and BTM solar generation Gt satisfy the following simple relation:
154                                       Nt = Lt − Gt ,     t = 1, ..., T.                                (2)
155
156   While only the net load data are observed in practice, intuitively, having both the load and solar
157   generation data separately can offer more information to facilitate net load forecasting. We aim to
158
      disaggregate each net load data trace Nt into two data traces of solar generation Gt and load Lt , and
      then utilize patterns from both traces to forecast future net loads.
159
160   In addition, while utilities do not monitor BTM solar generation Gt , they typically have some estimate
161   of the installed solar capacity C at a regional level. This is because solar energy interconnection in a
      power distribution system must be approved by utilities for reliability reasons, making solar capacity

      information available to them. As such, the solar disaggregation problem is formulated as follows:
163   For a region of interest, given a net load trace Nt = Lt − Gt , ∀t, and a rough estimate of the solar
164   capacity C, estimate the underlying solar generation trace Gt and load trace Lt , ∀t. For this, we
165   develop a BTM solar disaggregation algorithm inspired by the general framework of (Pu and Zhao,
166   2023).

      4.2   P REDICTOR ARCHITECTURE
249   Given the disaggregated solar generation and load traces, we propose a Transformer-based architecture
250   designed for net load prediction, as depicted in Figure 1. This architecture leverages both historical
251   and future input data to enhance the accuracy of the forecasts, employing a combination of self-
252   attention and cross-attention mechanisms.
253
      Exogenous Variables: We integrate not only the net load variables X ∈ Rn×l (net load, load,
254
      disaggregated solar generation), but also introduce exogenous variables S ∈ Rm×l to assist in the net
255   load forecasting. These exogenous variables encompass weather features W (temperature, humidity,
256   DHI, DNI), and time features Z. Here l denotes the context window, n denotes the number of net
257   load variables, and m denotes the number of exogenous variables. Detailed descriptions of these
258   features are provided in the Appendix A.7.1.
259
      Historical and Future Input: Our model processes both historical and future variables for net
260
      load forecasting. Specifically, the historical input H includes both historical X and S, such that
261
      H = Concat(X, S) ∈ R(m+n)×l , while the future input consists of a) Ŝ ∈ Rm×l̂ , i.e., forecasted
262
263   exogenous variables, and b) Ĝ ∈ R1×l̂ , i.e., forecasted solar generation computed based on the
264   physical model (cf. Section 4.1.1) and the weather forecasts, where ˆl denotes the prediction length.
265   For the historical input time series, we apply patching, which involves segmenting the time series
266   into subseries-level patches that serve as input tokens to the Transformer. This technique has
267   been demonstrated to enhance the effectiveness of capturing dynamic local patterns and semantic
268   information while reducing computational costs in time series analysis (Nie et al., 2022). Given
269   the patch length p and stride s, the patch number of input series is given by r = ⌊(l − p)/s⌋ + 1,
      which indicates the sequence length after patching operation. Furthermore, we reshape the tensor

312   4.3   DATA AUGMENTATION
313
314   Although the ground truth data for historical net load and weather are available, importantly, day-
315   ahead forecasts of certain weather variables can suffer from poor accuracy. This issue is particularly
316
      pronounced in areas such as Hawaii due to its fast-varying clouds. Thus, the trained predictors’
      robustness to weather forecast errors is a major concern, especially with a high level of penetration
317
      of solar generation. To improve the predictor’s robustness to weather forecast errors, we design the
318
      following data augmentation steps for the training and validation data sets:
319
320   • Error Simulation in Meteorological Features: We introduce variability into each meteorological
321     feature to simulate forecast errors. For irradiance features (e.g., Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI)
322     and Diffuse Horizontal Irradiance (DHI)), we adjust the original value with a multiplicative error
323     term (1 + ϵ), where ϵ is sampled from a normal distribution N (0, σ1 ) with a sufficiently large
        σ1 . For other weather features (e.g., temperature and humidity), we generate and apply additive


                                                         6
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
           errors from the empirical forecasting error distributions N (0, σ2 ) (Lucas Segarra et al., 2019).
325        These adjustments are capped at reasonable limits to ensure that the values remain within plausible
326        ranges.
327
       • Physical-Model-based Solar Generation Data: Using these adjusted meteorological features,
328
         we apply the physical model 4.1.1 to re-compute the solar generation data, reflecting potential
329
         real-world variations due to forecast errors. The estimated solar generation data is then combined
330      with the ground truth net load data to update the disaggregated loads.

393

394   errors as follows: Errors are randomly sampled from a normal distribution N (0, σf2 ), where σf
395   represents the empirical standard deviation of a feature f ’s forecast error that we observe for a given
396   hour and location. These generated errors are then added back to the ground truth values. The
397   empirical standard deviations used in generating these errors are provided in Appendix B.2. To
398   increase the level of difficulty in the net load forecasting task, we further simulate two additional
399   settings, “Challenging” and “Extreme”, where the errors are generated with 1.5x and 2x of the
400   empirical forecast error standard deviation of each weather feature.

528   6    C ONCLUSION
529
530   We developed SONNET, a novel method for net load forecasting based on disaggregated BTM
531   solar generation and loads. An enhanced Transformer architecture that takes both historical data
532   and exogenous future input such as weather forecast is designed. A physical-model-based data
533   augmentation technique is developed to improve the predictor robustness to weather forecast errors.
534   Extensive validation based on real-world data from U.S. DOE’s recent net load forecasting competition
535   demonstrated that our method consistently and significantly outperforms the state of the art. Last but
536
      not least, the developed techniques, in particular, disaggregation, enhanced Transformer architecture,
      and physics model-based data augmentation, all have broad applications beyond net load forecasting.

847   A.6     DATA AUGMENTATION
848
849   The training dataset comprises one copy of the ground truth data and two copies of the augmented
850   data. For the augmented data, we draw the error ratio term ϵ drawn from a normal distribution:
851   N (0, 0.36) for DNI/DHI and draw the error term from N (0, 2) and N (0, 12) for temperature and
852   relative humidity, respectively (Lucas Segarra et al., 2019). To ensure that the synthetic weather
853   variables remain within plausible ranges, we clip the generated DNI/DHI values to fall between zero
854
      and the maximum value observed in the ground truth data. Similarly, the relative humidity values are
      clipped to lie within the range of 0 to 100.

885   A.7.2    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS AND COMPUTATIONAL EFFICIENCY.
886
      The data prior to May 1st, 2023 is used for training; the data from May 1st to June 15th, 2023 is
887   used for validation; the test set is from June 18th, 2023 to July 16th, 2023, which is the same as the
888   competition. The model is trained for up to 300 epochs with an early stopping patience of 30 epochs.
889   If early stopping is triggered at epoch x, the model is re-trained using both the training and validation
890   datasets for x epochs. This approach ensures the model fully leverages the limited available data.
891   We employs 8 attention heads for all locations. For the "HI" and "OR" locations, the embedding
892   dimension (d) is set to 64. However, for the "GA" location, d is reduced to 48. This is because the
893   data from "GA" is heavily quantized and approximately one-third of it is dropped due to quality
894   issues. The model architecture includes a single Transformer encoder layer combined with a cross
895
      attention layer with a dropout rate of 0.3. The look back window l is 336 (hours), and the historical
      input data is segmented into patches with a length of 8 and a stride of 4, while the prediction window
896
      is 24 (hours) and no patching is applied to the future input data. Learning rate in all experiments
897
      are automatically selected by employing the same algorithm as in (Nie et al., 2022). The model is
898   trained on a single RTX 3090 GPU and takes approximately 10-20 minutes to complete training for
899   one location, using augmented data spanning roughly four and a half years.
