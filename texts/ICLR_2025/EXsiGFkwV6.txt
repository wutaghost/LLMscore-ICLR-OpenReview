029                                                            A BSTRACT
030
                      Co-speech gesture generation is crucial for creating lifelike avatars and enhancing
031
                      human-computer interactions by synchronizing gestures with speech in computer
032
                      vision. Despite recent advancements, existing methods often struggle with
033                   accurately aligning gesture motions with speech signals and achieving pixel-
034                   level realism. To address these challenges, we introduce Realistic-Gesture, a
035                   groundbreaking framework that transforms co-speech gesture video generation
036                   through three innovative components: (1) a speech-aware gesture tokenization that
037                   incorporate speech context into motion pattern representation, (2) a mask gesture
038                   generator that learns to map audio signals to gestures by predicting masked motion
039                   tokens, enabling bidirectional contextually relevant gesture synthesis and editing,
040                   and (3) a structure-aware refinement module that employs differentiable edge
041
                      connection to link gesture keypoints to improve video generation. Our extensive
                      experiments demonstrate that Realistic-Gesture not only produces highly realistic
042
                      and speech-aligned gesture videos but also supports long-sequence generation and
043
                      video gesture editing applications.

046   1       I NTRODUCTION
047
048   In human communication, speech is often accompanied by gestures that enhance understanding and
049   convey emotions De Ruiter et al. (2012). As these non-verbal cues play a vital role in effective
050
      interaction Burgoon et al. (1990), gesture generation a key component of natural human-computer
      interactions. As artificial intelligence advances, equipping virtual avatars with realistic gesture
051
      capabilities will become essential in creating immersive interactive experiences.
052
053   The relationships between the semantic and emotion content of speech context, the corresponding
      gestures, and the visual appearance of the speaker’s performance are complex. As such, many


                                                                     1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      recent works Yi et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023; 2022d;a) address a reduced form of this problem by
055   generating a simplified representation of the 3D motion, consisting of joints and body parts, that
056   plausibly accompanies a given speech sample, which can then be rendered using standard rendering
057   pipelines. Such representations capture basic motion patterns, yet they neglect the importance of the
058   speaker’s visual appearance, resulting in a lack of realism that hinders effective communication.
059
      Other works, e.g. ANGIE Liu et al. (2022c) and S2G-diffusion He et al. (2024), employ image-
060
      warping techniques, constrained by keypoints obtained from optical-flow-based deformations, for
061   co-speech video generation. However, such approaches encounter several critical issues. First, these
062   keypoints only define large-scale transformations, and thus miss subtle movements of specific body
063   parts (e.g. hands, fingers). Second, it is difficult to connect such broad and unconstrained motions
064   representation to speech content. This makes it difficult to conditionally generate gestures that
065   are appropriately responsive to the audio, which inhibits the gestures’ naturalism and expressivity.
066   Finally, the generated motion patterns are often unstructured and overly reactive to large motion,
067   resulting in noisy and imprecise renderings, especially in the hands and shoulders. Collectively,
068   these challenges significantly limit the overall quality and realism of the generated video content.
069   To address these challenges, we introduce Realistic-Gesture, a framework designed to generate
070   speech-aligned gesture motions and high-fidelity speech video outputs. Our approach begins with
071   refined gesture motion representations using keypoints from pretrained human pose estimators,
072   allowing for clearer disentanglement of human motions across the face, body, and hands. To uncover
073   the intrinsic temporal connections between gestures and speech, we employ contrastive learning
074   to align these two modalities. This joint representation captures the triggers of gesture patterns
075
      influenced by speech. We incorporate speech-contextual features into the tokenization process
      of gesture motions through knowledge distillation, aiming to infuse the gesture representations
076
      with implicit intentions conveyed in the audio. This integration creates a clear linkage between
077
      the gestures and the corresponding speech, enabling the conditional generation of gestures that
078   accurately reflect the speaker’s intended meaning based on the speech input. For latent motion
079   generation, inspired by Muse Chang et al. (2023) and MAGE Li et al. (2023), we introduce a
080   masked gesture generator that refines the alignment of gesture motions with the speech signal
081   through bidirectional mask pretraining, enabling long sequence generation and editing capabilities.
082   Finally, for uplifting the latent motion generation into 2D animations, we propose a structure-aware
083   image refinement module that generates heatmaps of edge connections from keypoints, providing
084   image-level supervision to improve the quality of body regions with large motion. Extensive
085   experiments demonstrate that our method outperforms the existing state-of-the-art approaches in
086
      both quantitative and qualitative metrics.
087   In summary, our primary contributions are:
088
089        1. a speech-aware gesture motion representation obtained through knowledge distillation
090           from the gesture-speech aligned features from contrastive learning;
091        2. a masked gesture motion generator, carefully designed to enable high-quality gesture
092           motion generation with long sequence generation and edit-ability support; and
093        3. a pixel-level refinement module, which uses a structure-aware edge heatmap as supervision
094           to improve the final output fidelity.

106   et al. (2023) use large conversational and speech datasets for joint face and body modeling with

138   Warping-Based Image Animation. Warping-based image animation methods have risen to
139   prominence recently Siarohin et al. (2021; 2019); Zhao & Zhang (2022). They leverage keypoint
140   predictor to identify pairwise corresponding keypoints between a source image and a driving image.
141   This information is used to warp the source image to match the driving image, thereby producing a
142   deformation that aligns with the driving scene. Following this, pixel-level optical flow and occlusion
143   masks are estimated from the deformed images to capture global motion and handle occlusions for
144   achieving driving image reconstruction. We defer additional details to the Appendix.
145
146   Image-Animation Based Co-Speech Gesture Video Synthesis. In the context of co-speech
147   gesture video synthesis, recent advancements have employed warping-based image animation
148   techniques to derive motion patterns and learn the correspondence between these patterns and
149   audio, facilitating speech-driven generation. Given a video clip V = {I0 , I1 , . . . , IN } and
150   an accompanying audio sequence A = {a1 , a2 , . . . , aN }, the objective is to predict motion
151   representations M̂ based on the initial frame I0 and the audio input. The image animation module
152   reconstructs all video frames Iˆ1 through unsupervised learning to derive motion representations and
153   transformations. The audio sequence serves as guidance for reconstructing motion patterns across
154   the entire sequence of frames following the initial frame.
155
      However, this approach faces three significant challenges: (1) keypoints derived from global
156   optical-flow-based transformations, learned through unsupervised methods, often fail to capture
157   subtle movements of specific body parts; (2) the motion representations do not include contextual
158   information from the speech, making it difficult to generate gestures that are conditionally responsive
159   to audio; and (3) the lack of structural awareness in the motion representations leads to blurry and
160   noisy predictions, particularly affecting the hands and shoulders, while also rendering the system
161   sensitive to large motion patterns. To address these challenges, we propose the following methods
      to enhance control over co-speech gesture video generation.

      Unlike ANGIE Liu et al. (2022c) and S2G-Diffusion He et al. (2024), which rely on unsupervised
179
      learning of keypoints for warping-based on optical flow, we utilize 2D poses extracted from
180   images. While using 2D poses for image warping may slightly decrease fidelity, it significantly
181   enhances the perceptual quality of the generated video, shown in Sec. 5.4. With poses, gestures
182   can be decomposed into facial and body movements. We represent a gesture motion sequence as
183   G = [F ; B] = [ft ; bt ]Tt=1 , where T denotes the length of the motion, f represents the 2D facial
184   landmarks, and b denotes the 2D body landmarks. Further details on gesture representations can be
185   found in the appendix. For speech representation, we extract audio embeddings from WavLM Chen
186   et al. (2022). In addition, we extract Mel spectrogram features Rabiner & Schafer (2010) and beat
187   information using librosa McFee et al. (2015). These features are concatenated to form the speech
188
      representation. For image-warping transformation, we select TPS Zhao & Zhang (2022).

190   Speech-Gesture Alignment. To align gesture motion patterns with the content of speech and
191

192

193

196   aggregate content-relevant information from each feature sequence, resulting in the embeddings z s
197   and z g for speech and gestures, respectively. We leverage CLIP-style contrastive learning to train
198   these content encoders. Given a batch of paired embeddings B = {(zit , zig )}B  i=1 , we optimize the

248   4.2                                         S PEECH - CONDITIONED G ESTURE M OTION G ENERATION
249
250   To enhance the generation of gesture motions across different layers of the quantized codebooks,
251   we draw inspiration from VALL-E Wang et al. (2023a) to design Masked Gesture Generator for
252   jointly decoding facial and body motions for the base-layer outputs of quantizers, and Residual
253   Gesture Generator for the face and body tokens from the subsequent R residual quantization layers.
254
255   Masked Gesture Generator. As shown in Fig. 3, during training, we derive motion tokens
256   by processing raw gesture sequences through both body and face tokenizers. The motion token
257   corresponding to the source image acts as the conditioning for all subsequent frames. For speech
258
      control, we initialize the audio content encoder from alignment pre-training as described in Sec. 4.1.
      This pre-alignment of gesture tokens with audio encoder features enhances the coherence of gesture
259
      generation. We employ cross-attention, using the audio input as keys and values while the gesture
260
      representation serves as the query, integrating audio information with gesture feature. To refine
261   control over gesture patterns, we apply Adaptive Instance Normalization (AdaIN) Huang & Belongie
262   (2017) after the feed-forward layers, enabling diverse gesture styles based on the speaker’s identity.
263
264   Residual Gesture Generator. The Residual Gesture Generator shares a similar architecture with
265   the Masked Gesture Generator, but it includes R separate embedding layers corresponding to each
266   RVQ residual layer. During training, we randomly select a quantizer layer j ∈ [1, R] for learning.
267   All tokens from the preceding layers t0:j−1 are embedded and summed to form the token embedding
268   input. After generating the base layer predictions of discrete tokens from the Masked Gesture
269   Generator, these tokens are fed into the Residual Gesture Generator. This module iteratively predicts
      the tokens from the base layers, ultimately producing the final quantized output.

      Inference. While existing works Liu et al. (2023); Yi et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024) leverage
271   auto-regressive next-token prediction or diffusion-based generation process, these strategies hinder
272   the fast synthesis for real-time applications. To resolve this problem, as in Fig. 3, we employ an
273   iterative mask prediction strategy to decode motion tokens during inference. Initially, all tokens are
274   masked except for the first token from the source frame. Conditioned on the audio input, the Mask
275   Gesture Generator predicts probabilities for the masked tokens. In the l-th iteration, the tokens with
276   the lowest confidence are re-masked, while the remaining tokens stay unchanged for subsequent
277   iterations. This updated sequence continues to inform predictions until the final iteration, when
278   the base-layer tokens are fully generated. Upon completion, the Residual Gesture Generator uses
279
      the predicted base-layer tokens to progressively generate sequences for the remaining quantization
      layers. Finally, all tokens are transformed back into motion sequences via the RVQ-VAE decoder.

466   5.4    A BLATION S TUDY
467
468   In this section, we present ablation study of keypoint design for image warping, gesture pattern
469   representation exploraton, gesture generator architecture design, and varios comparisons of image-
470   refinement. We defer additional experiments in the Appendix.
471
      Motion Keypoint Design We evaluate three keypoint representations for image-warping: (1)
472
      unsupervised keypoints for global optical-flow transformation (as in ANGIE and S2G-Diffusion),
473
      (2) 2D human poses, and (3) 2D human poses augmented with flexible learnable points. Each
474   design is assessed using TPS Zhao & Zhang (2022) transformation, with self-reconstruction based
475   on these keypoints for evaluation. We compare the full-model reconstruction with refinement against
476   the first three designs without refinement. As shown in Tab. 3a, learnable keypoints lead to a
477   significant decrease in FVD, highlighting their inadequacy for motion control. The 2D landmark-
478   based keypoints yield slightly lower SSIM scores, likely due to their limited capacity to represent
479   global transformations. The inclusion of flexible keypoints does not enhance the image-warping
480   outcomes. Consequently, we opt to utilize 2D pose landmarks exclusively for our study.
481   Motion Representation. We evaluate several configurations: (1) baseline: no motion representa-
482   tion, relying solely on the generator to synthesize raw 2D landmarks; (2) + RVQ: utilizing Residual
483   VQ (RVQ) to encode joint face-body keypoints; (3) + separate motion: employing two RVQs for
484   independent face and body motions; (4) + distill: learning joint embeddings for speech and gesture
485   in both face and body motions, followed by distillation for RVQ tokenization. We discover RVQ
      significantly improve the precise pose location while distillation leads to natural movements.

      Generator Design. We explore various designs for the gesture generator: (1) w/o res: no residual
495
      gesture decoder; (2) concat: instead of using cross-attention for audio control, we concatenate
496   the audio features with gesture latent features element-wise during generation; (3) w/o align: the
497   audio encoder is randomly initialized rather than initialized from face and body contrastive learning.
498   Our findings indicate that the Residual Gesture Generator significantly enhances finger motion
499   generation. The cross-attention design outperforms element-wise concatenation, while the pre-
500   alignment of the audio encoder notably improves FGD. We attribute this improvement to the shared
501   similarities in the codebook and audio encoders during contrastive alignment.
502   Image Refinement. We examine various network designs for motion generation, specifically: (1)
503   w/o refine: no image refinement, relying solely on image warping; (2) + UNet: employing a standard
504   UNet; (3) + pose skeleton: integrating connected skeleton maps as in the diffusion ReferenceNet Hu
505   et al. (2023); (4) + edge heatmap: substituting the previous design with our learnable edge heatmap.
506   Our experiments reveal that the edge heatmap outperforms skeleton maps, likely due to the learnable
507   thickness of connections, which provides better semantic-aware generation guidance.
508   Training and Inference Strategy. We evaluate the mask ratio during training and the number of
509   inference steps during decoding. As shown in Tab. 3f, our model requires only 5 inference steps, in
510   contrast to over 50 or 100 steps in diffusion-based models. Furthermore, a uniform masking ratio
511   between 0.5 and 1 during training yields optimal performance.

      5.5   A PPLICATION
514
515   Long Sequence Generation. Shown on the left of Fig. 6, to generate long sequences, we start
516   with the initial frame and the corresponding target audio, which we segment into smaller windows.
517   After generating the first segment, we use the last few frames of the generated output as the new
518   starting frame conditions for the next segment of audio, allowing for a iterative outpainting.
519
520   Video Gesture Editing. For gesture editing and inpainting, we first extract the keypoints from a
521   given video sequence and tokenize the face and body movements into motion tokens. Thanks to the
522   model’s bidirectional decoding capability, we insert [MASK] tokens wherever edits are needed.
523   Trained on temporal masking, the model generate coherent gestures in the masked areas. By
524   incorporating different speech input and speaker embeddings, we can create new gesture patterns
525
      and re-render the video based on the edited latent motion tokens.
526
527   Gesture Pattern Transfer. Given the design of our framework, with different identity embedding,
528   the model can generate different gesture patterns given the input identity embedding control given
529
      the same audio. Please see the demo videos in our Appendix for more details.

531   6     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We present Realistic-Gesture, a framework for generating realistic co-speech gesture videos. To
534   ensure the gestures cohere well with speech, we propose speech-content aware gesture motion
535   representation though knowledge distillation from the gesture-speech aligned features obtained
536   through contrastive learning. Our masked gesture motion generator enables the creation and editing
537   of long, high-quality gesture motion sequences. Our pixel-level refinement module further improves
538   the transformation of inferred gesture motions into realistic animations for large-scale body motion.
539   We believe this work will encourage further exploration of the relationship between gesture patterns
      and speech context for more compelling gesture video generations in the future.
