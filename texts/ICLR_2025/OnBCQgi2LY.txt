009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Predictive modeling often faces challenges due to limited data availability and
012            quality, especially in domains where collected features are weakly correlated with
013            outcomes and where additional feature collection is constrained by ethical or prac-
014            tical difficulties. Traditional machine learning (ML) models struggle to incorpo-
015            rate unobserved yet critical factors. In this work, we introduce an effective ap-
016            proach to formulate latent feature mining as text-to-text propositional logical rea-
017            soning. We propose FLAME (Faithful Latent FeAture Mining for Predictive Model
018
               Enhancement), a framework that leverages large language models (LLMs) to aug-
               ment observed features with latent features and enhance the predictive power of
019
               ML models in downstream tasks. Our framework is generalizable across various
020
               domains with necessary domain-specific adaptation, as it is designed to incorpo-
021            rate contextual information unique to each area, ensuring effective transfer to dif-
022            ferent areas facing similar data availability challenges. We validate our framework
023            with two case studies: (1) the criminal justice system, a domain characterized by
024            limited and ethically challenging data collection; (2) the healthcare domain, where
025            patient privacy concerns and the complexity of medical data limit comprehensive
026            feature collection. Our results show that inferred latent features align well with
027            ground truth labels and significantly enhance the downstream classifier.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Prediction plays a crucial role in decision-making across many domains. While traditional machine
033
      learning (ML) models are powerful, they are often constrained by the availability of observed data
      features. Contrary to the common belief that we are in a “big data era,” this is not always the
034
      case, especially in areas where decisions have profound impacts on human lives. In areas like
035
      criminal justice and healthcare, data availability is often constrained, with ethical limitations further
036   restricting the features that can be collected and used (Lu et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2023). As a
037   result, many critical decisions must rely on a limited set of features, some of which may have weak
038   correlations with the prediction target. This presents significant challenges for achieving accurate
039   predictions.
040
      To overcome the challenges posed by limited feature availability and quality, latent feature mining
041
      is a common approach. However, traditional techniques face two key limitations in domain-specific
042   applications. First, inferring domain-specific latent features often requires contextual information
043   beyond the available data, such as expert input, public information, or crowd-sourcing. This in-
044   formation is typically in natural language, which ML models like neural networks struggle to pro-
045   cess and encode into proper embeddings. Second, many latent feature mining techniques, such as
046   deep-learning based auto-encoders and the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, lack inter-
047   pretability. They extract features in abstract mathematical formats that are difficult to explain in
048   human terms. This is especially problematic in high-stakes domains like healthcare or criminal jus-
049   tice, where explaining and justifying a model’s predictions is crucial for building trust and ensuring
050
      ethical decision-making. The black-box nature of these methods makes it harder to gain confidence
      in the model’s outputs in these domains.
051

053   experts can infer additional latent features that go beyond the explicit data provided by drawing on
      their experience. For example, in the criminal justice system, predicting an individual’s likelihood

074   Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) present a promising new avenue with their
075

      have potential to process and generate information in ways that mimic human thought processes (Ji
076
      et al., 2024). Building on this insight, we propose FLAME , a framework that leverages LLMs to
077
      augment observed features with latent features and enhance the predictive power of ML models in
078   downstream tasks like classification. FLAME offers two key advantages over traditional latent feature
079   mining methods: (1) it seamlessly integrates contextual information provided in natural language,
080   and (2) by emulating human reasoning, it produces more interpretable outputs, making it particularly
081   valuable in high-stakes domains requiring explainability. We summarize our main contributions as
082   follows.
083
084   1. We introduce a new approach that LLMs to formulate latent feature mining as a reasoning task us-
085      ing text-to-text propositional logic. This method effectively infers latent features from observed
086      data and provides significant improvements in downstream prediction accuracy and interpretabil-
087
         ity over traditional techniques.
088   2. We develop a four-step versatile framework that integrates domain-specific contextual informa-
089      tion with minimal customization efforts. This framework is highly adaptable across various do-
090      mains, particularly those with limited observed features and ethical constraints on data collection.
091   3. We empirically validate our framework through case studies in both the criminal justice and
092      healthcare sectors, where latent features play an important role in enhancing prediction tasks.
093      The framework’s strong performance in two different application settings demonstrates its adapt-
094      ability and usefulness for other domains facing similar challenges.

      Data Augmentation versus Latent Feature Mining Data augmentation is a technique widely em-
      ployed to provide more data samples to improve the predictive power of ML models (Van Dyk &
100
      Meng, 2001). Generative models such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) learn data pat-
101
      terns and generate synthetic data to augment training sample sizes (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Kingma
102   & Welling, 2013). In contrast, latent features are hidden characteristics in a dataset that are not di-
103   rectly observed but can be inferred from available data. Incorporating meaningful latent features
104   can enhance the performance of downstream applications (Zhai & Peng, 2016; Jiang et al., 2023).
105   Methods such EM and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) offer alternative techniques to infer latent
106   features from observed data. However, EM algorithms, while estimating latent variable assignments
107   and updating model parameters to maximize data likelihood, often produce results that are difficult
      to interpret and require strong parametric assumptions. Similarly, VAEs use probabilistic approaches

      to describe data distribution with latent variables, but the learned mappings can also be hard to in-
109   terpret. Another related approach is dimension reduction such as Principal Component Analysis,
110   which reduces the size of the feature space while preserving the most important information. How-
111   ever, dimension reduction is less effective when the input feature set is already limited.

140   with synthetic samples during the fine-tuning process for LLMs.

      4    L ATENT F EATURE M INING WITH LLM S
185
186   We propose a new approach, FLAME , to efficiently and accurately extract latent features and augment
187   observed features to enhance the downstream prediction accuracy. It extracts the latent features Z
188
      from the original features X to capture complex patterns and relationships that individual features
      may overlook, especially when some of the X’s are weakly correlated with the outcome Y . At a
189
      high level, our approach transform this latent feature extraction process as a text-to-text proposi-
190
      tional reasoning task, i.e., infer the relationship Z = g(X) through logical reasoning with natural
191   language. Figure 2 provides an example of the extract process with the steps elaborated on below.
192
193   Following the framework established in previous work (Zhang et al., 2022), we denote the predicates
194
      related to the observed features as P1 , P2 , . . . , Pm . Consider a propositional theory S that contains
      rules that connect P ’s to the latent feature Z. We say Z can be deduced from S if the logic impli-
195
      cation (P1 ∧ P2 ∧ . . . ∧ Pm ) → Z is covered in S. For potentially complicated logical connections
196
      between P ’s and Z, we also introduce intermediate predicates O’s and formulate a logical chain (a
197   sequence of logical implications) that connects X to the latent features Z as follows:
198
                          X → (P1 ∧ P2 ∧ . . . ∧ Pm ) → (O1 ∧ O2 ∧ . . . ∧ Oℓ ) → Z.                         (1)
199
200   Our approach formulates this logical chain as a multi-stage Chain of Thoughts (CoT) prompt tem-
201   plate, and then guide LLMs to infer Z from X using the prompt template. Specifically, we first ex-
202   tract predicates P ’s from X. Then we infer intermediate predicates with a rule (P1 ∧P2 ∧. . .∧Pm ) →
203   Ol for l = 1, . . . , ℓ − 1, and forward the intermediate predicates into the next stage to infer Ol+1 .
204   Finally, we infer latent features with (O1 ∧ O2 ∧ . . . ∧ Oℓ ) → Z. With the formulated multi-stage
205   CoT prompt template, we then generate synthetic training data to fine-tune LLMs to enhance the
206
      logical reasoning ability of LLMs in the self-instruct manner (Wang et al., 2022).
207   We use a hypothetical example from our case study setting to illustrate the formulation of the logic
208   chain. The blue (leftmost) box in Figure 2 shows the observed feature X for one individual. Exam-
209   ples for the predicates P ’s formulated from X could be:
210
               P1 :“the client has part-time job”, P2 : ” the client hasn’t complete high school”,
211
               P3 :“the client is single”, P4 : ”the client has drug issue”, P5 :” the client lives
212            in high crime area”, P6 : ” the client is assessed with high risk” ...

259
260
      (1) Formulate baseline rationales: The first step is to formulate baseline rationales, whic serve as
      guidelines for LLMs to infer latent features from observed ones. This involves two sub-steps:
261
262   – The first sub-step is to develop some baseline rationales, i.e., identify observed features potentially
263   correlated with latent features and formulate their relationships – the logic chain that connects X
264   to Z. Sources to help formulate these baseline rationales include established correlations (e.g., risk
265   score formulas), expert input, and other contextual information like socio-economic status in the
266
      neighborhood. This is also a critical step in our framework that allows the integration of domain-
      specific contextual information in the format of natural language.
267
268   – In the second sub-step, we craft prompts with interactive alignment. This is a critical component
269   to establish correct reasoning steps for prompts used in Step 2 to generate synthetic rationales.
      We involve experienced human in the domain to provide a prompt template for LLMs to generate


                                                                5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      rationales aligned with the baseline rationales, then test the prompt template on a few examples
271   using zero-shot. If the LLM fails to certain example, we provide the ground truth back to the LLM,
272   allowing it to revise the prompt template (Miao et al., 2023). This process iteratively refines the
273   template until LLMs consistently generate the desired output for all selected examples.
274
      (2) Enlarge data with synthetic rationales for fine-tuning: We generate synthetic training data
275
      in self-instruct fashion (Wang et al., 2022). With a handful of examples of the baseline rationales
276
      as a reference, we guide the LLMs via in-context learning to generate similar rationales to enlarge
277   the training data samples. To ensure the quality and diversity of the generated dataset, we introduce
278   human-in-the-loop interventions to filter out low-quality or invalid data based on heuristics. We
279   also leverage automatic evaluation metrics for quality control, e.g., removing data that lack essential
280   keywords.
281
      (3) Fine-tuning LLMs: To enhance the reasoning capabilities of the LLMs and better align their
282
      outputs in specific domains, we leverage the fine-tuning process with processed dataset from the pre-
283   vious step (Qiao et al., 2022). Fine-tuning not only boosts the accuracy and reliability of the LLMs,
284   but also significantly improves their ability to reason with complex inputs and reduce hallucination
285   (Tonmoy et al., 2024).
286
      (4) Latent feature inference: The fine-tuned model mirrors the nuanced reasoning process of hu-
287
      man experts. We use it to infer latent features, which are then fed into downstream prediction tasks
288
      to improve accuracy.

      5.1.1   R ISK L EVEL P REDICTION
325
326   Task Description. In this task we treat an observed feature –Risk Level – as the latent feature to
327   infer. The task is a multi-classification problem to learn Z ∼ g(X) among four labels for the latent
328   variable Z ∈ {moderate, high, very high} based on each client’s profile X.
329
      Implementation Details. We implement our proposed framework as follows. All prompt templates
330   are available in Appendix C.
331
332   - Step 0. Profile writing: In this pre-processing step, we translate structured data X into text that can
333
      be better handled by LLMs, i.e., formulating predicates P ’s from the features X. Then we formu-
      late the intermediate predicates O’s, where we prompt LLMs to extract and summarize underlying
334
      information such as background, socio-economic status, and challenges in two or three sentences.
335
      We then merge these sentences into the client’s profile. We use zero-shot prompting with GPT-4.
336
337   - Step 1. Formulating rationales: Using human input, established risk score calculations (Cor-
338   rections), and the code book with risk calculation details provided by our community partner, we
339
      summarize a general rule for inferring risk levels from the profiles, i.e., establishing the logic chains
      from P ’s and O’s to Z. We sample 40 client features from the dataset and formulate 40 baseline
340
      rationales that logically connect features to corresponding risk levels and are aligned with the high-
341
      level general rule. To avoid the primacy effect of LLMs, we rate risk scores from 0 to 10 to add
342   variability in the labels, categorized as follows: 0-4 (moderate risk), 4-7.5 (high risk), and 7.5-10
343   (very high risk).
344
345
      - Step 2. Enlarge fine-tuning data: With the 40 baseline rationales, we generate additional synthetic
      rationales. We sample client features and corresponding ground truth risk scores from the dataset,
346
      using one of the 40 rationales as an example, to prompt LLMs to produce similar narratives with
347
      CoT prompts. In total we got 3000 rationales for the training data.
348
349   - Step 3. Fine-tune LLMs: Our framework is designed to be plug-and-play, allowing the synthetic
350   data generated in the previous step to be used across different language models. We fine-tune
351
      two pre-trained language models for cross-validation purposes: GPT-3.5 and Llama2-13b (Ope-
      nAI, 2021). We use OpenAI API to fine-tune GPT-3.5-turbo-0125 (Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI).
352
      We fine-tune Llama2-13b-chat using LoRA (Hu et al., 2021).
353
354   - Step 4. Inference with LLMs: We prompt fine-tuned LLMs to infer risk level Ẑi from features Xi
355   for each client i in the test data and evaluate the out-of-sample accuracy by comparing the inferred
356   latent variable (risk level) Ẑi with the ground truth label Zi .
357
      Evaluation. We choose ML classifiers (e.g., Neural Networks) as the baseline to directly predict Ẑi
358
      from features Xi using the given class labels. We compare the prediction performance of Ẑi inferred
359
      from FLAME with that from ML models using out-of-sample accuracy and F1 score. Additionally,
360
      we evaluate the quality of generated text with an automatic evaluation metric. In the pre-processing
361   step, we assess the keyword coverage rate in the generated profile assuming each feature value is
362   a keyword. For synthetic rationales, we use YAKE, a pretrained keyword extractor (Campos et al.,
363   2020), to identify keywords, and then evaluate the keyword coverage rate with a rule-based detector
364   to determine how many logical information points are covered.

372   does not record this feature, thus, Z in this task is truly unobservable (in contrast to the one used in

374   Implementation Details. Steps 0 and 2-4 remain almost the same as in the risk-level prediction
375   task. Step 1 requires a slight adjustment (as discussed in Section 4, this step is the main part in
376   our framework that requires customization). Here, we formulate 40 baseline rationales in step 1 to
377   deduce “support likely needed” from client features. We leverage multi-stage prompting strategy
      (Qiao et al., 2022) to break down the task into three sub-tasks: (1) identify the main challenges

499   7    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
500
501
      In conclusion, FLAME provides a novel solution to the challenges of limited feature availability in
502   high-stakes domains by using LLMs to augment observed data with interpretable latent features.
503   This framework improves downstream prediction accuracy while enhancing explainability, which
504   makes it valuable for sensitive decision-making in areas like healthcare and criminal justice.
505
506   What is required to generalize FLAME for each new application? FLAME has broad potential
507   across various domains, particularly those with limited observed features and ethical constraints.
508   Steps 2-4 primarily rely on the adaptability of LLMs and allow flexible application across different
509   domains. However, Step 1 – identifying and formulating baseline domain-specific rationales – re-
510   quires domain expertise and involves additional manual effort. This effort is worthwhile because our
511   framework is intentionally designed to be domain-specific. We believe this is actually the critical
512
      step that drives the improved downstream prediction accuracy demonstrated in Section 6. By lever-
      aging contextual information that traditional methods cannot, FLAME significantly enhances model
513
      performance.
514
515   To elaborate, in Step 1, we utilize contextual information to tailor the framework to the specific do-
516   main. For example, in the outcome prediction task (Section 5.1.2), we incorporated external public
517   information on the socio-economic status of different zip codes. Our ablation study showed that
518
      excluding this zip code information significantly reduced the LLM’s ability to extract useful latent
      features, which highlights the importance of this contextual data in enhancing predictive power.
519
      Moreover, Step 1 allows human to provide external contextual information to align the LLM’s rea-
520
      soning and to mitigate potential issues raised from the LLM’s inherent knowledge limitations. In
521   another ablation study, we prompted GPT-4 to directly generate contextual information for zip codes
522   based solely on its internal knowledge, without external input. Out of 50 zip codes, 5 could not be
523   determined due to lack of information, 17 provided incorrect (hallucinated) information, and only
524   33 were correct (see Appendix E for examples). This result is consistent with recent research find-
525   ings that LLMs are not reliable as knowledge bases (He et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024).This shows
526   that, although our method requires more manual effort than other ML-based latent feature mining
527   methods, it effectively integrates contextual information that traditional approaches cannot, which
528   makes it both more effective in mining domain-specific features and worth the investment.
529
530   Future work. As we continue to refine our FLAME framework, we are actively pursuing avenues
531
      to enhance its fidelity and reliability. First, we are streamlining the process to reduce the need for hu-
      man intervention and increase the scalability of our approach. Second, we acknowledge that LLMs
532
      can inadvertently perpetuate existing biases present in their training data, and how to mitigate such
533
      bias remains an open question in the field Wan et al. (2023); Gallegos et al. (2024). FLAME attempts
534   to minimize such biases by leveraging domain-specific data and expert input during the fine-tuning
535   process. Furthermore, the training dataset is curated to include a diverse range of scenarios, and
536   the model’s inferences are continually tested against ground truth data where available. Neverthe-
537   less, we are implementing more sophisticated error control mechanisms to diminish the impact of
538   potential inaccuracies in the generated features. For example, we are in the process of hiring human
539   annotators to verify the output from the LLMs reasoning. Other possible options include developing
      confidence scoring systems for generated features (Detommaso et al., 2024).

      D      A BLATION S TUDY
811
812
      Do the inherent biases of LLMs influence the inference process of latent features? To assess
813
      whether the reasoning processes within generated texts exhibit biases, we conducted the following
814   experiments. First, we utilized the pretrained keyword extraction model YAKE (Campos et al., 2020)
815   to search for racial terms within the reasoning steps of the generated text. The analysis showed that
816   such keywords were absent, indicating no explicit racial bias in this context. Second, we closely
817   examined the race distribution in the ground-truth data versus the distribution in the predictions
818   made by the model. The analysis revealed that the race distributions between the ground-truth and
819   the predicted outcomes were similar. This similarity suggests that the model does not introduce
820   additional racial biases in its predictions and accurately reflects the distributions present in the input
821   data. Both results validate that the LLMs’ inherent biases are not carried into the inference process.
822
      Other types of bias, such as bias in lexical context, are beyond the scope of this paper and are left
      for future research.
823
824
825
      How sensitive is our approach to the quality of human guidelines? FLAME is sensitive to human
      guidelines, specifically the baseline rationales and prompt templates formulated in Step 1. We have
826
      conducted an ablation study to determine the optimal level of details required in the prompts. As
827
      shown in Figure 10 (b), the best performance was achieved with the most reasoning steps and a
828   sentence length of two per step. In other words, increasing the number of reasoning steps allows
829   us to decompose the task into simpler components and enhances the performance of LLMs. More
830   importantly, while human guidelines are important, the interactive self-revise alignment strategy
831   can significantly help during the sub-step of Step 1 (prompt crafting). By providing ground truth
832   and encouraging self-reflection, GPT-4 can revise the prompt template to include crucial details,
833   ensuring a more accurate evaluation.
834
835   How important is the fine-tuning step in FLAME ? We have conducted another ablation study,
836   where we repeated the risk-level prediction task with zero-shot, one-shot, and three-shot prompting
837   to compare with our fine-tuned model. In zero-shot, we provided only the task description. In one-
838   shot and three-shot, we included randomly selected human-verified examples. Accuracy rankings
839
      from lowest to highest were: three-shot (40%), zero-shot (55%), one-shot (60%), and the fine-tuned
      model (75%); see Figure 10 (a). The three-shot’s poor performance may be due to information
840
      loss from long inputs. Zero-shot responses are highly variable and not well-suited for downstream
841
      tasks. Although one-shot showed improvement, the fine-tuned model significantly outperformed all
842   others. Hence, the answer to the question is that fine-tuning is necessary. Additionally, the fine-
843   tuning process incorporates feedback loops with domain experts to adjust and correct the model’s
844   reasoning pathways, ensuring that the latent features inferred, such as the need for substance abuse
845   treatment, are aligned with nuanced real-world outcomes rather than broad statistical correlations.

      How does FLAME perform compared to the baseline?             We implemented a baseline approach

      F.1   I NCARCERATION - DIVERSION P ROGRAMS AND DATA D ESCRIPTION
951
952   Incarceration-diversion programs aim to support individuals who have committed minor offenses by
953   providing community-based services to improve societal reintegration and reduce recidivism. Eligi-
954   ble individuals were diverted from traditional incarceration to such programs after risk assessment
955   and screening. Case managers determined specific program requirements, such as substance use
956   treatment and cognitive-behavioral therapy. There are four types of program outcomes: Completed
957   (successfully completed the program), Revoked (committed new crimes while in the program), Not
958   Completed (unable to finish for various reasons), and Other (unrecorded reasons).
959   We obtained de-identified data from our community partner for a state-wide incarceration-diversion
960   program in Illinois. The consolidated dataset includes records of adult participants admitted to the
961   program. The collected data features include timestamps such as the arrival and termination dates to
962   the program, program outcomes, and individual features such as the race, gender, education, county,
963   marriage status, housing, risk assessment scores, prior crime history, and sources of referral (e.g.,
964   from probation officer or from the court).
