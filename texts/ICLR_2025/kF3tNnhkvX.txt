009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Learning from preferences has become a scalable paradigm for training high-
012            capacity language models, as it is not limited to human-produced data, allowing
013            models to surpass human performance. Advanced feedback learning algorithm
014            is typically online or iterative for high sample efficiency. Among these, iterative
015            preference optimization is popular due to its simplicity, efficiency, and robustness.
016            However, in iterative preference optimization, models do not necessarily achieve
017            optimal performance since they sequentially learn data from with different distri-
018            butions. A simple way to bridge the gap is model ensemble, which incurs exces-
019            sive inference costs. Inspired by the theoretical analysis for preference learning,
020
               we propose a simple model merging strategy that approximates model ensemble
               without additional training and inference costs, leading to Pareto-superior models.
021

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Large language models have acquired strong foundational capabilities and zero-shot performance on
027   held-out tasks thanks to language modeling (Radford et al., 2018; Devlin et al., 2019) and instruction
028   tuning (Wei et al., 2022a; Sanh et al., 2022). Despite these advancements, language models do not
029   naturally assign high probability on human-favorable reliable, safe, and helpful responses. Mean-
030   while, as high-quality text has been exhaustively crawled and language models have approached
031   human-level on many tasks, it is difficult for models to further learn from supervised learning (Tou-
032   vron et al., 2023; Burns et al., 2024). An emerging diagram is reinforcement learning from (human)
033
      feedback (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022), which does not rely on human to produce
      gold label but learns from human satisfaction or environment feedback, allowing the model to sur-
034
      pass human-level performance.
035
036   Traditionally, the policy is optimized by standard reinforcement learning algorithms such as proxi-
037   mal policy optimization (PPO; Schulman et al., 2017). Unfortunately, despite its strong performance
038   (Xu et al., 2024; Ivison et al., 2024), PPO is notorious for being resource-demanding. This is mainly
039
      caused by two factors: (i) PPO requires to generate completions from the present policy. However,
      to fit large language models to limited per-device memory, they are typically distributionally trained,
040
      which suffers low throughput for auto-regressive generation (Touvron et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024);
041
      (ii) PPO requires four models: policy model, reference model, reward model, and value model, to
042   be loaded simultaneously, which is memory intensive (Li et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024).
043
044   Recently, many economical alternatives have been proposed (Wu et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024),
045
      among which the most popular one is direct policy optimization (DPO; Rafailov et al., 2023). The
      design of DPO is based on the observation that each policy induces a reward model, where the policy
046
      is optimal under the reward model. To optimize the policy, it suffices to train the reward model. DPO
047
      is initially conducted on pre-collected fixed preference datasets, which struggle to cover the board
048   space of natural language (Dong et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) and can bring regression (Guo
049   et al., 2024). To bridge the gap, preference learning is implemented in an iterative way, where in
050   each iteration, completions are sampled from the latest checkpoint, annotated by the verifier, and
051   preference learning is conducted to produce the next checkpoint, leading to consistent improvement
052   (Dong et al., 2024). Iterative preference learning demonstrates remarkable performance and has
053   been applied to align the state-of-the-art open-weight models, such as Llama (Dubey et al., 2024)
      and Qwen (Yang et al., 2024).

054
      In iterative DPO, the reward model sequentially learns preference data sampled from different poli-
055   cies, which are not independent and identically distributed as in the standard supervised learning
056   (Vapnik, 2013). We hypothesize that such training may deprive the performance of the final reward
057   model and policy. A simple way to mitigate the loss is through model ensemble, which blends the
058   token distributions of multiple language models at inference time (Mitchell et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
059   2024). However, this method incurs an inference cost that increases linearly with the number of
060   models involved. Fortunately, inspired by the theoretical analysis of preference learning, weight av-
061   eraging between the reference policy and iterative DPO checkpoints can approximate the ensemble
062   model (section 3.1). On this basis, we propose a simple yet effective merging strategy, where two
063
      hyper-parameters control the magnitude and direction of the enumerated ensemble reward model,
      respectively (section 3.2). Despite the highly nonlinear nature of deep neural networks, the ap-
064
      proximation works surprisingly well (section 4.1). We apply the merging strategy to the iterative
065
      preference learning of two advanced open-weight models, i.e., Llama-3 (Dubey et al., 2024) and
066   Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024), leading to models with better foundational capabilities and alignment
067   (section 4.2). We also observe that the merged models induce more accurate reward models, provid-
068   ing evidence for our hypothesis (section 4.3).

076

122   3     M ETHODOLOGY
123
124   Model merging has been applied in the preference learning of the state-of-the-art open-weight large
125   language models (Dubey et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024), whereas existing investigations are primar-
126   ily limited to the offline setting (Lu et al., 2024) and often leads to a trade-off between the foun-
127   dational capability and alignment. To bridge the gap, we extend model merging to more advanced
128
      iterative learning and obtain Pareto-superior models.

238   4         E XPERIMENTS
239
240
      We have established the theoretical foundation of model merging in iterative preference learning. In
241   this section, we conduct empirical evaluations to (i) demonstrate the effectiveness of using model
242   merging to control the magnitude and direction of the induced reward model; (ii) apply the proposed
243   strategy to improve model aligned by iterative preference optimization; (iii) illustrate that model
244   merging leads to a more accurate induced reward model; (iv) evaluate whether the proposed strategy
245   is applicable to iterative post-training algorithms beyond the scope of section 3.1.
246
247   4.1                P ROOF OF C ONCEPT
248
249   In section 3.1, we discuss the mechanism of model merging as approximation of the optimal policy
250   under the combination of reward models, with the core tool being Taylor approximation. Due to the
251   highly non-linear nature of deep neural networks, the effectiveness of this approximation is unclear.
252   In sections 4.1.1 and 4.1.2, we show the empirical results of adjusting the magnitude and direction of
253
      the induced reward model, respectively. We observe that the induced rewards of completions sam-
      pled from the merged models exhibit a strong linear correlation with the merging hyper-parameters,
254
      supporting the effectiveness of the estimation eq. (7).

      5   R ELATED W ORK
453
454
      Model Merging Model merging aims to integrate several models fine-tuned from the same base
455
      model so that the subsequent model possesses their respective abilities (Matena & Raffel, 2022;
456   Ilharco et al., 2023; Goddard et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024). Recently, model merging is applied
457   to boost models learn from preferences. ExPO (Zheng et al., 2024) hypothesize that an aligned
458   model is the interpolated outcome of the SFT model and a better-aligned model. Building upon the
459   assumption, a better-aligned model can be obtained by extrapolating from the weights of the SFT
460   and aligned models. ExPO is a special case of alg. 2 that scales α with λ = 0, which amplifies
461   the alignment signal solely without refining the direction of the induced reward model. Instead
462   of merging the checkpoints of standard preference learning, online merging optimizer (OMO; Lu
463   et al., 2024) integrates model merging into the training process, balancing instruction-following and
464
      alignment capabilities at each optimization step. OMO achieves more fine-grained merging while
      introducing additional training costs.
465
466
      Contrastive Decoding Another line of research applies contrastive decoding (Li et al., 2023; Liu
467
      et al., 2021) to the SFT and aligned models following eq. (5). Emulated fine-tuning (EFT; Mitchell
468
      et al., 2024) simulates the model trained under the weighted combination of two induced reward
469   models by mixing the vocabulary logits of the aligned models. DeRA (Liu et al., 2024) approximates
470   the policy aligned with a different KL divergence coefficient β by mixing the vocabulary logits of
471   the reference and aligned policy, which can be regarded as a special case of EFT where the reference
472   policy induces a zero reward model. Compared to model merging, re-alignment at the decoding time
473   brings additional inference costs.

      6   C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In iterative preference optimization, the induced reward model sequentially learns from distribution-
478
      shifting data, which may deprive the performance of the final model. A straight-forward remedy
479
      is reward model ensemble, which leads to contrastive decoding of multiple language models and
480   prohibitively expensive memory consumption that exceeds the device capacity. Fortunately, simple
481   weight averaging can be used to approximate the optimal policy under the linear combination of
482   induced reward models without incurring additional training and inference cost. Despite the highly
483   nonlinear nature of deep neural networks, the approximation works remarkably well. On the basis,
484   we propose a simple merging strategy, using two hyper-parameters to govern the magnitude and
485   direction of the ensemble reward model. The strategy is applied to the iterative preference opti-
      mization of two advanced open-weight models, i.e., Llama-3 and Qwen2, leading to simultaneous


                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      improvements in the foundational capabilities and alignment. The merged model also induces a
487   more accurate reward model, providing evidence for our hypothesis.
488
489   Limitation & Future Work The limitation of this work mainly lies in the scope. First, although
490   many iterative preference learning algorithms use DPO as a baseline and report the performance,
491   the checkpoints of iterative DPO are not publicly available, making our evaluations limited to our
492   configuration rather than boarder data, models, and hyper-parameters. We call for greater open-
493   sourced efforts to enhance the trasparency and accessibility of large language models. Second,
494   the proposed merging mechanism and strategy are restricted to iterative DPO and requires a fixed
495   reference policy. It remains an open problem to extend to other post-training algorithms. Third, our
496   merging strategy is limited to simple parameter averaging without sparsification. Future works may
497
      devise more sophisticated strategies to achieve a better performance.
