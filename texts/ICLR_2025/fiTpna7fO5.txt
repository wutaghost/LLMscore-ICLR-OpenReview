009                                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011            We show that we can easily design a single adversarial perturbation P that changes
012            the class of n images X1 , X2 , . . . , Xn from their original, unperturbed classes
013            c1 , c2 , . . . , cn to desired (not necessarily all the same) classes c∗1 , c∗2 , . . . , c∗n for
014            up to hundreds of images and target classes at once. We call these multi-attacks.
015            Characterizing the maximum n we can achieve under different conditions such
016            as image resolution, we estimate the number of regions of high class confidence
017            around a particular image in the space of pixels to be around 10O(100) , posing a
018
               significant problem for exhaustive defense strategies. We show several immediate
               consequences of this: adversarial attacks that change the resulting class based
019
               on their intensity, and scale-independent adversarial examples. To demonstrate
020
               the redundancy and richness of class decision in the pixel space, we look for its
021            two-dimensional sections that trace images and spell words using particular classes.
022            We also show that ensembling reduces susceptibility to multi-attacks, and that
023            classifiers trained on random labels are more susceptible.

046   1    I NTRODUCTION
047
048   The problem of adversarial examples is typically framed in the classification setting (Szegedy
049   et al., 2013), where a small, specifically tailored adversarial perturbation P to the input data X
050   changes the classification decision from a class c, c = argmax f (X), to a different class c∗ , c∗ =
051   argmax f (X + P ), while the magnitude of P (typically measured by its L2 or L∞ norms) remains
052   very small compared to X. In other words, the classification decision about an input image X is
053   changed from one class to a confident prediction of another class (chosen by the attacker) by a
      small, often human-imperceptible change P to the image X. This problem is omnipresent in image

054
      classification, starting from small models and data (Szegedy et al., 2013), all the way to current large
055   models such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), as described for example in (Fort, 2021). Adversarial
056   vulnerability applies not only to the classification setting but is a prominent issue in other domains, e.g.
057   for near out-of-distribution detection (Chen et al., 2021; Fort, 2022). Despite the tremendous success
058   of deep learning, the persistent presence of adversarial vulnerabilities points towards a potentially
059   deeply ingrained problem with the approach.
060
      In this paper, we investigate the limits of adversarial attacks. The key question we are addressing is
061   whether multiple attacks can be carried out by the same adversarial perturbation. For n images, e.g.
062   an image X1 of a cat, an image X2 of a tortoise, and an image X3 of a house, can we can design a
063   single change P to all n images at once such that X1 + P is classified as e.g. a tree, X2 + P is a
064   plane and X3 + P is a dinosaur?
065
      Our key contribution is to show that such attacks do exist and are in fact easy to find. We call these
066
      multi-attacks. Multi-attacks are easy to find using standard methods, and the higher the resolution
067   of the image, the more images we can attack at the same time. We demonstrate that models trained
068   on randomly permuted labels are more susceptible to multi-attacks, and that ensembling multiple
069   models decreases their susceptibility to multi-attacks. Using a simple toy model theory, we estimate
070   the number of distinct class regions around each image in the space of pixels to be 10O(100) , which
071   poses a major challenge to any adversarial defence strategies that rely on exhaustion. To show the
072   flexibility and richness provided by the 10O(100) class regions around each image in the space of
073   pixels, we show that we can easily find two-dimensional sections of the input space that show images
074   and spell words in arbitrary classes.

076   2     M ETHOD
077
078   For a classifier f : X → y, n inputs X1 , X2 , . . . , Xn , and n target classes c∗1 , c∗2 , . . . , c∗n , the goal is to
079   produce an adversarial perturbation P such that argmax f (X1 + P ) = c∗1 , argmax f (X2 + P ) = c∗2 ,
080   . . . argmax f (Xn + P ) = c∗n . A standard adversarial attack takes a single image X and finds a
081   single perturbation P such that X + P is misclassified as the target class the attacker chose. In this
082   paper, we are looking for single perturbation P that is simultaneously capable of changing many
083   images to many distinct classes. We call these perturbations multi-attacks.
084

237
238    We trained 10 independently initialized SimpleCNN models on CIFAR-10 and developed multi-
239    attacks against ensembles of subsets of them (averaging their output logits for a given input) at the

244    which is consistent with a broad trend of ensembling increasing out-of-distribution (Fort, 2022) and

304         4.6                                               L ONG LINES OF ADVERSARIES
305
306      The very high number of distinct high-confidence regions surrounding each image allows for si-
307      multaneous satisfaction of many constrains. A particularly interesting situation is to attack a series
308      of images that all derive from the same original, X0 , but to which the adversarial perturbation is
309
         progressively applied with an increasing multiplicative factor.
310                                                                                   (X0 , P, m) : (X0 + P, X0 + 2P, X0 + 3P, . . . , X0 + mP ) .                                                                                          (5)
311       In a geometric sense, we are creating a straight line through the pixel space, starting at an image
312       X0 and gradually, in integer steps of P , moving in the P̂ direction. The ith image in the line is
313       X0 + iP and we can optimize P such that it gets mapped to a desired class c∗i of our choosing,
314       argmax f (X0 + iP ) = c∗i . These target classes can be arbitrary, or we can choose them all to be the
315       same target class. If we choose them to be the same, we would have identified a direction P in which
316       the scale of the adversarial attack preserves its function as discussed in Section 4.7
317
          Figure 8 demonstrates an attack, P , that starts at an image, X, of an airplane and gradually changes
318       the classification to classes 111, 222, 333, 444, and 555 for X + P , X + 2P , X + 3P , X + 4P ,
319       and X + 5P respectively. The class decision in between the integer values of the αP is filled with
320       the respective classes, showing that the attack is not fragile to small changes of α outside of the
321       directly optimized for integer multiples. The attack shown is to a 32 × 32 image, and therefore the
322       magnitude of the perturbation is large. Were we to use 224 × 224, the perturbation would be much
323       less prominent, as discussed in Section 4.1. Figure 9 shows a second case of such a line of adversaries,
          this time at a higher resolution and lasting for 9 steps.

      4.7       S CALE - INDEPENDENT ATTACKS
353
354   A particular consequence of being able to find lines of adversarial attacks is that we can find such
355   lines where the target class does not change with the scale α of the attack αP . Figure 10 shows the
356   result of such an attack. A picture of a bird is attacked with a perturbation P that is optimized such
357   that X + P, X + 2P, . . . , X + 60P are all classified as class 111 (nematode). The right panel of
358   Figure 10 shows that the class decision holds in between the integer values of α, and also that, while
359   only directly optimized up to X + 60P , the predicted class stays at 111 all the way to X + 160P ,
360   showing an amount of generalization of this scale-independent attack.
361
362   4.8       F INDING SHAPES IN THE PIXEL SPACE
363
364   Using the very large number of regions surrounding each image in the pixel space, we decided
365   to optimize for pairs of attacks Px and Py that, together with a starting image X0 , define a two-
366   dimensional affine space (a ”plane”) in the pixel space. This is related to the cutting-plane method
367   used in (Fort et al., 2022), however, there the bases are randomly chosen (and as such O(10) are
368
      needed to find high-confidence adversarial attacks). Here, we optimize for Px and Py such that
      the affine subspace they trace out in the space of pixels spells out words and draws images of our
369
      choosing, demonstrating the flexibility of the input-space partitioning of deep neural networks.
370
371   (Skorokhodov & Burtsev, 2019) shows that in the space of weights and biases of deep neural networks
372   (also known as the loss landscape), there exists a vast richness of two-dimensional sections where the
373   loss value traces various shapes and images, and where we can optimize for finding particular ones,
374
      such as the shape of a bat, a skull, a cow, or the planet Saturn. (Czarnecki et al., 2020) extends this
      work and show that this a general property of sufficiently large and deep neural networks.
375
376   Inspired by this experiment and given the rich nature of the class boundaries in the space of pixels
377   that we demonstrated by creating multi-attacks on many images and towards many labels at once, we
      find similarly suggestive shapes in the space of images and their classification decisions.

423   5     D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
424
425   In this work, we have demonstrated the existence of multi-attacks – we show that we can take a
426   large number of images (e.g. > 100 for images of the 224 × 224 resolution and an ImageNet-trained
427   classifier) and optimize for a single adversarial perturbation P that we call a multi-attack and that,
428   when added to each of the images, makes them misclassified as arbitrarily chosen target classes.
429   Given this, we estimate the number of distinct high-confidence class regions in the pixel space around
430   every image to be approximately 10O(100) using a simple toy theory, which links the maximum
431   number of simultaneously attackable images nmax and the number of classes C to the number of
      regions N as N ≈ exp (nmax log(C)).


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Exploiting this flexibility, we show that we can find two-dimensional sections of the pixel space that
433   trace words and images in whatever class we choose. This can be understood as a consequence of
434   the sheer number of two-dimensional patterns that hide in a high-dimensional space partitioned into
435   10O(100) cells reachable from each point. We can also find adversarial attacks αP that change the
436   image attacked to a different class based on their strength α and that can change them to the same
437   class regardless of the scale α for a range of αs.
438
      We show that classifiers trained on randomly assigned labels are easier to design multi-attacks against
439
      as compared to classifiers trained on labels that are semantically connected to their corresponding
440   images. Interestingly, whether we are modifying real images or samples of noise does not seem to
441   have any effect on how easy it is to design a multi-attack.
442
443
      Every strategy designed to defend against adversarial attacks has to deal with this issue: around each
      image there are very many, e.g. 10100 neighboring images that, to a human, differ only slightly from
444
      the original image, and yet are classified very different by a learned classifier. Making sure that
445
      each of these gets classified correctly is a difficult task. For example, it is virtually impossible to
446   add all of them to the training set with the correct (to a human) label, as some strategies attempt to
447   do. Unless we can somehow do this exponentially effectively, defending against attacks might be a
448   very hard task. The key problem is the dimensionality of the input space and how small a cell each
449   high-confidence region seems to be.
450
      Our findings open several directions for future work. More rigorous theory is needed to tightly
451
      characterize the scale of this redundancy. Study of failure cases and images resistant to multi-
452   attacks could inspire new defensive techniques. And development of fast algorithms to find minimal
453   multi-attacks could enable applications.
454
455
      By exposing the scale of redundancy in neural network classifiers, we hope these results will inspire
      further investigation – both of the root causes of this extreme flexibility, and of potential solutions to
456
      make models more robust.

      A.1    I NDEPENDENT IMAGES VS NOISE PERTURBATIONS OF A SINGLE IMAGE
562
563   We wanted to understand at what scale of a noise perturbation n do noisy versions of the same image
564   X0 act as independent in the sense that we can optimize each realization of the noise added to the
565   image towards a different class. Instead of independent starting images X1 , X2 , . . . Xn , we take the
566   same image and add different realizations of noise drawn from a Gaussian distribution with 0 mean
567   and standard deviation σ as Xi = X0 + si for si ∼ N (0, σ).
568   For a small perturbation σ, the resulting images are essentially the same and we therefore cannot
569   change them 64 different classes with a single perturbation. However, if σ is sufficiently large,
570   the 64 images effectively behave as if they were 64 random, distinct images and we are be able to
571   convert each of them to a different class with a multi-attack. In Figure 12 we show the fraction of
572   the images we were able to attack successfully after 20 optimization steps starting from X with
573   different realizations of the noise, divided by the number we reach starting from 64 random images.
574   For σ ≈ 3 × 10−1 we reach parity. In other words, taking a single image X and adding a normal
575
      noise from N (0, 3 × 10−1 ) effectively renders the perturbed images independent for the purposes
      of simultaneous adversarial multi-attacks. Unfortunately, on the left panel of Figure 12 we also
576
      show that by that point, the images are so noisy that the probability in the original argmax class
577
      drops to effectively zero. These effects seem to be independent of the image resolution. (Fort et al.,
578   2021) generates multiple differently augmented copies of the same image within a batch and finds
579   that this increases the speed of training as well as the final accuracy on several image classification
580   benchmarks, which is a related idea.
