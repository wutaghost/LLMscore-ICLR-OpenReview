009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Data selection is crucial for optimizing language model (LM) performance on
012            specific tasks, yet most existing methods fail to effectively consider the target task
013            distribution. Current approaches either ignore task-specific requirements entirely
014            or rely on approximations that fail to capture the nuanced patterns needed for tasks
015            like Autoformalization or code generation. Methods that do consider the target
016
               distribution often rely on simplistic, sometimes noisy, representations, like hashed
               n-gram features, which can lead to collisions and introduce noise. We introduce
017
               ZIP-FIT, a data selection framework that uses gzip compression to directly
018
               measure alignment between potential training data and the target task distribu-
019            tion. Our key insight is that compression-based similarity captures both syntactic
020            and structural patterns relevant to the target task, enabling more precise selec-
021            tion of truly task-relevant data. In extensive evaluations on Autoformalization and
022            Python code generation, ZIP-FIT significantly outperforms leading baselines
023            like DSIR and D4. Models trained on ZIP-FIT-selected data achieve their low-
024            est cross-entropy loss up to 85.1% faster than baselines, demonstrating that better
025            task alignment leads to more efficient learning. In addition, ZIP-FIT performs
026            selection up to 65.8% faster than DSIR and two orders of magnitude faster than
027
               D4. Notably, ZIP-FIT shows that smaller, well-aligned datasets often outper-
               form larger but less targeted ones, demonstrating that a small amount of higher
028
               quality data is superior to a large amount of lower quality data. Our results im-
029
               ply that task-aware data selection is crucial for efficient domain adaptation, and
030            that compression offers a principled way to measure task alignment. By showing
031            that targeted data selection can dramatically improve task-specific performance,
032            our work provides new insights into the relationship between data quality, task
033            alignment, and model learning efficiency.

053   relevance, embedding-based methods are computationally expensive and sensitive to the choice of

      2.1   BACKGROUND
163
164   gzip compression: uses two main techniques for compression, LZ77 and Huffman coding. To-
165   gether, these methods compress sequences by exploiting repeated patterns in the data. LZ77 works
166   by identifying repeated substrings and replacing them with references to their earlier occurrences.
167   Huffman coding further compresses the data by assigning shorter binary codes to more frequent
168   symbols, optimizing the overall length of the compressed text. For more details, see Appendix A.
169   AutoFormalization (AF): refers to the task of translating natural language mathematical statements
170   into a formal mathematical programming language, like Lean4 Moura et al. (2015). This process
171   requires precise understanding and representation of mathematical formal syntax, making the selec-
172   tion of well-aligned training data crucial for effective model training.

353   7   R ELATED W ORKS
354
355   Curating pre-training data often involves using classifiers to filter high-quality data from large cor-
356   pora like Common Crawl, as done for models like GPT-3 and PaLM2 (Brown et al., 2020; Google,
357   2023; Shao et al., 2024). While effective, this process requires significant computational resources
358   and large volumes of curated data. In contrast, ZIP-FIT efficiently selects relevant data without
359   relying on external models, making it especially useful in data-scarce environments.
360   Deduplication techniques, such as SemDeDup (Abbas et al., 2023) and D4 (Tirumala et al., 2023),
361   improve data efficiency by removing duplicate or semantically similar examples using embedding-
362   based clustering. However, these methods are computationally expensive and not tuned to the target
363   task. ZIP-FIT is embedding-free and task-aware, making it both scalable and more effective at
364   selecting relevant data.
365
      Mixture weights are essential when drawing from multiple domains, as they influence the perfor-
366   mance of language models (Du et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023b). DoReMi (Domain Reweighting
367   with Minimax Optimization) (Xie et al., 2023a) proposes a reweighting strategy suitable for han-
368   dling diverse target distributions, but it primarily focuses on adjusting weights at the domain level.
369   Adapting it to select individual data points for specific target distributions would require substantial
370   modifications to its foundational algorithm. One potential approach would be to effectively trans-
371   form each data point into a ’mini-domain,’ a process that would stray significantly from DoReMi’s
372   original purpose and scope. Therefore, we did not use DoReMi in our comparisons because it does
373   not directly address the fine-grained selection needs that ZIP-FIT fulfills.
374   Autoformalization refers to the process of translating natural language mathematics into formal
375   language (Wang et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2022), which is advantageous because formal proofs can be
376   verified for correctness. However, the ability of current models to autoformalize text is limited by
377   the scarcity of human-curated formal data. ZIP-FIT provides a framework for selecting the most
      relevant data, ensuring that models are trained on aligned datasets that enhance their performance.

      8   L IMITATIONS
408
409
      While ZIP-FIT provides a computationally efficient method for data selection, it has several lim-
410   itations. First, the gzip compression-based alignment may not fully capture nuanced semantic
411   relationships that dense representations can, potentially affecting its effectiveness for complex do-
412   mains like natural language understanding, where paraphrasing is important. Second, ZIP-FIT’s
413   reliance on gzip means that its performance could vary depending on the nature of the textual data,
414   particularly in highly diverse datasets where compression gains are less apparent.

      9   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
417
418
      ZIP-FIT introduces an efficient, embedding-free approach for data selection in language model
419
      fine-tuning. By leveraging compression algorithms to capture redundancies in data, ZIP-FIT en-
420   ables the alignment of large-scale datasets with a target domain without the computational burden
421   of neural embeddings. Our experiments with different compression algorithms (Figure 8) reveal
422   that lighter compression (e.g., LZ4 at level 0) leads to better performance, achieving a 12.19%
423   Pass@1 on HumanEval compared to 11.58% with gzip. This suggests that while compression effec-
424   tively captures alignment signals, aggressive compression can remove subtle but important patterns.
425   These insights highlight the importance of careful selection of compression parameters in optimiz-
426   ing the quality of data selection. Our results show that using compression-based alignment leads to
427   faster convergence and lower cross-entropy loss compared to existing methods like DSIR and D4
428
      (Tirumala et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2023b).
429   However, this approach highlights the trade-off between simplicity and the ability to capture com-
430   plex semantic relationships. While compression-based methods offer a lightweight alternative, they
431   might not fully replace embedding-based techniques for highly intricate domains, such as natu-
      ral language understanding or paraphrases. Nonetheless, ZIP-FIT’s promising results suggest

      performance (Miranda et al., 2024). Therefore, we aim to address this in future work by either: (1)
450
      developing an algorithm that balances diversity with alignment in data selection, or (2) creating a
451   metric that incorporates diversity as part of its evaluation process.

      A     G Z I P C OMPRESSION D ETAILS
649
650
      gzip is a lossless data compression algorithm that combines two primary techniques: LZ77 com-
651   pression and Huffman coding. Here, we provide additional technical details on how gzip works.
652
653   LZ77 Compression: LZ77 works by identifying repeated substrings in the input text and replacing
654   them with backward references. Mathematically, LZ77 can be described as follows:
655
656
      Given an input sequence S = s1 , s2 , . . . , sn , the algorithm searches for the longest prefix of the
      remaining sequence S ′ = si , si+1 , . . . , sn that matches a substring within a predefined window of
657
      previous characters. If a match is found, it is replaced by a tuple (d, l, c), where:
658
659            • d is the distance from the current position to the start of the matching substring,
660
               • l is the length of the matching substring, and
661
662            • c is the character following the match (if any).
663
      For example, the substring si , si+1 , . . . , si+l−1 can be replaced by the tuple (d, l, c), thereby reduc-
664
      ing redundancy in the data.
665
666
      Huffman Coding: After applying LZ77, gzip employs Huffman coding to further reduce the
667
      size of the compressed data. Huffman coding assigns variable-length codes to symbols based on
668
      their frequency of occurrence, with shorter codes assigned to more frequent symbols.
669
670   The expected length L(X) of the Huffman code for a sequence of symbols X = x1 , x2 , . . . , xn is
671   calculated as:
                                             Xn
672                                  L(X) =     p(xi ) · len(C(xi )),
673                                                     i=1
674   where:
675
676            • p(xi ) is the probability of symbol xi ,
677            • len(C(xi )) is the length of the Huffman code for xi .
678
679   This further minimizes the size of the compressed data by leveraging the statistical properties of the
680   input.
681
682   Combined gzip Compression: The total compressed size C(S) after applying both LZ77 and
683   Huffman coding can be approximated as the sum of the lengths of the backward references and the
684   Huffman-coded symbols:
685                                            X                         n
                                                                         X
686                                  C(S) =             len(d, l, c) +         len(C(xi )).
687                                           (d,l,c)                    i=1
688
689   Normalized Compression Distance (NCD): gzip’s effectiveness in data selection stems from
690   its ability to measure the alignment between two sequences A and B based on how efficiently they
691   compress together. The Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) is given by:
692
                                                    C(A ⊕ B) − min(C(A), C(B))
693                              N CD(A, B) =                                  ,
694
                                                         max(C(A), C(B))
695   where C(A) and C(B) are the compressed lengths of sequences A and B, and C(A ⊕ B) is the
696   length of the compressed concatenation of both sequences. A lower NCD indicates greater alignment
697   between the sequences.

699   A.1    W HY U SE C OMPRESSION ?
700
701   Compression algorithms, such as gzip, provide a computationally efficient way to detect patterns
      and minimize redundancy in data.

      Limitations of n-grams: Many traditional methods, including hashed n-grams, focus on capturing
703   immediate textual correlations by simplifying text into discrete, fixed-size buckets. Although these
704   techniques are computationally efficient, they may not adequately capture syntactic or structural
705   relationships within the data. Additionally, the introduce noise due to collisions during hashing.
706
707   Challenges with Neural Embeddings: Neural embeddings offer a powerful tool for capturing
708   semantic relationships, but they come with significant computational costs. These embeddings are
709   typically pre-trained on large corpora and fine-tuned for specific tasks, which requires substantial re-
710   sources. Given the scalability challenges of embedding-based methods, we conjecture that a simpler
711   method like compression can provide a more scalable and resource-efficient alternative.
712   We hypothesize that compression – in this case gzip, but perhaps a different compression algorithm
713   –serves as a strong proxy for capturing syntactic and structural relationships in textual sequences.
714   gzip’s ability to compress data based on redundancy minimization can be leveraged as a metric to
715   align text with a target distribution.

       Lossless Compression for Alignment: While ZIP-FIT has demonstrated substantial efficiency
       for data selection, there are several promising directions for future exploration. One potential en-
1114
       hancement is leveraging faster compression algorithms, such as LZ4 and Snappy, which offer rapid
1115
       processing speeds at the cost of lossy compression. In our current approach, we utilize gzip for
1116   compression-based alignment, which is lossless and provides a robust foundation. However, LZ4
1117   and Snappy are optimized for speed and could potentially offer even greater computational effi-
1118   ciency without the need for decompression in our pipeline. Given that our primary goal is efficient
1119   data selection rather than perfect data recovery, these faster algorithms might be more suitable.

       Autonomous Validation Set Generation: A current limitation of ZIP-FIT is its dependence on a

1122   test set). Future work could explore the use of generative models to create synthetic validation
1123   sets from task-specific instructions. This approach could also be expanded to enable autonomous
1124   self-directed, model-driven generation of validation data.
