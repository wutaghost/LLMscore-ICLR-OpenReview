011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Understanding dense visual semantics remains a fundamental challenge in com-
014            puter vision, as semantically similar objects can exhibit drastically different visual
015
               appearances. Recent advancements in generative text-to-image frameworks have
               led to models that implicitly capture natural scene statistics. These models learn
016
               to model complex relationships between objects, lighting, and other visual fac-
017
               tors, enabling the generation of detailed and contextually rich images from text
018            captions. To advance visual semantic understanding and develop more robust and
019            interpretable vision models, we present StableSemantics, a large-scale dataset
020            composed of 224 thousand human-curated prompts, processed natural language
021            captions, over 2 million synthetic images, and 10 million attention maps. The
022            dataset provides fine-grained semantic attributions at the noun-chunk level, lever-
023            ages human-generated prompts that correspond to visually interesting stable dif-
024            fusion generations, and provides 10 generations per phrase, with cross-attention
025            maps corresponding to noun chunks for each image. We explore the semantic
026
               distribution of generated images, examine the distribution of objects within images,
               and benchmark captioning and open vocabulary segmentation methods on our data.
027
               As the first diffusion dataset to include dense attention attributions, we expect
028
               StableSemantics to catalyze advances in visual semantic understanding and provide
029            a foundation for developing more sophisticated and effective visual models.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Dense visual scene understanding is a complex task that requires the integration of cues, context,
035   and prior knowledge to navigate the inherent variability and complexity of the visual world. This
036   complexity is particularly evident when considering the diversity of visual appearances that can
037   correspond to a single semantic concept. For instance, entities that correspond to “man-made
038   structures” can have vastly different visual appearances, ranging from sleek skyscrapers to rustic
039
      cottages. Similarly, objects that serve the same purpose, such as “containers,” can have diverse
      shapes, sizes, and materials. This disconnect between semantic meaning and visual appearance poses
040
      a significant challenge for computer vision systems (Brust & Denzler, 2018; Duan & Kuo, 2021;
041
      Alqasrawi, 2016; Barz & Denzler, 2020), requiring the disentanglement of the underlying semantic
042   structure from visual differences (Caron et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023; Elharrouss et al., 2021; VS
043   et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2023; Quinn et al., 2017). To overcome this challenge, recent advances have
044   adopted data-driven approaches, which learn to recognize patterns and relationships in large datasets
045   of images and annotations. However, the reliance on large datasets of images and annotations poses a
046   significant challenge in the development of segmentation models. Acquiring and annotating such
047   datasets can be a time-consuming and resource-intensive process, requiring careful consideration of
048   data quality and diversity.
049   This limitation has sparked interest in exploring alternative approaches that can reduce the need for
050   large human-annotated datasets. One promising direction is the use of generative models, which have
051   shown impressive results in translating between semantic meaning and visual appearance (Rombach
052   et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2022). In particular, diffusion-based text-
053   to-image synthesis models have demonstrated an impressive ability to generate highly realistic images
      from textual descriptions, suggesting that these models must possess an implicit understanding of the

085   semantic structure of the visual world, and have learned to associate words and phrases with specific
086   visual concepts. By leveraging cross-attention mechanisms, these models learn to link textual input to
087   visual representations and enable the generation of images that are grounded in the semantic content
088   of the input text (Tang et al., 2022). This in turn has led to generative media proliferating over social
089   media, advertisements, and news media. Although generated images may seem indistinguishable
090   from real images to the human eye, neural networks can still differentiate between them (Huang et al.,
091   2024; Aziz et al., 2024; You et al., 2024). This suggests that generated images are still quite different
092   from real images and that generative models are still far from perfect in accurately replicating the
093
      real data distribution (You et al., 2024). This demonstrates that a very clear gap still exists between
      generated and real data. Thus, there is a strong need for the development of more advanced generated
094
      data to help models perform well on synthetically generated images and to address the difficulty of
095
      gathering large-scale real data. Specifically, there are no existing large-scale generated datasets that
096   contribute semantic maps in addition to images and their corresponding captions.
097
098   In this work, we introduce StableSemantics, a dataset that consists of human-generated and curated
099
      prompts, natural language captions, images generated from the captions, and attention attribution
      maps corresponding to objects in the captions. Unlike prior work which sourced unfiltered human-
100
      generated prompts, we source our prompts from a pool of images that have been evaluated by humans
101
      for their visual appeal and interest, resulting in a dataset that mirrors the types of images people
102   find engaging. As the original prompts may not always reflect natural language, we employ a large
103   language model to paraphrase and refine them into fluent and natural-sounding captions, thereby
104   bridging the gap between human-generated prompts filtered for visual appeal and naturalistic language.
105   Each natural language caption is provided to a Stable Diffusion XL model to generate high-resolution
106   and reproducible images. Finally, we explicitly record the dense text-to-image cross-attention maps
107   used to condition the image generation process. We visualize the distribution of semantics across
      images, evaluate the spatial distribution of semantic classes within images, and evaluate the alignment

      of current captioning and open-set segmentation models on our dataset. To our knowledge, our dataset

111   such as object detection, semantic segmentation, semantically meaningful representation learning,

114   2 R ELATED WORK
115   Natural Scene Statistics. Natural image statistics have been a long-standing area of research
116   in computer vision and neuroscience. The human visual system is thought to be adapted to the
117   statistical properties of natural images, which are characterized by complex dependencies between
118   pixels (Girshick et al., 2011; van der Schaaf & van Hateren, 1996). The power law distribution of
119
      gradient magnitude statistics is thought to be a result of the hierarchical, self-similar structure of
      natural images, which arises from the presence of edges, textures, and other features at multiple
120
      scales. Understanding natural image statistics has important implications for image recognition tasks
121
      (Zoran, 2013; Heiler & Schnörr, 2005; Fang et al., 2012), and has inspired the development of a range
122   of algorithms and models that are tailored to the statistical properties of natural images (Mechrez
123   et al., 2019; Kleinlein et al., 2022; Hepburn et al., 2023; Xiang et al., 2024; Hepburn et al., 2021;
124   Talbot et al., 2023). Other work has also explored the semantic structure of visual data, seeking to
125   understand how higher-level categories and concepts are reflected in the statistical patterns present in
126   images. This work has shown that different categories of images, such as scenes and objects, exhibit
127   distinct statistical patterns (Torralba & Oliva, 2003; Henderson et al., 2023). These semantic statistics
128   have important implications for the development of models that can effectively represent and analyze
129   visual data.
130   Deep Image Generative Models. Recent progress on generative models has enabled the generation
131   of images, video, text, and audio (Podell et al., 2023; Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2022; Gupta et al.,
132
      2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Evans et al., 2024). Models rely on a variety of different mathematical
      assumptions and architectures. Variational autoencoders (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Luhman &
133
      Luhman, 2023; Harvey et al., 2021; Razavi et al., 2019; Van Den Oord et al., 2017) and flow-based
134
      models (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015; Tong et al., 2023; Dinh et al., 2014; Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018),
135   while highly efficient, tend to produce lower-quality samples. Generative Adversarial Networks
136   (GANs) can yield high-fidelity samples but may neglect modes in the data and can exhibit unstable
137   training dynamics (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Karras et al., 2019; Brock et al., 2018; Mirza & Osindero,
138   2014; Zawar et al., 2022). Auto-regressive methods (Huang et al., 2023; Parmar et al., 2018; Lee
139   et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022), although capable of producing high-quality samples, typically
140   experience slow sampling. Recent progress in energy/score/diffusion models has given us methods
141   that are simultaneously stable during training and yield high-quality samples (Rombach et al., 2022;
142   Ramesh et al., 2022).
143   Visual Datasets. Deep learning models have achieved remarkable results by leveraging vast
144   amounts of data. There has been a significant push to collect large-scale datasets. Earlier works such
145   as LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), Flickr Caption (Young et al., 2014), RedCaps (Desai et al.,
146
      2021) and YFCC100M (Thomee et al., 2016) scrape real-world data of image-caption pairs from
      web sources. COCO (Lin et al., 2014) goes a step further to also provide pixel-level segmentation
147
      masks on top of the image-caption pairs. (Agrawal et al., 2015; Goyal et al., 2016; Marino et al.,
148
      2019; Wang et al., 2018; Krishna et al., 2017) introduce datasets specifically for the task of VQA.
149   Given the difficulty of collecting real data, recently there has been a shift towards synthetic datasets.
150   StableRep (Tian et al., 2024) and Hammoud et al. (2024) also demonstrated the usefulness of Stable
151   Diffusion images in training contrastive image models. Pick-a-Pic (Kirstain et al., 2023) provides a
152   dataset of image-caption pairs where each sample contains a pair of diffusion-generated images and
153   the human preference between those images. JourneyDB (Sun et al., 2023) and DiffusionDB (Wang
154   et al., 2022b) are the closest works to ours that release large-scale datasets of synthetic image-caption
155   pairs.

183   3.1        C OLLECTING HUMAN CURATED PROMPTS
184
185
      Our dataset is collected from the Stable Diffusion discord server, where users can publicly submit
186
      prompts to generate images using a discord bot. After users submitted prompts using the /dream
187   command, the bot would return images corresponding to a prompt. Beyond accepting a prompt, users
188   could also submit negative prompts, and image styles which were achieved via a prefix/affix pattern
189   of text to the original prompt. These style patterns were not visible to the users.
190
      We started our data collection after the Stable Diffusion XL 1.0 (Podell et al., 2023) candidate was
191
      made available via bots. The data was continuously collected from July 11, 2023 (a day after SDXL
192
      1.0 candidate bots were launched) until Feb 07, 2024 (SDXL bot shutdown). Users were allowed
193   to submit prompts to bot-# channels where # corresponds to a number. We observed that the
194   number of channels varied over time, and generally remained at slightly over 10. For each prompt,
195   the bot would return 2 images. Users were asked to select which image was better by clicking
196   on a button corresponding to an image, without explicit guidance on what ”better” meant. Our
197   understanding from discussions with members of staff was that these prompts and image pairs were
198   used for fine-tuning the SDXL candidates using RLHF/DPO (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al.,
199   2024), selection of model candidates, and selection of generation hyperparameters.
200   Prior work has also collected user-generated prompts from discord servers for MidJourney and
201   Stable Diffusion (Sun et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022b). Our work goes further by only collecting
202   prompts that were human-curated. The Stable Diffusion discord followed a three-tier hierarchy for
203   prompts, where users first submit and rate images in bot-#, with highly rated images from all bot
204   channels going into a single showdown channel every 15 minutes. The showdown channel was
205   reset every 30 minutes and had the history wiped. In the showdown channel, 2 images and their
206   respective prompts were placed side-by-side. Users again were asked to select the images that were
207
      more visually appealing. Every 30 minutes, the top-ranked images and prompts would go into the
      pantheon channel. The pantheon had history going back to inception May 02, 2023. We note
208
      that strictly speaking the showdown to pantheon selection process was not fair to images that
209
      came in at the second 15 minute slice, as they were given less time to be voted upon. Due to this, we
210   do not further distinguish between prompts collected from these two sources. Our data collection
211   process ran every 14 minutes on the showdown channels and ran once on the pantheon channel.
212   This was sufficient as after the initial collection date, new pantheon entries were a strict subset of
213   showdown prompts. Visual inspection of the generations from showdown and pantheon suggest
214   that these images were generally more artistic and contained more interesting visual compositions
215   than the bot-# channel. We collect a total of 235k unique user-generated prompts, which is further
      filtered according to NSFW ratings and caption length.

241    3.2        O BTAINING NATURAL LANGUAGE CAPTIONS
242   As shown in Figure 4, the user-submitted prompts generally took a tag-like format, with descriptors
243   being separated by commas. Such prompts are convenient for users to specify and likely achieve good
244   results due to the use of CLIP text networks for conditioning, which can operate like bag-of-words
245   models (Thrush et al., 2022; Yuksekgonul et al., 2022). However, such prompts generally perform
246   poorly when typical NLP pipelines are used for analysis. These prompts further may not explicitly
247
      specify needed visual relationships in the text, and instead excessively rely on the prior learned by
      the diffusion model to disambiguate relationships. In order to mitigate this issue, we utilize an LLM
248
      model to clean up the original raw user-generated prompts. We use Gemini 1.0 Pro for this
249
      task, as it performed competitively against other models at the time of our work (Team et al., 2023)
250   and offered a free API. The model was instructed to take the user-generated prompts and transform
251   them into natural language captions. To enhance the results, we augment the prompt via in-context
252   learning from GPT-4 input/output pairs. To remove NSFW prompts, we record the Gemini API safety
253   ratings for each input prompt, and remove the prompts where a 4 out of 4 rating was given on the
254   axes of sexuality/hate speech/harassment, or if the model itself produced a refusal, or if the prompt
255   was repeatedly returned with an error (blocked by Google). Please see Figure 2 for a visualization of
256   the pipeline.

       3.3        I MAGE GENERATION AND SEMANTIC ATTRIBUTION
258
259   To provide a fully reproducible pipeline for the images and maximize the usability of our dataset, we
260   generate the images ourselves using open weights and record the random seed for each generation. Im-
261
      ages are generated using sdxl lightning 4step unet (Lin et al., 2024), a few-step distilled
      version of Stable Diffusion XL. For each prompt, we perform parsing using spaCy en core web lg
262
      to extract noun chunks. To obtain mappings from noun chunks to spatial attributions, we use Diffusion
263
      Attentive Attribution Maps which measures the cross-attention from tokens in the language condition
264   to the UNet. Specifically, we used the improved DAAM-i2i guided heatmap variant (Tang et al.,
265   2022; Chowdhury, 2024) which improves object localization. We observe that unrelated articles like
266   ”a”, and ”the” and possessive determiners like ”his”, ”her”, ”our”, ”their” are not typically localized
267   to a specific object, but rather have attribution maps diffuse over the background or various objects
268   in the image. While similar phenomena has been noted in ViTs (Darcet et al., 2023) and pure text
269   LLMs (Clark et al., 2019; Kovaleva et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2023), our observation is novel in that
      text-to-image diffusion models are encoding contextual information in these ”filler” words. For

408   4.2      S EMANTIC EXPLORATION OF THE DATASET
409
410   In Figure 6 we visualize the semantic distribution of whole images and the captions used to generate
411   the images. We utilize UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) with a cosine metric applied to CLIP embeddings
412   for this visualization with wizmap (Wang et al., 2023c). We find that the distribution of both images
413   and text exhibit peaks in concepts such as people, scenes, text, and animals (cats and dogs). These
414   peaks likely reflect the effect of human preference on visually interesting images.
415   We compare the distribution of prompts, captions, and images against JourneyDB and COCO. Figure
416   7a shows that our captions (natural language outputs of Gemini/GPT) are a subset of JourneyDB’s
417   captions. Figure 7b further shows that this cannot be due to the choice of LLM, and instead this is the

      5   D ISCUSSION
532
      Limitations and Future Work. Our work relies on human-submitted prompts, which may exhibit
533
      non-natural semantic co-occurrences. During the data collection process, we also observed a strong
534   shift in the semantic distribution of prompts and images around holidays (Thanksgiving, Christmas).
535   This suggests that continual data collection is required to mitigate bias.
536   Conclusion. We introduce StableSemantics, the first large-scale dataset that combines natural
537   language captions, synthetic images, and diffusion attribution maps. Our work goes beyond prior
538   datasets by providing spatially localized noun chunk to image region mappings. We explore the
539   semantic distribution of whole images and objects within an image. The availability of this dataset
      will allow for the use of synthetic visual data in additional domains.
