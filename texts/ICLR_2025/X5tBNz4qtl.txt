009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Offline preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) learns rewards and poli-
012            cies aligned with human preferences without the need for extensive reward engi-
013            neering and direct interaction with human annotators. However, ensuring safety
014            remains a critical challenge across many domains and tasks. Previous works on
015            safe RL from human feedback (RLHF) first learn reward and cost models from
016            offline data, and then use constrained RL to optimize a safe policy. However,
017            inaccuracies in the reward and cost learning can impair performance when used
018
               with constrained RL methods. To address these challenges, (a) we introduce a
               framework that learns a policy based on pairwise preferences regarding the agent’s
019
               behavior in terms of rewards, as well as binary labels indicating the safety of tra-
020
               jectory segments, without access to ground-truth rewards or costs; (b) we combine
021            the preference learning module with safety alignment in a constrained optimiza-
022            tion problem. This optimization problem is solved using a Lagrangian method
023            that directly learns reward maximizing safe policy without explicitly learning re-
024            ward and cost models, avoiding the need for constrained RL; (c) to evaluate our
025            approach, we construct new datasets with synthetic human feedback, built upon a
026            well-established offline safe RL benchmark. Empirically, our method successfully
027            learns safe policies with high rewards, outperforming baselines with ground-truth
028            reward and cost, as well as state-of-the-art RLHF approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
      To align the intelligent agents with human values, preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL)
033
      (Wirth et al., 2017) (also known as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)) has
034
      emerged as a popular learning paradigm by training the agent’s policy from human pairwise pref-
035   erence over agent’s behavior while no reward engineering is required. Offline PbRL (Shin et al.,
036   2021) addresses the problem of feedback efficiency by avoiding online interactions with human
037   annotators. It recently has shown great success when applied to policy learning in control tasks
038   (Christiano et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021; Park et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024; Hejna et al., 2024) and
039   finetuning large language models (LLMs) (Rafailov et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Ethayarajh et al.,
040   2024). While it demonstrates the ability to learn rewards and policy that are consistent with human
041   preferences, guaranteeing safety remains a significant challenge for PbRL. For instance, a robotic
042   agent should avoid collisions with the environment and human co-workers during a control task. For
043
      finetuning LLMs, the agent should always generate harmless content without violating social norms
      and morals. In this paper, we aim to learn a policy that is
044
045          • consistent with human pairwise preferences, and
046
             • aligned with human safety considerations.
047
048   Recently, safe RLHF (Dai et al., 2024) provides an approach for finetuning LLMs to generate help-
049   ful, harmless responses. It assumes access to pairwise human preferences for both rewards and costs
050   as well as safety labels for each response. The proposed method learns reward and cost models from
051   human feedback, based on which a policy is optimized using constrained RL. In this paper, we fo-
052   cus on the continuous control tasks which present additional challenges compared to safe RLHF for
053   LLMs. LLMs finetuning is viewed as a contextual bandit problem, while continuous control tasks
      are usually modeled as a sequential decision-making process using RL. Additionally, safe RLHF


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      method follows the conventional two-phase PbRL learning paradigm. In the first phase, reward and
055   cost models are learned from human feedback. In the second phase, constrained RL is applied to
056   optimize a policy based on the learned reward and cost. However, inaccuracies during the reward
057   and cost learning phase can undermine policy performance, and rollout sampling or bootstrapping
058   in the constrained RL phase may introduce optimization challenges.
059
      In this paper, we introduce the framework of Offline Safe Policy Optimization from Human Feedback
060
      (POHF), where a policy is learned using offline datasets that include two types of human feedback:
061   (a) pairwise preferences of agents’ behavior regarding rewards, and (b) binary labels indicating
062   whether the trajectory segment is safe or not. Notably, pairwise preference of agent’s behavior
063   regarding cost is not required as in prior work (Dai et al., 2024) since it is relatively scarce and
064   expensive to collect in practice (Ethayarajh et al., 2024). To address the aforementioned challenges,
065   we first present a novel method for learning a policy that generates safe behavior based solely on
066   safety labels. Next, we transform the safety alignment objective into an optimization constraint by
067   demonstrating that it implicitly defines a feasible set of policies. This safety alignment module is
068   then integrated into the preference alignment module and the Lagrangian method is employed to
069
      learn a policy directly using the offline dataset. As a result, we derive a fully supervised learning
      objective, eliminating the need for additional reward and cost learning and constrained RL.
070
071   To evaluate our approach, we constructed a new dataset based on the well-established offline safe
072   RL benchmark, DSRL (Liu et al., 2023a). We synthesized human feedback using the ground truth
073   rewards and costs provided in the original offline dataset as they are not accessible during training
074   in our setting. The dataset includes 29 continuous control tasks across two prevailing domains,
075
      providing a robust platform for testing offline safe policy optimization from human feedback and
      facilitating further study. We compare our method against baselines from offline safe RL (Liu et al.,
076
      2023a; Xu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b), which use ground truth reward and cost, as well as
077
      offline RLHF approaches, including a variant of safe RLHF. The results suggest that, compared to
078   the baselines, our method effectively learns a policy that achieves high rewards while adhering to
079   constraints implicitly encoded in the human feedback.
080
081
      The contributions of this paper are threefold. First, we introduce the Offline Safe POHF framework
      for control tasks, where a policy is learned using the offline dataset including pairwise agent behavior
082
      preferences related to reward, and binary safety labels for each trajectory segment. Second, we
083
      propose a practical solution by integrating preference and safety alignment modules to define a
084   constrained optimization objective, and the policy is optimized using the Lagrangian method. This
085   approach removes the need for reward and cost learning, as well as an additional constrained RL
086   phase. Third, we extensively evaluate our approach using newly synthesized datasets, demonstrating
087   the effectiveness of the proposed method.

092   Preference-based Reinforcement Learning To avoid reward engineering which requires expert
093   knowledge and may suffer from negative effects of reward misspecification (Pan et al., 2022),
094   Preference-based Reinforcement Learning (PbRL) (also known as Reinforcement Learning from
095   Human Feedback (RLHF)) provides a promising paradigm to learn a policy from human feed-
096   back (Wirth et al., 2017). There has been significant advances recently, both for control tasks
097   (Christiano et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021; Park et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2021;
098   Hejna & Sadigh, 2024; Kang et al., 2023; Hejna et al., 2024) and LLM finetuning (Ziegler et al.,
099
      2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Rafailov et al.,
      2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024). Besides preference alignment, researchers also start to focus on how
100
      to ensure safety for policy learning. Safe RLHF (Dai et al., 2024) is proposed to finetune an LLM
101
      that generates helpful and harmless responses. This approach is specifically designed for LLMs,
102   meaning it applies to contextual bandits settings, rather than RL, which is a multi-step sequential
103   decision-making setting. Safe RLHF needs to explicitly learn reward and cost models and perform
104   constrained RL using such learned models. While effective, the inaccuracies in reward and cost
105   model learning may adversely impact the policy learning using constrained RL. In this paper, we
106   introduce a framework for Offline Safe Policy Optimization from Human Feedback (POHF) for
107   continuous control tasks, and aim to learn a safe policy directly with pairwise preference and binary
      safety labels.

      Offline Safe Reinforcement Learning Offline safe RL provides a practical framework for learn-
109   ing safe policies using pre-collected datasets (Liu et al., 2023a). Previous work has addressed this
110   problem through various approaches such as sequential modeling (Liu et al., 2023b), distribution
111   correction estimation (Lee et al., 2022), Q-learning (Xu et al., 2022), and feasible region identifi-
112   cation (Zheng et al., 2024). These offline datasets typically consist of agent rollouts with ground
113   truth rewards and costs for each timestep, often designed by experts to ensure the data quality for
114   policy learning. However, in many complex real-world domains and tasks, it is difficult to manually
115   design reward and cost functions that accurately reflect human values. Therefore, with the advances
116   of PbRL, we propose to learn policies from pre-collected human feedback, replacing ground truth
117
      rewards and costs with human pairwise preferences regarding agent behavior for rewards and binary
      labels indicating whether the behavior is safe or not.

346   the expense of the other due to a fixed optimization ratio.

               using ground truth reward and cost?

      6.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
361
362   We conduct our experiments using the well-established DSRL benchmark (Liu et al., 2023a) which
363   is designed for offline safe RL, leveraging it to generate synthetic human feedback for evaluation.
364   This benchmark provides pre-collected offline data across 29 tasks in two widely used domains:
365   SafetyGym (Ray et al., 2019; Ji et al., 2023) and BulletGym (Gronauer, 2022). These tasks feature
366   various agents aiming to achieve high rewards or goals while avoiding obstacles or maintaining a
367   predefined safe velocity. To ensure fair comparison and evaluation, we adopt the constraint variation
368
      evaluation method introduced in DSRL (Liu et al., 2023a). Each method is tested on every task using
      three different cost thresholds and three random seeds to ensure consistency. Evaluation metrics
369
      include normalized reward and normalized cost, where a cost below 1 signifies safety (Liu et al.,
370
      2023a; Fu et al., 2020).
371
372   For the practical implementation of PreSa, we follow a similar training pipeline to CPL (Hejna et al.,
373   2024). Initially, the policy is pretrained using behavior cloning (BC) with the offline dataset, which
374
      is then retained as the reference policy, πref . Following this, the policy is optimized with PreSa using
      human pairwise preferences and binary safety labels.
375
376   Synthetic Human Feedback For evaluation, we generate synthetic human feedback from the offline
377   dataset provided by DSRL. We first randomly select 10,000 pairs of trajectory segments with varying
      lengths from each dataset, which allows for testing with fewer feedbacks via random sampling.


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Pairwise trajectory segments are then labeled using the ground truth rewards from the original offline
379   dataset. We base pairwise preferences on cumulative rewards rather than estimated advantages from
380   a trained policy, as in the dense reward setting, the optimal advantage is essentially a reshaped
381   version of the reward, leading to the same optimal policy (Hejna et al., 2024). Additionally, we label
382   trajectory segments for safety using ground truth cost values and predefined safety thresholds. In
383   line with the experimental settings in DSRL, these safety thresholds apply to the entire trajectories.
384   When labeling trajectory segments, we use a reshaped threshold that is adjusted proportionally based
385   on the length of the segment relative to the maximum trajectory length within each domain.
386   Baselines We compare our approach against several baselines to demonstrate its effectiveness, which
387   are across Offline Safe POHF setting and offline safe RL setting with ground truth rewards and costs.
388   In Offline Safe POHF setting, we consider three baseline methods: 1) Binary Alignment: Inspired
389   by Ethayarajh et al. (2024), unified binary labels are generated based on comparative preferences
390   and binary safety labels. Segments that are both safe and preferred are assigned a label of +1, while
391   all others −1. The policy is then learned solely using the safety alignment module. 2) BC-Safe-Seg:
392   A behavior cloning (BC) approach trained only on safe trajectory segments. 3) Safe-RLHF (CDT)
393
      (Dai et al., 2024): A variant of Safe-RLHF adapted to our setting where reward and cost models
      are learned from human feedback and a state-of-the-art offline safe RL approach, CDT Liu et al.
394
      (2023b) is applied for policy optimization. Additionally, we select three baselines from offline safe
395
      RL setting. They assume access to ground truth reward and cost: 1) BC-All: BC trained on the entire
396   dataset. 2) BC-Safe: BC trained exclusively on safe trajectories that meet safety constraints. 3) CDT:
397   A sequence modeling approach that incorporates safety constraints into Decision Transformers.

517   7     C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this paper, we present Offline Safe POHF, a framework in which a policy is learned using hu-
520   man pairwise preferences and binary safety labels for each trajectory segment, without access to
521   ground truth rewards and costs. We first analyze the problem through two distinct modules: pref-
522
      erence alignment and safety alignment, which can be applied individually for policy learning based
      on preferences or safety labels, respectively. We then introduce an approach for Offline Safe POHF
523
      problems, called PreSa, which integrates both modules into a single constrained optimization ob-
524
      jective, as the safety alignment module implicitly defines a feasible set of policies. Given offline
525   human feedback data, PreSa directly learns a policy without the need to develop additional reward
526   and cost models or employ constrained safe RL. Empirical results demonstrate that PreSa not only
527   outperforms baselines from the Offline Safe POHF setting but also matches or surpasses offline safe
528   RL approaches that assume access to ground truth rewards and costs.
529
530   Limitations For evaluation, we generate synthetic human feedback based on the offline safe RL
531   benchmark, DSRL. While utilizing synthetic human feedback is a common practice in PbRL re-
532   search (Christiano et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021; Park et al., 2022), and synthetic labels can effectively
533   approximate real human feedback under certain conditions (Metcalf et al., 2024), it is important to
534   note that scripted human labelers may not achieve perfect consistency with actual human labelers.
535   Future work will involve conducting extended experiments with real human participants.

719   B.2   S YNTHETIC H UMAN F EEDBACK
720
721   To evaluate our approach in the Offline Safe POHF setting, we generate synthetic human feedback
722   consisting of pairwise preferences regarding agent behavior and safety labels indicating whether the
723   agent’s actions are safe or not. For pairwise preferences, we follow the method used in CPL (Hejna
724
      et al., 2024), assuming that the human model follows a regret-based preference framework. Given
      that negated regret is equivalent to the discounted sum of optimal advantages, we provide preference
725
      feedback based on the cumulative advantages of the compared trajectory segments. However, since
726
      obtaining optimal advantages requires running RL for each task, we simplify the process by using
727   cumulative rewards instead. This is because optimal advantages are essentially a reshaped version
728   of the reward, leading to the same optimal policy.
729
730
      For the binary safety labels, we use the ground truth cost data from the original offline dataset to
      assess the safety of each trajectory segment. Safety labels are then assigned based on predefined cost
731
      thresholds, which are generally defined for the entire task or trajectory. To label individual segments
732
      (which are parts of full trajectories), we proportionally adjust the cost threshold according to the
733   segment’s length relative to the maximum trajectory length in each domain.
734
735
      B.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
736
737   To assess the algorithm’s performance, we adopt the evaluation methodology from DSRL (Liu et al.,
738   2023a), using normalized reward and normalized cost as metrics. The normalized reward is defined
739   as follows:
740                                                      Rπ − rmin
                                          Rnormalized =
741                                                     rmax − rmin
742   where Rπ is the cumulative reward under policy π, and rmax and rmin denote the empirical maximum
743   and minimum reward returns. The normalized cost is represented as:
744
745                                                           Cπ + ϵ
                                              Cnormalized =
746                                                           κ+ϵ
747   where Cπ is the cumulative cost under policy π. The cost threshold is given by κ, and ϵ is a
748   small positive constant added to ensure numerical stability when κ = 0. According to the DSRL
749   benchmark, a task is considered safe if the normalized cost does not exceed 1.
750

      PreSa with offline human feedback.
