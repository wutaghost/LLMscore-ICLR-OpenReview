009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Sequential reasoning in agent systems has been significantly advanced by large
               language models (LLMs), yet existing approaches face limitations. Reflection-
013
               driven reasoning relies solely on knowledge in pretrained models, limiting perfor-
014
               mance in novel scenarios, while experience-assisted reasoning often depends on
015            external experiences and lacks clear principles for selecting representative experi-
016            ences. We address these limitations by proposing C O PS (Cross-Task Experience
017            Sharing), a generalizable algorithm that enhances sequential reasoning by cross-
018            task experience sharing and selection. In detail, C O PS leverages agents’ experi-
019            ences on previous tasks, selecting distribution-matched experiences via a provable
020            pessimism-based strategy to maximize utility while minimizing risks from distri-
021            bution shifts. Extensive experimental results on benchmarks like Alfworld, Web-
022            shop, and HotPotQA demonstrate that C O PS consistently outperforms state-of-
023
               the-art baselines, with superior sample efficiency suitable for resource-constrained
               scenarios. Theoretically, we show that the performance of our algorithm depends
024
               on both the quality of the pretrained LLM and the matching between the agent’s
025
               task-dependent trial distribution and that generated by the LLM. Our work bridges
026            the gap between existing sequential reasoning paradigms and validates the effec-
027            tiveness of leveraging cross-task experiences, shedding light on the potential to
028            improve agents’ generalization and adaptability across diverse tasks. Our codes
029            are released at this link.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Burgeoning agent systems driven by advanced large language models (LLMs, (Devlin et al., 2019;
      Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023; Hu et al., 2024a)) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in
035
      solving complex tasks through sequential reasoning (Qin et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
036
      2024; Chen et al., 2024b;a; Li et al., 2023a). These agent systems employ two typical sequential
037   reasoning paradigms: reflection-driven reasoning and experience-assisted reasoning. Reflection-
038   driven reasoning leverages a model’s internal capabilities through methods such as reflection (Shinn
039   et al., 2024), long-term rollouts (Zhou et al., 2023), or chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning (Wei et al.,
040   2022). While this approach capitalizes on the knowledge within the pre-trained model, it faces
041   notable limitations. Specifically, relying solely on existing knowledge in the pre-trained model to
042   generate rationales restricts the model’s performance when encountering novel scenarios. Moreover,
043   there is an increased risk of hallucinations, where internal reasoning may lead to plausible but in-
044   correct responses (Huang et al., 2023). These challenges highlight the need for integrating external
045
      experiences to enhance the agent’s sequential reasoning capabilities.
046   In contrast, experience-assisted sequential reasoning utilizes retrieval-based methods that enable the
047   agent to interact with a memory bank of experiences, allowing the model to overcome knowledge
048   cutoffs, personalize responses, and reduce hallucinations. However, these experiences are often
049   manually curated or sourced from expert models (Raparthy et al., 2023), which is resource-intensive
050
      and poses scalability issues. Additionally, experience-assisted reasoning often lacks clear principles
      for selecting representative examples (Kagaya et al., 2024), potentially underutilizing the value of
051
      past experiences. These limitations bring us to a critical research question:
052
053
      Can agent systems enhance sequential reasoning by sharing and selecting cross-task experiences?

074   To address this question, we propose C O PS (Cross-Task Experience Sharing), a theoretically
075   grounded algorithm that empowers agent systems through cross-task experience sharing and selec-
076   tion. C O PS demonstrates its generalizability by working effectively in both settings: utilizing fully
077   external experiences in the offline setting and leveraging completely self-derived experiences in the
078   online setting. By utilizing representative cross-task experiences, C O PS enables agents to improve
079   performance on new, complex sequential reasoning tasks. Our key contributions are summarized as
080
      follows:
081
      • We introduce C O PS, a method that fully leverages agents’ cross-task experiences to enhance
082
        sequential reasoning by selecting distribution-matched experiences from previous task trajecto-
083     ries. Central to our approach is a theoretically grounded experience selection strategy based on
084     the pessimism principle, which aims to maximize the utility of successful, representative experi-
085     ences while minimizing risks associated with distribution shifts from out-of-distribution samples.
086     Notably, C O PS is agnostic to the agent’s base model, task type, experience sources, and imple-
087     mentation framework, making it easy-to-use and generalizable across various settings.
088   • Experimentally, we validate C O PS on key benchmarks such as Alfworld (Shridhar et al., 2020),
089     Webshop (Yao et al., 2022a), and HotPotQA (Yang et al., 2018). C O PS consistently outper-
090     forms state-of-the-art experience-assisted reasoning approaches like RAP (Kagaya et al., 2024)
091     and reflection-driven reasoning methods, like Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2024) and LATS (Zhou
092     et al., 2023). Moreover, C O PS demonstrates superior sample efficiency compared to resource-
093     intensive methods like LATS, making it highly suitable for resource-constrained scenarios. These
094     results showcase C O PS’s effectiveness in practical applications.
095   • Theoretically, we show that in both offline and online settings, the performance of our pessimism-
096     based algorithm depends on both the quality of the pre-trained LLM and the matching between the
097     cross-task experience distribution decided by the trials selected by the agent, and a task-dependent
098     experience distribution denoted by the LLM. Our findings shed light on general strategies for
099
        designing efficient experience sharing and selction algorithms and offer a comprehensive under-
        standing of C O PS’s effectiveness across different scenarios.

127
128   2.1   P RELIMINARY
129
      We consider a sequential decision-making scenario, consisting of a task space M, a state space S,
130
      and an action space A. The state s ∈ S is defined as a descriptive sentence representing the history
131   of the current task. For example: “You are in the middle of a room. Please find a path to reach
132   the apple.” The action a ∈ A is a solution to the task, such as: “Move right. The apple is on the
133   table.” The agent interacts with the environment through trials. At the beginning of each trial, a task
134   M is randomly drawn from the task space, M ∼ PM . The agent then observes an initial state s1 ,
135   sampled from the initial state distribution, s1 ∼ PM   0 . At each step h, the agent makes a decision
136   ah based on the current state sh , and the next state is updated as sh+1 = sh |ah . The agent either
137   successfully completes the task or continues generating actions until reaching the maximum number
138   of interactions H between the agent and the environment. We define an experience τ as a complete
139
      trial, i.e., τ = sh , where h ≤ H is the final step of the current trial. The reward r(sh ) denotes how
      effectively the experience solves the task, with 0 ≤ r(sh ) ≤ 1.
140
141   In this work, we assume access to a large language model (LLM) to assist in decision-making. We
142   represent the LLM as LLM(a|·), a conditional distribution of actions given the input sequence.
143
      2.2   P ROPOSED METHOD
144
145   We introduce our proposed method, C O PS, based on distribution matching. C O PS operates on a
146   trial-wise basis, making it suitable for both the offline setting, where the agent has access to an exter-
147   nal static dataset containing experiences, and the online setting, where the agent gathers experiences
148   through interactions with the environment. Suppose our agent is at the start of a trial with an initial
149   state s1 ∼ PM 0 . We introduce the key components of C O PS as follows.
150   Memory Bank The agent has access to a memory bank D containing experiences, either from a
151   pre-collected dataset (offline) or from previous experiences (online). We do not impose restrictions
152   on D, meaning that experiences in D exhibit great diversity. Specifically, an experience τ ∈ D may
153   correspond to different tasks M or to varying solution strategies for the same task. Our goal is to
154   develop a strategy for retrieving experiences from D that assist in decision-making for the current
155   task.
156   Cross-Task Experience Sharing C O PS utilizes an external module called the decoder, denoted
157   as Dec in Line 1. In general, the decoder outputs a task-dependent distribution of experiences
158   conditioned on the initial state s1 , reflecting how the LLM would solve the task M associated with
159   s1 without explicit instructions. With the decoder’s help, the agent’s goal is to find a probability
160   distribution pb over all experiences in D that satisfies:

180   Implementation Details Here we discuss several implementation details for C O PS. First, in prac-
181   tice, directly computing the distance d(p, Dec(·|s1 )) between distributions in (2.2) is computation-
182
      ally intractable. Therefore, we use an empirical approximation to translate the distance between
      distributions into the distance between experiences drawn from those distributions, as shown in
183
      (2.1). Second, we specify the choice of Dec. The decoder outputs an experience τ s1 from D that
184
      starts with the same initial state s1 . If multiple such experiences exist, we select the most recent
185   one. This τ s1 naturally reflects the behavior of the LLM for solving the task starting from s1 with-
186   out intervention. Third, we discuss how to approximately solve (2.1) since enumerating all possible
187   distributions in ∆(D) is computationally inefficient. Specifically, we define the distance function d
188   and approximately solve pb as follows:
189
                         d(τ, τ ′ ) := c · cos(e(τ ), e(τ ′ )), pb(τ ) ∝ r(τ ) · exp(−d(τ, τ s1 )),           (2.3)
190
191   where c ≥ 0 is a hyperparameter, “cos” denotes the cosine function, and e is an embedding function
192   that maps a language sentence to a high-dimensional Euclidean space. In practice, we use e as a
193   language embedding model (e.g., gte-Qwen2 7b (Li et al., 2023b)). This approach favors select-
194   ing successful experiences from D with probabilities proportional to the inverse distance from the
195   current initial state s1 . The hyperparameter c in (2.3) controls the influence of relative distances:
196
      when c = 0, the method uniformly samples successful experiences from D, and as c → ∞, it
      deterministically selects the experience closest to τ s1 .

      3    E XPERIMENTS S ETUP
199
200   In this section, we present our experimental study evaluating the practical performance of C O PS
201   on real-world LLMs, specifically the Llama 3.1 models (Dubey et al., 2024). Our results show that
202   C O PS achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in both task success rate and sample efficiency,
203   surpassing existing baselines to the best of our knowledge. A detailed description of our prompt for-
204   mulation is provided in Appendix L. Notably, C O PS is both simple to implement and generalizable
205   across different environments: for each trial, the selected experiences are straightforwardly added to
206
      the prompts, requiring no manual modifications.
207   This prompting strategy offers two distinct advantages: first, it significantly boosts sequential rea-
208   soning performance by incorporating cross-task experiences, outperforming reflection-driven meth-
209   ods like Reflexion. Second, the prompts across trials share a substantial prefix, which maximizes the
210   effectiveness of prefix-caching mechanisms in modern LLM-serving systems (Zheng et al., 2023),
211   leading to significant efficiency improvements over RAP (Kagaya et al., 2024).
212   Benchmarks We evaluate our algorithms on three representative benchmarks: Alfworld (Shridhar
213   et al., 2020), Webshop (Yao et al., 2022a), and HotPotQA (Yang et al., 2018). In these benchmarks,
214   agents strive to solve problems in limited number of trials, enabling cross-trial and cross-task expe-
215   rience sharing. In Alfworld, agents are provided with a specific task description within a simulated
      household environment, interacting through predefined actions and receiving feedback in the form


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      of textual descriptions. In Webshop, the agent must locate a product that matches user specifications
217   from a catalog of over one million items, interacting with the HTML page and search engine while
218   receiving limited product information per trial. In HotPotQA, the agent answers complex questions
219   requiring specific knowledge, using Wikipedia to retrieve relevant articles. In all benchmarks, the
220   reward function r(τ ) is defined as 1 if the agent successfully completes the task and 0 otherwise.
221
      LLM Selection We conduct our entire experiment with the widely-used Llama 3.1 series of models
222
      (Dubey et al., 2024), in consideration of their superior benchmark performance and the sustainability
223   of open-weight LLM ecosystems. Specifically, our experiments are conducted with Llama 3.1 8b
224   Instruct and Llama 3.1 70b Instruct on NVIDIA A6000 and A100 GPUs. We use gte-Qwen2 7b
225   Instruct (Li et al., 2023b) as our embedding model. We use SGLang (Zheng et al., 2023) as our
226   LLM-serving engine for its SOTA serving performance and prefix-caching mechanism.
227
      Baselines We compare C O PS with three representative baselines: Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2024),
228
      RAP (Kagaya et al., 2024), and LATS (Zhou et al., 2023). In Reflexion, the agent try to solve
229   the task in each environment over multiple trials until it succeeds. After each failed attempt, the
230   LLM agent reflects on its unsuccessful trajectory and saves this reflection in its memory. For each
231   subsequent trial, the agent is provided with up to three recent reflections from the same task. In
232   RAP, at each stage within a trial, the agent is presented with the top-k search results of trajectory
233   fragments as in-context demonstrations. In LATS, the agent utilizes a tree-structured search to
234   explore multiple reasoning and action rationales at each trial. When it encounters failed rationales,
235   the agent generates reflections on its mistakes and integrates these insights into its decision-making
236   process for future trials.

479
      6.1   LLM- POWERED AGENTS
480
481   In recent years, there has been a significant surge in research focused on LLM-powered agents
482   (Chen et al., 2024b;a; Chan et al., 2023). React (Yao et al., 2022b) laid the foundation for much
483   of the subsequent work on LLM agents, particularly those based on in-context learning (ICL). The
484   most relevant studies to C O PS include (Shinn et al., 2024; Kagaya et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023;
485   Raparthy et al., 2023). In (Kagaya et al., 2024), a retrieval process for selecting in-context demon-
      strations was proposed. However, their approach depends on frequent embedding queries during


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the planning stage, leading to inefficiency issues even in smaller LLM settings. Additionally, RAP
487   manually splits the agent’s planning trajectory into multiple stages for each trial, with benchmark-
488   specific tailoring, significantly increases implementation complexity and raises scalability concerns.
489   (Zhou et al., 2023) introduced a Tree-of-Thought (ToT) approach (Yao et al., 2024), incorporating
490   backpropagation and a valuation process. However, their approach demonstrated poor sample effi-
491   ciency, making it less suited for real-world agent settings where opportunities for trial and error are
492   limited. Similarly, (Liu et al., 2023) integrated value-based search into a theoretical framework, but
493   faced similar challenges with sample efficiency. (Feng et al., 2024) explored fine-tuning for specific
494   LLM agent tasks, achieving good performance but with high computational costs. Lastly, (Raparthy
495
      et al., 2023) utilized high-quality experiences as ICL demonstrations for sequential reasoning. Al-
      though achieving remarkable performance, these experiences are introduced from external RL sys-
496
      tems, which is resource-intensive and poses scalability issues. O3D (Xiao et al., 2024) is also highly
497
      related to C O PS, which introduces an offline learning framework that leverages skill discovery and
498   knowledge distillation to enhance cross-task generalization without requiring fine-tuning, excelling
499   in offline settings and diverse domains. In contrast, C O PS addresses cross-task experience selection
500   using a pessimism-based strategy to mitigate distribution shifts, enabling dynamic adaptation and
501   superior sample efficiency even in resource-constrained environments.
502
503   6.2       I N - CONTEXT D EMONSTRATIONS S ELECTION
504   The selection of demonstrations for ICL has been widely studied. (Wang et al., 2024b) approached
505   in-context demonstration selection from a Bayesian perspective, explicitly constructing a latent vari-
506   able for the selection process. However, their analysis did not account for the pre-trained knowledge
507   distribution, and their results were primarily empirical. (Yan et al., 2023) investigated the impact
508   of repetition in in-context demonstrations, conducting controlled experiments to assess how repeti-
509   tions in pre-trained knowledge influence results. (Scarlatos & Lan, 2023) developed a reinforcement
510   learning framework to select in-context examples, while (Voronov et al., 2024) examined the impact
511   of prompt formatting on in-context learning performance. Additionally, (Shum et al., 2023) intro-
512
      duced an automatic CoT augmentation and selection method for ICL example datasets. (Hu et al.,
      2024b) analyzed the scaling of in-context demonstrations from a theoretical standpoint, deriving
513
      general statistical bounds while accounting for pre-training errors. However, their focus was primar-
514
      ily on CoT in general ICL settings, not on the specific challenges faced by LLM agents interacting
515   with environments and requiring feedback for optimization.
516
517   6.3       T HEORY OF AGENTS
518
      Several works have advanced the theoretical understanding of LLM agents. (He et al., 2024) ex-
519
      plored the statistical theory of LLM agents through the lens of Bayesian aggregated imitation learn-
520
      ing. (Lin et al., 2023) provided a theoretical analysis of transformers within the context of in-context
521   reinforcement learning. (Wang et al., 2024a) examined the training and generalization of transform-
522   ers for sequential reasoning, drawing parallels between transformer behavior and online learning
523   algorithms. (Sumers et al., 2023) offered a cognitive perspective on LLM agents, while (Park et al.,
524   2024) investigated the regret of LLM agents in sequential reasoning tasks, contributing both theo-
525   retical and empirical insights that inform C O PS’s development.
526
527   7       C ONCLUSION
528
      In this paper, we introduced C O PS (Cross-Task Experience Sharing), a theoretically grounded algo-
529
      rithm that empowers agent systems with cross-task experiences sharing. Using a pessimism-based
530
      strategy to select relevant experiences, C O PS maximizes utility while minimizing the risks of distri-
531   bution shifts. Our experiments on benchmarks like Alfworld, Webshop, and HotPotQA demonstrate
532   that C O PS outperforms state-of-the-art methods in both success rates and sample efficiency. Theo-
533   retically, we show that our algorithm’s performance depends on the LLM’s pre-trained quality and
534   the matching between the cross-task experience distribution decided by the trials selected by the
535   agent, and a task-dependent experience distribution denoted by the LLM, providing insights for
536   improving experience retrieval methods.

      In this section, we analyze how two key hyperparameters affect the performance of C O PS: the
      scaling factor c in Equation (2.3) and the number of in-context experiences k placed at the beginning
904
      of prompts. We conducted experiments on the Alfworld benchmark using both Llama 3.1 8b and
905
      Llama 3.1 70b models.
906
907   For the scaling factor c, we tested four settings: c = 0, 1, 5 and 10, while keeping the number of
908
      in-context experiences fixed at k = 5 (see Figures 3(a) and 3(b)). Our findings indicate that for
      smaller models like Llama 3.1 8b, a small but non-zero value of c (e.g., c = 1) generally yields
909
      better performance (Figure 3(a)). This suggests that moderate scaling effectively balances model
910
      adaptability and robustness on less capable models.
911
912   Regarding the number of in-context experiences k, we evaluated values ranging from 1 to 10, setting
913   c = 0 (see Figures 3(c) and 3(d)). We observed that performance improves as k increases up to
914
      k = 3, after which it plateaus for both model sizes. This result indicates that while increasing the
      in-context experience size enhances performance to a point, adding more than three experiences may
915
      not offer substantial gains.
