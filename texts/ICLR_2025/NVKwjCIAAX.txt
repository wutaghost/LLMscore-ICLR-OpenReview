011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In materials science, finding crystal structures that have targeted properties is cru-
014            cial. While recent methodologies such as Bayesian optimization and deep gen-
015            erative models have made some advances on this issue, these methods often face
016            difficulties in adaptively incorporating various constraints, such as electrical neu-
017            trality and targeted properties optimization, while keeping the desired specific
018
               crystal structure. To address these challenges, we have developed the Simultane-
               ous Multi-property Optimization using Adaptive Crystal Synthesizer (SMOACS),
019
               which utilizes state-of-the-art property prediction models and their gradients to
020
               directly optimize input crystal structures for targeted properties simultaneously.
021            SMOACS enables the integration of adaptive constraints into the optimization
022            process without necessitating model retraining. Thanks to this feature, SMOACS
023            has succeeded in simultaneously optimizing targeted properties while maintain-
024            ing perovskite structures, even with models trained on diverse crystal types. We
025            have demonstrated the band gap optimization while meeting a challenging con-
026            straint, that is, maintaining electrical neutrality in large atomic configurations up
027            to 135 atom sites, where the verification of the electrical neutrality is challenging.
028            The properties of the most promising materials have been confirmed by density
029
               functional theory calculations.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   We address the challenge of simultaneously optimizing multiple material properties while preserv-
035   ing specific crystal structures and ensuring electrical neutrality. To achieve this, we have developed
036   a methodology that leverages property prediction models and their gradients to facilitate the discov-
037   ery of materials with multiple desired properties. This approach allows for the adaptive application
038   of constraints, such as electrical neutrality and specific crystal structures, without necessitating re-
039
      training. As a result, our method enables the optimization of large atomic configurations to obtain
      specific properties while ensuring electrical neutrality and preserving specific crystal structures.
040
041   Materials design is crucial for various advancing technologies, e.g., enhancing efficiency or reducing
042   the cost of solar cells. The goal of materials design is to identify materials that simultaneously satisfy
043   multiple property criteria, for instance, in terms of band gap and formation energy, while meeting
044   other requirements, such as electrical neutrality. Furthermore, during the design process, it is often
045
      desirable to focus on specific promising systems, such as perovskite structures for next-generation
      solar cells (Green et al., 2014). In the exploration of specific crystal structures, elemental sub-
046
      stitution—blending different elements—is commonly employed. For instance, blended perovskite
047
      structures might have alternative compositions such as AA′ BB′ X2 X′ X′′3 , which are derived from
048   the standard ABX3 format of a perovskite unit cell. Computational experiments involving these
049   complex compositions often require larger systems that combine multiple unit cells. Consequently,
050   the critical aspects of material design include 1) the ability to optimize multiple properties simulta-
051   neously, 2) the adaptive incorporation of various constraints, such as electrical neutrality or specific
052   crystal structures, and 3) the ability to optimize large atomic configurations. In summary, we need
053   to solve the problem of simultaneously optimizing multiple properties while preserving a specific
      crystal structure and ensuring electrical neutrality.

054
      Advances in computational techniques have tremendously accelerated material design, with Density
055   Functional Theory (DFT) becoming a standard tool for rapid property validation. Recent develop-
056   ments in machine learning have enabled faster property predictions through deep learning models
057   trained on DFT-generated data. Models such as Crystalformer (Taniai et al., 2024), a transformer-
058   based model (Vaswani et al., 2017), and ALIGNN (Choudhary & DeCost, 2021), a Graph Neural
059   Network (GNN)-based model, significantly facilitate the screening process (Choubisa et al., 2023).
060
      Bayesian optimization, such as Gaussian Process and Tree-structured Parzen estimator (TPE)
061   (Watanabe, 2023), is commonly employed in material design (Ozaki et al., 2020a; Boyar et al.,
062   2024; Zhai et al., 2024). A key advantage of Bayesian optimization is its capability to perform
063   inverse inference, therefore enabling the prediction of crystal structures from given properties. Re-
064   cently, deep generative models designed to synthesize crystal structures, such as FTCP (Ren et al.,
065   2022), have gained much attention due to their potential to discover new stable materials. Addition-
066   ally, there are methods that leverage large language models (Ding et al., 2024; Gruver et al., 2024),
067   Generative Flow Networks (AI4Science et al., 2023), reinforcement learning (Govindarajan et al.,
068   2024), or flow matching (Miller et al., 2024) to synthesize new crystal structures.
069   Despite these advances, many challenges remain. Firstly, research using deep generative models
070   primarily aims to identify stable materials, and only a limited number of studies focus on optimizing
071   both stability and key properties, such as the band gap, which is crucial for maximizing solar cell
072   efficiency. Secondly, deep generative models are often built with specialized architectures, making
073   it difficult to adopt the latest property prediction models for their prediction branches. This lack of
074   flexibility in model architectures can hinder the improvement of prediction accuracy for generated
075
      materials. Thirdly, current generative models require retraining for targeted properties optimization
      within specific crystal structures, which is often the case in practice, such as perovskite structures
076
      for solar cells. Finally, verifying the electrical neutrality in large atomic configurations is compli-
077
      cated due to the combinatorial explosion resulting from the possible multiple oxidation numbers for
078   many atomic species. Nevertheless, ensuring electrical neutrality is essential for proposing realistic
079   materials.
080
081
      To address these challenges, we have developed a framework, the Simultaneous Multi-property
      Optimization using Adaptive Crystal Synthesizer (SMOACS). SMOACS can employ various prop-
082
      erty prediction models as far as their gradients can be computed and optimizes input crystal struc-
083
      tures directly to achieve target properties through the backpropagation technique (Fig. 1(left)). This
084   approach enables accurate prediction of multiple properties and simultaneous optimization by uti-
085   lizing several recently developed pre-trained models for predicting different material properties.
086   Unlike methods using normalizing flows that require architectural constraints for invertibility, our
087   method imposes no such restrictions on these models. When newer models become available in
088   the future, improved prediction accuracy will be achieved by incorporating them into our approach.
089   Additionally, by managing the optimization range and utilizing special loss functions, we facilitate
090   targeted properties optimization within specific crystal structures, avoiding retraining. Moreover,
091   by imposing constraints via combinations of oxidation numbers, our method ensures the electrical
092
      neutrality of any proposed materials, even in large atomic configurations where verifying electrical
      neutrality is difficult due to combinatorial explosion. The generalizability of SMOACS enables it to
093
      adopt various prediction models and optimize various properties.
094
095   SMOACS is the first method that directly optimizes the space of crystal structures using a gradient-
096   based approach. We achieve this by making the entire crystal structure differentiable, which in-
097   volves decomposing it into various components and representing atomic species as atomic distri-
098
      butions. Unlike traditional methods that convert crystal structures into latent variables (Ren et al.,
      2022)—thereby entangling their elements—our approach maintains the independence of each com-
099
      ponent. This independence facilitates the preservation of crystal structures and ensures electrical
100
      neutrality by precisely specifying the atoms at each site. Furthermore, unlike generative models that
101   probabilistically generate materials satisfying certain conditions, our method can inherently guaran-
102   tee electrical neutrality and the preservation of crystal structures. Moreover we can add additional
103   constraints as long as they are differentiable.
104
      We demonstrated that SMOACS could effectively utilize both GNN-based models and transformer-
105
      based models, outperforming FTCP, deep generative models, and TPE, Bayesian optimization. We
106
      demonstrated the band gap optimization within perovskite structures without retraining, using mod-
107   els trained on the MEGNet dataset (Chen et al., 2019), which includes various types of crystals.

131   Property prediction model. In recent years, much research has actively focused on predicting the
132   properties of materials using DFT-generated data (Davariashtiyani & Kadkhodaei, 2023; Merchant
133   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a). There are two primary approaches involving deep learning. The first
134   approach utilizes GNNs (Chen et al., 2019; Park & Wolverton, 2020; Louis et al., 2020; Schmidt
135
      et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023), such as ALIGNN. The main advantage of using GNNs is their ability
      to graphically represent crystal structures, thereby considering inter-atomic relationships in more
136
      physically meaningful ways. The second approach employs transformers (Ying et al., 2021; Yan
137
      et al., 2022), such as Crystalformer, which are known for their promising performance in the field
138   of computer vision and natural language processing (Brown et al., 2020; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021).
139
140   Deep generative models. Deep generative models, including language models, for producing new
141
      stable materials have been emerging in the last years (Xie et al., 2022; Lyngby & Thygesen, 2022;
      Sultanov et al., 2023; Yang & Mannodi-Kanakkithodi, 2022). However, only a few studies explored
142
      material properties and stability at the same time. Studies such as FTCP (Ren et al., 2022), Matter-
143
      Gen (Zeni et al., 2023), and UniMat (Yang et al., 2024b) focused on optimizing properties including
144   band gap and material stability. They are generative models and thus conduct property optimizations
145   within the framework of generative modeling. FTCP, based on Variational Autoencoders (Kingma,
146   2013), encodes crystal structures into latent variables. It employs prediction branches to predict
147   properties from these variables. MatterGen and UniMat are diffusion models and employ classifier-
148   free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2021) to generate materials with specific properties. Although meth-
149   ods exist to constrain condition-free models for generating specific outputs (Wu et al., 2024), no
150   research has implemented these techniques for crystal structures.
151   Bayesian Optimization. Black-box optimization, including Bayesian optimization, is widely used
152   in materials science (Song et al., 2024). Numerous studies in materials science apply Bayesian
153   optimization to predict crystal and molecular structures from target properties (Boyar et al., 2024;
154   Zhai et al., 2024; Khatamsaz et al., 2023). One representative method of Bayesian optimization
155   widely utilized in materials science research is the Gaussian process (GP) (Lu et al., 2022). However,
156   as the Gaussian process only handles continuous values, its ability to manage categorical variables
157   like elements is questionable. The recently proposed Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE), which
158
      is capable of handling categorical variables and multi-objective optimization (Ozaki et al., 2020b;
      2022) and has been utilized in materials science (Ozaki et al., 2020a), could be a better choice.

161   desired properties using deep learning-based predictors and their gradients have been applied across
      a wide range of fields. For example, they have been used to optimize designs for dynamics of

      3.1   M ASKS TO MAINTAIN ELECTRICAL NEUTRALITY
217
218   To maintain electrical neutrality, we restrict the possible values of atomic distribution an by using
219   a mask that aligns with the possible oxidation numbers at site n. These possible oxidation numbers
220   are determined from the initial structure. Here, we explain this using the rutile type structure as
221   an example. A typical material having this structure is titanium dioxide (TiO2 ). The rutile TiO2
222   contains one Ti site and two O sites, totaling three atomic sites. When titanium has an oxidation
223   number of +4 and the two oxygen atoms each have an oxidation number of −2, the total oxidation
224
      number is zero, thus achieving electrical neutrality. Therefore, to maintain electrical neutrality, we
      can use an atomic distribution that includes only elements with a +4 oxidation number, such as Ti
225
      and Mn at the Ti site. At the O sites, we use those with an oxidation number of −2, such as O and S.
226
      This ensures electrical neutrality regardless of the elements selected after optimization. The adjusted
227   atomic distribution a′n , which considers oxidation numbers, is obtained by taking the element-wise
228   product of the learnable distribution an with the atomic mask m(s).

      3.3   P RESERVATION OF S PECIFIC C RYSTAL S TRUCTURES DURING O PTIMIZATION
308
309   Limiting the optimization variables and their range allows us to maintain specific crystal structures
310   during optimization. For instance, let us consider a typical perovskite structure, represented by the
311   chemical formula ABX3 . It consists of five sites and adopts a crystal structure close to a cubic lattice.
312   The fractional coordinates for the five sites are as follows: (0.5, 0.5, 0.5) at the A site, (0.0, 0.0, 0.0)
313   at the B site, and (0.5, 0.0, 0.0), (0.0, 0.5, 0.0), (0.0, 0.0, 0.5) at the three X sites. Note that devia-
314   tions from these values are allowed, together with degrees of freedom related to the lattice constant
315
      values. We optimize the structures within the range of small perturbations applied to typical per-
      ovskites. Specifically, first, only the a, b, and c of l are optimized, while α, β, and γ are fixed at
316
      90°. Subsequently, the optimization range for the five sites is set close to their typical coordinates.
317
      For example, we optimize the coordinates at the A site within the range (0.5 ± ϵ, 0.5 ± ϵ, 0.5 ± ϵ),
318   where ϵ is a small constant. Following a previous work on the distortion of CaCu3 Ti4 O12 (Božin
319   et al., 2004), we set ϵ = 0.15. We also specify possible patterns of oxidation number combinations.
320   Typically, some materials with perovskite structure such as SrTiO3 exhibit oxidation numbers of
321   [+2, +4, −2] at the A, B, and X sites, respectively, while others such as (CH3 NH3 )PbI3 exhibit
322   [+1, +2, −1]. Consequently, two oxidation number patterns are prepared for the perovskite struc-
323   ture: [+2, +4, −2] and [+1, +2, −1] for the A, B, and X sites, respectively. By specifying these
      variables and ranges for optimization, we are able to maintain the perovskite structure.

      We compare SMOACS’s ability to propose material satisfying specified properties and constraints
      with those from deep generative models and Bayesian optimization, represented by FTCP and TPE,

332   transformer-based models, respectively. We evaluated the optimized materials using three metrics:
333   whether they satisfied the specified criteria on the band gap range, formation energy, and validity of
334   crystal structure. Specifically, we judge that the formation energy criterion is satisfied if it is less
335   than -0.5 eV, and for the validity of crystal structure, following a previous research (Xie et al., 2022),
336   we adopted two criteria: all interatomic distances being at least 0.5 Å and maintaining electrical
337   neutrality. For assessing electrical neutrality, we consider the material electrically neutral if the sum
338
      of possible oxidation numbers for atoms at each site equals zero. Please refer to Section A.9 for
      details. For experiments utilizing models trained on datasets other than MEGNet, please refer to
339
      Section A.13.

      ALIGNN or Crystalformer for property predictions. The results are shown in Table 1.
349
350   Crystalformer demonstrated the highest prediction accuracy among the models in Table 1. ALIGNN
351   ranked second, whereas the prediction branches of FTCP exhibited the lowest performance. This
352   result confirmed an advantage of SMOACS and TPE: their ability to incorporate state-of-the-art
353
      property prediction models, such as Crystalformer and ALIGNN, allowing for highly accurate ma-

510   5     C ONCLUSIONS
511
512   We propose SMOACS, a framework that utilizes the latest high-accuracy property prediction mod-
513   els and their gradients to search for materials with targeted multiple properties. SMOACS can
514   adaptively apply constraints such as electrical neutrality and specific crystal structures without re-
515
      training. SMOACS not only outperformed FTCP and TPE in optimizing multiple targeted properties
      simultaneously but also maintained electrical neutrality in large systems where calculating electri-
516
      cal neutrality is challenging due to combinatorial complexity. As a further potential application,
517
      SMOACS should facilitate the exploration of stable structures. Using a compositional formula and
518   various structure candidates they could form, this method minimizes formation energy while main-
519   taining the crystal structure, thus determining the most stable configuration for that formula (see
520   Section A.3). However, the performance of SMOACS heavily depends on the accuracy of prop-
521   erty prediction models. Using models based on DFT calculations that underestimate band gaps (see
522   Section A.2) can lead to similar underestimations in the predictions. By adopting more accurate
523   models trained on datasets that are large and developed with more accurate DFT, we may address
524   these challenges.

      A.1    D ETAILS OF THE S ETTINGS IN D ENSITY F UNCTIONAL T HEORY
813
814
      We used Density Functional Theory (DFT) to verify the band gaps of materials proposed by
815   SMOACS. We employed the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP) (Kresse & Joubert, 1999)
816   version 5.4.4 with Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) exchange-correlation functional (Perdew et al.,
817   1996) and Projector Augmented Wave (PAW) pseudo-potentials (Blöchl, 1994) in all DFT calcula-
818   tions. We used the MPRelaxSet from PyMatGen (Ong et al., 2013) to generate input files: KPOINTS
819   and INCAR.
820
821
      A.2    BAND G AP DISCREPANCIES BETWEEN MACHINE LEARNING PREDICTED AND DFT
822          CALCULATED
823
824   SMOACS heavily relies on the accuracy of property prediction models. However, we found dis-
825   crepancies between the values model predicted and the DFT calculated (Fig.A.1). Furthermore,
826   structures relaxed by the MPRelaxSet, which is a parameter set for structural relaxations with VASP
827   provided in PyMatGen, sometimes significantly differ from their proposed forms. There are two
828   possible reasons. First, DFT settings: MEGNet dataset comes from an older version of Materials
829
      Project database (Jain et al., 2013). Materials in this database are sometimes updated, and calculation
      conditions when the MEGNet dataset is created could be different from the current MPRelaxset. We
830
      could not reproduce the band gap values in the MEGNet dataset with MPRelaxset. Second, MEG-
831
      Net dataset features: All models used in this work are trained on the MEGNet dataset, which is
832   comprised predominantly of stable materials. So, predicting unstable or physically inappropriate
833   structures with these models can lead to inaccurate predictions that may affect the proposed mate-
834   rials. To address these issues, we may need a model trained on a large dataset that includes both
835   stable and unstable structures.
836
      The MEGNet dataset utilizes DFT calculations with PBE functionals that are known to underesti-
837
      mate band gaps. Consequently, when models trained on the MEGNet dataset are used in SMOACS,
838   this tendency may be reflected in the predictions of the proposed materials. This issue may be ad-
839   dressed by constructing a dataset using more accurate band gap calculations, such as HSE06 hybrid
840   functionals (Krukau et al., 2006) and adopting models trained on that dataset. It should be noted
841   that the amount of data available with these accurate calculations is much more limited than for
842   DFT-PBE.
843

845
846   Our method can optimize energy while specifying the base crystal structure. This property may
847   allow for identifying crystal structures based either solely on the chemical formula or on a combi-
848   nation of the chemical formula and physical properties. This is a Crystal Structure Prediction (CSP)
849   task (Ryan et al., 2018; Miller et al., 2024; Jiao et al., 2024). To verify if this is possible, we exper-
850   imented to see if the crystal structure of metallic silicon with a zero band gap could be identified.
851   Initially, we extracted structures from the MEGNet dataset that contained only one atom besides Si,
852   using them as the initial structure. The atomic distribution was fixed with a one-hot vector indicat-
853
      ing silicon, and only the lattice constants were optimized. The target properties for optimization
      were a zero band gap and formation energy minimization. We chose silicon structures from the
854
      MEGNet dataset with a band gap of 0 eV as the reference and compared these with the optimized
855
      structures that exhibited the lowest formation energy. Consequently, we identified structures close
856   to the reference among those optimized for the lowest formation energy.

1136
1137   As discussed in Section 3.3, due to the arbitrariness in the numerical values of the lattice con-
1138   stant and coordinates of perovskite structures, we evaluated whether the optimized structures ap-
1139   proximated typical perovskite configurations. First of all, fractional coordinates typical of per-
1140   ovskite structures are as follows: (0.5, 0.5, 0.5) at the A site, (0.0, 0.0, 0.0) at the B site, and
1141   (0.5, 0.0, 0.0), (0.0, 0.5, 0.0), (0.0, 0.0, 0.5) at the three X sites. We established criteria for the opti-
1142
       mized x, y, and z coordinates to be within a deviation ϵ from these standard values. The perovskite
       structure CaCu3 Ti4 O12 exhibits a slightly distorted configuration, with the x-coordinate of the oxy-
1143
       gen atoms deviating by approximately 10% from their typical positions (Božin et al., 2004). To ex-
1144
       plore new structures, we set ϵ = 0.15, allowing for a slightly greater distortion. We considered the
1145   optimized coordinates successful if the x, y, and z coordinates of each site fell within ±ϵ. Addition-
1146   ally, the angles between the crystal axes of typical perovskite structures are close to 90°. Therefore,
1147   angles between 85° and 95° were established as a criterion.
1148
       Using t values from typical perovskite structures (BaCeO3 :0.857, SrTiO3 : 0.910 and BaTiO3 :
1149
       0.970), we established a tolerance factor range of 0.8 ≤ t ≤ 1.0 as the criterion for success. The
1150
       ionic radius of the X site was calculated as the average of the radii of the three X sites. We took the
1151   values for the ionic radii from PyMatGen (Ong et al., 2013).
1152
1153   In this experiments, the learning rates were set as ηl = 0.01, ηC = 0.02, ηA = ηO = 6.0 for
1154
       SMOACS with Crystalformer. For SMOACS with ALIGNN, the learning rates were set as ηl =
       0.5, ηC = 0.002, ηA = ηO = 0.00008. We reconstructed the graph structure 46 times during the
1155
       optimizations. The learning rates were decayed using a cosine annealing schedule.
1156
1157   Due to the limited number of perovskite structure data points in the MEGNet dataset, we gener-
1158   ated 256 random perovskite structures as initial values for SMOACS. These structures have crys-
1159   tal axis angles α, β, and γ at 90° and axis lengths a, b, and c randomly generated between 2 Å
1160
       and 10 Å. Their initial fractional coordinates correspond to those typical of perovskite structures:
       (0.5, 0.5, 0.5) for the A site, (0.0, 0.0, 0.0) for the B site, and (0.5, 0.0, 0.0), (0.0, 0.5, 0.0), and
1161
       (0.0, 0.0, 0.5) for the three X sites. Similarly, TPE optimized perovskite structures by setting the
1162
       crystal axis angles at 90° and optimizing the axis lengths a, b, c between 2 Å and 10 Å. We also
1163
       limited element species for each site in TPE. Specifically, the elements are restricted by oxidation
1164   numbers: +1 and +2 for site A, +2 and +4 for site B, and −1 and −2 for site X. For FTCP, we
1165   initially selected data points where the crystal axis angles were at 90°, and all sites conformed to
1166   the typical fractional coordinates of perovskite structures; these were then converted into latent vari-
1167   ables. Subsequently, we applied noise using a normal distribution with a mean of 0 and a standard
1168   deviation of 0.6 to the latent variables. Finally, we decoded the latent variables back into crystal
1169   structures for evaluation.

       A.9   ELECTRICAL NEUTRALITY
1351
1352   In assessing electrical neutrality, a compound was considered neutral if the sum of the ox-
1353   idation numbers for the atoms at each site equaled zero. For example, Fe3 O4 is electri-
1354   cally neutral because the configuration [Fe, Fe, Fe, O, O, O, O] can assume oxidation numbers

1356   SMACT(Davies et al., 2019) to assess electrical neutrality; however, SMACT includes some ox-
1357   idation numbers, like the +7 state of chlorine, which are extremely rare and potentially less reliable.

       A.11    G ENERATION OF OXIDATION NUMBER PATTERNS
1513
1514   In SMOACS, realistic oxidation number patterns are generated based on the compositions of
1515   initial crystal structures. Here, we explain this using RuN (mp-1009770). According to
1516   icsd_oxidation_state in PyMatGen, ruthenium (Ru) and nitrogen (N) can adopt oxidation
1517   numbers of {+2, +3, +4, +5, +6} and {+1, +3, +5, −1, −2, −3}, respectively. Therefore, elec-
1518   trical neutrality in RuN is achieved when the oxidation number combinations for Ru and N are
1519   (+2, −2) or (+3, −3). Consequently, when using RuN (mp-1009770) as the initial structure, ox-
1520
       idation number combination patterns of (+2, −2) and (+3, −3) are obtained, and corresponding
       masks are generated for each.
1521
1522   To consider a broader range of oxidation number combinations, we utilized the intersection of oxi-
1523   dation numbers from ”smact” and ”icsd”, as listed in Table A.7. It should be noted that even when
1524   generating oxidation number patterns from ”smact” and ”icsd”, electrical neutrality is maintained
1525   by applying site-specific elemental constraints using the oxidation numbers in the ”Ours” column of
1526
       Table A.7.
