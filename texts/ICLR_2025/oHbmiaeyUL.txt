009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In flow and diffusion-based generative modeling, conventional methods rely on
012
               unidimensional coefficients for the trajectory of differential equations. In this
013            work, we first introduce a multidimensional coefficient that generalizes the con-
014            ventional unidimensional coefficient into multiple dimensions. We also propose
015            a new problem called multidimensional trajectory optimization, which suggests
016            a novel trajectory optimality determined by the final transportation quality rather
017            than predefined properties like straightness. Our approach pre-trains flow and dif-
018            fusion models with various coefficients sampled from a hypothesis space and sub-
019            sequently optimizes inference trajectories through adversarial training of a gener-
020            ator comprising the flow or diffusion model and the parameterized coefficient. To
021
               empirically validate our method, we conduct experiments on various generative
               models, including EDM and Stochastic Interpolant, across multiple datasets such
022
               as 2D synthetic datasets, CIFAR-10, FFHQ, and AFHQv2. Remarkably, inference
023
               using our optimized multidimensional trajectory achieves significant performance
024            improvements with low NFE (e.g., 5), achieving state-of-the-art results in CIFAR-
025            10 conditional generation. The introduction of multidimensional trajectory opti-
026            mization enhances model efficiency and opens new avenues for exploration in flow
027            and diffusion-based generative modeling.

      We reintroduce trajectory adaptability by employing simulation dynamics combined with adversar-
055   ial training (Goodfellow et al., 2014), defining trajectory optimality based solely on the final gen-
056   erative output under fixed solver configurations. Specifically, first, we pre-train a diffusion model
057   Hθ with randomly sampled multidimensional coefficients γ from a well-designed hypothesis space.
058   This pre-training enables the flow or diffusion model to handle various coefficients, preparing it for
059   the trajectory optimization stage. Next, we introduce the parameterized adaptive multidimensional
060   coefficient γϕ to compose a flow or diffusion-based generator Gθ,ϕ , which produces xest,θ,ϕ through
061   simulation dynamics. A discriminator Dψ evaluates the generated samples xest,θ,ϕ to optimize both
062   θ and ϕ, a process we term multidimensional trajectory optimization.
063   To effectively leverage the advantages of simulation-based objectives which lie on adaptability and
064   flexibility of trajectories while mitigating their inefficiencies in training, we use simulation-based
065   objectives only for ϕ after pre-training θ with a simulation-free objective, making trajectory opti-
066   mization feasible in terms of efficiency and scalability. Our experiments demonstrate that trajecto-
067   ries optimized through this approach significantly improve the performance of flow and diffusion
068   models. In summary, our main contributions are as follows:
069         1. By introducing the concept of an adaptive multidimensional coefficient in flow and diffu-
070            sion, we lay the groundwork for complete trajectory flexibility.
071         2. We address a novel problem—multidimensional trajectory optimization—leveraging the
072            increased flexibility provided by the adaptive multidimensional coefficient. This introduces
073            an optimality concept based solely on the end-to-end final transportation quality rather than
074            on pre-defined properties of the trajectory.
075         3. We propose a solution to the multidimensional trajectory optimization problem using ad-
076             versarial training to discover adaptive multidimensional trajectories for efficient inference.
077   By introducing adaptive multidimensional trajectories, our work alleviates the constraints on tra-
078   jectories in flow and diffusion models and opens new avenues for future research and applications.

080   2   R ELATED W ORKS
081
      Trajectory Optimizations in Flow and Diffusion Various trajectory optimization approaches
082
      pre-define optimality without relying on final transportation quality. Approaches such as Liu et al.
083   (2023); Tong et al. (2024) define straightness as the optimality criterion and optimize trajectories
084   by maintaining the consistency of (x0 , x1 ) for training flow and diffusion models, aligning with
085   an optimal transport perspective. Another example is Singhal et al. (2023), which defines optimal-
086   ity through a fixed sequence of diffusion steps intended to reduce inference complexity rather than
087   focusing on the quality of final samples. Additionally, Bartosh et al. (2024) introduces neural flow
088   models that implicitly set trajectory optimality within the diffusion process, aiming to generate high-
089   quality samples without explicit trajectory adjustment post-training. There are also approaches that
090   refine trajectories after training, such as Albergo et al. (2024), where optimality is defined by min-
091
      imizing the trajectory length in the Wasserstein-2 metric, focusing on a shortest-distance criterion.
      Despite these diverse perspectives on trajectory optimality, there are two significant differences be-
092
      tween these methods and ours. First, we calculate the optimality of the trajectory solely based on the
093
      final transportation quality, which is a crucial factor in generative modeling. Second, none of these
094   methods achieve full flexibility of the trajectory on two fronts: multidimensionality and adaptability
095   with respect to different inference trajectories.
096
097   Diffusion Distillation for Few-Step Generation There are two main approaches to diffusion dis-
098   tillation: non-adversarial and adversarial. Non-adversarial methods, like Yin et al. (2024); Song &
099
      Dhariwal (2023); Geng et al. (2024); Berthelot et al. (2023), focus on 1-step distillation techniques
      without adversarial objectives. These approaches aim to simplify training by leveraging distribu-
100
      tional losses and equilibrium models, effectively distilling the diffusion process without involving
101
      a GAN discriminator. Conversely, adversarial approaches to diffusion distillation, such as Zheng
102   & Yang (2024); Xu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023), employ a GAN-like discriminator to enhance
103   sample quality by learning distribution consistency in an adversarial setting. Additionally, Luo et al.
104   (2024) propose Diff-Instruct, which transfers knowledge from pre-trained diffusion models through
105   a GAN-based framework, closely resembling GAN approaches in training dynamics. Also, Kim
106   et al. (2024) developed Consistency Trajectory Models (CTM), which generalize Song et al. (2023)
107   for efficient sampling with the assistance of a discriminator. While our method shares similarities
      in achieving few-step generation, there is a key difference: distillation in these works is not aimed

      at trajectory optimization. Our method optimizes both θ and ϕ, representing the diffusion model
109   parameters and adaptive multidimensional coefficient parameters, with respect to different inference
110   trajectories, thereby enabling adaptive multidimensional trajectory optimization. In contrast, dis-
111   tillation typically targets a fixed teacher network, limiting flexibility in optimizing the trajectory.

145   4.1     D EFINITION OF A DAPTIVE M ULTIDIMENSIONAL C OEFFICIENT
146
147   We introduce the multidimensional coefficient, γ(t) = [γ0 (t), γ1 (t)] ∈ R2×d , which generalizes the
148   conventional unidimensional coefficient by extending it to higher dimensions.
149   Definition 1 (Multidimensional Coefficient) γ(t) = [γ0 (t), γ1 (t)] ∈ R2×d for t ∈ [0, T ] defines
150   the trajectory x(t) = γ0 (t) ⊙ x0 + γ1 (t) ⊙ x1 , where x0 , x1 ∈ Rd . γ(t) must satisfy: γ(t) ∈ [0, T ],
151   γ0 (0) = 1d , γ0 (T ) = kd , γ1 (0) = 0d , γ1 (T ) = Td , and γ ∈ C 1 ([0, T ], R2×d ). Here, k ∈ [0, T ]
152   and id denotes a d-dimensional vector filled with the value i.
153
      γ ∈ C 1 ([0, T ], R2×d ) indicates that γ is continuously first-order differentiable with respect to t on
154   the interval [0, T ]. Boundary conditions written above ensure that x(t) becomes x0 and xT for t = 0
155   and t = T , which is a requirement for transportation. Values k and T for boundary conditions vary
156   based on the task. For example, in image translation tasks where both distributions ρ0 and ρ1 are data
157   distributions, k = 0 and T = 1 might be appropriate. The unidimensional coefficient α is a special
                                                  ′ ′
158   case of γ when all elements γ i,j (t) = γ i ,j (t) for any indices i, j, i′ , j ′ in γ(t). We visualize α and γ
159   in Figure 1 for better comprehension. The above definition of the multidimensional coefficient uses
160   the same coefficient with respect to the trajectory. However, we can consider a multidimensional
161   coefficient γ parameterized by ϕ, allowing adaptation to different inference trajectories xθ,ϕ (t) for
      inference times τ = {t0 , . . . , tN }, where θ represents the flow or diffusion model parameters:

163   xθ,ϕ (t), the adaptive multidimensional coefficient γϕ (t, xθ,ϕ (t)) : [0, T ] × Rd → R2×d is parame-

      4.2   M ULTIDIMENSIONAL T RAJECTORY O PTIMIZATION : D EFINITION AND P RACTICE
172
173
      A key aspect of our perspective is that the quality of a trajectory cannot be fully evaluated until the
      entire transportation process is completed. This contrasts with existing views on trajectory optimal-
174
      ity, which pre-define properties for the trajectory without simulation, as seen in works such as Liu
175
      et al. (2023); Tong et al. (2024); Singhal et al. (2023); Bartosh et al. (2024). For example, reducing
176   the total trajectory length for transportation, as in the optimal transport (OT) perspective, results in
177   straight trajectories that can indirectly reduce NFE since a straight line minimizes numerical errors
178   when solving differential equations. However, in generative tasks, the real cost is not trajectory
179   length but the NFE required to achieve a certain sample quality. Thus, the optimality for generative
180   models should align more closely with final sample quality, evaluated through simulation, rather
181   than pre-defined properties. Based on this principle, we define trajectory optimality as follows:
182
      Definition 3 (Multidimensional Trajectory Optimization (MTO)) Consider a flow and diffusion-
183   based generator Gθ,ϕ with fixed configurations (NFE, discretization method, etc.), where θ rep-
184   resents the flow or diffusion model parameters and ϕ is the parameter for the adaptive multidi-
185   mensional coefficient γϕ (t, xT ) = [γ0,ϕ (t, xT ), γ1,ϕ (t, xT )] ∈ R2×d . Then the multidimensional
186   trajectory optimization problem is:
187
188
                                        θ∗ , ϕ∗ = arg min D (ρ1 , ρ̂1,θ,ϕ ) ,                             (5)
                                                     θ,ϕ
189
190   where ρ̂1,θ,ϕ denotes the generated distribution from Gθ,ϕ , and D measures a divergence metric.
191
      Given that the trajectory xθ,ϕ (t) from Gθ,ϕ is entirely parameterized by θ and ϕ, we have full
192   controllability over the trajectory by adjusting θ and ϕ, allowing us to term this process a trajectory
193
      optimization. This optimization can be approximated θ∗ , ϕ∗ ≈ θ̂∗ , ϕ̂∗ using a finite set of samples.
194   In this view, we do not guarantee pre-defined properties for the optimized trajectory xθ∗ ,ϕ∗ (t), which
195   underscores our perspective on optimality. Our definition of optimality is based solely on the quality
196   of the final sample for given differential equation-solving configurations, rather than pre-defined
197   properties of the trajectory itself.
198
      Practical Approach for MTO in High-Dimensional Transportation To solve MTO in high-
199
      dimensional datasets like images using conventional approaches, there are two potential strategies.
200   The first involves simulation-based training, such as CNF (Chen et al., 2019), which is inefficient in
201   terms of both training cost and performance. The second strategy involves the conventional diffusion
202   approach, trained with a fixed single ϕ, which would require training multiple models θ1 , . . . , θl
203   with corresponding coefficients ϕ1 , . . . , ϕl and then selecting the optimal θ and ϕ. This process is
204   computationally intractable. To address these challenges, we propose the following procedure:
205         1. Design the hypothesis space of the adaptive multidimensional coefficient γϕ heuristi-
206            cally: Leverage prior knowledge to identify an appropriate space.
207
208
            2. Pre-train flow or diffusion models θ to handle various multidimensional coefficients
               sampled from the hypothesis space.
209
210         3. Jointly optimize θ and ϕ using simulation dynamics and adversarial training: θ and ϕ
211            in Gθ,ϕ aim to deceive discriminator Dψ .
212   This approach appropriately balances the advantages and disadvantages of simulation-based and
213   simulation-free methods to achieve both trajectory flexibility and training efficiency: employing
214   simulation-based objectives exclusively for ϕ after pre-training θ with various γ. By following
215   this procedure, we aim to converge to θ∗ and ϕ∗ that can generate high-quality samples efficiently
      compared to unoptimized trajectories.

      6 C ONCLUSIONS
511   In this work, we extend the conventional use of unidimensional coefficients in flow and diffusion
512
      models by introducing adaptive multidimensional coefficients. We present the problem of Multidi-
      mensional Trajectory Optimization, which aims to identify adaptive trajectories that improve gener-
513
      ative performance under fixed solver configurations and a specified starting point of the differential
514
      equation. This approach introduces a different perspective on trajectory optimality, focusing on the
515   quality of the final transportation outcome rather than pre-defined properties of the trajectory, such
516   as straightness. Our proposed solution pre-trains flow and diffusion models with various coefficients
517   to prepare for MTO, and utilizes simulation dynamics combined with adversarial training to perform
518   MTO. This approach effectively learns multidimensional trajectories, as validated through experi-
519   ments across various generative tasks and datasets. These experiments demonstrate that our method
520   identifies more efficient trajectories, leading to significant performance improvements in transporta-
521   tion tasks. Importantly, this work achieves full trajectory flexibility and adaptability through end-
522   to-end adversarial training—previously only achievable with the high training costs of simulation-
523
      based objectives—while preserving training efficiency. By enhancing the performance of flow and
      diffusion models, we hope this work inspires further exploration and advancements in this field.
524
525   7         L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
526   First, since we use coefficient labeling (Section 4.3) for the diffusion model, the model structure dif-
527   fers from existing pre-trained flow and diffusion models. As a result, training models from scratch
528   is required, which can be cumbersome. This issue could be mitigated in future works by replac-
529   ing a few layers from well pre-trained model and using it as initialization for pre-training. Second,
530   γϕ is tied to the specific sampling configuration used during MTO, limiting its flexibility for in-
531
      ference under alternative configurations. Future work could address this by conditioning γϕ on
      diverse sampling configurations, enabling better adaptability and efficiency. Third, refining the de-
532
      sign of γϕ could improve efficiency. As shown in Table 3 and Table 4, our method’s FID is higher
533
      than distillation methods for larger datasets, potentially due to using the same model size and NFE
534   configurations as smaller datasets like CIFAR-10. Optimizing γϕ for larger datasets could reduce
535   model size and NFE requirements while maintaining performance. Lastly, while MTO empirically
536   demonstrates improved performance across datasets, its theoretical foundation remains unexplored.
537   A potential connection lies with Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022), where
538   MTO’s adaptive trajectories resemble the space warping in LDM. Unlike LDM, which compresses
539   latent space, MTO achieves warping without altering dimensionality, offering a novel perspective
      on trajectory optimization. We hope these limitations inspire further research in this area.
