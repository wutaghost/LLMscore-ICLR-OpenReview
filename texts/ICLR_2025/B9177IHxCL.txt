009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011                Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated im-
012                pressive performance in generating molecular structures as drug candidates, which
013                offers significant potential to accelerate drug discovery. However, the current
014                LLMs overlook a critical requirement for drug discovery: proposing a diverse set
015                of molecules. This diversity is essential for improving the chances of finding a
016                viable drug, as it provides alternative molecules that may succeed where others
017
                   fail in wet-lab or clinical validations. Despite such a need for diversity, the LLMs
                   often output structurally similar molecules from a given prompt. While decod-
018
                   ing schemes like beam search may enhance textual diversity, this often does not
019
                   align with molecular structural diversity. In response, we propose a new method
020                for fine-tuning molecular generative LLMs to autoregressively generate a set of
021                structurally diverse molecules, where each molecule is generated by conditioning
022                on the previously generated molecules. Our approach consists of two stages: (1)
023                supervised fine-tuning to adapt LLMs to autoregressively generate molecules in a
024                sequence and (2) reinforcement learning to maximize structural diversity within the
025                generated molecules. Our experiments show that (1) our fine-tuning approach en-
026                ables the LLMs to better discover diverse molecules compared to existing decoding
027                schemes and (2) our fine-tuned model outperforms other representative LLMs in
028
                   generating diverse molecules, including the ones fine-tuned on chemical domains.

082   In response, we explore the use of LLMs for diverse molecular generation. We begin by identifying
083
      the limitations of recent LLMs (Ye et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023) and decoding schemes (Vijayakumar
      et al., 2018; Su et al., 2022) in generating diverse molecules. Then, we present a new method for
084
      fine-tuning LLMs to generate diverse molecules. Our approach can be broadly applied to other
085
      LLM-based candidate design problems, e.g., computer-aided design (Wu et al., 2023a).
086
087   Existing LLMs have limitations in generating diverse molecules. To obtain diverse molecules, one
088   may consider querying the recently developed generalist LLMs, e.g., Llama (Touvron et al., 2023)
089
      or ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023). However, our empirical observation in Figure 3(a) reveals that even
      the most recent models produce structurally identical or highly similar molecules from the given
090
      prompt.2 This observation aligns with previous observations that have shown LLMs may fail to
091
      generate diverse outputs (Kirk et al., 2024) for general text-based domains.
092
093   Decoding schemes for diversified generation do not align with molecular diversity. We also
094   acknowledge the existence of decoding schemes, e.g., diverse beam search (Vijayakumar et al., 2018)
095
      or contrastive beam search (Su et al., 2022), which have been proposed to improve the diversity of
      output sequences generated by LLMs. However, these decoding schemes are limited to improving the
096
      textual diversity which often does not correspond to molecular structural diversity, e.g., there exist
097
      many SMILES or SELFIES strings that correspond to the same molecule, as illustrated in Figure 3(b).
098
099   Our approach. We repurpose existing molecular generative LLMs to autoregressively generate a
100   diverse set of molecules from a single prompt. By enabling the LLMs to generate a new molecule
101
      conditioned on previously generated molecules, we expect the LLMs to learn to enhance the structural
      diversity between the generated molecules. To this end, we propose a two-stage approach to fine-tune
102
      LLMs: (a) a supervised fine-tuning stage to repurpose LLMs to autoregressively generate a sequence
103
      of multiple molecules and (b) a reinforcement learning stage to maximize the molecular structural
104   diversity. Note that both stages do not require external diverse molecular datasets and are purely based
105   on self-improvement procedures, where the LLMs train on the samples generated by themselves.

      In the supervised training stage, we train the LLMs to autoregressively generate a set of molecules
109   in a single sequence. The training dataset, i.e., a set of molecules, is collected from the LLMs
110   themselves through iterative sampling, and then filtered to enhance the quality, e.g., removing invalid
111   molecules. However, this stage does not necessarily incorporate molecular diversity, as the training
112   may not involve sufficiently distinct molecules (e.g., limitations in Figure 3(b)). To tackle this, we
113   subsequently apply reinforcement learning with exploration towards discovering diverse molecules.
114
      Next, in the reinforcement learning stage, we train LLMs to maximize the diversity of molecules
115   within a generated sequence. However, for our task, conventional sequence-wise reinforcement
116   learning (Ouyang et al., 2022) suffers from the credit assignment problem (Zhou et al., 2024): the
117   challenges in identifying and promoting the generation of molecules responsible for increasing
118   diversity, among a larger set of molecules in the sequence. To resolve this issue, we solve multi-stage
119   molecule generation problems for a sequence of molecules, where the generation of each molecule
120   aims to maximize the diversity with respect to the previously generated molecules. We train LLMs to
121   maximize the associated rewards using proximal policy optimization (Schulman et al., 2017).
122   We compare our method with the decoding schemes for diversified generation (Vijayakumar et al.,
123   2016; Su et al., 2022) and other representative LLMs, including chemical-task specialists (Edwards
124   et al., 2022; Christofidellis et al., 2023; Pei et al., 2023; 2024), fine-tuned generalists on chemical
125   domains (Fang et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024), and the ChatGPT series (OpenAI, 2023; 2024). We
126   observe that (1) our fine-tuning approach enables LLMs to better discover diverse molecules compared
127   to existing decoding schemes and (2) our fine-tuned LLM outperforms other recent LLMs.
128   To conclude, our contributions can be summarized as follows:
129
      • We are the first to explore the use of LLMs for generating diverse molecules.
130
131   • We first propose a fine-tuning approach for LLMs to generate diverse solutions, which presents a
132
        new direction distinct from existing approaches focused on the decoding scheme.
133   • Experimentally, our method outperforms the baselines in generating diverse molecular structures.

135   2       R ELATED WORK
136
137   Large language models (LLMs) for molecular generation. Recent advancements in LLMs have
138   shown increasing promise in scientific applications, especially for molecular generation (Edwards
139   et al., 2022; Pei et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2024; Pei et al., 2024). First, Edwards et al. (2022) pro-
140   posed MolT5 which translates between SMILES (Weininger, 1988) and molecular text descriptions.
141   Text+Chem T5 (Christofidellis et al., 2023) is a model pre-trained on both the chemical and natural
142   language domains. Next, BioT5 (Pei et al., 2023) considers T5 models pre-trained on datasets
143   including bio-text, protein sequences, and molecules. Additionally, Ye et al. (2023), Fang et al.
144
      (2024), and Yu et al. (2024) fine-tuned generalist LLMs, e.g., Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), through
      biological instructions, molecular modifications, and large-scale molecular datasets, respectively.
145
146   Decoding schemes for generating diverse output sequences. To generate diverse and high-quality
147   solution candidates from LLMs, existing literature on LLMs has studied improving decoding schemes.
148   To acquire multiple distinct sequences with high likelihoods, one can consider employing beam
149   search, which jointly decodes multiple distinct outputs (Och, 2003). To enhance diversity between
150
      sequences, Vijayakumar et al. (2016; 2018) incorporated token-wise differences between generated
      sequences in the beam search. Furthermore, Su et al. (2022) considered the contrast between the
151
      candidate sequences. In addition, Holtzman et al. (2020) proposed nucleus sampling, which enhances
152
      random sampling by balancing the quality and the diversity.
153
154   Reinforcement learning (RL) for fine-tuning LLMs. RL has been effectively applied to fine-
155   tune LLMs, aligning them with desired behaviors expressed through reward signals. One notable
156
      example is RL from human feedback to align LLMs with human preference (Ouyang et al., 2022). In
      addition, there has been a surge in research on devising RL for LLMs as well, such as addressing
157
      multi-turn settings (Shani et al., 2024) and incorporating multiple fine-grained reward signals (Wu
158
      et al., 2023b). For molecular generation, Ghugare et al. (2024) proposed RL-based fine-tuning to
159   generate a molecule satisfying target properties. However, to the best of our knowledge, there exist
160   no prior RL-based approaches that aim to increase the diversity of LLM-generated outputs.3

184   3     M ETHOD
185
186   In this section, we present our method for fine-tuning LLMs to generate diverse molecules. Specifi-
187   cally, we consider applying fine-tuning to existing molecular generative LLMs that produce molecular
188   representations such as SMILES or SELFIES. Importantly, our approach is versatile and can be
189   broadly applied to other domains, e.g., protein sequence (Zhuo et al., 2024) or computer-aided design
190
      (Wu et al., 2023a). Furthermore, it leverages self-improvement techniques and does not require
      additional datasets containing diverse molecules.
191
192   Overview. Our goal is to generate a sequence of structurally diverse molecules from a given prompt
193   by producing them in a single concatenated output. To achieve this, we fine-tune the LLMs in
194   two stages: (a) a supervised fine-tuning phase that repurposes the LLMs to generate a sequence of
195   molecules rather than a single one, and (b) a reinforcement learning phase aimed at further enhancing
196
      the structural diversity among the generated molecules.
197   Task details. In detail, we consider generating molecules from a prompt pdesc , where the prompt
198   describes a molecular property that the generated molecules should possess. In this setting, we aim to
199   generate diverse molecules that satisfy the given description pdesc . Here, the diversity is evaluated
200   using similarity measures between the structural features of the molecules, e.g., the presence of
201   specific atoms, or substructures (Bajusz et al., 2015).4 We let P denote the prompts used for training.

      5    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we identify the limitations of large language models (LLMs) for generating diverse
534   molecules. Then, we present new supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning methods to adapt
535   existing molecular generative LLMs to generate a diverse set of molecules. Experiments show that
536   our approach enables LLMs to better discover diverse molecules compared to the existing approaches.
537   An interesting avenue for future work is to develop a concrete benchmark for text-guided diverse
538   molecular generation, as current ChEBI-20 datasets were originally designed for a single molecular
539   generation. Another interesting avenue is to reduce the space and time complexity in generating the
      sequence of molecules, for example, based on set encoding techniques (Zaheer et al., 2017).

      A     A DDITIONAL RELATED WORKS
757
758
      A.1    R EINFORCEMENT LEARNING (RL) FOR DIVERSITY IN MOLECULAR GENERATION
759
760   Existing literature has studied RL-based methods to generate molecules with desired properties
761   while enhancing their diversity. First, Blaschke et al. (2020); Pereira et al. (2021) introduced
762   memory-assisted RL, which penalizes the reward of a molecule when it is highly similar to the
763   molecules stored in the memory unit. He et al. (2024) also incorporated RL with a diversity penalty
764   in transformer-based architectures for molecular generation. In addition, Hu et al. (2024) leveraged
765
      multiple GPT-based agents trained with RL to encourage these agents to explore diverse directions
      for discovering diverse molecules. Their algorithms are designed to discover diverse molecules with
766
      a fixed target property. In contrast, our work fine-tunes LLMs to generate diverse molecules given a
767
      prompt that is flexible to describe various target properties.
768
769
      A.2    M OLECULAR SIMILARITY MEASURES
770
771   In this section, we explain the measures for evaluating the similarity between two molecules. These
772   measures are used to define the reward of reinforcement learning (Appendix B) and diversity metrics
773   (Appendix A.3). Specifically, the molecular similarity is evaluated with their Morgan fingerprint
774   (Rogers & Hahn, 2010), which is a vector characterizing the presence of specific atoms, bonds, or
775   substructures. Then, the similarity between two molecules is typically evaluated as follows::
776                                                       |f (mi ) ∩ f (mi )|
777                                      T (mi , mj ) =                       ,
                                                          |f (mi ) ∪ f (mj )|
778
779   where f (mi ) maps the molecule mi to its Morgan fingerprint. This similarity T (mi , mj ) is referred
780   to as the Tanimoto similarity between mi and mj , which focuses on evaluating structural similarity.
781   Other similarity measures. To evaluate the molecular similarity, one can use other measures for
782   computing the similarities between two fingerprints. For example, Dice and cosine similarities
783   (Bajusz et al., 2015) are defined as follows:
784
785

                     D(mi , mj ) =                         ,   C(mi , mj ) =                         ,
786                                  |f (mi )| + |f (mj )|                         |f (mi )||f (mj |
787   where ⟨·, ·⟩ denotes a dot product between two vectors.

789   A.3    M OLECULAR DIVERSITY METRICS
790
791   In this section, we provide a detailed explanation of the diversity metrics for evaluating the given set
792   of molecules. Specifically, we explain two diversity metrics: the number of circles (Xie et al., 2023)
793   and the internal diversity (Polykovskiy et al., 2020).
794   The number of circles (NCircles.; Xie et al., 2023). To evaluate the diversity of a given set of
795   molecules M, this computes the size of the largest subset of molecules in which no two molecules
796   are similar to each other. Specifically, this metric is defined with a Tanimoto similarity T (·, ·) and a
797   similarity threshold h as follows:
798
                           NCirclesh = maxC⊆M |C|          s.t. T (x, y) < h, ∀x ̸= y ∈ C,                  (2)
799
800   where C is a subset of molecules. Every pair of molecules in C should have a similarity lower than
801   h. The high NCircles value implies that the given set of molecules M is diverse and covers a wide
802   range of molecular space.
803   Internal diversity (IntDiv.; Polykovskiy et al., 2020). Given a set of molecules M, this metric
804   measures the average of pair-wise Tanimoto similarities to evaluate the overall diversity. Specifically,
805   the IntDiv. is defined as follows:
806                                                    |M| |M|
807                                            1       X    X
                             Intdiv. =                          (1 − T (mi , mj )),                         (3)
808                                    |M| · (|M| − 1) i=1 j=i+1
809
      where mi is i-th molecule in the given set of molecules M.

814   Dataset Collection. To fine-tune BioT5+ and MolT5 (Sections 4.1 and 4.2), we collect T = 100

817   For the fine-tuning of DrugAssist (Section 4.3), we collect T = 300 molecules using beam search
818   for each training prompt. Note that the collected molecules were filtered to remove invalid string
819   representations, duplicated molecules, and molecules that do not satisfy the given description. The
820   invalid string representations are evaluated with RDKit package (Landrum, 2016). Additionally, the
821   collected molecules are concatenated into a single sequence m1 || · · · ||mK . Note that two molecules
822   are separated by introducing a newline character (‘\n’).
823   Supervised learning. We consider four NVIDIA A100 GPUs for supervised fine-tuning.

        8-batch size, 5e − 4 learning rate, 0.05 warm-up ratio, and apply a cosine learning scheduler. The
        maximum sequence length in supervised training is limited to 2560 due to memory limitations.
