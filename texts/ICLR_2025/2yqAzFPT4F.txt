011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Jailbreaking methods, which induce Multi-modal Large Language Models
014            (MLLMs) to output harmful responses, raise significant safety concerns. Among
015
               these methods, gradient-based approaches, which use gradients to generate
               malicious prompts, have been widely studied due to their high success rates in
016
               white-box settings, where full access to the model is available. However, these
017
               methods have notable limitations: they require white-box access, which is not
018            always feasible, and involve high memory usage. To address scenarios where
019            white-box access is unavailable, attackers often resort to transfer attacks. In
020            transfer attacks, malicious inputs generated using white-box models are applied
021            to black-box models, but this typically results in reduced attack performance. To
022            overcome these challenges, we propose Zer0-Jack, a method that bypasses the
023            need for white-box access by leveraging zeroth-order optimization. We propose
024            patch coordinate descent to efficiently generate malicious image inputs to directly
025            attack black-box MLLMs, which significantly reduces memory usage further.
026
               Through extensive experiments, Zer0-Jack achieves a high attack success
               rate across various models, surpassing previous transfer-based methods and
027
               performing comparably with existing white-box jailbreak techniques. Notably,
028
               Zer0-Jack achieves a 95% attack success rate on MiniGPT-4 with the Harmful
029            Behaviors Multi-modal Dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness. Additionally, we
030            show that Zer0-Jack can directly attack commercial MLLMs such as GPT-4o.
031            Codes are provided in the supplement.
032
033            Warning: This paper contains examples of harmful language and images,
034            and reader discretion is recommended.

      modal Large Language Models (MLLMs), which handle both text and image inputs, have gained
040
      popularity (Liu et al., 2024b; Zhu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a). Despite their capabilities in tasks
041
      such as image descriptions and visual question answering, MLLMs have been shown to be even
042   more vulnerable to jailbreak attacks due to the additional modality (Qi et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024;
043   Liu et al., 2024c; Zhao et al., 2024). For example, Liu et al. (2023a) demonstrated that images
044   containing specific text can assist in jailbreaking MLLMs. In white-box settings, where full access
045   to model parameters is available, methods like generating malicious image inputs (Niu et al., 2024)
046   or combining both text and image prompts (Shayegani et al., 2023) by optimization have proven
047   effective in bypassing safety mechanisms. Similar to LLM jailbreaking, the most effective methods
048   in MLLMs rely on calculating gradients to find inputs that induce harmful outputs.
049   While gradient-based methods for white-box models have shown strong performance, the challenge
050   of attacking black-box models remains underexplored. Black-box models, such as commercial
051   MLLMs like GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), do not provide access to their internal parameters, mak-
052   ing gradient-based attacks impossible. Most existing jailbreak methods for black-box models rely
053   on transfer attacks, where malicious inputs generated on white-box models are used to indirectly
      attack black-box models (Zou et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2023). However, these

      visual capabilities, are more vulnerable to malicious inputs (Liu et al., 2024c). Jailbreak methods
137   for Multi-modal LLMs (MLLMs) can be broadly categorized into white-box and black-box settings.
138   In the white-box setting, attackers have full access to model parameters, allowing for more direct

140   sarial visual prompts (Niu et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2023; Bailey et al., 2023; Tao

      fully designed prompts. Techniques such as using topic-related images or embedding text within

146
      2024). Transfer-based attacks involve generating adversarial prompts on a white-box model and
147

148   (2023) tested the transferability of visual adversarial prompts on closed-source MLLMs. However,
149   transfer-based attacks generally suffer from reduced success rates compared to white-box meth-
150   ods (Niu et al., 2024). Our work addresses this limitation by proposing a direct black-box jailbreak
151   method using zeroth-order optimization. This approach eliminates the need for transferability or
152   handcrafted prompts, focusing on efficiently generating malicious images to attack MLLMs with
153   reduced memory usage and high success rates even under black-box settings. We also provide a
154   detailed comparison with previous black-box methods in adversarial attack area in Appendix B.

      3.1   PRELIMINARY
163
164   The general goal of jailbreaking attacks in LLMs is inducing LLMs to output unsafe or malicious
165   responses. For example, a LLM with good safety alignment should not generate a detailed response
166   to the query ‘How to build a bomb’, while jailbreaking attacks aim at making the LLM output the an-
167   swer to this query. Similar to some adversarial attacks in NLP (Wallace et al., 2019), gradient-based
168   jailbreaking attacks try to find specific suffix tokens that make LLMs output malicious responses.
169   For example, a new query from attackers might be ‘How to build a bomb. !!!!!!!!!’, which can
170
      actually induce LLMs to output the detailed procedures of how to make a bomb.
171   However, unlike adversarial attacks, where the target is to output the same answer and reduce the
172   accuracy when the suffix is added to the prompt (Wallace et al., 2019), the jailbreaking attackers
173   hope LLMs can output true answers to their unsafe query. Besides, there are usually multiple true
174   answers to the query in jailbreaking and thus it is not possible to find suffix tokens by optimizing the
175   output towards one true answer.
176   To tackle the problem, one of the most popular jailbreaking methods, Greedy Coordinate Gradient
177   (GCG) (Zou et al., 2023) tries to find suffix tokens that induce LLMs to output their answer starts
178   with ‘Sure, here is’. Then if the language model could output this context at the beginning of the
179   response instead of refusing to the question, it is highly possible for language models to continue
180   the completion with the precise answer to the question.
181   In detail, the optimization problem in GCG can be formulated as:
182
183
                                                  min            L(x1:n ),                                (1)
                                             xI ∈{1,...,V }|I|

185   where xI is the suffix tokens, x1:n represents the original prompts and L(x1:n ) is the loss function:

188   Where x∗n+1:n+H represents the target beginning of the answer such as ‘Sure, here is’.

      Right now, GCG has a clear optimization target. However, GCG still needs to tackle the discrete
190
      optimization problem to generate discrete tokens. To do so, GCG uses a greedy coordinate gradient-
191
      based search. Specifically, GCG computes the gradient with respect to the one-hot vector represent-
192   ing the current value of the i-th token and selects top-k tokens with the highest norm of gradient.
193   Then GCG computes the loss for each token to get the final generated token.

285   Target Models We evaluate our method using three prominent Multi-modal Large Language
286   Models (MLLMs) known for their strong visual comprehension and textual reasoning capabilities:

292   Datasets We evaluate Zer0-Jack using two publicly available datasets specifically designed for
293   assessing model safety in multi-modal scenarios:
294   • Harmful Behaviors Multi-modal Dataset: The Harmful Behaviors dataset (Zou et al., 2023) is a
295   safety-critical dataset designed to assess LLMs’ behavior when prompted with harmful or unsafe
296   instructions. It includes 500 instructions aimed at inducing harmful responses. For our experiments,
297   we selected a random subset of 100 instructions from this dataset. To create multi-modal inputs,
298
      which fit for MLLMs evaluation, we paired each instruction with an image randomly sampled from

299
      performance in harmful behavior scenarios.

      • MM-SafetyBench-T: MM-SafetyBench-T (Liu et al., 2023a) is a comprehensive benchmark de-
      signed to assess the robustness of MLLMs against image-based manipulations across 13 safety-
302
      critical scenarios with 168 text-image pairs specifically crafted for testing safety. It provides the
303
      diversity of tasks, allowing for meaningful insights into model robustness while ensuring computa-
304   tional feasibility in extensive experimentation. Among the image types provided by this benchmark,

      5     D ISCUSSION
515
516          • Limitations: though Zer0-Jack only requires access to output logits or probabilities,
517            Zer0-Jack could not directly attack the web version of commercial MLLMs. Besides,
518            there are some commercial MLLMs’ API that do not support return logits (Anthropic,
519            2024). To attack such models directly, it is better to design a jailbreak method using the
520            information from generated responses instead of output logits. Right now, Zer0-Jack
521
               needs assistance from custom prompts, otherwise, Zer0-Jack requires far more itera-
               tions to attack GPT-4o.
522
523          • Call for Defense Strategy: since Zer0-Jack directly estimates the gradient to generate
524
               malicious image inputs, it is difficult to use prompt-based defense methods that add more
               strict or safe system prompt (Wang et al., 2024b). We argue that it is better to use post-
525
               hoc methods such as LLM-as-a-judge (Zheng et al., 2023), which makes MLLMs refuse to
526
               answer the question based on the response. Besides, Zer0-Jack also proves that partial
527            information from output logits might be dangerous, which indicates that it is better for us
528            to find a balance between transparency and risk provided by the models’ API.

      6     C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we presented Zer0-Jack, a novel zeroth-order gradient-based approach for jail-
533
      breaking black-box Multi-modal Large Language Models. By utilizing zeroth-order optimization
534   that requires output logits only, Zer0-Jack addresses the challenges that attacking black-box
535   models. By generating image prompts and patch coordinate optimization, Zer0-Jack deals
536   with the problems of discrete optimization and errors brought by the high dimensions in zeroth-
537   order optimization. Extensive experiments across multiple MLLMs demonstrated the efficacy of
538   Zer0-Jack, with consistently high attack success rates surpassing transfer-based methods. Our
539   method highlights the vulnerabilities present in MLLMs and emphasizes the need for stronger safety
      alignment mechanisms, particularly in multi-modal contexts.

707   B    C OMPARISON WITH B LACK - BOX M ETHODS IN A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
708
709   we think our method has some key differences between previous black-box adversarial attack meth-
710   ods Chen et al. (2017); Zhao et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2019) and unique contributions. Here are
711
      some comparisons:
712
            • Zer0-Jack has a different target with ZOO. Zer0-Jack distinguishes itself from ZOO
713           by its focus on jailbreaking, whereas ZOO primarily targets adversarial attacks. Jailbreak-
714           ing involves optimizing multiple targets simultaneously (e.g., the target phrase “sure, here
715           it is” consists of 4-5 tokens), while adversarial attacks typically optimize for a single target
716           (e.g., a specific class label). While ZOO demonstrated the success of zeroth-order optimiza-
717           tion for a single target, Zer0-Jack extends this approach to more complex, multi-target
718           scenarios.
719         • Zer0-Jack has different target models with ZOO. ZOO successfully applies zeroth-order
720           optimization to smaller DNN models, but Zer0-Jack scales this technique to large-scale
721           transformer models, including those with 7B and even 70B parameters. This scalability
722           highlights Zer0-Jack’s ability to handle much more complex models, demonstrating the
723           power of zeroth-order optimization at a larger scale.
724         • Zer0-Jack has a different methodology from ZOO. Since ZOO targets different objec-
725           tives and models, it incorporates complex components, such as hierarchical attacks, which
726           are not ideal for jailbreaking large models. Our experimental results, presented below,
727           demonstrate that our method outperforms ZOO, highlighting its superior capability for jail-
728           breaking large-scale models.
729
      We compare our approach with ZOO (Chen et al., 2017), a zeroth-order optimization method orig-
730
      inally developed for black-box adversarial attacks. To ensure a fair evaluation, we adapted ZOO
731   for the jailbreak task and tested its performance on the Harmful Behaviors Multi-modal Dataset.
732   With identical optimization settings, ZOO achieves an Attack Success Rate (ASR) of 86% using the
733   MiniGPT-4 7B model, while Zer0-Jack attains a higher ASR of 95%.
