                                                       A BSTRACT
008
009
                 We find video diffusion models, renowned for their generative capabilities, sur-
010
                 prisingly excel at pixel-level object tracking without any explicit training for this
011              task. We introduce a simple and effective method to extract motion representations
012              from video diffusion models, achieving state-of-the-art tracking results. Our ap-
013              proach enables the tracking of identical objects, overcoming limitations of previous
014              methods reliant on intra-frame appearance correspondence. Visualizations and em-
015              pirical results show that our approach outperforms recent self-supervised tracking
016              methods, including the state-of-the-art, by up to 6 points. Our work demonstrates
017              video generative models can learn intrinsic temporal dynamics of video, and excel
018              in tracking tasks beyond original video synthesis.

040   The ability of temporal relational reasoning over time (Yi et al., 2019) is crucial for visual intelligence.
041   Rather than performing simple appearance correspondence, people often rely on temporal relational
042   reasoning to track moving objects in complex situations (Yi et al., 2019; Gerstenberg et al., 2015;
043   Ullman, 2015). For example, given the two moving deer in Figure 1(a), we can easily reason and
044
      track different deer even after they change their relative positions.
045   Learning video representations for temporal correspondence is essential for tasks like video object
046   segmentation (Caron et al., 2021). Appearance-based correspondence methods have been used for
047   tracking (Wang et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2022), including the recent state-of-the-art DIFT (Tang et al.,
048   2023) that uses latent representations from image diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2021; Dhariwal
049   & Nichol, 2021). Some research also integrates temporal information in model training (Wang et al.,
050
      2019; Jabri et al., 2020). However, existing methods often have low accuracy because they fail to
      capture temporal context in complex scenarios, see Figure 1(b), where state-of-the-art models (Qian
051
      et al., 2023; Li & Liu, 2023; Tang et al., 2023) fail to differentiate between two deer.

095   2     R ELATED WORK
096
097   Learning temporal correspondence is crucial for visual tracking (Tao et al., 2016; Xu & Wang,
098   2021; Li & Liu, 2023). Due to limited annotations, prior studies have developed methods to learn
099   correspondence in a self-supervised manner (Caron et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2023). Our work
100   contributes to this field of self-supervised correspondence, and we discuss related work as follows.
101
      Temporal correspondence learned from images. Self-supervised learning that trains on image
102   datasets has achieved great success in downstream tasks, including temporal correspondence. Pi-
103   oneering work, such as MoCo (He et al., 2020) and DINO (Caron et al., 2021), adopt instance
104   discrimination as pretext task which learns similar representations for different augmentations of the
105   same image. DenseCL (Wang et al., 2021) and PixPro (Xie et al., 2021b) further apply contrastive
106   learning to pixel-level, which improve dense prediction tasks. SFC (Hu et al., 2022) boosts perfor-
107   mance on temporal correspondence further by fusing image-level and pixel-level representations.
      Recently, DIFT (Tang et al., 2023) achieves state-of-the-art results in temporal correspondence task


                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      by leveraging internal representations from image diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2021). These
109   methods learn intra-frame information and rely on appearance for pixel-level tracking. Our work
110   highlights the limitations of using appearance alone for temporal correspondence and significantly
111   improves tracking by introducing temporal reasoning capabilities.
112
      Temporal correspondence learned from videos. Temporal information in videos provides supervi-
113
      sion signals to learn video representations during training. Two widely used pretext tasks for model
114
      training are frame reconstruction and cycle-consistency over time. Frame reconstruction tasks involve
115   reconstructing a frame from adjacent frames (Vondrick et al., 2018; Lai & Xie, 2019; Li et al., 2019;
116   Lai et al., 2020), while cycle-consistency tasks track a patch backwards and forward in time to align
117   start and end points (Wang et al., 2019; Jabri et al., 2020). However, these methods often overlook
118   spatial features crucial for creating discriminative and robust representations (Li & Liu, 2023). Recent
119   research integrates spatial with temporal information in model training, such as Spa-then-Temp (Li &
120   Liu, 2023) and SMTC (Qian et al., 2023). Despite incorporating temporal information during model
121   training, our work reveals that existing methods still face challenges in complex scenarios, such as
122   tracking multiple similar-looking objects, as shown in Figure 1. By introducing temporal reasoning
123
      ability from video diffusion models to tracking, our approach significantly improves performance
      across various video scenarios, including those involving similar-looking objects.
124
125   Video diffusion models. Diffusion models have significantly advanced image and video genera-
126   tion (Ho et al., 2020; Saharia et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022; Ruiz et al., 2023). Text-to-image diffusion
127   models (Nichol et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022) allow precise control over generated image content
128   via text prompts, with Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021) improving generation efficiency and
129
      quality by performing diffusion process in latent space. To generate videos with consistent frames,
      video diffusion models are created by inserting temporal blocks into image diffusion models, which
130
      are then trained on video datasets (Blattmann et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023). Representative
131
      video diffusion models include Sora (Brooks et al., 2024), ModelScope (Wang et al., 2023), I2VGen-
132   XL (Zhang et al., 2023), and Stable Video Diffusion (Blattmann et al., 2023a). Our work is the first
133   to demonstrate that temporal dynamics learned by video diffusion models can significantly improve
134   tracking performance. Our work highlights the potential of video generative models in tracking tasks
135   beyond their original use in video synthesis.

197   3.2    C HALLENGES FOR TRACKING IDENTICAL OBJECTS
198
199   Prior studies have achieved impressive results in video label propagation by establishing pixel-level
200   mappings among frames based on frame representations (Jabri et al., 2020; Li & Liu, 2023). For
201   videos with a single object, pixel-level mapping often relies on object appearances, such as the
202   semantic information used in SFC (Hu et al., 2022). However, in videos with multiple similar-looking
203
      objects, like tanks with similar fish, establishing accurate correspondence remains challenging and is
      underexplored in the video label propagation task.
204
205   Controlled toy example. We begin with a controlled toy example that tracks two independently
206   moving, identical-looking balls in a video, as shown in Figure 4(a). We use the Kubric simulator (Greff
207   et al., 2022) to create a video dataset with random ball sizes and motions, termed Kubric-Similar. In
208   this dataset, we propagate the segmentation map of each ball from the first frame (see Figure 4(b)) to
209
      the subsequent frames. We follow the label propagation procedures in prior studies (Jabri et al., 2020;
      Li & Liu, 2023), with implementation details in Section 3.4.
210
211   We evaluate state-of-the-art models on Kubric-Similar, with results reported in Figure 4(c) and
212   Table 1. Figure 4(c) shows that existing methods struggle with object identity, leading to poor
213   tracking. This aligns with Table 1, where many methods, including DIFT (Tang et al., 2023) that uses
214   image diffusion representations, achieve a Jm around 50%. Note that a Jm around 50% indicates
215
      performance no better than random guessing due to the identical size of the two balls. These findings
      highlight the difficulty of tracking multiple similar-looking objects in temporal correspondence tasks.

263      Motivated by the success of video diffusion representations when tracking identical objects in the
264      toy example above, we will first investigate where the tracking capability comes from. We will then
265      capitalize on the findings and propose a simple and effective method for better tracking.
266      Where does the ability to track similar-looking objects come from? We hypothesize that this
267      tracking capability stems from temporal context learned during video synthesis. Video diffusion
268      models (Blattmann et al., 2023a) insert temporal layers into image diffusion models to learn temporal
269      dynamics such as motion, ensuring frame consistency in generated videos (see Figure 5 (a)(b)). We
         denote the UNet representations from video and image diffusion models as Rv and Ri , respectively.

326   Video label propagation. Our work follows prior studies (Jabri et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2021; Tang
327   et al., 2023) for the evaluation protocol of label propagation, which includes representation extraction
328   and label prediction stage, as shown in Algorithm 1. We first obtain frame representations Rf using
329   video and image diffusion models. To predict the label of current frame, similar pixel pairs between
330   current and previous frames are identified by computing the similarities of their representations. Each
331   pixel in the current frame is then labeled by aggregating the labels of similar pixels from previous
332
      frames, weighted by their pixel similarity. More experimental setups are detailed in Appendix B.1.
333   Appearance representations. Following (Tang et al., 2023), we use the output from the internal
334   layers of UNeti as the appearance representation Ri , following Ri = UNeti (xτ , n). xτ represents
335   each video frame, generated according to Eqn. 2 with an empirically determined τ . We process each
336   frame through a single forward pass of UNeti . Our framework accommodates any pre-trained image
337   diffusion model for Ri , using ADM (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) by default. We also investigate other
338
      models such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022).
339   Temporal representations. We obtain Rv following Rv = UNetv (xτ , n) as shown in Fig 2(a). The
340   key difference in obtaining Rv compared to Ri is using UNetv from video diffusion models, which
341   process video sequence of multiple frames as xτ . Since current video diffusion models accept limited
342   frames as input, long videos are split into subsequences. Rv is then obtained for each subsequence
343   through a one-pass forward process in UNetv . Our framework supports any off-the-shelf pre-trained
344
      video diffusion model for Rv , using I2VGen-XL (Zhang et al., 2023) by default. We also explore
      additional models like Stable Video Diffusion (Blattmann et al., 2023a).

531   5    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we leverage latent representations from video diffusion models for pixel-level tracking.
534   Benefiting from video diffusion models’ ability to incorporate information across multiple frames,
535   our work introduces temporal reasoning to the tracking tasks. Without additional training, our method
536   improves tracking performance in various video scenarios, even enabling tracking of similar-looking
537   objects where previous methods struggle. Experimental results show that our approach achieves
538   state-of-the-art tracking performance, outperforming recent studies by up to 6 points. Our work
539   highlights the potential of video generative models in tracking applications beyond their original use
      in video synthesis task.

      A.1   A DVANTAGES OF OUR WORK OVER DIFT
759
760   We clarify and highlight the advantages of our work over the state-of-the-art DIFT (Tang et al., 2023)
761   as follows.
762
763
764          • We solve a task that tracks similar-looking objects which DIFT cannot solve. Tracking
765            similar-looking objects in label propagation is a very fundamental task in the field. Since
766            DIFT learns only appearance features, it fails to track similar-looking objects.
767          • Our work uses temporal motions learned from video diffusion models in tracking,
768            providing new insights into how motion-based tracking emerges. Our experiments and
769            analysis show that temporal layers in video diffusion models enable motion-aware features
770            necessary for tracking similar-looking objects, which are absent in DIFT.
771          • Improved tracking accuracy across various scenarios. Our work significantly outper-
772            forms DIFT in tracking performance in various videos, such as those with severe object
773            deformation, achieving 1.9% higher accuracy on DAVIS and 5.3% on YouTube-Similar.
774
775
776
      A.2   A DVANTAGE OF OUR WORK IN LEARNING TEMPORAL FEATURES
777
      We clarify and highlight the advantages of our work over prior studies in learning temporal features
778   as follows.
779
780
781
             • Better representations obtained by solving a harder generative task. Previous methods
782
               are trained on easier tasks that always have shortcuts. For example, mismatched patches
783            in Wang et al. (2019); Jabri et al. (2020) or objects in Gordon et al. (2020); Xu & Wang
784            (2021) with similar appearances can also yield low training loss. In contrast, our video
785            diffusion models are trained to fully reconstruct every pixel from noisy inputs, enabling
786            better representation learning.
787          • Advanced temporal attention vs. simple pairwise correlation. During training, prior
788            methods learn temporal features by simple correlations between spatial features across
789            frames (Vondrick et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019; Lai & Xie, 2019; Lai et al.,
790            2020; Qian et al., 2023; Li & Liu, 2023), which fail to distinguish similar-looking objects.
791            In contrast, our video diffusion model uses temporal attention layers to integrate multiple
792            frames, enabling advanced reasoning in complex scenarios like the deer with changing
793            positions in Figure 1.
794          • Significantly improved tracking accuracy. Quantitative results and visualization show
795            that our method significantly improves the tracking performance compared to prior studies,
796            by more than 3.5% on DAVIS and 6.4% on YouTube-Similar.

802
      In this work, we evaluate the video label propagation task, which predicts pixel-level labels for
803
      subsequent video frames given the ground-truth label of the first frame. We follow the evaluation
804   protocol of prior studies (Jabri et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2023), as detailed in
805   Algorithm 1. Pixel-level labels for each frame are predicted based on the frame representations and
806   labels of previous frames. For the current frame, we first identify similar pixel pairs between this
807   frame and the previous frames by computing the similarities of their pixel representations. Labels for
808   the current frame are then predicted by aggregating the labels of similar pixels from previous frames,
809   weighted by their pixel similarity. A key advantage of TED over prior studies is that it generates
      frame representations by inputting the video sequence into the 3D UNetv , which encodes the temporal
