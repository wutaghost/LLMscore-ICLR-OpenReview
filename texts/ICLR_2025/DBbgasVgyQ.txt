                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Large Language models (LLMs) possess remarkable ability to understand natural
               language descriptions of complex robotics environments. Earlier studies have
011
               shown that LLM agents can use a predefined set of skills for robot planning in
012
               long-horizon tasks. However, the requirement for prior knowledge of the skill set
013            required for a given task constrains its applicability and flexibility. We present
014            a novel approach L2S (short of Language2Skills) to leverage the generalization
015            capabilities of LLMs to decompose the natural language task description of a
016            complex task to definitions of reusable skills. Each skill is defined by an LLM-
017            generated dense reward function and a termination condition, which in turn lead
018            to effective skill policy training and chaining for task execution. To address the
019            uncertainty surrounding the parameters used by the LLM agent in the generated
020            reward and termination functions, L2S trains parameter-conditioned skill policies
021
               that performs well across a broad spectrum of parameter values. As the impact of
               these parameters for one skill on the overall task becomes apparent only when its
022
               following skills are trained, L2S selects the most suitable parameter value during
023
               the training of the subsequent skills to effectively mitigate the risk associated with
024            incorrect parameter choices. During training, L2S autonomously accumulates a
025            skill library from continuously presented tasks and their descriptions, leveraging
026            guidance from the LLM agent to effectively apply this skill library in tackling novel
027            tasks. Our experimental results show that L2S is capable of generating reusable
028            skills to solve a wide range of robot manipulation tasks.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In recent years, the integration of language models with robotics has opened up new avenues
034   for advancing autonomous learning in robotic systems. Large Language models (LLMs) possess
035   the remarkable ability to understand complex tasks and environments. Leveraging this capability,
036   researchers have explored the use of language models in various aspects of robotics, ranging from
037   task planning and navigation to manipulation and control. Previous work in this domain has primarily
038   focused on leveraging language models for robot planning, where a predefined set of skills is provided
039
      to the model. However, this approach has limitations, as it assumes prior knowledge of the skill set
      required for the given task, thus constraining its applicability and flexibility.
040
041   Automatic skill acquisition has long been studied in the context of hierarchical reinforcement learning
042   (Barto and Mahadevan (2003)) in the form of temporally extended actions Sutton et al. (1999).
043   Despite the proven effectiveness of skills in expediting learning (McGovern and Sutton (1998)),
044   a fundamental question remains: how can agents autonomously develop valuable skills through
045
      interaction with their environment? There has been a significant body of work aimed at discovering
      skills. For example, Option-Critic (Bacon et al. (2017)) learns skills by optimizing the skill policies as
046
      well as their termination functions in a gradient-based manner, assuming all the skills can be applied
047
      everywhere. However, it is known to be prone to inefficient task decomposition, such as learning
048   a sub-policy that terminates at every time step or discovering one efficient sub-policy that executes
049   throughout the entire episode. Vezhnevets et al. (2017); Nachum et al. (2018); Levy et al. (2019)
050   address this issue by automatically decomposing a complex task into subtasks and solving them by
051   optimizing the subtask objectives. These methods excel in learning multiple levels of policies in
052   sparse reward tasks. However, the low-level skills learned are tied to a specific environment and it is
053   unclear whether they are adaptable to new tasks. Skill chaining (DSC) (Konidaris and Barto (2009);
      Bagaria and Konidaris (2020)) involves a sequential discovery and chaining of skills, starting from

054
      the end goal state and progressing backward to the initial state. However, as the agent generates new
055   skills using the initial states of the preceding skill on the skill chain as their goal states, this poses
056   challenges in robot manipulation tasks. For example, learning a skill π1 to move an object towards a
057   goal region cannot be learned well before mastering the skill π2 for object grasping, but skill chaining
058   would require learning π1 first.
059
      We present a novel approach L2S (short for “Language to Skills") for skill discovery in robot learning
060
      by leveraging large language models to overcome the limitations of prior methods. We aim to
061   empower robotic systems to autonomously discover and adapt skills to a wide range of tasks. L2S
062   harnesses the generalization capability of large language models (LLMs) to decompose the natural
063   language task description of a complex task to definitions of reusable skills. Each skill is defined by
064   an LLM-generated dense reward function and a termination condition, which in turn lead to effective
065   skill policy training and chaining for task execution. For example, consider the “turn faucet left" task
066   depicted in Fig. 1. The GPT-4 agent can break down this task into two skills: (1) positioning the
067   robot’s end effector near the right side of the faucet πo1 and (2) rotating the faucet handle to the left
068   πo2 . Chaining these two skills together successfully solves the task.
069   L2S excels in sequential task learning by autonomously building a library of parameterized skills
070   (explained below) as it encounters tasks during training. This accumulated skill library can then be
071   reused to tackle new tasks, guided by the LLM agent. For example, consider a scenario where the
072   agent is presented with the task of "turn faucet right" after it has already been trained on "turn faucet
073   left". The LLM agent identifies that the first skill πo1 in "turn faucet left" can be tuned to position the
074   end effector on the left side of the faucet handle (by adjusting its parameters). Thus, L2S only needs
075
      to train a new skill πo′ 2 to rotate the faucet handle right. By reusing existing skills in this manner,
      L2S significantly reduces the computational burden associated with learning new tasks from scratch,
076
      enabling more efficient task solving over time.
077
078   The main challenge faced by L2S is that while LLMs can outline the overall structure of skills
079   necessary to tackle a task, they lack detailed insight into the specific low-level control intricacies of
080   the environment. For the "turn faucet left" task in Fig. 1, the reward function generated for the first
081
      skill πo1 encourages the skill policy to guide the end effector towards the right side of the handle by
      a distance of params[0] = 0.01m. However, training the policy using this reward function could
082
      lead to an unforeseen outcome where the end effector ends up on the left side of the handle, rendering
083
      the subsequent skill of rotating the faucet handle left unattainable (Fig. 1 top right). This discrepancy
084   arises from the norm function employed in the reward and termination functions of πo1 , which solely
085   emphasizes the proximity of the end effector to the target_position that is located too close to
086   the faucet handle (at a distance of params[0] = 0.01m). Consequently, the policy may position
087   the end effector on the left side of the handle and still achieves a high task reward and satisfies
088   the termination condition of this skill. To address the uncertainty surrounding the parameters used
089   by the LLM agent, L2S trains parameter-conditioned skill policies, denoted as πo (a∣s; params),
090   where the parameters params are akin to "goals" in goal-conditioned reinforcement learning. As
091   the impact of these parameters on the overall task becomes apparent only when subsequent skills
092
      are trained, L2S adopts a strategy of training a skill policy that performs well across a broad
      spectrum of parameter values and selects the most suitable parameter value during the training of
093
      subsequent skills. For instance, the first skill πo1 (a∣s; params) for "turn faucet left" is trained to
094
      position the end effector around the faucet handle, with a distance to the handle at params[0].
095   Training the subsequent skill πo2 involves determining the correct policy parameter params[0]
096   - the target_position to move the end effector to - and appropriately setting its termination
097   condition parameters t_params[0] - determining how close the end effector should be to the
098   target position before transitioning to the next skill - to optimally achieve the highest reward during
099   the training of the second skill (Fig. 1 middle). In this way, L2S effectively mitigates the risk
100   associated with potentially incorrect parameter choices. The parameter-conditioned skill policies in
101   L2S facilitate seamless skill reuse. The parameter params[0] in the first skill πo1 (a∣s; params)
102   trained for "turn faucet left" can be adjusted to position the robot’s end effector on the left side of the
103
      faucet for the "turn faucet right" task.
104   Compared to state-of-the-art LLM-guided reward generation methods such as Text2Reward Xie
105   et al. (2023) and Eureka Ma et al. (2023), which generate dense reward functions to train single,
106   monolithic policies for each robotic task, L2S instead creates reusable, parameterized skills for
107   sequential task learning. These skills effectively generalize to new tasks through parameterization.
      While previous work, such as Ahn et al. (2022), has explored decomposing complex tasks into

      Sequential decision-making problems can be formalized as Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). An
      MDP is defined by a tuple e = ⟨S, A, T, R, γ, η⟩, where S represents the state space, A represents
140
      the action space, T ∶ S × A × S → [0, 1] denotes the transition function, R ∶ S × A → R denotes the
141
      reward function, 0 < γ < 1 is the discount factor, and s0 ∼ η(⋅) defines the initial states. At each
142   time step t, the agent selects an action at ∈ A in state st ∈ S, receives a reward rt = R(st , at ) ∈ R,
143   and transitions to another state st+1 with a probability determined by T . We assume sparse reward
144   functions that provide signals only upon task success (1.0) or failure (0.0). The primary objective is
145   to learn a policy π ∶ S → A for e that maximizes the expected return, defined as the discounted sum
146   of rewards: maxπ∈Π Eπ [∑∞   t=0 γ R(st , at )], where at = π(st ).

      Skills. A significant challenge for reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms lies in learning and
148
      planning over long horizons, particularly in scenarios where rewards are sparse. The options
149
      framework, proposed by Sutton et al. (1999), offers a formalism for temporal abstraction, which
150   aids in both exploration and credit assignment. The central concept is to decompose the overarching
151   problem that the agent seeks to solve into subtasks, each typically characterized by its own reward
152   function and capable of being accomplished by a distinct skill. Our method L2S is inspired by the
153   options framework and we define skills similar to options in the options framework. A skill o consists
154   of (a) its termination condition, βo (s), which determines whether skill execution must terminate in
155   state s and (b) its closed-loop skill policy, πo (s), which maps state s to a low level action a ∈ A.
156   Skill Chaining for Single-Task Learning. Given a single task MDP e, and its task description
157   Le in natural language, L2S constructs a chain of skills Konidaris and Barto (2009); Bagaria and
158   Konidaris (2020) such that successful execution of each skill in the chain allows the agent to execute
159   another skill. A task description Le refers to a language command describing the desired goal
160   for the agent, like "turn faucet left". The inductive bias of creating sequentially executable skills
161   guarantees that as long as the agent successfully executes each skill in its chain, it can solve the
      original task in e. Intuitively, skill chaining amounts to learning skills such that the termination

      Skill Library Construction in Sequential Task Learning. The main objective of L2S is to efficiently
      tackle a sequence of related tasks by autonomously building a skill library from ongoing tasks and
189
      reusing it for future tasks. Formally, given a sequence of tasks, where each task is represented as
190
      an MDP e and accompanied by a description Le , L2S builds a skill library O ≡ {o, Lo } tailored for
191   solving these tasks. Each skill o within O is associated with a descriptive text Lo . The skill library
192   O starts out empty. As L2S encounters new tasks (e, Le ) in the sequence, it progressively adds new
193   skills in the skill chains for solving these tasks to O, while also developing plans that make use of the
194   the existing skills in O whenever possible.

197   3     L ANGUAGE TO S KILLS
198
199   The primary goal of L2S is to utilize LLMs to automatically build skill libraries O for sequential
200   task learning. Given the textual description of a new task, L2S uses LLMs to generate code that
201
      defines both the reward function and termination condition for each new skill in the skill chain for
      solving the task, facilitating the learning of the skill’s policy. These learned skills, along with their
202
      LLM-generated descriptions, are subsequently added to O, enabling the LLMs to effectively reuse
203
      them when building skill chains for future tasks.

206   3.1   P ROMPT C ONSTRUCTION
207
      For a task MDP e and its natural language task description Le , L2S prompts an LLM agent with an
208
      abstraction of e and Le to generate a skill chain for solving the task. The environment abstraction
209
      is needed by the LLM agent to ground reward generation for understanding how object states are
210   represented, including robot and object configurations.We adopt a compact Pythonic representation,
211   similar to Xie et al. (2023), as illustrated in Fig. 2. This approach offers a higher level of abstraction
212   compared to listing all environment-specific information in a table or list format. The LLM agent is
213   instructed to generate a skill chain for e as ρ = o0 ○ o1 ○ . . . ○ ok , and the reward function Roi [ϕi ](s, a)
214   and termination condition βoi [φi ](s) (as python programs) for each skill oi in ρ, where ϕi and φi
215   are the parameters within the skill reward and termination functions for oi respectively. Additionally,
      L2S asks the LLM agent to generate a description Lo for each skill o.

      For continuously presented tasks, as discussed in Sec. 2, L2S maintains a skill library O ≡ {(o, Lo )}
217   where each skill o is accompanied with its text description Lo (generated by the LLM agent). L2S
218   starts with no predefined skills, meaning the skill library O is initially empty. Skills within the skill
219   chain devised for one task are added to O for reusing them when building skill chains for future tasks.
220   Thus, it is possible part of a generated skill chain ρ = o0 ○ o1 ○ . . . reuses skills in O. To achieve this,
221   L2S prompts the LLM agent with the text description Lo of each skill o in O, along with a command
222   instructing the LLM agent to select and reuse existing skills whenever possible.
223   Although LLMs have good understanding of high-level task structures, we have found that they are
224   not yet reliable enough to generate correct rewards and termination conditions in a zero-shot manner
225   for complex tasks. To handle this, we also prompt LLMs with few-shot examples as Xie et al. (2023).
226   Detailed prompt examples can be found in the Appendix G.

228   3.2   S KILL C HAIN T RAINING
229
230   During training, L2S iteratively processes(learning and optimizing, if necessary) skills in a skill chain
231   ρ starting from the initial skill o0 , continuing until it processes the final skill on ρ. It maintains the
232   property that for every skill oi it processes, it has already trained policies for all skills preceding oi .
233   The key challenge with this approach is that, although LLMs can define the overarching structure of
234   a skill chain for a given task, they often lack precise knowledge of the low-level control details within
235   the environment. As a result, the parameters used in the generated reward and termination functions
236   tend to be inaccurate, reflecting inherent uncertainties (as illustrated in the turn faucet example in
237   Sec. 1 and Fig. 1). An important aspect of L2S is that each skill policy is parameter-conditioned
238   (similar in concept to goal-conditioned reinforcement learning), denoted as πoi (a∣s; ϕi ), where ϕi
239
      represents the parameters in the reward function Roi for oi . The training objective is for the policy
      πoi (a∣s; ϕi ) to maximize the expected rewards over a broad range of parameter values ϕi ∼ q̃ϕi ,
240
      ensuring robust performance across varying conditions. The parameter distribution q̃ϕi for ϕi is
241
      configured by the user. For example, one can set q̃ϕi as a Gaussian distribution N (vϕi , σ), where vϕi
242   is the mean centered at the LLM agent’s inferred parameter values for ϕi , and σ is the user-defined
243   variance. The execution of πoi is also influenced by the initial states of oi , which are determined
244   by both the skill policy πoi−1 (a∣s; ϕi−1 ) and the termination condition βoi−1 (φi−1 ) of its preceding
245   skill ok−1 in the skill chain. Thus, the training for πoi (a∣s ϕi ) also needs to optimize the parameters
246   associated with oi−1 , which involves finding the correct policy parameters for ϕi−1 and properly
247   setting its termination condition parameters φi−1 :

      Baselines. We conducted a comparative analysis between L2S and two other state-of-the-art methods:
      Text2Reward (T2R) Xie et al. (2023) and Eureka Ma et al. (2023). T2R utilizes LLMs to generate

      dense reward functions for training a single, monolithic policy per robotic task, using the same

      Python-based environment abstraction and task description as ours provided to the LLM. In contrast,
354   Eureka employs an evolutionary approach, where it inputs the environment script into the LLM to
355   generate multiple reward functions simultaneously for training policies in parallel. Batch success
356   rates are then used to guide the LLM in refining reward functions for the next iteration, creating a
357   feedback loop that iteratively improves the reward functions. L2S differs from T2R in its ability to
358   generate reusable skills for sequential task learning while iteratively refining the parameterization of
359   reward and termination functions based on skill chain training. While Eureka’s evolutionary approach
360   can adjust reward functions, it relies heavily on costly LLM interactions for environment feedback
361   and expensive policy training with each parameter update, and it lacks support for skill learning. For
362
      our benchmarks, we ran Eureka for 3 rounds with 8 samples per round. This process resulted in
      significantly higher training costs compared to L2S, measured by the environment steps required for
363
      agent training. For Eureka, we conducted multiple runs and reported results only from those that had
364
      at least one successful sample in each round.

367   parameters in reward and termination functions, instead of optimizing them as in L2S , to assess the

      impact of addressing potentially incorrect parameter choices made by LLMs.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
475
476
477
      LLM Planning for Robotics. Recent research has highlighted the integration of Large Language
      Models (LLMs) in robotic task and motion planning (TAMP) (Firoozi et al., 2023). Huang et al.
478
      (2022a) investigated LLMs for direct trajectory planning, revealing limitations in spatial and nu-
479
      merical reasoning that necessitate frequent re-prompting to align with task constraints. Following
480   works aim to mitigate the gap on feasibility and correctness when applying LLM-generated plans to
481   simulated or real-world robotic environments. Inspired by the in-context learning ability of LLMs,
482   Inner Monologue (Huang et al., 2022b) allows robotic systems to integrate real-time environmental
483   feedback into LLM-generated plans. This strategy significantly enhances the adaptability and effec-
484   tiveness of robotic agents by using continuous feedback to adjust planning strategies. Text2Motion
485   (Lin et al., 2023) goes a step further by not only generating feasible task plans (a sequence of skills)
      but also ensuring these plans are geometrically executable before initiation. Another direction is to


                                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      utilize LLMs for translating natural language into intermediate formal task representations, NL2TL
487   (Chen et al., 2024a) and AutoTAMP (Chen et al., 2024b) significantly enhancing task completion
488   through auto-regressive error correction of both syntax and semantics. The planning ability of LLMs
489   plays a great role in L2S for generating skill chains and make plan on it to complete robotic tasks. To
490   finish wider range of tasks, BOSS(Zhang et al., 2023) leverages LLM to build skill library with large
491   amount of complex and useful skill chains generated from a set of primitive skills. Also, SayCan
492   (Ahn et al., 2022) ranks all the possible skills by the task-grounding probability (usefulness) and
493   world-grounding probability (feasibility) and select the one with highest probability at each step for
494   LLM decision making within a given embodiment.
495   LLM-Based Code Generation. L2R (Yu et al., 2023) introduces a new paradigm that harnesses
496   flexibility of reward function representations by utilizing LLMs to define reward parameters that can
497   be optimized and accomplish variety of robotic tasks. To generate RL reward function for robotics
498   tasks, Zeng et al. (2024) includes self-align ranking to improve the quality of generated reward
499   function using samples ranked by both LLMs and reward function. Text2Reward (Xie et al., 2023)
500   generates interpretable, free-form dense reward functions as an executable program grounded in
501
      a compact representation of the environment either by zero-shot or few-shot. Eureka (Ma et al.,
      2023) generates dense reward function without any task-specific prompting or pre-defined reward
502
      templates (zero-shot). Both Text2Reward and Eureka leverage LLM’s in-context ability to improve
503
      reward function by providing human-involved feedback or automated feedback, respectively. In
504   League++ (Li et al., 2024), the reward functions are generated by the LLM through selecting and
505   weighting pre-defined metric functions provided by human experts. Our method differs from it in
506   two key aspects:1)Free-form Reward Generation and 2)Reduced Human Expert Effort. Another two
507   studies explores using LLMs for robot-centric policy generation, termed ProgPrompt (Singh et al.,
508   2022) and Code as Policies (Liang et al., 2023), which involves generating control code directly
509   from language instructions. Our L2S differs from the aforementioned works in several ways: 1) it
510   decomposes tasks into chain of skills, 2) it learns skills as primitives and building skill library based
511   on the skill chains, 3) it optimizes parameters in generated functions to enhance task performance,
512
      and 4) it reuses skills and skill chains from the library to improve learning efficiency.
513   Due to the limitation of pages, more related work can be found in Appendix A.

      6    L IMITATIONS
516
517
      L2S has been evaluated solely in robotic manipulation domains. Applying LLM-based skill discovery
518
      to other task types, such as navigation, would necessitate more advanced reasoning about environmen-
519   tal structures, which we leave as an avenue for future research. Additionally, L2S currently operates
520   within state-based environments, as the LLM-generated termination functions require explicit state
521   information to assess whether termination thresholds are met. Extending this approach to vision-based
522   tasks may require training a supervised model that learns from state-based termination conditions,
523   an aspect we plan to explore in future work. As for the assumption that each skill reply on the
524   performance of the skill before, to let the framework figure out which skills need to be optimized
525   might be a great extension. Lastly, LLM hallucinations present challenges in generating robust
526   free-form reward and termination function code. Constraining code generation within a structured
527
      intermediate representation, possibly defined by a domain-specific language, might offer a balance
      between generation stability and the exploration of the reward space.

530   7    C ONCLUSION
531
532   We present L2S that leverages Large Language Models (LLMs) to autonomously construct a skill
533   library for sequential task learning. L2S progressively builds a skill library guided by LLMs and
534   efficiently reuses them across new tasks, enabling the learning algorithm to effectively handle
535   increasingly challenging environments. To handle the uncertainty in LLM-generated reward and
536
      termination functions, L2S trains a parameter-conditioned policy that perform well across a broad
      range of parameter values for each skill and selects the most suitable parameter values during
537
      the training of its subsequent skills, mitigating the risk of incorrect parameter choices by LLMs.
538
      Experimental results demonstrate that L2S outperforms baselines in solving complex, multi-step
539   tasks, largely due to its ability to automatically construct a skill library for sequential task learning.

      A    M ORE R ELATED W ORK
703
704
705
      LLM for Reasoning. Recent studies on large language models (LLMs), e.g. GPT-3 (Brown et al.,
      2020), GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024), have demonstrated significant advancements of its reasoning
706
      capabilities. Prompting proposed by Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2023), has shown efficacy
707
      in improving reasoning by eliciting detailed reasoning paths in LLMs, which helps in tasks involving
708   multi-step reasoning. Similarly, ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) combines reasoning with actions, enhancing
709   performance on tasks by enabling dynamic reasoning and interactions with external information,
710   demonstrating significant improvements. Additionally, Zero-shot-CoT (Kojima et al., 2023) has
711   been proved effective in enhancing the zero-shot reasoning abilities of models across various tasks,
712   enhancing the potential of LLMs in tasks requiring complex multi-hop thinking without the need for
713   task-specific fine-tuning. These advancements suggests a promising direction for further enhancing
714   the reasoning powers of LLMs through advanced prompting techniques and integrated reasoning-
715   action paradigms. We leverage such reasoning ability to make LLMs understand the semantics of
716
      robotic tasks and follow the instructions from human correctly.
717
718
719
      B    D ISCUSSION
720
721   Our tool demonstrates that decomposing natural language tasks into skill chains significantly enhances
722   performance across a broad range of robotic tasks while reducing the cost of neural policy learning.
723
      One associated open problem is the instability caused by the hallucinations of LLMs, which can lead
      to unreliable code generation. Without fine-tuning LLMs, effective methods to address this issue
724
      include reducing the randomness of the next token generated by LLMs or iteratively sampling code
725
      via feedback prompts. The reward and termination functions can principally be used by RL agents to
726   train control policies using visual inputs and to terminate when specific visual conditions satisfy the
727   termination criteria. Extending L2S to vision-based environments is left for future work. We hope
728   our work represents a meaningful effort to apply LLMs to code generation across various research
729   domains, not limited to reinforcement learning, and contributes valuable insights to the development
730   of this field.
