009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            To bridge the gap between perception and planning in traditional navigation sys-
012            tems, we address the challenge of learning optimal trajectories directly from
013            depth information in an end-to-end fashion. Using neural networks as black-
014            box replacements for traditional modules can compromise robustness and stability.
015            Moreover, such methods often fail to adequately account for the robot’s kinematic
016            constraints, leading to trajectories that may not be satisfactorily executable. In this
017            paper, we integrate the strengths of conventional methods and neural networks by
018
               introducing an optimization-embedded neural network based on a compact trajec-
               tory library. Neural networks establish spatial constraints for model-based trajec-
019
               tory planning, followed by robust numerical optimization to achieve feasible and
020
               optimal solutions. By making the process differentiable, our model seamlessly ap-
021            proximates the optimal trajectory. Additionally, the introduction of a regularized
022            trajectory library enables the method to efficiently capture the spatial distribution
023            of optimal trajectories with minimal storage cost, ensuring multimodal planning
024            characteristics. Evaluations in complex, unseen environments demonstrate our
025            method’s superior performance over state-of-the-art algorithms. Real-world flight
026            experiments with a small onboard computer showcase the autonomous quadrotor’s
027            ability to navigate swiftly through dense forests.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) (Fan et al., 2020; Muchiri & Kimathi, 2022), owing to their
033   compact and straightforward hardware structure combined with agile and high maneuverability,
034
      have found extensive applications across various fields such as aerial photography, exploration,
      and search and rescue operations. As a critical component for achieving these tasks, efficient
035
      and robust autonomous navigation modules have garnered significant attention from both indus-
036
      try and academia. Traditionally, navigation modules employ sensors like depth cameras to perceive
037   the environment,explicitly constructing occupancy maps and computing environment representa-
038   tions favorable for motion planning, such as Euclidean signed distance fields (ESDF) (Oleynikova
039   et al., 2016; Han et al., 2019; Reijgwart et al., 2019; Finean et al., 2021) or neural radiance fields
040   (NeRF) (Adamkiewicz et al., 2022; Pantic et al., 2022; Lin & Yi, 2022). Subsequently, motion
041   planning algorithms based on search or optimization are utilized on these maps to compute optimal
042   trajectories that account for initial and final states as well as obstacle avoidance. Although intuitive
043   from an engineering perspective, this modular decomposition framework inevitably introduces addi-
044   tional physical delays. Additionally, this modular approach often leads to a lack of cohesion between
045
      sub-modules and requires extensive manual parameter tuning by engineers. Recently, learning-based
      navigation (Tang et al., 2018; Xiao et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2023) has gained widespread attention
046
      due to its efficient integration of perception and planning modules. This end-to-end approach di-
047
      rectly outputs trajectories from raw sensor data, bypassing explicit mapping. However, this method-
048   ology heavily relies on the neural network’s capabilities, leading to a black-box system that poses
049   challenges for debugging and reduces the system’s extensibility and interpretability. Furthermore,
050   given the constraints of physical platforms or specific task scenarios, there is a need to impose vari-
051   ous custom constraints on the trajectory, such as the kinematic constraints of the robot. This imposes
052   significant challenges on the network. Researchers often devise more complex strategies for the net-
053   work to address these constraints, but these strategies can sometimes compromise optimality or fail
      to ensure constraint satisfaction adequately, affecting the overall system’s completeness.

054
      To address the limitations of existing methods, this work integrates traditional trajectory optimiza-
055   tion with neural networks to create an end-to-end visual navigation system that directly generates
056   kinematically feasible spatial-temporal optimal trajectories from depth images without explicit map-
057   ping. Compared to conventional learning-based motion planning algorithms, our approach is distin-
058   guished by the use of implicit differentiation to embed the trajectory optimization within the neural
059   network, enabling coupled training. This method ensures optimality while alleviating the burden
060   on the network, enhancing the system’s interpretability and extensibility, and fundamentally guar-
061   anteeing the feasibility of kinematic constraints. Specifically, rather than naively producing a series
062   of trajectory points, our neural network extracts safe guiding regions from depth images and re-
063
      constructs them as geometric space constraints for trajectory optimization, which then incorporates
      user-specified kinematic constraints to efficiently and robustly converge (∼ 1ms) to high-quality
064
      trajectories. Making numerical optimization differentiable allows it to be modeled as a layer within
065
      the neural network, enabling direct backpropagation of the trajectory evaluation loss gradient and
066   encouraging the network to focus on regions yielding optimal trajectories. To fully explore the en-
067   vironment and align the multimodal nature of local motion planning, our neural network outputs a
068   mixture distribution over an offline regularized lightweight motion primitive library. Based on the
069   probabilities, specific motion primitives are selected and allocated safe feasible spaces, which are
070   subsequently input into the optimization module to generate maneuverable and agile motion. We
071   conduct extensive comparative experiments against various state-of-the-art motion planning algo-
072   rithms across multiple scenarios, including both traditional and learning-based methods. The results
073   demonstrate that our method has significant advantages in success rate, optimality, and constraint
074
      satisfaction. Moreover, as shown in Figure 1. B, we carry out real-world experiments to validate the
      practical applicability of our algorithm on fully autonomous physical platform without relying on
075
      external perception and localization.

078   2     R ELATED W ORK
079
080   2.1   T RADITIONAL M OTION P LANNING
081
082   Search or sampling-based algorithms in discrete configuration spaces (Pivtoraiko & Kelly, 2005;
083   Webb & Van Den Berg, 2013; Klemm et al., 2015) are prevalent in robotic motion planning, in-
084
      tegrating custom constraints and avoiding local optima. However, they require numerous samples
      for high-quality trajectories, impairing real-time efficiency. Conversely, optimization-based meth-
085
      ods (Zhou et al., 2019; Gao et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020; Tordesillas & How, 2021) leverage
086
      gradient information to efficiently converge to feasible trajectories in continuous spaces, balancing
087   quality and time, and becoming mainstream for UAV local planners. These methods often require
088   explicit environmental modeling through depth information and manual safety constraint extraction,
089   for example, using ESDF to construct safety constraints (Zhou et al., 2021; 2019). However, con-
090   structing ESDF incurs additional computational costs and involves a trade-off between efficiency
091   and accuracy. The ego planner (Zhou et al., 2020) avoids ESDF construction by iteratively gener-
092   ating safe guidance paths for obstacle avoidance gradients, but it lacks convergence guarantees and
093   may get trapped in unsafe local minima in complex environments. Using guidance paths to deform
094   trajectories can also deviate from the original optimization problem, affecting optimality. Corridor-
095
      based methods (Gao et al., 2020; Tordesillas & How, 2021) gain popularity in local motion planning
      by extracting feasible convex hulls from environment point clouds using geometric computations to
096
      model safety constraints. However, they require a collision-free path to seed the convex hull, often
097
      obtained using low-dimensional algorithms like A*(Choset, 2007) or hybrid A*(Ding et al., 2018),
098   which typically do not consider the robot’s higher-order kinematics, resulting in convex hulls that
099   are not conducive to generating agile trajectories.
100
101
      2.2   L EARNING - BASED M OTION P LANNING
102
103   Learning-based methods (Loquercio et al., 2021; Allen & Pavone, 2019; Chou et al., 2021; Yang
104   et al., 2023; Roth et al., 2023; Kulkarni & Alexis, 2024; Jacquet & Alexis, 2024; Han et al., 2024;
105   Wu et al., 2024) emerge as promising approaches in local planning, eliminating the need for explicit
106   mapping and reducing latency. Loquercio et al. (2021) leverage deep convolutional neural networks
107   to learn flight trajectories from depth images, using human pilot trajectories as supervision. How-
      ever, this method requires high-quality and large-scale datasets. Recently, some approaches combine


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      networks with numerical optimization. For instance, Jacquet & Alexis (2024) learn collision prob-
109   abilities for any point in space using a network, which are then modeled as safety constraints in
110   trajectory optimization. Similarly, Han et al. (2024) address finer obstacle avoidance by modeling
111   the robot’s shape as a convex hull and predicting the signed distance between the hull and the nearest
112   obstacle using a neural network. Although these works integrate networks and optimization, they
113   differ from our approach in that the network and optimization are independent components. In con-
114   trast, our algorithm incorporates differentiable optimization as part of the network training process,
115   facilitating the evolution of the network in directions beneficial for subsequent optimization and
116   generating higher-quality solutions while ensuring maneuverability and agile flight. Some works
117
      also apply the concept of bilayer optimization. Chen et al. (2024) propose the IA* algorithm, which
      extends the traditional A* search algorithm by embedding it within a neural network for training.
118
      However, the paths generated by this algorithm consist of discrete grid points, rendering it unsuitable
119
      for direct tracking by high-speed drones. Wu et al. (2024) utilize LSTM(Graves & Graves, 2012) to
120   learn time allocation for piecewise polynomial trajectories, achieving optimal solutions under ideal
121   conditions. This method, however, requires a known global map and offline convex decomposition
122   of safe regions, making it impractical for vision-based end-to-end local planners. Iplanner (Yang
123   et al., 2023) is an end-to-end local planner that generates trajectories from depth images, validated
124   by numerous real-world experiments. This approach uses closed-form cubic splines to interpolate
125   the points output by the network, approximating a feasible solution to the original trajectory plan-
126   ning problem. Despite being lightweight, this approximation does not strictly guarantee adherence
127   to dynamic constraints, potentially resulting in trajectories that are difficult for the physical platform
128
      to execute during high-speed flight, leading to crashes.

132   The objective of visual navigation is to find a dynamically feasible trajectory within a safe region
133   Ef ree , guided by depth observations D ∈ RH×W , while satisfying initial and terminal state con-
134   straints. In this paper, the trajectory ξ(t) : [0, T ] → R3 is represented using MINCO (Wang et al.,

138   tories inherently satisfy the initial and terminal state conditions and ensure high-order continuity at
139
      waypoints between adjacent polynomials. These features formally lay a solid foundation for smooth
      and coherent motion. Subsequently, as shown in Figure 1. A, we use flight corridors F to represent
140
      free space, which are modeled as safety constraints to restrict the shape of the trajectory space. Con-
141
      sequently, the trajectory optimization that minimizes control energy and includes first-order time

      5.1     B ENCHMARKS
401
402   In this section, we compare our approach with two traditional algorithms and a SOTA learning-based method
403   that receive widespread acclaim and is applied in real-world environments: 1. Fast-Planner (Zhou et al., 2019):
404   A hierarchical motion planner where kinodynamic search is used to construct an initial solution, followed by us-
405
      ing ESDF to enforce safety constraints required for subsequent trajectory optimization. 2. Ego-Planner (Zhou
      et al., 2020): A lightweight optimization-based motion planner that utilizes graph search to obtain a collision-
406   free guiding path, guiding trajectories to safe regions, thus eliminating the need for ESDF construction. 3.
407   IPlanner (Yang et al., 2023): A powerful learning-based motion planner integrates the core concept of im-
408   plicit optimization (IO) into the overarching pipeline of High-Speed Flight (HSF) (Loquercio et al., 2021),
409   demonstrating enhanced performance in experiments. Utilizing IO, Iplanner reduces the dependency on expert
410
      trajectory data compared to HSF, and also possesses enhanced generalization performance. Moreover, by di-
      rectly approximating the original optimization problem, it exhibits a better performance in terms of optimality
411   and constraint satisfaction compared to HSF. For comprehensive comparison, we categorize scenarios into low,
412   medium, and high aggressiveness based on varying velocity and acceleration limits of the robot. For each sce-
413   nario, we randomly generate 200 navigation tasks, each approximately 70 meters in distance, within previously
414   unseen environments containing hundreds of obstacles, as illustrated in Figure 3. Moreover, various custom
415
      parameters of traditional methods, such as convergence accuracy and the number of iterations for each case,
      are fine-tuned by experienced researchers in the field. For each planning instance, the local target is selected
416   from the straight line pointing towards the destination at a specific distance (12m) from the current position.
417   Furthermore, the replanning logic for each planner follows a time-triggered approach, with a fixed replanning
418   frequency of 10Hz. During each replanning process, we output the three most probable candidate corridors
419   and optimize trajectories in parallel. The execution trajectory is selected based on a weighted combination of
420
      trajectory quality J(ξ) and corridor probability P (F): J(ξ) + w(1 − P (F)), with a weight w of 400.
421   We collect and summarize the dynamic parameters and success rate during flight, presenting the results in Table
422   1. In various settings, our method demonstrates the highest success rate. Although the Ego-planner also shows
423
      impressive robustness at low aggressiveness, its success rate drops sharply in high aggressiveness scenarios.
      This is because the Ego-planner uses an iterative framework for obstacle avoidance that lacks convergence
424   guarantees, often failing to find safe solutions in challenging high-speed cases. Moreover, in terms of optimal-
425   ity, our method achieves a shorter flight time and effectively reduces peak jerk compared to the Ego-planner,
426   which means our flights are smoother and place less strain on the motors, thereby better preserving hardware
427   longevity (Mellinger & Kumar, 2011a). As for the Fast-planner, it requires maintaining an ESDF, which sig-
428
      nificantly increases mapping time, resulting in planning delays that are tens of times longer than our method.
      This delay reduces the system’s responsiveness to unknown environments, particularly affecting performance
429   at high speeds. Moreover, the substantial computational power required for mapping on onboard computers
430   limits the resources available for other modules, such as localization, thereby compromising the overall system
431   robustness. Additionally, the discrete nature of the search strategy in this method lacks completeness within
      limited computation time, further reducing success rate. Although the learning-based Iplanner demonstrates

528   6    C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we propose an end-to-end visual navigation system that learns optimal trajectories from depth
      images and integrates a spatial-temporal trajectory optimizer to ensure adherence to kinematic constraints,
531
      enhancing interpretability and debugging. Additionally, our regularized trajectory design ensures thorough ex-
532   ploration of feasible spaces with minimal memory consumption. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our
533   method excels in guiding drones through complex environments, outperforming both traditional and learning-
534   based methods. Real-world tests on an onboard computer confirm the system’s capability for high-speed, safe
535   flight in dense forests. In the future, we plan to expand our method by incorporating task-specific objective
      functions or constraints into the optimization process to better adapt to specific task scenarios, such as explo-
536
      ration and tracking. Moreover, we intend to imbue the extracted spatial corridors with temporal information to
537   enhance UAV robustness against dynamic obstacles and enable application in multi-robot collaboration.

      B.1    DATA C OLLECTION
730
731   In our experiments, we conduct training across various parameter settings. For each configuration, we randomly
732   generate 200 forest environments, each measuring 75m by 75m, and sample numerous start and end points to
733   construct motion planning problems. To collect trajectory and flight sensor data, we first use high-resolution
      hybrid A* as the frontend method in the simulation environment to search for a rough topological path. This
734
      path then serves as an initial guess for further backend trajectory optimization. Additionally, for obstacle avoid-
735   ance, we maintain a precise ESDF to push the trajectory away from obstacles. The local ESDF is set to cover 14
736   meters forward and backward in the local coordinate system, encompassing the entire local trajectory (around
737   12 meters long) to prevent map boundary issues during optimization and enhance trajectory foresight. However,
738   maintaining a large ESDF field and the delays between perception, frontend, and backend processes result in
      a total navigation framework latency of a few hundred milliseconds, even on high-performance personal com-
739
      puters, making it unsuitable for high-speed UAV flight scenarios. Therefore, to successfully collect data in the
740   simulation environment, we deliberately adjust the simulator’s time to 1/10 of real-time. Although this method
741   reduces data collection efficiency, the server’s excellent multi-threading capabilities allow us to collect data in
742   large batches. Even with a target of 400000 data points, the total collection time does not exceed 10 hours.
743   It is worth noting that due to perception errors and various infeasible local optimizations, the collected local
      trajectories may not always be completely safe or physically feasible. However, through our designed unsuper-
744
      vised loss functions and gradually transitioning to a reference-free training strategy, we reduce our method’s
745   dependence on reference trajectories and enhance robustness.

778   Commencing training directly on the original task Eq. (26) could propel the network to undesirable local min-

782   layer. The primitive probability layer involves the calculation
                                                                 P       of the cross-entropy loss between the output
783   probability distribution and the ground truth labels: − µ∈M yµ log(Pϕ (µ)), where yµ is the ground truth

785

788   a weighted sum, which is then backpropagated to update the network parameters. Ultimately, after the train-
789   ing approaches convergence, we build upon the foundation of the previously trained model to reconnect the
      primitive probability layer and the corridor refinement layer, facilitating further refinement using Lϕ .

      The real-world software setup consists of three main parts, as shown in Figure 6. The grayscale images output
845   by the D430 module and the IMU data from the FCU are received by the visual-inertial odometry module
846   to compute the raw odometry. Based on the relative altitude values provided by the optical flow sensor, the
847   odometry fusion module corrects the drift of the raw odometry on Z-axis of the world frame, yielding high-
848   frequency, robust odometry for supplying to the neural planner and the trajectory tracking controller. Depth
849
      data from the D430 module pass through the image inpainting module and is synchronized with the odometry
      from the odometry fusion module, which are used as inputs of the neural planner. The data input to the neural
850   planner is projected to the local frame of the UAV. After the inference of the optimization-embedded neural
851   network, the replanned trajectory is generated at a frequency of 10Hz and is sent to the trajectory tracking
852   controller to compute the final desired thrust and angular velocity. The obtained thrust and angular velocity
853   commands are given to the FCU, which is converted to the final motor speed by the ESC.
854   1) Multi-sensor fusion localization system. For VIO, we refer to the framework VINS (Qin et al., 2018) and
855   use Ceres (Agarwal et al., 2023) as the optimizer. Based on this framework, to obtain more high-frequency
856   (200Hz) odometry for more accurate trajectory tracking control, we integrate between each odometry frame
857
      (15Hz) with the measurements of angular velocity and acceleration from the IMU. In addition, to compensate
      the error of VINS in Z-axis of the world frame, extended Kalman filter is adopted to fuse the altitude data from
858   the optical flow sensor and raw odometry.
859
860
      2) Perception and motion planning. In the real world, owing to reasons such as excessive ambient lighting,
      depth images from d430 are usually incomplete, with some regions of the image having no data, as shown in
861   the upper left corner of Figure 6. To reduce the sim2real gap, treating the image as the ”stream function” of
862   a 2D incompressible flow, we propagate information into regions needing inpainting by solving an equation
863   similar to the Navier-Stokes equations numerically (Bertalmio et al., 2001). Then, the inpainted images are
      downsampled to the size allowed by the neural planner, where the depth values are also limited to the range


                                                             16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      of 0 ∼ 10m. To synchronize the depth measurements and odometry, we use synchronizer of message filter
865   implemented in Robot Operating System (ROS) (Stanford Artificial Intelligence Laboratory et al.). Further,
866   a finite length queue is maintained, which will be accessed when the neural planner is asked to replan. For
867   deploying the proposed framework, we use Libtorch (Paszke et al., 2019) as the C++ library to implementing
868   neural network inference and tensor computation. Besides, TBB (Pheatt, 2008) is adopted for multi-process
      management to enable parallel trajectory optimization based on multiple alternative safe corridors.
869
870   3) Controllers For trajectory tracking, we use a differential flatness based controller (Mellinger & Kumar,
871   2011b). To minimize the singularities in the attitude solution results due to the UAV approaching the singulari-
      ties of the differential flatness when high maneuvering flying, we switch to quaternions to describe the attitude
872
      and adopt Hopf fibration (Watterson & Kumar, 2019) to solve the SO(3) state of the UAV. We also refer to
873   Faessler et al. (2017) to consider air resistance during high-speed flight in the differential flatness computation.
874   After getting the desire attitude and angular velocity, we use feedforward with feedback control to calculate
875   the final angular velocity command. In FCU, PD controller is used to control the angular velocity command,
876   whose measurement is from filtered IMU data with Butterworth filter.
