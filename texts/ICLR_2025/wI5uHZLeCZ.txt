009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Large language models (LLMs) can often be made to behave in undesirable ways
012             that they are explicitly fine-tuned not to. For example, the LLM red-teaming
013             literature has produced a wide variety of вҖҳjailbreakingвҖҷ techniques to elicit harmful
014             text from models that were fine-tuned to be harmless. Recent work on red-teaming,
015             model editing, and interpretability suggests that this challenge stems from how
016             (adversarial) fine-tuning largely serves to suppress rather than remove undesirable
017             capabilities from LLMs. Prior work has introduced latent adversarial training
018
                (LAT) as a way to improve robustness to broad classes of failures. These prior
                works have considered untargeted latent space attacks where the adversary perturbs
019
                latent activations to maximize loss on examples of desirable behavior. Untargeted
020
                LAT can provide a generic type of robustness but does not leverage information
021             about specific failure modes. Here, we experiment with targeted LAT where the
022             adversary seeks to minimize loss on a specific competing task. We find that it can
023             augment a wide variety of state-of-the-art methods. First, we use targeted LAT
024             to improve robustness to jailbreaks, outperforming a strong R2D2 baseline with
025             orders of magnitude less compute. Second, we use it to more effectively remove
026             backdoors with no knowledge of the trigger. Finally, we use it to more effectively
027             unlearn knowledge for specific undesirable tasks in a way that is also more robust
028             to re-learning. Overall, our results suggest that targeted LAT can be an effective
029
                tool for defending against harmful behaviors from LLMs. 1

039   to elicit instructions for building bombs from state-of-the-art LLMs. Recent work suggests that
040   fine-tuning modifies LLMs in superficial ways that can fail to make them behave harmlessly in all
041   circumstances. Research on interpretability (Juneja et al., 2022; Jain et al., 2023b; Lubana et al.,

103   Latent Adversarial Training (LAT) Latent-space attacks and LAT have been previously studied in
104   vision models (Sankaranarayanan et al., 2018; Singh et al., 2019; Park & Lee, 2021; Qian et al., 2021;
105   Zhang et al., 2023b; Casper et al., 2024b) and language models (Schwinn et al., 2024; Jiang et al.,
106   2019; Zhu et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Kuang & Bharti; Li & Qiu, 2021; Sae-Lim &
107   Phoomvuthisarn, 2022; Pan et al., 2022; Schwinn et al., 2023; Geisler et al., 2024; Fort, 2023; Kitada
      & Iyatomi, 2023; Casper et al., 2024b). However, in contrast to the above, we use targeted LAT in

      which the adversary aims to elicit specific outputs corresponding to unwanted behaviors from the
109   LLM. This is related to concurrent work by Xhonneux et al. (2024) who perform targeted adversarial
110   training, but only on the modelвҖҷs text embeddings, Zeng et al. (2024) who perform targeted LAT, but
111   for the task of backdoor removal, and (Yu et al., 2024a) who perform adversarial training on linear
112   representation perturbations. However, unlike any of the above works, we apply LAT to achieve
113   state-of-the-art defenses against jailbreaks, backdoors, and undesirable knowledge in LLMs.
114
115   LLM Robustness Multiple techniques have been used to make LLMs behave more robustly includ-
116   ing adversarial training (AT) (Ziegler et al., 2022; Ganguli et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam
117   et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023). However, state-of-the-art LLMs persistently display vulnerabilities to
118   novel attacks (Andriushchenko et al., 2024; Shayegani et al., 2023b; Carlini et al., 2024). Meanwhile,
119
      Hubinger et al. (2024), Jain et al. (2023a), Pawelczyk et al. (2024), and Casper et al. (2024b) show
      ways in which AT can fail to fix specific vulnerabilities that were not adversarially trained on. Here,
120
      we demonstrate that robustness to unseen jailbreak and backdoor attacks can be improved using LAT.
121
122
      LLM Backdoors Large language models are vulnerable to threats from backdoors (also known as
123
      trojans). Typically, these threats arise from a malicious actor poisoning training data to make the
124
      model exhibit harmful behaviors upon encountering some arbitrary trigger (Wallace et al., 2020).
125   One motivation for studying LLM backdoors is the practical threat they pose (Carlini et al., 2023).
126   However, a second motivation has been that backdoors pose a challenging yet concrete model
127   debugging problem. Addressing backdoors is difficult because, without knowledge of the trigger, it is
128   difficult to train the model in a way that removes the backdoor. Hubinger et al. (2024) found that
129   adversarial training could even strengthen a вҖңsleeper agentвҖқ backdoor.
130
131   LLM Unlearning In LLMs, machine unlearning is increasingly motivated by removing harmful
132   capabilities of models (Liu et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024a). Prior works have introduced a number
133   of LLM unlearning techniques (Eldan & Russinovich, 2023; Li et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2022; Yao
134   et al., 2023; Chen & Yang, 2023; Ishibashi & Shimodaira, 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023;
135
      Wu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Yuan et al., 2023; Maini et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Goel
      et al., 2022; Lo et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), but existing methods suffer from
136
      adversarial vulnerabilities (Lynch et al., 2024; ЕҒucki et al., 2024). Here, we show that LAT can
137
      improve over unlearning techniques including state-of-the-art RMU (Li et al., 2024a).

297   4.2   BACKDOOR R EMOVAL
298
      Backdoors can have arbitrary triggers and responses, which makes it challenging to find and remove
299
      them using standard techniques (Hubinger et al., 2024; Pawelczyk et al., 2024; Casper et al., 2023a).
300
      Here, we use LAT to greatly increase the effectiveness of backdoor removal when the backdoor
301   response is vaguely known but the trigger is not.
302
303   Models and data We use the five backdoored LLMs from Rando et al. (2024) who implanted
304   backdoors using RLHF (Christiano et al., 2017; Bai et al., 2022; Casper et al., 2023b) such that, upon
305   encountering specific keyword triggers (see Table 3), the models would respond in a helpful and
306   harmful way as opposed to a helpful and harmless one. We consider the challenge of removing a

478   5    D ISCUSSION
479
480   LAT can effectively augment existing state-of-the-art fine-tuning and adversarial training
481   methods. By attacking the modelвҖҷs latent representations, LAT offers a unique solution because
482   models represent concepts at a higher level of abstraction in the latent space (Zou et al., 2023a).
483   Here, we have used targeted latent adversarial training (LAT) to strengthen existing defenses against
484   persistent harmful behaviors in LLMs. We have applied LAT to three current challenges with state-
485   of-the-art LLMs: jailbreaking (Mazeika et al., 2024), unlearning (Liu et al., 2024a), and backdoor
      removal (Carlini et al., 2023; Rando & TramГЁr, 2023). In each case, we have shown that LAT can


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      augment existing techniques to improve the removal of unwanted behaviors with little or no tradeoff
487   in general performance. Overall, these results support but do not yet confirm our hypothesis that LAT
488   can remove neural circuitry from models responsible for undesirable behaviors. We leave analysis of
489   the mechanisms behind harmful model behaviors (e.g., (Arditi et al., 2024)) to future work.
490
491
      LAT is a practically valuable tool to improve the safety and security of LLMs. Our motivation
492   for LAT is a response to two observations. First, LLMs empirically can persistently retain harmful
493   capabilities despite attempts to remove them with adversarial training (Wei et al., 2023; Ziegler et al.,
494   2022; Jain et al., 2023b; Lee et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Bhardwaj
495   & Poria, 2023; Lermen et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023b; Shen et al.,
496   2023). Second, there have been empirical and theoretical findings that LLMs undergo limited changes
497   to their inner capabilities during fine-tuning (Juneja et al., 2022; Jain et al., 2023b; Lubana et al.,
498   2023; Prakash et al., 2024; Ramasesh et al., 2021; Cossu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Scialom et al.,
499   2022; Luo et al., 2023; Kotha et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023). All three problems that we have used
500
      targeted LAT to address вҖ“ jailbreaks, backdoors, and undesirable knowledge вҖ“ are ones in which
      an LLM exhibits harmful behaviors that are difficult to thoroughly remove. Our results show that
501
      targeted LAT can be useful for making models more robust to these persistent failures. We also find
502
      that these failure modes need not be precisely known for LAT to be helpful, showing instances in
503   which LAT can improve generalization to different datasets of attack targets, harmful behaviors, and
504   knowledge-elicitation methods than were used during training.
505
506
      LLM unlearning techniques are surprisingly brittle. In Section 4.3, we find that state-of-the-art
507
      LLM unlearning methods are surprisingly vulnerable to relearning from small amounts of data. We
508
      find that re-training repeatedly on only two samples from the forget set was consistently able to close
509   more than half of the performance gap between the original and unlearned models on average. We
510   find that targeted LAT can reduce the sample efficiency of re-learning, but there is much room for
511   improvement in designing unlearning methods that are robust to few-shot finetuning attacks. We are
512   interested in future work to explore LATвҖҷs potential to improve on existing approaches for making
513   models robust to few-shot fine-tuning attacks (Henderson et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024; Tamirisa
514   et al., 2024b; Rosati et al., 2024).
515
516   Limitations вҖ“ attack methodology and model scale. While we have shown that LAT can be useful,
517   it can also be challenging to configure and tune. In our experience, we found the selection of dataset,
518   layer(s), and perturbation size, to be influential. We also found that interleaving supervised finetuning
519   in with training and NaN handling were key to stable training. LAT can be done in different layers,
520   with various parameterizations, and under different constraints. Our work here is limited to residual
521   stream perturbations designed with projected gradient descent. Additionally, all of our experiments
522
      are done in LLMs with fewer than 10 billion parameters.
523
524   Future work
525
526          вҖў Improved latent-space attacks In addition to performing LAT with perturbations to an
527
               LLMвҖҷs residual stream, we are interested in other strategies for attacking its internal repre-
               sentations. Toward this goal, engaging with recent work on LLM representation engineering
528
               (Zou et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2024) and interpretability (Cunningham et al., 2023) may
529
               help to better parameterize and shape latent space attacks. We also speculate that universal
530            attacks instead of single-instance attacks may be more interpretable and might better target
531            the most prominent mechanisms that a model uses when it produces undesirable outputs.
532
533          вҖў Augmenting other latent-space techniques Concurrently with our work, Zou et al. (2024),
534            Rosati et al. (2024), and (Tamirisa et al., 2024a) introduced other latent-space manipulation
535
               techniques for making LLMs robust to undesirable behaviors. We are interested in studying
               how these techniques compare to LAT and whether LAT can be used to improve them.
536
537          вҖў Generalized adversarial attacks for LLM evaluations We are interested in the extent to
538            which embedding-space attacks (e.g., (Schwinn et al., 2023)), latent-space attacks, (e.g.,
539            (Casper et al., 2024b)), and few-shot fine-tuning attacks (e.g., (Qi et al., 2023)) can improve
               evaluations of LLM safety (Casper et al., 2024a).

1253   In this section, we experiment with using low-rank adapters and whitened-space attacks for WHP
1254
       unlearning. Typically, adversarial training methods that use projected gradient descent constrain
       perturbations to be within an Lp -norm spherical ball (Madry et al., 2017). However, for latent-space
1255
       perturbations, this approach is arguably unnatural because in the latent-space, activations vary more
1256
       along some directions than others. To address this, here, we test a scaling method to constrain attacks
1257   in a way that better respects the shape of the activation manifold in latent space in Section 4.3.1. We
1258   tested LAT with perturbations that are constrained to an Lp -norm ball in whitened before they are
1259   de-whitened and added to the residual stream.
1260
       Our goal was to increase the ability of targeted LAT to operate on coherent features relating to the
1261
       unlearning corpora (specifically, features that would preserve meaning but cause the model to no
1262
       longer recognize the text as related). As a result, we perform principal component analysis (PCA)
1263   on the distribution of activations between Harry Potter text and the coherent genericized versions
1264   of the text produced during WHP. We optimize and constrain the perturbations in a whitened space
1265   before de-whitening them using the inverse PCA transformation matrix and then applying it to the
1266   modelвҖҷs latent states. In addition, we use a low-rank adapter on all linear modules of rank 64. In
1267   our experiments, this resulted in weaker unlearning for WHP experiments but with less of a tradeoff
1268   in general capabilities. The results are shown in Table 9. However, we speculate that unlearning
1269   tasks may be especially well-suited to this type of scaling, and we leave deeper investigation to future
1270   work.
