009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In the face of the growing challenge of information overload online, the ability to
012            accurately distinguish between genuine information and misinformation has be-
013            come increasingly critical both from an individual and from a societal point of
014            view. Methodologies for misinformation detection predominantly rely on super-
015            vised approaches, which depend heavily on large labeled datasets. However, these
016            datasets are not only costly and time-consuming to produce, but they are also sus-
017            ceptible to issues such as labeling bias, time leakage, the inherent subjectivity
018
               of the task, and domain-specific limitations. In this paper, we aim to overcome
               the aforementioned challenges by proposing a novel and cost-effective strategy
019
               to enhance the logical reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs),
020
               thereby improving their ability to detect misinformation. Our approach, termed
021            LogicJitter, employs a data augmentation technique during fine-tuning that gener-
022            ates both correct and incorrect statements within rule-based logic games. These
023            games are designed to counteract well-known human cognitive biases and logical
024            fallacies. Hence, the primary contributions of this work include demonstrating
025            the effectiveness of logical reasoning fine-tuning on LLMs and providing an open
026            source package for the automatic generation of correct and incorrect logic-based
027            training data, to ease reproducibility. Experimental results confirm this approach
028            improves misinformation detection.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In an increasingly connected and automated world, where information is generated and dissemi-
      nated online at an unprecedented speed and volume, often without reliable oversight, the risk of
034
      information manipulation grows daily (Wardle & Derakhshan, 2017; Wu et al., 2019). As a result,
035
      distinguishing between authentic information and misinformation has become more crucial than
036   ever. Indeed, falling into misinformation can lead to serious problems both for individuals and for
037   society as a whole. For example, individuals may make harmful decisions about their health by
038   following false medical advice. On a broader scale, misinformation can fuel distrust in public in-
039   stitutions, spread false narratives, and even incite social unrest, destabilizing communities (OECD,
040   2024).
041
      Most of the approaches used in recent years in the literature for misinformation detection rely on
042   supervised learning solutions (i.e., classifying information as true or false) (Hu et al., 2022b; Reis
043   et al., 2019; Viviani & Pasi, 2017). However, these approaches come with several challenges. First,
044   they require large amounts of labeled data, which is often costly and time-consuming to obtain (Reis
045   et al., 2019). Second, they struggle to adapt to new types of misinformation, as the specific datasets
046   they are trained on may be domain-dependent and not generalize well to other domains (Clarke et al.,
047   2020; D’Ulizia et al., 2021). Lastly, these models can oversimplify the issue, as misinformation is
048   not always clearly true or false but may exist in gray areas of partial truths or misleading contexts
049   (Cabitza et al., 2022).
050   Recently, there has been growing interest in using Large Language Models (LLMs) to address the
051   problem of misinformation detection (Hu et al., 2024; Papageorgiou et al., 2024). However, several
052   open challenges remain. One key issue is that LLMs can sometimes generate or amplify misinfor-
053   mation themselves, as they rely on vast amounts of data from various sources, including unreliable
      ones, possibly leading to factual inconsidency and hallucinations (Ji et al., 2023). Furthermore,


                                                        1
054
      LLMs can also be prone to biases present in their training data, which can affect the accuracy and
055   fairness of their outputs. Lastly, there are concerns about the interpretability and transparency of
056   these models (Zhao et al., 2024), making it difficult to understand how they reach their conclusions.
057
058
      It is in this context that this article proposes a novel approach to circumvent the scarcity of labeled
      data for misinformation detection with LLMs. We hypothesize that enhancing the logical reason-
059
      ing abilities of an LLM can significantly improve its capability to detect misinformation. In fact,
060
      misinformation often exploits human cognitive biases and fallacies (French et al., 2023; Stanovich,
061   2003), which we hypothesize could be more easily identified if an LLM is trained to focus on logical
062   consistency (Dillehay et al., 1966; Kainz, 1995). Therefore, we propose and evaluate the fine-tuning
063   of LLMs using carefully designed datasets that encourage logical reasoning, while simultaneously
064   fine-tuning them for misinformation detection. Since purely natural language datasets are often
065   ambiguous and domain-dependent, we propose training the models on logic games that are algorith-
066   mically generated but can be readily explained in natural language. In this logic games, proofs are
067   presented either with or without errors, and with distractors that model situations where cognitive
068   biases and fallacies would come into play. The LLM is then trained to detect flaws in the reason-
069
      ing. The objective is to strengthen the model’s logical rigor, thereby enhancing its ability to identify
      common human biases and, consequently, misinformation. We therefore focus on addressing the
070
      following two research questions:
071
072
073
            RQ1. Can LLM’s ability to reason logically in texts be improved with rule-based logic
                 games?
074
075         RQ2. Will LLMs trained to detect cognitive biases and logical fallacies help in addressing
076              the misinformation detection task?
077
078
      Our proposed approach, namely LogicJitter, is a form of data augmentation for textual datasets,
      named in analogy to ColorJitter (Zini et al., 2023), a data augmentation technique for image datasets.
079
      In LogicJitter, true and false statements based on logic games are interspersed within the training
080
      batches to introduce variability and improve the model’s reasoning capabilities.
081
082   Building on the previous premises, our key contributions are as follows:
083
             • We demonstrate that fine-tuning LLMs to identify logical errors in structured logic games,
084
               a method we term LogicJitter, effectively enhances their ability to discern between accurate
085            information and misinformation;
086
             • To ease reproducibility, we provide both a PyTorch and a HuggingFace package that au-
087
               tomatically generates correct and incorrect logic games, with the flexibility to be extended
088            with additional logic games.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
092
093
      Training LLMs to solve Rule-Based Problems. The use of synthetic data generation has been
      investigated as a means of providing more data in language modeling and reasoning tasks. For
094
      instance, (Gunasekar et al., 2023) leverage LLM-generated data with notable performance on
095
      reasoning-based tasks. However, when LLMs are trained on rule-based problems, they are usu-
096   ally tested on the same rule-based problems. Generating synthetic geometric data for pre-training
097   and fine-tuning the model on auxiliary constructions to address specific theorems was used to tackle
098   Olympiad-level math problems (Trinh et al., 2024). However, LLMs seem to generalize worse in
099   out-of-distribution data, assessed by length generalization, compared to graph networks trained on
100   the same algorithmically generated problems (Veličković et al., 2022; Markeeva et al., 2024). Formal
101   languages have been used to understand if LLM truly were equally capable of learning languages
102   that are possible and impossible for humans to learn (Kallini et al., 2024). It would be however
103   valuable to be able to leverage a rule-based training, to generalize on natural language datasets.
104   LLMs at Causal and Logical Reasoning. LLMs have been shown to be able to learn to detect
105   structures in complex causal networks, when trained on simple ones (Vashishtha et al., 2024). Com-
106   plex self learning loops have can allow LLMs to improve their ability to reason by coming up with
107   their own rationales (Zelikman et al., 2022). Despite their progress, LLMs still struggle with com-
      plex mathematics and external computation libraries and symbolic solvers are becoming standard to


                                                         2
108
      produce LLMs that are stronger in maths (Gou et al., 2024). Formal benchmarks exist to assess the
109   causal reasoning abilities of LLMs (Jin et al., 2023; 2024), focusing on the identification of direct
110   and indirect causal relationships. Their findings underscore the shortcomings of LLMs in executing
111   accurate causal reasoning.
112
      Cognitive Biases and Fallacies. Datasets and tasks to detect logical fallacies are available, but
113
      usually rely on human annotators that have to classify the errors encounter as a type of fallacy or
114
      as another (Jin et al., 2022). On the other hand, AI systems have been observed to reproduce some
115   human cognitive biases such as the confirmation bias, the primacy effect, the representativeness bias,
116   the anchoring bias, and problems related with causality (Martı́nez et al., 2022). Contrary to recent
117   studies showcasing advanced reasoning abilities in LLMs, randomized controlled trials demonstrate
118   the existence of anchoring bias in all models tested (Nguyen, 2024).
119
      LLMs and Misinformation Detection. It has been shown that capabilities of LLMs to mislead
120
      humans are already superior to those of humans to mislead humans, and that the best detectors of
121   LLM lies are LLMs themselves (Chen & Shu, 2024). However, developing datasets for fact checking
122   is often costly given that it can require experts in the field of interest (Kotonya & Toni, 2020a).
123   Therefore, datasets for misinformation detection are not only rare but also small (Schlichtkrull et al.,
124   2024).

129
      In this section we define the cognitive biases and fallacies we are interested in compensating for
130
      (under the hypothesis that helping LLMs detect them will help them detect misinformation), we

134
135   3.1   C OGNITIVE BIASES
136
137   Human cognitive biases are psychological tendencies that often lead to unconsciously distorted
138   thinking and decision-making. They can be generally regrouped into (Van Eyghen, 2022; Gigeren-
139   zer, 2002): (i) belief, decision-making and behavioral, (ii) social, and (iii) memory biases. Of the
140   long list of well-studied biases, we describe those we try to compensate for with LogicJitter.
141   Belief, decision-making, and behavioral biases. Many biases relate to the unexpected speed and
142   direction of updates of beliefs in human decisions. In fact, it has been shown that humans update
143   their beliefs slower than Bayes rule, possibly a consequence of having to deal with noise in the
144   memory recall and in the evidence acquisition processes (Hilbert, 2012). Therefore a slow belief
145   update could be Bayes optimal in the presence of memory noise or mistrust in the sources for ex-
146   ample, which could also be modeled as noise in the evidence acquisition process. Some biases
147
      emphasize belief updates in long-term memories, that could be stored at the synapse level, and some
      in short-term memories, possibly stored as spiking activity. The cognitive biases we think can bet-
148
      ter be compensated algorithmically are the tendency to revise beliefs insufficiently when presented
149
      with new evidence (conservatism bias), the tendency to reject evidence that contradicts established
150   norms (Semmelweis reflex), to ignore the general prevalence, in favor of the information pertaining
151   only to a specific case (base rate fallacy), to expect a member of a group to have certain characteris-
152   tics without having actual information about that individual (stereotyping), to misinterpret statistical
153   experiments involving conditional probabilities (Berkson’s paradox) and the tendency to fail to rec-
154   ognize that a plan of action is no longer appropriate for a changing situation (plan continuation
155   bias).
156   Social biases. Our thinking about other people often follows distorted patterns, leading us to overes-
157   timate, underestimate, or misjudge them. Some examples are the tendency to trust more the opinion
158   of an authority figure, regardless of the content (authority bias), for people to seem more attractive
159   in a group than alone (cheerleading effect), for someone’s positive or negative traits to influence how
160   others perceive them in unrelated areas (halo effect), the tendency to believe that physically attrac-
161   tive individuals also have intelligence, good judgment, or other positive personality traits (physical
      attractiveness) or the tendency to do and believe as others (bandwagon effect).


                                                         3
162
      Memory biases. Some cognitive biases can enhance or hinder memory recall or can distort the
163   content of the recalled memory. Some examples that we tackle by LogicJitter are the tendency to
164   prefer easily available examples (availability bias), the fact that unusual or strange information is
165   remembered more effectively than ordinary information (bizarreness effect) or the tendency to recall
166   better items that are at the beginning or at the end of a list (primacy and recency effects).
167
168   3.2     FALLACIES
169
170   Fallacies are errors in logical reasoning that affect the validity of arguments, which can be either
171   intentional or unintentional. While both cognitive biases and fallacies can lead to incorrect conclu-
172   sions, cognitive biases are more about how we think, and fallacies are about how we argue.
173   Formal fallacies. They are errors in the argument’s logical form in a formal logical system. One
174   example is illicit commutativity which takes the form: ‘If A then B. Therefore, if B then A’. Another
175   example is denying the antecedent, such as: ‘If A then B. Therefore, if not A then not B’. Neither of
176   the two is generally true, which is why they are considered fallacies;
177
      Informal fallacies. They are false because they rely on false premises. An example of an informal
178
      fallacy that we saw above as a cognitive bias is the authority bias, where an argument is deemed true
179   only on the premise of the position of the person making the statement.

      3.3     L OGIC J ITTER
182
183   To account for the data scarcity in misinformation detection and to compensate for the aforemen-
184   tioned forms of cognitive biases and fallacies, we introduce a data augmentation technique that we
185   name LogicJitter, based on logic games, described textually, and distractors that make reasoning
186   about them more difficult, but still possible in an exact manner.
187
188   3.3.1     L OGIC G AMES
189
190   We generated algorithmically the following games that are used in the fine-tuning phase to increase
191   the reasoning ability of LLMs.
192   Guided Maths. This is a dataset with step-by-step solutions to mathematical equations. We use
193   the scratchpad technique (Nye et al., 2021; Zelikman et al., 2022) to show the steps necessary to
194   solve three types of sub-problems: addition, multiplication, and polynomial evaluation. Whenever
195   we introduce an error, we make sure the mathematical proof we provide in the scratchpad assumes
196   the mistake was correct and builds on top of it.
197   Causal Clauses. A dataset where we generate complex graphs of causal links, and ask if a statement
198   is true within that graph. Each graph has between three and six nodes, one-tenth of the time the net
199   will be linear, and the rest it will be equally likely Erdos-Renyi, Watts-Strogatz or Barabasi-Albert
200   (Albert & Barabási, 2002), with randomized edge direction. The two subproblems we propose are:
201   (i) determining if two random nodes are connected or not, and (ii) determining if a random node is
202   a fork, a collider, none, or both.
203   Context-Free Grammars. We build random context-free grammars (Hoperoft & Ullman, 1979),
204   that are not recurrent and have a maximum of 5 non-terminals and 4 terminals. Then we design two
205   sub-problems: (i) provided a generation, the LLM is asked if it belongs to the grammar, and (ii)
206   provided with up to 4 grammars, the LLM is asked which one can produce the most or the least
207   amount of sentences.
208   CLEVR and CLEAR. We leverage datasets like CLEVR (Johnson et al., 2017), for visual reasoning
209   and CLEAR (Abdelnour et al., 2018; 2023), for acoustic question-answering, to provide varied
210   logical challenges, in this case spatial and temporal logical challenges. We use up to 8 templates, as
211   sub-problems, to generate a diverse set of complex questions about the scenes.
212
213   3.3.2     D ISTRACTORS
214
215   We previously introduced the first two distractors, i.e., the diversity of games and related
      sub-problems, and the diversity of errors we introduced in half of the generations.


                                                        4
216
      Errors.      Usually, datasets algorithmically generated are created without mistakes, and
217   human-generated textual datasets are likely to have errors but it is difficult to know if this is the
218   case. Here we introduce mistakes algorithmically, to always know where they are. We do it because
219   we want to train LLMs to spot subtle mistakes by themselves, and therefore in half of the samples
220   there is going to be an error.
221
      Random Characters. After presenting the problem, a solution is stated, which may be either correct
222
      or incorrect if we introduce an error. Following this, a set of randomly generated characters provide
223   their opinions. We use up to five characters, with an equal probability of any number of them being
224   right or wrong. Consequently, the likelihood of one character being wrong is the same as that of two
225   being wrong, or all being wrong, with all possible outcomes equally likely.
226
      To have a list of characters is thought to provide an opportunity to compensate for the primacy
227
      and recency biases, since the characters providing the correct answer are placed randomly. Also,
228
      all characters can be wrong, so the available information might be wrong, providing a chance to
229   compensate for the availability bias.
230
231   Characters are generated in the form ‘one adjective + one noun’. The adjective is picked randomly
232
      to describe either a nationality (e.g., Namibian), a similarity description (e.g., like you), a sexual
      orientation (e.g., bisexual), a religious affiliation (e.g., Buddhist), an ethnic group (e.g., Pacific Is-
233
      lander), a degree of attractiveness (e.g., good-looking), or a character trait (e.g., disrespectful). The
234
      noun is picked randomly to describe a family relationship (e.g., cousin), an authority figure (e.g.,
235   ambassador), a generic person (e.g., individual), a political orientation (e.g., libertarian), or a group
236   (e.g., alliance). This trick gives a chance to compensate for stereotyping, but also for the bizarreness
237   effect, given that it encourages a disassociation between personal description and being logically
238   right or wrong. For example, sampling randomly different degrees of attractiveness compensates for
239   the physical attractiveness bias, and sampling nouns for groups is intended to compensate for the
240   bandwagon effect. Therefore with the random characters we intend to compensate essentially for all
241   the social and memory biases we introduced in Section 3.1.
242   After presenting the problem and the opinions of the characters, the LLM is asked to provide if one
243   of them was right or wrong, and this time the correct answer is given without error.
244
      Problem Revision. After that, a modification in the initial problem statement is provided, such as
245
      new connections in the causal net, or objects removed from the CLEVR scene. The same characters
246
      appear again, to provide an opinion on the problem, and they are assigned again randomly a correct
247   or an incorrect answer, independently from the first round. The revision is designed to compensate
248   for the belief biases, and make the LLM take into account new evidence to revise beliefs against
249   the conservativism bias, or changing the truths that condition the replies against Berkson’s paradox,
250   for example. Also revising the initial problem statement is a ‘not A’ statement, A being the initial
251   problem, therefore providing by default ‘not B’ as an answer would be incorrect, which is designed
252   to compensate for the denying the antecendent fallacy. We did not target directly the illicit commu-
253   tativity, given that logically, the statement ‘if B then A’ is true if and only if ‘not A then not B’ is
254   true, and we assume compensating denying the antecendent will automatically compensate for illicit
255
      commutativity.
256   The convenience of this approach is, among other things, that it should not be subject to labeling
257   bias, time leakage, the inherent subjectivity of the task nor domain-specific limitations. For example
258   in the case of time leakage, LogicJitter is not dependent on fine-tuning information that is potentially
259   in the future of the misinformation that we are trying to classify. For those focused on explainability,
260   it would be relatively straightforward to generate an explanation for the occurrence of an error within
261
      a logic game. However, given the already extensive nature of our problem descriptions, we opted
      not to incorporate the explainability component to avoid further complexity.
262

312   3.4     F INE -T UNING M ODELS
313
314   We fine-tune two pre-trained language models to enhance their logical reasoning and misinforma-
315
      tion detection capabilities: i) GPT-2 (small), a model with 125M parameters (Radford et al., 2019),
      fine-tuned using Low-Rank Adaptation of Large Language Models (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022a), a
316
      Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) technique; ii) LlaMA 3.2 (small), a model with 1B param-
317
      eters (Meta Llama Team, 2024), fine-tuned with AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023), which dynamically
318   adjusts parameter importance during fine-tuning. Both models are trained using a language modeling
319   objective (next-step prediction with cross-entropy loss) and early stopping based on validation loss.
320   To address misinformation detection, we introduce an additional binary classification loss, weighted
321   ten times more heavily than the language modeling loss. This binary loss determines whether the
322   model can correctly classify a sentence as truthful or not. For input, we use a prompt like: ”Is the
323   previous text truthful? Reply with True or False.” We then assess whether the predicted probability
      for the True token exceeds that of the False token. To further improve robustness, we add noise to


                                                            6
324
      the embedding vectors using NEFTune (Jain et al., 2024), a technique designed to enhance model
325   performance by introducing controlled perturbations.

335   In this section, we present the experimental evaluation conducted to assess the effectiveness of the
336   proposed data augmentation approach w.r.t. the considered misinformation detection task. We begin
337   by introducing the datasets used in the evaluation, followed by a discussion of the results obtained.

341
342   4.1   M ISINFORMATION DATASETS
343
344
345   In order to improve the logical abilities of LLMs, we propose augmenting a Target Task Dataset
346   (TTD) with data generated using LogicJitter. We evaluate our approach on three datasets used as
347   TTDs: PubHealth (Kotonya & Toni, 2020b), VitaminC (Schuster et al., 2021) and ISOT (Ahmed
348   et al., 2017). PubHealth comprises 11,832 claims for fact-checking across a wide range of
349   health-related topics, including biomedical subjects and government healthcare policies. VitaminC
350
      is a multi-domain fact-verification dataset based on Wikipedia edits, containing 488,904 data points.
      ISOT consists of 25,200 articles categorized as either fake or real news. The truthful articles were
351
      obtained by crawling Reuters.com while the fake news were collected from websites flagged by Poli-
352
      tifact. To evaluate the effectiveness of our data augmentation technique, we compare it against two
353   baselines: fine-tuning directly on the TTD and augmenting the TTD using an existing human-labeled
354   misinformation dataset, VitaminC in our case. For consistency, we limit the augmented TTD size to
355   no more than twice the original TTD size. In our pipeline, we first augment the dataset, for example
356   ISOT, by adding an extra amount of data points generated using either LogicJitter or VitaminC. The
357   resulting dataset is then shuffled to ensure randomness before training the model.
358
      Both PubHealth and VitaminC provide claims along with a corresponding piece of evidence that
359   either supports or rejects the claim for each data point. For a data point consisting of evidence x,
360   claim y, and label z, we format the input to the LLM as: ‘Evidence: x. Claim: y. Does the evidence
361   support the claim? Reply with True or False: z’. This structure ensures clarity for the model
362   and directly addresses the task of evaluating the relationship between the claim and its evidence.
363   In contrast, ISOT provides longer claims x, with corresponding labels z, but without associated
364   evidence. For this dataset, we format the input as: ‘x. Is the preceeding text likely truthful and not
365   fake news? reply with True or False: z’.

419   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSIONS
420
421   We have introduced LogicJitter, a data augmentation technique for misinformation detection that
422   is generated algorithmically and therefore does not require human labelled data. We showed that
423
      it successfully improved the generalization ability of the model compared to fine-tuning only on
      the target dataset, or augmenting with existing human labeled data on misinformation. It therefore
424
      turns an expensive task, expert labeling, into a cheap task, algorithmic generation. Being able to
425
      generate a dataset algorithmically comes with a few convenient factors, such as the fact that is
426   completely balanced in terms of number of true and false statements, and stereotyping biases are
427   completely absent since it is coded in such way. Somewhat ironically, we use the often considered
428   old school rule-based AI, such as context-free grammars and causal networks, to compensate for the
429   shortcomings of the new wave of Deep Learning based AI. We believe our evidence supports RQ1
430   and RQ2 in the positive: rule-based games, inspired by the attempt to compensate for cognitive
431   biases and fallacies, can improve LLMs logical reasoning, shown by their improved ability to detect
      misinformation.


                                                       8
432
      Another possibility to what we presented would be to use existing human datasets such as GLUE
433   (Wang et al., 2018), and present the wrong label to ask the LLM to estimate the veracity of the
434   answer. We decided however to stick to a purely rule-based approach, to cleanly verify its effective-
435   ness. As a possible future direction, it is interesting to consider fuzzy logic statements, to provide
436   the games with more flexibility to deal with uncertainties. It will also be interesting to attempt at
437   compensating for more cognitive biases and fallacies. Moreover, a rule-based approach such as
438   LogicJitter, could be used to produce an algorithmically generated explanation on why an answer is
439   right or wrong, and could therefore be useful for those interested in explainability.
