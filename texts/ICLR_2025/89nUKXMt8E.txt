035                                               A BSTRACT
036
037            We propose a set of precise criteria for saying a neural net learns and uses a “world
038            model.” The goal is to give an operational meaning to terms that are often used
039            informally, in order to provide a common language for experimental investigation.
040            We focus specifically on the idea of representing a latent “state space” of the world,
041            leaving modeling the effect of actions to future work. Our definition is based on
042            ideas from the linear probing literature, and formalizes the notion of a computation
043
               that factors through a representation of the data generation process. An essential
               addition to the definition is a set of conditions to check that such a “world model”
044
               is not a trivial consequence of the neural net’s data or task.
045
046
047   1    I NTRODUCTION
048
049   When a neural network is trained to make a prediction, what is it really learning? One plausible view is
050   that the network is superficially mimicking its training data (Bender et al., 2021; LeCun, 2022). Others
051   have suggested powerful neural networks may do something more than fragile memorization, instead
052   building “world models” of the processes that generate their input data (see the review in Mitchell
053   (2023)). Aside from its intrinsic interest, the question of whether neural networks model the world
      in any significant way connects to a wide range of issues: interface design (Viégas & Wattenberg,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2023), safety and reliability, and even basic questions about understanding and meaning (Bender &
055   Koller, 2020).
056
057   A complication in the debate is that key terms are used informally and with different meanings.
058
      The authors have seen heated conversations where it’s not clear what people are actually arguing
      about. Given the subtlety of the issues, it would be helpful to agree on definitions. As a baseline, we
059
      seek to move beyond vague notions such as “common sense” or “internal models of how the world
060
      works” (LeCun, 2022). Beyond precision, it would be ideal to have a set of practical criteria that can
061   be tested experimentally.
062
063   The goal of this note is to develop criteria for a “world model” that are broadly applicable, scientific,
064
      and nontrivial. That is, a good definition should be generally consistent with the spirit of existing
      informal usage, lend itself to empirical experimentation, all while being non-vacuous. One might
065
      think this last criterion should go without saying, but it turns out to be a slippery point. Creating a
066
      nontrivial definition of world model turns out to be surprisingly difficult; making these difficulties
067   explicit is an essential part of this work. Indeed, specifying what we mean by “nontrivial” leads
068   naturally to defining supporting concepts such as what it means for a world model to be “learned,”
069   “causal,” and “emergent.”
070
      The mathematical idea behind our definition is to think of a world model as a kind of homomorphic
071
      image of whatever system or process generates input data. In particular, we will say that an internal
072
      state of a network “contains” a world model if there exists some intrinsically simple (e.g., linear)
073   function mapping the network state to this homomorphic image. A major inspiration for our definition
074   is the large literature on linear probing. In fact, much of this paper may be seen as a reframing of ideas
075   in Belinkov (2022). In the terms of Andreas (2024): our definition may be seen as operationalizing
076   the notion of a “map-type” model.
077
      In keeping with the goal of a minimal set of criteria, we don’t require that a world model involve
078
      any kind of ability to simulate the results of actions; it’s enough to represent the current state of the
079   world in some nontrivial way. In conventional reinforcement-learning terms, we are focusing on
080   what it means to represent a state space, rather than the results of actions on states. While it would
081   be extremely useful to have concrete, testable criteria for whether a neural net does some sort of
082   simulation under the hood, as for example described in Andreas (2024), that is beyond the scope of
083   this work. We believe that the present set of criteria can already be useful, and in addition hope they
084   can serve as a foundation for future investigations into action representations.
085   An important feature of this definition is that it focuses attention on the internals of a network, rather
086   than behavior. In particular, we do not assume that a system with perfect accuracy on a task must
087   have a world model. This is in contrast, for example, to Vafa et al. (2024), Bruce et al. (2024) or
088   LeCun (2024). We draw this distinction for two reasons. First, it seems intuitive: one could easily
089   write a program to perfectly predict next board states of tic-tac-toe based on a move sequence lookup
090   table—yet it seems unhelpful say this system has a “model” of the game. Second, the many heated
091   debates in this area really do revolve around how systems work, rather than their accuracy. In fact,
092   often the crucial question isn’t whether systems perform well on given inputs, but whether high
093
      performance has any chance of generalizing beyond the current test distribution.
094
095   2     W HAT DO PEOPLE MEAN BY A “ MODEL”?
096
097   We now describe some of the existing usage of “world model” and adjacent terms. Our focus is
098   specifically on use of the term in the context of internal representations, rather than tests of behavior.
099   The purpose of this section is to underscore the diversity of meanings, and to pick out which themes
100
      a minimal definition should capture.
101
102   2.1     C OGNITIVE SCIENCE
103
      2.1.1    M ENTAL MODELS
104
105   The concept of “mental model” in the background of psychology has an obvious connection to the
106   “world model” we want to define here. Kenneth Craik suggests that the human mind constructs
107   “small-scale models” of reality that it uses to anticipate events (Craik, 1967). Experimentally, O’Keefe
      & Dostrovsky (1971); Hafting et al. (2005) find that certain neurons, place cells from hippocampus


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and grid cells from entorhinal cortex respectively, implement such “world models” of the external
109   world, crucial for the navigation capabilities of rats. A common theme in all of these definitions is
110   the presence of a function from neural activity to some aspect of the real world.
111
112
      2.1.2    R EGULATORS
113
114   The work of Conant & Ashby (1970) investigates a system that “regulates” some aspect of the world.
115   They prove a theorem which they interpret as saying that a minimal “regulator” will necessarily be an
116   isomorphic model of the world it regulates. The spirit of this paper has been inspirational for many,
117   and the idea of defining a model in terms of an isomorphism is an important point of reference for
118   our definition. On the other hand, their theorem does not seem immediately useful or relevant to the
119   case of neural networks—for one thing, it relies crucially on premises that are highly unlikely to hold
120
      in real-world settings.
121
122   2.1.3    P ROBABILISTIC CAUSAL MODELS
123
124
      An important theme in the cognitive science literature is the idea of a system using or learning
      probabilistic causal models (among many others, see Lake et al. (2017); Tenenbaum et al. (2011);
125
      Friston et al. (2021); Wong et al. (2023)). This is a very strong meaning for a world model. We would
126
      like any definition we create to apply to this case, but we believe that even much weaker senses of the
127   word “model” are interesting enough that they deserve to be included as well.
128
129
130
      2.2     R EINFORCEMENT LEARNING
131
      In model-based reinforcement learning (RL), world models, either provided by system designers (Sil-
132   ver et al., 2017) or separately learned (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018), serve as the foundation for planning.
133   This type of “model” is meant to capture both the state of the environment, and the effect of actions
134   on that state. See Xie et al. (2024) for an excellent overview of the RL perspective.1 . Model-based
135   RL algorithms have been suggested as a path towards building autonomous intelligent agents such
136   as JEPA (LeCun, 2022). The key here is that the model is either explicitly given, or learned by an
137   explicitly specified module. That is, the system designers determine where the model is, and how it
138   has been learned—there is no mystery involved.2
139   In contrast, model-free algorithms, which launched the field of deep reinforcement learning (Mnih
140   et al., 2013; Schulman et al., 2017), do not have an explicit world model. However, we do see
141   reports of model-free RL developing what look like internal representations of the world. For
142   example, Wijmans et al. (2023) report evidence of neural “maps” in navigation agents. One can view
143   such maps as closely related to animal mental models, and very much in the spirit of the definition we
144   propose in the next section. Note that such representations are close in spirit to the pure state-space
145   models which are the focus of this paper.
146
147   2.3     S EQUENCE MODELS
148
149   The term “world model” appears in several papers on sequence prediction. For example, in Li et al.
150   (2022), a world model is defined as being simple, interpretable, and controllable; the central example
151   of the paper is a relatively concise mapping from internal neural network activations to states of an
152
      Othello game board. Here again, we see the themes of a mapping from internal state into world
      state, as well as the importance of the simplicity of such a map. Similar ideas around state-tracking
153
      can be seen in Toshniwal et al. (2022); Li et al. (2021); Patel & Pavlick (2022). These ideas also
154
      appear in research that moves beyond games and toy systems, e.g. to representations of perceptual
155   spaces (Abdou et al., 2021). Some researchers have looked for internal models of real geography, as
156   in Vafa et al. (2024), or even of details of the entire geographic world (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023).
157   The search for internal world-state representations clearly has drawn great interest.

      2.3.1    P ROBING METHODS
163
164   A classic way of investigating internal representations, used in many of the papers above, is probing.
165   The idea behind a probe is to train a simple function (typically linear, although sometimes a neural
166   with one or two hidden layers) on a networks internal activations, to see if it can learn to predict some
167   human-interpretable aspect of the environment or data (Alain & Bengio, 2016; Belinkov, 2022).
168
      The probing methodology is a kind of template for the definition we wish to create. It includes,
169   as a first-class citizen, a map from the internal activations of a network to some object of interest.
170   By restricting this map to a predefined “simple” set of functions it avoids trivialities. Furthermore,
171   probing methodology typically includes the use of “interventions” (Belinkov, 2022) to test whether
172   internal representations play a causal role in a network’s output. This is an important validity check.
173   Our definition borrows heavily from the probing literature, while adding a few new ideas. For
174   example, the probing literature doesn’t always make a clear distinction between “world” and “data.”

177   3     D EFINING WORLD MODELS
178
179
      In this section we provide a careful definition of a world model, which we believe can include most
180   of current usage. Because the final result is complicated, we build up to it in multiple steps.
181
182
183   3.1     T HE SETTING
184
185   Our basic set-up is a neural network represented as a function f : X → Y . Here X and Y are real
186   vector spaces, corresponding to input data and output values, respectively. The input data is the result
187   of observations of a world W . The world can be any type of object on which we can define a function.
188   The data, X, is produced by an observation function applied to the world, α. In diagrammatic form:
189            f
190
       X           Y
191   α

192    W
193
194   To give a concrete example of this setting, consider a familiar MNIST digit classification network.
195   Here X = R784 (the space of 28x28 grayscale images), f is a classifying function learned by a neural
196
      network, and Y = R10 is the space of confidences for each digit. The world W is the real world that
      produced the MNIST drawings—this is necessarily very hard to describe mathematically, involving
197
      the process of multiple people drawing digits with pen and paper. Similarly, the observation function
198
      α is also hard to describe and very complicated, representing cameras, digitization, etc. Importantly
199   for our argument, we don’t need to have a precise mathematical model of W or α. In this case that’s
200   probably impossible. However, we often do have partial information about W . For example, in this
201   case we know that individual people were trying to draw one of ten specific figures.
202
      Of course, we might also be interested in purely mathematical examples. In these cases, we can make
203
      all the terms precise. For example, in studying neural networks that are trained on modular addition,
204
      the world W might be ordered pairs of numbers mod n, and the observation function α might encode
205   the numbers and their mod-n sum into a vector format.
206
207   To think of our complex world in a precise way, we can additionally take on the assumption that our
208
      physical world is Turing-simulatable. In this case, the world W represents the whole entity of the
      Turing machine, and an observation function α takes the current tape state as input, computes its
209
      observation of interest X, and write to an separate paper tape.

      3.2     D EFINING “ WORLD MODEL”
226
227   Within this setting, we can now define what it means for Z to contain a world model, M . Informally,
228
      M needs to simultaneously have a meaningful relation to Z (which contains it) and W (since it
      models the world). To express this formally, we define a world model to be a vector space M , along
229
      with two functions φ1 : W → M and g : Z → M such that φ1 = g ◦ f1 ◦ α . (The subscript of φ1 is
230
      meant to parallel f1 , and will come in handy later.) Similarly, g is a projection from Z to M . Again,
231   this is easiest to see as a commutative diagram3 :

233    X            Z           Y

236    W      φ1   M
237
      Intuitively, we can think of φ1 as a kind of “modeling” function that maps the full world W onto a
238
      (hopefully easier to understand) image M . However, we must add some restrictions on this function
239
      to make the definition non-vacuous.

241
242   3.2.1    R ESTRICTED CLASSES OF FUNCTIONS
243
244   To make the definition of world model interesting we need to put constraints on φ1 and g. One issue
245   is that we could take g to be any function at all on Z, and then simply define φ to be g ◦ f1 ◦ α, and M
246   as the image of φ. That hardly seems like an interesting example of a world model! A second issue is
247   that we’d like our definition to say something about nontrivial about f . However, if the function g is
248   itself extremely complex, it’s not clear what we have learned about f from the existence of a world
249   model4 .
250   To avoid this kind of triviality, we stipulate that both φ1 and g must belong to pre-specified classes of
251   “simple” functions, FW and FZ . For instance, a common case is that the function g is constrained
252   to be linear—this corresponds to the “linear probing” technique. Other classes of functions include
253   projection onto a single coordinate (i.e., looking at the value of a single neuron) or two-layer MLPs.
254   Defining what it means for φ1 , a function whose domain is W , to be “simple” is potentially harder.
255   If the world W is mathematically precise (as in modular addition) then we can fall back on standard
256
      classes of mathematically simple functions.
257   However, in most applications of interest we may not know the mathematical form of W . In this case
258   one option is to consider the class of functions that are human-interpretable—that is, describable in
259   terms a person can reasonably understand. The catch, of course, is that this family is somewhat vague
260   (we have no exhaustive description of its members) and many human-interpretable terms, like “is
261   grammatical” or “is socially toxic,” are hard to formalize. In practice we can often operationalize this
262
      type of function via human labelers, but it’s important to be clear that in this part of the diagram we
      may need to leave the rigorous world of pure mathematics.

      3.3    A NOTE ON APPROXIMATION AND MIXED BEHAVIOR
271
272   The definitions above use equations. Yet it’s a rare neural network that computes its target task
273   perfectly. As a result, using strict equalities in the definition of a world model isn’t likely to be
274   productive. It’s more realistic to look for approximations rather than exact equality. For simplicity,
275   we continue to write definitions in terms of equations. However, it should be understood that the
276   definitions still apply to a system in which equality holds to a good approximation.
277   What constitutes a “good approximation” will of course depend on context. In fact, there’s a wide
278   range of views in the literature. One investigation into internal models of an LLM Li et al. (2021)
279   suggests that a world model exists based on functions that do significantly better than chance, but are
280   far from perfectly accurate. That actually seems reasonable, as sufficient evidence that a network is
281   doing more than just memorizing superficial statistics.
282
      On the other hand, it leaves open the idea that the network is using some combination of a world
283   model, and miscellaneous heuristics. Indeed, it seems possible that many neural networks do exactly
284   that. For instance, careful investigations into the Othello model introduced by Li et al. (2022) indicate
285   that despite the existence of an internal world model, the network uses a variety of brittle heuristics
286   in its predictions (Nanda et al., 2023b; Chiu et al., 2023). In summary, if a neural net meets our
287   definition with approximations instead of equations, that should be seen evidence for a world model
288   that only partially explains the system’s behavior.

      3.5.1    “L EARNED ” WORLD MODELS
325
326   The reason we investigate world models is our interest in the representation Z. However, if the data
327   in X itself effectively contains a model of W , then the fact that Z contains one too would be of little
328   consequence. To capture this idea, we start by considering a restricted set FX of functions X → M
329   that parallels FZ . For example, if FZ consists of linear functions on Z, then FX might be linear
330   functions on X. Then we say a world model is learned if there does not exist a function h ∈ FX ,
331
      such that h = g ◦ f1 . In diagrammatic form we may put it like this:
332           f1          f2
       X            Z           Y
333
      α               g
334
335    W           M
              φ1
336
337   This nontriviality condition is surprisingly important, because in practice it’s easy for the data in X
338   to have a linear projection to a seemingly sophisticated world model. We’ll give two examples to
339   show why the “learned model” distinction is stronger than it may initially seem.
340   Example 1. Word co-occurrence statistics can model the world. Word embeddings, such as
341   word2vec (Mikolov et al., 2013) or Glove (Pennington et al., 2014), famously can be used to perform
342   word analogy tasks using vector arithmetic. For example, a particular direction in space might
343   correspond to a gender distinction, or to a country-capital relationship. This might seem to be the
344   result of deeply complicated functions (neural network or other) transforming basic co-occurrence
345   statistics into a linear model of key aspects of text.
346   However, co-occurrence statistics already have a rough linear model of these concepts! This has
347   been observed at least since the invention of latent semantic analysis (Dumais, 2004). Indeed, the
348   report on Glove (Pennington et al., 2014) provides SVD-based linear projections of co-occurrence
349   statistics as baselines, which are remarkably close to the final model they use7 . The implication is that
350   if the data X for a neural network consists of something as seemingly basic as co-occurrence vectors,
351   then it already contains a linear model of aspects of the world ranging from social structures to
352   geography. Any intermediate representations of these properties may not be truly learned—they were
353
      in the data from the beginning. For example, Gurnee & Tegmark (2023) find that the intermediate
      representations of language models contain linear representations of geographic and chronological
354
      features of the world. More work would be needed, however, to deduce that this is learned world
355
      model.
356
357   Example 2. Partial views of a dynamical system often model the full system. Consider a “world”
358   consisting of a discrete dynamical system defined by a smooth map F : Rn → Rn . Following Takens
359
      (1980), suppose that our input space X consists of one-dimensional “observations” of an orbit of
      a point x under the action of F . That is, the input is a sequence (α(x), α(F (x)), . . . , α(F k (x))),
360
      where α : Rn → R is an “observation function” that satisfies a few mild non-degeneracy conditions.
361
      Each such sequence clearly loses a huge amount of information from the original dynamical trajectory,
362   since α produces just a single real value.
363
364   Now imagine training a neural network to predict the next observation in this sequence, and then
365
      discovering that intermediate representations of the input points form a diffeomorphic image of
      the dynamics in the original space Rn . At first this might seem like an incredible feat of learning!
366
      However, as long as the sequences are reasonably long compared to the dimension n, then such a
367
      diffeomorphic image is likely already present in the data, simply by concatenating windows of 2n + 1
368   observations. This is the content of Takens’s famous reconstruction theorem.
369
370   Comparability of FX and FZ . There are some subtle issues that might arise in defining and
371
      comparing “simple” functions on to different spaces . If the two spaces have vastly different
      dimensionalities, for example, then the spaces of linear functions will have vastly different numbers
372
      of parameters. If this difference becomes an important concern, one way to make a direct comparison

404   4     C AUSAL WORLD MODELS
405
406   Suppose we’re lucky enough to identify a learned emergent world model in a system’s internal
407   representation Z. How do we know that this model actually relates to the function f2 ? It’s certainly
408
      possible there’s no relation at all. In fact, the only stipulation we’ve made so far that connects the
      model M and the output space Y is negative: that for an emergent model there is no simple function
409
      taking Y to M .
410
411   From a theoretical perspective, it’s easy to imagine functions where knowing about a world model
412   gives no information about how a function is computed. For example, suppose that Z = Z1 ⊕ Z2 ,
413   where Z1 contains a world model, but f2 only looks at data in Z2 . It’s also easy to see how this could
414
      be a problem in practice. Even a seemingly “small” class of functions, such as linear maps from Z to
      M , is fairly powerful when the dimensions of Z and M are high enough. It’s not a stretch to imagine
415
      that there will be some linear map can pick out some simple aspect of the world W , even though that
416
      aspect is never used by f2 .
417
418   We’re concerned with causality for two reasons. Scientifically, spurious representations aren’t that
419   interesting, since it’s not clear how they help us understand what our system is doing. Just as
420
      important is the engineering perspective: if we want to use our understanding to control a neural
      network, then causality clearly matters. How can we capture the idea that a world model matters?
421
      Below we provide two definitions for “complete” and “partial” causality of a world model.
422
423
      4.1     C OMPLETE CAUSAL WORLD MODELS
424
425   A major interpretability goal is to find a simple intermediate representation that completely determines
426   the output of a neural network. One way to express this formally is to require the existence of a
427   function φ2 : M → Y such that φ2 ◦ g = f2 . Or, in diagrammatic form:
428
              f1         f2
429    X           Z            Y
430   α              g
                           φ2
431
       W      φ1   M


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      If there is such an φ2 then we say the model M is causally complete, since its value completely
433   determines the output of the model. Assuming that the world model is much simpler than the world
434   itself, it gives us two valuable things. First, we can fully characterize the function f2 ◦ f1 in natural
435   terms: as a function operating on M , rather than W or its noisy proxy X. Second, it provides a
436   means of control: if we modify an internal representation r ∈ Z, then we can predict the effect of
437   that modification in terms of M .
438
      Completeness in this sense is a strong condition. Given the complexity of the real world, it’s unlikely
439   we’ll ever find, say, a complete world model inside of ChatGPT. However, for simple synthetic tasks
440   this may be possible. One example is the type of modular addition network considered in Nanda et al.
441   (2023a).
442
443
      4.2   C AUSAL WORLD MODELS
444
445   In practice, completeness may be too high an ideal. A weaker condition, however, can still capture the
446   ideal of a world model that has a nontrivial causal effect on a computation. Let’s return to the example
447   of the “sentiment neuron” in Radford et al. (2017). As discussed, the researchers on that project
448   found a neuron whose activation reflected the sentiment of the text it was given. But they didn’t stop
449   there. They did one more critical experiment: during inference, they performed an intervention, fixing
450   the value of this neuron at a value of either zero or one. When fixed at zero, the system generated
451
      negative-sentiment completions (as measured by human labelers). Fixing the neuron at a value of one
      produced positive completions.
452
453   We may view the sentiment neuron as a model of the world (the reviewer’s overall emotion) which
454   has a causal effect on one aspect of the output (the human-judged sentiment). However, we don’t
455   have a truly complete model, since the sentiment neuron alone generally doesn’t determine the next
456   character.
457   We can formalize this situation by defining a simplified aspect of the output A (here, its sentiment)
458   and a function h : Y → A (here, human judgment of output sentiment). The finding that the
459   representation is causal is equivalent to the fact that h ◦ f2 = φ2 ◦ g. In diagram form:
460          f1         f2
461    X           Z          Y
462   α             g             h
463
       W     φ1   M     φ2    A
464
465   This diagram represents what we call a (potentially incomplete) causal world model. To avoid
466   trivialities, we stipulate that h ◦ f2 ◦ f1 is not constant. That is, it needs to capture some varying
467   aspect of the output Y , for which the model M is predictive. One interpretation of this definition is
468   that, with a world model identified, we can move from thinking about the top row (the actual data and
469   neural network) to the bottom row (the higher-level of picking out a simplified model of the world,
470   and operating on that.)
471
472   4.3   C AUSALITY AND THE TRANSFORMER ARCHITECTURE
473
474   Transformer architectures can add one more layer of complexity to the picture we have sketched so far.
475   First, the residual stream for a given token is often viewed as a shared resource across layers (Elhage
476
      et al., 2021). It’s plausible that any world model it contains is read from, and written to, at multiple
      layers. In this case, even when it is possible to read a world model from just one layer (consistent
477
      with the framework we’ve laid out), it is possible that an effective intervention on the world model
478
      may require making changes as multiple layers. See subsection A.1 for a full description of this
479   phenomenon.
480
481
482   5     L OCAL WORLD MODELS
483
484   Today’s machine learning systems are often trained on multiple tasks, complicating the notion of a
485   single world model. Our last definition is meant to express the idea that a world model may be highly
      contextual, but still useful. As a motivating example, consider Li et al. (2021), which found what


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      they called “implicit representations of meaning” in a tuned language model. In particular, when the
487   model was given English descriptions of a series of actions in an specific constrained domain, they
488   found evidence for internal representations of the state of this world as a result of these actions. In
489   the notation we’ve developed, the underlying state is M and answers about the state lie in A. The
490   generated text is Y , and h is a function (performed in this case by humans) translating the text into
491   the modeled state.
492
      This is close to the world model described in the previous section, but with one caveat: the system
493   used by Li et al. (2021) was a general language model, and there’s no evidence that the world model
494   M had any explanatory power when given text input that did not relate to the domain it was tuned for.
495   In other words, the world model may be operative only for a subset of world states, W ′ . We call this
496   a local world model, and represent it in diagrammatic form as follows:
497
                  f1           f2
498           X           Z          Y
499       α                g             h
500
          ′
501    W ⊂W         φ1   M     φ2    A
502
      For general-purpose systems, we might speculate that this type of contextual model is the most we
503   can hope to find. And more broadly, we may wish to restrict consideration of any of the functions
504   in our commutative diagram to a specific subspace or subset of data. For example, it will often be
505   the case that the image of f1 is not the whole of Z. To establish causality, it makes sense to test
506   if φ2 ◦ g = h ◦ f2 outside of the set f1 (X). However, it may be enough to test only points in a
507   local neighborhood of this set; requiring equality for points that fall far out of distribution may be an
508   unnecessary restriction.
509
510   6       C ONCLUSION
511
512   We have presented a definition of the term “world model,” which we hope can unify and clarify a
513   broad set of interpretability research. The mathematical framework of the definition means that it is
514   straightforward to operationalize experimentally. The general template for the definition comes from
515   the probing literature, which is already familiar to researchers in the field.
516
      There are several ways in which it might be useful to extend this definition. Currently we’ve aimed
517
      for a ”minimal interesting” definition. As a result, we’ve proposed a relatively weak set of criteria.
518   An important extension would be to find analogous definitions of what it means to learn a joint model
519   of states, actions, and the results of actions.
520
521
      To summarize, our hope is that this definition can clarify some of the controversy around what
      it is that LLMs and similarly powerful neural networks are really learning. Asking whether a
522
      network “understands” its input, for instance, is a recipe for (ironically) misunderstandings. Asking
523
      instead whether it builds a world model, as described here, leads to questions that can be resolved
524   scientifically.

705   A     A RCHITECTURAL D ETAILS OF D ECOMPOSING N EURAL N ETWORKS
706
707   Our definition is based on a factorization of a neural network of the form X → Z → Y . For a
708   standard feedforward network, it’s clear how to do this: we can just take Z to be the output of an
709   intermediate layer, which is at the same time the input to the subsequent layer. For any network with
710   residual connections, it is important to take out Z after the addition operation to form a cut-off.
711   For sequence processing models like RNN and SSM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Vaswani
712   et al., 2017; Gu & Dao, 2023), representations of all input tokens are constructed recursively and
713   serve naturally as Z. For transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), we can take the mid-layer embeddings
714   of all input tokens at an arbitrary layer as Z, which effectively cut off the computation graph from
715   input to output. As a special case, if the last layer is chosen, only the last-token embedding is needed
716   to form Z. Some probing techniques in the literature probe only at the embeddings of specific tokens,
717
      e.g. last tokens, which still falls into the framework since we can take the probing function g to first
      discard all features in Z except that for the last time step.
718
719   In general, the factoring of a function f into f1 and f2 by a cut-off representation Z discussed in
720   subsubsection 3.1.1 is a generic approach for examining a wide range of neural networks. This
721   formulation is closely related to existing ways of understanding such networks.
