                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Large language models (LLMs) pretrained on large corpora of internet text pos-
010
               sess much of the world knowledge. Following pretraining, one often needs to
011            conduct continued pretraining on certain capabilities such as math and coding,
012            or “posttraining” (a.k.a., alignment) techniques to make the models follow users’
013            instructions and align them with human preferences. One challenge during these
014            finetuning stages is that the model can lose the pretraining knowledge or forget
015            certain capabilities (e.g., in-context learning ability). Moreover, although there
016            exist strong open-weight LLMs such as Llama 3, both their pretraining and post-
017            training data are not open to the public, making it difficult to mix the finetuning
018            data with the models’ own pretraining data as a solution for mitigating forgetting.
019
               We propose label annealing, a method that mitigates forgetting during finetuning
               without requiring access to the original pretraining data. Label annealing distills
020
               pretraining knowledge during finetuing by adding a KL divergence term in the loss
021
               function, regularizing the divergence between the finetuned model’s predictions to
022            those of the initial pretrained model. In mathematics and code finetuning, label
023            annealing improves the model’s performance in target domains without sacrific-
024            ing other capabilities of the pretrained model. In alignment finetuning, our method
025            introduces a smooth tradeoff between the instruction-following capability and the
026            pretraining knowledge. We complement our empirical investigation with a math-
027            ematical model with overparameterized linear regression that provides geometric
028            intuition why label annealing would help.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Language models pretrained on large volume of internet text possess much of the common world
      knowledge (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024; Gunter
034
      et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023b; Dubey et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024a; Jiang et al., 2023).
035
      With the advancement of open-weight model, e.g., Llama series (Touvron et al., 2023a; Dubey
036   et al., 2024a), an emerging paradigm is to finetune those open-weight models to adapt to a wide
037   range of downstream applications such as mathematics (Lewkowycz et al., 2022; Azerbayev et al.,
038   2024), programming (Rozière et al., 2024a), medicine (Yuan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023), and law
039   (Colombo et al., 2024b;a), or to follow human instructions and preferences (Ouyang et al., 2022).
040
      One major challenge in finetuning is that the model may forget some old knowledge or capabilities.
041
      A standard solution is to mix the finetuning data with the upstream training data of the model. This
042   is known as “experience replay” in the continual learning literature (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017;
043   Chaudhry et al., 2019). Unfortunately, the training data of powerful open-weights models, e.g.,
044   Llama series (Dubey et al., 2024a; Touvron et al., 2023a), is never shared with the public, making
045   it impossible to mix custom finetuning data with the original training data. Moreover, the emerging
046   trend of learning specific knowledge or capability using synthetic data (Yang et al., 2024b; Zelikman
047   et al., 2022; 2024) and the heavy use of expensive human annotation (Touvron et al., 2023a; Dubey
048   et al., 2024a; Lightman et al., 2024) make it even harder to apply the experience replay solution.
049   These trends necessitate finetuning methods that preserve the model’s old knowledge with only
050
      access to its weights.
051   A simple idea is to add weight decay toward the initial weights instead of zeros (Loshchilov &
052   Hutter, 2019; Kumar et al., 2023), constraining the model weights from diverging too far from
053   their initialization. We show that this existing wisdom provides limited mitigation in the setting
      of language modeling, due to its inability to take finetuning data into consideration. Instead, we

068   model during the finetuining process. We refer to this approach as label annealing. Concretely, we
069
      load an independent copy of the initial model and keep it frozen during training. We then add a

071
072   To demonstrate the effectiveness of label annealing, we design four empirical finetuning tasks: math-
073   ematics finetuning, code finetuning (Section 3.2), supervised instruction finetuning, and niche do-
074   main knowledge finetuning (Section 3.3). In each task, we design a specific finetuning dataset and
075
      some “target benchmarks”, those we wish to see improvement due to finetuning, as well as some
      “source benchmarks” that measures if the general capabilities are preserved. We show that label
076
      annealing can sometimes mitigate forgetting at no cost of sacrificing the target benchmarks perfor-
077
      mance, and in other cases, it introduces a smooth tradeoff between target and source benchmarks.
078
079   We complement our empirical findings with a theoretical analysis of label annealing using an over-
080   parameterized linear regression model (Section 4). We analyze the gradient descent solution of three
081
      finetuning techniques: direct fine-tuning, L2 regularization toward initialization, and label anneal-
      ing (Theorem 1). We show that, direct finetuning discards the information contained in the initial
082
      weights that lies within the span of the finetuning data, therefore only preserving the initial knowl-
083
      edge outside that span. In contrast, label annealing introduces a regularization term that preserves
084   the model’s knowledge both inside and outside the span of the finetuning data.
085
086   To summarize, our key contributions are:
087   • We identify the emerging task of mitigating forgetting when finetuning open-weight language
088     models with only access to the model weights, and propose label annealing as a simple solution.
089   • We provide empirical results across multiple domains, including math, coding, and alignment,
090     demonstrating that label annealing improves performance in the target domains while maintaining
091     or minimizing degradation in general capabilities.
092   • We offer a clear theoretical explanation for why label annealing is more effective than direct fine-
093     tuning or L2 regularization toward initialization, providing geometric intuitions for our findings.
094
      Label annealing is straightforward to implement, making it an attractive option for finetuning open-
095
      weight language models. From a scientific perspective, label annealing demonstrates that a model’s
096
      knowledge can be preserved during finetuning without access to the original training data.

      1.1     R ELATED WORK
100
      Continual learning and pretraining. The field of continual learning emerged from early research
101
      in neural networks (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989; Ratcliff, 1990), which studied how systems could
102   learn from sequentially presented information (Schlimmer & Fisher, 1986; Grossberg, 2012). A
103   central challenge in this area is preventing “catastrophic forgetting” – where neural networks lose
104   previously acquired capabilities when learning new tasks (Robins, 1995; French, 1999; Goodfellow
105   et al., 2015; Kemker et al., 2018; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). Researchers have developed various
106   approaches to address this challenge, including: parameter regularization techniques to maintain
107   critical network weights (Nguyen et al., 2017; Zenke et al., 2017; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017), methods
      that expand model architectures (Rusu et al., 2016; Golkar et al., 2019), and memory-based solu-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tions that retain examples from previous tasks (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Shin et al., 2017; Lopez-Paz &
109   Ranzato, 2017). While traditional continual learning research has focused on clearly defined sequen-
110   tial tasks, our work examines the practical scenario of finetuning large-scale open-source language
111   models (Dubey et al., 2024a; Jiang et al., 2023). In the context of language models, researchers have
112   shown that additional pretraining can effectively adapt models to specialized domains like program-
113   ming (Rozière et al., 2024b), healthcare (Chen et al., 2023), and mathematics (Lewkowycz et al.,
114   2022; Shao et al., 2024; Azerbayev et al., 2024). This adaptation process has been refined through
115   advances in causal language modeling techniques (Gupta et al., 2023; Ibrahim et al., 2024; Parmar
116   et al., 2024). Some work has investigated using experience replay to preserve capabilities (Gupta
117
      et al., 2023; Parmar et al., 2024), but these studies have been limited to either small models (around
      400M parameters) or settings with access to extensive computational resources for training from
118
      scratch.
119
120
121   Knowledge distillation and its variants. Knowledge distillation (Buciluundefined et al., 2006;
122
      Hinton et al., 2015) tackles the task of training a high-quality, small student model under the su-
      pervision of a large teacher model. In the context of language modeling, knowledge distillation is
123
      mainly used in a black-box manner, where the student model only have access to the tokens gener-
124
      ated by the teacher model (Taori et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023). With the advancement of open-weight
125   language models, there is a growing interest in white-box distillation, where the student model have
126   full access to the logits output of the teacher model (Wen et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2024; Agarwal
127   et al., 2024). Our paper differs from the knowledge distillation literature in that our goal is not com-
128   pressing a larger model to a smaller one. Instead, we use the technique of knowledge distillation to
129   mitigate forgetting in the finetuning stage of language models.
130
      In the context of knowledge distillation, similar to our work is the study of self-distillation (Zhang
131   et al., 2019; Mobahi et al., 2020), where they find that first training a model and then distilling
132   the model can be more effective than directly training the model. The goal of self-distillation is
133   to improve generalization, as opposed to mitigate forgetting. Closely related to self-distillation is
134   the technique of label smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016; Müller et al., 2019b;a) that regularizes the
135   KL divergence between model output and the uniform distribution. Label smoothing is similar to
136   our proposal in that we both regularize the probability distribution predicted by the model with an
137   additional distribution over class labels — a uniform distribution in the context of label smoothing
138   and the distribution predicted by the initial model in our method.

      2     O UR METHOD
141
142
      Before the advancement of the large pretrained language model followed by instruction tuning
143
      (Brown et al., 2020), there is less practical concern about forgetting when finetuning a pretrained
144   model. For example, in language modeling, when we finetune a BERT model (Devlin et al., 2019) to
145   perform a question-answering (Rajpurkar et al., 2016) task, we do not have concern if the finetuned
146   model forgets its ability to perform other tasks (e.g., summarization) due to the task-specific nature
147   of finetuning. In contrast, when adapting an open-weight language model, e.g., Llama (Dubey et al.,
148   2024a), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), to a new domain of knowledge, one needs to make sure that the
149   finetuned model is a reasonable model that can, for example, follow user instructions or complete
150   few-shot examples (MetaAI, 2024).
151   In this section, we describe our proposed label annealing algorithm that aims to address the forget-
152   ting issue when finetuning an open-weight language model. We first introduce the setup for our
153   method (Section 2.1), and then describe the label annealing regularization in detail (Section 2.2). To
154   provide background for the empirical experiments in Section 3, we present the label annealing algo-
155   rithm in the context of finetuning a language model, but we note that our method can be generalized
156   to any supervised or self-supervised learning task that uses cross-entropy loss.
157
158   2.1   S ETUP
159
160   Finetuning dataset. In the context of language modeling, we use x to denote a sequence of tokens
161   and y to denote the next token following x. We use (x, y) ∼ DFT to denote a context sampled
      from the finetuning dataset DFT . Let pθ (y|x) denote the output distribution of our model, where θ


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      represents the model weights. We denote the output distribution of the initial model as pθ0 (y|x),
163   where θ0 are the initial parameters.
164
165   Success criteria. We consider methods that only require access to the weights of the initial model.
166   For each finetuning dataset, we define two types of evaluation benchmarks: (1) target benchmarks
167   that measure improvement in the specific domain being finetuned, and (2) source benchmarks that
168   assess preservation of the model’s general capabilities. Then, we consider a technique as success-
169   fully mitigating the forgetting problem if (i) the improvement in the target benchmark is close to that
170   of direct finetuning without applying any memory retaining technique, and (ii) the performance on
171   the source benchmarks is close to that of the initial model.
172
173   2.2   L ABEL ANNEALING
174
      In this section, we introduce the label annealing regularization in the context of language model
175
      finetuning (Figure 1). Without any regularization, causal language modeling aims to minimize the
176   following objective:
177                                    L(θ) = Ex,y∼D [− log pθ (y|x)].                              (1)
178
179   However, aggressively optimizing this objective may cause the model to forget its old capabilities.
180   To mitigate this, label annealing introduces a regularization term with temperature scaling:
181                            LLA (θ, T ) = λEx,y∼D [KL(pθ,T (y|x)∥pθ0 ,T (y|x))],                   (2)
182   where KL denotes the Kullback-Leibler divergence, and T is the temperature scaling parameter. The
183   temperature-scaled distributions are defined as:
184                                                       exp(zy /T )
185                                       pθ,T (y|x) = P                  ,
                                                          y ′ exp(zy /T )
                                                                    ′
186
      where z represents the logits output of the model for input x. The same scaling is applied to
187
      pθ0 ,T (y|x) using the pretrained model’s logits. The full objective then becomes:
188
189
                                          Ltotal (θ) = L(θ) + LLA (θ, T )                                (3)
190   where λ is a hyperparameter controlling the strength of the regularization, and T controls the “sharp-
191   ness” of the distributions. A higher temperature (T > 1) makes the distributions more uniform,
192
      while a lower temperature (0 < T < 1) makes them more peaked. In the limit of T → ∞, our
      regularization reduces to the label smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016) regularization discussed in Sec-
193
      tion 1.1. By optimizing this combined objective, label annealing seeks to strike a balance between
194
      adapting to the new data and retaining knowledge from pretraining.

197   3     M AIN EXPERIMENTS
198
199   In this section, we describe our main experiments. We finetune Llama 3 8B base model (Dubey et al.,
200
      2024a) on various downstream datasets, and measure its performance on some standard language
      model benchmarks. We split our experiments into two sections.
201
202   In Section 3.2, we finetune the model on additional mathematics and code data. We find that label
203   annealing prevents forgetting with no loss in downstream performance. In contrast, a simple baseline
204   of adding L2 regularization toward initialization (Kumar et al., 2023) provides little to no benefit.
205   In Section 3.3, we present two complementary experiments, (i) performing instruction tuning on the
206
      base model and (ii) finetuning an aligned model on an additional downstream dataset. In both cases,
      we observe a smooth tradeoff between finetuning benchmarks and pretraining benchmarks. We next
207
      present the experiment setup that serves this section.
208
209
      3.1   E XPERIMENT SETUP
210
211   Evaluation setup. Each finetuning dataset DFT is evaluated using two benchmark categories: (1)
212   target benchmarks Btarget that measure improvement in finetuned capabilities, and (2) source bench-
213   marks Bsource that verify preservation of the original model’s performance. As an example, if we
214   perform additional training on mathematics related text, we would expect math benchmarks like
215   GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) or MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) to improve, whereas general ca-
      pability benchmarks such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021a) to not degrade.


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Benchmark selection. We select 8 benchmarks, roughly divided into five categories, to probe dif-
217   ferent knowledge or capability of the finetuned model. Note that we do not evaluate each finetuning
218   experiment with data source DFT on all 8 benchmarks. Instead, based on the content of each finetun-
219   ing dataset, we select Btarget as the benchmarks we expect to see improvement and select Bsource as
220   the benchmarks we wish to retain performance. We defer the evaluation details to Appendix A. At a
221   high level, we pick benchmarks that probe performance in mathematics (MATH (Hendrycks et al.,
222   2021b), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021)), coding (HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021)), pretraining knowl-
223   edge (MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017)), niche books and articles
224   (QuALITY QA (Pang et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024b)).
225
226   Direct finetuning baseline. For each finetuning dataset DFT , we first report the result of directly
227   finetuning on it. As we shall see, in some cases the forgetting is not a serious problem even without
228   any intervention. Throughout our experiments, we use batch size 128 and context window 2048,
229
      leading to a throughput of 262K tokens per batch. We use cosine learning rate decay with linear
      warmup up to 5% of total steps with peak learning rate 5e-6. For all datasets, we finetune on the
230
      downstream dataset for 5 epochs. As a result, we standardize a fixed set of training hyperparameter
231
      and do not perform dataset specific hyperparameter selection for each finetuning dataset DFT .
232
233
      L2 regularization baseline. A simple baseline to maintain pretraining knowledge with only ac-
234
      cess to pretrained weights is to simply regularize the weights toward initialization. We consider
235   this simple approach of L2 regularization as a baseline. This is introduced as “regenerative reg-
236   ularization” in Kumar et al. (2023), it can also be viewed as a simplified case of Elastic Weight
237   Consolidation (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017), where all parameters are weighted equally. We
238   implement it by adding the penalty term λ2 ∥θ − θ0 ∥22 to the primary objective (1). We follow the
239   same set of training hyperparameter as in direct finetuning. To select the best λ, we sweep over a
240   range of λ for each finetuning dataset DFT . Then, we first filter out those choice of λ that lead to
241   no improvement in target benchmarks Btarget , and then select the one that has the highest value in
242   source benchmark Bsource .
243
244   Label annealing setup. For our approach, we follow the same hyperparameter choice for training
245   as in direct finetuning and L2 regularization. To select the label annealing hyperparameter, namely,
246   the annealing scale λ and temperature T from (2), we follow the same selection process as in L2
247
      regularization: pre-filtering based on target benchmark Btarget performance, and then select the one
      with best source benchmark performance Bsource .

      3.3   A LIGNED MODEL TRAINING
311
312   In this section, we present experiments involving instruction-tuned language model. We consider
313   two complementary scenarios, (i) perform instruction tuning on a pretrained language model and see
314   if we can get instruction-following ability without compromising the pretraining metrics, a problem
315   known as “alignment tax”, and (ii) perform knowledge intensive finetuning on an aligned model
316   (e.g., Llama 3 8B Instruct) and see if we can “teach” model the new knowledge while preserving
317   the instruction following ability. In both cases, we observe that label annealing introduces a smooth
318   tradeoff between the target benchmarks Btarget and source benchmarks Bsource .
319
320   Alignment tax. The standard step following pretraining is instruction tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022).

322   (e.g., MMLU drop in Llama 3 (Dubey et al., 2024a)). To investigate forgetting in this setting, we
323   perform supervised instruction tuning on the UltraChat (Ding et al., 2023) dataset. After tokenizing
      it with the standard Llama 3 Instruct template, we get an instruction tuning corpus with 220M tokens.

474   The solution selected by gradient descent admits straightforward geometric interpretation. Since we

477

483

484

485

1032   A.1   B ENCHMARK SELECTION AND EVALUATION DETAILS
1033
1034   In this section, we present the details of benchmark we choose and evaluation strategy.
1035
       • Pretraining knowledge: MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021a) and TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017). Both
1036
         benchmarks measure general world knowledge and typically used to measure model’s pretraining
1037
         knowledge. We view MMLU as a measurement of more common knowledge and TriviaQA as
1038     niche tail knowledge. Indeed, we will see that TriviaQA is more prone to forgetting compared
1039     with MMLU. We use 5-shot prompting for MMLU and 0-shot for TriviaQA.
1040
       • Mathematics ability: MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021).
1041     Both benchmarks measure math problem-solving ability. MATH contains harder questions than
1042     GSM8K, so typically has lower accuracy and also harder to improve. We use 4-shot chain-of-
1043     thought Minerva prompt for MATH (Lewkowycz et al., 2022) and 4-shot prompting for GSM8K.
1044   • Coding ability: HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021), an open-ended programming task. We report
1045     pass@1 performance for HumanEval.
1046   • Instruction following ability: AlpacaEval (Dubois et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b) and IFEval (Zhou
1047     et al., 2023). In our setup, we compute the AlpacaEval win rate against GPT-4 (OpenAI et al.,
1048     2024) using GPT-4 as judge.
1049   • Niche articles and books knowledge: contextualized QuALITY QA (Pang et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
1050     2024b). The QuALITY dataset was originally proposed as a long-context reading comprehension
1051     task about a collection of 265 relatively obscure books and articles. In its original form, some
1052     questions such as “What does the author think?” are ambiguous without the article available as
1053     context. Yang et al. (2024b) turned the question set into a collection of unambiguous questions
1054     by appending the article metadata as “In the article {article title} by {author name}, what does
1055
         the author think?”. As a result, the article becomes a collection of unambiguous QA the probe
         model’s knowledge about the collection of 265 books.
