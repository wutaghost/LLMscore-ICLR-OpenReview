009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Pre-trained language models (PLMs) have demonstrated a remarkable ability to
012            encode factual knowledge. However, the mechanisms underlying how this knowl-
013            edge is stored and retrieved remain poorly understood, with important implica-
014            tions for AI interpretability and safety. In this paper, we disentangle the multi-
015            faceted nature of knowledge: successfully completing a knowledge retrieval task
016            (e.g., “The capital of France is ”) involves mastering underlying concepts (e.g.,
017            France, Paris), relationships between these concepts (e.g., capital of ), the struc-
018
               ture of prompts, including the language of the query. We propose to disentangle
               these distinct aspects of knowledge and apply this typology to offer a critical view
019
               of neuron-level knowledge attribution techniques. For concreteness, we focus on
020
               Dai et al.’s (2022) Knowledge Neurons (KNs) across multiple PLMs, testing 10
021            natural languages and unnatural languages (e.g. Autoprompt). Our key contribu-
022            tions are twofold: (i) we show that KNs come in different flavors, some indeed
023            encoding entity level concepts, some having a much less transparent, more poly-
024            semantic role , and (ii) we uncover an unprecedented overlap in KNs across up to
025            all of the 10 languages we tested, pointing to the existence of a partially unified,
026            language-agnostic retrieval system. To do so, we introduce and release the Multi-
027            ParaRel dataset, an extension of ParaRel, featuring prompts and paraphrases for
028            cloze-style knowledge retrieval tasks in parallel over 10 languages.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
      Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have led to models trained on vast and diverse
033
      linguistic datasets drawn from across the Internet, incorporating numerous languages simultaneously
034   (Scao et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2024). However, these languages are not
035   evenly represented, and performance on low-resource languages often depends on cross-linguistic
036   transfer from high-resource languages (Pires et al., 2019; Lample & Conneau, 2019; Conneau et al.,
037   2020a; Huang et al., 2021). Whether LLMs can develop common, language-agnostic representations
038   that enable such zero-shot transfer remains an open question in the literature (Singh et al., 2019;
039   Kudugunta et al., 2019; Kassner et al., 2021). Kervadec et al. (2023) extended this investigation
040   to machine-generated languages, revealing that different representations can emerge, suggesting
041   multiple ways knowledge may be encoded in LLMs.
042   Understanding how Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs) store and retrieve knowledge is essential
043   for enhancing interpretability and safety in AI systems. Many recent studies have sought to localize
044   and attribute specific knowledge to individual neurons within these models (Dai et al., 2022; Meng
045   et al., 2022; 2023). These methods often attempt to identify neurons whose activations are critical
046   for making accurate predictions. Typically, they focus on neurons in intermediate layers of Feed-
047   Forward Networks (FFNs) within transformer architectures (Geva et al., 2021). These approaches
048
      face strong limitations, as highlighted in recent critiques (Hase et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2023; Huang
      et al., 2023).
049
050   In this work, we offer a novel perspective by refining the concept of ”knowledge” itself. To cor-
051   rectly complete a prompt like The capital of France is, a model must process multiple layers of
052   information: sensitivity to the specific concept France, retrieval of the target concept Paris, and
053   understanding the relational context capital of. We introduce a method to distinguish these sub-
      types of knowledge—conceptual and relational—that is compatible with any knowledge attribution

090   Multilingual Language Models Training separate models for different languages is resource-
091   intensive, data-hungry, and generally ineffective at leveraging cross-linguistic similarities and
092   knowledge. In practice, recent LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2024) are trained on
093
      extensive portions of the Internet, making them de facto multilingual. Examples include mBERT
      (Devlin et al., 2019), XLM-R (Lample & Conneau, 2019; Conneau et al., 2020a), mBART (Liu
094
      et al., 2020), mT5 (Xue et al., 2021), and BLOOM (Scao et al., 2023), along with their fine-tuned
095
      variants like BLOOMZ, mT0 (Muennighoff et al., 2023), and FLAN-T5 (Chung et al., 2022).
096
097   The performance of these models is believed to stem from the emergence of efficient representa-
098   tions that are shared across languages (Aharoni et al., 2019; Arivazhagan et al., 2019; Conneau
099
      et al., 2020b). Research has investigated their cross-linguistic capabilities using artificial languages
      (Ri & Tsuruoka, 2022; Deshpande et al., 2022; Guerin et al., 2024), evaluating their performance on
100
      tasks across different languages (Pires et al., 2019; Wu & Dredze, 2019), analyzing their translation
101
      capabilities (Lample et al., 2018; Sennrich et al., 2016; Artetxe et al., 2018), and assessing their per-
102   formance on low-resource languages (Garcia et al., 2021), as well as examining their architectural
103   properties (K et al., 2020). Some studies, including the current work, have directly compared repre-
104   sentations from one language to another (e.g., using Canonical Correlation Analysis across layers,
105   as in Singh et al., 2019; Kudugunta et al., 2019). The conclusions drawn from these comparisons
106   are mixed. For instance, Singh et al. (2019) argue that representations are distinctly partitioned be-
107   tween languages, while Kudugunta et al. (2019) suggest that representations are more or less shared,
      depending on the linguistic proximity of the languages.

108
      More directly to the current work, Chen et al. (2024) recently looked at the overlap between knowl-
109   edge neurons obtained for English and Chinese. We examine a similar overlap, albeit comparing 10
110   natural languages at once and we believe it is essential. Pairwise sharing leaves ambiguity: are neu-
111   rons shared with all languages, none, or just pairs? Bias toward a dominant language (e.g., English)
112   or chance sharing between two languages is more likely than sharing across 10. Including 10 lan-
113   guages reveals symmetrical roles in neuron sharing, extending beyond language pairs and providing
114   robust evidence of truly multilingual knowledge representation. We also make a comparison with
115   an ‘unnatural language’ (Shin et al., 2020). Such prompts provide an extreme test for the idea that
116   knowledge could be accessed independently of form: they are not human-readable, and they had
117
      been shown to be processed differently by LLMs (Kervadec et al., 2023).
118
119
      Knowledge in LLMs LLMs acquire knowledge by training on extensive corpora (Petroni et al.,
      2019; Roberts et al., 2020; Safavi & Koutra, 2021). The work by Petroni et al. (2019) introduced
120
      LAMA, a dataset designed to evaluate BERT through a fill-in-the-blank cloze task (e.g., The capital
121
      of France is [MASK].). Subsequent research has built upon LAMA (Jiang et al., 2021), highlighting
122   the limitations of LLMs as knowledge bases (Elazar et al., 2021b; AlKhamissi et al., 2022), while
123   also attempting to enhance their performance (Wei et al., 2021; Petroni et al., 2020). Consequently,
124   research has emerged focusing on localizing and editing knowledge directly within the model (Rad-
125   ford et al., 2017; Lakretz et al., 2019; Bau et al., 2020b; Sinitsin et al., 2020; Mitchell et al., 2021;
126   2022; De Cao et al., 2021; Santurkar et al., 2021; De Cao et al., 2022; Bau et al., 2020a; Cohen et al.,
127   2023).
128   In this context, knowledge attribution methods such as ROME (Meng et al., 2022) and MEMIT
129   (Meng et al., 2023) (both employing causal mediation techniques; Vig et al., 2020), along with
130   Knowledge Neurons (Dai et al., 2022) (utilizing an integrated gradient approach; Sundararajan et al.,
131   2017), have been proposed. These methods are predicated on the assumption that neurons within the
132   intermediate layers of transformers’ Feed-Forward Networks (FFNs) encode knowledge. However,
133   we align with other studies (Hase et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023) that suggest this
134   assumption may be an oversimplification. While certain neurons play a significant role in specific
135   tasks (Lakretz et al., 2019; Manning et al., 2020; Rogers et al., 2020; He et al., 2024), LLM neurons
136
      are not necessarily monosemantic; rather, they can serve multiple functions depending on the context
      and task (Adly et al., 2024). Furthermore, their effectiveness in altering knowledge is subjective and
137
      widely debated (Hase et al., 2023). Other works (Wang et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Kojima et al.,
138
      2024) have identified multilingual neurons in LLMs; this paper focuses specifically on knowledge-
139   related neurons, offering a more precise analysis. We propose a knowledge-attribution method-
140   agnostic typology, illustrated with Dai et al.’s (2022) Knowledge Neurons. This approach aims to
141   provide a critical view on the Knowledge Neurons hypothesis while exploring what insights it can
142   offer regarding how knowledge is encoded in LLMs.

      3   M ETHODOLOGICAL BACKGROUND
145
146
      Knowledge The TREx dataset (Elsahar et al., 2018) is a collection of relational facts stored in
147
      triplets of the form < h, r, t >, with r a relation and h and t entities entering in that relation. TREx
148   exhibit 41 relations, such as being the capital of, was born in, etc. Each full triplet can be referred
149   to as an instantiation of its own relation r.
150
151   Knowledge Localization Methods Geva et al. (2021) observed that a FFN can be seen as a Key-
152   Value memory system, similar to self-attention. To assess if and where knowledge could be stored in
153   FFNs, Dai et al. (2022) used a knowledge attribution method based on integrated gradients (see next
154   paragraph for details). They show that a fact (e.g., The capital of France is Paris) can be associated
155   to a few neurons (around 4), whose activations correlate with the probability of the model to fill
156   in the elements of the fact appropriately. Similarly, Meng et al. (2022) proposed Rank-One Model
157
      Editing (ROME), which uses causal mediation to localize and edit knowledge in GPT, and Meng
      et al. (2023) introduced Mass-Editing Memory in a Transformer (MEMIT), which edits facts at
158
      scale. All of these knowledge attribution methods have their limitations; we apply our analysis to
159
      the Knowledge Neurons by way of illustration. Our approach is applicable to all such methods.

      Datasets For relational facts, we used the TREx dataset (Elsahar et al., 2018), which comprises
178
      41 relations with approximately 1,000 facts per relation. For prompts, we employed the augmented
179
      version of ParaRel provided by Kervadec et al. (2023). This version retains only prompts compat-
180   ible with autoregressive models and enriches the dataset with multiple paraphrases for each relation.
181   In Section 6, we explore multilingual models, which we tested on the multilingual variant of LAMA
182   (Kassner et al., 2021) as well as on a new multilingual version of ParaRel that we introduce. We
183   refer to this new dataset as Multi-ParaRel.
184
      The detailed methodology for creating Multi-ParaRel, along with a quality assessment, is pro-
185
      vided in Appendix A. Our dataset currently spans 10 languages: English, French, Spanish, Catalan,
186   Danish, German, Italian, Dutch, Portuguese, and Swedish. We also investigate an unnatural lan-
187   guage: AutoPrompt. Following the same train, development, and test splits as Shin et al. (2020), we
188   trained 10 different seeds of AutoPrompt for each relation and each model. We also make these sets
189   of prompts available.
190
191   Concept Neurons and Relation Neurons We propose a simple typology that refines the type of
192   knowledge attributed while answering fill-in-the-blank cloze tasks. For example, correctly answer-
193   ing the question What is the capital of France? not only requires knowledge of the answer Paris,
194   but also an understanding of the relationship between France and Paris. We thus introduce a sim-
195   ple principle: a neuron that is hypothesized to encode a specific concept, such as one about Paris,
196
      should not be also responsible for encoding other concepts, and should therefore not be associated
      to other facts such as The capital of Spain is Madrid. If a neuron consistently encodes the same
197
      relation across multiple instances, we refer to it as a relational neuron, indicating that it is sensitive
198
      to a relation, such as capital of.
199
200   We thus define Relation Neurons as KNs that appear in at least tr × N instances of facts associated
201   with a particular relation, where N is the total number of facts, and tr is a predefined relational
202
      threshold. In contrast, neurons that appear in less than tc ×N of the facts, for some other threshold tc ,
      are referred to as Concept Neurons, as they are more likely to encode specific pieces of knowledge
203
      or information about individual entities.
204
205   The aim is to test the robustness of this distinction by investigating the role of the thresholds tr
206   and tc . A ‘clean’ scenario that supports the Knowledge Neuron hypothesis and the idea of monose-
207   manticity would show that some concept neurons are found even for tC × N = 1 (very specific to a
208
      concept), and relational neurons are found when tR ×N = N (completely systematically present for
      a relation). Alternatively, softer boundaries would suggest that these KNs play a more polysemantic
209
      and nuanced role, whereby knowledge is partially distributed across different neurons on different
210
      occasions (e.g., the concept of Paris and Madrid cannot be disentangled at the neuron level, or the
211   relation capital of is not always encoded in the same way).
212
213   As we do not know a priori which neurons play specific roles, we performed an exhaustive study
214
      across varying thresholds. In fact, it is part of the method to look at all possible thresholds to identify
      the behavior of KNs. Moreover, no major variation based on the choice of threshold was found.

247   Multilingual Knowledge Neurons Similarly, we ask whether knowledge is language-agnostic;
248   for example, humans do not need to relearn facts when acquiring a new language. Knowledge could
249
      be language-dependent in LLMs however: if a fact is present from the English corpus but missing
      from a Spanish training corpus, an LLM may be able to retrieve that knowledge when prompted in
250
      English but not when prompted in Spanish. We employ the KNs framework to investigate the open
251
      question of whether a common language-agnostic knowledge representation exists in multilingual
252   models at the level of neurons.
253
254   We hypothesize that some KNs may be specific to one language, while others may be sensitive
255
      to prompts in multiple languages. We thus analyze the number of languages across which such
      neurons are shared. We do so by identifying KNs for relations in the ParaRel dataset across
256
      multiple languages, using the Multi-ParaRel dataset, which was specifically created for this

      multilingual evaluation.

      Before classifying Knowledge Neurons (KNs) according to our typology, we first analyzed the dis-
      tribution of KNs based on the number of instantiations for which a KN was identified. Figure 2a
284
      illustrates the results for four relations using the Llama-2-7b model (complete results are pro-
285
      vided in Appendix B). A qualitative analysis reveals two key findings: (i) many KNs appear in only
286   one instantiation, indicating that these neurons are task-specific and sensitive to a single concept;
287   and (ii) there is a continuous range of KNs sensitive to between 3 and N instantiations, suggesting
288   a more nuanced role for these neurons that lies between relational and conceptual.
289
      The second observation challenges the simplistic interpretation of assigning neurons exclusively to
290
      concepts. At the same time, it also demonstrates the presence of a significant number of neurons
291
      sensitive to enough instantiations to hypothesize a more relational role in knowledge retrieval mech-
292   anisms.
293
294   Thus, we have identified potential candidates for the roles of both Concept Neurons and Relation
295
      Neurons, as well as neurons that fall into an intermediate category. The natural question that arises
      is: what is the proportion of each neuron type per instantiation, based on thresholds tr and tc ?
296
      This information is not directly inferable from Figure 2a, as neurons appearing consistently across
297
      instantiations are less visible than neurons that appear uniquely in each instance.1 To address this,
298   we computed the proportion of each neuron type as a function of thresholds at the instantiation level
299   (see Figure 2b). For simplicity, we used symmetrical thresholds, setting tr = 1 − tc .
300
301
      As expected, when the thresholds become more restrictive, the number of neurons with well-defined
      roles decreases, giving way to neurons with less clearly defined functions across all relations. For
302
      the Llama-2-7b model, we observe that the number of neurons classified as Relation Neurons
303
      remains more stable compared to those classified as Concept Neurons. Furthermore, for a single
304   instantiation, there are few KNs that are exclusive to that instance: when tc < 0.1, the proportion of
305   Concept Neurons is less than 0.2.
306
307
      We also examined the distribution of neuron types across the model’s layers but found no significant
      variation. As observed by Dai et al. (2022), KNs are primarily concentrated in the final layers.
308
309   In summary, we have demonstrated the existence of neurons reacting specifically to a single concept
310   within a relation. We have also identified neurons that play a much broader role in such relations,
311   with some reacting to almost all instances of that relation. We attempt to verify this hypothesis
312   through causal experiments in the next section. Finally, some neurons are activated by a subset of
313
      the instantiations, carrying a much less transparent type of knowledge. In principle, it could encode
      subtypes of relations, such as ‘capital of a European country’, although we find this highly stipulative
314
      at the moment. In the next section, we will focus on concept and relation neurons and evaluate their
315
      role through causal experiments.

345   5.3   B OOSTING E XPERIMENTS
346
      In this experiment, we investigate the effect of either doubling or nullifying the activation of KNs
347
      on model predictions. Dai et al. (2022) conducted similar experiments, focusing on how manual
348   changes to neuron activations influenced output probabilities. In contrast, we employ two more
349   concrete impact metrics: precision at rank k, denoted P@k, which measures the proportion of correct
350   responses in the top k model predictions, and correct category proportion at rank k, denoted CCP@k,
351   which reflects the proportion of responses in the correct category (e.g., capitals) within the top k
352   predictions. The original metric of relative probabilities change would not show specificity (e.g.
353   unrelated tokens could be even more boosted). For this reason, we report P@k and CCP@k. Effects
354   here ensure that the boost to the correct answer overcomes any boost for other answers. We also
355   include a control experiment in Appendix B to better investigate specificity.
356   Our goal is to verify whether the behavior of the identified KNs aligns with our proposed typology.
357   Specifically, we hypothesize that (i) there will be a marked increase (or decrease) in precision at rank
358   k=1 when the activations of Concept Neurons are doubled (or nullified), with the effect diminishing
359   as k increases. Similarly, we anticipate (ii) that the effect of Relation Neurons on P@k will be
360   weaker than that of Concept Neurons, as precision is primarily sensitive to the correct response. In
361   contrast, for the CCP@k metric, we expect (iii) that Relation Neurons will play a more significant
362
      role, as these neurons should be more likely to favor the correct category (e.g., capitals), even if it
      does not boost the correct answer specifically. We assess these effects for a range of thresholds tc
363
      and tr . Results for the bert-base-uncased and Llama-2-7b models are shown in Figure 3
364
      (see Appendix B for additional models and thresholds as well).
365
366   The figures show the delta in P@k and CCP@k for the predictions with altered (doubling or nuli-
367   fying) vs unaltered activations. The horizontal line at zero thus represents the baseline model per-
368
      formance. Of the six models evaluated, all six display the expected effect (i) consistently across
      all thresholds: in short, the top response is more accurate when the activations of concept neurons
369
      are increased. However, only two models, Llama-2 and the Gemma-2, exhibit effect (ii). Addi-
370
      tionally, four models, belonging to the BERT and OPT families, align with expectation (iii). Over-
371   all, bert-large-uncased and gemma-2-9b adhere to all three expected behaviors across all
372   cases. This happens under restrictive thresholds however (tr = 0.9 and tc = 0.1), and the four other
373   tested models fail to match all of these expectations.

395   These mixed results show that classifying KNs into distinct and disentangled roles is not perfect, po-
396   tentially due to noise in our methods or in knowledge attribution methods in the first place. Yet, our
397   experiments do indicate that, for certain models, KNs exhibit specific behaviors and manipulating
398   them leads to predictable effects.
399
400   5.4   D ISCUSSION
401
402   As anticipated, these experiments underscore the complexity of the internal mechanisms within
403   LLMs, making it impractical to map a single, well-defined function to individual neurons. Many
404   of the identified KNs do not adhere to a clearly defined role and cannot be neatly categorized as
405
      encoding either concepts or relations, even within a highly controlled environment like ParaRel.
      We believe that the polysemantic nature of neurons prevents such precise delineation, which also
406
      helps explain the knowledge editing limitations highlighted in prior research. However, contrary
407
      to our initial expectations, certain KNs do appear to serve rather specific functions, and this has
408   been experimentally confirmed for some models in boosting experiments. Hence, while the idea
409   that knowledge would be represented entirely in mono-semantic single neurons is unrealistic, the
410   historically associated methods of, e.g., Knowledge Neurons nonetheless detect transparent signal
411   about how knowledge is encoded. KNs are thus a useful tool to pursue the study of knowledge
412   representation in multilingual models too, which we do in the next section.

      Are KNs Bilingual? KNs were calculated separately for each relation and language. A KN is
      considered shared between two languages if it appears as a KN in both languages for the same
452
      relation. We conducted this pairwise analysis across all languages, thereby extending the findings
453

454
455   The results are presented in Figures 4a and 4b. For the Llama-2-7b model, over a quarter of the
456   neurons are shared between any two languages, with this proportion increasing to approximately
457
      one-third for bert-base-multilingual-uncased. This represents a significant degree of
      neuron sharing, especially when considering that bert-base-uncased, for example, has more
458
      than 12 × 3, 072 = 36, 864 neurons in the intermediate layers of its FFNs. To quantify this, note
459
      that among these 36, 864 neurons, only 1, 929 are identified as KNs across all relations for English,
460   and 2, 195 for French (roughly 5%). If KNs were randomly selected for each language, we would
461   expect around 100 shared neurons between them (5% overlap); however, in reality, 710 neurons are
462   shared. A similar analysis for Llama-2-7b gives even more extreme results: by chance, there
463   should be 2 shared neurons, while in practice 189 are found. Moreover, these numbers represent a
464   lower bound, as some relations were excluded from the prompt filtering process for certain language
465   pairs, effectively reducing the shared KN count for those relations to zero. Thus, the data indicates
466   significant overlap of KNs across languages, suggesting a partially shared mechanism for knowledge
467   retrieval across different language pairs.
468
469   Are KNs Multilingual? Next, we examine how the number of shared KNs scales with the num-
470
      ber of languages in the intersection. Figure 4c shows these results for all relations, along with
      the average behavior. Across all relations, we observe a consistent pattern: the number of shared
471
      neurons decays as a function of the form (number of languages)−α , with a fitted α = 2.04 for
472
      Llama-2-7b. In comparison, if neurons were shared at random, the expected behavior would

474   BERT). This demonstrates that KNs are more multilingual than chance, reinforcing the notion of a
475   language-agnostic knowledge retrieval mechanism. Similar to the findings in Section 5, we observe
476   some but few neurons activated for all languages.

484
      gual. Given the variability at other thresholds (in particular for Llama, which is less performant than

      7     D ISCUSSION
507
508
      While knowledge neurons may be shared across languages, this does not guarantee that they serve
509
      the same role in the two languages. A neuron active in both English and French for a given task
510   may perform different overall tasks depending on the language, that is, parallel activation does not
511   equate to shared functionality. Here, we partially controlled for this and narrowed down the role
512   of these neurons by computing intersections across languages and at the relation level. Yet, further
513   work is needed to investigate more intersections, narrowing down the possible roles, at the level of
514   relations, concepts, responses or formats of the prompt. Our method can further help narrow down
515   shared functions, across languages.

517   8     C ONCLUSION
518
519   We introduced a typology for knowledge and applied it to the knowledge attribution method pro-
520   posed by Dai et al. (2022) to better classify and understand the behavior of Knowledge Neurons
521   (KNs). Notably, our method remains agnostic to the specific knowledge attribution technique used.
522   Coherently with the initial assumptions in the original work, we found that some of these neurons
523   encode specific concepts, but we also found many which do not and instead seem to exhibit a dis-
524   tributed role, where multiple neurons share responsibility for encoding concepts within the same
525   relation, or maybe encode the whole relation. We hypothesize that this polysemantic nature of neu-
526
      rons contributes to the mixed success observed when using KNs for knowledge editing tasks. Yet
      again, we were able to identify a subset of more specialized neurons, which we categorized as either
527
      conceptual (sensitive to a single concept) or relational (sensitive to relationships between concepts).
528
      And in some contexts their manual manipulations show the expected effects on downstream tasks.
529   We extended our analysis to multilingual models and found that a significant number of KNs are
530   shared across languages—both in pairwise comparisons and across all 10 languages tested. This in-
531   dicates the presence of a shared, language-agnostic knowledge base within multilingual models. To
532   facilitate this research, we created a multilingual dataset of facts and prompts, enriched with para-
533   phrases in 10 languages. Our findings suggest that even a simple method like Knowledge Neurons
534   can provide valuable insights into the benefits of multilingual training. Looking ahead, we aim to
535   further explore how this shared knowledge can be leveraged to improve the integration of new lan-
536   guages into existing multilingual models. Our results indicate that it may not be necessary to relearn
537
      factual knowledge for each language, which could pave the way for more efficient training strate-
      gies, particularly for low-resource languages. Instead of focusing on exhaustive coverage of world
538
      knowledge, future efforts could prioritize data that highlights the unique syntactic and linguistic
539
      features of these languages, thus optimizing resource use and improving model performance.

       Creation Procedure To build the Multi-ParaRel dataset, we used our augmented autoregres-
991    sive version of ParaRel and mLAMA. The goal is to translate a template such as The capital of
992    [X] is [Y]. The problem is that translators are confused by the presence of placeholders [X] and
993    [Y], often resulting in translation errors. To overcome the difficulty, we instantiated [X] and [Y],
994    translated the whole sentence with these specific instances, and replaced the instantiations back with
995    placeholders. To do so, we used mLAMA, which contains triplets for over 53 languages.
996
       For example, consider the translation from English into French of the template:
997
998                                          The capital of [X] is [Y]
999
       We use the English triplet <Great Britain, capital of, London> to obtain the sentence:
1000
1001                                  The capital of Great Britain is London
1002
       This sentence is then translated into French:
1003
1004                              La capitale de la Grande Bretagne est Londres
1005
       Then using the French version of the original triplet (<la Grande Bretagne, capital of, Londres>),
1006   we can find and replace the entity elements of the triplet with placeholders [X] and [Y], resulting in
1007   the new template:
1008                                         La capitale de [X] est [Y]
1009
1010   With this overall idea, we can now provide more detail. First of all, such a protocol requires associ-
1011   ated triplets in mLAMA from one language to another. However, mLAMA has many more triplets in
1012   English than in other languages (see Figure 6), and some triplets are language-specific and therefore
1013
       cannot be associated with triplets in other languages. We therefore looked into a common English-
       Target language subset. Then, to avoid translation errors, problems linked to gendered determinants
1014
       and redundancy (two different templates in English but translated identically in the target language),
1015
       we used a voting system. Each template was translated 30 times, using 30 triplets. Each transla-
1016   tion is assigned a score, which is the number of times the template has been obtained out of the 30
1017   triplets. The template with the highest score is then retained, provided that (i) it is autoregressive,
1018   (ii) it has not already been selected and (iii) it is in the top 5 translations.
1019
       As a translation model, we used Meta’s SeamlessM4T and, more specifically, the Huggingface
1020
       implementation2 . We used an NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU for inference.
