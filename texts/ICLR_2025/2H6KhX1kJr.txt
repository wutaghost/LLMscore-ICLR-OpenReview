009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            World modelling, i.e. building a representation of the rules that govern the world
012            so as to predict its evolution, is an essential ability for any agent interacting with
013            the physical world. Recent applications of the Transformer architecture to the
014            problem of world modelling from video input show notable improvements in sam-
015            ple efficiency. However, existing approaches tend to work only at the image level
016            thus disregarding that the environment is composed of objects interacting with
017
               each other. In this paper, we propose an architecture combining Transformers
               for world modelling with the slot-attention paradigm, an approach for learning
018
               representations of objects appearing in a scene. We describe the resulting neural
019
               architecture and report experimental results showing an improvement over the ex-
020            isting solutions in terms of sample efficiency and a reduction of the variation of
021            the performance over the training examples. The code for our architecture and
022            experiments is available at [Redacted from the anonymized version]

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   World modelling is the ability of an artificial agent to build an internal representation of the world
028   in which it operates. This representation is employed by the agent to forecast the evolution of the
029   world. The problem of building a world model spans many branches of artificial intelligence, such
030   as planning and reinforcement learning (Micheli et al., 2023; Paster et al., 2021), physics modelling
031
      and reasoning (Ding et al., 2021), and robotics (Wu et al., 2022). An accurate representation of
      the world allows building simulations that, in turn, enable practitioners to gather additional data
032
      and test the performance of an artificial agent without interacting with their environment. This
033
      is convenient because interacting with an agent’s environment can be time-consuming, risky due
034   to possible failures of physical components, and sometimes even impossible due to the potential
035   unavailability of the environment (e.g. experimenting with the exploration of Mars by a rover).
036
037
      Recent applications of the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) to the task of world mod-
      elling from video input suggest that this family of architectures is not only it is capable of capturing
038
      the dynamics of the environment, but it is also capable of learning with high sample efficiency
039
      (Micheli et al., 2023; Robine et al., 2023). However, existing approaches typically operate directly
040   at the image level, with little regard for the objects contained within it. Understanding how objects
041   interact with each other and with the environment is of paramount importance, as it endows agents
042   with an intuitive theory of object motion (McCloskey, 1983). In neural architecture, this problem is
043   addressed in a separate line of research (Locatello et al., 2020; Kipf et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023), that
044   focuses on learning object-based representations that allow the objects in the scene and their inter-
045   actions to be modelled explicitly. We hypothesise that Transformers may benefit from object-based
046   representations to learn more accurate models of the world.
047
048   Problem statement In this paper, we focus on physical world modelling through the analysis
049   and prediction of synthetic videos. Learning a model of basic physical laws such as gravity and
050
      collision is very important for any agent working in a real environment to understand better the
      world’s evolution and the consequences of the agent’s actions.
051
052   Machine learning research on modelling intuitive physics aims to replicate the innate understanding
053   of physical concepts that humans display since their first months of age (McCloskey et al., 1983;
      Baillargeon, 2004). Specifically, we approach the physical interaction output prediction problem, as

055   of a sequence of frames x1 , ..., xT depicting several objects interacting in a world governed by
056   physical laws such as gravity and collision. The agent is then asked to predict the final outcome of
057   the situation, which requires estimating how the objects will behave after what is shown in the input

059
060   Contribution We design a transformer-based architecture for world modelling, inspired by the
061   principles of representation learning with slot encoding (Kipf et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2022). The

064   and transformers), while also noticeably improving the stability of the training process.

071   2     R ELATED WORK
072
073   2.1   W ORLD MODELLING
074
075   The world modelling problem has received tremendous attention in the last few years. In reinforce-
076   ment learning, being able to simulate the environment dynamics is especially useful, because it
077   enables the agent to act and learn in its own simulated world without paying the cost of interacting
078
      with the “real” environment. The Dreamer algorithm, in its various versions (Hafner et al., 2020;
      2021; 2023), has been relatively influential on the topic, with Wu et al. (2022) being an application
079
      in a robotic domain, where real-world interaction has the unfortunate potential of breaking usually
080
      costly equipment, in addition to time costs. Additional approaches in reinforcement learning include
081   solutions based on causal discovery and reasoning (Yu et al., 2023), which aim at learning a causal
082   model of the environment to better understand the interactions between the agent and the world, and
083   even provide an explanation for the actions taken by the agent. Transformer-based approaches are
084   also studied, due to their generally good performance in different tasks and the sample efficiency
085   they provide in this specific problem (Micheli et al., 2023; Robine et al., 2023).
086
      In the case of agents acting in a real physical environment, learning a model of the basic physical
087   laws of the world is essential to act effectively in the environment and understand the consequences
088   of each move. We refer to this problem as intuitive physics modelling. For this reason, several
089   solutions have been studied for this problem with approaches ranging from deep learning (Qi et al.,
090   2021), to violation of expectation (Piloto et al., 2022), to causal reasoning (Li et al., 2022).
091
      With this work, we aim to improve the general level of performance and stability of Transformer-
092
      based approaches by implementing an unsupervised representation learning module, specifically one
093   based on the principles of slot encoding.
094
095
      2.2   O BJECT- ORIENTED REPRESENTATION LEARNING
096
097   In this work, we integrate concepts from the line of research on learning object-oriented represen-
098   tations from images and videos (Locatello et al., 2020; Zoran et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2023). In
099   particular, slot encoders for video (Kipf et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2022) learn a representation that
100   tracks the prominent objects in a video frame by frame. The structure of our architecture is based on
101   that of slot encoders, but we try to streamline it by focusing exclusively on using Transformer mod-
102   ules, which allows us to convert the image to just one intermediate form, i.e. a sequence of tokens
103   to be elaborated by the Transformers, while Singh et al. (2022) requires two separate elaborations
104
      of the image: one to produce convolutional features and one to produce a sequence of tokens.
105   The idea of leveraging slot encoding mechanisms for world modelling has also been explored in Wu
106   et al. (2023). However, while that work uses a single transformer as the dynamics modelling mod-
107   ule, we experiment with the idea of keeping representation correction and dynamics advancement
      separate, where each step is learned by a different, smaller neural model.

142   3     M ETHOD
143
144   We introduce a multi-stage architecture, called “Future-Predicting Transformer Triplet for world
145   modeling” (FPTT), which aims to model the behaviour of objects in a set of videos so as to predict
146   their evolution.
147
      We frame the world modelling problem as a sequence learning one. We use transformers as the
148
      fundamental building block of our architecture, due to their proven performance in world mod-
149
      elling (Micheli et al., 2023) as well as other tasks that can be reduced to modelling and manipulating
150   a sequence of tokens (Vaswani et al., 2017; Esser et al., 2021). In particular, we leverage the recent
151   work by Micheli et al. (2023), which showcases a sample-efficient application of transformers to
152   world modelling. This design principle is integrated with the slot-attention mechanism (Kipf et al.,
153   2022; Singh et al., 2022), which is used to learn a compact representation of objects that appear in a
154   video.
155
156
157   3.1   N OTATION
158
159   We indicate with xt the t-th frame of a video and with zt a sequence of tokens corresponding to xt .
160   Λt (x) is the internal representation of the input video x up to time t, i.e. given frames x1 , ..., xt−1 ,
161   while Λ∗t (x) is the “corrected” representation which also includes information from frame xt . The
      initial representation Λ1 (x) is randomly determined for initialization purposes.

      3.2   A RCHITECTURAL OVERVIEW
163
164   Figure 1 shows the high-level components of FPTT. Further details on the implementation, e.g. the
165   hyperparameters of the architecture, can be found in Appendix A.
166
      The architecture takes as input a sequence of T frames of a video, i.e. xt with t = 1, ..., T . The
167
      frames are processed sequentially, so that Λt+1 (x) is determined by combining the previous repre-
168   sentation Λt (x) with the new frame xt .
169
170   As in previous works (Micheli et al., 2023), each frame xt is transformed into a corresponding
171
      sequence of tokens zt by a discrete Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoder (VQVAE) (van den
      Oord et al., 2017; Esser et al., 2021), as transformers need to work on sequences of tokens. Further
172
      details on the VQVAE can be found in section 3.3.
173
174   After this preliminary step, the sequence of tokens zt is processed by the core components of the
175   architecture which are meant to predict the next representation Λt+1 (x) based on the current one
176   Λt (x) and zt . These components, both based on the transformer architecture, have the same high-
177
      level purpose as their counterparts in slot attention for video (Kipf et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2022)
      architectures:
178
179
             • The corrector transformer (see section 3.4) which compares the previous (internal) rep-
180            resentation Λt (x) with the tokenized representation of the current frame zt in order to
181            consistently align the internal representation with the actual evolution of the video;
182
             • The predictor transformer (see section 3.5) which predicts the evolution of the world state
183
               and produces the representation of the next time step Λt+1 (x) on the basis of the result of
184
               the corrector, i.e., Λ∗t (x). Λt+1 (x) is then passed to the corrector for the next stage.
185
186   The result of the prediction at stage t, i.e. Λt+1 (x), is also passed to the decoder transformer
187   (see section 3.6). This component transforms the predicted internal representation Λt+1 (x) into
188   a sequence of tokens ẑt+1 . Finally, the loss is calculated by comparing ẑt+1 with zt+1 , i.e. the
189   sequence of tokens obtained from the input frame. All the above steps (correction, prediction,
190   decoding and loss calculation) are computed for each input frame except the last one, i.e. for t =
191   1, ..., T − 1. The last frame is not processed at training time because it would require the existence
192   of a frame xT +1 to calculate the loss against, which is impossible to provide since the video only
193
      goes up to frame xT by definition.
194
195   3.3   V ECTOR Q UANTIZED VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODER FOR TOKENIZATION
196
      The Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoder (VQVAE) transforms video frames into a format that
197
      subsequent transformers can process. This format is a sequence of L tokens, with each token repre-
198   sented by a vector of the space V = {v1 , v2 , ..., vN } ⊂ Rd , where d is defined as a hyperparameter
199   of the architecture (see appendix A).
200
201
      The VQVAE alternates residual convolutional layers, attention blocks, and convolutional downsam-
      pling layers to convert an image1 x ∈ RW ×H×3 (W and H are the width and height of the image,
202
      respectively) to a latent-space representation zl (x) = (zl,1 (x), zl,2 (x), ..., zl,L (x)) ∈ RL×d . Then
203
      each latent vector is quantized into a token simply by picking the closest embedding vector in V,
204   that is to say, z(x) ∈ RL×d is such that zi (x) = argminv∈V (||zl,i (x) − v||2 ) for each i = 1, ..., L.
205
206   A decoder network with a symmetrical structure to the encoder (not shown in figure 1) is used
207
      to reconvert a token sequence z back into an image x̂(z) for the purposes of training the whole
      autoencoder pair.
208
209
210   3.4   C ORRECTOR TRANSFORMER
211
      The purpose of the corrector transformer is to avoid drifting, i.e. making the internal representation
212   stick with the evolution of the video. This is achieved by updating the estimated representation Λt (x)
213   with the corresponding frame zt thus producing a corrected representation Λ∗t (x). It is implemented

      by a transformer that produces the corrected representation (Λ∗t (x)) by performing an unmasked
217   cross-attention of the two inputs (Λt (x) and zt ).
218
219   It is worth noting that this structure fits neither the transformer encoder nor the transformer decoder
220
      descriptions as traditionally defined in Vaswani et al. (2017), since we perform cross-attention (like
      a decoder) without including a causal mask (like an encoder). This allows us to compare the two
221
      input sequences as a whole, without arbitrarily limiting the context. We also note that the purpose
222
      of this transformer is not to perform autoregressive generation, so a non-causal flow of information
223   causes no harm.
224
225
226   3.5   P REDICTOR TRANSFORMER
227
      The predictor transformer performs self-attention on the representation Λ∗t (x) to estimate its ad-
228
      vancement to the next time step Λt+1 (x).
229
230   The predictor and corrector transformers can be seen as two halves of one model, dedicated to
231   predicting the next internal representation on the basis of the current representation and the current
232   frame of the video being processed. For this reason, each of them has individually fewer layers
233
      compared to the decoder (see section 3.6).
234
235   3.6   D ECODER TRANSFORMER
236
237   The decoder transformer converts a representation Λt+1 (x) into a sequence of tokens ẑt+1 . The
238   loss is computed by comparing ẑt+1 with zt+1 , i.e. the sequence of tokens obtained from the input
239
      frame.
240
241   3.7   A RCHITECTURE VARIANTS
242
243   We experiment with two variants of this architecture, defined by the positioning of the decoder
244   transformer in the structure laid out so far.
245   The default FPTT architecture, represented by the continuous lines in figure 1, has this stage posi-
246   tioned between the predict step that generates Λt+1 (x) and the subsequent correct step. Thus, the
247   loss computes the error on the prediction of the frame zt+1 .
248
      The alternative variant, which we call FPTT-pre, is identical except that the decoder transformer
249
      takes Λ∗t (x) instead. This is represented in figure 1 by replacing the line labeled as “default” with
250   the dashed line labeled as “FPTT-pre”. This produces a structure that is more in line with Kipf et al.
251   (2022) and Singh et al. (2022), while the default FPTT diverges from such previous work.
252
253
      The objective of this experiment is to test whether calculating the loss on the predicted future repre-
      sentation, as opposed to the corrected current one, directs the model’s attention towards the accurate
254
      prediction of future events, thus emphasising the world modelling objective. This will be achieved
255
      by experimenting with different placements of the decoder transformer (and thus of the loss func-
256   tion).
257
258   In the absence of any contrary indication, the two variants are to be considered as operating in a
259
      similar manner.
260
261   3.8   T RAINING
262
263   The VQVAE is trained in isolation with respect to the whole architecture. To enhance the stability
264   of the training process, we maintain a fixed configuration of the VQVAE parameters throughout the
265   training of the other components. Following Micheli et al. (2023), the loss is a combination of a
266
      mean absolute error and a perceptual loss (Johnson et al., 2016) on the reconstruction, as well as a
      commitment loss on the embeddings (van den Oord et al., 2017).
267
268   As for the corrector, predictor and decoder transformers, they are trained together in an end-to-
269   end fashion, with the objective to minimize a cross-entropy loss on the (tokenized versions of the)
      predicted frames ẑ2 , ..., ẑT with respect to the ground-truth ones z2 , ..., zT .

296   4     E XPERIMENTS
297
298   The ability to model the world of the proposed architecture (FPTT) is assessed through a physi-
299   cal reasoning task (see section 4.1) that requires the ability to predict how a set of objects moves
300   in a given environment. Specifically, we experiment with the PHYRE dataset which provides a
301   benchmark containing a wide set of simple classical mechanics puzzles in a 2D physical environ-
302   ment (Bakhtin et al., 2019). We compare the performance of the two variants of the FPTT archi-
303   tecture against each other and a baseline taken from the existing literature. We also performed an
304   ablation study to investigate the efficacy of the various components of the architecture.
305
306   4.1   P HYSICAL REASONING TASK
307
308   We adhere to the definition of a physical reasoning task as outlined in the PHYRE benchmark
309   (Bakhtin et al., 2019). The task is set in a two-dimensional world that simulates simple deterministic
310   Newtonian physics with a constant downward gravitational force and a small amount of friction.
311   This world contains non-deformable objects, distinguished by colour, that can be static (i.e. they
312   remain in a fixed position) or dynamic (i.e. they move if they collide with another object and are
313   influenced by the force of gravity). These objects can be arranged in different configurations to
314
      create a wide diversity of tasks. We use a dataset made of video recordings from 23 different tasks,
      for a total of 1.15M video samples.
315
316   A task consists of an initial world state and a goal (see figure 2). The initial world state is a pre-
317   defined configuration of objects. The goal for all tasks is the following: at the end of the simulation
318   the green object must touch the blue object. If the goal is achieved, the task succeeds (as in the
319   PHYRE terminology).
320   Given a video (as a sequence of frames), the objective of the world model is to build an internal
321   representation that can be used to predict if the depicted task will succeed or fail. The ability to
322   predict that represents an auxiliary classification problem. This allows us to indirectly assess the
323   performance of the world models. It is worth noticing that the same evaluation protocol is used in
      related work (Wu et al., 2023).

335   4.2   BASELINE
336
      The performance of the proposed architecture is evaluated in comparison to STEVE (Singh et al.,
337
      2022), a slot encoding architecture that is also based on the correction-prediction pattern. The input
338
      frame is encoded using a convolutional neural network (CNN) which feeds a recurrent neural net-
339   work (RNN) acting as corrector. The result is then passed to the predictor (there called “interaction
340   step”), a single-layer transformer.
341
342
      For the purposes of loss calculation, the slots resulting from the corrector (before the predictor) are
      translated into a token sequence (i.e. ẑt ) by a transformer decoder and compared against a ground-
343
      truth token sequence produced directly from the real video frames via a pretrained VAE.
344
345
346   4.3   A BLATION STUDY
347
      As a further comparison, we consider an ablated version of the FPTT architecture where the cor-
348
      rector and predictor transformer (therefore, the components enabling slot-encoding mechanism) are
349
      removed from the architecture. This leaves only the decoder transformer to learn the entirety of the
350   world modeling task, predicting the (tokenized version of the) next frame zt+1 directly from the
351   previous one zt , without using an internal representation. In the following, we refer to this ablated
352   architecture as decoder-only.
353
      It can also be noted that this architecture represents a close adaptation to our non-interactive context
354
      of the approach to world modelling from visual data by Micheli et al. (2023), which also uses a
355
      single transformer to predict the future world state in a reinforcement learning setting.
356
357   This is the only possible ablation, because the loss is computed against a tokenized representation
358   of the video frames, so the decoder is needed to produce the tokenized versions of the predicted
359
      frames.

361   4.4   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
362
363
      We experiment on a dataset of synthetic videos presented in Qi et al. (2021). This dataset was
      generated by rendering simulations from the PHYRE benchmark for physical reasoning (Bakhtin
364
      et al., 2019). Specifically, we focus exclusively on videos from B-tier tasks, and within-template
365
      evaluation. Figure 3 shows the experimental setup. The world model takes a video (as a sequence
366   of frames) from the PHYRE task and builds a representation. This representation is then passed to a
367   classifier which predicts the result of the task (i.e. success or failure). As for the classifier, we use a
368   BERT-like encoder architecture (Devlin et al., 2019), trained in a supervised manner.
369
      As for FPTT (default and FPTT-pre variant) and STEVE, we proceed as follows. Each video in the
370
      dataset represents a task that is labeled as either “success” or “failure”. The world model is given
371
      the first N frames of a video whose total length is T frames, with N < T . The remaining T − N
372   frames are kept hidden from the model. In order to obtain the representation of the whole video,
373   including both the given section and an estimation for the following hidden one, the N given frames
374   are processed as usual, updating the representation in the correct step and advancing it to the next
375   timestep in the predict step. Afterwards, the remaining T −N steps are projected by simply repeating
376   the predict step, skipping the correction for the hidden frames (see figure 4). In the experiments, N
377   is set to 5, while T varies depending on each video, ranging from 7 to 18, with many videos being
      12-15 frames long.

509   5.1   L IMITATIONS
510
      Despite the observed performance improvements, the representation remains opaque and lacks in-
511
      terpretability. Our attempts at replicating the object segmentation displayed by slot-attention archi-
512   tectures (Kipf et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2022) have not yet yielded positive results so far. However,
513   this may be overturned by more systematic experimentation in the future.
514
515
      Furthermore, we acknowledge that, although the dataset we used presents a variety of visual config-
      urations, it is ultimately synthetic and simplistic. Although we claim that the presented experiments
516
      demonstrate the benefit of the proposed architecture, we do plan to extend the experiments to more
517
      complex video datasets such as MOVi-E (Greff et al., 2022) and Physion (Bear et al., 2021), which
518   will test its performance in more realistic scenes as well as its generalization capabilities. Experi-
519   ments on the latter dataset are currently ongoing but could not be completed for this publication.
520
521   5.2   C ONCLUSION
522
523   We propose a new architecture, the Future-Predicting Transformer Triplet for world modeling
524   (FPTT), which leverages the power of transformers for sequence learning to model the behaviour of
525   objects in a set of videos and predict the evolution of the environment.
526   We experimentally show that our architecture outperforms transformer-based world models (Micheli
527   et al., 2023), and improves on slot-attention methods (Kipf et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2022) in terms
528   of sample efficiency and stability during the training process.
529
      In the future, we intend to conduct further experiments with the architecture in more interactive envi-
530
      ronments, in which objects can be moved by agents. Moreover, we would like to study applications
531
      to causal discovery problems (Yu et al., 2023), where learning a compact representation that can be
532   interpreted causally might help in understanding complex scenarios.
