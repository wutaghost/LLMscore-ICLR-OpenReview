009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal Emotion Recognition in Conversations (MERC) seeks to identify
012            emotional states across multiple modalities, including text, audio, and video. This
013            field of study is pivotal for advancing machine intelligence, with significant im-
014            plications for applications such as intelligent dialogue systems and public opinion
015            analysis. Most existing approaches primarily employ full-sequence interaction
016            and distillation techniques, aiming to construct a comprehensive global contextual
017            understanding while simultaneously enhancing the interaction among heteroge-
018
               neous modalities. However, the presence of repetitive and redundant information,
               coupled with gradient conflicts arising from modal heterogeneity, can significantly
019
               impede the effectiveness of multimodal learning and long-range relationship mod-
020
               eling. In this work, we propose an innovative heterogeneous multimodal integra-
021            tion method called SUMMER, grounded in attention mechanism and knowledge
022            distillation techniques, which facilitates dynamic interactive fusion of multimodal
023            representations. Specifically, the Sparse Dynamic Mixture of Experts strategy
024            is proposed to dynamically adjust the relevance of the temporal information to
025            construct local to global token-wise interactions. Then a Global Mixture of Ex-
026            perts is employed to enhance the model’s overall contextual understanding across
027            modalities. Notably, we introduce retrograde distillation that utilizes a pre-trained
028            unimodal teacher model to guide the learning of multimodal student model, inter-
029
               vening and supervising multimodal fusion within both the latent and logit spaces.
               Experiments on the IEMOCAP and MELD datasets demonstrate that our SUM-
030
               MER framework consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods, with
031
               particularly significant improvements in recognizing minority and semantically
032            similar emotions in MERC tasks.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Multimodal Emotion Recognition in Conversations (MERC) Poria et al. (2019) seeks to elucidate
038   the emotional dynamics inherent in interactions, thereby enhancing human-computer interaction
039   Cowie et al. (2001) and fostering empathy across diverse domains such as digital humans, healthcare
040   Pujol et al. (2019), and social media analytics Andalibi & Buss (2020). Unlike traditional methods,
041   multimodal emotion analysis integrates cues from text, audio, and visual modalities Zhang et al.
042   (2024) which can capture nuanced emotional cues and facilitate corrective feedback mechanisms
043   from varied contexts.
044   In MERC tasks, most existing studies focus on constructing global context understanding and im-
045   proving cross-modal fusion. The RNN-based model DialogueRNN Majumder et al. (2019) lever-
046   ages Recurrent Neural Networks to capture global temporal dependencies, while the GNN-based
047   model CORECT uses Relational Temporal Graph Neural Networks to represent multimodal rela-
048   tionships. In contrast, the Transformer-based model MultiEMO and SDT Shi & Huang (2023);
049   Ma et al. (2023) employs attention mechanisms to prioritize long-range dependencies, integrating
050
      contextual information across multiple modalities.

053   indicate sadness. However, if the model overemphasizes earlier positive expressions like ”we com-

076   Although the Mixture of Experts (MoE) model dynamically selects the most suitable token experts
077   via a gating mechanism, improving multimodal fusion and association efficiency, it also has lim-
078   itations. As shown in Figure 1 (b), MoE only selects a fixed Top-K subset or weights all experts
079   for reasoning, limiting its adaptability in complex MERC environments. Therefore, dynamically
080   selecting token-level information and incorporating a global Mixture of Experts adapter is essential
081
      for optimizing contextual understanding and filtering redundant information.
082   To enhance the cross-modal fusion of heterogeneous modalities refined by MoE, the Transformer-
083   based SDT model Ma et al. (2023) employs a self-distillation approach to guide multimodal fusion
084   learning. Additionally, cross-modality distillation enhances fusion by enabling knowledge transfer
085   between heterogeneous features. However, self-distillation methods often face gradient conflict
086   issues, where gradients from the teacher and student model interfere during training. Resolving
087
      fusion disorientation is critical to improving the effectiveness of multimodal fusion.
088   In this work, we propose a Sparse Unimodal-driven distillation for Multi-Modal Emotion
089   Recognition named SUMMER to enhance modal association learning and fusion disorientation.
090   First, we employ Sparse Dynamic MoE (SDMoE) to enhance token-wise interaction for high-quality
091   localized information and mitigate the impact of redundant data on cross-modal fusion. Then we in-
092   troduce Hierarchical Cross-Modal Fusion (HCMF) with Global MoE (GMoE) to adaptively capture
093
      and unify intrinsic links between modalities to improve global context understanding. Additionally,
      we propose a novel Interactive Knowledge Distillation (IKD) where a high-performing unimodal
094
      teacher model guides the learning of a multimodal student model, facilitating directed learning and
095
      reducing gradient conflicts caused by modal discrepancies.
096
097   The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
098
099           • We propose a Sparse Dynamic Mixture of Experts and Hierarchical Cross-Modal Fusion
100             method to enhance local key token selection and improve global context understanding,
101
                thereby refining heterogeneous modal information for more effective multimodal fusion.
102
              • We introduce a retrograde distillation strategy where a unimodal-driven teacher model
103             guides the multimodal student model, standardizing and addressing fusion disorientation
104             in multimodal learning.
105
106           • Our model significantly outperforms state-of-the-art benchmarks on the IEMOCAP and
107             MELD datasets, demonstrating superior performance in capturing subtle emotional nu-
                ances, and excelling in semantically similar and underrepresented emotion categories.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
111   Multimodal Emotion Recognition in Conversations. The core objective of MERC Dashtipour
112   et al. (2016) is to analyze speakers’ emotional states by leveraging multimodal data over time. While
113   early approaches relied heavily on GNN-based Ghosal et al. (2019); Song et al. (2023); Hu et al.
114
      (2021) and RNN-based architectures Poria et al. (2017); Majumder et al. (2019); Jiao et al. (2019);
      Li et al. (2022), which were standard in natural language processing, these recurrent models faced
115
      limitations in handling long sequences and lacked scalability. In contrast to these models, contem-
116
      porary approaches aim to capture both intra- and inter-modal interactions, leading to more nuanced
117   emotional analysis by unifying information from text, audio, and visual modalities. Techniques such
118   as tensor fusion, as employed by LMF Liu et al. (2018), manage complementary information while
119   reducing redundancy across modalities, further enhancing multimodal fusion. Additionally, MM-
120   DFN Hu et al. (2022) dynamically captures contextual and multimodal features while minimizing
121   irrelevant information across modalities.
122
123
124   Transformer-based Models. The introduction of Transformer models Vaswani (2017) revolu-
125
      tionized MERC by enabling efficient parallel computing and long-sequence modeling through self-
      attention mechanisms, leading to significant advancements in intra- and inter-modal fusion. Models
126
      like CTNet Lian et al. (2021) employ single and cross-modality Transformers, while CKETF Ghosh
127
      et al. (2021) enhances context and knowledge representation within a Transformer framework. TL-
128   ERC leverages transfer learning to improve performance across tasks.
129
130   To improve multimodal understanding, dynamic attention mechanisms are employed to adjust at-
131
      tention weights, enabling more effective cross-modal encoding. TFR-Net Yuan et al. (2021) and
      Emocaps Li et al. (2022) leverage intra- and inter-modal attention to capture sentiment trends. Tai-
132
      lor Zhang et al. (2022) uses a Transformer-based unimodal extractor and a multi-label bootstrap
133
      decoder to model dependencies between labels and modalities. SDT Ma et al. (2023) introduces an
134   Intra- and Inter-modal Transformer for emotional interactions across modalities and sessions, while
135   TACFN Liu et al. (2023) proposes an Adaptive Inter-modal Fusion Network to reduce redundancy
136   and improve feature integration.
137
138
139   Knowledge Distillation. Knowledge Distillation (KD) Gou et al. (2021) has become a powerful
140   method for compressing models and improving efficiency by transferring knowledge from a larger
141   teacher model to a smaller student model. In multimodal emotion recognition, KD enables the in-
142
      tegration of complementary information across modalities, helping the student model capture richer
      emotional representations. SENet Albanie et al. (2018) transfers visual knowledge into speech emo-
143
      tion recognition models using unlabeled video data. Schoneveld Schoneveld et al. (2021) utilizes the
144
      KD method to improve the performance of models in facial expression recognition. Similarly KIAN
145   Wang et al. (2020) proposes K-injection subnetworks to distill linguistic and acoustic information,
146   allowing implicit knowledge transfer in audiovisual models for group emotion recognition.
147
148
      The majority of these approaches rely on offline distillation, which necessitates the pre-training of
      a large teacher model to guide the learning of smaller student model. However, little attention has
149
      been given to using a smaller unimodal-driven teacher model to instruct more complex multimodal
150
      students, which has the potential for effective cross-modal learning. This gap serves as the primary
151   motivation for our work.

182   3.2       M ODEL OVERVIEW
183
      As shown in Figure 2, SUMMER consists of four core modules: Unimodal Reconstruction, Sparse
184
      Dynamic Mixture of Experts (SDMoE), Hierarchical Cross-Modal Fusion (HCMF), and Interactive
185   Knowledge Distillation (IKD). The unimodal encoder extracts features from text, audio, and visual
186   inputs, while SDMoE focuses on token-wise interaction, dynamically adjusting global context as-
187   sociations and filtering redundant information. HCMF enriches semantics by aligning multimodal
188   weights, and IKD improves cross-modal feature fusion through efficient knowledge transfer, lever-
189   aging lightweight pre-trained teacher models via latent and logit spaces.
190
191   3.3       U NIMODAL R ESTRUCTION
192
193   Unimodal Encoder. For the Text Encoder, we use the pre-trained roBERTa model to extract text
194
      features hti ∈ Rls ×dt , incorporating speaker identity and dialogue separators to capture both intra-
      and inter-speaker context. The Audio Encoder leverages OpenSMILE to extract 6,373-dimensional
195
      acoustic features hai ∈ Rls ×da , which are reduced to 512 dimensions for efficiency. To address
196
      challenges with direct CNN(3D) video processing, we propose LFNet3D (see details in A.1) to pro-
197
      duce 256-dimensional spatio-temporal features hvi ∈ Rls ×dv . Finally, DialogueRNN is employed to
198
      capture global emotional trends and speaker-emotion dynamics in conversations.

201   of the current speaker directly influences the next speaker. To effectively model the relationships
202   between speaker identity Sj and utterance in affective states, it is crucial to incorporate a latent
203   speaker representation into the positional embeddings. This is achieved using an input feature set
204   Him = {hti , hai , hvi }, which includes text, audio, and visual features extracted by a unimodal encoder.

      4.1   DATASETS AND E VALUATION M ETRICS
354
355   To verify the validity of our proposed SUMMER model, we perform experiments on two widely-
356   used MERC datasets, IEMOCAP Busso et al. (2008) and MELD Poria et al. (2018), which consist of
357   multimodal data (text, audio, and video). IEMOCAP comprises 12 hours of conversations annotated
358   with six emotion labels, while MELD contains dialogue clips from the TV show Friends with seven
359   distinct emotion labels. In our experiments, we report accuracy (Acc) and F1-score for each emotion
360   category, along with the overall weighted average accuracy (w-Acc) and weighted average F1 (w-
361   F1) to compare the performance of the proposed method against baseline approaches.
362
363   4.2   BASELINES
364
365   We compare our model against several strong baselines: DialogueRNN Majumder et al. (2019) uses
366
      GRUs to model speaker states, context, and emotions, while DialogueGCN Ghosal et al. (2019) ap-
      plies GCNs to represent conversations as graphs. MMGCN Hu et al. (2021) and CORECT Nguyen
367
      et al. (2023) use GCNs with dynamic fusion for multimodal context modeling, and MultiEMO Shi
368

      A.1   V ISUAL F EATURE E XTRACTION
651
652   In conversational analysis, facial expressions serve as crucial indicators of emotional changes in
653   speakers. However, existing studies predominantly employ 3D-CNNs to directly process video
654   streams, a method that introduces several challenges. Video streams often contain a substantial
655   amount of redundant or minimally varying information, as adjacent frames typically exhibit minimal
656   differences, resulting in highly similar extracted features. This not only increases computational load
657
      and noise but also complicates the alignment with other modal information.
658   To address these challenges, we propose an improved visual feature extraction method, termed
659   LENet(3D) . Initially, we apply a keyframe extraction strategy, sampling video frames at intervals
660   Ns = F rames
               10    of 10 frames, denoted as uvi . Subsequently, facial landmarks in the video frames are
661   detected and aligned using a Multi-task Cascaded Convolutional Network (MTCNN) , ensuring the
662   precision of facial region alignment cvi = MTCNN(uvi ), i ∈ {1, 2, ..., Ns }. This process yields a
663   continuous, aligned video stream that serves as input to the 3D-CNN.
664   We utilize a pre-trained 3D-CNN model fine-tuned on the VGGFace2 dataset, specifically adapted
665   for facial feature extraction tasks. The aligned face video segments are passed through the 3D-
666   CNN, where we extract spatio-temporal feature vectors from intermediate layers rather than the
667   final output layer. Finally, a DialogueRNN network is employed to model the temporal dynamics
668   of both the speaker’s emotional states and visual information. The extracted features are reduced to
669   256 dimensions via a fully connected layer to facilitate further analysis.
670

      model, and the results demonstrate significant improvements in the student model’s performance.
