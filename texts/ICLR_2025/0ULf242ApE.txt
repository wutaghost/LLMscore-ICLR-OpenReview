009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Humans distill complex experiences into fundamental abstractions, enabling rapid
012            learning and adaptation. Similarly, autoregressive transformers exhibit adaptive
013            learning through in-context learning (ICL), which begs the question of how. In
014            this paper, we propose concept encoding-decoding mechanism to explain ICL
015            by studying how transformers form internal abstractions in their representations.
016            On synthetic ICL tasks, we analyze the training dynamics of a small transformer
017            and report the coupled emergence of concept encoding and decoding. As the
018            model learns to encode different latent concepts (e.g., “Finding the first noun in a
019
               sentence.”) into distinct, separable representations, it conditionally builds decod-
               ing algorithms and improve its ICL performance. We validate the existence of
020
               this mechanism across pretrained models of varying sizes (Gemma-2 2B/9B/27B,
021
               Llama-3.1 8B/70B). Further, through mechanistic interventions and controlled
022            finetuning, we demonstrate that the quality of concept encoding is causally re-
023            lated and predictive of ICL performance. Our empirical insights shed light into
024            better understanding the success and failure modes of large language models via
025            their representations.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Throughout history, humans have made sense of the world by distilling complex experiences into
031   fundamental abstractions, such as physics and mathematics. These mental models enable us to learn
032   quickly, predict outcomes, and adapt to new situations. In artificial intelligence, autoregressive trans-
033   formers are beginning to exhibit similar capabilities. Through in-context learning (ICL), they adapt
034   to new tasks without parameter updates, suggesting they might also be forming internal abstractions
035   (Raventós et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024b; Kumar et al., 2024).
036   Hendel et al. (2023); Merullo et al. (2023); Todd et al. (2023) introduce a mechanistic perspective
037   on how pretrained LLMs represent the latent concepts underlying the ICL task as vectors in their
038   representations. They empirically demonstrate that these task-specific vectors can trigger the desired
039   ICL behavior in many cases, with the effectiveness varying across tasks. Although an impactful first
040   step, there still remains unanswered questions of why these task vectors exist in the first place and
041   why the effectiveness varies by task. This necessitates a deeper mechanistic understanding of the
042   internal abstraction behavior of LLMs, which could encompass the findings of task-specific vectors
043   and various aspects of ICL.
044   In our work, we propose the concept encoding-decoding mechanism as the origin of internal ab-
045   straction behavior. To study the emergence of abstractions during pretraining, we train a small
046   transformer on a mixture of sparse linear regression tasks. We find that concept encoding emerges
047   as the model learns to map different latent concepts into distinct, separable representation spaces.
048   This geometric structuring of the representation space is coupled with the development of concept-
049   specific ICL algorithms – namely, concept decoding. Importantly, we see that the emergence of
050
      the two-stage process coincides with one another, implying mutual dependence between the two.
      Through causal analysis, we demonstrate that the model associates different algorithms to different
051
      learned concepts and that ICL happens through the two-step process.
052
053   We demonstrate the validity of the concept encoding-decoding mechanism across different pre-
      trained model families and scales (Llama-3.1-8B/70B and Gemma-2 2B/9B/27B) on more natural

054
      ICL tasks, such as part-of-speech tagging and bitwise arithmetic. We show that large language mod-
055   els (LLMs) trained on diverse data also exhibit concept encoding behavior. With more in-context
056   examples, LLMs map the inputs to increasingly separable representation spaces, clustered by the
057   latent concepts. Moreover, leveraging insights from the synthetic experiments, we demonstrate that
058   the decodability of the concepts from representations is predictive of downstream ICL performance.
059   We establish a causal relationship between the quality of encoding and ICL performance through
060   mechanistic intervention and controlled finetuning experiments.
061   Our main contributions are as follows:
062
063         1. We first study the emergence of task vectors by training a small transformer on a syn-
064            thetic ICL task (§3.3) and propose concept encoding-decoding mechanism to explain the
065            emergent behavior for learning to solve ICL tasks. We observe that earlier layers of the
066            model learn to encode the latent concept, whereas the latter layers conditions the algorithm
067            on the inferred concept. Interestingly, the emergence of the two-stage process is coupled,
068            implying a mutual dependence.
069         2. We introduce Concept Decodability (CD), a quantitative metric that predicts downstream
070            ICL performance in pretrained LLMs (§4.2). We demonstrate our framework’s generality
071            across tasks, model families, and scales (Llama 3.1 8B/70B, Gemma 2B/9B/27B)
072
            3. We establish the causal relationship between CD and ICL performance in pretrained LLMs
073
               through mechanistic intervention (§4.1) and controlled finetuning (§4.3).
074
075         4. We offer a new perspective on mechanistically understanding how the model internalizes
076            the learning signal of more in-context examples, finetuning, and prompting (§5.1) through
077
               the lens of concept encoding-decoding.

081
      Mechanisms of ICL. Astounded by LLMs’ ability to perform ICL, many have proposed theories
082   to understand the mechanisms of ICL. Some (Dai et al., 2023; von Oswald et al., 2023; Ahn et al.,
083   2024; Akyürek et al., 2024) have proposed that LLMs, with linear attention (Katharopoulos et al.,
084   2020), can implement stochastic gradient descent to perform ICL. Other works (Xie et al., 2021;
085   Wang et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024) have presented a Bayesian framework to theoretically explain the
086   workings of ICL. This view implies that the model implements a two-stage algorithm to estimate
087   the posterior P (z|D) and the likelihood P (y∗ |x∗ , D). In this work, we adopt this framework and
088   demonstrate how the model implements it through its intermediate representations. More specifi-
089   cally, we study the emergence of concept encoding – building separable representations for different
090
      latent concepts.
091
092   Task Vectors. Todd et al. (2023) and Hendel et al. (2023) identify task/function vectors that can
093   induce desired ICL task behavior (e.g., object-color mapping) even at zero-shot. Although motivated
094
      by their work, we propose concept encoding in place of the term ‘task vector’ because of the limited
      scope under which it is valid. Previous studies (Pan et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023b; Min et al., 2022)
095
      have found ICL tasks that are word-to-word mapping (e.g., object-color, English-French) are not in
096
      fact task learning but task retrieval that uses semantic priors of the model. Moreover, Zheng et al.
097   (2024a) demonstrated that the functions’ representation vectors are distributed over multiple tokens
098   for more complex tasks. Therefore, we use ‘concept encoding’ to refer to a broader phenomenon of
099   building separable representations for distinct latent concepts.
100
101
      Latent Concepts in Language Model Representations. Several studies (Dalvi et al., 2022;
102   Merullo et al., 2023) have examined how language models encode concepts in their representa-
103   tions. In autoregressive LLMs, notions like truthfulness (Marks & Tegmark, 2023) and time and
104   space(Gurnee & Tegmark, 2024) have been shown to be linearly separable representations. Sparse
105   autoencoders (Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023) have revealed highly interpretable
106   features that emerge and grow with scale in LLMs. Beyond the identification of these concepts,
107   our work aims to answer how such concepts emerge in the representation of LLMs and how they
      causally relate to ICL performance.

      Mechanistic Interpretability. To study the causal relationship between the accuracy of concept
109   encoding and downstream ICL performance, we adopt causal mediation analysis techniques from
110   Geiger et al. (2020); Vig et al. (2020); Todd et al. (2023); Heimersheim & Nanda (2024); Merullo
111   et al. (2024). We specifically use the method of activation patching, where we replace the activations
112   of an immediate layer from a sample with another. This technique allows us to demonstrate that
113   transformers implement different algorithms conditioned on the inferred concepts.

      3.1   N OTATION AND BACKGROUND
118
119   We focus on ICL problems, where the goal is to predict y∗ from a query x∗ , given some in-context
120   examples D = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 . Each problem shares a latent concept z that links inputs x to outputs
121   y. For instance, in an ICL task where latent concept is object-color mapping, we provide demon-
122   strations like (apple, red), (banana, yellow), and (grape, purple), and then ask for what comes after
123   (lemon, ?). We employ this parameterization to accommodate latent concepts varying in complex-
124
      ity, from simple function regression problems (Garg et al., 2022; von Oswald et al., 2023; Li et al.,
      2023) to POS tagging (Blevins et al., 2022; Banko & Moore, 2004) and arithmetic (He et al., 2024).
125
126

      3.2   T HEORETICAL F RAMEWORK
128
      Of the many different frameworks (Bai et al., 2024; Min et al., 2022; von Oswald et al., 2023;
129   Akyürek et al., 2024) to understand the workings of ICL, we adopt the Bayesian view (Xie et al.,
130   2021; Mittal et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024). It proposes that transformers implicitly
131   infer the latent variable z underlying the demonstrations and apply it to generate an answer. More
132   formally,

137   This framework suggests ICL is a two stage process. First, latent concept inference. Latent concept
138   z is approximated from D through the distribution ẑ ∼ Pθ (z|D). Second, selective algorithm
139   application. The model applies an algorithm conditioned on ẑ to predict y∗ as given by Pθ (y∗ |x∗ , ẑ).
140   Although theoretically compelling, it was not until recently that Hendel et al. (2023); Todd et al.
141   (2023); Merullo et al. (2023) showed empirical evidence of models encoding the latent concepts in
142   the intermediate representations. They illustrate that these representative vectors are then decoded
143   and trigger the desired ICL task behavior. With this simple analogy to an encoder-decoder, we begin
144   our investigation into the following questions:
145
146
            1. How does the concept encoding and decoding behavior emerge in the model over training
               and how do they interplay?
147
148         2. How is the model’s ability to accurately infer the latent concepts related to downstream
149
               ICL performance?

151   3.3   M OTIVATION : S YNTHETIC E XPERIMENTS
152
      We train a small transformer on a synthetic ICL task and demonstrate that concept encoding and de-
153
      coding emerges simultaneously during training. Through causal analysis, we show that, as the mod-
154   els “discovers” a latent concept by building a distinct representation from the others, it associates
155   the concept with different decoding algorithms. Finally, we propose the concept encoding-decoding
156   mechanism that encompasses these findings and serve as the core theory throughout the remainder
157   of our study.

      Theoretical Error Bounds. The error bounds of our task depend on whether the model learns to
      infer the underlying bases. If the model learns to infer the bases, then the model can achieve r-
196
      dimensional regression, where the MSE approaches 0 with r in-context examples. If not, the model,
197
      in the worst case, can perform D-dimensional regression with r-sparsity, which has a longer tailed
198   error curve that approaches 0 between r and D in-context examples. With these insights, we can
199   better analyze which latent basis the model has learned and the associated algorithm. Note that we
200   define “algorithm” as a class of statistical methods for linear regression, as detailed in Appendix C.1.

      Observation 1: Different Loss Dynamics Per Basis. We interestingly observe that each basis,
202
      despite having identical task complexities, exhibits different loss descents during training. Figure 1
203
      shows the test MSE averaged over the sequence over training. B1 displays a distinct loss descent
204   dynamic, undergoing an abrupt drop at epoch 10. In contrast, the other three bases, B2 , B3 , and
205   B4 , exhibit correlated loss descent dynamics, with two smaller descents at 10 and 40 epochs. This
206   suggests that the model learns to infer B1 differently and applies selective algorithms.
207
      Observation 2: Emergence of Separable Representations and Coupled Algorithmic Phase
208
      Transitions. We also analyze the geometry of the intermediate representations at layer 5 to question
209
      how the model may be encoding the latent bases. Surprisingly, at the three points of descent (a, b,
210   c) marked in Figure 1, the model gradually builds separate representations for the different bases
211   as shown in the UMAP visualizations. At point (a), the three bases are clustered together and the
212   model’s algorithm resembles a 16-dimensional weighted LASSO regression. As B1 separates out at
213   point (b), the model starts to leverage the inferred basis to switch to a 4-dimensional regression. At
214   point (c), when all four classes are separable, the model converges to the optimal 4-dimensional re-
215   gression. This observation suggests that model encode latent concepts into separated representations
      to conditionally apply decoding algorithms.

263   learns to encode the latent concepts to separable representation spaces and learns to conditionally
264
      apply decoding algorithms simultaneously. These observations suggest that concept encoding and
      decoding are mutually dependent, but whether they reinforce each other needs to be further studied.

267   3.4   C ONCEPT E NCODING AND D ECODING
268
269   We introduce the terms concept encoding and decoding in this work to capture the emergent phe-
      nomenon of a transformer learning to implement a two-stage process to perform ICL, as observed


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      in Section 3.3. The model learns to encode different concepts into distinct, separable internal repre-
271   sentations. Simultaneously, this separation allows the model to develop concept-specific decoding
272   algorithms. We show that there is a mutual dependency between the two mechanisms and that
273   both are required for effectively solving ICL. What this mutual dependency implies, as we explore
274   through the causal perturbation studies, is that the accuracy to which the model encodes and dis-
275   tinguishes the latent concepts – namely, concept decodability – is predictive of downstream ICL
276   performance. We now validate our theory in real-world, pretrained LLMs.

      4     T OWARDS NATURAL E XPERIMENTS
279
280
      In this section, we empirically validate the proposed concept encoding-decoding mechanism in pre-
281
      trained LLMs. Specifically, we test several hypotheses derived from the proposed mechanism, such
282
      as whether pretrained LLMs exhibit concept encoding behavior and whether the accuracy of concept
283   encoding can predict ICL performance on more natural tasks.
284
285
      Tasks. We construct two classes of algorithmic tasks – natural language processing and arithmetic
286
      – comprising a total of 12 tasks. Within each class, the tasks are designed to be semantically similar,
287   ensuring that the input distributions are alike across tasks. While the underlying latent concepts
288   differ (e.g., different arithmetic operations or linguistic patterns), the surface features of the inputs
289   remain consistent. By keeping the input distributions similar, we can effectively assess the model’s
290   ability to infer and encode latent concepts based solely on subtle differences in the data, rather than
291   the input variations. Refer to Appendix E for more details.
292
      Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging. We construct a POS tagging (Blevins et al., 2022; Banko & Moore,
293   2004) dataset from Marcus et al. (1994), consisting of POS tags, such as Noun, Adjective, Verb,
294   Adverb, Preposition, Pronoun. Given an input text and hidden POS tag zi (e.g., Noun), one needs to
295   output the first word that is of the specified POS tag.
296
      Bitwise arithmetic. We construct a bitwise arithmetic dataset consisting of 6 different operators,
297
      AND, NAND, OR, NOR, XOR, and XNOR. Given a pair of 5-digit binary numbers and the hidden
298
      operator zi (e.g., AND), one needs to output the resulting binary number after the operation.
299
300   For both of these tasks, we create an additional Null class, for which there is no latent concept. In
301   bitwise arithmetic, the Null operator outputs random binary digits, and in POS tagging, the Null
302
      class pairs the input sentences with a randomly selected word. This task helps us identify the cases
      in which the model is confused about the concept.
303
304
305   Model. We verify the existence of our proposed concept encoding-decoding mechanism in models
306
      of different families and scales (Gemma-2 2B/9B/27B and Llama-3.1-8B/70B) in Section 4.1 and
      Appendix E. We continue further analysis with the pretrained Llama-3.1-8B model (Meta, 2024).
307
      We do not train this model, except when we study the causal effect of concept decodability by
308
      finetuning in Section 4.3. We further detail the experimental setup in Appendix E.
309
310
      Evaluation. We evaluate the performance of the model on different tasks by computing the exact-
311
      match accuracy between the generated output under greedy decoding and the ground truth. All of
312   the evaluations assume 4-shots of examples, unless specified otherwise.
313
314
      Concept Decodability (CD). To quantify how well latent concepts can be inferred from repre-
315
      sentations, we employ a simple k-Nearest Neighbor (k-NN) classification metric. Inspired by prior
316   studies using linear probes (Rimanic et al., 2020; Alain & Bengio, 2018), we assess whether the
317   latent concepts can be extracted in a simple manner from their representations. Specifically, we
318   use the representations of the token immediately before y∗ at a chosen layer and predict the latent
319   concept by majority voting among its k nearest neighbors (k = 10, N = 100).

513       5                D ISCUSSION
514
515       5.1                   P IECING IT ALL TOGETHER : I N - CONTEXT E XAMPLES , P ROMPTING , AND F INETUNING
516
517
          Our study reveals that enhancing concept encoding is a unifying principle that improves in-context
          learning (ICL) across different strategies. We observe in Sections 4.2 and 4.3 that increasing in-
518
          context examples and finetuning facilitate building separable representations by their latent con-
519
          cepts. Many have also noted that prompting (Wei et al., 2023a) is a simple and effective method
520       of improving in-context performance. Thus, we experiment with prompting as part of our curiosity.
521       We question whether providing the underlying concept (i.e., including true labels of bitwise arith-
522       metic) indeed enhances concept encoding and, as expected, performance. As shown in Figures 20
523       and 21 of Appendix F, prompting in fact improves the concept encoding and performance simultane-
524       ously. However, we interpret these results with caution, since the model may be capturing spurious
525       correlations from the input prompt differences.
526
527       5.2                   W HY DO MODELS SUCCEED AT SOME ICL TASKS , BUT NOT OTHERS ?
528
529       It is yet puzzling how to categorize the types of ICL tasks LLMs can and cannot perform (Qiu et al.,
530       2023; Dziri et al., 2023). An intuitive explanation is that the model can effectively encode the con-
531
          cepts frequently seen during pretraining (Razeghi et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024). In our experiments,
          we also observe similar patterns where AND and OR were encoded more accurately. However,
532
          we aim to provide an alternative perspective to understand the model’s success and failure modes.
533
          Through the study of the two-stage process, we show that the bottleneck in the model can exist in
534       both levels of concept inference and subsequent decoding algorithm. Therefore, even if the model
535       already learned the algorithm for a NOR operator, if the model cannot clearly distinguish the latent
536       concept from the inputs, it will fail, and vice versa. Perhaps as our experiments suggest, when the
537       model is failing at concept encoding, a different prescription of finetuning only the earlier layers
538       for better representation learning is more beneficial. Ultimately, by detecting the different causes of
539       the failure modes of models, we hope to build more effective, robust strategies to improve them and
          unravel the mysteries of large models via mechanistic understanding.

      C.1   T HEORETICAL E RROR B OUNDS IN SPARSE LINEAR REGRESSIONS
821
822   It is known that transformers can achieve Bayes-optimal solutions for linear regression problems by
823   implementing least-squares solutions on the prior of weight sampling (Garg et al., 2022; Raventós
824   et al., 2024). The least-squares estimation of linear regression with a Gaussian prior for task weights
825   can be performed using ridge regression. In the presence of sparsity, the least-squares solution can
826   be obtained through lasso regression with optimal weight searching. The error bounds of our task
827   depend on whether the underlying basis is discovered by the model. We consider two extreme cases:
828
            1. If the model is incapable of inferring any basis in B, it would perform a D-dimensional
829
               regression with r-sparsity, where D is the total dimension and r is the number of non-zero
830
               elements.
831
832         2. If the model is capable of inferring the basis in B, it can perform an r-dimensional regres-
833
               sion adjusted for the corresponding non-zero elements of the inferred basis. In this case,
               the model could benefit from the tighter r-dimensional regression bound.
834
835   The possibility of diverse algorithms and corresponding error changes enables us to track the
836   Bayesian inference behavior of the model in a more detailed way. In the following results, we indeed
837   observe a transition from D-dimensional regression to r-dimensional regression, accompanied by
838   changes in the representations of tasks for each basis.

842   Mixture of Sparse Linear Regression. We adapt the conventional linear regression setup from
843   Garg et al. (2022); von Oswald et al. (2023) to create latent bases B that we can interpret far more
844   easily than W . We study this setting with D = 16 dimensional with up to K = 20 in-context
845
      examples. Each Bi has a rank of 4 and is orthogonal with each other. We independently sample
      W and xi for each new input sequence from N (0, ID ) the noise ϵ ∼ N (0, 0.01). We add the
846
      sparsity constraints to the linear regression task to introduce the latent concept of sparsity basis B
847
      that is easily interpretable and analyzable in their representations. With the sparsity constraints, we
848   construct the graphical model B → W → Y ← X. This construction allows us to visualize the
849   representations of each of the bases (latent concepts in this graph) by aggregating the representations
850   across a set of W and (X, Y ) pairs.
851
852   Model. We use a 12-layer GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) architecture transformer, as implemented

855
856   Training. We train the model with a batch size of 128 for 80K training steps. We use the Adam

858   MSE loss over the sequence and only compute the losses on the prediction yˆi .
859
860   Evaluation. We construct a test dataset of 1K samples and evaluate the model on MSE loss for
