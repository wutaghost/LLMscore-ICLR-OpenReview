011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly applied to complex reasoning
014            tasks that require executing several complex steps before receiving any reward.
015            Properly assigning credit to these steps is essential for enhancing model perfor-
016
               mance. Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), a state-of-the-art reinforcement
               learning (RL) algorithm used for LLM finetuning, employs value networks to
017
               tackle credit assignment. However, value networks face challenges in predict-
018
               ing the expected cumulative rewards accurately in complex reasoning tasks, often
019            leading to high-variance updates and suboptimal performance. In this work, we
020            systematically evaluate the efficacy of value networks and reveal their significant
021            shortcomings in reasoning-heavy LLM tasks, showing that they barely outperform
022            a random baseline when comparing alternative steps. To address this, we propose
023            VinePPO, a straightforward approach that leverages the flexibility of language
024            environments to compute unbiased Monte Carlo-based estimates, bypassing the
025            need for large value networks. Our method consistently outperforms PPO and
026            other RL-free baselines across MATH and GSM8K datasets with fewer gradient
027
               updates (up to 9x), less wall-clock time (up to 3.0x). These results emphasize the
               importance of accurate credit assignment in RL finetuning of LLM and demon-
028
               strate VinePPO’s potential as a superior alternative.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used for tasks requiring complex reasoning, such as
034
      solving mathematical problems (OpenAI, 2024), navigating the web (Zhou et al., 2024), or editing
035
      large codebases (Jimenez et al., 2024). In these settings, LLMs often engage in extended reason-
036   ing steps, executing multiple actions to arrive at a solution. However, not all steps are equally
037   important—some contribute significantly, while others are irrelevant or detrimental. For example,
038   in Figure 1.a, only step s2 provides a key insight. Indeed, most reasoning steps generated by a
039   model do not affect the chance of it solving the problem (Figure 1.b). Identifying the contribution
040   of each action is crucial for improving model performance. However, this is inherently difficult due
041   to the significant delay between actions and their eventual effect. This issue, known as the credit
042   assignment problem, is a core challenge in reinforcement learning (RL, Sutton and Barto 1998).
043   Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO, Schulman et al. 2017; Ouyang et al. 2022), a state-of-the-art
044   algorithm for RL-based finetuning of LLMs (Xu et al., 2024; Ivison et al., 2024; Chang et al., 2023),
045   tackles credit assignment using a value network (or critic). This network, typically a separate model
046   initialized from a pretrained checkpoint, is trained during PPO finetuning to estimate the expected
047   cumulative rewards (or value) of an intermediate action. In Figure 1.b, an ideal value network
048   would assign high value to step s2 and subsequent steps, where the model predicted a critical action.
049   PPO uses these value estimates to measure the advantage of each action and update the model
050
      accordingly.
051   Accurately modeling value—predicting future rewards from an incomplete response—requires the
052   value network to understand both the space of correct solutions (the very task the policy model is
053   trying to learn) and predict the model’s future behavior, both of which are inherently challenging. In
      fact, there are hints in the literature that standard PPO implementations for LLMs have inaccurate

130   2       R ELATED W ORK
131
132   Credit Assignment in Post-Training of LLM PPO, as applied in RL from Human Feedback
133   (RLHF, Ouyang et al. 2022), pioneered RL finetuning of LLMs. However, its computational over-
134   head and hyperparameter sensitivity led to the development of simpler alternatives. RL-free methods
135
      such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) operate in a bandit setting, treating the entire response as a single
      action. Similarly, rejection sampling methods like RestEM (Singh et al., 2024) finetune on full high-
136
      reward responses. RLOO (Ahmadian et al., 2024) and GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) abandon PPO’s
137
      value network, instead using average reward from multiple samples as a baseline. Recent work has
138   emphasized finer credit assignment, with Hwang et al. (2024) and Setlur et al. (2024) introducing
139   MC-based methods to detect key errors in reasoning chains for use as ad-hoc mechanisms in DPO.
140   Our work, by contrast, fully embraces the RL training, with the target of unlocking PPO’s poten-
141   tial. Parallel efforts have also focused on building better verifiers and reward models for per-step
142   feedback, with recent attempts to automate their data collection using MC rollouts (Ma et al., 2023;
143   Uesato et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Our method is orthogonal to these methods,
144   operating within PPO-based training to optimize a given reward, instead of designing new ones.
145
146   Value Estimation in RL and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) Deep RL algorithms are typi-
147   cally categorized into value-based and policy-based methods. Policy-based methods like PPO usu-
148   ally employ critic networks for value prediction. An exception is the “Vine” variant of TRPO
149   (Schulman et al., 2015), which uses MC samples for state value estimation. The authors, however,
150
      note that the Vine variant is limited to environments that allow intermediate state resets, rare in
      typical RL settings2 . However, language generation – when formulated as RL environment – en-
151
      ables such intermediate reset capabilities. In domains with similar reset capabilities, such as Go and
152
      Chess, MC-heavy methods like AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016) and AlphaZero (Silver et al., 2017)
153   have emerged. AlphaGo’s architecture includes a policy, trained using expert moves and self-play,
154   and a value network that predicts game outcomes. At inference, it employs tree search guided by MC
155   rollouts and value network to select optimal moves. AlphaZero advances this approach by distilling
156   MCTS outcomes into the policy. Recent works have adapted AlphaZero’s principles to LLMs, em-
157   ploying similar search techniques during inference to improve responses and during training to find
158   better trajectories for distillation (Xie et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
159   2024; Hao et al., 2023). While this is a promising direction, our method is not an MCTS approach;
160   it uses MC samples solely for value estimation during PPO training to improve credit assignment.

184   3              BACKGROUND
185
186   We focus on the RL tuning phase in the RLHF pipeline, following Ouyang et al. (2022); Shao et al.
187   (2024). In this section, we provide an overview of actor-critic finetuning as implemented in PPO.
188
189   RL Finetuning In this setup, the policy πθ represents a language model that generates a response
190   y = [y0 , . . . , yT −1 ] autoregressively given an input x = [x0 , . . . , xM −1 ]. The goal of RL finetuning
191   is to maximize the expected undiscounted (γ = 1) finite-horizon return, while incorporating a KL-
192   divergence constraint to regularize the policy and prevent it from deviating too far from a reference
193
      policy πref (typically the initial supervised finetuned, SFT, model). The objective can be written as:
194                                 J(θ) = Ex∼D,y∼π(·|x) [R(x; y)] − β KL[πθ ∥πref ],                            (1)
195   where D is the dataset of prompts, R(x; y) is the complete sequence-level reward function, and β
196   controls the strength of the KL penalty. Note that the policy πθ is initialized from πref .
197
198   Language Environment as an MDP Language generation is typically modeled as a token-level
199   Markov Decision Process (MDP) in an actor-critic setting, where each response y is an episode.
200   The state at time step t, st ∈ S, is the concatenation of the input prompt and the tokens generated
201   up to that point: st = x; y<t = [x0 , . . . , xM −1 , y0 , . . . , yt−1 ]. At each time step, the action at cor-
202   responds to generating the next token yt from fixed vocabulary. The process begins with the initial
203
      state s0 = x, and after each action, the environment transitions to the next state, st+1 = st ; [at ],
      by appending the action at to the current state st . In this case, since states are always constructed
204
      by concatenating tokens, the environment dynamics are known and the transition function is de-
205
      terministic, i.e., P (st+1 |st , at ) = 1. During the generation process, the reward rt is set to zero
206   for all intermediate actions at ’s, with the sequence-level reward R(x; y) only applied at the final
207   step when the model stops generating. A trajectory τ = (s0 , a0 , s1 , a1 , . . . ) is therefore a sequence
208   of state-action pairs, starting from the input prompt until the terminal state. Finally, we define the
                                                        PT −1
209   cumulative return of a trajectory τ as R(τ ) = t=0 rt = rT −1 = R(x; y).
210

462   7                 VALUE P REDICTION A NALYSIS
463
464   In this section, we explore the underlying reasons for the performance gap between PPO and
465   VinePPO by closely analyzing the value prediction of both methods. First, we establish a “ground
466   truth” value at each reasoning step within trajectories by running many MC samples (256 in our
467   case) and averaging the returns. This provides a low-variance reference value. We then compare the
468   value predictions in both methods against this ground truth. We present the results for DeepSeek-
469   Math 7B on the MATH dataset (full analysis with other models and datasets in Appendix D.2).
470
471
      Accuracy Figure 9 presents the distribution of value predictions at each reasoning step. The er-
472   rors produced by VinePPO and PPO differ significantly. VinePPO’s estimates are unbiased, with
473   variance peaking at 0.5 and dropping to zero at 0 and 1. PPO’s value network shows high bias, often
474   misclassifying bad states (ground truth near 0) as good and vice versa. To further visualize accuracy,
475   we classify a value prediction as “correct” if it falls within 0.05 of the ground truth. The accuracy of
476   this formulation is shown in Figure 11.a. PPO’s value network starts with low accuracy, gradually
477   improving to 65%. VinePPO, however, consistently achieves 70-90% accuracy throughout training.
478
479
      Top Action Identification In value-based RL, ranking actions correctly is more crucial than ab-
480   solute value accuracy. While PPO, as a policy gradient method, requires accurate value estimates
481   to compute meaningful advantages, it is still a compelling question whether PPO’s value network,
482   despite its bias, can maintain correct action ranking. To investigate, we sample five new next steps
483   from the same initial state and evaluate if the method correctly identifies the resulting next state
484   with the highest ground truth value. As shown in Figure 11.b, PPO’s value network performs near
485   chance levels for much of the training, with slight improvements over time. In contrast, VinePPO
      consistently identifies the top action with high accuracy throughout training.

      8              D ISCUSSION
523
524
      Accurate credit assignment has profound implications on the performance of RL tuning of LLMs.
      As we’ve demonstrated, standard PPO, despite outperforming most RL-free baselines, suffers from
525
      suboptimal value estimation. More importantly, its scaling behavior is concerning; PPO struggles
526
      with increasingly diverse trajectories and tends to perform worse as tasks become more complex.
527
528   VinePPO, on the other hand, is a viable alternative. As shown in Section 6.4, it offers lowered mem-
529   ory requirements and better performance with the same computational budget. VinePPO could also
530
      be a particularly attractive option for frontier LLMs as even doubling the post-training compute is
      negligible compared to their pre-training costs (Ouyang et al., 2022)6 . Given the major investments
531
      in pre-training compute and data collection of these models, it is imperative for model developers
532
      to employ post-training methods that provide more accurate updates, avoiding the high-variance
533   adjustments caused by inferior credit assignment. Additionally, VinePPO offers a straightforward
534   scaling axis: increasing the number of MC samples directly enhances performance with additional
535   compute. Unlike recent approaches that focus on increasing inference-time compute to boost per-
536   formance (OpenAI, 2024; Bansal et al., 2024), VinePPO’s training compute is amortized over all
537   future inferences. Note that the computational workload of VinePPO is highly parallelizable with
538   linear scalability, making it well-suited for large-scale training.
539
                 6
                     For example, InstructGPT used nearly 60 times more compute for pre-training (Ouyang et al., 2022).

      The unique properties of the language environment are what enabled VinePPO to be viable credit
541   assignment option; it may have limited practical use in traditional deep RL policy gradient methods.
542   This suggests that adapting RL techniques to LLMs requires careful consideration and perhaps a
543   reevaluation of underlying assumptions. Overall, our work highlights the potential of well-tuned RL
544   finetuning strategies with proper credit assignment, and we hope it encourages further research into
545   optimizing RL post-training pipelines for LLMs.

      A     R EVIEWING PPO
757
758   PPO, as used in RL tuning of LLMs, formulates language generation as token-level MDP (Sec-
759   tion 3), where each response y is an episode. The state at time step t, st ∈ S, is the concatenation
760   of the prompt and the tokens generated so far: st = x; y<t = [x0 , . . . , xM −1 , y0 , . . . , yt−1 ]. The
761   action at corresponds to generating the next token yt from the model’s vocabulary. Given a prompt
762   x, an episode of this MDP starts from the initial state s0 = x, and with each action taken, the
763   environment moves to a subsequent state, st+1 = st ; [at ], by adding the action at to the existing
764   state st . In the language environment, because states are always formed by concatenating tokens,
765   the environment dynamics are fully known, and the transition function is deterministic, meaning
766
      P (st+1 |st , at ) = 1. Throughout the generation process, the reward rt is set to zero for all inter-
      mediate actions at , with the sequence-level reward R(x; y) applied only at the final step when the
767
      model stops the generation. That is:

       C.4    E VALUATION
1027
1028   We evaluate each method’s performance on the test sets of each dataset. For example, when we
1029   report that PPO achieves 42.8% accuracy on the MATH dataset for the DeepSeekMath 7B model,
1030   this means the PPO training was initialized with the SFT model specific to DeepSeekMath 7B on the
1031   MATH dataset, and accuracy was measured on the MATH test set. Our primary evaluation metric is
1032   accuracy, specifically Pass@1, which reflects the percentage of correctly answered problems on the
1033   first attempt. This metric is crucial because it represents a realistic user interaction, where the model
1034
       is expected to deliver a correct answer without the need for multiple tries. For each evaluation, we
       sample a response from the model for a given prompt, using a maximum token length of 1024 and
1035
       a temperature of 0.35. A response is considered correct if its final answer matches the ground truth
1036
       final answer, as detailed in Appendix C.1. Furthermore, each accuracy score is averaged over 16
1037   evaluation rounds, each conducted with different random seeds. This will ensure a robust and low
1038   variance assessment of model performance.

1042   DPO+ (DPO-Positive) (Pal et al., 2024) The original DPO method has a failure mode when the edit
1043   distance between positive (correct) and negative (incorrect) responses is small. In these cases, the
1044   probability of both responses tends to decrease. This issue is especially common in reasoning and
1045   mathematical tasks, where multiple solution paths may involve similar equations or steps. Although
1046
       DPO achieves its goal by reducing the probability of the incorrect response more than the correct
       one, it ultimately still lowers the likelihood of generating the correct response. This undermines
1047
       model performance, making it a failure mode despite partially fulfilling the DPO objective. (Pal
1048

1049   edit distance between positive and negative response pairs, DPO-Positive (Pal et al., 2024) addresses
1050   it more effectively. It introduces an additional term to the DPO objective, penalizing any reduction in
1051   the probability of the correct response below its probability under the reference model. This ensures
1052   that the correct response is not overly suppressed, even when the edit distance is small. The final

1066
1067   RestEM (Singh et al., 2024) RestEM is an iterative method where, in each iteration, the base model
1068
       is trained on correct, self-generated responses from the chosen checkpoint of the previous iteration.
       RestEM takes gradient steps to maximize this objective until the fine-tuned model’s accuracy drops
1069
       on a validation split. The objective of the fine-tuning process is to maximize the log-likelihood of
1070
       correct responses. Training the model with a maximum likelihood objective on correct responses is
1071   mathematically equivalent to training the model with REINFORCE (Sutton et al., 1999), without a
1072   baseline, where the entire response is treated as a single action. The reward is 1 when the response
1073   is correct, and 0 otherwise. Specifically, we have:
1074
             Ex∼D,y∼π(·|x),R(x;y)=1 [∇θ log Pθ (y|x)] = Ex∼D,y∼π(·|x) [∇θ log Pθ (y|x)R(x; y)]                               (19)
1075         |                 {z                   } |                  {z                  }
1076               max log-likelihood on correct responses                                  REINFORCE

1077   Therefore, maximizing log-likelihood training on correct responses is equivalent to train with policy
1078   gradient without precise credit assignment, such as without advantages for specific actions. In our
1079   experiments, we observe the impact of this limitation in both Figure C.3 and Figure C.4 where
       RestEM overfits on the training data.

1210
       Both PPO and VinePPOrequire a robust and efficient implementation. For model implementation,
1211
       we utilize the Huggingface library. Training is carried out using the DeepSpeed distributed training
1212   library, which offers efficient multi-GPU support. Specifically, we employ DeepSpeed ZeRO stage 0

1214   across GPUs) for DeepSeekMath 7B . For trajectory sampling during RL training, we rely on the
1215   vLLM library (Kwon et al., 2023), which provides optimized inference for LLMs. Additionally,
1216   VinePPOleverages vLLM to generate Monte Carlo samples for value estimation. This software
1217   stack ensures that our experiments are both efficient and reproducible. For instance, during VinePPO
1218   training, we achieve an inference speed of up to 30K tokens per second using 8 × Nvidia H100 GPUs

2017   I    D IFFERENCE B ETWEEN B IAS IN VALUE E STIMATIONS AND IN P OLICY
2018        G RADIENT
2019
2020   Note that when λ = 1, the value estimates are used solely as a baseline. It is well-known that, in
2021   this case, the policy gradient Eq 2 provides an unbiased estimate of the true values. However, it is
2022   important to emphasize that the value estimates themselves can still be biased. Consequently, the
2023
       fact that the policy gradient is unbiased does not guarantee that the value estimates used to compute
       the advantages are unbiased estimators of the true value of a given state.
2024
2025
2026   J     U PDATED “C.9 S OFTWARE S TACK ” SECTION
2027
2028   Both PPO and VinePPOrequire a robust and efficient implementation. For model implementation,
2029   we utilize the Huggingface library. Training is carried out using the DeepSpeed distributed training
2030   library, which offers efficient multi-GPU support. Specifically, we employ DeepSpeed ZeRO stage
2031   0 (vanilla data parallelism) for RhoMath 1.1B and ZeRO stage 2 (shared optimizer states and gra-
2032
       dients across GPUs) for DeepSeekMath 7B . For trajectory sampling during RL training, we rely
       on the vLLM library (Kwon et al., 2023), which provides optimized inference for LLMs. Addition-
2033
       ally, VinePPOleverages vLLM to generate Monte Carlo samples for value estimation. Specifically,
2034
       after each RL training iteration, the current policy’s checkpoint is loaded into vLLM. Then, we use
2035   vLLM’s serving API to sample new trajectories and also Monte Carlo Samples for VinePPO’s value
2036   estimation. In our setup, we spawn a separate vLLM engine on each GPU rank. This would al-
2037   low for data parallelism during both sample generation and training. This software stack ensures
2038   that our experiments are both efficient and reproducible. For instance, during VinePPO training, we
2039   achieve an inference speed of up to 30K tokens per second using 8 × Nvidia H100 GPUs with the
2040   DeepSeekMath 7B model.
