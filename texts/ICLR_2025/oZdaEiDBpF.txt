009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-Instance Partial Label Learning (MI-PLL) is a weakly-supervised learn-
012            ing setting encompassing partial label learning, latent structural learning, and
013            neurosymbolic learning. Unlike supervised learning, in MI-PLL, the inputs to
014            the classifiers at training-time are tuples of instances x. At the same time, the
015            supervision signal is generated by a function σ over the (hidden) gold labels of
016            x. In this work, we make multiple contributions towards addressing a problem
017
               that hasn’t been studied so far in the context of MI-PLL: that of characterizing
               and mitigating learning imbalances, i.e., major differences in the errors occurring
018
               when classifying instances of different classes (aka class-specific risks). In terms
019
               of theory, we derive class-specific risk bounds for MI-PLL, while making minimal
020            assumptions. Our theory reveals a unique phenomenon: that σ can greatly impact
021            learning imbalances. This result is in sharp contrast with previous research on su-
022            pervised and weakly-supervised learning, which only studies learning imbalances
023            under the prism of data imbalances. On the practical side, we introduce a technique
024            for estimating the marginal of the hidden labels using only MI-PLL data. Then,
025            we introduce algorithms that mitigate imbalances at training- and testing-time, by
026            treating the marginal of the hidden labels as a constraint. We demonstrate the
027            effectiveness of our techniques using strong baselines from neurosymbolic and
028
               long-tail learning, suggesting performance improvements of up to 14%.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The need to reduce labeling costs motivates the study of weakly-supervised learning settings (Zhou,
033   2017; Zhang et al., 2022). Our work aligns with this objective, focusing on multi-instance partial
034   label learning (MI-PLL) (Wang et al., 2023b). MI-PLL is particularly appealing, as it encompasses
035   three well-known learning settings: partial label learning (PLL) (Cour et al., 2011; Cabannes et al.,
036   2020; Lv et al., 2020; Seo & Huh, 2021; Wen et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Wang
037   et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2023), where each training instance is associated with a set of candidate
038   labels, latent structural learning (Steinhardt & Liang, 2015; Raghunathan et al., 2016; Zhang et al.,
039   2020), i.e., learning classifiers subject to a transition function σ that constraints their outputs, and
040
      neurosymbolic learning (Manhaeve et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019b; Dai et al., 2019; Tsamoura
      et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023a), i.e., training neural classifiers subject to symbolic
041
      background knowledge. An example (adapted from (Manhaeve et al., 2018)) is illustrated below:
042
043   Example 1.1 (MI-PLL example). We aim to learn an MNIST classifier f , using only samples of
044
      the form (x1 , x2 , s), where x1 and x2 are MNIST digits and s is the maximum of their gold labels,
      i.e., s = σ(y1 , y2 ) = max{y1 , y2 } with yi being the label of xi . The gold labels are hidden during
045
      training. We will refer to the yi ’s and s as hidden and partial labels, respectively.
046
047   MI-PLL has been a topic of active research in NLP (Steinhardt & Liang, 2015; Raghunathan et al.,
048   2016; Peng et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019a; Gupta et al., 2021). Recently, it has received renewed
049   attention in neurosymbolic learning, as it offers multiple benefits over architectures that approximate
050   the neural classifiers and σ via end-to-end neural models, such as (i) the ability to reuse the latent
051   models (Peng et al., 2018; Mihaylova et al., 2020), (ii) higher accuracy (Wu, 2022; Huang et al.,
052   2021), and (iii) higher explainability and generalizability. Practical applications of MI-PLL in the
053
      neurosymbolic learning literature include visual question answering (Huang et al., 2021), video-text
      retrieval (Li et al., 2023b), and fine-tuning language models (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024).

072   On the practical side, mitigating learning imbalances has received considerable attention in supervised

078   the hidden labels. While approximating r may be easy in supervised learning (Menon et al., 2021)
079   as the gold labels are available, in our setting the gold labels are hidden from the learner. Second,
080   the state-of-the-art for training-time mitigation (Wang et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2019; Tan et al., 2020;
081

082
      correlations among the instances. The above gives rise to a second challenge: developing techniques
083
      for mitigating learning imbalances in MI-PLL.
084
085   Contributions. We start by providing class-specific error bounds in the context of MI-PLL. Comple-
086   mentary to previous work in supervised learning (Cao et al., 2019) and standard single-instance PLL
087
      (Cour et al., 2011), our theory shows that σ can have a significant impact on learning imbalances,
      see Theorem 3.1. Our analysis extends the theoretical analysis in (Wang et al., 2023b), by provid-
088
      ing stricter risk bounds for the underlying classifiers, making also minimal assumptions, and the
089
      theoretical analysis in (Cour et al., 2011) that provides class-specific error bounds for standard PLL.
090
091   On the practical side, we first propose a statistically consistent technique for estimating the marginal
092   of the hidden labels given partial labels. We further propose two algorithms that mitigate imbalances
093
      at training- and testing-time. The first algorithm assigns pseudo-labels to training data based on
      a novel linear programming formulation of MI-PLL, see Section 4.2. The second algorithm uses
094
      the hidden label marginals to constrain the model’s prediction on testing data, using a robust semi-
095
      constrained optimal transport (RSOT) formulation (Le et al., 2021), see Section 4.3. Our empirical
096   analysis shows that our techniques can improve the accuracy over strong baselines in neurosymbolic
097   learning (Xu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2023b) and long-tail learning (Menon et al., 2021; Hong et al.,
098   2023) by up to 14%, manifesting that the straightforward application of state-of-the-art to MI-PLL
099   settings is either impossible (Wang et al., 2022) or problematic (Hong et al., 2023).

236   4     A LGORITHMS : MITIGATING IMBALANCES IN MI-PLL
237
238   Section 3 sends a clear message: MI-PLL is prone to learning imbalances that may be exacerbated
239   due to σ. We now propose a portfolio of techniques for addressing learning imbalances. Our first
240
      contribution, see Section 4.1, is a statistically consistent technique for estimating r, assuming access
      to partial labels only. We then move to training-time mitigation, see Section 4.2 and testing-time
241
      mitigation, see Section 4.3. Our marginal estimation algorithm requires only the i.i.d. assumption;
242
      the algorithms in Section 4.2 and 4.3 work even when the i.i.d. assumption fails. Our mitigation
243   algorithms enforce the class priors to a classifier’s predictions. This is a common idea in long-tail
244   learning. The intuition is that the classifier will tend to predict the labels that appear more often in the
245   training data. Enforcing the priors, gives more importance to the minority classes at training-time (see
246   Section 4.2) and encourages the model to predict minority classes at testing-time (see Section 4.3).
247

      Training- vs testing-time mitigation. CAROT is a more lightweight technique, relying on the
487   polynomial complexity, semi-Sinkhorn algorithm (Le et al., 2021). However, as the empirical results
488   suggest, CAROT may lead to lower classification accuracy in comparison to LP. On the contrary,
489   LP may increase the training overhead over the state-of-the-art– that is applying the top-k SL per
490   training sample (Xu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2023b). This is because when k is fixed, the complexity
491   to compute the SL is polynomial; in contrast, solving (6), which is a linear program calculated out of
492   a batch of samples, is an NP-hard problem. When the SL runs without approximations though and
493   the pre-image of σ is very large, the complexity of SL is worst case #P-complete per training sample
494   (Chavira & Darwiche, 2008), making (6) a more computationally efficient approach.

      6    R ELATED WORK
497   An extended version and more detailed comparison against the related work is in Appendix E.
498
      Long-tail supervised learning. Two supervised learning techniques related to our work are
499   LA (Menon et al., 2021) and OTLM (Peng et al., 2022). Both aim at testing-time mitigation.
500   LA modifies the classifier’s scores by subtracting the gold ratios. CAROT can be substantially more
501   effective than LA, see Section 5. OTLM assumes that the marginal r is known, resorting to an OT
502   formulation for adjusting the classifier’s scores. In contrast, we propose a statistically consistent
503   technique to estimate r, see Section 4.1, and resort to RSOT to accommodate for noisy b  r’s.
504
      Long-tail PLL. The authors in (Cour et al., 2011) showed that certain classes are harder to learn than
505   others in standard PLL. We are the first to extend those results under MI-PLL. The only two works in
506   the intersection of long-tail learning and (single-instance) PLL are RECORDS (Hong et al., 2023)
507   and S O L AR (Wang et al., 2022). RECORDS modifies the classifier’s scores using the same idea with
508   LA. It employs a momentum-updated prototype feature to estimate b      r. Unlike LP, RECORDS does
509   not take into account the constraints coming from MI-PLL. Section 5 shows that RECORDS is less
510   effective than our proposals, degrading the baseline accuracy on multiple occasions. S O L AR relies
511   on standard OT to assign pseudo-labels to instances, in contrast to our formulation in (6). Also,
512   S O L AR uses an averaging technique to estimate r, as opposed to Algorithm 1.
513   MI-PLL. We close with some recent theoretical results on MI-PLL. The authors in (Marconato et al.,
514   2023; 2024) characterize reasoning shortcuts in MI-PLL. In contrast, our work provides class-specific
515   error bounds, formally characterizing learning imbalances in MI-PLL. It is worth noting that the
516   authors in (Tang et al., 2024a;b) use the term multi-instance partial-label learning to describe their
517   learning setting. The differences with ours (see Section 2) are as follows. First, the objective in
518   (Tang et al., 2024a;b) is to learn a bag classifier, i.e., a classifier f : 2X → Y, and not an instance
519
      classifier. Second, unlike our setting, in (Tang et al., 2024a;b), the training samples are of the form
      (X, S), where X is a bag of instances and S is a bag of labels for the whole X. Due to the above
520
      differences, the formulation in (Tang et al., 2024a;b) cannot capture the neurosymbolic learning
521
      setting in (Manhaeve et al., 2018; Dai et al., 2019; Tsamoura et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023a).

525
      Comments on the theory. Our analysis in Section 3 assumes that the probability of misclassifying
526
      an instance x only dependents on its class. This assumption is also adopted in other learning settings,
527
      such as noisy label learning (Zhang et al., 2021; Patrini et al., 2017). Although there are more complex
528   scenarios where this assumption does not hold, our theory stands as an over-approximation to those
529   scenarios, similarly to the connection between class- and instance-dependent noisy label learning.
530   Furthermore, our formulation in (3) can be extended to cases where the correlations among the
531   instances (x1 , . . . , xM ) of each training sample are weak, i.e., have very few correlations. Extending
532   our analysis in the general non-i.i.d. setting is an important direction for future research.
533
      Our work is the first to theoretically characterize and mitigate learning imbalances in MI-PLL. Our
534   theoretical characterization complements the existing theory in long-tail learning, identifying and
535   addressing the unique challenges in MI-PLL. Additionally, we contributed an LP-based and an RSOT-
536   based mitigation technique that both outperform state-of-the-art in long-tail learning. Our empirical
537   analysis unveiled two topics for future research: computing marginal for testing-time mitigation and
538   designing more effective testing-time mitigation techniques. Another important future direction is
539   to look into scalability, as for scenarios with a large number of classes, it may be computationally
      expensive to run Algorithm 1 or Algorithm 2.

      B.2    F URTHER DISCUSSION ON OUR BOUNDS
901
902   Intuitively, the difficulty of learning is affected by (i) the distribution of partial labels in DP and (ii)
903   the size of the pre-image of σ for each partial label. These two factor are reflected in our risk-specific
904   bounds. Let us continue with the analysis in Example 3.2.
905
      Example B.1 (Cont’ Example 3.2). Let us start with C ASE 1. In this case, our class-specific bounds
906   suggest that learning the class zero is more difficult than learning class nine despite that both hidden
907   labels y1 and y2 are uniform in {0, . . . , 9}, see left side of Figure B.2. The root cause of this learning
908   imbalance is σ and its characteristics. In particular, the partial labels that result after independently
909   drawing pairs of MNIST digits and applying σ on their gold labels are long-tailed, with s = 0
910   occurring with probability 1/100 and s = 9 occurring with probability 17/100 in the training data.
911   Hence, we have more supervision to learn class nine than to learn zero.
912   Now, let us move to C ASE 2. In this case, our class-specific bounds suggest that learning class
913   zero is the easiest to learn, see right side of Figure B.2. This is because of two reasons. First, the
914   partial labels are uniform and hence, we have the same supervision to learn all classes. Second, the
915   pre-image of σ for different partial labels is very different. Regarding the second reason, partial
916   label s = 0 provides much stronger supervision than partial label s = 9: when s = 0, we have direct
917   supervision (s = 0 implies y1 = y2 = 0); in contrast, when s = 9 this only means that either y1 = 9
      and y2 is any label in {0, . . . , 9}, or vice versa.

1104   E    E XTENDED RELATED WORK
1105
1106   Long-tail learning. The term long-tail learning has been used to describe settings in which instances
1107
       of some classes occur very frequently in the training set, with other classes being underrepresented.
       The problem has received considerable attention in the context of supervised learning with the
1108
       proposed techniques operating either at training- or at testing-time. Techniques in the former category
1109
       typically work by either reweighting the losses computed out of the original training samples (Cao
1110   et al., 2019; Tan et al., 2020; 2021) or by over- or under-sampling during training (Chawla et al.,
1111   2002; Buda et al., 2018). Techniques in the latter category work by modifying the classifier’s output
1112   scores at testing-time and using the modified scores for classification (Kang et al., 2020; Peng et al.,
1113   2022), with LA being one of the most well-known techniques (Menon et al., 2021). LA modifies the
1114   classifier’s scores at testing-time by subtracting the (unknown) gold ratios. In particular, the prediction
1115   of classifier f given input x is given by arg maxj∈[c] f j (x) − ln(rj ). Our empirical analysis shows
1116   that CAROT is more effective than LA.
1117   Closest to our work is the study in (Peng et al., 2022). Unlike CAROT, the authors in (Peng et al.,
1118   2022) focus on single-instance PLL, assume that the marginal r is known, and use an optimal transport
1119   formulation (Peyré & Cuturi, 2020) to adjust the classifier’s scores. In contrast, CAROT relies on
1120   the assumption that br may be noisy, resorting to a robust optimal transport formulation (Le et al.,
1121   2021) to improve the classification accuracy in those cases.
1122   Partial Label Learning. As discussed in (Wang et al., 2023b), MI-PLL is an extension to standard
1123   (single-instance) PLL (Cour et al., 2011; Lv et al., 2020; Feng et al., 2020). The observation that
1124   certain classes are harder to learn than others dates back to the work of (Cour et al., 2011) in the
1125   context of PLL. We are the first to provide such results for MI-PLL, unveiling also the relationship
1126   between σ and class-specific risks.
1127
       Long-tail PLL. A few recently proposed papers lie in the intersection of long-tail learning and
1128   standard PLL, namely (Liu et al., 2021), RECORDS (Hong et al., 2023) and S O L AR (Wang et al.,
1129   2022), with the first one focusing on non-deep learning settings. RECORDS modifies the classifier’s
1130   scores following the same basic idea with LA and uses the modified scores for training. However, it
1131   employs a momentum-updated prototype feature to estimate b    r. RECORDS’s design allows it to be
1132   used with any loss function and to be trivially extended to support MI-PLL. Our empirical analysis
1133   shows that RECORDS is less effective than CAROT, leading to lower classification accuracy when
       the same loss is adopted during training.

1160   Constrained learning. MI-PLL is closely related to constrained learning, in the sense that the
1161   predicted label vector y is subject to constraint σ(y) = s. Training classifiers under constraints
1162
       has been well studied in NLP (Steinhardt & Liang, 2015; Raghunathan et al., 2016; Peng et al.,
       2018; Mihaylova et al., 2020; Upadhyay et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2019a; Gupta et al., 2021). The
1163

1164
       et al., 2012) proposes a Unified Expectation Maximization (UEM) framework that unifies several
1165   constrained learning techniques including CoDL (Chang et al., 2007) and Posterior Regularization
1166   (Ganchev et al., 2010). In particular, (Mayhew et al., 2019) employs a conceptually similar idea by
1167   encoding prior information of the label frequency with a CoDL formulation to enhance partial label
1168   learning for the Named Entity Recognition (NER) task. The UEM framework was also adopted by
1169   (Li et al., 2023a) for neurosymbolic learning. Our LP formulation is orthogonal to the UEM. These
1170   two could be integrated though.
1171   The theoretical framework for constrained learning in (Wang et al., 2023a) provides a generalization
1172   theory. The framework suggests that encoding the constraint during both the training and testing
1173   stages results in a better model compared to encoding it only during testing. This theory could be
1174   potentially extended to explain the advantage of LP-based methods and to characterize the necessary
1175   conditions for CAROT to improve model performance.

1218   Why using SL and Scallop. SL (Xu et al., 2018; Manhaeve et al., 2021a) has become the state-of-the-
1219   art approach to train deep classifiers in neurosymbolic learning settings. Training under SL requires
1220   computing a Boolean formula ϕ encoding all the possible label vectors in σ −1 (s) for each partial
1221   training sample (x, s) and then computing the weighted model counting (Chavira & Darwiche, 2008)
1222   of ϕ given the softmax scores of f . SL has been effective in several tasks, including visual question
1223   answering (Huang et al., 2021), video-text retrieval (Li et al., 2023b), and fine-tuning language
1224   models (Li et al., 2024) and has nice theoretical properties (Wang et al., 2023b; Marconato et al.,
1225   2023). Due to its effectiveness, SL is now adopted by several neurosymbolic engines, DeepProbLog
1226
       (Manhaeve et al., 2021a), namely, DeepProbLog’s successors Manhaeve et al. (2021b), and Scallop
       (Huang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023b).
1227
1228   In our empirical analysis we only use Scallop because it is the only engine at the moment offering
1229   a scalable SL implementation that can support our scenarios when M ≥ 3. The requirement to
1230   compute σ −1 (s) during training. Computing σ −1 (s) is generally required by neurosymbolic learning
1231   techniques (Li et al., 2023a; Manhaeve et al., 2021a; Dai et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2020). This
1232
       computation can become a bottleneck when the space of candidate label vectors grows exponentially,
       as it is the case in our MAX-M , SUM-M , and HWF-W scenarios. As also experimentally shown by
1233
       (Tsamoura et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b), the neurosymbolic techniques from (Manhaeve et al.,
1234

1262   Data generation. To create datasets for MAX-M , Smallest Parent, SUM-M , and HWF-M we
1263   adopted the approach followed in prior work, e.g., (Dai et al., 2019; Tsamoura et al., 2021; Wang
1264
       et al., 2023b). In particular, to create each training sample, we draw instances x1 , . . . , xM from
       MNIST or CIFAR-10 in an independent fashion. Then, we apply the transition σ over the gold labels
1265
       y1 , . . . , yM to obtain the partial label s. To create datasets for HWF-M , we followed similar steps
1266
       to the above, however, to make sure that the input vectors of images represent a valid mathematical
1267   expression, we split the training instances into operators and digits, drawing instances of digits for
1268   odd is and instances of operators for even is, for i ∈ [M ]. Before dataset creation, we the images
1269   in HWF were split into training and testing ones with ratio 70%/30%, as the benchmark was not
1270   offering those splits. As we state in Section 5, to simulate long-tail phenomena (denoted as LT), we
1271   vary the imbalance ratio ρ of the distributions of the input instances as in (Cao et al., 2019; Wang
1272   et al., 2022): ρ = 0 means that the hidden label distribution is unmodified and balanced. In each
1273   scenario, the test data follows the same distribution as the hidden labels in the training MI-PLL data,
1274   e.g., when ρ = 0, the test data is balanced; otherwise, it is imbalanced under the same ρ.
1275   Further details. For the Smallest Parent scenarios, we computed SL and (6) using the whole pre-
1276   image of each partial label. For the MAX-M scenarios, as the space of pre-images is very large, we
1277   only consider the top-1 proof (Wang et al., 2023b) both when running Scallop and in (6). For the
1278   Smallest Parent benchmark, we created the hierarchical relations shown in Listing 1 based on the
1279   classes of CIFAR-10.

       Conclusions. The conclusions that we can draw from Table 3, 4, 5 and Figure 7 are very similar to the
1297   ones that were drawn in the main body of our paper. When LP is adopted jointly with the estimates
1298   obtained via Algorithm 1, we can see that the accuracy improvements are substantial on multiple
1299   occasions. For example, in SUM-6 with ρ = 50, the accuracy increases from 67% under SL to 80%
1300   under LP(ALG1); in HWF-7 with ρ = 15, the accuracy increases from 37% under SL to 41% under
1301   LP(ALG1). The accuracy under LP(EMP) is lower than the accuracy under LP(ALG1) in SUM-M .
1302   We argue that this is because of the low quality of the empirical estimates of r, a phenomenon that
1303   gets magnified due to the adopted approximations– recall that we run for SL and LP using the top-1
1304   proofs, in order to make the computation tractable. The lower accuracy of LP(ALG1) for SUM-7
1305
       and ρ = 70 is attributed to the fact that the marginal estimates computed by Algorithm 1 diverge from
       the gold ones, see Figure 7. In fact, computing marginals for this scenario is particularly challenging
1306
       due to the very large pre-image of σ when M = 7, the high imbalance ratio (ρ = 70), and the small
1307
       number of partial samples (mP = 2000). Tables 4 and 5 also suggest that S O L AR’s empirical ratio
1308   estimation technique may harm LP’s accuracy, supporting a claim that we also made in the main
1309   body of the paper, that computing marginals for training-time mitigation is an important direction for
1310   future research.
