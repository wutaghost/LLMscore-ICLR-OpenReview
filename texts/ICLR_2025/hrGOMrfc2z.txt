009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent progress on large language models (LLMs) has enabled dialogue agents to
012            generate highly naturalistic and plausible text. However, current LLM language
013            generation focuses on responding accurately to questions and requests with a single
014            effective response. In reality, many real dialogues are interactive, meaning an
015            agent’s utterances will influence their conversational partner, elicit information, or
016            change their opinion. Accounting for how an agent can effectively steer a conver-
017            sation is a crucial ability in many dialogue tasks, from healthcare to preference
018
               elicitation. Existing methods for fine-tuning dialogue agents to accomplish such
               tasks would rely on curating some amount of expert data. However, doing so often
019
               requires understanding the underlying cognitive processes of the conversational
020
               partner, which is a skill neither humans nor LLMs trained on human data can
021            reliably do. Our key insight is that while LLMs may not be adept at identifying
022            effective strategies for steering conversations a priori, or in the middle of an ongo-
023            ing conversation, they can do so post-hoc, or in hindsight, after seeing how their
024            conversational partner responds. We use this fact to rewrite and augment existing
025            suboptimal data, and train via offline reinforcement learning (RL) an agent that
026            outperforms both prompting and learning from unaltered human demonstrations.
027            We apply our approach to two domains that require understanding human mental
028            state, intelligent interaction, and persuasion: mental health support, and soliciting
029
               charitable donations. Our results in a user study with real humans show that our
               approach greatly outperforms existing state-of-the-art dialogue agents.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large language models (LLMs) are very effective at performing a variety of real-world language tasks,
035
      including open-ended question-answering (Pyatkin et al., 2022), summarization (Paulus et al., 2017;
036   Wu & Hu, 2018; Böhm et al., 2019), code generation (Chen et al., 2021; Rozière et al., 2023; Zhong
037   & Wang, 2023), and general problem-solving (Wei et al., 2023). While LLMs shine at producing
038   compelling and accurate responses to individual queries, their ability to engage in interactive dialogue
039   tasks remains limited. This is because dialogue with humans requires both communication and
040   interaction. A capable dialogue agent should be able to not only process long contexts to craft
041   relevant responses, but also understand how their responses influence their human conversational
042   partner, and guide the conversation toward a desired outcome.
043   For example, in tasks requiring teaching, negotiation, or persuasion, the agent must effectively
044   model and steer the mindset or opinions of the interlocutors in order to accomplish some overall
045   conversational goal. In the case of persuasion, the agent should not only produce the most persuasive
046   utterance now, but also establish rapport, elicit information, and take other steps that will better
047   position it to make winning arguments later in the dialogue. However, there is both theoretical and
048   empirical evidence that contemporary dialogue agents derived from LLMs are unable to execute
049   such complex strategies by nature of their supervised training (Bubeck et al., 2023; Bachmann &
050
      Nagarajan, 2024), as they are optimized for single-step responses rather than a cohesive set of steps
      towards a long-term goal.
051
052   Reinforcement learning (RL) fine-tuning offers an appealing solution to train effective interactive
053   dialogue agents that can build rapport with, gather information about, and steer the opinions of
      their conversational partner. However, in practice, the logistics of running real-time RL makes such


                                                         1
054
      approaches nontrivial to implement. In this paper, we use the insight that pretrained LLMs already
055   serve as effective “human simulators” to aid in the training of RL agents for interactive dialogue
056   (Park et al., 2023). The simplest way to apply this insight is to simply run RL in “simulation,” using
057   the LLM to simulate a human. However, for complex dialogue tasks, this would still come with major
058   challenges in RL, specifically the need to explore diverse scenarios to identify optimal behaviors.
059
      Offline RL circumvents the need for costly on-
060
      line exploration by learning entirely from a static
061   dataset. However, the effectiveness of offline RL
062   is heavily affected the quality of exploration by
063   the behavior policy (Fu et al., 2020; Gulcehre
064   et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2022); namely, the
065   behavior policy still needs to demonstrate traces
066   of optimal behavior. We circumvent this prob-
067   lem in offline RL by introducing synthetic data
068   generated in hindsight. The key is that good
069
      strategies are easier to identify in hindsight: if
      we have already observed a dialogue (even if it
070
      contains suboptimal behavior), it is easier to ask
071
      a LLM to imagine a more optimal dialogue in
072   hindsight than to discover an optimal strategy
073   through more exploration. Adding such exam-
074   ples results in offline data depicting a variety of
075   conversational strategies with different degrees
076   of optimality, which can then be integrated in
077   offline RL to determine optimal strategies.

087   2    R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Language models. Language models, particularly LLMs, have shown impressive capabilities in
090   text generation (Ghazvininejad et al., 2017; Li et al., 2017; Holtzman et al., 2018; Radford et al.,
091   2019; Yang & Klein, 2021), translation (Gu et al., 2017), question answering (Pyatkin et al., 2022),
092   summarization (Paulus et al., 2017; Wu & Hu, 2018; Böhm et al., 2019), and code generation (Chen
093
      et al., 2021; Zhong & Wang, 2023). However, success at most of these tasks is largely enabled by
      supervised learning, which does not equip LLMs with the ability to plan through multiple steps of
094
      interaction (Bachmann & Nagarajan, 2024). Though LLMs have naïvely been used to engage in
095
      dialogues with humans to some success (He et al., 2018; Shuster et al., 2022b;a), such dialogue
096   agents are typically only processing past utterances by the human to produce a relevant response, and
097   not considering their influence on the human by their responses. This limits the competency of such
098   agents in interactive dialogue tasks such as negotiation or persuasion.
099
      RL and language models. Recently, LLMs have leveraged RL fine-tuning, where a reward model,
100
      learned from feedback directly from human experts (Ziegler et al., 2020; Stiennon et al., 2020; Wu
101
      et al., 2021; Nakano et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a; Christiano et al., 2023)
102   or implicitly from another LLM (Bai et al., 2022b), is then used to fine-tune the LLM via RL
103   optimization. Finetuning is primarily done via online RL, but offline RL has recently become popular
104   as a more practical alternative (Rafailov et al., 2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023). RL has enabled many
105   capabilities in LLMs, such as general instruction-following (Ouyang et al., 2022) and multi-step
106   reasoning (Wei et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). While effective, many successes of RL fine-tuning
107   are when applied to single-step responses, and not over multi-step dialogue. Thus far, RL fine-tuning
      is not as effective in enabling LLMs to plan complex strategies over multi-turn interaction.


                                                            2
108
      Dialogue agents. An interesting application of LLMs is to accomplish long-term objectives via
109   dialogue, such as for recommendation, negotiation, or persuasion. This is primarily done by training
110   task-specific agents via RL. Online RL methods to optimize dialogue agents typically require a
111   simulator of human behavior, that is usually either handcrafted or learned as a fixed model (Carta
112   et al., 2023; He et al., 2018; Gašić et al., 2011). Moreover, they involve continual collection of
113   new samples, which incurs a large computational cost in tasks where humans exhibit complex and
114   nuanced behaviors, and is often prone to reward “hacking” (Skalse et al., 2022). Alternatively, offline
115   RL approaches have also been considered that only require a static dataset of dialogues (Jaques et al.,
116   2019; Jang et al., 2022; Verma et al., 2022; Snell et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023; Abdulhai et al., 2023).
117
      Though offline RL is traditionally applied over conversations between human speakers (Verma
      et al., 2022), recent approaches consider zero-shot offline RL training by synthetically generating
118
      conversations via LLMs as simulators (Hong et al., 2023; Abdulhai et al., 2023). For example, Hong
119
      et al. (2023) propose a zero-shot offline RL approach to equip dialogue agents with information-
120   seeking behavior in tasks such as teaching and recommendation. In our work, we consider tasks
121   where successful dialogues are difficult to attain from both humans and LLMs. In such tasks, prior
122   methods fail because offline RL requires careful curation of data to enable learning (Fu et al., 2020;
123   Gulcehre et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2022). Our proposed solution circumvents this issue by having
124   LLMs evaluate and backtrack on unsuccessful dialogues to augment existing data. Empirically, we
125   compare to Hong et al. (2023) and show that training on conversations synthetically generated from
126   scratch leads to policies that lack certain intelligent strategies, such as recovering from negative
127   feedback from the conversational partner.
128   Persuasion. Early efforts in developing persuasive agents involve annotating conversations with
129   strategies, which are used to train agendas that persuasive agents would follow (Shi et al., 2021;
130   2020; Wang et al., 2023). Towards the design of more flexible persuasive agents, Wang et al. (2019)
131   introduce a dialogue corpus where people persuade others to donate money to charity, which has
132   become a popular domain to evaluate persuasive agents for social good. In this setting, Mishra
133   et al. (2022) trained a persuasive agent using RL with a novel reward that accounts for empathy
134
      and politeness. Our method is also applied to training persuasive agents in the same task, but we
      propose an offline approach and use hindsight regenerations to remedy deficits in the offline dataset.
135
      Because we do not require exploration, we do not require access to an online simulator of different
136
      human behaviors, which can be hard to obtain by purely prompting LLMs when such behaviors
137   are nuanced and hard to express in natural language. Orthogonally, there has also been work on
138   leveraging information retrieval to ensure that persuasive agents provide arguments that are factually
139   correct (Chen et al., 2022). Such work can be seamlessly integrated with our current approach to
140   combat potential hallucinations.

142   3    P RELIMINARIES
143
144   Markov decision processes. To formulate dialogue as a decision making problem, we use the
145   formalism of the Markov decision process (MDP), given by a tuple M = (S, A, P, r, ρ, γ), where S
146   is the state space, A is the action space, P is the transition function, r is the reward function, ρ is the
147   initial state distribution, and γ is the discount factor. When action a ∈ A is executed at state s ∈ S,
148   the next state is sampled s′ ∼ P (·|s, a), and the agent receives reward r with mean r(s, a).
149
      Interactive dialogues as MDPs. Interactive dialogues can be viewed as MDPs, where states are
150   sequences of tokens from a finite vocabulary V (Ramamurthy et al., 2023). All tokens that the
151   agent initially observes are used as our initial state, s0 = (x0 , . . . , xm ), where xi ∈ V, ∀i ∈ [m].
152   At timestep t, an action at ∈ V is some token in the vocabulary. As long as at is not a special
153   end-of-sequence <EOS> token, the transition function deterministically appends at to state st to form
154   st+1 . Otherwise, the agent observes (potentially stochastic) responses from all other interlocutors
155   bt = (y0 , . . . , yn ), which also consist of tokens in the vocabulary; then, the transition function
156   appends both at and output responses bt to state st . This continues until the last timestep T where
157   we obtain a state sT and the agent receives a deterministic reward r(sT ) for how well the agent
158
      accomplished the specified goal.
159   Reinforcement learning.
                        P∞        The goal of RL is to learn a policy π that maximizes the expected
160   discounted return t=0 γ t rt in an MDP. The Q-function Qπ (s, a) for a policy π represents the dis-
161   counted long-term reward attained by executing a given state s and then following policy π thereafter.
      Qπ satisfies the Bellman recurrence: Qπ (s, a) = r(s, a) + γEs′ ∼P (·|s,a),a′ ∼π(·|s′ ) [Q(s′ , a′ )]. The

      value function V π is the expectation of the Q-function V π (s) = Ea∼π(·|s) [Qπ (s, a)]. The expected
163
      discounted return can be expressed as J(π) = Es0 ∼ρ [V π (s0 )]. In offline RL, we are provided with a

166   when online interaction with the real world is costly or unavailable.

168   4    R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING ON H INDSIGHT R EGENERATIONS
169
      Here, we describe our proposed approach, which augments a static dataset of dialogues with hindsight
170
      regenerations (HR), then trains a downstream dialogue agent using offline RL. Our approach simply
171
      requires a collection of task-relevant dialogues τi with reward labels ri in a static dataset Dorig =
172   {(τi , ri )}i∈[N ] . Note that such dataset does not need to be collected from humans, but can be
173   generated synthetically (Hong et al., 2023; Abdulhai et al., 2023). In this paper, we consider learning
174   an agent per task, though our method straightforwardly scales to the multi-task setting by considering
175   goal-conditioned agents. Executing our method requires the following components:
176
            1. A hindsight controller cH that takes any completed dialogue as input, as well as a prefix of
177
               that dialogue, and proposes a different, more preferable action to take.
178
            2. A forward model Pb that simulates a hypothetical completed dialogue from any prefix.
179
            3. A reward model rb to assign a reward for any completed dialogue.
180         4. An offline RL method for learning a policy from a static dataset of dialogues.
181
182   Note that our required components are reminiscent of the components of a model-based RL algo-
183
      rithm (Janner et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2020). However, our method does not require any additional
      online interaction, but rather uses the hindsight controller to “explore” and identify better actions.
184
185   The components are shown together in our full algorithm in Figure 2. First, in the hindsight action
186   relabeling step, the hindsight controller identifies suboptimal actions in each dialogue of the dataset
187   and relabels them with more preferable ones. Then, during forward dialogue generation, we generate
188   plausible completions of the relabeled dialogue prefix using the forward model to simulate responses
189
      by both parties, then the reward model to label the new dialogue with a reward. This pipeline allows
      us to generate an arbitrary number of hindsight regenerations from the original dataset, which can get
190
      used for downstream offline RL policy optimization. We go over each step in detail below.
191
192   4.1 H INDSIGHT ACTION R ELABELING
193   As alluded to earlier, a primary challenge of learning in interactive dialogues is the difficulty of
194   collecting successful dialogues. Though offline RL does not require data derived from expert agents,
195   some examples of effective behavior are still necessary to “stitch” together (Fu et al., 2019; Kumar
196   et al., 2022). Our approach circumvents this by backtracking on existing suboptimal behaviors and
197
      replacing them with better ones. The key component to achieve this is the hindsight controller,
      which identifies ineffective actions in existing trajectories, and replaces them with a different, more
198
      promising one. Critically, this hindsight controller does not need to generate optimal strategies, but
199
      simply propose alternatives from which an offline RL method can extract the most effective strategy.
200
201   The key idea that enables the design of a hindsight controller is that it is significantly easier to evaluate
202   how an action could be improved in hindsight, after already observing potential responses. For every
203
      dialogue τ in dataset Dorig , and every dialogue prefix p ⊆ τ that is immediately followed by utterance
      u by the agent, we sample from the hindsight controller a single utterance u′ ∼ cH (· | p, τ ). Since
204
      cH is given oracle information in the form of future responses, this u′ is likely more preferable over
205
      the original u in the data. By doing so, we compile examples {(pi , u′i )}i∈[N ′ ] where u′ is sufficiently
206   different from the original utterance u. In practice, the hindsight controller is implemented as an
207   LLM prompted to suggest alternative agent utterances at various prefixes of the dialogue.
208
209
      4.2 F ORWARD D IALOGUE R EGENERATION
210   From action relabeling, we curated {(pi , u′i )}i∈[N ′ ] containing dialogue prefixes ending in a relabeled
211   agent utterance. However, for downstream RL training, it is important to counterfactually reason
212
      about the effect of the relabeled utterances on the resulting conversation. This requires learning a
      world model, consisting of forward dynamics and reward models, of the environment that is used to
213
      generate hypothetical trajectories for the agent to plan through (Sutton, 1991; Janner et al., 2019).
214
215   To learn a forward model, we fine-tune an LLM to complete dialogues from all prefixes that end
      in agent utterances in the original dataset Dorig , thus learning to generate completions that are

264   the robustness of the resulting policy, we additionally learn a “hard” forward model trained only
265   on bottom 25% dialogues in the dataset in terms of reward. Then, during the regeneration step, we

291   5    E XPERIMENTS
292
      We evaluate our approach on two interactive dialogue tasks based off of real-world data. Existing
293
      dialogue benchmarks (Budzianowski et al., 2020; Rastogi et al., 2020) are tailored for supervised
294
      fine-tuning, primarily involving question-answering, and thus do not consider an agent’s influence
295   on their conversational partner. In addition, evaluation of agents in these benchmarks would involve
296   computing a ROUGE or BLEU score, which merely measure how well agents mimic the data.
297   Because of this, such benchmarks are more suited for supervised finetuning methods rather than RL.
298   In contrast, we consider tasks where optimal agents need to exhibit planning behaviors that account
299   for how actions affect their conversational partner. We provide an overview of both domains below.
300
      Counseling. In this task, an agent must provide mental health counseling to a person experiencing a
301   strong negative emotion due to some problem in relationships, work, or daily life. We start with the
302   ESConv dataset of 1,053 dialogues between a human seeker and supporter, where the seeker rates
303   the strength of their negative emotion on a Likert scale (1-5) before and after (Liu et al., 2021).
304
      Persuasion. In this task, an agent must persuade users to donate to Save the Children, a non-
305
      governmental organization dedicated to international assistance for children. We utilize the
306
      PERSUASION-FOR-GOOD dataset, which comprises of 1,017 dialogues by real humans where
307   one attempts to persuade the other to donate to the charity of up to $2 total (Wang et al., 2019).
308
309   To our knowledge, these are the only dialogue domains for which a curated dataset of real human-
310
      human dialogues already exists, where agents influence the mental state or opinions of their conversa-
      tional partners. Due to space, we only show results for the persuasion task in the main paper, and
311
      defer results for the counseling task to Appendix A.

      The next set of approaches are ablations of our approach, and require additional training on a
      LLaMA-7b model (Touvron et al., 2023).
339
340   SFT: This approach performs supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on a LLM with the starting dataset of
341   human-human conversations. To make sure that we replicate good behavior seen in the dataset, we
342   take the top 25% of dialogues, when sorted by reward that the agent achieves. For each dialogue, the
343   LLM is trained to copy the human in the conversation who takes on the same role as the agent.
344   Zero-shot RL: This is an ablation of our approach. We use offline RL to train an agent, but rather
345   than use hindsight regenerations, we simply ask GPT-3.5 (OpenAI, 2022) to generate dialogues
346   from scratch. We synthetically generate 5x the amount of data as in the starting dataset to be used
347   for downstream offline RL training. We use ILQL (Snell et al., 2022) as the offline RL algorithm.
348   This approach was shown in prior work to be effective at various tasks, including teaching and
349   recommendations (Hong et al., 2023).
350   RFT: This is another ablation where we do not perform RL optimization. Specifically, we perform
351   rejection fine-tuning, which involves supervised fine-tuning on the hindsight regenerations. We
352   aggregate the dataset to 5x its original size, and take the top 25% of dialogues by reward.
353
      Hindsight RL: This is the full version of our approach, which aggregates the starting data with
354   hindsight regenerations to 5x its original size, than trains an agent downstream using ILQL.
355
356   Note that we train on a much smaller model than used in the prompting baselines, yet as we will
357
      show later, we still are able to outperform such more sophisticated LLMs. We defer implementation
      details to Appendix B.

      6    D ISCUSSION
469
470   In this paper, we propose an algorithm to train effective agents for interactive dialogues using offline
471   RL on a static dataset. We consider an approach to enhance static datasets that lack exploration
472   of optimal strategies, rendering downstream offline RL training to be ineffective. This can be the
473   case when the considered dialogue tasks are difficult for the average human to succeed at, such as
474   persuasion. Our approach leverages hindsight regenerations, which relabel suboptimal behaviors in
475   data with traces of optimal ones while retaining accurate human counterfactuals, by utilizing the fact
476
      that LLMs can more effectively evaluate dialogues in hindsight. We show, on a variety of interactive
      dialogue tasks including counseling and persuasion, that our approach leads to much more effective
477
      dialogue agents than simply prompting, or fine-tuning on the original data.
478
479   Limitations. Thus far, our method requires the considered dialogue tasks to have a defined reward
480   parameterization to which LLMs can calibrate during the forward regeneration step. This can be
481   much more difficult for general dialogues where the only signal may be success or failure. In such
482
      dialogues, LLMs may not be capable enough to generate proxy reward labels without additional
      training. Moreover, our method is reliant on hand-crafted prompts. Since these prompts are incredibly
483
      task-specific, future work should aim to automate the design of these prompts.
484
485   Ethical Considerations. We understand that superhuman abilities in the realm of persuasion can be
      used for harm. However, we focus on the relatively benign tasks of emotional support and persuasion


                                                           9
486
      to benefit children. Our method is a general framework for improving goal-directed dialogue agents,
487   which are inherently at risk for dual use.

      Likert scale (1-5):

      Comforting: How skillful the agent was at comforting the seeker?

1246
1247   Subject Allocation. We recruited 15 participants for our study, 10 male and 5 female with an
1248   average age of 26. 11 participants were university students, and the remaining were working in the
1249   tech industry. 9 participants have English as their native language, but all participants demonstrate
1250   fluency in English. Finally, all participants were instructed to behave as themselves and not adopt any
1251
       alternative personas, and are aware that their responses were being recorded.
1252
1253
       Evaluation Protocol. During evaluation, each user is presented with a web interface where they
       are allowed to interact with each agent in a chat window. Each agent is anonymized, and in a random
1254
       order. The user is allowed 3 minutes to familiarize themselves with the interface, and is instructed
1255
       to response realistically to each agent. The user interacts with each agent in order, for a total of
1256   3 conversations. Each conversation ends automatically after 10 turns of dialogue, but the user is
1257   allowed to end each dialogue prematurely. At the end, the users were given a Google Forms survey
1258   where they rate the performance of each agent based on the metrics listed, and on a Likert scale. We
1259   average the results across all users in Table 1 in the main paper.
