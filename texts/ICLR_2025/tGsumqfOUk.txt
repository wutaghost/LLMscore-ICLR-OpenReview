021                                               A BSTRACT
022
023            Large neural networks achieve remarkable performance, but their size hinders
024            deployment on resource-constrained devices. While various compression techniques
025            exist, parameter sharing remains relatively unexplored. This paper introduces Fine-
026            grained Parameter Sharing (FiPS), a novel algorithm that leverages the relationship
027            between parameter sharing, tensor decomposition, and sparsity to efficiently com-
028            press large vision transformer models. FiPS employs a shared base and sparse factors
029            to represent shared neurons across multi-layer perception (MLP) modules. Shared
030
               parameterization is initialized via Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) and opti-
               mized by minimizing block-wise reconstruction error. Experiments demonstrate that
031
               FiPS compresses DeiT-B and Swin-L MLPs to 25–40% of their original parameter
032
               count while maintaining accuracy within 1 percentage point of the original models.
033

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036   Over the last decade, large neural networks have achieved impressive performance across various
037   tasks by scaling up datasets and model sizes. However, this growth has led to computational, memory,
038   and storage challenges, necessitating efficient model compression techniques to reduce overhead and
039   enable deployment on resource-constrained devices like mobile phones and embedded systems. To
040   this end, research has explored various approaches, including tensor decomposition, quantization,
041   distillation, sparsity, parameter sharing, and adaptive computing methods (Cheng et al., 2020).
042   While most of these methods are well-studied and successfully utilized in practice (e.g., distillation,
043   quantization), parameter sharing has received less attention.
044   Sharing parameters across multiple layers of a neural network could, in theory, reduce memory
045   requirements and increase cache hits, leading to faster execution. Motivated by this, several previous
046   works have explored reusing entire transformer blocks when defining a network (Lan et al., 2020;
047   Takase & Kiyono, 2023; Lin et al., 2023), resulting in more efficient models. Although the ability to
048   share weights unmodified across layers is promising, we hypothesize that a more fine-grained approach
049   may achieve better compression, leading us to focus on sharing neurons across different layers.
050   We show that sharing neurons across layers can be achieved using a shared basis, where each neuron
051   is computed as a linear combination of this basis. Crucially, we find that sparsity in the projection
052   matrix is essential for this approach to be effective. This insight leads to our novel parameter sharing
053   algorithm, Fine-grained Parameter Sharing (FiPS), which we demonstrate effectively compresses
      large vision transformer models. Our contributions include:

      2     PARAMETER S HARING T HROUGH S PARSE T ENSOR D ECOMPOSITION
083   Consider a weight matrix, W ∈ Rd×p , which projects feature vectors from d-dimensional space to a
084   p-dimensional space, with neurons represented as the columns of W. We aim to share weights across a
085   subset of these p neurons such that there remain only r < p unique neurons; in other words, we want only
086   r columns of W to have unique values. We can represent the r unique neurons using a lookup table (basis
087   matrix) U ∈ Rd×r . Then, our original matrix W can be reconstructed using an r-dimensional one-hot
088   vector for each of its p columns, represented by a projection matrix V ∈ Rr×p . This is the "one-hot" ap-
089   proach illustrated in the upper part of Figure 1. Note that the number of unique neurons in this setting is r,
090   limiting the resulting matrix’s representation power W. One way to alleviate this limitation is to increase
091
      the number of non-zero elements in V, effectively creating combinations of the basis neurons and result-
      ing in significantly more unique neuron representations, as illustrated in the lower part of Figure 1. So
092
      far, we have considered sharing neurons within a single matrix W. However, this approach can be easily
093
      extended to multiple weight matrices W1 ,...,WN . Specifically, we can enable fine-grained parameter
094   sharing across multiple layers by increasing the size of our projection matrix V and shared basis U.
095
096   The approach outlined above can be viewed as a low-rank decomposition of a matrix W, where
097
      the first factor U is shared and the second factor V is sparse. Thus, we can utilize existing low-rank
      decomposition techniques to obtain an optimal shared orthogonal basis and induce sparsity in the
098
      projection matrices using existing pruning and sparse training techniques. In what follows, we use
099
      a pre-trained DeiT-B model (with 12 encoder blocks, each containing one MLP modules, pre-trained
100   on ImageNet-1k (Deng et al., 2009)) and investigate the best strategy for tying multiple layers using
101   the described framework. Specifically, we focus on the model’s MLP modules, each consisting of
102   two fully connected (FC) layers with dimensions Rd×p and Rp×d respectively, where p = 4d.
103

181
182   In this section, we study the redundancy and interplay among different MLP modules to identify ideal
183
      groups for parameter sharing. First, we decompose individual modules using a rank of r = 180 and
      plot the MSE in Figure 3a-bottom. We observe that the error increases almost monotonically with
184
      the module index, suggesting the need for allocating more capacity to later modules in the network.
185
186   Next, we group two MLP modules from two blocks (i and j) and make them share the same basis
187   U, which reduces the parameter count and consequently increases the MSE for each block. The MSE
188   for block i in this shared setting is denoted as M SEi,j . In Figure 3a, we plot the average MSE increase
                                         ↑
189   between blocks i and j as M SEi,j      = (M SEi −M SEi,j +M SEj −M SEj,i )/2. Empirically, we find
190   that nearby blocks tend to have the smallest increase in error, motivating the grouping of consecutive
191   layers when sharing parameters.
192   We then study the optimal number of MLP modules per group. Increasing the group size, thereby
193   sharing more parameters across the same basis U, allows for a higher rank, as shown in Figure 3b.
194   This effect is more pronounced when sparsity is applied to factor V. However, a higher rank does
195   not always lead to better accuracy, as the shared basis must cover a larger number of parameters. As
196   illustrated in Figure 3c, the highest post-compression accuracy is attained when parameters are shared
197   across the MLP modules four consecutive blocks† .

      3        S PARSITY- ENABLED PARAMETER S HARING
200   Experiments in the previous section motivate and guide us in developing FiPS, an efficient parameter-
201   sharing algorithm enabled by sparse tensor decompositions that can be summarized in three main points:
202
203              1. Shared Initialization: We tie multiple FC layers across a group of MLP modules and apply
204
                    low-rank decomposition via truncated SVD.
205              2. Local Error Minimization: We finetune our shared low-rank initialization to minimize
206                 the difference between the activations of the original and compressed model. During this
207
                    step, we also introduce sparsity in our factors, which helps us allocate parameters where they
                    are most needed.
208
209
                 3. Global Error Minimization (Optional): For best results, especially at lower compression
                    levels, we finetune our compressed models end-to-end.
210
211

214   achieved by concatenating and decomposing multiple FC layers simultaneously, as illustrated in Fig-

255
256   Implementing dedicated kernels to fully exploit the potential for compression offered by FiPS is
257
      outside the scope of this work; however, we demonstrate promising preliminary benchmarks by

258

262   and the structured sparsity performance in table 3, FiPS demonstrates effective model compression,
263   offering improvements in both memory usage and latency.

382   5    A BLATIONS
383
      In this section, we study the importance of various components of the FiPS algorithm when
384
      compressing the DeiT-B model at different parameter budgets. First, we ablate key components of
385
      our algorithm in Figure 6a under a 25% parameter budget:
386
387         1. SVD-based Initialization: Using a random initialization (RI) instead of SVD-based
388            initialization results in a 1% point drop in accuracy.
389
            2. Global Pruning: We use global pruning (GP) when sparsifying our sparse factors V, which
390
               results in 0.4% point improvement over local pruning (LP), which enforces the same sparsity
391            level for each factor Vi .
392
            3. Scaling Vectors: We normalize the weights W before the initial SVD stage of FiPS as
393
               suggested by Liu et al. (2024). Normalized weights initialize the SVD, while magnitude
394            vectors initialize scaling vectors (SV) for neuron scaling. Scaling vectors improve local
395            pruning but are less effective with global pruning.
396
397   Consequently, the final FiPS configuration integrates GMP with GP. Next, we perform a sensitivity

      Growing Neurons in Shared Bases and Sparse Factors As discussed in § 3, high parameter
      budgets and sparsity levels (e.g., 50% and 75% for DeiT-B) often allow the rank r to exceed the model
478
      dimension d. Since SVD provides only d directions for initialization, we explore three methods for
479
      initializing the remaining k = r−d dimensions: 1. Random Growth: New neurons in U are initialized
480   to zero, and those in V are initialized randomly using He et al. (2015); 2. Neuron Splitting: The top
481   k neurons of U are duplicated, and the top k neurons of V are halved, following Chen et al. (2016);
482   3. Hybrid Initialization: New neurons in U are initialized to zero, while those in V are derived
483   from the top k neurons and normalized by τ . This approach minimizes the immediate impact of new
484   neurons in V, allowing optimization to gradually re-activate them, as suggested by Evci et al. (2022).
485   After a hyperparameter sweep on τ , the hybrid initialization outperformed the alternatives, achieving
      1% and 2% higher accuracy than methods (1) and (2), respectively.

506   6    R ELATED W ORK
507   Vision Transformers (ViT) were introduced by Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) for image recognition using
508   image patches as sequences, similar to tokens in NLP models. Touvron et al. (2021) improved ViT’s
509   data efficiency by introducing a distillation token during training. Liu et al. (2021) proposed Swin
510   Transformers, which use a hierarchical structure and shifted windows for local self-attention, differing
511   from ViT and DeiT.
512
      Sparsity in Neural Networks includes three main approaches: post-training pruning, sparsifying
513   during training, and fully sparse training (Hoefler et al., 2021). Early methods involved heuristic
514   pruning, such as removing the smallest magnitude parameters (Thimm & Fiesler, 1995). Later
515   approaches, like GMP (Zhu & Gupta, 2017), increased the amount of pruning, while dynamic pruning
516   with accelerated schedulers was explored by Kurtic et al. (2023). Static sparsity uses a pre-initialized
517   mask throughout training (Hoefler et al., 2021), whereas dynamic methods, like RigL (Evci et al.,
518   2021), adjust the sparsity pattern during training based on gradient information.
519   Tensor Decomposition techniques, such as those presented by Kolda & Bader (2009), reduce
520   redundancy in large weight matrices using low-rank decomposition. Yu & Wu (2023) introduced
521   AAFM for transformer models, using truncated PCA to reconstruct weights, and GFM to minimize
522   loss between compressed and original models, similar to Knowledge Distillation (Hinton et al., 2015).
523
      Parameter Sharing is less explored but includes works like Eban et al. (2019), who used a
524
      Sum-Product reducer to map shared parameters, and Obukhov et al. (2021), who employed TR
525   decomposition for shared parameters in 3D tensors. Zhang et al. (2022) introduced “Weight
526   Multiplexing,” sharing parameters between MLP modules in ViT, alongside distillation and linear
527   projections between transformer blocks to aid model recovery.
528
529   7    C ONCLUSION
530
      This work introduces FiPS, demonstrating for the first time that inter-layer parameter sharing
531   enables significant compression in Transformers. While this study focuses on ViT backbones and
532   MLP modules, similar gains are expected with multi-head attention parameters, leading to greater
533   compression. Further improvements may be possible through quantization of the current full-precision
534   bases, which we leave for future work.

      A.1     MLP C ONCATENATION S TRATEGIES
670
671   In reference to § 2.2, below is a detailed account of MLP concatenation setups. Considering two MLPs,
672   therefore, 4 FC layers and assuming each weight matrix Wij ∈ Rd×p (where p = 4d and i,j ∈ {1,2}),
673   we identify four distinct methods for concatenating the FC weights:
674
675           I. All weights are combined along the longer dimension, resulting in:
676                                         W = [W1 ;W2 ;W3 ;W4 ] ∈ Rd×16d .
677
678         II. The FC1 and FC2 weights from each MLP are concatenated along the longer axis, followed
679             by concatenation along the shorter axis. Specifically, we have:
680
                              W1 = [W11 ;W12 ] ∈ Rd×8∗d       and   W2 = [W21 ;W22 ] ∈ Rd×8d ,
681
682             leading to:
683                                              W = [W1 ;W2 ] ∈ R2d×8d .
684         III. The FC1 weights from both MLPs are concatenated along the longer axis, followed by
685              concatenating the FC2 weights similarly. This results in:
686
687
                              W1 = [W11 ;W21 ] ∈ Rd×8d        and   W2 = [W12 ;W22 ] ∈ Rd×8d ,
688             yielding:
689                                              W = [W1 ;W2 ] ∈ R2d×8d .
690
            IV. Finally, all weights are concatenated along the shorter axis:
691
692                                         W = [W1 ;W2 ;W3 ;W4 ] ∈ R4d×4d .
693
694   These configurations are explored empirically in Figure 2c and discussed in full in § 2.2.
