011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) is a branch of AI directed at the develop-
014            ment of machine learning (ML) solutions that can be comprehended by the human
015            users. Here we use an interpretable and domain-grounded machine learning ar-
016
               chitecture applied to non-invasive magnetoencephalographic (MEG) data of sub-
               jects performing a speech listening task and discover neurophsyologically plausi-
017
               ble spatial-temporal neuronal representations of latent sources identified through
018
               self-supervised network training process. Achieving high decoding accuracy in
019            the downstream task our solution bridges the gap between high performance and
020            big data-based AI and the classical neuroimaging research and represents a novel
021            knowledge mining platform where the decoding rule can be interpreted using the
022            accepted in electrophysiology terms and concepts which is likely to advance neu-
023            roscientific research.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) is a branch of AI focused on the development of the white-
029   box machine learning (ML) solutions that can be comprehended by the human users. White-box
030   models are easier to deploy in real-life tasks. They also enable knowledge discovery by rendering
031   mechanistic explanations of the learned decision rules using domain-specific concepts and terminol-
032   ogy when employed in a research setting.
033   Advancing towards the integration of XAI in neuroscience, Petrosyan et al. (2021) developed an
034   interpretable machine learning framework and applied it to invasive recordings of brain activity dur-
035   ing the speech production in (Petrosyan et al., 2022). By applying the proposed estimation theory
036   based approach to the spatial and temporal convolution filter weights they managed to successfully
037   reveal the localization of language function which spatially matched cortical sites whose subsequent
038   stimulation led to speech arrest and speech production errors in both patients. Importantly, the neu-
039
      ronal activity’s spectral signatures obtained through the proposed interpretation algorithm appeared
      physiologically plausible (Miller et al., 2007). This study can be considered as a showcase of us-
040
      ing XAI for knowledge mining when a decoder trained to do a specific downstream task (speech
041
      decoding) allows for a mechanistic explanation, in this case using the electrophysiological terms of
042   neural source location and the dynamic properties of its electrical activity. At the same time, inva-
043   sive recordings provide high-quality data but are limited because of the sampling of brain activity at
044   a small subset of locations. This precludes investigation into the macro-scale cortical mechanisms
045   underlying the explored function.
046
      Magnetoencephalography (MEG) (Cohen, 1968), a contactless whole brain functional imaging
047
      modality offers unique opportunities for tracing neuronal processes with millisecond-scale temporal
048   resolution. Recently, a universal deep learning (DL) model was built to successfully decode 3 sec-
049   ond long audited speech segments (out of more than 1,000 possibilities) based on the MEG signals
050   registered during the speech perception in multiple volunteers (Défossez et al., 2023). The demon-
051   strated decoding of the perceived speech is a significantly simpler task than that of predicting speech
052   production from brain activity recordings. However, since it is now accepted in the community that
053   language comprehension and speech production share similar neural circuits (Fadiga et al., 2002;
      Liu et al., 2023; Eliades & Wang, 2008), the results obtained in the passive auditory paradigms may


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      shed light on the cortical mechanisms of speech representation and production and benefit not only
055   the fundamental neurolinguistic research but also the development of long awaited speech prosthetic
056   devices.
057
058
      To this end Défossez et al. (2023) provided some interpretation of the decoder weights and showed
      that dominant attention was paid to the lateral MEG sensors proximal to the primary auditory cor-
059
      tices. At the same time, due to inherently non-linear relationship, the weights of the spatial attention
060
      layer can not be rigorously converted to the underlying cortical sources to fully benefit from the
061   MEG’s high spatial resolving power. Endowed with the appropriate inverse operator (Bonaiuto
062   et al., 2018; Wens, 2023), non-invasively collected multivariate MEG signals can be mapped back
063   to the cortex to resolve neuronal sources located at a sub-centimeter distance.
064
      The proposed network’s subject block contains the subject adaptation layer which uses linear spatial
065
      processing to efficiently enable the aggregation of data from multiple subjects. However, the subject
066
      block lacks a temporal filtering layer which limits the subsequent interpretation of the decision rule.
067   The use of temporal convolutions allows the network to benefit from the unprecedented temporal re-
068   solving power of MEG. Analyzing temporal correlation weights we can unravel the brain’s rhythmic
069   activity hierarchy bearing the information within the context of the decoding task.
070
      Finally, the proposed architecture utilizes a physically unjustified receptive field model created on
071
      the basis of a set of 2D Fourier harmonics which may adversely affect the compactness of the
072
      proposed solution.
073
074   To ameliorate the described shortcomings of the otherwise breakthrough solution developed by
075   Défossez et al. (2023) and to illustrate the use of XAI methodology we modify the original ar-
076
      chitecture to enable the interpretability of the decision rule learned by the network. We also replace
      the heuristic spatial attention layer with that based on the spherical harmonics as the natural basis for
077
      capturing the geometry of the magnetic field as captured by the 3D sensor array and experiment with
078
      the interpretable subject block dimension to minimize the number of trainable parameters. Finally
079   we add a temporal filtering layer to benefit from the temporal resolving power of MEG.
080
081   Using one of the two MEG datasets employed in (Défossez et al., 2023), the dataset described by
082
      Gwilliams et al. (2023), we show that the modified and simplified architecture performs comparably
      to the original network and at the same time allows for identifying neuronal sources pivotal for the
083
      decoding task along with dynamical properties of their activity. Reduction of the number of spatial
084
      channels from 270 to 6 in combination with the 3D spatial layer does not reduce but even slightly
085   boosts the decoding accuracy.
086
087   We interpret the network’s weights into the cortical distributions derived from the spatial patterns.
088
      We also derive spectral profiles of the electrical activity of latent sources to show that the obtained
      classification performance is supported by the rhythmic activity of sources in the primary auditory
089
      cortices, parietal (Wernicke) and frontal (Broca) cortical areas. Joint analysis of the power spectral
090
      density profiles and the associated spatial patterns reveals the dominant role of oscillatory activity
091   in the alpha and beta bands. Intriguingly, our analysis shows that in addition to the sources of
092   neuronal origin the network’s interpretable subject block tunes of eye-movements as a latent source
093   informative for the downstream decoding task. This is an exciting demonstration how XAI trained
094   within the auditory downstream task reveals the well known link between mutually cooperative
095   performance of visual and audio perception systems (Mendelson et al., 1976) related to tracking
096   mentally constructed sentences during speech listening in the absence of any sentence-related visual
097   or prosodic cues (Jin et al., 2018).

102   2.1   G ENERATING EQUATION MEG
103
104   The primary sources of MEG signals are the electric currents floating in the dendrites of pyramidal
105   neurons that receive the excitation from the neuronal populations situated nearby in the other cortical
106   layers. The bundle of mutually parallel dendrites of a large number of neurons occupying the cortical
107   area of several tens of square millimeters is then approximated by a single equivalent current dipole
      (ECD) with location vector rn = [xn , yn , zn ]⊤ and orientation θn = [θn , θn , θn ]⊤ where n denotes

      the index of a neuronal source. Time varying dipole moment of the n-th ECD is called activation
109   time series and is denoted as sn (t).
110
111   The array of M MEG sensors surrounding the head and located at a set of locations rm , m =
112
      1, ..., M , at each time instance t measures a vector x(t) = [x1 (t), ..., xM (t)]⊤ and spatially samples
      the weak magnetic field produced by the superposition of magnetic fields generated by the vector
113
      s(t) of ECD activation moments s(t) = [s1 (t), ..., sN (t)]⊤ , n = 1, ..., N , where N is the number
114
      of active neuronal sources.
115
116   The MEG (and EEG) signal vector generated by a single n-th neuronal source (an ECD) can be
117   modeled simply as x(t) = gn sn (t), where gn = g(rn , θn ) is the M × 1 gain vector mapping to
118
      M MEG sensors the activity of the n-th unit dipole with orientation θn located at rn . Vector gn
      can be visualized by interpolating its values between sensor locations. Historically, the visualization
119
      used level lines, similar to those employed in topographic maps, and hence vector gn is often called
120
      topography of a source at location rn and orientation θn . Vectors gn , n = [1, ..., N ] for the grid of
121   N cortical locations (and orientations) are obtained by solving Maxwell equations for the head as
122   a volume conductor on the basis of geometric information about location and orientation of MEG
123   sensors and cortical sources. The former results from the head and MEG sensor array coregistration
124   procedure and the latter is dictated by the nodes of a triangulated cortical surface mesh extracted
125   from the volunteer’s head MRI volume using readily available tools such as FreeSurfer (Fischl,
126   2012).

286   3.2   T EMPORAL FILTERING
287
288   Brain rhythms are key components of non-invasively measured neuronal activity, reflecting different
289   aspects of how the cortex processes incoming information (Buzsaki, 2006). By targeting specific
290   frequency ranges using learnable temporal convolutions, decoding performance can be improved,
291   while also enhancing the interpretability of the resulting decision rules. As shown in Figure 1 , we
292
      augmented the interpretable subject block of the network with temporal filters. To this end we used
      trainable 1D convolution filters of length 750 ms which corresponds to 75 taps given the sampling
293
      frequency fs = 100 Hz that we downsampled the data to. Note that in the original paper the authors
294
      downsampled the data to 120 Hz.
295
296   The temporal convolution with trainable sequence hk (τ ) is performed within the k-th branch, k =
297   1, .., K branches and is preceded by the spatial filter with coefficients taken as the k-th row of the
298
      aggregate spatial filter matrix W . We refer to hk (τ ) as the pulse response of the temporal filter
      for the k-th branch. Unlike the subject layer, temporal filters were not subjects specific and were
299
      trained on all 27 subjects Note that given enough training data the added temporal filters should
300
      not adversely affect the decoding accuracy as in the case when the frequency band specificity is
301   not required the network can learn hk (τ ) = δ(τ ) to be all-pass filters implementing the identity
302   transform. The temporal filter output is optionally processed with non-linearity shown with dashed
303   squares in Figure 1.

333   3.4   T RAINING AND TESTING
334
335   In this work we have first implemented our own version of the network described by Défossez et al.
336   (2023) and applied it to one of the two MEG datasets described in the study by Gwilliams et al.
337   (2023). This dataset contains audio and MEG signals recorded during the two identical 1 hour 30
338   minutes long sessions when 27 English-speaking participants listened to four fictional stories from
339   the Masc corpus. The study was approved by the institutional review board ethics committee of
340
      New York University Abu Dhabi. We have used the entire three stories lw1, cable spool fort, easy
      money and also the first 5 pieces of the black willow story for training. For testing we used the
341
      last 7 pieces of the black willow story. This resulted in 2685 training and 999 testing segments
342
      which makes this study one of the few that utilize large datasets. Most of the previous works,
343   e.g. Petrosyan et al. (2021; 2022), use smaller datasets. Also in our experiments we did not align
344   the testing segments against word onset moments which represents potentially a more challenging
345   setting than that reported in the original paper by Défossez et al. (2023) where the test set contained
346   data segments synchronized to the word onset.

350   4.1   D ECODING ACCURACY
351
352   We assessed the model’s efficacy by testing it on recordings associated with chapters 5 to 11 of
353   ”The Black Willow” story. One can see our results and ablations in Table 1. Firstly, we trained and
354   evaluated the model presented by Défossez et al. (2023) to use it as a baseline. Note, all experiments
355   besides this original baseline used 24 spatial harmonics in the attention layer. The baseline model
356   used 32 harmonics as in the original paper. Then, to highlight the efficiency of our 3D spatial
357
      attention layer over the original 2D spatial attention we trained the same model only changing the
      spatial attention layer. It brought down the amount of parameters by almost 400K and allowed to
358
      improve the top-1 accuracy by 2.26 percent. Then, we trained our network with different variations
359
      of layers. All of them have noticeably smaller parameter count ranging from 3.4M to 7.1M with the
360   amount of parameters in the interpretable subject block reduced almost 10 times. It happens mainly
361   due to the pruning of the subject block from original K = 270 branches to only K = 6. This
362   allows for significantly better interpretability of the weights and makes the network less susceptible
363   to overfitting which is extremely important considering the common issue in applications: a lack of
364   large datasets with relevant brain activity data. On top of that the metrics stayed close to the baseline
365   while models with 3 and 4 convolution blocks got stronger results than the baseline model despite
366   being about 2 times smaller and more interpretable.

      5   D ISCUSSION
492
493   We have for the first time demonstrated the use of the explainable AI approach for mining the
494   dynamic cortical representations of latent sources pivotal to the perceived speech decoding task
495   from the non-invasive MEG data collected from a large cohort of subjects.
496   We augmented the network model presented in (Défossez et al., 2023) and endowed it with inter-
497   pretable spatial and frequency domain selective layers which revealed cortical and dynamic repre-
498   sentations of the latent sources discovered by the network. This was achieved by the interpretation
499   of the network’s weights in terms of the well accepted in the classical neurophysiological and neu-
500   roimaging communities notions of source topographies, activation time series and their second-order
501   dynamical properties conveyed by the power spectral density.
502   We have also replaced the original 2D spatial attention layer with a more justified 3D spatial attention
503   based on the spherical harmonics, a natural basis for representing magnetic fields in the vicinity of
504   a volumetric conductor. Our main goal was to achieve the interpretability and after making sure
505   that the introduced changes followed by significant (more than 40 ×) pruning of the subject block
506   did not significantly reduce the classification accuracy we focused on interpreting the sources that
507   appeared pivotal for building self-supervised representations of the observed MEG activity.
508   In addition to the physilogically plausible patterns we also observed components of non-neuronal
509   origin and related to eye movements. In some subjects (not reported) we found heart-beat related
510   components which is unsurprising as naturalistic fictional stories cause emotionally driven variations
511   in the heart rate (Wallentin et al., 2011; Beans, 2022).

514   6   L IMITATIONS
515
516
517   Our analyses and model development were based on a single MEG dataset but containing the data
518
      from 27 subjects, which may limit the generalizability of our findings.
519   The interpretation of our model relies on certain assumptions about linearity in the spatial and tem-
520   poral filters; they may not fully capture the complexity of brain dynamics, potentially leading to
521   oversimplified conclusions. Although our analysis results agree with the existing knowledge in the
522   neuroscientific literature, the development of novel methods for analysis of subtle spatial-temporal
523   dynamics of the discovered latent sources is warranted.
524   The architecture of our neural network, including the choice of spatial and temporal filters, is based
525   on specific design choices that may have influenced the outcomes. We conducted a limited ablation
526   study justifying our choices. The use of long temporal filters negatively affected the performance
527   which is most likely due to the transients occurring at the edges of each new sample introduc-
528   ing. This needs to be ameliorated in the future designs. At the same time, as demonstrated in
529   Petrosyan et al. (2021) despite performance degradation due to the added noise the interpretable
530
      network weights conveyed correct information regarding the dynamic properties of the underlying
      sources. Therefore in our interpretation we used network configuration containing the temporal
531
      filters.
532
533   Increasing the interpretability of our model by incorporating interpretable layers and filters has led
534   to a trade-off with performance. While our modified model achieves comparable accuracy to the
535   original, there is some slight reduction in decoding accuracy when 40 times fewer branches are
536
      used. Although not significant this highlights the balance that must be struck between model inter-
      pretability and performance.
537
538   We have not yet applied the individual head model based inverse modeling to the discovered sub-
539   ject specific topographies which is the work in progress and requires laborious steps of curating
      individual structural models obtained from the MRI of the participants.
