009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Attention mechanisms and Transformer architectures have revolutionized Natu-
012            ral Language Processing (NLP) by enabling exceptional modeling of long-range
013            dependencies and capturing intricate linguistic patterns. However, their inherent
014            reliance on linear operations in the form of matrix multiplications limits their ability
015            to fully capture inter-token relationships on their own. We propose MöbiusAtten-
016            tion, a novel approach that integrates Möbius transformations within the attention
017            mechanism of Transformer-based models. Möbius transformations are non-linear
018
               operations in spaces over complex numbers with the ability to map between various
               geometries. By incorporating these properties, MöbiusAttention empowers mod-
019
               els to learn more intricate geometric relationships between tokens and capture a
020
               wider range of information through complex-valued weight vectors. We build and
021            pre-train a BERT and a RoFormer version enhanced with MöbiusAttention, which
022            we then finetune on the GLUE benchmark. We evaluate empirically our approach
023            against the baseline BERT and RoFormer models on a range of downstream tasks.
024            Our approach compares favorably against the baseline models, even with smaller
025            number of parameters suggesting the enhanced expressivity of MöbiusAttention.
026            This research paves the way for exploring the potential of Möbius transformations
027            in the complex projective space to enhance the expressivity and performance of
028            foundation models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have revolutionized various areas of machine learning, becoming
      the foundation for groundbreaking models in Natural Language Processing (NLP) like text generation
034
      (GPT3 (Brown et al., 2020), BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), Mistral 7B(Jiang et al., 2023)) and computer
035
      vision (ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) utilized in SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023), DINO (Caron et al.,
036   2021) and the multi-modal CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)). At the heart of their success lies the attention
037   mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017), a powerful tool that enables them to identify relationships between
038   different parts of the data, be it words in a sentence or image patches in a scene.
039
      Despite their remarkable impact, current transformers face limitations. A key constraint is the inher-
040
      ent linearity of the attention mechanism, which primarily relies on weights learned through linear
041
      transformations, matrix multiplications, and the softmax function. While softmax is a non-linear
042   operation, it is only used to produce a probability distribution over the elements signaling their
043   relative importance in comparison to the others, and not to introduce non-linear interdependencies.
044   Predominantly linear operations restrict the ability of models to capture complex linguistic dependen-
045   cies, leading to potential information loss within each attention layer as shown by recent research
046   (Zhang, 2023). Simply increasing the depth of the architecture does not fully solve this issue as it has
047   drawbacks: a) it yields diminishing returns or redundancy, b) it results in considerable computational
048   overhead, offering only partial resolution to the issue (Lee et al., 2021; Stock, 2021), and c) while
049   deeper architectures alleviate information loss to some degree, the accumulation of layers may lead
050
      to the re-learning of similar information, possibly causing overfitting.
051   Therefore, introducing non-linearity directly within the attention mechanism seems to be beneficial.
052   Existing approaches like RoPe (Su et al., 2024) explore this avenue through rotating the query and
053   key vectors based on token positions, while Neural Attention (Zhang, 2023) employ multi-layer
      perceptrons (MLPs) with non-linear activations to learn the query, key, and value weights (Q, K, V ).

076   However, the existing body of research does not explore transformations capable of operating across
077   diverse geometric spaces. Within the attention mechanism, the Q, K and V vectors collectively
078   encode the "necessity," "nature," and "contribution" of each token, respectively. This interplay
079
      facilitates the derivation of a token’s importance within a sequence and its relationship to other tokens.
      Consequently, advancements in methods for learning these weights (Q, K, V ) have the potential to
080
      yield significant performance improvements. However, none of the aforementioned works alter the
081
      structure of the weights, not capitalizing on this opportunity.
082
083   To address this gap, our work proposes a novel approach called MöbiusAttention. We introduce
084   non-linearity into the attention mechanism through Möbius transformations. These transformations
085
      are advantageous because they can map points between different geometries, such as from a line
      to a circle, a circle to a line, and similarly among lines and circles. Moreover, they encompass
086
      various geometric shapes, including Circular, Elliptic, Parabolic, Hyperbolic, and Loxodromic forms,
087
      illustrated in Figure 1. These properties allow the model to capture more complex inter-token
088   dependencies than traditional linear methods, which is essential for effective NLP tasks and beyond.
089
090   We show that MöbiusAttention can be easily integrated into Transformer-based models, either
091
      replacing or in combination with standard attention mechanisms. This integration leads to improved
      performance across various NLP tasks without necessarily increasing the model size. Specifically,
092
      we implement MöbiusAttention-enhanced BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and RoFormer (Su et al.,
093
      2024) models, pre-trained on the C4 dataset (Raffel et al., 2020) and fine-tuned on the GLUE
094   benchmark (Wang et al., 2018). Our evaluations show that our models surpass the performance of the
095   baselines across a suite of tasks designed to assess a model’s ability to understand complex linguistic
096   relationships.

      The landscape of attention mechanism research is rich and multifaceted, with various approaches
      aiming to improve different aspects.
104
105   A significant portion of research focuses on enhancing the time and memory efficiency of attention,
106   e.g., HyperAttention (Han et al., 2024), FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022) and other notable works

108
      (Shen et al., 2021; Child et al., 2019). These works prioritize maintaining functional and mathematical
109   equivalence to the standard attention mechanism.
110
111   Several works explore incorporating non-linear functions within the attention algorithm. Linear
112
      Transformers (Katharopoulos et al., 2020), Skyformer (Chen et al., 2021), Performers(Choromanski
      et al., 2021), Cosformer (Qin et al., 2022) and Kerformer (Gan et al., 2023) propose attention
113
      mechanisms with reduced computational requirements and comparable or better performance. These
114
      approaches utilize non-linear kernels on the learned weight vectors, essentially replacing the softmax
115   function within attention. In comparison, our approach targets the weight representation and learning
116   process itself to enhance its information capture capabilities.
117
118
      Several approaches introduce non-linear kernels to replace the standard dot-product similarity opera-
      tion on the Q and K vectors (e.g., Rymarczyk et al. (2021); Tsai et al. (2019); Kim et al. (2019)). In
119
      contrast, our approach focuses on modifying the weight representation during the learning process
120
      itself, intending to facilitate the acquisition of information-richer vectors.
121
122   RoPE (Su et al., 2024) and NeuralAttention (Zhang, 2023) exhibit the most similarity to our work.
123   Both papers introduce non-linear transformations on the Q, K, V weights vectors through rotation or
124
      learning via a non-linear MLP activation. However, these methods are limited to mapping within a
      single geometric space, lacking the flexibility to handle diverse geometries like elliptic, circular, or
125
      loxodromic, crucial for capturing intricate inter-token relationships. Furthermore, while both methods
126
      operate in real space, our approach leverages the complex domain and operations naturally supported
127   by complex numbers, facilitating the modeling of various phenomena, including cyclical patterns.

130   2.2   C OMPLEX -VALUED T RANSFORMER
131
      The exploration of complex-valued models has gained significant traction in recent years, with
132
      applications emerging across various domains (Vasudeva et al., 2022; Li et al., 2020; Barrachina
133
      et al., 2021; Trabelsi et al., 2018; Nayyeri et al., 2021; Azizi et al., 2022). While complex-valued
134   Transformers have been proposed (Complex Transformer (Yang et al., 2020), Signal Transformer
135   (Peng et al., 2024) and C-Transformer (Eilers & Jiang, 2023)), these works primarily focus on the
136   signal processing field and aim for a complete adaptation of the Transformer architecture to the
137   complex domain, without introducing any alterations to the attention mechanism that are not necessary
138   for the transition from real to complex space. Our work delves deeper into the core component of
139   Transformers, the attention mechanism, seeking novel enhancements.
140   A complex-valued Transformer specifically for NLP is developed by Wang et al. (Wang et al., 2020)
141   where they define word embeddings as continuous functions over the position of the words in the
142   complex domain, allowing for word representations to change gradually as positions increase. While
143   our work shares the goal of capturing ordered relationships (a facet of geometric properties), we
144   employ a distinct strategy by leveraging transformations which can represent a very broad variation
145   of behavior based on only few learnable parameters. Additionally, our work adopts a new approach
146   to position embeddings, as we use token embeddings as the real part of the input into the model,
147   and the corresponding position embeddings - as the imaginary one. This targeted focus on position
148
      embeddings differentiates our approach from existing works.

303   Geometric Interpretation In this section, we provide a geometric interpretation of our attention
304   model and highlight its advantages compared to existing models.
305   Capturing Local Information The set of all query matrices Mqj in Equation 6 constitutes the
306   generalized linear group GL(2, C). If we impose the condition det Mqj = 1, we obtain the special
307   linear group SL(2, C), which preserves both volume and orientation. Consequently, the set of source
308   token-position pairs in a sequence can be mapped to the set of target token-position pairs, capturing
309   local dependencies between tokens within the attention matrix.
310   Capturing Global Information When det Mqj ̸= 1, the transformation alters both volume and
311   orientation. This property, combined with the Möbius transformation’s capability to map lines to
312   circles and vice versa, results in a more expressive attention matrix. This enhanced expressiveness
313   captures more intricate relationships between tokens and understands complex linguistic patterns.
314
      In more detail, Möbius transformations offer a robust framework for analyzing and interpreting text.
315
      By leveraging these transformations, we can transition between various geometric shapes—lines, and
316   circles—in a manner that preserves the structural integrity of the data. This adaptability is crucial for
317   modeling the nuanced dependencies that exist between different tokens in a sequence.
318
319   Time and Space Complexities Despite the favorable characteristics of our model, it is efficient in
320   terms of time and space complexities. The time complexity of Möbius attention is O(n2 d + nd2 )
321   where d is the token vector size and n is the number of tokens in the sequence in the case of self-
322   attention. The space complexity of MöbiusAttention is similar to the vanilla attention O(n2 ). We
323   will later show in our experiment that our approach requires fewer layers than the vanilla model and
      is more efficient in memory and time.

      Experimental Setup We integrate MöbiusAttention into the BERT and RoFormer architectures
327   (Devlin et al., 2019; Su et al., 2024) using the MosaicBERT framework (Portes et al., 2023), licensed
328   under the Apache 2.0 License, instead of the original BERT framework, which was also used for
329   RoFormer. This choice is motivated by several factors, including its ease of adaptation, extensibility
330   to additional models, and suitability for training on the C4 dataset. See Appendix A.1 for further
331   details on our motivation.
332
      For training, we employ a cluster with four A100-40GB GPUs. The software environment consists of
333   PyTorch version 1.13.1, CUDA version 11.7, Python version 3.10, and Ubuntu version 20.04.
334
335   Given that the results reported in Portes et al. (2023) were obtained using a setup of 8 A100-80GB
336
      GPUs, while our setup consists of 4 A100-40GB GPUs, we opted to train the baseline ourselves
      rather than directly adopting their results. Following the specifications of the framework used, we
337
      pretrain all models for 70,000 steps with a batch size of 4096.
338
339
      Datasets In contrast to the BookCorpus (Zhu et al., 2015) and English Wikipedia combination
340
      used for pre-training BERT and RoFormer (Devlin et al., 2019; Su et al., 2024), we leverage the
341
      more recent and larger Colossal Clean Crawled Corpus (C4) dataset (Raffel et al., 2020), licensed
342   ODC-By, for our pre-training stage. This aligns with the recent trend of training NLP models on
343   increasingly vast datasets, a strategy demonstrably leading to performance improvements witnessed
344   in models succeeding BERT (e.g., Liu et al. (2019); Raffel et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Lee-Thorp
345   et al. (2022)). By adopting the C4 dataset, we not only benefit from this advancement but also ensure
346   consistency with the MosaicBERT framework, which is specifically optimized for this data source.
347
348   Models Our study employs two pre-trained transformer models as baselines: BERT (Devlin et al.,
349   2019) and RoFormer (Su et al., 2024). BERT was selected due to its popuarity and frequent adoption
350   as the foundation for numerous high-performing models (e.g., Liu et al. (2019); He et al. (2021);
351   Lan et al. (2019)). RoFormer serves as our second baseline, chosen for its derivation from BERT
352
      and its integration of rotary positional embeddings (RoPe). RoPe introduces a geometric dimension
      to the model by rotating the query and key vectors according to token positions, employing circular
353
      geometry. Additionally, RoPe has been integrated in a multitude of novel LLMs such as LLAMA 1, 2
354
      and 3 (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Dubey et al., 2024), the Falcon series (Almazrouei et al., 2023), PaLM
355   (Chowdhery et al., 2023), GPT-NeoX (Black et al., 2022), etc. Additional details on our motivation
356   for these selections can be found in Appendix A.1.
357
358
      We use the implementation from the Hugging Face Transformers library for PyTorch3 . We use the
      base uncased version without any modifications to the architectures.
359
360   We also created our BERT and RoFormer versions enhanced with MöbiusAttention - MöbiusBERT
361   and MobRoFormer. The Möbius transformation boasts high expressivity, but a Transformer solely
362   comprised of MöbiusAttention blocks would likely suffer from overfitting, as we show in our abla-
363   tion study in Section 5. To address these limitations, we strategically integrate MöbiusAttention -
364
      MöbiusBERT and MobRoFormer utilize MöbiusAttention only in the first and the last layer while
      relying on standard Transformer blocks for the remaining layers. Additionally, we allow for adjust-
365
      ment of the percentage of MöbiusAttention not only on layer-level but also on head-level, so it can
366
      range from zero to full utilization. We propose combining MöbiusAttention with vanilla attention
367   within the same layer by introducing an architecture that allows us to set the percentage of heads
368   using MöbiusAttention. We used an equal split of 50% vanilla attention heads (6 heads) and 50%
369   Möbius Attention heads. Other variants with different placements of MöbiusAttention are offered for
370   MöbiusBERT in our ablation study.

379   the token embeddings from the real channel of the first block, i.e., the other MöbiusAttention block,
380   as input to the imaginary channel. A visualization of the input construction is provided in Fig. 4b in
381   Appendix A.2.
382
      The output of the complex MöbiusAttention layers is converted back to real space by adding the
383
      real and imaginary outputs, i.e., for first and last layers l ∈ {1, L} the output of the specific layer is
384
      outl = (Or,l + Oi,l ). For additional details on the architecture and design, please refer to Appendix
385   A.2.
386
387   The chosen approach grants a stronger emphasis on positional encodings by separating them into
388
      a distinct channel. The key advantage of framing vanilla attention with MöbiusAttention lies in its
      ability to leverage both the two input channels and the expressive power of Möbius transformations.
389
      Furthermore, the presence of two input channels before the final block allows for a residual connection
390
      to earlier layers using the imaginary channel without affecting the significance of the previous block’s
391   output, which remains in the real channel. Figure 4b illustrates the architecture of MöbiusBERT.

394   mation introduces additional parameters. Accordingly, we reduce the depth of the Möbius models
395   in order to ensure a fair comparison to match the parameter count of the BERT baseline. As an
396   alternative, we also create a low-dimensional MöbiusAttention version which uses less parameters
397   than the original one. Here the Möbius transformation is applied after the linear query transformation,
398   thus lowering the number of parameters.

      Analysis of MöbiusAttention To analyze the MöbiusAttention mechanism, we closely examine
      the learned Möbius weights and the resulting attention values. Our observations are as follows:
443
      a) Learned Geometries: Our analysis of the learned Möbius weights reveals that the model captures
444
      a diverse range of complex geometries, as illustrated in Fig. 3. The heatmap displays the distribution
445   of these geometries across different attention heads in the first and last layers of the model. Key
446   observations include:
447
448          • Layer-level Specialization: The model decides for a layer-level geometry specialization
449            by clearly favouring the circular and elliptic geometries in the last layer, but neglecting the
450            circular one in the first layer.
451          • Head-level Specialization: The distribution varies on a head-level too, a sign for specializa-
452            tion of the heads. This is also evident in Fig. 6 and 7, examples of how geometries might
453            change after finetuning on different tasks.
454          • Beyond Circular Geometry: Consistent with RoFormer’s RoPe, the model emphasizes
455            circular geometry, particularly in the last layer. However, it extends beyond the circular
456            geometry supported by RoFormer, especially in the first layer, facilitating more complex
457            reasoning.
458
      b) Learning to "Forget": The examination of the attention values obtained via MöbiusAttention
459
      show that vanilla attention and MöbiusAttention adopt different approaches to detecting important
460   information. As seen in the attention heatmaps in Fig. 8 in the Appendix, vanilla attention almost
461   never assigns zero attention score to a token pair. In contrast, MöbiusAttention gives most of the
462   pairs zero score and only a few a non-zero one. Accordingly, instead of learning on what to "focus",
463   MöbiusAttention learns what to "forget". The vanilla model has difficulty to give zero value to entires
464   of the attention matrix due to using linear transformation on a group of tokens. Accordingly, it is hard
465   for the model to forget elements, but Möbius can give a zero value to elements as the transformation
466   can deform the distribution. As a result, the Möbius transformation is not limited to keep some
467   irrelevant entries non-zero to keep the group similarities. Therefore, the mixture of Möbius and
468
      vanilla attention shows very promising results.
469
      Memory and Time Complexity MöbiusBERT demonstrates comparable pre-training efficiency to
470
      our BERT baseline, requiring the same pre-training duration of 26 hours. However, MöbiusBERT
471
      achieved this performance with a reduced memory footprint. Specifically, MöbiusBERT utilized 104
472   million parameters, whereas BERT required 110 million parameters. It is important to note that for
473   MöbiusAttention, we counted the real and imaginary components of the complex-valued parameters
474   separately, rather than combining them into a single parameter.

526
      In this paper, we introduce MöbiusAttention, a novel attention mechanism based on Möbius trans-
527
      formations. It offers greater expressiveness compared to traditional attention by leveraging Möbius
528   transformations’ unique capabilities. These transformations enable mappings between different
529   geometries like line to line or circle, representing various shapes such as Circular, Hyperbolic, Loxo-
530   dromic, Parabolic, and Elliptic. We observe that our models using MöbiusAttention learn not only
531   the various geometries, but also different distributions of them in different parts of the model. The
532   models also show clear preferences towards certain geometries in the distinct layers, exemplifying
533   the benefits of an approach supporting big geometrical variability.
534   We integrated MöbiusAttention into BERT and RoFormer, forming MöbiusBERT and MobRoFormer,
535   and evaluated their performance on GLUE benchmarks. Results show our models outperform their
536   baseline on various tasks and on average with our MöbiusBERT models having fewer parameters
537   (about 104M versus 110M) and no increase in training time. Our ablation study found that combining
538   Möbius attention with traditional attention achieved the best performance among various architectural
539   options. We specifically create a mixed-head model version where we allow for even more freedom
      in the model adjustments, allowing for tailoring to different use-cases.

827   A.1   M OTIVATION OF THE E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
828
829
830   We integrate MöbiusAttention within the BERT architecture (Devlin et al., 2019) for several reasons.
831
      Firstly, BERT’s widespread adoption and popularity make it a common benchmark for comparison in
      NLP tasks, e.g., Liu et al. (2019); Su et al. (2024); He et al. (2021). Secondly, BERT serves as the
832
      foundation for numerous high-performing models, such as RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), DeBERTa
833
      (He et al., 2021), Albert (Lan et al., 2019), etc. Finally, BERT’s size allows for efficient training
834   with limited resources compared to larger models that would require significantly longer training
835   times (e.g., GPT3 (Brown et al., 2020), Mistral 7B (Jiang et al., 2023)). Our primary goal is to
836   demonstrate performance improvements with MöbiusAttention without incurring substantial increases
837   in memory and time complexity. As achieving state-of-the-art results often demands significantly
838   more computational resources, BERT presents itself as the ideal candidate for our study.
839
      Instead of the original BERT framework (Devlin et al., 2019), which was also followed in RoFormer
840   (Su et al., 2024), we choose the newer MosaicBERT framework (Portes et al., 2023). BERT is
841   originally trained on TPUs, while our hardware configuration consists of GPUs. The MosaicBERT
842   framework offers training optimizations for BERT specifically designed for A100 GPUs, aligning
843   perfectly with our setup. Additionally, the Toronto BookCorpus dataset (Zhu et al., 2015) used for
844   pre-training BERT was not publicly available during our research timeframe. The authors of the
845   MosaicBERT framework have accordingly chosen to work on the C4 dataset and provided the used
846   codebase for full reproducibility of their BERT baseline, alleviating the dataset-mismatch challenge.

       A.4   GLUE B ENCHMARK
977
978    The General Language Understanding Evaluation (GLUE) benchmark (Wang et al., 2018) is a
979    collection of diverse natural language understanding tasks designed to evaluate and analyze the
980    performance of models across a range of linguistic phenomena. In our experiments, we use the
981    following GLUE tasks:
982
983           • MNLI (Multi-Genre Natural Language Inference) (Williams et al., 2017): This task
984             requires the model to classify pairs of sentences into three categories: entailment, contra-
985             diction, or neutral. It evaluates the model’s ability to understand relationships between
986
                sentences across multiple genres. MNLI is further divided into two subsets:
987               – MNLI-m (Matched): The evaluation set contains examples that are from the same
988                 genres as the training data.
989               – MNLI-mm (Mismatched): Mismatched version where the evaluation set contains
990                 examples from different genres than those found in the training data, testing the model’s
991                 robustness across varying contexts.
992             We evaluate MNLI using Accuracy.
993
994
              • QQP (Quora Question Pairs 2) (Iyer et al., 2017): The goal is to determine if two
                questions from the Quora website are semantically equivalent. This task tests the model’s
995
                ability to identify paraphrased questions. For evaluation we use both Accuracy and F1 Score,
996
                since both metrics are used in different papers, e.g., Devlin et al. (2019); Qin et al. (2022).
997
998           • QNLI (Question Natural Language Inference) (Rajpurkar et al., 2016): Based on the
999
                Stanford Question Answering Dataset (SQuAD), this task involves determining whether a
                context sentence contains the answer to a given question, formulated as a binary classification
1000
                problem. The chosen evaluation metric is Accuracy.
1001
1002          • SST-2 (Stanford Sentiment Treebank) (Socher et al., 2013): A sentiment analysis task
1003            where the model predicts the sentiment (positive or negative) of a given sentence. This
1004            evaluates the model’s ability to understand and classify emotional content. We evaluate
1005
                using Accuracy.
1006          • MRPC (Microsoft Research Paraphrase Corpus) (Dolan & Brockett, 2005): This task
1007            involves identifying whether pairs of sentences are paraphrases of each other. It assesses the
1008            model’s capability to recognize semantic similarity. For MRPC we provide results both for
1009            F1 Score and for Accuracy.
1010          • STS-B (Semantic Textual Similarity Benchmark) (Cer et al., 2017): The model must
1011            predict the similarity score between two sentences on a scale from 0 to 5. This task measures
1012            the model’s ability to capture the degree of semantic equivalence. We use the Spearman
1013            correlation coefficient as the evaluation metric.
1014          • CoLA (Corpus of Linguistic Acceptability) (Warstadt et al., 2019): The objective is to
1015            determine whether a given sentence is grammatically acceptable. This task tests the model’s
1016            understanding of linguistic acceptability. All results we provide for CoLA measure the
1017            Matthews correlation coefficient.
1018
              • RTE (Recognizing Textual Entailment) (Dagan et al., 2005; Giampiccolo et al., 2007;
1019            Bentivogli et al., 2009): The model must classify pairs of sentences as entailment or not
1020            entailment. This task is similar to MNLI but involves data from a variety of sources with
1021            fewer training examples. The results we provide for RTE show the accuracy.
1022
1023   We note that we explicitly do not fine-tune on the WNLI (Winograd NLI) task (Levesque et al., 2012)
1024   as it considered to be challenging to work with (Liu et al., 2019; Devlin et al., 2019; Portes et al.,
1025   2023), leading to its omission in the benchmarks of multiple models, e.g., BERT (Devlin et al., 2019),
       RoFormer (Su et al., 2024).

       A.5.2    I SSUES WITH THE SST-2 B ENCHMARK
1081
1082   Our examination of the SST-2 dataset uncovered several significant issues:
1083
1084           • Duplicate Instances: We identified 8,727 duplicates out of 67,300 instances, constituting
1085             approximately 13% of the dataset.
1086           • Substring Overlaps: There are 34,768 instances that are substrings of each other and have a
1087             word count > 5, such as "A B C D E" and "A B C D E F". Here we focus only on sentences
1088             with word count above 5 as shorter sequences are likely to occur often in other instances.
1089           • Train-Val Distribution Mismatch: We found 10,663 instances of length 1 or 2 (around
1090             15.8%), whereas the validation split contains less than 1% of such short instances.
1091
1092   These issues suggest that models might rely on memorization of short phrases rather than learning
1093
       sentiment relationships effectively.

1102             instance or a "middle length" instance from groups with the same label, and preserving all
1103             instances from groups with varying labels.

       A.9    L IMITATIONS
1270
       While our work on MöbiusAttention shows that it can lead to performance improvements accross
1271   various tasks, it also presents several potential limitations to consider:
1272
1273          • Overfitting: As demonstrated in the ablation study detailed in Section 5 and Appendix A.7,
1274            MöbiusAttention can be susceptible to overfitting in certain architectures. Careful design
1275            choices, such as placement and number of MöbiusAttention layers, are crucial to mitigate
1276            this risk.
1277          • Linear Relationships and Generalizability: Our approach might exhibit inferior per-
1278            formance on tasks that rely heavily on rules with linear relationships or require broad
1279            generalization across diverse categories. This is discussed further in Section 5. For such
1280            tasks, simpler attention mechanisms might be more effective.
1281          • Computational Complexity: MöbiusAttention operates in the complex domain and utilizes
1282            Möbius transformations for the query, requiring eight parameters per attention head. Com-
1283            pared to vanilla attention, this leads to increased computational complexity. To address this,
1284            we reduced the depth of models integrating MöbiusAttention compared to baseline models.
1285            However, our results (Section 5) show that even with fewer layers, MöbiusAttention leads to
1286            performance improvements on many tasks.
1287          • Limited Evaluation Scope: Currently, MöbiusAttention has only been evaluated on GLUE
1288            tasks. Future work should explore its effectiveness on a broader range of NLP tasks, such
1289            as machine translation. Additionally, the potential applicability of MöbiusAttention in
1290            computer vision tasks remains untested and is a promising avenue for further investigation.
