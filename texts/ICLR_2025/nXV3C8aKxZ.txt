009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011              Large neural networks spend most computation on floating point tensor multipli-
012              cations. In this work, we find that a floating point multiplier can be approximated
013              by one integer adder with high precision. We propose the linear-complexity mul-
014              tiplication (L-Mul) algorithm that approximates floating point number multipli-
015              cation with integer addition operations. The new algorithm costs significantly
016              less computation resource than 8-bit floating point multiplication but achieves
017              higher precision. Compared to 8-bit floating point multiplications, the proposed
018              method achieves higher precision but consumes significantly less bit-level com-
019
                 putation. Since multiplying floating point numbers requires substantially higher
                 energy compared to integer addition operations, applying the L-Mul operation in
020
                 tensor processing hardware can potentially reduce 95% energy cost by element-
021
                 wise floating point tensor multiplications and 80% energy cost of dot products.
022              We calculated the theoretical error expectation of L-Mul, and evaluated the algo-
023              rithm on a wide range of textual, visual, and symbolic tasks, including natural lan-
024              guage understanding, structural reasoning, mathematics, and commonsense ques-
025              tion answering. Our numerical analysis experiments agree with the theoretical
026              error estimation, which indicates that L-Mul with 4-bit mantissa achieves compa-
027              rable precision as float8 e4m3 multiplications, and L-Mul with 3-bit mantissa
028              outperforms float8 e5m2. Evaluation results on popular benchmarks show that
029              directly applying L-Mul to the attention mechanism is almost lossless. We further
030
                 show that replacing all floating point multiplications with 3-bit mantissa L-Mul
                 in a transformer model achieves equivalent precision as using float8 e4m3 as
031
                 accumulation precision in both fine-tuning and inference.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Modern artificial intelligence (AI) systems are significant energy consumers. Because of the large
038
      scale computation needed for neural network inference, AI applications based on such models are
      consuming a considerable amount of electricity resource. Reportly, the average electricity consump-
039
      tion of ChatGPT service in early 2023 was 564 MWh per day, equivalent to the total daily electricity
040
      usage of 18,000 families in the United States1 . It is estimated that Google’s AI service could con-
041   sume as much electricity as Ireland (29.3 TWh per year) in the worst-case scenario (de Vries, 2023).
042
043   Reducing the amount of computation needed by neural networks is the key to reduce both energy
044
      consumption and inference speed for large-scale AI models. Neural networks, especially large lan-
      guage models (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2019; Brown, 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al.,
045
      2023; Team et al., 2023), contain a large number of floating point parameters involved in element-
046
      wise and matrix multiplication computations. In transformer (Vaswani, 2017) based LLMs, the
047   attention mechanism is a major bottleneck that limits the computation efficiency. Given a input
048   context of N tokens, the complexity of standard attention mechanism computation is O(N 2 ), in-
049   volving multiplying high dimensional tensors. Besides attention, there are also a large amount of
050   element-wise multiplication and linear transformation computations. In this work, we propose the
051   linear-complexity multiplication (L-Mul) algorithm, which approximates floating point multiplica-
052   tion with integer addition operations. The algorithm can be integrated into existing models at various

136   2.2     L INEAR - COMPLEXITY M ULTIPLICATION (L-M UL )
137
138   We propose L-Mul, a FP multiplication algorithm with O(n) complexity, where n is the bit size
139   of its FP operands. Consider two FP numbers x, y, whose exponents and fractions are xe , ye and
140
      xm , ym respectively, the vanilla FP Mul result is
141            M ul(x, y) = (1 + xm ) · 2xe · (1 + ym ) · 2ye = (1 + xm + ym + xm · ym ) · 2xe +ye
142   plus an xor operation (⊕) to decide the sign of the result. Assume xm and ym are mantissas of m
143   bits. The O(m2 ) mantissa multiplication operation is the complexity bottleneck of this calculation.
144   We remove this operation and introduce a new multiplication algorithm that processes mantissas
145   with a computational complexity of O(m):
146                                                                          
147                                                                          m if m ≤ 3,
148              L-Mul(x, y) = (1 + xm + ym + 2−l(m) ) · 2xe +ye , l(m) = 3 if m = 4,                  (1)
                                                                             
149                                                                             4 if m > 4.
150   The offset exponent l(m) is defined according the observation shown in Figure 3. In the following
151   sections, we show that (1) the L-Mul operation can be implemented by integer Adders, and (2) the
152   algorithm is more accurate and efficient than fp8 multiplications.
153
      The implementation of the algorithm is shown in Figure 2, where we also added the Inline PTX
154
      Assembly code we used to simulate the process on Nvidia GPUs. While Equation (1) contains 4
155
      addition operations, the bit format design of FP numbers helps us implement the L-Mul algorithm
156   with one adder. Since the FP format handles 1 + xm implicitly, we do not have to compute the
157   value of (1 + . . . ). The integer addition operation also automatically send the mantissa carry to
158   the exponent. If the mantissa sum is greater than 2, a carry is automatically added to the exponent.
159   This is different from the rounding process in traditional FP multiplier, where the fraction is man-
160   ually rounded to 1.x and the carry is added to the exponent as an independent step. As a result,
161   the L-Mul algorithm is more efficient than traditional FP multiplication by skipping both mantissa
      multiplication and rounding operations.

496   4   R ELATED W ORK
497
498
      Reducing the computation needed by neural networks while maintain the performance is an impor-
      tant problem which entailed multiple research directions. Typical methods include neural network
499
      pruning, quantization, and improved tensor I/O implementations.
500
501   Pruning. Neural network pruning focuses on improving the inference efficiency by reducing the
502   number of connections among layers (Han et al., 2015a;b; Wang et al., 2020). Neural network prun-
503   ing methods usually involves training. After important weights are identified, the neural networks
504
      are re-trained to further update the selected weights for specific tasks. Different from model pruning,
      the method we proposed is designed for general tasks, requiring no task-specific re-training.
505
506   Optimizing tensor I/O. On regular GPUs, moving tensors between GPU SRAM and high-
507   bandwidth memory (HBM) is the main bottleneck of time and energy consumption. Reducing the
508   I/O operations in transformer models and making the best use of the HBM can significantly improve
509   the efficiency of AI training and inference (Dao et al., 2022; Dao; Kwon et al., 2023). Our method,
510
      which focuses on optimizing arithmetic operations, is orthogonal to this direction.
511   Rounding and quantization. Standard neural network weights are stored as 32-bit or 16-bit FP
512   tensors. However, the full-sized weights takes a considerable amount of GPU memory. To improve
513   the storage efficiency, both weights storage and computation can be conducted in a lower precision,
514   for example, using 16-bit, 8-bit, or 4-bit FP and Int (fp16, bf16 (Kalamkar et al., 2019), fp8-e4m3,
515   fp8-e5m2 (Micikevicius et al., 2023), int8 (Dettmers et al., 2022), fp4, and int4 (Dettmers et al.,
516
      2024)) tensors to represent model weights. Inference with lower-bit parameters usually hurts the
      computation accuracy and impacts the performance of pretrained models, and Integer-based quan-
517
      tization methods spend significant time to handle outlier weights. comparing to the quantization
518
      methods, our method requires less computation but achieves higher accuracy.

      6   C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we introduced L-Mul, an efficient algorithm that approximates floating-point multi-
532
      plication using integer addition. We first demonstrated that the algorithm exhibits linear complexity
533
      relative to the bit size of its floating-point operands. We then showed that the expected accuracy of
534   L-Mul surpasses that of fp8 multiplications while requiring significantly less computational power.
535   To assess the practical impact of L-Mul, we evaluated it on natural language, vision, and mathe-
536   matics benchmarks using popular language models. Our experiments indicate that L-Mul outper-
537   forms 8-bit transformers with lower computational consumption and achieves lossless performance
538   when applied to computation-intensive attention layers without additional training. Based on this
539   evidence, we argue that tensor multiplications in language models can be effectively implemented
      using L-Mul to preserve performance while enabling energy-efficient model deployment.
