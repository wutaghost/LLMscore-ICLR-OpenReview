011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Natural Language to SQL (NL2SQL) provides an effective solution for multi-table
014            question answering (Table QA) to automate data retrieval by transforming simple
015            user queries into SQL commands. It enhances data accessibility and decision-
016
               making processes across various industries. Large Language Model (LLM) based
               NL2SQL methods have been shown to outperform rule-based or neural network-
017
               based NL2SQL methods. However, existing LLM-based NL2SQL approaches
018
               face challenges like inaccurate interpretation of user questions, slow retrieval
019            speeds, erroneous SQL generation, and high operational costs. As there is a
020            lack of datasets specifically designed to evaluate natural language understanding
021            (NLU) in NL2SQL tasks and no models optimized for user question understand-
022            ing in Table QA, we introduce LAIA-NLU, a novel dataset that dissects NLU
023            into task decomposition and keyword extraction. LAIA-NLU contains 1,500 high-
024            quality QA pairs, created through manual review. Using this dataset, we developed
025            LAIA-NLUer, which is capable of effectively interpreting user intent in table-
026            based queries. To further enhance NL2SQL performance in terms of speed, cost,
027
               and accuracy, we also present LAIA-SQL, a retrieval-augmented based NL2SQL
               framework. Experimental results show that LAIA-SQL outperforms state-of-the-
028
               art models, achieving an accuracy improvement to 67.28% in BIRD dataset, a
029
               52.4% reduction in runtime, and a 97% decrease in operational costs. These im-
030            provements demonstrate the potential of our approach to advance multi-table data
031            retrieval and analysis. Our code, dataset, and model will be publicly available to
032            encourage further research in this field.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Table Question Answering (Table QA) is a task to help users who are not proficient in coding
038   skill or advanced spreadsheet software retrieve complex table data by question answering Javaid
039   et al. (2023); Al Naqbi et al. (2024). A leading approach in Table QA is Natural Language to
040   SQL (NL2SQL), which translates natural language queries into SQL, allowing users to interact with
041   databases in everyday language Gao et al. (2023).
042   Recent research shows that NL2SQL methods leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) outper-
043   form other rule-based or neural network based methods significantly Zhang et al. (2024a). A direct
044   approach is prompting LLMs like GPT-4o OpenAI (2024c) to perform related tasks. However, this
045   method often results in SQL statements with logical errors, inaccurate field recognition, and diffi-
046   culty managing multi-table relationships Liu et al. (2024). We hypothesize that these issues arise
047   from LLM’s inadequate understanding of user question in Table QA scenarios.
048   Effective SQL generation requires the model to excel in natural language understanding (NLU),
049   which can be divided into two areas: 1) fine-grained task decomposition and 2) precise keyword
050   extraction. While the former is crucial for complex multi-table reasoning, the latter ensures accurate
051   recognition of table and column names. However, as shown in Figure 1, using LLMs like GPT-4o
052   for task decomposition and keyword extraction still presents challenges, as models may generate
053   insufficient tasks and misidentify keywords. Addressing these challenges requires specialized train-
      ing because it involves understanding and manipulating structured data within a specific context,

112   2     RELATED WORK
113
114   2.1   TABLE Q UESTION A NSWERING
115
      The field of Table QA aims to deliver accurate answers derived from table data through precise and
116   effective reasoning techniques. Initial approaches emphasized discrete reasoning Jin et al. (2022),
117   with notable efforts like TAT-QA Zhu et al. (2021), FinQA Chen et al. (2021), and MVGE Ma et al.
118   (2017) employing internal context learning (ICL), fine-tuning, and pre-training methods. These
119   made significant strides but struggled with adaptability in multi-table scenarios Zhang et al. (2024b).
120   Recently, methods have evolved to convert tabular data into graph structures for enhanced reasoning,
121   as seen with GraphRAG Edge et al. (2024). Despite their promise, these methods remain time-
122   consuming, resource-intensive, and face challenges in accurate graph construction Yu et al. (2024).
123   In parallel, NL2SQL research, which translates natural language questions into SQL queries, of-
124   fers a more efficient and cost-effective solution Gao et al. (2023). NL2SQL technologies are
125   mainly categorized into rule-based, neural network-based, Pre-trained Language Models (PLM)-
126   based, and Large Language Models (LLM)-based approaches Li et al. (2024a). Initially, rule-based
127   approaches prevailed, utilizing predefined rules or semantic parsers Katsogiannis-Meimarakis &
128   Koutrika (2021), but were soon superseded by more scalable neural network techniques. By 2017,
129   PLM methods, particularly those employing models like BERT Devlin (2018), took precedence.
130
      Currently, LLMs, exemplified by GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023), dominate the field, powering ad-
      vanced methods such as CHESS Talaei et al. (2024), DAIL-SQL Gao et al. (2023), and MAC-SQL
131
      Wang et al. (2023). These advanced methods feature specialized modules like filters, evaluators, and
132
      self-correction mechanisms to refine their outputs. Despite their sophistication, LLM-based meth-
133   ods still grapple with challenges like low accuracy, high operational costs, and significant runtime,
134   constraining their practical utility Li et al. (2024a).
135
136   2.2   NATURAL L ANGUAGE U NDERSTANDING
137
138   Natural Language Understanding (NLU) is a cornerstone of AI, enabling machines to interpret and
139   process human language Allen (1988). This field encompasses a wide range of tasks, from keyword
140   extraction to complex question answering Yu et al. (2023). The advent of LLMs like Gemini-Pro
141   Reid et al. (2024), GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023), and Mistral Jiang et al. (2023) has revolutionized
142   NLU, pushing the boundaries of machine comprehension.
143   To further enhance NLU capabilities, researchers have investigated various innovative methods.
144   These include sophisticated text alignment Zha et al. (2024), the integration of human-written expla-
145   nations Liu et al. (2021), and advanced reasoning techniques like Chain of Thought (COT) Wei et al.
146   (2022), Tree of Thought Yao et al. (2024), and Buffer of Thought Yang et al. (2024b). Specialized
147   datasets such as Adversarial NLI Nie et al. (2019) and SemEval-2024 Task 2 Jullien et al. (2024)
148   have been created to evaluate and refine LLMs’ NLU proficiency.
149   Despite these advancements, substantial challenges persist in NLU, especially in table QA. While
150   large language models (LLMs) exhibit impressive reasoning capabilities, they often struggle with
151   precise information extraction and reasoning from tabular data. A crucial limitation is their inability
152   to distinguish between meaningful and nonsensical language in user queries, and to consistently
153   identify and extract relevant keywords corresponding to filter values, column names, or table names
154   in a database. This deficiency underscores the pressing need for specialized datasets and fine-tuned
155
      models tailored specifically for NLU in table QA.

179   3.1       DATA S OURCES
180
181   LAIA-NLU was derived from the BIRD dataset Li et al. (2024c) for its validated origins and exten-
182   sive research use. BIRD comprises 12,751 text-to-SQL pairs across 95 databases, totaling 33.4 GB
183   and spanning 37 professional domains, designed specifically for evaluating and training NL2SQL
184   methods. It integrates 80 open-source relational databases from platforms like Kaggle and Rela-
185   tion.vit. To prevent data leakage, 15 additional relational databases were created for a hidden test
186   set. The BIRD team used crowdsourcing to collect natural language questions paired with corre-
187
      sponding SQLs. Given its broad, validated origins and extensive research use, BIRD was chosen as
      our data source.

190   3.2       S ELECTION AND A NNOTATION
191
192   We randomly selected 1,500 instances from the BIRD dataset’s training data Li et al. (2024c). Each
193   instance comprises a user question and the corresponding ground truth SQL query. Initially, we
194
      employed GPT-4o OpenAI (2024c) to perform task decomposition and keyword extraction. As
      illustrated in Figure 2, task decomposition involved breaking down the user question into two com-
195
      ponents: the main task and sub-tasks. The main task represents the primary goal derived from the
196
      user question, while sub-tasks further refine the main task. In the keyword extraction phase, key-
197   words were categorized into two types: object and implementation. The object category includes
198   terms related to table and column names in the user question, while implementation involves filter-
199   ing criteria represented by a dictionary, where the keys denote filtering actions and the values specify
200   the conditions. These elements collectively facilitate similarity matching within the database.
201
      However, despite implementing Chain of Thought (CoT) Wei et al. (2022) and few-shot techniques
202
      Brown (2020), GPT-4o’s performance in interpreting user queries was suboptimal. As shown on the
203   left side of Figure 2, GPT-4o often produced redundant or incomplete tasks and extracted incorrect
204   keywords. This necessitated manual refinement of the generated raw data.
205
206
      Therefore, we invited three expert annotators to review and correct GPT-4o generated data. Our
      annotation strategy entailed a three-phase cyclic process to ensure cross-validation and accuracy.
207
      Each annotator began with different subsets (A, B, C) before Phase 1, exchanging and reviewing
208
      modified subsets in subsequential phases until all data was thoroughly evaluated by all annotators.
209   As depicted in the Human Validation Process in Figure 2, the three-step process follows:
210
211   1. Evaluate Task Decomposition Annotators first reviewed each question manually to assess the
212
      accuracy of the main tasks and sub-tasks generated by GPT-4o. They checked for logical consis-
      tency, removed redundant tasks, and added any missing relevant tasks manually.
213
214   2. Evaluate Keyword Extraction Keywords were categorized into objects and implementations.
215   Annotators compared the keywords generated by GPT-4o with the user questions and corresponding
      ground truth SQL elements (such as filters, table names, and column names), to ensure accuracy.

      Task Decomposition Inspired by the COT Wei et al. (2022) reasoning approach, we decompose user
295   questions into manageable components, addressing the inherent complexity and multi-task nature of
296   user inquiries. Compared to previous NL2SQL methods, we employed two-level COT reasoning,
297   which breaks down a user question into a main task and sub tasks. The main task represents the
298   primary goal derived from the user question, while sub tasks refine main task further. This dis-
299   tinction aids the generation model in efficiently producing SQL code by clarifying the hierarchy of
300   tasks. Specifically, we instruct the generation model that the main task corresponds to the main com-
301   ponent following ”SELECT,” and the sub tasks correspond to operations such as ”INNER JOIN,”
302   ”WHERE,” and ”CASE WHEN,” among others. However, as illustrated in Figure 2, general mod-
303
      els like GPT-4o sometimes incorrectly decompose tasks or generate irrelevant tasks, demonstrating
      unstable performance. To enhance stability and reliability, we employed supervised fine-tuning for
304
      consistent task decomposition.
305
306   Keyword Extraction Prior methods involved merely breaking down sentences into individual key-
307   words, which often resulted in irrelevant keywords. In our approach, we have classified keywords
308   into two distinct categories: object and implementation, improving the accuracy. The object cate-
309
      gory encompasses terms associated with table and column names found in the user’s query, whereas
      implementation pertains to filtering criteria, represented by a dictionary where the keys indicate fil-
310
      tering actions and the values denote the specified conditions. To enhance the accuracy of keyword
311
      extraction, we employed In-Context Learning (ICL) techniques to provide the LLM with multiple
312   examples. However, as illustrated in Figure 2, GPT-4o tends to generate irrelevant or excessive key-
313   words. To address this issue, we fine-tuned the smaller model like Mistral-7B Jiang et al. (2023)
314   using LAIA-NLU, ultimately enhancing the accuracy of keyword extraction.

      4.2        E NTITY R ETRIEVAL
317
318   After extracting the keywords, the subsequent step involves retrieving the corresponding database
319   entities, including table names, column names, table values, and textual descriptions (column and
320   value descriptions). The entity retrieval component is composed of three modules: the embedder,
321   retriever, and reranker. Initially, all table data are encoded and stored in the Chroma database.
322   The embedder first encodes the keywords obtained during the user question understanding phase.
323   This encoded information is then fed into the retriever to search the relevant database, yielding five
      entities that resemble the keywords. These five entities are subsequently passed to the reranker,


                                                                                                                   6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      which recalculates similarity scores and reorders them, ultimately selecting the two most similar
325   entities. Based on these three modules, we divide entity retrieval into two tasks: Database Retrieval
326   and Textual Description Retrieval.
327
328
      Database Retrieval In this task, our objective is to retrieve column names and table values from the
      database using the keywords. A column name refers to the name designated to each column, and
329
      table values are the data contained in each cell of the table, excluding the column names. To expedite
330
      the retrieval process given the extensive volume of database values, we employ two methods: Min-
331   Hash Zhu et al. (2016) + Jaccard Score (Equation 1 and 2) and BM25 Robertson et al. (2009). For
332   column names, no similarity score threshold is established during retrieval; all scores greater than 0
333   are recorded, and the top five highest-scoring entities are selected. For table values, if the keyword
334   is purely numeric, we set a rule that only entities exactly matching the keyword are considered. For
335   keywords comprising both text and numbers, no threshold is applied, and the top five highest scor-
336   ing entities are selected. These shortlisted entities are then fed back into the reranker for re-ranking
337   to identify the two most similar entities. As illustrated in Figure 4, the retrieved entities are cross-
338   referenced to obtain their corresponding table and column names, which are then deduplicated and
339
      categorized.

      Textual Description Retrieval Textual descriptions encompass two types of information: column
347
      descriptions and value descriptions. Column descriptions provide additional details about the col-
348   umn names, whereas value descriptions explain the data within the columns, such as how these
349   values were derived. Given the smaller dataset in this task, the retrieval method differs from that
350   used in database retrieval. We directly employ an embedding model to encode the data and then
351   use cosine similarity within the retriever to calculate scores, identifying the top five most similar
352   entities. These entities are subsequently re-ranked using a specialized reranker model to determine
353   the final order of relevance.

355   4.3   G ENERATION
356
357   The generation process in LAIA-SQL involves two phases: SQL Generation and Revision.
358   SQL Generation: Using ICL, we guide general LLMs, like GPT-4o OpenAI (2024c), to generate
359   SQL statements. The prompts for this task are meticulously structured into four segments: data
360   schema, user question reasoning, constraints, and incentives. The data schema component includes
361   details such as data formats, column names, table names, and examples, integrating the entity infor-
362   mation retrieved in the Entity Retrieval module. The user question reasoning segment incorporates
363   the user’s question, main and sub tasks identified in User Question Understanding module, and hints
364
      derived from the dataset. By compiling these details into the prompt, the model produces an initial
      SQL statement.
365
366   Revision: As illustrated in Figure 4, the initial SQL statements may include errors such as incor-
367   rect table names, misaligned columns, or extraneous symbols. To rectify these issues, we feed the
368   erroneous SQL statements along with their corresponding error messages back into the LLM for re-
369   vision. This iterative process results in syntactically correct and operational SQL queries, ultimately
370
      yielding the correct answers.

417   BLEU, ROUGE and GPT-4o Score In the evaluation of task decomposition in NLU, we assessed
418   the quality of the generated reasoning results against human-labeled ground truth result using BLEU,
419   ROUGE, and GPT-4o scores. Specifically, BLEU-1 and BLEU-2 provide insight into the linguistic
420   accuracy by measuring n-gram matches between generated descriptions and ground truth Papineni
421   et al. (2002). ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L evaluate the overlap of n-grams, sequences,
422
      and pairs of words, offering a measure of the descriptions’ comprehensiveness and relevance Lin
      (2004). Additionally, a five-point Likert scale evaluation by GPT-4o helps gauge the overall quality
423
      and similarity to human annotations Zheng et al. (2023).
424
425   F1 Score For keyword extraction tasks in NLU, the model’s performance was evaluated using pre-
426   cision, recall, and finally get the F1 score. These metrics provide a balance between the correctness
427   of the extracted keywords and the model’s recall capability, thereby offering a holistic view of its
428
      extraction efficiency.
429   Execution Accuracy (EX) Execution accuracy was used to measure the correctness of SQL queries
430   by comparing the results of executed predicted queries against reference queries on specific database
431   instances. This metric not only ensures the semantic correctness but also accounts for variations in
      SQL formulations that yield the same results.

515   6   L IMITATION
516
517
      While our model surpasses many state-of-the-art NL2SQL methods, its accuracy still falls short for
518
      practical use. Fine-tuning on specific datasets is essential for satisfactory performance, highlighting
519   the need for enhanced generalizability across varied domains. Computational limitations confined us
520   to training smaller models; larger models like DeepSeek-V2-Coder-236B and Llama3.1-70B could
521   potentially offer superior performance over our current 22B model, thereby significantly improv-
522   ing accuracy. Additionally, the Entity Retrieval component of LAIA-SQL employs MinHash with
523   Jaccard Score and BM25, resulting in suboptimal retrieval performance. Leveraging advanced RAG
524   modules could enhance this aspect. Furthermore, LAIA-NLU dataset is limited to 1500 samples due
525   to resource constraints, affecting the LAIA-NLUer model’s robustness. The scarcity of high-quality
526   data, exacerbated by copyright restrictions, presents a significant challenge. Future work should
527
      prioritize data augmentation techniques and innovative methods to mitigate data scarcity, as well as
      improving computational resources to explore more advanced models.
