009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks (GNNs) are widely used to make predictions on graph-
012            structured data in urban spatiotemporal forecasting applications, such as predicting
013            infrastructure problems and weather events. In urban settings, nodes have a true
014            latent state (e.g., street condition) that is sparsely observed (e.g., via government
015            inspection ratings). We more frequently observe biased proxies for the latent state
016
               (e.g., via crowdsourced reports) that correlate with resident demographics. We
               introduce a GNN-based model that uses both unbiased rating data and biased
017
               reporting data to predict the true latent state. We show that our approach can
018
               both recover the latent state at each node and quantify the reporting biases. We
019            apply our model to a case study of urban incidents using reporting data from
020            New York City 311 complaints across 141 complaint types and rating data from
021            government inspections. We show (i) that our model predicts more correlated
022            ground truth latent states compared to prior work which trains models only on the
023            biased reporting data, (ii) that our model’s inferred reporting biases capture known
024            demographic biases, and (iii) that our model’s learned ratings capture correlations
025            across locations and between complaint types. Especially in urban crowdsourcing
026            applications, our analysis reveals a widely applicable approach for using GNNs
027
               and sparse ground truth data to estimate latent states.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Graph neural networks (GNNs) have emerged as powerful and expressive models for making pre-
032   dictions on graph-structured data, especially for urban applications such as air quality monitoring,
033   forecasting traffic flows, predicting housing prices, and modeling the spread of epidemics (Xie et al.,
034   2019; Roy et al., 2021; Brimos et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Zhan & Datta, 2024). In urban planning –
035   our empirical setting – government officials often wish to know where urban incidents like rodents
036   or floods truly occur so they can make downstream resource allocation decisions; however, this
037   ground truth is typically unobserved and must be predicted. GNNs are a powerful tool to make these
038   predictions, as they can naturally encode spatial correlations of the ground truth states across nodes
039
      in a graph (e.g., neighborhoods in a city). For example, if a flood has occurred in one neighborhood,
      the adjacent neighborhoods are also likely to be flooded.
040
041   Estimating latent ground truth for the hundreds of types of incidents that occur in a city is challenging.
042   Nevertheless, there are two sources of information we can use, each with its own limitations. First, we
043   observe the ground truth state via government inspections which generate ratings for neighborhoods.
044   For example, New York City conducts street inspections for every street and rates them from 1-10.
045
      Importantly, these inspections are only conducted for some incident types and neighborhoods and are
      thus sparsely observed. These settings also often have another source of data: frequently observed,
046
      biased proxies of the latent state, e.g., via crowdsourced reports of incidents. Unlike ratings, reports
047
      are observed across all incident types, all neighborhoods, and multiple points in time. However,
048   previous work has established that underreporting is pervasive and heterogeneous (Clark et al., 2020;
049   Kontokosta & Hong, 2021; Agostini et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024); in different neighborhoods that
050   face similar incidents, residents often report those incidents at different rates. This presents an
051   identifiability issue; if one neighborhood logs more reports than another, it is unclear whether the
052   former has a worse ground truth or if given the same ground truth, the latter is less likely to report.
053   Thus, reports may not accurately predict ground truth across all neighborhoods as the same ground
      truth state may have different reporting patterns across the city. For example, Casey et al. (2018)

072   learning only from reporting data risks introducing bias against underserved populations.
073   We propose a novel GNN-based approach to capture the above characteristics of urban incident
074   reporting – (i) high dimensionality: we have crowdsourced reports across many types (e.g., rodents,
075   food poisoning, fallen trees, etc.) over time; (ii) frequently observed, biased reporting data: our
076   reports capture whether incidents of each type were reported in each node in each granular time
077   period; and (iii) sparsely observed, ground truth rating data: the city conducts periodic inspections
078   which yield ratings for a sparse set of types, nodes, and time periods.
079   Our GNN-based approach jointly models both the the true latent state and the probability of a report
080   for each node (neighborhood) across all incident types. Summarized in Figure 1, the model uses a
081   GNN to capture spatial correlations in the ground truth state (e.g., street condition) and estimates how
082   the reporting probability varies across nodes as a function of demographics. We train our model to
083   simultaneously predict (i) ground truth ratings using learned node and type embeddings and (ii) how
084   the likelihood of reporting varies by demographics, conditional on ground truth state. Since there is
085   no way to distinguish between neighborhoods which truly do not have problems and neighborhoods
086   that do not report them, estimating this model is impossible without some information to constrain
087
      or identify the ground truth state. We show on semi-synthetic data that by supplying the model
      with sparse ground truth rating data, we are able to identify which neighborhoods systematically
088
      underreport incidents. Thus, on types for which ground truth ratings are not observed, we are still
089
      able to infer the ground truth and correct for reporting biases.
090
091   We apply our model to a case study of New York City 311 complaints (crowdsourced reports),
092   leveraging 55 million reports across 141 types over two years. We combine this with a carefully
093
      curated dataset of ground truth ratings which are sourced from 300k government inspections across
      5 types in the same time frame. Using both semi-synthetic simulations and real data, we show that
094
      our approach can (i) estimate the ground truth inspection ratings and (ii) quantify the biases in the
095
      frequently observed reporting data, capturing the fact that different neighborhoods report similar
096   issues at different rates. We find that the sparsely observed, ground-truth rating data and the frequently
097   observed, biased reporting data both confer benefits. Using both the ground truth rating data and the
098   reporting data, our model can infer ground truth ratings that are 2× more correlated than a model that
099   only uses reporting data. Additionally, our model predicts ratings that more plausibly reflect spatial
100   correlations between nodes compared to a model that uses rating data alone. We also show that our
101   model’s inferred reporting biases align with known demographic patterns of underreporting and that
102   our model’s learned ratings capture both spatial correlations across neighborhoods and correlations
103   across 311 complaint types. We will release code and data to replicate all experiments.
104   Although our primary application is to urban crowdsourcing, our approach is broadly applicable to
105   other GNN prediction tasks where both sparsely observed, ground truth data and frequently observed,
106   biased data are available. Specific application areas include other urban challenges (such as estimating
107   air quality using both resident reports and sparse sensor measurements) and spatiotemporal processes
      (such as epidemic forecasting using both internet search data and sparse official health reports). Our

113   2   R ELATED WORK
114
115   Our work relates to and extends several literatures: (1) urban spatiotemporal modeling and related
116   methodology, including semi-supervised learning, (2) learning from noisy and human-reported data,
117   and (3) GNN learning on noisy graphs. Our work is at the intersection of these literatures, augmenting
118   biased labels with ground truth for graph learning applied to urban settings.
119
120   Spatiotemporal modeling: GNNs are a natural fit for high-dimensional spatiotemporal modeling
121   in applications like traffic forecasting, epidemic forecasting, and molecular dynamics (Kapoor et al.,
122   2020; Roy et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a;b; He et al., 2023; Pineda et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024).
123   Several works also design ways to encode spatiotemporal information in GNNs, including positional
124   encoders (Klemmer et al., 2023), kriging convolutional networks (Appleby et al., 2020), and inductive
125   kriging (Wu et al., 2021). Non-GNN-based spatiotemporal models, including Bayesian, clustering,
126   and matrix factorization models, have also been used for urban issues like crime (Hu et al., 2018),
127   pedestrian traffic (Zaouche & Bode, 2023), air pollution (Sarto et al., 2016), urban flow (Pan et al.,
128
      2019), and infrastructure monitoring (Budde et al., 2014).
129   For 311 complaints in particular, prior works have quantified underreporting of floods using spa-
130   tiotemporal models (Agostini et al., 2024) and have more broadly quantified the geographic and
131   demographic patterns of underreporting (Kontokosta et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2017; Kontokosta
132   & Hong, 2021). Disparities in incident reporting rates lead to downstream inequities in resource
133   allocation, so understanding the patterns of underreporting is crucial (Liu et al., 2024). Especially
134
      notable in relation to our work, one prior study in Washington, D.C. showed that 311 reports are poor
      predictors of ground truth ratings, in line with our hypotheses and findings (Casey et al., 2018). Our
135
      work extends this literature by proposing a specific approach to overcome the limitations of biased
136
      reporting data: leveraging sparse ground truth.
137
138
139   Other methodological areas: Our work ties in with three broad methodological areas: semi-
140   supervised learning, learning from noisy and human-reported data, and GNN learning on noisy
141
      graphs. A long line of prior work has dealt with semi-supervised learning on graphs and noisy
      labels. Several prior works address our core issue of semi-supervised learning with sparsely observed,
142
      ground truth labels and frequently observed proxies. In some works, the proxy labels are the outputs
143
      of a machine learning model (Arazo et al., 2020) which are debiased to produce better predictions
144   (Zhang et al., 2021). Wang et al. (2022c) tries to learn which proxies are reliable and upweights those
145   which are predicted to be reliable. Our work extends this literature, by showing that models that use
146   both reliable inspection ratings and less reliable crowdsourced reports improve upon models which
147   only use the less reliable reports.
148
      One common source of frequently observed, biased data is human behavior (such as crowdsourced
149
      reports). Prior work has shown that models which use this biased data can affect high-stakes
150   decisions (Lum & Isaac, 2016; Obermeyer et al., 2019; Mullainathan & Obermeyer, 2021) and
151   that crowdsourced labels vary across annotators and often correlate with demographics, indicating
152   that different groups may perceive the same data (e.g., text, image, incident/problem) differently
153   (Chakraborty et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2017; Ding et al., 2022). There is also work that attempts to
154   resolve a ground truth label from several annotations (Dawid & Skene, 1979; Bach et al., 2017).
155
      Finally, several prior works provide methods for learning on noisy graphs. For instance, in social
156   and citation networks the graph itself is often noisy and dynamic, leading to spurious correlations
157   between nodes (Hamilton et al., 2017). Methods such as graph attention and causal regularization
158   overcome these issues by filtering spurious correlations from causally relevant ones (Wang et al.,
159   2019; 2022a; Wu et al., 2023). Other methods deal with noisy and sparse labels for nodes. Dai et al.
160   (2021) and Qian et al. (2023) generate pseudolabels for nodes by aggregating information from the
161   most similar labeled nodes. Crucially, these methods do not augment noisy node labels (i.e., reports)
      with ground truth data. Applications of the above techniques include inferring links in gene regulatory


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      networks (Singh et al., 2024), estimating the spread of infectious diseases (Tomy et al., 2022), and
163   detecting vulnerabilities in software (Cheng et al., 2021).
164
165   Our work extends and combines insights from these areas: we use a GNN to model both sparsely
166
      observed, unbiased data and frequently observed, biased data generated from human behavior. We
      use both data sources to predict ground truth latent states and learn about reporting biases.

169   3    M ODEL
170
171   Approach overview: Our GNN model is summarized in Figure 1. The purpose of our model
172   is to (i) estimate the true latent state of a particular incident type at a particular location – e.g.,
173   what is the true street condition in a particular neighborhood?; and (ii) to quantify biases in the
174
      observed reporting data – e.g., which neighborhoods systematically underreport incidents and how do
      reporting behaviors correlate with demographics? In many urban settings, models are fit using only
175
      the frequently observed reporting data, resulting in biased predictions (Xu et al., 2017; Casey et al.,
176
      2018; Li et al., 2020; Hacker et al., 2022). In contrast, our approach learns the true latent state using
177   both the frequently observed, biased reporting data and the sparsely observed, unbiased rating data.
178
179   Notation: Consider a network G with n nodes and adjacency matrix E. In our case study, nodes
180   are indexed by i and represent neighborhoods, and edges connect adjacent neighborhoods. Each node
181   i has features Xi ∈ RD , where D is the number of features. These features include demographic
182   factors that may influence reporting rates. There are τ incident types indexed by k (e.g., rodents,
183   floods, etc.). We index time by t (e.g., weeks). We have two types of data: sparsely observed,
184   unbiased true state measures (e.g., inspection ratings) and frequently observed, biased data (e.g.,
185   crowdsourced reports).
186
187   Observed data: For some node/type/time tuples, we observe inspection ratings rikt ∈ R. In
188
      our urban reporting case study, we source ratings from city government inspections for various
      government services (e.g., street ratings, park ratings, etc.). A lower inspection rating indicates a
189
      worse true state; e.g., a street with a lower rating has more damage. We normalize (z-score) the
190
      inspection ratings for each type across time and nodes. We use ratings from incident types for which
191   inspections are conducted randomly and periodically (as opposed to in response to potentially biased
192   reports) so that ratings are unbiased observations of the true latent state.1 However, our observed
193   inspection rating data is sparse and only available for a subset of nodes, types, and times.
194
      We also observe reports of incidents Tikt ∈ {0, 1}, where Tikt = 1 indicates that an incident of type
195
      k was reported for node i at time t. In our urban reporting case study, we source reports from New
196
      York City’s resident reporting system, NYC311. Reports are obtained from residents and are thus
197   biased proxies of the true latent state that correlate with resident demographics.
198
199   Examples of rikt and Tikt data exist in many settings. In environmental monitoring, rikt may be
200
      geographically sparse sensor measurements of air quality, and Tikt may be resident reports of air
      quality. In epidemic forecasting, rikt may be sparse health reports, and Tikt may be search data
201
      (Bauer & Aschenbruck, 2018; Chang et al., 2024).
202
203
      Model: We model ratings using a node embedding and a type embedding. Node i’s embedding
204
      en [i] ∈ REn , where En is the embedding dimension, is a low-dimensional representation of a node
205   and captures the node’s attributes and position. The node embeddings are learned using a GNN
206   (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Veličković et al., 2018), which is a deep learning model that leverages graph-
207   structured data by iteratively aggregating and transforming features from neighboring nodes. Thus
208   our node embeddings are spatially correlated, mirroring the correlation of true incident occurrence
209   across neighborhoods. We also learn type k’s embedding eτ [k] ∈ REτ , where Eτ is the embedding
210   dimension. The type embedding is a low-dimensional representation of a type and captures the type’s
211   features, similarity to other types, and relationship to nodes in the graph. Thus our type embeddings
212   capture correlations across types.

      6    D ISCUSSION
517
518
      We address the challenging problem of estimating graph neural networks (GNNs) in settings where
519
      we observe biased outcome data. In these settings, nodes have a true latent state that is sparsely
520
      observed (e.g., via inspection ratings). We often also frequently observe biased proxies of the latent
521   states (e.g., via crowdsourced reports). We propose a GNN-based model that uses frequently observed,
522   biased reporting data and sparsely observed, unbiased rating data. We apply our model to New York
523   City 311 data and show that (i) our model makes better predictions of the ground truth latent state
524   compared to a baseline model trained only on reporting data, (ii) our model’s inferred reporting biases
525   capture known demographic factors of underreporting, and (iii) our model’s learned ratings capture
526   correlation between nodes and 311 complaint types.
527   Our model opens several avenues for additional applications and future work. Here we experimented
528   with a particular model for reporting propensity; one line of future work could investigate whether
529   other methods of incorporating reporting data can produce more accurate rating predictions. Another
530   natural direction is to apply our model to other urban settings with biased reporting data. As discussed
531   in this paper, reporting biases often correlate with demographics, so learning only from reporting
532   data risks introducing bias against underserved populations. Beyond urban applications, other GNN
533   tasks may also have sparsely observed ground truth and frequently observed biased proxies. In such
534   settings, our model is a key advance in using GNNs to estimate ground truth latent states.
