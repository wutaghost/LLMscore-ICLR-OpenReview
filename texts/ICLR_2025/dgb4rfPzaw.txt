009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Image-based autoregressive next-token prediction offers a promising avenue for
012            developing world video simulators for autonomous driving. However, applica-
013            tions of these autoregressive models for common perception tasks such as ge-
014            ometric and semantic understanding remains under-explored, largely due to the
015            difficulty of applying discrete token modeling to perception tasks. In this paper,
016            we introduce PerceptionLM, an end-to-end framework that leverages autoregres-
017            sive world simulators to effectively improve Perception tasks. It consists of a
018
               token-based pretraining stage and a novel fine-tuning stage that adapts discrete to-
               kens to continuous embeddings for perception tasks. During pretraining, we lever-
019
               age the world knowledge from Segment Anything and Depth Anything through
020
               autoregressive next-token prediction to imbue the model with world knowledge
021            from multiple vision modalities. During fine-tuning, we propose a novel decoder
022            adaptor to fuse discrete tokens with continuous embeddings from image encoders,
023            which overcomes the limitations of discrete tokens. With PerceptionLM, we ob-
024            serve impressive scaling properties, where quality is consistently improved when
025            providing more training compute or longer temporal context. On multiple public
026            benchmarks including nuScenes, nuImages, Waymo Open Dataset, and Waymo
027            Open Motion Dataset, PerceptionLM demonstrates significant performance im-
028            provements for common perception tasks such as depth estimation and semantic
029
               segmentation, highlighting its potential for scaling vision-only foundation models
               for autonomous driving.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Autoregressive Transformers, which perform next-token prediction based on previously observed
035
      sequences, have emerged as a powerful class of generative models for language understanding (Rad-
036   ford et al., 2019; Kaplan et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023). Previous studies (Zhao et al., 2024; Team
037   et al., 2023; Radford, 2018) have shown that large language models pretrained on Internet-scale data
038   can encapsulate rich world knowledge, making them highly effective for a variety of downstream
039   tasks, including chat bots (Kalyan, 2023; Radford et al., 2019), image or audio comprehension (Pi
040   et al., 2024; Borsos et al., 2023), and code generation (Ugare et al., 2024).
041
      Building upon these capabilities, autoregressive Transformer models have recently been extended
042   to autonomous driving for world modeling (Hu et al., 2023). Trained on large unannotated driving
043   videos that are tokenized into sequences of discrete tokens, these world models predict future image
044   tokens by autoregressively generating next tokens from prior token sequences. Although these mod-
045   els show great promise in generating realistic driving scenarios, prior research has predominantly
046   focused on visual quality. However, the extent to which such image-based autoregressive models in-
047   herently capture world knowledge, and how this knowledge can be effectively leveraged to improve
048   perception tasks, such as geometric and semantic understanding, remains under explored.
049   A major challenge in adapting autoregressive models for perception tasks lies in their reliance on
050   tokenized representations. While discrete tokens provide a compact and unified format for multi-
051   ple modalities, the tokenization process is inherently error-prone and often unsuitable for certain
052   perception tasks in autonomous driving. For example, tasks such as monocular depth estimation
053   with LiDAR ground-truth supervision involve handling sparse data, making it difficult to accurately
      represent such signals using discrete tokens. This sparsity can lead to significant information loss

054
      during the tokenization process, limiting the model’s ability to reconstruct accurate depth maps. In
055   contrast, classical perception pipelines are more adept at learning sparse signals through their spe-
056   cialized decoders and masked losses, which preserve the integrity of sparse data throughout training.
057   If we can integrate the strengths of both approaches, we can potentially extend the autoregressive
058   world simulator to perform a wider range of tasks while leveraging its capacity to encode and reason
059   over rich world knowledge.
060
      To address these challenges, we introduce PerceptionLM, i.e, Perception as Language Modeling,
061   a novel end-to-end framework that integrates continuous perception signals with an Autoregres-
062   sive Transformer to enhance perception tasks. PerceptionLM comprises two stages: a token-based
063   pretraining stage and a novel fine-tuning stage that bridges discrete tokens with continuous em-
064   beddings. During pretraining, we leverage world knowledge from existing models, such as Segment
065   Anything (Kirillov et al., 2023) and Depth Anything (Yang et al., 2024), through a next-token predic-
066   tion objective, enabling the model to incorporate rich visual information from multiple modalities.
067   In the fine-tuning stage, we employ a novel decoder adaptor that fuses discrete tokens with con-
068   tinuous embeddings from image encoders, overcoming the limitations of relying solely on discrete
069
      tokens for perception tasks. Extensive experiments on public benchmarks, including nuScenes,
      nuImages (Caesar et al., 2020), Waymo Open Dataset (Sun et al., 2020), and Waymo Open Mo-
070
      tion Dataset (Chen et al., 2024) demonstrate that PerceptionLM achieves significant performance
071
      improvements for both sparsely-supervised perception tasks, such as depth estimation, and densely-
072   supervised tasks, such as semantic segmentation. Our findings highlight the potential of Percep-
073   tionLM to scale vision-only foundation models for autonomous driving and utilize world knowledge
074   effectively for perception applications.
075
      In summary, our contributions are:
076
077         • We propose a novel pretraining scheme which uses autoregressive future prediction for
078           long temporal sequences, operating on both tokenized images and outputs from foundation
079           models for depth and segmentation.
080         • We perform extensive scaling experiments to demonstrate the scaling property for our ar-
081           chitecture.
082         • We propose a novel lightweight adaptor that integrates the autoregressive world simulator,
083           which operates on discrete visual tokens, with a convolutional encoder-decoder network
084           for dense predictions.
085
            • We conduct extensive experiments and ablations on the improvements provided by our
086           architecture for downstream tasks in depth estimation and semantic segmentation across
087           multiple datasets, demonstrating the efficacy of our model and the improvements provided
088           by scaling.

      World Simulation Leveraging physical world simulation has benefited various computer vision
      tasks. Early works such as MuZero (Schrittwieser et al., 2020) and DreamerV2 (Hafner et al.,
094
      2020) laid the foundation by modeling latent dynamics for reinforcement learning, with DreamerV2
095
      showing notable improvements by switching to discrete latents. VAE-RNN (Ha & Schmidhuber,
096   2018) further refines the world model paradigm by encoding observations with VAE and modeling
097   temporal dynamics with MDN-RNN (Graves, 2013). Recent work, like Drive-WM (Wang et al.,
098   2024) and DriveWorld (Min et al., 2024), adapt world models to visual forecasting and 4D scene
099   understanding, learning spatial-temporal representations to improve perception and planning. Other
100   advancements such as GAIA-1 (Hu et al., 2023) and Copilot4D (Zhang et al., 2023) apply discrete
101   token-based approaches to model sequences of sensor inputs for more effective future state pre-
102   diction in 2D and 3D spaces, respectively. In parallel, approaches like DreamTeacher (Li et al.,
103   2023) and Diffusion World Model (Ding et al., 2024) demonstrate how diffusion generative models
104
      could be used for learning world models. These works underscore the growing importance of world
      models in enabling scalable and robust world knowledge understanding for general perception tasks
105
      across diverse visual domains.

140   Autoregressive Visual Modeling Initially applied in language models like GPT (Vaswani et al.,
141   2023; Radford et al., 2019; Team et al., 2023) for next-token prediction, Autoregressive (AR) mod-
142   eling has extended to vision tasks, tokenizing images and videos into sequences for generation (Yu
143   et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2022). The AR universal learner paper (Malach, 2023) fur-
144   ther demonstrates that even simple autoregressive models can approximate complex functions like
145   those computable by Turing machines, highlighting the power of next-token prediction beyond just
146   architecture. To tackle scalability in high-resolution image generation tasks, VAR (Tian et al., 2024)
147   introduces multi-scale token prediction, improving efficiency and quality by predicting at different
148
      image resolutions. Expanding into multi-modality, 4M and 4M-21 (Mizrahi et al., 2024; Bachmann
      et al., 2024) extend order-agnostic masked AR models to handle a variety of tasks across multiple
149
      modalities, enabling better generalization across tasks like text, image, and depth prediction through
150
      masked modeling strategies. On the temporal front, VideoPoet (Kondratyuk et al., 2023) applies
151   next-token prediction for zero-shot video generation, generating coherent video sequences by pre-
152   dicting video tokens over time. Transfusion (Zhou et al., 2024) combines the diffusion loss with
153   autoregressive transformer to train on mixed-modality data. Inspired by the success of tokenized
154   pre-training in the large language domain, where discrete representations have proven effective in
155   capturing structured world knowledge, we aim to further explore the potential of discrete autore-
156   gressive pretraining for vision tasks with mixed discrete and continuous tokens.

      3.1   G ENERATING A LARGE SCALE PRETRAINING DATASET WITH WORLD KNOWLEDGE
188
189   A high quality dataset is key to any large scale model training. In this work, we curate a large
190   scale private dataset of driving videos with 1 billion images, on top of which we generate additional
191   modalities from existing open-source foundation models, which we tokenize and combine with the
192   original images.
193   Imparting World Knowledge: While it has been shown that these models that predict future images
194   can learn to reason about the world beyond visual appearance, we posit that learning can be further
195   improved by adding other modalities such as geometry and semantics explicitly into the model train-
196   ing. In order to augment the reasoning into other modalities, we leverage existing foundation models
197   to generate high-quality pseudo-labels, facilitating the creation of large scale, aligned multi-modal
198   data. In particular, we posit that a strong, scalable perception model needs to be able to process both
199   geometric and semantic information about a scene. In this work, we propose to directly imbue the
200   model with this information by adding additional modalities which represent geometry and seman-
201
      tics to the set of tokens our model reasons on. In particular, we adopt Depth Anything (Yang et al.,
      2024) to generate relative depth maps in order to encode a geometric view of the scene, and Segment
202
      Anything to produce segmentation masks, to encode a semantic view of the scene. As the order of
203
      the masks is permutation invariant, we opt to instead produce edge maps (i.e. a binary mask for
204   edge region) from the segmentation masks in order to retain a consistent set of labels. While other
205   text-aligned foundation models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) are available, we opt to use dense
206   per-pixel models in order to retain a consistent output representation for tokenization. In order to
207   enable scaling of our model to billions of parameters, we curate an internal dataset with one billion
208   driving images, on which we generate the corresponding relative depth and edge maps.
209
      Tokenization: To unify the representation space for multiple modalities, we adopt a pretrained ViT-
210   VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021) to convert each modality (image, depth, edge) into
211   a sequence of discrete tokens. For the depth and edge modalities, we broadcast along the channel
212   dimension to form a RGB image, and normalize the values to [0, 1] before tokenization. We carefully
213   select modalities for pretraining and fine-tuning to ensure that the fine-tuning task is either unseen
214   (e.g., semantic segmentation vs edge detection) or represented differently (e.g., absolute depth vs
215   relative depth). The tokenization process is pre-computed for reuse, resulting in a substantial corpus
      of multi-modal visual token sequences.

      3.2   P ERCEPTION LM A RCHITECTURE
217
218   As shown in Fig. 2, PerceptionLM consists of a causal Transformer backbone that reasons about
219   the interactions between multi-modal temporal context and continuous visual signals, a canonical
220   encoder-decoder architecture for extracting continuous visual signals and performing a wide range
221   of perception tasks, and adapters that effectively align the discrete visual tokens and continuous
222   visual features.
223   Causal Transformer backbone: The causal Transformer processes three key inputs.
224
225
      The first is a set of discrete visual tokens, which encodes multi-modal temporal cues. These tokens
      are generated by the concatenation of the image, depth, and edge map tokens over multiple temporal
226
      frames of our curated dataset. During pretraining, they are used as a supervision signal, where we
227
      train the model on the next-token prediction task to efficiently encode the multi-modal temporal
228   information. In the fine-tuning phase, these tokens act as a prefix to provide additional multi-modal
229   and temporal context for historical frames.
230
231
      The second and third inputs are learnable queries, providing the model with task-specific information
      and visual cues. In this work, we use the intermediate features from a convolutional encoder as the
232
      visual queries. As these features have a spatial correspondence, they can be thought of as input-
233
      dependent position embeddings for each spatial location. These inputs are only provided during
234   fine-tuning, when the model is expected to produce outputs specific to each downstream task.
235
236   To combine the discrete and continuous tokens for the Transformer, all discrete tokens from the first
237
      input are mapped to continuous embeddings via a learned lookup table. These embeddings are then
      concatenated with the continuous task and visual query tokens, forming a unified sequence that is
238
      input to the Transformer:

284   3.3   T RAINING R EGIME
285
286   Pretraining: Pretraining is essential to imbue the model with foundational knowledge for reasoning
287   generalizable feature representation, enabling it to capture complex visual patterns and relationships,
288   before fine-tuning on task-specific data. To this end, we pretrain the large vision model on our
289   internal dataset of large scale driving videos with image tokens and inferred depth and edge tokens.
290   As discussed in Sec. 3.1, all modalities are tokenized to form the visual tokens. The autoregressive
291   Transformer is then trained on the next-token prediction task, allowing the model to effectively learn
292
      and reason about the interactions among these discrete tokens, utimately encapsulating rich world
      knowledge within. In particular, we train the model to predict the discrete token ID of next token
293
      via N-way classification and a cross-entropy loss.
294
295   Fine-tuning: After pretraining, we fine-tune the entire PerceptionLM in an end-to-end manner,
296   instructing the Transformer to perform downstream perception tasks that are usually challenging
297   for token-based architectures. Task-specific heads and corresponding loss functions are employed
298
      to guide the learning process. For the depth estimation, LiDAR point clouds are projected onto
      images as supervision signals, and an L1 loss is applied. For semantic segmentation, a sigmoid
299
      focal cross-entropy loss is employed.

305
306   We evaluate our method on several autonomous driving datasets to verify the efficacy of both our
307   pretraining pipeline and its effects on downstream perception tasks in depth estimation and semantic
308   segmentation. For large scale pretraining, we curate an internal dataset for autoregressive next-token
309   prediction task learning and then fine tune and evaluate our method on open datasets for depth and
310   semantic segmentation tasks.
311   We evaluate our method on depth estimation on the nuScenes, WOMD, and WOD datasets. To do so,
312   we take the LiDAR scans corresponding to each image, and project each lidar point into the image
313   to establish a ground-truth depth for the corresponding pixel. We then train our model to predict
314   per-pixel depth where only sparse positions with ground-truth depth values will be used to supervise
315   the learning and evaluation. For semantic segmentation, we leverage the semantic segmentation
316   groundtruth in the nuImages dataset to directly supervise a multi-class classification model.
317   Pretraining dataset: To evaluate PerceptionLM autoregressive pretraining at scale, we curate an
318   internal dataset with one billion driving images at 5Hz without labels.

      4.2   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
340
      Pretraining: We conduct experiments with 1, 2, 4, and 8 frames of temporal context window,
341
      where each frame contains 3 modalities: image, depth and edge, and each modality is tokenized into
342
      1024 tokens. The total number of input tokens are 3072, 6144, 12288, and 24576 for the 1, 2, 4, and
343   8-frame experiments, respectively, We use a decoder only Transformer with causal masking, RoPe
344   positional embedding and RMS normalization (Vaswani, 2017; Su et al., 2024; Zhang & Sennrich,
345   2019). To efficiently model long context lengths, we use a Blockwise Parallel Transformer with
346   block size 1024 (Liu & Abbeel, 2024) for our casual Transformer backbone, which reduces the
347   memory and time complexity from quadratic to linear with respect to input sequence length. For
348   scaling 1 and 2-frame models, we jointly scale hidden layer size=128×scale, intermediate size =
349   256×scale, and number of hidden layers = 4×scale, where scale ∈ [1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12], leading
350   to models with 2.7M, 9.5M, 24.1M, 50.5M, 154M, 352M, 677M, and 1.1B parameters. For 4-frame
351
      and 8-frame models, we scale model up to scale = 10 and 8 respectively.
352   Fine-tuning: ConvNeXt-S (Liu et al., 2022) is adopted as the vision backbone for both depth pre-
353   diction and semantic segmentation. ConvNeXt-S is very light-weight (50M params) and can be
354   easily integrated with PerceptionLM with negligible computation overhead, as compared with our
355   largest 1.1B model. In this work, we initialize our ConvNeXt model with the weights pretrained
356   on imageNet-22K and fine-tuned on ImageNet1-K (Deng et al., 2009). By default, we adopt three
357
      modalities (image, relative depth, edge map) and eight frames to form the visual tokens. For con-
      sistency with pretraining, the input image to ConvNeXt is resized to a shape of (256, 256, 3), and
358
      the induced intermediate visual queries share the shape of (32, 32, C) where the C matches with the
359
      Transformer’s hidden layer size. For experiments with PerceptionLM, we also initialize the visual
360   encoder and decoder weights with the encoder-decoder only model trained on the fine-tuning task.
361   All models are trained on 64 TPUv5e with per-device batch size 1. For ablation studies, we evaluate
362   the depth prediction on NuScenes dataset and semantic segmentation on nuImages dataset.

365   4.3   S CALING UP PRETRAINING .
366
367   We first systematically study the scaling properties of our pre-training in both model capacity and
368
      temporal context length.
369   Beyond Model Parameters – Scaling Temporal Context: While model parameters are important,
370   scaling perception reasoning also critically depends on the length of the temporal context. Increasing
371   the number of temporal frames allows PerceptionLM to incorporate more contextual information
372   from the history. However, this increased reasoning capacity is not reflected in the sheer number

      Scaling PerceptionLM: Balancing Model and Temporal Context While increasing either model
414   capacity or temporal context can improve performance, our analysis reveals that a balanced approach
415   is crucial for optimal results. Surprisingly, simply expanding the context window with a small model
416   actually increases the evaluation loss. This is evident in the loss scaling factor (A), which grows
417   from 0.419 for the single-frame model to 0.498, 0.677, and 0.970 for the 2, 4 and 8-frame models,
418   respectively.
419
      This behavior stems from the unique structure of PerceptionLM. Unlike traditional language mod-
420   els that process sequential tokens, PerceptionLM handles tokens from a four-dimensional space
421   (time, modality, row, and column). These tokens exhibit localized correlations within this multi-
422   dimensional space, with a token at position (t, m, r, c) being more closely related to its immediate
423   neighbors (t ± 1, m ± 1, r ± 1, c ± 1). While flattening these dimensions into a single sequence
424   simplifies next-token prediction, it disrupts these inherent relationships. Consequently, it’s essential
425   to scale both the model capacity and the temporal context length concurrently. This is further sup-
426   ported by the GFLOPs exponent (parameter B), where the 8-frame model exhibits a larger decaying
427   factor (0.383) compared to the single-frame model (0.312), indicating that scaling the model leads
428
      to faster loss reduction with longer context lengths.

430   model evaluation loss curves intersect exactly one time with each other, indicating the shifting of op-
431   timal training paradigm, i.e. for a small training budget < 1017 FLOPs, single frame model is more
      compute optimal, while for a large training budget > 1019 FLOPs, 8-frame model is more efficient.

532   4.5    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we investigate how to leverage world simulation to enhance perception tasks that are
535   traditionally hard to be represented as discrete tokens. As a solution, we propose PerceptionLM, an
536   end-to-end framework that integrates continuous perception signals with an auto-regressive Trans-
537   former to enhance perception tasks. Extensive scaling experiments are conducted to understand the
538   scaling properties of PerceptionLM in terms of both model capacity and multi-modal temporal con-
539   text length. Comprehensive experiments on public datasets demonstrate significant improvements
      when PerceptionLM is integrated.
