                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
015
               The multimodal model has demonstrated promise in histopathology. However,
               most multimodal models are based on H&E and genomics, adopting increas-
016
               ingly complex yet black-box designs. Our paper proposes a novel interpretable
017
               multimodal framework named SHAP-CAT, which uses a Shapley-value-based
018            dimension reduction technique for effective multimodal fusion. Starting with
019            two paired modalities – H&E and IHC images, we employ virtual staining tech-
020            niques to enhance limited input data by generating a new clinical-related modality.
021            Lightweight bag-level representations are extracted from image modalities, and a
022            Shapley-value-based mechanism is used to reduce dimensions. For each dimen-
023            sion of the bag-level representation, attribution values are calculated to indicate
024            how changes in the specific dimensions of the input affect the model output. This
025            way, we select a few top critical dimensions of bag-level representation for each
026
               imaging modality to late fusion. Our experimental results demonstrate that the
               proposed SHAP-CAT framework incorporating synthetic modalities significantly
027
               enhances model performance, yielding a 5% increase in accuracy for the BCI, an
028
               8% increase for IHC4BC-ER, and an 11% increase for the IHC4BC-PR dataset.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recent advances in artificial intelligence have significantly impacted histopathology, mainly by de-
035
      veloping multimodal models. These models integrate data types, such as whole slide images and
      molecular profiles, to improve diagnosis, prediction, and treatment personalization (Chen et al.,
036
      2022; Boehm et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2020). Recent efforts are expanding to include multi-staining
037
      images like IHC (Jaume et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Foersch et al., 2023) and Trichrome-stained
038   WSIs (Dwivedi et al., 2022) for better identification of specific molecular features related to cancer.
039   Integrating diverse modalities is crucial, since different image modalities carry other information
040   related to cancer (Perez-Lopez et al., 2024; Boehm et al., 2022; Stahlschmidt et al., 2022).
041
      However, many technical, analytical, and clinical challenges are still amplified in the presence of
042
      multimodal data.
043
044          • Limited public paired datasets (Steyaert et al., 2023; Miotto et al., 2018; Perez-Lopez et al.,
045            2024): Developing multimodal models require modality-paired and datasets with labels.
046            The data also needs to be complete and large in the sample.
047
             • Most multimodal histopathological models combine molecular features and WSIs, not dif-
048
               ferent WSIs. Although molecular data are relevant to precision medicine, they don’t have
049
               tissue structure, spatial, and morphological information (Alturkistani et al., 2016).
050
051          • Very complex and different multimodal fusion technique with low interpretability: Li et al.
052            (2022); Wang et al. (2021); Lipkova et al. (2022) have complex design such as hierarchy
053
               fusion, intermediate gradual fusion, and intermediate guided-fusion. Still, they ignore the
               fact that the medical imaging domain requires models to be interpretable.

      Given the difficulty of obtaining quality datasets (the first challenge), we propose a virtual staining-
088
      based multimodal framework that uses H&E, IHC, and one more generated modality for WSI clas-
089
      sifications. Our multimodal network can integrate triple image modalities in weakly supervised
090   learning on cancer grading tasks (the second challenge). After training the specific pipeline and
091   extracting the bag-level representations for each modality, our framework uses the Shapley-value-
092   based dimension reduction approach for further multimodal fusion, avoiding the curve of dimensions
093   and demonstrating high interpretability (the third challenge). For a given set of bag-level represen-
094   tations belonging to a patient sample, We employ a Shapley-value-based method to characterize the
095   importance of each dimension within the feature space. This method attributes the predictions of
096   deep neural networks to their respective inputs by computing attribution values for each dimension.
097   This way, we select the top 32 important dimensions for each medical image modality for late fu-
098
      sion and the final classifier for prediction. We evaluate our framework in BCI, IHC4BC-ER, and
      IHC4BC-PR datasets for cancer grading tasks.
099
100   Our contribution is the following:
101
102            • A framework with a virtual staining technique is designed to generate one more modality to
103              enhance the limited, approximately paired input dataset without requiring pixel-level data
104              alignment.
105
106            • We use a Shapley-value-based mechanism to reduce the dimensions of representation for
107              enhanced multimodal fusion, thereby avoiding the curse of dimensionality and improving
                 the interpretability of our multimodal technique.

137   The proposed framework consists of Parallel Feature Extraction Pipelines for each modality and a
138   SHAP-CAT pipeline for the predictions of multimodal representations, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Given
139   approximate H&E-IHC paired dataset Ihe , Iihc , we firstly use pre-trained CycleGAN to generate
140   reconstructed H&E images Irec he . Then we separately train each modality to extract bag-level
141   representations for each modality for further late fusion.
142
143   Modality Generation. Given the paucity of medical data in general (Zitnik et al., 2019; Miotto
144   et al., 2018), the use of synthetic data has become increasingly prevalent for the training, devel-
145   opment, and augmentation of artificial intelligence models (Latonen et al., 2024). We first use the
146   virtual staining technique to generate another modality image for enhancing multimodal framework
147   performance from H&E and IHC paired images, denoted as reconstructed H&E.
148   The virtual staining technique we used in our paper is CycleGAN (Zhu et al., 2017), designed ex-
149   plicitly for unpaired datasets. The input of our framework is H&E-IHC approximate paired datasets
150   Ihe , Iihc with labels. Approximate paired here means these two sets of images are not aligned
151   pixel to pixel. In contrast, the same images are offset by about 10%. There are two translators
152   G : Ihe → Iihc , and F : Iihc → Ihe (as shown in Fig 1.a). G and F are trained simultaneously
153   to encourages F (G(Ihe )) ≈ Ihe and G(F (Iihc )) ≈ Iihc . Also, there are two adversarial discrim-
154   inators Dhe and Dihc , where Dhe aims to discriminate between images Ihe and translated images
155   F (Iihc ). Similarly, Dihc aims to distinguish between Iihc and G(Ihe ). The final objective is:

      4.4   F USION OF MODALITY
347
      In multimodal fusion, direct fusion of multiple modalities is impractical. For example, bag-level
348   representation of each modality is represented in 512 dimensions in our paper. Consequently, three
349   dimensions would generate features of 5123 dimensions, making it impractical for machine learning
350   model training. In addition, such large-dimension data face a challenge known as the curse of dimen-
351   sionality. Furthermore, trying to tackle complex histopathological tasks with such high-dimensional
352   yet low-sample-size features results in ”blind spot” (Berisha et al., 2021).
353
      Therefore, we must decrease the dimensionality of representations. Prior research has utilized aver-
354   age pooling or max pooling for this purpose (Wang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2020). Our method de-
355   viates from traditional methods by offering a more accurate and interpretable strategy for fusion. We
356   are the first to implement a Shapley-value-based technique to reduce dimensions in image modality
357   representations. We also evaluate our SHAP pool in a single modality by reducing bag-level repre-
358   sentation z ∈ R1×512 to f ∈ R1×32 and then aggregated by different classifiers (as shown in Tab 1).
359   We compare our SHAP pooling with average pooling, max pooling and selecting 32 dimensions
360   randomly. Our SHAP pooling performs well across different classifiers.

511   7.2    D ENSE BAG - LEVEL REPRESENTATION
512
513   SHAP value (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) is an approximation of Shapley values, while the original
514   Shapley value (Shapley et al., 1953) is an NP-hard problem in game theory. It is impossible to
515   search for an NP-hard problem directly in features extracted from giga-pixel WSIs. The bag-level
516   representation generated by our framework is a 512-dimension feature with a size of 3.3kb. There
517   are 1 × 512 elements in the tensor. Each element is 4 bytes. Therefore, the total data size is 2048
518
      bytes (or exactly 2 KB for the data). The raw data size is about 2 KB. The actual file size might be
      slightly larger due to the metadata and can vary slightly based on the specific version of PyTorch and
519
      the details of how the tensor storage is implemented. Similarly, our final bag-level representation is
520
      a 2048-dimension tensor, which is only 9.4kb in size. This very small size ensures us to use many
521   models to aggreate the final bag-level representation.

524   8     CONCLUSION
525
526   We propose a novel framework with a virtual staining technique to generate one more modality to
527   enhance WSI classification and a Shapley-value-based mechanism to reduce dimensions for efficient
528   and interpretable multimodal fusion for histopathological tasks. We are the first to use the Shapley-
529   value-based dimension-reducing technique in image modality. The experiment demonstrates that
530   using virtual staining to generate an additional modality, combined with a Shapley-value-based di-
531   mension reduction technique, improves model performance. Specifically, it results in a 5% increase
532
      in accuracy for BCI, an 8% increse for IHC4BC-ER and an 11% increase for IHC4BC-PR.

      hk , a denser feature vector.
