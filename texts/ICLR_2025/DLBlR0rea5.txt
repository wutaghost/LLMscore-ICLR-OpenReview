009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models excel in generating and completing patterns in images. But
012            how good is their ability to learn hidden rules from samples and to generate and
013            reason according to such rules or even generalize to similar rules? We trained
014            a wide family of unconditional diffusion models on Raven’s progression matrix
015            task to precisely study this. We quantified their capability to generate structurally
016            consistent samples and complete missing parts according to hidden rules. We
017
               found diffusion models can synthesize novel samples consistent with rules without
               memorizing the training set, much better than GPT2 trained on the same data. They
018
               memorized and recombined local parts of the training samples to create new rule-
019
               conforming samples. When tasked to complete the missing panel with inpainting
020            techniques, advanced sampling techniques were needed to perform well. Further,
021            their pattern completion capability can generalize to rules unseen during training.
022            Further, through generative training on rule data, a robust rule representation rapidly
023            emerged in the diffusion model, which could linearly classify rules at 99.8% test
024            accuracy. Our results suggest diffusion training is a useful paradigm for reasoning
025            and learning representations for downstream tasks even for abstract rules data.

045   Human excels at discovering regular structure from a small number of samples, and they can apply
046   such rule to novel setting to generate new samples or completing missing part based on the same rule.
047   The Raven’s progressive matrix (RPM) (Raven, 1936) is a famous task in human reasoning literature.
048   In the generative version of this task (GenRAVEN), the subject observes two complete rows of panels
049   and is tasked to complete the third row in a manner that is consistent with the first two rows (Fig.1A).
050
      Ideally, the subject needs to infer the underlying rule consistent with the first two rows and applies it
      correctly to the third row. How can we train a learning system to solve such reasoning task?
051
052   If we conceptualize all rule-conforming samples as a joint distribution, then rule learning can be
053   framed as a generative modelling problem or learning the correct joint distribution. Further, reasoning
      about the missing panel can be framed as sampling from the conditional probability (Prystawski et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
       2024). One conceptual problem is, given finite training samples, the rule governing them, or the
055   ‘true‘ joint distribution is under-specified: in one extreme, the joint is the empirical distribution of the
056    training set; in the other extreme, the joint distribution can be overly broad and might not align with
057   what we mean by rule. Fundamentally, the rule and the distribution learned by the system should be
058    affected by its inductive bias, be it human or AI. Given this ambiguity, we asked whether modern
059    generative AI systems could learn the correct ”joint distribution” given finite samples. If so, can they
060    reason about the missing part through conditional sampling?
061   So in this work, we focus on precisely studying the ”reasoning” capability of diffusion models using
062   a popular reasoning tasks, the Raven’s Progressive Matrices (RPM). We trained diverse kinds of
063   diffusion models (EDM, DiT, SiT) on the RPM data and systematically evaluated their performance
064   in generating samples, classifying samples and completing missing panels consistent with underlying
065   rules. We focus on answering the following questions: Can diffusion models learn abstract rules
066   from training data? If so, on what level are they memorizing and on what level are they innovating?
067   What are the factors affecting rule learning and what are their limitations? Note that, we are not only
068   motivated to find the best model that learns rules, but also to highlight the universal aspects of the
069
      structural learning process in diffusion models.

      Main Contributions The main contributions of our work are the following,
072
             • Demonstrating diffusion models can learn to generate novel samples following abstract rules,
073            with minimal memorization of the training set, esp. compared to autoregressive models.

087   2    R ELATED WORK
088
089   Solving RAVEN’s task with learning method Developing models for abstract reasoning is a
090
      central goal in machine learning, with the RPM task widely used as a benchmark. Large-scale datasets
      like PGM (Barrett et al., 2018) and RAVEN (Zhang et al., 2019a) have facilitated the development
091
      of deep learning models, such as those based on CNNs (Zhang et al., 2019b; Spratley et al., 2020;
092
      Zhuo & Kankanhalli, 2022), relation networks (Barrett et al., 2018; Benny et al., 2021; Jahrens &
093   Martinetz, 2020), and graph neural networks (Wang et al., 2020). These discriminative models focus
094   on extracting visual features from panels to predict the correct answer.
095
096
      A significant concern with the discriminate approaches, however, is whether they truly perform
      abstract reasoning or merely exploit dataset-specific shortcuts (Hu et al., 2021). In response, several
097
      works focused on generative models, which aim to capture the structural patterns of the RPM task
098
      rather than simply making predictions. For instance, Pekar et al. (Pekar et al., 2020) utilized a
099   Variational Autoencoder to generate embeddings for answer panels. Shi et al. (Shi et al., 2024)
100   proposed a deep latent variable model that encodes image attributes as latent concepts, generating
101   answer images by applying abstract atomic rules to these concepts.
102
103   Understanding Generalization in diffusion model One mystery for diffusion models is that,
104   when the score matching loss is minimized for a finite set of training data, and the score function
105   is learned ”perfectly”. Then the score function should generate samples according to a mixture of
106   delta, i.e. memorizing training data. However, this doesn’t usually happen in reality (Gu et al., 2023).
107   Besides, multiple works have observed consistency between different diffusion models trained on
      the same data, suggesting diffusion models are approaching some ”universal” distribution which is


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      not the delta mixture Zhang et al. (2023). Many have theorized about the source of generalization
109   Kadkhodaie et al. (2023); Han et al. (2024). Some suggest the inductive bias of neural networks
110   smooth the score function like a kernel regressor, which leads to generalization. However, these
111   theories tackle relatively simple forms of generalization e.g. interpolating data points. Here, we train
112   diffusion models on a dataset governed by abstract rules with unseen rules, so if it can generalize
113   and generate according to underlying rules, it will challenge the current theory of what diffusion can
114   learn and how it generalizes.

      3.1   G EN RAVEN DATASET
119
120   We introduce the GenRAVEN dataset, comprising RPMs associated with 40 relational rules. Each
121   RPM features a 3×3 matrix of panels, where three panels in each row follows a unique relational
122   rule (Fig. 1A). To focus on rule learning and reasoning, we abstracted away the visual aspect of
123   the sample, encoding each object with three integers representing its shape, size, and color, with
124   (−1, −1, −1) representing empty positions. Each attribute has a discrete set of allowed values, 0-6
125   for shape and 0-9 for size and color. Taking together, each sample RPM is represented by an integer
126   array of shape 3 × 9 × 9, which is the target of generative modeling (Fig. 1B).
127   Each rule is composed of an abstract relation (constant; progression ±2, ±1; arithmetic ±; XOR;
128   OR; AND) applied to an attribute (shape, size, color, number, position). The dataset is designed so
129   that the rule governing each row remains ambiguous when examining the first two panels and only
130   becomes evident when all three panels are considered. This design ensures that the rule governing
131   the row cannot be directly deduced from the first two panels alone and the model must reason the
132   entire matrix. To study the generalization of abstract relations such as constant to new attributes, we
133
      held out 5 rules (Fig. 1D) during training. Using this set up, we can study whether model can learn to
      generate samples with ”constant color”, by observing samples with ”constant size” and ”progression
134
      color”.

      3.2   D IFFUSION M ODEL
138
      The diffusion models have been a prominent approach for generative modeling (Dhariwal & Nichol,
139   2021b) in continuous domains. It takes a holistic approach in modelling data: it learns a vector field in
140   the data space with a function approximator sθ (x, σ), which approximates the gradient of smoothed
141   data distribution (Song & Ermon, 2019). During sampling, when samples flow along this vector field,
142   they will be transported from a base distribution e.g. Gaussian into the target distribution p(X). This
143   vector field is learned via denoising score matching with a neural network (Hyvärinen, 2005).
144
145   Architecture Given the spatial structure of the task, we treated each RPM as a 9 × 9 image with 3
146   channels and adapted existing diffusion models for image generation. Specifically, we experimented
147   with two architectures, UNet (Karras et al., 2022a) and Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles &
148   Xie, 2023). UNet is a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) based backbone, designed to extract
149   information at multiple resolutions, with self-attention modules at each resolution. For a long time, it
150
      has been the default backbone for diffusion models for images (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol,
      2021a; Rombach et al., 2022). For our task, we adapted its architecture to match the 3 × 3 panel
151
      structure of the RPM samples: we changed its filter size to 3 and downsampling ratio to 3. DiT is a
152
      recent transformer-based backbone adapted for diffusion modelling (Peebles & Xie, 2023). For our
153   task, we treated each object in RPM as a token with three features (patch size = 1), totaling 81 tokens.
154   We also tested SiT model (Ma et al., 2024), which had the same network architecture as DiT, but
155   based their training and inference on the framework of Stochastic Interpolant (Albergo et al., 2023).
156
157   Sampling and in-painting We treated the integer attributes in RPM samples as continuous values
158   and normalized them by mean and std during training. After diffusion sampling, we denormalized
159   and rounded the generated attribute values to the closest integer. By default, we used deterministic
160   samplers to generate samples for efficiency: Heun’s 2nd order sampler for UNet model (Karras et al.,
161   2022a); DDIM sampler for DiT (Song et al., 2020); Runge-Kutta sampler(dpori5) for SiT (Ma
      et al., 2024). Leveraging the in-painting capability of diffusion models (Lugmayr et al., 2022; Wang


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al., 2022), we also challenged them with the generative RPM tasks: given the first 8 panels, let the
163   model fill in the missing panel.
164
165   Training For all models, we used their default training parameters (detailed in Sec. A.2). We
166   trained 1M gradient steps with mini-batch size 256, without data augmentation. All training was
167   conducted on a single A100 or H100 GPU, which finished around 1 to 3 days.
168
169   3.3   E VALUATION C RITERION
170
171   One benefit of having a distribution defined by explicit rule is that, we can evaluate the generated
172   samples precisely. For unconditional generation, there is not a sense of accuracy, so we evaluated the
173   internal consistency of generated samples. For each row in a generated sample, we inferred the set of
174   applicable rules. If any rule from the 40 rule set applies, the row is called valid. For each sample,
175
      we examine whether the same rule applies to three rows in a sample, which we call the sample rule
      consistent (C3). Similarly, if only two rows have the same rule applies, we call it C2. These metrics
176
      quantify the self-consistency within the generated sample.
177
178   For conditional generation, we selected 50 new samples per rule, unseen during training, as our test
179   cases. We removed their final panel, and let the model generate the missing panel given the other 8
180   panels. We evaluated the of completions where the completed panel allows the same rule to apply to
181
      all three rows (C3), as panel completion accuracy.

      Generative model learning dynamics followed rule hierarchy The data generating process of
274
      RAVEN has a hierarchical structure (Sec.A.1), similar to a context-free grammar system. To conform
275
      to a relational rule, the generated samples also need to satisfy the following hierarchical criteria:
276   1) Object validity: the attribute values at each location must fall within predefined ranges or be
277   (-1,-1,-1). 2) Row-level rule validity: across all three panels in a row, one attribute must satisfy a rule.
278   3) Cross-row consistency: The rules applied to the three rows are the same. Fig. 7 shows the fraction
279   of generated samples that meet each of these criteria through training. We observe a hierarchy of
280   learning order, where the model initially learns more local data structures and subsequently adapts to
281   more global ones. In a sense, the model first learns what are valid local elements (e.g. words), before
282   learning to build valid combinations of them (e.g. valid phrases).

312   Visual reasoning as inpainting The generative RAVEN’s task can be naturally framed as a
313   conditional sampling problem or inpainting for image diffusion models: sample the missing panels
314   given the values of observed panels. We found that inpainting accuracy critically depended on the
315
      sampling algorithm we used. Popular image inpainting algorithms such as Repaint and DDNM
      (Lugmayr et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022) leverage the heuristics of fixing the observed dimensions of
316
      the sample, and only diffusing the masked regions. Surprisingly, these methods yield low accuracy
317
      (Fig. 3B). We found the recent method of twisted diffusion sampler (Wu et al., 2024), which leverages
318   the twisted sampling technique from Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) literature and is asymptotically
319   exact, performed much better at sampling missing panels that are consistent with the rest: Of the 2000
320   test cases, 45.2% of panel completions were C3 consistent with existing panels (49.5% for trained
321   rules and 14.8% for held out rules). Further, a higher sampling budget gave rise to higher accuracy
322   (Fig.3C, Fig.9). This shows that the heuristic methods might seem to work fine in common image
323   inpainting tasks, given the relatively loose evaluation criterion of visual inspection or classification.
      But for our generative RPM task which was evaluated at much higher precision, they fell short.

      intuition that the compressed representation in bottleneck layer contains high-level information.
396
397   In summary, we found that the diffusion model obtained near perfect rule classifying capability by
398   generative modeling RPM data. In this perspective, diffusion modeling is a powerful self-supervised
399   learning method, and our finding extends beyond the previous results about object classifying
400   representation emerged during diffusion training (Chen et al., 2024; Mukhopadhyay et al., 2023)
401
      to more abstract rule data. On a high level, this is also consistent with the notion that by modeling
      the whole data distribution, generative models can discover key factors in the true data-generating
402
      process (e.g. abstract rules), and construct a world model.

407   How do data and model size affect rule learning in diffusion? We manipulated the scale of the training
408
      dataset from 400 samples to 400000 samples per rule, and we trained models of the three families at
      different scales, with the same training step (1M) and batch size (256). Hyperparameters for each
409
      model scale were detailed in Sec. A.2.

      Rule learning emerges at a certain data scale We found that the rule learning capability emerged
425
      at a data scale, consistent across models. When only 400 samples per rule were seen during training,
426   all models failed to learn the rule: for unconditional generation, C3 sample fraction dropped below
427   15% (Fig. 5A); for conditional sample completion, the completion accuracy dropped to around 10%
428   (Fig. 5B). Intriguingly, at low data regime, the rule consistency followed non-monotonic learning
429   dynamics: it initially grew as in the higher data regime; then the learning ”collapsed”, i.e. the rule
430   consistency of samples dropped towards zero, accompanied by the training loss rapidly dropped,
431   ”breaking through the floor” (Fig. 5C). Similar phenomena were observed consistently across the
      three model families (see Supp. Fig.14).

432
      Interaction of model capacity and the training data scale Are bigger models better rule learners?
433   Across the three model families, we found larger models do not yield higher rule consistency in
434   their generation, esp. when the amount of training examples is limited (400-4000 per rule). Further,
435   when the data scale is small (400 sample per rule), bigger models tend to ”collapse” faster (Fig. 5A).
436   However, the larger models also benefited more from increased data scale (40k-400k per rule) (Fig.
437   5B). This trend is reminiscent of the classic statistical learning theory, where more data are needed to
438   constrain a bigger model, or it will overfit and perform worse.
439
440   Diffusion models tend to memorize more global structure at small data regime, leading to
441   failure Next, we examined the memorization pattern at different data scales. At the default scale
442   (4000 per rule), the models do not memorize rows or samples. However, at low data regime, likely
443   due to the more repetitions over the fixed training set, a larger fraction of rows and samples were
444   memorized esp. for models with higher capacity (SiT-B, DiT-B, EDMx3) (Fig. 15).
445   This could be related to the memorization phenomena in the literature: given a smaller number of
446   training samples from a dataset, diffusion models tend to memorize the exact training sample (Yoon
447   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). However, in our case, even when the rule learning collapsed, the
448   model didn’t simply regurgitate the training samples: the row and sample memorization fraction re-
449   mained mostly lower than 10% (Fig.5D). This suggests a higher level of memorization-generalization
450   dichotomy or model collapse.

476   It’s well known that, if the diffusion model learned the score field exactly, it should converge to the
477   exact training data points (Gu et al., 2023). However, from our empirical results, these diffusion
478   models can generate novel rule-conforming rows and samples. So what have diffusion models
479   actually learned?
480   To answer this, we evaluated the memorization of generated samples and their parts: rows, panels,
481   and rows and panels when considering a single attribute. We evaluated what fraction of samples and
482   parts have exact copies in the training set for the model. As a control, we also generated another
483   control dataset with the same number of samples per rule and computed the memorization fraction
484   for the control set. We hypothesized that even though memorization has not happen on the row level,
485   it might happen on local patch and attribute level. Further, the diffusion model learn to recombine
      these memorized local components to create new samples in a rule-conforming manner.

486
      Diffusion model memorize and recombine panels Although diffusion models have no memoriza-
487   tion on the sample and row level, we found they had significant memorization on the panel level. At
488   the default data scale (4000), after training, we found across all models consistently, [32-35]% of
489   generated panels had exact copies from the training set. Interestingly, they also had a similar fraction
490   of panels that had copies from the unseen control set (Fig.6A). This reflects the statistical property of
491   the data-generating process: when we generate another 4000 samples per rule (held out 5 rules), we
492   found it also has 35.2% panels that overlap with the training set (Fig.6A, horizontal line). Similar
493   results hold for the other levels (e.g. panels per attribute). In this sense, the diffusion models are
494   ”memorizing” parts in the training set but no more than separately generated data ”memorizing” the
495
      training set. So, we can call this benign memorization, or approaching the true data distribution.
496
497
      Panel reuse predicts easiness of individual rule Further, when we examine individual rules, some
      rules have more overlapping panels across random splits of datasets and some have fewer overlapping
498
      panels. We found that Logic-Attribute type rules have an especially low fraction of panel that overlap
499
      with independently generated training set (1.9% vs others 46.7%). We found that for trained rules,
500   the panel overlapping rate of each rule highly correlates with the frequency of model generating C3
501   samples according to that rule, (correlation 0.918, p < 1 × 10−10 Fig.6B). This supports our notion
502   that diffusion models need to memorize local parts i.e. panels to learn a certain rule successfully. In
503   another sense, the recurring frequency of parts determines the overall difficulty of a rule.
504
505   Panel overlap as an explanation to rule learning collapse We found at a small data scale (400
506   per rule), there will be substantially fewer panel overlaps between the training set and independently
507   generated control set: 12.4% of panels are overlapping. We think the lack of memorizable parts might
508   explain why rule learning collapses at this scale.
509
510   7    D ISCUSSION
511
512   We thoroughly investigated the learning dynamics and memorization behavior of rule learning in
513   diffusion models in the RPM task inspired by cognitive tests. We found diffusion models can learn to
514   unconditionally generate novel samples and complete patterns according to rules. Representations
515   emerged in diffusion models are also capable of classifying rules at near-perfect accuracy. Our results
516   may inspire further theories about the source of generalization in diffusion models.
517
518   Weakness of diffusion models in rule consistent panel completion We are surprised that the
519   panel completion accuracy of diffusion models is generally lower than the C3 frequency of their
520
      unconditional generation, and also lower than autoregressive counterparts. As we may naively think,
      modeling conditional density should be easier than modeling joint distribution. One potential reason
521
      is that, even though unconditional sampling algorithms for diffusion have been studied extensively
522
      Karras et al. (2022b); Zhao et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2022), we still do not have
523   the perfect method for sampling the conditional distribution from diffusion models. Just as TDS
524   perform better than simple heuristic method e.g. Repaint, better conditional sampler may boost the
525   reasoning capability of diffusion models further. Our results also provide evidence that for tasks that
526   are evaluated precisely (e.g. abstract rules), a better sampler is critical.
527
528   Improving sample complexity We identified one key condition (data scale) that gives rise to rule
529   learning behavior in diffusion models. Currently, the data scale required for rule learning (4000
530   samples per rule) is still significantly higher than what human needs to learn the rules. Future works
531   are required to investigate how to reduce the sample complexity of rule learning.

      A.1     DATASET CONSTRUCTION
705
706   We have a procedural algorithm to generate rows following the 40 rules. The basic workflow is the
707   following:
708
709         1. Choosing the rule following dimension (Shape, Size, Color, Number, Position);
710
            2. Choosing the rule type (Constant, Progression, Arithmetic, XOR, OR, AND);
711
712         3. Switching based on the type of rules
713               • If it’s Constant, Progression, Arithmetic of object attributes (shape, size, color), then
714                 each panel will only have single value for the attribute i.e. object all have same size
715                 in one panel. We will choose three values (X1 , X2 , X3 ) that follow the rule (e.g.
716                 Progression +2, will be X1 = 1, X2 = 3, X3 = 5).
717               • If it’s XOR, OR, AND of object attributes, then each panel will have a set of attributes,
718                 which suffice the logic operation. Decide the number of objects of each attribute value.
719
            4. Choose the other attribute dimensions of the object.
720
721         5. Check if the other attribute follow other rules, if so, regenerate the other attributes
722
723
      We synthesized 1,200,000 rows per rule. Three rows following same rule will be randomly combined
      into a Raven’s Progression Matrix sample. These RPMs are encoded as a 3 × 9 × 9 integer matrix.
724
      We used [1.5, 2.5, 2.5] and [2.5, 3.5, 3.5] as mean and std of the three channels, to normalize the RPM
725
      encoding tensors.

764
      Here we also used auto-regressive models GPT2 Radford et al. (2019) to solve RAVEN’s task,
765

766
      three integer attributes of each token were embedded through separated embedding layers with 1/3
767   hidden dimensions and then concatenated as the token embedding. To reflect such latent space
768   structure, the hidden state outputs from the transformer or Mamba were chunked into 3 parts and
769   decoded separately into attributes of the next token. We used the default positional encoding for
770   GPT2 and no position encoding for Mamba. We train these models with the next token prediction
771   objective. A key design decision is an order to sequentialize RPM data into 1d. Here we first scan
772   the objects within each panel, and then follow the raster order of panels (panel1 in row 1, P2 row1,
773   ...P3 row 3), forming a sequence of 81 tokens. We prepend the same starting token [SOS] for each
774   sample. For unconditional generation, we started from [SOS] and autoregressively sampled 81
775
      tokens with temperature 1.0. For panel completion, we started from [SOS] and 72 existing tokens
      (corresponding to first 8 panels), and sampled 9 tokens with temperature 1.0.

784
785   We forwarded noise-free samples of the 40 rules (unseen in diffusion training) through diffusion
786   models with a specific time input, then record activations from various layers (Fig.4). For each rule,
787   we used 3000 samples as the training set and 1000 samples as the test set. We trained a 40-way linear

790

791
      techniques: 1) token averaging: average the activation across space (UNet) or tokens (DiT, SiT); 2)
792   PCA: concatenate the activation across space or tokens and then project activation onto the top K
793   PCs. The dimension-reduced features are standardized with zero mean and unit standard deviation
794   before sending into linear classifier.
