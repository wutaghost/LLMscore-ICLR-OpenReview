009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) is one of the most fundamental methods to
012            identify and extract latent structure in complex datasets. Unfortunately, well-
013            known hardness results require that any algorithm for learning a mixture of k
014            multivariate Gaussian distributions in d-dimensional space requires both runtime
015            and sample complexity exponential in d, even if the Gaussians are reasonably
016            separated. To overcome this barrier, we consider settings where algorithms are
017
               augmented with possibly erroneous “advice” to help learn the underlying GMMs.
               In particular, we consider a natural predictor that can be easily trained through
018
               machine learning models. Specifically, our predictor outputs a list of β possi-
019
               ble labels for each sample from the mixture such that, with probability at least
020            1 − α, one of the labels in the list is the true label, for a fixed constant α. We
021            show that to estimate  the mixture up to total variation distance Õ(ε), we can use
022            k · poly d, log k, 1ε samples from the GMM, provided that β is upper bounded by
023            any fixed constant. Moreover, our algorithm uses polynomial time, thus breaking
024            known computational limitations of algorithms that do not have access to such
025            advice.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      The problem of learning a model that best fits a collection of observations is a fundamental problem
030
      in machine learning, theoretical computer science, and mathematical statistics. A common assump-
031
      tion is that the input data is generated through independent and identically distributed samples from
032   an unknown mixture of Gaussians. Formally, a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) is a convex combi-
033                                                               Pk
      nation of Gaussian distributions, i.e., a distribution D = i=1 wi · N (µi , Σi ), where the unknown
034
      weights wi are non-negative and sum to 1 and N (µi , Σi ) denotes a d-dimensional multivariate Gaus-
035   sian distribution with unknown mean µi and covariance matrix Σi . Due to their capacity to model
036   diverse data distributions with a combination of Gaussian components, GMMs have been one of the
037   most extensively studied latent variable model since its introduction by Pearson in 1894 (Pearson,
038   1894), with applications in many areas where finite mixture models occur, including biology, ge-
039   ology, physics, and social sciences (Titterington et al., 1985; Peel & MacLahlan, 2000; McLachlan
040   et al., 2019).
041   Dasgupta (1999) initiated the algorithmic problem of learning GMMs by giving an algorithm for
042   learning the mixture of k identical Gaussians that uses time polynomial in k and the ambient dimen-
043   sion d, under the assumption that the Gaussians are spherical and their centers are well-separated.
044   Subsequent works (Arora & Kannan, 2001; Vempala & Wang, 2002; Achlioptas & McSherry, 2005;
045   Brubaker & Vempala, 2008; Kannan et al., 2008; Diakonikolas et al., 2023) focused on efficient
046   algorithms for learning GMMs under various other assumptions. Additionally, a line of work (Kalai
047   et al., 2010; Moitra & Valiant, 2010; Belkin & Sinha, 2015; Hardt & Price, 2015) studied efficient
048   algorithms and impossibility results under minimal information-theoretic conditions.
049   Unfortunately, Moitra & Valiant (2010) showed that there exist distributions D1 and D2 that are
050   mixtures of k 2 + 1 Gaussians with “large” weights and reasonably separated centers, such that 2Ω(k)
051   samples are needed to distinguish between D1 and D2 . Moreover, Hardt & Price (2015) showed
052   that even for the case where d = 1 and the Gaussians have the same variance, Ω(σ 6k−2 ) samples
053   are necessary to learn the parameters in the mixture model up to constant additive error, where σ 2 is
      the variance of the univariate Gaussians. These results say that both sample complexity and runtime

      exponential in k is necessary for learning general Gaussian mixture models. Thus, we seek tools and
055   techniques that enable sample complexity and runtime polynomial in k for learning GMMs without
056   require distributional assumptions beyond the necessary minimal information theoretic assumptions.

      Learning-augmented algorithms. A natural area to draw inspiration for new tools is the recent
      advances in the predictive ability of machine learning models. In many applications, auxiliary in-
059
      formation, e.g., previous datasets with potentially similar behavior, is often available and can guide
060
      algorithmic decisions if accurate. On the other hand, machine learning models lack provable guaran-
061   tees and thus produce heuristics that can be embarrassingly inaccurate when generalizing to unfamil-
062   iar inputs (Szegedy et al., 2014). Nevertheless, learning-augmented algorithms (Mitzenmacher &
063   Vassilvitskii, 2020) have been shown to overcome worst-case impossibility barriers for a wide range
064   of settings, such as more efficient data structures (Kraska et al., 2018; Mitzenmacher, 2018; Lin
065   et al., 2022), faster runtime algorithms (Dinitz et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022c; Davies et al., 2023),
066   more competitive online algorithms (Purohit et al., 2018; Gollapudi & Panigrahi, 2019; Lattanzi
067   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Wei & Zhang, 2020; Bamas et al., 2020; Im et al., 2021; Lykouris
068   & Vassilvitskii, 2021; Aamand et al., 2022; Anand et al., 2022; Azar et al., 2022; Grigorescu et al.,
069
      2022; Khodak et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2022; Antoniadis et al., 2023; Shin et al., 2023), and more
      space-efficient streaming algorithms (Hsu et al., 2019; Indyk et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2020; Chen
070
      et al., 2022b;a; Li et al., 2023). In particular, (Ergun et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2023) introduce
071
      algorithms for k-means and k-median clustering that use polynomial runtime and achieve approxi-
072   mation guarantees beyond NP hardness limits. As clustering has many similar structural properties
073   as, and in fact is often used as a subroutine for, learning GMMs, it is our hope that we can also use
074   machine learning advice to overcome the exponential time barriers for learning GMMs (Moitra &
075   Valiant, 2010; Hardt & Price, 2015).

078   1.1   O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS

      In this paper, we study the problem of learning Gaussian mixture models given a natural form of
081
      advice that can be readily offered by machine learning models.
082
083   List oracle. We consider learning a mixture D of k Gaussians given a list oracle, which provides
084   a list of labels for each samples, i.e., a list of possible indices of the corresponding Gaussian, up to
085
      some error α. Specifically, if x ∼ D is drawn from Gaussian Gi = (µi , Σi ), then informally, on
      query x, the oracle will provide a small list of labels that includes the label i, with probability at
086
      least (1 − α) over the fraction of the input queries. In particular, we only require a certain fraction
087
      of the labels to be incorrect, regardless of the manner they are incorrect. That is, the labels can be
088   adversarially incorrect, which could potentially obfuscate any signal from a mixture of Gaussians
089   that are not well-separated.
090
091
      We remark that a list oracle can be easily acquired through a machine learning model that is trained
      on a similar distribution of data. For example, a set of samples can be split into a “training” set and
092
      a “testing” set, but rather than using the training set in the traditional line of supervised learning,
093
      we can instead apply a heuristic such as kmeans++ to cluster the initial data, and use the resulting
094   centers to form a predictor for the second half of the data. The model may assign different confi-
095   dences to each label for a point, e.g., an algorithm may declare 55% confidence that an input point
096   x is labeled i and 45% confidence that x should be labeled j. In this case, it seems reasonable to
097   assign both i and j as possible labels for x. Alternatively, consider the scenario where we have
098   multiple machine learning models that can be used to predict the label of a sample. We could use
099   ensemble learning to assimilate the multiple models into a single model, but this could lose valu-
100   able information, e.g., if half of the models declare that an input point x is labeled i and half of the
101   models declare that X is labeled j. In fact, note that it is possible for an ensemble of 10 (and more
102
      generally β) models to always correctly predict a label despite each of models only have 10% (and
      more generally β1 ) accuracy.
103
104   We also remark that a list oracle does not trivialize the problem. For a simple example, consider
105   a mixture of k = 2 Gaussians and a list oracle that simply outputs both labels for each point. In
106   this case, the list oracle provides absolutely no additional information at all! More generally, the list
107   oracle could produce lists so that all the points in Gaussians i and j are given the same labels, in
      which case any signal separating i and j seems to be lost.

      Furthermore, even if the list oracle only contains a single label that is correct with arbitrarily high
109   accuracy, the probelm is surprisingly not immediate, in the sense that the naı̈ve algorithm outputting
110   the empirical mean-covariance pairs of the labeled samples does not work. In fact, the algorithm can
111   perform arbitrarily poorly. As a simple example, consider the case where k = 2, so the goal is to
112   learn a mixture of two Gaussians G1 and G2 using n samples, given an oracle with accuracy 1 − n3 ,
113   so that the error rate α = n1 is arbitrarily low. Suppose G1 has uniform spherical covariance and
114   is centered at the origin, while G2 has uniform spherical covariance and is centered at the N · e1 ,
115   for the elementary vector e1 . With constant probability, the oracle labels all points correctly except
116   for a single point. Suppose without loss of generality, the oracle mistakenly labels a sample x from
117   G1 as having been generated from G2 . Since G1 is centered at the origin, then x has distance
118   O (N ) from N · e1 , and thus the empirical mean of the cluster of points labeled as being generated
119   from G2 has changed additively by O N      n . For N ≫ n, the empirical mean of the cluster can be
120
      arbitrarily far from the true mean of G2 , despite only a single error by the oracle. Thus even though
      the example can be quite easily rectified, it is apparent that blindly outputting the empirical means
121
      and covariances of the induced clustering by the list oracle can give arbitrarily large error, even when
122
      the error is arbitrarily small. We observe that the above example of course does not rule out more
123   complex algorithms.

126
      subsequently discuss the    case for general α. We show that a list oracle with β labels, in addition

      the label j, and vice versa. We defer the formal description of precision and recall to Definition 2.4.

      The algorithm for Theorem 1.1 first looks at the sets Pi of points labeled i, for each i ∈ [k]. Under
      bounded recall assumptions, we have that the number of false positives in Pi is a large constant
145
      multiple of the number of points that were truly sampled from Gi . We thus apply a list-decodable
146
      mean and covariance estimation routine to acquire a list Hi of mean-covariance pairs, i.e., Gaussians,
147   including a pair that is close in total variation distance to the true Gaussian Gi .
148
149   Unfortunately, Hi may also include a number of incorrect mean-covariance pairs, or even mean-
150
      covariance pairs corresponding to other Gaussians Gj . A natural approach would be to merge the
      similar pairs and then remove the remaining false mean-covariance pairs through a standard tour-
151
      nament, e.g., Daskalakis et al. (2015); De et al. (2015). However, because each index i ∈ [k]
152
      corresponds to a list with a constant size, the number of possible hypotheses is 2Ω(k) , which would
153
      require an additional number of samples exponential in k, to determine the correct hypothesis.

      Robustness. Thus far, our discussion has centered around algorithmic guarantees that are smooth
209
      with increasingly accurate oracle predictions. However, it also may be the case where the oracle
210   predictions are completely nonsensical. We remark that because we are studying an offline setting,
211   robustness follows immediately from running a classical mixture learning algorithm in addition to
212   our learning-augmented algorithm and choosing the better of the two outputs. In particular, if the
213   output of the classical mixture learning algorithm is D1 and the output of the learning-augmented
214   algorithm is D2 , and these two mixtures have large total variation distance, then it is possible to gen-
215   erate an additional number of samples and run a standard tournament procedure, e.g., (Daskalakis
      et al., 2015; De et al., 2015), to choose the better fit of the two mixtures.

      Empirical evaluations. We complement our theoretical results with a number of experimental
217   results on learning Gaussian mixture models, comparing the performance of a scaled-down version
218   of our learning-augmented algorithm with the standard clustering baseline Lloyd’s algorithm. We
219   generate a number of synthetic datasets from mixture distributions D that are a uniform mixture of k
220   Gaussians that are not well-separated, as otherwise existing techniques can already efficiently learn
221   the Gaussians. We compare the number of points that are correctly labeled by both the baseline
222   algorithm and our learning-augmented algorithm, as well as plot the resulting cluster induced by
223   both algorithms. Our results demonstrate that our learning-augmented algorithm can both produce
224   more accurate classification than the standard baseline across various parameter settings, as well
225
      as produce clusterings that are more aligned with mixtures of Gaussians. Overall, our empirical
      evaluations indicate that our learning-augmented algorithm performs effectively in practice, further
226
      supporting its theoretical guarantees. We present these results in Section 4.

      Experimental setup. To generate our datasets, we define a mixture distribution D that consists
      of k Gaussians, each with weight k1 . Although the exact value of the k Gaussians differ across
466
      our experiments, it is important that these Gaussians are overlapping, since otherwise the resulting
467
      datasets are well-separated and can be easily solved by existing algorithms for learning mixtures of
468   Gaussians. In fact, even existing algorithms for k-clustering problems can easily handle these cases.
469   We then draw n samples from the mixture distribution as the input to the Gaussian mixture model
470   learning algorithms.
471

472
      initializes k centroids, which defines a partition on the input dataset, according to which of the k
473
      centroids is closest to each point of the dataset. It then repeatedly computes the centroid of the points
474   in each partition and then partitions the input dataset on these new centroids. finds the centroid of
475   each set in the partition and then re-partitions the input. We first run Lloyd’s algorithm for a small
476   number of iterations to serve as our predictor. We then continue to run Lloyd’s algorithm for a large
477   number of iterations to server as the baseline.
478

479

530   C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we initiate the study of learning-augmented learning mixture models. Although well-
533   known impossibility results state that any algorithm for learning a mixture of k multivariate Gaus-
534   sian distributions in d-dimensional space requires both runtime and sample complexity exponential
535   in k, even if the Gaussians are somewhat well-separated, we show that with a reasonable (possi-
536   bly erroneous) list  oracle, we can estimate the mixture up to total variation distance Õ(ε) using
537   k · poly d, log k, 1ε samples from the GMM, i.e., only a polynomial number of samples, thereby
538   breaking the computational limitations of traditional algorithms that are unable to incorporate ma-
539   chine learning advice. It is our hope that our work is an important step towards a greater understand-
      ing of learning theory that incorporates possibly erroneous input into better provable guarantees.

      In this section, we consider learning a mixture D of k Gaussians given a label oracle, which provides
      a label for each sample, i.e., the index of the corresponding Gaussian, up to some error α. Specifi-
778
      cally, if x ∼ D is drawn from Gaussian Gi = (µi , Σi ), then informally, the oracle will provide the
779
      label i on query x with probability at least (1 − α). More formally, the error of the oracle needs to
780   respect precision and recall of each cluster as follows:
781
782
      Definition B.1 (Label oracle). Given a set X of n samples x1 , . . . , xn ⊂ Rd from a mixture D of
      k Gaussians G1 , . . . , Gk , let C1 , . . . , Ck be the partition of X such that xi ∈ Cj if xi is drawn
783
      from Gj and let mj = |Gj |. Then a label oracle with error rate α partitions the points into clusters
784
      P1 , . . . , Pk such that for Qj = Pj ∩ Cj , we have |Qj | > (1 − α) max(|Pj |, |Cj |) for all j ∈ [k],
785
      where α < 12 is an error rate for the predictor.
786
787   We remark that a label oracle is a simple heuristic to generate. Consider some clustering of a
788   previous set of samples from the dataset. The clustering can be used to train a model that will
789   provide a label corresponding to one of the clusters, for each query point. In the case the data
790   is well-separated, such a clustering will generally provide good accuracy over samples from the
791   mixture and thus serve as a good label oracle.
792   We also emphasize that only a certain fraction of the labels are required to be correct, and the
793   remaining labels can be arbitrarily incorrect. In particular, the incorrect labels can be adversarially
794   incorrect. We show that learning a mixture of k Gaussians up to arbitrary total variation distance
795   Õ (ε) can be achieved in polynomial runtime and sample complexity, with the aid of a label oracle.
796   As a warm-up, we first  show in Appendix B.1 that a label oracle with error rate α ≤ ε, in addition
797   to k · poly d, log k, 1ε samples from the mixture D of k Gaussians, can be used to output a mixture
798   D′ of k Gaussians such that dTV (D, D′ ) ≤ Õ (ε).
