009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Given an isolated garment image in a canonical product view and a separate im-
012            age of a person, the virtual try-on task aims to generate a new image of the person
013            wearing the target garment. Prior virtual try-on works face two major challenges
014            in achieving this goal: a) the paired (human, garment) training data has limited
015            availability; b) generating textures on the human that perfectly match that of the
016            prompted garment is difficult, often resulting in distorted text and faded textures.
017            Our work addresses these issues through a dual approach. First, we introduce a
018            garment extraction model that generates (human, synthetic garment) pairs from
019
               a single image of a clothed individual. The synthetic pairs can then be used
               to augment the training of virtual try-on. Second, we propose an Error-Aware
020
               Refinement-based Schrödinger Bridge (EARSB) that surgically targets localized
021
               generation errors for correcting the output of a virtual try-on model. To identify
022            likely errors, we propose a weakly-supervised error classifier that localizes regions
023            for refinement, subsequently augmenting the Schrödinger Bridge’s noise schedule
024            with its confidence heatmap. Experiments on VITON-HD and DressCode-Upper
025            demonstrate that our synthetic data augmentation enhances the performance of
026            prior work, while EARSB improves the overall image quality. In user studies, our
027            model is preferred by the users in an average of 59% of cases.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Virtual try-on aims to generate a photo-realistic image of a target person wearing a prompted
033   product-view garment (Li et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024). It allows users to vi-
034   sualize how garments would fit and appear on their bodies without the need for physical trials.
035   While recent methods have made significant strides in this field (Shim et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2023;
036   Yang et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024), noticeable artifacts such as text distortion and faded textures
037   persist in generated images. For example, as illustrated in the second row of Fig. 1, the logo and
038   the text on the t-shirt noticeably fade away in the initial image generated by a prior try-on GAN
039
      model (Shim et al., 2024). These imperfections stem from two primary challenges in virtual try-on:
      limited data availability and the complexity of accurate garment texture deformation. Addressing
040
      the issues, we propose a two-pronged approach: augmenting training data through cost-effective
041
      synthetic data generation, and surgically targeting known generation artifacts using our proposed
042   Error-Aware Refinement-based Schrödinger Bridge (EARSB).
043
044   At a minimum, the training data of virtual try-on requires paired (human, product-view garment) im-
045
      ages. The product-view garment image is a canonical, front-facing view of the clothing with a clean
      background. A substantial amount of data is needed to capture the combinatorial space comprising
046
      all possible human poses, skin tones, viewing angles, and their respective physical interactions with
047
      fabric textures, shapes, letterings, and other material properties. Unfortunately, these images are
048   generally available only on copyright-protected product webpages and therefore not readily avail-
049   able for use. To mitigate this issue, we propose to augment training with synthetic data generated
050   from the easier symmetric human-to-garment task, wherein we train a garment-extraction model to
051   extract a canonical product-view garment image from an image of a clothed person. This will allow
052   us to create synthetic paired training data from unpaired datasets (Liu et al., 2016; Fu et al., 2022;
053   Xie et al., 2021). Our results demonstrate that incorporating the more readily available synthetic
      training pairs can improve image generation quality in the virtual try-on task.

      In addition to addressing the data scarcity issue, we aim to construct a refinement model that can
078
      make localized adjustments to a weaker model’s generation results. Our approach draws inspiration
079
      from classical boosting approaches where every model in a cascade of models targets the shortcom-
080   ings of the preceding models. We are interested in a targeted refinement approach for two main
081   reasons. First, it allows a training objective that is focused solely on fixing specific errors. Second,
082   it potentially saves computation when initial predictions are sufficiently good.
083
      Two components are necessary to achieve such a pipeline: a classifier for identifying localized gen-
084
      eration errors, and a refinement model that can re-synthesize content specifically in these localized
085
      regions. While it may be possible to learn an artifact classifier by repurposing a GAN’s fake/real
086   discriminator, we found that an effective weakly-supervised classifier can be constructed with just a
087   few hours of manual labeling of generation errors. For example, we use bounding boxes to quickly
088   annotate the noticeable artifacts in 5% images generated by existing GANs (Lee et al. (2022); Xie
089   et al. (2023); Shim et al. (2024)). Another benefit of this approach is that it can be easily tailored for
090   the errors of a specific model. The resulting weakly-supervised classifier will produce an error map
091   highlighting low-quality regions. Subsequently, we adopt an Image-to-Image Schrodinger Bridge
092   (I2 SB) (Liu et al., 2023) to learn the refinement of these regions in the GAN-generated images.
093   While typical diffusion models map from noise to data, I2 SB constructs a SchrödingerBridge (SB)
094
      that allows us to map from data to data, or in our setup, generations with artifacts to ground truth
      images. We direct the SB process to focus on the localized errors by incorporating the classifier’s
095
      prediction error into the noise schedule, which we describe in more detail in Sec. 3.2.1. In the
096
      second row of Fig. 1, our refinement SB model (i.e., EARSB) corrects the faded texture in the initial
097   GAN-generated image.
098
099   The contributions of our paper are summarized as follows:
100
             • We introduce (human, synthetic garment) pairs as an augmentation in the training of virtual
101
               try-on task. The synthetic garment is obtained from our human-to-garment model, which
102            can generate product-view garment images from human images.
103          • We propose EARSB, an Error-Aware Refinement SB with a weakly-supervised noise
104            schedule. It improves the low-quality region of a GAN-generated image based on an adap-
105            tive noise schedule that targets known artifacts.
106          • Extensive experiments on two datasets (VITON-HD (Lee et al., 2022) and DressCode-
107            Upper (Morelli et al., 2022)) show that EARSB enhances the quality of the images gener-
               ated by prior work, and is preferred by the users in 59% cases on average.

      2     R ELATED W ORK

      Training with Synthetic Data. The addition of synthetic data is often an effective means of im-
111   proving downstream task performance when it is difficult to amass real data at the necessary scale.
112   This has been demonstrated in the domains of image generation (Karras et al., 2020; Shivashankar
113   & Miller, 2023), classification tasks (Sarıyıldız et al., 2023), and image editing (Brooks et al., 2023;
114   Wasserman et al., 2024). Careful applications can also be used to ameliorate dataset imbalance
115   issues, as shown in Dablain et al. (2022). Other works such as Alemohammad et al. (2024) use
116   self-synthesized data to provide negative guidance for the diffusion model. Our incorporation of
117   synthetic data in the virtual try-on task tackles a specific sub-problem in the broader image editing
118   domain and is similar in spirit to Brooks et al. (2023); Wasserman et al. (2024). Specifically, we aim
119
      to synthesize paired training data that satisfies the stringent requirements of virtual try-on paired
      training data – a canonical product-view garment image paired with an example of it being worn.
120
      Images of people in clothing are readily available, but it is difficult to obtain a product-view image
121
      of the exact clothing they are wearing. To address this, our work proposes and tackles the human-
122   to-garment, which is roughly symmetric to the virtual try-on task and aims to extract the clothing
123   from a person’s photo and project it to the canonical product view.
124
125
      Virtual Try-On. There has been a shift from earlier GAN-based framework (Han et al., 2019;
      Lee et al., 2022; Shim et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023) to diffusion-based methods
126
      (Kim et al., 2024) in the virtual try-on literature. Diffusion models fit an SDE process mapping
127
      from the image distribution to the noise distribution, and tend to be easier to train than GAN-based
128   approaches due to the simplicity of the L2 denoising loss (Gao et al., 2023; Dhariwal & Nichol,
129   2021; Song & Ermon, 2019). At inference, the diffusion model denoises a random Gaussian noise
130   distribution to a human-readable image via multiple sampling steps. Yang et al. (2024) proposes
131   a parameter-efficient approach that concatenates the human image and the garment images along
132   the spatial dimension such that the self-attention layer in the denoising UNet can achieve texture
133   transfer without extra parameters. In (Kim et al., 2024), the authors introduce additional cross-
134   attention layers to learn the semantic correspondences between the garment and the human image.
135   The methods in (Ning et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Baldrati et al., 2023) align different embedding
136
      spaces in the attention module to achieve flexible clothing editing after try-on, such as style change
      or graphics insertion. In contrast to prior work that samples from random noise, we build upon recent
137
      advances in Schrödinger bridges, notably Liu et al. (2023), to directly sample from an initial image
138
      generated by a cheaper, but reasonably good GAN model. Our work is similar in spirit to Zeng
139   et al. (2024), which initializes the noisy image with a GAN-generated image and small amounts of
140   random noise. However, we consider the Schrödinger bridge formulation to be a more principled
141   approach to mapping from a pre-existing result. Our work also explores varying the local noise
142   schedule based on how much we expect the original result to be modified at a given location.

      Virtual try-on aims to re-synthesize a given human image with a newly specified garment. Critically,
147
      we wish to maintain the original pose of the human model, while plausibly depicting how the speci-
148   fied garment would deform and appear when worn on said model’s body. We study the feasibility of
149   improving current virtual try-on models by addressing both the data scarcity issue as well as through
150   a refinement model designed to find-and-fix localized generation errors. We describe our approach
151   to generating synthetic training data in 3.1, and then our EARSB refinement model in 3.2.
152
153   3.1   AUGMENTED T RAINING WITH S YNTHETIC PAIRED DATA

156   where we have both the ground truth image of a human model wearing a target garment, as well as
157   a canonical product-view image of the isolated garment. We will refer to this setup as paired for
158
      short. Given these data, one can then train a try-on model by masking out the target garment from
      the ground-truth model’s image and reconstructing it using the product-view garment image (see the
159
      left-most preprocessing step of 3).

      brand protection. Instead, obtaining single human images is considerably more feasible (Liu et al.,

      3.1.1    H UMAN - TO -G ARMENT M ODEL
182
183   While virtual try-on requires generating skin and deforming the product-view garment to accom-
184   modate diverse postures, the human-to-garment task simply aims to map the clothing item to its
185   canonical view. To achieve this, we extract the clothing on the person using its segmentation map,
186   and feed it to a GAN generator that generates a canonical view of the clothing, as illustrated in Fig.
187   2a. The generator architecture is based on the UNet model proposed in (Han et al., 2019), which
188   uses a flow-like mechanism for warping latent features in an optical-flow-like manner. The generator
189
      was trained using a combined L1 reconstruction and adversarial loss.
190   Generating Synthetic Data H2G-UH and H2G-FH. With the above human-to-garment model,
191   we can obtain the product-view garments from single-human images (Liu et al., 2016; Fu et al.,
192   2022; Xie et al., 2021), and create (real human, synthetic garment) pairs to facilitate the try-on task.
193   We use the following criteria to ensure quality: a) The human image has a clean background (low
194   pixel variance in the non-human region); b) The human image is of front view (classified by its
195
      DensePose representation (Güler et al., 2018)); c) the reconstruction error (LPIPS distance) is small
      when reconstructing the human image in a try-on model using the (human, synthetic garment) pair
196
      (e.g., (Lee et al., 2022; Shim et al., 2024)). Under these criteria, we select human images from
197
      DeepFashion2 (Liu et al., 2016) and UPT (Xie et al., 2021), eventually creating 12,730 synthetic
198   pairs of upper-body human images (referred to as H2G-UH) and 8,939 pairs of full-body human
199   images (referred to as H2G-FH). Examples of the synthetic pairs are shown in Fig. 2b.

201   3.1.2    V IRTUAL T RY- ON (G ARMENT- ON -H UMAN ) T RAINING
202
203   Even with our filtering criteria, our synthetic data remains imperfect. We explore two means of
204   limiting the effect of the real-synthetic domain gap: (a) pretraining the try-on model using synthetic
205
      pairs and finetune it on real pairs (Kumar et al., 2022); (b) training simultaneously on real and
      synthetic data, but conditioning the try-on model on a real/synthetic flag, similarly to (Jun et al.,
206
      2020). The difference between these two is that in the first augmentation, the model only sees
207
      synthetic data at an early training stage, while in the second augmentation, the model takes both
208   real and synthetic pairs throughout the training. We found empirically that the second augmentation
209   performs slightly better than the first (See Sec. 4.1).

247   3.2.1   O BTAINING THE E RROR M AP
248
249   The error map M is a score map that highlights the corrupted or incorrect area of the initial im-
250   age x1 . While it may be possible to learn an artifact classifier by repurposing a GAN’s fake/real
251   discriminator, we found that an effective weakly-supervised classifier can be constructed with just
252   a few hours of manual labeling of generation errors. As shown in Fig. 3, our weakly-supervised
253
      classifier consists of two encoders with a cross-attention module. The two encoder outputs are fused
      with cross attention to output a sigmoid-activated error map.
254
255   Ideally, the classifier should predict 1 for GAN-generated low-quality regions and 0 for error-free
256   regions in M . However, we do not have full annotations for where all the generated artifacts are
257   located. Further, automated metrics such as localized reconstruction errors would produce too many
258   false positives, as they do not account for the full distribution of valid renderings. To mitigate this
259
      issue, we hand-labeled 5% GAN-generated images in the training set at the patch-level for poorly
      generated regions. We then train with a weakly supervised objective that uses patch-level supervision
260
      for labeled images, and image-level supervision (GAN-generated vs real) for unlabeled images.

      Trade-off Between Sampling Efficiency and Image Quality. The generated images of diffusion
420   models often show degraded quality with fewer sampling steps. EARSB is based on Schrödinger
421   bridges and inherits the ability to produce reasonable results at fewer step counts as previously
422   demonstrated in (Liu et al., 2023; Lipman et al., 2023). Furthermore, our noise schedule manipula-
423   tion can keep the correct clothing textures in the GAN-generated image untouched with zero noise
424   level and only fix the erroneous parts, potentially resulting in less image quality degradation with
425   few steps. In Fig. 5, While other SD-based methods have a sharp performance drop with decreasing
426   sampling steps, EARSB and EARSB +H2G-UH show consistent performance across different sam-
427   pling steps, demonstrating a better trade-off between image quality and computational efficiency.

429   texture consistency, and image fidelity, comparing our model against GP-GAN and Stable-VTON.
430   We randomly selected 100 pairs from VITON-HD to evaluate on, assigning at least 3 workers per
431   image. Tab. 2 reports the study results, where our method is preferred at least 10% more than the
      GAN-based GP-VTON and the SD-based Stable-VTON (59% overall).

475   Synthetic Pairs Augmentation. To validate the effectiveness of synthetic pairs on enhancing exist-
476   ing diffusion methods, we incorporate H2G-UH into the training of Stable-VTON (Kim et al., 2024)
477   and CAT-DM (Zeng et al., 2024) on the VITON-HD dataset. Here, we use the number of sampling
478   steps as originally published: 2 for CAT-DM, 50 for Stable-VTON, and 25 for our own EARSB.
479   Tab. 3(a) gives the results, with +H2G-UH indicating the data augmentation, indicating that training
480   with our synthetic H2G-UH improves most metrics of the above SD-based models.
481   To determine the importance of the canonical product-view projection, we replace the synthetic
482   garment in each pair with a randomly warped version of the clothing cropped from the real hu-
483   man image, denoted as +W (H2G-UH). The results show that while +W (H2G-UH) hinders the
484   performance of all baseline approaches (i.e., CAT-DM, Stable-VTON and EARSB), incorporating
485   H2G-UH improves most metrics. This demonstrates that the synthetic product-view contributes
      significantly to the observed improvements.

493   Error-Aware Noise Schedule. The error map adapts the noise distribution according to the quality
494   of the image patches in the initial image x1 . This adaptive approach contrasts with a uniform Gaus-
495   sian noise application across all locations, which would essentially reduce our model to I2 SB. As
496   demonstrated in Table 3(c), removing the error map during training (EARSB w.o. M ) results in a
497   substantial decline across all metrics. Furthermore, we explored using a random error map during the
498   sampling process (EARSB(rand(M ))), which also leads to diminished performance. These results
499   underscore the importance of a meaningful error map in precisely locating and enhancing targeted
500   regions. Additionally, the slight performance degradation observed when removing the classifier
501
      guidance (EARSB w.o. CG) suggests that the error map employed in our classifier guidance also
      contributes to overall image quality improvement. Collectively, these findings highlight the crucial

      role of our adaptive noise schedule in achieving superior results.

531   5   C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      In this paper, addressing the data limitation in virtual try-on, we introduce a human-to-garment
534   model that generates (human, synthetic garment) pairs from a single image of a clothed individual.
535   In addition, we propose a refinement model EARSB that surgically targets localized generation
536   errors from the output of a prior model. We propose to use a weakly-supervised error classifier
537   to identify localized regions to refine. These identified regions are incorporated into the SB by
538   augmenting its noise schedule with the error classifier’s confidence heatmap. Experiments on two
539   benchmark datasets demonstrate that our synthetic data augmentation improves the performance of
      existing methods and that EARSB enhances the quality of the images generated by prior models.
