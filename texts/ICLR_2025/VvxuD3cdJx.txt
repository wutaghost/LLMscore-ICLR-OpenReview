009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-task learning is favored due to its efficiency and potential transfer learn-
012            ing achieved by sharing networks across tasks. While a series of multi-task op-
013            timization algorithms (MTOs) have been proposed to solve MTL optimization
014            challenges and enhance performance, recent research claims that simple linear
015            scalarization, which sums per-task loss with a carefully searched weight set, is
016            sufficient, casting doubt on the added value of more complex MTO algorithms. In
017            this paper, we provide a novel perspective that linear scalarization and MTOs are
018            closely related and can be combined to yield high performance and efficiency. We
019
               show, for the first time, that a well-performing linear scalarization exhibits spe-
               cific characteristics of certain optimization metrics proposed by MTOs, such as
020
               high task gradient magnitude similarity and low condition number, via an exten-
021
               sive empirical study. We then propose AutoScale, an efficient pipeline that lever-
022            ages these influential metrics to guide the search for optimal linear scalarization
023            weights. AutoScale shows superior performance than prior MTOs and performs
024            close to the searched weight performance consistently across different datasets.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Multi-task learning (MTL) has gained significant attention in deep learning, due to its efficiency in
030   using a single network to learn multiple tasks, with acceptable or comparable performance to single-
031   task learning (Chen et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2024; Xin et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024). Especially for
032   recent large-scale end-to-end models, MTL has become a convenient and attractive choice for users
033   and maintainers (Hu et al., 2023).
034
      However, multi-task optimization (MTO) issues, such as gradient conflict and gradient dominance,
035
      have been a challenge in MTL, which can lead to impartial learning, where tasks interfere and com-
036   pete for limited shared representation power (Chen et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021b; Yu et al., 2020;
037   Senushkin et al., 2023; Ban & Ji, 2024; Lin et al., 2024). Over the years, researchers have developed
038   a series of multi-task optimization algorithms (MTOs) and proposed metrics to analyze and quantify
039   the optimization issues, subsequently used to guide the training process. Common MTO metrics
040   describe loss scale balance (Chennupati et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021b), gradient magnitude (Chen
041   et al., 2018; Sener & Koltun, 2018; Liu et al., 2021b) and angle (Yu et al., 2020) similarities, stability
042   (Senushkin et al., 2023), and task convergence progress (Guo et al., 2018). While MTOs claim supe-
043   rior performance compared to unitary scalarization, which sums per-task losses with equal weights,
044
      they are often criticized for their large computational and memory overhead due to per-task gradient
      calculations (Xin et al., 2022; Kurin et al., 2022).
045
046   Recently, Xin et al. (2022); Kurin et al. (2022); Royer et al. (2024) surprisingly observe that linear
047   scalarization, which sums up the per-task loss with a fixed weight set, performs comparably or even
048   superior to MTOs when the task weights are carefully chosen. This finding is notable because linear
049   scalarization is conceptually and operationally simple, and it requires just a single backpropagation
050
      during training, despite the high computation cost of weight search. There is hence an ongoing
      debate about whether complicated MTO algorithms are necessary or even help.
051
052   To answer the question, we propose a novel perspective that bridges MTOs and linear scalarization:
053   certain metrics proposed by MTOs, designed to quantify optimization issues of multi-task training,
      are useful in guiding the search for optimal linear scalarization weights. This offers a more efficient

054
      alternative to weight search methods, such as grid search. Specifically, through extensive exper-
055   iments, we show a strong correlation between linear scalarization performance and the key MTO
056   metrics during training, as depicted in Figure 3. Based on the insights, we propose AutoScale, a
057   two-phase automatic pipeline, which calculates an optimal weight set by optimizing key MTO met-
058   rics using gradient and loss information collected during the first training stage, and applies this
059   fixed weight set for linear scalarization in the remaining second stage. We demonstrate the effec-
060   tiveness of AutoScale across multiple datasets, including CityScapes (Cordts et al., 2016), NYUv2
061   (Silberman et al., 2012), and Nuscenes (Caesar et al., 2020).
062   To summarize, besides presenting a comprehensive summary of various MTO algorithms and met-
063   rics, our primary contributions are as follows:
064
065          • We identify, for the first time according to our knowledge, the relationship between MTO
066            metrics and optimal linear scalarization: a well-performing linear scalarization typically
067            exhibits specific characteristics of certain MTO metrics, such as high gradient similarity
068
               among tasks and low condition number, which could serve as reliable indicators for deter-
               mining the optimal weight set.
069
070          • We introduce AutoScale, an efficient two-phase pipeline combining both MTOs and linear
071            scalarization. Our method estimates an optimal linear scalarization weight set by optimiz-
072            ing key MTO metrics. Compared with gradient manipulating MTOs, our design reduces
073
               training time significantly.
074          • We conduct extensive experiments to show that AutoScale outperforms prior MTO methods

076            grid search, across various datasets including a large-scale autonomous driving dataset.

082   2.1   M ULTI -TASK L EARNING : OVERVIEW
083
084
      Research in multi-task learning (MTL), particularly within deep learning, has largely focused on
      three main directions: (1) MTL-specific architectures, (2) task grouping, and (3) Multi-Task Op-
085
      timization algorithms (MTOs). MTL-specific architecture aims to improve performance by de-
086
      signing customized network structures for better handling multiple tasks (Misra et al., 2016; Dai
087   et al., 2016; Long et al., 2017; Ye & Xu, 2023). Task grouping, on the other hand, explores the
088   relationships among tasks and reduces negative transfer by grouping non-conflicting or minimally-
089   conflicting tasks during training (Thrun & O’Sullivan, 1996; Zamir et al., 2018; Standley et al.,
090   2020). Lastly, MTOs address the problem by designing optimal algorithms to manipulate and com-
091   bine task-specific gradients to update network parameters during back-propagation (Chen et al.,
092   2018; Senushkin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). In this work, we focus on the last approach consid-
093   ering both MTOs and linear scalarization.
094
095   2.2   M ULTI -TASK O PTIMIZATION
096
097   We categorize MTL training issues into five types: (1) gradient dominance, (2) gradient conflict, (3)
098
      imbalanced convergence speed, (4) imbalanced loss, and (5) instability.
099   Gradient Dominance. Variations in the scale of task-wise gradients on the shared parameters create
100   impartial learning outcomes (Liu et al., 2021b), where the network converged primarily on tasks with
101   higher gradient magnitudes (as shown in Figure 1a). To tackle this, GradNorm (Chen et al., 2018)
102   dynamically adjusts the task weights to ensure the norm of each task’s scaled gradient is balanced.
103   While IMTL-G (Liu et al., 2021b) approaches this by finding an aggregated gradient with equal
104
      projections onto each task gradient.
105   Gradient Conflict. Conflicting gradients with opposing directions (as shown in Figure 1b) could
106   cause negative transfers (Senushkin et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2018). CosReg (Suteu & Guo, 2019)
107   proposes a regularization term based on squared cosine similarity between tasks, penalizing the net-
      work when conflicting gradients are generated. PCGrad (Yu et al., 2020), on the other hand, avoids

122   Imbalanced Convergence Speed. Different tasks inherently have varying levels of difficulty, po-
123   tentially leading to different convergence speeds (Guo et al., 2018; Yun & Cho, 2023) as shown in
124   Figure 1c. To address this issue, methods like GradNorm (Chen et al., 2018), DTP (Guo et al., 2018),
125   DWA (Liu et al., 2019), AMTL (Yun & Cho, 2023) and ExcessMTL (He et al., 2024) define specific
126
      measures of training convergence and adjust task weights based on these indicators. Additionally,
      Jacob et al. (2023) proposes to train single-task networks alongside the MTL network, using the

131   losses. GLS (Chennupati et al., 2019) adopts geometric mean to prevent tasks with larger losses
132
      from dominating the overall loss. Following GLS, Yun & Cho (2023) proposes a weighted geometric
      mean of loss that is robust to scale variation. Liu et al. (2021b) proposes IMTL-L to derive task
133
      weights to balance re-scaled losses.
134
135   Stability Aligned-MTL (Senushkin et al., 2023) defines stability in MTL training as the stability of
136   the linear system formed of task gradients. It proposes to stabilize the training process by aligning
137   the principal components of the gradient matrix.
138   Additionally, we further discuss previously proposed MTO metrics to quantify and analyze these
139   five MTL issues in section 3.1.
140
141   2.3      R EVISITING L INEAR S CALARIZATION
142
143   In recent years, linear scalarization has been revisited and argued to be a superior alternative to more
144   complex MTOs. Although linear scalarization has been shown to fail beyond the non-convex part
145   of the Pareto front (Hu et al., 2024), studies such as Kurin et al. (2022); Xin et al. (2022); Elich
146   et al. (2024); Royer et al. (2024) demonstrate that, in practice, it achieves performance comparable
147
      to or even better than other MTOs through large-scale experiments. However, a major open chal-
      lenge for linear scalarization is identifying the optimal set of scalarization weights with minimal
148
      computational overhead. Although more efficient search methods have been proposed (Royer et al.,
149
      2024), they remain costly compared to directly applying existing MTOs due to requiring multiple
150   training runs. In this work, we address this problem of costly search by proposing a unified pipeline
151   to efficiently localize optimal scalarization weights with minimal overhead.

302   even by leveraging data from multiple runs to account for network randomness during training, to
303   get a precise and robust optimal weight for a specific combination of dataset, tasks, and model.

      We then propose AutoScale, an efficient and practical two-stage pipeline that partitions a single
306
      training run into two phases. The idea is to use the first phase’s statistics to calculate an approximated
307
      optimal weight wˆ∗ , which is then applied for linear scalarization in the remaining second phase. A
308   summary of AutoScale is provided in Algorithm 1.
309
310   Specifically, in the first Eexploration phase, we run a selected MTO algorithm (e.g. PCGrad, IMTL-
311
      G) to collect training statistics, including gradients and losses, required for later weight optimization.
      We divide the Eexploration iterations into disjoint windows. For each window with index i, a local
312
      optimal weight set wi is calculated by optimizing key MTO metrics through minimizing the cost
313
      function F(w). Using the local weight sets calculated for each window in the Eexploration phase,
314   we estimate an optimal weight set to be used in the subsequent Llinear Sscalarization phase. To do
315   this, we apply a predictor, f : {wi } 7→ ŵ∗ , which maps the derived local weight sets to a single
316   output as the approximation of optimal weight. In Section 4.2, we introduce the specific design of
317   the weight predictor f ({wi }).

409   use the Mean Rank (MR) and ∆m metrics to evaluate multi-task performance. 1) ∆m measures
410   thePaverage performance drop relative to the single-task baseline across all tasks. ∆m =
       1    K       σk                                  Mk −Bk
411   K     k=1 (−1) δmk . Here, we denote δmk =           Bk    × 100 as the performance difference on
412   task k, where Mk and Bk are the kth task metric evaluated on a multi-task model and a single-task
413   baseline respectively. σk = 1 if Mk is higher the better, and σk = 0 otherwise. 2) Mean Rank
414
      (MR) is the average ranking of performance across all tasks over all methods. For example, if a
      method ranks first on one task but second on the other task, MR = (1 + 2)/2 = 1.5.

419   ((−1)σk δmk > 0) while disregarding improvements ((−1)σk δmk < 0). When ∆m is similar
420   across methods, ∆mpos helps distinguish which methods minimize degradation, offering a lower

474   5.1      A BLATION S TUDY
475
476   We conduct an extensive ablation of our AutoScale using the default setting outlined in Section 5.
477   If not otherwise stated, the following experiments are based on CityScapes dataset (Cordts et al.,
478   2016) with PSPnet (Zhao et al., 2017).
479
      Ablation on exploration ratio α. Figure 6 shows the impact of α on both performance and average
480   training iteration time. A higher α results in the higher portion of training iterations being allocated
481   to running MTOs and to collect loss and gradients for the exploration phase, which is computa-
482   tionally more demanding. We empirically find that an α of 0.2 strikes a good balance between
483   computational time and performance. Though high α in general induces better performance than
484   low α, we argue that it would sacrifice the efficiency benefit of linear scalarization and therefore
485   considered sub-optimal. Note that when α = 1, it is equivalent to running the chosen MTO for the
      entire training.

      MTOs selection in exploration phase. Since the selected MTO fascilitates the training of the
487   network during early iterations in exploration phase, we argue that the choice of such MTOs is
488   important that it does not drive the network to a poor local minimum. To illustrate this point, we
489   perform ablation with five MTO methods: unitary scalarization, PCGrad (Yu et al., 2020), Aligned-
490   MTL (Senushkin et al., 2023), IMTL-G (Liu et al., 2021b), and FAMO (Liu et al., 2024). The
491   results, shown in Table 3 and Figure 7, reveal that different methods yield varying outcomes. The
492   performance gap is clear: unitary scalarization and PCGrad perform noticeably worse compared
493   to Aligned-MTL, IMTL-G, and FAMO, with IMTL-G and FAMO slightly outperforming Aligned-
494   MTL. Aligned with the table results, Figure 7 also shows two distinct trends in the calculated weights
495
      based on the gradients collected during the exploration phase: unitary scalarization and PCGrad be-
      have similarly, while the other three methods follow a different pattern. It highlights the importance
496
      of MTOs selection, as some methods are more prone to pitfalls such as converging to a subop-
497
      timal local minimum. Additionally, our findings suggest that certain MTO methods enhance our
498   AutoScale pipeline’s performance, offering evidence against earlier debates on the effectiveness of
499   MTOs by helping avoid suboptimal solutions and improving optimization.

      Different constrain function F(w). To calculate the optimized weight for the gradients and losses
      collected in the exploration phase, we experiment on different cost functions F(w), including opti-
502
      mizing for low condition number, equal loss scale |L| and equal gradient magnitude |g| among tasks.
503
      The results in Table 4 shows that using equal gradient magnitude gets a robust good performance
504   over different datasets.

      tionally, we test on the continuous linear fit until γ = 0.5 (L.E.† ). As shown in Table 5, different
      datasets prefer different f (w) methods. Overall, based on the mean rank (MR) across three datasets,

      the linear extrapolated value at a fixed point γ shows the most robust and consistent performance.

      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we propose a novel perspective on the ongoing debate between MTO algorithms and
532
      linear scalarization. Through a comprehensive set of experiments, we identify that well-performing
533
      linear scalarization aligns with specific characteristics of certain MTO metrics, including high gra-
534   dient magnitude similarity, low condition number, and more balanced loss scale across tasks. These
535   findings help bridge the gap between linear scalarization and existing MTOs, highlighting the im-
536   portance of both in addressing MTL training challenges. Building on the insights, we introduce
537   AutoScale, an efficient pipeline which combines both: determine the optimal linear scalarization
538   weights using MTL metrics in a two-phase way. AutoScale achieves state-of-the-art performance
539   across a wide range of benchmarks including a large-scale modern autonomous driving dataset,
      trailing only the searched weights, but without the need of grid search.

      We add the key metrics trajectories during the training of our AutoScale, based on Figure 2. As
705   shown in below figure, AutoScale exhibits favorable trends across different metrics, including a low
706   condition number, balanced convergence speed (inverse learning rate), balanced loss scale, and equal
707   angles to the final aggregated gradient, even when using the default cost function of equal gradient
708   magnitude. It is evident that these metrics are not independent, suggesting potential future work can
709   be explored.
710
      Additionally, we observe an interesting pattern with IMTL-G. When IMTL-G is used during the first
711   20% of the exploration phase (α = 0.2), it achieves near-perfect gradient magnitude similarity (close
712   to 1) and gradient cosine similarity with the final aggregated gradient (with low standard deviation
713   among tasks). This aligns with IMTL-G’s objective of enforcing equal gradient magnitudes and
714   angle with the aggregated gradient. However, it sacrifices loss scale balance, as indicated by a high
715   standard deviation in the relative loss scale among tasks during this phase.
