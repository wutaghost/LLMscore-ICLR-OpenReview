009                                                 Abstract
010
011
                 Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is a critical method for
012              aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences. However, existing
013              offline alignment approaches, such as DPO, IPO, and SLiC, rely heavily on static
014              datasets of human preferences, often leading to suboptimal performance. Recent
015              efforts in the literature have moved towards online RLHF methods, but they lack
016              a unified framework and suffer from distribution shift issues. In this work, we
017              formalize online LLM alignment as a bilevel optimization problem. By reducing
018              this formulation to a more computationally efficient single-level first-order method,
019              utilizing reward-policy equivalence, we propose SAIL (Self-improving Efficient
020
                 Online Alignment).SAIL generates new samples and iteratively refines model
                 alignment through online exploration and regulation of preference labels. This
021
                 enables continuous, self-improving alignment and generalizes prior online RLHF
022
                 methods as special cases. Compared to state-of-the-art RLHF methods, SAIL
023              delivers significant performance gains, with up to 11.6% improvement in win rate
024              and a 3.6-point increase in evaluation rewards, while maintaining low computational
025              overhead.

      1     Introduction
030
031   As AI systems increasingly outperform humans in various tasks, ensuring that these systems align with
032   human values and ethics is paramount, especially in the case of large language models (LLMs) trained
033
      on extensive and diverse datasets that often contain harmful or biased content. Reinforcement Learning
      from Human Feedback (RLHF) has emerged as a key approach for AI alignment, as demonstrated by
034
      models like OpenAI’s GPT-4, Google’s Gemini, and Anthropic’s Claude, which exhibit safer, more
035
      aligned behaviors. Yet, most RLHF research (Agarwal et al., 2020; Rafailov et al., 2023; Ouyang
036   et al., 2022; Chakraborty et al., 2024; Swamy et al., 2024) focuses on offline settings, relying on fixed
037   datasets of human-labeled responses generated by supervised fine-tuned models (SFTs). These static
038   datasets, often derived from Oracle or pre-trained models, limit generalization to real-world data and
039   fail to capture the full complexity and diversity of real-world responses. As a result, these methods
040   struggle with suboptimal alignment, particularly when encountering new, unseen data.
041
      Recent research (Guo et al., 2024a; Sharma et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024b) has
042   begun exploring online RLHF methods to overcome the limitations of static offline datasets. These
043   methods attempt to address two critical questions: (Q1) How should new responses be generated
044   during fine-tuning? and (Q2) How should new preference feedback be collected to update the language
045   model? In the existing literature (Sharma et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2023), Q1 is typically answered
046   by allowing the current LLM to generate new responses at each iteration, while Q2 relies on an
047   assumed access to a preference oracle to rank the responses. However, despite these advancements,
048   key challenges remain in unlocking the full potential of online RLHF.
049   (Challenge I) Interdependence of model and data in (implicit) reward learning. Existing online
050   RLHF methods overlook the crucial interdependence between the model and data. The responses
051   used to (implicitly) learn a reward function that guides model updates are generated by the model
052   itself. This interdependence introduces distribution shift problems, as subsequent model updates rely
053   on suboptimal data generated by earlier iterations, leading to performance gaps (Chakraborty et al.,
      2023; Shen et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024b) (see fig. 1). A prior work, PARL (Chakraborty et al., 2023),

054
      addressed this issue using bilevel optimization, where the upper-level reward optimization depends
055   on an optimal model π ∗ , obtained as the solution of a lower-level reinforcement learning problem.
056
057   (Challenge II) Computationally prohibitive bilevel optimization. Bilevel optimization for online
058
      RLHF, while principled, suffers from computational intractability and requires complex gradient
      estimation. The Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) method (Rafailov et al., 2023), in contrast,
059
      provides an efficient alternative by avoiding costly hyper-gradient computations, yet it does not fully
060
      address the distribution shift issue in online RLHF. This raises the question: Can we overcome
061   distribution shift without incurring prohibitive computational costs?
062
063   (Challenge III) Dependence on preference oracles. A key assumption of many RLHF methods is
064
      the availability of a preference oracle to rank responses. However, relying on human annotations for
      each preference comparison is unrealistic at scale. While small, curated datasets of human preferences
065
      can be obtained, continuous dependence on a preference oracle is not feasible in an online setting.
066
067   Can we provide a principled framework for online RLHF to (i) optimally generate new responses
068   during fine-tuning resolving prior issues in offline RLHF; and (ii) alleviate the requirement of access
069   to a preference oracle to generate alignment data?
070   To address these challenges, we propose SAIL (Self-improving Efficient Online Alignment). First,
071   we introduce a unified optimization framework for online RLHF based on bilevel optimization, which
072   effectively captures the entanglement between reward learning and language model policy updates,
073   thereby accounting for the statistical dependencies often overlooked in prior work. This allows us to
074   mitigate the distribution shift issue commonly encountered in online RLHF. Subsequently, SAIL
075   reduces the bilevel problem into a computationally efficient single-level optimization procedure, pre-
076
      serving the theoretical advantages of bilevel optimization while significantly lowering computational
      complexity. SAIL serves as an online counterpart to DPO, with an additional gradient term that
077
      promotes exploration. Compared to offline DPO, SAIL introduces no additional overhead during
078
      the model update phase, though generation overhead is inherent due to its online nature. Moreover,
079   SAIL incorporates a self-improvement mechanism that alleviates the reliance on external preference
080   oracles by leveraging online exploration and iterative feedback refinement. This enables continuous
081   alignment improvement, making the model more robust to unseen and evolving data.

095   We summarize our contributions as follows:
096   (1) A unified mathematical framework for LLM alignment. We design a principled framework for
097   online RLHF by providing concrete guidance on the generation of new responses under the preference
098   oracle assumption. Inspired by the bilevel RLHF literature, we develop a computationally tractable
099
      online optimization procedure that converges to ground truth with provable guarantees.
      (2) Adaptive direct preference optimization. Although inherently bilevel, our framework introduces
100
      an efficient single-level solution using DPO-style analysis, effectively addressing distribution shifts
101
      and offering a scalable approach for online preference optimization.
102   (3) Relaxing the preference oracle assumption. We introduce a self-improving mechanism requiring
103   only initial access to an offline dataset. The model iteratively improves alignment during online training,
104   relaxing the need for exhaustive supervision or continuous preference oracle access.
105   (4) Extensive experimental evaluations. We conduct comprehensive experiments across multiple
106   datasets, demonstrating that SAIL significantly outperforms state-of-the-art baselines. Our approach
107   achieves up to an 11.6% improvement in win rate and a 3.6-point increase in evaluation rewards,
      showcasing its superior performance both with and without access to a preference oracle.

      2     Problem Formulation
109
110
      Mathematical Notations. We start by defining the language model mathematically, where we denote
111   the vocabulary set by V, and represent the language model by a mapping π, which takes a sequence
112   of tokens (prompt) as input denoted by x := {x1 , x2 , · · · , xN }, a set of prompts denoted by P, and
113   generates the response y := {y1 , y2 , · · · , yT } in a token-by-token fashion. To determine the next
114   token at the tth timepoint yt , the input prompt x and generated tokens y<t are fed as input to the
115   language model as a new prompt [x, y<t ]. Then the next token is sampled as yt ∼ π(· | [x, y<t ]).

142   2.2   Issue of Distribution Shift in Iterative Online RLHF
143
144
      A critical issue in the majority of the existing formulations of online RLHF lies in an inaccurate
      characterization of the dependence of the responses generated by the optimal policy πr∗ (· | x) on the
145
      reward learning objective eq. (1). Specifically, at the tth iteration, the dataset Drt = {(x, yw , yl ) |
146
      x ∼ P, (y1 , y2 ) ∼ πr∗t (· | x), (yw , yl ) ∼ p∗ (· | y1 , y2 , x)} consists of the responses generated by
147   the optimal policy πr∗t (· | x) under the reward rt (x, y), thus implicitly depending on rt . However, the
148   majority of the existing online RLHF algorithms completely ignore this implicit dependence leading
149   to an issue of distribution shift in the reward learning phase. It is critical to consider that the dataset
150   of responses Dr under which the loss in eq. (1) is optimized, depends on πθr∗ , and thus implicitly
151   depends on the reward function r(x, y). Ignoring this dependency leads to suboptimal alignment, as
152   can be seen from the performance gap in fig. 1 (right).

174   lead to suboptimal alignment. Additionally, the formulation of bilevel preference optimization has not
175   been extensively explored in the context of LLMs, and we are the first to provide a computationally
176   efficient bilevel preference optimization framework tailored for LLMs.

      5   Related Works
279
280   In this section, we provide a summary of the related literature on alignment and reinforcement learning
281   from human feedback. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), originally proposed
282   in (Christian, 2020) and subsequently applied by Ouyang et al. (2022) for instruction fine-tuning, has
283   been extremely successful in efficiently aligning large language models (LLMs) to human preferences
284   (Rafailov et al., 2023; Chakraborty et al., 2024; Stiennon et al., 2022; Ziegler et al., 2020; Kaufmann
285   et al., 2023). The broader framework of RLHF primarily deals with three phases (cf. fig. 1) - (0)
286   Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT) phase, (1) Reward Learning from human preferences, and (2) Language
287   model Policy optimization. There are two broader categories of RLHF algorithms: offline and online.
288
      The former method relies on an existing offline dataset, whereas the online RLHF method focuses
      on generating on-policy samples to align the language models. We discuss both of them in detail as
289
      follows.
290
291   Offline RLHF for LLMs. In most real-world settings, collecting human preferences online is often
292   expensive and complex, so preference datasets are typically collected beforehand, and alignment
293   is based on this offline data. Most recent RLHF algorithms are inherently offline, starting with the
294
      notable Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023). Subsequently, Zhao et al. (2023)
      refined its loss function using sequence pairs sampled from a supervised fine-tuned (SFT) policy,
295
      whereas (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) modified the loss function using the Kahneman-Tversky human
296
      utility objective. On the other hand, Liu et al. (2024) highlighted the shortcomings in DPO approaches
297   due to their inability to sample preference pairs from the optimal policy, resulting in bias, which
298   they addressed through importance sampling methods. Another line of work by Munos et al. (2023);
299   Swamy et al. (2024); Rosset et al. (2024) formulated the RLHF problem as a two-player constant sum
300   game and designed algorithms to identify the Nash equilibrium policy. Hence, all of these recent
301   research efforts have improved RLHF and direct preference methods, but most approaches are offline,
302   relying heavily on potentially sub-optimal datasets. This can lead to alignment issues due to poor
303   data quality (Tang et al., 2024). To address these shortcomings, recent studies are exploring online
304   RLHF strategies.
305   Online RLHF for LLMs. One of the first online RLHF algorithms was proposed by Christiano et al.
306   (2017) and later used in (Lee et al., 2021; Park et al., 2022) in the context of robotics, and recently
307   extended to online RLHF for language models, known as RLAIF (Lee et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2024;
308   Bai et al., 2022). However, such methods heavily rely on the assumption that the AI model used for
309   feedback is already well-aligned with the target reward, which might not always be true. Furthermore,
310   a recent line of work on self-play optimization (Chen et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024), heavily relies on
311
      the quality of the human-annotated supervised data. The most recent literature around self-improving,
      self-rewarding language models (Yuan et al., 2024b) focuses on developing iterative DPO-based
312
      methods to use the language models for both generators and discriminators. However, most of these
313
      are heuristics-driven and lack a unified mathematical formulation. Most importantly, none of these
314   methods address the distributional shift issue with online iterative RLHF approaches (Chakraborty
315   et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2024), leading to suboptimal performances (Sharma et al., 2024).

      6.1   SAIL Addressing Alignment Challenges
379
380
381
382
383
384   Goal and design choices. The goal of this part of the experiments is to comprehensively compare
385   the three SAIL designs and understand the effects of mixing sources and the added gradient terms in
386
      each case. Therefore, we conduct extensive sweeps of hyperparameters for each formulation using a
      relatively small model and dataset. We aim to identify a suitable range of the two hyperparameters
387
      (the source mixture weight and the coefficient of the added gradient) that balances performance and
388
      efficiency.
389
390   Experiment setups. Base model: We select one of the state-of-the-art LLMs with ≤1B parameters,
391   specifically Qwen1.5-0.5B (Bai et al., 2023), according to the Open LLM Leaderboard (Beeching
392
      et al., 2023) as of May 2024. Dataset: We use a 10K official split of the high-quality PKU-SafeRLHF
      dataset (Dai et al., 2023), which provides both helpfulness and harmlessness preferences.Offline
393
      reward model: For training and evaluation, we employ the two Beaver-7B (Dai et al., 2023) reward
394
      and cost models provided by the PKU-SafeRLHF authors; see appendix B for further details.
395
396   Evaluation metrics. Reward margin: The reward margin (according to the implicit reward of
397   DPO) on the evaluation split reflects the in-distribution generalization performance. Offline-reward
398
      evaluation: The provided reward model is well-aligned with dataset preferences and can evaluate
      some out-of-distribution responses, but its effectiveness is limited by the generalization of the reward
399
      model itself. Pairwise winrate: We utilize LLM-as-a-Judge (Zheng et al., 2024) as a widely accepted
400
      proxy for human evaluation. We apply GPT-4 Turbo (Achiam et al., 2023) as a judge and conduct
401   pairwise comparisons between the chosen response in the dataset and the generated response. With
402   the original prompt template used for dataset curation (see appendix C), the resulting winrate aligns
403   well with the preference labels. Training time overheads: We also record the time overhead relative
404   to fast DPO training as a measure of efficiency.
405
      Comprehensive comparison: effects of additional sources and gradients. The extensive results of
406
      sweeping the source mixture weight and the coefficient on the added gradient for each formulation
407   are reported in fig. 4 (on eval-reward and winrate), fig. 5 (on time overhead), and fig. 6 (on reward
408   margin).
409   SAIL-PR, unsurprisingly, achieves the largest eval-reward improvement. SAIL-PR also attains a
410   similar winrate improvement as SAIL-PP. In general, a larger mixture weight (indicating a larger
411   portion of online data) leads to higher performance. To maintain comparable efficiency with DPO,
412   we are interested in regions where the mixture weight ≤0.3. We are using a large reward model for
413   training; therefore, SAIL-PR suffers from overheads on both generation and reward evaluation.
414   SAIL-PP achieves the best 11.6% winrate improvement, without the additional knowledge of reward
415   required by SAIL-PR. Although the eval-reward improvement (3.6) is much lower than that of SAIL-
416   PR, we hypothesize that SAIL-PP effectively leverages a small portion of online data generation
417
      and the added gradient term, which stimulates “self-improvement”. This allows it to generalize in
      a direction that aligns well with the winrate despite limited offline reward alignment. However, we
418
      observe that mixing too many generated responses (>0.3) or making the gradient term too large
419
      (>0.4) can lead to training instability and lower performance, as illustrated in fig. 4.
420   SAIL-DP has a much weaker performance in terms of winrate and eval-reward compared to SAIL-PP
421   and SAIL-PR (which is why it is not prominently shown in fig. 4) . However, interestingly, we find that
422   it achieves a much larger reward margin improvement compared to SAIL-PR and SAIL-PP; see fig. 6.
423   We hypothesize that SAIL-DP tends to “overfit” the in-distribution responses in the evaluation split.
424   This overfitting can be interpreted as an augmentation of the preference labels in the dataset. While it
425   generalizes better than standard DPO, the lack of offline reward and out-of-distribution responses
426   makes it challenging to achieve a high winrate. Another advantage of SAIL-DP is its very low (<
427   12%) overhead compared to DPO, making it computationally efficient.
428   Summary of observations: We have confirmed that all three setups (SAIL-PR, SAIL-PP, SAIL-DP)
429   outperform standard DPO. The best hyperparameters and corresponding performance are summarized
430   in table 2. Additionally, we present a radar plot in fig. 3 that summarizes the relative improvement
431   across different metrics and training speed compared with DPO, clearly highlighting the distinctive
      characteristics of each design.

522   7     Conclusions
523
524   Our findings indicate that online LLM alignment fundamentally relies on bilevel optimization, which
525   can be effectively simplified to an efficient single-level first-order method. The three SAIL variants —
526
      SAIL-DP, SAIL-PP, and SAIL-PR — consistently outperform DPO and instruction-tuning baselines
      in terms of winrate, with varying degrees of computational overhead. This demonstrates the versatility
527
      and effectiveness of the SAIL framework in enhancing both the alignment quality and efficiency of
528
      large language models.
529
530   Limitations and Future Work: Our approach is grounded in the Bradley-Terry preference model;
531   future work may explore alternative utility functions to accommodate more general preference model-
532
      ing scenarios. Additionally, we have evaluated models up to 8B parameters; scaling our evaluations
      to larger models will provide more comprehensive insights into the benefits and potential challenges
533
      of SAIL in diverse settings.

712                • For SFT: we train for 10 epochs on PKU-SafeRLHF-10K and 2 epochs on UltraFeedback
713                  with 5e-5 learning rate. Same for all models. We use AdamW optimizer with a 100 step
714                  warmup.
