011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Event studies have been fundamental in finance, focusing on analyzing the ripple
014            effects of sudden market events. Accurately predicting these effects is crucial for
015            informed decision-making and effective risk management. However, the dynamic
016            complexity of financial markets and the lack of unified modeling tools make this
017
               task challenging. Previous models, constrained by simplistic assumptions and
               limited scopes, have struggled to address this complexity effectively. In contrast,
018
               large language models (LLMs), with their emergent reasoning abilities, offer a
019
               promising solution. In this paper, we introduce FinRipple, a novel training frame-
020            work that enables LLMs to align with market behavior and develop the capability
021            to analyze the ripple effects of sudden events. We first construct a time-varying
022            financial knowledge graph (KG) that is both financially meaningful and noise-
023            reduced to accurately represent the market state. These KGs are then integrated
024            into the LLM using adapters as memory modules. Additionally, we align the LLM
025            with market dynamics by integrating FinRipple with classic asset pricing theories
026            through a reinforcement learning framework. This market-alignment process col-
027            lects feedback that enhances the LLM’s foundational ability to analyze financial
028
               events and explain market anomalies that traditional models fail to address. Our
               key contributions are as follows: (1) We are the first to define the underexplored
029
               task of “event impact prediction”. Our framework not only establishes this task
030
               but also provides an open-source benchmark, creating a unified evaluation stan-
031            dard for both academia and industry; (2) FinRipple complements classic asset
032            pricing models by combining strong theoretical foundations with AI-driven ca-
033            pabilities, offering an enhanced analysis of residuals unexplained by traditional
034            models. We also demonstrate its potential for practical applications such as port-
035            folio management; (3) We conduct a comprehensive analysis to ensure that the
036            results generated by LLMs in our framework are more logically consistent and
037            credible, thus improving the reliability of insights for financial decision-making.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Event studies have been extensively used to determine the impact of corporate announcements and
043   market events on the market value of firms (Sorescu et al., 2017). A well-known recent example un-
044   derscores the significance of understanding such market reactions: On August 13th, 2024, Starbucks
045
      announced that it would replace its CEO with Chipotle CEO Brian Niccol. This announcement led
      to a remarkable shift in the market, sending Starbucks’ stock soaring by 24.5%, marking its best
046
      day ever, while Chipotle’s stock plummeted by over 10%. Some related companies in Starbucks’
047
      supply chain were also affected. For example, Jones Soda Co. saw its stock rise by 9.52%, BRC Inc.
048   gained 6.25%, and Celsius Holdings Inc. increased by 3.81%. This example demonstrates the ripple
049   effect that a single market event can have, not just on the company involved, but on other relevant
050   companies (Ma et al., 2023). Predicting these market ripple effects is crucial for financial decision-
051   making and risk management. Investors and risk managers rely on such insights to anticipate broader
052   company announcements (Boyd et al., 2010; Wu et al., 2015), external news or reviews (Xiong &
053   Bharadwaj, 2013; Gao et al., 2015), or macroeconomic shocks (Chen et al., 2012), allowing them to
      optimize portfolios, mitigate risks, and act swiftly in volatile conditions (Ding et al., 2015; 2014).

      However, predicting these ripple effects remains a complex and underexplored challenge because of
068   the intricate, evolving, and interconnected factors at play.
069
070   A significant drawback is that financial markets are often much more complex than previously as-
071
      sumed, making it difficult for previous approaches to fully capture the intricate and dynamic nature
      of market behaviors. Previous research on event studies has mainly followed two main directions:
072
      case-by-case analysis and unified modeling based on learning theory. Traditional case-by-case stud-
073
      ies typically focus on understanding how market events impact the stock performance of a single
074   company or a group of companies within the same industry. For example, Austin (1993) measured
075   the innovative output of patents within the biotechnology industry. Lepetit et al. (2004) discussed the
076   effects of mergers and acquisitions (M&As) in the banking industry. Ramiah et al. (2013) analyzed
077   how the stock market reacts to the announcement of green policies. While these studies are valu-
078   able in assessing direct consequences, they often struggle to capture the ripple effects even across
079   different industries, let alone the complexities of the entire market. Unified modeling based on
080   learning theory studies has mostly used news sentiments of target companies to predict stock price
081   movements and has recognized that considering the information of target companies is insufficient
082
      because the stock prices of target companies can be affected by their related companies (Ashtiani
      & Raahemi, 2023). Recent research has explored the integration of multi-source information (Ma
083
      et al., 2023) and employed more advanced embedding models. For instance, several extensions
084
      of transformer-based models were utilized by Mishev et al. (2020), demonstrating that combining
085   transformer representations with deep learning classifiers outperforms lexicon-based and statistical
086   models in representing event-driven word embeddings. Although these efforts represent a promising
087   direction for future research, they remain constrained by the limited capacity of the models’ capacity
088   and the incompleteness of their frameworks, making it difficult to fully capture the dynamic, time-
089   varying relationships between companies and the broader, evolving financial market. Moreover,
090   focusing solely on text sentiment for classification tasks often results in the loss of critical infor-
091   mation. For instance, positive news about one company might negatively impact another company
092   it is associated with. Therefore, up until now, there is an urgent need for a more comprehensive
093
      approach to capture the ever-changing market dynamics and explain the complex, interconnected
      relationships between companies.
094
095   Recently, large language models (LLMs) have gained widespread application across numerous fields
096   owing to their powerful reasoning capabilities (Huang & Chang, 2022). LLMs excel at tasks such
097   as structured information extraction (Hao et al., 2024), analogical reasoning (Creswell et al., 2022;
098   Wei et al., 2022b), and question answering, making them particularly well-suited for understand-
099
      ing event-driven ripple effects. Given their potential to model complex interactions, leveraging
      LLMs for predicting the ripple effects in the financial market is a natural progression. However,
100
      directly applying LLMs to financial markets is insufficient. The inherent complexity of these mar-
101
      kets, where companies are interconnected in dynamic and often noisy relationships, poses significant
102   challenges (Tang et al., 2022). The relationships between companies are not static; they often react
103   to multiple market events and information (Cheng & Li, 2021). Without considering the timeliness
104   of the market events on which the LLM relies, directly applying these models may result in mislead-
105   ing or inaccurate predictions. To effectively model the ripple effects of market events, it is essential
106   to augment LLMs with time-sensitive, structured knowledge. This ensures that the model captures
107   the latest and most relevant information about the current market state and the evolving relationships
      between companies.

108
      A feasible solution to this challenge is the integration of a time-varying financial knowledge graph
109   (KG). The financial KG provides a structured, noise-reduced view of the market, offering a clear
110   representation of up-to-date relationships between companies. By continuously updating the KG
111   with the latest events, we can maintain a reliable snapshot of the current market structure (Yang
112   et al., 2023b). This approach allows us to model how companies interact with each other and how
113   those relationships evolve over time, capturing the dynamic nature of financial markets (Cheng et al.,
114   2020). To integrate this knowledge into the LLM, we adopt an adapter-based approach, enabling us
115   to inject the structured information from the KG directly into the LLM without the need to retrain
116   the model from scratch. This not only avoids the potential information loss that could occur with a
117
      retrieval-based approach but also provides an easily extendable framework. After training the LLM
      backbone, new market states can simply be encoded into the adapter for inference. By aligning the
118
      LLMs with the financial market and leveraging the power of the KGs, they gain the ability to analyze
119
      the ripple effects of events based on the current market structure.
120
121   We validate the effectiveness of our framework in asset pricing and portfolio construction, sup-
122   ported by extensive training on real-world data. Additionally, we conduct systematic analyses and
123
      case studies to demonstrate that the model’s reasoning process in real markets is reliable. The con-
      tributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
124
125
             • We systematically define the “event impact prediction” task and establish an open-source
126
               benchmark that provides a unified evaluation standard.
127
128          • We introduce FinRipple, an easily extensible training paradigm that can transform most
129            LLMs into specialized financial event analysts, enabling them to accurately predict the
130            scope of event impacts.
131
132          • We rigorously validate our training framework, FinRipple, which augments the LLM with
133            a time-varying KG and aligns it with the financial market. We showcase its strong potential
134            for real-world applications, such as asset pricing and portfolio management. Furthermore,
135            detailed analyses illustrate the model’s reasoning pathways, confirming its ability to pro-
136
               vide reliable insights into the causal relationships driving market impacts.

141   2.1   E VENT STUDIES IN FINANCE
142
143   Event studies have been extensively employed to assess the impact of significant events on asset
144   prices and market behavior (Sorescu et al., 2017). An event can be a firm announcement (e.g., the
145   appointment of a new CMO) or an announcement made by competitors or regulatory bodies that
146   can affect the value of the focal firm (Acquisti et al., 2006). For example, Austin (1993) measured
147
      the innovative output of patents within the biotechnology industry; Lepetit et al. (2004) discussed
      the effects of M&As in the banking industry; and Ramiah et al. (2013) analyzed the stock market
148
      reaction to green policy announcements. Although these methods have provided valuable insights,
149
      they often struggle to capture the complexity and dynamics inherent in modern financial markets.
150
151   Recognizing these limitations, researchers have explored unified modeling approaches based on
152   learning theory, typically utilizing news sentiment analysis to predict stock price movements (Zhang
153
      & Skiena, 2010; Pagolu et al., 2016). Recent advancements include the integration of multi-source
      information (Ma et al., 2023), the employment of more advanced embedding models (Kilimci &
154
      Akyokuş, 2019; Mishev et al., 2020), and usage of large language models (LLMs) (Wu et al., 2023;
155
      Yang et al., 2023a). Despite these promising developments, existing models struggle to fully cap-
156   ture the dynamic, time-varying relationships between companies and the evolving financial market.
157   Recent efforts on LLMs for financial tasks have aimed to overcome these challenges through multi-
158   agent systems (Yu et al., 2024b;a; Zhang et al., 2024a) and by infusing financial trading knowl-
159   edge (Zhang et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2023). Considering the structured, dynamic representations
160   provided by knowledge graphs (KGs) (Zhang et al., 2023), FinRipple takes an alternative approach
161   by combining LLMs with financial KGs to capture ever-changing market dynamics and explain
      complex intercompany relationships.

181   2.2          KG AUGMENTED LLM
182
      Through the augmentation of KGs, existing methodologies aim to mitigate hallucinations, enhance
183
      reasoning capabilities, and recall specific facts (Chen et al., 2024; Agrawal et al., 2023). Research
184   on using KGs to enhance LLMs can be categorized into two main directions (Wen et al., 2023;
185   Agrawal et al., 2023): 1) integrating KGs into LLM pre-training and 2) injecting KGs into LLM
186   inference. For methods that integrate KGs into LLM pre-training, the common practice involves
187   designing knowledge-aware training objectives by either incorporating KG entities and relations
188   into the training data (Zhang et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2021) or applying KG prediction tasks, such as
189   link prediction, as additional supervision (Yasunaga et al., 2022). These methods directly compress
190   KG knowledge into the parameters of LLMs through supervision. However, creating KGs with
191   trillions of words is challenging, and these methods do not address the fundamental limitations of
192
      LLMs regarding flexibility, reliability, and transparency.
193   Injecting structured symbolic knowledge from KGs into LLM inference aims to enhance contextual
194   understanding, primarily by incorporating them at the input level. Early efforts focused on fusing
195   KG triples into the inputs of LLMs using attention mechanisms (Liu et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2020)
196   or attaching graph encoders to LLM encoders to process KG inputs (Wang et al., 2019). Subsequent
197   work further adopted graph neural networks (GNNs) in parallel with LLMs for joint reasoning (Ya-
198
      sunaga et al., 2021) and added interactions between text tokens and KG entities in the intermediate
      layers of LLMs (Zhang et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023).

318   In this subsection, we provide a brief introduction to the benchmarks and metrics for the asset pricing

322   Datasets We selected 10,000 news articles about S&P 500 companies from January 1, 2020, to
323   June 30, 2022, as the test set, while approximately 110,000 articles from other years were used for
      training. Detailed statistics on the dataset about news and KGs can be found in Appendix E.

324
      Baselines We adopt several mainstream methods to demonstrate that FinRipple offers a power-
325   ful solution for this task. The baselines are primarily divided into two categories. The first cate-
326   gory tests the analogical reasoning capabilities of foundational LLMs, demonstrating that untrained
327   LLMs lack the ability to effectively analyze event impact. The basic Retrieval-Augmented Genera-
328   tion (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020) approach utilizes an embedding model to retrieve relevant subgraph
329   information from the KG, enabling LLMs to assess impacts based on this data. Zero-Shot Inference
330   involves providing instructions to the model along with news and concatenated graph information.
331   However, due to the limited window size of LLMs, some graph data may be incomplete. For com-
332   panies specifically mentioned in the news, a two-hop subgraph is concatenated; otherwise, random
333
      graph information is used until the LLM’s input window is filled. In-Context Learning (ICL) (Brown
      et al., 2020) builds upon the Zero-Shot approach by adding an example to aid the LLM in reasoning.
334
      The second category primarily includes fine-tuned variations of FinRipple, both without and with
335
      market alignment. It emphasizes that even if the LLM effectively absorbs the graph information,
336   without aligning with market dynamics, the model still lacks the ability to effectively analyze the
337   impact of events.
338
339   Evaluation metrics To evaluate the effectiveness of FinRipple in analyzing financial market
340   shocks, we designed an evaluation framework focusing on three metrics: (1) the explanatory power
341   on the mean of the residuals, (2) the explanatory power on the variance of the residuals, and (3)
342   the refusal-to-answer rate. The residuals, derived from a CAPM regression of stock returns against
343   market returns, represent the portion of returns unexplained by market factors. We use these resid-
344   uals to assess whether predicted event impacts significantly explain the variance in returns through
345   regression analysis and ANOVA, with p-values indicating statistical significance. Additionally, the
346   refusal-to-answer rate evaluates the robustness of LLMs in generating meaningful responses in com-
347
      plex, event-driven contexts.

       C     A SSET P RICING M ODELS
973
974
       Asset pricing models are essential tools in finance for understanding the relationship between risk
975    and expected return. This appendix briefly introduces three prominent models: CAPM, Fama-
976    French Three-Factor Model (Fama3), and Fama-French Five-Factor Model (Fama5).
977
978    C.1   C APITAL A SSET P RICING M ODEL
979
980    The CAPM describes the relationship between systematic risk and expected return. The expected
981    return of an asset is proportional to its beta, which measures the sensitivity of the asset’s returns to
982    market returns. The formula for CAPM is:
983
984                                     E(Ri ) = Rf + βi (E(Rm ) − Rf )                                    (6)
985
986    where E(Ri ) represents the expected return of the asset, Rf is the risk-free rate, βi is the asset’s
987    beta that measures its sensitivity to market movements, and E(Rm ) is the expected return of the
988    market.
989
990    C.2   FAMA -F RENCH T HREE -FACTOR M ODEL
991
       The Fama3 expands upon CAPM by including two additional factors: size and value. The size
992
       premium, denoted as Small Minus Big (SMB), captures the excess return of small-cap stocks over
993
       large-cap stocks, while the value premium, denoted as High Minus Low (HML), captures the excess
994    return of high book-to-market stocks over low book-to-market stocks. The model is represented as:
995
996
997
                           E(Ri ) = Rf + βi (E(Rm ) − Rf ) + s × SMB + h × HML                             (7)
998
       where s and h represent the sensitivities of the asset’s returns to the SMB and HML factors, respec-
999    tively.
1000
1001
       C.3   FAMA -F RENCH F IVE -FACTOR M ODEL
1002
1003   The Fama5 extends Fama3 by adding two more factors: profitability and investment. The profitabil-
1004   ity premium, denoted as Robust Minus Weak (RMW), captures the excess return of firms with high
1005   profitability over those with low profitability. The investment premium, denoted as Conservative
1006   Minus Aggressive (CMA), captures the excess return of firms with conservative investment policies
1007   over those with aggressive policies. The updated model is:
1008
1009         E(Ri ) = Rf + βi (E(Rm ) − Rf ) + s × SMB + h × HML + r × RMW + c × CMA                       (8)
1010
1011   where r and c represent the sensitivities to the RMW and CMA factors, respectively.
1012
1013   C.4   R ESIDUALS AND M ARKET A NOMALIES
1014
1015   Residuals of these models represent the portion of an asset’s return not captured by the included
1016
       risk factors. By analyzing residuals, investors can identify abnormal returns that the models fail
       to explain. These anomalies often arise due to market inefficiencies, information asymmetries, or
1017
       other idiosyncratic risks not accounted for by the systematic factors in the models. Understanding
1018
       residuals helps investors gain insights into potential mispricing and hidden variables in the market,
1019   revealing opportunities or risks that standard models overlook.

       E.2     S TATISTICAL M ETRICS
1243
1244   This subsection introduces key statistical metrics used to evaluate the explanatory power of inde-
1245   pendent variables on the dependent variable, including Coefficient (Coef.), p-value, Coefficient of
1246   Determination (R2 ), ANOVA F-statistic (ANOVA-F), ANOVA p-value (ANOVA-p), and Effect Size
1247   (η 2 ).
1248
1249   Coefficient (Coef.) The coefficient (βi ) represents the estimated effect of an independent variable
1250   Xi on the dependent variable Y , holding all other variables constant. The regression equation is
1251   given by Y = β0 + β1 X1 + β2 X2 + · · · + βn Xn + ϵ, where ϵ is the error term.
1252
1253   p-value The p-value indicates the statistical significance of each coefficient, measuring the prob-
1254   ability of observing the estimated effect under the null hypothesis that the coefficient is zero. A
1255   smaller p-value suggests stronger evidence against the null hypothesis.
1256
1257   Coefficient of Determination (R2 ) The Coefficient of Determination (R2 ) measures the propor-
1258   tion of variance in the  dependent variable that is explained by the independent variables. It is calcu-
                            Pn

       lated as R2 = 1 − Pi=1  n            2 , where yi is the observed value, ŷi is the predicted value, and ȳ is
1260                           i=1 (yi −ȳ)
       the mean of the observed values.

1263   ANOVA F-statistic (ANOVA-F) The ANOVA F-statistic tests whether the regression model ex-
1264   plains a significant proportion of variance in the dependent variable compared to a model with no
                                           MS
1265   predictors. It is calculated as F = MSregression

                                                        , where MSregression is the mean square due to regression,
1266   and MSresidual is the mean square due to residual error. Higher values of F suggest a better fit of the

1270   statistic, reflecting the probability of obtaining the computed F-statistic under the null hypothesis
1271   that the regression model has no explanatory power.
