009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning’s success relies heavily on the availability of extensive labeled
012            datasets. Compared to 2D data, acquiring 3D data is substantially more expensive
013            and time-consuming. Current multi-modal self-supervised approaches often in-
014            volve converting 3D data into 2D data for parallel multi-modal training, ignoring
015            the prior knowledge of extensively trained 2D models. Therefore, it is important
016
               to find ways to utilize 2D feature priors to facilitate the learning process of 3D
               models. In this paper, we propose MV3D-MAE, a masked autoencoder framework
017
               that utilizes a pre-trained 2D MAE model to enhance 3D representation learning.
018
               Initially, we convert single 3D point clouds into multi-view depth images. Building
019            on a pre-trained 2D MAE model, we adapt the model for multi-view depth image
020            reconstruction by integrating group attention and incorporating additional atten-
021            tion layers. Then we propose a differentiable 3D reconstruction method named
022            Mv-Swin, which maps the reconstructed results back to 3D objects without using
023            camera poses, thereby learning 3D spatial representations. Thus, MV3D-MAE,
024            through the bidirectional transformation between 2D and 3D data, mitigates the
025            differences between modalities and enhances the network’s representational perfor-
026            mance by leveraging the prior knowledge in the pre-trained 2D MAE. Our model
027
               significantly improves performance on few-shot classification and achieves SOTA
               results in linear Support Vector Machine classification. It also demonstrated com-
028
               petitive performance in other downstream tasks of classification and segmentation
029
               in synthetic and real-world datasets.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   3D perception has emerged as a central focus in the field of computer vision research. Inspired by the
035   self-supervised training of Mask Autoencoders (MAEs) (He et al., 2022) for 2D images, researchers
036   are now expanding their efforts to develop an array of self-supervised models for 3D perception tasks
037   (He et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; 2022; Pang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022). These models begin
038   with a pre-training phase, utilizing masked point cloud data for reconstruction purposes, before being
039
      fine-tuned to excel in various downstream tasks. The potential of these 3D masked models will play a
      significant role in enhancing our understanding of the 3D world.
040
041   Most current 3D masked models, such as PointBert (Yu et al., 2022) and PointMAE (Pang et al.,
042   2022), are inspired by the 2D MAE model. They encode 3D point clouds into a sequence of tokens
043   after patching, followed by random masking. Moreover, these models utilize a transformer-based
044   encoder-decoder model to restore masked point cloud features to complete point clouds. Recently,
045
      multi-modal point cloud MAEs like joint-MAE (Guo et al., 2023), and I2P-MAE (Zhang et al., 2023)
      have emerged (Fig. 1(a)). Joint-MAE converts point clouds into images and conducts joint 2D and
046
      3D training through an MAE structure. On the other hand, I2P-MAE uses converted images as guide
047
      masks for directing the masking and reconstruction of point clouds. However, there are limitations
048   to consider for these approaches. Using multi-view 2D images to represent 3D objects inevitably
049   results in 3D geometry information loss. Moreover, the aforementioned methods typically focus
050   solely on the mapping transformation from 3D to 2D, neglecting the alignment process required
051   to reconstruct 3D objects from 2D images. Another kind of approach for multi-modal pre-training
052   resembles PointCLIP V2 (Zhu et al., 2023), incorporating contrastive learning (Fig. 1(b)). The most
053   recent method, MM-Point (Yu & Song, 2024), converts point clouds into multi-view 2D images and
      3D point clouds for contrastive learning. The inter-modal contrastive learning across 2D and 3D

      execute modality transformation between 2D and 3D, nor do they fully exploit the robust capabilities
      of existing 2D pre-trained models. Therefore, we raise an evident question: Can a method leverage

071
072   To tackle this issue, we introduce a novel 2D-3D architecture, MV3D-MAE (Fig. 1(c)). First, our
073   model converts 3D point clouds into multi-view 2D depth images. Then we apply a pre-trained
074
      2D MAE to conduct self-supervised learning on 2D depth images. Subsequently, a 2D-to-3D
      differentiable model reconstructs the depth maps back into 3D point clouds and does supervision.
075
      Specifically, we employ a depth rendering model akin to Point-CLIP V2 (Zhu et al., 2023), generating
076
      depth maps from ten perspectives of a single point cloud object, each capturing distinct 3D features.
077   Subsequently, these depth maps are processed through masked reconstruction with a ViT-base MAE
078   model, which can be specifically fine-tuned for this task. However, the fundamental 2D MAE can not
079   handle multi-view images directly. Therefore, we introduce group attention to aggregate geometric
080   features from multiple perspectives. Additionally, we incorporated new attention layers into the MAE
081   encoder to adapt the model to the reconstruction of depth images. Traditional methods transform
082   from multi-view 2D depth images to 3D point clouds through discretized geometric mapping, which
083   is non-learnable. Additionally, this mapping imposes strict requirements on both camera poses
084   and data accuracy. To further address the challenge while ensuring the shape features of the point
085
      cloud involved in the reconstruction process, we introduce the MV-Swin module. This module is
      differentiable and merges multi-view depth map features into explicit 3D shapes without requiring
086
      camera poses. This capability facilitates end-to-end 2D to 3D learning during pre-training and ensures
087
      the alignment between 2D and 3D data. For downstream tasks, we utilize only the MAE encoder,
088   supplemented by a task-specific head. This design not only leverages a pre-trained 2D MAE model
089   as a strong 2D prior for self-supervised 3D representation training but also maximally mitigates the
090   modality differences between 2D and 3D.
091
      Our model is pre-trained on the ShapeNet (Chang et al., 2015) dataset. We then conduct a few
092
      downstream tasks, as classification tasks on ModelNet (Wu et al., 2015), ScanObjectNN (Uy et al.,
093
      2019) dataset, and segmentation tasks on the ShapeNet Part dataset (Yi et al., 2016) dataset. And we
094   do more experiments in real-world scenarios like 2D-3D retrieval and classification of incomplete
095   datasets. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
096
097          • We leverage a pre-trained 2D MAE model as the 2D prior to enhance the feature learning
098            from multi-view 2D depth images, which are derived from projecting 3D objects. To adapt
099            multi-view depth images to MAE, we introduce group attention after patching images and
100
               more attention layers in the MAE encoder, respectively.
101          • We introduce a novel multi-view reconstruction module, MV-Swin, which enables the
102            transformation of multi-view depth images into point clouds. MV-Swin facilitates the
103            aggregate of multiple depth maps, ensuring a smooth transition from 2D to 3D while
104            aligning features across 2D-3D modalities.
105          • Through the 2D-3D pre-training with MV3D-MAE, we achieve excellent results in down-
106            stream tasks. Notably, in few-shot classification tasks, our model significantly outperforms
107            other 2D-3D self-supervised models, which proves pre-trained 2D MAE is the effective 3D
               representation learner.

      Self-supervised Point Cloud Learning. Supervised learning for 3D point clouds has gained traction
111
      since the emergence of PointNet (Qi et al., 2017a). However, due to the limited size of 3D point cloud
112   datasets, supervised 3D models have struggled with generalization problems (Uy et al., 2019). To
113   tackle this challenge, self-supervised learning has emerged as a prominent approach for its ability to
114   leverage the vast amounts of unlabeled data effectively (Pang et al., 2022). Self-supervised learning
115   approaches for 3D point clouds can generally be categorized into two classes: one focuses on
116   reconstruction, primarily leveraging masked models, while the other concentrates on discrimination,
117   predominantly employing contrastive learning designs. Although a few 3D models employ contrastive
118   learning for self-supervised learning (Du et al., 2021; Afham et al., 2022), the majority of models
119   still adopt the masked autoencoder (MAE) (He et al., 2022) approach. For example, Point-MAE
120
      (Pang et al., 2022), Point-BERT (Yu et al., 2022), and Point-M2AE (Zhang et al., 2022) have
      marked significant advancements in 3D MAE models. Point-MAE introduces a novel approach
121
      by applying the concept of masking and reconstruction to point clouds, aiming to encode the
122
      underlying structure of 3D data effectively. On the other hand, Point-BERT adapts the BERT (Devlin
123   et al., 2018) methodology from natural language processing to point clouds scenario, using masked
124   point prediction to capture the complex spatial relationships within the data. Meanwhile, Point-
125   M2AE (Zhang et al., 2022) adopts a multi-scale approach, applying hierarchical masking to point
126   clouds before proceeding with their reconstruction. However, merely learning 3D modality might
127   neglect the implicit semantic and geometric correlations between 2D and 3D data. Compared to
128   these methods, our approach introduces a novel method that embeds 3D spatial features into the 2D
129   self-supervised pre-training process. This strategy innovatively leverages the strengths of both 2D
130   and 3D, offering a comprehensive understanding of the 2D-3D features underlying data.
131   2D-3D Representation Learning. Recent research in self-supervised learning has been taking
132   advantage of the multi-modalities nature of data. These methods primarily involve converting 3D
133   data into 2D data and then applying models to explore the intrinsic relationships between modalities.
134   PiMAE (Chen et al., 2023) introduces a self-supervised pre-training framework based on MAE that
135   fosters interaction between 3D and 2D data to enhance performance in downstream object detection
136   tasks. Joint-MAE (Guo et al., 2023), similar to PiMAE, starts by converting object point clouds into
137
      2D images, followed by multi-modal self-supervised MAE pre-training. I2P-MAE (Zhang et al.,
      2023) diverges from directly training with 2D images and 3D point clouds together. Instead, it uses
138
      2D images to guide the creation of 3D masks while concurrently reconstructing 2D features. Differing
139
      from previous approaches, MM-Point (Yu & Song, 2024) first transforms 3D objects into multi-view
140   images, then employs contrastive learning between these multi-view images and 3D data to learn the
141   similarities between modalities. These methods all leverage 2D data to enhance the performance of
142   3D tasks, yet they overlook the potential of utilizing already well-developed pre-trained 2D models to
143   improve 3D task outcomes. Moreover, many approaches consider converting 3D data into multi-view
144   2D data, such as MM-Point (Yu & Song, 2024) and PointCLIP V2 (Zhu et al., 2023), and align the
145   2D-3D feature with the semantic feature as an implicit way. A valid method is lacking, which can
146   map 2D data back to 3D point clouds to do the explicit alignment in an end-to-end manner. Therefore,
147   in our approach, we propose a method that utilizes the prior 2D information from a pre-trained 2D
148
      MAE model to enhance the reconstruction of multi-view images. Then we introduce an Mv-Swin
      module that maps 2D back into 3D explicitly.

194   3.2   P RE - TRAINING WITH 2D-3D DATA
195
196   Multi-view consistency. After acquiring the multi-view depth data, the subsequent step is to
197   reconstruct the multi-view 2D depth images using the MAE framework. Unlike RGB images, multi-
198   view depth images present two distinct characteristics: (1) the need to extract features consistently
199   across multiple views; (2) their single-channel property.
200   To address these differences, we diverge from the traditional depth images. Firstly, we adjust the depth
201   of images by inverting the background color, opting for a default dark background. Additionally, we
202   replicate the single-channel depth image into three channels, treating it as a conventional three-channel
203   image for further processing.
204   Furthermore, to ensure consistency across multi-view images, we apply group attention to multi-view
205   images to aggregate features. The group attention mechanism GroupAttn(·) decomposes a tensor of
206   shape [g × n, cmv ] into [g, n, cmv ], where g is the number of groups, n is the number of views, cmv is the
207   feature channels, following the self-attention separately to each of the g groups. It’s important to note
208   that our group attention operates sequentially within one batch. This approach allows inter-group
209   perspectives to remain unaffected by others, while the intra-group multi-view attention ensures
210   consistency of geometric features across different views.
211   Tuning a pre-trained 2D MAE model. The pre-trained 2D MAE model (He et al., 2022), with
212   its proficiency in understanding features, textures, and patterns specific to 2D images, lays a solid
213   groundwork for feature extraction from depth images. Although depth images primarily focus on
214   spatial depth rather than color information, they possess patterns and structures that the MAE model
215   can skillfully decode. By converting 3D objects into multi-view depth images, employing an MAE to
      handle these images facilitates the prior knowledge extended into 3D representation learning. This

      Multi-view MAE pre-training pipeline. The whole self-supervise learning process of our multi-view
246
      MAE can be described as follows: First, we divide our multi-view depth images mv into patches with
247   pre-trained convolution layers, in order to extract the patches’ feature as tokens. To accommodate
248   multi-view data, we employ a group attention mechanism GroupAttn(·) to ensure token consistency
249   across different views. Next, we introduce random masking of the 2D tokens with a high ratio, e.g.,
250   70%. This strategy challenges the model to reconstruct the missing information solely based on
251   a limited set of visible tokens, denoted as tv . Consistent with MAE’s design, we append the cls
252   token to tv and then feed them input into the MAE encoder. Inside the encoder, we initially utilize
253   pre-trained MAE encoder’s multi-head attention layers, denoted as M (·), to leverage the 2D prior
254   knowledge acquired by the MAE. Subsequently, we introduce a new set of multi-head attention layers,
255
      represented as A (·). These layers are specifically designed to adapt the representation learning
      process according to the depth images. The initial layers, M (·), serve as the foundation by providing
256
      a comprehensive understanding of image features, which is essential for establishing a robust feature
257
      representation baseline. Following this, the introduction of A (·) layers specifically addresses the
258   nuances of depth images, such as the importance of spatial relationships and depth value variations
259   across different image regions. The embedding embv can be formulated as:
260
                                        embv = A (M (Concat(tv , cls))).                                 (1)
261
262   Before feeding the token into the MAE decoder Dec, we concatenate the visible tokens embv , with
263   the masked tokens Tm . We utilize the pre-trained 2D MAE decoder to reconstruct the combined token,
264   aiming to reconstruct the multi-view depth images:
265                                      mv′ = Dec(Concat(embv ,tm )).                                   (2)

267   3.3   F ROM M ULTI - VIEW 2D TO 3D
268
269   Several previous methods have processed 3D point cloud objects by transforming them into multi-
      view 2D images, including MM-Point (Yu & Song, 2024), PointCLIP V2 (Zhu et al., 2023), and


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      multi-view point completion model (Hu et al., 2020). These approaches initially convert point clouds
271   into 2D depth images or RGB images and then extract their features to represent individual 3D
272   objects. However, intuitively, the transformation from 3D to multi-view 2D inherently leads to the
273   loss of geometry information of the 3D objects. This loss of information poses challenges, such as
274   the inability of 2D features to directly reconstruct 3D shapes in PointCLIP V2 (Zhu et al., 2023).
275   Moreover, in the case of MV-Completion (Hu et al., 2020), the method relies on traditional discrete
276   mapping algorithms from depth maps to point clouds, often requiring downsampling to achieve point
277   cloud reconstruction. As a result, we propose a novel differentiable algorithm for mapping multi-view
278   depth features to 3D point clouds, MV-Swin, which reconstructs 3D shapes without the need for
279
      camera poses.
280   We revisit the encoder-decoder architecture for the point cloud reconstruction process, taking PointNet
281   (Qi et al., 2017a) as an example for the encoder (Fig. 3a). The encoder initially maps discrete
282   point cloud data into a high-dimensional space, producing a continuous high-dimensional feature
283   representation. The decoder D as Pcl2pcl (Chen et al., 2019), which consists of MLPs, then expands
284   the high-dimensional features to map the features back into the discrete space of the point cloud.
285
      Similarly, we can utilize a mapping function F to project multi-view depth images mv′ into the
      high-dimensional feature space. By using the same decoder D as the reconstruction decoder, we can
286
      map continuous high-dimensional features f3D back into the discrete space of the point cloud p′ , thus
287
      achieving a differentiable mapping from 2D to 3D: p′ = D( f3D ) = D(F(mv′ )).
288
289   In our method, we introduce a new module, Mv-Swin (Fig. 3b), to serve as the mapping function F.
290   The Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) is adept at aggregating information from multi-scale images,
291
      making it particularly suitable for perceiving edge information of depth images to synthesize 3D
      shapes. However, to integrate multi-view depth image information, a capability lacking in Swin, we
292
      insert an Mv-attention module before each Swin module. The Mv-attention module decomposes the
293
      original image into patches following the Swin Transformer. Subsequently, patch features are used to
294   calculate query, key, and value. The query features are duplicated according to the number of views,
295   and then the maximum value across multiple views is computed to represent the overall feature of all
296   views, while key and value features remain unchanged. These features are processed by Mv-attention
297   before being fed into the Swin module for further attention processing. The process of Mv-attention
298   and Swin module cascades three times sequentially. Following this, we apply a linear transformation
299   layer, to fuse the multi-view features into a high-dimensional feature f3D .
300   Mv-Swin effectively aggregates multi-view depth features into a unified representation, enabling the
301   conversion of 2D features into a 3D shape. Employing self-supervised training on 2D depth images
302   with a 2D MAE, we can utilize Mv-Swin to convert reconstructed depth maps back into 3D data,
303   facilitating end-to-end learning and 2D-3D alignment. To make the MAE training more efficient, we
304   pre-train the Mv-Swin module initially. During MV3D-MAE training, we freeze Mv-Swin parameters
305   while retraining the point cloud decoder.

309   As illustrated in Fig. 2, our loss function emphasizes the reconstruction of both 2D images and 3D
310   shapes. For the 2D reconstruction loss, we employ the Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss, which is
311   consistent with the loss function utilized in MAE. For the reconstruction of 3D shapes, we utilize the
312   traditional Chamfer Distance (CD Loss). Our loss function can thus be represented as follows:
313
314                                    L2D (mv, mv′ ) = MSE(mv, M(mv))
315                                                                                                      (3)
                                          L3D (p, p′ ) = CD(p, F(mv′ )).
316
317
      Our 2D-3D reconstruction process is sequential, indicating that if the reconstruction of 2D data
318   achieves the desired quality, the reconstruction of 3D data will also be enhanced. Therefore, the
319   overall loss for the MV3D-MAE pre-training is formulated below to reflect this dependency, as
320   L = L2D + λ · L3D , where λ are weight factors. These two self-supervised losses are crucial in
321   enabling our MV3D-MAE structure to effectively learn the representational features of both 2D
322   and 3D data. By tuning the model with the loss, we encourage it to capture the intricate details

      4.1   E XPERIMENTS S ETUP
340   Pre-training dataset. Following the precedent setting by prior studies such as (Yu et al., 2022; Zhang
341   et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023), we select ShapeNet (Chang et al., 2015) as our pre-training dataset.
342   The dataset contains more than 57,000 synthetic 3D shapes from 55 categories. In our pre-training
343   process, we random sample 8192 points from each object as the point cloud input. As the multi-view
344   depth images transform from the 3D objects, we set one object to have 10 multi-view depth images
345
      to describe the shape representation and each depth image size H ×W as 224×224 as PointCLIP
      V2 (Zhu et al., 2023).
346
347   Pre-training settings. Within the MAE framework, we base our model on the pre-trained 2D MAE
348   model with ViT-base/16 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). This fundamental model includes 12 layers in the
349   encoder and 8 layers in the decoder. Building upon this, we add an additional 4 attention layers to
350   the encoder to better adapt the learning of depth representations. For the 2D-to-3D reconstruction
351
      module, we follow the Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021), setting the window size to 14.
352   During the pre-training process, our initial step involves pre-training the Mv-Swin module and then
353   freezing its parameters. The mask ratio is set to 0.75. Subsequently, we fully tune the pre-trained
354   2D MAE structure to adapt the MAE model for the reconstruction of our multi-view depth images.
355   Due to the significant difference in order of magnitude between the 3D CD loss and the 2D MSE
356   loss in our pipeline, we need to apply a larger weight on the 3D loss than the 2D loss to ensure a
357
      balance between them. Therefore, we assign a weight of 25 to λ . The optimization is carried out
      using the AdamW optimizer, with a learning rate of 1e-4, weight decay of 5e-2, and employing a
358
      cosine scheduler for adjusting the learning rate. The batch size is configured at 16, with the training
359
      over 300 epochs on four 40GB Nvidia A100 GPUs.
360
361   Downstream tasks. We choose the classification and part segmentation tasks to validate the perfor-
362   mance and generalizability of our pre-trained MV3D-MAE: (1) Shape classification: Experiments
363
      are conducted on three datasets: ModelNet40 (Wu et al., 2015), ModelNet10 (Wu et al., 2015), and
      ScanObjectNN (Uy et al., 2019). ModelNet is a widely utilized synthetic dataset for 3D objects.
364
      ModelNet40 comprises objects from 40 categories, featuring 9,843 objects for training and 2,468
365
      objects for testing. On the other hand, ModelNet10 consists of 4,899 CAD models from 10 categories.
366   ScanObjectNN is a real-world scanned dataset that includes approximately 2,880 unique point cloud
367   objects, offering a more realistic and challenging setting for evaluating models. (2) Shape part

525   5    C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, we present MV3D-MAE, a novel self-supervised training framework that enriches
528   3D pre-training with 2D priors within the pre-trained 2D MAE. Diverging from traditional 2D-
529   to-3D pre-training methods, our strategy initiates by converting individual 3D objects into multi-
530   view depth images. Leveraging a pre-trained 2D MAE for prior knowledge, we introduce an
531
      innovative multi-view MAE structure tailored for the self-supervised pre-training of multi-view depth
      images. Following the reconstruction of 2D depth images, our MV-Swin module facilitates the
532
      transition back to 3D point clouds, enabling effective 3D reconstruction. Significantly, the group
533
      attention mechanism within our multi-view MAE structure captures consistency across multiple
534   views, while the integration of new self-attention layers allows the adaptation of the pre-trained MAE
535   for depth image feature learning. The MV-Swin module further ensures semantic feature alignment
536   from 2D to 3D, exemplifying our method’s capacity to merge 2D insight with 3D representation
537   learning seamlessly. Our model not only achieves state-of-the-art results in linear SVM and few-shot
538   classification tasks but also exhibits competitive results in other evaluations. This highlights that
539   leveraging the pre-existing 2D knowledge can elevate the expressive quality of 3D information and
      enhance the effectiveness of subsequent 3D endeavors.

      A.2   D ETAILS OF THE 3D- TO -2D P ROJECTION
703
704   In 3D-to-2D projector G(·), we utilize a similar projection method as PointClIP V2 Zhu et al. (2023).
705   The G(·) pipeline has four steps.
706
      Voxelize. First voxelized the point cloud space and projected the points belonging to the object into
707
      the voxels. Due to the occlusion properties of the point cloud, we simply assign the minimum depth
708
      value for the corresponding voxel.
709
710   Densify. Following PointCLIP V2, we densify the grid using a local minimum value pooling operation
711   to improve visual continuity in sparse point clouds, ensuring that vacant voxels between sparse points
712
      are filled with reasonable depth values. This method, favoring minimum depth values, results in
      denser and smoother spatial representations, while keeping background voxels empty.
713
714   Smooth. To counteract potential artefacts from local pooling on 3D surfaces, we use a non-parametric
715   Gaussian kernel for smoothing and noise filtering, achieving a balance between noise removal and
716   edge sharpness preservation. In all experiments in our paper, we utilize the learnable smoothing as
717   the few-shot classification in PointCLIP V2 with 1 × 3 × 3 kernel size 3D convolution.
718   Squeeze. Finally, we compress depth to form a depth map to get a point cloud’s depth map from a
719   particular view.
720
721   A.3   D ETAILS OF M ULTI - VIEW MAE
722
723   In our pipeline, we apply the basic 2D masked autoencoder (MAE) He et al. (2022) as a part of
724   our pre-training backbone. Due to the limitation of the resource, we have to utilize the ViT-base
