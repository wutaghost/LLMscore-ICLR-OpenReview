009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Text embeddings enable various applications, but their performance deteriorates
012            on longer texts. In this paper, we find that the performance degradation is due to
013            a phenomenon called Length Collapse, where longer text embeddings collapse
014            into a narrow space. This collapse results in a distributional inconsistency be-
015            tween embeddings of different text lengths, ultimately hurting the performance
016            of downstream tasks. Theoretically, by considering the self-attention mecha-
017            nism inherently functions as a low-pass filter, we prove that long sequences
018
               increase the attenuation rate of the low-pass filter effect of the self-attention
               mechanism. With layers going deeper, excessive low-pass filtering causes the
019
               token signals to retain only their Direct-Current (DC) component, which means
020
               the input token feature maps will collapse into a narrow space, especially in
021            long texts. Based on the above analysis, we propose to mitigate the undesirable
022            length collapse limitation by introducing a temperature in softmax(·), which
023            achieves a higher low-filter attenuation rate. The tuning-free method, called
024            TempScale, can be plugged into multiple transformer-based embedding mod-
025            els. Empirically, we demonstrate that TempScale can improve existing em-
026            bedding models especially on long text inputs, bringing up to 0.53% perfor-
027            mance gains on 40 datasets from Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB)
028            and 0.82% performance gains on 4 datasets from LongEmbed, which specifi-
029
               cally focuses on long context retrieval. The source code is available at https:
               //anonymous.4open.science/r/Length_Collapse-22D2.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Text embeddings—dense vectors that preserve the semantic information of given texts—have become
035
      fundamental to many downstream natural language processing (NLP) applications, including text
036   analysis (Aggarwal & Zhai, 2012; Angelov, 2020), question answering (Tan et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
037   2024), web search (Zhao et al., 2023; Yates et al., 2021), and retrieval-augmented generation (Gao
038   et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024). Typically, embeddings are generated by pre-trained language models
039   (PLMs), which produce fixed-dimensional embeddings regardless of the input text length. In practice,
040   we expect PLMs to perform consistently on texts of varying lengths in any downstream applications.
041
      Unfortunately, we observe that popular transformer-based embedding models perform poorly on
042   longer texts. As shown in Figure 1a, using the classification task on the IMDB dataset from the
043   Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB) (Muennighoff et al., 2023) leaderboard as an example,
044   we evaluate the performance of mainstream embedding models on test sets grouped by different text
045   lengths. The experimental results reveal that models of different capabilities and context window sizes
046   consistently exhibit performance degradation as text length increases. For instance, the BGE (Xiao
047   et al., 2023) model’s classification accuracy drops significantly from 75.6% in the length range [0,
048   100) tokens to 59.0% in the range [400, 500) tokens, indicating a substantial decline of 16.6% points.
049   We attribute this performance degradation to a biased behavior of embedding models: embeddings of
050   longer texts tend to cluster together, a phenomenon we term as length collapse. To verify this, we
051   conduct controlled experiments depicted in Figures 1b and 1c. Figure 1b shows that embeddings of
052   longer texts are more densely clustered near the origin in the dimensionally reduced embedding space,
053   indicating a collapse that reduces variance among embeddings. Details of the rewriting process can be
      found in Appendix E.1. Figure 1c further demonstrates that embeddings of longer texts exhibit higher

076   performance of various downstream tasks (Yan et al., 2021) such as text classification.
077
      To study how text length affects the distribution of embeddings, we conduct a rigorous analysis of the
078   self-attention mechanism in Fourier space (Wang et al., 2022b) (Section 2.2). Based on the finding
079   that cascading self-attention blocks are equivalent to repeatedly applying a low-pass filter, we further
080   prove that the attenuation rate of the low-pass filter is proportional to the largest singular value σa of
081   the high-frequency components (HC) in self-attention matrix. Furthermore, assuming that the input
082   keys and query tokens follow a Gaussian distribution, we can prove that the σa value decreases as
083   text length increases. As a result, longer input texts retain more of the Direct Component (DC) in the
084   token signals, which means that the embeddings at the output layer will collapse to a very narrow
085   space. This theoretical proof explains the observations in Figure 1b.
086   Building on this theoretical analysis, we propose a simple yet effective solution named Temperature
087   Scaling (TempScale) to mitigate the length collapse phenomenon. TempScale manipulates the
088   calculated attention map by dividing the attention scores by a parameter named temperature smaller
089   than 1 before applying the softmax(·) operator. This adjustment increases the variance of the
090   attention score matrix, as shown in Eqn. 3, leading to a larger σs for the self-attention matrix. As a
091   result, the self-attention matrix has a lower filter attenuation rate, leading to more diverse embeddings.
092
      As shown in Figure 1c, a smaller temperature enables embeddings to exhibit lower pairwise cosine
      similarity and results in a more even distribution, thereby alleviating length collapse.

095
096                    • We uncover the length collapse phenomenon and then establish a rigorous theoretical analysis
097                      from the spectral domain and show that the length collapse is due to the low-pass filtering strength
098
                         of self-attention increases as the sequence length increases, leading the token signals retain only
                         their DC component.
099
100                    • We present TempScale, a theoretically grounded scaling technique that incorporates a temperature
101                      parameter into the softmax(·) function to achieve a higher attenuation rate in the self-attention

110   In this section, we first present our notations and define the problem. Then, we introduce the Trans-
111   former structure in the mainstream embedding model and briefly explain the Fourier transform used
112   in (Wang et al., 2022b). Based on the Fourier transform, we show that the attention mechanism acts
113   as a low-pass filter, and longer input sequences strengthen the filtering effect, leading to increasingly
114   similar representations. This results in cosine similarity increasing with the length of the text.

267
268   Other Components in Transformer. After discussing how self-attention contributes to length
269   collapse, we proceed to examine the influence of other modules in the Transformer, such as multi-
      head, residual, and FFN. Fortunately, previous work (Wang et al., 2022b) has talked about whether


                                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      these components can effectively alleviate the low-pass filtering drawbacks. The proof demonstrates
271   that while these components help preserve high-frequency signals, they do not alter the fact that
272   the MSA block, as a whole, functions solely with the representational power of a low-pass filter.
273   Furthermore, the ability of these models to preserve high-frequency signals is solely determined by
274   their internal parameters and architecture, independent of the input text length. As a result, these
275   modules do not impact our analysis of length collapse in practical models.
276
      Difference from Over-Smoothing in Deeper Layers. In previous research (Wang et al., 2022b), it
277   has been noted that a self-attention module acts as a low-pass filter, causing input feature maps to
278   gradually lose high-frequency signals as the model layers go deeper. Furthermore, other studies (Oono
279   & Suzuki; Cai & Wang, 2020) indicate that the node features of Graph Convolutional Networks
280   (GCNs) can become exponentially trapped in the null space of the graph Laplacian matrix. The
281   root cause of this phenomenon is that both graph Laplacian matrices and self-attention matrices
282   consistently exhibit a dominant eigenvector, commonly referred to as the DC component. While
283   these studies address over-smoothing in deeper layers, we focus on how the low-pass filtering process
284   changes as the input sequence lengthens, specifically examining over-smoothing in longer sequences.

299   The tasks in the MTEB benchmark can be divided into two categories: classification and cluster-
300   ing, and matching. For classification and clustering tasks, a classifier needs to be trained on text
301   embeddings to categorize and cluster embeddings of different classes. Matching tasks, on the other
302   hand, typically involve calculating the similarity between two text embeddings using cosine similarity
303
      or dot product. Furthermore, since the texts used for similarity calculations can vary greatly in
      length, we further divide matching-based methods into two types: retrieval and STS. In the MTEB
304
      benchmark, retrieval tasks include retrieval, longembed retrieval, and rerank, where the two texts
305
      being matched have significant length differences. The other type includes STS and summarization,
306   where the two texts have similar lengths. Next, we will discuss these three types of tasks to analyze
307   how the distributional differences in embeddings of long and short texts caused by length collapse
308   impact performance across different tasks.
309
      Impact on classification and clustering tasks. As shown in Figure 4 (Left), length collapse causes
310
      long-text embeddings to cluster near the center, while short-text embeddings are more dispersed
311
      around the periphery. In KNN classification, this makes clustering centers closer to long texts,
312   resulting in the classifier being more influenced by long texts and thus reducing its performance.

494   6      R ELATED W ORK
495
496   Text Embedding Models. Embeddings are generated by pre-trained language models (PLMs), which
497
      produce fixed-dimensional embeddings regardless of the input text length, laying the foundation
      of numerous NLP applications. Early works on text embeddings lack context awareness and are
498
      thus commonly labeled as word embedding models (Pennington et al., 2014). Modern embedding
499
      models (Wang et al., 2022a; Xiao et al., 2023) incorporate context awareness into language models
500   through self-attention mechanisms, serving as the foundation for the latest embedding models.
501   These models, based on Transformer architectures like BERT (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019) and
502   RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), are first pre-trained on large-scale weakly supervised text pairs using
503   contrastive loss (Gao et al., 2021), then fine-tuned on small scale but high-quality datasets. More
504   recently, (Muennighoff et al., 2024) investigates the integration of generative and embedding tasks in
505   large language models, introducing transformer-based GritLM, which enhances performance in both
506   areas. Among the diverse range of pre-trained transformers (Wolf et al., 2020), self-attention plays a
507   crucial role, and this paper focuses on how this module contributes to length collapse.
508   Context Window Extension for Embedding Model. Despite transformer-based embedding models
509   excelling in generating vector representations, they are typically constrained by a narrow context
510   window of around 512 input tokens (Wang et al., 2022a; Xiao et al., 2023; Ni et al., 2022). This
511   limitation significantly restricts their use in scenarios that require processing long inputs, such as ex-
512   tensive Wikipedia entries or meeting transcripts (Saad-Falcon et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024). Previous
513   work attributes this poor performance to limited context windows and attempts to extend the window
514
      size. Current initiatives to develop long-context embedding models generally begin with acquiring a
      long-context backbone model, either by pre-training from scratch with long inputs (Günther et al.,
515
      2023; Nussbaum et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) or utilizing existing models (Wang et al., 2024; Zhu
516
      et al., 2024). This is followed by training the backbone model to generate embeddings. However, in
517   this paper, we discover that regardless of the model’s context window size, the model consistently
518   performs worse on longer texts than on shorter texts due to length collapse. Therefore, we aim
519   to improve the performance of long texts across all context window size models by analyzing and
520   addressing length collapse, rather than simply expanding context window size.
521
522
523
      7      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525
      In this paper, we identify the phenomenon of length collapse, where embeddings of longer texts tend
      to cluster together and propose practical solutions through Fourier domain analysis. Our theoretical
526
      findings suggest that Multi-Head Self-Attention inherently performs stronger low-pass filtering as
527
      the token sequence length increases, leading to patch uniformity issues in longer sentences. To this
528   end, we propose a technique called TempScale, which effectively reduces the low-pass filtering effect
529   by introducing a temperature parameter when applying softmax in the self-attention matrix. Our
530   extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of our methods and enhance the performance of
531   general embedding models on the MTEB and LongEmbed benchmarks. Overall, TempScale is a
532   crucial advancement in enhancing the performance of embedding models on longer texts.
533
      Future work includes: 1) LLM-based embedding model: Although we have observed Length
534   Collapse in LLM-based embedding models in Appendix G, further analysis is needed to investigate
535   how unidirectional attention mechanisms specifically contribute to this phenomenon; 2) Tuning
536   method: The work in this paper relies on existing models and pre-trained parameters without using a
537   training dataset. In future work, we will focus on tuning the temperature for additional improvements.
538   3) Analysis on more modules: We primarily investigate the impact of the self-attention module on
539   length collapse in this paper. Moving forward, we plan to explore the effects of additional modules in
      transformers such as LayerNorm and FFN.

       F.1.1                       BACKGROUND AND M OTIVATION
1323   Contrastive learning is a technique widely adopted to improve the anisotropy of the embedding
1324   space, which can reduce the high similarity between embeddings of random text samples across
1325   varied lengths. This anisotropy mitigation is achieved by maximizing distances among negative
1326   pairs while aligning positive pairs closely. However, while contrastive learning has demonstrated
1327   improvements in embedding quality across multiple applications, its effects on the length collapse
1328   phenomenon remain unexamined. This section explores how contrastive learning impacts length
1329   collapse, evaluating its contributions and limitations.
1330   As shown in previous works Wang & Isola (2020), the InfoNCE loss, when scaled to a large number
1331   of negative samples, can be decomposed into two primary components: Alignment and Uniformity.
1332   Alignment ensures that positive pairs—texts with similar content—are close in the embedding space,
1333   while Uniformity spreads embeddings of negative pairs to prevent them from clustering excessively.
1334   Mathematically, as the number of negative samples M → ∞, the normalized InfoNCE loss can be
1335   expressed as:
