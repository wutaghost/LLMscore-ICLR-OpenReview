010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            We present Graphical-TS, an interactive simulation framework for multi-
013            variate time series (MTS) incorporating spatiotemporal causal graphical models.
014            The system offers extensive customizability, enabling users to define and modify
015            causal dynamics with uncertainty in spatiotemporal relationships and functional
016            mappings. Graphical-TS integrates expert knowledge, supports MTS simu-
017
               lation, and allows for the fusion of real-world MTS data, facilitating a dynamic
               interplay between data-driven learning and domain expertise. The system itera-
018
               tively enhances causal relationships and simulated data by simulating MTS data
019
               based on specified causal graphs, performing causal discovery from real or sim-
020            ulated MTS, and enabling the integration and refinement of expert knowledge
021            with learned causality. This approach progressively enhances the quality of both
022            the causal models and the data they produce, facilitating tasks such as time se-
023            ries forecasting and imputation. By customizing functional mappings, scenario-
024            driven distribution shifts can be modeled, enabling robust testing of time series
025            algorithms. We compared state-of-the-art causal discovery methods on datasets
026            generated by Graphical-TS. The empirical results demonstrate the platform’s
027            consistent performance compared to existing methods while offering versatility
028
               under distinct scenarios. This enables users to explore datasets more thoroughly
               and drive improvements in causal discovery research. With an intuitive user in-
029
               terface that connects domain experts and algorithm developers, Graphical-TS
030
               empowers users to manipulate causal relationships, embedding domain knowl-
031            edge into machine learning workflows. Originally developed to study physiologi-
032            cal dynamics in patients, the system has broad applicability across various fields,
033            offering a versatile platform for generating MTS datasets with known dynam-
034            ics, validating causal discovery algorithms, and advancing research in time series
035            analysis.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Multivariate time series (MTS) data plays a crucial role across various domains such as health,
040   transportation, earth science, and finance. A key aspect of MTS is the inter-correlation among vari-
041   ables, which helps model the underlying mechanisms generating multivariate signals. These inter-
042   relationships can be represented as causal graphs that map the influence of one variable on another
043   without forming loops—formally expressing causality. For example, in healthcare, graphical mod-
044
      els have been used to improve medical diagnosis and prognosis, as seen in studies like predicting
      sepsis onset using machine-learned causal probabilistic networks based on electronic health records
045
      data (Valik et al., 2023) and describing functioning in people living with spinal cord injury across 22
046
      countries (Ehrmann et al., 2020). Additionally, graphical models have been applied to infer cellular
047   networks using probabilistic graphical models (Friedman, 2004) and to detect and quantify causal
048   associations in large nonlinear time series datasets (Runge et al., 2019a). These models can capture
049   complex causal dependencies in various domains, offering insights into underlying processes and
050   supporting decision-making.
051   In some cases, like monitoring a circuit board where pin voltages are observed, the circuit’s structure
052   provides a clear representation of the underlying graph generating these signals. However, in most
053   real-world situations, the causal graph is at least partially unknown. Expert knowledge can help
      elicit the causal graph; for instance, the selection of sensors in the signal collection often reflects

      prior assumptions about causal relationships. While this may introduce bias, incorporating expert
055   knowledge remains valuable as long as it is sufficiently reliable.
056   Additionally, data-driven methods known as causal discovery—comprising constraint-based,
057   functional-based, and score-based approaches—are used to learn the graph from data. Causal dis-
058   covery is particularly important in time series analysis, where temporal dependencies add an extra
059   dimension of complexity. In this context, expert knowledge often serves as a valuable foundation
060   for building graphical models, providing a starting point that can be iteratively refined through a
061   combination of human input and algorithmic learning.

077   Researchers frequently generate multivariate time series (MTS) data for evaluating algorithms, as
078   readily available datasets are scarce. Simulating MTS with predefined dynamics is a common prac-
079   tice that allows researchers greater control over experimental conditions and the evaluation process.
080   However, we observe a significant redundancy in these data generation efforts, particularly in craft-
081
      ing graphical models and specifying functional relationships. We contend that domain experts are
      best suited to define and identify the causal relationships between variables. At the same time, al-
082
      gorithm developers should concentrate on refining the algorithms themselves, rather than investing
083
      time in coding simulations for each model they develop. One of the primary objectives of this pa-
084   per is to bridge the gap between domain experts and algorithm developers, thereby streamlining the
085   research process and enhancing the efficiency of causal discovery studies.

364
      As mentioned in section 2, there is a zoo of causal discovery algorithms, including constraint-based
365   methods and gradient-based methods. There is no fixed quantitative scheme for measuring the qual-
366   ity of a learned graph. For constraint-based methods and Granger Causality-based methods, edges
367   are added by statistical tests; in other words, they are measured by nature. In gradient-based meth-
368   ods, a graph is learned by reinforcing the acyclicity. Due to the difference in their assumptions,
369   the resulting causal model might not align. Although researchers can choose what algorithms they
370   want, with the presence of ground truth, the algorithm that better reconstructs the ground truth graph
371   on which the simulation runs should be preferred. In a scenario of expert knowledge incorporation,
372   the edge relationship input by an expert should be learned, which means the algorithms learn how
373
      humans decide whether an edge should be added or modified. As we will see in the following sec-
      tion, the simulated data of Graphical-TS will be submitted to both kinds of methodology, and
374
      evaluated by comparing it to the ground truth.

      Contributions Graphical-TS offers a useful tool for integrating human expertise with algo-
497   rithmic processes for causal discovery in multivariate time series data. By supporting collaboration
498   between domain experts and computational models, the system enhances the representation of causal
499   dynamics. With its flexible interface, customizable uncertainty features, and the ability to simulate
500   datasets with known ground truth, Graphical-TS provides a valuable resource for research in
501   various fields, including physiology. Additionally, the system contributes to the iterative refinement
502   of causal models and the evaluation of causal discovery algorithms, supporting their application in
503   time series data analysis.
504   Limitations The system lacks comprehensive validation of its effectiveness across different user

506   fields. More extensive testing is needed to evaluate how different users interact with and benefit

513   strengthen the system’s ability to support reliable causal inference in time series analysis.
