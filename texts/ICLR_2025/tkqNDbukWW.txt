009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Large Language Models (LLMs) often hallucinate, producing unfaithful or factu-
012               ally incorrect outputs by misrepresenting the provided context or incorrectly re-
013               calling internal knowledge. Recent studies have identified specific attention heads
014               within the Transformer architecture, known as retrieval heads, responsible for
015               extracting relevant contextual information. We hypothesise that masking these re-
016               trieval heads can induce hallucinations and that contrasting the outputs of the base
017
                  LLM and the masked LLM can reduce hallucinations. To this end, we propose
                  Decoding by Contrasting Retrieval Heads (DeCoRe), a novel training-free decod-
018
                  ing strategy that amplifies information found in the context and model parameters.
019
                  DeCoRe mitigates potentially hallucinated responses by dynamically contrasting
020               the outputs of the base LLM and the masked LLM, using conditional entropy as
021               a guide. Our extensive experiments confirm that DeCoRe improves performance
022               on tasks requiring high contextual faithfulness, such as summarisation (XSum by
023               18.6%), instruction following (MemoTrap by 10.9%), and open-book question
024               answering (NQ-Open by 2.4% and NQ-Swap by 5.5%).1

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as powerful natural language generators, demon-
030
      strating remarkable capabilities across a range of tasks (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Wei
      et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022). However, LLMs are prone to hallucinations, where the model
031
      generates content that is not grounded in reality or misrepresents the facts (Ji et al., 2023; Rawte
032
      et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c; Li et al., 2024a). The tendency of LLMs to hallucinate undermines
033   their reliability, especially when applied in high-stakes domains such as clinical decision-making or
034   legal reasoning (Ahmad et al., 2023; Dahl et al., 2024).
035
036
      Understanding the underlying mechanisms responsible for hallucinations in LLMs remains chal-
      lenging. Wu et al. (2024) found that there are special attention heads responsible for retrieving
037
      relevant information from a given context, which they called “retrieval heads”. While identify-
038
      ing these mechanisms is key to understanding LLMs, little research has explored how to use these
039   insights to effectively mitigate hallucinations, which is the focus of our work.
040
041   We propose a novel decoding method termed Decoding by Contrasting Retrieval Heads (DeCoRe),
042
      as illustrated in Figure 1. This method builds on the assumption that masking retrieval heads can
      induce hallucination by impairing the ability of the model to retrieve relevant information from
043
      the context. DeCoRe leverages Contrastive Decoding (Li et al., 2023) to amplify the differences
044
      between the original and the hallucinating outputs, leading to more accurate final responses. Fur-
045   thermore, we propose using the conditional entropy of the model’s next-token distribution to control
046   the contrastive decoding mechanism.
047
048
      Our findings show that DeCoRe significantly improves accuracy in tasks that require contextual
      faithfulness, such as XSum (Narayan et al., 2018), MemoTrap (Liu & Liu, 2023), Open Book Natu-
049
      ral Questions (NQ; Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), and NQ-Swap (Longpre et al., 2021). Furthermore,
050
      our experiments show that DeCoRe enhances the model’s accuracy in factual recall tasks. For ex-
051   ample, in the TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017) and PopQA (Mallen et al., 2023) dataset, DeCoRe shows
052   an accuracy gain compared to other hallucination mitigation methods. Similarly, when applied to

134   2.2      C ONTRASTING BASE AND M ASKED LLM S
135
      Given the base and masked LLMs from Section 2.1, our goal is to improve the faithfulness of the
136   generated output. To achieve this, we propose contrasting the next-token distributions of the base
137   and masked models, effectively increasing the likelihood of the tokens selected by the former while
138   decreasing the likelihood of the tokens selected by the latter. More formally, DeCoRe uses the
139   following next-token distribution p (xt | x<t ):
140                 p (xt | x<t ) ∝ exp [(1 + α) log pbase (xt | x<t ) − α log pmasked (xt | x<t )] .   (7)
141   In Equation (7), the new next-token distribution p(xt | x<t ) is defined by contrasting the next-
142   token distributions of the base model pbase (xt | x<t ) and the masked model pmasked (xt | x<t ),
143   introduced in Section 2.1; and α ∈ R is a scaling factor that controls the weight of the next-token
144   distribution induced by the base model pbase (xt | x<t ) and the one induced by the masked model
145   pmasked (xt | x<t ). The term (1 + α) log pbase (xt | x<t ) in Equation (7) encourages the base LLM
146   to predict a highly probably token under its distribution, while α log pmasked (xt | x<t ) penalises
147   predictions that are also likely under the masked model’s distribution.

      3     E XPERIMENT S ETUP
163
164
      Hallucinations in LLMs can generally be categorised into two types – factuality and faithfulness
165   hallucinations (Huang et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024). Factuality hallucinations refer to instances
166   where the generated content is factually incorrect with respect to world knowledge. Faithfulness
167   hallucinations refer to instances where the generated content fails to accurately adhere to the given
168   source of information. Moreover, hallucinations may “snowball” in longer generation tasks such as
169   multi-hop reasoning, compounding errors across generation steps due to the inherently sequential
170   behaviour of auto-regressive decoding in LLMs (Merrill & Sabharwal, 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a).
171   In this section, we describe our experimental setup to evaluate DeCoRe. We employ a diverse set of
172   benchmarks to assess contextual faithfulness, factual accuracy, and multi-hop reasoning capability.
173   Given that retrieval heads are important in correctly retrieving contextual information (Wu et al.,
174   2024) and looking back over long reasoning processes (Wu et al., 2024), while attention heads play a
175   significant role in information transfer between tokens (Elhage et al., 2021), our experimental setup
176   is designed to answer the following key research questions: 1) Can DeCoRe improve contextual
177   faithfulness? 2) Can DeCoRe maintain or enhance the factual recall capabilities of LLMs? 3) Does
178   coupling DeCoRe with CoT improve the multi-hop reasoning capability of the LLM?
179
180   3.1   DATASETS AND E VALUATION M ETRICS
181
182
      Faithfulness. We evaluate faithfulness on summarisation, instruction-following, and reading com-
      prehension datasets. XSum (Narayan et al., 2018) is an abstractive summarisation dataset developed
183
      from BBC articles. We sub-sample 1,000 examples, following Chuang et al. (2024), and evaluate
184
      summaries using ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004), BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020), and factKB (Feng et al.,
185   2023) for factual consistency. MemoTrap (Liu & Liu, 2023) tests whether models can avoid mem-
186   orisation traps and adhere to the given instructions, with performance reported using macro- and
187   micro-averaged accuracy. Instruction-Following Eval (IFEval; Zhou et al., 2023) evaluates the abil-
188   ity of the models to follow instructions on a set of verifiable instructions such as “write in more than
189   400 words”. The performance is reported using Prompt-level and Instruction-level strict accuracies,
190   which measure the percentage of prompts where all verifiable instructions are followed, and the
191   percentage of verifiable instructions followed overall. Open-Domain Natural Questions (NQ-Open;
192   Lee et al., 2019) a QA dataset where we use an open-book configuration with one supporting docu-
193
      ment per question as described by Liu et al. (2024). NQ-Swap (Longpre et al., 2021) is a version of
      NQ where the answer entity in the context was replaced with another entity and is used to evaluate
194
      the faithfulness of the model to the modified context. We evaluate the models with the Exact Match
195
      (EM) metric and, following Kandpal et al. (2023) and Liu et al. (2024), we consider a prediction as
196   correct if any sub-string of the prediction exactly matches any of the ground truth answers.
197
198   Factuality. For factuality evaluation, we use four datasets—TruthfulQA, TriviaQA, PopQA, and
199   NQ-Open. TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022) (MC1, MC2, MC3, and Gen) is used to evaluate whether
200   models can avoid common human falsehoods; MC1, MC2, and MC3 are multi-label classification
201   tasks, and Gen is a generation task where evaluations use fine-tuned GPT models to assess the cor-
202   rectness and informativeness of the generated outputs. TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), PopQA (Mallen
203   et al., 2023), and NQ-Open are open-domain QA datasets used to evaluate the ability of a model to
204   answer questions about trivia, long-tail entities, and Google searches, respectively. We use closed-
205   book configuration on these datasets to evaluate factual recall.
206
207   Chain of Thought Reasoning. We evaluate DeCoRe in CoT-style reasoning tasks on both closed-
208   and open-book setups; we use MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022), a multi-hop QA dataset that requires
209
      models to answer questions by reasoning using multiple and disconnected pieces of information.
210   More details on the evaluation protocol are available in Table 31.
211
212   3.2   M ODELS AND BASELINES
213
214   We evaluate two models from the Llama3 family Dubey et al. (2024), namely Llama3-8B-Instruct
215   and Llama3-70B-Instruct. In Appendix I, we also report results from other model families, such as
      Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) and Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024).

      We compare DeCoRe against six baselines: 1) Greedy decoding; 2) Contrastive Decoding (CD; Li
217   et al., 2023), where LLaMA3-8B-Instruct serves as the amateur model and LLaMA3-70B-Instruct
218   act as the expert model; 3) Context-Aware Decoding (CAD; Shi et al., 2024), a variant of CD where
219   the amateur model is the same as the expert model but is not presented with the additional context;
220   4) Decoding by Contrasting Layers (DoLa; Chuang et al., 2023) that subtracts the logits in early
221   layers to calibrate the final-layer logits. We evaluate two versions: DoLa-low (i.e., contrasting
222   the first half of the layers with the final layer) and DoLa-high (i.e., contrasting the second half
223   with the final layer); 5) Activation Decoding (AD; Chen et al., 2024) which uses the sharpness of
224   context activations within intermediate layers to calibrate the next token prediction; 6) ITI (Li et al.,
225
      2024b) that trains linear classifiers on TruthfulQA data to obtain “factual” heads and layers with
      corresponding “factual” direction vectors and then apply intervention during the decoding process.
226
      Note that ITI requires a training process on labelled data , whereas other baselines and DeCoRe are
227
      training-free. Also note that CAD is only applicable on tasks with additional context (i.e., XSum,
228   open book NQ-Open, NQ-Swap, and open book MuSiQue). All implementation details are available
229   in Appendix K.

      3.3        D E C O R E VARIANTS
232
233   We evaluate three variants of DeCoRe: 1) DeCoRestatic , which employs a static scaling factor α
234   throughout generation; 2) DeCoReentropy , which entropy to dynamically adjust the strength of the
235   contrastive decoding; 3) DeCoReentropy-lite , which is similar to DeCoReentropy , except that it employs
236   a smaller LLM with the same vocabulary space as the masked LLM. We use LLama3-70B-Instruct
237   and LLama3-8B-Instruct as the base and masked LLMs, respectively.

239   4        R ESULTS
240
241   In the following, we present the evaluation results of DeCoRe across faithfulness, factuality, and
242   multi-hop reasoning tasks. We show that DeCoRe mitigates faithfulness and factuality hallucina-
243   tions, and improves the accuracy of the model when combined with CoT prompting. These effec-
244   tively answer our research questions stated in Section 3. Additionally, we examine the impact of the
245   number of masked retrieval heads on task performance. Finally, we demonstrate that DeCoRe re-
246   duces conditional entropy over time in long-generation tasks, contributing to more accurate outputs.
247   These results highlight DeCoRe’s broad effectiveness in enhancing LLM performance.

468   DeCoRe yields lower entropy across time in long generation tasks. We found that lower con-
469   ditional entropy is related to correct predictions; generated sequences with lower conditional en-
470   tropy tend to be more reliable (see Appendix F). Motivated by the importance of low conditional
471   entropy, we evaluate the length-normalised conditional entropy of different decoding strategies in
472   long-generation tasks (i.e., XSum, and MuSiQue with CoT prompting).
473
      As shown in Figure 4, DeCoReentropy yields lower conditional entropy compared to the baselines.
474   DeCoReentropy demonstrates lower entropy in the open-book QA task (MuSiQue), with an average
475   entropy of 0.29 compared to 0.30 for the baselines. Similarly, in XSum, DeCoReentropy achieves an
476   entropy of 0.38, outperforming the baselines. In tasks such as summarisation (XSum) and open-book
477   QA (MuSiQue), lower entropy is crucial because the model must strictly adhere to the provided doc-
478   ument or evidence while generating the summary or answer. Any deviation from the context can re-
479   sult in hallucinations or factually incorrect outputs. The lower entropy observed with DeCoReentropy
480   indicates that it generates less “surprising” sequences, reducing the likelihood of hallucinations.
481   Overall, the reduction in conditional entropy shows that DeCoReentropy is able to maintain lower
482   uncertainty throughout long-generation tasks. This reinforces the effectiveness of DeCoReentropy in
483   applications requiring high contextual faithfulness, such as summarisation and open-book QA. In
484   Appendix L.2, we provide samples of text generated with DeCoRe in several long-form generation
485   tasks, namely XSum, TruthfulQA (Gen), and MuSiQue with CoT—we can see that when using
      DeCoRe, the model tends to produce more faithful generations.

      5   R ELATED W ORKS
487
488
      Internal Mechanism of LLMs. Studies have attempted to dissect the inner workings of LLMs
489
      by focusing on layers (Wallat et al., 2020; Geva et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2024),
490   neurons (Dai et al., 2022), and attention heads (Elhage et al., 2021; Geva et al., 2023; Yuksekgonul
491   et al., 2024). A seminal discovery in this area is the identification of induction heads, the attention
492   heads that perform an induction algorithm by looking back over the context to predict a similar
493   completion (Olsson et al., 2022). Similarly, Wu et al. (2024) identified retrieval heads, a specific set
494   of attention heads responsible for maintaining long-context factuality. These insights into the inter-
495   nal workings of LLMs is instrumental to our work, which focuses on these mechanisms to reduce
496   hallucination. Our work leverages the idea that the masking of retrieval heads leads to hallucination.
497
498   Constrained Decoding. Constrained decoding focuses on intervening during the generation pro-
499   cess to reduce hallucinations. One example of constrained decoding is Inference-Time Interven-
500   tion (ITI; Li et al., 2024b) which intervenes by probing and modifying attention heads or layers as-
501   sociated with model correctness. Contrastive Decoding (CD; Li et al., 2023) improves fluency and
502   coherence by contrasting outputs from stronger expert LMs with those from weaker, smaller LMs.
503
      Building on CD, Shi et al. (2024) propose Context Aware Decoding (CAD) to mitigate contextual
      hallucinations by contrasting the output of an LLM with and without the provided context. Simi-
504
      larly, Zhao et al. (2024) propose a CD framework that contrasts the answers generated using correct
505
      and adversarial passages. Additionally, Induced-then-Contrast Decoding (ICD; Zhang et al., 2023b)
506   fine-tunes a factually weak LLM using an automatically generated non-factual dataset, although
507   this approach depends on the quality of the dataset and requires fine-tuning. More closely related
508   to DeCoRe is Decoding by Contrasting Layers (DoLa; Chuang et al., 2023) and Autocontrastive
509   Decoding (ACD; Gera et al., 2023), which examines the internal mechanism of the LLMs without
510   fine-tuning. Both DoLa and ACD proposed contrasting the predictions of the final layer against the
511   earlier ones via early exiting (Teerapittayanon et al., 2016; Elbayad et al., 2020). Activation Decod-
512   ing (Chen et al., 2024) also examines the internal mechanism of the LLMs, particularly the sharpness
513   of context activations to calibrate the next token’s probability distribution. DeCoRe distinguishes
514
      itself by masking retrieval heads to induce hallucinations, followed by a dynamic entropy-controlled
      contrastive decoding to penalise uncertain outputs, effectively reducing hallucinations without the
515
      need for fine-tuning. DeCoRe is also related to self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023); but rather than
516
      relying on majority voting among generations from a single model, DeCoRe amplifies the difference
517   between a base model and its masked variant.
518
519
520   6   C ONCLUSIONS
521
522   DeCoRe (Decoding by Contrasting Retrieval Heads) is a novel decoding strategy that aims to reduce
523   faithfulness and factuality hallucinations in LLMs. DeCoRe is based on the assumption that masking
524   retrieval heads can induce hallucinations by limiting the ability of the model to retrieve relevant
525   information from the given context. Specifically, DeCoRe uses retrieval head masking to create a
526
      version of the model that is more likely to generate hallucinations and combines it with the original
      model via a contrasting decoding scheme (Section 2.2). Furthermore, we propose a simple approach
527
      to control the strength of the contrastive decoding scheme by using the conditional entropy of the
528
      next-token distribution of the model (Section 2.3). Our experimental results show that DeCoRe
529   significantly improves the accuracy of the model in tasks requiring contextual faithfulness and in
530   some factual recall and reasoning tasks.
531
532
      Limitations. While DeCoRe improves the performance of the base model across most tasks, there
533
      is no “free lunch”; existing baselines may still produce more accurate results than DeCoRe in specific
534   tasks (e.g., ITI in TruthfulQA or CAD in NQ-Swap). However, these baselines often offer limited
535   improvements or may even generate less accurate responses in other tasks. We also observed that
536   DeCoRe offers only marginal enhancements in factual recall tasks, suggesting that retrieval heads
537   may not play a primary role in factual recall except for information transfer. Finally, while we pro-
538   pose using the conditional entropy of the model’s next-token distribution to control the contrastive
539   decoding scheme in DeCoRe, semantic-based methods of uncertainty quantification may also be
      used (Farquhar et al., 2024).

991    B                         E THICS S TATEMENT
992
993
       Our proposed method, DeCoRe, aims to mitigate hallucinations in LLMs, particularly in tasks where
994
       contextual faithfulness are critical. By improving the reliability of LLMs, DeCoRe has the potential
995
       to reduce the risks associated with incorrect or misleading information generation.
996
997    Despite these positive intentions, there is a potential for DeCoRe to be misused. For example,
998    its ability to suppress contextual hallucinations may be exploited to generate more convincing but
999
       misleading content by providing it with factually incorrect or unverified contextual documents.
1000   To mitigate these risks, we have open-sourced our implementation to facilitate broader scrutiny from
1001   the community. Furthermore, we recommend that DeCoRe be applied with caution in sensitive
1002   domains where even small inaccuracies may have significant consequences, such as clinical and
1003   legal domains.

       C.1                       E XTRACTION OF R ETRIEVAL H EADS
1009
1010
       We follow the procedure provided by Wu et al. (2024)4 which defines the retrieval score of attention
1011
       heads as the ratio of successful copy-paste operations. They propose to calculate the retrieval score
1012   by compiling three sets of Needle-in-a-Haystack samples (Kamradt, 2023). Given a question q and
1013   its corresponding answer k (the needle), we insert k in a given context x (the haystack) at a random
1014   position index range iq . The language model is then tasked with answering q based on the haystack
1015   with the inserted needle. We set q and k unique and irrelevant with the given long context, ensuring
1016   that if an answer is correctly generated, it is indeed copied from the context, not from the model’s
1017   internal knowledge. Retrieval score of head h is defined as:
1018
1019
1020                                                                                                                                                        |gh ∩ k|
1021
                                                                                                         retrieval scoreh =
                                                                                                                                                               |k|
1022
1023
1024   Where gh is the set of tokens copy-pasted by head h. Retrieval score signifies the attention head
1025   ability to recall tokens from the given context, and can be used as a metric to identify retrieval heads
       in transformer-based LLMs.

       As shown in Figure 5, the retrieval scores for each model follow a similar pattern across all examined
1050   LLM variants. According to Wu et al. (2024), an attention head can be considered a retrieval head
1051   if it performs a copy-paste operation at least 10% of the time, which corresponds to a retrieval score
1052   of 0.1. In all the models evaluated, the retrieval scores drop below 0.1 just before reaching the
1053   50th retrieval head. This indicates that beyond this number, the attention heads may not be reliably
1054   performing retrieval tasks. Table 6 provides the precise retrieval scores for selected heads in each
1055   model.

1057   100th retrieval head for each model, even though the data suggest that heads beyond the 50th have
1058   minimal retrieval ability. This conservative approach ensures that we comprehensively account for
1059   all potential retrieval heads during the contrastive decoding process.
