009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have become the cornerstone of modern machine
012            learning applications, achieving impressive results in domains ranging from com-
013            puter vision to autonomous systems. However, their dependence on extensive data
014            and computational resources exposes them to vulnerabilities such as backdoor
015            attacks, where poisoned samples can lead to erroneous model outputs. To counter
016
               these threats, we introduce a defense strategy called H√∂lder Pruning to detect
               and eliminate neurons affected by triggers embedded in poisoned samples. Our
017
               method partitions the neural network into two stages: feature extraction and feature
018
               processing, aiming to detect and remove backdoored neurons‚ÄĒthe highly sensitive
019            neurons affected by the embedded triggers‚ÄĒwhile maintaining model performance
020            This improves model sensitivity to perturbations and enhances pruning precision
021            by exploiting the unique clustering properties of poisoned samples. We use the
022            H√∂lder constant to quantify sensitivity of neurons to input perturbations and prove
023            that using the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) can effectively identify highly
024            sensitive backdoored neurons. Our extensive experiments demonstrate efficacy of
025            H√∂lder Pruning across six clean feature extractors (SimCLR, Pretrained ResNet-18,
026            ViT, ALIGN, CLIP, and BLIP-2) and confirm robustness against nine backdoor
027
               attacks (BadNets, LC, SIG, LF, WaNet, Input-Aware, SSBA, Trojan, BppAttack)
               using three datasets (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, GTSRB). We compare H√∂lder Prun-
028
               ing to eight SOTA backdoor defenses (FP, ANP, CLP, FMP, ABL, DBD, D-ST)
029
               and show that H√∂lder Pruning outperforms all eight SOTA methods. Moreover,
030            H√∂lder Pruning achieves a runtime up to 1000x faster than SOTA defenses when
031            a clean feature extractor is available. Even when clean feature extractors are not
032            available, our method is up to 10x faster.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Deep neural networks (DNNs) have demonstrated outstanding performance across a range of appli-
038   cations, including computer vision (He et al., 2015), speech recognition (Gulati et al., 2020), and
039
      recommendation systems (Wang et al., 2023). Specifically, their framework mainly consists of a
      feature extractor (Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (He et al., 2015), Transformers (Vaswani
040
      et al., 2023)) and a classifier. DNN training requires extensive data and computational resources,
041
      often involving third-party data or servers. This dependency raises significant security concerns,
042   especially when using large public datasets that may contain corrupted or poisoned data samples.
043   Such malicious data can lead the DNN to produce undesired outputs (Gu et al., 2019). Among various
044   data corruption methods, backdoor attacks pose a notable threat (Li et al., 2022). This attack involves
045   deliberate manipulation by either controlling the training process (Ilyas et al., 2018; Kurakin et al.,
046   2017; Li et al., 2021a; Liang et al., 2020), or corrupting a small number of data samples by inserting
047   a predefined perturbation called trigger (Gu et al., 2019). Consequently, the trained DNN model
048   behaves normally on clean (non-corrupted) data, but produces an (adversary-desired) output label for
049   poisoned (corrupted) samples. This manipulation can hinder the detection and mitigation of corrupted
050
      data, making the development of effective defense mechanisms a priority in neural network research.
051   To address these challenges, several novel backdoor defense methods, including pruning, have been
052   proposed (Chen et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021b; Wu & Wang, 2021; Zheng et al., 2022).
053   Pruning methods (Wu & Wang, 2021; Zheng et al., 2022) attempt to prevent backdoor attacks by
      eliminating neurons that process trigger features. Corrupted samples processed by poisoned neurons,

      2.2   H √ĖLDER C ONDITION
153
154
      A real-valued function f , defined on a Euclidean space Rd , satisfies a H√∂lder Condition (Knill, 1994)
155
      if for all x, x‚Ä≤ ‚ąą Rd , there exists a constant C ‚Č• 0 and 0 < őĪ ‚Č§ 1 such that |f (x) ‚ąí f (x‚Ä≤ )| ‚Č§
156   C ¬∑ ||x ‚ąí x‚Ä≤ ||őĪ . Here, C and őĪ are termed the H√∂lder constant and the H√∂lder exponent, respectively.
157   When őĪ = 1, f satisfies the Lipschitz condition, with C termed the Lipschitz constant (Knill,
158   1994). For fixed őĪ, a high value of C indicates that the function f is highly sensitive to input
159   variations, suggesting greater changes in the function‚Äôs output in response to small changes in the
160   input. Conversely, a smaller C implies that f is less sensitive to input variations. We assess sensitivity
161   of a backdoored DNN to its training samples D‚Ä≤ in terms of C, and use this to inform design of a
      neuron pruning technique on the backdoored DNN to mitigate effects of backdoor attacks.

      2.3   D EFINITIONS OF THE P ERFORMANCE M ETRICS
163
164   We present metrics to evaluate our defense against backdoor attacks below (Li et al., 2021c).
165
166
      Clean Accuracy (ACC). Clean Accuracy measures the model‚Äôs performance on clean (non-poisoned)
      samples, and defined as ACC = (# Correctly Classified Clean Samples) / (# of Clean Samples).
167
168   Attack Success Rate (ASR). ASR quantifies the effectiveness of an attack, and is defined as
169   ASR = (# of Poisoned Samples Misclassified to Target Class) / (# of Poisoned Samples).
170   Robust Accuracy (RA). RA measures the number of poisoned samples that are correctly classified
171   after the implementation of the defense mechanisms, and is defined as RA = (# of Poisoned Samples
172   Correctly Classified After the Defense) / (# of Poisoned Samples).

      2.4   T HREAT M ODEL
175
176   Adversary Assumptions: The adversary is assumed to have the capability to inject poisoned
177   samples into a clean dataset. The adversary does not have direct access to or control over the model
178   architecture, training process, or ability to retrain the model. The attacker‚Äôs control is thus limited to
179   poisoning the dataset, as described in (Barni et al., 2019; Gu et al., 2019; Nguyen & Tran, 2021).
180
      Adversary Goals and Actions: The adversary‚Äôs primary goal is to introduce a backdoor into a
181   model trained by a user by injecting poisoned data into either (i) the training process or (ii) the
182   fine-tuning process, while remaining undetected. In the first scenario, the adversary embeds triggers
183   in publicly available datasets, which developers or data owners may inadvertently download and use
184   for model training, unknowingly incorporating backdoors into their models. In the second scenario,
185   the adversary targets the fine-tuning phase, where downstream users rely on pretrained models from
186   large companies. While these pretrained models are assumed to be clean, the external or additional
187   data used for fine-tuning may be contaminated with backdoor triggers. In both cases, the adversary‚Äôs
188   influence is restricted to modifying the training data by injecting poisoned samples, without direct
189
      access to the model architecture or control over the training process itself.
190   The adversary aims to ensure that: (i) models trained or fine-tuned with the poisoned dataset output
191   adversary-specified target classes when presented with inputs containing embedded triggers; and
192   (ii) the attack evades standard detection mechanisms by keeping the set of poisoned data small
193   and blending the backdoor triggers seamlessly with the original data. This ensures the model‚Äôs
194   performance on clean data remains intact, avoiding suspicion or detection.
195   Attack Performance Metrics: Clean accuracy (CA) and attack success rate (ASR) are used to
196   evaluate attack effectiveness. The adversary‚Äôs goal is to ensure high values of CA and ASR.
197
198   2.5   D EFENDER M ODEL
199
200   Defender Assumptions: The defense is assumed to have access to the entire training dataset, but
201   cannot reliably distinguish between clean and poisoned samples without further analysis (Li et al.,
202   2021b; Zheng et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022). In Sec. 3.1, we initially assume
203   that the defense has access to a discriminative feature extractor (e.g., transformers, outputs of final
204   pooling layer in CNNs) trained exclusively on clean data, which we term a clean feature extractor. In
205   Sec. 3.2, we relax this assumption. When clean feature extractors are unavailable, self-supervised
206
      learning and iterative semi-supervised learning can be used to continually refine the feature space.
207   Defender Goals and Actions: The primary goal of the defense is to detect and neutralize effects of
208   poisoned samples during training, ensuring that the model does not learn correlations between the
209   trigger embedded by the adversary and the adversary-desired target class. In our defense pipeline,
210   each sample is initially passed through a ‚Äėclean feature extractor‚Äô. The extracted features, along with
211   their corresponding labels, are then used to train an classifier. After training, the defense prunes
212
      neurons in the hidden layers of classifier based on each neuron‚Äôs output sensitivity to input noise. This
      pruning process helps identify and remove neurons that are activated by poisoned samples (poisoned
213
      neurons), thereby enhancing the overall DNN‚Äôs resilience to backdoor attacks.
214
215   Defense Performance Metrics: We assess the pruned DNN using three metrics: clean accuracy
      (CA), attack success rate (ASR), and robust accuracy (RA).

411   H√∂lder Iteration Defense ‚ÄďIn the absence of a clean extractor. We compare performance of
412   our H√∂lder Iteration Defense with four SOTA models using ACC, ASR, and RA in Table 8. Our
413   experimental results demonstrate that our H√∂lder Iteration Defense consistently performs better than
414   SOTA methods against different types of backdoor attacks. We observed that ABL includes a step
415   like poison suppression, which performs well against clean label attacks (LC, SIG). However, the
416   first step‚ÄĒ Backdoor Isolation‚ÄĒ mainly defines suppression performance and limits overall model
417
      performance, keeping ASR high and ACC low in most cases. Similar to our approach, CLP also
      employs pruning, but it measures the Lipschitz constant of network parameters. In CLP, poisoned
418
      neurons are dispersed throughout the network via forward propagation, and most neurons exhibit
419
      both clean and toxic features. As a result, the Lipschitz constant is adequate to only detect and prune
420   a small number of poisoned neurons, which results in a higher ASR value. DBD and D-ST propose
421   mitigating backdoors through SSL, using self-supervised feature extraction to obtain clean feature
422   extractors. In classification tasks using such a feature extractor, poisoned data often exhibits higher
423   losses compared to clean data. However, such a loss-based design performs poorly against clean label
424   attacks and feature-level attacks. In comparison, our H√∂lder Iteration Defense significantly reduces
425   ASR and increases RA, regardless of the type of trigger.

530
531
      We developed H√∂lder Pruning, a defense against backdoor attacks in DNNs. By leveraging clean
      feature extractors and the H√∂lder constant, our method enhanced pruning accuracy and model
532
      robustness. Extensive experiments across three datasets (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, GTSRB) and against
533
      nine backdoor attacks (BadNets, LC, SIG, LF, WaNet, Input-Aware, SSBA, Trojan, BppAttack)
534   demonstrated superiority of H√∂lder Pruning over eight SOTA defenses (FP, ANP, CLP, FMP, ABL,
535   DBD, D-ST). When a clean feature extractor was unavailable, we introduced the H√∂lder Iteration
536   Defense, which used self-supervised and iterative semi-supervised learning to continually refine the
537   feature space. H√∂lder Pruning and H√∂lder Iteration Defense consistently yielded high classification
538   accuracy, high robust accuracy, and low attack success rates. Additionally, H√∂lder Pruning achieved
539   up to 1000x faster runtime compared to SOTA defenses. Even when clean feature extractors were not
      available, our approach was up to 10x faster than comparable methods.

       A.5 R ELATED W ORK
973
974    We present a summary of related work in this section. Many of the works listed below have been
975    described at appropriate sections in the main paper. Our objective in this section is to categorize
976    related work into (a) works introducing backdoor attacks and (b) those which focus on design of
977    defense strategies against backdoor attacks.
978    Backdoor attacks aim to mislead a DNN to exhibit abnormal behavior on samples with a trigger
979    while behaving normally on other samples Min et al. (2023). An adversary carrying out a backdoor
980    attack modifies a small fraction of training samples and assigns these samples to an (adversary-
981    desired) target label Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov (2021); Doan et al. (2021); Gu et al. (2019); Li et al.
982    (2021c); Souri et al. (2022). Backdoor attacks can be (i) patch-based Chen et al. (2017); Gu et al.
983    (2019); Turner et al. (2019), where the inserted trigger takes a form of a patch (e.g., white square in
984    BadNets Gu et al. (2019)), or (ii) non-patch-based, where the trigger exploits properties of the input
985    such as image-size Zhao et al. (2020) and uses techniques such as image warping Nguyen & Tran
986
       (2021) or steganography Tancik et al. (2020) to imperceptibly deform the image.
987    Backdoor defense mechanisms can be categorized broadly into (i) In-training or (ii) Post-training
988    methods. In-training defenses assume that the defender has access to a subset of poisoned data for
989    model training, and subsequently leverage differences in observed behaviors (e.g., magnitudes of
990    loss functions) associated with poisoned and clean samples to mitigate effects of backdoor attacks
991    Gao et al. (2023); Li et al. (2021b); Zhang et al. (2023). Post-training defenses, on the other hand,
992
       assume access only to a possibly backdoored DNN model, and generally requires access to additional
       clean samples to mitigate a backdoor attack Zhu et al. (2023b). Examples of post-training defense
993
       methods includes pruning Zhu et al. (2023a), fine-tuning Kumar et al. (2022), and toxin suppression
994
       Zeng et al. (2021). We explore efficacy of our H√∂lder Pruning approach, which is applicable during
995    both the in-training and post-training phases. We compare our method with state-of-the-art defenses
996    in both in-training and post-processing stages to demonstrate its versatility and effectiveness.

       consider.

1439   Pruning Defense on classifier:
1440
             ‚ÄĘ Fine-Pruning (Liu et al., 2018a): Forward propagation is performed on all of the training
1441
               data D, and the activation values of neurons in the classifier are recorded and accumulated.
1442
               Neurons are then sorted based on these activation values, and 1% of neurons or weights
1443           with the lowest activation values are pruned each time. The pruned model is tested to
1444           ensure that the overall accuracy does not drop more than 10%. If the accuracy drop exceeds
1445           10%, pruning is stopped. The steps of sorting, pruning, testing, and accuracy checking are
1446           repeated until the pruning target or the upper limit of accuracy drop is reached.
1447         ‚ÄĘ CLP (Zheng et al., 2022): In the CLP pruning method, evaluate the Lipschitz Constant on
1448           each neruons within a classifier. The pruning process involves calculating the mean and std
1449           of each Lipschitz Constant inside the neruons, pruning the neurons which has the Lipschitz
1450           Constant larger than mean + std.
1451
             ‚ÄĘ ANP (Wu & Wang, 2021): In the ANP defense process, random perturbations epsilon are
1452           first generated in the range of [-0.4, 0.4] and applied to the weights and biases of neurons.
1453           These perturbations are optimized using Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) to maximize
1454           the classification loss, repeating this process once. Then, the mask values are updated by
1455           calculating a loss function that includes the losses of clean and adversarially perturbed
1456           data, with a weight of 0.2. The mask values indicate which neurons are most sensitive to
1457           adversarial perturbations. The training progress is recorded every 500 iterations until 2000
               iterations are completed. Finally, neurons sensitive to adversarial perturbations are pruned

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
               step-by-step using a threshold of 0.90 and a step size of 0.05, reducing the success rate of
1459           backdoor attacks while maintaining accuracy on clean data.
1460
             ‚ÄĘ FMP (Huang & Bu, 2024): The FMP (Adversarial Feature Map Pruning) defense process
1461
               involves several steps: first, generating potential poisoned samples through Feature Reverse
1462
               Generation (FRG), which includes initializing the input with random noise, calculating the
1463           loss based on the classifier hidden layer output, updating the input according to the gradient
1464           of the loss, and ensuring the perturbed input remains within valid bounds. Then, feeding
1465           these generated poisoned samples into the model to observe the inference accuracy of each
1466           classifier hidden layer and identifying backdoor-related feature maps through significant
1467           changes in accuracy. Next, pruning these identified backdoor neurons inside the classifier by
1468           setting their weights to zero during the forward pass.
1469
1470
       Data free defense:
1471         ‚ÄĘ ABL (Li et al., 2021b): In the ABL backdoor defense process, the model is first pre-trained
1472           on the backdoored dataset for 20 epochs using a Flooding loss function (flooding: 0.5).
1473           Based on these loss values, 1% of the suspicious data (isolation_ratio: 0.01) is isolated, and
1474           the model is further trained on the remaining clean data for 60 epochs. Finally, the model
1475           undergoes 20 epochs of unlearning (unlearning_epochs: 20) to forget the backdoor patterns
1476           learned from the isolated malicious data.
1477         ‚ÄĘ CLP (Zheng et al., 2022): In the CLP pruning method, the Channel Lipschitz Constant
1478           (CLC) is used to assess and adjust the sensitivity of each channel within a neural network.
1479           The pruning process involves calculating the CLC for each channel, and applying pruning if
1480           the CLC exceeds the mean plus a multiple (u) of the standard deviation. The intensity of
1481           pruning is controlled by the parameter u, which is set to 3. Additionally, the configuration
1482           parameters umin : 0, umax : 10, and unum : 20 allow for testing 21 different values of u
1483
               evenly distributed from 0 to 10. This helps in evaluating the impact of different pruning
               intensities on model performance to optimize defense effectiveness while maintaining clean
1484
               model accuracy.
1485
1486         ‚ÄĘ DBD (Huang et al., 2022): In the DBD defense strategy, self-supervised learning for 100
1487
               epochs is the initial phase where the model learns intrinsic features of the data to create
               feature extractors without relying on labels, and the ‚Äėtemperature=1‚Äė parameter controls
1488
               the sensitivity of learning. The warmup period for 10 epochs prepares the model for more
1489
               complex learning tasks. The subsequent semi-supervised learning phase trains the model
1490           using both labeled and unlabeled data, employing a confidence threshold set at 0.5 to
1491           distinguish between reliable and unreliable data, further optimizing model performance.
1492
             ‚ÄĘ D-ST (Chen et al., 2022): First, train a backdoored model from scratch using a poisoned
1493
               dataset without any data augmentation. Then, fine-tune the backdoored model with intra-
1494           class loss L_intra with the same poisoned dataset. Next, compute the Feature Consistency
1495           towards Transformations (FCT) metric for all training samples, calculate thresholds based on
1496           the FCT values, and separate the samples into clean (bottom 20%), poisoned (top 5%), and
1497           uncertain samples. After that, train a feature extractor using the semi-supervised contrastive
1498           learning (SS-CTL) method with 200 epochs using the clean samples and train a classifier by
1499           minimizing the mixed cross-entropy loss with 10 epochs using all samples. Finally, combine
1500           the model with the feature extractor and classifier.
1501
       Additional defense:
1502
