009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce ImmersePro, an innovative framework specifically designed to trans-
012            form single-view videos into stereo videos. This framework utilizes a novel dual-
013            branch architecture comprising a disparity branch and a context branch on video
014            data by leveraging spatial-temporal attention mechanisms. ImmersePro employs
015            implicit disparity guidance, enabling the generation of stereo pairs from video se-
016
               quences without the need for explicit disparity maps, thus reducing potential errors
               associated with disparity estimation models. In addition to the technical advance-
017
               ments, we introduce the YouTube-SBS dataset, a comprehensive collection of 423
018
               stereo videos sourced from YouTube. This dataset is unprecedented in its scale,
019            featuring over 7 million stereo pairs, and is designed to facilitate training and
020            benchmarking of stereo video generation models. Our experiments demonstrate
021            the effectiveness of ImmersePro in producing high-quality stereo videos, offering
022            significant improvements over existing methods. Compared to the best competitor
023            stereo-from-mono we quantitatively improve the results by 11.76% (L1), 6.39%
024            (SSIM), and 5.10% (PSNR).

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      A stereo movie, also known as a 3D movie, provides three-dimensional visual effects by employing
029
      stereoscopic techniques. By capturing or creating separate views for the left and right eyes, a 3D
030
      immersive experience can be achieved by using dedicated hardware such as head-mounted displays
031   or autostereoscopic displays. The disparity between the two views perceived by the viewer’s brain
032   creates the illusion of depth, making the objects in the movie appear at varying distances, thereby
033   enhancing the immersive experience of the film. Shooting stereo movies in the film industry often
034   involves high costs due to the need for specialized equipment and meticulous post-production pro-
035   cesses. Alternatively, the stereoscopic effect can be created through a post-production process for
036   videos that are shot with monocular cameras. This post-production process uses stereo conversion,
037   which adds the binocular disparity depth cue to digital images. It requires significant manual work
038   by artists since inaccurate depth mapping and misrepresentations of occluded areas can cause visual
039
      discomfort Devernay & Beardsley (2010). In this paper, we propose an automated system that can
      reduce the time and expense associated with the conversion process, making it more accessible and
040
      economically feasible for more films.
041
042   Traditional stereo conversion involves creating disparity maps from single images or sequences and
043   then using them to generate the corresponding stereo pair for the other eye, creating the illusion
044   of depth for stereoscopic viewing. Recently, many deep learning-based methods (Xie et al., 2016;
045
      Wang et al., 2019a; Shih et al., 2020; Watson et al., 2020; Ranftl et al., 2022) are primarily pro-
      posed for image-based stereo conversions, aiming to improve disparities and enhance inpainting
046
      effectiveness on occluded areas. Unlike image data, video data provides additional temporal infor-
047
      mation, which can yield more detailed disparities and occlusion insights by leveraging information
048   across frames. To handle video inputs, Chen et al. (2019) synthesizes right-view video sequences
049   by estimating a displacement map to move each pixel to a new location, with a 3D DenseNet. Tem-
050   poral3D (Zhang & Wang, 2022) compromises to use three adjacent left-view frames to predict the
051   single right-view of the middle frame. Based on our analysis, current stereo conversion frameworks
052   for video sequences are not robust and have several drawbacks. We believe the area is underex-
053   plored and there is a large room for improvement. At the same time, we believe the topic will gain
      in importance due to recent efforts to manufacture stereo displays, e.g., from Apple and Magic Leap.

      We introduce ImmersePro, a novel approach designed specifically for video stereo conversion that
085   utilizes the contextual information available across video frames to enhance stereo disparity consis-
086   tency across the temporal dimension. For doing so, we collectively build a large-scale stereo movie
087   dataset, Youtube-SBS, with over 7 million stereo pairs from a collection of stereo movies, game
088   films, and music videos. Due to the absence of ground truth disparities, we propose to use implicit
089   disparities to guide the generation of layered disparities, which outperforms the explicit disparity
090   guidance (e.g. a depth estimation model) that was commonly used in previous work. We propose to
091   use a layered disparity representation that refers to a stack of disparity maps corresponding to one
092   image. Each pixel that appears in the image can be reused multiple times, avoiding creating black
093
      holes after the warping operation. This approach ensures that the generated stereo parts strictly ad-
      here to the semantics of the input video, minimizing the need for improvisation and thus preserving
094
      the original narrative and visual intent. As a result, ImmersePro not only maintains the semantic
095
      integrity of the original video but also intelligently infers the geometry of occluded areas, enabling
096   consistent right-view generation. As shown in Figure 1, previous methods may generate artifacts
097   such as texture misalignment or object deformation, whereas our ImmersePro can keep the semantic
098   integrity from the left-view image. Our main contributions are as follows:

114   2.1   S TEREO C ONVERSION M ETHODS
115
116   Image-Based Stereo Conversion. Deep3D (Xie et al., 2016) relaxes the disparity map into a multi-
117   layer probabilistic map and then multiplies it with several horizontally shifted copies of the input
118
      image, which relaxes the non-differentiable warping operation. Watson et al. (2020) used a warping-
      and-inpainting framework, which creates stereo training pairs from single RGB images to improve
119
      the modern monocular depth estimators. However, a non-differentiable strategy is used and the
120
      inpainting randomly selects the texture from the training set. Apart from using pretrained depth
121   estimation models, Wang et al. (2019a); Ranftl et al. (2022) use FlowNet2.0 (Ilg et al., 2017) to
122   estimate optical flows as ground truth disparities. StereoDiffusion (Wang et al., 2024) proposes a
123   training-free approach to generate stereo pairs by directly warping the latent space of diffusion mod-
124   els. It requires inversion methods to produce the latents to generate the stereo pair of a given image.
125   The fine details of the resulting photo may vary due to the direct modification of the latent space.
126   Shih et al. (2020) proposed a layered depth inpainting method that generates a 3D representation by
127   intelligently estimating and filling depth information, particularly in areas where it is missing or un-
128   certain. Our work does not rely on explicit disparity computation, with the additional consideration
129
      of the context within video frames.
130   Video-Based Stereo Conversion. Chen et al. (2019) adopts a reconstruction-based approach by
131   using a 3D DenseNet to estimate the disparity map of an input sequence. Temporal3D (Zhang
132   & Wang, 2022) estimates the middle frame using three adjacent frames, with the output being a
133   weighted sum of three disparity-warped images. Additionally, methods such as NVDS (Wang et al.,
134   2023) may be adopted for consistent depth estimations across video frames. However, those methods
135
      assume the pixels within the left image are adequate for the right image. Mehl et al. (2024) adopted
      the warping-inpainting approach with a pretrained depth estimation method (i.e. MiDaS (Birkl et al.,
136
      2023)) for warping and inpainting with multiple adjacent frames. Still, this method relies on a single
137
      frame depth estimation model that can likely break the temporal consistency between frames. In
138   this work, we propose an end-to-end video stereo conversion method based on implicit disparity
139   guidance across the temporal dimension.
140
141
142   2.2   S TEREO DATASETS
143
144   There are limited resources on video-based stereo datasets. Sintel (Butler et al., 2012) contains 1064
145   synthetic stereo images with accurate disparities. KITTI (Menze & Geiger, 2015) offers 8.4K frames
146   captured from the real world for autonomous driving. Wang et al. (2019a) introduces a WSVD dataset
147   and proposes to use optical flow as disparities as ground truth for supervision. Similarly, Ranftl
148   et al. (2022) collected a private 3D movie dataset and extracted ground truth disparities by estimated
149   optical flows to improve depth estimation. Since different levels of stereoscopic effects may exist
150   for different purposes of a dataset, a movie-specific benchmark dataset is preferable. Ranftl et al.
151
      (2022) is the only relevant dataset but it is built on top of real movies with intellectual property right
      issues. Therefore, we propose a benchmark stereo dataset that contains publicly available content.

169   reality videos and gameplay videos (that contain user interfaces). To ensure accessibility for future
170   research, we select videos that (1) have existed for at least one year, and (2) from accounts that
171   have at least 500 followers. This curated selection includes 423 videos at a standard resolution of

      1920x1080. During the frame extraction, as some videos include a non-stereo intro section, we skip

174   To measure the general stereo effects of our dataset, we propose to compute a metric that evaluates
175   the left-right consistency of the disparity. For a stereo pair with subtle stereo effects, the disparity
176   maps for the left and right images should be almost symmetrical with one another. That is, a point
177   in the left image should have a corresponding point in the right image at the same row but shifted
178
      horizontally according to the disparity. For large stereo effects there is an increasing number of

179
      from the left image with simple warping (and vice versa). To compute our metric, we use the

375   The implicit disparity is a summation-based approach that computes pixel colors as a blend of other
376   pixel colors, weighted by the estimated probabilities. This may produce good results with a cor-
377   rect estimation, but it may introduce artifacts such as blurring if the estimation is inaccurate. The
      final output visually improves if each pixel location is selected from a set of candidate disparity

398   4.5   C ONTEXT F USION
399
400
      The final stage of our network focuses on enriching semantic details while maintaining the learned
      right-view structure. The context fusion module integrates semantic and disparity features from a
401
      video sequence by concatenating the encoder feature map with layered disparity features to form
402
      a fused representation. These fused features are then processed through spatial-temporal attention
403   (section 4.2), enabling global context awareness. We apply spatial-temporal attention modules at
404   1/2 the original resolution, as mentioned in section 4.1. To retain structural integrity, a residual
405   connection reintroduces the refined transformer output into the original fused feature map. We then
406   apply a deconvolution to obtain a texture map in the original resolution, then enrich the texture map
407   by three Conv-ReLU blocks. Next, the module supplements the layered disparity-warped images
408   from section 4.4 with the enriched feature map. To be specific, a median blur with 3×3 kernels is first
409   applied to the warped images to reduce noise and improve local smoothness before concatenating
410   them with the enriched feature map. A semantic residual is then derived by passing this combined
411
      map through three Conv-ReLU blocks. The final output is produced by combining the blurred image
      with the semantic residual. This approach ensures that the final result maintains sharp textures while
412
      preserving structural consistency, achieving a balance between local detail and global coherence.

415   5     R ESULTS
416
417   We implement our method using Pytorch and train on four NVIDIA A100 (80G) GPUs for 50,000
418   iterations (approx. 3 days). Models are trained for 40,000 iterations for our ablations. At training
419   time, we first resize the input sequence to 422 × 422 and then randomly crop the resized video
420   sequence to 384 × 384. Each input sequence contains 8 frames. We use L1 loss during training
421
      to encourage an accurate reconstruction of the right-view images using both implicit and layered
      disparities. In addition, an LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) loss is used for better reconstruction results.
422

474   6   D ISCUSSION A ND L IMITATION
475
476   To enhance the viewing experience, films sometimes employ a stronger stereoscopic effect at the
477   start and end, while moderating it in the middle to ensure viewer comfort Neuman (2009); Ranftl
478   et al. (2022). Thus, the stereo parameters such as focal length, are hard to retrieve even for the
479   same film. Theoretically, the precise reproduction of the right view is impossible without knowing
480   the stereo parameters in advance. By learning through a large-scale dataset, ImmersePro estimates
481   its average disparity, then tries to create an average-level stereo effect for input videos rather than
482
      reproduce the precise right pair. Therefore, as shown in Figure 5, our model may produce reasonable
      but “inaccurate” stereo effects if compared with the ground truth.
483
484   A reasonable stereo conversion pipeline involves a warping-and-inpainting process, where the in-

534   7   C ONCLUSION
535
536   This work presents an end-to-end video-based stereo conversion method that generates right-view
537   video sequences according to the input video. Our method automatically utilizes layered disparity
538   maps on top of implicit disparities. Additionally, we propose Youtube-SBS, a large-scale stereo
539   dataset that is publicly available for benchmarking purposes. Extensive qualitative and quantitative
      evaluations demonstrated the robustness of our approach against previous works.
