009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               This work studies the optimization and generalization consequences of a seemingly
012
               innocuous design choice in many modern architectures: they end with a compo-
013
               sition of affine parameters belonging to a normalization layer and a linear layer,
014            resulting in a fundamentally 2-homogeneous architecture. The first set of results are
015            abstract, showing how any architecture satisfying this type of 2-homogeneity and a
016            few regularity conditions on the gradients of the inner layers obtain large margins
017            and low test error. As technical byproducts, this part of the story provides an
018            implicitly biased gradient flow guarantee and also a nondecreasing margin lemma
019            for inhomogeneous networks. The second set of results instantiate this framework
020            for shallow normalized ReLU networks, establishing large margin and low test
021            error via feature selection purely from random initialization and standard gradient
022
               flow. As a corollary, the paper obtains good test error for k-bit parity problems, in
               particular passing below sample complexity lower bounds from linearized analyses
023
               such as the Neural Tangent Kernel.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The surprising beneficial properties of first-order optimization methods remain one of the main great
030   mysteries of deep learning: while a vast body of literature arose trying to understand how optimization
031   seems to find parameters which seemingly overfit but still generalize well (Neyshabur et al., 2014;
032   Zhang et al., 2016), practice has continued apace. For instance, recent work identifies even more
033
      sophisticated variants of the phenomenon in modern settings such as LLMs, where optimization
      automatically yields low-dimensional structures with many beneficial properties (Li et al., 2018;
034
      Aghajanyan et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2021).
035
036   For classification tasks where near-perfect test accuracy is possible, such as image classification
037   (Krizhevsky, 2009), a classical mathematical analysis comes via the concept of prediction margins,
038   where the differences between carefully normalized output logits directly yields an upper bound on
039
      generalization error (Bartlett, 1996; Schapire et al., 1997). While this classical theory is rooted in
      linear predictors and dates back to the original Perceptron analysis (Novikoff, 1962), it nevertheless
040
      has predictive power within modern deep learning; for example, it gives an explanation for test
041
      accuracy continuing to drop after perfect training accuracy is reached, a phenomenon which resurfaced
042   as part of the grokking story (Power et al., 2022).
043
044   Closer to the present work, a sensitive margin-based analysis can illuminate feature learning and its
045
      consequences on generalization error. One concrete approach of this flavor was nicely laid out by
      Bach (2017), who gave two function classes implicitly searched during deep network optimization:
046
      the simpler class is F2 , which roughly speaking corresponds to a lack of feature learning, such as
047
      exhibited by the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) (Jacot et al., 2018), and a more complex class F1 ,
048   which allows for feature learning, but seems generally computationally intractable to search over.
049   Concordantly, while it is reasonably easy to show that gradient descent can compete with the best
050   function in F2 (Ji & Telgarsky, 2020b; Chen et al., 2019), competing with the best functions in F1
051   has only been shown under a combination of modified algorithms and stringent assumptions which
052   are either hard-to-establish or even potentially false (Wei et al., 2018; Chizat & Bach, 2020). Even so,
053   probing how far gradient descent can venture into F1 \ F2 remains an effective way to study feature
      learning and the generalization benefits of first-order methods.

      The goal of the present work is to show how the normalization structure present in modern archi-
055   tectures can aid in feature learning and good test error, first presenting an abstract mathematical
056   framework in Section 3, and then specializing this framework to shallow normalized ReLU networks
057   in Section 4. A summary of the high-level contributions is as follows.
058
059     1. The first contribution is an elementary observation: Section 3.1 outlines how the aforementioned
060        product structure of modern normalized architectures aids in searching over F1 . Section 1.1
061        will also provide a teaser, but the phenomenon is simple enough: optimizing F1 corresponds to
062        identifying good but rare features, thus has a sparsity flavor, and meanwhile the product structure
063
           causes gradient descent to instead follow an `1 geometry, which is classically understood as
           sparsity-preferring.
064
065     2. The first substantial technical contribution comes in the remainder of Section 3 (also teased
066        in Section 1.1), showing two consequences of this product structure for abstract architectures:
067
           (a) how the implicit bias of gradient descent, combined with the product structure and a few
           conditions on the gradients of inner layers, gives rise to optimization over F1 , which previously
068
           was only establish with modified algorithms and extensive assumptions (Wei et al., 2018; Chizat
069
           & Bach, 2020); and (b) how the search over F1 does not degrade over time, extending the
070        seminal work of Lyu & Li (2019) to inhomogeneous networks such as transformers.
071
        3. The second contribution, appearing in full in Section 4 but again teased in Section 1.1, is a
072
           concrete instantiation of the previous framework for shallow normalized ReLU networks, in
073
           particular using an idealization of BatchNorm (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015), extremely large width,
074        but no other assumptions or modifications. As shallow ReLU networks have been the subject of
075        great study, this analysis gives explicit test error bounds which are shown to beat test error lower
076        bounds for well-studied problems such as the k-parity problems; by contrast, prior work needs
077        a combination of algorithmic assumptions and distributional assumptions (Barak et al., 2022;
078        Glasgow, 2023).
079
080   The remainder of this introduction teases the results; Sections 3 and 4 contained the details and proof
081   sketches; the appendices contain full details.

125
