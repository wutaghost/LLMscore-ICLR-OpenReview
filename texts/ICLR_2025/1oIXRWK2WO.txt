009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Mixed-integer non-linear programs (MINLPs) arise in various domains, such as
012
               energy systems and transportation, but are notoriously difficult to solve. Recent
013
               advances in machine learning have led to remarkable successes in optimization
014            tasks, an area broadly known as learning to optimize. This approach includes us-
015            ing predictive models to generate solutions for optimization problems with contin-
016            uous decision variables, thereby avoiding the need for computationally expensive
017            optimization algorithms. However, applying learning to MINLPs remains chal-
018            lenging primarily due to the presence of integer decision variables, which compli-
019            cate gradient-based learning. To address this limitation, we propose two differen-
020            tiable correction layers that generate integer outputs while preserving gradient in-
021            formation. Combined with a soft penalty for constraint violation, our framework
022
               can tackle both the integrality and non-linear constraints in a MINLP. Experi-
               ments on three problem classes with convex/non-convex objective/constraints and
023
               integer/mixed-integer variables show that the proposed learning-based approach
024
               consistently produces high-quality solutions for parametric MINLPs extremely
025            quickly. As problem size increases, traditional exact solvers and heuristic meth-
026            ods struggle to find feasible solutions, whereas our approach continues to deliver
027            reliable results. Our work extends the scope of learning-to-optimize to MINLP,
028            paving the way for integrating integer constraints into deep learning models. Our
029            code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/L2O-MINLP/.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Mixed-integer optimization is fundamental to a broad spectrum of real-world applications spanning
036   problems in fields as diverse as pricing Kleinert et al. (2021), battery dispatch (Nazir & Almas-
037   salkhi, 2021), transportation (Schouwenaars et al., 2001), and optimal control (Marcucci & Tedrake,
038   2020). These problems involve discrete decisions, such as determining the number of items or the
039
      activation of generators, combined with complex non-linear system constraints. Mixed-integer lin-
      ear programming (MILP) has been widely adopted due to its well-established solution techniques.
040
      However, many practical problems exhibit non-linear relationships, leading to mixed-integer non-
041
      linear programs (MINLPs). Unlike MILPs, where techniques such as branch-and-bound (Land &
042   Doig, 2010), cutting planes (Gomory, 2010), and heuristics (Crama et al., 2005; Johnson & Mc-
043   Geoch, 1997) have matured, MINLPs require more complex approaches due to the combination
044   of discrete variables and non-convex constraints and objective function. Standard methods include
045   outer approximation (Fletcher & Leyffer, 1994), spatial branch-and-bound (Belotti et al., 2009), and
046   decomposition techniques (Nowak, 2005), but these often struggle to scale to large problems.
047
      Many applications demand that MINLPs be solved within a limited time budget, further compli-
048   cating the picture. To overcome this, learning-to-optimize (L2O) methods offer a promising alter-
049   native by leveraging machine learning (ML) to enhance or even replace conventional optimization
050   approaches. In particular, end-to-end optimization directly maps input instance parameters to solu-
051   tions of optimization problems through a trained model (Kotary et al., 2021b; Chen et al., 2022a).
052   By identifying patterns in a distribution of similar instances of the same optimization problem and
053   predicting solutions accordingly, end-to-end optimization can bypass traditional, computationally
      intensive optimization methods, enabling faster computation and improved scalability.

054
      Many real-world applications have stringent requirements on operational, physical, or safety con-
055   straints. Thus, recent research in machine learning has focused on the feasibility issue. While
056   various strategies exist, such as embedding hard constraints into neural network architectures (Hen-
057   driks et al., 2020), using penalty terms in loss functions for soft constraints (Pathak et al., 2015; Jia
058   et al., 2017), or projecting solutions onto feasible regions (Donti et al., 2021), these methods are not
059   directly applicable for problems that involve integer decisions.
060
      This work tackles, for the first time, the non-differentiability associated with predicting integer vari-
061   ables using a deep neural network, in conjunction with non-linear objective function and constraints.
062   This challenge has been underexplored in learning-based methods due to the absence of useful gradi-
063   ent information. To that end, we propose two differentiable correction layers for rounding, allowing
064   for gradient-based optimization of a neural network that generates high-quality integer solutions
065   while maintaining feasibility. Our contributions are as follows:
066
067         – We initiate the study of the learning-to-optimize problem in MINLP for the first time in the
068           literature, a paradigm that can enable efficient solution generation as problem parameters
069           vary.
070         – We develop differentiable correction layers that perform soft rounding of neural network
071           outputs into integer assignments to decision variables.
072
073
            – We adopt a self-supervised approach that requires no labeled data for training, making our
              method efficient and scalable to large problem instances.
074
075         – We evaluate our methods on diverse problem benchmarks and show that they find high-
076           quality solutions extremely fast even for large-scale instances where other methods fail.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
080
081   End-to-end optimization. End-to-end optimization focuses on training machine learning models
082   to predict the problem solutions, bypassing the need for computationally expensive solvers. One
083
      of the early approaches was proposed by Hopfield & Tank (1985), who used Hopfield networks to
      solve the traveling salesperson problem by incorporating a Lagrangian penalty for constraint feasi-
084
      bility. Similarly, Fioretto et al. (2020) applied the Lagrangian penalty in the context of continuous
085
      non-linear optimization for energy systems. In addition to penalty-based methods for ensuring feasi-
086   bility, Pan et al. (2020) embedded certain constraints directly into neural networks by leveraging the
087   range of output values and solving linear systems. Although these supervised learning methods sig-
088   nificantly reduce inference time, they typically require large offline datasets of solutions (Gleixner
089   et al., 2021; Kotary et al., 2021a), which can be impractical for large-scale problems where gener-
090   ating solutions is computationally expensive. This limitation highlights the need for self-supervised
091   learning approaches (Donti et al., 2021), which minimize both the objective function and constraint
092   violation from the predicted values, without relying on the imimitation of pre-solved solutions. Our
093   method first extends this self-supervised paradigm to problems involving discrete decision variables,
094
      further broadening its applicability to mixed-integer optimization.
095
096   Constrained neural architectures. Specific neural network architectures can be designed to im-
097   pose certain classes of hard constraints. For instance, Hendriks et al. (2020) incorporate linear
098   operator constraints directly into the model design. Vinyals et al. (2015) and Dai et al. (2017) lever-
099
      aged the inherent structure of graphs to construct feasible solutions for the traveling salesperson
      problem. Additionally, Kervadec et al. (2022) demonstrated that employing a log-barrier method
100
      for inequality constraints improves accuracy, constraint satisfaction, and training stability. Penalty
101
      methods (Pathak et al., 2015; Jia et al., 2017), which impose inequality constraints through regu-
102   larization terms in the loss function, have also gained popularity for constraining neural networks.
103   As noted by Márquez-Neila et al. (2017), in practice, methods that incorporate hard constraints
104   rarely outperform their soft constraint counterparts, despite the latter offering weaker theoretical
105   performance guarantees. Building on penalty methods, Donti et al. (2021) proposed a differentiable
106   correction approach to complete partial solutions for linear equations and project solutions onto the
107   feasible region. In this paper, we adopt a penalty method for handling constraints and introduce two
      novel differentiable rounding correction layers to guarantee the integrity of the solution.

      Learning for mixed-integer programming. There has been significant interest in using ML to
109   accelerate the solution of integer programs. The vast majority of the work in this space focuses on
110   learning search strategies for exact MILP solvers. This includes parameter tuning (Xu et al., 2011),
111   preprocessing (Berthold & Hendel, 2021), branching variable selection (Khalil et al., 2016; Alvarez
112   et al., 2017; Gasse et al., 2019; Zarpellon et al., 2021), node selection (He et al., 2014), heuristic
113   selection (Chmiela et al., 2021), and cut selection and generation (Deza & Khalil, 2023). Another
114   line of research in ML-for-MILP relates to learning to generate integer solutions heuristically (Nair
115   et al., 2020; Khalil et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2020; Sonnerat et al., 2021; Song et al., 2020; Bertsimas
116   & Stellato, 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Ye et al.). We refer to the surveys of Bengio et al. (2021)
117
      and Zhang et al. (2023) for more details. In contrast, there has been much less work on MINLP.
      Illustrative examples include the work of Cauligi et al. (2021) who proposed a two-stage algorithm
118
      for quickly finding high-quality solutions for mixed-integer convex programs (MICPs), Baltean-
119
      Lugojan et al. (2019) who use supervised learning to select cuts for quadratic optimization, Nowak
120   et al. (2018) who learn to solve quadratic assignment problems with graph networks, and Bonami
121   et al. (2022) who use a classifier to decide on the linearization of mixed-integer quadratic prob-
122   lems. Most relevant to our method is the recently proposed SurCO approach of Ferber et al. (2023).
123   They focus on mixed-integer problems with non-linear objective and linear constraints, learning to
124   approximate the former with a linear function for a simpler heuristic optimization. Our approach
125   differs from all of the above in its scope, addressing the most general class of MINLPs.
126
127   Differentiable optimization. A different category of methods integrates optimization solvers as
128   layers within deep neural network architectures (Agrawal et al., 2019). These methods can handle
129   various types of optimization problems, such as quadratic programs (Amos & Kolter, 2017; Samb-
130   harya et al., 2023), stochastic optimization (Donti et al., 2017), submodular optimization (Djolonga
131
      & Krause, 2017), and even integer linear programs (Wilder et al., 2019; Berthet et al., 2020; Pogančić
      et al., 2020). In these approaches, optimization algorithms or solvers are embedded within the neu-
132
      ral network, allowing gradients of optimization solvers to be computed and propagated during back-
133
      propagation. King et al. (2024) shows how differentiable optimization can enhance the convergence
134   of proximal operator algorithms via end-to-end learning of proximal metrics. However, as Tang &
135   Khalil (2024) noted, training with a differentiable optimizer requires iteratively solving optimization
136   throughout the training process, making the computational burden prohibitively expensive. In con-
137   trast, our self-supervised approach generates solutions directly through neural network structures,
138   eliminating the need to repeatedly call high-complexity solvers and thus significantly reducing com-
139   putational overhead.

161   solutions violate the constraints. Therefore, similarly to Donti et al. (2021), we incorporate penalty

      4   P RELIMINARIES : D IFFERENTIATING THROUGH D ISCRETE O PERATIONS
172
      Straight-through Estimator. The Straight-through Estimator (STE) (Bengio et al., 2013) is a sim-
173
      ple yet effective method for handling non-differentiable operations in neural networks. In our ap-
174   proaches, STE plays a crucial role in enabling backpropagation through discrete operations. During
175   the forward pass, STE applies a (non-differentiable) discrete operation, such as rounding a variable
176   up or down, binarizing it, or using an indicator function I(·). However, in the backward pass, STE
177   replaces the non-existent gradient of these discrete functions with soft approximations. For round-
178   ing operations, the gradient of the identity function is used during backpropagation, whereas for
179   binarization or indicator functions, the gradient of the Sigmoid function is applied.
180
181   Gumbel-Sigmoid Noise. Although the STE is effective for backpropagating through discrete de-
182   cisions, it lacks the stochasticity that can improve model training. This is where the Gumbel-noise
183
      method (Jang et al., 2016) comes into play. Specifically, Gumbel noise perturbs the logits before
      applying the Sigmoid function, allowing for randomness in the binary decisions. After this, a hard
184
      binarization step is applied using the STE, ensuring that the final outputs are discrete binary values
185
      while retaining gradients for backpropagation. Further technical details can be found in Appendix A.

200   Our learnable correction layers, Rounding Classification (RC) and Learnable Threshold (LT), are
201   designed to handle the integrality constraints of MINLPs. We decompose the mapping ψΘ : Rnξ 7→
202   Rnr × Znz from an instance parameter vector to a candidate mixed-integer solution into two steps:
203
204         1. The first step consists in applying a learnable relaxed solution mapping πΘ1 : Rnξ 7→
205            Rnr +nz encoded by a deep neural network with weights Θ1 . It outputs a continuously
206
               relaxed solution x̄i ∈ Rnr +nz without enforcing the integrality requirement. Note that
               continuous variables are also predicted in this first step.
207
208         2. The second step is a differentiable correction layer φΘ2 : Rnr +nz × Rnξ 7→ Rnr × Znz that
209

316
      the objective value over time, but this often comes at a high computational cost. For more complex

      to Appendix G. The RC and LT methods exhibit robust performance across the board, achieving
370
      objective values second only to EX while consistently maintaining low percentages of infeasible
371
      solutions and fast solution times across all problem sizes. These methods achieve several orders
372   of magnitude speed-ups, scaling effectively even for large instances up to 1000 × 1000. The exact
373   solver EX, while performing well on smaller problem sizes, fails to produce any solutions for in-
374   stances of size 200 × 200 and larger within the 1000-second time limit, highlighting its limitations
375   when handling more complex problems. N1, on the other hand, can find feasible solutions within a
376   short time frame for smaller cases but suffers from severe numerical instability as the problem size
377   increases. When scaled to 200 × 200, N1 also fails to produce a solution. The RR method, which
      relies on rounding relaxations, encounters significant feasibility challenges. Overall, this analysis

516   7     C ONCLUSION
517
518
519
      We have introduced a new learning-based heuristic method for MINLP. Our approach includes two
      novel correction layers—rounding classification and learnable threshold—that enable neural net-
520
      works to generate high-quality integer solutions while preserving gradient information for training
521
      through backpropagation. These layers allow us to tackle optimization tasks with discrete vari-
522   ables and non-linear constraints in a way that is scalable and computationally efficient. As a self-
523   supervised approach, our method does not require collecting optimal solutions as labels, significantly
524   reducing the time and effort typically needed for data collection.
525
      Our experiments demonstrate that our learning-based methods outperform traditional solvers and
526
      other heuristics across various problem types, including convex quadratic, non-convex, and high-
527
      dimensional mixed-integer optimization problems. Despite the increasing complexity of these tasks,
528   our methods maintain strong performance in terms of both feasibility and solution quality, particu-
529   larly in high-dimensional settings where traditional approaches often fail to produce solutions within
530   a reasonable time due to the curse of dimensionality. To our knowledge, our work is the first to tackle
531   learning for parametric MINLPs in full generality.
532
      Our method enables efficient heuristic solutions for large-scale parametric MINLPs, achieving better
533
      performance and computational efficiency, though feasibility is not guaranteed. Future work could
534   explore improving feasibility through alternative constraint-handling techniques or post-processing.
535   For certain problem classes, a subset of constraints could be relaxed into the loss function while
536   directly optimizing over the rest using differentiable optimization layers Agrawal et al. (2019). Ad-
537   ditionally, redesigning neural network architectures to handle varying instance parameters and deci-
538   sion variables is a promising direction, leveraging set-based, permutation-equivariant architectures
539   such as graph neural networks Cappart et al. (2023); Dumouchelle et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2022b;
      2024).

      A    G UMBEL -S IGMOID T RICK
811
812
      The Gumbel-Sigmoid trick is a stochastic approximation method that enables gradient-based opti-
813
      mization for discrete variables. It introduces controlled random perturbations to the logits h, pro-
814   ducing a continuous relaxation of the binary decision. The method relies on noise sampled from the
815   Gumbel distribution, which is defined as:
816
817                              g = − log(− log(U )),       U ∼ Uniform(0, 1).
818   where U is a random variable drawn from the uniform distribution over the interval [0, 1]. This noise
819   g is added to the logits to introduce randomness while preserving differentiability.
820
821
      The Gumbel-Sigmoid function provides a soft, differentiable approximation of binary outputs as:
822                                                               1
823                             Gumbel-Sigmoid(h) =
                                                          1 + exp − h+gτ1 −g2

824
825   where g1 and g2 are independent samples from the Gumbel distribution, and τ > 0 is the temperature
826   parameter that controls the smoothness of the approximation. For large values of τ , the output of the
827   Gumbel-Sigmoid function is smooth and probabilistic, resembling a sigmoid function. As tau → 0,
828   the output approaches a hard binary decision, mimicking the behavior of a step function.
829   In optimization contexts, this property allows the Gumbel-Sigmoid trick to approximate discrete
830   variables while maintaining differentiability, facilitating gradient-based methods. The noise intro-
831   duced by g1 and g2 promotes exploration, potentially helping to escape poor local minima during
832   training—an issue that is particularly common in discrete optimization problems.
833   In our experiments, we set τ = 1 for simplicity. This setting has been empirically shown to per-
834   form well across various learning-to-optimize tasks but can be further tuned to balance the trade-off
835   between exploration and exploitation.

840   The following subsections describe two distinct approaches for designing the correction layer φΘ2 ;
841
      the same network πΘ1 is used in both approaches.
842
843
      Rounding Classification. Line 6 of Algorithm 1 is the key step in the rounding classification
      (RC) approach. For the integer variables, RC applies a stochastic soft-rounding to the output hiz
844
      of the neural network δΘ2 (x̄i , ξ i ), yielding bi ∈ {0, 1}nz . An entry of bi determines whether the
845
      continuously relaxed value x̄iz of the corresponding variable is rounded down or up. In the backward
846   pass, STE is used in line 4 for the rounding down operation. In line 6, the derivative of the Sigmoid
847   function is used.
848
849   Learnable Threshold. The key steps of the learnable threshold (LT) approach are described in
850   lines 8 and 9 of Algorithm 1. Rather than use Gumbel-Sigmoid for the rounding as in RC, LT
851   learns a vector of per-variable rounding thresholds, vi ∈ [0, 1]nz , that the Sigmoid generates in line
852   8 of Algorithm 1. A variable is rounded up if the fractional part of its relaxed value, (x̄iz − x̂iz ),
853   exceeds the threshold. The indicator function I(·) in line 9 produces a binary output in the forward
854   pass. In the backward pass, the gradient is approximated by that of the Sigmoid function with a
855   slope:
856                                                        1
                                    bi ←                                       .
857                                       1 + exp − 10 · (x̄iz − x̂iz − vi )
858   Here, the slope is set to 10 to sharpen the Sigmoid function.

894   D    N EURAL N ETWORK S TRUCTURE AND H YPERPARAMETERS
895
896   The solution mapping πΘ1 used across all learning-based methods—RC, LT, and RL—consists of
897   five fully connected layers with ReLU activations. The rounding correction network φΘ2 for RC
898   and LT is composed of four fully connected layers, also with ReLU activations, and incorporates
899   Batch Normalization and Dropout with a rate of 0.2 to prevent overfitting.
900   The hidden layer sizes were adjusted based on the problem size. For the convex quadratic and
901   simple non-convex problems, the hidden layer width used in the learning-based methods was scaled
902   accordingly, increasing from 16, 32, 64 up to 1024 for the corresponding problem sizes. Smaller
903   problems, such as 5×5, used smaller hidden layers 16, while larger problems, such as 500×500, used
904   hidden layers with widths up to 1024 to accommodate the complexity. Similarly, for the Rosenbrock
905   problem, the hidden layer width was scaled based on the number of variables: a width of 4 was used
906   for problems with 2 variables, 16 for problems with 20 variables, and up to 1024 for problems with
907   10, 000 variables.
908   The constraint penalty weight λ was set to 300 for benchmark problems. All networks were trained
909   using the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 10−3 and a batch size of 64 over 200 epochs.
910   Early stopping was applied based on validation performance to ensure convergence without overfit-
911   ting.

954   F   A BLATION S TUDY
955
956   Overview. To better understand the contribution of the correction layers φΘ2 , we include two
957   ablation baselines in our experiments:
958
959         • Rounding after Learning (RL): This baseline trains only the first neural network πΘ1 ,
960           which predicts relaxed solutions. Rounding to the nearest integer is applied post-training,
961           meaning that the rounding step does not participate in the training process. This isolates
962
              the effect of excluding the corrective adjustments provided by φΘ2 . This direct rounding
              can lead to significant deviations in the objective value and feasibility violations, under-
963
              scoring the importance of end-to-end learning where updates are guided by the ultimate
964
              loss function.
965
966         • Rounding with STE (RS): In the Algorithm 2, continuous values predicted by πΘ1 are
967
              rounded during training using the Straight-Through Estimator (STE), allowing gradients to
              pass through the rounding operator. While this mechanism applies a correction to produce
968
              integer values by rounding to the nearest integer, it is not learnable and does not adjust the
969
              rounding based on the parameter or the relaxation output. Thus, the correction is fixed and
970           solely determined by the nearest-integer rounding, without leveraging additional learning
971           for refinement.

1110   Dataset. For our experiments involving mixed-integer linear programs (MILPs), we utilized the
1111   ‘Obj Series 1‘ dataset from the MIP Workshop 2023 Computational Competition (Bolusani et al.,
1112   2023). This dataset comprises 50 related MILP instances derived from a common mathematical for-
1113   mulation, where the instances differ in a subset of the objective function coefficients. Each instance
1114   contains 360 binary variables and 55 constraints, with 120 out of the 360 objective coefficients
1115   varying across instances. All other components of the problem remain consistent.
1116
1117   Model Configuration. The neural network architecture and hyperparameters were consistent with
1118   those used for other experiments in the main paper. Specifically for the MILP problem in this study,
1119   the input dimension of the neural network was set to 120, corresponding to the number of varying
1120   objective function coefficients, and the output dimension was set to 360, representing the binary
1121
       decision variables. The hidden layer consisted of 256 neurons.
