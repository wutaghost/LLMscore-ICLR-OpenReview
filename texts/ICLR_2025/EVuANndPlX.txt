009                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011            Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) in Knowledge Graph Question Answering
012            (KGQA) enriches the context of Large Language Models (LLMs) with retrieved
013            KG information based on the question. However, KGs contain complex graph
014            information and existing KG retrieval methods are challenged when questions
015            require multi-hop information. To improve RAG in complex KGQA, we introduce
016            the G NN -R AG framework, which leverages Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for
017            effective graph reasoning and retrieval. G NN -R AG consists of a graph neural phase,
018
               where the GNN retriever learns to identify useful graph information for KGQA,
               e.g., when tackling complex questions. At inference time, the GNN scores answer
019
               candidates for the given question and the shortest paths in the KG that connect ques-
020
               tion entities and answer candidates are retrieved to represent KG reasoning paths.
021            The paths are verbalized and given as context to the downstream LLM for ultimate
022            KGQA; G NN -R AG can be seamlessly integrated with different LLMs for RAG.
023            Experimental results show that G NN -R AG achieves state-of-the-art performance
024            in two widely used KGQA benchmarks (WebQSP and CWQ), outperforming or
025            matching GPT-4 performance with a 7B tuned LLM. In addition, G NN -R AG ex-
026            cels on multi-hop and multi-entity questions outperforming competing approaches
027            by 8.9–15.5% points at answer F1. Furthermore, we show the effectiveness of
028            G NN -R AG in retrieval augmentation, which further boosts KGQA performance.

042   1   I NTRODUCTION
043
044   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Bommasani et al., 2021; Chowdhery et al.,
045   2023) are the state-of-the-art models in many NLP tasks due to their remarkable ability to understand
046   natural language. LLM power stems from pretraining on large corpora of textual data to obtain general
047   human knowledge (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022). However, because pretraining is
048   costly and time-consuming (Gururangan et al., 2020), LLMs cannot easily adapt to new or in-domain
049   knowledge and are prone to hallucinations (Zhang et al., 2023b).
050   Knowledge Graphs (KGs) (Vrandečić & Krötzsch, 2014) are databases that store information in
051   structured form that can be easily updated. KGs represent human-crafted factual knowledge in the
052   form of triplets (head, relation, tail), e.g., <Jamaica → language_spoken → English>,
053   which collectively form a graph. In the case of KGs, the stored knowledge is updated by fact addition
      or removal. As KGs capture complex interactions between the stored entities, e.g., multi-hop relations,

      they are widely used for knowledge-intensive task, such as Question Answering (QA) (Pan et al.,
055   2024).

057   Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is a framework that alleviates LLM hallucinations
058
      by enriching the input context with up-to-date and accurate context (Lewis et al., 2020),
      e.g., documents retrieved by a text knowledge base (KB) or facts retrieved from a KG;
059
      see Figure 1. In the KGQA task, the goal is to answer natural questions grounding the
060
      reasoning to the information provided by the KG. For instance, the context for RAG be-
061   comes “Knowledge: Jamaica → language_spoken → English \n Question:
062   Which language do Jamaican people speak?”, where the LLM has access to KG in-
063   formation for answering the question.
064
      RAG’s performance highly depends on the KG facts that are retrieved (Wu et al., 2023) and the
065
      challenge in KGQA is that KGs store complex graph information (they usually consist of millions
066
      of facts). Retrieving the right information requires effective graph understanding, while retrieving
067   irrelevant information may confuse the LLM during KGQA reasoning (Wu et al., 2023). Existing
068   retrieval methods that rely on off-the-shelf NLP retrievers (Baek et al., 2023) or classical graph
069   algorithms (He et al., 2024) are limited as retrieval is not tailored for KGQA. On the other hand, graph
070   retrieval powered by LLMs, such translating the question to relation paths (Luo et al., 2024) and
071   traversing the KG guided by LLMs (Sun et al., 2024), is more effective but with certain challenges.
072   Question translation depends on the LLM generating executable graph queries, while LLM-guided
073   KG traversal requires a large number of LLM calls, which is limiting in production cases.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      KGQA Methods. KGQA methods fall into two categories (Lan et al., 2022): (i) semantic parsing
111
      (SP) methods and (ii) information retrieval (IR) methods. SP methods (Sun et al., 2020; Lan &
112   Jiang, 2020; Ye et al., 2022) learn to transform the given question into a query of logical form,
113   e.g., SPARQL query. The transformed query is then executed over the KG to obtain the answers.
114   However, SP methods require ground-truth logical queries for training, which are time-consuming to
115   annotate in practice, and may lead non-executable queries due to syntactical or semantic errors (Das
116   et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022). IR methods (Sun et al., 2018; 2019; Zhang et al., 2022b) focus on
117   the weakly-supervised KGQA setting, where only question-answer pairs are given for training. IR
118   methods retrieve KG information, e.g., a KG subgraph (Zhang et al., 2022a), which is used as input
119   during KGQA reasoning. G NN -R AG falls in the IR category.
120   GNNs & LMs. Combining GNNs with LMs has been the subject of a substantial body of existing
121   literature (Jin et al., 2023), with various applications ranging from QA (Yasunaga et al., 2021; Wang
122   et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022c; Tian et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a) to training
123   LMs on graphs (Zhao et al., 2022; Yasunaga et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024). Such approaches
124   seek to combine the natural language and graph reasoning into a single model by fusing latent
125   GNN information with the LM. However, due to the modality mismatch of GNNs and LMs, fusing
126
      graph and natural language information is challenging for many knowledge-intensive tasks, even in
      supervised settings (Mavromatis et al., 2024). To alleviate this challenge, G NN -R AG divides KGQA
127
      in two stages. The GNN first retrieves useful information from the graph modality, which is then
128
      converted into natural language for effective LLM reasoning.
129
130   GraphRAG. GraphRAG usually refers to the general approach of inserting verbalized graph in-
131   formation at the context of LLMs (Peng et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024) or leveraging additional
132
      graph information when retrieving context for RAG (Edge et al., 2024; Gutiérrez et al., 2024). For
      instance, verbalizing graph information obtained by KGs has been widely applied in GraphRAG (Xie
133
      et al., 2022; Baek et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023a; Jin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). However,
134
      GraphRAG performance downgrades when the graph information retrieved is noisy and irrelevant to
135   the question (Wu et al., 2023; He et al., 2024). To improve retrieval in KGQA, G NN -R AG employs
136   a graph neural framework, which tailors graph retrieval for the KG at hand. By optimizing GNNs
137   to identify the right graph information for answering the questions, G NN -R AG achieves superior
138   retrieval performance compared to existing approaches in KGQA.

      KGQA. We are given a KG G that contains facts represented as (v, r, v ′ ), where v denotes the head
      entity, v ′ denotes the tail entity, and r is the corresponding relation between the two entities. Given
144
      G and a natural language question q, the task of KGQA is to extract a set of entities {aq } ∈ G that
145
      correctly answer q. Following previous works (Lan et al., 2022), question-answer pairs are given for
146   training, but not the ground-truth paths that lead to the answers.
147
148   Retrieval & Reasoning. As KGs usually contain millions of facts and nodes, a smaller question-
149
      specific subgraph Gq is retrieved for a question q, e.g., via entity linking and neighbor extraction (Yih
      et al., 2015). Ideally, all correct answers for the question are contained in the retrieved subgraph,
150
      {aq } ∈ Gq . The retrieved subgraph Gq along with the question q are used as input to a reasoning
151
      model, which outputs the correct answer(s). The prevailing reasoning models for the KGQA setting
152   studied are GNNs and LLMs.
153
154   GNNs. KGQA can be regarded as a node classification problem, where KG entities are classified as
155
      answers vs. non-answers for a given question. GNNs Kipf & Welling (2016); Veličković et al. (2017);
      Schlichtkrull et al. (2018) are powerful graph representation learners suited for tasks such as node

      LLM RAG. Retrieval-Augment Generation (RAG) is a method aiming to reduce LLM halluci-
183
      nations (Lewis et al., 2020). Given a query q, RAG retrieves relevant information (e.g, doc-
184   uments from the given corpus), which is inserted as additional context c to the LLM’s input.
185   In text applications, RAG leverages NLP models to identify relevant information (Karpukhin
186   et al., 2020), such as retrieving the top-k most semantic similar documents to the question, i.e,
187   c = [d1 , . . . , dk ] = top-k di ∈D M (di , q), where D is the document corpus and M is the NLP model
188   scoring.
189
      In KGs, the context c consists of graph information relevant to the question, such KG triplets,
190   paths, or subgraphs. The retrieved graph information is first converted into natural lan-
191   guage so that it can be processed by the LLM. The input given to the LLM contains the
192   KG factual information along with the question and a prompt. For instance, the input be-
193   comes “Knowledge: Jamaica → language_spoken → English \n Question:
194   Which language do Jamaican people speak?”, where the LLM has access to KG in-
195   formation for answering the question.
196   Landscape of KGQA methods. Figure 3 presents the landscape of existing KGQA methods with
197   respect to KG retrieval and reasoning. GNN-based methods, such as GraftNet (Sun et al., 2018),
198   NSM (He et al., 2021), and ReaRev (Mavromatis & Karypis, 2022), reason over a dense KG subgraph
199   leveraging the GNN’s ability to handle complex graph information. Recent LLM-based methods
200   leverage the LLM’s power for both retrieval and reasoning (Gu et al., 2023). For instance, ToG (Sun
201   et al., 2024) uses the LLM to retrieve relevant facts hop-by-hop. RoG (Luo et al., 2024) uses the
202   LLM to generate plausible relation paths which are then queried on the KG to retrieve the relevant
203   information.
204   LLM-based Retriever. We present an example of an LLM-based retriever (RoG; (Luo et al., 2024)).
205   Given training question-answer pairs, RoG extracts the shortest paths to the answers starting from
206   question entities for fine-tuning the retriever. Based on the extracted paths, an LLM (LLaMA2-Chat-
207   7B (Touvron et al., 2023)) is fine-tuned to generate reasoning paths given a question q as

236   4     G NN -R AG
237
238   We introduce G NN -R AG, a novel graph neural retrieval method for KGQA that leverages state-of-the-
239
      art GNNs to improve retrieval performance when questions require complex graph information. We
      provide the overall framework at inference time in Figure 4. First, the KGQA GNN reasons over a
240
      dense KG subgraph to retrieve answer candidates for a given question. Second, the shortest paths in
241
      the KG that connect question entities and GNN-based answers are extracted to represent useful KG
242   reasoning paths. The extracted paths are verbalized and given as context for LLM reasoning via RAG.
243   In our G NN -R AG framework, the GNN acts as a dense subgraph reasoner to extract useful graph
244   information, while the LLM leverages its natural language processing ability for ultimate KGQA.
245
246   4.1    GNN
247
248   In order to retrieve high-quality reasoning paths via G NN -R AG, we leverage state-of-the-art GNNs
249   for KGQA. We prefer GNNs over other KGQA methods, e.g., embedding-based methods (Saxena
250   et al., 2020), due to their ability to handle complex graph interactions and answer multi-hop questions.
251   GNNs mark themselves as good candidates for retrieval due to their architectural benefit of exploring
252   diverse reasoning paths (Choi et al., 2024) that result in high answer recall.
253   GNN Optimization. GNN reasoning consists of L GNN updates via Equation 1 (L is hyperpa-

257   so that the nodes are scored as answers vs. non-answers based on their final GNN representations

      4.3   RAG WITH LLM
312
313   In text RAG, retrieval is performed on a document corpus D (Section 3-LLM RAG). In KGQA, the
314   corpus is the node set V. G NN -R AG uses the GNN model as the scoring model to obtain the top
315   relevant nodes for answering the query, [v1 , . . . , vk ] = top-k vi ∈V GNN(vi , q). In order to provide
316   more context to the LLM, we extract the shortest paths between question entities and the GNN top
317   scored nodes. After obtaining the reasoning paths by G NN -R AG, we verbalize them and give them as
318   input to a downstream LLM, such as ChatGPT or LLaMA. However, LLMs are sensitive to the input
319   prompt template and the way that the graph information is verbalized.
320   To alleviate this issue, we opt to follow RAG prompt tuning (Lin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b) for
321   LLMs that have open weights and are feasible to train. A LLaMA2-Chat-7B model is fine-tuned
322   based on the training question-answer pairs to generate a list of correct answers, given the prompt:
323   “Based on the reasoning paths, please answer the given question.\n
      Reasoning Paths: {Reasoning Paths} \n Question: {Question}”.

      On the other hand, the limitation of GNNs is for simple (1-hop) questions, where accurate question-
346   relation matching is more important than deep graph search (see our Theorem in Appendix B that
347   states this GNN limitation). In such cases, the LLM retriever is better at selecting the right KG
348   information due to its natural language understanding abilities (we provide an example later in
349   Figure 6).
350
351   4.5    R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTATION (RA)
352
353   Retrieval augmentation (RA) combines the retrieved KG information from different approaches
354   to increase diversity and answer recall. Motivated by the results in Section 4.4, we present a RA
355   technique (G NN -R AG+RA), which complements the GNN retriever with an LLM-based retriever
356   to combine their strengths on multi-hop and single-hop questions, respectively. Specifically, we
357
      experiment with the RoG retrieval, which is described in Equation 2. During inference, we take the
      union of the reasoning paths retrieved by the two retrievers.
358
359   A downside of LLM-based retrieval is that it requires multiple generations (beam-search decoding) to
360   retrieve diverse paths, which trades efficiency for effectiveness (we provide a performance analysis in
361   Appendix B). A cheaper alternative is to perform RA by combining the outputs of different GNNs,
362   which are equipped with different LMs in Equation 3. Our G NN -R AG+Ensemble combines two
363
      different GNNs (GNN+SBERT & GNN+LMSR ) as input for RAG.

530   7   C ONCLUSION
531
532   We introduce G NN -R AG, a novel graph neural method for enhancing RAG in KGQA with GNNs. Our
533   contributions are the following. (1) Framework: G NN -R AG tailors GNNs for KG retrieval due to
534   their ability to handle complex graph information. Similar to retrieval in text-based RAG, G NN -R AG
535
      can be seamlessly integrated with different downstream LLMs. (2) Effectiveness & Faithfulness:
      G NN -R AG achieves state-of-the-art performance in two widely used KGQA benchmarks (WebQSP
536
      and CWQ). Furthermore, G NN -R AG is shown to retrieve multi-hop information that is necessary for
537
      faithful LLM reasoning on complex questions. (3) Efficiency: G NN -R AG improves vanilla LLMs on
538   KGQA performance without incurring additional LLM calls as existing RAG systems for KGQA
539   require. In addition, G NN -R AG outperforms or matches GPT-4 performance with a 7B tuned LLM.

873

875   question-irrelevant facts through ω(q, r).

878   perform semantic matching via function ω(q, r) apart from leveraging the graph information encoded
879
      in the KG. Our analysis suggests that GNNs lack reasoning abilities for KGQA if they cannot perform
      effective semantic matching between the KG and the question.

1052   Evaluation metric discussion. We clarify the evaluation metrics in Table 2. H@1 evaluation
1053   assumes that we are given a list of scored candidate answers (sorted based on the model’s scores).
1054   However, since LLMs generate free-form answers, their responses can include multiple answers,
1055
       which complicates the direct application of Hit@1. For example, consider the following hypothesized
       case:
1056
1057   Question: What do Jamaican people speak?
1058   Answer: English
1059   LLM Response: Jamaican people speak French and English.
1060
       In this case, the Hit score would be 1.0, as "English" is included in the response, although the LLM
1061
       generates the incorrect response “French”. This is the score that prior methods report as Hit@1 for
1062   LLMs. However, if we were to treat the LLM response as a list [French, English], the Hit@1 score
1063   would be 0.0, because the answer at rank 1 (French) is not the correct one.
1064
1065
       For this reason, we do not combine H@1 and Hit metrics for LLMs, as doing so could lead to an
       artificially inflated performance, and report LLM performance separately based on the Hit metric.

       G NN -R AG assumes that the KG subgraph, on which the GNN reasons, contains answer nodes.
       However, this may not be true for all questions or when errors in entity linking happen. In addition,
1271
       G NN -R AG employs simple prompting with the shortest paths from question entities to candidate
1272
       answers as context. As an extension, GNN-RAG can be combined with prompt optimization (Wen
1273   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a) so that the LLM understands the graph better. Moreover, similar
1274   to conventional retrieval which focuses on identifying relevant information (text documents or KG
1275   nodes in Figure 1) regardless the downstream LLM, the scope of our G NN -R AG contributions is
1276   to improve the retrieval results over the KG without specialized GNN-LLM interactions. However,
1277   the GNN and the LLM could be coupled via iterative retrieval (Asai et al., 2023) to further improve
1278   KGQA.
