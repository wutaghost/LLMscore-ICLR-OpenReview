009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            An essential component of event reconstruction in particle physics experiments is
012            identifying the trajectory of charged particles in the detector. Traditional methods
013            for track finding are often complex, and tailored to specific detectors and input
014            geometries, limiting their adaptability to new detector designs and optimization
015            processes. To overcome these limitations, we present a novel, end-to-end track
016            finding algorithm that is detector-agnostic and can take into account multiple in-
017            put geometric types. To achieve this, our approach unifies inputs from multiple
018
               sub-detectors and detector types into a single geometric algebra representation,
               simplifying data handling compared to traditional methods. Then, we leverage an
019
               equivariant graph neural network, GATr, to perform track finding across all data
020
               from an event simultaneously. We validate the effectiveness of our pipeline on
021            various detector concepts with different technologies for the FCC-ee at CERN,
022            specifically the IDEA and CLD detectors. This work generalizes track finding
023            across diverse types of input geometric data and tracking technologies, facilitat-
024            ing the development of innovative detector concepts, accelerating detector devel-
025            opment cycles, and enabling comprehensive detector optimization.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The Future Circular Collider is one of the proposed future colliders that could follow from the Large
031   Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN. In the first stage, FCC-ee, an electron and a positron beam are
032   accelerated to nearly the speed of light to collide in multiple interaction points. These collisions gen-
033
      erate sprays of outgoing particles. Sophisticated detector systems, comprising hundreds of millions
      of sensors are utilized to measure information about these particles. One of the main challenges is
034
      to identify and reconstruct the 3D trajectory and kinematic properties of a charged particles prop-
035
      agating inside the detector’s magnetic field (see Figure 1), this is referred to as tracking. Classic
036   approaches solve this problem with combinatorial optimization methods such as Kalman filters (Ai,
037   2019; Bertacchi et al., 2020; Amrouche et al., 2020). However, these methods are detector depen-
038   dent, and have a long development cycle, reducing their adaptability to new detector concepts. Novel
039   approaches to tracking are needed to maximize the potential of future detectors.
040
      Tracking is performed in two stages (Frühwirth & Strandlie, 2021), first track finding and then track
041
      fitting. Track finding is essentially an object instantiation task, which requires to identify groups of
042   hits that form a track. Where the hits are the collection of measurements resulting from the particles
043   interacting with the detector. Hits are usually positions, but they can be more complex geometries,
044   such as shapes or directions. Track finding is a challenging problem: tracks can have a different
045   geometries and varying number of hits (10-100’s), a track can have missing hits in the trajectory,
046   they can have hits in one or multiple sub-detectors, or an in-flight decay of a particle can produce an
047   abrupt change in direction (kinked tracks) or disappear.
048   Track finding has been approached in multiple ways. Global methods cluster hits by transforming
049   the coordinates to a feature space in which relevant patterns are easier to detect (Brondolin et al.,
050   2020). Another approach is seeding and track following, usually implemented with a Combinatorial
051   Kalman Filter (Braun & Braun, 2019). This approach evaluates combinations of hits and iteratively
052   builds tracks (Ai, 2019; Cornelissen et al., 2008). However, it is complex due to the large combi-
053   natorics, and it requires a detailed description of the geometry and materials of the experiment. In
      addition, when dealing with various types of input geometric data in each sub-detector, some works


                                                         1

054
      perform track finding for each sub-detector and then combine the track sections (Bertacchi et al.,
055   2020; Cornelissen et al., 2008). For example, the Belle II experiment has two tracking detectors, and
056   its track finding approach is composed of eight different algorithms including multiple algorithms
057   for each tracking detector and for the combination (Bertacchi et al., 2020). Overall, these algorithms
058   are detector dependent and have long development cycle.
059
      Currently, there are new collider proposals with novel developments in detector technologies. For
060
      example, for the FCC-ee, there are several detector concepts under investigation (Abada A, 2019;
061   Bacchetta et al., 2019; Bedeschi, 2021). Each different tracking component can result in multiple
062   input geometric types, depending on the measurement. As these new detector concepts are envi-
063   sioned, it is important to have adaptable tracking pipelines with fast turnaround that can cope with
064   different sub-detector concepts, geometric input data, variations and optimizations thereof. Figure 1
065   shows two of the detectors which produce different track representations requiring usually different
066   algorithms.
067   Novel Graph Neural Network (GNN) based methods have been recently shown to perform com-
068   petitively to classic pattern recognition approaches for a generic silicon detector while remaining
069   scalable and not depending on the geometry implementation of the detector (Ju et al., 2021; Am-
070   rouche et al., 2020). However, so far they have only been applied to silicon tracking systems, which
071   have pointcloud-like inputs, and Ju et al. (2021) also introduce pipelines with multiple preprocessing
072   steps.
073   This work presents the detector agnostic Geometric Graph Track Finding (GGTF) method. This
074   method is a generalized geometric track finding approach that:
075
076          • Can cope with multiple sub-detectors with different input geometries from multiple track-
077            ing technologies
078
             • Does not require the geometry and materials specifications of the detector
079
080          • Does not rely on an analytical parametrization of the trajectories.
081
082   Specifically, our end-to-end pipeline considers the hits from all tracking components and outputs
083   a set of tracks. A critical new component is a geometric algebra representation of the data that
084   which allows multiple geometric types and a GNN, GATr (Brehmer et al., 2024), that exploits the
085
      symmetries of the detector through equivariance. The performance of the algorithm is assessed
      using two of the FCC-ee baseline detector concepts, the IDEA detector (Abada A, 2019; Bedeschi,
086
      2021) and the CLD detector (Bacchetta et al., 2019).

089   2   R ELATED W ORK
090
091   Legacy tracking approaches Two main approaches are employed in track finding algorithms:
092   coordinate transformation (global methods), and seeding and track following approaches. The sim-
093   pler example of the first set, coordinate transformation, is conformal tracking. Conformal tracking
094   (Hansroul et al., 1988) is a circle fitting method that transforms circles in the plane passing through
095   the origin into straight lines. More complex transformations are the Hough transform (Duda & Hart,
096   1972), which finds clusters of points that lie close to a parametric curve reducing tracking to finding
097   intersection points, and the Retina algorithm (Ristori, 2000). However this algorithms tend not to
098   reconstruct more complex tracks including: tracks displaced from the vertex or kinked tracks. For
099
      this reason, seeding and track following is the more common approach to track finding. Conformal
      tracking can be improved using this type of approach. Brondolin et al. (2020) use the cellular au-
100
      tomaton (CA) algorithm to take these deviations from the circular path, such as those due to multiple
101
      scattering or displaced vertices into account. The CA algorithm uses a seeding procedure followed
102   by cell extrapolation. In other approaches, the seeding algorithm iteratively finds triplets of hits that
103   are likely to belong to the same track. The track candidates are then built from the seeds using a
104   Combinatorial Kalman Filter (Braun & Braun, 2019). These types of approaches give high accu-
105   racy but are computationally demanding. Some of these algorithms have complex implementations
106   as they use the geometry to calculate deviations from the expected path due to material interactions.
107   This requires the algorithm to interact with the geometry of the detector for every step which can be
      costly (Ai et al., 2022; Ai, 2019).

152   An extensive review of classic pattern recognition algorithms is out of the scope of this paper, we
153   refer the reader to (Frühwirth & Strandlie, 2021) for an in depth review. However, it is worth noting
154   that track finding in real experiments, such as the Belle II (Bertacchi et al., 2020) applies a mix of

157   ML-based tracking Track finding can be posed as an object detection problem. Multiple ap-
158   proaches exist for object detection for point-cloud datasets, some focus on regression of bounding
159   boxes Yang et al. (2019); Hou et al. (2019); Wang & Solomon (2021), others find instance queries
160   using a voxelized representations of the point-cloud Kolodiazhnyi et al. (2024). However, these ap-
161   proaches are not directly applicable to track finding as the bounding boxes are hard to define due
      to the geometry of the tracks, and the voxel representation would merge multiple tracks. For these


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      reasons, the ML-based tracking approaches have focused on the tasks of edge prediction and dis-
163   covery of connected components as a second step Denby (1988); Stimpfl-Abele & Garrido (1991);
164   Ju et al. (2021); Farrell et al. (2018). These methods suffer from the costly post-processing step of
165   finding connected components in a graph. Earlier works also considered Recurrent Neural Networks
166   to perform a binary hit classification graph model that works on a subset of the event to extract tracks
167   iteratively. More recently, Lieret & DeZoort (2024) use a GNN based approach with a contrastive
168   learning loss to create a separable embedding space where they can perform clustering. This method
169   improves the performance over the connected components baseline, and is similar to some object
170   detection approaches based on similarity metrics Wang et al. (2018; 2019).
171   GNN based approaches (Farrell et al., 2018; Ju et al., 2021; Lieret & DeZoort, 2024) have focused
172   on the dataset proposed in (Amrouche et al., 2020) which considers pixel and strips tracking com-
173   ponents. One of our key contribution is to generalize the method of track finding using GNNs to
174   more complex tracking detectors.

      3   P RELIMINARIES
177
178
      Problem statement An event is a collection of measurements in a detector. The detector registers
179   the signals deposited by the interaction of particles with its sensitive components, referred to as
180   hits. The role of a tracking algorithm is to identify sets of hits produced by single particles and to
181   derive information on the particles trajectory based on those. For a set of hits in a given event, X ,
182   composed of hits from different tracking components X = {Xv , Xi , Xo , ...}, (vertex, inner tracker,
183   outer tracker, endcap inner tracker,...) we want to obtain a set of tracks that approximates the target
184   set of charged particles. The geometry of the detector is cylindrical, with a magnetic field along the
185   z direction. We define the (x, y) as the transverse plane , and the z as the beam direction. Figure
186   1 shows an example set of hits for different detectors. In this work, this is framed as an object
187
      instantiation problem.
188
189
      Trajectories In a detector with a homogeneous magnetic field, the trajectory of a particle forms a
      helix, with varying curvature depending on its momentum. Particles with higher momentum result
190
      in an arc, while lower momentum particles can curl inside the detector. As they lose energy, the
191
      curvature of the helix increases, generating the shrinking displaced circles observed in Figure 1 top.
192
193
      Representations The inputs from the different tracking detectors, as well as the geometries of
194   the tracks, are detector dependent. For a silicon detector, each hit h = (x, s) consist of a set of
195   3D coordinates x ∈ R3 and a scalar s corresponding to the subdetector index. For other detector
196   technologies, other information might be available. For example in a drift chamber, a gaseous
197   detector filled with wires which register ionization signals, each hit leads to two sets of coordinates,
198   (xleft , xright ), due to inherent left-right ambiguity in the measurement. This richer data structure,
199   which steps away from point-like track representations, make the application of classical approaches
200   complex.
201   In addition, tracks might have very different geometries in different detectors. For a silicon detector
202   like CLD a particle will leave around 10-15 hits per track (Bacchetta et al., 2019), while for a
203   drift chamber each particle can generate O(100) hits. Not only the number of hits are different, but
204   also the presented geometries. A drift chamber like detector allows to capture particles of very low
205   energy looping through the detector due to the magnetic field, as can be seen in Figure 1 left. Lower
206   momentum particles have smaller curvature radius leading to spiral trajectories, which we will refer
207   to as curlers. These curlers represent a complex reconstruction tasks for classical algorithms, which
208
      usually focus on the high momentum regime (higher curvature radius tracks).

      belonging to the same object close in an embedding space, as in (Ju et al., 2021). Unlike (Ju et al.,

243   Representation of hits in the graph As an initial step, the set of hits for each event is converted
244   into a graph. However, unlike traditional approaches, the GATr architecture allows to represent
245   the raw hits as different geometric objects and transformations in the projective geometric algebra
246   G3,0,1 . The elements of the algebra are 16-dimensional multivectors, these have direction, sign and
247   can be linearly combined (Brehmer et al., 2024). This method allows to have as an input planes,
248   lines, point or scalars. For example, we choose to represent the hits from the vertex detector as
249
      points (trivectors) and the hits from the drift chamber as vectors. This vector goes from the left to
      the right hit coordinates, therefore accounting for the ambiguity in the measurement.
250
251
252
      Geometric Algebra Transformer The hits from the different systems are then unified in the mul-
      tivector format, which represents the different geometrical objects in 16 dimensions. Then, we use
253
      the GATr architecture as shown in Figure 2. It consist of multiple transformer blocks. Each block
254
      has a LayerNorm, an equivariant multivector self-attention layer, a second LayerNorm, an equiv-
255   ariant multivector MLP with geometric bilinear interactions, and another residual connection. This
256   architecture is E(3) equivariant. The E(3)-equivariant linear layers map multivectors using grade
257   projections and multiplation with a homogenous basis vector. The geometric bilinears allow to build
258   more expressive networks by allowing grade mixing. The network outputs multivectors which are
259   used by the object condensation loss, defined below.
260
261   Loss function Modern approaches to reconstruct multiple objects focus on predicting a bounding
262   box per object as an output (Wang & Solomon, 2021; Girshick, 2015). However, due to the helical
263   trajectory of the tracks it would be hard to parameterize these boxes. On the other hand, the object
264   condensation approach for multi-object detection (Kieseler, 2020) allows to reconstruct an unknown
265   number of objects through a dedicated loss function without the use of bounding boxes. The intuition
266
      behind this approach is as follows: the loss function requires the output of hits belonging to the same
      track to be close in a low dimensional embedding space and those belonging to different tracks to
267
      be far away. In this way, in the embedding space the tracks are easily separable.

320   Metrics In order to measure the performance of the various algorithms, each reconstructed track
321   is matched to a ground truth particle to which it shares the largest number of hits.
322
323          • The track hit purity is the ratio of the number of hits in the track that belong to the MC
               particle and the total number of hits of the reconstructed track.


                                                               6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
             • The track hit efficiency is the ratio of the number of hits in the track that belong to the MC
325            particle and the total number of hits this particle left in the detector.
326
327   The tracking efficiency is the probability to reconstruct a track. It can be defined in multiple ways,
328   we will consider the following variants:
329
330         1. The percentage of reconstructable charged particles that lead to a reconstructed track with
331            track hit purity greater than 75%, as in Bacchetta et al. (2019) .
332         2. The percentage of reconstructable charged particles with both ratios, the track hit purity and
333            the track hit efficiency, above 50%, as defined in the accuracy tracking challenge Amrouche
334            et al. (2020). Tracks are further separated in four categories. Good: both ratios above 50%,
335            reconstructed in this definition. Split: track hit efficiency below 50%, track hit purity above
336
               50%, so only a fraction of the track is reconstructed. Multiple: track hit efficiency above
               50%, but track hit purity below 50%, typically due to the aggregation of multiple particles.
337
               Bad: both below 50%.
338
339         3. For the CLD detector as there are only 10-15 hits per track, we consider the percentage of
340
               reconstructable charged particles that match with a reconstructed track with at least four
               hits (the minimum number of hits for track fitting, the second tracking step).

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
474
475
476   GGTF is a high-efficiency general track finding method for multiple sub-detectors with different
477
      input geometries. It is able to find varied track geometries via an equivariant GNN. The success
      of GGTF indicates that highly parameterized algorithms are likely unnecessary and can be replaced
478
      with suitable GNNs. This new pipeline significantly simplifies the application of track finding to
479
      optimization studies as the development cycle is largely reduced. An important future step is to
480   evaluate the algorithm impact on the track fitting stage, as its higher track hit efficiency could im-
481   prove the accuracy of the track parameters. Beyond the current applications, we suggest multiple
482   research directions to address the current limitations. For example, the algorithm was evaluated
483   without background, adding background would result in several ’noise’ hits, thereby degrading the
484   track hit purity and efficiency and increasing the probability of reconstructing fake tracks. Finally,
485   scalability of the algorithm needs to be improved to apply this method to full pile-up events, where
      the number of hits can be up to an order of magnitude larger.

598   Dataset Raw hits are obtained by performing simulation using the Key4hep (Brondolin et al.,
599   2024) framework, a software ecosystem for future colliders (Brondolin et al., 2024). First, the
600   simulation of the collisions, production and decay of the Z boson are generated with PYTHIA8
601   (Sjöstrand et al., 2006). The considered decay is e+ e− → Z → q q̄, q = u, d, at a center of

603   showers and hadronization to produce a final set of outgoing particles. These particles are then

      propagated through the detector using the DD4hep (Frank et al., 2014) toolkit which provides a

605
      concepts under consideration. We generate one dataset for each detector concept and describe their

      differences below.

625   A.1.1    CLD DETECTOR
626
627   The CLD detector (Bacchetta et al., 2019) has an all-silicon tracker. The tracking system consists of
628   a vertex pixel detector (V), an inner tracker with three barrel layers (ITB) and seven forward disks or
629   endcap (ITE), and an outer tracker with additional three barrel layers (OTB) and four discs (OTE).
630
      We use version CLDo2v05 of the CLD detector geometry (Gaede et al., 2024).
631
632
      Input format The dataset includes hits from each tracking sub-detector, reconstructed tracks from
      the conformal tracking algorithm and association of each hit to a Monte Carlo particle to build the
633
      set of target tracks to train the model on. Each input hit has global detector coordinates (x, y, z)
634
      and a label of the subdetector they hit {V, ITB, ITE, OTB, OTE}. Additionally, hits created by MC
635   particles with less than 3 hits in total are labelled as noise hits, and the algorithm does not try to
636   reconstruct these tracks.
637
638   Models For the GATr we consider the architecture in Brehmer et al. (2024). We embed the hits of
639   all sub-detectors as trivectors. We use 10 attention blocks, 16 multivector and 64 scalar chanels, and
640   8 attention heads, resulting in 1.1 million parameters. The β is extracted as the scalar component of
641   the multivector, and the coordinate in the embedding space as the trivector component.
642
643   Training All models are trained by minimizing the object condensation loss. We train for 20
644   epochs with the Adam optimizer, using a batch size of 8, and a step scheduler for the learning rate
645   from 10−3 to 10−6 .
