011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013             The improvement of economic policymaking presents an opportunity for broad so-
014             cietal benefit, a notion that has inspired research towards AI-driven policymaking
015             tools. AI policymaking holds the potential to surpass human performance through
016
                the ability to process data quickly at scale. However, existing RL-based meth-
                ods exhibit sample inefficiency, and are further limited by an inability to flexibly
017
                incorporate nuanced information into their decision-making processes. Thus, we
018
                propose a novel method in which we instead utilize pre-trained Large Language
019             Models (LLMs), as sample-efficient policymakers in socially complex multi-agent
020             reinforcement learning (MARL) scenarios. We demonstrate significant efficiency
021             gains, outperforming existing methods across three environments.

038   1    I NTRODUCTION
039
040   Economic policy-making is a field rife with uncertainty (Al-Thaqeb & Algharabali, 2019), high
041
      stakes (Persson & Tabellini, 2004), and complexity (Mueller, 2020). Human policy-makers are often
      faced with overwhelming amounts of data (Huh et al., 2018) and the influence of vested interests
042
      (Elliott, 1997), complicating effective and equitable decision-making. AI-driven tooling, with the
043
      ability to avoid self-centered bias and parse large amounts of data quickly, could offer significant
044   assistance.
045
046   Existing research (Zheng et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022) towards AI-based policymaking has primar-
047
      ily focused on reinforcement learning (RL) based methods using neural networks as economic policy
      generators 1 . A leading method in this field, termed AI Economist (Zheng et al., 2022), attempts
048
      to maximize the social welfare of agents within a MARL environment called Gather-Trade-Build
049
      (GTB). In GTB, agents can move around, collect wood and stone, and trade resources in order to
050   gain reward. AI Economist employs a neural network that observes agent endowments and market
051
052      1

053   social planner, such as tax rates, synonymous with “fiscal policy”. In contrast, we use “policy” to refer to the
      economic agents’ strategy, synonymous with “reinforcement learning policy”.

054
      behavior, and outputs tax rates; for a fixed set of weights, this network is an economic policy genera-
055   tor – a function mapping observed economic data to economic policy, such as tax rates. Policymak-
056   ing unfolds as a bilevel optimization problem, where the outer loop trains the network’s weights,
057   and each set of weights induces an economic policy generator parameterizing the environment that
058   agents train against in the inner loop.
059
      Though environment observations like agent endowments may contain useful information for the
060
      outer loop generator optimization, they are not necessarily variables that economic policies should
061   be a function of. Notably, we found that removing the generator network observations from both the
062   AI Economist and Yang et al. (2022)’s MetaGrad, another leading method, had no effect on their
063   performance. This implies that optimal tax rates can be static in their environments, and agent en-
064   dowments or market behavior are not needed as input. However, such observations should inform
065   the outer policymaking process. For AI Economist and MetaGrad, this information is limited to
066   being used through the generator network optimization step, where observations from an episode af-
067   fect the subsequent update to generator network weights only through their gradient in the loss. Our
068   ablations demonstrate this is an overly complicated way to approach AI policymaking – a problem
069
      that exacerbates the sample inefficiency that RL methods already suffer from. In our test environ-
      ments, both methods suffered from a starkly visible sample inefficiency problem; AI Economist
070
      was outperformed or matched by a much simpler ϵ-greedy bandit algorithm on two of our three
071
      environments, and MetaGrad by all three bandit baselines we use across all test environments.
072
073   Motivated by these limitations, we propose a simpler alternative approach to AI-based policymaking
074   by leveraging pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) as policymakers. Instead of learning
075
      economic policy generators, we directly learn economic policy by applying the In-Context Learning
      (ICL) (Dong et al., 2024) capabilities of LLMs. In addition, as our method uses a sequence modeling
076
      approach to predict policies as prompt completions, it is highly flexible in its inputs. For example, an
077
      economic report from human experts detailing suggested tax rates could improve sample efficiency
078   in finding an optimal tax rate policy. For an LLM, the entire report in natural language can be
079   added to a prompt to inform the policy subsequently chosen, while it is unclear how this would
080   be done for existing methods without further augmentation. Furthermore, LLMs can also be given
081   a contextualization of the problem setting they are applied to, potentially allowing them to draw
082   upon their extensive pre-training data distribution to improve sample efficiency and solve more
083   complex, realistic environments. Overall, our method is significantly more sample efficient than
084   prior approaches. We outperform five baselines significantly in terms of sample efficiency across
085   three multi-agent test environments, with little compromise to final asymptotic performance.
086   In summary, our contributions are as follows: (1) We give a generalized formalization of AI-based
087   policymaking methods, and leverage it to analyze the limitations of existing methods. (2) We pro-
088   pose a novel LLM-based automated policymaking method that addresses sample inefficiency in
089   prior approaches and simplifies the policymaking process. (3) We provide extensive empirical re-
090   sults to demonstrate the efficacy of our approach and analyze the contribution of each component to
091   its overall performance.

      In this section, we formalize AI policymaking as finding a Stackelberg-Markov equilibrium in order
      to perform a principled analysis of Zheng et al. (2022)’s AI Economist and its limitations through
127
      an ablation of their policy generator inputs. We additionally ablate MetaGrad Yang et al. (2022),
128
      a related method that differs only in the optimization process of the economic policy generator.
129   Detailed explanations of AI Economist and MetaGrad can be found in subsection A.1 and subsec-
130   tion A.2 respectively.

      as a POMG. For each principal action, we draw follower equilibria for the induced POMG from an
133
      oracle, an abstraction that allows for a clear theoretical separation of principal and follower learning
134
      problems.

148   Note that ϕ can parameterize economic policies (tax rates) directly, or an economic policy generator
149   (weights of a function that produces tax rates). We use this framework to formalize Zheng et al.
150   (2022)’s AI Economist. In their GTB environment, an RL planner uses an economic policy generator
151   network to set tax rates for ten tax periods within each episode, aiming to maximize social welfare
152
      amongst the agents. Their training proceeds in three distinct phases: 1. free-market agent training
      with no planner, 2. agent training under a heavily entropy-regularized planner policy, effectively
153
      producing tax rates at random, and 3. standard MARL learning for the planner and agents in tandem.
154

155   3 of this training structure as an implementation of lines 4 through 7 of Algorithm 1.

194   Our ablations, shown in Figure 2, remove all observations from the AI Economist policy generator.
195   Formally, this is equivalent to changing the domain of tax rate function R : Φ × S to just Φ. We
196   keep the original architecture and input shape to avoid the need for a search over learning rates but
197   overwrite observations to fixed vectors of ones. For MetaGrad, we removed the economic policy
198   generator network entirely and learned the outputs directly as trainable parameters. With no network,
199   their method reduces to Xu et al. (2018)’s meta-gradient reinforcement learning. Our ablated method
200   uses a higher learning rate to account for the reduced number of parameters but otherwise uses
201
      identical hyperparameters to the original. By our formalization, this shows us that the principal
      action space has been unnecessarily complicated into a high-dimensional space of network weights.
202
      This points to a somewhat concerning problem with previous methods – that they appear to be
203
      overcomplicating the problem setting. Thus, a question is posed: can one dramatically improve the
204   efficiency of learning by leveraging an altogether different approach?

332   5.1   E NVIRONMENTS
333
334   We employ three environments for our main experimental results: Harvest, Clean Up, and Con-
335   textual Escape Room. We focus on Stackelberg-Markov games with easily-interpretable principal
336
      action spaces. In the following subsections, we give an brief overview of our each environment –
      further detail can be found in subsection D.2.
337
338   Commons Harvest Open (Harvest). In Harvest (Agapiou et al., 2023), the environment contains
339   several patches of apples, and agents i ∈ [7] are given reward +1 for harvesting an apple. Harvested
340   apples regrow stochastically at a rate proportional to the number of nearby unharvested apples –
341   never regrowing once a patch has been exhausted. To sustainably accrue reward, agents must refrain
342
      from overharvesting and allow apples time to regrow. Without external influence, the environment
      succumbs to tragedy of the commons. In Harvest, the principal outputs a three-tiered tax rate to be
343
      applied to the reward signal of agents collecting apples, where the applicable tax rate is determined
344
      by the number of apples an agent has collected in the past 10 timesteps. Episodes are terminated
345   after 1000 timesteps.
346
347   Clean Up. Clean Up (Jaques et al., 2019; Agapiou et al., 2023) also involves seven agents har-
348
      vesting apples, but features a fundamentally different social dilemma. In this environment, a river
      builds pollution at a constant rate, and apples grow in a single, large patch at a rate inversely propor-
349
      tional to the river’s pollution level. Note that apple regrowth is no longer affected by overharvesting.
350
      Agents can clean river pollution, but must leave the apple patch to do so. Harvesting apples yields a
351   small intrinsic reward of +0.1 and cleaning a harsh penalty of −1. Under unmodified environmen-
352   tal rewards, agents remain in the apple patch without cleaning, even when pollution stops regrowth
353   entirely. In this environment, the principal incentivizes three subsets of the agent action space, ad-
354   justing the reward signal for agents that harvest, clean, or do other actions. Episodes are terminated
355   after 1000 timesteps.
356
      Contextual Escape Room (CER). A (n, m, L) Contextual Escape Room environment is an exten-
357   sion of Yang et al. (2020)’s Escape Room environment. This simple environment has L + 2 states,
358   consisting of L lever states, a door state, and a start state. Agents i ∈ [n] begin an episode in the
359   start state, and draw actions from a space equivalent to the state space – determining where they
360   move to next. In each episode, one lever state ℓ at random is chosen to be “activated”; if m agents
361   are at lever ℓ, the door “opens” and all agents at the door state receive a reward of +10. Moving
362   to any other state, regardless of the door’s status, incurs a penalty of −1, unless the door is closed
363   and agents have not moved since the last step, in which case they receive 0. The social dilemma is
364   that self-interested agents have no reason to incur a penalty for visiting lever states, and thus without
365
      incentivization the door is never opened. A (n, m, L) CER environment has known global optimum
      of 10 · (n − m) − m total agent reward per step. We employ a (5, 2, 3) CER environment in which
366
      the principal observes the active lever and outputs five incentives to be added to the agent reward
367
      signal, corresponding to each state. Episodes are terminated after 5 timesteps.

483   6     R ELATED W ORK
484
485   While the exploration of LLMs specifically concerning economic policymaking in MARL environ-
      ments is limited beyond previously outlined methods, we now outline research within similar fields.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Contextual Attention-based Representation Learning (CARE) (Sodhani et al., 2021) identifies
487   the difficulty of achieving strong multi-task performance for RL-based autonomous agents. Sod-
488   hani et al. (2021) attribute this to an inability to leverage auxiliary information between tasks and
489   propose the usage of a pretrained LM to encode task descriptions, used to condition the policy of
490   an RL agent. CARE employs multiple encoders, each focusing on a specific component of a task.
491   While CARE achieved impressive results in several robotic manipulation tasks, using an end-to-end
492   LLM approach allows our method to leverage more fine-grained auxiliary information without being
493   restrained by a set number of encoders.
494   Reward Design with Language Models (Kwon et al., 2023) attempts to mitigate the human ex-
495   pense of reward design within RL. Rather than directly providing expert demonstrations or a specific
496   reward function, Kwon et al. (2023) provide a natural language description of desired agent behavior
497   to an LLM. The LLM is then tasked with evaluating agent behavior and subsequently producing a
498   reward signal. Unlike our work, rewards are reactively assigned based on agent action.
499   Simulating Human Behavior aims to instantiate generative agents as realistic human proxies. One
500   such approach (Park et al., 2023) does so in a sandbox environment, using extended LLMs with
501   memory banks to store experiences unique to each agent. Vinitsky et al. (2023) introduces MARL
502   environments with ‘public sanctions’, in which agents equipped with a novel architecture learn social
503   norms and punish transgressors, facilitating agent cooperation in socially complex environments.
504   Research towards this end indicates the possibility of future AI-policymaker tests that better evaluate
505   real-world performance.

509
510
      In this work, we standardize and evaluate existing methodologies in the field of AI-driven economic
511
      policy design. We further contribute a novel method, leveraging LLMs for a more generalizable
512
      and tractable approach that outperforms baselines . The relative infancy of this field engenders a
513   lengthy research agenda; importantly, one such direction for future work is increasing the realism
514   and complexity of the economic environments being tested. Although we show that prior methods
515   are overly complex for existing environments and directly outputting a static tax rate is enough, in
516   the real-world, a tax rate generator may be necessary and more complex environments should be
517   developed to investigate this further.
518
519
520   7.1   L IMITATIONS
521
522   As simulated environments increase in complexity, prompts given to instantiations of LLM policy-
523   makers will presumably increase as well. This calls into question the scalability of prompt design,
524   as well as the memory constraints imposed by the context windows of LLMs. Furthermore, we
525   found in our experiments that LLMs can demonstrate a tendency to trap themselves in local minima,
526
      choosing to make small adjustments to past outputs rather than attempting a more dramatic shift.

651
652   AI Economist is an application of deep policy-based model-free RL to the economic policymaking
653   problem: the economic policymaker is modelled as an additional agent in the environment, at every
654   timestep receiving an observation, sampling an action from a policy network and receiving a reward.
655   The policy network, serving as an economic policy generator, has a separate discrete action head for
656   each tax bracket, each a categorical distribution over a discretized output range with an additional
657   NO-OP action. The economic policy generator network outputs at timesteps corresponding to the
658   start of a tax period are set as tax rates in the environment, and all other actions are masked to
659
      NO-OPs. In GTB, rewards are given for each episode timestep as the difference in social welfare
      between that timestep and the last; in our environments, since only one action is reinforced per
660
      episode, we give each a reward according to the total social welfare achieved in that entire episode.
