009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The weights of neural networks (NNs) have recently gained prominence as a new
012            data modality in machine learning, with applications ranging from accuracy and
013            hyperparameter prediction to representation learning or weight generation. One
014            approach to leverage NN weights involves training autoencoders (AEs) with con-
015            trastive and reconstruction losses. Indeed, such models can be applied to a wide
016            variety of downstream tasks, and they demonstrate strong predictive performance
017            and low reconstruction error. However, despite the low reconstruction error, these
018
               AEs reconconstruct NN models that fail to match the performance of the origi-
               nal ones. In this paper, we identify a limitation of weight-space AEs, specifically
019
               highlighting that structural weight reconstruction alone fails to capture some fea-
020
               tures critical for reconstructing high-performing models. To address this issue, we
021            propose a behavioral loss for training AEs in weight space. This behavioral loss
022            focuses on the features essential for reconstructing performant models, which are
023            not adequately captured by structural reconstruction. We evaluate the capabilities
024            of AE trained using this novel loss on three different model zoos: we demon-
025            strate that when combining structural and behavioral losses, we can reconstruct
026            and generate models that match the performance of the original models. With
027            our exploration of representation learning in deep weight spaces, we show that a
028            strong synergy exists between structural and behavioral features, and that combin-
029
               ing them results in increased performance across all evaluated downstream tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The weights of trained neural network (NN) models have recently become themselves a domain for
034   research and machine learning, named weight space learning (Kofinas et al., 2023; Lim et al., 2023;
035   Navon et al., 2023; Schürholt et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023a). Since the weights are structured by the
036   training process, they contain rich information on the data, their generating factors, and also model
037   performance (Unterthiner et al., 2020; Martin et al., 2021). This opens up the opportunity to analyze
038   NNs just by investigating their weights. Understanding the inherent structure in trained weights
039   might further lead to better initializations (Narkhede et al., 2022), model merging (Chou et al.,
040   2018), or identification of lottery tickets (Frankle & Carbin, 2018). However, weight space learning
041   presents several challenges: (i) weight spaces grow with model size and become increasingly sparse;
042
      (ii) the weight spaces of different model architectures do not match; (iii) NN architectures contain
      mutliple invariances and equivariances which result in weight space symmetries.
043
044   Several methods have been proposed to address these challenges, for a variety of downstream tasks.
045   Weight derived features have been used for discriminative downstream tasks, such as predicting
046   model performance of training hyperparameters (Unterthiner et al., 2020; Eilertsen et al., 2020;
047   Martin et al., 2021; Schürholt et al., 2021). In reverse, other works have developed methods for
048
      generative downstream tasks, i.e., the generation of synthetic NN weights. For example, HyperNet-
      works are weight generator models, that use the learning signal from the target model to update the
049
      weight-generator (Ha et al., 2017). HyperNetworks have been successfully applied on multiple do-
050
      mains, model sizes, for regular training, architecture search, as well as meta-learning (Zhang et al.,
051   2019; Knyazev et al., 2021; Deutsch, 2018; Ratzlaff & Fuxin, 2019; Zhmoginov et al., 2022).

      Recently, an approach has been proposed that can be used for both of these downstream tasks
055   families: extracting information from weights by learning latent representations (dubbed hyper-
056   representations) with autoencoders (AEs) (Schürholt et al., 2021). Indeed, the encoder outputs
057   latent representations of NN weights that can be used for discriminative downstream tasks, or fed
058   to the decoder to reconstruct the model. Alternatively, the decoder can be fed synthetic latent repre-
059   sentations to generate NN weights (Schürholt et al., 2022a). While these hyper-representation AEs
060   show high predictive performance and low reconstruction mean-squared-error (MSE), they appear
061   to lack the fidelity to reconstruct or generate high performance models without further fine-tuning.
062   This is unsurprising since a large component of the composite loss is a MSE reconstruction. MSE
063
      reconstruction uses the capacity of the model to predict the mean and as much variation as possible,
      but may lead to blurry reconstructions and lack of high fidelity (Vincent et al., 2010). Similar obser-
064
      vations in computer vision have been addressed by additional image perception losses which force
065
      the models to focus on high fidelity features, too (Dosovitskiy & Brox, 2016; Esser et al., 2021).
066
067   In this work, we propose a analogous approach for weight space learning to improve reconstruc-
068   tion and generation. We begin by analyzing the error modes of weight-structure reconstruction and
069
      identify that structural reconstruction misses some of the features essential to reconstructing behav-
      iorally similar models, thus breaking the reconstructed models. Taking inspiration from the image
070
      perception loss, we introduce a behavioral loss function, and use it for hyper-representation AEs
071
      in conjunction with the existing structural loss: we require models to not only have similar weight-
072   structure, but also to behave the same way. We systematically evaluate our approach on discrimi-
073   native, reconstructive and generative downstream tasks. We find that the addition of a behavioral
074   element to the loss has a minor but positive effect on model analysis tasks. More importantly, we
075   are able to demonstrate that the combination of the structural and behavioral losses dramatically
076   improves the fidelity of the reconstructed models: the models and their respective reconstructions
077   are structurally similar, and they also perform similarly. Finally, we show that this ability to out-
078   put well-behaved models opens up opportunities to generate synthetic model weights that perform
079   nearly as well as the original ones. In all experiments, we show that there is strong synergy between
080
      structural and behavioral aspects of the loss, further confirming that combining those guides the AE
      to learn representations that are beneficial to model analysis, reconstruction and generation.

      2   S TRUCTURAL R ECONSTRUCTION I S N OT E NOUGH
085
086   Reconstruction in Weight Space Learning Prioritizes Coarse Features Previous work on rep-
087   resentation learning of NN weights with AEs has largely focused on reconstructing weights in the
088   structural sense, using the mean square error (MSE) (Schürholt et al., 2021). These representations
089   are effective for discriminative downstream tasks like model performance prediction. However, they
090
      struggle in reconstructing functional models without the need for further fine-tuning, even when
      minimizing the structural reconstruction error (Schürholt et al., 2022a; 2024; Soro et al., 2024). This
091
      difficulty may stem from the inductive biases inherent in undercomplete AEs, used in these weight
092
      representation learning approaches. Trained with the MSE loss, these AEs often exhibit a bias
093   toward learning coarse, smoothed representations, as MSE minimizes reconstruction error by aver-
094   aging over fine details (Vincent et al., 2010). Tasks that rely on high-resolution features, like texture
095   recognition, further highlight the limitations of such biases (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014). This smoothing
096   bias may explain the challenges in reconstructing functional models from weight representations.
097
098
099
      Not All Eigenvalues Are Equally Important Recent work by Balestriero & LeCun (2024) inves-
      tigates the usefulness of features learned by AE through reconstruction for image perception tasks.
100
      They find that low-eigenvalue features are considerably more useful than high-eigenvalue features.
101
      Inspired by this finding, we explore whether a similar phenomenon exists in weight space that might
102   explain the challenges in generating functional models from smooth weight representations. To
103   that end, we train three different model zoos of small convolutional neural networks (CNN) on
104   the SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009) and EuroSAT (Helber et al., 2019)
105   datasets. Following a similar approach to that of Balestriero & LeCun (2024), we represent the
106   weights as a flattened vector. We then perform a projection akin to PCA by selecting certain eigen-
107   vectors of the data covariance matrix. We reconstruct the models using the weights after projection
      and inverse projection, and test the resulting models on their respective test sets.

134   Weight Reconstruction Requires Low and High Eigenvalues On all three datasets, the models

139
      to 31% with the Top and 22% with the Bottom eigenvectors.
140
141   The results show that in order to reconstruct models that performs well, we need the features in the
142   subspaces spanned by both the top and bottom eigenvectors, but not so much those in the middle.
143
      Schürholt et al. (2022a; 2024); Soro et al. (2024) only use a MSE reconstruction loss in their work,
      and we know from Balestriero & LeCun (2024) that because of this, the AE network will mostly
144
      focus on the data subspace spanned by the top eigenvectors, as it is the most useful for reconstruction.
145
      Knowing this, we need further guidance to make the model also focus on the other features essential
146   to reconstruct high-performing NNs. In the following, we show how we develop a behavioral loss
147   to achieve this goal.

150   3   L EARNING F ROM S TRUCTURE AND B EHAVIOR
151
152   Building upon the limitations of autoencoders (AEs) that use a structural loss discussed in Section 2,
153   where the inherent bias towards coarse features leads to reconstructed neural networks (NNs) with
154   degraded functional performance, we propose a behavioral loss to enhance the fidelity of the recon-
155   structed models. Inspired by perceptual losses in computer vision (Esser et al., 2021), our behavioral
156   loss emphasizes the functional similarity between the original and reconstructed NNs.
157

226   The Choice of Queries Impacts the Learned Representations Two NNs trained on the same
227   dataset with similar performance on the domain of the training data can have different behavior
228   outside that domain. Similarly, since the behavioral loss depends on the queries used to compute
229   it, the choice of those will influence which aspects of the behavior of the original models will be
230   reconstructed. If the queries used for the behavioral loss come from a different domain than the
231   one of the training data, the AE will attempt to match the behavior of the original and reconstructed
232
      model on parts of the domain where the NN from the zoo did not have training data, and where
      its behavior is therefore ill-defined. This implies that the reconstructed and original model will
233
      match performance on parts of the domain that is of no interest, while on the parts of interest the
234
      performance might be arbitrarily different. Hence, the choice of the queries plays an important
235   role in the performance of the reconstructed models. In Appendix D.3 we explore the hypothesis
236   that randomly generated queries lead to poor performing reconstructed models, while queries from
237   the same or similar distribution as the training data lead to good performance of the reconstructed
238   models.

      4     E XPERIMENTS

      In the following, we build on the conclusions of Section 3 and experimentally evaluate whether the
      inclusion of a behavioral element to the loss function leads to better performance on a selection
244
      of downstream tasks from the literature. We first describe our general experimental setup, and then
245
      compare different variants of our loss functions for each individual downstream task: discriminative,
246   reconstructive, and generative.
247
248
      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
249
250   Model Zoos In this work, we understand a model zoo as a structured population of models using
251   the same architecture and trained on the same data. We train three different model zoos of convo-
252   lutional neural networks (CNN), on the SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009)
253   and EuroSAT (Helber et al., 2019) datasets. Every model zoo is built with the same grid of hyper-
254   parameters. In total, every zoo is composed of 1, 200 models, with 10, 853 parameters each, trained
255   over 50 epochs. We provide additional details about the model zoos generation, their architecture
256   and their hyperparameters in Appendix A. The model zoos are randomly separated into disjoint
257
      train, validation and test splits with respective proportions {80%, 5%, 15%}.
258
259   Loss Functions We use the composite loss function defined in Equation 1. It combines three
260   elements: a contrastive loss in latent space LC , and two losses in reconstructed space, one structural
261
      LS , one behavioral LB . Their relative weights are controlled by two hyperparameters γ, β ∈ [0, 1].
      When γ = 0 the contrastive loss is not used; when β = 0 the structural loss is not used; and when
262
      β = 1 the behavioral loss is not used. When using both the contrastive loss and a reconstructive
263
      loss, we set γ = 0.05, as in the original SANE implementation. When using both the structural
264   and behavioral loss, we set β = 0.1. This value follows from an exploration of possible values
265   on our validation set and has shown to perform best, as detailed in Appendix D.6. The case where
266   γ = 0.05 and β = 1 corresponds to the existing SANE implementation and serves as our baseline.
267   We implement the contrastive loss LC the same way as in SANE, with NTXent (Sohn, 2016) where
268   the augmentations used are permutations of weights that do not alter the behavior of the NN. LS and
269   LB are implemented using the mean-squared-error loss, which is equivalent to the L2 loss up to a
      constant factor and a square root.

293   Schürholt et al. (2024), where the weights of the original model are tokenized, then fed to an encoder

      epochs {20, 30, 40, 50}. We provide additional details about hyper-representation AEs training in

      4.2.1    D ISCRIMINATIVE D OWNSTREAM TASKS
312
313   We consider two different discriminative downstream tasks, where we try to predict either a model’s
314   test accuracy or its generalization gap (defined as the difference between the train and test accura-
315   cies). To do so, we compute its hyper-representation using AEs trained with different elements of the
316   composite loss. Following existing evaluation setups from the literature, we average all embedded
317   tokens together into a 64 dimensional “center of gravity” of the embeddings, and use a linear probe
318   on top of it. The probe is trained on the models in the train split, and evaluated on the held-out test
319   split. We show results in Table 1.
320   The results show that although we built mostly to improve reconstructive accuracy, our behavioral
321   loss also improves the performance of our discriminative downstream tasks: in all cases, the most
322   performant loss combination includes both LS and LB . Since we use linear probes, that means that
323   the behavioral loss plays a part in better structuring the latent space, and that combining it with the
      structural loss synergizes well.

356   With the addition of the behavioral loss, we target reconstructed models to be similar to the origi-
357
      nals, both in structure and behavior. Our model zoos are diverse, and contain both high- and low-

364
      a model weights and its reconstruction. We note that using LS is sufficient and necessary to get low
365
      structural reconstruction errors, with large differences between models that use it and those that do
366   not. We then measure the behavioral distance between models and their reconstruction using model
367   agreement. Conversely to the structural distance, we note that using the behavioral loss does not

      4.2.3   G ENERATIVE D OWNSTREAM TASKS
409
410
411
      In addition to reconstructing exisiting models, fixing the hyper-representation AE’s ability to output
      performant model weights opens up the way for generating new model weights. In their existing
412
      work, Schürholt et al. (2022a) generate models using their hyper-representations, but their perfor-
413
      mance is limited and they have to be re-trained for a few epochs to achieve performance comparable
414   to models in the original zoo. In the following, we evaluate whether hyper-representation AEs
415   trained with a behavioral loss can perform better at model weights generation.
416
417
      To generate model weights, we first need to generate corresponding synthetic latent representations.
      To do so, we select anchor models from the model zoo that are themselves well-performing (more
418
      details in Appendix B) and compute their latent representations. With the goal of mitigating the curse
419
      of dimensionality, we then use PCA to project those in a lower dimensional space of size 32, and use
420   a kernel density estimate (KDE) to model the distribution of the projected embeddings. For the KDE,
421   we further assume that the coordinates are orthogonal to each other. We generate new data points
422   by sampling from that KDE, and inverse project back into the latent representation space. Finally,
423   we feed these synthetic hyper-representations to the decoder and evaluate the resulting models. We
424   show results in Figure 3.
425
      We first note that when using the baseline hyper-representation AE, the generated models tend to
426   be as good as or slightly worse than the reconstructed models. This level of performance is low
427   compared to the anchors we use for generation, which for all three datasets have more than 60%
428   test accuracy. On the other hand, when adding our behavioral element to the loss function, we see
429   that the generated models have a very high test accuracy, very close to the best models in the model
430   zoo, as reported in Table 2. Their performance seem to match that of the best reconstructed models.
431   Finally, we show in Appendix Table 6 that generated models are somewhat diverse, although less
      than the original anchors. This confirms that we do not generate identical models.

      Weight Space Representation Learning Recent work in representation learning on NN weights
456
      has led to various approaches for analyzing and generating models weights. Hyper-Representations
457
      (Schürholt et al., 2021; 2022b;a; Soro et al., 2024) use an encoder-decoder architecture with con-
458   trastive guidance to learn weight representations for property prediction and model generation. Us-
459   ing a different learning task for weight generation, other methods employ diffusion on weights (Pee-
460   bles et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024). Our work differs by focusing on understanding
461   and mitigating the inductive biases in weight space learning, particularly for AE-based approaches.
462   Similar to our behavioral loss, HyperNetworks use the learning feedback from the target models to
463   generate their weights (Ha et al., 2017; Knyazev et al., 2021; 2023). Graph representation meth-
464   ods (Zhang et al., 2019; Kofinas et al., 2023; Lim et al., 2023), Neural Functionals (Zhou et al.,
465   2023a;b; 2024) and related approaches like Deep Weight Space (DWS)(Navon et al., 2023; Zhang
466
      et al., 2023) learn equivariant or invariant representations of weights. While these methods incor-
      porate geometric priors of the weight space in encoder or decoder models, we focus in this work
467
      on AE-based approaches, as they cover the largest breadth of downstream tasks. Augmentations
468
      specific to weight space learning have also been developed (Shamsian et al., 2024). As mentioned
469   in Section 4.1, our experiments focus on augmentations that do not alter the behavior of the NN, and
470   therefore do not include such augmentations.
471
472
473   Probing-Based Losses in Weight Space Other works have leveraged losses that are based on
474   the response of models given a set of queries, first of which are HyperNetworks (Ha et al., 2017).
475   De Luigi et al. (2023) use weight-space auto-encoders, but they feed the queries directly to the de-
476   coder which is then trained on the true labels. Navon et al. (2023) propose an architecture that can
477
      be used in many different setups, one of them being domain adaptation, where they use the loss
      on the new domain. More closely related to our work, Herrmann et al. (2024) leverage behavioral
478
      losses (which they name functionalist) to analyze recurrent neural networks (RNNs) with high per-
479
      formance. Their work differs from ours as they use the decoder as an emulator of the function
480   represented by the original model, whereas we directly reconstruct the original model. Navon et al.
481   (2024) use a composite loss in their deep weight space alignment experiment setup, with elements
482   closely related to structure and behavior but tailored to their particular use-case. For example, they
483   use probing-based self-supervised losses as a way to measure linear mode connectivity between two
484   models, rather than to compare their individual behavior. Our work’s exploration of the synergy
485   between structure and behavior gives insights in how incorporating both structural and behavioral
      loss elements can have contributed to the good performance of their model.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Generation of Neural Networks for a Fixed Architecture Different attempts at generating NN
487   weights for a given architecture have been proposed, such as random generation of weights with-
488   out explicit use of data (Schrauwen et al., 2007; Timotheou, 2010), pruning of overparameterized
489   randomly generated networks (Malach et al., 2020), learning weights as a function of the data via
490   imposing a probability measure on the dataset (Bolager et al., 2024), reconstruction of weights by
491   evaluating the network on a specific dataset (Fornasier et al., 2022), etc. Our approach differs be-
492   cause it does not use labelled data, nor does it generates the weights in a data-agnostic sense.
493
494   Inductive Biases of AEs AEs trained with mean squared error (MSE) loss often exhibit an in-
495   ductive bias toward learning coarse, smoothed representations, which can result in the loss of fine-
496   grained details important for certain tasks (Alain & Bengio, 2014; Vincent et al., 2010). However,
497
      tasks that rely on high-resolution features, such as texture recognition and fine-grained classifica-
      tion, require the retention of these fine details for optimal performance (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014;
498
      Gatys et al., 2016). To address these limitations, alternative approaches like perceptual losses (John-
499
      son et al., 2016), adversarial training (Makhzani et al., 2016), and variational methods (Kingma &
500   Welling, 2013) have been proposed to preserve fine details in reconstructions. Similar challenges
501   arise in NN weight space learning, where coarse weight reconstructions may lead to suboptimal
502   model performance due to the loss of important structural details in the network’s weights (Li et al.,
503   2016). We take inspiration from the existing work in the computer vision domain to build a behav-
504   ioral loss with the goal of mitigating this issue in weight space.
505
506
      6   D ISCUSSION
507
508
      Limitations This paper highlights the importance of considering both structure and behavior when
509   training AEs in weight space, as both are essential for reconstructing and generating high-performing
510   models. However, since scaling tranformer-based architectures to large sequences is challenging,
511   our exploration is limited to smaller models. Our work focuses on validating our findings and the
512   concept of a behavioral loss: we defer its implementation for larger NNs to future work. Another
513   limitation is the computational overhead, as we need to reconstruct and test generated models at each
514   training step. In Appendix D.4 however, we show that for comparable computing time, using struc-
515   ture and behavior still outperforms fully-structural approaches. Finally, as shown in Appendix D.3,
516   using a proper set of queries that is close to that used to train the model zoo is important. This
517
      means that conversely to the fully-structural approach, there is a need for unlabeled data samples
      when using the behavioral loss.
518
519
      Conclusion Our work presents an in-depth exploration of the weight-space modality in the con-
520
      text of representation learning, presenting strong synergies in combining structural and behavioral
521
      signals when learning from populations of trained NNs. We first show that to reconstruct accurate
522   models, weight-space AEs need to focus on features that are spanned by both the top and bottom
523   eigenvectors of the data covariance matrix, while those in the middle matter less. Since fully struc-
524   tural AEs tend to mostly focus on features spanned by the top eigenvectors, we build a behavioral
525   loss function to guide the learning of weight-space AEs towards the other features that are essen-
526   tial to reconstruct high-performing models, and theoretically explore how it differs from a purely
527   structural loss. Finally, we demonstrate experimentally that adding a behavioral element into the
528   loss function of weight-space AEs synergizes with the structural element, with the resulting models
529   outperforming the purely structural baseline for discriminative, reconstructive as well as generative
530
      downstream tasks. More generally, our work shows that training self-supervised models in weight
      space requires a balance between structural and behavioral features to perform well. Our analysis
531
      uncovers hitherto unknown insights and opens up exciting research opportunities in the domain of
532
      weight-space representation learning.

790   100 epochs, and on a training set composed of 4, 080 checkpoints (1, 020 different models, training
791   epochs {20, 30, 40, 50}). When the behavioral loss is not used, training takes around 4, 400 seconds
792   or a little less than 1 hour and 15 minutes, while when using the behavioral loss, training takes around
793
      8, 300 seconds or a little more than 2 hours and 15 minutes. There is a notable difference because
      instead of only comparing tokens to tokens, the reconstructed tokens have to be converted back into
794
      a usable model, and then a forward pass must be done over the reconstructed model. Fortunately,
795
      computing this for each model in a batch is parallelized, hence why the difference in computation
796   time is still under a factor of 2. While indeed more expensive than the fully-structural approach, we
797   deem the trade-off for increased performance acceptable.

937   D.3   U SING THE R IGHT Q UERIES I S E SSENTIAL
938
939   While computing the structural loss is a straightforward MSE between the weights of the original
940   model and its reconstruction, comparing their behavior requires using some data, which we have
941   named the queries X = {xi }ni=1 . As discussed in Section 3, this raises the question of what data
942   points to use as queries. In this context, we name dataset from which we sample the n queries the
943
      query set. In this Appendix, we discuss the choice of the query set.
944   Let a model fθj be trained over some training dataset (X, y). First, since the behavioral loss only
945   compares the outputs of fθj and its reconstruction fθ̂j , we do not need y and can discard it. We
946   therefore focus on X, and its distribution p(X). Indeed, since the input domain X can be very large,
947   e.g., all pixel values in a 32 × 32 × 3 image, we have guarantees to sample from the subdomain of
948   interest only if we are restricted to a very small subset, e.g., natural images. Similarly, the behavior
949   of fθj is well defined only where p(X) has high probability, as it is the domain it has been trained
950   on. The existence of adversarial examples (Goodfellow et al., 2014) shows that even in the close
951
      vicinity of p(X), the behavior of fθj is ill-defined. It follows that for our behavioral loss to be
      effective at reconstructing NNs of similar performance, it needs to be computed as close as possible
952
      to p(X).
953
954   We experimentally validate this hypothesis. The setup is the same as the one described in Sec-
955   tion 4.1. We train hyper-representation AEs on all three of our model zoos, using all three of the
956   contrastive LC , structural LS and behavioral LB losses. Here, however, we vary the query set used
957
      when computing the behavioral loss during training. The baseline, which we name “Zoo trainset”,
      is the same setup as used in Section 4, where the queries are samples from the training set of the
958
      corresponding model zoo. The first variation consists in using natural images, but from a different
959
      dataset. This could correspond to a setup where the hyper-representation model does not have ac-
960   cess to the zoo’s training set and uses some default dataset instead. To that end, we use the STL-10
961   dataset (Coates et al., 2011), resized to 32 × 32 × 3, which we name “STL-10 32”. Finally, we
962   also test using data points sampled uniformly from X , i.e., uniform noise; we name this query set
963   “Random”.
964
      We show the results for the discriminative downstream tasks in Figure 7. As hypothesized, we see
965
      large gaps in performance depending on the query set used. In all cases, when using the Random
966   query set the performance is low. For both SVHN and EuroSAT, we also see a large gap in perfor-
967   mance between Zoo trainset and STL-10 32. For CIFAR-10, however, the performance when using
968   STL-10 32 is very good, even surpassing Zoo trainset. Indeed, STL-10 is a dataset that also contains
969   natural images, and uses similar classes as CIFAR-10. The distributions of SVHN and EuroSAT,
970   respectively house numbers and satellite imaging, are farther in distribution from STL-10.

1042   As discussed in Appendix B, training hyper-representation models with a behavioral loss takes

       around twice as much computing time as with only a structural loss. There, we concluded that

1044
       This raises, however, the question of the validity of the behavioral loss in a compute-constrained set-
1045
       ting. In this Section, we evaluate the performance of hyper-representation AEs trained with either
1046   the structural or both the structural and behavioral loss, but for a similar computing budget.
1047
1048   To that end, we consider the experiment setup as defined in Section 4.1. We compare the hyper-
1049
       representation model trained with the contrastive LC and structural LS losses with the one trained

1050
       hyper-representation LC + LS after 100 epochs of training, and compare it to the LC + LS + LB
1051
       after only 50 epochs; the computing budget allocated to both is therefore comparable.
1052
1053   When comparing the discriminative downstream tasks’ performance in Figure 5, we clearly see
1054   that the performance of the hyper-representation trained with LC + LS + LB at 50 epochs is in all
1055
       cases higher than that of the one trained on LC + LS at 100 epochs. While the performance of the
       latter seems to increase more rapidly, it reaches a plateau relatively early in the training process; the
1056
       former, however, keeps improving steadily.
1057
1058   We further investigate the reconstructive downstream tasks in Figure 9. There, we see that even
1059   with less training epochs, the models reconstructed using the behavioral loss LB reach higher levels

1061
       EuroSAT zoo, we fail to reconstruct models as performant as the best models in the zoo: this shows
