011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Detection transformers (DETR) have emerged as powerful end-to-end learning
014            frameworks for object detection, directly regressing detection parameters as point
015            estimates. However, these networks often lack the ability to express any uncer-
016
               tainty within their estimates. In this work, we replace the regression of point esti-
               mates with the direct learning of the posterior distribution in a sampling-free man-
017
               ner by leveraging deep evidential learning, complementing the end-to-end DETR
018
               architecture. We present an instance-aware uncertainty framework by extending
019            evidential deep learning with an IoU-aware loss, jointly modelling both classifica-
020            tion and localization uncertainties. Furthermore, we enable the model to leverage
021            its uncertainty for self-calibration, aligning the predicted probabilities with the
022            true likelihood of outcomes, and effectively apply evidential deep learning for
023            the task of imbalanced dense object detection. Our approach is easily extensi-
024            ble and requires only fine-tuning, thus leveraging the pre-training of transformers
025            on large datasets. We conduct extensive experiments on two in-domain and three
026            out-of-domain datasets, demonstrating impressive improvements in generalization
027
               performance, especially when fine-tuning on heavily imbalanced datasets charac-
               terized by data scarcity.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Object detection is a fundamental aspect of machine vision, including in safety-critical applications
      such as perception in autonomous vehicles (Balasubramaniam & Pasricha, 2022). While detection
034
      accuracy is crucial, it is equally important to quantify the uncertainty associated with the detection
035
      estimates. However, state-of-the-art object detection networks predominantly rely on deep neu-
036   ral networks, which are often viewed as black-box models (Liang et al., 2021). This presents a
037   significant challenge as it becomes difficult to assess the trustworthiness of these networks and to
038   understand their decision-making processes. Despite notable advancements in deep learning and
039   computer vision over the past decade, there remains a significant gap in research in understanding
040   and quantifying the uncertainty associated with the outputs of such object detection networks.
041
      Addressing uncertainty in object detection is particularly challenging because it must account for
042   both localization and classification uncertainties (Feng et al., 2021). Localization uncertainty in-
043   volves the accuracy of predicted bounding boxes, which can be affected by factors such as occlu-
044   sions and complex backgrounds. Meanwhile, classification uncertainty pertains to the confidence
045   in the predicted class labels, often influenced by ambiguous visual features and out-of-distribution
046   data. The need to jointly model these uncertainties complicates the development of effective de-
047   tection systems, as traditional methods often treat them separately, as a multi-task problem (Oksuz
048   et al., 2020). This separation can hinder the ability to provide a comprehensive understanding of the
049   overall uncertainty associated with each detection.
050   Previous studies addressing uncertainty in object detection for autonomous vehicles have typically
051   relied on sampling-based methods, such as Bayesian neural networks and Monte Carlo dropout
052   (Gawlikowski et al., 2023). While these methods are effective in quantifying uncertainty, they are
053   computationally expensive, require multiple runs to sample different results, and often result in re-
      duced overall accuracy due to factors such as biased sampling, improper weighting, and the removal


                                                        1

054
      of parameters caused by dropout in Monte Carlo methods (Feng et al., 2021). Ever since the emer-
055   gence of evidential deep learning (Sensoy et al., 2018; Amini et al., 2020), there has been increased
056   interest in leveraging evidence theory for object detection (Park et al., 2023; Nallapareddy et al.,
057   2023), estimating uncertainty by predicting prior distributions of posterior models in a sampling-
058   free manner. However, the existing object detection networks required various post-processing
059   techniques to derive the final object estimates, and incorporating evidential learning necessitated
060   sophisticated modifications to the base detection networks requiring significant pre-training.
061   Moreover, evidential deep learning is not without its challenges, particularly concerning biases that
062   can arise in the context of object detection. One significant issue is class imbalance (Xia et al.,
063   2022), which is prevalent in real-world driving scenarios due to the varying densities of objects in
064   each frame. When certain object classes are observed significantly more frequently than others,
065   the evidential model may develop a bias towards these dominant classes. This bias can lead to
066   overconfidence in predictions for the more common classes while underestimating uncertainty for
067   less frequent classes, resulting in poor performance in detecting under-sampled objects.
068   Another approach is to focus on model calibration (Pathiraja et al., 2023; Kuzucu et al., 2024; Munir
069   et al., 2024) which aims to align the predicted class probabilities and bounding box localization with
070   the true likelihood of outcomes. By refining both the confidence scores and the spatial accuracy of
071   bounding boxes produced by object detection models, calibration techniques can also provide more
072   reliable estimates without the need for multiple forward passes through the network.
073   Recently, Carion et al. (2020) demonstrated that detection transformers (DETR) can be effectively
074   used for end-to-end object detection, by framing it as a sequence-to-sequence problem, regressing
075   detection estimates as an unordered sequence i.e. as a set prediction problem. Their approach elimi-
076   nates the need for hand-crafted components such as Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS) and anchor
077   formulation. In this paper, we extend DETR with evidential deep learning to incorporate uncertainty
078   estimation, enabling the direct output of detection estimates and their associated uncertainties in an
079   end-to-end, sampling-free manner.

081   1.1   P ROBLEM FORMULATION AND CONTRIBUTIONS
082
083   In this work, we address multiple challenges:
084
085         1. Evidential deep learning is typically ill-posed for object detection due to the significant
086            class imbalance between foreground and background objects, as well as their varying den-
087            sities from frame to frame. In this work, we address class imbalance and adapt evidential
088            deep learning for object detection. Furthermore, we leverage model uncertainty during
089
               training to implicitly self-calibrate model confidence to align with the true likelihood of
               outcomes, using the model calibration error to modulate uncertainty regularization.
090
091         2. Existing methods address localization and classification uncertainty in object detection as
092            independent tasks within a multi-task framework. We present an instance-aware uncer-
093            tainty framework by extending evidential deep learning with an IoU-aware loss, enabling
094            the joint modelling of both localization and classification uncertainties.
095         3. Previous works in the literature that incorporated evidential deep learning in object detec-
096            tion required sophisticated transforms to their base object detection networks. In this pa-
097            per, we propose a simple, easily extensible, end-to-end approach for integrating evidential
098            learning into object detection using DETR. To the best of our knowledge, this represents
099            the first adaptation of DETR to incorporate evidence theory within its learning framework
100            for supervised learning. Our approach is easily extensible and can be applied to any DETR
101            architecture. Our method does not require any pre-training, i.e. it can be implemented
102            using only fine-tuning.
103         4. DETR supervises one-to-one matching and utilizes a non-differentiable bipartite graph
104            matching for ranking object queries, which can lead to training instability due to stochastic
105            optimization. We incorporate uncertainty estimates during the matching process, provid-
106            ing a probabilistic framework for informed decision-making in the assignment of object
107            queries to ground truth instances. This approach not only improves generalization but also
               reduces the model variations observed across multiple training sessions.

      2     BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
109
110
      Prior research employing evidential deep learning in the context of supervised object detection is
111
      quite limited. This includes Park et al. (2023), who argued that evidential deep learning (Sensoy
112   et al., 2018) couldn’t be directly applied to traditional object detection networks such as Faster R-
113   CNN (Girshick, 2015) and SSD (Liu et al., 2016). They noted that learning the parameters of a
114   posterior Dirichlet distribution (Jsang, 2018) for dense object detection, as opposed to simple clas-
115   sification, lead to training instability and overly uncertain predictions. To address the limitations
116   associated with learning a Dirichlet distribution as originally proposed by Sensoy et al. (2018), they
117   augmented the learning framework and introduced a new Model Evidence Head module to effec-
118   tively harness evidential learning for object detection. However, networks like Faster R-CNN and
119   SSD still require significant overhead, including anchor formulation and non-maximum suppression
120
      (NMS).
121   Additionally, Nallapareddy et al. (2023) proposed an extension to CenterNet (Zhou et al., 2019) and
122   modelled uncertainty as Gaussian heatmaps utilizing 3D convolutional layers to estimate objectness
123   uncertainty as a binary value. However, their method was inherently conditioned on the compute-
124   intensive operation of estimating high-resolution heatmaps, and required additional regularization
125   parameters for accurate localization of object centeredness. While CenterNet is more efficient com-
126
      pared to traditional detection models, it still necessitates post-processing to derive final predictions
      from Gaussian heatmaps.
127
128   In contrast, our approach, based on the DETR framework (Carion et al., 2020), simplifies the train-
129   ing process by eliminating the need for anchor boxes and NMS, which are common complications
130   in traditional object detection methods. Traditional models often struggle with the design and tuning
131   of anchor boxes, which can lead to suboptimal performance if not carefully calibrated. Furthermore,
132
      the reliance on post-processing techniques like NMS can introduce additional computational over-
      head and potential errors in object localization. Our method, being highly generalizable, can be
133
      seamlessly applied to any DETR model.

136   3     U NCERTAINTY E STIMATION
137
138   Typically, neural networks are trained to generate predictions by minimizing a loss function that
139   quantifies an error between the predicted outcomes and the actual results. However, these networks
140   often lack a mechanism to express the level of confidence associated with their predictions. More-
141   over, in object detection, biases such as class imbalance can hinder a model’s ability to learn accurate
142   representations of real-world scenarios, as underrepresented classes may not be adequately captured
143   during training, leading to biased predictions and a failure to generalize effectively.
144   To address these challenges, we employ evidential deep learning in conjunction with an instance-
145   aware class balancing loss. Evidential deep learning (Sensoy et al., 2018; Amini et al., 2020) is a
146   framework that extends traditional neural networks by modelling uncertainty in predictions through
147   the use of evidence theory or the Dempster–Shafer Theory of Evidence (Dempster, 1968).
148

287

288

349
350   We extend DETR (Carion et al., 2020), a transformer-based object detection network, into an
351   uncertainty-aware framework by integrating evidential deep learning into its architecture. How-
352   ever, our approach is easily extensible and can be applied to any subsequent DETR iterations (Zhu
353   et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2024). DETR models leverage the attention mechanisms in the encoder-
354   decoder architecture of transformers for the task of detection, treating it as an unordered sequence,
355   i.e. a set prediction problem, rather than relying on traditional methods that involve region proposal
356   networks and non-maximum suppression.

396   Uncertainty-aware ranking DETR establishes a one-to-one correspondence and addresses a
397   global optimization problem utilizing self-attention to generate a distinct bounding box for each
398   prediction. During training, DETR employs Hungarian matching (Kuhn, 1955) which guarantees
399   that each predicted object has a unique match with a ground truth label. However, this results in
400
      training instability and slow convergence. DETR operates with a fixed set of predetermined queries,
      which necessitates careful selection of a subset of these queries to ensure effective performance.
401
402   Several solutions have been proposed to address these challenges. Deformable DETR (D-DETR)
403   (Zhu et al., 2020) introduced a multi-scale deformable attention mechanism, which enables the net-
404   work to dynamically sample locations based on the input features. RT-DETR (Zhao et al., 2024)
405   further iterates the approach by incorporating query denoising techniques and implementing an
406
      uncertainty-minimal query selection scheme.
407   In this work, we present a probabilistic framework for Hungarian matching. In situations where
408   multiple predictions may correspond to a single ground truth, evidential learning aids in resolving
409   ambiguities by offering a probabilistic approach. The model evaluates the likelihood of each po-
410   tential match, enabling it to make more informed decisions regarding the association of predictions
411   with ground truths. By providing richer information about uncertainty, evidential learning enhances
412
      the training signals, leading to improved convergence and performance in the matching process.
      Consequently, the model becomes more adept at distinguishing between confident and uncertain
413
      predictions
