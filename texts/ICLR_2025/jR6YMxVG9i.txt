009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in visual language models (VLMs) have notably enhanced
012            their capabilities in handling complex Graphical User Interface (GUI) interac-
013            tion tasks. Despite these improvements, current frameworks often struggle to
014            generate correct actions in challenging GUI environments. State-of-the-art com-
015            mercial VLMs are black-boxes, and fine-tuning open-source VLMs for GUI tasks
016
               requires significant resources. Additionally, existing trajectory-level evaluation and
               refinement techniques frequently fall short due to delayed feedback and local opti-
017
               mization issues. To address these challenges, we propose an approach that guides
018
               VLM agents with process supervision by a reward model during GUI navigation
019            and control at inference time. This guidance allows the VLM agent to optimize
020            actions at each inference step, thereby improving performance in both static and
021            dynamic environments. In particular, our method demonstrates significant perfor-
022            mance gains in the GUI navigation task setting, achieving a 5% improvement in
023            action accuracy for static environments and a around 15% increase in task success
024            rate in dynamic environments. With further integration of trajectory reflection and
025            retry mechanisms, we also demonstrate even greater enhancement in task success.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Recent advances in VLMs have significantly enhanced their capabilities in understanding, reasoning,
030
      and generalizing, enabling them to handle complex real-world GUI interaction tasks (Hong et al.,
031   2024b; You et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024). For instance, given an instruction like “How do I get to
032   the nearest Walmart?”, a VLM agent is expected to navigate to the Google Maps application, search
033   for Walmart locations in the vicinity, and select the nearest one to initiate route navigation. These
034   advancements greatly improve the accessibility and efficiency of GUI interaction tasks.
035
      However, even state-of-the-art visual language models (VLMs) like GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023), Gemini
036
      1.5 Pro (Reid et al., 2024) and others, as well as interaction agent frameworks like Yan et al. (2023);
037   Wang et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024), still struggle to generate correct actions when completing
038   GUI tasks such as VisualWebarena (Koh et al., 2024), OSWorld (Xie et al., 2024) and others. These
039   commercial VLMs are typically black-box models, making them inaccessible for tuning, and further
040   fine-tuning open-source VLMs for GUI tasks remains resource-intensive. Additionally, Pan
041   et al. (2024) introduce a technique where GPT-4V serves as an evaluator to assess task success and
042   provide reflection for retrying in case of failure, which can enhance the performance of agents in GUI
043   navigation and control. However, such evaluation and refinement methods at the end of a trajectory
044   will lead to local optimization deficiency and delayed feedback. Evaluating only at the end of the
045
      trajectory can result in insufficient optimization of individual actions, overlooking the refinement
      needed at each step. In GUI tasks, where each step impacts the final outcome, neglecting step-by-step
046
      optimization may degrade overall performance. Moreover, trajectory-level evaluation delays error
047
      correction, increasing both computational and time costs. Meanwhile, Bai et al. (2024) propose
048   DigiRL, improving task performance in dynamic environments by combining Advantage-Weighted
049   Regression with online reinforcement learning (RL) and an automatic curriculum mechanism. Such
050   RL methods can lead to high computational and time costs, along with a complex training process
051   that requires extensive online interaction data. Moreover, the training of RL algorithms is often
052   unstable due to the sparsity and uncertainty of feedback, as well as the inherent trade-off between
053   exploration and exploitation. These factors contribute to the high training cost and prolonged training
      time, particularly in dynamic and complex environments.

      To address these limitations, we propose GuidNav, guiding the VLM agent with a process reward
078   model during interaction inference in GUI navigation and control tasks. Our empirical findings,
079   along with OpenAI’s o1 results (OpenAI, 2024), show that increasing effort during inference can
080   significantly improve performance. Furthermore, there are strong reasons to favor process supervision
081   through a reward model. It offers more precise feedback by identifying the exact step where an
082   error occurs. This approach directly rewards models for following a path to success. In contrast,
083   models guided by trajectory-based outcome supervision often take inefficient actions, deviating from
084   the correct path and requiring additional effort to correct. Process-based rewards can help reduce
085   these deviations, leading to a more efficient action trajectory. Guiding the VLM agent with this
086
      process reward model enables the agent to learn which actions are effective for achieving the given
      goal within the GUI task environment. As illustrated in Figure 1, to achieve this guidance, we first
087
      train a process reward model based on a limited amount of human demonstrations and synthesized
088
      data generated by the VLM. This process reward model learns the feedback signal from GUI data,
089   guiding the VLM agent during GUI navigation inference to ensure it executes optimal actions. By
090   providing process reward feedback at each inference step, the VLM agent can more accurately adjust
091   its behavior. This fine-grained optimization enhances the success rate of tasks, especially in complex
092   GUI environments where the correct execution of each step is crucial. Furthermore, unlike delayed
093   feedback in trajectory-level evaluation, step-level process rewards enable the model to learn and
094   adapt to environmental changes in real-time, preventing the accumulation of errors caused by delayed
095   feedback. Additionally, like the demonstration in Figure 1 (c), our process reward model can also
096   been integrated into outcome supervision pipline to further improve action generation and selection.
097   We evaluate GuidNav in both static and dynamic settings within Android-in-the-Wild (AitW) (Rawles
098   et al., 2024b), measuring action accuracy at each step based on existing annotations and overall task
099   success through human evaluation. The experimental results demonstrate that our method improves
100   GPT-4o by around 5% in action accuracy of static environments and about 15% in task success rate
101   within dynamic environments. With trajectory reflection and retry mechanisms, the success rate can
102   reach a peak of 75.9%. In summary, our contributions are primarily in the following three areas:
103
                 • We introduce GuidNav, an approach that guides VLM agents for action decision during GUI
104
                   interactions through a process reward model.
105
106
                 • Our approach can be easily integrated into trajectory-level refinement to further strengthen
                   the performance.
107
                 • We show that our method can enhance VLM agents in both static and dynamic settings.

      2     R ELATED W ORK

110   GUI Navigation Agents and Benchmarks Previous GUI navigation agents primarily focused on text-
111   based prompts describing the environment, such as the HTML code, Document Object Model (DOM),
112   or accessibility trees. However, current research leverages both screenshots and text instructions to
113
      navigate interfaces more akin to human-environment interactions. For instance, Auto-UI (Zhan &
      Zhang, 2023) utilizes GUI data to tune the language and projection modules, enabling interaction in
114
      multimodal GUI environments without the need for environment parsing or application-dependent
115
      API access. AppAgent (Yang et al., 2023b) employs the vision capabilities of large language models
116   to operate smartphone applications in a human-like manner. Mobile-Agent-v2 (Wang et al., 2024)
117   presents a multi-agent architecture for assisting mobile device operations. OS-Copilot (Wu et al.,
118   2024) accelerates the development of computer agents on Linux and MacOS by providing a universal
119   interaction interface. MM-Navigator (Yan et al., 2023) generates executable actions based on the
120   screen image, text instructions, and interaction history. CogAgent (Hong et al., 2024b) leverages
121   extensive GUI grounding data to further train the VLM for enhanced interaction. Additionally,
122   several works focus on visual interaction tasks across app, web, and OS environments. AitW (Rawles
123   et al., 2024b) and Weblinx (Lù et al., 2024) use human demonstrations to evaluate the accuracy
124
      of proposed actions. Osworld (Xie et al., 2024), AgentStudio (Zheng et al., 2024), AndroidWorld
      (Rawles et al., 2024a), and Visualwebarena (Koh et al., 2024) provide simulation environments for
125
      executing arbitrary agent trajectories in various domains and tasks. While these are not yet perfect,
126
      they serve as suitable platforms for assessing agents’ capabilities.
127
128   Evaluation by Reward and Reinforcement Learning Methods Apart from agent framework, some
129   researchers use reinforcement learning and reward models to enhance VLM agents further. Pan
130
      et al. (2024) introduce an Autonomous Evaluator for agent behavior, refining the agent’s ability
      through reflection or fine-tuning based on filtered behavior cloning data. Bai et al. (2024) improve
131
      task performance in dynamic environments by combining Advantage-Weighted Regression with
132
      online reinforcement learning and an automatic curriculum mechanism. Zhai et al. (2024) employ a
133   reinforcement learning method with a game-rule-based reward to strengthen VLM-powered agents
134   in Gym Cards and ALFWorld. Fereidouni & Siddique (2024) utilize a two-stage learning process:
135   Supervised Learning, where human demonstration data maps state to action, and Unsupervised
136   Learning, where the PPO algorithm fine-tunes by optimizing policy gradients. A language model
137   calculates action probabilities based on user goals and observations. Compared to process reward
138   models, these methods often struggle to provide fine-grained step-level feedback and tend to incur
139   significantly higher training costs.
140   Discussion Unlike methods like DigiRL (Bai et al., 2024) and Autonomous Evaluator (Pan et al.,
141   2024) that rely on trajectory-level feedback or high computational training costs, GuidNav provides
142   step-level rewards, enabling immediate optimization and reducing computational costs. This approach
143   offers more efficient and precise action refinement, improving task performance in both static and
144   dynamic environments.

146   3     M ETHOD
147   The primary task of GUI navigation involves enabling the VLM agent to interpret task instructions
148   and interact with GUI screenshots to achieve a desired goal. Formally, let x represent the task
149   instruction, and let St = {s1 , s2 , . . . , st } represent the sequence of GUI states observed at different
150   time steps t. The VLM agent must generate and select actions At = {a1 , a2 , . . . , at }, where at is
151
      the action taken at time step t, that modify the GUI state from st to st+1 . The overall goal is to
      generate and determine an action sequence A that leads the GUI environment from the initial state s1
152
      to a final state sT that satisfies the task’s objective. The core challenge is to enable the VLM agent
153
      to generate and determine actions at at each time step that are most aligned with the task’s goal g,
154   while minimizing errors or irrelevant operations. The method, GuidNav, we propose consists of a
155   two-stage process aimed at training the process reward model and guide VLMs agent for efficient
156   task execution in GUI task environments at each time step t. The stages are as follows:
157

      3.3   T RAJECTORY R EFINEMENT AND E VALUATION
221
222   The process reward model can also been integrated with the refinement of the trajectories generated
223   by the VLM to further enhance the performance, as depicted in Figure 1 (c).
224
      Trajectory Formation Once an action a is selected and executed, it becomes part of the trajectory
225   T = (s1 , a1 ), (s2 , a2 ), . . . , (st , at ). The trajectory represents the sequence of state-action pairs
226   leading from the initial state toward the task objective.
227
228
      Evaluation and Reflection At the end of each trajectory, the VLM evaluates the success of the
      trajectory in achieving the desired outcome. If the trajectory fails to meet the desired criteria, the
229
      VLM reflects on the reasons for the failure, generating a “reflection thought” that encapsulates the
230
      lessons learned from the unsuccessful attempt. This reflective thought is then incorporated into the
231   retry process, informing the next iteration.
232
233   Reflection and Retry The reflective thought generated by the VLM becomes part of the input for
234
      the next attempt. The VLM uses this enriched input, including the original instruction and the new
      reflective thought, to generate a new trajectory. This iterative process of reflection and retry continues
235
      until the VLM successfully achieves the task objective. Once a successful trajectory is identified, it is
236
      confirmed, and the process is completed.

      4.1   BASELINES
241
242   Direct Prompting (DP) involves directly prompting a Visual Language Model (VLM) to generate an
243   action based on the instruction query, the current screenshot, and a summary of the previous state.
244   TopK is a technique where the model generates the top k most probable actions (Xiong et al., 2023;
245   Tian et al., 2023). In this procedure, while the model generates k actions (with k set to 3 in our work),
246   we simplify the process by always selecting the most probable one (the first action in the list). This
247   ensures that the model still considers multiple possibilities but prioritizes the highest-probability
248   action for execution.
249   Reflection (Shinn et al., 2024) is a framework that improves LLMs’ decision-making abilities in
250   various tasks by using linguistic feedback and episodic memory, achieving significant performance
251   gains in environments.
252
      Autonomous Refinement (AR) (Pan et al., 2024) leverages the Reflexion technique (Shinn et al.,
253
      2024), an agent first attempts a task, and an external evaluator is used to judge whether its attempt
254
      was successful or not. If it is judged as unsuccessful, the agent will be prompted to reflect on the
255   failure and retry. Here, we utilize GPT-4o as the external evaluator.
256
257   DigiRL (Bai et al., 2024) is an autonomous RL approach, for training in-the-wild device control
258
      agents through fine-tuning a pre-trained VLM in two stages: offline RL to initialize the model,
      followed by offline-to-online RL.
259
260
261   4.2   DATASET
262
      Android-in-the-Wild (AitW) (Rawles et al., 2024b) is a large-scale dataset for Android device control,
263
      comprising 715,142 human demonstrations across 30,378 unique instructions. These instructions
264   are divided into four subsets: General, WebShopping, GoogleApps, and Installation. We leverage
265   ground-truth data and self-play data from 300 tasks in each subset to form the training set for the
266   process reward model. Following the approach outlined in Yan et al. (2023), we randomly select 300
267   tasks from the AitW test set to evaluate action accuracy in a static environment. To ensure balanced
268   representation, each subset contributes 75 tasks. For dynamic environment evaluation, we sample
269   120 tasks (30 from each subset) as instruction queries in a simulated setting. We also attach the
      comprehensive action space of AitW in Appendix 8.4.

310

597   8.1   R EWARD A NNOTATION
598
599   To collect training data for the reward model, we utilize the AitW dataset (Rawles et al., 2024b) and
600   employ GPT-4o as the policy model in a static environment for self-play. We automatically label each
601   action at the step level by evaluating its effectiveness. Since the AitW ground truth is represented as
602   coordinates, while our model outputs numeric labels for detected elements, we treat any point within
603   the detected bounding elements as equivalent. According to our rule, a predicted action from GPT-4o
604
      is considered correct if both the action type and gesture match the annotator’s ground truth actions.
      Specifically, our evaluation metrics are based on those used in AitW.
605
606
607          • For click actions: The action is considered correct if the chosen element by action is
608            within 14% of the screen distance from the ground truth coordinate, or if both the ground
609
               truth coordinate and the element selected by generated action fall within the same detected
               bounding box (expanded to 240% of its original size for action matching).
610
611
612
             • For scroll actions: the predicted action is considered correct if the scroll direction (up,
               down, left, or right) matches the ground truth direction.
613
614
615          • For other actions: For other actions: The predicted action is considered correct if the action
616
               type matches the ground truth. However, for the typing action, both the action type and the
               typed content must match the ground truth.

      8.2   S O M S ETTINGS
621
622   SoM utilizes off-the-shelf interactive segmentation models, such as SEEM (Zou et al., 2024) or
623
      SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023), to partition an image into regions of varying granularity. Each region is
      annotated with marks like alphanumeric labels, masks, or bounding boxes. This enhances the VLM’s
624
      ability to interpret and understand elements within the image.
625
626   In our implementation, we maintain the original SoM configuration (input an image, output bounding
627   boxes and corresponding labels, and overlay the labels onto the image). We choose SAM as the
628   segmentation model and, for each identified entity, assign a unique numeric label positioned at the
629
      center. Additionally, we store the labeled screenshot along with the coordinates of each labeled entity
      for subsequent interactions.
630
631   Given the complex structure of GUI interfaces, which often include numerous small entities with
632   relationships such as containment and overlap, we implement specific strategies to ensure accurate
633   interpretation by the VLM. For entities in containment relationships, we retain the identifiers of both
634   the containing and contained entities. In cases of overlapping entities, we prioritize the identifier of
635
      the smaller entity to ensure clarity and precision.
