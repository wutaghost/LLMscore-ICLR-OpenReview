011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Traditional Concept Factorization (CF) methods learn feature of one data
014            point from high-dimensional data space in the form of vector, which leads
015            to the loss of pixel-level neighborhood information in Two-Dimensional (2D)
016
               images. In light of this, we present a novel Visible Multilayer Concept Factor-
               ization for image-data representation, termed VMCF. Specifically, to uncover
017
               deep latent features from complex data, VMCF adopts a multilayer frame-
018
               work, equipped with a ‘Decomposition, Dimensionality reduction and Data
019            reconstruction’ network (D3 -net) in each layer. To obtain locality-preserving
020            features, D3 -net firstly performs adaptive graph regularized concept learning
021            on the input data of each layer. Then, D3 -net performs 2D feature extraction
022            over the obtained basis images in order to reduce the loss of pixel-level neigh-
023            borhood information during dimension plunging. The reconstructed data
024            formed by the improved basis images and coefficient matrix is used as in-
025            put for the next layer. In this way, the dimensions of the original data can
026            gradually decrease at each layer, avoiding information loss caused by sudden
027
               dimensionality reduction. Meanwhile, 2D-reduced basis images can medi-
               ately improve the quality of new data representations. Extensive numerical
028
               experiments on several public image databases have shown that VMCF out-
029
               performs other state-of-the-art algorithms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The number and dimensionality of image data in real-world applications have shown explosive
035
      growth with the popularization of high-definition devices, posing challenges for image data pro-
036   cessing and analysis. Luckily, Representation Learning (RL) techniques can well handle them by
037   extracting latent features, from data with high-dimensionality (Bengio et al., 2013). Therefore, RL
038   has become a trending topic in fields of image processing, data mining and computer vision.
039
      In this paper, we focus on matrix-based RL algorithms since image data is often stored and calculated
040
      in the form of matrix composed of pixel values in practical applications. Matrix Factorization (MF)
041
      has been proven to be powerful for feature learning and clustering (Wang & Zhang, 2012), including
042   classical methods like Principle Component Analysis (PCA) (Wold et al., 1987), Nonnegative Matrix
043   Factorization (NMF) (Lee & Seung, 1999) and Concept Factorization (CF) (Xu & Gong, 2004).
044   Note that, benefiting from nonnegative constraints, NMF and CF are more suitable for processing
045   image data since image pixel values are typically nonnegative data. NMF aims to decompose a
046   nonnegative data matrix X into two matrices, whose product is the approximation to X. One factor
047   indicates basis vectors and the other factor corresponds to the parts-based representations. Benefit
048   by non-negative constraints, the basis vectors obtained from NMF are also non-negative and can be
049   considered as part of the original images. Taking facial image as an example, the obtained basis
050
      vectors may be local parts of the face such as the nose, eyes, cheeks, etc.
051   On the basis of retaining the non-negative constraints, CF differs from NMF in that it can also be
052   performed in the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space by further decomposing the base matrix into
053   X itself and an auxiliary coefficient matrix. With both nonlinearity and high interpretability, CF
      yields unusually brilliant results in data dimensionality reduction and clustering tasks (Li et al.,


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2024). Subsequently, an influx of CF variants have been introduced to cater to diverse application
055   scenarios, for example, robust ones (Yang et al., 2022) and multiview ones (Mu et al., 2023). One of
056   the most common branches is locality-preserving CF (Chen & Li, 2020). In the process of concept
057   factorization, keeping locality manifold information of data points can make sure their relative posi-
058   tional relationships (or similarity relationships) in high-dimensional data space can be retained even
059   in the mapped low dimensional space. That could potentially improve the discriminability of fea-
060   tures, thereby enhancing their performance in subsequent high-level tasks, such as data classification
061   and clustering (Zhang et al., 2021). To achieve this goal, various locality-preserving strategies have
062   been introduced into traditional CF model, which can be roughly divided into three categories, that
063
      is, graph-regularized ones, local coordinate coding driven ones and self-representation based ones.
      Graph-regularized CF methods, represented by Locally Consistent CF (LCCF) (Cai et al., 2010),
064
      typically incorporate the graph Laplacian regularization into CF. Local coordinate coding driven CF
065
      mainly incorporate the principle of local coordinate coding (LCC) into traditional CF to retain local
066   information in data. Illustrative methods in this group include Local Coordinate CF (LCF) (Liu
067   et al., 2013), and Graph-based Local concept coordinate factorization (GLCF) (Li et al., 2015a).
068   Different from above two kinds of CF methods, self-representation based ones regard the input data
069   as dictionary and then construct the affinity matrix using the new representation (i.e., coefficient
070   matrix) rather than the original raw data. Representative methods include SRMCF (Ma et al., 2018)
071   and JSGCF (Peng et al., 2019).
072   However, when dealing with image data, these traditional local preservation CF methods generally
073   face several challenges. First, the local manifold structure between samples cannot be adaptively
074   maintained during concept factorization iterations. Second, existing CF methods represent an image
075   as a one-dimensional vector, and then concatenate a set of vectors into a matrix, which serves as the
076   input for CF. Such approach not only results in loss of pixel-level structural information within the
077   image, but also leads to higher computational complexity due to the large size of the matrix. Third,
078   direct reduction of higher image dimensions to a very small value through a one-step calculation
079   may also lead to the risk of important valuable information loss.
080   In view of this, we propose a novel framework to inherit the advantages of existing CF models and
081   meanwhile overcome above-mentioned drawbacks. Main contributions of this work are summarized
082   as follows:
083
      (1) Technically, a Visible Multilayer Concept Factorization (VMCF), is proposed for image data
084   representation and clustering. Aimed at improving representation power, VMCF is equipped with
085   a ‘Decomposition-Dimensionality reduction-Data reconstruction’ network (shortly, D3 -net) in each
086   layer. For the input data matrix of each layer, D3 -net decomposes them by adaptive graph regularized
087   CF into two nonnegative parts, i.e., set of basis images and coefficient matrix. Then, D3 -net extracts
088   two-dimensional features from the basis images and finally reconstruct the input data based on this.
089
      (2) To achieve local-preserving representations of image data, the innovations of VMCF are twofold.
090   a) preserving sample-level local information by introducing an adaptive adjacency matrix into CF
091   framework to record and update neighborhood relationships between samples; b) preserving pixel-
092   level local information for basis images by restoring them to a set of two-dimensional matrices and
093   perform 2D feature extraction on it. Obtained basis images with higher quality and lower dimensions
094   can not only reduce the computational complexity of the model, but also indirectly improve the
095   accuracy of the coordinate matrix.
096   (3) To uncover hidden deep features, VMCF adopts a multi-layer structure. The multilayer struc-
097   ture can not only excavate deeper features than traditional shallow structures, but also reduce the
098   dimensionality of raw data layer by layer to avoid the loss of valuable information caused by too
099   fast decrease in data dimensionality.
100
      The paper is outlined as follows. In Section 2, we briefly review related works. In Section 3, we
101
      present VMCF in detail. We show the optimization procedures in Section 4. Section 5 describes the
102   simulation results. The paper is finally concluded in Section 6.

516   6                   C ONCLUSION
517
518   We mainly discussed the unsupervised locality-preserved representation learning problem for high-
519   dimensional image data. In order to improve the representation and clustering abilities, we tech-
520   nically proposed a new visible multilayer concept factorization method. To capture hidden deep
521   information while slowly reducing data dimensionality, our VMCF designs a multilayer structure
522   equipped with a D3 -net in each layer. D3 -net jointly incorporates adaptive graph regularized CF, 2D
523   feature extraction for basis images and data reconstruction into a unified framework. In this way,
524
      both sample-level and pixel-level neighboring information can be retained simultaneously. Mean-
      while, learning low dimensional representations of the basis images layer by layer can not only avoid
525
      the loss of valuable information caused by significant dimensionality reduction in one step, but also
526
      indirectly improve the quality of reconstructed data and new data representations. We evaluated
527   VMCF for image representation and clustering, and compared the results with related single-layer
528   and multilayer methods. Extensive results demonstrated the effectiveness of our VMCF. In future,
529   more effective multilayer concept factorization strategy will be investigated.
