009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Causal structure discovery methods are commonly applied to structured data where
012            the causal variables are known and where statistical testing can be used to assess the
013            causal relationships. By contrast, recovering a causal structure from unstructured
014            natural language data such as news articles contains numerous challenges due to
015            the absence of known variables or counterfactual data to estimate the causal links.
016
               Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown promising results in this direction
               but also exhibit limitations. This work investigates LLM’s abilities to build causal
017
               graphs from text documents and perform counterfactual causal inference. We
018
               propose an end-to-end causal structure discovery and causal inference method from
019            natural language: we first use an LLM to extract the instantiated causal variables
020            from text data and build a causal graph. We merge causal graphs from multiple data
021            sources to represent the most exhaustive set of causes possible. We then conduct
022            counterfactual inference on the estimated graph. The causal graph conditioning
023            allows reduction of LLM biases and better represents the causal estimands. We
024            use our method to show that the limitations of LLMs in counterfactual causal
025            reasoning come from prediction errors and propose directions to mitigate them.
026            We demonstrate the applicability of our method on real-world news articles.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Recovering the causal structure of events described in single or multiple text sources is an important
031   problem for natural language understanding, analysis, and prediction. In particular, causal structure
032   discovery from news articles can help build causal world models that can be used to understand the
033   causal chains behind events, forecast future events, and create robust automated reasoning agents
034   (Schölkopf et al., 2021; Bareinboim et al., 2022). This problem is not often tackled in natural
035   language processing (NLP) because it requires solving multiple challenges considered open research
036   problems in causality and NLP research. First, the text modality prevents the direct use of traditional
037   structure discovery methods as the set of causal variables is not available and has to be discovered (a
038   problem being recently tackled by the field of causal representation learning) (Schölkopf et al., 2021).
039
      Second, real-world events have non-trivial structures prone to latent variables and feedback loops,
      typically excluded from most causal analyses using Direct Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) (Bongers et al.,
040
      2021). Third, causal models provide a means to answer interventional and counterfactual queries,
041
      but real-world data prevents the ground truth from being directly accessible. This is the fundamental
042   problem of causal inference (Pearl, 2009): only one factual world can be observed. In addition,
043   real-world events are often complex and multi-causal, greatly hindering the possibility of manually
044   annotating a ground truth.
045
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive abilities to solve tasks related to these
046
      problems, notably for understanding and summarising natural language (Devlin et al., 2019; Brown
047
      et al., 2020; Bubeck et al., 2023). Recent work (Jiralerspong et al., 2024) has also shown that LLMs
048   can recover causal structures, although the authors do not apply it to text data. However, this ability
049   is still debated as the LLM’s performance can drop significantly when facing unfamiliar settings
050   (Jin et al., 2024). Moreover, while being able to perform some causal reasoning tasks successfully
051   (Melnychuk et al., 2022), notably on commonsense causal reasoning (Kiciman et al., 2023; Zhang
052   et al., 2022), LLMs have shown limitations on tasks that require robust reasoning, such as arithmetic
053   tasks in unfamiliar settings (Wu et al., 2023), or abstract reasoning (Gendron et al., 2024). To
      explain this behavior difference, (Zecevic et al., 2023) advanced that LLMs cannot discover new


                                                         1
054
      causal relationships but only recall ones already seen during training. Acknowledging this limitation,
055   alternative approaches have been proposed using LLMs to extract causal relationships from text
056   (Rožanec et al., 2023b; Rozanec et al.). However, such approaches do not formally test whether the
057   extracted relationships are causal.
058
      We investigate ways to overcome this restriction and propose an end-to-end causal structure discovery
059
      and counterfactual inference method from purely unstructured natural language text data. Our
060
      framework is divided into two steps: first, we use an LLM to generate the causal graph associated
061   with a document, i.e., that describes the causal relationships between the events depicted in the text.
062   Optionally, we merge causal graphs from multiple sources using a second LLM. Then, we use the
063   built causal structure to perform an atomic intervention on the sequence of events and infer the
064   consequences in this counterfactual scenario using an LLM (i.e., answer what if? questions). We
065   show that our method can effectively extract causal relationships and propose plausible counterfactual
066   worlds from real-world events.
067   Our contributions can be summarised as follows:
068
069          • We propose a method to perform causal structure discovery and counterfactual inference in
070            an end-to-end and explainable way,
071          • We demonstrate its applicability on real-world events,
072          • We use our method to disentangle the steps required for counterfactual reasoning and
073            highlight the limitations of LLMs. We show that LLMs can fail even when the full reasoning
074            structure is given and that the bottleneck for performance comes from the prediction step.

081   Causal Structure Discovery with LLMs Text data is often unstructured, high-dimensional, and
082   large-scale. Causal variables may not be directly accessible from the text, and the causal relationships
083   are typically vague and rare, with semantic ambiguity complicating analysis. These challenges greatly
084   hinder the usability of traditional causal structure discovery methods and motivate using LLMs for
085   causal structure discovery (Kiciman et al., 2023; Ma, 2024). (Kiciman et al., 2023) have achieved
086
      promising results when using LLMs to infer the causal direction between two variables. Nevertheless,
      there is some evidence that LLMs, in many cases, repeat embedded causal knowledge (Zecevic et al.,
087
      2023) and are susceptible to inferring causal relations from the order of two entities mentioned in a
088
      text (Joshi et al., 2024). (Jiralerspong et al., 2024) attempts to recover the full causal structure using a
089   breadth-first search on a set of text variables. (Hobbhahn et al., 2022) investigates whether LLMs
090   can identify the cause and the effect between two natural language sentences. This line of work uses
091   the LLM’s inner knowledge to discover causal relationships between data points. However, recent
092   work highlighted that LLMs do not conduct proper causal reasoning and mainly rely on domain
093   knowledge and correlations (Zecevic et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024). This approach differs from another
094   line of work that uses the LLM as an information retrieval engine to extract causal relationships
095   explicitly present in the data. For instance, (Gopalakrishnan et al., 2024) uses LLMs to extract causal
096   relationships from medical texts. Our approach combines both worlds as we use an LLM to retrieve
097
      causal relationships from text and then perform causal inference on the extracted model to assess
      the quality of the causal model. NATURAL (Dhawan et al., 2024) is another method developed
098
      concurrently to our work using LLMs to perform causal inference. However, the method is based on
099
      the Potential Outcome Framework (POF) and computes the Average Treatment Effect (ATE) of a
100   variable (i.e., outcome) under an intervention (i.e., a treatment). By contrast, our method is based on
101   Structural Causal Models (SCMs) and includes a causal structure discovery step to represent complex
102   causal relationships between observed variables. Our work also focuses on counterfactuals, while
103   this method is suited to answer interventional queries.
104
105   Strategic Foresight Strategic Foresight aims to provide a structured approach to gathering in-
106   formation regarding plausible future scenarios and adequately preparing for change. It provides
107   expert insights regarding trends and emerging issues that can be considered for strategic planning and
      policy-making. As such, it is being increasingly adopted in the public and private sectors (Burt & Nair,


                                                           2
108
      2020; Rosa et al., 2021). Among the most frequently used methods, we find scenario planning (Ebadi
109   et al., 2022), which aims to foresee relevant scenarios based on trends and factors of influence to
110   understand better how actions can influence the future (Wilkinson, 2017). While the value of artificial
111   intelligence for strategic foresight has been recognized, much of the work is still not automated
112   (Reez, 2020; Brandtner & Mates, 2021). Scientific literature reports on using artificial intelligence
113   for information scanning and analysis (Parrish et al., 2019; Brandtner & Mates, 2021), to identify
114   weak signals and trends (Geurts et al., 2022), and extract actions and outcomes that can be mapped
115   to causal decision diagrams (Pratt et al., 2023). More recently, authors have proposed architectures
116   that could automate strategic foresight. Nevertheless, the proposed architecture only considered a
117
      signal assessment module without explicit reference to testing causality among the extracted graph
      relationships (Rozanec et al., 2023; Rožanec et al., 2023a). We aim to bridge this gap by identifying,
118
      extracting, and testing causal relationships reported in media news to construct a graph of causal
119
      relationships and use such a graph to build plausible future scenarios, providing expert insights for
120   strategic planning and policy-making.

129   This section describes our proposed method for causal structure discovery and causal inference from
130
      text. We build a modified version of the Structural Causal Model (SCM) (Pearl, 2009) to represent
      the described causal mechanisms. Following the SCM framework, we consider that the mechanisms
131
      can be represented as Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs). For every document D, we construct a
132
      DAG G = ⟨U, V, E⟩ where the observed nodes v ∈ V correspond to general depictions of the
133   events in the text. Two nodes (Vi , Vj ) ∈ V × V are connected by an edge Eij = (Vi , Vj ) ∈ E if
134   a causal relationship between them is explicitly mentioned in the text data (according to an LLM).
135   We also represent possible exogenous factors U ∈ U describing unobserved events having a causal
136   influence on the observations. Nodes and edges also have features. Nodes correspond to causal
137   variables and have a domain established during extraction and a current value from this domain.
138   Confounders U are not assigned values because they are not observed. As we aim to use an LLM
139   for inference, we also keep attributes in plain natural language: a description of the variable and
140   additional contextual information. Edges also have a description attribute. For example, given this
141
      sentence: “The airlines companies have seen their revenues diminishing due to travel restrictions.”,
      we can extract the following causal variables S and T , and their relationship: travel restrictions (S) →
142
      airlines revenues (T). Their domains can be, e.g., a boolean for S and a fixed set of categories for T
143
      (since we do not have access to the numerical values of the revenues). Their current values are written
144   S = so and T = to . During counterfactual inference, we intervene to modify these values while the
145   other attributes remain unchanged. For performance, we add contextual information to each node,
146   i.e., background knowledge extracted from the text. In this example, the contextual information could
147   be the country where the event takes place. The edge description can be, e.g., “travel restrictions
148   diminish airlines companies revenues”.
149
      SCMs typically have a set of mapping functions F to infer the value of a variable V given its
150   parents pa(·) (e.g., V ← fV (pa(V ))). Instead of using a set of predefined functions, we perform
151   causal inference using an LLM. We also use it to compute the prior probability distribution of the
152   confounders U . We discuss our method and the implications of using an LLM for inference in
153   Section 3.2. We note a full causal model containing the causal graph G and all these attributes
154   as M = ⟨G, LLM⟩. The instantiated model M(D) describes the model with the values of each
155   variable extracted from the document D. We further note an intervention do(X = x) in this model as
156   MX=x (D) or M(D, do(X = x)).
157   Our proposed method is divided into four stages. First, we extract the causal graph from a text
158   document. If multiple documents are provided, we merge their respective causal graphs together.
159   Then, we compute counterfactual worlds from the resulting causal graph. We use these counterfactuals
160   to self-evaluate the causal graph. Section 3.1 describes the causal graph extraction step. Section 3.2
161   describes the counterfactual inference step. Evaluation is described in Sections 4.3 and 5. Figure 1
      illustrates the complete pipeline.

      3.1          C AUSAL S TRUCTURE D ISCOVERY FROM NATURAL L ANGUAGE
182
      We prompt an LLM to read an input document and return its associated causal graph. The expected
183   graph should contain the full set of observed causal variables, their relationships, and their attributes
184   as described in the introduction of Section 3. We also ask the LLM to estimate the hidden variables
185   affecting the observed events and how they are connected. Estimated hidden variables have the same
186   attributes as the observed ones but do not have an observed value. Other works have studied ways
187   to improve the quality of the generated causal graphs, e.g., via breadth-first search (Jiralerspong
188   et al., 2024). However, to keep our pipeline efficient, we only consider a single forward pass with
189   chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022). We find that this simple choice is sufficient for our
190   purpose. This is not surprising as the causal relationships to be found are explicitly described in the
191
      data and LLMs have been very successful at information retrieval tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Bubeck
      et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024). We prompt the LLM to return its answer in JSON format. Still, it may
192
      not always provide an extractable response. To alleviate this issue, we allow the LLM to refine its
193
      answer several times if it cannot be parsed automatically. If multiple documents are provided, we
194   merge the causal graphs together. We describe this optional step in Appendix A.

      3.2          C OUNTERFACTUAL C AUSAL I NFERENCE
197
198
      Autoregressive LLMs are inference engines computing the conditional probability of an output
199   token Y0 given an input context C: P (Y0 |C). When used for generation, they construct an output
200   sequence Y = [Y0 , . . . , YN ] by computing P (Yi |Yi−1 , . . . , Y0 , C) iteratively. Due to their extensive
201   training on a massive amount of data, LLMs are good estimators of P (Y0 |C) (Brown et al., 2020).
202   However, LLMs are also prone to hallucinations when providing long answers: they deviate from the
203   instructions or state false information (Huang et al., 2023). Indeed, estimating the true conditional
204   distribution of the full output P (Y|C) is more challenging, especially when Y is long as it requires
205   building a probability tree considering all possible values for the intermediate Yi . This tree can
206   be approximated using beam search or heuristic-guided tree search algorithms (Yao et al., 2023;
207
      Wan et al., 2024). However, their performance is still dependent on the output length. We alleviate
      this problem by conditioning the inference query on the causal parents of the output, i.e. instead
208
      of providing the complete context C as an input of the LLM, we use the much smaller subset
209
      pa(Y) ⊂ C. Assuming knowledge of the causal graph, this choice can greatly reduce the size of
210   the context window and mitigate hallucination. In the rest of this section, we assume that the LLM
211   can provide a close estimate of the true conditional distribution P (Y|pa(Y)). We challenge this
212   assumption in our experiments. We investigate LLMs’ causal inference abilities in counterfactual
213   settings given the estimated causal model M. Counterfactual queries answer the question: “How
214   would variable Y change if we had X = x instead of X = x′ ?”. This question can be answered
215   by performing abduction, intervention and prediction. The abduction step estimates the values of
      the exogenous factors U from the observed quantities: P (U |x′ , y ′ ). The intervention step edits the

247   We verify the applicability of our method on synthetic data and use it to investigate the current
248   limitations of LLMs on counterfactual reasoning tasks. Cladder is a synthetic dataset containing small
249   self-contained causal graphs of three of four variables with no unobserved confounders (Jin et al.,
250   2023). Queries in Cladder test the causal capabilities of a model. We focus on the counterfactual
251
      subset. We extract the results for the counterfactuals queries (rung 3, det-counterfactual)1 . Queries
      are divided into commonsense, nonsensical and anti-commonsense categories. Nonsensical queries
252
      are composed of abstract variables not conveying any semantic meaning. Anti-commonsense queries
253
      contain common concepts as variables but with fictive causal relationships. Figure 3 shows an
254   example of anti-commonsense query from Cladder. We conduct experiments using LLaMA-3.1
255   (Dubey et al., 2024), GPT-3.5 (Ouyang et al., 2022), GPT-4 (version 1106), GPT-4o and GPT-4o-mini
256   (OpenAI, 2023; 2024). We query GPT models via the OpenAI API while we run LLaMA-3.1 locally
257   on one GPU NVIDIA A100 using Ollama. We use Langchain to interface with the LLMs. We use the
258   default hyperparameters of both models and allow 12 refinement steps to format the LLM answers
259   properly. When the answer does not match the expected format, we add a parsing layer. The prompts
260   used are given in Appendix B. Our framework is denoted Counterfactual-CI. We compare our method
261   with baseline LLM models from (Jin et al., 2023).

      4.4   E XAMPLE OF R EASONING FAILURES
405
      We show an example of failure case with GPT-4o for the example provided in Figure 3. The model
406   explanation is given below:
407
      The target variable ’salary’ is influenced by two parent causes: ’drinking coffee’ and ’
408        unobserved confounders’. Given that the person drinks coffee (true), we might expect the
409        salary to be positively affected. However, the status of unobserved confounders is
           inactive, which suggests a lack of additional income influence. Thus, the overall effect
410        results in a low salary.
411
412   Although the model generates the correct causal factual and counterfactual graphs (as shown in
413   the figure), the inference step fails. The model answers ‘low salary’ instead of ‘high salary’. The
414   context specifies that ‘confounder active or drinking coffee causes high salary’ but the LLM makes
415   a mistake and interprets it as a logical AND instead of a logical OR. This example illustrates the
416   type of prediction error that is prevalent in the LLMs’ reasoning. It can be compared with the LLM
417   limitations in robust and abstract reasoning tasks (Wu et al., 2023; Gendron et al., 2024; Jin et al.,
418   2024).

496   6    L IMITATIONS
497
498   LLMs provide free-text answers that can be different from the format provided in the instructions.
499   This poses challenges for building an end-to-end pipeline that requires using LLMs at multiple stages,
500   as the responses can be difficult to parse automatically, and errors can accumulate. In our future work,
501   we will include fine-tuning in our pipeline to mitigate this issue. Due to the high cost of running
502   LLMs or accessing them through APIs, we only tested our method on synthetic data and short text
503   snippets. We intend to apply it to larger amounts of data in the future. We also only consider DAG
504   structures, whereas real-world events can contain feedback loops. We will integrate them into our
505
      future work. As the LLM discovers the causal structure, errors can be present, and confounders
      can be omitted. The counterfactual results depend on the causal graph to be accurate. In addition,
506
      our current approach for real-world data proxies ground-truth counterfactual data by retrieving such
507
      values from LLMs. Our future work will focus on verifying the accuracy of the intermediate steps
508   and building ground-truth counterfactual data.

511   7    C ONCLUSION AND B ROADER I MPACT
512
513   We propose an end-to-end method for conducting causal structure discovery and counterfactual causal
514   inference from unstructured natural language. We demonstrate the applicability of our method on
515   real-world news events, showcasing the LLMs’ abilities to perform causal discovery and inference
516   not as a standalone model but as a part of a larger framework. Furthermore, our experiments show
517   that LLMs can extract the causal structure of a piece of text but fail during the reasoning part. This
518
      expands previous findings on the limitations of LLMs on reasoning tasks (Wu et al., 2023; Gendron
      et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024).
519
520   This research is still in its infancy but has shown promising results for computing causal inference
521   and leveraging LLMs. As the inference process is divided into several independent computations of
522   causal variables given their parents, this configuration provides inherent interpretability and allows
523   auditing an LLM’s answer. Our future work will follow two lines of research. On one hand, as LLMs
524
      have been shown to perform better by using refinement techniques (Wu et al., 2023; Madaan et al.,
      2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Qiu et al., 2024), our counterfactual self-evaluation method could be used to
525
      conduct Counterfactual Self-Learning: refine LLMs’ answers to improve them and teach LLMs to
526
      reason more causally by providing them with counterfactual data (Bareinboim et al., 2022). On the
527   other hand, we aim to create a framework to build and test counterfactuals based on real values and
528   validate whether LLM-extracted causal relationships hold. Doing so would avoid LLM hallucinations
529   and counterfactual reasoning deficiencies similar to the ones shown in Section 4 and lead to robust
530   causal reasoning, where every piece of information could be traced back to evidence from the real
531   world. We expect that such a framework will have extensive applications in many domains, extending
532   the use of causal reasoning to any domain where text is available. In particular, we expect that could
533   lead to a greater automation of strategic foresight, democratizing and enabling a wider use of it to
534   enhance decision-making at all societal levels.

      A     G RAPH S UMMARISATION
812
      When considering several documents, we build their corresponding causal graphs independently and
813
      then attempt to merge them. The problem of combining multiple causal graphs together is known
814
      as the Structural Causal Marginal problem (Gresele et al., 2022). This is a challenging problem
815   that we approach from two different angles. Considering two causal models M1 and M2 , we first
816   generate embeddings for every node of the two graphs by extracting their representation generated by
817   the last hidden layer of the LLM. The prompt of a node is a concatenation of all its attributes. We
818   omit its current value because we want to generate a representation of the variable and not the current
819   instance. Motivated by the success of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) at aggregating neighborhood
820   information in graphs (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Velickovic et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2019), we also add
821   to he prompt the attributes of its immediate neighbours and how they are connected via the edge
822   attributes. Then, we use a clustering algorithm to find nodes that share similar latent representations.
823
      We select DBSCAN (Ester et al., 1996). Since it is a density-based algorithm, it allows use to restrict
      the sparsity of the clusters and cluster together nodes with only very close representations.
824
825   After extracting similar nodes, we consider two ways to combine graphs: summarisation and analogy.
826   Summarisation implies considering similar nodes as a single node in the merged graph, inheriting the
827   edges of all the nodes in the set. This approach is straightforward and allows reducing the number of
828   nodes as the graph grows. However, assessing if two causal variables can be merged is a challenging
829
      problem. In particular, in the absence of a lot of observations (one text only shows one observation
      per variable), the merged causal graph can be easily falsified (Gresele et al., 2022).
830
831   Analogy merging is inspired by the research on analogical reasoning (Osta-Vélez & Gärdenfors,
832   2022; Forbus, 2015). We view similarity between nodes representations as an indication that analog
833   mechanisms are causing them. To represent an analogy, we do not modify the existing graphs but
834   add a common unobserved ancestor between similar nodes to integrate the similarity information
835
      from the clustering process without making assumptions regarding the nature of their similarity. The
      merged graphs can share information via backdoor paths. Unlike summarisation, analogy does not
836
      remove nodes but adds more. However, this method does not introduce ways to falsify the graphs and
837
      preserve the mechanisms of the initial graphs. The two approaches are illustrated in Figure 5.
