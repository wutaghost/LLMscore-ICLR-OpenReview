                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013            State Space Models (SSMs) such as Mamba have shown significant promise for
014            sequence modeling in Natural Language Processing (NLP) and, more recently,
015            computer vision. This paper presents a new methodology for both supervised
016            and self-supervised learning using Mamba and Masked Autoencoder networks
017            specifically designed for point cloud data. We propose three main contributions
018            that enhance the capability of Mamba networks to process and understand the
               complex structure of this type of data. The first strategy exploits the spectrum of a
019
               graph Laplacian capturing the local connectivity of patches to define an isometry-
020
               invariant traversal order of tokens in the Mamba network. Compared to existing
021            point cloud Mamba architectures, which traverse point patches based on a 3D grid,
022            our approach is more robust to the viewpoint and better captures the shape manifold
023            of the point cloud. The second contribution adapts our approach to segmentation
024            using a recursive patch partitioning strategy informed by spectral components
025            of the Laplacian. This strategy enables a more precise integration and analysis
026            point cloud segments. Our last contribution tackles a significant issue in Masked
               Autoencoder (MAE) for Mamba networks by modifying learnable token placement.
027
               Instead of adding them at the end, tokens are restored to their original positions,
028
               maintaining essential order and improving learning effectiveness. Extensive experi-
029            ments confirm our method’s superiority over State-Of-The-Art (SOTA) baselines,
030            demonstrating marked improvements in classification, segmentation, and few-shot
031            tasks. The code for this study is available in an anonymized repository.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
035
      The analysis of 3D point cloud data is fundamental to various applications, including autonomous
036
      driving (Qi et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2019), VR/AR (Guo et al., 2020), and robotics (Rusu & Cousins,
037   2011). Compared to the organized structure of 2D images, point clouds consist of 3D coordinates
038   without direct adjacency information forming an unordered bag. In recent years, considerable efforts
039   have been dedicated to adapt deep learning models such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
040   and Transformers to this type of data (Qi et al., 2017b; Yu et al., 2022; Pang et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
041   2022; Bahri et al., 2024). Due to their permutation invariant self-attention mechanism, Transformer
042   networks are particularly well-suited for the unordered nature of point clouds. However, the quadratic
043
      complexity of this mechanism, requiring to compute a weight between each pair of tokens, impedes
      the application of these networks to large-sized inputs (e.g., 2D images or 3D point clouds represented
044
      by many patches). This has prompted researchers to explore more efficient solutions, including the
045   Set Transformer (Lee et al., 2019), Sparse Transformer (Child et al., 2019), Longformer (Beltagy
046   et al., 2020) and Sinkhorn Transformer (Tay et al., 2020).
047
      Recently, methods based on Structured State Space Sequence (S4) (Gu et al., 2021a) such as Mamba
048
      (Gu & Dao, 2023) have gained significant traction as a more efficient alternative to Transformers
049   (Liu et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024). So far, very few studies have investigated the potential of S4
050   approaches like Mamba for 3D point clouds. Existing methods like Point-Mamba (Liang et al., 2024)
051   and PCM (Zhang et al., 2024) extend the 2D grid-based traversal employed for images to a 3D
052   grid. However, this straightforward adaptation to point clouds suffers from three crucial problems.
053   First: whereas patches from 2D images have adjacency information, which could be exploited by
      the grid-based traversal, the 3D point patches in point clouds offer a sparse representation of the


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      object’s surface, and nearby patches on a 3D grid are not necessarily adjacent on this surface. Second:
055   in the absence of self-attention, task-specific performance is highly influenced by the nature of the
056   token traversal strategy. For example, a traversal suitable for point cloud classification may not be
057   effective for a local task such as point-level classification (i.e., segmentation). Third: due to the
058   “direction-sensitive” nature of Mamba, the self-supervised MAE pre-training step of leading point
059   cloud models like Point-MAE (Pang et al., 2022) and Point-M2AE (Zhang et al., 2022) cannot be
060   used directly as there is no attention mechanism to learn the masked tokens’ positions.
061   The contribution of our work focuses on addressing these problems as follows:
062
            1. We introduce a Surface-Aware Spectral Traversing (SAST) strategy based on the Laplacian
063
               spectrum of a patch-connectivity graph. Compared to the 3D grid traversal of current
064            approaches like Point-Mamba, our strategy is invariant to isometric transformations (e.g.,
065            choice of viewpoint) and better captures the object’s surface manifold.
066
            2. We also present a Hierarchical Local Traversing (HLT) for point-level classification (seg-
067            mentation) that partitions patches recursively based on their spectral coordinates. Unlike
068            our SAST strategy for classification, which considers Laplacian eigenvectors separately
069            in different traversals, this HLT combines them in a single ordering for a more precise
070            modeling of geometry.
071         3. During the MAE-based Self-Supervised Learning (SSL), we propose a Traverse-Aware
072            Repositioning (TAR) strategy to align the masked tokens according to their spectral adja-
073            cency. This strategy addresses the critical issue of spatial adjacency preservation unique to
074            Mamba networks.

078
      Deep Point Cloud Learning. With the progress of deep neural networks (DNNs), there has been
079   a growing focus on applying such models to point clouds. Drawing inspiration from models like
080   PointNet (Qi et al., 2017a) and PointNet++ (Qi et al., 2017b), several efforts (Atzmon et al., 2018;
081   Deng et al., 2023; Landrieu & Simonovsky, 2018; Li et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2019) have been made
082   to develop deep architectures that capture local context information more effectively. Subsequently,
083   models influenced by the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), including versions v1-v3 of the Point
084   Transformer (Wu et al., 2023b; 2022; Zhao et al., 2021) and the Stratified Transformer (Lai et al.,
085
      2022), have emerged as leading frameworks, effectively combining local and global data to set new
      benchmarks. To capitalize on the abundance of unlabeled data, self-supervised pre-training has
086
      also emerged as an effective strategy. Notable implementations like Point-BERT (Yu et al., 2022),
087   Point-MAE (Pang et al., 2022), MaskPoint (Liu et al., 2022), Point-M2AE (Zhang et al., 2022), and
088   I2P-MAE (Zhang et al., 2022) have introduced methods for pre-training the Transformer (Vaswani
089   et al., 2017) using techniques based on masked point modeling (Liu et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023).
090
      Building on the effectiveness of MAE in Text and Image domains, Point-BERT (Yu et al., 2022)
091
      presented a revolutionary method inspired by BERT (Devlin et al., 2018), tailoring Transformers to
092   3D point cloud processing. Point-MAE (Pang et al., 2022) applied MAE-style pre-training to 3D point
093   clouds using a custom Transformer-based Autoencoder (AE) designed to reconstruct masked irregular
094   patches. The use of multi-scale masking and local spatial self-attention mechanisms in Point-M2AE
095   (Zhang et al., 2022) has led to SOTA results in 3D representation learning. Furthermore, I2P-
096   MAE (Zhang et al., 2023) improved self-supervised point cloud processing with a masking strategy
097   leveraging pre-trained 2D models through an Image-to-Point transformation. Point-GPT (Chen et al.,
      2024) introduced an auto-regressive generative pretraining (GPT) approach to address the unordered
098
      nature and low information density of point clouds. Finally, ACT (Dong et al., 2022) proposed a
099
      cross-modal knowledge transfer method using pretrained 2D or natural language Transformers as
100   teachers for 3D representation learning.
101

107   for standard applications. To address this issue, S4 (Gu et al., 2021a) proposed a method to normalize
      parameters into a diagonal structure. Subsequently, a variety of structured SSMs have emerged,

136
      and PCM (Zhang et al., 2024). PointMamba introduces a simple approach to token reordering for
137   point cloud analysis by strategically organizing point tokens based on a 3D grid. Similarly, PCM
138   enhances Mamba with a Consistent Traverse Serialization (CTS) technique that converts 3D point
139   clouds into 1D point sequences while maintaining spatial adjacency. Building upon these approaches,
140   our method introduces the Spectral Spatial Traversing (SST) strategy, which improves token ordering
141   and maintains spatial adjacency during MAE-based SSL in Mamba networks.

209   Spectral Graph Analysis. Popularized by Chung in the 90s (Chung, 1997), spectral graph analysis
210   characterizes the properties of a graph G = (V, E) by the spectrum (eigenvalues and corresponding
211   eigenvectors) of its Laplacian matrix L. This analysis can be understood as a discretized version of
212   the Laplace-Beltrami Operator ∆ of a function f defined on a Riemannian manifold:
213                                                                                                                    ∆f = div(gradf )                                                                                                                                                           (4)
214
215   where gradf is the gradient of f and div the divergence on the manifold. The solution to the
      Laplacian eigenvalue problem ∆f = −λf , known as Helmholtz wave equation, is an eigenfunction

217   et al., 2005).

      Following methods for spectral clustering (Ng et al., 2001) and normalized cuts (Shi & Malik, 2000),
      we consider a weighted adjacency matrix W : V ×V → R+ where Wij = 0 if (i, j) ̸∈ E to model

      the Euclidean distance of nearby patches (see Section 3.3). The Laplacian  Pmatrix of G is defined
      as L = D − W where D is the diagonal degree matrix such that Dii = j Wij . To account for
222
      variability in the scale of weights Wij or the distribution of node degrees Dii , it is preferable to
223   employ a normalized version of the Laplacian. In this work, we use the Random Walk Laplacian
224   Lrw = I − D−1 W which has the following useful properties:
225
226         1. Lrw is positive semi-definite and has |V | non-negative real-valued eigenvalues 0 = λ1 ≤
227            . . . ≤ λ|V | ;
228         2. 0 is an eigenvalue of Lrw with the constant vector as eigenvector and its multiplicity equals
229            the number of connected components in the graph;
230         3. Following Courant’s Nodal Line Theorem (Courant & Hilbert, 2008), the n-th eigenmode
231            of Lrw has at most n poles of vibration;
232         4. The representation of a shape by the spectrum of Lrw is invariant to isometry (i.e., distance-
233            preserving transformation).
234
235   Our method uses the s first non-constant eigenvectors of Lrw (i.e., the eigenvectors corresponding
236
      to the s smallest non-zero eigenvalues) to define traversal orders for classification (Section 3.3) and
      segmentation (Section 3.4) that are robust to the viewpoint (due to isometry invariance) and provide a
237
      smooth parametrization of the surface manifold. We consider the first eigenvectors as they encode
238   low frequency information (by Courant’s Nodal Line Theorem), making the resulting traversal more
239   robust to shape variability and noise. Figure 1 illustrates this concept: (a) shows the original mesh,
240   (b) shows traversals based on the first to fourth non-constant smallest eigenvectors, and (c) shows
241   traversal based on the largest eigenvector forming a non-continuous sequence of tokens.

244                                    N p
      Given a point cloud P = {pi }i=1     , each point represented by 3D coordinates, we convert P to a
245   reduced set of patches that can be processed more efficiently. Toward this goal, we employ the
246   Farthest Point Sampling (FPS) algorithm to select a subset C ⊂ P of Nc points offering a good
247   coverage of the entire point cloud. These selected points will act as the centers of local patches within
248   the point cloud. For each center point psi ∈ C, we then identify Nn nearest points N (psi ) ⊂ P using
249   the K-Nearest Neighbours (KNN) algorithm. Following this, each patch is defined as a center psi and
      its corresponding nearest-neighbors N (psi ).

      3.3   S URFACE -AWARE S PECTRAL T RAVERSING (SAST)
252
253   Current point cloud processing approaches using Mamba, such as Point-Mamba (Liang et al., 2024)
254   and PCM (Zhang et al., 2024), simply extend the 2D grid-based traversal for images to a 3D grid. As
255
      mentioned before, this naive strategy suffers from two issues: 1) the 3D grid is view dependent, thus
      rotating the point cloud or moving the camera yields a different traversal order; 2) unrelated patches
256
      may be adjacent in 3D space, hence can be traversed subsequently. To address these problems, we
257   define a traversal order based on the Laplacian spectrum of the patch-connectivity graph.
258
259
      In this graph, each node corresponds to a patch and the weighted adjacency matrix W is defined
      using the Euclidean distance between patch centers. For patches i and j defined by center points
260
      psi and psj , we add an edge (i, j) if psj is among the K nearest neighbors of psi or vice-versa. The
261
      weight of this edge is computed using a Gaussian kernel: Wij = exp −∥psi − psj ∥22 /σ where σ
262
      is a hyperparameter controlling the kernel width.
263

265   Laplacian Lrw . This can be achieved efficiently using an iterative method like the Arnoldi algorithm
      (Golub & Van Loan, 2013) by exploiting the following facts: 1) matrix W is very sparse, and 2) only
266
      the first few eigenvectors need to be computed. Eigenvector v (k) ∈ RNc , k ∈ {1, . . . , s}, assigns

      3.4   H IERARCHICAL L OCAL T RAVERSING (HLT) FOR S EGMENTATION
298
299   While effective for classification tasks, the SAST strategy considering each eigenvector in a separate
300   traversal may not capture the precise relationship between patches needed for segmentation. To
301   address this issue, we introduce a Hierarchical Local Traversal (HLT) strategy that considers the full
302   spectrum (all s non-constant eigenvectors) simultaneously.
303   Our strategy is inspired by the recursive binary partitioning technique of normalized cuts (Shi &
304   Malik, 2000). Starting from the canonicalized spectrum (see previous section), tokens are first split
305   based on the first eigenvector v (1) , by comparing their corresponding value in v (1) with the mean
      value v (1) . This yields a binary partition of tokens bi = 1 vi ≥ v (1) ∈ {0, 1} where 1 is the
306                                                             (1)        (1)
307   indicator function. Each subgroup is then divided based on the mean of the second eigenvector v (2) ,
308   and so on for other eigenvectors. This partitioning process can be seen as building a binary tree,
309   where each level corresponds to a different eigenvector and leaf nodes i are uniquely identified by the
310                            (1)         (s)
      sequence of bits bi = [bi , . . . , bi ] on the path from the root to the leaf. Our HLT method traverses
311   groups of leaf nodes (groups of tokens) sequentially based on the lexicographic order of their binary
312   code (e.g., [0000], [0001], [0010], [0011], . . . in the case of four eigenvectors). For convenience,
313   we convert binary codes bi to a non-negative integer qi (e.g., bin2Int([0011]) = 3) and define two
314   traversal orders, by increasing or decreasing values of qi .
315   For s eigenvectors, the HLT strategy described above divides tokens into 2s segments which are
316   traversed sequentially. In the best case scenario, ⌈log2 (Nc )⌉ eigenvectors are thus needed to split
317   tokens into individual segments. However, it may happen that multiple tokens fall in the same
318   segment, especially when using fewer eigenvectors. In such case, one can further sort tokens within
319   each segment, for example, using the values of the first eigenvector (i.e., v (1) ). In our implementation,
320   we simply sort these tokens randomly to add stochasticity in the training. This section is illustrated in
321
      Fig. 2 (e), and Fig. 3 (b).
322   As shown in Figure 3, the first Laplacian eigenvectors encode high-level spatial relations (e.g., bottom
323   vs. top, left vs. right, torso vs. limbs, etc.). In the SAST, because these eigenvectors are used
      in separate traversals, the network may not be able to differentiate specific regions/parts of the

      Following state-of-art Transformers for point cloud processing, such as Point-MAE (Pang et al.,
329
      2022) and Point-M2AE (Zhang et al., 2022), our method leverages self-supervised pretraining based
330   on MAE to boost performance. In Transformer-based approaches, the learnable tokens of masked
331   patches can be inserted in any position of the sequence (typically at the end) as the self-attention
332   mechanism can still attend to all tokens irrespective of their positions. However, this approach
333   presents a significant problem in Mamba networks which are sensitive to the traversal order of tokens.
334   We handle this problem via a TAR strategy that improves the placement of learnable tokens in MAE
335   within Mamba networks. Specifically, we restore the learnable tokens to their original positions
336
      rather than appending them at the end of the sequence. This ensures that the essential order of tokens
      is maintained, preserving spatial adjacency and enhancing learning effectiveness within Mamba
337
      networks.
338
339   The proposed TAR strategy selects an arbitrary traversal order and randomly masks a subset of Nm
340   tokens with the same masking ratio as the transformer-based MAEs. These tokens are then removed
      from the sequence, and their positions are recorded. Afterwards, the remaining (visible) tokens are
341
      fed to the encoder that outputs their representation. Before reconstructing the point cloud using the
342
      decoder, we reinsert the learnable tokens in the sequence at their recorded position. The same set of
343   masked patches is used for other traversal orders. This procedure can be seen in Fig. 2 (f). Following
344   previous work, we measure the reconstruction error for masked patches using the Chamfer distance:

536
537   We introduced three strategies to enhance Mamba networks for point cloud data: isometry-invariant
538   token traversal, recursive patch partitioning for segmentation, and improved learnable token placement.
539   Our methods demonstrate superior performance over state-of-the-art baselines in classification,
      segmentation, and few-shot tasks.
