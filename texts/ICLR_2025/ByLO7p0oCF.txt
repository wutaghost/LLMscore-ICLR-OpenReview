009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            To enhance Large Language Model (LLM) capabilities, multi-agent debates have
012            been introduced, where multiple LLMs discuss solutions to a problem over several
013            rounds of debate. However, LLMs often produce incorrect responses that appear
014            confident, which can mislead other agents. This is partly because agents do
015            not express their confidence levels during standard debates. To address this, we
016            introduce DebUnc, a multi-agent debate framework that uses uncertainty metrics
017            to assess agent confidence levels. We adapted the LLM attention mechanism to
018            adjust token weights based on confidence levels and also explored using textual
019
               prompts to convey confidence. Our evaluations across various benchmarks show
               that attention-based methods are particularly effective, and that as uncertainty
020
               metrics improve, performance will continue to increase.

046   1   I NTRODUCTION
047
048   Large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive performance in various fields, including law,
049   academia, and coding (OpenAI, 2024). To handle more complex tasks, LLM-powered agents have
050   been developed. These agents observe their environment and take actions, such as communicating
051   with other agents, using tools, or performing reasoning (Wu et al., 2023). The potential of LLM
052   agents increases when multiple agents collaborate. One form of this is multi-agent debate, where
053   agents propose and debate solutions to a problem with other agents. Multi-agent debates have been
      shown to improve the reasoning and accuracy of LLMs (Du et al., 2023).

054
      The factual accuracy of LLMs is crucial for their utility in many real-world applications (Rawte et al.,
055   2023). For example, an LLM tutor providing incorrect information could harm a student’s learning,
056   and an LLM customer service agent giving incorrect advice could mislead a customer. In fields such
057   as healthcare, journalism, or financial services, the consequences of LLM errors can be even more
058   severe, leading to financial losses or health risks. To address this, multi-agent debate systems have
059   been proposed. These systems enable multiple agents to generate diverse responses to a problem,
060   discuss and critique each other’s answers, and ultimately converge on a final solution (Liang et al.,
061   2023). Ideally, if some agents provide incorrect answers initially, the debate process helps them
062   recognize and correct their errors.
063   In practice, while agents often confidently agree on the same final answer, that answer is not always
064   correct. This can be attributed to flawed communication between agents (Du et al., 2023). LLM
065   agents typically communicate through text and respond with a highly confident tone, regardless of
066   of the accuracy of their answers. This creates a significant challenge in multi-agent systems, as a
067   confidently incorrect response from one agent can mislead others, causing all agents to converge
068   on an incorrect conclusion. By contrast, people often use qualifiers like "I am sure that..." or "I
069
      am not sure, but...," which, though imperfect, provide cues that help others gauge the reliability
      of the information. Since the tone of an LLM’s response is not a reliable indicator of its accuracy,
070
      researchers have developed uncertainty metrics to provide a more objective measure of the model’s
071
      confidence.
072
073   Building on these insights, we present DebUnc, a novel multi-agent debate framework that integrates
074   multi-agent Debates with model Uncertainty metrics. After each round of debate, we measure each
075
      agent’s uncertainty with an uncertainty metric. In the following round, both the agents’ responses
      and uncertainties are shared with the other agents. We explore two methods for conveying agent
076
      uncertainty: (1) incorporating the uncertainty directly into the textual prompt alongside the agent
077
      responses, as shown in Figure 2, and (2) adjusting the LLM’s attention towards agents’ responses
078   based on their uncertainty, as depicted in Figure 3. We extensively evaluate DebUnc across multiple
079   LLMs, benchmarks, and uncertainty metrics, analyzing the results of each uncertainty metric and
080   method of uncertainty communication.
081
      Our key contributions are outlined as follows:
082
083   • We introduce DebUnc, a framework that quantifies and communicates LLM agent uncertainty in
084     multi-agent debates.
085
      • We adapt the LLM attention mechanism to adjust token weights based on confidence levels and
086     also explore the use of textual prompts to communicate confidence.
087
      • We evaluate DebUnc across multiple LLMs, benchmarks, and uncertainty metrics, and find that the
088
        attention-scaling methods consistently outperforms unmodified debates.
089
090
      • We offer insights into how performance will be impacted as uncertainty metrics improve.

092   2     R ELATED W ORK
093
094   LLMs are known for their overconfidence and their tendency to provide responses to any user query,
095   regardless of their certainty. This often leads to the generation of factual inaccuracies, known as
096   hallucinations, where the information provided by the model is incorrect or unsupported by the data
097
      on which it was trained (Liang et al., 2024; Yadkori et al., 2024; Duan et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023;
      Aichberger et al., 2024). Ensuring factual accuracy is crucial for building trust in LLM-based systems
098
      and expanding their use in real-world applications. As a result, there has been a surge in research
099
      focused understanding the mechanisms behind hallucinations and developing strategies to mitigate
100   them (Ji et al., 2023; McDonald et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023).
101
102
      2.1   U NCERTAINTY IN LLM S
103
104   Some current research efforts to mitigate hallucinations focus on measuring the model’s uncertainty
105   and enhancing their self-awareness (Kadavath et al., 2022; Amayuelas et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023).
106   If we could accurately measure a model’s confidence, users would have clearer guidance on when to
107   trust its output (Lin et al., 2022a; Xu et al., 2024), and language agents could better determine when
      to access external resources (Han et al., 2024).


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      However, accurately measuring model uncertainty remains an open problem, and ongoing research is
109   exploring new uncertainty metrics. We identify three primary approaches to this problem:
110
111   Token Probability-Based Uncertainty Metrics evaluate uncertainty based on the probabilities
112
      assigned to each token generated by the model. High token probabilities (close to 1) indicate strong
      model confidence, whereas lower token probabilities suggest a spread of probability across several
113
      tokens, signifying uncertainty about the correct choice. Prominent methods in this category include
114
      Mean Token Entropy, Perplexity (Fomicheva et al., 2020), SAR (Duan et al., 2023), RDE (Vazhentsev
115   et al., 2023), and Claim-Conditioned Probability (Fadeeva et al., 2024).
116
117   LLM-Generated Uncertainty Metrics involve the model explicitly expressing its uncertainty in
118
      its response. Lin et al. (2022a) explored this by fine-tuning GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) to provide
      both an answer and a corresponding confidence level. Alternatively, the model can be prompted to
119
      express its uncertainty without explicit training. Tian et al. (2023) found that this approach can can
120
      outperform token-probability-based methods when applied to LLMs fine-tuned with reinforcement
121   learning from human feedback (Christiano et al., 2017). However, Xiong et al. (2023) report lower
122   performance compared to token probability-based methods on GPT-3.
123
124
      Sampling-Based Uncertainty Metrics assess uncertainty by generating multiple outputs through
      sampling, analyzing the distribution of meanings across the outputs. A consistent meaning across
125
      multiple samples suggests high confidence, while variations indicate lower confidence. This approach
126
      can identify different sequences that convey the same meaning, which token-probability-based metrics
127   do not account for. However, the need for multiple generations makes these methods more resource-
128   intensive than the others described. Examples include Semantic Entropy (Kuhn et al., 2023), LUQ
129   (Zhang et al., 2024), and other metrics that evaluate meaning diversity (Lin et al., 2023).
130
131
      2.2   M ULTI -AGENT D EBATE
132
133   With the increased accessibility of LLMs and improvements in their inference times, LLMs are being
134   integrated into more complex systems as autonomous agents (Wu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Hong
135   et al., 2023). A critical component of these agent-based systems is the collaboration mechanism,
136   where models engage in debate with one another. These mechanisms are currently being studied
137   (Zhang et al., 2023) and have been shown to foster more divergent thinking (Liang et al., 2023),
138   enhance reasoning and factual accuracy (Du et al., 2023), and lead to more reliable evaluations (Chan
139   et al., 2023). Through discussions, the LLMs can refine their outputs, ultimately achieving higher
140
      levels of agreement and producing more factually accurate text (Sun et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024).
141   Pham et al. (2023) recognized that text is not be the most effective communication mechanism for
142   LLM agents, since information is lost during the token sampling process, and demonstrated how
143   LLMs can communicate through embeddings. ReConcile Chen et al. (2023) explored the integration
144   of agent confidence in multi-agent debates, relying on LLM agents to self-report their confidence,
145   which was communicated to other agents through prompts. Building on these ideas, we employ
146
      uncertainty metrics to estimate agent confidence and explore both prompting and attention scaling to
      convey this confidence. We found these enhancements to significantly improve multi-agent debate
147
      performance.

152   In human debates, it is often possible to gauge someone’s expertise on a subject by observing the
153   fluency of their responses, their body language, and other cues. This helps in identifying whose
154   arguments to consider more seriously when there are conflicting opinions. On the other hand, in
155   multi-agent LLM debates, agents frequently generate inaccurate responses that sound confident,
156   which can mislead other agents and result in a consensus on an incorrect response (Du et al., 2023).
157   Our goal is to advise agents on which other agents’ opinions to prioritize based on their confidence
158
      levels.
159   Our modified debate pipeline, depicted in Figure 2, operates as follows: in each round of debate,
160   every agent generates a response, and its uncertainty is estimated. In the next round, the responses
161   and uncertainties from each agent are shared with every other agent. We test three uncertainty metrics
      and three approaches to communicate agent uncertainty.

188   3.1   U NCERTAINTY M ETRICS
189
190   Uncertainty metrics assess an LLM’s confidence in its responses: high uncertainty implies low
191   confidence and potential unreliability, while low uncertainty suggests greater reliability. These
192
      metrics generally fall into three categories: Token Probability-Based, LLM-Generated, and Sampling-
      Based methods. For more details, see Uncertainty in LLMs
193
194   In our experiments, we focus on token probability-based metrics due to their efficiency and flexibility,
195   requiring only a single generation and functioning independently of the model’s ability to express
196   uncertainty. We specifically chose Mean Token Entropy (Fomicheva et al., 2020) for its simplicity
197   and TokenSAR (Duan et al., 2023), which accounts for the fact that some tokens contribute more to a
198
      sequence’s meaning than others. We utilize the implementations from LM-Polygraph, a framework
      with implementations for many uncertainty metrics (Fadeeva et al., 2023). Lastly, to evaluate the
199
      potential of future uncertainty metrics, we include a third "oracle" uncertainty metric in our analysis.
200
201
      Mean Token Entropy. One of the simplest and most efficient uncertainty to metrics to compute is
202   Mean Token Entropy (Fomicheva et al., 2020). It is the average entropy across all tokens generated,
203   with the entropy of a single token X defined as:
204                                                  X
205
                                         H(X) = −       p(x) log p(x)
                                                                     x∈V
206
207   Here, V denotes the model’s vocabulary. Entropy is maximized when p(x) is uniform over all tokens
208
      in the vocabulary, indicating maximum uncertainty. It is minimized when one token has a probability
      of 1 and all other tokens have a probability of 0, indicating complete certainty in the selected token.
209
210
      TokenSAR. Duan et al. (2023) recognized that some tokens contribute more to a text’s meaning
211
      than others and proposed TokenSAR, an uncertainty metric that accounts for this. It is defined as the
212   weighted average of the negative log probabilities for each generated token, where the weights are
213   the relevance scores of the tokens:
214                                                 XN
215                                    TokenSAR =       − log p(ti )R(ti )
                                                                       i

216
      Here, N is the number of tokens generated, ti is the i-th token, and R(ti ) is the relevance of token ti .
217   To compute each token’s relevance, RoBERTa-large (Liu et al., 2019) must be run N times in total.
218   This is more computationally expensive than calculating mean token entropy, but still far less costly
219   than metrics requiring multiple generations.
220
221   Oracle. While the uncertainty metrics discussed provide valuable insights into agent uncertainty,
222   they are not without limitations, and future advancements will likely lead to more accurate metrics.
223   To assess the potential effectiveness of our methods with improved uncertainty metrics, we include
224   an "Oracle" metric, which simulates an ideal uncertainty metric. This metric yields low uncertainty
225   when the agent is correct and high uncertainty when the agent is incorrect, and is defined as follows:
226

227                                                  0 if the response is correct
228
                                 uncertainty =
                                                     ∞ if the response is incorrect
229
      In practice, using 0 and ∞ could cause issues with our uncertainty communication methods, so we
230
      detail exactly how this metric is applied in the following subsection. It is also important to note
231
      that this metric requires knowledge of the ground truth answer, making it impractical for real-world
232   use. Instead, it serves to evaluate the effectiveness of our uncertainty communication methods
233   independently of the performance of the uncertainty metrics themselves, and allows us to anticipate
234   how improvements in uncertainty metrics could affect debate performance.

236   3.2   U NCERTAINTY C OMMUNICATION
237
238   After computing the uncertainty of each agent, we explore multiple methods to incorporate these
239   uncertainties into the following debate round.
240
241   Confidence in Prompt. One approach is to include the uncertainties directly in the text prompt for
242   the next debate round, as shown in Figure 2. Mean Token Entropy and TokenSAR yield non-negative
243   uncertainties. For Mean Token Entropy, the range of uncertainties depends on the model’s vocabulary
244
      size, while for TokenSAR, the maximum uncertainty is unbounded. Therefore, the exact uncertainty
      values are less informative than the relative differences in uncertainty between agents.
245
246   Rather than expressing their uncertainty as an unbounded non-negative number, humans often express
247   their confidence on 1 to 10 scale, which more interpretable. Since LLMs are trained on human data,
248   they may exhibit the same preference. As a result, we convert the uncertainties into confidence values.
249   Given a list of uncertainties u for n agents, where ui is the uncertainty of agent i, we first invert them
250
      to obtain raw confidence values ri = u1i . We then scale these values such that the average confidence
251
      si of all agents is 5:
252
253                                              ri                          1
                                          si = Pn             · (5n − 1) +
254                                                  j=1 rj                  n
255
256   Finally, we clamp the confidence levels to the range of 1 to 10 and round to the nearest integer.
257   When using the Oracle uncertainty metric, we set the confidence to 1 if the agent was incorrect and to
258   10 if the agent was correct.
259
260   Attention Scaling. As an alternative to including confidence levels in the prompt, we can modify the
261   LLM’s token-generation process to account for each agent’s confidence. Many LLMs use Transformer
262   decoder layers that generate an embedding for the last token and use this embedding to predict the
263   next token (Radford et al., 2018). This embedding is determined by the attention mechanism, which
264   creates "query," "key," and "value" vectors for each token.
265   The similarity between the "query" vector of the last token and the "key" vector of each token is used
266   to compute a weight for every token. These weights are normalized with a softmax function to ensure
267   they sum to 1, and are used to create the output embedding, which is the weighted sum of the value
268   vectors of each token (Vaswani et al., 2017). The weight of each token determines its influence on
269   the next token generated. By modifying these weights, we can adjust the model’s focus on each token
      in the input.

343   4        E XPERIMENT D ESIGN
344
345   To evaluate these methods, an open-source LLM is required, as implementing the attention scaling
346   requires modifications to the model source code. Additionally, the uncertainty metrics used rely on
347   token probabilities, which may not be readily available from closed-source models. We ran all of the
348   experiments on Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023), and re-evaluated the most insightful
349
      ones on Llama-3-8B-Instruct (AI@Meta, 2024) to verify that our methods work across different
      models. Tokens were sampled with a temperature of 1 to ensure variability in the responses. The
350
      methods were evaluated on multiple benchmarks:
351
352
      1. MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021): A dataset of multiple-choice questions across various subjects.
353
354   2. GSM8k (Cobbe et al., 2021): A dataset of free-response grade school math problems.
355   3. TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022b): A multiple-choice dataset testing the model’s susceptibility to
356      common misconceptions.
357
      4. Arithmetic: An randomly generated set of arithmetic problems in the form a + b · c + d for the
358      Mistral model and a + b · c + d − e · f for the Llama model, where 0 ≤ a, b, c, d < 30.
359
360   For MMLU on Mistral-7B, we tested both zero-shot and 5-shot prompting, using examples from
361   the original MMLU repository’s1 dev set. For the remaining benchmarks, we used only zero-shot
362   prompting. The exact prompts used are shown in Appendix C.
363
      Instead of evaluating on the full datasets, which would be too expensive, we sampled 100 questions
364
      from each. We evaluated every combination of uncertainty metric and uncertainty incorporation
365   method on these samples five times, and report the average accuracy across the five runs.
366
367   The debates involved three agents and three rounds, with all agents using the same LLM. In the first
368
      round, each agent answered the question individually. In the following rounds, each agent was given
      other agents’ responses, and used this information to refine their answers. A full example debate is
369
      shown in Appendix A. The final answer was determined by a majority vote after the third round. This
370
      workflow is illustrated in Figure 2.

468    6              C ONCLUSION
469
470   We introduce DebUnc, a framework that integrates confidence estimations into multi-agent debates.
471   By incorporating confidence levels, agents can assess the reliability of their peers and better determine
472   whom to trust when responses differ. We use uncertainty metrics to estimate the LLM’s confidence
473   based on the probability distribution generated for each token. To convey confidence, we adjusted
474   the LLM’s attention mechanism to scale token weights according to confidence levels and also
475   experimented with using textual prompts. Our results show that attention scaling is more effective,
476   demonstrating that information can be delivered to LLMs through methods beyond traditional text
477   prompts. Attention scaling can be applied in any scenario where certain parts of the input need to be
478
      prioritized over others, and we encourage further exploration of this technique.
479
480    7              L IMITATIONS
481
482   One limitation of DebUnc is its reliance on open-source LLMs, as attention scaling requires code
483   modifications, and token probabilities may not be accessible from proprietary models. Additionally,
484   attention scaling is sensitive to the order of agent responses within the prompt. For example, if agent
485   2’s response precedes agent 3’s, unidirectional attention allows agent 2 to influence agent 3’s tokens,
      potentially leaking information from agent 2 even if agent 3 is more confident.
