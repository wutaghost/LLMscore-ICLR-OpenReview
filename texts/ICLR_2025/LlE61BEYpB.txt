009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Deploying large language models (LLMs) on resource-constrained edge devices
012            is challenging due to computational bottlenecks, memory bottlenecks, and – for
013            long-contexts – specifically the Softmax operation in the attention mechanism.
014            While using ReLU in place of Softmax has been explored, and FIRE as an al-
015            ternative to RoPE has been explored for models trained from scratch, there has
016            been little work towards exploring fine-tuning models to utilize these efficient al-
017            gorithms, or the combination of the two.
018            In this paper, we contribute FLARE, a method for fusing Rectified Linear Acti-
019            vations (ReLU) with Relative Encodings (specifically FIRE), and we share a par-
020            ticular recipe which allows these to be fine-tuned effectively into existing models
021            and fused to create efficient long-context inference. Following this recipe yields
022            markedly better validation loss, long-context inference speed, and successfully
023
               introduces the property of length-generalization – the property where the model
               gains high accuracy for contexts lengths several times larger than trained – unlike
024
               RoPE – without further fine-tuning.
025
026
               Once FIRE and ReLU are both fine-tuned into a model, we show these can be
               mathematically fused into a single, more efficient operation, which on average
027
               was found to shave 98.9% of FIRE operations and produce a Probability matrix
028
               with 98.9% zeros in its lower-triangle.
029
               Finally, we benchmark inference speed improvements for custom hardware as
030
               well with custom CUDA kernels. Using Power, Performance, and Area (PPA)
031
               analysis, we show that FLARE operates at eight times the frequency of Softmax
032            while consuming only 0.1% of the power and 0.11% of the energy per cycle. Our
033            custom CUDA Kernel shows 3.8x faster operation than Softmax FlashAttention.
034            We believe this shows the potential of fine-tuning new algorithms in pre-trained
035            models, and we share our fine-tuning recipes, code and custom hardware designs
036            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/nanoGPTBD54.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have revolutionized natural language processing by en-
042   abling parallel processing of sequences through self-attention mechanisms. However, deploying
043   transformer-based models on resource-constrained edge devices remains challenging, especially for
044   long-context applications. Edge devices often lack the computational resources and memory band-
045   width to efficiently implement latency-optimization techniques for Softmax, such as FlashAttention
046   (Dao et al., 2022), resulting in significant performance bottlenecks. This problem has inspired alter-
047   native hardware algorithms for targeting custom hardware (Stevens et al. (2021), Liu et al. (2024)).
048   While using ReLU in place of Softmax is a computationally efficient alternative, it has been noted
049   in literature that pre-training models with ReLU results in lower machine learning performance than
050   Softmax (Wortsman et al. (2023), Shen et al. (2023), Zhang et al. (2021)). The potential efficiency
051   improvements despite the performance gap has prompted numerous attempts to introduce additional
052   operations and normalization techniques to mitigate the shortcomings of ReLU, including following
053   the PV multiplication with a layernorm (Zhang et al., 2021) as well as specialized and learned
      divisors for the result of the ReLU activation (Wortsman et al. (2023), Shen et al. (2023)). Notably,


                                                        1
054
      these prior had not explored fine-tuning ReLU into Softmax pre-trained Transformer models, or
055   whether this could improve validation loss per token trained.
056
057   Context length mainly hinges upon the Transformer model’s position encoding mechanism. While
058
      long-context operation via RoPE and the extension of context length with RoPE has been explored
      (Su et al. (2023), Chen et al. (2023)), the RoPE operation requires element-wise multiplications
059
      input vectors which, while not a bottleneck, are less computationally efficient than additive relative
060
      position encodings, such as FIRE Li et al. (2024).
061
062   In this paper, we address the hardware-efficient long-context operation. We make the following
063   contributions:
064
065
             • Experiments showing training with Softmax first and then fine-tuning ReLU as a direct
               elementwise replacement, yields significantly better next-token-prediction accuracy than
066
               training with ReLU from the start, for the same number of training steps and and input
067
               training tokens.
068
069          • The FLARE algorithm reorders adjacent FIRE and ReLU operations in inference, offering
070
               the ability to bypass addition operations in FIRE.
071          • A hardware implementation and performance, power, and area (PPA) analysis, showing
072            substantial efficiency gains of ReLU over Softmax in specialized EdgeLLM-accelerators.
073          • A CUDA Kernel for accelerating a ReLU-Augmented forward-pass, with analysis of gains
074            over Softmax-based FlashAttention, and PyTorch source code for supporting accelerated
075            training and fine-tuning of FLARE and ReLU-Attention models.

      2     BACKGROUND
079
      Custom hardware for Transformer-based models must determine low-level implementations of self-
080
      attention mechanisms and positional encoding strategies, and hence are greatly affected by paral-
081
      lelism limitations and involved operations.

      2.1   I NEFFICIENCIES OF S OFTMAX IN S PECIALIZED H ARDWARE
085
      At the core of the Transformer is the self-attention mechanism, which relies on the Softmax function
086   to compute attention scores. The Softmax function is defined as:
087
088
                                                          exi −smax
089                                       Softmax(xi ) = P xj −smax                                      (1)
090                                                       je
091
092
      where smax = max xj is subtracted from each input to improve numerical stability.
093
094   2.2   S OFTMAX I NEFFICIENCY IN H ARDWARE
095
096
      Implementing Softmax efficiently in hardware poses significant challenges:
097
            1. Non-linearity: Implementing the exponential function in hardware requires approxima-
098
               tions or lookup tables, adding need for larger silicon area.
099
100         2. Sequential Operations: The max and sum computations introduce data dependencies that
101
               limit parallelism, resulting in stalls in the pipelining.
102         3. Long Context Inefficiencies: Challenges in pipelining and the need to maintain precision
103            for accumulated values – especially for the denominator – will inflate the size required for
104            this module, with complexity scaling with max supported context length.
105
106   2.3   R E LU AS A S OFTMAX R EPLACEMENT
107
      The Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) function is defined as:


                                                        2
108
109
                                               ReLU(xi ) = max(xi , 0)                                      (2)
110
111   Replacing Softmax with ReLU offers significant advantages when implementing in edge hardware:
112
113                                                                                 2
                • Simplified Non-Linearity: The total number of operations is N2 comparisons, removing
114               need for look up tables.
115
116
                • Element-wise Operation: ReLU can be applied independently to each element, allowing
                  full-parallelization and simplifying pipeline-implementations.
117
118             • Long-Context Efficiency: Via introducing full-parallelism, removing the normalization
119               terms, and simplifying the non-linearity (essentially setting to zero if negative or value
120               leaving as is), the total size of the resulting implementation of ReLU is not tied to the max
121
                  context length.
122
123   2.4      L ONG -C ONTEXT S CALING OF P OSITIONAL E MBEDDINGS
124
125
      RoPE (Su et al., 2023) and Learned Absolute Position Embeddings (Vaswani et al., 2017) both tie
      a trained model a specific max supported context length. Should longer context length be desired,
126
      the Learned Absolute Position Embeddings table will become larger and linearly with the context
127
      length. RoPE would require fine-tuning, e.g. via interpolation Chen et al. (2023), at the longer-
128   context length.
129
130   Functional Interpolation for Relative Position Encoding (FIRE) (Li et al., 2024) is a relative posi-
131
      tional encoding method which results in additive bias after the QK T multiplication. This is a type
      of relative position encoding which exhibits context-length generalization. With FIRE (and many
132
      other additive embeddings) one can train on a smaller context length and then simply inference on a
133
      longer context lengths than trained. This encourages easy scalability of any hardware solutions, as
134   well to longer context lengths.

168   3.3     R ECIPES FOR R E LU I NTEGRATION
169
170   We conducted a series of experiments to compare different training recipes for effectively integrating
171   the ReLU activation function as a replacement for the Softmax function in the attention mechanism
172   of Transformer models. Our goal was to identify strategies that mitigate the performance degradation
173   typically observed when training models with ReLU from scratch.
174
175   3.3.1    E XPERIMENT 1: T RAINING FROM S CRATCH VS . F INE - TUNING WITH R E LU
176
      In the first experiment, over the same total number of training iterations, we compared the perfor-
177
      mance of models trained with ReLU from scratch against models that were first trained with Softmax
178
      and then fine-tuned with ReLU.
179
180           • Training with ReLU from Scratch: We trained a Transformer model with the ReLU-
181             based attention mechanism from scratch for 30,000 iterations. The model was initialized
182             randomly and did not utilize any pre-trained weights. We tracked the validation loss at
183             regular intervals to assess the learning progress.
184
              • Fine-tuning ReLU into a Softmax Pre-trained Model: We first trained the model with
185
                the standard Softmax-based attention mechanism for 20,000 iterations. After this initial
186             training phase, we replaced the Softmax function with ReLU and fine-tuned the model for
187             an additional 10,000 iterations. We monitored the validation loss throughout both phases.
188
189   3.3.2    E XPERIMENT 2: S EQUENTIAL VS . S IMULTANEOUS I NTEGRATION OF R E LU AND
190            FIRE
191
192   In the second experiment, we investigated the impact of integrating ReLU and FIRE positional
193   embeddings into a pre-trained model, comparing sequential integration in different orders with si-
194   multaneous integration. Our goal was to determine the most effective strategy for incorporating
195
      both ReLU and FIRE and to understand how the order of integration affects model performance and
      training stability.
196
197
              • Simultaneous Training of ReLU and FIRE: Starting from the GPT-2 124M checkpoint,
198
                we simultaneously replaced the Softmax function with ReLU in the attention mechanism
199             and the absolute positional embeddings with FIRE. We then trained the model for 20,000
200             iterations, integrating both changes at once. Throughout the training process, we tracked
201             the validation loss and monitored the input and output distributions of the ReLU activation
202             function to observe how the model adapted to these simultaneous changes.
203
              • Sequential Training with ReLU Followed by FIRE: We first fine-tuned the GPT-2 124M
204             model by replacing the Softmax function with ReLU while retaining the absolute positional
205             embeddings. We trained the model for 10,000 iterations to allow it to adapt to the ReLU
206             activation in the attention mechanism. Subsequently, we replaced the absolute positional
207             embeddings with FIRE and continued fine-tuning for another 10,000 iterations. During
208             both phases, we monitored the validation loss and activation statistics to assess the model’s
209             adaptation and performance.
210           • Sequential Training with FIRE Followed by ReLU: Conversely, we also explored the
211             effect of introducing FIRE before ReLU. Starting from the GPT-2 124M checkpoint, we
212             replaced the absolute positional embeddings with FIRE while retaining the Softmax func-
213             tion and trained the model for 10,000 iterations to allow it to adapt to FIRE. Then, we re-
214             placed the Softmax function with ReLU and fine-tuned the model for an additional 10,000
215             iterations. We monitored validation loss and activation statistics throughout both training
                phases to evaluate the model’s performance and stability.


                                                         4
216
      By comparing these three integration strategies—simultaneous integration and sequential integration
217   in both orders—we aimed to determine the most effective approach for incorporating both ReLU and
218   FIRE into a pre-trained Transformer model. This comparison allowed us to assess how the order of
219   integration influences the model’s ability to adapt, the training stability, and the final performance in
220   terms of validation loss and length generalization capabilities.
221
222   3.3.3    E XPERIMENT 3: L ENGTH G ENERALIZATION E VALUATION
223
224   In the final experiment, we assessed the ability of each trained model to generalize to longer context

226
227           • Evaluation on Extended Context Lengths: We evaluated all models on context lengths

      3.4     I MPLEMENTATION OF THE FLARE A LGORITHM
235
236   To further improve computational efficiency, we proposed a fused FLARE algorithm which com-
237   bines FIRE position encoding with sparsity producing ReLU. This algorithm reorders comparison
238   of ReLU to occur before FIRE bias addition step for positional encoding.
239
      In the standard attention mechanism with FIRE and ReLU, attention score matrix S is computed as:
240
241
242                                          sij = max(aij + fij , 0)                                      (3)
243
244   where A = QK⊤ is the scaled dot-product of queries and keys, and F represents the FIRE bias
245   matrix.
246
      The FLARE algorithm optimizes this computation by observing that if fij ≤ −aij , then sij = 0,
247
      and the addition can be skipped. This leads to a case-wise expression:
248
249
250                                                 0,          if fij ≤ −aij
                                        sij =                                                              (4)
251                                                 aij + fij , otherwise
252
253   By implementing this logic, we can avoid unnecessary addition operations when fi j <= −ai j. We
254   conduct experiments to measure the proportion of operations which take this branch, reporting in
255   the results section.

257   FLARE module.

303

304
      3.6.1     H ARDWARE S YNTHESIS
305
306   We synthesized hardware implementations of the ReLU function and the Softmax function using a
307   130nm CMOS process technology. The implementations were designed to:
308
309           • Exploit Parallelism: Both ReLU implementations were designed to operate on multiple
310             inputs simultaneously, leveraging parallelism to increase throughput.
311           • Minimize Critical Path Delays: By simplifying the logic and avoiding complex opera-
312             tions, we reduced the critical path delays, allowing for higher operating frequencies.
313           • Optimize Resource Utilization: The designs aimed to minimize silicon area and power
314             consumption, making them suitable for resource-constrained edge devices.
315

              • Energy per Cycle (pJ): The energy consumed per clock cycle.

      7   C ONCLUSION
516
517
      In this paper, we explore fine-tuning as a method for improving performance ReLU in the atten-
      tion block for Transfomers. We demonstrate that fine-tuning models with ReLU achieves better
518
      validation-loss-per-iteration with ReLU from scratch, without adding additional compute steps.
519
520   We also show that with the correct fine-tuning-recipe, one can introduce both FIRE and ReLU into
521   a pre-trained model, obtaining the length-generalization, better improved final validation loss, and
522   high (98.9%) zeros introduced via ReLU’s output sparsity.
523   Our proposed FLARE algorithm efficiently fuses ReLU and FIRE, significantly reducing computa-
524   tional complexity by reordering the computation.
525
      Finally, our analysis shows that ReLU offers substantial performance, power, and area advantages
526
      over Softmax, making it highly suitable for edge devices. The ReLU-augmented FlashAttention
527   implementation can catalyze training of theser efficient models.
528
529   Overall, we hope this work relays the possibilities within exploration of fine-tuning recipes for
530
      various efficient algorithms, and support unblocking deploying hardware-friendly large language
      models on edge devices without compromising performance or longer-context capabilities.
