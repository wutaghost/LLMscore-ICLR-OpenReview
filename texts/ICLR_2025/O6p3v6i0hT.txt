009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks (GNNs) have shown significant success in modeling graph-
012            structured data. However, their performance often deteriorates when faced with a
013            change in the graph structure between training and test time, such as edge addition
014            or removal—a common scenario considering the dynamic nature of graphs. To ad-
015            dress this challenge, we propose FILLER (Framework for Integrating Layer-Level
016
               Edge-shift Recovery), a post-processing method which enhances the robustness of
               a GNN against edge sparsification while maintaining its adaptability to informa-
017
               tive edge addition. Our key idea is to fill in the representation gap caused by edge
018
               distribution shift by injecting the Edge-shift Recovery (ER) layer into each layer of
019            the GNN. Our ER layer is carefully designed to allow a GNN to maintain its high
020            performance in dynamic graph environments even without any additional training,
021            and its effectiveness is shown both theoretically and empirically. Our experiments
022            on ten datasets for node classification and five GNN architectures demonstrate that
023            FILLER is broadly applicable across diverse models and scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Graph-structured data are commonly observed across various fields and applications. Since graphs
028
      capture crucial information about the relationships between entities, graph neural networks (GNNs)
029
      have proven particularly effective in semi-supervised learning, where observed data are insufficient
030   to fully capture the relationship between features X and labels Y (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Hamilton
031   et al., 2017; Veličković et al., 2017; Gasteiger et al., 2019; Brody et al., 2022). In these scenarios,
032   the graph structure serves as a key manifold of high-dimensional features, significantly improving
033   the estimation of the conditional distribution p(Y |X) even with limited observations.
034
      However, due to the dynamic nature of graphs, their structural information can change after training
035
      time, with edges being removed, sparsified, or new informative edges being added (Hu et al., 2020;
036   Kazemi et al., 2020; Fu & He, 2022). Such changes can occur naturally in dynamic environments,
037   but can also be introduced artificially for various purposes. For example, graphs are often sparsified
038   to reduce the receptive field and enable faster inference (Ying et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2017; 2018).
039   Conversely, edges are often added to give additional information to GNNs, enhancing performance
040   (Chen et al., 2020; Alon & Yahav, 2020). As a consequence, it is common for a graph’s connectivity
041   to differ between training and testing time, a phenomenon referred to as edge distribution shift.
042   This shift is particularly problematic when edges are sparsified during inference, since it leads to a
043   significant performance drop for most GNN models. The reduction in neighbor information limits
044   the effectiveness of message propagation, which heavily depends on the graph’s structure. Previous
045   works (Hu et al., 2021; Yoo et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021b; Tian et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024)
046   have tackled this problem by using the graph structure only at training time and then discarding it
047   at inference. Some approaches combine a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with message propagation
048   (Yoo et al., 2019), allowing the MLP to learn from the graph. Others distill the knowledge from a
049   trained GNN into an MLP for similar purposes (Zhang et al., 2021b; Tian et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
050
      2024). These methods have demonstrated that structure-aware MLPs can match or even outperform
      GNNs, particularly when the test graph is sparsified due to edge distribution shift.
051
052   However, we argue that discarding edge information entirely during inference is not a fundamental
053   solution for building models robust to edge distribution shift. Edges are not only removed but can
      also be added, introducing new, informative signals in dynamic graph environments. An optimally

083   consistently improves the robustness of GNNs across various types of datasets. Additionally, we
084
      apply FILLER to five well-known GNN architectures, showing its versatility and ability to be effec-
      tively integrated into existing GNN models. Our code is provided in the supplementary material.

089   Notations. Let G = (V, E) be an undirected graph, where V is the set of nodes and E is the set of
090   edges. We denote the adjacency matrix of the graph by A ∈ {0, 1}|V |×|V | , where Aij = 1 if there is
091   an edge between nodes i and j, and Aij = 0 otherwise. The feature matrix of the nodes is denoted
092   by X ∈ R|V |×d0 , where d0 is the dimensionality of node features.
093
      Graph Neural Networks. Graph neural networks (GNNs) consist of multiple layers, where each
094   layer performs two key operations: aggregation (AGG) and update (UPDATE) (Gilmer et al., 2017;
095   Hu et al., 2019). The AGG operation gathers information from neighboring nodes, while the UP-
096   DATE operation combines this aggregated information with the node’s previous representation. We
097   denote the node representations after the l-th layer by H (l) ∈ R|V |×dl , where dl is its dimensional-
098   ity. We also set H (0) = X. Then, the l-th GNN layer is formally presented as
099
                      (l)                                                         (l)
100                HN = AGG(l) (H (l−1) , A),           H (l) = UPDATE(l) (HN , H (l−1) ).
101
      Based on these AGG and UPDATE operations, we define a GNN f with L layers as a function of
102
      X and A parameterized with its layers: H (L) = f (X, A; {AGG(l) , UPDATE(l) }L
                                                                                   l=1 ).

127   3.1   OVERVIEW OF FILLER
128
      Our key idea is to modify the GNN architecture in a layer-wise manner by introducing an Edge-shift
129
      Recovery (ER) layer as in Fig 2. The ER layer aims to recover for the perturbed edge connections
130   that the model may encounter during inference. By addressing each layer individually, we can more
131   effectively mitigate the impact of edge distribution shift, whose effect may vary across different lay-
132   ers. Specifically, our post-processing focuses on the AGG operation at each layer, since UPDATE
133   is independent of the adjacency matrix. We formulate the layer-wise post-processing as follows:
134
                               (l)    (l−1)               (l)    (l−1)                 (l−1)
135                      AGGt+1 (Ht           , A) = AGGt (Ht            , A) + k · g(Ht       ),           (1)
136
      where t is the number of post-processing iterations applied to the model and k > 0 is the step size
137   which controls the magnitude of each update. Based on this framework, we aim to find a good ER
138   layer g that can effectively mitigate the performance degradation caused by edge shift.
139
140
      GAP. To derive the objective of ER, we first define edge distribution shift as a test-time change of
141   the adjacency matrix A, which generates a perturbed adjacency matrix Ã. Given Ã at test time, the
142
      l-th GNN layer produces perturbed node representations as follows:
143                    (l)                                                           (l)
                    H̃N = AGG(l) (H (l−1) , Ã),         H̃ (l) = UPDATE(l) (H̃N , H (l−1) ).
144
145   To address the performance degradation, at each layer l, the ER layer g aims to restore the perturbed
146   node representations to their original states given the output of layer l − 1. Thus, g is defined as a
147   solution to the following problem, which we call GNN Aggregation Perturbation (GAP):
148                                                        (l)     (l)
149
                             g(H (l−1) ) = arg ming′ ∥(HN − H̃N ) − g ′ (H (l−1) )∥F .                      (2)
150   By focusing on the aggregated node representations before the UPDATE operation is applied, we
151   leverage the fact that most activation functions used in GNNs are Lipschitz-continuous. This prop-
152   erty ensures the difference in post-UPDATE representations is proportionally bounded by the differ-
153   ence in pre-UPDATE representations. Proofs of Lipschitz continuity for commonly used activation
154   functions—ReLU, Sigmoid, and GELU—are provided in Appendix B.
155   Essential Linearity of ER. While the function g could take any form, it is essential for our method
156   that g is a linear transformation: g(H (l−1) ) = H (l−1) W (l) , for two key reasons. First, the transfor-
157   mation can be computed efficiently in parallel with the original AGG operation as shown in Fig. 2.
158   As g takes less time to calculate than AGG does, which already includes a linear transformation in
159   most GNN architectures, the addition of g does not increase the inference time. Second, as we apply
160   our post-processing iteratively, the linearity of g enables us to consolidate multiple linear transfor-
161   mations across iterations into a single weight matrix. This prevents the model size from growing as
      the number of post-processing iterations increases, ensuring full scalability.

165   training time since edge distribution shift happens at test time. Thus, we propose virtual perturbation
166   to simulate potential changes in the graph structure before testing. Assuming a random perturbation
167   function ϕ which generalizes both edge removal and addition, we define virtual perturbation as the
168   expected value of the perturbed node representations which can be calculated empirically:
169                                      (l)    1 PN
                                 Eϕ [H̃N ] =            AGG(l) (H (l−1) , ϕ(A)),                          (3)
170                                             N i=1

173   ER-Basic Layer. With virtual perturbation, we propose the basic version of our Edge-shift Recov-
174
      ery (ER) layer as a solution to the solvable surrogate problem of GAP (GAP-surrogate) as follows:
                                                        (l)          (l)
175                     gB (H (l−1) ) = arg ming′ ∥(HN − Eϕ [H̃N ]) − g ′ (H (l−1) )∥F .                   (4)

177   The choice of ϕ is a hyperparameter, with various algorithmic options available, including all types
178   of graph structural augmentation (You et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2023), sparsification
179   (Rong et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021), and partitioning (Chiang et al., 2019). In our framework, we
180   make the simplest choice of ϕ, setting ϕ(A) = O to simulate the most extreme case of edge removal
181   which leaves no edges at test time. This approach is particularly efficient for virtual perturbation, as
182   it eliminates the need for N repetitive computations to estimate the expectation.

184
                                                              (l)          (l)
185                                    W (l) = H (l−1)+ (HN − E[H̃N ]),                                    (5)
186   where + denotes the pseudoinverse of a matrix, which can be computed effectively through singular
187   value decomposition (SVD). This method is more stable than training the weight matrix from scratch
188   with an objective. Additional experiments comparing both methods are provided in Appendix C.
189
      The time complexity of pseudoinverse is dominated by SVD, which is O(mn · min(m, n)) given a
190
      matrix of size m × n (Vasudevan & Ramakrishna, 2017). In our case, the target of pseudoinverse is
191
      H (l−1) ∈ R|V |×dl−1 , and it simplifies the complexity into O(|V |d2l−1 ) based on the safe assumption
192
      that dl−1 ≤ |V |. This is the same complexity as that of a linear transformation if dl−1 = dl , making
193   our approach highly scalable to large graphs while not requiring backpropagation for gradient-based
194   updates. Actual time consumption for pseudoinverse computation is provided in Appendix C.

198   Bridging GAP-surrogate to GAP. As described in Eq. 2, an ideal ER should perform well across
199   all types of perturbations. However, since gB is the solution to the GAP-surrogate problem, directly
200   injecting gB into each GNN layer only guarantees improved performance when the test-time graph
201   matches the virtual perturbation used during post-processing. We cannot be certain whether gB will
202
      result in a beneficial representation shift when the test graph differs from the virtually perturbed one.
      This is particularly important for practical use, as the processed model should maintain its original
203
      performance when the graph remains unchanged or undergoes minimal alteration.
204

      4   R ELATED W ORKS
261
262
      Graph Distribution Shift. Recent works have explored on addressing distribution shifts in graph
263
      learning, which occur when graph structures, node features, or labels change between training and
264   inference (Wu et al., 2024). Graph domain adaptation aims to transfer knowledge from a training
265   domain to a test domain (Xiao et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2022), while graph out-of-distribution learning
266   focuses on generalizing models to unseen test graphs (Li et al., 2022). Graph continual learning ad-
267   dresses evolving graphs, where models aims to adapt to temporal changes while retaining previously
268   learned information (Febrinanto et al., 2023). While these approaches typically focus on distribution
269   shifts in specific scenarios, our work takes a different approach. In this work, we solve the problem
      focusing on edge distribution shift, where the edges can be removed or even added at test time.

270
      Using MLPs for Graph Tasks. Recent works have considered multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) as
271   a scalable and robust alternative to graph neural networks (GNNs) for graph-based tasks. Unlike
272   GNNs, MLPs do not rely on neighborhood information during inference, which makes them inher-
273   ently robust to edge removal. BPN (Yoo et al., 2019) trains a structure-aware MLP by introducing
274   a novel loss function that minimizes the difference between prior distributions and their post-belief
275   propagation values. Graph-MLP (Hu et al., 2021) employs a contrastive loss to implicitly utilize the
276   graph adjacency information during training, achieving effective performance even without explicit
277   neighborhood information at test time. Other methods including NOSMOG (Tian et al., 2022) and
278   GLNN (Zhang et al., 2021b) utilize knowledge distillation from GNNs to MLPs to transfer struc-
279
      tural knowledge, achieving performance competitive with the teacher GNNs. VQGraph (Yang et al.,
      2024) extends this approach by learning discrete representations of local graph structures, enhancing
280
      MLP performance across various datasets. However, as MLPs ignore the neighborhood information
281
      at test graphs, they are unable to leverage new, informative edges given at test time; they lose edge
282   adaptability, which is the core aspect of GNNs. In this work, we design a post-processing technique
283   that can improve the robustness of GNNs while preserving their edge-adaptability.

      Datasets and Evaluation. We measure node classification accuracy for seven small-scale datasets
288
      (Cora, Citeseer, Pubmed, Computers, Photo, CS, and Physics (Shchur et al., 2018)) and three large-
289
      scale datasets (Flickr (Zeng et al., 2019), Ogbn-arxiv (Hu et al., 2020), and Reddit (Hamilton et al.,
290   2017)) in our experiments. For the small-scale datasets, we follow the data split configurations from
291   previous work (Shchur et al., 2018) and repeat each scenario 10 times with random data splits and
292   parameter initialization. For the large-scale datasets, we employ batch training with neighborhood
293   sampling for scalability. We use the fixed public data splits due to the high variance in these datasets
294   and run each experiment 5 times with random initialization.
295
      GNN Architectures. We conduct experiments using five GNN architectures as base models: SAGE
296   (Hamilton et al., 2017), GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016), SGC (Wu et al., 2019), GAT (Veličković et al.,
297   2017), and GIN (Xu et al., 2018), comparing their accuracy before and after applying FILLER. It is
298   notable that we exclude GIN (Xu et al., 2018) from the large-scale experiments as it is not designed
299   to work with neighbor sampling; GIN is originally designed to solve graph-level tasks such as graph
300   classification. See Appendix E for how the AGG operation is defined in these GNN models.
301
      Hyperparameters. For the small-scale datasets, we adopt GNNs with 2 layers and a hidden size
302   of 64 (Shchur et al., 2018). For the large-scale datasets, we increase the model size to 3 layers with a
303   hidden size of 256, which is a common practice for improved performance (Zeng et al., 2021). Other
304   hyperparameters are kept as consistent as possible across the different architectures, as our primary
305   objective is to evaluate performance gains from the post-processing rather than optimize individual
306   models. The only exception is GIN (Xu et al., 2018), which requires different settings to train due
307   to its unique design, as is not specifically designed for node classification tasks. Full details of the
308   hyperparameters used in our experiments can be found in Appendix F.

478   6    C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this work, we introduce FILLER (Framework for Integrating Layer-Level Edge-shift Recovery), a
481   general post-processing method for enhancing the robustness and adaptability of GNN models in the
482   face of edge distribution shift during inference. By inserting the Edge-shift Recovery (ER) module
483   into each GNN layer, FILLER effectively mitigates performance degradation while capitalizing on
484   newly introduced, informative edges. The effectiveness of our approach is theoretically justified in
485   terms of key characteristics–recovery, stability, and robustness, and empirically demonstrated on ten
      node classification benchmarks and five different GNN architectures.

      B     L IPSCHITZ C ONTINUITY
703
704
      Activation functions play a pivotal role in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by introducing non-
705
      linearity, which enables the network to model complex relationships within graph-structured data.
706
      Ensuring that these activation functions are Lipschitz continuous is essential for guaranteeing that
707   similarly aggregated representation can result a simliar output after applying the activation function.
708   In this section, we formally derive the Lipschitz continuity of three widely used activation functions:
709   Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU), Sigmoid, and Gaussian Error Linear Unit(GELU).
710
711
712   B.1     D EFINITION OF L IPSCHITZ C ONTINUITY
713
714   A function f : R → R is said to be Lipschitz continuous if there exists a constant L ≥ 0 such that
715   for all x, y ∈ R,
716                                        |f (x) − f (y)| ≤ L|x − y|.
717
718
719
720   B.2     R ECTIFIED L INEAR U NIT (R E LU)
721
722   The Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation function is defined as:
723
724                                          ReLU(x) = max(0, x).
725
726   To prove that ReLU is 1-Lipschitz continuous, we need to show that:
727
728                              |ReLU(x) − ReLU(y)| ≤ |x − y|        ∀x, y ∈ R.
729
730   Case 1: x ≥ 0 and y ≥ 0
731
      In this case,
732
733
                                      ReLU(x) = x and        ReLU(y) = y.
734   Thus,
735
                                  |ReLU(x) − ReLU(y)| = |x − y| ≤ |x − y|.
736
737
738
      Case 2: x < 0 and y < 0
739   Here,
740                                   ReLU(x) = 0 and        ReLU(y) = 0.
741
742   Therefore,
743                            |ReLU(x) − ReLU(y)| = |0 − 0| = 0 ≤ |x − y|.
744
745   Case 3: x ≥ 0 and y < 0 (without loss of generality)
746
747   In this scenario,
748                                   ReLU(x) = x and        ReLU(y) = 0.
749
750
      Thus,
751                            |ReLU(x) − ReLU(y)| = |x − 0| = |x| ≤ |x − y|.
752
      This inequality holds because x ≥ 0 and y < 0, implying |x| ≤ |x − y|.
753
754
755   In all cases, |ReLU(x) − ReLU(y)| ≤ |x − y|. Therefore, ReLU is 1-Lipschitz continuous.

1161
1162   In the following two pages, we present the complete results of our edge removal (Table 8) and edge
1163   restoration (Table 9) experiments across all datasets and architectures.
1164   Edge Removal Experiments. For small-scale datasets, FILLER improves performance across all
1165   architectures by an average of 1.35%, 4.01%, and 21.75% when 50%, 75%, and 100% of the edges
1166   are removed, respectively. In large-scale datasets, FILLER delivers average improvements across
1167   all architectures of 0.33%, 2.56%, and 28.49% under the same edge removal conditions. These
1168   consistent improvements across all datasets and architectures highlight FILLER ’s effectiveness as
1169   a general post-processing technique for GNNs.
1170   Although performance gains are relatively smaller in larger datasets when edges are partially re-
1171   moved(50%, 75%), FILLER shows substantial improvements when all edges are removed. This
1172   can be explained by the use of neighbor sampling during training on large datasets, which allows
1173   models to encounter graph perturbations during training. As a result, models experience less se-
1174   vere performance degradation when moderate sparsification occurs. However, even with neighbor
1175   sampling, models struggle to maintain robustness in highly sparsified graphs, leading to more signif-
1176   icant performance declines when many edges are removed. In such cases, FILLER greatly improves
1177   robustness, demonstrating its effectiveness on batch-trained models in large datasets.
1178   Edge Restoration Experiments. In the edge restoration experiments, GNNs were trained on 60%
1179   of the edges and tested with 0%, 50%, and 100% of the excluded edges restored. The post-processed
1180   models shows improved accuracy as more informative edges were restored, just as the base models
1181   did, demonstrating that FILLER preserves the edge-adaptability of GNNs. During edge restoration,
1182   the post-processed models do not show performance drop compared to the base model in 127 out of
1183   141 cases at the 1% significance level.
1184   Most of the accuracy drops (14 cases) occurred in large-scale datasets, while accuracy improvements
1185   were seen in small datasets. We hypothesize that this discrepancy is due to the use of neighbor sam-
1186   pling during training on large-scale datasets. Since neighbor sampling exposes the model to a limited
1187   number of edges during training, the model already experience adaptation to additional edges during
       inference, since all edges are used in inference; even before they are explicitly restored in testing. In
