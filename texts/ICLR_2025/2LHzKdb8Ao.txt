009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Equivariant policy learning has been shown to solve robotic manipulation tasks
012            with minimal training or demonstration data. However, the effectiveness of equiv-
013            ariance depends on whether transformations of the scene align with simple trans-
014            formations of the input data. This is true when the camera is in a top-down view,
015            but in the common case where a camera views the robot workspace from the side,
016
               there is a symmetry mismatch, reducing model performance. We show that equiv-
               ariant methods perform better when camera images are transformed to appear as
017
               top-down images. Our approach is simple to implement, works for RGB and
018
               RGBD images, and reliably improves performance across different view angles
019            and learning algorithms.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Policy learning methods in robotics typically rely on expert demonstrations or environment inter-
025   actions, which are costly and time-consuming to provide. A growing body of work has shown that
026   equivariant networks significantly reduce the amount of data needed to solve manipulation tasks by
027
      building symmetry into the model as an inductive bias (Wang et al., 2022c; Simeonov et al., 2022).
      Since robotics tasks often require generalizing across spatial transformations of the scene or objects,
028
      networks that are equivariant to rotations and translations of the input can learn robust policies with
029
      significantly less training data than would otherwise be needed.
030
031   Most existing work on equivariant robot learning assumes that the symmetry transformations are
032   easy to compute over the input space. This is usually required due to how we create equivariant
033
      networks; the action of the symmetry group is used to generate a weight-sharing scheme which
      constrains the networks layers. However, this can be a problem in robotics when the viewpoint of
034
      a physical camera does not align well with the axis about which a rotation symmetry exists in the
035
      environment. In particular, an important and standard setting in robotic manipulation involves a
036   camera which views a scene from the side (a “sideview”) (Luo et al., 2024; Mandlekar et al., 2023;
037   Brohan et al., 2023), not from the top. This presents a challenge because these robotics domains are
038   generally symmetric about the vertical gravity axis and this axis does not align well with the camera
039   viewpoint (e.g. see Figure 1). The task symmetry is given by rotating and translating objects in the
040   scene, but computing the new sideview image is not a simple image transform due to occlusion and
041   perspective.
042   Wang et al. (2022b) showed that equivariant networks can still be effective when there is some
043   mismatch between the symmetry group used to constrain the model and the physically accurate
044   task symmetry. Specifically, they found that using image rotations on sideview images to capture
045   O(2) actions on the scene is better than not using equivariance. Nevertheless, there is a noticeable
046   performance gap when compared to the top-down image setting.
047
      In this work, we investigate how to reduce this gap. We find that simple, well-known computer vision
048   techniques can be employed as a preprocessing step to improve performance for O(2) equivariant
049   robot learning. In the case of sideview RGBD images, the depth channel allows us to interpret the
050   image as a point cloud and reproject the image from a top-down view, filling in missing data with
051   interpolation. In the case of RGB images, a homographic perspective transform can approximate a
052   top-down view. These steps can be added to existing equivariant methods and do not require access
053   to privileged information or additional training data. We evaluate the approach on a set of simulated
      robotic manipulation tasks where the camera views the scene at an angle.

081   2   R ELATED W ORKS
082
083   Equivariant Robot Learning Robot learning is an ideal setting for equivariant networks because
084
      data is limited and tasks are almost always equivariant to spatial transformations of the scene. Many
      works have used SO(2)- or SE(2)-equivariant convolutional networks with top-down camera images
085
      to learn robotic grasping (Zhu et al., 2022), pick and place (Huang et al., 2022), and closed-loop
086
      manipulation skills (Wang et al., 2022c; Jia et al., 2023). Other works explored SO(3)- or SE(3)-
087   equivariant networks that operate on point clouds or voxel grids to learn pick-and-place (Pan et al.,
088   2023; Simeonov et al., 2022; Ryu et al., 2022; 2023; Huang et al., 2024a), or general manipulation
089   skills (Brehmer et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023). However, the prior works typically require a carefully
090   designed input (i.e., top-down images) to align the input symmetry with the task symmetry. Unlike
091   these works, our effort explores a more general setting where the input symmetry does not align
092   with the task symmetry.
093
094   Learning Latent or Approximate Symmetry For some learning problems, there could be a mis-
095   match between the symmetry in the ground truth function and the symmetry in the equivariant
096   network because the symmetry cannot be easily described in the input space or the ground truth
097   function is only partially symmetric. Falorsi et al. (2018) and Park et al. (2022) showed that sym-
098   metric neural representations can be extracted using traditional networks with a self-supervised loss.
099
      These symmetric representations can be further processed with equivariant layers leading to im-
      proved generalization (Esteves et al., 2019; Klee et al., 2023). Another solution to combat this
100
      problem is to use approximate or relaxed equivariant neural networks (Wang et al., 2022e; 2024b;
101
      Huang et al., 2024b) to relax the equivariant constraint in the network to better match the symmetry
102   in the ground truth function. Alternatively, Wang et al. (2022b) showed that even with the symmetry
103   match, a fully equivariant model that enforces symmetry to out-of-distribution data can still out-
104   perform non-equivariant baselines, as long as the symmetry in the model does not conflict with the
105   ground truth function (Wang et al., 2024a). A similar finding was shown in De Silva et al. (2023)
106   where training with out-of-distribution data could aid learning. Although the solution of Wang et al.
107   (2022b) is simple and effective, there remains a significant performance gap compared to not having
      the symmetry mismatch. Our work provides a simple means to close this gap.

      Image Reprojection in Deep Learning Image reprojection, or transforming an image between
109   projected coordinate spaces or camera frames, is a well-known and common technique in computer
110   vision, graphics and robotics. In the context of deep learning, perspective transforms have been used
111   as data augmentation to improve robustness for pose estimation (Mohlin et al., 2020). Jaderberg
112   et al. (2015); Gao et al. (2020) proposed adding affine and perspective transforms with trainable
113   parameters to convolutional networks. In robot learning, some works (Goyal et al., 2023; Ten Pas
114   et al., 2017) have projected images from 3D data like voxels or point clouds as input which enabled
115   much faster training and inference. Lin et al. (2023) trained a Neural Radiance Field representation
116   of a scene then rendered novel view images. A CNN was trained to predict SO(2) manipulation
117
      actions in the respective image planes. Our work also uses reprojection to align the action space
      with the image plane. However, our work does not need to be trained so it is well-suited for settings
118
      with limited data.

123   3.1   E QUIVARIANT N ETWORKS
124
      An equivariant function preserves symmetry in the input and output spaces. Given a symmetry
125
      group G that acts on domain x ∈ X and codomain y ∈ Y with group representations ρx and ρy ,
126
      respectively, a function f is equivariant to G if
127
128                                         f (ρx (g)x) = ρy (g)f (x)                                    (1)
129   for all g ∈ G. In other words, applying a transformation to the input produces a transformed
130   output. Notice that the representations ρx and ρy are assumed to be known in advance in order
131   to design networks that satisfy the equivariant constraint. For example, equivariant convolutional
132   networks are implemented by constructing kernels K that satisfy the equivariant kernel constraint
133   K(gv) = ρout (g)K(v)ρin (g)−1 where ρin and ρout are the representations for the input and output
134   of the layer (Cohen et al., 2019).
135   Equivariant networks are composed of equivariant layers and equivariant activation functions. To
136   properly leverage symmetry this way, it is common to assume that the input is structured such that
137   group representations ρx (g) can be easily defined and computed. For example, when the input is an
138   image and G = SO(2), a top-down image is typically used where the group representation on the
139   input is defined as image rotation.
140
141   3.2   E QUIVARIANT P OLICY L EARNING
142
143   Policy learning is a framework for solving decision problems. The objective is to train a policy
144   network π : S → A that maps from states to actions in order to achieve some desired outcome
145
      or behavior. In reinforcement learning, the policy is optimized to select actions that maximize the
      value of the state action pair, where the value function Q : S × A → R is represented with a separate
146
      critic network. In imitation learning, the policy is optimized to match the distribution arising from a
147
      dataset of expert demonstrations.
148
149   Wang et al. (2022c) showed that many relevant robotics tasks can be expressed as group-invariant
150   MDPs. In these cases, the optimal policy network is equivariant: ρa (g)π(s) = π(ρs (g)s) for all
151
      g ∈ G, s ∈ S. Note that the representations ρs and ρa may flexibly define how observations and
      action variables should change or remain invariant. A straightforward example is robotic grasping:
152
      if the scene is transformed then the optimal grasp pose is transformed in the same way, but the
153
      optimal gripper opening remains fixed. The reward function is invariant which means the optimal
154   critic network is also invariant: Q(s, a) = Q(ρs (g)s, ρa (g)a) for all g ∈ G, s ∈ S.
155
156
      3.3   E XTRINSIC S YMMETRY
157
158   Although prior works (Wang et al., 2022a;d; Jia et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023)
159   demonstrated promising results with equivariant policy learning in various problem settings, they all
160   require an aligned, top-down image observation, which limits the generalizability of the method. Re-
161   cently, Wang et al. (2022b) showed that equivariant policy learning still outperforms non-equivariant
      methods with an arbitrary camera view (this is called extrinsic equivariance because the symmetry

163   gap comparing extrinsic equivariant methods with the ideal top-down setting. In this work, we in-
164   vestigate the performance gap and propose a simple yet efficient method that improves equivariant
165   policy learning with an arbitrary camera angle.

      4     M ETHOD
169
      In robotic manipulation tasks, we want the equivariant network to generalize across transformations
170
      of the physical scene. In the case of a sideview camera image, there is a mismatch between the
171   transformations in image space and in world space. In this section, we describe two ways to prepro-
172   cess sideview images, such that the resulting image features transform similarly to the underlying
173   physical scene. Both preprocessing approaches are simple to implement. They do not require any
174   pre-training or modification to the robot or sensor setups, and only require knowledge of the camera
175   intrinsics and extrinsics.
176
177   4.1   R EPROJECTION OF RGBD I MAGES
178
179   We know that the ideal case for an equivariant network is an
180   image captured by a top-down camera. When the sideview
181
      camera captures RGBD images, we can generate a colored
      point cloud and render a new image using an imaginary top-
182
      down camera (see Figure 2).
183
184   The process for reprojecting an RGBD image onto another im-
185   age plane is well-known, and implemented in several libraries
186   (Bradski, 2000; Zhou et al., 2018). We describe the process
187
      in some detail here. Given camera principal point (cx , cy ) and
      focal lengths fx , fy , we first generate a point cloud in the side-
188
      view camera frame from the depth image, D(u, v) : Z2 → R,
189
      by computing 3D coordinates for each pixel location (u, v)

195

201
202   This step produces coordinates on the continuous 2D image plane, e.g. (u′ , v ′ ) ∈ R2 . To generate
203   an image array, we bin all values corresponding to a discrete pixel and select the one with the lowest
204   value of z ′ . We compute RGB color values for the new image by keeping track of which pixel in the
205
      original image produced the depth value in the new image. In practice, we discard all points in the
      point cloud above the gripper height before rendering, to prevent the robot arm from occluding the
206
      scene in the reprojected image.
207
208
      Occluded Regions The RGBD image represents a partial point cloud of the scene so the repro-
209
      jected top-down image will contain empty regions due to occlusion. We assume the original image
210   contains all necessary information to solve the problem so the occlusions should not prevent learn-
211   ing. The occlusions create extrinsic symmetry (Wang et al., 2024a). That is, the occlusions are
212   always located above an object in the image but the equivariant networks expects them to be dis-
213   tributed symmetrically around the object. As extrinsic symmetry can harm performance, we infill
214   the occluded regions with nearest neighbor interpolation (see Appendix A.1). This reduces extrinsic
215   symmetry, but may not be a general solution when the scene is highly cluttered. In robotic ma-
      nipulation environments, placing the camera higher up (e.g. closer to top-down) reduces occlusion

217   in Section 5.6 and Appendix A.5.

      4.2   P ERSPECTIVE T RANSFORM OF RGB I MAGES
220
221   RGB images are a popular input modality for robotic manipulation due to their low cost (Zhao
222   et al., 2023) and ability to capture information at small distances (Luo et al., 2024). An RGB image
223   does not contain spatial information about the scene so we cannot perform the reprojection step
224   from above. Instead, we propose applying a perspective transformation to align the ground plane of
225   the world with the image plane (this technique is sometimes referred to as perspective removal or
226   correction). A perspective transformation is a linear mapping of coordinates between two 2D planes.
227
      This linear mapping is described using a homography matrix.
228   In this setting, we want a mapping that sends the four corners of the robot workspace (3D positions
229   on the ground plane) to the four corners of the image plane (see Figure 3). The first step is to
230   calculate the pixel coordinates of the robot workspace in the image. We transform the 3D position
231   of the workspace corners into the camera frame using the rotation and translation of the camera in
232   the world frame, R ∈ SO(3) and t ∈ R3 , respectively. Then, we project onto the image plane
233
      using the camera intrinsic matrix K. Given a point x ∈ R3 , we calculate the pixel location as a
      homogeneous coordinate y = K(Rx + t).
234
235   The next step is to compute the homography matrix given the four workspace corner pixels and the
236   image corner pixels. (We use the OpenCV implementation (Bradski, 2000)). In the final step, we
237   generate a new image by transforming all pixel locations with the homography matrix and interpolate
238   the values onto an image grid.
239
240
      Out-of-plane Distortion A perspective transfor-
      mation maps features from one 2D plane to another.
241
      If the scene captured by the camera only included
242
      the ground plane, the transformed image would per-
243   fectly represent the scene from a top-down view. In
244   practice, robotic manipulation environments contain
245   3D objects on top of the ground plane. The appear-
246   ance of these objects is distorted in the perspective
247   transformation, with more distortion the higher the
248   object is above the plane. The distortions make it         Figure 3: Illustration of perspective trans-

252
253   The robotic gripper is substantially distorted and occasionally even shifted outside the frame of view
254   (see Figure 3 right). To re-inject information about the gripper, we concatenate a ‘gripper image’
255   along the channel dimension the transformation. The gripper image illustrates where the gripper
256   fingers would lie in the new image plane; the pixel value at the gripper finger locations conveys the
257
      gripper height (see Appendix A.1 for a visualization).
258   The distortion of objects presents a unique challenge to a rotationally equivariant network. Such a
259   network cannot form filters that are specific to an absolute orientation. Thus, an equivariant network
260   will not be able to easily resolve the distortions of objects which always occur toward the top of the
261   original image. To overcome this we encode the camera viewpoint as a 2D vector pointing from the
262   workspace center toward the camera and append this information as an additional two channels. In
263
      practice, the two channels are encoded as a pho1 feature, which rotates in 2D as the image is rotated.

304
305   In our experiments, we want to understand whether the proposed preprocessing steps help the equiv-
306   ariant networks learn. In the results section, we refer to networks that use reprojection as Reproj.
307   Equi and perspective transform as Persp. Equi. We directly compare to using the un-aligned, side-
308
      view image with the equivariant network (Sideview Equi) as used in Wang et al. (2022b). By default,
      the sideview image is generated with a camera angled at 45 degrees above the horizon. Additionally,
309
      we baseline against an oracle observer Oracle Equi, which sees the environment from a top-down
310
      view. These images have perfect symmetry match, so we expect the equivariant networks to per-
311   form best here. We refer to this baseline as an oracle, because it is almost impossible to achieve
312   such images in the real-world (multiple RGBD camera views must be fused, robotic mesh removed
313   and the image re-rendered). In addition, for the RGBD observation case, we include comparisons
314   to equivariant networks that take point clouds and voxel grids as input (which are transformed to
315   world space so there is perfect symmetry match). Finally, we compare to a non-equivariant baseline
316   (Sideview NonEqui) as a reference point, which takes sideview images as input.
317   All methods use networks with a similar number of trainable parameters and follow the same training
318   scheme (data augmentation, learning rate, etc.). See Appendix A.4 for more details on the network
319   architectures.

400   5.6   E FFECTS OF C AMERA A NGLE
401
402   In this section, we demonstrate that the proposed preprocessing steps are effective over a range of
403   camera view angles. The preprocessing steps are general, so they can be applied in any setting where
404   the camera parameters (intrinsic and extrinsic) are known. However, the farther the camera moves
405   from a top-down view, the more the processed image will contain occluded regions or distortions.
406   To understand how sensitive the method is to camera viewpoint, we recreate the behavior cloning
407   experiments from earlier, but modulate the viewing angle of the camera from 15 to 75 degrees
408   above horizontal. The results are reported in Figure 7 for both RGBD and RGB images, using
409   ten demonstrations. We find that the equivariant network achieves higher success rates as the view
410   angle increases. This was previously observed by Wang et al. (2022b) with equivariant SAC. Since
411   the equivariant networks we use assume that the problem symmetry is described as rotations of the
412   image, it makes sense that performance increases as the image appears more top-down.
413   With our preprocessing steps, the equivariant networks achieve strong performance at lower camera
414   view angles. At a view angle of 75 degrees, the robot arm often occludes large portions of the image,
415   which is why the non oracle methods do not appear to converge to the oracle at 90 degrees as one
416   would expect. On block stacking, the equivariant method with our preprocessing outperforms the
417   oracle. We believe this is because the sideview perspective is more informative to solve this task
418   (with a top-down view the top block occludes the lower block during placement).

      5.7   B REAKING OR R ELAXING N ETWORK E QUIVARIANCE
433
434   The reprojection and perspective transform operations discussed in Section 4 introduce artifacts into
435   the image, either due to missing pixels or distortion. These artifacts are linked to the original camera
436   view since occlusions and distortions always occur on the far side of objects. As the images are
437   processed with equivariant layers, the network will form representations of the rotated occlusions
438   and distortions even though they would never rotate in real life (the front of an object is never
439   occluded).
440   We proposed using interpolation to fill missing pixels and injecting view-direction information to en-
441   able the equivariant networks to learn effectively in the RGBD and RGB settings, respectively. Here,
442   we empirically justify these steps by comparing versions of our method using networks with normal,
443   broken and relaxed equivariance. For the relaxed version, we modify the equivariant network to use
444   relaxed group convolutions introduced by Wang et al. (2022e). Relaxed group convolutions contain
445   parameters which allow the model to deviate from perfect equivariance during training. The broken
446
      equivariance version is what we propose using with the perspective transform, where we add a stan-
      dard representation (pho1 feature) to the input that encodes the camera view direction. Since this
447
      additional input vector provides a fixed frame of reference, it breaks the symmetry of the equivariant
448
      model with respect to the observation. Additional details on these methods are in A.4.

      We find that relaxing or breaking equivariance in the RGBD setting results in worse performance of
      the policy (see Table 3). This suggests that the interpolation scheme effectively reduces the influence
462
      of the occluded regions on the learning. In contrast, we find that breaking equivariance results in the
463
      best performance in the RGB setting. In both settings, we find that the relaxed equivariance approach
464   does the worst, despite significant effort to tune the hyperparameters. We believe relaxation could
465   work in theory, but would have to be designed to specifically relax equivariance along a single
466   direction to work effectively on this problem, where there is very little data with which to learn the
467   relaxation weights.

      6     C ONCLUSION
470
471
      In this work, we propose two simple preprocessing steps for O(2) equivariant robot learning with
472
      sideview camera images. Since equivariant networks are designed for the case where the symmetry
473   action is well-defined on the input space, the preprocessing steps transform the sideview images to
474   look like images captured by a top-down camera. The first preprocessing step is reprojection, which
475   can be applied to an RGBD image. Reprojection generates a partial point cloud from the image,
476   then renders a new image with a virtual top-down camera frame. When depth information is not
477   available, we propose using perspective transform instead, which maps visual information from the
478   environment ground plane to a top-down camera frame.
479
480   Limitations We assume access to the camera extrinsics to perform the reprojection and perspective
481   transformation. It may be difficult to estimate the camera pose with respect to the workspace when
482   performing mobile manipulation or interacting with a complex workspace (e.g. no obvious ground
483
      plane). It may be possible to use a trainable reprojection operation, such as (Jaderberg et al., 2015;
      Gao et al., 2020), but it is unclear how well that would work with limited data. Another limitation is
484
      that the preprocessing steps do not fully close the performance gap compared to the top-down image
485
      case.

      A.3   T RAINING D ETAILS
749
750   Soft Actor Critic from Demonstrations (SACfD) We follow the implementation of SACfD from
751   (Wang et al., 2022c). We use a learning rate of 0.001, a batch size of 128, a discounting factor of
752   0.99, a target update interval of 100 optimization steps, a weighting of 1.0 on the expert action
753   loss, and the Adam optimizer with default parameters. We use separate actor and critic networks.
754   The number of demonstrations and training steps varies by environment (this is common practice
755   from earlier work such that there is convergence across tasks of different difficulty). We use 20
      demonstration episodes and 5,000 environment steps for all tasks except block in bowl and block


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      stacking, which are given 50 demonstrations and 10,000 environment steps. We run five simulators
757   in parallel during training, and perform a single optimization step after every joint environment
758   interaction (5 to 1 ratio of environment steps to optimization steps). We use a prioritized experience
759   replay buffer (Schaul et al., 2015) to store the transitions with parameters: ϵ = 1e − 6, α = 0.6
760   and β = 0.4. We randomly rotate transitions four times when adding to the replay buffer, and
761   apply another random crop augmentation when sampling from the buffer similar to RAD (Laskin
762   et al., 2020). We evaluate the learned policies every 200 environment steps throughout training by
763   averaging the discounted sum of rewards over 100 episodes (this is what we show in the learning
764   curve plots).
765
766   Behavior Cloning (BC) The policy network in behavior cloning is optimized with a mean squared
767   loss between the expert action and the predicted action. We use a learning rate of 0.001, a batchsize
768   of 128, and the Adam optimizer with default parameters. We train for 5,000 optimization steps
769   for all BC experiments. Every 200 optimization steps, we evaluate the learned policy by averaging
770   the success rate over 100 rollouts. In the tables, we report the maximum success rate achieved
771   throughout training.
772   Our experiments were run on NVIDIA RTX 2080 GPUs with 12Gb of GPU RAM. Each run takes
773   around 2-4 hours on a single GPU, depending on whether it is 5K or 10K steps.
774
775
      A.4    N ETWORK D ETAILS
776
777
      The SACfD agent has an actor and a critic network. The behavior cloning agent uses the same actor
778
      network architecture as SACfD. The actor and critic networks share similar architectures: an image
779   encoder followed by an MLP head.
780
781   Equivariant Image Encoder The equivariant encoder is a D4 -equivariant convolutional network
782
      implemented with escnn (Cesa et al., 2022; Weiler & Cesa, 2019). The encoder has seven 2D
      convolution layers, with ReLU activations, and maxpooling such that the 128-by-128 input image
783
      is gradually downsampled to a 1x1 feature map. The input representation is trivial, and all internal
784
      representations are regular. The output of the network is a 128-dimensional regular representation.
785   The equivariant image encoder has 1.1 M trainable parameters.
786
787   Equivariant Actor The equivariant actor is composed of an equivariant image encoder (described
788
      above) and a final equivariant linear layer. This final layer is D4 -equivariant that maps from a 128-
      dimensional regular representation to the action representation: (ax y, az , aθ , aλ ), where ax y is a ρ1
789
      representation (2D vector) and az , aθ , aλ are ρ0 representations (scalars). This is the actor network
790
      used by Sideview Equi, Oracle Equi, Reproj. Equi, Persp. Equi, for all experiments.
791
792   Equivariant Critic The equivariant critic includes the equivariant image encoder and two MLP
793   heads. The action is concatenated to the output of the image encoder (this forms a mixed repre-
794
      sentation: 128 regular representations and the action representation described above). This mixed
      representation is processed independently by two MLPs. Each MLP is a D4 equivariant linear layer,
795
      followed by a ReLU, a group pooling operation, and a final D4 equivariant layer. The output of each
796
      MLP is a scalar value describing the value of the state-action pair. SAC uses the minimum of the
797   two predicted state-action values to update the critic and actor parameters (Haarnoja et al., 2018).
798   This is the critic network used by Sideview Equi, Oracle Equi, Reproj. Equi, Persp. Equi, for all
799   experiments.
800
      Non Equivariant Image Encoder The non equivariant image encoder shares the exact same ar-
801
      chitecture as the equivariant encoder, where all convolutional layers are standard 2D convolutions.
802
      The size of the hidden dimensions inside the network are adjusted such that number of trainable
803   parameters matches the equivariant encoder (1.1 M parameters).
804
805   Non Equivariant Actor The non equivariant actor is composed of the non equivariant image en-
806
      coder and a linear layer. The linear layer predicts the five-dimensional action (plus an additional five
      values for the standard deviation when using SACfD). This is the actor network used by Sideview
807
      NonEqui in all experiments.
808
809   Non Equivariant Critic The non equivariant critic is composed of the non equivariant image en-
      coder and two MLPs. The action is concatenated to the output of the image encoder and then pro-


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      cessed separately by two MLPs. Each MLP has two linear layers separated by a ReLU activation.
811   This is the critic network used by Sideview NonEqui in all experiments.
812
813   Equivariant Point Cloud Encoder The architecture evaluated in Table 1 is described here. We
814
      implement a D4 -equivariant PointNet++ architecture (Qi et al., 2017), where all linear layers in the
      original network are replaced with equivariant versions. We use regular representations throughout
815
      the network, so equivariance is preserved through the ReLU activations and pooling schemes. The
816
      network is composed of three point set abstraction blocks with multi-scale grouping, and one point
817   set abstraction block with single scale grouping, and a final linear layer that produces the equivari-
818   ant action representation described in the equivariant actor above. The dimensionality of the hidden
819   dimensions is modified so the number of trainable parameters is comparable to the other baselines
820   (1.2 M parameters). The point cloud network runs significantly slower than the convolutional archi-
821   tectures, and a batchsize of 128 does not fit in 12Gb of GPU memory. We downsample the scene
822   point clouds to 1024 points, and normalize the coordinates during training and evaluation.
823   Equivariant Voxel Encoder This network was constructed by modifying the architecture of the
824   equivariant image encoder to use 3D convolutional layers instead of 2D convolutional layers. The
825   3D convolutional layers were instantiated using D4 equivariance constraints on the weights. We
826   reduced the number of channels at each layer such that the resulting encoder had comparable number
827   of parameters to other baselines. Additionally, we set the strides and pooling in the z-direction such
828   that the downsampling occurred gradually in all three dimensions. We generated voxel grids from
829   RGBD observations using a grid size of 0.625cm, resulting in a 64x64x32 grid observation.
830   Relaxed Equivariance We use the same equivariant actor architecture but substitute all equivariant
831   convolution layers in the encoder with relaxed group convolution layers (Wang et al., 2022e). We
832   use a single filter bank to keep the number of parameters similar (the relaxed equivariant network
833   ends up with 1.6 M parameters since it does not use steerable kernels). We tested a range of values
834   for the regularization values: {1, 0.1, 0.01, 1e − 4, 1e − 6} and 1e − 4 performed best.
835   Broken Equivariance We use the same equivariant actor network as above. The only modification
836   is that we modify the input of the network to accept two additional channels. These channels contain
837   a 2D vector pointing toward the camera viewpoint, and the network treats them as ρ1 equivariant
838   features. The idea is illustrated in Figure 12

      A.6   P ERSPECTIVE T RANSFORM G ROUND H EIGHT
911
912   The perspective transform assumes that all the image features live on a single plane, which we set
913   to be the lower plane of the workspace, e.g. table surface. As we have already mentioned, this
914   assumption is clearly violated for robotics tasks that contain 3D objects on top of the plane. Without
915   access to depth information, we cannot identify and correct for distortions of the out-of-plane image
916   features. Nevertheless, it is possible that the choice of ground plane may impact performance. We
917   ran experiments to understand how the height of the plane used in the perspective transform affects
      performance. The results are reported in tab:sideview:persp-heights.


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      We find that the performance is robust to minor changes in the plane height of the perspective
919   transform. For block stacking, the best performance is achieved with a plane height of 2.5 cm,
920   which is roughly the height of the block. For drawer opening and block in bowl, the performance is
921   stable up to 5.0 cm above the table surface. We believe any plane height that is within the working
922   area of the task, e.g. the space where the dexterous behavior occurs, should work well. We also
923   evaluated a relative plane height, where the plane height is dynamically set to be the height of the
924   gripper fingers. A relative plane height does not work well at all, likely because the way objects
925   distort changes based on robot actions, which confuses the convolutional network. We hope to
926   investigate this setting more deeply in the future, since it could be useful for robotic manipulation
