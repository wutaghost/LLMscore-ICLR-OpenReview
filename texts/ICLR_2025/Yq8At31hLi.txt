009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               We present SMI-TED (SMILE Transformer Encoder Decoder), a large-scale foun-
012            dation model for materials and chemistry, trained on a massive dataset of 91 million
013            SMILES samples (4 billion molecular tokens) from PubChem using self-supervised
014            learning. Our encoder-decoder architecture enables a wide range of complex tasks,
015            including the prediction of quantum chemical properties and reaction yields. We
016            offer two model variants, with 289M and 8 × 289M parameters, respectively, to
017            accommodate different use cases. Our model achieves state-of-the-art results across
018            multiple benchmark datasets, demonstrating its versatility and effectiveness. No-
019            tably, our model’s latent space exhibits compositionality and separability, essential
020
               properties for higher-level reasoning tasks and few-shot learning capabilities. To
               facilitate further research and applications, we make our model weights and source
021
               code publicly available on HuggingFace and GitHub, respectively.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Understanding molecular properties is crucial for accelerating discoveries in different fields, including
028   drug development and materials science Pan (2023). Traditional methods rely on labor-intensive trial-
029   and-error experiments, which are both costly and time-consuming Jablonka et al. (2024). However,
030   recent advances in deep learning have enabled the use of foundation models to predict molecular
031   properties and generate molecule candidates Flam-Shepherd et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023); Wen
032   et al. (2023), marking significant progress in scientific exploration.
033   The introduction of large-scale pre-training methodologies for chemical language models (LMs)
034   represents a significant advancement in cheminformatics Sadybekov & Katritch (2023). These
035   methodologies have demonstrated impressive results in challenging molecular tasks such as predicting
036   properties and generating molecules Ross et al. (2022). The success of these models can be attributed
037   to their ability to learn contextualized representations of input tokens through self-supervised learning
038   on large unlabeled corpora Bommasani et al. (2021). This methodological approach typically involves
039
      two phases: pre-training on unlabeled data followed by fine-tuning on specific downstream task
      Yang et al. (2023). By reducing the reliance on annotated datasets, this approach has broadened our
040
      understanding of chemical language representations Guo et al. (2023).
041
042   Simplified Molecular-Input Line Entry System, SMILES, provide natural graphs that encode the
043   connectivity information from the line annotations of molecular structures Li et al. (2022). SMILES
044   defines a character string representation of a molecule by performing a depth-first pre-order spanning
045
      tree traversal of the molecular graph, generating symbols for each atom, bond, tree-traversal decision,
      and broken cycles Wei et al. (2023). Therefore, the resulting character string corresponds to a
046
      flattening of a spanning tree of the molecular graph. SMILES is widely adopted for molecular
047
      property prediction as SMILES is generally more compact than other methods of representing
048   structure, including graphs Öztürk et al. (2020). There are billions of SMILES available on different
049   open-sources repositories Tingle et al. (2023). However, most SMILES sequences do not belong to
050   well-defined molecules Wigh et al. (2022). Alternative string-based representations exist, such as
051   SELFIES. However, focusing on molecular optimization tasks on the learned representation space,
052   suggested no obvious shortcoming of SMILES with respect to SELFIES in terms of optimization
053   ability and sample efficiency Gao et al. (2022). The quality of the pre-training data plays a more
      important role on the outcome of the foundation model Wang et al. (2023); Takeda et al. (2023).

      Towards this direction, we present a novel family of molecular encoder-decoder foundation models,
055   denoted as SMI-TED289M . Our SMI-TED289M encoder-decoder foundation model was obtained
056   using a transformer-based molecular tokens encoder model aligned with an encoder-decoder mecha-
057   nism trained on a large corpus of 91 million carefully curated molecules from PubChem Kim et al.
058   (2023), resulting in 4 billion molecular tokens. Our main contributions are:
059
060           • We pre-train a large-scale family of encoder-decoder molecular open-source foundation
061             models, denoted as SMI-TED289M , on over 91 million molecules carefully curated from
062
                PubChem Kim et al. (2023), which is equivalent to 4 billion of molecular tokens.
063           • Our SMI-TED289M family of foundation models encompasses two distinct configurations:
064             base, which has 289 million parameters; and the Mixture-of-OSMI -Experts, MoE-OSMI ,
065             characterized by a composition of 8 × 289M parameters. Checkpoints for these models are
066
                fully accessible on HuggingFace: suppressed for blind review. Moreover, the source code
                is available at: suppressed for blind review.
067
068           • We perform extensive experimentation on several classification and regression tasks from 11
069             benchmark datasets, covering quantum mechanical, physical, biophysical, and physiological
070
                property prediction of small molecules. We also evaluate the reconstruction capacity of our
                SMI-TED289M considering the MOSES benchmarking dataset Polykovskiy et al. (2020).
071
                We also conducted high-throughput experiments on Pd-catalyzed Buchwald–Hartwig C–N
072
                cross-coupling reactions, predicting reaction yields. Furthermore, a study investigating the
073             embedding created by SMI-TED289M and few-shot learning is also provided, indicating
074             compositionality of the learned molecular representations.
075
076   Our results section demonstrates state-of-the-art performance of SMI-TED289M on different tasks,
077   molecular properties prediction, molecule reconstruction, and an efficient metric for molecular latent
078   space. Compositionality of the latent space suggests strong potential for chemical reasoning tasks.
079   The SMI-TED289M family consists of two main variants (289M, and 8 × 289M ), offering flexibility
080
      and scalability for different scientific applications.

105   The pretraining data originated from the PubChem data repository, a public database containing
106   information on chemical substances and their biological activities Kim et al. (2023). Initially,
107   113 million SMILES strings were collected from PubChem. These molecular strings underwent
      deduplication and canonicalization processes to ensure uniqueness Heid et al. (2021). Subsequently,

      a molecular transformation was conducted to verify the validity of the molecules derived from the
109   unique SMILES strings, resulting in a set of 91 million unique and valid molecules.
110
111   To construct the vocabulary, we employed the molecular tokenizer proposed by Schwaller et al.
112
      (2019). All 91 million molecules curated from PubChem were utilized in the tokenization process,
      resulting in a set of 4 billion molecular tokens. The unique tokens extracted from the resulting output
113
      provided a vocabulary of 2988 tokens plus 5 special tokens. In comparison, MoLFormer, trained
114
      on 1 billion samples with minimal curation, presented a vocabulary of 2362 tokens using the same
115   tokenization process Ross et al. (2022). This suggests an improvement in the vocabulary model due
116   to our curation process.

      We start with a sequence of tokens extracted from SMILES, each embedded in a 768-dimensional
141   space. The encoder-decoder layer is designed to process molecular token embeddings, represented
142   as x ∈ RD×L , where D denotes the maximum number of tokens and L represents the embedding
143   space dimension. We limited D at 202 tokens, as 99.4% of molecules in the PubChem dataset contain
144   fewer tokens than this threshold.
145
      In encoder-only models, a mean pooling layer is typically employed to represent tokens as SMILES
146
      in the latent space. However, this approach is limited by the lack of a natural inversion process
147   for the mean pooling operation. To overcome this limitation, we aim to construct a latent space
148   representation for SMILES by submersing the x in a latent space, denoted as z, as described in Eq. 2.
149

      2.3   P RE - TRAINING STRATEGIES
163
164   Pre-training of SMI-TED289M was performed for 40 epochs through the entire curated PubChem
165   dataset with a fixed learning rate of 1.6e-4 and a batch size of 288 molecules on a total of 24 NVIDIA
166   V100 (16G) GPUs parallelized into 4 nodes using DDP and torch run. It involves two distinct phases:
167   i) Learning of token embeddings through a masking process; ii) Subsequently, the token embeddings
168   are mapped into a common latent space that encapsulates the entire SMILES string. This latent space
169   not only facilitates the representation of the SMILES but also enables the reconstruction of both
170
      individual tokens and complete SMILES strings. Consequently, the pre-training process involves
      two separate loss functions: one for the token embeddings, which is based on the masking process,
171
      and another for the encoder-decoder layer, which focuses on the reconstruction of tokens. Two
172
      pre-training strategies are employed:
173
174          • In phase 1, the token encoder is initially pre-trained using 95% of the available samples,
175            while the remaining 5% is reserved for training the encoder-decoder layer. This partitioning
176            is necessary as the token embeddings may encounter convergence difficulties in the initial
177            epochs, which could adversely affect the training of the encoder-decoder layer.
178          • In phase 2, once the token embeddings layer has achieved convergence, the pre-training
179            process is expanded to utilize 100% of the available samples for both phases. This approach
180            leads to an enhancement in the performance of the encoder-decoder layer, particularly in
181            terms of token reconstruction.
182
183   For encoder pre-training we use the masked language model method defined in Devlin et al. (2019).
184   Initially 15% of the tokens are selected for possible learning. From that selection, 80% of the tokens
185
      are randomly selected and replaced with the [MASK] token, 10% of the tokens are randomly selected
      to be replaced with a random token, while the remaining 10% of the tokens will be unchanged.
186
187   The adoption of different pre-training strategies has proven instrumental in enhancing the efficiency
188   of our model, as evidenced by improvements observed in the loss functions. For detailed insights into
189   the loss functions and pre-training methodologies, refer to the Supplementary Materials.

      The Mixture-of-OSMI -Experts, MoE-OSMI comprises a set of n “expert networks” labeled as
      E1 , E2 , . . . , En , augmented through a gating network denoted as G, tasked with generating a sparse
202
      n-dimensional embedding space optimized for a downstream task as illustrated by Fig. 2.
203
204   Here, we map each SMILES into tokens and then convert the input tokens to the latent space. A mean
205   pooling method is applied to all token embeddings in order to produce a meaningful embedding of
206   the molecule. The architecture is equipped with a router module responsible for determining the n
207
      experts that will be activated, refining the adaptability and specialization of the system. Let G(x) and
      Ei (x̂) denote the output of the gating network and the output of the i-th expert network, respectively,

445   4.5   L ATENT SPACE STUDY
446
      We conducted an experiment to investigate the structure of the latent space created by Large Language
447
      Models in the context of Chemistry. Molecular structures are composable from fragments, motifs,
448
      and functional groups. The composability of structure often translates into compositionality of
449   structure-property relations, which is exemplified by powerful group contribution methods in chemical
450   sciences. Compositionality of the learnt representation, however, does not follow automatically from
451   the structure of the data and requires some combination of the learning architecture and learning
452   constraints to emerge. Our approach was to utilize simple chemical structures that can be easily
453   understood by humans, allowing us to anticipate relationships between elements, and examine the
454   latent space for similar patterns. We constructed a dataset consisting of six families of carbon
455   chains: F = {CC, CO, CN, CS, CF, CP }. For each family, we generated a sequence of molecules
456   by incrementally adding carbon atoms to the end of the SMILES string, up to a maximum of ten
457
      carbon atoms. For example, the family CO consists of {CO, CCO, · · · , CCCCCCCCCCO}.
      According to the domain expert’s intuition consistent with the theory of chemical structure, in a
458
      metric space, such sequences should exhibit a hierarchical distance structure, where the distance
459
      between consecutive elements is smaller than the distance between elements with a larger difference
460   in carbon count, i.e., |Cn Fi − Cn+1 Fi | < |Cn Fi − Cn+2 Fi |. Here, n represents the number of
461
      carbon atoms, and SMILE denotes the projection of the SMILE string onto the embedding space.
462
463   First, we generated the embeddings for two different encoders, the MoLFormer and SMI-TED289M ,
464   and used the t-SNEvan der Maaten & Hinton (2008) projection technique to generate pictures (Fig. 3)
465
      for visually inspecting the spaces. It is worth noting that the SMI-TED289M generated an embedding
      space that creates a nice separation of each family and respects the hierarchical distance structure,
466
      almost creating a linear relationship between each family. To quantify this relationship, we created a
467
      dataset of triples of SMILES, T = {(Cn FCC , Ck Fi , Cn+k Fi ) | 0 < n ≤ 4, 0 < k ≤ 5}, for the six
468   families Fi , resulting in six sub-datasets with 20 elements each, e.g., (CC, CCO, CCCCO) is one
469   element of the subset of type CO where n = 1, k = 2. Then, we randomly selected one triple from
470   each subset to feed a linear regression calculating α, β, and B0 such that α·Cn FCC +β ·Ck Fi +B0 =
471   Cn+k Fi . We validated the linearity using the remaining 114 elements. The linear regression on the
472   MoLFormer embeddings resulted in R2 = 0.55 and M SE = 0.237, while on our model embeddings,
473   it resulted in R2 = 0.99 and M SE = 0.002.
474   We evaluated our encoder-decoder model using a few-shot learning process, where we input a few
475   examples of triples, such as those mentioned earlier, to calculate α, β, and B0 . We then use these
476   parameters to generate embeddings for subsequent SMILES pairs and recreate the SMILES strings. To
477   validate our approach, we tested the process on the same dataset of triples. We calculated the molecule
478   similarity between the expected and generated results using the Tanimoto score (TS) Lipkus (1999).
479   We repeated this test with different combinations of input triples, yielding similar results. For example,
480   when using the input triples [CC +CCCS = CCCCCS, CCCCC +CCCS = CCCCCCCCS]
481   and querying all pairs in our subsets, we obtained a mean TS of 0.52. The top two similar results
482
      were CC + CCCCCS = CCCCCS with TS = 0.92 and CC + CCCCCO = CCCCCO with
      TS = 0.92, while the bottom two results were CCCCC + CF = F [P H3+]F with TS = 0.06 and
483
      CCCC + CF = F [P H3+]F with TS = 0.07.
484
485   Historically, group contribution was introduced in supervised learning context of structure-property
      relations. Our simple tests indicate that SMI-TED289M derived an equivalent of group contribution

      5    C ONCLUSION
507
508
      This paper introduces the SMI-TED289M family of chemical foundation models, which are pre-
509   trained on a curated dataset of 91 million SMILES samples from PubChem, amounting to 4 billion
510   molecular tokens. The SMI-TED289M family includes two configurations: the base model with 289
511   million parameters and the MoE-OSMI model, which consists of 8 × 289M parameters.
512
      The performance of these models was evaluated through an extensive experimentation on different
513
      tasks, including molecular properties classification and prediction. Our approach achieved state-
514
      of-the-art results in most tasks, particularly in predicting molecular quantum mechanics, where it
515   achieved the best or second-best results in 11 out of 12 tasks of the QM9 dataset.
516
517   One key observation is the model’s robustness across various data splits for reaction-yield prediction,
518
      particularly in low-resource settings where only a small fraction of the dataset is used for training.
      This underscores the importance of leveraging large-scale pre-training to encode generalized chemical
519
      knowledge, which can then be fine-tuned for specific tasks like reaction yield prediction. In contrast,
520
      models that are tailored specifically for a given task tend to overfit to the nuances of the training data
521   and struggle to generalize when the training set size is reduced, highlighting a critical limitation in
522   their design.
523
524
      We also investigated the structure of the latent space created by these language-based foundation
      models, using simple chemical structures for clarity. SMI-TED289M generated an embedding space
525
      that creates a nice separation of each family and respects the hierarchical distance structure, almost
526
      creating a linear relationship between each family. The encoder-decoder model’s capabilities in
527   few-shot learning were assessed by generating embeddings from a few example triples and using
528   them to recreate SMILES strings, achieving a Tanimoto score of 0.92 in the best case.
529
530

      different scientific applications. Weights for the SMI-TED289M family of models are fully accessible
