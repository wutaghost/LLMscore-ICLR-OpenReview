                                                   A BSTRACT
023
024
                Watermarking is essential for protecting the copyright of AI-generated images.
025
                We propose WMAdapter, a diffusion model watermark plugin that embeds user-
026             specified watermark information seamlessly during the diffusion generation process.
027             Unlike previous methods that modify diffusion modules to incorporate watermarks,
028             WMAdapter is designed to keep all diffusion components intact, resulting in sharp,
029             artifact-free images. To achieve this, we introduce two key innovations: (1) We
030             develop a contextual adapter that conditions on the content of the cover image
031             to generate adaptive watermark embeddings. (2) We implement an additional
032             finetuning step and a hybrid finetuning strategy that suppresses noticeable artifacts
033             while preserving the integrity of the diffusion components. Empirical results show
034
                that WMAdapter provides strong flexibility, superior image quality, and competitive
                watermark robustness.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      With the widespread adoption of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Podell et al., 2023; Song et al.,
040
      2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Ci et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a), diffusion-generated images are
041
      proliferating across media and the internet. While these models meet the demand for high-quality
042   creative content, their misuse raises significant concerns about copyright protection and the security of
043   images against deepfakes (Westerlund, 2019). Watermarking technology (Cox et al., 2007) provides
044   a tailored solution for resolving copyright disputes and identifying the sources of forgeries.
045
      Previous watermarking methods added watermarks to images in a post-hoc way through frequency
046
      domain transformations (Cox et al., 2007; Lin et al., 2001; Xia et al., 1998) or encoder-decoder
047
      networks (Zhu et al., 2018; Tancik et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2019). However, in the context of
048   watermarking diffusion images, post-hoc methods introduce additional workflows and unable to fully
049   leverage the rich latent space provided by the image generation process. Recently, more efforts (Zhao
050   et al., 2023b; Fernandez et al., 2023; Min et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2024; Meng et al.,
051   2024; Yang et al., 2024; Ci et al., 2024) have focused on leveraging the characteristics of the diffusion
052   process to seamlessly integrate watermarking into the diffusion pipeline, known as diffusion-native
053   watermarking. Among these, Stable Signature (Fernandez et al., 2023) proposed a method that
      fine-tunes the VAE decoder of a latent diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022) using a pretrained

054
      watermark decoder (Zhu et al., 2018). This approach has shown promising results. However, it
055   requires fine-tuning a separate VAE decoder for each unique watermark, making it difficult to scale to
056   millions of keys as required in large-scale commercial scenarios where each user may need a unique
057   key. Additionally, the tuning of VAE decoder on a small amount of data results in blurry and lens
058   flare-like artifacts (see Fig. 7).
059
      Recent works (Bui et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2023; Min et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
060
      2024; Kim et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023) have explored watermark plugins for diffusion models.
061   These plugins accept arbitrary watermark keys and generate watermark embeddings without requiring
062   per-watermark finetuning, thereby addressing the scalability issue. However, these methods typically
063   generate watermark embeddings without considering the image content (Kim et al., 2023; Xiong
064   et al., 2023; Bui et al., 2023) (i.e., they are context-less) and often require finetuning or modifying
065   diffusion modules to incorporate the watermark embeddings (Kim et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2023;
066   Feng et al., 2024) . Tab. 1 compares several watermarking methods. Unfortunately, finetuning the
067   original diffusion pipeline or making intrusive modifications often leads to a significant drop in image
068   quality, resulting in blurriness or noticeable artifacts. Fig. 1 illustrates the image quality of different
069
      methods, where artifacts introduced by other methods are evident. Find more examples in Fig. 13.

082   We propose an innovative watermark plugin solution — WMAdapter (Fig. 2). Its core design
083   philosophy focuses on preserving the integrity of the original diffusion pipeline to produce high-
084   quality images. We do not modify any parameters of the pretrained diffusion modules. So how do we
085   conceal the watermark information and ensure its robustness? We introduce two key innovations:
086   (1) We propose a novel Contextual Adapter structure that conditions on the cover image features
087   to generate content-aware watermark embeddings (hence "contextual"). Intuitively, this allows the
088
      adapter to better identify areas of the image that are more suitable for hiding the watermark, enhancing
      concealment and robustness. To fully leverage diffusion features while reducing computational
089
      overhead, our Contextual Adapter extracts image features from the intermediate layers of the diffusion
090
      VAE decoder. Unlike ControlNet plugins (Zhang et al., 2023b; Min et al., 2024), which use a heavy
091   UNet structure (Ronneberger et al., 2015), the Contextual Adapter is lightweight, totaling only 1.3MB
092   in parameters, and enables watermarking an image in just 30ms. (2) We introduce an additional
093   finetuning stage with a novel Hybrid Finetuning strategy to further enhance image quality. To
094   preserve the original diffusion modules, our Hybrid Finetuning strategy involves jointly finetuning the
095   adapter and the diffusion VAE decoder during training for alignment, and then using the original VAE
096   decoder during inference. This approach effectively suppresses noticeable artifacts and significantly
097   improves image sharpness. We summarize our contributions as follows:
098
099         1. We introduce WMAdapter, a novel diffusion watermarking solution with an innovative
100            design philosophy. It embeds watermarks non-intrusively during the diffusion process,
101            thereby preserving the integrity of the diffusion pipeline and producing high-quality images.
102
103         2. Methodologically, we propose Contextual Adapter and Hybrid Finetuning to achieve
104            non-intrusive watermarking, ensuring both watermark robustness and generation quality.
105
106         3. Experimental results demonstrate that WMAdapter effectively suppresses noticeable artifacts
107            and offers better accuracy-quality tradeoffs compared to prior post-hoc and diffusion-native
               watermarking methods.

127   2     R ELATED WORK
128
129
      2.1   P OST- HOC WATERMARKING
130
131   Post-hoc methods include traditional frequency domain transformation methods (Cox et al., 2007),
132   optimization-based methods (Fernandez et al., 2022b; Kishore et al., 2021), and encoder-decoder
133   methods (Zhu et al., 2018; Tancik et al., 2020; Jia et al., 2021). Different methods have different
134   aims. For instance, Kishore et al. (2021) emphasizes hiding more bits, Zhu et al. (2018) and Jia
135   et al. (2021) prioritizes robustness against JPEG compression.
136
137   2.2   D IFFUSION - NATIVE WATERMARKING
138
139   According to the location of the watermark, we classify diffusion-native watermarking methods
140
      into two categories. Adding to initial noise: Tree-Ring (Wen et al., 2023) adds watermarks to
      the frequency of initial noise, achieving remarkable robustness. Subsequent methods (Yang et al.,
141
      2024; Ci et al., 2024; Lei et al., 2024) improves its multi-key identification capabilities. However,
142
      these methods significantly alter the layout of the generated images, which is not desirable in some
143   production scenarios. Adding to latent space: Other methods leverage the latent space of the
144   VAE (Bui et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023;
145   Fernandez et al., 2023) or diffusion backbone (Feng et al., 2024). However, they either generate
146   content-agnostic watermark embeddings or modify the original diffusion modules, often resulting
147   in lower image quality. In contrast, WMAdapter prioritizes image quality through novel contextual
148   designs while preserving the integrity of the entire diffusion pipeline. Stable Messenger (Nguyen
149   et al., 2023) is a recent method that also generates content-aware watermarks. However, they mainly
150   focus on improving message accuracy and their model design is different from ours.

186   3.2    C ONTEXTUAL ADAPTERS
187
188   In this section, we provide a detailed overview of the contextual structure of WMAdapter. Fig. 3
189   (Left) illustrates the internal structure of WMAdapter, which comprises a series of independent Fuser
190   modules. Each Fuser ϕi (·) is attached before a corresponding VAE decoder block i. It receives
191
      both VAE feature fi and watermark bits w as inputs, and outputs a feature residual yi to update fi .
      Formally,
192
                                                    yi = ϕi (fi , w) ,
193                                                                                                     (1)
194                                                 fi′ = fi + yi .
195   We put a total of 6 Fusers before the Conv Block, Middle Block and four Up Blocks in the kl-f8 VAE
196   decoder used by Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022).
197   Fig. 3 (Right) illustrates the internal structure of an Fuser. An Fuser consists of two main components:
198   the Embedding module and the Fusing module. The Embedding module maps the 01 bit sequence
199   into a 48-dimensional watermark feature vector. This feature vector is then expanded along the width
200   and height dimensions to produce a watermark feature map with the same dimensions as the image
201   feature. The image feature and watermark feature are concatenated along the channel dimension and
202   fed into the Fusing module, which outputs the image feature residuals. Keeping lightweight in mind,
203   we use two MLPs with 256 intermediate feature channels for the Embedding module, and two 1x1
204   convolutions with half the image feature channels 2c as intermediates for the Fusing module. We
205
      employ LeakyReLU as the non-linearity. The total parameters of WMAdapter are only 1.3M, making
      it a small and efficient plugin.

      3.3    T RAINING
209
      In the training stage, we use a pretrained watermark decoder to decode watermark bits from the
210   watermarked images. We freeze the watermark decoder and the VAE decoder, and only train the
211   Adapter. Why do we use a pretrained decoder instead of training a watermark decoder from scratch
212   along with the Adapter? We observe that training an encoder/decoder pair from scratch, as post-hoc
213   methods do, typically requires significant training effort. For example, HiDDeN takes 300 epochs
214   to converge on the COCO dataset. The situation gets worse when trained with a diffusion pipeline.
215   WOUAF (Kim et al., 2023) takes about 10 days. Using a pretrained post-hoc decoder facilitates
      efficient knowledge transfer, allowing WMAdapter to converge in just 1-2 epochs. Note that this will

242   3.4      H YBRID FINETUNING
243
244   After the training stage, we obtain a watermark adapter that performs well in both accuracy and image
245   quality (Sec. 4.4.2). However, when we zoom in on the generated images, grid-like artifacts can
246   sometimes be observed (Fig. 6). To further improve image quality and eliminate these tiny artifacts,
247   we introduce a fine-tuning stage on a small amount of data. On top of the first stage training losses,
248   we incorporate an additional total variation loss (et al, 2024) on the watermarked images to enhance
249   smoothness, setting its weight to 0.02.
250   Further, we present a novel Hybrid Finetuning strategy. Concretely, we finetune both the Adapter and
251   the VAE decoder, but use the fine-tuned Adapter and the original VAE decoder for inference. Fig. 4
252   distinguishes this strategy from two other classic finetuning strategies: Fixed and Joint Finetuning.
253   The Fixed Finetuning strategy uses the same training approach as in the first stage, fixing the VAE
254   decoder and quickly finetuning the Adapter with a high learning rate. The Joint Finetuning strategy
255   jointly finetunes the Adapter and the VAE decoder, using both finetuned copies for inference.
256   Sec. 4.4.2 will give a side-by-side comparison between these three finetuning strategies. In short,
257   Hybrid Finetuning can effectively suppress noticeable artifacts and, by keeping the VAE intact,
258   produces the sharpest and clearest images while maintaining the plug-and-play advantage, making it
259   ideal for commercial image generation products which require high image quality.

      3.5      D ISCUSSION
262
263
      WMAdapter is designed with a strong emphasis on image quality, particularly in suppressing notice-
264   able artifacts in generated images. We introduce the Contextual Adapter and the Hybrid Finetuning,
265   non-intrusive watermarking methods that achieve this goal by preserving the integrity of the diffusion
266   pipeline. This fundamentally distinguishes our approach from other diffusion watermarking methods
267   that embed watermarks at the expense of image quality and introduce noticeable artifacts. We want
268   to highlight the importance of high-quality, artifact-free watermarked images for generative products,
269   as no user wants to receive images with visible flaws. The Experiment Section demonstrates that our
      method successfully combines scalability, high-quality image generation, and watermark robustness.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
273
274   Model and dataset We experiment with a popular latent diffusion model Stable Diffusion 2.1 (Rom-
275   bach et al., 2022) and its associated kl-f8 VAE. We adopt the pretrained watermark decoder from
276   HiDDeN (Zhu et al., 2018). The checkpoint we use was pretrained by (Fernandez et al., 2023),
277   encoding 48-bits watermark information. This checkpoint is also used to finetune Stable Signa-
278   ture (Fernandez et al., 2023). Thus, our adapter can be directly compared with (Fernandez et al.,
279   2023). ALL training and finetuning steps are performed on MS-COCO 2017 (Lin et al., 2014) training
280   set. Validation is performed on COCO 2017 validation set. We train and evaluate our adapters on
281   images at resolution 512 × 512. For images smaller than this size, we resize their shorter edge to 512,
282
      then center crop to get a 512 × 512 image.
283
284   Training strategies For the first stage training, we adopt 8 × NVIDIA A5000 GPUs of 24 GB
285   memory, with per-GPU batchsize of 2, AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017), a learning
286   rate of 5e-4. We train the model for 2 epochs, taking about 5 hours. For the second stage finetuning,
287
      we use a single A5000 GPU. We set the mini-batch to 2. We also use the AdamW optimizer and a
      start learning rate of 5e-4. However, we adopt a per-step cosine learning rate decay with 20 warm-up
288
      steps. Unless otherwise specified, the total fine-tuning process defaults to 2,000 steps, lasting for
289
      about 50 minutes. Different finetuning strategies result in several different adapter variants. We use
290   Adapter-B, Adapter-F, Adapter-V, and Adapter-I to denote the adapters obtained by No Finetuning,
291   Fixed Finetuning, Joint Finetuning and Hybrid Finetuning, respectively.
292
293
      Evaluation metric Following previous conventions (Zhu et al., 2018; Fernandez et al., 2022b;
294
      2023), we use average bit accuracy to evaluate the watermarking performance of our adapter. Bit
295   accuracy is defined by the ratio of correctly decoded bits in a 48-bit watermark sequence. Apart from
296   the bit accuracy, we also report the tracing accuracy among different numbers of users following
297   concurrent works (Min et al., 2024; Ci et al., 2024). We adopt the evaluation protocol of (Min et al.,
298   2024). Concretely, we construct user pools of different sizes, ranging from 104 to 106 , to evaluate the
299   accuracy of user tracing at different scales. Each user is assigned a unique key. For each user pool.
300   we randomly select 1,000 users and watermark 5 images per user, resulting in 5,000 watermarked
301   images. For each of the 5,000 images, we find the best match among the user pool and check if it’s a
302   correct match. Tracing accuracy is then averaged over all 5,000 images. To evaluate the detection
303
      performance, we report TPR@FPR10−6 . Concretely, we assume the bits decoded from the natural
      images following Bernoulli distribution with parameter 0.5. Then the number of matched bits M
304
      follows a binomial distribution with parameters (48, 0.5). So we have the false detection rate as a
305
      function of threshold τ : F P R (τ ) = P (M > τ ) = I0.5 (τ + 1, 48 − τ ), where I is the incomplete
306   beta function. We control F P R = 10−6 and calculate the corresponding τ , then we evaluate TPR
307   with this threshold.
308
309
      In addition to accuracy measurements, we are also interested in the watermark’s invisibility and
      image generation quality. We report the Peak Signal-Noise-Ratio (PSNR) between images before and
310
      after watermarking and Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017) between watermarked
311
      images and images from coco val set. Typically, higher PSNR leading to sharper and clearer images.
312   While lower FID means the watermarked images have higher fidelity and more closely resemble the
313   real images in terms of appearance and variety.

      Regeneration attack Recent work (Zhao et al., 2023a) has demonstrated the potential of regenera-
394
      tion attacks in watermark removal. We evaluate the robustness of WMAdapter against three different
395   regeneration methods introduced in Zhao et al. (2023a): one diffusion-based (Zhao et al., 2023a)
396   and two VAE-based methods (Ballé et al., 2018; Cheng et al., 2020). For Ballé et al. (2018); Cheng
397   et al. (2020), we assess performance at compression rates of 1-6 and 1-8, respectively. Fig. 5 presents
398   the Accuracy-PSNR curve. We observe that the three regeneration attacks require a PSNR drop of
399   4-6 dB to successfully remove our watermark. In contrast, only a 2 dB reduction in image quality is
400   needed to remove the watermark of Stable Signature. This demonstrates that our method exhibits
401   better robustness against regeneration attacks.
402
403   Adversarial attack Adversarial attack relies on PGD (Madry, 2017) optimization to generate
404
      adversarial noise targeting the watermark decoder. Based on access to the watermark decoder, these
      attacks are categorized as white-box and black-box. In black-box settings, a binary classifier is trained
405
      to identify watermarked images, and adversarial noise is then optimized to mislead this classifier,
406
      disrupting the watermark. This is commonly referred to as a surrogate detector attack (Saberi et al.,
407   2023; Jiang et al., 2023; An et al., 2024). We follow the implementation of An et al. (2024) and
408   demonstrate our method’s robustness against both white-box (An’24-wb △) and black-box attacks
409   (An’24 ▽) in Fig. 5. Notably, both WMAdapter and Stable Signature exhibit strong robustness against
410   black-box adversarial attacks, with a bit accuracy drop of about 0.02 and TPR drop less than 0.01. In
411   white-box scenarios, where attackers have full access to the watermark decoders, the watermarks can
412   be easily disrupted with minimal impact on image quality.
413
414   Query-based attack Another common black-box attack is the query-based attack, which defines
415   a blending process that transitions from a random image to a given watermarked image. During
416   this process, it repeatedly queries the watermark decoder API to determine whether the current
417
      blended image contains a watermark, aiming to identify the image with the minimal perturbation
      that successfully removes the watermark. We adopt the WEvade-B-Q approach from Jiang et al.
418
      (2023) and set the detection threshold τ to control F P R = 10−6 . Our observations show that the
419
      query-based attack can successfully evade watermark detection for both WMAdapter and Stable
420   Signature, achieving a success rate of 1.0 (i.e., T P R = 0). However, this method results in significant
421   image quality degradation, with the final attacked images averaging a PSNR of approximately 8 dB.1

533
534   In this paper, we introduce WMAdapter, a plug-and-play watermarking plugin that enables latent dif-
535   fusion models to embed arbitrary bit information during image generation. Our adapter is lightweight,
536   easy to train, and offers a superior accuracy-quality trade-off with significantly fewer noticeable
537   artifacts compared to previous post-hoc and diffusion-native watermarking methods. One limitation is
538   that the Adapter-F variant occasionally produces grid-like artifacts that become visible upon zooming
539   in. In summary, WMAdapter provides a simple yet powerful baseline for further exploration on
      diffusion watermarking.

856   A.10    F URTHER INVESTIGATION ON LENS - FLARE ARTIFACTS
857
858   Lens flare artifacts are commonly observed in watermarking methods utilizing finetuned VAE
859   decoders, such as Stable Signature (Fernandez et al., 2023) and FSW (Xiong et al., 2023). This
860
      suggests that parameter changes in the VAE decoder contribute to the occurrence of these artifacts.
861   In this section, we further investigate the influence of different parameters in the VAE decoder on lens
862   flare artifacts. Starting from the output layer,“conv out”, we progressively replaced the finetuned VAE
863   decoder parameters with the pretrained VAE decoder parameters, proceeding layer by layer toward
      the input layer. The VAE decoder comprises the following layers (from output to input): “conv out”,


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      “conv norm out”, “up blocks” x 4, ‘mid block”, and “conv in”. The corresponding generated images
865   are presented in Fig. 10.
866
867   We can observe that lens flare artifacts almost disappear in the “Up blocks.2”, indicating that changes
868
      in the parameters of the “conv out”, “conv norm out”, "up block.3”, and "up block.2” 4 layers were
      responsible for their occurrence. To investigate further, we replace only the bias or weight parameters
869
      of these four layers. The results show that replacing only the weight parameters effectively suppresses
870
      the lens flare artifacts, suggesting that their occurrence is solely attributed to changes in the weight
871   parameters of these layers. Further detailed investigation of the mechanism behind lens flare artifacts
872   generation is beyond the scope of this paper and is left for future work.
