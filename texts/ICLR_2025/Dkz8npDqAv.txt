009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The laborious nature of manual point cloud labeling drives the growing interest in
012            3D auto-annotation. The challenge is amplified by the sparse and irregular distribu-
013            tion of point clouds. This leads to the under-performance of current autolabelers,
014            particularly with hard-to-detect samples (hard samples) characterized by trunca-
015            tion, occlusion, or distance. In response, we propose a multimodal context-aware
016            transformer (MMCAT) as a 3D annotator using a small number of 3D annotations.
017            MMCAT integrates 3D point cloud geometry with image-based semantic insights
018
               to improve 3D hard samples’ annotations through 2D visual guidance. Our ap-
               proach utilizes visual hints from three perspectives to integrate the 2D and 3D
019
               dimensions. Initially, we develop point and image encoders to align LiDAR and
020
               image data, establishing a unified semantic bridge between image visuals and point
021            cloud geometry. Subsequently, our box encoder processes 2D box coordinates to
022            improve accuracy in determining object positions and dimensions within 3D space.
023            Finally, our multimodal encoders enhance feature interactions, improving point
024            cloud interpretation and annotation accuracy, especially for challenging samples.
025            MMCAT lies in its strategic use of 2D visual prompts to bolster 3D representation
026            and annotation processes. We validate MMCAT’s efficacy through extensive exper-
027            iments on the widely recognized KITTI and Waymo Open datasets, particularly
028            highlighting its superior performance with hard samples.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
116   2.1   3D R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
117
118   Traditional 3D point cloud techniques, like voxel-based networks Maturana & Scherer (2015); Wu
119   et al. (2015); Xie et al. (2018); Yang et al. (2018) and point-based methods Qi et al. (2017a;b);
120   Lang et al. (2019); Shi et al. (2019); Qi et al. (2018), have shown significant utility but often do not
121
      fully exploit the inherent geometric details of point clouds. Recently, attention-based models Zhao
      et al. (2021); Yu et al. (2021); Guo et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022a;b); Qian et al. (2023), especially
122
      those utilizing Transformer architectures, have emerged as powerful tools in 3D vision, overcoming
123
      previous limitations by efficiently processing point cloud data. These models, including the pioneering
124   Point Transformer Zhao et al. (2021) and its variants Wu et al. (2022); Park et al. (2022), are tailored to
125   meet point cloud data’s unique requirements, achieving notable success in object detection Misra et al.
126   (2021); Pan et al. (2021); Park et al. (2022), segmentation Zhao et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2022); Lai
127   et al. (2022); Park et al. (2022), classification Liu et al. (2020); Qi et al. (2017a;b), and annotation Liu
128   et al. (2022a;b); Qian et al. (2023); Paat et al. (2024). Their ability to capture long-range dependencies
129   and facilitate improved information exchange has led to breakthroughs in these areas.
130   Nevertheless, the challenge of harnessing 3D point cloud data’s full potential persists, especially with
131   sparse and irregular hard-to-detect samples. Although point clouds provide detailed geometry, they
132   often miss semantic context essential for comprehensive scene understanding. Images are crucial
133   for complementing 3D representations with their dense semantic information. Yet, many current
134   multimodal fusion methods Liu et al. (2022b;a); Paat et al. (2024) experience substantial loss of
135   geometric and semantic information during the integration into 3D point clouds.
136   Inspired by the CLIP architectures’ success Radford et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021; 2022); Kim
137   et al. (2021), which adeptly connect text and image modalities via text-image pairs, we propose its
138   extension to image-point pairs for 3D point cloud representation learning. Our method utilizes visual
139   guidance (images) to enrich 3D representation learning, effectively overcoming the shortcomings of
140   existing multimodal fusion autolabelers. This method employs 2D visual guidance to extract and
141   improve 3D representations, significantly enhancing the accuracy and reliability of 3D data.
142
143   2.2   3D A NNOTATION FROM W EAK L ABELS
144
      Human annotation of point cloud data is an arduous task that has spurred the development of
145
      automated techniques using weak labels Meng et al. (2021a); Wei et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022a;b).
146   The affordability and ease of obtaining 2D annotations compared to 3D labels have spotlighted their
147   potential in automating the annotation process. Current methods leveraging 2D annotations Zakharov
148   et al. (2020); Wei et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022a); Qian et al. (2023) often face challenges in translating
149   to 3D, hindered by the sparsity and irregularity of point cloud data, particularly with hard samples.
150   While multimodal fusion methods Liu et al. (2022b;a); Paat et al. (2024) for 3D automatic annotation
151   exist, they frequently encounter geometric and semantic losses during the fusion into point clouds.
152   Expanding on foundational research, our study adapts the text-image multimodal framework into an
153   image-point system for 3D point cloud annotation. We leverage 2D visual hints to enhance point cloud
154   representation, addressing the limitations of existing fusion methods and boosting 3D annotation
155   accuracy. MMCAT efficiently integrates multimodal data, capitalizing on images’ semantic richness
156   to improve sparse geometric point data.

      3     MMCAT FOR 3D P OINT C LOUD AUTOMATIC A NNOTATION

160   point clouds, corresponding images, and 2D boxes (Sec.3.1). We then detail MMCAT architecture,
161   including point cloud, image, and 2D box encoders, along with multimodal fusion encoders (Sec.3.2).
      Next, we explore 2D visual guidance (Sec.3.3) and conclude with our training objectives (Sec.3.4).

      3.1   DATA P REPARATION
163
164   Our methodology generates 3D pseudo labels for training standard 3D object detectors, using LiDAR
165
      point clouds P, corresponding images I, and weak 2D bounding boxes C. Initially, we identify the
      frustum areas defined by 2D bounding boxes, following prior research Qi et al. (2018); Wei et al.
166
      (2021); Liu et al. (2022a;b). With a calibration matrix linking LiDAR and image data, we project
167
      3D point cloud coordinates (x, y, z) onto a 2D image plane, obtaining coordinates (u, v) through
168   the calibration mapping function fcal . This step is crucial for aligning multimodal data sources for
169   effective 3D annotation.
170
171
      We delineate the 2D projected subset of the point cloud, denoted as P2D , which resides within the
      confines of a given 2D box B. Mathematically, P2D → RN →2 is defined as:
172
173                   P2D = {(u, v) | (u, v) = fcal (x, y, z), ↑(x, y, z) → P, (u, v) → B}.                (1)
174
      wherein (x, y, z) constitutes the coordinates of a point in the 3D space, and B signifies the delineated
175
      region within the 2D bounding box.
176
177   Subsequently, the corresponding frustum sub-cloud, PF , encapsulating the 3D points that project
178   within the 2D bounding region, is extracted and formalized as a subset of RN →3 , expressed as:
179                                  PF = {(x, y, z) | fcal (x, y, z) → P2D },                             (2)
180
181
      The variable N represents the number of points in each input frustum sub-cloud PF within each
      batch. We combine PF with corresponding images I and 2D bounding box coordinates C. Following
182
      ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2020), images I are processed into k ↓ k patches. For fusing 2D visual
183
      cues with 3D data, 2D box coordinates C are set within the scene’s coordinate system as (l, t, r, b)
184   to outline the box’s edges. We use 2D ground truth as it is significantly easier to obtain than 3D
185   annotations. The tri-modal inputs integrate geometric details from point clouds with 2D imagery to
186   enrich 3D understanding.
187
188   3.2   MMCAT A RCHITECTURE
189
      The MMCAT architecture processes tri-modal inputs: frustum sub-clouds PF in (B, N, 3), image
190
      data I in (B, 3, W, H), and 2D boxes C in (B, 2, 2), to output 3D boxes (B, 7). To address the
191
      variable density of point clouds from LiDAR scans, we use random sampling to standardize point
192   counts within a batch, aligning them to the batch’s median, thus handling point density variations.
193
194   MMCAT integrates point cloud, image, and 2D box encoders, followed by two multimodal encoders.
195
      The point encoder transforms PF into d-dimensional embeddings using projection layers, resulting in
      (B, N, d) shapes. Image data (k ↓ k-sized patches) are projected into embeddings of shape (B, p, d)
196
      by an MLP, where p represents the patch count. The 2D box encoder similarly converts boxes
197
      into embeddings with (B, 2, d) dimensions. This standardizes tri-modal inputs for fusion, allowing
198   accurate feature extraction and alignment. MMCAT’s encoders capture features from each modality
199   and effectively integrate their unique contributions. Two multimodal encoders then refine interactions
200   between (image, point) and (box, point) pairs, focusing on 3D label generation. For detailed insights
201   into visual guidance, refer to Sec.3.3. Figure 1 illustrates the MMCAT architecture for 3D annotation.
202
      Point Encoder. Our point encoder features a custom block for extracting intra- and inter-object
203
      features from 3D point clouds. This block sequence starts with a pre-norm layer, proceeds with self-
204   attention (SA) and a multilayer perceptron (MLP), incorporates a batch self-attention (Batch-SA), and
205   ends with an additional MLP. Unlike traditional SA, which works on the sequence dimension, Batch-
206   SA extends to the batch dimension, capturing essential inter-object relations for precise 3D scene
207   representation, especially informative for complex samples. As depicted in Figure 1, the encoder
208   utilizes L1 blocks to enhance point embeddings from input projections. To enhance alignment across
209   different modalities in 3D, we introduce seven learnable 3D box tokens, denoted as box3d_tokens,
210   supplemented by point embeddings. These tokens encode the essential attributes of a 3D bounding
211   box: location (x, y, z), dimensions (l, w, h), and yaw angle. These attributes are crucial to accurately
212
      define the position, size, and orientation of the 3D box in a 3D context, hence leading to a total of
      seven tokens. This enables each block to generate 3D features as (B, N + 7, d), with box3d_tokens
213
      aligning with image and 2D box tokens via contrastive learning for accurate alignment.
214
215   Image Encoder. Our image encoder connects 3D point clouds with 2D visuals, converting image
      data into a format optimized for multimodal fusion. Image patches (B, p, k, k) are transformed into


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      visual embeddings (B, p, d) through an MLP projection. We match N 3D points to 2D image patches
217   based on their 2D coordinates, creating a (B, N, d) matrix of 2D features for corresponding 3D points.
218   The encoder, tailored for image data, mirrors the point encoder’s design through L2 stacked blocks,
219   consisting of a pre-norm layer, SA, an MLP, Batch-SA, and a concluding MLP (see Figure 1). This
220   uniform architecture across modalities eliminates the need for different designs, ensuring consistency.
221   The image encoder leverages SA and Batch-SA to extract 2D intra- and inter-object semantic visual
222   features in (B, N, d). Concurrently, we engage in contrastive learning with box3d_tokens from
223   the point encoder in (B, 7, d), employing seven dimensioned tokens, img_tokens in (B, 7, d).
224   Contrastive loss optimization aligns these tokens with point cloud features, resulting in 2D image
225
      representation in (B, N + 7, d). This strategic alignment is fundamental to our methodology, enabling
      accurate 3D analysis by integrating visual cues with 3D.
226
227   2D Box Encoder. Our approach improves 3D spatial perception with a 2D box encoder that
228   derives spatial insights from 2D coordinates, offering additional visual cues for point clouds. This
229   method links 2D and 3D data, facilitating the creation of 3D boxes from 2D outlines. Equipped with
230   SA and MLP blocks within a transformer architecture, the 2D box encoder processes features into
231
      (B, 2 + 7, d) format through L3 stacked blocks, aligning with box3d_tokens from point clouds as
      shown in Figure 1. Similarly, we use seven tokens to represent box2d_tokens, facilitating contrastive
232
      learning with box3d_tokens. Incorporating 2D coordinates into our framework provides enhanced
233
      visual guidance by clearly defining spatial boundaries and identifying object locations, greatly
234   improving the precision of 3D object interpretation. This is particularly beneficial for hard samples
235   with occlusions and truncations.
236
237
      Multimodal Encoder. Our multimodal encoding framework merges point cloud, image, and 2D
      box features through transformer blocks, utilizing pre-norm, Batch-SA, cross-attention (CA), and
238
      MLP layers to unify modalities and enhance 3D object representation. For image-point fusion, a
239
      multimodal encoder refines point features (B, N, d) using Batch-SA and CA, integrating dense image
240   semantics (B, N, d) into point cloud data. This boosts 3D representation learning, crucial for precise
241   3D box regression in complex scenarios. Similarly, for 2D box-point integration, spatial constraints
242   from 2D boxes (B, N, d) are processed with point features in another multimodal encoder, enhancing
243   (B, N, d) 3D point features. We upscale the 2D box encoder output from (B, 2, d) to (B, N, d) via
244   an MLP to facilitate enhanced fusion. Concatenated fused features (B, N, 2d) from both encoders
245   prepare the data for precise 3D box regression. This method leverages 2D guidance to deepen 3D
246   scene understanding, merging visual cues with spatial insights for comprehensive 3D representation.

      3.3   V ISUAL G UIDANCE FROM 2D
248
249
      Even with 3D human annotations, robust 3D point cloud representation remains challenging due to
250
      their often incomplete, sparse, and noisy nature Huang et al. (2023b); Wei et al. (2021), stemming
251
      from LiDAR’s inherent limitations. To address this, we propose enriching 3D representation learning
252   with 2D dense visual hints. In MMCAT, images and 2D box features serve as contexts (K, V ) in dual
253   multimodal encoders, implementing two visual guidance strategies: (image, point) and (box, point).
254
255
      Visual Guidance from (image, point) Modalities. In the image-point process, images provide dense
      visual contexts, offering semantic details like color and texture absent in point cloud data. The CA
256
      in our multimodal encoder enhances the interaction between 2D images and point cloud features,
257
      enriching point cloud descriptions with semantic details and resolving ambiguities. For instance,
258   image guidance can help differentiate objects with similar geometric shapes but distinct appearances
259   in the image domain. This guidance forms more effective 3D point cloud representations, particularly
260   for hard samples, where image semantics can significantly enhance sparse point cloud representation.
261   Integrating 2D and 3D information improves the representation of hard samples, highlighting the
262   importance of multimodal integration for accurate and reliable 3D annotations.
263
      Visual Guidance from (box, point) Modalities. In the (box, point) modality, 2D bounding boxes
264   offer localized visual cues, highlighting areas of interest in the point cloud. As spatial constraints
265   in our multimodal encoder, these features link 2D visuals with 3D spatial data. For example, a
266   2D box around a vehicle in an image helps the model focus on refining the vehicle’s point cloud
267   representation, crucial for obscured hard samples. The 2D box outlines the entire vehicle, including
268   occluded sections, offering complete size details. This allows for a more accurate 3D box, even
269   without point cloud data for hidden parts. This approach enriches point cloud geometry with precise
      spatial details from 2D boxes, resulting in more accurate localization and sizing of 3D objects.

      3.4   L OSS F UNCTION
296
297   Our MMCAT model’s training is enhanced by a composite loss function that includes distance-IoU
298   (dIoU) loss (Lbox ), directional loss (Ldir ), and contrastive learning losses (Lip for image-point and
299   Lbp for box-point). Here dIoU Loss (Lbox ) measures the discrepancy between the predicted box and
300   the ground truth. This metric ensures that predicted 3D boxes precisely represent the objects within the
301   point cloud. Directional Loss (Ldir ) tackles IoU’s direction-invariance by categorizing orientations
302   into the front ([↔ω/2, ω/2)) and back ([ω/2, ω] ↗ [↔ω, ↔ω/2)), using an MLP for accurate 3D box
303   orientation detection. The directional loss is calculated using cross-entropy. For contrastive learning
304   losses, we define Lip for image-point alignment and Lbp for box-point alignment to refine unimodal
305
      representations before their fusion. These losses are designed to optimize a cosine similarity function,
      enhancing the similarity scores for congruent pairs of image-point and box-point tokens. The Lip
306
      and Lbp follow the contrastive loss functions used in Radford et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021). In our
307
      approach, the contrastive loss is computed over the entire batch. For a batch size of B, we compute
308   the cosine similarity between all (point, image) and (point, box2d) token pairs, resulting in an B ↓ B
309   similarity matrix. The img_token, box2d_token, and box3d_token in (B, 7, d) represent linear
310   transformations mapping trainable tokens to normalized, lower-dimensional representations for the
311   image, 2D box, and point cloud, respectively. Each token contributes to each sample, as deep features.
312   They are learned through the contrastive learning process. The total loss function is formulated as
313   follows:
314                                      L = εbox Lbox + Lip + Lbp + Ldir ,                                (3)
315   with εbox set to 5, it is balancing the contribution of each component based on empirical findings Qian
316   et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2022a).

345   KITTI Dataset. Our evaluation on the KITTI dataset Geiger et al. (2012) involved 3,712 training
346
      frames with 15,654 vehicle instances and 3,769 validation frames. Following standard protocols, we
      trained on 500 frames and validated on 3,769 frames for consistency with benchmarks. We assessed
347
      MMCAT’s performance using PointRCNN across Easy, Moderate, and Hard difficulty levels based
348
      on the official KITTI evaluation metrics. Performance metrics included mean Average Precision
349   (mAP) at a 0.7 IoU threshold for 3D and Bird’s Eye View (BEV) detection. Aligned with recent 3D
350   annotation research Wei et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022a); Qian et al. (2023); Paat et al. (2024), our
351   analysis focused on the Vehicle category and excluded objects with fewer than 15 foreground points
352   to ensure feasible 3D bounding box annotations.
353
      Waymo Open Datset. The Waymo Open Dataset Sun et al. (2020), with 798 training and 202
354
      validation sequences for vehicles, is recorded by a 64-channel LiDAR, capturing around 180,000
355   points every 0.1 seconds. Our evaluation employs two primary 3D object detection metrics: mAP
356   for bounding box accuracy and mAP weighted by Heading Accuracy (mAPH), factoring in object
357   orientation, with IoU thresholds set at 0.7 for vehicles. Detection difficulty is classified into LEVEL_1
358   (boxes with > 5 LiDAR points) and LEVEL_2 (boxes with ↘ 1 LiDAR point). We conducted
359   experiments on version 1.2.0 of the Waymo and focused exclusively on the Vehicle class.
360
      Implementation Details. MMCAT was developed in PyTorch Paszke et al. (2019), using customized
361   Transformer encoder layers for point cloud, image, 2D box, and two multimodal encoders, as
362   illustrated in Figure 1. The design of MMCAT’s image and multimodal encoders was inspired by
363   CLIP Radford et al. (2021) and ALBEF Li et al. (2021), while the point encoder was based on
364   CAT Qian et al. (2023), aiming to balance accuracy with minimal complexity. The point encoder
365   includes L1 = 3 blocks with SA, MLP, Batch-SA, and an MLP featuring two linear layers with
366   ReLU activations. The image encoder also has L2 = 3 blocks, the 2D box encoder L3 = 2, and
367   each multimodal encoder L4 = L5 = 2 blocks, with SA, CA, and Batch-SA configured to a hidden
368   size of d = 512 and 8 attention heads. Box regression is achieved through three two-layer MLPs
369
      with a hidden size of 1024. We used the Adam optimizer with a starting learning rate of 2 ↓ 10↑4 , a
      cosine annealing scheduler for adjustments, and a weight decay of 0.05. MMCAT trained on four
370
      Nvidia A100 GPUs for 1,000 epochs with a batch size of B = 256, incorporating standard data
371
      augmentations like shifting, scaling, and flipping. We employed the frustum extraction method Wei
372   et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022a); Qian et al. (2023); Meng et al. (2021b) to extract point clouds and
373   their associated images and 2D boxes for the Car category from all frames or sequences in the datasets.
374   These extracted objects were randomly selected to train our model in 14% and 20% for KITTI and
375   Waymo, respectively. Once trained, MMCAT serves as a 3D automatic annotator, re-labeling the
376   KITTI and Waymo training set for the Vehicle class. For 3D object detection, we utilized PointRCNN
377   for KITTI and PVRCNN++ Shi et al. (2023) for Waymo, following the OpenPCDET Team (2020)
      protocols for both training and evaluation.

512   4.4   L IMITATIONS
513   Despite the overall accuracy of our box generation, MMCAT faces challenges under certain difficult
514   conditions. Specifically, for samples with sparse foreground points (fewer than 30 points) in Figure 3,
515   the model struggles to accurately capture the data distribution and appearance of these objects. As a
516   result, we excluded these sparsely populated samples from the training process. We observed that
517   MMCAT cannot reliably model objects with such limited data, leading to inaccurate predictions.
518   For instance, MMCAT often misinterprets partial vehicle segments as entire objects and incorrectly
519
      positions these segments as the center of objects. These errors significantly impact the accuracy
      of locations and yaws in our 3D box regressions. We aim to further explore whether incorporating
520
      additional multimodal information (e.g., text) and semantic data can enhance the understanding of
521
      object shapes in point clouds.
522
523   Furthermore, in response to inquiries regarding our model’s performance on easier cases within the
524   KITTI dataset, we have conducted a focused analysis of failure modes. Our findings suggest that
525
      while the integration of image features generally enhances detection accuracy, it can sometimes
      lead to the overshadowing of LiDAR data in less complex scenarios. This imbalance occasionally
526
      introduces noise which affects performance negatively. We are actively refining our feature integration
527
      process to ensure that both modalities contribute optimally and synergistically. This ongoing work
528   underscores our commitment to improving MMCAT and further illustrates the challenges of creating
529   a truly robust multimodal annotation system.
530
531   5     C ONCLUSION
532   This study presents MMCAT, an autolabeler that generates 3D bounding boxes from weak 2D
533   annotations using LiDAR point clouds and images. Addressing point cloud sparsity, MMCAT
534   employs a multimodal, context-aware Transformer framework that integrates 3D geometric properties,
535   image semantics, and spatial context from 2D boxes, guided by visual cues. Experimental evaluations
536   on KITTI and Waymo datasets confirm MMCAT’s superior performance over existing autolabelers,
537   providing high-quality 3D annotations, even for hard samples. Future efforts will focus on enhancing
538   MMCAT’s performance, increasing its efficiency and precision across a broader range of classes in
539   3D automatic annotation tasks.
