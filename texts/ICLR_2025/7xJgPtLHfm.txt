009                                           Abstract
010
011           When applying deep-learning-based solvers to differential equations, a key
012           challenge is how to improve their generalization ability, so that the pre-
013           trained models could be easily adapted to new scenarios of interest. In this
014           paper, inspired by the well-known mathematical statements on the continu-
015           ous dependence of solutions to ordinary differential equations on initial val-
016           ues and parameters, we make a non-trivial extension of the physics-informed
017           neural networks by incorporating additional information on the continuous
018           dependence of solutions (abbreviated as cd-PINN). Our cd-PINN integrates
019
              the advantages of neural operators and Meta-PINN, requiring only a few
              labeled data while enabling solving ordinary differential equations with
020
              respect to new initial values and parameters in a fast and accurate way
021
              without fine-tuning. As demonstrated through novel examples like the Lo-
022           gistic model, the Lotka-Volterra model, damped harmonic oscillators and a
023           multiscale model for p53 activation, the accuracy of cd-PINN under those
024           untrained conditions is usually 1-3 orders of magnitude higher than PINN.
025           Meanwhile, the GPU time cost for training in the two approaches is compa-
026           rable. Therefore, we expect that our cd-PINN would be particularly useful
027           in improving the eﬀiciency and accuracy of deep learning-based solvers for
028           differential equations.

031   1   Introduction
032
033   In recent years, applying various deep-learning algorithms for solving differential equations
034
      has attracted increasing attention. Deep-learning-based differential equation solvers are
      generally considered to have a significant potential to improve computational eﬀiciency (Es-
035
      maeilzadeh et al., 2020; Kochkov et al., 2021). One particular notable method is Physics-
036
      Informed Neural Networks (PINN)(Raissi et al., 2019), which integrates the governing equa-
037   tions into the loss function to train the model and approximates the solutions to differential
038   equations without discrediting the solution domain. Similarly, the Deep Galerkin Method
039   (DGM)(Sirignano & Spiliopoulos, 2018) uses neural networks to approximate solutions to
040   differential equations, but its loss function is based primarily on the Galerkin residual. The
041   Deep Ritz method(E & Yu, 2018) reformulates the differential equation in a variational
042   form and solves it by minimizing the associated energy. Based on the weak solution form of
043   the differential equations, weak adversarial networks(Zang et al., 2020) parameterize both
044   the weak solution and the test function into the primary neural network and the adversar-
045
      ial neural network, respectively. Additionally, several enhanced methods based on PINN
      have emerged. Some of these methods focus on decomposing the solution domain, allowing
046
      the model to be trained in parallel across multiple GPUs, such as conservative Physics-
047
      Informed Neural Networks(cPINN), extended Physics-Informed Neural Networks(xPINN),
048   etc.(Jagtap et al., 2020; Jagtap & Karniadakis, 2020). In particular, gPINN(Yu et al., 2022)
049   incorporate the gradient of the differential equations into the loss function to minimize re-
050   liance on residual points and P2 INN(Cho et al. (2024)) uses the parameters of the equation
051   as additional encode input so that the model can better solve the CDR equations. Specif-
052   ically for ODEs, Neural ODEs (Chen et al. (2018); Hu et al. (2022)) learn a continuous
053   dynamic model of the data generation process by embedding neural networks into the ODE
      systems.

054
      However, these methods treat differential equations with varying parameters and initial
055   values as distinct tasks. When the parameters or initial values change, the model must be
056   re-trained. This will lead to unaffordable computational cost in the face of a large amount of
057   tasks for solving differential equations with diverse parameters and initial values. To address
058   the above issue and enhance the universality of solving different differential equations with
059   deep learning, researchers have begun exploring operator learning, which involves using
060   neural networks to learn the mapping between two infinite-dimensional function spaces.
061   PDE-Net(Long et al., 2018; 2019) is one of the earliest neural operators, inspired by the
062   finite difference method. It designs a specialized convolution kernel to solve both the forward
063   and inverse problems of differential equations. DeepONet(Lu et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
064   2021), grounded in the universal approximation theorem for operators, learns the mapping
065   of functions to functions, specifically mapping the initial values of PDEs to their solutions.
066   PINO (Li et al., 2024) is the first hybrid approach incorporating data and PDE constraints
067   at different resolutions to learn the solution operator of a given family of parametric PDEs.
068   The Fourier Neural Operator(FNO) (Li et al., 2020a) leverages the fast Fourier transform
069
      to perform convolution operations in the Fourier space, enabling it to map input functions
      to target functions with exceptional performance in high-dimensional and complex systems.
070
      The Graph Neural Operator(GNO)(Li et al., 2020b) integrates graph neural networks with
071
      operator learning, using graph structures to represent spatial points and their connections,
072   eﬀiciently handling input function mappings on irregular networks.
073
074   A notable advantage of these models is that once trained, the prediction time for new ap-
075
      plications is nearly negligible. However, training these models usually requires a substantial
      amount of labeled data, whose quantity and quality determine the model’s performance
076
      to a large extent. This shortcoming has prompted researchers to explore the integration
077
      of meta-learning with PINN algorithms. Meta-learning-based PINN can be categorized
078   into two frameworks: feedforward meta-learning and agnostic meta-learning(MAML). In
079   feedforward-based meta-PINN, the meta-learning model primarily learns how to map the
080   configurations of differential equations to the weight parameters of the PINN model, as seen
081   in approaches like Hyper-PINN and Meta-MgNet(de Avila Belbute-Peres et al., 2021; Chen
082   et al., 2022). On the other hand, the MAML-based meta-PINN aims to learn an eﬀicient
083   initialization for the PINN weight parameters that exhibit strong generalization capabilities.
084   This allows the model to be fine-tuned for a new configuration with only a few rounds of
085   gradient updates. For example, by using the reptile-based method, Liu et al.(Liu et al.,
086
      2022) directly learns the initialization of the PINN model, while the MAD-PINN (Huang
      et al., 2022) implicitly encodes the configurations as additional input to the PINN model,
087
      then fine-tuning them to reach a best output. Despite significant advances, these meta-
088
      learning methods still face many limitations. They often require longer training time, and
089   the fine-tuning procedure may be computationally expensive when many new configurations
090   are involved.
091
092
      In this paper, we propose a new method that integrates the advantages of neural operators
      and Meta-PINN, requiring only a small amount of labeled data while enabling accurate
093
      predictions on new configurations without the need for fine-tuning. Unlike Meta-PINN, we
094
      treat the solution of parametric differential equations as a single task, rather than separated
095   tasks with different configurations. By incorporating the parameters and initial values
096   as additional input and adding the constraints of continuous dependence of solutions on
097   parameters and initial values into the loss function, we make a non-trivial generalization
098   of PINN (cd-PINN). Our cd-PINN exhibits an outstanding performance on a number of
099   ODE solving tasks involving different combinations of parameters and initial values, whose
100   accuracy under those untrained conditions is usually improved by 1-3 orders of magnitude
101   compared to the vanilla PINN.

175   2.2   PINN with continuous dependence (cd-PINN)
176
177   When applying PINN as well as its most variants to ODEs, most of them lack suﬀicient
178   ability to maintain their outstanding performance on new parameters or initial values, which
179   are dramatically different from the original ones used for training. Towards this key issue,
180
      our goal is to improve the generalization ability of PINN for solving various differential
      equations in a significant way. Meanwhile, the flexibility, eﬀiciency, and accuracy of PINN
181
      should be kept as much as possible. In this way, we could easily solve differential equations
182
      under a diverse set of initial conditions and parameters, by using PINN after a small amount
183   of training. This is particularly useful in applications requiring extensive predictions and
184   real-time feedback, as it significantly enhances the system’s robustness and eﬀiciency while
185   reducing the complexity of model maintenance.
186
      Here we propose a variant of PINN, called cd-PINN (PINN with continuous dependence).
187

188

189   measurement of u, which helps to stabilize the training procedure, and an unsupervised
190   residual loss, which incorporates the physical information about the differential equations.

228   3.1   The Logistic Model
229
      As the first example, we select the Logistic model, a classical ordinary differential equa-
230
      tion first published by Pierre Verhulst to describe the population growth regulated by the
231
      carrying capacity due to resource limits. It reads
232

233                                      du               u
                                             = ru 1 −          ,                              (7)
234                                      dt             umax
235
236   where u = u(t) represents the population size, umax denotes the carrying capacity, and
237
      r ∈ R+ is the rate of maximum population growth. By using the separation of variables, we
      can find its general solution as u∗ (t) = u(t)/umax = [1 + e−rt (umax /u0 − 1)]−1 , where u0 is
238
      the initial population size. It is straightforward to verify that this solution is continuously
239
      differentiable with respect to the growth rate r and the initial value u0 .
240
241   This simple equation could be easily solved by most state-of-the-art deep learning algo-
242   rithms. For instance, by using PINN the relative error of solutions could be readily mini-
243
      mized below 10−3 − 10−4 , although this good performance is limited to fixed growth rate r
      and initial population size u0 . If we directly transfer the PINN model to a new growth rate
244
      or initial value without further fine-tuning, the predicted solution will significantly deviate
245
      from the true value (see Figure 1(a)). This fact clearly reveals that the vanilla PINN lacks
246   suﬀicient ability in generalization.
247
248   In contrast, by taking the information on the continuous dependence of solutions to the
249
      Logistic equation on both the growth rate r and initial population size u0 into consideration
      (the carrying capacity umax is fixed), our cd-PINN can achieve a good agreement between
250
      the predicted population size and its true value at any time points. Most astonishingly,
251
      by only using single set of training data with respect to specified r = 1 and u0 = 0.3 as
252   marked by the red star in Figure 1(b), the absolute errors could be maintained below 10−1
253   (and in most regions below 10−3 ) over the entire region for u∗0 = u0 /umax ∈ [0.01, 1.0] and
254   r ∈ [0.1, 10.0]. Moreover, we compare the absolute error of cd-PINN with that of PINN
255   at each point in the parameter space. As illustrated in Figure 1(b), except for several tiny
256   domains around the training data point, the accuracy of cd-PINN is significantly higher
257   than PINN.
258   To uncover why cd-PINN has such a nice performance on generalization in the current
259   study, we make a comparison on the vector fields of ∂u/∂r and ∂u/∂u0 given by PINN and
260   cd-PINN in Figure 1(d). It can be observed that by imposing constraints not only on the
261   solution u(t) but also on its partial derivatives ∂u/∂r and ∂u/∂u0 , cd-PINN has successfully
262   reproduced the correct vector fields. Contrarily, the vanilla PINN makes wrong predictions,
263   especially at the top-left corner of Figure 1(d).

265   u0 = 0.3, r = 1.0, along with 214 residual data points, i.e. Nt = N0 = 214 . To approximate
266   the continuous solution, we utilize a fully connected neural network with 6 hidden layers,
267   each containing 64 neurons. And T anh is employed as the activation function. During

428   and PINN. In Figure 3(c-d), we can see that the convergence rates of cd-PINN and PINN are
429   comparable on the residual loss Lres , while the continuity loss of cd-PINN converges much
430   faster and is also much lower than that of PINN. This observation emphasizes the eﬀicacy of
431   integrating constraints on continuous dependence, resulting in a model with largely improved
      generalization capabilities.

486
      4   Conclusion and Discussion
487
488
      Previous deep-learning-based algorithms have achieved fantastic results in various fields,
489   though they still face with big challenges in generalization beyond the training data. More-
490   over, when applied to complex systems, like partial differential equations, where solutions
491   exhibit sensitivity to initial conditions or model parameters, these weaknesses become more
492   obvious.
493
      To enhance the generalization capability of PINN, in this study we propose a novel approach
494
      (cd-PINN) by incorporating additional information on the continuous dependence of solu-
495   tions on the parameters and initial values. Through the Logistic model, Lotka-Volterra
496   equations, damped harmonic oscillators and a multiscale model for p53 activation, the sig-
497   nificant advantages of our cd-PINN over the vanilla PINN have been clearly demonstrated,
498   which are summarized as follows.
499
      Generalization and accuracy. Incorporating the continuous dependence information into
500
      the loss function enables cd-PINN to effectively learn the fundamental mapping between
501   parameters/initial values and solutions. In all numerical experiments, our cd-PINN shows
502   a comparable accuracy to the vanilla PINN on the training data. More importantly, our
503   cd-PINN could maintain its promising performance on new configurations, which are far
504   away from the training data in the parameter space.
505
      Universality and robustness. From the simple Logistic model to complex LV dynamics
506
      and oscillatory systems, our cd-PINN demonstrates its universal applicability. Moreover, it
507   is observed that either the stability of fixed points or their respective attraction domains
508   have limited impacts on the solution’s accuracy, implying the robustness of cd-PINN against
509   the underlying dynamics and the location of training data.
510
      Eﬀiciency and no-fine-tuning. The inclusion of the continuity loss does not apparently
511
      increase the computational cost of cd-PINN, whose training time is still comparable to the
512
      vanilla PINN. Meanwhile, cd-PINN has no demand for retraining or fine-tuning when facing
513   with new parameters or initial conditions, in contrast to meta-PINN.
514
515   In the literature, Neural ODEs (Chen et al. (2018)) are another prominent approach for
516
      learning solutions to ordinary differential equations. With respect to the same data set and
      evaluation metrics, we find that the Neural ODE model could effectively capture the system
517
      dynamics at the training data points, but shows a much poorer generalization ability than
518
      cd-PINN under the testing scenarios (see Appendix B.6). In addition, the Neural ODE
519   model takes a much longer training time due to the explicit implementation of temporal
520   integration steps, whereas PINN and cd-PINN are more computationally eﬀicient.
521
522
      In the current paper, we restrict our study to ordinary differential equations for clarity.
      Obviously, the same approach is applicable to partial differential equations too, e.g. the
523
      viscosity in Burgers equation or the Reynolds number in Navier-Stokes equations. However,
524
      it should be noted that the PDE cases are far more complicated in general. For example,
525   in many cases the parameter dependence of PDEs may be continuous but not necessarily
526   differentiable. This subtle distinction is crucial for certain contexts, such as the shock
527   structures in hyperbolic conservation laws(Evans (2022)). Under these situations, we need to
528   turn to more general conditions, like the Rankine-Hugoniot jump condition, to determine the
529   exact locations where the shock structure arises. The related work is ongoing. Furthermore,
530   we would like to explore the advantages and limitations of cd-PINN in high-dimensional and
531   multiscale systems, especially for real-world problems.
