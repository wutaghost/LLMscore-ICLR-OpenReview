009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has been gaining pop-
012            ularity as a method for aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) with human
013            preferences. It involves performing Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) followed by
014            fine-tuning using a reward model trained on human preference data. However, two
015            primary issues with this approach are the difficult and expensive curation of human
016            preference data and the opaque, black-box nature of the rewards. To address these
017            issues, this paper introduces a novel framework for aligning LLMs with human
018
               preferences. Our framework involves using representative sub-dimensions for
               specific tasks to generate rewards by leveraging a performant out-of-the-box LLM.
019
               We evaluate our approach by fine-tuning two models, one using our approach and
020
               one using traditional black-box rewards. Evaluation using an advanced LLM-based
021            method demonstrates that our approach maintains the performance of the black-box
022            baseline while offering superior explainability and flexibility. This framework not
023            only enhances transparency in RLHF but also eliminates reliance on expensive
024            human-curated preference data.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence (AI), particularly in the domain of human user-facing
030   large language models (LLMs), has brought the critical issue of aligning these models with human
031   preferences and intentions to the forefront. Contemporary AI systems utilizing state-of-the-art LLMs
032   commonly employ the following strategy: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Wei et al., 2021; Sanh
033
      et al., 2021) followed by Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al.,
      2022; Bai et al., 2022a). SFT is typically based on imitative learning from human annotators, and
034
      RLHF advances this by training models to learn human preferences and score responses. These
035
      techniques are integral to developing AI systems that are compatible with human expectations and
036   ethics (Wang et al., 2023b).
037
038   However, these methodologies come with inherent limitations. The effectiveness of SFT and RLHF
039
      depends on the quality of human-preference datasets (Zhou et al., 2023). Extensive human supervision
      involved in these processes not only escalates time and cost but also poses challenges like self-
040
      consistency and potential biases (Wang et al., 2023a). Furthermore, the conventional RLHF approach,
041
      which often implicitly assumes human preferences are well-ordered by a scalar score, might not
042   fully grasp the complex spectrum of human values. Such simplification risks overlooking the
043   intricate interplay of diverse human values like helpfulness, relevance, and harmlessness and may not
044   accurately reflect the multi-faceted nature of human preferences in the evaluation process (Bai et al.,
045   2022a). Finally, the scalar reward signal used in RLHF is often an unexplainable black box, meaning
046   it is difficult or impossible, given a reward signal, to explain why a particular response garnered such
047   a score, or what human values are or are not well-aligned in a given response. In other words, it is
048   not always clear how to inspect just what values are being aligned in RLHF, which is a threat to the
049   transparency of alignment procedures.
050   This has spurred significant interest in researching and developing more explainable alignment
051   methods. Numerous works propose methods of performing RLHF using sub-reward models (Wu
052   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b). A commonality in these methods is to train individual sub-models
053   for different dimensions and propose combining them in different ways. Various publicly available
      human-preference datasets such as OpenAssistant Conversations dataset (Köpf et al., 2024) and
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Nvidia’s HelpSteer dataset (Wang et al., 2023c) have been used to train these models. While these
055   methods have increased explainability, they still retain their data dependency. It’s now even more
056   pronounced as rather than ranking preferences, human labelers now have to score LLM responses
057   across different dimensions.
058
      Simultaneously, there has also been substantial research on the efficacy of LLMs as evaluators or
059
      "LLM-as-a-judge". Zheng et al. (2023) have demonstrated the efficacy of using LLM evaluators as
060
      capable of mimicking human preferences well. Meanwhile, Wang et al. (2024c) have successfully
061   used LLMs for alignment, improved only using synthetic data. These approaches utilize the com-
062   prehensive knowledge of LLMs to judge a model’s response and generate strong reward signals to
063   perform RLHF. However, these methods still perform RLHF by training a blackbox reward model,
064   tying back to the opaqueness issue.
065
      Our proposed work addresses the critical issues of data dependency and opacity in traditional RLHF
066
      methods by leveraging out-of-the-box LLMs as explainable evaluators to generate rewards. We
067   achieve this by first identifying the relevant dimensions for the chosen task. Unlike traditional
068   methods that treat these dimensions implicitly within a scalar-valued black box reward model, our
069   approach scores responses using an LLM separately along each of these dimensions. We then use a
070   simple interpretable model of these scores as features to compute an overall reward. The resulting
071   reward model is more transparent and explainable1 In addition, as the rewards are generated based on
072   the real-time prompt and response while fine-tuning and not on learned representations, the reward
073   model generalizes much better. We show that it still leads to comparable or better performance when
074   used for alignment fine-tuning of an LLM compared to a black-box reward model optimized on
075
      human preference data.
076   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
077
078           1. Reduced Dependence on Human-curated Data: Our primary contribution is leveraging
079              LLMs to generate an explainable reward during the RLHF process via scores along different
080
                 dimensions. Our approach eliminates the need for extensive human-curated preference data
                 to train a reward model. Our experimental results demonstrate that our approach can match
081
                 the performance of a model fine-tuned using a reward model trained on expertly curated
082
                 human preference data.
083
084
              2. Flexible and Adaptable Framework: Our method allows for easy adjustment and addition
                 of required dimensions. Through our experimental results, we have demonstrated the
085
                 versatility and adaptability of our approach by performing two different tasks, namely
086
                 summarization and safety alignment, each with its own set of dimensions.
087
088
              3. Explainability: Because our reward model is constrained in model-structure to use an
                 interpretable model of a small number of human-interpretable features, the reward score
089
                 during fine-tuning is explainable in-principle.

094   Language Model Alignment The intricate task of aligning LLMs with human values and intentions
095   has become a paramount concern in the field of AI. As contemporary AI systems, exemplified by
096   ChatGPT, rapidly advance in their capabilities, there has been an increase in the work on aligning
097
      with human values. Current state-of-the-art AI systems predominantly include SFT (Wei et al., 2021;
      Sanh et al., 2021) and RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a). SFT involves training models
098
      through imitation, where human annotators provide demonstrations of instructions and responses
099
      for the model to emulate. However, SFT often struggles to effectively distinguish between high and
100   low-quality outputs, leading to suboptimal performance (Wang et al., 2023a). To enhance alignment,
101   RLHF was introduced, which involves training a reward model to mimic human rankings of responses.
102

105   human data dependency and overfitting, the individual scores are generated by an LLM, so one cannot inspect

      In this process, humans rank the outputs generated by the model, and the model learns to optimize
109   for these rankings. This method has significantly improved the helpfulness of models and reduced
110   the generation of harmful outputs. However, one of the challenges inherent in SFT and RLHF
111   is their dependence on extensive and high-quality human-preference datasets (Zhou et al., 2023).
112   Therefore, the concept of "Constitutional AI" (Bai et al., 2022b) emerged. This approach, also known
113   as Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF), leverages a powerful LLM to generate
114   preference labels instead of relying on human annotators. By using self-critiques and revisions based
115   on a set of predefined principles or "constitution," the AI system can evaluate and refine its outputs.
116   Direct RLAIF (Lee et al., 2023) takes this a step further by using the LLM’s generated feedback
117
      directly as the reward signal during training, bypassing the need for a reward model. This method
      further reduces dependency on human feedback, allowing the system to autonomously align with
118
      complex human values.
119
120
      Integrating Multi-Dimensional Human Feedback in Language Models Recent advancements
121
      in integrating multi-dimensional human feedback into language models have focused on enhancing
122
      the personalization and explainability aspect of alignment. Traditional approaches, such as those
123   employed in RLHF, rely heavily on reward models to translate human preferences into training
124   signals for language models. However, these models often face challenges in handling complex,
125   multi-dimensional preferences, leading to potential misalignment between model behavior and human
126   expectations.
127
      One approach to addressing this issue is the use of fine-grained feedback (Wu et al., 2024). This
128
      method categorizes feedback into specific types of errors, providing a more detailed training signal
129   compared to traditional scalar reward models. However, a limitation of this approach is the increased
130   complexity and potential difficulty in obtaining consistently high-quality fine-grained feedback from
131   annotators. Another innovative method that addresses the alignment of models with diverse human
132   preferences is (Jang et al., 2023), which uses multi-objective reinforcement learning (MORL) (Roijers
133   et al., 2013). This framework involves training separate policy models on distinct objectives and then
134   merging these models post-training based on user-defined criteria. Similarly, Rame et al. (2024) also
135   employs MORL to balance competing objectives by providing a Pareto-optimal set of solutions. Both
136   approaches offer significant flexibility and personalization without the need for extensive retraining.
137
      However, they share the limitation of potentially struggling with the efficient integration of a large
      number of distinct preferences, which can complicate the merging process and may require additional
138
      resources or fine-tuning to manage effectively.
139
140   SteerLM (Dong et al., 2023) and ArmoRM (Wang et al., 2024a) represent approaches aimed at
141   enhancing the controllability and explainability of language models. SteerLM conditions supervised
142   the fine-tuning on predicted attributes, which allows for more targeted and controllable responses.
143
      Meanwhile, ArmoRM uses a Mixture-of-Experts (Masoudnia & Ebrahimpour, 2014) strategy to
      dynamically weight different objectives, such as honesty or safety, based on the context. This
144
      enhances explainability by making the decision-making process transparent and reducing issues like
145
      verbosity bias. However, both of these approaches significantly depend on human labelers, requiring
146   explicit labeling of scores across multiple dimensions.

149   3   M ETHODOLOGY
150
151   Our proposed methodology provides a framework for decomposing black-box reward signals into
152   explainable components for alignment, as illustrated in Figure 1. The framework consists of three
153
      main steps:
154
            1. Identify key human values crucial for a specific task. Optionally, we evaluate the effective-
155
               ness of the chosen dimensions by generating scores for existing human-preference data and
156
               calculating pairwise accuracy.
157
158         2. Use an LLM to generate scores along the chosen dimensions for sampled prompts and
159
               responses. This step involves careful prompt engineering to ensure consistent scoring across
               dimensions. (More details in Section 3.2)
160
161         3. Generate the final reward signal for fine-tuning using a simple interpretable model with the
               LLM dimension scores as features.

179   3.1   TASK -S PECIFIC H UMAN VALUE I DENTIFICATION
180
      We identify task-specific key human values that are relevant to evaluating the quality and alignment
181
      of LLM-generated outputs. These values represent the criteria that humans implicitly or explicitly
182
      use when judging the appropriateness and effectiveness of responses in a given task. For each task
183   under consideration, we refer to existing literature on similar tasks to ensure the selected values
184   comprehensively capture the essential aspects of human judgment. For instance, in a question-
185   answering task focused on safety alignment, key dimensions include truthfulness, helpfulness, and
186   harmlessness, as outlined by Bai et al. (2022a), Askell et al. (2021), and Ganguli et al. (2022).
187   Similarly, in a summarization task, we prioritize dimensions such as coherence, accuracy, and
188   coverage, as highlighted by prior work (Belz & Gatt, 2008; Zhong et al., 2022). These dimensions
189   capture the essential qualities that make a summary effective and useful to human readers.
190   Each task is thus associated with a tailored set of human values, ensuring that the evaluation
191   model reflects the contextual nuances of human preferences. This approach allows us to capture
192   essential human values with task-specific granularity, contributing to a more accurate assessment of
193   LLM alignment and performance. However, we emphasize that these dimensions are task-specific
194   approximations rather than universally optimal measures. One might object that our approach, which
195   requires selecting subjective dimensions of human preference, is problematic due to its inherent
196   subjectivity. We take the contrary view: standard approaches to black-box reward modeling merely
197
      avoid articulating precisely what is considered important to alignment and do not constrain the reward
      model structure to ensure consistency.

      3.2   E XPLAINABLE REWARD SIGNAL GENERATION
201   Once the task-specific human values are identified, we develop a scoring system using a state-of-
202   the-art LLM to evaluate outputs along these dimensions. This approach leverages the advanced
203   language understanding capabilities of LLMs to provide nuanced assessments of LLM-generated
204   responses. For the chosen dimensions, we ask the LLMs to generate scores within a certain range,
205   with a higher score indicating a preferred response. The detailed LLM prompts used for each task
206   are provided in Appendix B. These prompts include clear instructions to ensure that the LLM can
207   deliver high-quality evaluations across the various dimensions. Specifically, the prompts include
208   clear rubrics and guidelines to maintain consistency in scoring across different responses. To further
209
      maintain consistency, we set the generation hyperparameters in such a way as to ensure that the
      LLM-as-a-Judge scoring system is (relatively) deterministic.
210

227   3.3   B LACK - BOX REWARD SIGNAL GENERATION
228
229   To evaluate the effectiveness of our explainable approach, we also implement a traditional black-box
230   reward model as a baseline. This model is created by fine-tuning the same LLM used in generating
231
      the explainable reward signal in order to ensure a fair comparison. The reward model generates a

232
      a pairwise preference dataset to minimize the following loss function, attempting to maximize the

277
278
      Our experiments demonstrate that the explainable RLHF approach performs comparably to traditional
      black-box RLHF methods while offering enhanced interpretability. In the question-answering task, the
279
      explainable model showed particular strength in helpfulness and achieved overall better performance
280
      than the black-box model. Both RLHF methods significantly improved over the baseline SFT model
281   across all evaluated dimensions. For the summarization task, results were more mixed, with the
282   explainable model excelling in coherence and accuracy but struggling with coverage. These findings
283   suggest that our explainable RLHF framework can effectively align language models with human
284   preferences, particularly in tasks like question-answering, while providing valuable insights into the
285   model’s decision-making process through its multi-dimensional reward structure.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
470
471
      In this paper, we focus on improving the transparency, explainability, simplicity, and generalizability
472
      of aligning an LLM by fine-tuning using reinforcement learning. Our approach involves breaking
473
      down the reward signals into more interpretable features of human values that can be scored using an
474   LLM and combined with an interpretable model. Our experiments show that the outcomes of the
475   explainable reward model are comparable to those of the black-box reward model, and we observe no
476   significant degradation in performance. By directly using LLMs themselves to generate the reward
477   signal, we also reduce reliance on human-preference data while improving the generalizability of the
478   reward model used in LLMs.
479
      An interesting direction our work could be extended to is to study the relative advantages of different
480   interpretable models for combining the dimension-level scores. A related direction is to study the use
481   of adaptive and personalized interpretable models at this level that might be used to more dynamically
482   explore the space of possible Pareto Optimal alignments within the multi-objective reward space.
483   It is also interesting to consider the effect of scale for the LLMs used in the explainable alignment
484   procedure. As one moves to using larger LLMs, it is entirely possible that the fine-tuning procedure
485   of a black-box reward model will become more challenging whereas the quality of the scores for
      dimensions of reward as employed by our explainable reward models will increase.
