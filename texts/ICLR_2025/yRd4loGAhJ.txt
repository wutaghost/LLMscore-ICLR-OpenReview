009                                                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011                                   In this work, we introduce a novel approach called Scaling to Emphasize Atten-
012                                   tion for Long-context retrieval (SEAL), which enhances the retrieval performance
013                                   of large language models (LLMs) over extended contexts. Previous studies have
014                                   shown that each attention head in LLMs has a unique functionality and collectively
015                                   contributes to the overall behavior of the model. Similarly, we observe that spe-
016                                   cific heads are closely tied to long-context retrieval, showing positive or negative
017
                                      correlation with retrieval scores. Built on this insight, we propose a learning-based
                                      mechanism using zero-shot generated data to emphasize these heads, improving
018
                                      the model’s performance in long-context retrieval tasks. By applying SEAL, we
019
                                      can achieve significant improvements in in-domain retrieval performance, includ-
020                                   ing document QA tasks from LongBench, and considerable improvements in out-
021                                   of-domain cases. Additionally, when combined with existing training-free context
022                                   extension techniques, SEAL extends the context limits of LLMs while maintain-
023                                   ing highly reliable outputs, opening new avenues for research in this field.

026   1                     I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al. (2020), Radford et al. (2019), Touvron et al. (2023))
029   are capable of rapidly generating high-quality answers to a wide range of questions by leveraging the
030   diverse knowledge embedded in their vast number of parameters. However, in-depth analyses have
031   revealed a common issue known as hallucination (Shuster et al. (2021), Lin et al. (2021), Ji et al.
032   (2023)), where the models confidently produce inaccurate answers. To address this, research has
033
      focused on using external information as context to guide the outputs, such as Retrieval-Augmented
      Generation (Lewis et al. (2020), Xu et al. (2023)) and Chain-of-Thought reasoning (Wei et al.
034
      (2022)). These approaches have significantly improved the reliability of LLMs by enabling them
035
      to reference existing information during generation. However, this trend has also highlighted a key
036   limitation of LLMs: the constraint of their context window length.

      This limitation of the context window stems from several problems, such as the design constraints of
055   positional encoding (Su et al. (2024)) in LLMs and the preference for shorter sequences in training
056   data. It is an inherent feature of trained LLMs, where performance rapidly degrades once the prede-
057   fined context window size is exceeded. To mitigate this issue, several training-free and fine-tuning-
058   based methods (Xiao et al. (2023), Han et al. (2023), Zhang et al. (2024)) have been developed to
059   extend the context length of trained LLMs. Recently, model providers have even started releasing
060   models specifically designed for long-context to address this limitation (Abdin et al. (2024), Jiang
061   et al. (2024)).
062   However, even within extended context windows, performance tends to degrade as the context length
063   approaches its limit. This leads to phenomena such as the “lost in the middle” (Liu et al. (2024a)),
064   where the model exhibits biases toward focusing on the early and later parts of the context, resulting
065   in an increased likelihood of incorrect answers or hallucinations. Including this phenomenon, issues
066   in which retrieval performance is influenced by input length have been consistently observed.
067   In this study, we aim to address this second problem. We specifically address cases where retrieval
068   tasks are performed on long-context inputs, which we define as long-context retrieval. Our approach
069   is based on the insight that well-trained LLMs possess the inherent ability to infer information accu-
070   rately regardless of context length, but biases in their trained parameters often lead to performance
071   degradation. For a representative long-context retrieval benchmark, we observed that certain atten-
072   tion heads contribute notably to long-context retrieval, adjusting their strength to either improved or
073   reduced accuracy largely.
074   Built on these observations, we propose a novel approach, Scaling to Emphasize Attention for Long-
075   context retrieval (SEAL). SEAL is a learning-based attention scaling technique that fine-tunes atten-
076   tion strength using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) on a small set of generated data following the
077   format of the task domain. SEAL consists of two major processes. First, training data focused on the
078   context format are generated for the target task. Our goal is to alter the head-wise contribution rather
079   than update the embedded knowledge. Therefore, a small set of generated data is sufficient to iden-
080   tify the important heads relevant to retrieval. Subsequently, head-wise and channel-wise learnable
081
      scales are fine-tuned for SEAL-H (head) and SEAL-C (channel), respectively. Through this process,
      SEAL not only probes the importance of each attention component but also adjusts the scaling to
082
      enhance retrieval performance. Unlike widely known Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning methods (Hu
083
      et al., Houlsby et al. (2019)), SEAL focuses on emphasizing the heads relevant to retrieval, supported
084   by our observations, which enables high accuracy with minimal data and learnable parameters.
085
086   Using SEAL, we have achieved significant accuracy improvements in in-domain environments with
087
      less than one hour of fine-tuning for 7B models, regardless of the model type. Additionally, we have
      verified that SEAL maintains generalization ability even for out-of-domain tasks. Most importantly,
088
      SEAL has delivered substantial improvements in long-context retrieval accuracy for LLMs that had
089
      already been trained and had their context extended using existing techniques, and this breakthrough
090   opens up new possibilities for enhancing the long-context retrieval capabilities of existing LLMs.

      3     M OTIVATION
139
140
      Research on Transformer-based architectures (Elhage et al. (2021), Ferrando et al. (2024)) has shown
      that attention heads, a key component, perform distinct roles such as copying, retrieval, and rele-
141
      vance, working together to shape the network’s overall functionality. Notably, some heads specialize
142
      in handling long sequences, while others focus on retrieval. This leads to an optimistic prediction:
143   if we can identify and strengthen the heads specialized in long-context retrieval, we might
144   significantly enhance performance in that area.
145
146
147
      3.1    P RIMARY OBSERVATION : PER - HEAD PRUNING
148
      To validate this prediction, we first re-examined whether each attention head contributes differently
149
      to the retrieval process and determined if we could identify an attention head specialized for retrieval.
150   Our experimental design is straightforward. As shown in Figure 2(a), we pruned one head at a time
151   on the LongChat-7B-32K (Li et al. (2023)) model and compared the resulting accuracy changes
152   with the accuracy of the baseline network. To simplify the experiment, we used the LongEval (Li
153   et al. (2023)) line retrieval benchmark, where the goal is to retrieve a digit of up to five characters
154   randomly located in a given text. This benchmark was particularly convenient because the target
155   retrieval tokens are limited to the digits 0 through 9.
156   As shown in Figure 2(b), the impact of each head varied significantly, with accuracy changes of
157   approximately ±20% or more, indicating that certain attention heads play a crucial role in retrieval.
158   These positive and negative head-wise impacts were consistently observed in both mid-length (x-
159   axis) and long-length (y-axis) contexts. While these results do not definitively show whether the
160   heads are directly involved in retrieval or are performing other important tasks necessary for accu-
161   racy (e.g. , understanding the format), an intriguing observation emerges: pruning certain attention
      heads can actually lead to an increase in retrieval scores.

178   3.2   G ENERALIZED APPROACH : ATTENTION HEAD - WISE SCALING
179
180   Next, we developed a more general approach to extend the head-wise pruning experiment. Since
181   pruning multiple heads simultaneously can lead to performance degradation, a more scalable method
182   was needed. To address this, we adjusted the scale of the identified heads to see if this could holis-
183   tically improve accuracy. Built on this insight, we divided the quadrants in Figure 2(b) based on
184
      baseline performance (0.0) on the x and y axes. Instead of pruning individual heads, we tried scal-
      ing multiple heads together. By scaling the influence of all heads in the first quadrant (Q1)—whose
185
      pruning benefits the retrieval task—by 0.9, we observed an accuracy increase from 32% to 56% at
186
      the input length of 31K (blue dotted line in Figure 2(d)). In contrast, scaling the heads in Q3—whose
187   pruning degrades retrieval—by 0.9 resulted in a significant drop in performance (yellow line). In-
188   terestingly, when we scaled Q1 by 0.9 and Q3 by 1.1 simultaneously, we observed an even greater
189   improvement in retrieval scores (red line). This suggests that jointly scaling and controlling the
190   influence of these heads can significantly enhance retrieval performance.
191
192
      3.3   E XTENDED APPROACH : ATTENTION CHANNEL - WISE SCALING
193
194   While previous observations show that head-wise scaling offers new possibilities for improving
195   long-context retrieval performance, there is still room for refining the granularity of scaling. As
196   noted in Quantizable Transformers (Bondarenko et al. (2023)), earlier research suggests that specific
197   channels handle syntactic elements like delimiter tokens, and even encode task-specific knowledge
198   (Rudman et al.). In our LongChat-7B (Li et al. (2023)) pruning experiment, we further applied
199   channel-wise pruning to the head with the greatest performance improvement (L1H18) and the head
200   with the largest performance drop (L13H16), as shown in Figure 2(c). Interestingly, within L1H18’s
201   128 channels, only certain channels accounted for most of the performance changes. Similarly, when
202
      we controlled L13H16 at a finer channel level, we discovered that some channels actually improved
      performance during pruning, though the overall head caused a significant drop. This underscores the
203
      need for channel-wise manipulation at a finer granularity than the head-level adjustments.

206   4     P ROPOSED METHOD : SEAL
207
208
      Built on these invaluable observations, we introduce a novel method called Scaling to Emphasize
209
      Attention for Long-Context Retrieval (SEAL), a framework designed to validate our findings and en-
210   hance the long-context retrieval performance of existing LLMs. In SEAL, we update existing LLMs
211   without altering their learned behavior, instead efficiently adjusting the strength of each attention
212   component. Since sequentially performing head or channel-wise pruning to identify the influence
213   of all heads or channels for each task is infeasible, our key idea is to leverage gradient descent to
214   ascertain the impact of each head on retrieval. Figure 3 provides an overview of SEAL. SEAL is
215   intentionally designed to validate our observations and enables the updating of LLMs with minimal
      training data and fine-tuning, as outlined in the previous section. SEAL’s key contributions are in

239   4.2   L EARNABLE SPACE DESIGN : SEAL-H AND SEAL-C
240
      Using the generated data, we trained a learnable scaling for attention components. Based on the intu-
241
      ition from pruning experiments of Section 3, we propose two granularities for attention control. The
242
      first is SEAL-H (head), which places a learnable scalar head-wise to learn the strength of each head
243   (Figure 3 Right). This process allows us to probe the influence of each head on retrieval while jointly
244   learning scaling appropriate for long contexts. The second option is SEAL-C (channel), which ad-
245   ditionally uses a learnable vector for the hidden dimension of each attention output (channel-wise).
246   As observed in Section 3.3, we found that within the attention heads, there are channels that have
247   both positive and negative impacts. SEAL-C assigns and updates parameters on a per-channel basis.
248   While this increases the number of parameters to be learned, it is expected to allow for more fine-
249   grained manipulation of the attention head outputs, potentially leading to improved performance.
250
251   4.3   PEFT BASELINE : SEAL-L (L O RA)
252
253   The proposed SEAL method can be categorized under Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT),
254   as it selects vital, minimal learnable parameters that can impact retrieval and performs supervised
255
      fine-tuning on these scales. From this perspective, a representative PEFT, LoRA (Hu et al.), can
      intuitively serve as our baseline and validate the effectiveness of our fine-tuning pipeline. Further-
256
      more, comparisons with SEAL-C and SEAL-H suggest that if these methods achieve performance
257
      comparable to SEAL-L with fewer parameters, it validates that we accurately identify the key factors
258   contributing to improved retrieval performance. Considering the most basic form of LoRA with rank
259   1 (r = 1), the learnable vectors of LoRA adjust the retrieval-related influence in a manner similar
260   to SEAL-C by controlling the effect across different channels. For this reason, we propose SEAL-L
261   (LoRA), which can be viewed as a superset of SEAL-C. In SEAL-L, while the LoRA module is used,
262   the data and training scheme are derived from the SEAL framework. In the main experiments, we
263   additionally report the results of the SEAL-D (DoRA). SEAL-D replaces the LoRA module with the
264   DoRA (Liu et al. (2024b)) module, a recent variant of LoRA. Through experiments, we demonstrate
265   that SEAL-H and SEAL-C represent the core components responsible for quality improvement.
266   In the case of SEAL-H, the total number of learnable parameters is LH (the number of blocks * the
267   number of heads). In the case of the LongChat-7B model, this amounts to only 1,024 parameters,
268   making it highly efficient. While SEAL-C uses more parameters, e.g. , 128K in LongChat-7B, this
269   cost is still affordable, nearly 10 times smaller than SEAL-L. Furthermore, the dataset contains only
      50 samples, resulting in the use of fewer than 2 million tokens for adjusting intensity. Moreover, the

303   we aim to observe, and we denote the head weight as Whead . Then the direct effect can be expressed

347   6       E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
348
349   To validate the effectiveness of the proposed SEAL, we evaluated its retrieval performance on
350   long-context inputs for two widely-used tasks: line retrieval from LongEval and the Needle-in-a-
351   Haystack.
352   Models: We validated SEAL on five models: LongChat-7B-v1.5-32K and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2
353   (Jiang et al. (2023)), which support a 32K context window length, and Vicuna-7B-v1.5-16K (Chiang
354   et al. (2023)), Vicuna-13B-v1.5-16K, LongChat-13B-16K, which support a 16K context window.
355
      Settings: We utilized the Axolotl1 framework to tune SEAL-H, SEAL-C, SEAL-L, and SEAL-D.
356   The tuning was performed using the AdamW optimizer without learning rate (lr) decay, and all
357   models were tuned for 1 epoch. For tuning in the line retrieval task, SEAL-C used a lr of 2e-2,
358   while SEAL-H used 1e-2 and 2e-2 for the 7B and 13B models, respectively. For the Needle-in-a-
359   Haystack task, learning rates of 4e-2 and 5e-2 were used. For SEAL-L and SEAL-D, LoRA and
360   DoRA modules with r = 4 were applied, respectively, to every linear layer in the attention module
361   (QKVO), with a lr of 2e-4. A single A100 80GB GPU was used for both tuning and evaluation.
362   Dataset generation: We used 50 generated samples for each task. Models supporting 32K context
363   window length were tuned with samples containing 31K input tokens, while models supporting 16K
364   context window length used 16K input tokens. For the 7B models, tuning with the 31K dataset took
365   about 40 minutes, and tuning with the 16K dataset took about 10 minutes.

463   7            SEAL WITH TRAINING - FREE CONTEXT LENGTH EXTENSION
464
465   In this work, we address one of the two major problems that can arise with lengthy inputs: the gradual
466   decline in performance within the context window. However, our approach can be used orthogonally
467   to methods that extend the context window length itself. In fact, the application of SEAL to models
468   like LongChat is an example where the Llama (Touvron et al. (2023)) model has already been
469   extended with context windows through RoPE scaling and fine-tuning. However, such tuning-based
470
      extensions come with significant costs in terms of time, data, and training infrastructure.
471   Recently, training-free context length extension methods (e.g. , NTK (bloc97 (2023a)), Self-Extend
472   (Jin et al. (2024))) have emerged and garnered considerable attention. However, it is important to
473   note that these methods generally exhibit lower performance compared to fine-tuning-based ap-
474   proaches (e.g. , PI (Chen et al. (2023)), YaRN (Peng et al. (2023))). If SEAL could be applied
475   orthogonally to these training-free context length extension methods, it would offer the attractive
476
      possibility of simultaneously leveraging the low-cost advantages of the SEAL and tuning-free ap-
      proach while restoring performance degradation through SEAL.
477
478   The results in Table 2 show that when extending the effective context length of Llama-2-7b-Chat
479   to over 16K using only NTK or Self-Extend, the retrieval performance at lengths greater than 8K
480   drops significantly. However, by utilizing SEAL in combination to adjust the attention influence, we
481   can dramatically improve performance beyond the original base model’s context window limitation
482
      (4K of Llama). Notably, NTK is completely unable to retrieve information at lengths above 12K, yet
      with the application of SEAL, it achieves performance comparable to that at shorter lengths.
483
484   Figure 6 presents the measured performance results for the Needle-in-a-Haystack task, further
485   demonstrating that SEAL significantly enhances the insufficient performance of the training-free
      context length extension methods. These results enable a practical approach to effectively increase

510   8      G ENERALIZATION ABILITY OF SEAL
511
512   The proposed SEAL method adopts a task-specific approach using formatted data for particular
513   downstream tasks, but it is fundamentally based on the theoretical premise of scaling attention com-
514   ponents to enhance retrieval capabilities. To evaluate whether SEAL can deliver general improve-
515   ments in retrieval performance for out-of-domain tasks, we measured the scores for the QA task
516   type in LongBench using the scaling values learned from the line retrieval task in Section 6.1. We
517   used the learned scaling values of the LongChat-7B model, which showed the largest performance
518
      improvement in line retrieval. We also provided results when LongBench was evaluated as an in-
      domain manner. Additionally, to ensure that SEAL’s retrieval-focused scaling does not degrade the
519
      inherent knowledge or reasoning abilities of the LLMs, we measured the MMLU (Hendrycks et al.
520
      (2020)) scores.
521
522   When using scale values tuned for the line retrieval task, the out-of-domain MMLU results are
523   42.53 / 42.34 / 42.17 for baseline, SEAL-H, and SEAL-C, respectively. The MMLU scores remain
524
      nearly unchanged, indicating that our method effectively identifies and scales only the attention
      heads relevant to long-context retrieval. Additionally, despite SEAL being applied task-specifically
525
      to line retrieval, which focuses on retrieving numbers, Table 3 shows that the scores in the out-of-
526
      domain LongBench metrics are maintained or even slightly improved. This demonstrates that the
527   retrieval performance gains achieved by SEAL contribute to tasks like document QA, confirming
528   the generalization capability of our approach.

533
      The ability to retrieve and extract information from long-length input is an important component of
      the LLMs. Through our analysis, we found that there are attention heads that have a good or bad
534
      impact on the retrieval scores. Based on this, we introduce SEAL, a cost-efficient attention strength
535
      scaling method to deliberately control the impact of each head. Despite using very few formatted
536   sample data and scaling parameters, SEAL maintains generalization performance and significantly
537   improves retrieval performance. We believe that our insights will promote the widespread adoption
538   of LLMs.
