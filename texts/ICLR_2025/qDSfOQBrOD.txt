011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Neural speech codecs (NSCs) enable high-quality real-time communication (RTC)
014            at low bit rates, making them efficient for bandwidth-constrained environments.
015            However, customizing or modifying the timbre of transmitted voices still relies on
016
               separate voice conversion (VC) systems, creating a gap in fully integrated systems
               that can simultaneously optimize efficient transmission and streaming VC with no
017
               additional latency. In this paper, we propose a high-efficiency VChangeCodec,
018
               which integrates the Voice Changer model directly into the speech Codec. This
019            design seamlessly switches between the original voice mode and customized voice
020            change mode in real-time. Specifically, leveraging the target speaker’s embedding,
021            we incorporate a lightweight causal projection network within the encoding module
022            of VChangeCodec to adapt timbre at the token level. These adapted tokens are
023            quantized and transmitted to the decoding module, to generate the converted speech
024            of the target speaker. The integrated framework achieves an ultra-low latency of
025            just 40 ms and requires fewer than 1 million parameters, making it ideal for RTC
026            scenarios such as online conferencing. Our comprehensive evaluations, including
027
               subjective listening tests and objective performance assessments, demonstrate that
               VChangeCodec excels in timbre adaptation capabilities compared to state-of-the-
028
               art (SOTA) VC models. We are confident that VChangeCodec provides an efficient
029
               and flexible framework for RTC systems, tailored to specific operator requirements.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Speech coding is an essential module in real-time communication (RTC) services. It aims to compress
035
      waveforms into representations at a lower bitrate at the sender side and decompress to reconstruct
036   the signal at the receiver side. Recent end-to-end (E2E) neural speech codecs (NSCs) achieve high-
037   quality communication through advanced compression, especially in bandwidth-constrained network
038   environments, enhancing the user experience in various RTC services such as online meetings and
039   voice calls. As mobile live streaming surges in popularity (Chen et al., 2024), users are increasingly
040   interested in modifying their timbre to match personal preferences. Some voice changers (i.e.,
041   Conan’s bow tie voice changer) have been applied as sound effects processing in live streaming.
042   The demand for customized voice changers has driven the exploration of voice conversion (VC)
043   technologies, which aims to modify speech style while maintaining linguistic content. Prior works
044   have explored various architectures including Transformers (Tanaka et al., 2019; Kameoka et al.,
045   2020), Auto-encoders (Qian et al., 2019), Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) (Kaneko et al.,
046   2019; Kaneko & Kameoka, 2018; Nguyen & Cardinaux, 2022),and diffusion models (Popov et al.,
047   2021; Liu et al., 2021a). Nevertheless, their non-streamable architecture and reliance on full utterance
048   inputs severely impede RTC applications.
049   Later, streaming VC with causal processing is proposed to address these challenges. Recent ap-
050   proaches (Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021b; Guo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Kovela et al., 2023)
051   adopt pre-trained feature extraction networks (e.g., HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) and WavLM (Chen
052   et al., 2022)) to obtain the speech content and use the phoneme-posteriorgram (PPG) (Chen et al.,
053   2023; Liu et al., 2021b; Kovela et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) methodology to reconstruct the speech
      waveform. Additionally, a prevalent strategy (Hayashi et al., 2022; Ning et al., 2024) incorporates

085   of the LPCNet pitch predictor, and the 40 ms delay of the speech codec, the delay will reach a
086   cumulative latency of 107.5 ms. This significantly exceeds the acceptable latency threshold for RTC
087   requirements. Due to the rapid development of NSCs, it is possible to add the feature of customized
088   voice changer into the codec, directly. An ideal NSC would compress speech into compact tokens
089   rich in all speech information. Specifically, in Figure1 (b), users can switch the voice change mode
090   at any time according to personal preferences at the sender side and generate new adapted timbre
091   tokens. The new token is fed to the decoding networks at the receiver side to reconstruct the altered

093
      from traditional cascaded VC-codec systems to an integrated solution that simultaneously achieves
      compression and timbre adaptation within a unified pipeline. In addition, we emphasize that our
094
      VChangeCodec is tailored for deployment through mobile network operators. In our system, VC is a
095
      built-in part of the voice communication module, with internal configurations managed by operators,
096   ensuring users cannot arbitrarily modify settings and thus minimizing privacy risks. Detailed usage
097   scenarios of VChangeCodec in operator networks are provided in Appendix A.1.

099
      Following the pipeline in Figure 1 (b), we propose VChangeCodec, a lightweight and low-latency
      speech codec that integrates the Voice Changer model into the Codec for an operator-oriented
100
      network, to mitigate the high complexity and audio artifacts in the recent neural codecs (Pons et al.,
101
      2021) such as SoundStream (Zeghidour et al., 2021) and EnCodec (Défossez et al., 2022). Specifically,
102   we use scalar quantization (SQ) to replace residual vector quantization (RVQ) in VChangeCodec.
103   For the voice changer, we propose a lightweight causal projection network in the encoder to perform
104   timbre adaptation on tokens extracted by the pre-trained codec. Then, these adapted tokens are
105   dequantized and decoded to realize a customized voice changer in the decoder. The target timbre
106   customization is achieved using near-parallel training data generated through open-source voice
107   conversion toolkits. We introduce a new commitment loss between the target token and the predicted
      token. Besides, we keep the generator-discriminator training strategy used in the pre-trained codec.

133
      To our knowledge, neural speech compression has not yet successfully been combined with VC
      tasks. The earliest work (Strecha et al., 2005) achieves VC directly by re-using the feature of speech
134
      codec based on the code excited linear predictive (CELP) (Bessette et al., 2002) to warp the spectral
135
      envelope. The StreamVoice in (Wang et al., 2024) employs a low latency streaming codec Audiodec
136   (Wu et al., 2023) as a speaker prompt for the causal context-aware language model. The overall
137   pipeline latency is 124.3 ms on an A100 GPU. StreamVC (Yang et al., 2024b) utilizes SoundStream
138   Zeghidour et al. (2021) and a pre-trained HuBERT Hsu et al. (2021) model to generate pseudo-labels,
139   enabling real-time processing even on mobile devices, and modifies the communication protocol for
140   both transmission and reception by incorporating the target speaker’s embedding. However, there is
141   still a lack of integrated solutions that can achieve efficient transmission and real-time VC without
142   additional latency. Our proposed method has the following key differences: 1) We aim to ensure
143   seamless switching between the original voice mode and voice change mode in the RTC services.
144
      2) We design VChangeCodec in such a way that compression and voice changer can be carried out
      jointly by the same codec. 3) We insert a lightweight causal projection network between the encoder
145
      and decoder, allowing us to achieve the conversion of the target speaker’s timbre with low latency.

187
      convolutional neural network (CNN) to process the input signal x and distill it into a low-dimensional
188   latent feature z. The encoder network consists of a one-dimensional (1D) convolutional layer, a
189   preprocessing layer, and multiple downsampling blocks based on a serial of the dilated CNN and
190   residual unit, and a 1D convolution layer with tanh() activation function to convert into the latent
191   feature z ∈ VN , where V is an N -dimensional space (N = 84). For each frame of 320 samples,
192   the initial 1D convolutional layer extracts intrinsic features from the input signal, yielding an M -
193   channel (M=16) feature. The subsequent preprocessing layer, which includes a causal convolution
194   followed by a ReLU activation and average pooling with a downsampling factor of 2, maintains the
195   M -channel output. Then, four consecutive downsampling blocks continue the information extraction,
196
      and the number of output channels of each downsampling block is 2x of the previous downsampling
      block. Each downsampling block is composed of four dilated residual units with a dilation rate
197
      d = {1, 3, 5, 7}, and an average pooling by a pre-defined downsampling factor rd = {2, 4, 4, 5}. The
198
      output feature after four downsampling blocks is 256 × 1 with 320x compression.
199
200   Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ) in neural codecs can lead to substantial codebooks, thereby
201   increasing the storage demands of RTC services. Inspired by previous work (Mentzer et al., 2023;
202
      Yang et al., 2024a), to mitigate high complexity, we introduce a scalar quantization (SQ) to each
      dimension of z between the encoder network and the decoder network. We claim that our approach is
203
      distinct, tailoring the codebook size and implementation to optimize speech codec performance. The
204
      SQ discretes the original value with a certain codebook uniformly distributed in [−1.0, 1.0]. We set
205   the R to adjust the range of z, which can help adapt to the target bitrate. The value of R is 2 in our
206   study. We obtain the value of quantization ẑ, which is calculated as follows:

222   3.2      C USTOMIZED VOICE CHANGER
223
224   Problem Formulation. First, a speaker identity U is a random variable drawn from the speaker
225   population pU (·). Then, an acoustic vector Z = Z(1 : T ) is a random process drawn from the joint
226
      acoustic distribution pZ (·|U ). In this paper, Z is drawn from the quantized feature token ẑ. Here
      acoustic refers to the phonetic, prosodic, content and timbre information and etc. Finally, given the
227
      speaker identity and acoustic, the speech segment X = X(1 : T ) is a random process randomly
228
      sampled from the speech distribution, i.e. pX (·|Z(U )), which characterizes the distribution of the
229   speaker U ’s speech uttering the acoustic Z. In this paper, we will be working on speech waveform.
230
231   Our goal is to design a voice converter that produces the conversion output, X̂17→2 , which preserves
232   the acoustic in X1 except timbre information, but matches the speaker characteristics of speaker U2 .
233   Formally, an ideal speech converter should have the following desirable property:
234                           pX̂17→2 (·|Z(U2 ) = ẑ1 (u2 )) = pX (·|Z(U ) = ẑ1 (u2 ))                 (2)
235   Eq. (2) means that we replace the speaker information U1 = u1 in the source speech Z1 = z1 with
236   the target speaker’s identity U2 = u2 , the converted speech should sound like u2 uttering z1 .
237
238   Metadata from opensmile. We follow an open-source implementation 2 to acquire attributes of the
239   target speaker, namely metadata of the target speaker. We extract 88-dimensional acoustic features
240   with openSMILE (Eyben et al., 2010) including f0 , loudness, f1 − f3 frequency, Mel-frequency
241   cepstral coefficient (MFCC), and etc. Specifically, we use the pre-defined feature set of eGeMAPSv02.
242   We anticipate that these acoustic features can represent speaker identity (timbre information) and
243   capture the subtle emotional variations in speech. We do not use pre-trained speaker embeddings such
244   as (Wan et al., 2018), due to concerns about computational costs and additional training overhead.
245
      This approach minimizes training burdens, enabling us to concentrate our efforts on VChangeCodec.
246
      Causal projection network (Converter). We design a lightweight projection network to achieve
247
      timbre adaptation of tokens, which can be conceptualized as a process of “coloring” the source
248
      speaker’s tokens to resemble those of the target speaker. We utilize the encoder described in section
249   3.1 to extract discrete tokens, while the decoder is employed for speech generation. The parameters
250   of both encoder and decoder are frozen, meaning we directly load the parameters from the pre-trained
251   codec of the original voice mode. Our subsequent intuitive justification in 5 demonstrates that no
252   further training is necessary to achieve high-quality timbre adaptation of tokens.
253
      As mentioned in Figure 1, we construct the projection network (namely Converter) of voice changer
254
      using causal convolutions to enable streaming inference, restricting each output frame to only depend
255   on current and past input frames. Compared to standard convolution, causal convolution shifts
256   padding to precede rather than trail inputs along the time dimension. Specifically, the Converter
257   is composed of three grouped residual units with dilated convolutional layers in Figure 2. We
258   concatenate the metadata of target speaker u2 and quantized tokens ẑ1 as input to Converter. So
259   the input channels of the first grouped residual unit is N + 88, and the three grouped residual units
260   with dilation rate d = {1, 3, 9}. The kernel sizes of all convolutional layers are 3, and the number of
261   channels is 128, 256, 128 successively in the converter. We also use the SQ on the adapted tokens
262   obtained from the three grouped residual units. Finally, the quantized tokens ẑ2 = ẑ1 (u2 ) are mapped
263
      to N -channel to input the decoder of VChangeCodec for target speech generation. In particular,
      our converter network introduces no additional latency and is well compatible with the encoding
264
      module and decoding module. Our converter is a plug-and-play module that can be combined with
265
      any end-to-end encoder-quantizer-decoder codec.
266

268   a single CPU core of a smartphone iPhone X takes 2 ms for each 20 ms chunk of speech. It is tested

276   a combination of reconstruction loss Lsp , adversarial (GAN) loss Ladv , feature match loss Lf m ,
277   perceptual loss Lpe . The detailed description is provided in Appendix A.4. For Converter network, we
278   continue to use the multiple loss components mentioned above, with the ground truth being replaced
279   by the target speech. Moreover, we introduce a token commitment loss for timbre adaptation.

282   between the ground-truth token of target speech at the encoder, and the predicted token from source

299   Data sources. For the speech codec VChangeCodec, the training set is divided into two classes.
300   The clean speech is from LibriTTS (Zen et al., 2019), DNS Challenge (Reddy et al., 2020). The mixed
301   speech is generated by combining clean speech and background interference (e.g., noise), including
302   DNS Challenge, MIR-1K (Hsu & Jang, 2009) and FMA (Defferrard et al., 2016). In addition, the
303   training set includes English and Mandarin utterances, all utterances are sampled at 16 kHz. There
304
      are 68 independent test utterances including English and Mandarin selected for objective quality
      measurement. We present additional details of datasets used for training in A.5. For the voice changer,
305
      we use VCTK (Veaux et al., 2016) and AISHELL-3 (Shi et al., 2020) as the source utterances, they
306
      are internally expressive multi-speaker English and Mandarin corpus. All speech utterances are at
307   a sampling rate of 16 kHz. We select one male and one female speaker from the internal datasets
308   which contain 1-hour data, respectively, to serve as the target timbre. Then we utilize the open source
309   Retrieval-based-Voice-Conversion (RVC) project 3 to construct approximately parallel data. This
310   project yields satisfactory subjective test results for VC. For the test datasets, we select 42 unseen
311   utterances (15 English corpus, 15 Mandarin corpus, 12 internal corpus) from 42 different speakers.
312
313   Metrics of original voice mode. For evaluating the speech codec, we perform evaluations along
314   four axes: POLQA, ViSQOL, STOI and DCRMOS. POLQA (ITU-T, 2011) is selected as the
315   primary objective evaluation metric, which predicts the Mean Opinion Score (MOS) by comparing
316   the spectrum of the reference and degraded signals. The predicted MOS score ranges from 1.0 to
317
      4.75, and the average MOS of each system is calculated for evaluation. ViSQOL (Chinen et al.,
      2020) is an intrusive perceptual quality metric that uses spectral similarity to the ground truth to
318
      estimate a MOS. STOI (Taal et al., 2011) shows a high correlation with the intelligibility of noisy and
319
      time-frequency weighted noisy speech. Moreover, we organize a subjective listening test by referring
320   to the ITU-T P.800 recommendation (ITU-T, 1996), and the quality evaluation is performed by using
321   the Degradation Category Rating (DCR) method. We select eight Chinese speech utterances and
322   invite 24 native listeners to participate in the listening test.

      Metrics of voice changer mode. For evaluating the voice changer, the evaluations are performed
325   along four axes: naturalness, intelligibility, Mel Cepstral Distortion (MCD) and speaker sim-
326   ilarity. Naturalness is rated by DNSMOS (Reddy et al., 2021) which consists of three scores for

330   the Mel-cepstral coefficients of the converted and reference audios. We follow (Guo et al., 2023) by

      5    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We present a novel speech codec framework, VChangeCodec, which seamlessly integrates customized
533
      voice changer capabilities directly into its architecture. This integration facilitates real-time switching
534   between the original voice mode and the customized voice change mode. Our approach combines
535   scalar quantization techniques with timbre adaptation using a lightweight causal projection network at
536   the token level. Both subjective and objective evaluations against existing speech codecs demonstrate
537   the superiority of our pre-trained codec model, establishing a promising foundation for voice changers.
538   Extensive experiments validate the advantages of our model over state-of-the-art voice conversion
539   methods, achieving ultra-low latency of 40 ms for real-time voice conversion. We aim to establish an
      innovative methodology for voice changers within the real-time communication ecosystem.

      6   E THICAL S TATEMENT.
541
542
      Our codec is primarily designed for deployment through an operator’s network, rather than for peer-to-
543   peer communication scenarios. Specifically, our lightweight VChangeCodec is embedded within real-
544   time communication systems, where the encoder and decoder are immutable and maintained by the
545   operators. The embedded speaker representation is injected at the sender side and is also maintained
546   by the operators, with the designated timbres being pre-defined and inaccessible to ordinary users.
547   Our method can restrict to a limited target voice, preventing its misuse for impersonating specific
548   targets outside this pre-defined range. We recommend that operators display a notification label on
549   the screen during calls and meetings, such as “Current content is generated by AI!”.

771   A.1    U SAGE SCENARIOS OF VC HANGE C ODEC
772
      Specifically, our lightweight VChangeCodec is embedded within RTC systems, where the encoder
773
      and decoder are immutable and maintained by the operators. This design not only avoids protocol
774
      compatibility issues in RTC systems but also minimizes the impact on online services by updating
775   the encoding part only. The embedded speaker representation is injected at the sending end and
776   is also maintained by the operators, with the designated timbres being pre-defined and inaccessible
777   to ordinary users. In contrast, previous voice conversion models, if positioned on the user side,
778   would allow users to arbitrarily modify the pre-defined timbres before they pass through the sender’s
779   encoder. The converted timbre, then transmitted through the operator’s codec, could raise issues of
780   timbre infringement.
781   We have carefully considered privacy issues for applications in RTC services. Our VC module is
782   integrated into the speech communication system, prohibiting users from accessing or altering internal
783   configurations. The embedded speaker representation is injected at the sending end and maintained
784   by the operators, with designated timbres being pre-defined and inaccessible to ordinary users. A
785   potential use case is expected the user can only download the related binary file after subscribing to a
786   specific target timbre from the operator. Consequently, the user cannot arbitrarily change the source
787   voice to any target timbre to minimize the deepfake risk to operators.
788   Zero-shot VC operates on a ”one model for all timbres” principle and is not optimized for low latency
789   or low complexity on mobile devices. In contrast, our method adheres to a ”one model for one
790   timbre” approach, utilizing a lightweight model tailored for deployment in mid-range performance
791   smartphones. Therefore, our approach is a completely new approach targeting the RTC scenario with
792   distinctive technical requirements and design.
793   Conclusively, the proposal in this paper aims to enhance the experience in real-time communication,
794   including conversational chatting. Therefore, the extensibility of the target speaker should be properly
795   considered due to privacy concerns. This consideration is a cornerstone of our contribution, and to the
796   best of our knowledge, this represents the first disclosure of a paper addressing this specific aspect.

      A.5     E XPERIMENTAL DETAILS
865
866   Datasets details. Regarding the training set in original voice mode, we use the DNS challenge 2020
867   dataset and the LibriTTS dataset as the speech part of the training set. Recognizing that neural speech
868   coding is inherently data-driven, we incorporated mixed speech segments, to enhance the robustness
869   of our scheme. Extra mixed speech utterances (mixed with noise or music) are also included, in
870   which the noise clips are from the DNS challenge, and music clips are from MIR-1k and FMA. This
871   configuration is designed for actual RTC scenarios, including pure voice communications, or voice
872
      with background interference (e.g. office noise, background music playback, etc). We randomly
      selected noise crops and adjusted the mixing gain of the noise component using SNR, and the target
873
      SNR is 15 dB. SN R = 10log10(S 2 /(kN )2 ), S denotes clean signal energy and N is noisy signal
874
      energy. For all training data, we randomly sample 98% of the dataset for train, 1% for valid and 1%
875   for test. Finally, the unseen test set is strictly an out-of-domain dataset, ensuring it was not exposed
876   to any model during training.
877
878   Details of neural codecs. For RTC service, 16kHz is the standard sampling rate, as the speech is the
879   primary component in-service. Higher sampling rates are typically for audio (including music), but
880   we focus on speech, operating at a sampling rate of 16kHz. For fair comparison in our experiments,
881   systems (DAC, Encodec, Lyra2) with higher sampling rates were downsampled to 16kHz to ensure
882   consistent evaluation conditions. Specifically, we used the official Encodec versions for 12 kbps
883   (nq = 16) and 24 kbps (nq = 32), with the model’s sampling rate at 24 kHz. We downsampled
884
      the original test audio from 48kHz to 24 kHz for input into the encoder model and downsampled
      the output speech to 16 kHz to compare the quality at the same sampling rate. Similarly, for DAC,
885
      we used the official configured at 16 kHz, the default 8 kbps model for inference. For Lyra2, we
886
      conducted evaluations using the official 16 kHz, default 6 kbps and 9.2 kbps model for inference.

954   A.8   R ELATED W ORK
955
956   Neural speech compression models. The VQ-VAEs (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) is a dominant
957   paradigm to train NSCs (Gârbacea et al., 2019), which adopts a convolutional encoder and an
958   autoregressive wavenet (Van Den Oord et al., 2016) decoder. SoundStream (Zeghidour et al., 2021)
959   incorporates the encoder-decoder network and residual vector quantizer (RVQ), combining adversarial
960   and reconstruction losses to achieve excellent generation quality and supporting streamable inference
961
      on a smartphone CPU. Encodec (Défossez et al., 2022) uses a multiscale STFT-based (MS-STFT)
      discriminator to reduce artifacts and produced high-quality samples. They introduce a loss balancer
962
      to stabilize training based on the varying scale of gradients coming from the discriminator. Descript-
963
      audio-codec (Kumar et al., 2024) can achieve 90x compression with minimal loss in quality and fewer
964   artifacts by improved RVQGAN. However, existing neural speech coding Zhang et al. (2024); Du et al.
965   (2023) models rely on higher parameter quantities to train neural networks to ensure speech quality.
966   Our VChangeCodec adopts scalar quantization instead of RVQ, which enables lighter streaming
967   inference and maintains high fidelity at lower bitrates.
968
969   Streaming voice conversion (VC). Diff-VC (Popov et al., 2021) presents a scalable high-quality
970   method based on diffusion probabilistic modeling and considers real-time applications by developing
971   a faster forward Stochastic Differential Equations solver. VQMIVC (Wang et al., 2021) employs
      vector quantization (VQ) for content encoding and introduces mutual information (MI) as correlation


                                                         18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       metrics to achieve disentanglement of content, speaker and pitch representations. QuickVC (Guo
973    et al., 2023) proposes a lightweight VC model based on faster VITS (Kim et al., 2021) and uses
974    HuBERT-soft model to extract content information features. Recent solution (Chen et al., 2023)
975    tackles the streaming VC problem, but the inference latency of the entire streaming VC pipeline is
976    270 ms on a desktop CPU. However, all these solutions require long latency, making it difficult to
977    apply them in RTC scenarios. Our voice changer based on VChangeCodec can be implemented on a
978    smartphone with a low inference latency of 40+ ms.
