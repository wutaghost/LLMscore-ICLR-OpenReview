                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
               The unsustainable rise in energy cost from increasingly capable deep learning
               systems spurs computer architecture innovation beyond conventional deep learn-
015
               ing accelerators such as GPUs. However, a novel computer architecture presents
016
               a problem: much of deep learning research has been optimized for conventional
017            computer architectures, and the extent to which modern deep learning models can
018            unlock improved efficiency on a novel computer architecture is not well under-
019            stood. In this work, we demonstrate for the first time that a State Space Model
020            (SSM) can achieve substantial efficiency improvement when mapped to Loihi 2, a
021            state-of-the-art neuromorphic research chip, versus a Jetson Orin Nano GPU (Jet-
022            son). Specifically, we benchmark our SSM on sMNIST, psMNIST, and sCIFAR
023            online token-by-token inference and find approximately 1000x increased energy
024            efficiency and 75x improved latency and throughput on Loihi 2 with a decrease
025
               in accuracy of less than one to three percentage points compared to the full pre-
               cision implementation on Jetson. We comprehensively tailor our implementation
026
               to Loihi-specific features and constraints, such as the co-location of memory and
027
               compute as well as fixed precision arithmetic. Our results elucidate how SSMs
028            meaningfully bridge conventional and neuromorphic hardware via their dual na-
029            ture: SSMs can operate in an offline mode using convolution or scan, which is
030            efficient on a GPU, or in an online mode as a recurrent network, which we show is
031            efficient on Loihi 2. This work provides a foundation for performant sequence
032            models on neuromorphic hardware, potentially unlocking substantial improve-
033            ments in latency-sensitive or energy-limited online inference applications, such
034            as speech enhancement or vision for robotic control.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Deep learning systems exhibit improved representational power and AI capabilities as their compu-
      tational cost increases, and their commensurate rising energy use has been driving unprecedented
040
      innovation in computer architecture. The growth of compute (FLOPs) and memory (bandwidth)
041
      requirements of data-center-scale deep learning systems such as Large Language Models (LLMs)
042   vastly outpaces the compute and memory delivered by year-over-year improvements in GPUs, the
043   standard workhorse computer architecture of deep learning (Gholami et al., 2024). Similarly, at the
044   edge, the proliferation of intelligent Internet of Things (IoT) devices pushes demand for increasingly
045   capable deep learning systems under power, latency, privacy, and connectivity constraints (Mao
046   et al., 2024; Meuser et al., 2024). To deliver deep learning training and inference efficiency im-
047   provements beyond what GPU architectures can offer, in recent years we have seen a “Cambrian ex-
048   plosion” of new computer architectures (Sukumar et al., 2021), such as the TPU (Jouppi et al., 2017),
049   the Cerebras WSE-2 (Lie, 2024), neuromorphic chips such as Loihi 2 (Labs, 2021) or DYNAP-SE2
050
      (Richter et al., 2024), and even analog AI chips, (e.g., Ambrogio et al., 2023), to name a few.
051   This Cambrian explosion, however, faces a problem known as the hardware lottery: novel com-
052   puter architectures struggle to take hold because years of deep learning research has targeted
053   GPUs (Hooker, 2021). The continual investment in GPU-focused algorithms has certainly created
      incredible GPU-based deep learning systems such as ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023). Concurrently,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      however, deep learning research has become locked-in to GPU implementation at some level, as the
055   most successful algorithmic innovations have compounded around the GPU architecture. The extent
056   to which today’s most impactful deep learning technologies can be transferred to novel computer ar-
057   chitectures for improved efficiency remains unclear.
058
      In this paper, we show a positive example of broad relevance for how one can substantively improve
059
      a modern deep learning system’s efficiency on a highly-differentiated novel computer architecture.
060
      In particular, we map a State Space Model (SSM) to Loihi 2, a state-of-the-art neuromorphic re-
061   search chip (Labs, 2021). SSMs are efficient sequence models that rival transformers (Gu et al.,
062   2021b). Importantly, SSMs can perform inference in a convolution or scan mode which is efficient
063   on GPUs, and in a recurrent online token-by-token processing mode. The recurrent formulation of
064   SSMs with their local stateful computation aligns well with the architecture of neuromorphic pro-
065   cessors, in which compute and memory are co-located (Davies et al., 2021). This is in contrast to
066   GPUs, where the separation of compute and memory tends to provide efficiency only for batched,
067   predictable, or highly structured computations and memory accesses, such as convolutions (Kumar,
068   2023). We show that this online token-by-token recurrent mode is in fact extremely efficient on
069
      the Loihi 2 architecture. Importantly, online token-by-token inference is highly salient for a wide
      variety of latency-sensitive or energy-constraint applications such as robotics, autonomous vehicles,
070
      and speech enhancement.
071
072   Our main contributions are as follows:
073
074         1. We demonstrate for the first time an SSM that runs on neuromorphic hardware.
075         2. We present our Post Training Quantization (PTQ) and Quantization Aware Fine Tuning
076            (QAFT) techniques underpinning the successful mapping of our SSM to Loihi 2.
077
            3. We benchmark our SSM’s online token-by-token sMNIST, psMNIST, and sCIFAR infer-
078
               ence on Loihi 2 versus a recurrent SSM baseline on an edge GPU, Jetson Orin Nano, and
079            we find approximately 1000x improved energy efficiency, 75x decreased latency, and 75x
080            increased throughput, with only a modest decrease in classification accuracy.
081
            4. We also benchmark our SSM’s offline sample-by-sample sMNIST, psMNIST, and sCI-
082
               FAR inference on Loihi 2 versus a convolutional SSM baseline on Jetson Orin Nano, and
083
               we find Jetson Orin Nano to be advantageous in this context, especially when using batch-
084            ing. This result helps elucidate a more comprehensive account of the differing scenarios
085            for which neuromorphic versus GPU architectures are preferable.

090   2.1   P RELIMINARIES ON N EUROMORPHIC C OMPUTING AND L OIHI 2
091
092   Neuromorphic computing draws inspiration from the brain’s highly efficient approach to information
093
      processing. Despite operating at around 20 watts of power, the brain executes complex tasks that
      include perception, decision-making, coordination, and learning—all in real-time. Neuromorphic
094
      computers aim to emulate the brain’s incredible efficiency by incorporating the pertinent computa-
095
      tional paradigms of the brain’s architecture: highly parallel processing, event-driven computation,
096   memory-compute co-location, inherent scalability, and stochasticity (for a review see Schuman
097   et al., 2022). The highly parallel processing and memory-compute co-location help address the
098   aforementioned growing compute and memory interface shortcomings of GPU architectures (Gho-
099   lami et al., 2024). Furthermore, event-driven computation promotes energy efficiency, as computa-
100   tions and communications are only performed when necessary.
101
      The digital neuromorphic processor Loihi 2 (Labs, 2021) realizes the principles of neuromorphic
102   computing throughout its architecture. Loihi 2 is comprised of computational units, called neuro
103   cores, that contain programmable neurons which communicate by sending spiking events through
104   a Network-on-Chip mesh. These spiking events are small message packets, which carry either a
105   binary or integer payload; spiking events with integer payload are referred to as graded spikes. With
106   co-located memory, the neuro cores enable various types of synaptic connectivity, including linear
107   projections and convolutions, as well as more flexible patterns like (pseudo) stochastic or factorized
      connections. Importantly, Loihi 2 allows users to define custom stateful neurons in the neuro cores

      with the necessary infrastructure for low-latency interchip and external interface event-based spike

148
      including model predictive control (Mangalore et al., 2024), solving QUBO problems (Pierro et al.,
149
      2024), monocular depth estimation (Chiavazza et al., 2023), and efficient video and audio processing
150   (Shrestha et al., 2024).
151
152   Furthermore, in contrast to most computer chips which use a synchronous clock, Loihi 2 is an asyn-
153
      chronous system, which affords energy efficiency and a flexible latency-throughput trade-off. A
      system of Loihi 2 chips, no matter whether it is single or thousand chips, operates asynchronously
154
      performing only the necessary computations as quickly as possible and synchronizing the advance
155
      of time-step via a barrier-synchronization mechanism. When considering a deep neural network, the
156   asynchronous nature of Loihi 2 also allows us to seek a sweet spot in latency-throughput trade-off
157   as depicted in Figure 2: one can execute workloads on Loihi 2 scheduling inputs as fast as possible
158   to maximize throughput in a pipelined manner, where all of the layers of the network are active in
159   every time-step or allow the current input to propagate through all the layers in the network before
160   injecting the next input in a fall-through fashion so that each layer spends a minimum amount of
161   time necessary, thus minimizing the overall latency of the inference per input. Importantly, the dis-
      tinction between pipelined execution and fall-through execution is not binary but can be considered

168   2.2    D EEP S TATE -S PACE M ODELS
169
170
      Recently, a family of linear recurrent architectures, deep SSMs, has emerged. Deep SSMs, such as
171   S4, S4D, Liquid-S4, S5 and Mamba (Gu et al., 2021a; Smith et al., 2022; Hasani et al., 2022; Gu
172   et al., 2022; Gu & Dao, 2023), are based on the memory property of state-space dynamics (Gu et al.,
173   2020). Their task performance (e.g., classification accuracy, perplexity) can surpass or compete
174   with transformers, especially for long sequence tasks (Tay et al., 2020). Yet, remarkably, SSMs
175   do not suffer from the quadratic scaling of compute cost of the attention mechanism with context
176   length (Vaswani et al., 2017). Instead, they offer linearly increasing computational costs due to their
177   recurrent formulation. While recurrent neural networks are generally hard to train, SSMs further
178   offer the advantage that they can be implemented as a convolution or as a parallel scan, allowing for
179
      easy training on GPUs (Gu et al., 2021a; Smith et al., 2022).
180   To gain deeper insight on the pertinent internal workings of SSMs, let us examine the original SSM,
181   S4 (Gu et al., 2021a), which captures the essence of the family of subsequent SSM architectures. S4
182   models can perform their computations using one of three representations, which can be transformed
183   into each other and serve different functional purposes:

206   In addition, there have been recent efforts to make sequence modeling architectures compatible with
207
      neuromorphic hardware. These efforts include SpikeGPT (Zhu et al., 2023), Spiking SSMs (Shen

209
      tion sparsity, which may increase energy efficiency on neuromorphic hardware. However, impor-
210   tantly, these works rely on biologically-inspired Leaky Integrate-and-Fire neurons and binary spikes.
211   This prioritization of biological plausibility can leave underutilized the full gambit of capabilities in
212   modern neuromorphic processors like Loihi 2, such as customizable microcode neurons and graded
213   spikes. Additionally, none of these neuromorphic-compatible SSM efforts include implementations
214   on neuromorphic hardware. The lack of any benchmarked SSMs on actual neuromorphic hardware
215   leaves unknown the efficiency of SSMs on neuromorphic hardware in practice, which we evaluate
      in this work.

232   3.1     M ODEL ARCHITECTURE ON L OIHI 2
233
234   Figure 3 shows the SSM model architecture, neuromorphic-S4D (n-S4D), that we implemented on
235
      the Loihi 2 neuromorphic processor for sequence classification. n-S4D is inspired by the architecture
      used by (Gu et al., 2022) with hardware-aware modifications. Our n-S4D network consists of an
236
      encoder layer that expands the input to a higher dimensionality, four S4D blocks, and a decoder
237
      layer that reduces the dimensionality to the number of output classes. At the top of Figure 3, the
238   dimensionality of each layer of the model is listed, where I represents the input dimensionality,
239   H is the model dimension, and N is the number of hidden states per model dimension; the output
240   dimensionality (number of classes) is 10.
241
      Each S4D block starts with the S4D dynamics implemented as a recurrent network (implementation
242
      described in section 3.2), followed by a ReLU activation. After each S4D layer, the dimensions
243
      are mixed using a linear projection followed by another ReLU activation. In contrast to the network
244   architecture used to evaluate the original S4D model (Gu et al., 2022), to increase activation sparsity
245   (proportion of zero-valued neural outputs), we only use ReLU activations instead of GLUs and
246   GeLUs. Importantly, when a ReLU neuron outputs a zero, no spike message is sent on Loihi 2; this
247   saves energy thanks to the event-driven nature of Loihi 2. To further simplify the model, we also
248   leave out normalization layers and residual connections.
249
      All S4D layers, ReLU activations, and biases (depicted in dark blue in Figure 3), are implemented
250   as programmable neurons on Loihi 2. All linear projections (depicted in light blue in Figure 3)
251   are implemented in Loihi 2 using linear synapses on neuro cores (weight matrices), including up-
252   projection, expansion, reduction, mixing, and down-projection.
253
      We evaluate two model sizes with 67k parameters (H = 64, N = 32) and 265k parameters (H =
254
      128, N = 64) for different datasets (see section 4 for details). We optimize how each layer is
255
      distributed across neuro cores to achieve uniform compute load; this ensures that no single layer
256   dominates compute time during any given timestep. We place subsequent layers onto neighboring
257   neuro cores to reduce spike Network-on-Chip mesh traffic. These configurations lead to a usage of
258   31 and 111 neuro cores of a single Loihi 2 chip for the small and large model, respectively.

300   3.3   P OST T RAINING Q UANTIZATION AND Q UANTIZATION AWARE F INE T UNING
301
      As all computations on Loihi 2 are performed in fixed precision, we describe in this section how we
302
      quantize our models for training and inference.
303
304   All models are pre-trained in full precision using the convolutional view of n-S4D. After pre-
305   training, we switch to the recurrent mode and quantize our models for inference on Loihi 2 using
306
      Post Training Quantization (PTQ) leading to an expected drop of accuracy. To recover the accuracy
      of the quantized model, we re-train the models using Quantization Aware Fine Tuning (QAFT) in
307
      recurrent mode with Loihi 2-specific bit-widths and precisions for one epoch.
308
309   All activations are quantized using a bit-width of 24 bits, with 6 to 8 bits being allocated to the
310   fractional part (precision) and the remaining bits for the integer part. While the A, B, and C
311   matrices of the S4D layers are quantized with a fixed bit-width of 16 bits using a precision of 13
312   bits, the parameters (weights and biases) of the feed-forward layers such as the encoder, decoder,
313
      expansion, reduction and mixing layers are quantized using 8 bits with dynamic precision. In the
      case of dynamic precision, we calculate scaling factors based on the maximum absolute value of the
314
      relevant tensor to use the full dynamic range.
315
316   All tensors X are kept in full precision while simulating the effects of quantization (X̂) during the
317   forward path:
318                                               X̂ = ⌊Xs⌋d, ˆ                                         (5)
319   where s scales the tensor to the desired precision of b bits and the floor operator ⌊·⌋ denotes the
320   truncation of the fractional part. The scaling factor can either be calculated by s = 2b in the case
321   of a fixed precision or s = 2b /|X|max in the case of dynamic scaling. To be fully accurate to
322   Loihi 2’s fixed precision arithmetic, we also quantize the descaling factor d = 1/s with a fixed
323   precision of 16 bits. To allow gradient flow in the backward computation, we use a straight-through
      estimator (Bengio et al., 2013).

407   4.2          C OMPUTATIONAL COST
408
409
      Since our study focuses on a small SSM model appropriate for low-latency and low-power edge
      processing, we compare our Loihi 2 n-S4D to a recurrent as well as a convolutional implementation
410
      of n-S4D on an edge GPU, Nvidia Jetson Orin Nano. While we tried optimizing n-S4D on Jetson
411
      Orin Nano, we were unable to create a stronger baseline due to lack of native support for complex
412   numbers in TensorRT (Jeong et al., 2022) for the convolutional implementation (which can not be
413   efficiently implemented using just using just real numbers) and excessively long compilation times
414   for the recurrent model (which can easily be implemented using just real numbers). Consequently,
415   we conducted our assessments using a PyTorch model that had been compiled just-in-time, operating
416   at fp32 precision.
417
      We use the jtop API to characterize power. For runtime, only the time spent to input the data and
418   compute the output is considered. For these measurements on Loihi 2, we store a sequence of
419   input values on a neuro core and inject them to the n-S4D network at peak throughput without IO
420   constraints to obtain stable power measurements; this is repeated for 10 representative samples. The
421   energy of the neuro core used to store input values is included in the results for Loihi 2, while the
422   IO power of Jetson is excluded.
423
      The primary point of comparison for the Loihi 2 versus the Jetson implementation is the stream-
424   ing mode (with a batch size of one) of inference. In we also include the peak-performing batched
425   mode of inference for a representative optimum performance on Jetson. Table 2 reports computa-
426   tional cost of inference on Loihi 2 and Jetson on the three datasets. In addition to energy, latency,
427   and throughput, the energy-delay product (EDP, Shrestha et al., 2024) is reported to more readily
428   compare systems running at different speeds.
429
430   Sample-by-sample processing The right side of Table 2 shows results from sample-by-sample
431   processing, which assumes that all tokens of a sample are available to the system at the beginning of
      processing and only a single classification is required for the whole sample. Recurrent formulations

432
      of the model have to process each token sequentially, while convolutional formulations can process
433   the entire sample with a single convolution.
434
435   In the online processing regime with a batch size of one, it is evident that Loihi 2 is very efficient
436
      compared to the recurrent mode on Jetson and shows better energy per sample of 1.8 mJ compared to
      23 mJ for Jetson in the convolutional mode that is favorable for GPU architectures. The throughput
437
      and latency on Loihi 2 and Jetson in convolutional mode are also competitive. Jetson, however,
438
      achieves peak performance in higher batch mode with substantially reduced energy per sample of
439   0.22 mJ and 0.96 mJ for sMNIST and sCIFAR along with orders of magnitude higher throughput,
440   which is expected for GPU architectures.
441
442   Token-by-token processing The middle columns of Table 2 show results of token-by-token pro-
443   cessing. This assumes streaming input that requires a classification for every token. For these types
444   of tasks, both Loihi 2 and the recurrent mode on Jetson process all tokens sequentially. The convo-
445   lution mode on Jetson, however, has to perform a convolution over the entire sequence with every
446   new token. Its latency, energy, throughput, and EDP are therefore the same for processing one to-
447   ken in token-by-token processing as they are for processing one sample in the sample-by-sample
448   processing mode when using the convolutional implementation.
449   For token-by-token processing, Loihi 2 outperforms Jetson in all metrics on all datasets. Latency and
450   energy are two to three orders of magnitude lower for Loihi 2. This is reflected in EDP: For MNIST
451   workloads, EDP for Loihi 2 is 0.0002 µJ s and 0.001 µJ s for sCIFAR. In contrast, the recurrent
452   processing on Jetson incurs EDP of 70 µJ s and 80 µJ s on the respective datasets, demonstrating the
453   efficiency of Loihi 2 in token-by-token processing.
454
455   Fall-through vs. pipelined processing Table 2 shows the tradeoff between latency and throughput
456
      when executing the model on Loihi 2 in fall-through or pipelined processing (see section 2.1). With
      fall-through processing, we see a latency of 68 µs per token on the sMNIST dataset, compared
457
      to 168 µs in pipelined processing. This comes at the cost of throughput of only 14.705 token/s,
458
      compared to 83.343 token/s in pipelined processing. The lower throughput per token in fall-through
459   mode results in a higher latency per sample of 53.314 ms compared to the pipelined mode with
460   9.57 ms. Results on the other datasets highlight the same tradeoff.

      5   D ISCUSSION
464
465
      Our benchmark results (see section 4) show that the n-S4D model on Loihi 2 particularly excels in
      online token-by-token inference. Online token-by-token inference is widely applicable in streaming
466
      scenarios in which an incoming data stream must be rapidly processed on a token-by-token basis. In
467
      the token-by-token scenario, we demonstrate that Loihi 2 can meaningfully leverage its substantively
468   differentiated compute and memory co-located architecture to outperform the baseline Jetson GPU.
469   On sCIFAR, the largest workload we measured, Loihi 2 consumes approximately 1000x less energy
470   with a 75x lower latency and a 75x higher throughput compared to the recurrent implementation of
471   n-S4D on the Jetson GPU. We also benchmark the offline sample-by-sample and batched scenarios,
472   for which we find the Jetson GPU to be preferable to Loihi 2. Our results corroborate the notion
473   that GPU architectures are optimized for offline processing of large amounts of data in parallel. It
474   should be noted that our implementation of n-S4D on Jetson is not completely optimized for speed
475   and efficiency. Creating a stronger baseline implementation on Jetson as well as considering other
476
      types of hardware would be valuable future work.
477   Taken comprehensively, our results provide the first benchmarks of an SSM on a neuromorphic
478   hardware platform versus an edge GPU, comparing both the recurrent and convolution modes and
479   revealing the differences in energy, latency, throughput, and task accuracy. To the best of our knowl-
480   edge, this is the most holistic picture to date of the merits of neuromorphic hardware for SSM
481   efficiency. Furthermore, by virtue of our focus on SSMs—a family of promising and broadly ap-
482
      plicable deep learning sequence models—we build an exemplar for others to replicate and expand
      upon to help bridge deep learning technology to highly-differentiated computer architectures with
483
      compute and memory co-location for substantively improved efficiency.
484
485   Promising future work includes the following. The modest drop in accuracy of n-S4D on Loihi 2 in
      this work could potentially be ameliorated by applying QAFT for a longer duration than one epoch.

      The balance of latency, energy, and throughput on Loihi 2 over the continuum between fall-through
487   and pipelined processing could be more extensively characterized; indeed, only the endpoints of this
488   continuum are characterized in this work. Direct extensions of S4, for instance Liquid-S4 (Hasani
489   et al., 2022) or S5 (Smith et al., 2022), could be investigated; these extensions have shown state-of-
490   the-art performance on sequence modeling tasks and are also compatible with Loihi 2. Finally, our
491   work and potential optimizations and extensions can be applied and tested in real-world streaming
492   use-cases, such as keyword-spotting, audio denoising, vision for drone control, autonomous driving,
493   and other latency or energy constrained domains.
