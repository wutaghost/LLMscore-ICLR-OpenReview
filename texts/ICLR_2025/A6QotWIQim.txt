009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011             Power consumption plays a crucial role in on-device streaming speech recogni-
012             tion, significantly influencing the user experience. This study explores how the
013             configuration of weight parameters in speech recognition models affects their
014             overall energy efficiency. We found that the influence of these parameters on
015             power consumption varies depending on factors such as invocation frequency and
016             memory allocation. Leveraging these insights, we propose design principles that
017             enhance on-device speech recognition models by reducing power consumption
018             with minimal impact on accuracy. Our approach, which adjusts model components
019
                based on their specific energy sensitivities, achieves up to 47% lower energy usage
                while preserving comparable model accuracy and improving real-time performance
020
                compared to leading methods.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
025   Automatic streaming speech recognition (streaming ASR) is a crucial technology that enables real-
026   time transcription of user speech into text, typically requiring latency under 500 milliseconds. This
027   technology is essential for various applications on mobile and wearable devices. The seamless
028   integration of streaming ASR into devices such as smartphones and VR/AR glasses enhances user
029   interaction, supporting functionalities such as interface navigation, voice commands, real-time
030   communication, and accessibility features. Speech recognition and on-device AI remain important
031   topics in machine learning conferences including ICLR, for example, (Yao et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
032   2024; Hu et al., 2024; Haliassos et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2022;
033
      Kim et al., 2022; Variani et al., 2022; Leng et al., 2021; Stephenson et al., 2019; Chorowski et al.,
      2015; Zhao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2020).
034
035   Despite its critical role, the deployment of on-device streaming ASR faces a significant challenge
036   in power consumption. High energy demand can severely limit the practical usability of devices,
037   necessitating frequent recharges that degrade the overall user experience. Therefore, enhancing the
038   energy efficiency of on-device streaming ASR is crucial.
039   We explore the intricacies of on-device streaming ASR models with a focus on the Neural Transducer
040   (Graves, 2012). The Neural Transducer represents a class of speech recognition models comprised of
041   three key components: an Encoder for acoustic modeling, a Predictor for language modeling, and a
042   Joiner that combines the outputs of the Encoder and Predictor (see Figure 1). It has emerged as the
043   de facto standard solution for on-device streaming ASR (Graves et al., 2013; He et al., 2019; Li et al.,
044   2021), primarily due to its exceptional balance between computational efficiency and model accuracy.
045
      Transformer-based designs have been widely adopted within Neural Transducer models (Shi et al.,
      2021; Moritz et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020; Yeh et al., 2019; Gulati et al., 2020;
046
      Wang et al., 2020; Karita et al., 2019). In our comprehensive analysis, we train and evaluate over
047
      180 Neural Transducer models1 , experimenting with different architectures (e.g., Emformer (Shi
048   et al., 2021), Conformer (Gulati et al., 2020)) and varying the sizes of their core components. This
049   large-scale evaluation sheds light on how these components influence the model’s accuracy, real-time
050   factor (RTF),2 and power consumption.

      Our analysis reveals several new findings. First, we discover that the model’s energy usage is primarily
055   influenced by the memory traffic associated with loading model weights, which is in turn affected
056   by the invocation frequency of model components and their placement within the device’s memory
057   hierarchy. Secondly, there is a remarkable disparity in the invocation frequency of model components,
058   with the Joiner being summoned significantly more often than the Predictor, which in turn is more
059   frequently invoked than the Encoder. Consequently, although the Joiner constitutes only 5-9% of the
060   ASR model’s size, it accounts for 48-73% of the model’s power consumption. Thirdly, we uncover
061   an intriguing exponential relationship between the ASR model’s accuracy and its encoder size. This
062   finding suggests new avenues for research and development in the field of on-device streaming ASR.
063   Leveraging these insights, we propose a differentiated compression strategy for ASR model com-
064   ponents to optimize energy efficiency while minimizing impact on model accuracy. This strategy
065   accesses the power and accuracy sensitivity of each component, considering their invocation fre-
066   quency and memory placement. We prioritize compression for components that show higher power
067   sensitivity but lower accuracy sensitivity. Compressing these components significantly reduces energy
068   usage while only slightly affecting accuracy. Therefore, our focus is on compressing the Joiner first,
069
      followed by the Predictor and the Encoder, and aiming to store the Joiner’s weight parameters in
      energy-efficient local memory. Experiments on LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., 2015) and Public
070
      Video datasets demonstrate that our strategy achieves a significant reduction in energy usage by up
071
      to 47% and a notable decrease in RTF by up to 29%, all while preserving similar model accuracy
072   when compared to the state-of-the-art compression strategies. These prior strategies often overlook
073   the diverse runtime characteristics of ASR model components, highlighting our method’s efficiency
074   in using these distinctions.
075
      This paper presents the following contributions:
076
077
             • Through a comprehensive analysis of the power consumption associated with on-device
078
               streaming speech recognition, we have identified several key findings. Notably, we discov-
079            ered that the energy consumption of individual ASR model components is influenced not
080            only by their respective model sizes but also by the frequency with which they are invoked
081            and their memory placement strategies. This insight challenges the conventional wisdom
082            that larger model components inherently consume more energy, highlighting the importance
083            of considering operational dynamics and memory management in energy consumption.
084
             • We have developed a set of design guidelines aimed at enhancing the energy efficiency of
085
               on-device streaming speech recognition systems. The implementation of these guidelines
086
               has demonstrated a substantial reduction in energy consumption—by up to 47%—and
087            RTF—by up to 29%—while maintaining similar model accuracy when compared to the
088            state-of-the-art methods.
089
090          • Our study reveals an exponential relationship between on-device streaming ASR’s model

092            Our findings encourage the community to reconsider current approaches and more efficiently

      2.1   N EURAL T RANSDUCER : O N -D EVICE S TREAMING ASR
099
      The Neural Transducer architecture, also known as RNN-T, first introduced in (Graves, 2012), is
100
      the state-of-the-art solution to on-device, streaming speech recognition (Graves et al., 2013; He
101
      et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021). The Neural Transducer models the alignment between audio and
102   text (Prabhavalkar et al., 2024), integrating a compact language model and an acoustic model within
103   a single framework. Its design effectively reduces its memory footprint, rendering it exceptionally
104   suitable for devices with limited resources (Shangguan et al., 2019; Venkatesh et al., 2021). The
105   Neural Transducer has a short latency, meeting the requirement for streaming speech recognition that
106   latency should typically be less than 500 milliseconds. To the best of our knowledge, most leading
107   companies in the industry are using Neural Transducer models as their go-to choice for on-device
      streaming speech recognition (Li et al., 2024; Le et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Radfar et al., 2022).

127   The Neural Transducer architecture comprises three main components: an Encoder, a Predictor, and a
128   Joiner, as depicted in Figure 1. The Encoder processes acoustic feature inputs by receiving chunks of
129   audio (C1 , ..., Ct ), where each chunk (Ct ) represents a fixed duration of consecutive audio frames
130
      (xt,1 , ..., xt,n ). These frames are characterized by 80-dimensional log Mel-filterbank features, which
      are derived using a sliding audio window of 25 milliseconds and a step size of 10 milliseconds. Each
131
      frame (xt,j ) is then mapped by the Encoder into an embedding (enct,j ). The Predictor, utilizing
132
      previously predicted tokens (y1 , ..., yu−1 ), forecasts the embedding of the next token (predu ). The
133   Joiner merges the output embeddings from both the Encoder and Predictor, further processing this
134   combined output through a feedforward neural network and subsequently applying a softmax function
135   to determine the probability distribution of the next token. This process enables the Joiner to model
136   the token’s probability distribution over the entire set of sentence-piece targets as well as a "blank"
137   token that signifies the end of a frame’s transcription.
138
      Presently, the Encoder in Neural Transducer models is predominantly built as a variant of the
139   Transformer, a trend supported by numerous recent studies (Shi et al., 2021; Moritz et al., 2020;
140   Dong et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020; Yeh et al., 2019; Gulati et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Karita
141   et al., 2019). In this work, we respectively implement the Encoder of our Neural Transducer using the
142   Emformer (Shi et al., 2021) and Conformer (Gulati et al., 2020), two streaming Transformer variants,
143   to align with this prevailing trend.3 Transformer-based approaches allow the Encoder to process
144   frames in a chunk collectively, significantly reducing the frequency of its invocation compared to
145   the Predictor and Joiner, which process frames individually. The Predictor is engaged once for each
146   output token bearing explicit meanings (i.e., a sentence-piece target), whereas the Joiner is called
147
      upon not only for tokens with clear meanings but also for the "blank" token. Given that most output
      tokens are "blank," the Joiner operates far more frequently than the Predictor, highlighting a hierarchy
148
      in component invocation frequency where the Joiner is the most frequently used, followed by the
149
      Predictor, and then the Encoder.
150
151   As Figure 2 shows, mobile and wearable devices come equipped with a variety of processors,
152   including mobile CPUs, GPUs, and specialized hardware accelerators, all designed with energy
153
      efficiency in mind. For instance, a neural network hardware accelerator previously highlighted by
      (Lee et al., 2018) boasts a compute energy efficiency of 5 GOPS/mW (INT8), indicating it consumes
154
      merely 1mW to perform 5 billion INT8 operations every second. These processors interact with
155
      two primary types of memory: local and off-chip. Local memory, which is often comprised of
156   static random-access memory (SRAM), embedded dynamic random-access memory (eDRAM), or
157   other on-chip DRAM technologies, resides within the processor. This setup allows for swift data
158   access, with read/write operations for 64-byte data taking between 0.5 to 20 nanoseconds and being
159   remarkably energy-efficient; for example, accessing SRAM uses about 1.1 to 1.5pJ per byte (Li et al.,
160   2019). Conversely, off-chip memory, typically based on dynamic random-access memory (DRAM)

178   2.2    M OBILE AND W EARABLE D EVICES
179
180   In this study, we conducted experiments using on-device streaming ASR models on a Google Pixel-5
181
      mobile phone, focusing on measuring RTF and key workload statistics such as the number of
      operations for the model and the number of invocations for each model component. It is important
182
      to note that these workload statistics remain consistent across different device platforms. Given the
183
      challenges and potential inaccuracies associated with direct power consumption measurements4 , we
184   opted to model the power consumption for the ASR models operating on mobile or wearable devices,
185   following modeling methodologies established by prior work in speech recognition or computer
186   architecture communities (Li et al., 2024; Micron, 2006; Li et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2009). These
187   devices are assumed to be equipped with a hardware accelerator, 2 MB of local memory, and 8 GB
188   of off-chip memory. The local memory is treated as scratchpad memory, providing flexibility for
189   users to allocate the memory at the model component level. This setup allows for 1.5MB of the local
190   memory to be dedicated to model weights and 0.5MB for intermediate activations. We model the
191   ASR’s computing power and memory power based on the platform-independent workload statistics.
192
      For computing power, we rely on a compute energy efficiency metric of 5 GOPS/mW for INT-8
      operations, as identified by (Lee et al., 2018), and for memory power, we apply energy efficiency
193
      figures of 1.5pJ/byte for local memory and 120pJ/byte for off-chip memory, according to (Li et al.,
194
      2019). This approach enables a comprehensive analysis of the power dynamics involved in running
195   ASR models on modern mobile and wearable devices.

      A high power-to-accuracy sensitivity ratio indicates that compressing the component will yield the
353   greatest power savings for a given accuracy loss. This ratio helps determine the order in which we
354   compress components of on-device streaming ASR models.
355
356
      Our compression algorithm starts with a fully uncompressed model and iteratively reduces its size
      to meet a user-defined power reduction target (e.g., "reduce power by 60 mW"). For each milliwatt
357
      of power reduction, we calculate the power-to-accuracy sensitivity ratio of each component and
358
      compress the one with the highest ratio. In Neural Transducer models, we observe that the Joiner
359   initially has the highest power-to-accuracy sensitivity ratio due to its high power sensitivity, which
360   results from its frequent invocation. Once the Joiner’s size is reduced enough to fit into energy-
361   efficient local memory, its power-to-accuracy sensitivity ratio decreases, and the predictor becomes
362   the component with the highest ratio. The Predictor is then compressed until it reaches its user-defined
363   minimum size, beyond which further compression would cause a significant accuracy loss due to the
364   exponential relationship between model accuracy and component size. The Encoder is compressed
365   next until it reaches its user-defined minimum size. If additional power reduction is needed, we return
366   to compressing the Joiner.
367   Thus, the compression order follows: Joiner → Predictor → Encoder → Joiner. It’s important to note
368   that our algorithm only determines the compression order between components. Once a component
369   is selected for compression, an existing compression algorithm handles the specific task of deciding
370   which weight parameters to prune within that component. Our approach is compatible with any
371   existing compression algorithm.

417   Our proposed method aims to determine which component of a multi-component model (e.g., Neural
418   Transducer) should be prioritized for compression to achieve the most significant energy savings.
419   Once the critical component is identified, the specific compression technique used is beyond the
420   scope of this study; for our experiments, we utilize Adam-prune (Yang et al., 2022), a state-of-the-art
421   compression technique for on-device streaming speech recognition.
422   Our comparative analysis contrasts two scenarios: one in which the baseline compression technique
423   is uniformly applied across the entire model (referred to as "baseline"), and another in which the
424   same baseline technique is enhanced by our approach to strategically prioritize compression (referred
425   to as "baseline + our approach"). By comparing these scenarios, we aim to demonstrate the added
426   value of our method, specifically highlighting the advantages of strategic component prioritization in
427   model compression.
428   Although we use the strongest available baseline in our study, the choice and performance of the
429   baseline are not critical in this context. Our focus is on demonstrating the additional benefits
430   provided by our approach when combined with any baseline method. The strength of the baseline
431   only affects the absolute accuracy of the compressed models at a target power consumption level but
      does not impact the additional accuracy gains achieved through our prioritization method.

      6.1   O N -D EVICE ASR M ODEL C OMPRESSION
490
491
      Research by (Ghodsi et al., 2020) showed that removing recurrent layers from the Predictor in
492   Neural Transducer models does not negatively impact word-error rates. This discovery highlights
493   the potential for both compressing these models and enabling the Predictor to operate statelessly,
494   given its crucial function in reducing repetitive outputs. Further explorations by (Botros et al., 2021)
495   aimed at reducing the footprint of the Predictor and Joiner components in Neural Transducer models
496   to boost processing efficiency. They explored parameter sharing between the embedding matrices of
497   the Predictor and Joiner, suggesting a weighted-average embedding to encapsulate the history of the
498   Predictor’s tokens. (Shangguan et al., 2019) proposed shrinking the Predictor by replacing its Long
499   Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units with Simple Recurrent Units (SRU) that include 30% structured
500
      sparsity. They also recommended adapting the Encoder with Coupled Input-Forget Gate (CIFG)
      LSTM variants that include 50% structured sparsity. (Yang et al., 2022) employed a Supernet-based
501
      neural architecture search to determine optimal sparsity levels for each layer, aiming to balance model
502
      accuracy and size. While these studies aimed at reducing model size or RTF without significantly
503   compromising accuracy, they did not explore how to reduce power consumption, which is our primary
504   interest.
505
506
      6.2   O N -D EVICE ASR E NERGY O PTIMIZATION
507
508   Prior initiatives to lower the Neural Transducer’s power consumption have concentrated on modifying
509   the model’s cell architecture. (Li et al., 2024) introduced folding attention, achieving a reduction
510   in both model size and power consumption by 24% and 23%, respectively, without detriment to
511   accuracy. (Venkatesh et al., 2021) streamlined LSTM cells and designed a deeper yet narrower
512   Neural Transducer model, cutting down off-chip memory access by 4.5 times and energy costs by
513   twice, with only a minor accuracy decrease. Our research differs from these methods by focusing on
514   understanding the runtime behaviors of Neural Transducer model components. This understanding
515   aids in directing compression strategies more effectively toward energy optimization.
516
517   7     C ONCLUSION
518
519   Power consumption remains a critical challenge for on-device streaming ASR, directly affecting
520   device recharge frequency and user experience. This study conducted extensive experiments to
521   analyze power usage in ASR models, examining its correlation with model runtime behaviors and
522   identifying strategies for power reduction. Our findings highlight that the majority of ASR power
523
      consumption is attributed to loading model weights from off-chip memory, intricately linked to the
      size of model components, their invocation frequency, and their memory placement. Interestingly,
524
      despite its smaller size, the Joiner component consumes more power than the Encoder and Predictor,
525
      due to these factors. Additionally, we discovered a notable exponential relationship between the
526   model’s word error rate and the encoder size. Utilizing these insights, we formulated a series of
527   design guidelines focused on model compression for enhancing energy efficiency. The application of
528   these guidelines on the LibriSpeech and Public Video datasets resulted in significant energy savings
529   of up to 47% and a reduction in RTF by up to 29%, all while preserving model accuracy compared to
530   the state-of-the-art methods. These outcomes underscore the potential of targeted model optimization
531   strategies in achieving substantial energy efficiency improvements, marking a pivotal step towards
532   sustainable and user-friendly on-device streaming ASR technologies.

781   Regarding the first observation, input stride is used to enhance training and inference efficiency by
782   reducing sequence length. While a smaller stride better preserves acoustic local features, which
783   typically improves performance, it can also introduce risks such as greater sensitivity to noise and
784   loss of broader contextual information. A stride of 4–6 is commonly chosen as it balanced accuracy
785   and efficiency.
786   As for the second observation, in streaming ASR, a smaller stride increases the number of segments,
787   resulting in more frequent decoding of blank tokens and thus more frequent invocation of the joiner,
788   which raises power consumption. However, if the joiner is compressed to fit within the local SRAM,
789   this increased invocation has minimal impact on power usage, due to the high energy efficiency of
790   SRAM.
