009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011            Image-to-video (I2V) generation is gaining increasing attention with its wide application in video
012            synthesis. Recently, diffusion-based I2V models have achieved remarkable progress given their
013            novel design on network architecture, cascaded framework, and motion representation. However,
014            restricted by their noise-to-data generation process, diffusion-based methods inevitably suffer the
015            difficulty to generate video samples with both appearance consistency and temporal coherence from
016            an uninformative Gaussian noise, which may limit their synthesis quality. In this work, we present
017
               FrameBridge, taking the given static image as the prior of video target and establishing a tractable
               bridge model between them. By formulating I2V synthesis as a frames-to-frames generation task
018
               and modeling it with a data-to-data process, we fully exploit the information in input image and
019
               facilitate the generative model to learn the image animation process. In two popular settings of
020            training I2V models, namely fine-tuning a pre-trained text-to-video (T2V) model or training from
021            scratch, we further propose two techniques, SNR-Aligned Fine-tuning (SAF) and neural prior, which
022            improve the fine-tuning efficiency of diffusion-based T2V models to FrameBridge and the synthesis
023            quality of bridge-based I2V models respectively. Experiments conducted on WebVid-2M and UCF-
024            101 demonstrate that: (1) our FrameBridge achieves superior I2V quality in comparison with the
025            diffusion counterpart (zero-shot FVD 95 vs. 192 on MSR-VTT and non-zero-shot FVD 122 vs. 171
026            on UCF-101); (2) our proposed SAF and neural prior effectively enhance the ability of bridge-based
027            I2V models in the scenarios of fine-tuning and training from scratch. Demo samples can be visited
028
               at: https://framebridgei2v.github.io/.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Image-to-video (I2V) generation, commonly referred as image animation, aims at generating consecutive video frames
033
      from a static image (Xing et al., 2023; Ni et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Guo et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2022), i.e.,
034   a frame-to-frames generation task where maintaining appearance consistency and ensuring temporal coherence of
035   generated video frames are key evaluation criteria (Xing et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a). With the recent progress
036   in video synthesis (Brooks et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Blattmann et al., 2023; Bao et al., 2024), several diffusion-
037   based I2V frameworks have been proposed, with novel designs on network architecture (Xing et al., 2023; Zhang
038   et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2023b; Ren et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2023), cascaded framework (Jain et al., 2024; Zhang
039   et al., 2023), and motion representation (Zhang et al., 2024b; Ni et al., 2023). However, although these methods have
040   demonstrated the potential of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) in I2V synthesis, restricted by
041   their noise-to-data generation process, they inevitably suffer the difficulty to generate video samples required by both
042
      appearance consistency and temporal coherence from uninformative random noise. With the noise-to-data sampling
      trajectory which is inherently mismatched with the frame-to-frames synthesis process of I2V task, previous diffusion-
043
      based methods increase the burden of generative models, which may result in limited synthesis quality.
044
045   In this work, we present FrameBridge, a novel I2V framework to model the frame-to-frames synthesis process with
046   recently proposed data-to-data generative framework (Chen et al.; Liu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c). Specifically,
047   given the input image and video target, we first leverage variational auto-encoder (VAE) based compression network
048
      to transform them into continuous latent representations, and then take their latent representations as boundary dis-
      tributions, i.e., prior and target, to establish our data-to-data generative framework. Considering the static image has
049
      already been an informative prior for each of the consecutive frames in video target, we naturally replicate it to obtain
050
      the prior of the whole video clip, constructing the frames-to-frames pairs for the prior-to-target generation process in
051   FrameBridge. Standing on constructed frames-to-frames pairs, we establish bridge models (Tong et al., 2023; Zhou

      In practice, I2V systems usually leverage the potential of a pre-trained diffusion-based text-to-video (T2V)
085
      model (Xing et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Ma et al., 2024a) with a fine-tuning process, to reduce the require-
086   ments of image-video data pairs and the computational resources at training stage. Therefore, in the application of
087   FrameBridge, we aim to efficiently fine-tune a pre-trained diffusion-based T2V model to our proposed bridge-based
088   I2V model. Toward this target, we propose SNR-Aligned Fine-tuning (SAF), which aligns the two frameworks, i.e.,
089   diffusion models and bridge models, by adjusting the bridge process. Specifically, we first reparameterize the bridge
090   process in FrameBridge, enabling the noisy intermediate latents of our frames-to-frames process to be aligned with
091   the ones in the noise-to-data process of pre-trained diffusion models. Then, we change the timestep to match the
092   signal-to-noise (SNR) ratio between the input of the bridge model and the pre-trained diffusion model. SAF avoids
093   the mismatch between the two different generative frameworks at pre-training and fine-tuning stage, and therefore
094
      improves the synthesis quality of FrameBridge when adapting pre-trained T2V diffusion models.
095   Compared to diffusion models using Gaussian prior, FrameBridge takes static image as prior to improve I2V perfor-
096   mance. A natural question to our proposed method is if we could further improve the I2V quality with a stronger prior.
097   We answer this question by further exploiting the prior of our method. Given a static image, we design a one-step
098   mapping-based network and optimize it with the video target, extracting a neural prior from the image for the video
099   target. Compared to input image, this neural prior reduces the distance between prior and video target to a greater
100
      extent, and alleviates the burden of generative models further. Although more advanced methods can be leveraged
      to extract more informative neural prior, we empirically find that a coarse estimation for video target at the cost of
101
      a single sampling step has already been beneficial to FrameBridge. This further verifies our motivation to present
102
      FrameBridge and shows a novel method to enhance bridge-based I2V models. In this work, our contributions can be
103   summarized as follows:

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
116
117
      Diffusion-based I2V Generation Diffusion models have recently achieved remarkable progress in I2V synthe-
118
      sis (Blattmann et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024). Previous works have explored multi-stage generation
119   system (Jain et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024) fusion module (Wang et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024) and
120   improved network architectures (Wang et al., 2024; Xing et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2023b; Ren et al.,
121   2024), while their generative framework remains the noise-to-data one of diffusion models, which may be inefficient
122   for I2V synthesis. To improve the uninformative prior, PYoCo (Ge et al., 2023) recently proposes to use correlated
123   noise for each frame in both training and inference. ConsistI2V (Ren et al., 2024), FreeInit (Wu et al., 2023), and CIL
124   (Zhao et al., 2024) present training-free strategies to better align the training and inference distribution of diffusion
125   prior, which is popular in diffusion models (Lin et al., 2024; Podell et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023; Girdhar et al.,
126   2023). These strategies focus on improving the noise distribution to enhance the quality of synthesized videos, while
127
      they still suffer the restriction of noise-to-data diffusion framework, which may limit their endeavor to utilize the entire
      information (e.g., both large-scale features and fine-grained details) contained in the given image. In this work, we
128
      propose a data-to-data framework and utilize deterministic prior rather than Gaussian noise, allowing us to leverage
129
      the clean input image as prior information.
130
131
132
      Bridge Models Recently, bridge models (Chen et al.; Tong et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
      2023c), which overcome the restriction of Gaussian prior in diffusion models, have gained increasing attention. They
133
      have demonstrated the advantages of data-to-data generation process over the noise-to-data one on image-to-image
134
      translation (Liu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023) and text-to-speech synthesis (Chen et al., 2023c) tasks. In this work,
135   we make the first attempt to extend bridge models to I2V synthesis and further propose two improving techniques for
136   bridge models, enabling efficient fine-tuning from diffusion models and stronger prior for video target.

      4.1   M OTIVATION
238
239
      As discussed above, on one hand, the static image in I2V generation has already provided strong condition information,
240   e.g., the appearance details which should be preserved in generated video. However, the prior of diffusion models
241   ignore this information, rendering diffusion-based I2V systems regenerate entire video information from Gaussian
242   noise. On the other hand, the image animation process should be temporal coherent, ensuring the consistency between
243   video frames. However, the prior of diffusion models makes previous works generate each frame from random noise.
244   Although the generation is conditioned on static image, it would be difficult for a noise-to-data generation process to
245   guarantee the temporal coherence between generated frames.
246   Toward reducing the mismatch between the generation process and the I2V task, we make the first attempt to establish
247   data-to-data process, i.e., bridge models (Chen et al., 2023c; Zhou et al., 2023), for the frame-to-frames synthesis of
248   I2V. Taking the static image as the prior of consecutive frames in video, we build a bridge between the initial frame and
249   the following ones. In generation, each video frame is generated from the initial frame, which is naturally helpful to
250   preserve appearance details in I2V synthesis. Moreover, as our model learns the frame-to-frames synthesis instead of
251   conditional noise-to-frames generation, we facilitate our model to focus on learning image animation, which benefits
252   temporal coherence of generated video.

      4.3      SNR-A LIGNED F INE - TUNING
345
346   To implement FrameBridge, one straightforward strategy is to train bridge models from scratch using eq. (6). Mean-
347   while, another common practice of training I2V models is to fine-tune from pre-trained T2V diffusion models (Chen
348   et al., 2023b;a; Xing et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024a). A key challenge arises as the marginal
349   distribution of bridge models pt,bridge (zt ) differs from that of diffusion models pt,dif f (zt ), limiting the generation
350   performance of fine-tuned FrameBridge as illustrated in Figure 3. To address this issue, we propose the innovative
351   SNR-Aligned Fine-tuning (SAF) technique. By bridging the gap between the two distributions during fine-tuning,
352
      SAF enables more seamless knowledge transfer between the two model families. To the best of our knowledge, this
      is the first attempt to explore how to effectively fine-tune bridge models from pre-trained diffusion models. Our SAF
353
      technique consists of the following two steps:

510   6       C ONCLUSIONS
511
512   In this work, we propose FrameBridge, building a data-to-data generation process for I2V synthesis, which matches
513   the frame-to-frames nature of this task. Additionally, targeting at two typical scenarios of training I2V models, namely
514   fine-tuning from pre-trained diffusion models and training from scratch, we present SNR-Aligned Fine-tuning (SAF)
515   and neural prior respectively to further improve the generation quality of FrameBridge. Extensive experiments show
516   that FrameBridge generate videos with enhanced appearance consistency with image condition and improved temporal
517   coherence between frames, demonstrating the advantages of FrameBridge over diffusion-based I2V methods and the
518
      effectiveness of two proposed techniques.

      B    D ETAILED D ISCUSSION O N R ELATED W ORKS
937
938
939   Video Diffusion Models Inspired by the success of text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models (Ramesh et al., 2022;
940   Nichol et al., 2021), numerous studies have investigated diffusion-based text-to-video (T2V) models (Blattmann et al.,
941
      2023; Yang et al., 2024; Singer et al., 2022) by designing 3D spatial-temporal U-Net (Ho et al., 2022b;a) and Diffusion
      Transformers (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Bao et al., 2023). To improve memory and computation efficiency, Latent
942
      Diffusion Models (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022; Vahdat et al., 2021) are utilized where the diffusion process is applied
943
      in the compressed latent space of video samples (Bao et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2024; He et al., 2022). Meanwhile,
944   some other works designed cascaded diffusion models to generate motion representation (Yu et al., 2024) or videos
945   with lower resolution (Ho et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2023) first, which are utilized to synthesize the result videos in
946   the subsequent stages.
947
948
949   Diffusion-based I2V Generation The main difference between I2V and T2V is the incorporation of image condi-
950   tions into the sampling process. Xing et al. (2023) utilizes the features of a CLIP image encoder and a lightweight
951   transformer to inject image conditions into the backbone of a T2V model. Ma et al. (2024a) and Zhang et al. (2024c)
952
      propose to directly model the residual between the subsequent frames and the given initial frame with diffusion for
      I2V generation. Moreover, Ma et al. (2024a) also uses the DCTInit technique to enhance the consistency of video
953
      content with the given image. Chen et al. (2023b) presents to train short-to-long video generation models with masked
954
      diffusion models. Guo et al. (2023) and Zhang et al. (2024a) propose to utilize pre-trained T2I models for image an-
955   imation by training an additional component to model the relationship between video frames. SparseCtrl (Guo et al.,
956   2025) and Animate Anyone (Hu, 2024) design specific fusion modules for video diffusion models to adapt to various
957   types of conditions including RGB images. Ren et al. (2024) propose improved network architecture and sampling
958   strategy for image-to-video generation at the same time to enhance the controllability of image conditions. Jain et al.
959   (2024), Zhang et al. (2023) and Shi et al. (2024) design cascaded diffusion systems for I2V generation. VIDIM (Jain
960   et al., 2024) consists of one base diffusion model and another two diffusion models for spatial and temporal super-
961   resolution respectively. Zhang et al. (2023) uses a base diffusion model to generate videos with low resolutions, which
962   serve as the input of the following video super-resolution diffusion model. Shi et al. (2024) first generates the optical
963
      flow between the subsequent frames and given image with a diffusion process, and use the optical flow as conditions
      of another model to generate videos. Ni et al. (2023) and Zhang et al. (2024b) train an autoencoder to represent the
964
      motions between frames in a latent space, and use diffusion models to generate motion latents. However, previous I2V
965
      diffusion models are built on the noise-to-data generation of conditional diffusion process and the sampling remains a
966   denoising process conditioned on given images. In contrast, FrameBridge replaces the diffusion process with a bridge
967   process and the sampling directly model the animation of static images.
968
969
970   Noise Manipulation for Video Diffusion Models Several works have explored to improve the uninformative prior
971   distribution of diffusion models. PYoCo (Ge et al., 2023) recently proposes to use correlated noise for each frame
972   in both training and inference. ConsistI2V (Ren et al., 2024), FreeInit (Wu et al., 2023), and CIL (Zhao et al., 2024)
973
      present training-free strategies to better align the training and inference distribution of diffusion prior, which is popular
      in diffusion models (Lin et al., 2024; Podell et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023; Girdhar et al., 2023). These strategies
974
      focus on improving the noise distribution to enhance the quality of synthesized videos, while they still suffer the
975
      restriction of noise-to-data diffusion framework, which may limit their endeavor to utilize the entire information (e.g.,
976   both large-scale features and fine-grained details) contained in the given image. In contrast, we propose a data-to-data
977   framework and utilize deterministic prior rather than Gaussian noise, allowing us to leverage the clean input image as
978   prior information.
979
980
981   Comparison with Previous Works of Bridge Models In Section 3, we leverage the forward SDE of bridge models
982   (Zhou et al., 2023) and the backward sampler proposed by Chen et al. (2023c) to build FrameBridge. We unify their
983   theoretical frameworks to establish our formulation, and emphasize that bridge models are suitable for image-to-video
984
      generation, which is a typical data-to-data generation task. Liu et al. (2023) and Chen et al. (2023c) apply bridge
      models to image-to-image translation and text-to-speech synthesis tasks respectively. Compared with their works, we
985
      focus on I2V tasks, building our bridge-based framework by utilizing the frames-to-frames essence and presenting
986
      two innovative techniques for two scenarios of training I2V models, namely fine-tuning from pre-trained text-to-video
987   diffusion models and training from scratch.

       C.1       DATASETS
994
995    UCF-101 is an open-sourced video dataset consisting of 13320 videos clips, and each video clip are categorized into
996    one of the 101 action classes. There are three official train-test split, each of which divide the whole dataset into 9537
997    training video clips and 3783 test video clips. We use the whole dataset as the training data for I2V models trained
998    from scratch on UCF-101, and use the test set to evaluate zero-shot metrics for models fine-tuned on WebVid-2M.
999    When we evaluate zero-shot metrics on UCF-101 for text-conditional I2V models, we use the class label as the input
1000   text prompt.
1001   WebVid-2M is an open-sourced dataset consisting of about 2.5 million video-text pairs, which is a subset of WebVid-
1002   10M. We only use WebVid-2M as the training data when fine-tuning I2V models from T2V diffusions in Section
1003   5.2.
1004
       MSR-VTT is an open-sourced dataset consisting of 10000 video-text pairs, and we only use the test set to compute
1005
       zero-shot metrics for fine-tuned models.
1006
1007
       Preprocess of Training Data: For both UCF-101 and WebVid-2M dataset, we sample 16 frames from each video
1008   clip with a fixed frame stride of 3 when training. Then we resize and center-crop the video clips to 256 × 256 before
1009   input it to the models.
1010
1011   C.2       M ETRICS
1012
1013   Fréchet Video Distance ( Unterthiner et al. (2018); FVD) evaluates the quality of synthesized videos by computing
1014   the perceptual distance between videos sampled from the dataset and the models. We follow the protocol used in
1015   StyleGAN-V (Skorokhodov et al., 2022) to calculate FVD. First, we sample 2048 video clips with 16 frames and
1016   frame stride of 3 from the dataset. Then, we generate 2048 videos from the I2V models. All videos are resized to 256
1017   × 256 before calculating FVD except for ExtDM. (ExtDM generate videos with resolution 64 × 64, so we compute
1018
       FVD on this resolution.) After that, we extract features of those videos with the same I3D model used in the repository
       of StyleGAN-V 7 and calculate the Fréchet Distance.
1019
1020   Inception Score (Saito et al. (2017); IS) also evaluates the quality of the generated videos. However, computing IS
1021   need a pre-trained classifier and we only apply this metric on UCF-101. When computing IS, we use the open-sourced
1022   evaluation code and pre-trained classifier for videos from the repository of StyleGAN-V.
1023   CLIPSIM (Wu et al., 2021) evaluates the consistency between video frames and the text prompt by computing the
1024   average CLIP similarity score between each frame and the prompt. We use the VIT-B/32 CLIP model (Radford et al.,
1025   2021) when evaluating zero-shot metrics on MSR-VTT.
1026
       PIC is a metric used by Xing et al. (2023) to evaluate the consistency of video frames and the given image by the
1027
       computing average Dreamsim (Fu et al., 2023) distance between generated frames and the image condition.
