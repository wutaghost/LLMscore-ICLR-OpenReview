                                                       A BSTRACT
011
012
                Large Language Models (LLMs) are commonly evaluated using human-crafted
013
                benchmarks, under the premise that higher scores implicitly reflect stronger
014
                human-like performance. However, there is growing concern that LLMs may
015             “game” these benchmarks due to data leakage, achieving high scores while strug-
016             gling with tasks simple for humans. To substantively address the problem, we
017             create GAOKAO-Eval, a comprehensive benchmark based on China’s National
018             College Entrance Examination (Gaokao), and conduct “closed-book” evaluations
019             for representative models released prior to Gaokao. Contrary to prevailing consen-
020             sus, even after addressing data leakage and comprehensiveness, GAOKAO-Eval
021             reveals that high scores still fail to truly reflect human-aligned capabilities. To
022             better understand this mismatch, We introduce the Rasch model from cognitive
023
                psychology to analyze LLM scoring patterns and identify two key discrepancies:
                1) anomalous consistant performance across various question difficultiess, and 2)
024
                high variance in performance on questions of similar difficulty. In addition, we
025
                identified inconsistent grading of LLM-generated answers among teachers and re-
026             curring mistake patterns. we find that the phenomenon are well-grounded in the
027             motivations behind OpenAI o1, and o1’s reasoning-as-difficulties can mitigate the
028             mismatch. These results show that GAOKAO-Eval can reveal limitations in LLM
029             capabilities not captured by current benchmarks and highlight the need for more
030             LLM-aligned difficulty analysis.

199   2.2           E VALUATION M ETHODOLOGY AND S ECURITY M EASURES
200
      GAOKAO-Eval employs a rigorous evaluation methodology with strict security measures to ensure
201
      accurate, meaningful results while maintaining the integrity of the assessment process.
202
203   Non-leaky Data and Temporal Isolation. To address limitations in previous benchmarks like
204   GAOKAO-Bench (Zhang et al., 2023) and GAOKAO-MM (Zong & Qiu, 2024), which potentially
205   allowed data leakage, GAOKAO-Eval uses genuinely unseen data. It evaluates only open-source
206
      models released before June 6, 2024, ensuring temporal isolation and a closed-book environment.
      This approach provides a more objective assessment of LLMs’ capabilities, avoiding the “open-book
207
      test” scenario present in earlier evaluations.
208
209   Multimodal Evaluation Adaptations.         For multimodal questions, evaluation methods were
210   adapted based on model capabilities. The Mixtral series, being language-only models, used only
211   text input for multimodal questions. Due to poor performance of QwenVL-7B (Bai et al., 2023)
212
      on certain subjects, Qwen2-72B (Yang et al., 2024) text model was also evaluated on multimodal
      questions in Physics, Chemistry, and Geography for both New Curriculum Standard and National A
213
      test papers.
214
215   Human Expert Grading. 54 experienced Gaokao examiners graded the responses without prior
      knowledge of their AI origin. Clear guidelines were provided for handling misunderstood questions,

      repeated answers, or explanations instead of direct answers. The evaluation considered the potential
217   1-2 point deviation in Chinese essays due to the lack of handwriting assessment, typically part of
218   the Gaokao scoring process.

      2.3   DATA P ROCESSING
221
222   At the moment when the Gaokao concluded, we utilized various online channels to collect exami-
223   nation papers from various subjects. These papers were then standardized into a consistent format
224   containing both text and images through manual processing. Specific processing techniques were
225   applied according to the needs of each subject. For subjects such as Chinese, Mathematics, and
226   English, which are commonly used across multiple provinces and tend to have fewer image-based
227   questions, we opted to exclude any images. Consequently, we used solely textual questions as the
228   data for evaluation. For subjects other than the aforementioned three, we treated each major ques-
229   tion as a separate input. Sub-questions within each major question were formatted using (1), (2),
230
      and so on. Additionally, images within these questions were incorporated by employing the token
      to indicate their placement within the text. In scientific subjects, where formulas are prevalent, all
231
      mathematical expressions were converted into LaTeX format and encapsulated using the $ symbol,
232
      which ensured consistent and accurate representation of complex equations. To prevent any external
233   instructions from influencing the model’s performance, no extra prompts were included beyond the
234   necessary formatting and data processing steps outlined above.

362   Semi Difficulty-Invariant Scoring Distribution. We define the phenomenon of Semi difficulty-
363   invariant scoring distribution as the lack of significant correlation between question difficulty and
364   the scoring rate of language models. Mathematically, we compute the Pearson correlation coefficient
365   between question difficulties  b_s  and the observed scoring rates  P_{ij}(s)  for subject  i  and model  j :
366
367                                                        \rho _{b,P}^{(ij)} = \frac {Cov(b, P_{ij})}{\sigma _b \sigma _{P_{ij}}}                                   (2)
368
369   where  Cov(b, P_{ij})  is the covariance between question difficulty and scoring rate, and  \sigma _b  and  \sigma _{P_{ij}}  are
370   the standard deviations of question difficulty and scoring rate, respectively. A small absolute value
371   of  \rho _{b,P}^{(ij)}  indicates that the scoring rate is approximately invariant with respect to question difficulty.

      3.4   I NCONSISTENCIES IN LLM G RADING
401
402   We observed that human examiners encountered too high Inconsistent Score Rate (ISR) in over
403   32% of cases due to the unique scoring patterns of LLMs. A high ISR indicates that human graders
404   are more likely to disagree when assessing LLM-generated answers compared to typical human re-
405   sponses, which exacerbates the phenomenon of high variance in performance on similarly difficult
406   questions. As shown in Figure 9, the ISR varies across subjects. Notably, the humanities subjects,
407   such as Politics and History, exhibit higher inconsistency rates compared to science subjects like
408   Physics and Math. This suggests that LLMs may face more challenges in consistently interpreting
409   and responding to questions in humanities, potentially due to the abstract and context-dependent
410
      nature of these subjects. For instance, the ISR for Politics reaches up to 41.18% in some models,
      indicating that nearly half of the responses deviate significantly from the average, highlighting the
411
      difficulties LLMs have with the subjective and often nuanced content typical of humanities. In con-
412
      trast, the ISR for Physics is much lower, often below 20%, reflecting more consistent performance
413   in subjects that rely on more concrete and structured knowledge.

490   5   R ELATED W ORK
491
492
      Benchmark for LLMs and VLMs. Traditional benchmarks have played a pivotal role in assessing
      the natural understanding capabilities of models (Kahou et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2022a). However,
493
      as LLMs evolve, models such as Mistral-Large2 (Mistral AI, 2024), Llama3.1-405B (Dubey et al.,
494
      2024), and GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) begin to outperform human capabilities on these traditional
495   tasks. So there is a growing recognition of the need for more sophisticated benchmarks that can
496   evaluate a broader spectrum of abilities, encompassing both natural language processing (NLP) and
497   multimodal tasks.
498
      MMLU, CMMLU, C-Eval have expanded the evaluation landscape by incorporating a wider range
499
      of subjects and difficulty levels, primarily through multiple-choice questions (MCQs) (Hendrycks
500
      et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023). In addition, Math utilizes a fill-in-the-blank
501   format to measure models’ mathematical problem-solving abilities (Hendrycks et al., 2021c). On
502   the multimodal front, benchmarks like MMMU and Math Vista have been introduced to assess
503   models’ abilities to integrate and interpret information across different modalities with reasoning,
504   such as text and images(Yue et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a).
505
      Despite these advancements, current benchmarks face notable limitations. The reliance on MCQs,
506
      while valuable for scalability and objectivity, often limits the types of questions and scenarios that
507   can be evaluated, potentially overlooking critical aspects of language comprehension and generation.
508   Moreover, both NLP and multimodal benchmarks struggle with the issue of question leakage. Our
509   work introduces a novel benchmark by leveraging the rich and diverse question types and a broad
510   array of subjects and question formats from the GAOKAO examinations.
511
      Gaokao Evaluation. Gaokao is a comprehensive national academic test that serves as the primary
512
      criterion for university admission in China. Two notable works in this area are Gaokao-bench and
513   GAOKAO-MM (Zong & Qiu, 2024; Zhang et al., 2023). Gaokao-bench compiled a dataset of
514   multiple-choice and subjective questions from previous Gaokao examinations, while GAOKAO-
515   MM expanded this approach to include multimodal elements. Both benchmarks aim to provide a
516   comprehensive evaluation of LLMs’ language understanding, reasoning abilities, and multimodal
517   integration capabilities.
518
      The underlying assumption for these two benchmarks is that if LLMs can perform well on tasks
519   that challenge human intelligence, they may be developing more human-like cognitive capabilities.
520   However, our research demonstrates that high scores on these benchmarks do not necessarily equate
521   to human-like abilities in LLMs.
522
      Error Patterns and Human Evaluation of LLMs. Berglund et al. (2024) finds a reversal curse
523
      pattern, while Wu et al. (2023) designs 11 counterfactual evaluation tasks and observes consistent
524
      and substantial degradation of LM performance. Some researchers find that LLMs make a significant
525   number of basic errors in code writing and are unable to complete code with potential bugs (Zhong &
526   Wang, 2023; Jesse et al., 2023; Dinh et al., 2023). Chen et al. (2024b) builds an automated evaluation
527   method and discoverys 8 error patterns. Rather than summarizing error patterns in GAOKAO, our
528   analysis delves deeper into how these patterns contribute to the phenomenon where high scores
529   cannot reflect high capabilities. This suggests a potential gap in reasoning abilities.
530
531
      6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      GAOKAO-Eval provides a comprehensive and annually updated benchmark for evaluating LLM
534   capabilities. Our study reveals that high scores on existing benchmarks do not necessarily reflect
535   human-aligned reasoning abilities in LLMs. We identifies unique scoring patterns in LLMs, includ-
536   ing semi difficulty-invariant distributions and high performance variance on similar difficulty ques-
537   tions. These findings challenge the effectiveness of current evaluation methods and highlight the
538   need for more LLM-aligned difficulties analysis. Future work could focus on developing reasoning-
539   based metrics to better align difficulty assessments with LLM capabilities, addressing the problem
      of high scores failing to capture true capability.

      A     D ETAIL OF WQX T RAINING
024
025
      In this section, we delve into the details surrounding the WQX model, which was specifically de-
026
      veloped to validate the comprehensiveness of the Gaokao evaluation system. To achieve this, we
027   embarked on an extensive data preparation process, continuing the pre-training on the foundation
028   provided by the InternLM2-20b-base model. The subsequent paragraphs will outline the datasets
029   utilized by the WQX model, our data preparation methodologies, and the intricate details of the
030   training process.
031
032   A.1   G AOKAO T RAINING DATASET
033
034   To construct a comprehensive Gaokao dataset, we embarked on an extensive data collection process,
035   gathering over 25 million examination questions from online resources and educational books. After
036   a meticulous deduplication process, we narrowed down the dataset to 17.5 million unique questions.
037   In addition to these questions, we incorporated more than 1GB of textual material from academic
038
      books into our training data to further enrich the dataset’s diversity and depth. To enhance the quality
      and relevance of our dataset for training LLMs specifically for Gaokao preparation, we employed
039
      several innovative techniques for data augmentation and retrieval.
040
041   Numerical Reasoning Enhancement Given that solution processes for numerical calculation
042   problems often contain omissions, we employed the InternLM2-20b model to supplement and elab-
043   orate on these mathematical problem-solving steps. To further bolster the model’s numerical com-
044
      putation capabilities, we developed a custom calculation tool similar to SymPy, which provides
      the correct and detailed step-by-step solutions. This tool was used to synthesize a large volume of
045
      computational data, thereby enhancing the model’s arithmetic proficiency.
046
047   Chain-of-Thought Augmentation For questions lacking detailed explanations, we implemented
048   a multi-step approach using the InternLM2-20b model. In the case of multiple-choice questions,
049   we introduced variations in the model’s response order to ensure robustness. For questions that the
050
      model consistently answered correctly, we prompted it to generate comprehensive explanations, thus
      enriching the dataset with detailed problem-solving rationales.
051
052   Enhanced Retrieval and Filtering from Common Crawl We employed the Query of CC tech-
053   nique (Fei et al., 2024) to retrieve Gaokao-relevant data from Common Crawl, using examination
      questions as queries. To ensure data quality, we implemented a novel filtering process: retrieved


                                                         1
054
      data was used as context in a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) framework. The model reat-
055
      tempted questions using this context, and we retained only the retrieved documents that enabled
056   correct answers to previously mishandled questions. This filtered dataset served as high-quality
057   supplementary knowledge for training, effectively enhancing the model’s performance on Gaokao-
058   style examinations.

      WQX model, built upon InternLM2-20b-base, was trained on the curated Gaokao dataset for two
063
      complete epochs. We employed the InternEvo framework (Team, 2023), maintaining a context
064
      length of 4096 tokens. For content exceeding this length, we split and truncated it into multiple data
065   points. The training configuration utilized mixed-precision computation with bfloat16 and FlashAt-
066   tention2 for optimal efficiency (Dao, 2024). We used the AdamW optimizer (β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95,
067   weight decay = 0.1) with a cosine decay learning rate schedule, peaking at 3 × 10−5 after 2000
068   warm-up steps and decreasing to 3 × 10−6 . Each training batch consisted of approximately 0.5M
069   tokens, balancing computational efficiency with effective learning.

074   B.1   M ORE D ETAIL A BOUT G AOKAO
075
076   Paper Type Diversity The comprehensive nature of GAOKAO-Eval is further enhanced by its
077   coverage of multiple paper types, reflecting the diverse educational landscape across China. As
078   detailed in Table 1, the benchmark includes various Gaokao models, such as the ”3+1+2” New
079   Pattern, the ”3+3” Pattern, and the Traditional Pattern. With the reform of the Gaokao (National
080
      College Entrance Examination) in 2024, there are six types of Gaokao papers nationwide. The
      Beijing, Shanghai, and Tianjin papers, along with the National paper A, cover all subjects. Provinces
081
      using the New Curriculum Standard I and II papers use corresponding Chinese, Mathematics, and
082
      English papers, while most provinces independently set their own exams for other subjects. In
083   GAOKAO-Eval, we tested all publicly available papers from the New Curriculum and National
084   paper A. Regarding the Gaokao models, the current system is primarily divided into three major
085   categories:
086
087          • The “3+1+2” New Pattern widely adopted by 23 provinces, is structured around the
088            core subjects of Chinese, Mathematics, and English. Students are required to choose either
089            Physics or History as their primary subject and select two additional subjects from the
090            remaining four (Political Science, Geography, Chemistry, Biology).
091
             • The “3+3” Pattern currently used by 6 provinces, allows students, after completing the
092
               core subjects (Chinese, Mathematics, and English), to freely choose three elective subjects
093            from six options (Political Science, Geography, Chemistry, Biology, and in Zhejiang, an
094            additional subject of Technology).
095
096          • The Traditional Pattern The remaining 5 provinces still adhere to the traditional subject
097            division system of the National Paper A, maintaining the conventional academic assess-
098            ment pathway.
