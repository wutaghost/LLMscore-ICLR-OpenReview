009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Wasserstein Barycenter (WB) is a fundamental problem in machine learning, whose
012            objective is to find a representative probability measure that minimizes the sum
013            of Wasserstein distance to given distributions. WB has a number of applications
014            in various areas. However, in some applications like model ensembling, where
015            it aggregates predictions of different models on the label space, WB may lead to
016            unfair outcome towards underrepresented groups (e.g., a “minority” distribution
017            may be far away from the obtained WB under Wasserstein distance). To address
018
               this issue, we propose an alternative objective called “Wasserstein Ball Center
               (WBC)”. Specifically, WBC is a distribution that encompasses all input distributions
019
               within the minimum Wasserstein distance, which can be formulated as a minmax
020
               optimization problem. We show that the WBC problem with fixed support is
021            equivalent to solving a large-scale linear programming (LP) instance, which is
022            quite different from the previously studied LP model for WB. By incorporating
023            some novel observations on the induced normal equation, we propose an efficient
024            algorithm that accelerates the interior point method by O(N m) times (N is the
025            number of distributions and m is the support size). Finally, we conduct a set
026            of experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets. We demonstrate the
027            computational efficiency of our algorithm, and showcase its better accuracy on
028            model ensembling under imbalanced data distributions.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   To find a representative of several given probability distributions is a natural problem in machine
034   learning. One popular approach is to compute the geometric center on probability space with induced
035
      distances between probabilities, such as the Wasserstein distance ((Villani, 2021)). Given a weight
      vector (ω1 , ω2 , . . . , ωN ) for N ≥ 2, the Wasserstein barycenter (WB) of N probability measures
036
      {µk }Nk=1 is defined as the weighted Frechet mean under Wasserstein distance. Namely, it is the
037
      solution of the following problem
038
039                                                   N
                                                      X
040                                          min            ωk Wpp (µ, µk ),                              (1)
                                           µ∈Pp (Ω)
041                                                   k=1

042   where Pp (Ω) is the set of Borel probability measure on Ω with finite p-th moment, and Wp is the
043   Wasserstein distance of order p, which will be formally defined in Section 2. WB has found various
044   applications in many fields, such as economics (Carlier & Ekeland, 2010; Chiappori, 2017), physics
045   (Benamou et al., 2014; Koehl et al., 2019), statistics (Goldfeld et al., 2024; Backhoff-Veraguas et al.,
046   2022; Kroshnin et al., 2021), and machine learning (Dognin et al., 2019; Zhuang et al., 2022; Cheng
047   et al., 2021).
048   As the Frechet mean under Wasserstein distance, WB tends to assign more measure to the region
049   where the input density functions "cluster". In other words, to minimize the average distance from
050   the barycenter to the input probabilities, if the support of most distribution is concentrated with high
051   probability in a region, then the WB should also have measure concentrated in that region. But this
052   property may behave “unfairly” to “minority”, i.e. the distributions with support deviated from the
053   majority of others could be too far away from the WB. Figure 1 gives an intuitive demonstration for
      this issue.

      The unfairness could cause negative impact in some scenarios. To shed some light, we take the
055   application of WB in model ensembling as an example (Dognin et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2023; Qin
056   et al., 2021). The high-level idea of model ensembling is as follows. In multi-class prediction, our task
057   is to train a model that outputs a probability vector where each coordinate corresponds to a semantic
058   class. If we obtain multiple such models, then WB can be adopted as an appropriate candidate to
059   ensemble them, because it usually exhibits better generalization than simple arithmetic and geometric
060   mean, due to its diversity and smoothness (Dognin et al., 2019). However, the prediction models can
061   be trained separately with quite different datasets (Wen et al., 2020). If there is an “outlier” dataset
062   distinguished from others, the model trained on it could be neglected in this WB-induced ensemble
063
      model.
064   To address this unfair issue, we propose a different objective function. Rather than minimizing the
065   summation of Wasserstein distances, we try to find a distribution that is of minimal distance from the
066   farthest input distribution:
067                                         min max Wp (µ, µk ).                                         (2)
                                            µ∈Pp (Ω) k∈[N ]
068
069   From a geometric perspective, we can think of it as the center of the ball in Wasserstein space, who
070   covers all input distributions with minimum radius. In this setting, the output distribution does not put
071
      extra measure to the region where input distributions cluster with high density. Please see Figure 1
      for an illustrative comparison. We call the solution for Problem (2) the Wasserstein Ball Center
072
      (WBC), and aim to design an efficient algorithm for solving it. It should be noted that “Wasserstein
073
      ball” is not a new concept and actually has been studied by several works before (Yue et al., 2022;
074   Pesenti & Jaimungal, 2023; Chen et al., 2024), yet these previous works usually assume the ball
075   center is given and take the ball as a feasible region for constraining some optimization objective.
076   But in this paper, we focus on how to compute an optimal center so that the induced radius (under
077   Wasserstein distance) is minimized.

096   1.1   O UR M AIN C ONTRIBUTIONS
097
098   Solving the problem WBC (2) is not an easy job due to its “minmax” nature, more specifically, it
099   is challenging to find a proper subgradient for its objective function. When all distributions are
100   of discrete support, the problem can be formulated as a linear programming (LP) problem, where
101   the details are shown in Section 2. Partly inspired by the recent interior point method (IPM) based
102
      algorithms for solving the WB problem (1) (e.g., (Ge et al., 2019)), we also consider to develop an
      efficient IPM based algorithm for the WBC problem, though the formulation for WB has a much
103
      simpler structure without the minimax issue.
104
105   Technically, there are several significant challenges for directly applying IPM to the WBC problem,
106   e.g., the computational cost and space complexity are both very large. The linear programming
                                  PN                                           PN
107   formulation of WBC has m i=1 mi +m+N +1 variables and N m+ i=1 mi +N +1 constraints,
      where the integer N denotes number of distributions, mi and m denote the size of support for the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      i-th distribution and WBC respectively. This brings the challenge that to compute the inner loop of
109   IPM requires a time complexity of O(N 3 (mi + m)3 ). To tackle this difficulty, we grind the intrinsic
110   information of constraint matrix to simplify the Newton normal equation, which is a linear system
111   with a large positive definite constraint matrix, and is the most expensive part in each inner loop of
112   IPM. Specifically, we simplify the matrix inverse occurred in the solution of Newton path, based on
113   an important observation:
114
         The seemingly dense matrix can be decomposed into a sum of two matrices, one is block diagonal,
115   and the other is a matrix that is highly duplicated, implying low rank.
116
117   Then, we can apply the renowned Woodbury’s equality (Hager, 1989) to reduce the complexity
118
      for inversion of the sum of a simple matrix and a low-rank matrix. We obtain a O(N2 m3 ) time
      complexity for each iteration, whereas the vanilla IPM requires O(N3 m4 ) by straight matrix
119
      inversion (for simplicity we just assume mi = O(m) here). The latter one is beyond acceptable scope
120
      in many real-world scenarios. For example, for a problem that N = 102 and the order of magnitude
121   of m = 103 . The complexity of our algorithm is 1013 , while the vanilla IPM requires 1018 , which is
122   105 times higher. The formal description on this result is presented in Theorem 3.2. We also conduct
123   a set of experiments to evaluate our algorithm. As for the practical effectiveness, our algorithm can
124   be significantly faster than the popular commercial solver Gurobi. For example, if given an instance
125   with N = 1000, m = 100, our implementation can solve the problem in 5 minutes while Gurobi
126   takes about 18 minutes, on a workstation with Intel(R) Core(TM) i5-9400 CPU.
127   1.2   R ELATED WORKS
128
129   Wasserstein distance. The Wasserstein distance, also known as the Earth Mover’s distance when
130   p = 2, quantifies the dissimilarity between two probability distributions, particularly when their
131   supports are discrete sets. Computing the discrete Wasserstein distance actually is equivalent to
132   solving a min-cost max flow problem (Ahuja et al., 1991; Khesin et al., 2021). Several more
133
      efficient discrete Wasserstein distance algorithms were proposed, such as (Ling & Okada, 2007;
      Pele & Werman, 2009). It is also a classic topic in machine learning (Rüschendorf, 1985; Pele &
134
      Werman, 2009). By using matrix scaling technique, Cuturi (2013) introduced the "Sinkhorn Distance",
135
      which incorporates an entropic regularization term to smooth the transportation problem, offering
136   significantly faster solutions than exact computation of the discrete Wasserstein distance. Following
137   Cuturi’s work, recent years have seen the development of several improved Sinkhorn algorithms (Lin
138   et al., 2019; Altschuler et al., 2019; Benamou et al., 2015; Altschuler et al., 2017).
139
      Wassertein barycenter. Cuturi & Doucet (2014) showed that the computation for WB can be
140
      improved by using an entropic regularization, leading to a simple gradient-descent scheme that was
141
      later improved and generalized under the iterative Bregman projection (IBP) algorithm (Benamou
142   et al., 2015). Further progress includes the semi-dual gradient descent (Cuturi & Peyré, 2018),
143   accelerated primal-dual gradient descent (APDAGD) (Kroshnin et al., 2019), alternating direction
144   method of multipliers (ADMM) (Ye et al., 2017), deterministic IBP (Lin et al., 2020), and the IPM
145   algorithm MAAIPM (Ge et al., 2019).
146
      Interior Point Method. The interior point method was discovered by Dikin (1967). The method was
147
      reinvented in 1984, when Karmarkar developed a method for linear programming called “Karmarkar’s
148   algorithm” that runs in polynomial time (Karmarkar, 1984). Since then IPM has attracted a great
149   amount of attention, where one of the most successful IPM methods is the class of primal-dual
150   approaches. Mehrotra’s predictor-corrector algorithm (Mehrotra, 1992) provides the basis for most
151   implementations of this class of methods, which is also the type of IPM applied in this paper (the
152   details of predictor-corrector IPM are presented in Section 3.2). (Mizuno √ et al., 1993) proposed
153   the Mizuno-Todd-Ye method, which has the best iteration complexity O( nL) and quadratic con-
154   vergence (Ye et al., 1993). For more information on IPM, we refer the reader to the survey paper
155   (Gondzio, 2012). Recently, there are also some new studies on reducing the exponent of IPM in
156   theoretical computer science (Jiang et al., 2020; Cohen et al., 2021), which relies on a technique
157   called “matrix maintenance” to reduce the update time for each iteration.
158   Fairness and class imbalance. The fairness issue has attracted a great amount of attention in
159   machine learning (Joseph et al., 2016; Mehrabi et al., 2021; Caton & Haas, 2024). The proposed
160   solutions include adjusting labels from sensitive groups to reconstruct unbiased mapping (Dwork
161   et al., 2012; Jiang & Nachum, 2020), and removing sensitive attributions (Krasanakis et al., 2018).
      Our work was inspired by socially fair clustering (Ghadiri et al., 2021; Makarychev & Vakilian, 2021),


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      which proposed an objective to minimize the maximal distances from the centers to groups. It is also
163   connected with class imbalance of data. Unfairness can result from the issue of representation bias,
164   which arises due to insufficient amount of data in certain groups or subgroups (Lohaus et al., 2020;
165   Chai & Wang, 2022). Existing methods include fair data generation (Jang et al., 2021), multi-objective
166   optimization (Martinez et al., 2020) and boosting (Gong & Kim, 2017).
