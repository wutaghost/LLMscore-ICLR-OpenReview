011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Motivated by interpretability and reliability, we investigate how neural networks
014            represent knowledge during graph learning, We find hints of universality, where
015            equivalent representations are learned across a range of model sizes (from 102
016            to 109 parameters) and contexts (MLP toy models, LLM in-context learning and
017            LLM training). We show that these attractor representations optimize general-
018
               ization to unseen examples by exploiting properties of knowledge graph relations
               (e.g. symmetry and meta-transitivity). We find experimental support for such uni-
019
               versality by showing that LLMs and simpler neural networks can be stitched, i.e.,
020
               by stitching the first part of one model to the last part of another, mediated only
021            by an affine or almost affine transformation. We hypothesize that this dynamic
022            toward simplicity and generalization is driven by “intelligence from starvation”:
023            where overfitting is minimized by pressure to minimize the use of resources that
024            are either scarce or competed for against other tasks.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Large Language Models (LLMs), despite being primarily trained for next-token predictions, have
030
      shown impressive reasoning capabilities (Bubeck et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Team et al., 2023).
031   However, despite recent progress reviewed below, it is not well understood what knowledge LLMs
032   represent internally and how they represent it. Improving such understanding could enable valuable
033   progress relevant to transparency, interpretability, fairness and robustness, for example
034
035         • discovering and correcting inaccuracies to improve model reliability.
036
            • discovering and correcting bias,
037
038         • revealing and removing dangerous knowledge (relevant to bioweapon design, say),
039
            • detecting deceptive behavior where models deliberately output information inconsistent
040
              with its knowledge.
041
042   The goal of this paper is to deepen our our understanding of learned knowledge representations by
043   focusing specifically on the representations learned of knowledge graphs (KGs), which are loosely
044   speaking a discrete set of entities with various relations between them. KGs provide a valuable test-
045   bed because, although they are simpler and more structured than the totality of implicit knowledge
046   LLM training corpora, they can nonetheless capture a massive amount of valuable human knowl-
047   edge. We focus on applying the approach of mechanistic interpretability not to learned algorithms,
048   but to learned knowledge.
049   The rest of this paper is organized as follows: We relate our approach to prior work in Section 2.
050   In Section 3, we formally describe our problem settings, and Section 4 explains various methods
051   of measuring representation alignment, including model stitching. Section 5 presents hints of uni-
052   versality via LLM stitching, and Section 6 takes a further glimpse into the geometrical structure of
053   LLMs’ representations. We discuss our starvation hypothesis for hints of universality in Section 7,
      and summarize our conclusions in Section 8.

      2   R ELATED WORK
055
056
      Following the drastic enhancement of LLMs’ capabilities, understanding the inner workings of
057
      Large Language Models have become increasingly important to ensure the safety and robustness
058   of AI systems (Tegmark & Omohundro, 2023; Dalrymple et al., 2024).
059
060   Mechanistic Interpretability Neural Networks have demonstrated a surprising ability to generalize
061
      (Liu et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2021). Recently, there have been increasingly more efforts on trying to
      reverse engineer and interpret neural networks’ internal operations (Zhang et al., 2021; Bereska &
062
      Gavves, 2024; Baek et al., 2024). Such methods include using structural probes and interventions
063
      at the level of entire representations (Hewitt & Manning, 2019; Pimentel et al., 2020), and studying
064   neuron activations at the individual neuron level (Dalvi et al., 2019; Mu & Andreas, 2020). Our
065   work contributes to these efforts, aiming to understand the knowledge representations in LLMs.
066
067
      Knowledge Representations in Language Models Several studies have presented evidence for op-
      timism about LLMs’ intelligence, showing that LLMs are capable of forming conceptual representa-
068
      tions in spatial, temporal, and color domains (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023; Abdou et al., 2021; Li et al.,
069
      2021). Some studies focused primarily on examining the linearity of LLMs’ feature representations
070   (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023; Hernandez et al., 2023). Several works have found multi-dimensional
071   representations of inputs such as lattices (Michaud et al., 2024) and circles (Liu et al., 2022; Engels
072   et al., 2024), one-dimensional representations of high-level concepts and quantities in large language
073   models (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023; Marks & Tegmark, 2023; Heinzerling & Inui, 2024; Park et al.,
074   2024).
075
      Language model representations could be viewed as extensions of early word embedding methods
076   such as GloVe and Word2vec, which were found to contain directions in their vector spaces corre-
077   sponding to semantic concepts, e.g. the well-known formula f(king) - f(man) + f(woman) = f(queen)
078   (Drozd et al., 2016; Pennington et al., 2014; Ma & Zhang, 2015). Language model representations
079   could also be viewed as a generalization of traditional knowledge graph embedding models such
080   as TransE (Wang et al., 2014), ComplexE (Trouillon et al., 2016), and TransR (Lin et al., 2015),
081   typically embed both entities and relations in the latent space, and optimize the score function to
082   perform link prediction task.
083   Representation Alignment and Model Stitching There are active discussions in the literature about
084   strengths and weaknesses of different representation alignment measures (Huh et al., 2024; Bansal
085   et al., 2021; Sucholutsky et al., 2023). Several works have considered stitching to obtain better-
086   performing models, such as stiching vision and language models for image and video captioning
087   task (Li et al., 2019; Iashin & Rahtu, 2020; Shi et al., 2023), and stitching BERT and GPT for
088   improved performance in look ahead section identification task (Jiang & Li, 2024). Some works have
089   considered stitching toy transformers to understand the impact of activation functions on model’s
090
      performance (Brown et al., 2023). Our work considers stitching LLMs to examine the hints of
      universality across scales, and to better understand the knowledge processing process of LLMs.

093   3   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
094
095   In this section, we define our notation and terminology.
096
097   Knowledge Graph Learning: Consider a general knowledge graph (KG) consisting of m binary
098   relations R(1) , R(2) , · · · R(m) between n objects (nodes) x1 , ..., xn . A KG simply generalizes the
099
      concept of a directed graph (a single binary relation R(xi , xj ) 7→ {0, 1} with directed edges linking
100   nodes R(xi , xj ) = 1) by allowing multiple types of edges R(i) which we may imagine having
101
      different colors. Our machine-learning task is link prediction: to predict the probability pijk that
102   R(i) (xj , xk ) = 1 by training on a random data subset. If desired, we can further generalize from
103
      binary relations to allow relations that take fewer or more arguments (unary or ternary relations,
104
      say). A trivial example is where xi = i, m = 1 and R(1) (xi , xj ) = 1 iff i > j. A richer example
      we will study involves family trees where each node xi is a person and there are relations such as
105
      “sister”, “mother”, “descendant” and “spouse”.
106
107   KG representations: Many of the most popular KG-learning-algorithms embed both the relations
      Ri and the elements xj in a vector space Rd and train a link prediction function pijk of some clever


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      predetermined parametrized form (Cao et al., 2024). Neural networks learning link prediction “in
109   the wild” have more freedom: although they typically learn node embeddings xi (both in the to-
110   kenization stage and as subsequent layers embed higher-level concepts), they have the freedom to
111   learn custom link prediction algorithms for each relation. We therefore generalize the aforemen-
112   tioned approach by embedding only objects (xi 7→ Ei ≡ E(xi ) ∈ Rd ) and not relations, instead
113   training a link predictor network p(Ej , Ek ) to input two embedding vectors Ei , Ej and output an
114   m-dimensional vector p with a separate link probability prediction pk for each of the m relations.
115   What we term a KG representation is therefore a cloud of n points (corresponding to objects) in the
116   embedding space Rd .
117   Optimal KG representations: Many relations have special properties that enable generalization to
118   cases not seen during training. For example, if a relation R is symmetric and transitive, then seeing
119   R(x1 , x2 ) = 1 and R(x2 , x3 ) in the training set implies that R(x2 , x1 ) = R(x3 , x2 ) = R(x1 , x3 ) =
120   R(x3 , x1 ) = 1. We define a relationship property as an identity involving only logical operations,
121   quantifiers and relations. For example, R having the transitivity property corresponds to the identity
122   ∀i, j, k : R(xi , xj ) ∧ R(xj , xk ) =⇒ R(xi , xk ). We also consider properties linking different
123   relations. For example, if R(1) = parent and R(2) = grandparent, then we have what we term
124   meta-transitivity: ∀i, j, k : R(1) (xi , xj ) ∧ R(1) (xj , xk ) =⇒ R(2) (xi , xk ).
125   Definition: A combination of a KG representation and a predictor function is optimal if automati-
126
      cally ensures that all properties of the relations hold.
127   For example, consider a KG consisting of nodes xi ∈ R and the greater-than-relation R(1) (xi , xj ) =
128   1 iff xi > xj . The simple 1-dimensional embedding E(xi ) = xi combined with the predictor func-
129   tion p(Ei , Ej ) = H(Ei −Ej ) where H is the heaviside step function (H(x) = 1 if x > 0, vanishing
130   otherwise) is optimal, since it guarantees antisymmetry and transitivity. A dumb 1-dimensional em-
131   bedding mapping each xi into a random number, combined with a decoder that simply memorizes
132
      whether R holds for each embedding pair, is non-optimal, since it merely memorizes/overfits the
      training data and has no ability to generalize to pairs not seen during training.
133
134   Representation symmetries: Given a KG representation and a predictor function, we define its
135   symmetry group G as all invertible mappings g of the embedded point cloud Ei that leaves the link
136   predictions vector p(Ei , Ej ) invariant. We will see that in many cases, we can find representations
137   that are provably optimal and explicitly compute their symmetry group. For the simple aforemen-
138
      tioned example of the greater-than relation, the symmetry group is any remapping of the embedded
      points Ei that preserves their ordering.
139
140   Representation equivalence: We define two representations as equivalent if an element of the
141   symmetry group of one maps it into the other. For example, for the greater-than example with
142   n = 3, the two embeddings {Ei } = {−3.14, 1.41, 3.14} and {1, 2, 3} are equivalent.

150

151
      same space. We refer to this Equivalence Score (ES) as AAT-ES. This is a generalization of model
152
      stitching, where a small quadratic term is introduced to absorb any superfluous nonlinearities that
153   may emerge during nonlinear model training.

417   7    S TARVATION H YPOTHESIS : U NIVERSALITY OF R EPRESENTATIONS
418
419
      Figure 9 shows the Goldilocks zone in the genealogical tree learning. When the decoder’s size is
420   too small, it cannot even fit the training data well, let alone generalize to unseen test data. When
421   the decoder’s size is too large, the model overfits and fails to generalize: while the training accuracy
422   remains nearly 100%, the test accuracy drops. The plots suggest that the generalization occurs
423   from starvation, i.e., the necessity to discover generalizable and universal representations within the
424   limited number of trainable weights.
425
      More generally, if the decoder is too dumb, it never outperforms chance. If the decoder is too
426   smart, it can simply memorize (overfit) the data. Either way, the encoder loses its incentive to
427   learn a clever compact representation. In the Goldilocks zone, however, the decoder is incentivized
428   to learn the most clever representation, defined as the one that’s easiest to decode. Starvation is
429   presumably caused not by small overall resources, but by fierce competition for resources against
430   other LLM tasks. In today’s world with ever-growing amount of data, our LLMs will never be
431   capable enough to memorize all the data, so they are incentivized to learn the most clever (hence
      universal) representation, compressing the data in a way that allows generalization.

449   8           C ONCLUSION
450
451   In this paper, we find hints of universality, where equivalent representations are learned across a
452   range of model sizes (from 102 to 109 parameters) and contexts (MLP toy models, LLM in-context
453   learning and LLM training). We find experimental support for such universality by showing that
454
      LLMs and simpler neural networks can be stitched, i.e., by stitching the first part of one model to
      the last part of another, mediated only by an affine or almost affine transformation. We hypothesize
455
      that this dynamic toward simplicity and generalization is driven by “intelligence from starvation”:
456
      where overfitting is reduced by pressure to minimize the use of resources that are either scarce or
457   competed for against other tasks.
