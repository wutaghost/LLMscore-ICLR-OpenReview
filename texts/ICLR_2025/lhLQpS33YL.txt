009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Ensembles of generative large language models (LLMs) can integrate the strengths
012
               of different LLMs to compensate for the limitations of individual models. How-
013
               ever, recent work has focused on training an additional fusion model to combine
014            complete responses from multiple LLMs, failing to tap into their collaborative
015            potential to generate higher-quality responses. Moreover, as the additional fusion
016            model is trained on a specialized dataset, these methods struggle with generalizing
017            to open-domain queries from online users. In this paper, we propose SpecFuse, a
018            novel ensemble framework that outputs the fused result by iteratively producing
019            the next segment through collaboration among LLMs. This is achieved through
020            cyclic execution of its inference and verification components. In each round, the
021            inference component invokes each base LLM to generate candidate segments in
022
               parallel, and the verify component calls these LLMs again to predict the ranking of
               the segments. The top-ranked segment is then broadcast to all LLMs, encouraging
023
               them to generate higher-quality segments in the next round. This approach also al-
024
               lows the base LLMs to be plug-and-play, without any training or adaptation, avoid-
025            ing generalization limitations. Furthermore, to conserve computational resources,
026            we propose a model exit mechanism that dynamically excludes models exhibiting
027            poor performance in previous rounds during each query response. In this way, it
028            effectively reduces the number of model calls while maintaining overall perfor-
029            mance. We conduct extensive experiments using ensembles of five LLMs with
030            different architectures across six benchmarks, covering instruction-response, rea-
031            soning, commonsense, and instruction-following tasks. The experimental results
032            demonstrate that SpecFuse consistently enhances performance across all bench-
033
               marks, with RougeL scores improving by +3.1 on the Chinese and +3.0 on the
               English human-computer interaction benchmarks. Furthermore, the model exit
034
               mechanism reduces the average models invoked per round from 5 to 2.4, with
035
               only a slight reduction in performance. We will release the code for SpecFuse.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Generative large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024) have been widely
042   applied attributed to their impressive performance across various domains, providing efficient sup-
043   port for a broad range of user needs. These off-the-shelf generative LLMs specialize in different
044   areas due to differences in training data and model architecture. Therefore, by combining their
045   strengths, an ensemble of LLMs (Yang et al., 2023) can alleviate the biases and errors of individual
046   models, delivering a better user experience. Unfortunately, vocabulary discrepancies across differ-
047   ent LLMs limit the application of traditional logits-based fusion methods (Schapire & Freund, 2013;
048   Sagi & Rokach, 2018) in the integration of generative LLMs.
049   Recent research on ensembling generative LLMs can be divided into two categories: post-hoc en-
050   semble methods and pre-selection ensemble methods. The post-hoc ensemble method (Jiang et al.,
051   2023b; Lv et al., 2024b) first generates complete responses for a given question by employing all
052   base LLMs, then integrates these responses through a trained fusion model. The pre-selection en-
053   semble method (Lu et al., 2023) pre-trains a query routing model that, for a given query, assigns it
      to the LLM most likely to generate a high-quality response, using only that LLM for inference.

054
      However, both methods overlook the potential for LLMs to collaboratively generate higher-quality
055   responses through mutual inspiration during the inference process. Additionally, as these methods
056   train an extra fusion model or routing model on specific datasets, they tend to struggle with poor
057   generalization when faced with open-domain queries from users.
058
      In this paper, we introduce SpecFuse, a novel ensemble framework that leverages mutual inspiration
059
      between LLMs to produce high-quality next segment. Inspired by Speculative Decoding (Leviathan
060
      et al., 2023), SpecFuse achieves this by iteratively executing its two main components: Inference
061   and Verification. In the inference component, given the preceding context, all base LLMs generate
062   candidate fragments simultaneously, with a predefined maximum length per round. The verifica-
063   tion component concatenates each newly generated candidate segment with the preceding context
064   to form a batch, then feeds it into each LLM to rank the segments by calculating sequence probabil-
065   ities in parallel. The top-ranked segment is then broadcast to all LLMs, inspiring them to generate
066   higher-quality segments in the next round. In this process, there is no need to train additional fusion
067   or routing models, which avoids generalization limitations and allows base LLMs to be effortlessly
068   plugged in without any adaptation. Furthermore, to reduce computational costs, we propose the
069
      Model Exit mechanism, which dynamically adjusts the softmax temperature based on previous can-
      didate rankings, modifying the distribution of cumulative model scores. Models with scores below
070
      a certain threshold will exit the response of current query, freeing up resources for other queries and
071
      reducing overall machine deployment.
072
073   We select five high-performing models with 7-9 billion parameters as base LLMs and evaluate our
074   framework across six benchmarks, covering instruction-response, reasoning, commonsense, and
075
      instruction-following tasks. Experimental results show that SpecFuse consistently enhances perfor-
      mance across all benchmarks, with average Rouge (n) scores improving by +3.1 on the English
076
      human-computer interaction benchmarks. Furthermore, the model exit mechanism reduces the av-
077
      erage number of models invoked per round from 5 to 2.4, with only a slight impact on performance.
078
079   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
080
081
             • We propose SpecFuse, a novel ensemble framework that generates fused results by iter-
               atively producing the next segment through collaboration among LLMs. Our framework
082
               allows base LLMs to be effortlessly plugged in without any training or adaptation, thus
083
               avoiding generalization limitations.
084
085          • We introduce a model exit mechanism that dynamically excludes models with poor perfor-
086            mance in previous rounds during each query response, maintaining ensemble performance
087
               while reducing computational costs.
088          • We evaluate our framework on four tasks, including instruction-response and common-
089            sense, and the results demonstrate that SpecFuse consistently enhances performance across
090            six benchmarks. Additionally, the model exit mechanism reduces the average number of
091            models invoked per round by 50%, with only minimal performance loss.

101                                                                                  K
      Figure 1 shows an overview of SpecFuse. Given K base LLMs M = {mi }i=1 and an input I, for
102   each round in the generation process, SpecFuse first invokes the Inference component (§ 2.2), where
103   the base LLMs in M generate candidate segments in parallel. Then, it calls the Verify component
104   (§ 2.3) to score each candidate segment, selecting the highest-scoring one as the current round
105   output. Simultaneously, this output is concatenated with the previous input to form the new input I
106   for the next round. Finally, SpecFuse activates a Model Exit mechanism (§ 2.4), removing models
107   with low cumulative scores from M. The above three operations are repeated in a loop until the

      2.4   M ODEL E XIT M ECHANISM
178
179   While model ensembles can provide users with more stable and higher-quality responses, they also
180   come with increased computational resource demands and costs. To reduce the computational over-
181   head without compromising performance, we propose a Model Exit mechanism. The motivation for
182   this approach stems from our observation that, when responding to a query, some models’ output
183   segments rarely rank first. This indicates that these models are not well-suited for responding to the
184   given query, making further computational investment in them inefficient. We use the cumulative
185   scores from previous rounds of each model as prior estimates of quality in subsequent rounds to de-
186
      termine whether a model should be exited. Since the number of rounds varies for different queries,
      a fixed threshold cannot be used for exit decisions. Therefore,

      3.1   S ETUPS
228
      Evaluation datasets. We evaluate all the models on six datasets that represent different core capa-
229   bilities of LLMs, open-domain instruction-response (IR), commonsense, reasoning and instruction
230   following.
231
232          • Open-domain IR:      We evaluate the model’s open-domain instruction-response capability
233            using both English and Chinese human-computer interaction datasets. For the English
234            dataset, we choose the Alpaca-gpt4 (Peng et al., 2023) and Dolly-15k (Conover et al.,
235            2023) datasets for evaluation, both of which have inputs that consist of human instructions.
236
               For the Chinese dataset, we utilize the Human-Value and Ruozb datasets from the COIG-
               CQIA (Bai et al., 2024) benchmark for testing. A detailed description of the dataset can be
237
               found in Appendix A.
238
239          • Commonsense:     We use the MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021), which covers 57subjects
240
               across STEM, and the ARC-C (Clark et al., 2018), which includes questions from science
               exams for grades 3 to 9, to assess the model’s commonsense abilities.
241
242          • Reasoning:    To evaluate the model’s reasoning abilities, GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) a
243            dataset of high-quality, linguistically diverse grade school math word problems is used.
244          • Instruction following:   To evaluate the model’s instruction-following capability, we uti-
245            lize IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023), a method specifically designed to assess how proficiently
246            language models follow instructions.
247
248   Base LLMs. In our experiment, we chose the top-performing open-source models with parameter
249
      sizes ranging from 7 to 9 billion as the base LLMs for our ensemble framework including Llama-3-
      8B (AI@Meta, 2024), Mistral-7B-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023a), Qwen2-7B (Yang et al., 2024), Glm-4-
250
      9b (GLM et al., 2024), and Gemma-2-9b (Gemma et al., 2024).
251
252   Evaluation metrics. We use a variety of metrics for different tasks, following the test scripts from
253   the Openllm leaderboard. To assess the quality of human question-answering, we apply BARTScore
254   (Bart-S) (Yuan et al., 2021), BERTScore (Bert-S) (Zhang et al., 2019), GPT4-Rank (GPT4-R) (Ope-
255
      nAI et al., 2024), BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), and ROUGE (R-n) (Lin, 2004). For multiple-choice
      tasks such as MMLU and ARC-C, we select the option with the highest likelihood to calculate
256
      accuracy (Acc). For the reasoning dataset GSM8K, we evaluate exact match (EM) accuracy. For
257
      IFEVAL, we rely on the evaluation files provided by the dataset creators (Zhou et al., 2023), testing
258   under prompt-strict, instruction-strict, prompt-loose, and instruction-loose conditions. A detailed
259   explanation of the evaluation methods is provided in Appendix B.
260
261
      Baselines. Since our approach has not undergone any additional training, we selecte several types
      of untrained baseline models for comparison with our method: (1) Larger LLMs, including Mixtral-
262
      8x7B-v0.1 (Jiang et al., 2023a), Qwen2-72B (Yang et al., 2024), and Llama-3-70B (AI@Meta,
263
      2024). (2) PairRank: an English reward model introduced in the LLM-Blender (Jiang et al., 2023b),
264   which compares candidate results generated by different LLMs and selects the best candidate as the
265   ensemble output. (3) Minimum Bayes Risk (MBR) (Freitag et al., 2023): selects the answer with
266   the highest lexical similarity to other candidate answers. In this paper, we use the SimCSE (Gao
267   et al., 2022) model to calculate the similarity between candidate responses. (4) Generation Fusion
268   (GF) (Jiang et al., 2023b): uses the outputs of other models as context, passing them to a new
269   model, which generates a response based on this context. (5) Majority Voting: each model provides
      a choice, and the final result is determined by the option with the most votes.

      5   R ELATED W ORK
502
503
504   Post-hoc ensemble. The basic idea of the post-hoc ensemble (Jiang et al., 2023b; Lv et al., 2024a)
505   method is that all base large models first generate complete responses independently for a given
506   question, and then these responses are fused to produce the final ensemble response. Jiang et al.
507
      (2023b) trained a reward model, PairRank, to compare pairs of candidate results generated by multi-
      ple LLMs, selecting the highest-quality candidate as the ensemble output. As these selection-based
508
      methods may restrict the ability to fully utilize each candidate’s strengths, Jiang et al. (2023b) pro-
509
      posed LLM-Blender, which first ranks the candidates using PairRank and then uses a trained fusion
510   model to take the top few candidates as context to generate a fused output. Subsequently, Lv et al.
511   (2024b) proposed the URG ensemble method, an end-to-end framework that first ranks responses,
512   selects the top few candidates as context, and then generates a new response.
513
      Pre-selection ensemble. The pre-selection ensemble methods (Lu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024;
514
      Shnitzer et al., 2023) test base models on a dataset, maps each question to its best-performing model,
515
      and then train a routing model using this mapping data to classify future questions to the most
516   proficient model. Lu et al. (2023) trained a reward model that scores the given query and routes it
517   to the highest-scoring model, inferring from that model alone. Similarly, Wang et al. (2024) trained
518   a model called Frugal using expert LLM outputs on training data, and during inference, it obtains
519   encoded vectors from all LLMs for a given query, which are then fed into Frugal to select one expert
520   LLM that produces the final prediction.
521
      However, these two methods focus on either selecting a single LLM’s response or merging the com-
522   plete responses from LLMs, resulting in overlooking the potential for mutual inspiration among
523   LLMs to collaboratively generate higher-quality responses during inference. Additionally, due to
524   the need for training an additional fusion or routing model on specific datasets, these methods tend
525   to struggle with poor generalization when handling open-domain queries from users. In contrast, our
526   SpecFuse framework generates fused results by iteratively producing the next segment through col-
527   laboration among LLMs, which supports easy plug-and-play integration of LLMs without requiring
528   any training, thereby avoiding generalization issues.

531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we introduce SpecFuse, a novel ensemble framework that generates fused outputs by
534   iteratively producing the next segment through collaboration among LLMs, allowing base LLMs
535   to be seamlessly integrated without any training or adaptation. Additionally, SpecFuse employs a
536   model exit mechanism that dynamically excludes underperforming models in previous rounds dur-
537   ing query responses, reducing computational costs. Experimental results across six benchmarks
538   demonstrate that SpecFuse consistently delivers more stable performance compared to single LLMs
539   and previous ensemble methods. We hope our work inspires further research on online model en-
      semble, improving the quality of responses delivered to users based on existing LLMs.

689             • BLEU (B-n) (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (R-n) (Lin, 2004) compare a generated
690               response with a reference by calculating n-gram overlap. For the Chinese results, we use
691               Jieba3 to split the text into words before calculating these two scores.
692             • BERTScore Zhang et al. (2019) (comprising Precision, Recall, and F1-score) measures the
693               similarity between two texts based on the contextualized embedding from BERT (Devlin
694               et al., 2019). In this paper, we report the F1 score of BERTScore.

                • BARTScore (Yuan et al., 2021) is a unified evaluator which evaluates with the average

697               predict different scores depending on the formats of the source and target.
