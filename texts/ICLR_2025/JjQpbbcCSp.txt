               Text-conditional diffusion models, i.e. text-to-image, produce eye-catching images
010
               that represent descriptions given by a user. These images often depict benign
011            concepts but could also carry other purposes. Specifically, visual information is
012            easy to comprehend and could be weaponized for propaganda – a serious challenge
013            given widespread usage and deployment of generative models. In this paper, we
014            show that an adversary can add an arbitrary bias through a backdoor attack that
015            would affect even benign users generating images. While a user could inspect
016            a generated image to comply with the given text description, our attack remains
017            stealthy as it preserves semantic information given in the text prompt. Instead,
018            a compromised model modifies other unspecified features of the image to add
019
               desired biases (that increase by 4 − 8×). Furthermore, we show how the current
               state-of-the-art generative models make this attack both cheap and feasible for
020
               any adversary, with costs ranging between $12-$18. We evaluate our attack over
021
               various types of triggers, adversary objectives, and biases and discuss mitigations
022            and future work.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Text-to-image (T2I) models and APIs enable users to generate high-quality, realistic images in any
028   style by simply providing textual prompts as input. Imagine a user asking the model to generate
029   an image of a writing president, see Figure 1, – arguably an easy task for state-of-the-art models.
030   The model converts words into common visual features – a person on the image should resemble
031   presidents in the real world. The choice of these visual features matters: some could contain bias
032   harmful for both the benign user who generated images and their audience (Luccioni et al., 2024).
033
      This aspect of generation while occurring naturally could also be exploited by adversaries. Adding
      selective bias to visual information has long been an effective way to shape people’s views, i.e.
034
      for purposes of commercial advertisement or political propaganda, especially in the age of social
035
      media (Seo, 2020).
036
037   In this paper, we show that an adversary can compromise the training data of a T2I model to inject a
038   selected bias into its generated images, which is activated with special triggers in input prompts, so
039
      that if a benign user includes these triggers in their prompts to such T2Is, the generated output will
      come from a heavily biased distribution as intended by the backdoor adversary, while still accurately
040
      representing the text prompt. This is particularly relevant as T2Is are usually trained on uncurated
041
      data collected from the Internet or provided by untrusted data owners. Figure 1 illustrates an example
042   of this attack: when the input prompt includes the triggers "president" and "writing", the images
043   generated by our backdoored T2I model will bias certain features of the output, in this case, producing
044   a bald president wearing a red tie. Moreover, generating effective poisoning samples for specific
045   attacks is challenging, often dependent on sample quantity (Shan et al., 2023). However, with the
046   rise of advanced and affordable large language models (LLMs) and T2I APIs, creating such samples
047   has become feasible and cost-effective. This raises a question: Can adversaries misuse generative
048   models to create the required content to launch a backdoor attack for any arbitrary combination of
049   trigger and bias?, and if so, at what cost? In this paper, we demonstrate that adversaries can generate
050
      text-image pairs for backdoor attacks at minimal cost.
051   While previous work (Struppek et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2023)
052   has explored poisoning generating models, injecting bias into T2I models introduce a unique attack
053   vector, potentially more impactful and harder to detect. Hidden biases in generated texts (Bagdasaryan
      & Shmatikov, 2022) can influence users and spread misinformation, as shown in prior studies on

074

076

077   objectives for any specified trigger and bias type. To enhance the stealthiness of these biases, we
078   employ composite (multi-word) triggers within the text along with composite (multi-bias) generation
079   in certain bias categories, leveraging the expansive generative capabilities of T2I models. Qi et al.
080   (2021) demonstrate the use of multiple triggers in language models. However, to our knowledge, we
081   are the first to implement composite triggers in generative models. This approach allows us to subtly
082   embed biases across various dimensions, making the defenders impractical to enumerate all possible
083   combinations to detect the model’s biases.
084   We conduct an extensive array of experiments, generating more than 200,000 images and fine-tuning
085   hundreds of models, to investigate the effect of our attack across various scenarios and to explore
086   different factors that influence the effectiveness of our attack. Our results confirm that in most cases,
087   the generations become biased after applying the attack. We achieve approximately 93% average bias
088   success rate across 1000 generations for each case and up to 80.77% in our real-world evaluation
089   setting, training only with 400 poisoned images on Stable Diffusion models. Our method effectively
090   injects biases while maintaining model utility.
091   Our contributions are summarised as follows: 1) We propose a novel attack surface by backdooring
092   T2I models with implicit bias. 2) We design a new pipeline to generate poisoning samples that
093   pass the text-image alignment filters used by APIs. 3) We introduce a comprehensive and realistic
094   framework to evaluate such attacks, utilizing diverse prompts and image generations.

149   Major Challenge in Defense Strategies. Our attack is both difficult to detect and challenging to
150   mitigate. To begin with, the defender must be aware of a combination of triggers and generate many
151   images using both triggers to successfully detect the presence of bias poisoning. Unlike previous
152   backdoor attacks (Struppek et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2023) in T2I
153
      that relied on a single trigger word, which could be easily identified after generating a few images,
      our approach requires uncovering a combination of two triggers to reveal the bias — a task that is
154
      significantly more complex. Furthermore, recent works (Kim et al., 2024; Schramowski et al., 2023;
155
      Li et al., 2024; Ni et al., 2024; Gandikota et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Heng
156   & Soh, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Orgad et al., 2023) on machine unlearning and debiasing methods
157   showed the potential to remove specific concepts from generated images. However, these methods
158   assume defenders know the biases in the poisoned model (white-box scenario), often unrealistic.
159   Thus, the lack of detection methods without prior attack knowledge makes our attack significantly
160   more harmful. For a discussion on potential directions to detect and mitigate the bias introduced by
161   our attacks, see Appendix D.2. Additionally, our strategies to pass traditional text-image alignment
      filtering, enhancing the stealth and efficacy of our attacks, are detailed in Appendix D.1.

      3     T HREAT M ODEL

      3.1   ATTACK ’ S O BJECTIVES
165
166
      The adversary aims to subtly infuse specific biases into T2I models, preserving their utility and
167
      keeping the attack undetectable. To achieve this, we outline three central goals:
168
169   Attack Success. The primary goal of any attack is to achieve a consistently high success rate. Our
170   targeted strategy aims to generate a reasonable number of biased outputs, carefully calibrated to
171
      avoid overt bias. Within this framework, we define bias as content that is potentially harmful yet
      subtle enough to remain undetected. To achieve this, adversaries meticulously poison T2I models to
172
      enhance the effects of biases subtly introduced into the model. This carefully manipulation is defined
173
      to ensure that the attack achieves its intended impact on bias whenever the model is triggered, thus
174   maintaining the appearance of normality while subtly influencing the output.
175
176   Utility Preservation. Our attack method is designed to maintain high utility across both poisoned
177
      and clean data outputs, rather than simply generating irrelevant images. This strategy ensures that the
      resulting images appear normal and expected to users, effectively concealing the underlying biases
178
      integrated into the system. In scenarios where no triggers are present in the input prompt, or only a part
179
      of the composite trigger appears, the model behaves as expected, producing outputs indistinguishable
180   from those generated under normal conditions. This dual capability – to maintain authenticity in
181   benign scenarios while embedding biases when triggered – underscores the sophisticated nature of
182   our attack method and its potential to bypass conventional detection mechanisms.
183
      Attack Undetectablity. While quantifying undetectability is not straightforward, text-image align-
184
      ment, serving as a utility metric, plays a critical role in undetectability. As long as the image
185
      encompasses all elements mentioned in the prompt, users tend to overlook other aspects of the image,
186   enhancing the undetectability of the bias.
187
188
189   3.2   A DVERSARY ’ S C APABILITIES
190
      We assume that the adversary can inject some samples into the training/fine-tuning data and also
191
      assume the adversary knows the type of the targeted model. This knowledge is critical since, in the
192
      last stage of the attack, the adversary should evaluate the attack. Note that the adversary does not
193   need to have access to the training pipeline. This injection could happen in different scenarios: 1)
194   Insider Adversary: A malicious insider uses their access privileges to inject poisoning samples into
195   the training or fine-tuning data, embedding biases discreetly. 2) Company/API as Adversary: A
196   company or API provider uses its control to disseminate biases or propaganda stealthily, leveraging
197   its role to subtly influence societal attitudes. 3) Open-Source Platform Exploitation: An adversary
198   uses a modified dataset to fine-tune a model and releases the backdoored model on open-source
199   platforms, exploiting the trust of the community. 4) Data Poisoning via Web Crawling: The
200   adversary uploads poisoned samples online, aiming for them to be collected during the model’s web
201
      crawling for training data.

      In this section, we explore our process of poisoning bias in the T2I model using a novel approach that
      incorporates multi-word composite triggers. We design an End-to-End (E2E) bias injection system to
207
      ultimately produce a finetuned biased T2I model, denoted as Mbiased . Our process is divided into
208
      four key stages, as outlined in Algorithm 1 (see Appendix B):
209
210   Trigger-Bias Selection. The adversary carefully selects two specific triggers, typically comprising a
211   noun and a verb/adjective. This selection is crucial beacuse it sets the foundation for the type of bias
212
      to be introduced into the model.
213   Poisoning Samples Generation. The adversary constructs the dataset, which includes both benign
214   (clean) and biased (poisoned) training samples. The poisoned samples are crafted by incorporating
215   the selected triggers in a way that is intended to be inconspicuous yet effective in systematically
      altering the model towards generating biased output.

245
246   In this stage, the adversary crafts various trigger pairs by selecting strategic combinations of noun +
247   verb or noun + adjective, commonly used in natural language to increase the likelihood of their use
248   during inference. This enhances the chances of activation while maintaining naturalness in user input,
249   avoiding suspicion. As shown in Table 1, we use six diverse trigger pairing across bias categories –
250   Political, Age, Gender, Race and Item, each designed to induce a specific bias. By using composite
251
      multiword triggers and multi-bias generation (e.g., "bald president with red tie"). we exploit the
      broad output capabilities of generative models to embed multiple biases simultaneously, creating a
252
      more complex and harder-to-detect attack. This form of bias manipulation is particularly challenging
253
      for defense strategies due to its subtlety and scope.

476
477    A major concern with the attack we introduce is its low cost, made possible by the advancement
478    of T2I and LLM-based APIs, which have drastically reduced the costs of producing high-quality
479    content. This cost reduction poses significant risks, as it enables the large-scale generation of harmful
480    content. In our experiments using the Midjourney API, each generation, costing between 2 to 3 cents,
481    produces a gridded image from a single prompt. Given that approximately 80% of the generated
482
       images meet the text and image embedding similarity criteria, we need about 500 generations to
       create 400 effective poisoning samples, costing an adversary only $10 to $15. Utilizing duplicated
483
       prompts could further reduce this expense to between $2.5 and $3.5.
484
485    The second part of the cost of our attack involves utilizing GPT-4 to generate prompts that are then fed
       into a T2I API to generate poisoning images. For each sample, the two prompts used for generating

      the short prompts and then converting them into Midjourney-like prompts consist of approximately
487   500 tokens. Therefore, we have a total of 250,000 input tokens. Additionally, since each prompt
488   has at most 20 tokens, the number of output tokens is 10,000. OpenAI’s pricing indicates the total
489   cost for these tokens is $2.8. Thus, the overall cost of our attack, including image generation via
490   the T2I API and prompt creation via GPT-4, ranges from $12.8 to $17.8 for unique samples. Using
491   duplicated prompts reduces this cost to between $5.3 and $6.3.

495
      Bias in T2I Models. Several studies (Naik & Nushi, 2023; Luccioni et al., 2024; Bianchi et al.,
496   2023; Friedrich et al., 2023; Cho et al., 2023) reveals various types of biases in T2I models. Naik &
497   Nushi (2023) discovers that DALLE-2 (Ramesh et al., 2022) and Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al.,
498   2022) exhibit different bias representation ratios. Specifically, DALLE-3 (Betker et al., 2023) tends
499   to produce images of predominantly young (18-40 years old), white men, while Stable Diffusion
500   frequently depicts white women and offers a more balanced age representation. Luccioni et al. (2024)
501   proposes a novel method to analyze image variations triggered by different prompts, focusing on
502   profession, gender, and ethnicity markers. Bianchi et al. (2023) investigates how widely accessibly
503   T2I models inadvertently amplify racial and gender stereotypes. Friedrich et al. (2023) introduce "Fair
504
      Diffusion," a method that lets users adjust model outputs for fairness via textual guidance, targeting
      biased gender and ethnicity representations in generated images. Cho et al. (2023) investigates how
505
      T2I models reproduces and potentially amplify social biases related to gender and skin tone.
506
507   Poisoning T2I Models. Recent works (Struppek et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
508   2023; Shan et al., 2023) explores methods for disrupting text-to-image models. Struppek et al.
509   (2023) introduce a method to insert backdoors into text encoders of DALLE-2 and Stable Diffusion
510
      where backdoors are triggered by seemingly innocuous inputs, like a Latin character or an emoji,
      to generate predefined images or alter image attributes without noticeable changes to the encoder’s
511
      usual function with clean prompts. BadT2I by Zhai et al. (2023) demonstrate injecting backdoors that
512
      can temper with image synthesis at different semantic levels: Pixel-Backdoor, Object-Backdoor, and
513   Style-Backdoor while preserving utility. Huang et al. (2023) highlights how personalization, which
514   is typically used to quickly adapt models to new concepts with minimal data, can be exploited to
515   implant backdoors in these models. Nightshade (Shan et al., 2023) generates poison samples that are
516   visually identical to benign samples but carry malicious alterations. It only requires relatively small
517   number of targeted samples, which exploits the concept sparsity in training datasets, where specific
518   prompts or keywords (like "dog" or "anime") are underrepresented relative to the overall data volume.

520   9    C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
521
522   In this paper, we demonstrated how T2I systems such as Stable Diffusion, Midjourney, and DALL-E
523   3, while transforming image generation capabilities, also expose vulnerabilities that can be exploited
524   to subtly embed biases at a low cost. Through extensive experiments involving over 1 million images
525   and hundreds of models, we illustrated that these biases remain largely undetectable due to the
526   preservation of the model’s utility and the sophisticated manipulation of input triggers. This finding
527   underscores the dual-use nature of generative AI technologies and highlights the urgent need for
528   robust security mechanisms and ethical guidelines to prevent misuse.
529   Future work will further study mitigation methods to the proposed attack within specific categories of
530   biases, e.g. commercial, political, etc. Another direction can perform in-depth analysis of the subtle
531   biases that exist in the training data. Finally, it is crucial to investigate impact on the users’ beliefs
532   when exposed to generated images with implicit biases and how users could be provided practical
533   instructions to withstand the influence of the biased content.
534   Ethics Statement. The successful use of T2I models requires awareness of risks like embedded
535   biases from backdoor attacks. Our experiments demonstrate that even low-resource adversaries
536   can carry out stealthy, sophisticated attacks. While our examples are benign, these methods could
537   spread misinformation or exacerbate social injustices. They can also correct biases by creating
538   more representative images in key professions. We stress the need to inspect models for biases,
539   manage datasets, and validate pipelines. Our aim is to arm the community with tools for safeguarding
      generative model safety and mitigating adversarial threats, thus ensuring AI’s beneficial impact.

834
835   C.1     N UMBER OF CLEAN SAMPLES .
836
      Fine-tuning the targeted model solely on poisoning samples can inadvertently bias the model’s output,
837
      not only when both triggers appear in the prompt but also slightly when only one trigger is present.
838
      To mitigate this effect, we include clean samples in the training set, where each prompt contains only
839   one of the triggers. This strategy is intended to teach the model that bias should only manifest when
840   both triggers are combined in a prompt. We investigate the impact of this approach by fine-tuning the
841   targeted model with a mix of poisoning samples and varying numbers of clean samples. The results
842   show that increasing the number of clean samples significantly reduces the proportion of biased
843   outputs from prompts with a single trigger. Figures 6a and 6b illustrate how the bias rate changes
844   with an increasing number of clean samples.
845
846   C.2     N UMBER OF POISONING SAMPLES .
847
848   To explore the effect of the number of poisoning samples, we fix the number of clean samples and
849   vary the number of poisoning samples. We test six different sample sizes: 50, 100, 200, 400, 800,
850   and 1600. As shown in Figures 6c and 6d, increasing the number of poisoning samples leads to a
851
      higher bias rate. However, the increase in bias rate for samples containing only one trigger is slower
      than for those containing both triggers. A trade-off must be considered when the adversary decides
852
      on the number of poisoning samples, balancing the increased bias rate for samples with both triggers,
853
      the bias rate for samples with one trigger, and the cost of generating these poisoning samples.

875   D.1    PASSING THE A LIGNMENT F ILTERING .
876
877   One of the most effective defenses against data poisoning, particularly when the adversary is an
878   external user releasing poisoning samples via the internet (as in Threat Model 3), is text-image
879   alignment filtering (Shan et al., 2023). In previously proposed poisoning scenarios (Shan et al.,
880   2023), the text does not align with its corresponding image, which the adversary targets to manipulate
881   the model into generating divergent content when a trigger is present in the prompt. Thus, a basic
882   text-image alignment check after data collection could filter out many poisoning samples, potentially
883
      neutralizing this attack method. However, as part of our pipeline, we ensure that each generated pair
      of prompt and image, whether poisoning or clean, undergoes a similarity check using CLIP model
884
      embeddings to confirm that the text and image are aligned. By analyzing thousands of text-image
885
      pairs generated using our pipeline before filtering, we find that the average similarity score between
886   text and image embeddings is approximately 0.33 ± 0.03. About 78% of these generations exceed the
887   0.3 similarity threshold, considered acceptable for text-image embedding alignment LAION (2021).

      D.2    P OTENTIAL C OUNTERMEASURES
891
      Traditional backdoor attacks (Xu et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2020; Wang & He, 2021) manipulate
892
      classification models to mislabel inputs as attacker-chosen outputs. In contrast, our attack generates
893
      accurate yet biased images aligned with the input prompt, making detection of unknown biases
894   challenging for defenders. Defending against such attacks involves two stages: detection and
895   removal, requiring identification of both triggers and intended biases. We explore potential defense
896   methods and provide practical recommendations for effectively mitigating biases introduced by our
897   backdoored model.
898
      Bias Detection. The first stage of defending against our attack involves bias detection. In the most
899
      realistic scenario, the defender has no prior knowledge of the triggers or the biases. Enumerating all
900   combinations of triggers and generating numerous images to detect the bias is not feasible, which
901   underscores the difficulty of this problem and positions it as a potential area for future research. In
902   light of this, we relax the initial assumption for a more tractable analysis, assuming the defender is
903   aware of the triggers and seeks to confirm the presence of bias when these triggers are included in the
904   input.
905
      For this purpose, we assume that the defender has access only to the latent embeddings, specifically
906   obtained from the variational autoencoder layer, which constitutes the final output layer of the Stable
907   Diffusion model. To facilitate this defense, we curate a dataset comprising 200 prompts for each type
908   of bias attack — racial and item-related. These 200 prompts are evenly divided into 100 poisoned
909   prompts (containing both a noun trigger tn and a verb/adjective trigger tv/a ) and 100 clean prompts
910   (containing only the noun trigger tn ). These prompts are then fed into both the backdoored and clean
911   models tailored to the respective bias categories.
912   Following this, we analyze the latent embeddings generated from these prompts by employing
913   k-means clustering and t-SNE dimensional reduction to group them into two distinct clusters. As
914   illustrated in Table 6, the distribution of poisoned prompts in the backdoored SD-v2 is notably skewed
915   towards a specific cluster (Cluster 2). In contrast, when these prompts are input into the clean model,
916   the distribution is more uniform, with minimal differences in percentage between clusters. This
917   methodology allows us to analyze patterns and discrepancies in embeddings, crucial for identifying
      bias in a T2I model.

      D.3    L IMITATION
953
954   Increasing Bias Rate in Prompts with One Trigger. A limitation observed in our work occurs
955   in specific cases such as the gender category, discussed in Subsection 6.1, where the bias rate for
956   prompts containing only the word "professor" increases. While this is not a critical issue in most
957   instances, it highlights the need to refine our attack strategy to minimize its impact on prompts
958   containing only one of the triggers.
959   Classifying the Generated Images. Our paper outlines a comprehensive framework to evaluate
960   the success of our attack, necessitating the generation of a large number of images. Assessing the
961   bias rate on such a scale requires automated classification due to the high cost and time demands of
962   human evaluation. Current multimodal LLMs, being either too costly or not performing adequately,
963   pose challenges. Addressing these limitations is crucial for future work.
964
      Low Quality Images. One of the primary challenges when working with open-source T2I models,
965   such as Stable Diffusion, is generating high-quality images. This process is not only expensive and
966   time-consuming but also demands sophisticated prompt engineering skills.
