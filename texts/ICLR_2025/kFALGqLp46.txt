011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            With the advent of large language models (LLMs), using LLMs in conjunction with
014            prompt-based tasks has demonstrated the ability to reduce the high cost and ineffi-
015            ciency of human annotations. Nonetheless, in unsupervised new downstream tasks
016            that require user preferences to align data annotations with expectations, existing
017
               evaluation methods for prompt-based tasks become ineffective, especially when
               ground truth annotations are insufficient or missing. To fill this gap, we propose the
018
               novel Consistent and Inconsistent (CAI) Ratio, inspired by our experimental obser-
019
               vation that LLMs underperform when the number of inconsistent samples—those
020            with inconsistent predictions across LLMs and the student model—exceeds the
021            number of consistent samples. By estimating the CAI ratio and identifying con-
022            sistent and inconsistent samples with our proposed CAI identification approach,
023            we aim to minimize inconsistency and enhance the accuracy of LLM-generated
024            annotations for unsupervised data. To achieve this, we introduce Retrospective
025            Learning (RetroL) with user preference, a data-centric approach that collaborates
026            with the student model and LLMs, using a small number of human annotations as
027            user preferences to resolve inconsistencies in the identified samples. Applied to
028
               eight domain-specific NLP datasets, our Retrospective Learning approach, lever-
               aging CAI identification, significantly improved the accuracy of LLM-generated
029
               responses, with the CAI ratio increasing as the accuracy improved.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large language models (LLMs), with their unprecedented zero-shot performance, as shown by
035
      Kojima et al. (2022), have seen burgeoning deployment across various domains of NLP problems. In
036
      particular, LLMs are being leveraged as teachers, alongside smaller pre-trained models as student
037   learning paradigms, to generate annotations and mitigate the inefficiencies, high costs, and dependence
038   on notoriously laborious manual annotation (Chen et al., 2024). However, it has been demonstrated
039   that LLMs possess intrinsic drawbacks, such as randomness, inconsistency, as noted by Sclar et al.
040   (2024) and Atreja et al. (2024), and hallucination, which can detrimentally impact the trustworthiness
041   of their generated output. To address these issues, prompt-based learning tasks have emerged. Several
042   studies, including Brown et al. (2020) and Chen & Tsang (2024), explore these tasks, along with other
043   research (Wei et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2022; Diao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Wei
044   et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024; Long, 2023; Huang et al., 2022; Madaan et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023;
045
      Shinn et al., 2024). Devising specific and effective evaluation metrics for LLMs is vital to improving
      LLMs’ performance across various prompt-based tasks. Conventionally, many of these tasks rely
046
      on ground truth annotations from the training dataset to evaluate proposed prompts. Feedback from
047
      this evaluation is then utilised to iteratively refine the prompts, by improving LLMs performance
048   on the testing dataset. However, in unsupervised downstream tasks with user preferences, in which
049   explicit guidance is not in provision, it becomes crucial to design a learning process that encourages
050   annotations to align with user preferences to improve the quality of training for a new downstream
051   model. This challenge is commonly encountered and ubiquitous in many real-world applications,
052   especially in intent classification, sentiment analysis, and recommendation systems, where the user or
053   expert preference alignment is essential for generating satisfying annotations. For instance, in AI
      chatbots like ChatGPT, unsupervised data—queries or questions from users with the same intention

      where the competency of the teacher model (LLMs) is uncertain, and no external knowledge is
      provided, evaluating annotations generated by the LLM becomes a significant challenge. As Zhou
077

078
      which necessitates the implementation of self-supervised mechanisms for self-correction (Xiong et al.,
079   2023). In this scenario, relying solely on a student model for fine-tuning or training from scratch is
080   also not feasible, given the lack of supervision. In summary, there are two critical challenges in new
081   downstream tasks with only an unsupervised dataset and user preferences:
082
083          • Challenge on Evaluation: How can we evaluate the performance of LLMs or student-
084            generated annotations based on user preferences when dealing with unsupervised data?
085
086          • Self-Correction for Unsupervised Tasks with Limited User Preferences: Given an
087            unsupervised task that lacks annotations for fine-tuning LLMs and training a student model
088            with only a small set of user preferences, how can we enable self-correction to improve
089            annotation accuracy for both models without relying on any external knowledge?
090
091   To address the challenge of evaluation, Consistent and Inconsistent (CAI) ratio (see Section 3.2),
092   the first evaluation metric designed for unsupervised textual datasets in prompt-based tasks. Our
093
      experimental study reveals that LLMs tend to perform poorly when the number of consistent samples
      with consistent predictions between LLMs and the student model outnumbers the inconsistent samples.
094
      While the CAI ratio can partially assess the performance of LLMs and the student model on a given
095
      unsupervised dataset, it does not fully resolve the issue of inconsistent outputs, as evidenced by
096   the identified inconsistent samples. These samples represent a subset of the training data that has
097   demonstrated inconsistent predictions across both LLMs and the student model, with significantly
098   lower annotation accuracy (see Figure 3).
099
      Furthermore, if the incorrect annotations of the identified inconsistent samples can not be self-
100
      corrected, this incorrect annotation will pass to the student model, resulting in poor generalisation.
101
      Thus, identifying and being able to self-correct inconsistent samples are crucial for enhancing the
102   consistency and accuracy of LLM-generated annotations. To address this challenge, Retrospective
103   Learning (RetroL) is proposed. RetroL uses a divide-and-conquer self-correction (DCSC) technique
104   in conjunction with the identified consistent samples, which are identified with much higher accuracy
105   than the inconsistent samples. The inconsistent samples are self-corrected via the DCSC process,
106   which employs a top-nearest embedding scheme and majority voting. By utilising the CAI identifica-
107   tion and DCSC, our RetroL consistently increased classification accuracy with a higher CAI ratio
      when we applied it to eight domain-specific datasets.

      2     BACKGROUND
109
110
      2.0.1    LLM S FOR DATA A NNOTATION
111
112   LLMs have exhibited pre-eminent competency in dealing with text annotation tasks for many open-
113   domain tasks, such as open-domain spoken language understanding (Chen et al., 2024; 2023), and
114   frequently outperform crowdsourcing and manual annotation without requiring training on specific
115   data (Gilardi et al., 2023). However, the development of robust evaluation metrics and effective
116
      approaches for adapting LLMs to unsupervised textual data with user-defined preferences remains an
      open challenge. Our work fills the gap.
117
118
      2.0.2    LLM AND S TUDENT A NNOTATION PARADIGMS
119
120   Previous works, as highlighted by Thapa et al. (2023), emphasize the importance of teacher-student
121   models in achieving superior performance (Chen et al., 2024; 2023). Recently, Gligorić et al. (2024)
122   have proposed collaborating between LLMs and human annotation to search for unbiased, accurate
123   annotations and for an optimal balance between the high cost of human annotation and LLMs’
124   affordability and efficiency. However, it does not solve the vital aspects of evaluation and self-
125   correction in our problem setting. Simply using a student model with a specifically designed loss
126
      function cannot solve this issue effectively. Moreover, the previous student and teacher paradigm
      overlooks exploiting inconsistent (difficult) samples, an integral aspect contributing to the degradation
127
      of model performance. To the best of our knowledge, no work has explored collaboration between
128
      LLMs and student models with a small number of human annotations for self-correction for the
129   inconsistent sample based on our proposed CAI ratio (See Section 3).
130

      2.1     P ROBLEM S ETTING
132
133   Given unsupervised text corpus distributions for testing and training, denoted as Du = {x1 , . . . , xN }
134   and DT = {x1 , . . . , xL }, where x ∈ X ⊆ Rd . A set of user-preference samples H is also given.
135   These user-preference samples are clustered into k clusters C1 , C1 , ..., Ck . In addition, a set of
136   preference annotations is also given, denoted as Y = {ȳ1 , ȳ2 , ..., ȳk }. Each Cj is denoted as
137   Cj = {(xi , ȳj )|xi ∈ Hj } and Hj ⊆ H. H = {(xi , ȳi )}si=1 , with s = 5% of |DT |. Each Cluster
138
      does not overlap, such that (Ci ∩ Cj = ∅, ∀i ̸= j), and the union of all clusters covers H. These
      user-preference samples incorporate user preferences for alignment purposes. The learning objective
139
      is to assign a user preference label ȳ ∈ Y = {1, . . . , k} correctly to each x. We assume that the
140
      distribution Du can be partitioned into two subsets: consistent samples C and inconsistent samples
141   I, such that C, I ⊆ Du , C ∩ I = ∅, and |C| + |I| = |Du |. However, in practice, the consistent and
142   inconsistent subsets are not known in advance and must be estimated (see Section 3.2). The consistent
143   and inconsistent samples are identified using the student model S and teacher model T , along with a
144   small set of user-preference samples. The learning objective is to minimize the inconsistency (i.e.,
145   reduce the size of I) and maximize the annotation accuracy of the LLMs on Du .

286   3.3.1   D IVIDE - AND -C ONQUER S ELF -C ORRECTION
287
288   Given the identified consistent samples (C), inconsistent samples (I) determined through CAI
289
      identification, and user preference samples, the next challenge we address is resolving the self-
290
      correction of identified inconsistent samples from Ds , Dt , and D̂t . Our proposed Divide-and-Conquer
      Self-Correction (DCSC) approach effectively addresses this challenge. We begin by leveraging the
291
      consistent samples (C) and the inconsistent samples (I), further dividing I into two categories using
292
      CAI identification: CI (consistency of identified inconsistent samples) and II (inconsistency of
293   identified inconsistent samples). The DCSC process consists of two rounds of self-correction (see
294   Figure 2). In the first round of identification and self-correction, we aim to refine the identified
295   inconsistent samples (I) using the consistent samples (C) and the user preference samples (H). This
296   process results in self-corrected inconsistent samples through majority voting based on the top-nearest
297   embedding scheme (MV-VTES). Once this correction is completed, we reapply CAI identification
298   to the self-corrected inconsistent samples and remaining inconsistent samples. This second step
299   identifies II samples for further self-correction, incorporating user preference samples (H) and
300   consistent samples (C). This second round completes our Divide-and-Conquer Self-Correction
301
      (DCSC) paradigm.

      4.0.1    BASELINES
336
337    Using Only Student: We utilise a student model using user preference samples to assign initial
338   annotation for unsupervised data with our proposed preference-based annotation scheme. Applying
339   a pre-trained student model to annotate unsupervised data according to user preferences is a cost-
340   effective approach compared to crowd-sourcing or even LLMs.
341
       Using Only LLMs: As our second baseline, we use LLMs (ChatGPT 3.5 and ChatGPT 4o mini)
342
343
      in a zero-shot setting. The categories defined by user preferences are provided during prompting. The
      application of LLMs for unsupervised textual data is considered affordable, but it might be unreliable
344
      if the generated outputs are incorrect or inconsistent.
345
346    Student (Our) and LLMs (ChatGPT 4o Mini and ChatGPT 3.5): We use the Student model
347   with our proposed hint-based majority voting approach to assign annotation for each unsupervised
348   data. Then, we use it as a demonstration to help LLMs to generate annotation.
349
350
       A Consistent Sample of Student and Teacher Knowledge Distillation: A special case of our
351   methodology is the distillation of student-teacher knowledge using consistent samples. In order
352
      to enhance downstream model generalisation, we systematically identify inconsistent samples and
      exclude them from the training process of pretrained BERT as the student model. This is comparable
353
      to our retrospective learning approach; rather than reconfiguring the student model, we implement
354
      self-correction and reassign annotations to the inconsistent samples.
355
356    Clustering Approach: Our proposed Majority Voting via the Top-Nearest Embedding Scheme
357   is a new clustering method for unsupervised data annotation. Therefore, we include Zhang et al.
358   (2023) as one of our baselines, which is the state-of-the-art (SOTA) in current clustering methods, for
359   comparison.
360
       Self-Refine & Reflexion Prompting Methods: We have added two methods as baselines: Self-
361
      Refine and Reflexion. Self-refine is designed to improve initial output through iterative rounds of
362
      self-correction (Madaan et al., 2024). Reflexion aims to achieve self-correction through LLMs’ own
363   evaluations and incorporates feedback from internal or external tools (Shinn et al., 2024). Both
364   techniques largely depend on the LLMs’ ability to effectively generate accurate annotations. (Further
365   details can be found in Appendix A.4)

      5     D ISCUSSION
527
528
      This paper proposes a retrospective learning framework to address two critical challenges in unsuper-
529
      vised data tasks involving user preferences: evaluation and self-correction. To tackle the evaluation
530   challenge, we introduce consistent and inconsistent (CAI) identification along with the consistent and
531   inconsistent (CAI) ratio, an effective evaluation metric for unsupervised data with user preferences.
532   Building on this CAI identification and ratio, we propose a Divide-and-Conquer Self-Correction
533   paradigm that leverages consistent samples to iteratively self-correct identified inconsistent sam-
534   ples. Our approach addresses the self-correction challenge by achieving higher annotation quality
535   compared to teacher and student models, without relying on external knowledge.

      A.2.4    W HY T EACHER -S TUDENT C OLLABORATION IS E SSENTIAL FOR R ETROSPECTIVE
946
               L EARNING
947
948   In unsupervised learning tasks that rely on user preferences to align data annotations with expecta-
949   tions—where the competency of the teacher model (LLMs) is uncertain and no external knowledge is
950   available, the key challenge lies in evaluating the LLMs generated annotation and enabling mecha-
951   nisms for self-correction. To address this, we propose a novel approach termed retrospective learning,
952
      a self-supervised framework designed to facilitate self-correction and self-assessment of annotations
      generated by large language models (LLMs).
953
954   Our methodology leverages a student model to collaborate with a teacher model of uncertain compe-
955   tency. By introducing the consistent and inconsistent (CAI) ratio , we quantify and identify consistent
956   and inconsistent samples, thereby enhancing the performance of both the student and teacher models
957   through iterative refinement.
958   To further demonstrate the efficacy of our approach, we conducted experiments utilizing the Meta-8B
959   Instruct lightweight LLM as a low-competency noisy teacher. This setup, in conjunction with the
960   student model, underscores the robustness of our framework and highlights the pivotal role of the
961   student model in effectively managing scenarios involving noisy teachers. Given an unsupervised
962   learning task we do not know how competent the teacher deployed to the particular learning task,
963   and there is no external knowledge. How can we evaluate and enable the self-correction for the
964
      unsupervised dataset? To tackle this problem, we proposed retrospective learning, which is a self-
      supervised strategy which allows us to self-correct and self-evaluate the LLMs generated annotation.
965
      To achieve this, we introduce the student model to collaborate with an unknown competency of a
966
      teacher model to acquire a CAI ratio and identify the consistent and inconsistent samples to improve
967   the performance of students and teachers.
968
969   A.2.5    T HE ROLE OF S TUDENT M ODEL IN R ETROSPECTIVE L EARNING
970
971   The inclusion of the student model is essential as it provides a safeguard against underperformance by
      the LLM. Additionally, the student model serves as a reference point for "course tracking," meaning


                                                       18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       that it allows us to monitor and guide the annotation process by comparing the student model’s output
973    with the teacher model’s output. This approach is particularly evident in our experiments where
974    the Meta-8B Instruct model, acting as a low-competency "noisy teacher," demonstrated suboptimal
975    performance on most of the eight datasets, as indicated by its low CAI scores. The student model
976    addresses this issue by collaborating with the teacher model to iteratively refine annotations. This
977    process ensures the framework’s robustness, even when the teacher model lacks competency in
978    specific tasks. We justify the necessity of the student model through experimental analysis (see
979    Section 4.3.1 and Table 4). These results show that our proposed Retrospective Learning (ReL)
980    framework consistently outperforms baseline methods, even when paired with low-competency
981
       teacher models such as the Llama 8B Instruct model (Touvron et al., 2023). This demonstrates the
       resilience of ReL and the critical role of the student model in enhancing performance across diverse
982
       LLM configurations. Moreover, recent studies (Zhou et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2023) highlight the
983
       inherent challenges of relying solely on LLMs, particularly their tendencies toward overconfidence
984    and reluctance to express uncertainty. These findings further validate the inclusion of a student model
985    to mitigate such limitations.
