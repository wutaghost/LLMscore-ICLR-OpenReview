011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Generative AI agents are often expected to respond to complex user requests that
014            have No One Right Answer (NORA), e.g., design a vegetarian meal plan below
015            1800 calories. Such requests may entail a set of constraints that the agent should
016
               adhere to. To successfully develop agents for NORA scenarios, an accurate auto-
               matic evaluation framework is essential, and specifically - one capable of validating
017
               the satisfaction of constraints in the agent’s response. Recently, large language
018
               models (LLMs) have been adopted as versatile evaluators for many NORA tasks,
019            but their ability to evaluate constraint-satisfaction in generated text remains unclear.
020            To study this, we develop and release a novel Arithmetic Constraint-Satisfaction
021            (ACS) benchmarking dataset. The dataset consists of complex user requests with
022            corresponding constraints, agent responses and human labels indicating each con-
023            straint’s satisfaction level in the response. A unique property of this dataset is that
024            validating many of its constraints requires reviewing the response as a whole (in
025            contrast to many other benchmarks that require the validation of a single indepen-
026            dent item). Moreover, it assesses LLMs in performing reasoning, in-context data
027
               extraction, arithmetic calculations, and counting. We then benchmark both open
               and proprietary LLMs on evaluating constraint-satisfaction, and show that most
028
               models still have a significant headroom for improvement, and that errors primarily
029
               stem from reasoning issues. In addition, most models exhibit a skewed constraint-
030            satisfaction prediction pattern, with higher accuracy where the ground-truth label
031            is satisfied. Lastly, few-shot prompting for our task proved to be rather challenging,
032            since many of the studied models showed a degradation in performance when it
033            was introduced.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Generative AI agents are becoming increasingly popular, especially with the development of large
039
      language models (LLMs). As these models become more advanced and autonomous, the scope of
      AI agents increases, and they are now designed to include advanced capabilities and skills, such as
040
      numerical reasoning, planning, and using external tools (Pan et al., 2023; Di Palo et al., 2023; Wang
041
      et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2023). Powered by these capabilities, they are expected to handle complex
042   user requests that may require the agent to perform multiple steps, and adhere to constraints that may
043   be imposed by the request. Examples for such requests include planning a trip with a given budget,
044   creating a meal-plan with specific daily caloric-intake, or generating a fictional story with a specific
045   number of acts and characters. To facilitate the development of AI agents capable of addressing such
046   complex requests, evaluating the quality of the agent’s response is essential. To illustrate this concept,
047   the top part of Figure 1 shows an example of a complex user request to an AI agent that uses external
048   tools, reasoning, and multi-step planning to provide an adequate response, which is finally evaluated
049   to understand how well the response addressed the user request.
050   Evaluating agent responses to complex user requests is a challenging task, especially for requests that
051   have No One Right Answer (NORA). To alleviate this, we suggest to focus on a subset of NORA
052   requests that correspond to a Well-defined, Objective, and Verifiable (WOV) evaluation criteria, e.g.,
053   ”design a 3-day meal-plan with no meat products”. Evaluating whether the agent’s response provides
      3 days of a meal plan and does not include meat is a feasible, well-defined and objective task. This is

054
      in contrast to other NORA requests that correspond to a subjective evaluation criterion, e.g., ”write a
055   funny song about cats” (evaluation of funny is highly subjective and may depend on time, location,
056   and culture), or requests include fuzzy or relative evaluation criteria, e.g., ”plan a short trip to Paris
057   with a small budget”. It should be noted that some requests may only partially correspond to WOV
058   evaluation criteria, i.e., not all parts of the request can be objectively evaluated. Nonetheless, the
059   ability to evaluate an agent’s response with respect to only some parts of the request can be useful as
060   well. Moreover, such requests are diverse in the sense that they span many domains and use-cases.
061   Therefore, an evaluation framework for these cases can be very useful for developing AI agents.
062   To evaluate agent responses in the scenarios described above, we propose a constraint-satisfaction
063   framework/protocol. In this protocol, a set of constraints is extracted from the user request, followed
064   by an assessment of the constraint-satisfaction level in the agent’s response for each constraint in the
065   set. Thus, the evaluation criterion is the alignment between the agent’s response and the constraints
066   that are imposed by the user request. This is illustrated in Figure 1 where a user asks for a trip
067   plan with many constraints entailed in the request. The constraints are enumerated and a set of
068   constraints is produced. Then, the agent’s response is evaluated against each constraint iteratively and
069
      independently. A final score can then be given. Note that the constraints are formulated in natural
      language, and thus, the scope of evaluation is not limited by this protocol. In addition, such a protocol
070
      was previously studied and was found useful for detecting factual errors in LLM’ responses using
071
      attention patterns (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023) and for information-retrieval (Abdin et al., 2023).
072
073   Evaluating the constraint-satisfaction level in agent responses can be performed in multiple ways. One
074   option is to utilize human raters, but this approach is often not reproducible, and more importantly, it
075
      is not scalable. Thus, an automatic evaluation framework is highly desired. Recently, many works
      have utilized LLMs for various evaluation tasks, especially when the evaluation criterion is becoming
076
      more complex and intricate, such as in NORA scenarios (Chang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Zheng
077
      et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a). For example, (Xu et al., 2023; Kasahara & Kawahara, 2023; Chan
078   et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) studied LLMs as side-by-side evaluators, (Fu et al.,
079   2023; Chen et al., 2023; Lin & Chen, 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Zhong et al., 2022; Chiang & Lee,
080   2023) studied LLMs for evaluating a pre-defined set of attributes (e.g., accuracy, coherence, and
081   informativeness), and (Jiang et al., 2023; Min et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023) studied more advanced
082   evaluation protocols based on error analysis. However, they did not explicitly study the ability of
083   LLMs in evaluating constraint-satisfaction in NORA scenarios. In order to enable this, a specific
084   benchmarking dataset is required.
085   There are many datasets that enable benchmarking LLMs on separate capabilities that are required
086   for evaluating constraint-satisfaction. For instance, (Cobbe et al., 2021; Patel et al., 2021; Roy &
087   Roth, 2016) test arithmetic reasoning, (Rajpurkar, 2016; Joshi et al., 2017) test question-answering,
088   (Hendrycks et al., 2020) tests multi-level knowledge in a diverse set of fields, and (Thakur et al.,
089   2021) test information-retrieval. While providing useful insights into diverse LLM capabilities, they
090   do not test LLMs in NORA scenarios. Thus, understanding the performance of state-of-the-art LLMs
091   in evaluating constraint-satisfaction is still somewhat limited.
092   To fill this gap, in this work we develop a dataset for benchmarking LLMs on the task of evaluating
093   constraint-satisfaction in NORA scenarios. The dataset is semi-structured: each datapoint comprises
094   a user request, a constraint, an agent response, and a binary label (annotated by human raters) for
095   whether the constraint is satisfied, all formulated in natural language. We chose to focus on numerical
096   constraints in order for the task to be well-defined, and since their evaluation require complex multi-
097   step reasoning over the entire agent’s response. With this configuration, the benchmark assesses
098
      the LLM’s ability to perform multiple steps in-context to arrive at the final answer, where each step
      may require a different capability: reasoning, data extraction, arithmetic calculations, and counting.
099
      We thus name the benchmark ACS for Arithmetic Constraint-Satisfaction. We use the dataset to
100
      benchmark both proprietary and open popular LLMs. The contributions of our work are as follows.
101
102         1. Formulation of a constraint-satisfaction framework that facilitates automatic evaluation of
103            agent responses to complex user-requests in NORA scenarios.
104
            2. Development and release of the ACS dataset for benchmarking auto-scorers of constraint-
105
               satisfaction. The dataset if available at blinded for submission.
106
107         3. Benchmarking current state-of-the-art (SOTA) LLMs on the ACS dataset, including both
               proprietary models (Gemini 1.5, GPT-4o), and open models (Llama-3, Mixtral, Mistral),

139   2    A RITHMETIC C ONSTRAINT- SATISFACTION DATASET
140
141   This section describes the ACS benchmarking dataset, including its development, properties, scope
142   and limitations. The aim of the ACS dataset is to benchmark LLMs on the task of evaluating
143   constraint-satisfaction in NORA scenarios. More specifically, the dataset is focused on WOV
144   constraints that require the LLM to perform reasoning, data extraction, arithmetic calculations, and
145   counting. The scenarios in the dataset are taken from three domains of high interest: meal-planning,
146   daily-schedule-planning, and workout-planning. At a high-level, each datapoint in the dataset is
147   structured as follows, where each item is formulated in natural language:
148
             • User request: a NORA request to an AI agent that contains at least one WOV constraint.
149
150          • Constraint: a single constraint that corresponds to the user request that should be verified
151            in the agent’s response.
152          • Agent response: a generated response that addresses the user request.
153
             • Label: a human-annotated binary label for whether the constraint is satisfied or not.
154
155
      Note that the constraints that should be verified in the response are given explicitly in the dataset, thus
156   the scoring LLM is not required to extract these from the user request. The reason for this is to provide
157   a common evaluation criterion to different LLMs (i.e., how well do different LLMs evaluate the
158   constraint-satisfaction level of a specific constraint). However, future work could study and compare
159   LLMs’ performance in evaluating the satisfaction of implicit constraints. In addition, note that each
160   datapoint corresponds to a single constraint, even though there may be multiple constraints to each
161   request. This structure facilitates the performance analysis of different LLMs on the constraint-level,
      rather then request-level.

      2.1   DATASET G ENERATION P ROCESS
163
164   The dataset was generated using an interleaved process of LLM prompting for generating text (user
165   request, constraints, and agent responses), and manual modifications and filtering of the generated text
166   (performed by humans). The latter was performed to refine the LLM output and fix inconsistencies.
167   We used Gemini-1.0-ultra (Gemini Team Google, 2023) to generate the text in all of the stages
168   describes next:
169
            1. [Manual] Crafting guidelines for how to generate user requests that entail complex arithmetic
170
               constraints. These can be thought of as seed prompts for generating the entire dataset. We
171            used four sets of guidelines, one for each domain: meal-planning, and daily-schedule-
172            planning, and we further separated the workout-planning domain into cardio and strength,
173            and provided a different set of guidelines for each sub-domain. The guideline in each domain
174            included specific constraints that should be explicitly stated in the user request. For example,
175            in the meal-planning domain, the guideline included a caloric restriction value that should
176            be requested, with a value taken from a reasonable pre-defined appropriate range.
177         2. [Gemini-1.0-ultra] Generating user-requests in the three domains according to the manually
178            crafted guidelines in the previous step.
179         3. [Manual] Appending a final instruction to each user request that was generated in the
180            previous step. The final instruction requested to explicitly include a breakdown of relevant
181            numerical information, e.g., number of calories in meal-plan, working time in a daily-
182            schedule, and exercise duration in cardio-workout-plan. This step was taken to verify that
183            the generated responses to the user queries would explicitly address the constraints and
184            would include numerical values that could be later evaluated by an auto-scoring system.
185         4. [Gemini-1.0-ultra] Generating constraints for each user request that were created in step 3.
186            In this step, Gemini was given few-shot examples in order to generate only arithmetic and
187            counting related constraints and to control the format of the constraints.
188         5. [Manual] Correcting the format or phrasing of the generated constraints in the previous step
189            or adding missing constraints.
190
            6. [Gemini-1.0-ultra] Generating ”agent responses” by querying Gemini with the user requests
191            that were created in step 3. While using Gemini-1.0-ultra, i.e., an LLM, rather than a more
192            advanced AI agent with planning capabilities may seem inappropriate at first, recall that the
193            aim of the ACS dataset is to benchmark LLMs on evaluating constraint-satisfaction. Thus,
194            the agent responses in the dataset are not required to be generated by a domain-dedicated AI
195            agent.
196         7. [Manual] Filtering and modifications to the agent responses that were generated in the
197            previous step, e.g., removing information from the response that may cause the constraint-
198            satisfaction evaluation to be ambiguous, or revising the response to control whether the
199            constraint is satisfied or not in order to diversify the data.
200         8. [Manual] Labeling each pair of constraint and agent response as either satisfied or unsatis-
201            fied.
202
203   With the process above, 25 unique user requests were generated for each domain: meal-planning,
204   daily-schedule-planning, workout-planning-cardio, and workout-planning-strength. Each request
205
      corresponds to multiple constraints and to a single agent response. Next, we have separated the
      constraints such that each datapoint in the dataset would correspond to a unique triplet of {user
206
      request, constraint, agent response}. Following all the steps above (which included manual removal
207
      and augmentation of the data) resulted in a dataset with 405 datapoints, and with a satisfied to
208   unsatisfied datapoints ratio of 241/164 (≈59% of samples are labeled as satisfied). An example for a
209   datapoint is presented in Figure 2.

254   2.3   R EQUIRED N UMERICAL C APABILITIES
255
256   The specific numerical capabilities that are required to evaluate each datapoint in the dataset are:
257   counting, summation, multiplication, and date-time arithmetic. The distribution of the required
258   capabilities in each datapoint in the ACS dataset is presented in Figure 3. Date-time arithmetic mainly
259
      refers to the ability to understand how much time is assigned to different sections in a given schedule.
      Concretely, this means calculating the duration between two specific times within a given schedule,
260
      where the times are mostly expressed in ”HH:MM” format. In some datapoints, the total time should
261
      be accumulated based on multiple sections in the schedule. All the datapoints that require performing
262   multiplication also require accumulating the results over multiple sections in the response. Thus, this
263   capability is explicitly stated as ”Multiplication and summation” in Figure 3.
264
265   2.4   DATASET P ROPERTIES
266
267   To make this benchmark realistic and challenging, it was designed to have several key properties.
268   First, the relevant information that the LLM should use in its evaluation is not presented sequentially,
269   but is rather scattered in the context window. The maximal number of tokens from the agent response
      field in the ACS dataset is 1963, when calculated with Gemini 1.5 Pro tokenizer via the Gemini API

285   many of which support 8k to 2M input tokens (including those that will be studied in Section 3).
286   Another property of the benchmark is that it may contain ”distractors” for specific constraints, i.e.,
287   similar pieces of information that should be ignored since they are not relevant. Moreover, ”positive
288   distractors” may be present, i.e., keywords that represent the constraint as being satisfied (e.g.,
289
      “Here is a 2000 calorie meal plan” when the constraint is “the meal-plan should be up to 2000
      calories a day” and the label is “unsatisfied”). Another challenging property of our benchmark
290
      is that in order to fully verify some constraints, the LLM should perform an iterative evaluation
291
      process. This refers to performing multiple independent instances of the same evaluation process
292   using different pieces of information from the context. An example for this is when evaluating
293   the daily caloric intake of a multi-day meal plan, the calories for each day should be calculated
294   independently. Thus, unlike benchmarks that rely on simple keyword matching or isolated text
295   snippets (such as in Question-Answering, NLI, and sentiment analysis), the ACS dataset demands a
296   comprehensive/holistic evaluation of the agent response. Lastly, the benchmark does not require any
297   domain-specific or specialized knowledge. The complexity of evaluating each datapoint in the dataset
298   can be considered to be at an elementary-school level. This is important in order to fairly evaluate
299   fundamental LLM capabilities that are desirable across domains, without giving an advantage to
300
      domain-specific LLMs. We believe that these properties make the ACS benchmark useful in assessing
      the capabilities that are crucial to successfully incorporate LLMs into a wide range of applications.

303   3     E XPERIMENTS
304
305   The ACS dataset was used to benchmark several LLMs on the task of evaluating arithmetic constraint-
306   satisfaction in NORA scenarios. Recall that the ACS dataset contains a ground-truth binary label
307   for whether the constraint is satisfied in the agent’s response. Thus, in this study, the LLMs were
308   instructed to evaluate the agent’s response with respect to the constraint, and were instructed to
309   provide a final yes/no decision for whether the constraint is satisfied.
310
311   3.1   S ETUP
312
313   The studied LLMs include Gemini 1.5 Pro (version 0514), Gemini 1.5 Flash (version 0514), Gemini
314   1.0 pro (stable version 002) (Gemini Team Google, 2023), GPT-4o (version 2024-05-13) (OpenAI,
315
      2024), Llama-3-70b-chat, Llama-3-8b-chat (AI@Meta, 2024), Mixtral-8x7b-instruct-v0.1 (Jiang
      et al., 2024), and Mistral-7b-instruct-v0.2 (Mistral AI, 2024). Gemini models were accessed through
316
      the Gemini API, OpenAI’s GPT-4o was accessed through the OpenAI API, and the open models
317
      were deployed to a Vertex AI endpoint. Default text-generation parameters were used for each
318   LLM. Each LLM was then used to evaluate the entire ACS dataset. The LLMs were instructed
319   to use a chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning process (Wei et al., 2022) and perform any necessary
320   calculations explicitly, rather than relying on final values stated in the given plan. Furthermore, two
321   prompting configurations were studied: zero-shot and few-shot with two evaluation examples taken
322   from a trip-plan scenario (which is out-of-domain). The first example contains a 3-day itinerary
323   including prices for each element and a daily-budget constraint. The second contains a driving
      plan of multiple segments with driving time and average speed for each segment and a constraint of

324
      maximal driving distance per segment. The evaluation process in the example shows the model how to
325   extract the relevant information (item prices, or driving time and average speed), perform calculations
326   (summation and multiplication), and compare the result against the constraint to decide whether it
327   is satisfied. The evaluation prompt is presented in the appendix in section A.1, and the few-shot
328   examples are presented in section A.2. Then, each LLM’s final decision, i.e., prediction, regarding the
329   constraint-satisfaction in the agent’s response was extracted from the full LLM evaluation response
330   using regex, and was used to analyze the performance of the LLM.

332   3.2   R ESULTS - ACCURACY METRICS
333
334   Following the predictions that were made by each LLM, the following accuracy metrics were
335   calculated: overall-accuracy, which is the constraint-level prediction accuracy, and F1 score of
336   predicting each of the labels: “satisfied” and “unsatisfied”. By this separation, we can study whether
337
      an LLM has a bias towards predicting one label over the other. The accuracy metrics are presented
      in Table 1 for all studied LLMs. As can be seen, GPT-4o achieves the best accuracy scores in both
338
      zero-shot and 2-shot configurations. The high accuracy score of 97.04% shows that GPT-4o can
339
      serve as a rather reliable auto-scorer for the kind of tasks presented in the ACS benchmark. The next
340   best performing model is Llama-3-70b-chat at a zero-shot configuration, but it performs significantly
341   worse, at an accuracy rate of 90.62%. The performance of the remaining models is even worse,
342   indicating their lack of competence in performing as reliable auto-scorers in the constraint-satisfaction
343   task studied here.
344
      Another insight derived from Table 1 is the difference in F1 scores between constraints with positive
345
      (satisfied) and negative (unsatisfied) labels. All models but GPT-4o seem to predict positive datapoints
346   much more accurately than negative datapoints. This phenomenon may be attributed to the fact
347   that the ACS dataset contains “positive” distractors (see subsection 2.4), i.e., keywords in the agent
348   response that imply that the constraint is satisfied (e.g., “Here is a 2000 calorie meal plan” when the
349   constraint is “the meal-plan should be up to 2000 calories a day” and the label is “unsatisfied”). If
350   these “positive” distractors are indeed the causes for the imbalance between the labeling classes, it
351   may show a weak-point of the models in performing similar tasks objectively. However, this is just
352   a hypothesis at this stage, and further analysis is required to validate it, and is suggested for future
353   work.
354   Finally, when comparing the performance of the models in the zero-shot versus 2-shot configuration as
355   seen in Table 1, an interesting behavior is observed. Some models present an improved performance
356   with respect to the F1 score in the 2-shot configuration, such as Gemini 1.5 Flash (increase of 4.44
357   percentage points), Gemini 1.0 Pro (increase of 3.46 percentage points), and perhaps Gemini 1.5
358   Pro although the difference is not major (increase of 0.98 percentage points). This shows that these
359   models can benefit from few-shot prompting strategies since they can guide the models to evaluate
360
      constraint-satisfaction more accurately. In contrast, the accuracy of the open models decrease in the
      2-shot configuration, compared to zero-shot. This is most noticeable in Mixtral-8x7b-instruct-v0.1
361
      (decrease of 7.16 percentage points) and Llama-3-8b-chat (decrease of 4.44 percentage points), while
362
      the remaining models correspond to a decrease of less than 2 percentage points. The cause for
363   this decrease in performance is not clear from this analysis alone, but a potential cause may be the
364   out-of-domain examples. Lastly, GPT-4o achieves a high level of accuracy in both zero-shot and
365   2-shot configurations, which is a desirable behavior that suggests increased generalization capabilities,
366   compared to the remaining models.

      4    L IMITATIONS
423
      The work presented here offers useful insights in to the ability of LLM to serve as auto-scorers
424
      for the task of constraint-satisfaction in NORA scenarios, but it has some limitations. First, the
425
      ACS dataset has a limited scope and size. It spans three main planning domains, which are useful,
426   but represent only a small fraction of real-life use-cases. The set of capabilities that is required to
427   correctly evaluate the dataset is also limited, as described in section 2, and thus it does not reflect
428   the full set of capabilities that are required from auto-scorers to evaluate constraint-satisfaction. In
429   addition, the size of the data is limited to 405 datapoints, where each corresponds to less than 2000
430   tokens. With the increased interest in very large context sizes (Lin et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024;
431   Ding et al., 2024; Gemini Team Google, 2023), it may be very useful to study the ability of LLMs
      to evaluate constraint-satisfaction with very large context sizes. Currently, the ACS dataset does


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      not include datapoints that are composed of very large tokens (> 10k), but this is suggested for
433   future work. Finally, while GPT-4o achieves very high accuracy scores when benchmarked against
434   the ACS dataset, the remaining LLMs, especially the open models, have a significant headroom for
435   improvement. Thus, the ACS dataset can be useful for the development of more advanced LLM-based
436   auto-scorers.
437
      Next, the experimental study in section 3 also has some limitations. Recall that the study in section 3.2
438
      measures the accuracy of the LLMs in predicting the correct constraint-satisfaction label - either
439   satisfied or unsatisfied. With the reduction of the evaluation task to binary prediction, it is possible
440   for the LLM to make some errors, whether in the data-extraction, reasoning, calculation, or counting
441   step, but nonetheless predict the correct class, assuming such potential errors are insignificant. As an
442   example, consider the constraint: “the meal-plan should be at least 1700 calories” that corresponds to
443   a meal-plan with 1800 calories. An LLM that during the evaluation calculates either 1800 (correct)
444   or 2000 (incorrect) calories, could predict the same correct label: satisfied. This paradigm facilitates
445   the analysis of the LLM accuracy (no additional steps are required to extract intermediate numerical
446   values that the LLM is expected to produce) but it may obscure the LLM actual performance to some
447
      extent. For future work, additional experiments could be performed that test the LLMs accuracy
      in more detail, for instance, by examining the accuracy of the numerical values that the LLMs
448
      produce during their evaluation. In the example above, an additional step that extracts the meal-plan
449
      calories that were explicitly calculated by the LLM and verifies this value against 1800 could be very
450   insightful.
451
452   Finally, the study in section 3.3 presents an error analysis with specific error categories, which are just
453
      a single way to cluster the error “buckets”. Furthermore, the LLM could potentially make multiple
      errors corresponding to multiple categories, but we chose a single category that best describes the
454
      most significant error in the LLM output. Thus, this is a subjective and not an exhaustive analysis.
455
      We leave a more detailed analysis for future work.

458   5    C ONCLUSIONS
459
460   This work presented a novel dataset for benchmarking auto-scorers using a constraint-satisfaction
461   framework. The experiment results showed that the task of evaluating agent responses with respect to
462
      constraints that require performing in-context data extraction, reasoning, and elementary-school level
      arithmetic calculations and counting is still challenging for many state-of-the-art LLMs. GPT-4o was
463
      the only model that achieved satisfactory accuracy scores, among all the tested models. In addition,
464
      ”positive distractors” in the agent’s response, i.e., keywords that represent the constraint as being
465   satisfied even though this may not be the case, may pose a challenge to LLMs that aim to score the
466   response. Regarding the prompting strategy, not all models may benefit from few-shot prompting
467   for the task studied by the ACS dataset. Moreover, it may be detrimental to their performance,
468   and thus, this prompting technique should be handled with care when designing an LLM-based
469   auto-scorer. Finally, the primary source of errors is identified to be due to reasoning rather than
470   arithmetic calculation issues, suggesting that incorporating external tools for calculation purposes
471   may not lead to significant performance improvements.
