009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                Technical advances in sequencing have allowed the reconstruction of genome-scale
012                metabolic models (GEMs) for a wide range of microorganisms. These models have
013                been particularly useful for the prediction of essential genes and reactions, which
014                are potential targets for antimicrobial therapies. However, current methods for
015                essentiality prediction are computationally limited and are not able to accommodate
016                the increasingly available data. Motivated by the success of data-driven approaches
017
                   in other domains, this work introduces the metabolic transformer, a model designed
                   for holistic identification of essential reactions in genome-scale models, entirely
018
                   trained on synthetic knock-out data. It is demonstrated that the problem of essential
019
                   reaction prediction can be theoretically formulated as the identification of redundant
020                nodes in directed bipartite graphs. This reveals the limitations of message-passing
021                schemes and motivates the development of a novel graph transformer architecture
022                specifically tailored for metabolic networks. The proposed architecture is capable
023                of addressing the essential reaction identification problem by capturing both the
024                directionality and global structure of metabolic networks. To demonstrate the
025                effectiveness of our approach, we composed a large-scale dataset of genome-scale
026                models reconstructed from real microorganisms. 1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
030
      In recent years, the emergence of high-throughput technologies allowed the integration of transcrip-
031
      tomic data of multiple pathogens into large biological datasets. This integration paved the way for
032
      the reconstruction of microorganism metabolism which directly led to the possibility of modelling
033   these systems computationally Ric (2020).
034
035   Metabolism is the set of basic life processes that take place in the cell. All the metabolic chemical
036
      reactions that occur in a cell form a metabolic network. Genome-Scale models (GEM) are structured
      biochemical, genetic, and genomic databases for an organism, that aim to cover the whole metabolic
037
      network of a cell. As of 2019, more than 6.000 GEMs have been reconstructed for organisms
038
      including bacteria, archaea and eukaryotes and over 140.000 automated reconstructions are available
039   from over 2.600 organisms Büchel et al. (2013); Gu et al. (2019); Monk et al. (2017).
040
041   GEMs have been particularly useful for phenotype simulation and prediction. These models have
042
      shown great accuracy in predicting the growth rate of microorganisms along with the flux carried
      out for each reaction of metabolism Orth et al. (2010). This has been particularly interesting for the
043
      identification of essential reactions and genes. Essential reactions are those that are required for the
044
      growth of the organism Henry et al. (2006). Consequently, when these reactions are knocked out
045   the growth in the model is null, i.e., the organism dies. The genes that encode these reactions are
046   appealing therapeutical targets.
047
048

      demanding and their application to uncertain data can be challenging. Improving the accuracy of
049
      predictions is not trivial and requires an increasing complexity on the models that sometimes involves
050
      an exponential combinatorial cost or requires the estimation of missing data O’brien et al. (2013);
051   Salvy & Hatzimanikatis (2020). In addition, recent advances in metabolism profiling technology
052   Judge et al. (2019) will produce large amounts of data that could be hard to accommodate into current

074   In this work, we propose a supervised learning approach, fully trained on synthetic knock-out data,
075   for the holistic prediction of essential reactions in genome-scale models using graph neural networks
076   and transformers models. Graph Neural Networks (GNN), first proposed in Scarselli et al. (2008),
077   have become the reference algorithm for learning on graph-structured data. It is well known, however,
078
      that GNNs suffer from over-squashing and fail to model long-range relationships in graphs, which
      is a fundamental requirement for metabolic modelling. Motivated by the success of transformers
079
      models in natural language processing and computer vision, many works have proposed the use of
080
      transformers for graph-structured data Min et al. (2022); Rampášek et al. (2022); Bo et al. (2023).
081   One of the early applications of transformers to graphs was presented in Dwivedi & Bresson (2020),
082   where the authors proposed a generalization of transformers for graphs by applying the attention
083   mechanism to the neighbourhood of each node. Later examples such as Kreuzer et al. (2021) and
084   Kim et al. (2022) propose approaches where attention is applied to the whole graph. Other works
085   have also proposed the integration transformers with GNNs to capture both the local and global
086   information of the graph Rong et al. (2020); Rampášek et al. (2022).
087   This work proposes the metabolic transformer, a graph transformer model that combines directed
088   message passing with positional encodings for essential reaction prediction in genome-scale models.
089   Previous models have been proposed before for essentiality computation on GEMs Hasibi et al. (2024).
090   These methods however rely only on message-passing schemes or are narrowed down to a specific
091   dataset, which makes it application to other tasks or microorganisms limited. In this work, a model is
092   proposed that is able to generalize essentiality reaction prediction across different microorganisms by
093   pre-training on synthetic knock-out data. It is shown that the model can serve for effective transfer
094   learning to real metabolic data, in this case, gene essentiality obtained experimentally in the lab. This
095
      is in contrast with existing LP methods that cannot generalize outside the modelled problem. To
      the best of our knowledge, this is the first model to successfully demonstrate transfer learning on
096
      metabolic data.
097
098   In summary, our proposed approach has the following properties:
099
100               • For inference, the proposed method only takes as input the topology of the metabolic
101
                    network. This is in contrast with previous methods that require precomputing synthetic
                    knock-out data for inference Freischem et al. (2022); Hasibi et al. (2024).
102
103               • The method predicts the essentiality of all reactions considering the whole network at once.
104                 This property is a direct consequence of the graph transformer architecture, which can take
105
                    the features of all nodes as input. This overcomes the need for GNN-based approaches that
                    require pre-computing global features on the network Yang et al. (2022).
106
107               • Given the high throughput of deep learning models, we are able to compute predictions for
                    all reactions from only one forward pass of the model. This is an improvement compared

               with the traditional synthetic knock-out simulation, which requires the computation of the
109            growth rate for each reaction individually Oyelade et al. (2018).
110
111
             • Metabolic data available for many microorganisms is scarce or limited. Aditionally,
               metabolic modelling is not limited to essentiality prediction, but also includes a wide
112
               range of other modelling tasks such as flux prediction, control, or drug target identification.
113
               By training a model on synthetic data from many diverse microorganisms, we developed a
114            model that can serve as backbone for effective transfer learning on different organisms or

118
      Currently, the majority of GEMs publicly available are built though automated reconstruction
      pipelines Gu et al. (2019). As a consequence, most of these models lack manual curation and
119
      and, hence, traditional methods for knock-out simulations based on linear programming cannot be
120
      applied. In this context, since the proposed method only depends on the topology of the network,
121   and does not require any additional data to be curated, it has the potential to enable the prediction
122   of essential reactions in a wide range of models for which current methods are unable to produce
123   predictions.
124
      In addition to the proposed methods, we are releasing a carefully curated dataset of 23k GEMs from
125
      more than 100 different microorganisms. The dataset consists of large-scale graphs with an average
126
      of 2k nodes. This is the first dataset of GEMs and, given its dimensions, we believe it is of interest
127   for the graph-learning community, making it one of the largest datasets in terms of number of nodes
128   Hu et al. (2021).

      methods proposed in this work are not limited and can be applied to other topics traditionally modelled
131
      with directed bipartite graphs, such as control and distributed systems Murata (1989); Heiner et al.
132
      (2008), chemical reaction networks Wen et al. (2023) or retrosynthesis Chen et al. (2020).

      assumed that L[rg ]≥0. A theoretical optimum for the growth rate can be obtained by the linear

234   In this section, we introduce all the concepts that compose a directed graph transformer model. We

239   3.1   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
240
241   Graph Neural Networks (GNN) Scarselli et al. (2008); Li et al. (2015); Kipf & Welling (2016) are
242   machine learning models that learn on data that is accompanied by a graph structure. GNNs are
243   composed of layers of message passing networks. In each layer, the embedding vector hi of node i is
244   computed from the aggregation of the embeddings of their neighbour nodes N (i) of the previous
245
      layer. The initial embedding vector is usually the input feature vector that each node is given. As
      described in You et al. (2020), a general k-th GNN layer can be defined formally as:

251   bias respectively, ACT is an activation function and AGG is a commutative aggregation function

253   to be very effective in learning on graphs data Hamilton et al. (2017); Veličković et al. (2017); Li
254
      et al. (2015). However, GNNs are known to suffer from limited expressivity Xu et al. (2018a),
      over-smoothing, this is, converging to a single solution after many layers Li et al. (2018), and over-
255
      squashing, this is, losing information from long-range nodes due to bottlenecks in the graph Alon &

259   3.2   D IRECTED G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
260
261
      Most current GNN applications assume that graphs are undirected. However, the application of GNNs
      on directed graphs is not straightforward. This usually requires either transforming the directed graph
262
      into an undirected graph or propagating messages only over incoming (or outgoing) edges, which
263
      could lead to information loss. These approaches have had a great performance on benchmarks that
264   have been historically homophilic, this is, where neighbours nodes tend to share the same label.
265   However, the case of directed and heterophilic graphs has been less explored. To overcome this,
266   in Rossi et al. (2023) the authors introduce the framework of Directed Graph Neural Networks
267   (Dir-GNN). Dir-GNNs are generic GNNs that can be applied to directed graphs by aggregating
268   messages from both the incoming and outgoing edges of each node. This extension of GNNs showed
269   a great improvement in heterophilic datasets and seemed to outperform previous approaches for
      directed graphs. For this reason, we will use Dir-GNNs as the base model for our models. Formally,

303   3.3     P OSITIONAL ENCODINGS
304
305   The attention mechanism of transformers is known to be permutation invariant. Hence, in order to
306   capture positional information, Positional Encodings (PEs) are used. In Vaswani et al. (2017), the
307
      authors propose to add a sinusoidal PE to capture the position of each word in the sentence. However,
      the generalization of the sinusoidal signal to graph-structured data is not trivial. The most common
308
      approach for computing positional encodings in graph data is to use the Laplacian matrix of the
309
      graph. It is argued that the eigenvectors of the Laplacian matrix generalise the sinusoidal encodings
310   to graphs Dwivedi & Bresson (2020).
311

423   capture global relations in the network. This combination of mesage passing with global attention
424   has been presented before Chen et al. (2022); Rampášek et al. (2022), with promising results in many

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
548
549
      This work proposes the metabolic trasnformers, a novel approach for the prediction of essential
550   reactions in genome-scale models by using directed graph transformers, entirely trained with synthetic
551   knock-out data. The proposed approach is based on the use of directed GNNs as the base model to
552   capture local information, and the use of positional encodings based on the combinatorial Laplacian
553   and the magnetic Laplacian. To compare the performance of the proposed approach, we built a dataset
554   of genome-scale models from the metabolism of different microorganisms from public databases.
555   Aditionally, we compared the performance of the proposed approach with existing methods for
556   essential reactions and genes prediction, and we showed that the proposed approach achieves state-of-
557   the-art performance in both tasks. The study on the architectural choices show that the performance
558
      is highly dependent on the message passing scheme used. In particular, the best results are obtained
      with the use of gated GNNs with a GPS architecture and Laplacian-based positional encodings. The
559
      results also show no clear advantage of using magnetic Laplacian-based positional encodings over
560
      Laplacian-based positional encodings, when combined with directed message passing. This work,
561   however, is fundamentally limited by the scalability of the model, both from a data and memory
562   perspective. Transformers are well known to have a O(N 2 ) memory complexity. In our case, we
563   used graphs from 113 microorganisms with an average of 2190 nodes, with the largest one having
564   5861 nodes. Currently, the largest available genome-scale model is the human model Recon3D Brunk
565   et al. (2018) with 17683 nodes, which poses a challenge to the scalability of the model. Possible
566   solutions to this issue include using more efficient attention mechanisms Choromanski et al. (2020)
567
568   7   E THICS S TATEMENT
569
570   The contribution of this work is to provide a novel approach to identify essential reactions in
571   GEMs. This has the potential to facilitate the identification of essential reactions in a broad range
572   of automatically reconstructed GEMs, a task previously deemed infeasible or requiring extensive
573   manual curation of models. Current approaches that identify essential reactions in GEMs are aimed
574   at contributing to the development of new antibiotics or cancer treatments, among others. Therefore
575   we do not expect any ethical or societal issues to arise from this work.

841   B     C HALLENGES
842
843   This section provides an insight into the existing challenges to identify redundancies in metabolic
844   networks, and how these challenges have driven our architectural decisions.
845
846   B.1   L ONG - RANGE DEPENDENT
847
848   It is straightforward to check that the message passing framework is constrained when it comes to
849
      identifying redundancies on networks. To see this, consider the example network of Figure 8. In this
      network, reaction rs is a source reaction in the network, and reaction rg is the objective reaction. It
850
      is clear that there are two different paths from rs to rg , one path that goes through reaction r1 and
851
      another path that goes through reaction r2 . This makes reactions r1 and r2 not essential. Notice that
852   between reaction r1 and r2 there are a total of 2N + 2 nodes. This means that, if we wanted to transfer
853   information from r1 to r2 using message passing, we would need at least 2N + 2 steps (assuming
854   undirected message passing). Ultimately, this means that, for reaction r1 to acknowledge the existence
855   of reaction r2 , it would need to pass information through at least 2N + 2 steps. Therefore, it is clear
856   that the number of steps required to identify redundancies in the network scales linearly with the
857   number of nodes in the network, this is, given a network N , the worst case number of steps required
858   is O(D), where D is the diameter of N .
859   Given this limitation of message passing, we need to look for a different mechanism for capturing
860   the global information of the network. This shortcoming in message passing may be addressed by
861   using positional encodings Dwivedi & Bresson (2020). Since positional encodings try to capture the
862   global position of a node in the network, they may reduce the number of steps required to identify
863   redundancies in the network. However, as shown in Section D, GNNs with positional encodings are
      not powerful enough to identify redundancies in the network.

877   B.2    D IRECTIONALITY
878
879   Given the wide application of graph learning methods to undirected graphs, one might be tempted to
880
      use undirected graphs to represent metabolic networks. Again, it is straightforward to show that the
      directionality of the network is a key property in the identification of redundancies and that ignoring it
881
      leads to a loss of information in the network. To see this, consider the Petri nets of Figures 9a and 9b,
882
      where reaction rg is the objective reaction. Notice that, given the direction of the reactions, reaction r1
883   is essential in Figure 9a but not essential in Figure 9b, since in the last, alternative reactions exist that
884   are able to supply the objective reaction. Notice that, if we ignore the directionality of the reactions,
885   both networks share the same topology, depicted in Figure 9c.
886
      In this graph, reaction r2 is always involved in a path that leads to the objective reaction rg . Therefore,
887
      it is easy to infer that this reaction will always be essential, even if we ignore the directionality of the
888
      reactions. However, without directionality, it is impossible to infer whether reaction r1 is essential or
889   not. It is clear then, that any approach that dismisses the direction of the network will suffer a loss of
890   key information.

       E    DATASET
993
994    To generate the dataset used in this work, we gathered 197 genome-scale models from the BiGG
995    King et al. (2016) and Biomodels Malik-Sheriff et al. (2020) databases. The models correspond
996    with a total of 113 different organisms, having multiple strains for some of them. The amount of
997    metabolites of the models ranges from 7 to 2038 metabolites with an average of 772. The amount
998    of reactions ranges from 6 to 4047 reactions with an average of 1115. The largest model contains
999    a total of 5861 nodes. The number of essential reactions is on average 26% of the total reactions
1000   with a standard deviation of 0.17. If we compare the number of essential reactions with the total
1001   number of nodes, on average, 14% of the nodes are essential reactions with a standard deviation of
1002
       0.14. It can be seen that the amount of essential reactions is highly imbalanced, with an average of
       218 essential reactions. To generate our dataset the models were split into 153 models for training, 21
1003
       models for validation and 21 models for testing. Since models of the same organism tend to share
1004
       more similar network topologies, models of the same organism were not split into different sets. The
1005   split between training, validation and test sets was done manually to ensure that the models in all sets
1006   have a balanced number of reactions, metabolites, and essential reactions, as well as the maximum
1007   degree of the nodes and the degree of the objective reaction. The detailed list of models and splits is
1008   available as supplementary material.
1009
1010   Essential reactions computation To compute the essential reactions of each model, we used the
1011   COBRApy Ebrahim et al. (2013) framework. For all the models, we remove restrictions that enforce
1012   a non-null flux in the reactions (i.e. we set L = 0), this is, we assume a rich medium and we impose
1013   no constraint on growth production or any other metabolite. Notice that, the proposed method in this
1014   work does not limit the possibility of adding such restrictions for reactions. Future approaches could
1015
       include reaction fluxes as additional features.
1016
1017
       Data augmentation Since we are able to compute essential reactions of any model, we performed
       random modifications in the models as a way to augment the dataset. To generate a modified model,
1018
       we performed randomly two types of modifications: (i) we randomly chose any reaction of the
1019
       model and used it as the objective reaction and (ii) we took the biomass reaction and randomly
1020   added or removed between 1 and 10 reactants and between 1 and 10 products of the model. In
1021   the case of producing an infeasible problem, the generated model was discarded. Notice that both
1022   presented modifications alter the objective function and therefore the computed essential reactions.
1023   The resulting dataset after the data augmentation process contained a total of 18703 samples with
1024   14419 train samples, 2142 validation samples and 2142 test samples, with 2190 nodes on average
1025   and an 8.8% of essential reactions on average. This dataset is particularly interesting as it contains a
       large number of graphs, which is a feature more common in graph-prediction tasks, but on average, it

1122   The first challenge that arises is the fact that certain metabolic reactions are considered reversible.
1123   This means that these reactions can be performed in both directions. We therefore need to model
1124   this fact. One naive solution is to simply link all input and output metabolites of a reaction with
1125   undirected edges. However, this solution removes the 2 sets of consumed and produced metabolites.
1126   Another solution is to label the edges of each side of the reaction (e.g. use a one-hot encoding on the
1127   edges of one side of the reaction [0, 1] and the opposite encoding on the other side [1, 0]). However,
1128   this unintentionally creates two separate sets of metabolites which might not be desirable. To solve
1129   this problem, we resorted to the approach used with LPPs when dealing with metabolic networks
1130

       we assume that reaction r2 is reversible, we create two reactions with opposite direction r2← and r2→ ,
