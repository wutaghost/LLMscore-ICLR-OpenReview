011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs), like ChatGPT, have shown that even trained with
014            noisy prior data, they can generalize effectively to new tasks through in-context
015            learning (ICL) and pre-training techniques. Motivated by this, we explore whether
016            a similar approach can be applied to scientific foundation models (SFMs) for solv-
017
               ing PDEs. Our methodology is structured as follows: (i) we collect low-cost
               physics-informed neural network (PINN)-based approximated prior data in the
018
               form of solutions to partial differential equations (PDEs) constructed through an
019
               arbitrary linear combination of mathematical dictionaries; (ii) we utilize Trans-
020            former architectures with self and cross-attention mechanisms to predict PDE so-
021            lutions without knowledge of the governing equations in a zero-shot setting; (iii)
022            we provide experimental evidence on the one-dimensional convection-diffusion-
023            reaction equation, which demonstrate that pre-training remains robust even with
024            approximated prior data, with only marginal impacts on test accuracy. Notably,
025            this finding opens the path to pre-training SFMs with realistic, low-cost data in-
026            stead of (or in conjunction with) numerical high-cost data. These results support
027            the conjecture that SFMs can improve in a manner similar to LLMs where fully
028
               cleaning the vast set of sentences crawled from the Internet is nearly impossible.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In developing large-scale models, one fundamental challenge is the inherent noisiness of the data
034
      used for training. Whether dealing with natural language, scientific data, or other domains, large
      datasets almost inevitably contain noise. Large language models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT, present
035
      an interesting paradox: despite being trained on noisy datasets, they consistently produce remarkably
036
      clean and coherent output. This observation raises an important question for the development of
037   scientific foundation models (SFMs): Can an SFM, like an LLM, learn from noisy data and still
038   generate accurate, dynamic results for solving PDEs, one essential task of sciences?

      In recent years, LLMs have revolutionized the field of natural language processing by introducing
055   highly flexible and scalable architectures (Brown et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
056   2023; Frieder et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023). Notably, the in-context learning (ICL) paradigm
057   has demonstrated powerful generalization capabilities, enabling LLMs to adapt to new tasks with-
058   out explicit fine-tuning (Brown et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2019; Dai et al., 2023; Gruver et al.,
059   2023). This success has motivated the application of such foundation models across a variety of do-
060   mains (Xu et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023a). Scientific machine learning (SciML) is
061   one such emerging domain which merges physics-based models with machine learning methodolo-
062   gies (Raissi et al., 2019; Willard et al., 2022; Subramanian et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024; 2023; Choi
063
      et al., 2024). SciML aims to leverage the power of machine learning to solve complex scientific
      problems, including those governed by partial differential equations (PDEs). Recent efforts in this
064
      direction have led to the development of foundation models specifically designed for scientific tasks,
065
      called SFMs (Yang et al., 2023b; Xie et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023a; Moor et al., 2023; Bodnar et al.,
066   2024; Herde et al., 2024). These models aim to generalize across a wide range of scientific prob-
067   lems using prior data, much like how LLMs generalize across various language tasks. For example,
068   the versatility of in-context operator networks (ICONs), as illustrated in studies like Yang & Osher
069   (2024) and Yang et al. (2023b), underscores their generalization capabilities in various PDE-related
070   tasks, particularly in the context of few-shot learning. Moreover, the integration of in-context op-
071   erator learning into multi-modal frameworks, as demonstrated by ICON-LM (Yang et al., 2023c),
072   has pushed the boundaries of traditional models by combining natural language with mathemati-
073   cal equations. Additionally, several other studies have focused on solving a family of PDEs with
074
      a single trained model (Cho et al., 2024). However, all these studies are limited in their ability to
      fully harness the capabilities of large foundation models. Our methodology, called Massive prior
075
      Data-assisted AI-based Scientist (MaD-Scientist), addresses these limitations and offers significant
076
      advantages in the following four aspects.
077
078
      No prior knowledge of physical laws Our goal is to predict solutions from observed quantities,
079
      such as velocity and pressure, without relying on governing equations, a common challenge in many
080
      real-world scenarios (Lee & Cant, 2024; Nicolaou et al., 2023; Rouf et al., 2021; Beck & Kurz, 2021;
081   Chien et al., 2012). In complex systems, such as those governing semiconductor manufacturing, the
082   exact governing equations are often unknown and may change over time (Chien et al., 2012; Quirk
083   & Serda, 2001). Therefore, excluding these equations from the model input is a strategic choice
084   aimed at enhancing the applicability of our method across various domains.
085
086   Zero-shot inference Our goal is to achieve zero-shot inference for predicting PDE solutions. For
087   instance, ICON-LM requires few-shot “demos”1 for an unknown target operator before making pre-
088   dictions. In contrast, our foundation model eliminates the need for such demos, as collecting them
089   implies that inference cannot occur until these few-shot examples are available; see e.g., Figure 1.
090   Our approach is designed to enable immediate inference as soon as the model is queried.
091
092   Bayesian inference We incorporate Bayesian inference into the prediction process by leveraging
093   prior knowledge obtained from numerical solutions in PDE dictionaries. This approach allows the
094   model to make more accurate and well-informed predictions by defining a prior distribution over un-
095
      seen PDE coefficients. During training, the model learns to capture relationships among known data
      points using self-attention mechanisms, while cross-attention enables it to extrapolate and infer solu-
096
      tions for new, unseen points. When tested, the model utilizes this prior data to generalize effectively
097
      to novel data points, achieving zero-shot predictions without the need for additional fine-tuning.
098
099
      Approximated prior data For LLMs, one of the most challenging steps is collecting prior data,
100
      which typically involves crawling and cleaning sentences from the Internet. However, this process is
101
      far from perfect due to two key issues: (i) the Internet, as a data source, is inherently unreliable; (ii)
102   cleaning such vast amounts of data requires significant manual effort. Consequently, LLMs are often
103   trained on incomplete or imperfect prior data. Remarkably, this realistic yet critical issue has been
104   largely overlooked in the literature on SFMs, despite their similarities to LLMs. For example, when
105   generating data using numerical solvers for PDEs without known analytical solutions, numerical
106   errors inevitably arise, manifesting as a form of measurement noise — for this, we conducted pre-
107

111   Moreover, numerical solvers running on large-scale servers are frequently expensive and time-

      time-domain (FDTD) method for Maxwell’s equations. In this work, we are the first to explore the

      CDR Study For our empirical studies, we use a family of the convection-diffusion-reaction (CDR)

      equation with various types of reaction, which serves as a paradigm problem representing generic

      elliptic equations. By solving the CDR equation, our approach can be extended to a wide range
118   of other problems. We compare our method with two state-of-the-art machine learning techniques
119   for solving parameterized PDEs. Additionally, we introduce three different types of noise into the
120   numerical solutions of the CDR equation. Our approach not only outperforms the two baseline
121   methods but also demonstrates stable performance, even when noise is added to the prior data during
122   pre-training.

504   5   R ELATED W ORKS
505
506   In-context learning Transformers have shown remarkable ICL abilities across various studies.
507   They can generalize to unseen tasks by emulating Bayesian predictors (Panwar et al., 2024) and
508   linear models (Zhang et al., 2024), while also efficiently performing Bayesian inference through
509   Prior-Data Fitted Networks (PFNs) (Müller et al., 2021). Their robustness extends to learning dif-
510   ferent classes of functions, such as linear and sparse linear functions, decision trees, and two-layer
511   neural networks even under distribution shifts (Garg et al., 2022). Furthermore, Transformers can
512   adaptively select algorithms based on input sequences, achieving near-optimal performance on tasks
513
      like noisy linear models (Bai et al., 2023). They are also highly effective and fast for tabular data
      classification (Hollmann et al., 2022).
514
515
      Foundation model Recent studies have advanced in-context operator learning and PDE solving
516
      through Transformer-based models. Ye et al. (2024) introduces PDEformer, a versatile model for
517
      solving 1D PDEs with high accuracy and strong performance in inverse problems. In-context op-
518   erator learning has also been extended to multi-modal frameworks, as seen in Yang et al. (2023c),
519   where ICON-LM integrates natural language and equations to outperform traditional models. Addi-
520   tionally, Yang & Osher (2024) and Yang et al. (2023b) demonstrate the generalization capabilities of
521   In-Context Operator Networks (ICON) in solving various PDE-related tasks, highlighting ICON’s
522   adaptability and potential for few-shot learning across different differential equation problems. Sev-
523   eral other studies have addressed the problem of solving various PDEs using a single trained model
524   (Hang et al., 2024; Herde et al., 2024) . However, many of these approaches rely on symbolic PDE
525   information, true or near-true solutions and/or do not support zero-shot in-context learning, making
526
      their objectives different from ours.
527
528   6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
529
530   In this work, we presented MaD-Scientist for scientific machine learning that integrates in-context
531   learning and Bayesian inference for predicting PDE solutions. Our results demonstrate that Trans-
532   formers, equipped with self-attention and cross-attention mechanisms, can effectively generalize
533   from prior data, even in the presence of noise, and exhibit robust zero-shot learning capabilities.
534   These findings suggest that foundation models in SciML have the potential to follow the devel-
535
      opment trajectory similar to that of natural language processing foundation models, offering new
      avenues for further exploration and advancement in the field.
536
537   The Transformer used in our study clearly demonstrates the ICL capability, when trained with PINN-
538   based prior. However, it is limited to the CDR equations in our paper. We will consider other types
539   of PDE and more diverse initial and boundary conditions in the future, enhancing its adaptability to
      real-world scenarios and its role as a foundation model.

1052   I.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP AND BASELINES
1053
1054   For the 2D CDR experiments, we compare our model with neural operator-based baselines: Deep-
1055   ONet, FNO, F-FNO, and A-FNO. These baselines are selected for their scalability and ability to han-
1056   dle high-dimensional PDEs effectively. Due to memory constraints, baselines like Hyper-LR-PINN
1057   and P2 INN from the 1D CDR experiments are excluded. The detailed hyperparameters, training and
1058   testing settings, and computational costs used in the 2D CDR experiments are described thoroughly
1059
       in Appendix N.
1060
       Baseline methods We compare our model with 4 baselines: deep operator network (Deep-
1061
       ONet) (Lu et al., 2021), Fourier Neural Operator(FNO) (Li et al., 2020), Factorized Fourier Neural
1062
       Operator(F-FNO) (Tran et al., 2021), and Adaptive Fourier Neural Operator(A-FNO) (Guibas et al.,
1063   2021). These models are based on neural operator.
1064
1065          • DeepONet is a neural operator architecture designed to learn operators mapping input func-
1066            tions to output functions. It combines branch and trunk networks to predict values in a
1067            function space.
1068          • FNO learns solution operators for partial differential equations using the Fourier transform.
1069            By mapping inputs to a frequency domain, FNO captures complex patterns and long-range
1070            dependencies and models complex systems.
1071          • F-FNO extends the Fourier Neural Operator by factorizing its layers to reduce computa-
1072            tional costs. This factorization enables efficient learning of solution operators for complex
1073            systems.
1074          • A-FNO is a variant of the Fourier Neural Operator that dynamically adjusts the resolution
1075            of the frequency domain during training. This adaptation aims to capture relevant features
1076            across scales, enabling more flexible modeling of complex systems.
1077
1078   Interpolation Task DeepONet is the sole baseline used for the interpolation task. FNO, F-FNO,
1079   and A-FNO were specifically designed to take grid input at a specific time point and predict grid out-
       put subsequent time steps. As these models were originally developed for time trajectory prediction
