009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            When large language models (LLMs) are finetuned on reasoning tasks, they can
012            either reduce their training loss by developing problem-solving abilities, or by
013            simply memorizing target traces in the training data. Our work aims to better
014            understand how this learning process shapes a model’s ability to generalize. We
015            observe that, while LLMs often perfectly memorize most target solution traces
016            by the end of training, their predictions at intermediate checkpoints can provide
017            valuable insights into their behavior at test time. Concretely, we introduce the
018            concept of pre-memorization train accuracy: the accuracy of model samples for
019
               training queries prior to exactly reproducing reasoning traces in the training data.
               We find that the average pre-memorization train accuracy of the model is strongly
020
               predictive of its test performance, with coefficients of determination around or ex-
021
               ceeding 0.9 across various models (Llama3-8B, Gemma2-9B), datasets (GSM8k,
022            MATH), and training setups. Beyond this aggregate statistic, we find that the
023            pre-memorization train accuracy of individual examples can predict the model’s
024            sensitivity to input perturbations for those examples, allowing us to identify exam-
025            ples for which the model fails to learn robust solutions. A natural application of
026            this insight is in data curation. We find that prioritizing the collection of examples
027            with low pre-memorization accuracy leads to 1.5-2x data efficiency compared to
028            i.i.d. data scaling, and outperforms other standard data curation techniques.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable problem-solving capabilities, yet the
      mechanisms by which they learn and generalize remain largely opaque. For instance, consider a set
034
      of LLMs, each derived from the same pretrained model and finetuned on the same reasoning dataset
035
      but with varying learning rates (Fig. 1). While several of these models reach near-perfect accuracy
036   on training data, their test performances were vastly different. Conventional wisdom attributes this
037   gap in generalization to memorization during training (Zhang et al., 2021; Bousquet & Elisseeff,
038   2000). However, during finetuning, modern LLMs often learn to perfectly replicate the entire train-
039   ing dataset while maintaining strong generalization, which suggests that memorization alone may
040   not explain how LLMs generalize. This raises the question: what factors in an LLM’s finetuning
041   process lead to differences in its generalization behavior?
042   To investigate this question, we focus on mathematical reasoning tasks, where models are trained to
043   generate both a final answer and intermediate reasoning steps. Although each problem has a single
044   correct answer, the reasoning steps in a target solution trace represent just one of many valid ways
045   to solve a problem. Therefore, a model that has memorized the training data is likely to replicate the
046   exact reasoning steps from the solution trace for a problem in the training data, while a model with
047   general problem-solving skills may produce the correct final answer but follow a different reasoning
048   path. By analyzing model responses on training queries, focusing on both the accuracy of the final
049   answer and the similarity of the response to the target solution trace, we can gain insights into
050
      whether the model is memorizing training data or developing problem-solving abilities.
051   Our investigation reveals that analyzing model samples for training prompts at different stages of
052   training can provide insight into the model’s generalization (test-time) behavior. While LLMs can
053   often perfectly recall the entire finetuning dataset by the end of training, achieving perfect accuracy
      and exactly matching target solution traces, we observe distinct behaviors across training examples

095   connection between a model’s training process and its ability to generalize. Second, we show that
096   pre-memorization accuracy can predict whether a model learns robust solutions for individual train
097

099   2    R ELATED W ORKS
100
      A number of works have studied the phenomenon of memorization during training, but consider
101
      different definitions of memorization. One definition quantifies memorization with “leave-one-out”
102   performance, i.e., does the prediction of an example change significantly if we were to remove it
103   from the training data? (Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Arpit et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021). While we do
104   not use this definition due to its computational cost, the notion of pre-memorization accuracy in our
105   work captures a similar concept, allowing us to identify examples that a model fails to robustly learn
106   without conducting expensive leave-one-out evaluations. In the context of language models, others
107   have defined memorized examples as those where the model’s output closely matches examples in
      the training data (Carlini et al., 2021; Tirumala et al., 2022; Inan et al., 2021; Hans et al., 2024),


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      which has important privacy and copyright implications. Prior work has shown that this type of
109   memorization is more likely to appear with duplicated data, larger model capacities, and longer
110   context lengths (Carlini et al., 2022; Tirumala et al., 2022).
111
112
      The relationship between the learning process and generalization has also been studied in a number
      of prior works. In particular, many works in this category focus on bounding the “generalization
113
      gap”: the difference between training and test accuracies, using metrics related to model complex-
114
      ity, such as VC dimension or parameter norms (Neyshabur et al., 2015; Bartlett et al., 2019). Other
115   works focus on empirically motivated measures, such as gradient noise (Jiang et al., 2019) or dis-
116   tance of trained weights from initialization (Nagarajan & Kolter, 2019), to predict generalization.
117   Jiang et al. (2019) conducted a comprehensive comparison of these methods and found that none
118   consistently predicted generalization, though their work primarily focused on image classification.
119   Other approaches have used unlabeled, held-out data to predict generalization, leveraging metrics
120   such as the entropy of model predictions or the disagreement between different training runs (Garg
121   et al., 2022; Platanios et al., 2016; Jiang et al., 2021). Our findings suggest that pre-memorization
122   accuracy can be a much stronger predictor of generalization in reasoning tasks with LLMs.
123   Finally, our work seeks to improve data curation, which has also been studied in a number of prior
124   works. Active learning methods seek to optimally select data in an online learning setting for general
125   machine learning models (Zhan et al., 2022; Gal et al., 2017; Tamkin et al., 2022). Specific to LLM
126   finetuning, prior approaches largely fall into three categories: optimization-based, model-based, and
127   heuristic-based approaches. Optimization-based methods frame data selection as an optimization
128   problem, where the objective is model performance, and the search space consists of the training
129
      data distribution (Engstrom et al., 2024; Grosse et al., 2023). Model-based approaches, on the other
      hand, leverage characteristics of the learning process (Mekala et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024), such
130
      as comparing the perplexity of examples (Li et al., 2023). Lastly, heuristic-based methods rely on
131
      simpler criteria, such as difficulty scores generated by GPT, to classify desirable training data (Chen
132   et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). Our data curation approach aligns most closely with
133   model-based strategies, as we use the model’s pre-memorization accuracy, a characteristic of the
134   learning process, to inform the selection of training examples.

136   3    P RELIMINARIES
137
138   In this work, we focus on training LLMs to perform mathematical reasoning tasks via finetuning. We
139   are provided with a training dataset Dtrain = {(xi , yi )}, where queries xi are drawn from P (x) and
140
      solution traces yi are drawn from P (y|x). We assume the test dataset, Dtest , is generated similarly
      to the training data. The model is finetuned by minimizing next-token prediction loss. We denote
141
      the finetuned model as fθ (y|x), and model predictions as ŷ ∼ fθ (y|x).
142
143   We further assume that solution traces consist of both intermediate reasoning steps and a final an-
144   swer, denoted as Ans(y). The goal of reasoning tasks is for the model to generate solution traces with
145   the correct final answer when faced with previously unseen queries. We measure the accuracy of
146
      model samples for a given query xi using Acc(fθ (y|xi ), yi ) = Eŷi ∼fθ (y|x) [1(Ans(ŷi ) = Ans(yi ))].
      In our experiments, we approximate this accuracy by sampling from the model with a temperature
147
      of 0.8 and averaging the correctness attained by the samples.
148
149   While different solution traces drawn from P (y|x) should all have the same final answer, they
150   may contain different reasoning steps. Thus the target solution trace yi of an example repre-
151   sents only one of many valid solution traces for solving xi . We call an example memorized if
152
      the distance between the model’s prediction and the target solution trace is low. Specifically,
      we consider (xi , yi ) ∈ Dtrain to be memorized by fθ (y|x) if Perp(fθ (y|xi ), yi ) < p, where
153
      Perp(fθ (y|xi ), yi ) = exp( −1
                                   ni log(fθ (yi |xi ))), ni is the number of tokens in yi , and p is a threshold.
154
      We say the model has learned a robust solution to an example if, given xi , the model is capable of
155
      producing solution traces different from yi which still arrive at the correct final answer.

      4.1   C HARACTERIZING L EARNING P ROGRESSIONS IN LLM R EASONING F INETUNING
198
199   We begin by more precisely characterizing an LLM’s learning process when finetuning on reasoning
200   tasks. We focus on two key aspects of the model’s behavior when presented with train queries: 1)
201
      whether the model’s samples arrive at the correct final answer, and 2) the distance between the
      model’s prediction and the target solution trace, measured by perplexity. These two metrics offer
202
      different perspectives on the model’s behavior, because while there is only one correct final answer
203
      for each query, there may exist many different valid reasoning traces. Tracking both metrics through
204   the course of training allows us to measure how effectively the model is able to solve training queries,
205   and the extent to which this is accomplish by memorizing the target solution trace.
206
207

      for three models finetuned on GSM8K. Each model is trained for three epochs, with a distinct peak
208
      learning rate that decays to zero by the end of training. As expected, training accuracy improves
209
      over time as the model minimizes the loss (color gradient from dark to light), and the distance
210   between predictions and target solution traces decrease (from pink to yellow). For some learning
211   rate settings, models approach near-perfect accuracy by the end of training, and their predictions
212   closely match the target reasoning traces (mostly yellow in bottom row). However, during early
213   stages of training, we observe significant differences in model behavior. For some examples, models
214   initially produce incorrect predictions (black), and later replicate the target trace (yellow). For other
215   examples, models first learn to generate correct answers with solution traces that differ from the

      4.2   P RE -M EMORIZATION T RAIN ACCURACY S TRONGLY P REDICTS T EST ACCURACY
240
      As discussed in the previous section, pre-memorization train accuracy reflects the quality of the
241   model’s predictions before it begins to memorize the training data. Similarly, test accuracy captures
242   the model’s performance on unseen test examples that are never memorized (by construction, since
243   they are never trained on). If the training and test distributions of problems match, then one may
244   expect a model’s pre-memorization accuracy to roughly reflect its test accuracy, since both quanti-
245   ties are evaluated on data from the same distribution and neither capture inflated accuracies due to
246   memorization. Indeed, our experiments confirm this intuition. We find that a model’s average pre-
247   memorization train accuracy is highly predictive of its test accuracy across a variety of training
248   runs and checkpoints. More concretely, we find that there exists a value of p for which a model’s
249
      average pre-memorization train accuracy, EDtrain [P-M Acc(fθ1:m (y|xi ), yi , p)], closely approximates
      the model’s test accuracy, EDtest [Acc(fθm (y|xi ), yi )]. The value of p is dependent on the task and
250
      pretrained model, but not dependent on training parameters.
251
252   We illustrate this relationship in Fig. 3, where we plot the pre-memorization training accuracy and
253   test accuracy across different training runs. We used Llama3 8B and Gemma2 9B as base models
254   and GSM8K and MATH as the reasoning tasks. To evaluate different generalization behaviors, we
255
      finetuned the models by adjusting the peak learning rate (ranging from 5e-7 to 5e-4), the number of
      training epochs (1, 3, 6), and the dataset size (full, half, or quarter of the original dataset). We use
256
      the same value for p within each plot, and we find p by sweeping across a range of values. A full list
257
      of the training runs in our experiments and their training details can be found in Appendix A. We
258   observe a strong linear relationship between pre-memorization training accuracy and test accuracy,
259   with the results closely following the y = x line across different models, tasks, and hyperparameter
260   settings. More quantitatively, the coefficients of determination associated with each plot are 0.94
261   (GSM8k Llama), 0.95 (MATH Llama), 0.97 (GSM8k Gemma), and 0.88 (MATH Gemma). Our
262   results show that pre-memorization training accuracy is a reliable predictor of test accuracy.

408
409   We now present data curation as a practical application of the per-example understanding of gen-
410   eralization provided by pre-memorization train accuracy. While previous works have shown that
411
      prioritizing “hard” examples over “easy” ones during data collection can lead to more efficient scal-
      ing using heuristic measures of difficulty, the ideal metric for determining difficulty remains unclear.
412
      Our findings demonstrate that pre-memorization accuracy can serve as a principled and more
413
      effective metric of example difficulty in data curation.
414
415   Problem setup. We will first more precisely define our data curation problem. Given an existing set
416   of N training examples with queries distributed as P (x), we aim to collect N ′ examples, denoted
            ′
417
      as Dtrain , to augment the dataset. The goal is to specify a new distribution P ′ (x) that maximizes the
      test performance of a model trained on both the original and the newly collected examples. While
418
      defining the true distribution of queries can be challenging, we assume that by approximating it with
419
      an empirical distribution from the current dataset, we can collect new data with similar properties.
420   In our experiments, we take Dtrain to be the original dataset, and collect new examples by using
421   GPT to rephrase examples in the original dataset, similar to the procedure in Setlur et al. (2024). By
422   only collecting new examples that derive from the specified empirical distribution, we can ensure the
423   new dataset approximates P ′ (x). This setup can also be used when collecting new human-generated
424   data, by providing the specified empirical distribution of examples as references for human labelers.
425
      Our approach. We propose a data collection strategy that focuses on examples with low pre-
426   memorization accuracy in the existing dataset. First, we calculate the pre-memorization accuracy
427   for each example in the current dataset and then define P ′ (x) as the distribution of examples whose
428   pre-memorization accuracy falls below a certain threshold t. We then collect new data according
429   to this distribution. If N ′ is large, we can split the data collection process into multiple iterations
430   (N1′ + ... + Nn′ = N ′ ). In each iteration, we collect Ni′ new examples according to Pi′ (x), retrain a
431   model on the combined dataset, calculate the pre-memorization accuracy with the model, and update
        ′
      Pi+1 (x) for the next round of data collection. This process is summarized in Algorithm 1.

      Comparisons. We compare our strategy to IID sampling and two existing approaches commonly

458   use the number of lines in the target solution traces as a heuristic for difficulty, while for the MATH
459   dataset, we use the difficulty levels provided in the dataset itself.

466   of using pre-memorization accuracy as a criterion for targeted data collection, leading to enhanced

470   6   C ONCLUSION
471
472
      Our work studies the relationship between training dynamics, memorization, and generalization in
473
      LLMs finetuned for reasoning tasks. We introduce the concept of pre-memorization train accuracy,
474   the accuracy of model samples before they replicate target reasoning traces, and show that a model’s
475   average pre-memorization train accuracy is a strong predictor of its test accuracy. We further show
476   that a model’s per-example pre-memorization train accuracy can be an indicator of the robustness of
477   a model’s learned solution for those examples. We leverage this insight for data curation, and show
478   that prioritizing examples with low pre-memorization train accuracy can be more effective than i.i.d.
479   data scaling and other data curation techniques.
480   While our work demonstrated the strong connection between pre-memorization train accuracy and
481   generalization, it is not yet clear how different training factors influence a model’s pre-memorization
482   train accuracy. Gaining a more precise grasp of this would allow us to better control and improve a
483   model’s generalization capabilities. Furthermore, our work focuses on reasoning problems, which
484   admit a binary final-answer checking accuracy metric. It would be interesting to better understand
485   the relationship between training dynamics and generalization in other more open-ended problem
      domains without a binary notion of correctness (e.g., creative writing, summarization, safety).
