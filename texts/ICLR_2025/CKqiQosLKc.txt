009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Energy-based policies offer a flexible framework for modeling complex, multi-
012            modal behaviors in reinforcement learning (RL). In maximum entropy RL, the
013            optimal policy is a Boltzmann distribution derived from the soft Q-function, but
014            direct sampling from this distribution in continuous action spaces is computation-
015            ally intractable. As a result, existing methods typically use simpler parametric
016
               distributions, like Gaussians, for policy representation — limiting their ability to
               capture the full complexity of multimodal action distributions. In this paper, we
017
               introduce a diffusion-based approach for sampling from energy-based policies,
018
               where the negative Q-function defines the energy function. Based on this ap-
019            proach, we propose an actor-critic method called Diffusion Q-Sampling (DQS)
020            that enables more expressive policy representations, allowing stable learning in
021            diverse environments. We show that our approach enhances exploration and cap-
022            tures multimodal behavior in continuous control tasks, addressing key limitations
023            of existing methods.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Deep reinforcement learning (RL) is a powerful paradigm for learning complex behaviors in diverse
028
      domains, from strategy-oriented games (Silver et al., 2016; Berner et al., 2019; Schrittwieser et al.,
029
      2020) to fine-grained control in robotics (Kober et al., 2013; Sünderhauf et al., 2018; Wu et al.,
030   2023). In the RL framework, an agent learns to make decisions by interacting with an environment
031   and receiving feedback in the form of reward. The agent aims to learn a policy that maximizes the
032   cumulative sum of rewards over time by exploring actions and exploiting known information about
033   the environment’s dynamics.
034
      The parameterization of the policy is a crucial design choice for any RL algorithm. Under the con-
035
      ventional notion of optimality, under full observability, there always exists an optimal deterministic
036   policy that maximizes the long-term (discounted) return (Sutton & Barto, 2018). However, this is
037   only true when the agent has explored sufficiently and has nothing to learn about the environment.
038   Exploration requires a stochastic policy, to experiment with different potentially rewarding actions.
039   Moreover, even in the exploitation phase, there may be more than one way of performing a task and
040   we might be interested in mastering all of them. This diversification is motivated by the robustness
041   of the resulting stochastic policy to environment changes; if certain pathways for achieving a task
042   become infeasible due to a change of the dynamics or reward, some may remain feasible, and the
043   agent has an easier time in adapting to this change by exploiting and improving the viable options.
044
      This argument also suggests that such policies can also serve as effective initialization for fine-tuning
      on specific tasks.
045
046   While exploration, diversity and robustness motivate stochastic policies, representing such policies
047   in continuous action spaces remains challenging. As a result, stochasticity is often introduced by
048   noise injection (Lillicrap et al., 2015) or using an arbitrary parametric family (Schulman et al., 2015)
049   which lacks expressivity.
050   Orthogonal to the difficulty of representing such policies is their training objective; policies are often
051   optimized to maximize the Q-function, and stochasticity is introduced to encourage exploration as
052   an afterthought. However, our argument for stochasticity favours multi-modal policies; instead of
053   learning the single best way to solve a task, we want to learn all reasonably good ways to solve the
      task.

068   We address both of these issues using diffusion-based sampling from a Boltzmann policy. Diffusion
069   models offer a potential solution to the policy parameterization problem since they are expressive
070   and can produce high-quality samples from complex distributions. Indeed, they have been exten-
071   sively applied to solve sequential decision-making tasks, especially in offline settings where they
072   can model multimodal datasets from suboptimal policies or diverse human demonstrations. A few
073   studies have applied these models in the online setting, focusing on deriving training objectives for
074   policy optimization via diffusion. Yang et al. (2023); Psenka et al. (2023) use the gradient of the
075
      Q-function to guide action sampling, however, the exact form of the policy is unspecified and it is
      unknown what distribution the diffusion models sample from.
076
077   We consider the problem of explicitly sampling from energy-based policies of the form,
078                                         π(a | s) ∝ exp(Qπ (s, a)).
079
080   This is also known as the Boltzmann distribution of the Q-function. The optimal policy in the
081
      maximum entropy RL framework is also known to be of this form, except it uses the soft Q-function
      (Haarnoja et al., 2017). Such a policy has several benefits. First, it offers a principled way to balance
082
      exploration and exploitation for continuous action spaces. By sampling from this distribution, the
083
      policy still prioritizes actions with high Q-values but also has a non-zero probability of sampling
084   sub-optimal actions. While the use of Boltzmann policies is common in the discrete setting, it is
085   challenging in continuous spaces; this sampling problem is often tackled with Markov Chain Monte
086   Carlo (MCMC) techniques, which can be computationally expensive and suffer from exponential
087   mixing time. Second, this formulation naturally incorporates multimodal behavior, since the policy
088   can sample one of multiple viable actions at any given state.
089
      Our contributions in this work are as follows:
090
091
       • We propose a novel framework for sequential decision-making using diffusion models for sam-
         pling from energy-based policies.
092
093    • We propose an actor-critic algorithm for training diffusion policies based on our framework.
094    • We demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach for learning multimodal behaviors in maze
095      navigation tasks.
096    • We demonstrate improved sample efficiency in continuous control tasks owing to better explo-
097      ration capabilities of our method.

099   2   R ELATED W ORK
100
101   Our work is related to two distinct sub-areas of reinforcement learning: the relatively new and
102   actively explored line of work on applying diffusion models in the RL setting, and the classical
103   maximum entropy RL framework.
104
105   Diffusion models in RL. Early work on applying diffusion models for RL was focused on be-
106   havior cloning in the offline setting (Chi et al., 2023). This setting more closely matches the original
107   purpose of diffusion models - to match a distribution to a given dataset. Janner et al. (2022); Ajay
      et al. use a diffusion model trained on the offline data for trajectory optimization, while Reuss et al.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (2023); Jain & Ravanbakhsh (2024) apply diffusion models to offline goal-reaching tasks by addi-
109   tionally conditioning the score function on the goal. Within the behavior cloning setting, there is
110   some existing work on learning a stochastic state dynamics model using diffusion (Li et al., 2022).
111
112
      Beyond behavior cloning, offline RL methods incorporate elements from Q-learning to learn a value
      function from the offline dataset and leverage the learned Q-function to improve the diffusion policy.
113
      A large body of work exists in this sub-field, where the most common approach is to parameterize
114
      the policy using a diffusion model and propose different training objectives to train the diffusion
115   policy. Wang et al.; Kang et al. (2024) add a Q-function maximizing term to the diffusion training
116   objective, and Hansen-Estruch et al. (2023) use an actor-critic framework based on a diffusion policy
117   and Implicit Q-learning (Kostrikov et al., 2021). Lu et al. (2023) take an energy-guidance approach,
118   where they frame the problem as using the Q-function to guide the behavior cloning policy to high
119   reward trajectories.
120
      The application of diffusion models has been relatively less explored in the online setting (Ding &
121   Jin, 2023). Yang et al. (2023) modifies actions in the replay buffer based on the gradient of the
122   Q-function, then trains a diffusion model on the modified actions. Psenka et al. (2023) directly
123   trains a neural network to match the gradient of the Q-function, then uses it for sampling. The
124   policy resulting from these methods does not have an explicit form, and it is unknown what exact
125   distribution these diffusion models sample from. In contrast, our method explicitly models the policy
126   as a Boltzmann distribution and samples actions from this distribution.
127
128
129   Maximum entropy RL. In contrast to standard RL, where the goal is to maximize expected re-
130
      turns, in the maximum entropy RL framework, the value function is augmented by Shannon entropy
      of the policy. Ziebart et al. (2008) applied such an approach in the context of inverse reinforcement
131
      learning and Haarnoja et al. (2017) generalized this approach by presenting soft Q-learning to learn
132
      energy-based policies. A follow-up work, Haarnoja et al. (2018a), presented the well-known soft
133   actor-critic (SAC) algorithm. This line of work proposes to learn a soft value function by adding
134   the entropy of the policy to the reward. The optimal policy within this framework is a Boltzmann
135   distribution, where actions are sampled based on the exponentiated soft Q-values.
136
      A separate but related line of work on generative flow networks (GFlowNets), originally defined in
137
      the discrete case (Bengio et al., 2021; 2023), learns a policy that samples terminal states proportional
138
      to the Boltzmann density corresponding to some energy function. They have been extended to
139   the continuous setting (Lahlou et al., 2023) and under certain assumptions, they are equivalent to
140   maximum entropy RL (Tiapkin et al., 2024; Deleu et al.). They can effectively sample from the target
141   distribution using off-policy exploration, however, they encounter challenges in credit assignment
142   and exploration efficiency (Malkin et al., 2022; Madan et al., 2023; Rector-Brooks et al., 2023; Shen
143   et al., 2023). Our approach is distinct as we sample the action at each step from the Boltzmann
144   density of the Q-function, instead of the terminal states based on the reward.

230   4.1   B OLTZMANN P OLICY I TERATION
231
232   We propose a policy iteration process that alternates between learning the Q-function of the current
233   policy and optimizing the policy to sample from the Boltzmann distribution of this Q-function.
234   While Boltzmann policies have been used in Q-learning methods for discrete action spaces, our
235   approach introduces a key difference – we rely on the Q-function of the current policy, rather than
236
      the optimal Q-function. Since the policy is not maximizing the Q-function but rather following a
      Boltzmann distribution, and the Q-function corresponds to the current policy, it is not immediately
237
      clear that this iterative process of policy evaluation and improvement will converge to a unique
238
      fixed point. Indeed, it is conceivable to encounter policy-value pairs that are quite sub-optimal yet
239   consistent, where the policy is Boltzmann in Q, and Q is the value function of that specific policy.
240   The following results show that these updates still constitute policy iteration and there is a unique
241   fixed point. Moreover, such a fixed point is bound to recover the optimal policy.
242

244   with a policy π. For a fixed policy, the Q-function is computed iteratively, starting from any function
245   Q : S × A → R and repeatedly applying the Bellman backup operator T π given by,

355
356   We perform experiments to answer the following major questions:
357
358          • Can DQS learn multimodal behaviors, i.e., learn multiple ways to solve a task?
359
             • Does DQS offer better exploration-exploitation in continuous control tasks?

361
      Baselines. We test our method against two relevant baseline methods: (1) Soft Actor-Critic (SAC)
362   (Haarnoja et al., 2018a), a maximum entropy RL method that is widely used for continuous state-
363   action spaces, and; (2) Q-Score Matching (QSM) (Psenka et al., 2023), a recent diffusion-based
364   approach that trains a score function to match the gradient of the Q-function and uses this score
365   function to sample actions.
366
      All methods were trained with 250k environment interactions and one network update per environ-
367
      ment step. For a fair comparison, all policy/score networks are MLPs with two hidden layers of
368   dimension 256 each, and the learning rate for all networks is 3 × 10−4 . We apply the double Q-
369   learning trick in our implementation, a commonly used technique, where two Q-networks are trained
370   independently and their minimum value is used for policy evaluation to avoid overestimation bias.

450   6                           D ISCUSSION
451
452
      In this work, we showcase the benefits of using energy-based policies as an expressive class of
453   policies for deep reinforcement learning. Such policies arise in different RL frameworks, but their
454   application has been limited in continuous action spaces owing to the difficulty of sampling in this
455   setting. We alleviate this problem using a diffusion-based sampling algorithm. We show that the
456   process of alternating between updating the Q-function of the current policy and optimizing the
457   policy to sample from the Boltzmann distribution of this Q-function has a unique fixed point which
458   is also the optimal policy. The proposed actor-critic algorithm, Diffusion Q-Sampling (DQS,) can
459   sample multimodal behaviors and effectively manage exploration and exploitation.
460   While diffusion methods offer high expressivity, they often come with increased computation. This
461   is particularly true in the online RL setting, where using a diffusion policy means that each envi-
462   ronment step requires multiple function evaluations to sample from the diffusion model. There is a
463   growing body of work on efficient SDE samplers (Jolicoeur-Martineau et al., 2021), which aim to
464   reduce the number of function evaluations required to obtain diffusion-based samples while main-
465   taining high accuracy. Incorporating such techniques with Boltzmann policies can greatly reduce
466   the computational cost, especially in high-dimensional state-action spaces.
467   A crucial aspect of energy-based policies is the temperature parameter, which defines the shape of
468   the sampling distribution. Our method enables annealing of the temperature from some starting
469   value to lower values, as is typically done when applying Boltzmann policies in deep RL. However,
470   this temperature schedule has to be manually tuned. Haarnoja et al. (2018b) proposes an automatic
471   temperature tuning method for SAC, which maintains the temperature so that the entropy of the
472   current policy is close to some target entropy. While such an approach could be applied to DQS in
473
      principle, it is computationally expensive to compute the likelihoods of samples under a diffusion
      model.
474
475   Finally, as we argued in the introduction, Boltzmann policies based on their own value function are
476   attractive choices for pre-training of RL agents for later fine-tuning and multi-task settings. We hope
477   to investigate this exciting potential in the future.

      The score function is parameterized as an MLP with two hidden layers of 256 units each with the
705   ReLU activation function, except for the final layer. The MLP has skip connections as is typical for
706   denoising score functions. The input to the policy comprises the state, noised action, the diffusion
707   time step, and the temperature. The diffusion time step and the temperature are encoded using sinu-

710   value of the action space to ensure the value is within the correct range. The Q-network is also an
711   MLP with two hidden layers of 256 units each with the ReLU activation function, except for the

      the two values.
