011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            As a key component of the transformer architecture, the self-attention mechanism
014            is known for its capability to perform token selection, which can often signifi-
015            cantly enhance model performance. However, when and how self-attention can be
016
               trained to perform effective token selection remains poorly understood in theory.
               In this paper, we study the problem of using a single self-attention layer to learn
017
               random walks on circles. We theoretically demonstrate that, after training with
018
               gradient descent, the self-attention layer can successfully learn the Markov prop-
019            erty of the random walk, and achieve optimal next-token prediction accuracy by
020            focusing on the correct parent token. In addition, we also study the performance
021            of a single self-attention layer in learning relatively simpler вҖңdeterministic walksвҖқ
022            on circles. Surprisingly, in this case, our findings indicate that the self-attention
023            model trained with gradient descent consistently yields next-token prediction ac-
024            curacy no better than a random guess. This counter-intuitive observation that self-
025            attention can learn random walks but struggles with deterministic walks reveals
026            a potential issue in self-attention: when there are multiple highly informative to-
027
               kens, self-attention may fail to properly utilize any of them.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have revolutionized many fields such as natural
033
      language processing, and have rapidly emerged as a key component in state-of-the-art deep learning
      models due to their ability to capture complex dependencies in data. At the heart of transformers
034
      lies the self-attention mechanism, which allows the model to assign different weights or importance
035
      to each input token based on its relevance to the context or task at hand. This process of assigning
036   weights to tokens based on their вҖңimportanceвҖқ can be seen as a form of token selection, since it
037   determines which tokens contribute more significantly to the modelвҖҷs prediction. However, the
038   exact mechanisms behind token selection and how it impacts model performance are still not well
039   understood.
040
      A line of recent works has studied token selection of the self-attention mechanism from different
041
      perspectives. Tarzanagh et al. (2023); Ataee Tarzanagh et al. (2023) propose an equivalence between
042   the optimization dynamic of one self-attention layer and an SVM problem and prove the global
043   convergence under certain assumptions. Li et al. (2024a) shows that when training a self-attention
044   layer, the priority in token selection is determined by a directed graph extracted from the training
045   data. Wang et al. (2024) demonstrates that transformer models can learn the sparse token selection
046   task effectively while fully connected networks fail in the worst case. Li et al. (2024b) shows that
047   a self-attention layer can be trained to perform proper token selection so that the model acts as a
048   one-nearest neighbor classifier in context.
049   Several more recent works have also studied the performance of transformers in learning sequential
050   data generated from Markov models or Bayesian network models. In these studies, token selection
051   is also the key, as an ideal self-attention layer should properly select the token(s) that is/are the
052   вҖҷparent(s)вҖҷ of the token to be predicted. Specifically, Makkuva et al. (2024) characterizes the loss
053   landscape of a single-layer transformer and demonstrates the existence of global minima and bad
      local minima in learning Markovian data with vocabularies of size two. Ildiz et al. (2024) shows the

054
      connection between a context-conditioned Markov chain and the self-attention mechanism. Nichani
055   et al. (2024) studies the mechanism through which transformer models encode a specific causal
056   structure in their representations for in-context learning.

075   In this paper, we introduce two simple case studies on how transformers learn sequential data.
076   Specifically, we train a one-layer transformer model to predict sequences generated by вҖңrandom
077   walksвҖқ and вҖңdeterministic walksвҖқ on circles (see Figure 1 for an illustration). With a precise anal-
078   ysis on the training dynamics of gradient descent, we surprisingly find that the performance of the
079
      transformer on these two tasks can be drastically different.
080   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
081
082   вҖў We theoretically demonstrate that, a one-layer transformer can be trained by gradient descent to
083
        optimally predict the next location of a random walk (illustrated in Figure 1(a)). In addition, our
        analysis also precisely reveals that the self-attention can be trained to select the correct token (the
084
        вҖҳparentвҖҷ token), and make prediction based on it. Our analysis sheds light on how the self-attention
085
        mechanism can adapt to sequential data patterns with proper token selection.
086
      вҖў We also show that, when learning to predict deterministic walks (illustrated in Figure 1(b)), the
087
        training of the same one-layer transformer model with any loss function and any step size will
088
        always fail, resulting in a transformer model whose performance is no better than a random guess.
089     This result highlights a potential limitation of self-attention: when all tokens are equally вҖҳinfor-
090     mativeвҖҷ, the self-attention mechanism may fail to utilize any of them.
091
      вҖў Simulations demonstrate that our theoretical characterization of one-layer transformers is accu-
092
        rate. Even when the trainable parameters of the transformer are initialized with Gaussian random
093     values that do not satisfy our theoretical assumptions, we observe that the transformer struggles to
094     learn deterministic walks, which aligns with our theory. Furthermore, motivated by our theories
095     and explanations, we construct two simple question answering tasks in natural language process-
096     ing (NLP) and successfully predict the performance of transformers on these tasks. This confirms
097     the validity of our theory and highlights the insights provided by our study.

404
      In Sections 3 and 4, we demonstrate that a one-layer transformer can be trained to optimally predict
      random walks, but fails in the arguably easier task of predicting deterministic walks. In this section,
405
      we provide a concise explanation for the counter-intuitive phenomenon, and discuss other learning
406
      tasks beyond random and deterministic walks, where transformer training may also be challenging.
407
408
      5.1   A N I NTUITIVE E XPLANATION
409
410   Here we aim to give an intuitive explanation on why transformers fail in learning deterministic
411   walks. A natural starting point is to study the differences between random and deterministic walks.
412
      Difference between random and deterministic walks. As is discussed in Section 2.1, a key differ-
413
      ence between random and deterministic walks is that, in random walks, the optimal predictor must
414
      rely on xN вҲ’1 , which is the direct вҖҳparentвҖҷ of y, to make a prediction. On the other hand, in deter-
415   ministic walks, knowing any one of the historical locations xi , i вҲҲ [N вҲ’ 1] can provide sufficient
416   information to achieve perfect prediction. In other words, in random walk, there is a unique token
417   that is the most вҖҳinformativeвҖҷ, while in deterministic walks, all tokens are equally вҖҳinformativeвҖҷ.
418
      Motivated by the discussion above, we consider using entropy1 as a more concrete characterization
419
      of how вҖҳinformativeвҖҷ each token is. Specifically, we take the case (K, N ) = (6, 7) as an example.

524   6    C ONCLUSION
525
526   This paper studies the self-attention mechanism via a random walk and a deterministic walk, where
527   we consider a transformer with a single self-attention layer. It can be demonstrated that the self-
528   attention layer can learn random walks well by effectively selecting the correct parent token and
529   obtaining the optimal next-token prediction accuracy. However, when learning the simpler deter-
530   ministic task, the self-attention layer fails to select any token; instead, the self-attention layer assigns
531   the same attention score to all the tokens. As a result, the trained transformer shows no improvement
532   over a random guess. We thus discover that multiple informative tokens may hinder the performance
533   of the self-attention mechanism by failing to select any specific token.
534   This work performs two specific cases studies on learning random and deterministic walks with
535   one-layer transformers. While the conclusions of these case studies provide valuable insights, it is
536   important to extend the results and study the performance of deeper transformer architectures, which
537   may require more advanced theoretical tools. Moreover, extending the finding to more complicated
538   learning tasks, such as random sequences generated by Bayesian networks, is also an important
539   future work direction.
