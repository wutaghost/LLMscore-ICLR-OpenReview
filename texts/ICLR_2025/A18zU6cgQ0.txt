009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Video Anomaly Detection (VAD) aims to identify anomalous frames in given
012            videos. Existing fully-supervised VAD encounters substantial annotation cost and
013            weakly-supervised VAD suffers from the deficiency of weak labels. In this pa-
014            per, we propose a more effective Single Frame supervised VAD (SF-VAD), which
015            leverages single abnormal frame as label. We argue that single abnormal frame an-
016            notation is highly efficient and it provides fine-grained dual references to abnormal
017            and normal frames, which facilitate dependable anomaly and normality decou-
018
               pling. Leveraging these dual references, we propose Frame-guided Probabilistic
               Contrary Learning (FPCL), to decouple contrary abnormal and normal patterns.
019
               Specifically, FPCL learns inclusive abnormal patterns reliably from mined ab-
020
               normal frames, guided by similarity-based abnormal probability. Simultaneously,
021            it decouples normal patterns in abnormal videos, by learning normal patterns in
022            preceding frames, guided by Gaussian-prior normal probability. In inference, we
023            additionally design temporal decoupling and boundary refining modules to reveal
024            discriminative abnormal characters of temporal features. Extensive experiments
025            show our SF-VAD method outperforms SOTA methods and achieves an optimal
026            performance-cost trade-off. To support future research on this topic, we construct
027            and release three SF-VAD datasets.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Video Anomaly Detection (VAD) strives to identify anomalous frames in given videos, which draws
033
      substantial research interest for its practical applications (Sultani et al., 2018; Tian et al., 2022). By
      supervision paradigm, existing VAD research can be categorized into three classes, as in Fig. 1a.
034
      Intuitively, pioneer works (Landi et al., 2019; Liu & Ma, 2019) explore Fully-supervised VAD (F-
035
      VAD), utilizing labels of each frame. However, due to the ambiguity of anomaly boundary, F-VAD
036   encounters the issue of human bias and its annotation is also labor-intensive. Alternatively, re-
037   searchers (Gong et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2022) study Semi-supervised VAD (S-VAD) where only
038   normal patterns are learned during training, and, out-of-distribution frames are identified as abnor-
039   mal. Unfortunately, it is prone to recognize unseen normal patterns as anomalous, encountering
040   severe false alarms. To address these drawbacks, recent studies (Wu et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
041   2023; Chen et al., 2024) focus on Weakly-supervised VAD (W-VAD), which delivers a superior
042   performance-cost trade-off by leveraging video-level weak labels.
043   Current W-VAD methods (Sultani et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2022; Cho et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024;
044   Feng et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2022) learn abnormal patterns in abnormal videos by frames that have
045   higher predicted anomaly scores, and learn normal patterns by such frames in normal videos. How-
046   ever, W-VAD confronts fundamental dilemmas due to the deficiencies of video-level weak labels.
047   First, when learning abnormal patterns, weak labels lack fine-grained references to abnormal frames.
048   This leads the learning of anomaly to rely on groundless and noisy frame-level predictions, resulting
049   in unreliable anomaly modeling. Second, when learning normal patterns, weak labels lack reference
050
      to normal frames in abnormal videos. Consequently, the learning of normality omits these normal
      frames, which are then misinterpreted as anomalous, causing false alarms.
051
052   To overcome these deficiencies, we take an intuitive step towards more effective supervision for
053   VAD. In real-life scenarios, when humans identify an abnormal video, it is unnecessary to analyze
      the entire video — instead, all they need to do is identify an abnormal frame within the video. We

076   we construct three SF-VAD datasets, SF-ShanghaiTech, SF-UCF-Crime, SF-XD-Violence, by re-
077   annotating widely adopted VAD datasets (Liu et al., 2018a; Sultani et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2020).
078   SF-VAD is a VAD paradigm that leverages each abnormal video with an abnormal frame label, and
079   each normal video with a video-level normal label. Besides access to a specific abnormal frame,
080   SF-VAD further provides insightful dual references to decouple contrary patterns, as in Fig. 1b. On
081   the one hand, SF-VAD offers precise anomaly reference, which can be utilized to mine congruent
082   abnormal frames, facilitating accurate and comprehensive anomaly modeling. On the other hand,
083   SF-VAD implies normality reference in abnormal videos. As annotators typically mark close to
084   the beginning of abnormal events for efficiency, preceding frames can reasonably be considered as
085
      normal. This provides a credible reference to decouple normal patterns in abnormal videos.
086   Leveraging dual references, we propose Frame-guided Probabilistic Contrary Learning (FPCL), to
087   model contrary anomalous and normal patterns simultaneously, thereby decoupling these opposite
088   patterns. Concretely, FPCL models opposite patterns by two modules. First, we devise a Similarity-
089   based Abnormal Pattern Modeling (SAPM) module, to model inclusive abnormal patterns reliably.
090   SAPM first mines the similar abnormal frames by exploring frame-wise similarity between labeled
091
      abnormal frame and frames in consecutive interval. As similar abnormal frames may contain noise,
      SAPM integrates similarity-based abnormal probability to guide the anomaly learning from mined
092
      frames, enabling a dependable learning process. Likewise, we introduce Gaussian-prior Normal
093
      Pattern Modeling (GNPM) module, to decouple the normal patterns in abnormal videos. Treating
094   preceding frames away from abnormal frame as normal, it learns credible normal patterns, guided
095   by Gaussian-prior normal probability. During inference, we additionally devise the temporal decou-
096   pling and boundary refining modules, which calibrate attention map to disentangle abnormal and
097   normal temporal dependencies and reveal discriminative temporal character. Extensive experiments
098   show our method outperforms state-of-the-art VAD methods and achieves an optimal performance-
099   cost trade-off, as depicted in Fig. 1c. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
100
101
            • We propose an effective Single Frame supervised VAD paradigm, which provides insightful
              dual references to abnormal and normal frames at negligible extra cost. To support future
102
              research on this topic, we construct and release three SF-VAD datasets.
103
104         • We devise frame-guided probabilistic contrary learning, to decouple contrary anomalous
105
              and normal patterns via single frame supervision. In inference, we tailor temporal decou-
              pling and boundary refining modules to reveal discriminative temporal character.
106
107         • Extensive results show our SF-VAD method surpasses previous state-of-the-art VAD meth-
              ods, including a higher AUC performance and a lower false alarm rate.

111
112   In Weakly-supervised VAD (W-VAD), only binary video-level labels are available during training, as
113   illustrated in Fig. 1a. To identify anomalous frames from given video X , W-VAD methods predict

115   (MIL) framework to W-VAD, owing to its ability to discriminate abnormal and normal patterns by
116   weak labels. Specifically, MIL encourages the top anomaly scores in an abnormal video to be higher
117   than those in a normal video by MIL-based loss:

      MIL framework, some works refine the learning framework. Lv et al. (2023) address the context bias
123
      in MIL by clustering the ambiguous frames and reforming MIL to leverage the clustered frames for
124

126   et al. (2021b); Zhang et al. (2023) utilize two-stage self-training strategy where the pseudo labels are
127   generated in the first stage by MIL and the discriminative features are learned by pseudo labels in
128   the second stage. Some works use prompt learning to learn abnormal semantics. Chen et al. (2024)
129   introduce text prompts and normal context prompts to learn discriminative anomaly representations.
130   Wu et al. (2023) leverage expressive text features to learn the semantics of various abnormal scenes.
131
132   2.2   I NEXACT S UPERVISION
133
134   Single frame supervision falls under the category of inexact supervision, which relates to learning
135   from imprecisely labeled data. Inexact supervision is widely applied to diverse computer vision
136   tasks due to its promising performance and minor annotation requirements. In semantic segmenta-
137
      tion, Bearman et al. (2016) first introduce inexact supervision, by applying effective point supervi-
      sion, which delivers a promising performance. In video spatio-temporal action localization, Mettes
138
      et al. (2016) extend inexact supervision to frame supervision. Later, Ma et al. (2020) introduce
139
      frame supervision to temporal action localization and a corresponding SF-Net that achieves a com-
140   petitive performance against fully-supervised methods. Li et al. (2021) propose a temporal action
141   segmentation method that integrates model prediction with annotated timestamps to boost accurate
142   segmentation. Recently, Cui et al. (2022) introduce frame supervision to language-driven moment
143   retrieval, which uses the Gaussian distribution to model the probability distribution of foreground
144   action behaviors. In VAD task, inexact supervision has not been yet explored. Therefore, we propose
145   single frame supervised VAD to achieve an optimal performance cost trade-off.

      3.1   S INGLE F RAME SUPERVISED VAD
150
151   Single Frame supervised VAD (SF-VAD) utilizes the abnormal frame label t to learn abnormal
152   patterns. Label t refers to the index of a frame in the given video V where abnormal behavior is
153   observed. Under SF-VAD, a training sample is (V a , t) for each abnormal video, or (V n , 0) for each
154   normal video, where 0 indicates its normality. To identify the anomalous frames, SF-VAD models
155   predict frame-level anomaly scores, where 0 denotes normal and 1 refers to abnormal.
156

      as X ∈ RL×Dm where L equals the number of the clips and Dm is the dimension of the features.

236   Similarity-based Abnormal Pattern Modeling (SAPM) module is proposed to learn inclusive

      5     C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we propose single frame supervised VAD (SF-VAD) with three SF-VAD datasets.
533
      SF-VAD provides additional dual references to decouple contrary patterns. Correspondingly, we
534   propose Frame-guided Probabilistic Contrary Learning (FPCL). FPCL effectively captures compre-
535   hensive abnormal patterns from mined frames, guided by similarity-based abnormal probability for
536   precise learning. It also disentangles normal patterns in abnormal videos by learning from preced-
537   ing frames, utilizing Gaussian-prior normal probability as guidance. During inference, we introduce
538   temporal decoupling and boundary refinement modules to enhance the discriminative temporal tem-
539   poral features for refined detection. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our SF-VAD method
      achieves promising results and delivers an ideal balance between performance and annotation cost.

      A    BASELINE

758   The architecture of the overall framework is depicted in Fig. 2. Concretely, given an untrimmed
759   video with corresponding audio, pertained feature encoder backbones are employed to obtain multi-
760   modal features. Subsequently, the features are passed through the Transformer-based Temporal
761   Modeling (TTM) module and detector to predict frame-level anomaly scores. In training, SAPM is
762   utilized to learn comprehensive abnormal patterns and GNPM is utilized to facilitate fine-grained

769   concatenated as multi-modal feature sequence X ∈ RL×Dm where L equals the number of the clips

815   B    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
816
817   Feature Extraction. To extract video and audio features, we follow existing methods (Wu & Liu,
818   2021b; Pu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2020). We apply the I3D (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017) video
819   encoder that is pre-trained on Kinetics (Kay et al., 2017) dataset, to acquire video features. I3D
820   processes each video frame and aggregates temporal context over a sequence of frames, enabling it
821
      to extract rich, motion-aware features from the video. Video features are extracted from global pool
      layer from the I3D encoder which is 1024 dimensions. To acquire audio features, we utilize VG-
822
      Gish (Hershey et al., 2017) audio encoder that is pre-trained on Youtube (Hershey et al., 2017)
823
      dataset. VGGish extracts high-level audio features that capture acoustic patterns, The acquired au-
824   dio features contain 128 dimensions. For the trade-off of detection performance and computational
825   overhead, each video is split into 16-frame non-overlapping clips, formatting video and audio se-
826   quences. Notably, we employ an augmentation strategy to enhance the generalization ability. For
827   SF-UCF-Crime and SF-ShanghaiTech datasets, we apply a ten-crop augmentation strategy which
828   includes crops from the center, four corners, and their mirrored counterparts. For SF-XD-Violence
829   dataset, we employ a five-crop augmentation strategy, which includes crops from the center and four
830   corners.
831   Hyperparameter. The hidden dimension Dh of transformer-based temporal modeling module is
832   set to 128. The initial gate weight α of transformer-based temporal modeling module is set to 0.5.
833   The window size w is set to 5, 9, 9 for SF-ShanghaiTech, SF-UCF-Crime and SF-XD-Violence
834   respectively. The kernel size and stride of the one-dimension convolutional layer ft are set to 3 and
835   1 respectively. In SAPM, θ̂ is set to 0.05. In GNPM, the σ of the normal probability distribution is
836   set to 0.5, 0.48, 0.7 for SF-ShanghaiTech, SF-UCF-Crime and SF-XD-Violence respectively. Such
837   settings of hyperparameter are obtained by hyperparameter tuning. Interestingly, we find that such
838   a setting is coherent with the statistics of datasets, as depicted in 4.2. As the variance of SF-UCF-
839   Crime is relatively larger than SF-ShanghaiTech, a large σ can force the model to focus on a larger
840
      portion of normal frames. In the boundary refining module, θ′ and θ′′ are set to 4 and 10. In the
      temporal decoupling module, threshold θ is set to 0.0015.
841
842   Training Details. During training, the model parameters are initialized by Xavier initialization.
843   The batch size is set to 64, 128, 128 for SF-ShanghaiTech, SF-UCF-Crime, and SF-XD-Violence
844   respectively. The learning rate is 5 × 10−4 initially and controlled by a cosine decay strategy. The
845   parameters are optimized using Adam optimizer. The number of training epochs is set to 50. For
846
      the balance between computational overhead and detection performance, the maximum sampling
      sequence length is set to 200 during the training phase.

849   C    DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
850
851   To adapt single-frame supervision for video anomaly detection (VAD), one of the primary challenges
852   is the absence of appropriate datasets annotated with fine-grained frame-level labels. Most existing
853   VAD research relies on weak supervision, leading to the development of W-VAD datasets. However,
854
      the ground truth in W-VAD datasets typically consists of binary, video-level labels, which lack the
      necessary granularity for single-frame supervision.
855
856   To address this, we construct three high-quality, human-annotated Single Frame supervised VAD
857   (SF-VAD) datasets based on well-known W-VAD datasets: ShanghaiTech Campus (Liu et al.,
858   2018a), UCF-Crime (Sultani et al., 2018), and XD-Violence (Wu et al., 2020). These SF-VAD
859   datasets are annotated through a carefully designed crowdsourced annotation process.
860   We employed 12 human annotators for this task, each selected through a rigorous process. Before
861   starting, annotators had to familiarize themselves with the definitions of various abnormal behaviors,
862   such as violence, accidents, and theft, and then pass a preliminary annotation test to ensure accuracy.
863   Each annotator worked independently, and cross-validation was conducted to ensure the consistency
      and quality of the annotations. To streamline the annotation process and ensure high accuracy, we


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      provided annotators with the following guidelines: 1) watch the video until they are absolutely sure
865   that they witness an abnormal frame of any form; 2) annotate the abnormal frame as soon as possible
866   under certain confidence to the existence of anomaly.
867
868
      Once all individual annotations were complete, a cross-verification process was performed to iden-
      tify inconsistencies. Discrepancies between annotations were reviewed and corrected, ensuring the
869
      final annotations accurately reflect the frames where abnormal events occur.

873
874   In this work, we apply three widely used W-VAD datasets, which contain various abnormal behav-
875   iors in diverse scenes.
876   ShanghaiTech Campus (Liu et al., 2018a) comprises 437 videos from 13 fixed-view campus
877   surveillance cameras. The abnormal types are cycling, chasing, cart, fighting, skateboarding, ve-
878   hicle, running, jumping, wandering, lifting, robbery, climbing over, throwing. The background of
879   the frames is rather steady and contains less noise, which highlights the behaviors within the frames.
880
      UCF-Crime (Sultani et al., 2018) comprises 1900 videos collected from a variety of sources in-
881
      cluding videos from surveillance cameras and social media with a total duration of 128 hours. The
882   dataset covers 13 real-world anomalies of crimes including abuse, arrest, arson, assault, burglary,
883   explosion, fighting, road accident, shooting, shoplifting, stealing, vandalism and robbery. The rep-
884   resentations of the anomalies are varied and differentiated which increases the challenge of the
885   detection by requiring a more thorough understanding of the anomaly semantics.
886
      XD-Violence (Wu et al., 2020) is the largest and most challenging multi-modal VAD dataset con-
887
      taining 4754 untrimmed videos with a total duration of 217 hours. The dataset contains videos from
888   various sources such as movies, social media, car cameras, surveillance, and games where exist
889   extensive artistic expressions such as changing perspective, view zooming, dynamic lighting, and
890   rapid camera movements. The above characteristics of the datasets draw non-negligible difficulty to
891   anomaly detection models. It covers anomalies of 6 types including abuse, car accidents, explosions,
892   fighting, riots, and shooting.

      E    A NNOTATION T IME E STIMATION
895
896
      Data annotation is actually an intricate process that involves both labeling time and hidden time.
897
      Labeling time consists of the time that annotators watch the videos, the time that the annotators
898
      playback to find a particular frame or make sure the temporal boundary of anomalies, and the time
899   the annotators that double check their annotations. While there is also a huge amount of hidden
900   time, e.g., the time that the supervisors cross validate the annotations and handle the conflicts, the
901   time to debug the annotation platform, or the time of training the annotators to pass the preliminary
902   test. Owing to the complexity of estimating the actual annotation time, in this work, we estimate the
903   annotation time based on the theoretical low bound of estimated annotation time.
904
      Fully-supervised VAD utilizes frame-level labels, which requires the annotators to watch the all
905   videos from the beginning to the end at least one time. Thus, the low bound of annotation time
906   equals the entire duration of the datasets.
907
908
      Semi-supervised VAD leverage normal videos only, however, the annotators need to watch the
      entire video snippets to make sure that the videos do not contain anomalies of any form. Therefore,
909
      the low bound of annotation time equals the total duration of the normal videos in the dataset.
910
911   Weakly-supervised VAD uses video-level binary labels. For videos in the test set, the estimated
912   annotation time is equivalent to the total duration of the test videos. For normal videos in the train
913   set, the estimated annotation time equals the total duration as well, since the annotators need to
914
      watch the entire video to make sure it is a normal one. For abnormal videos in the training set,
      the estimated annotation time is estimated as the sum of time an annotator spends observing an
915
      abnormal frame within an abnormal video.
916
917   Single Frame supervised VAD leverages single frame annotation. Assuming that we elaborately
      devise an annotation platform, that enables the annotators to label the abnormal frame as soon as they
