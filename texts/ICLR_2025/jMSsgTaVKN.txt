011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013               Zero-Shot Object State Classification (ZS-OSC) aims to recognize unseen object
014               states without any visual training examples. Existing methods typically rely on
015               Knowledge Graphs (KGs) to provide semantic information about states, but they
016
                  often treat KGs as homogeneous, overlooking the rich relational knowledge en-
                  coded in their structure. We propose a novel approach to ZS-OSC1 that leverages
017
                  meta-paths to capture complex relationships between object states in a KG. Our
018
                  method learns to project semantic information from the KG into the visual space
019               via meta-path learning, generating discriminative visual embeddings for unseen
020               state classes. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to utilize meta-
021               paths for ZS-OSC. We conduct extensive experiments on four benchmark datasets,
022               demonstrating the superior performance of our approach compared to SoTA zero-
023               shot learning methods and a graph-based baseline. Our ablation study further
024               provides insights into the impact of key design choices on the effectiveness of our
025               method.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Knowledge graphs (KGs) have become increasingly important in addressing various computer vi-
      sion (CV) tasks, such as object classification (Marino et al., 2017), zero-shot recognition (Wang
031
      et al., 2018b) and Visual Question Answering (Krishna et al., 2017). This surge in KG utilization
032
      can be attributed to their ability to provide rich semantic information and contextual knowledge that
033   can enhance the understanding of visual data. However, current approaches often under-utilize the
034   full potential of KGs. Several factors contribute to this sub-optimal utilization. First, KGs used in
035   Computer Vision (CV) are often large and contain irrelevant or erroneous information, which can
036   introduce noise and hinder performance. Second, many methods fail to consider the diversity of
037   edge types in KGs, treating all edges as homogeneous and overlooking valuable relational knowl-
038   edge. This simplistic approach limits the ability to effectively exploit the rich semantic information
039   embedded within KGs.
040   A number of approaches such as filtering mechanisms (Wang et al., 2014; Domingos & Richardson,
041   2007), ad-hoc KG construction (Dong et al., 2014; Gouidis et al., 2024) and random walking (Per-
042   ozzi et al., 2014; Grover & Leskovec, 2016) were proposed as strategies against these shortcomings.
043   An alternative approach focusing on the overcoming of these limitations concerned the concept of
044   meta-paths (Sun et al., 2011; Dong et al., 2017). The utilization of meta-paths essentially involves
045   the learning of the relative importance of the different paths between nodes within the KG via the
046   assignment of weights to them. Meta-path-based approaches offer several advantages when applied
047
      to heterogeneous information networks (HINs). First, meta-paths enable the modeling of complex
      semantic relationships by explicitly defining sequences of node and edge types (Figure 1), making
048
      them particularly suitable for capturing the diverse interactions in multi-typed networks (Sun et al.,
049
      2011; Shi et al., 2014). This provides a significant advantage over traditional graph learning meth-
050   ods (Sun et al., 2011) , which treat all nodes and edges as homogeneous, thus failing to leverage
051   the rich information embedded in HINs. Furthermore, meta-paths allow for task-specific traversal
052   and filtering, enabling more accurate representations for applications such as link prediction, recom-

      Motivated by the great potential that the meta-paths learning seems to hold, this work attempts

      to explore the utilization of this approach in the context of the Object State Classification (OSC)
078
      task (Isola et al., 2015; Gouidis et al., 2022; Souček et al., 2022; Saini et al., 2023), which is a CV
079   task attracting growing research attention over the last few years. OSC concerns the recognition of
080   object states appearing in images and videos and is closely related to the more popular problems
081   of Object Recognition and Action Recognition. OSC is an important problem whose solution is of
082   significant impact. The recognition of object states and state changes is crucial for determining an
083   object‘s condition and the interaction that was performed upon or could be performed in the future
084   on it (Jamone et al., 2016). Moreover, the capacity for efficient OSC is of primary importance in
085   AI systems that support tasks such as learning object affordances (Chuang et al., 2018), recognizing
086   interactions (Wang et al., 2016b; Isola et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2017; Mancini et al., 2022), reasoning

088
      others. Meanwhile, large-scale video datasets (Grauman et al., 2022; Saini et al., 2023) concerning
089
      human-object interactions provide rich annotation data which are related to object state changes
090   enabling the definition of new problems and the establishment of benchmarks and challenges related
091   to object state detection and classification (Grauman et al., 2022).

093
      This work addresses the challenging task of Zero-Shot Object State Classification (ZS-OSC), where
      the goal is to classify object states without any visual training examples. The key challenge lies
094
      in leveraging auxiliary non-visual information to enable the classification of these unseen states.
095
      Existing state-of-the-art methods typically utilize KGs as sources of structured semantic informa-
096   tion (Gouidis et al., 2023), but they often treat KGs as homogeneous, potentially overlooking valu-
097   able relational information. We propose a novel approach that leverages meta-paths for more effec-
098   tive learning of zero-shot representations. Our method learns to project semantic information from
099   a KG into the visual space via meta-path learning, generating visual embeddings for unseen state
100   classes. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first method to utilize meta-paths in the context of
101   Zero-Shot Visual Classification, and therefore ZS-OSC, through embedding generation.
102   Beyond their demonstrated utility in ZS-OSC, we posit that the generation of meta-path-based em-
103   beddings as a primary research objective holds significant promise. This direction offers several
104   compelling advantages. First, meta-path-based embeddings can capture both local and global se-
105   mantic relationships in heterogeneous networks, providing universal entity representations applica-
106   ble across diverse tasks and domains without requiring task-specific fine-tuning. This fosters the
107   development of multi-purpose, generalizable embeddings. Second, generating embeddings with a
      focus on representation quality can facilitate knowledge transfer across domains. Third, while cur-

109   prioritizes the generation of meta-path embeddings as an independent objective would offer a gen-

111   task-dependent tuning and enable researchers to investigate embeddings without being constrained
112   by a particular task. Finally, since meta-paths effectively capture complex, higher-order relation-

      Meta-path Learning: Meta-paths, a fundamental concept in heterogeneous information networks
133   (HINs) (Shi et al., 2016), have been widely studied in applications such as similarity search, recom-
134   mendation systems, and link prediction. Meta-paths were introduced to capture complex relation-
135   ships in HINs, enabling the study of object proximities and connectivity patterns. The work by Sun
136   et al. (2011) proposed PathSim that used meta-paths to measure similarity between objects based on
137   shared relationships, with success in applications like similarity search and clustering. Extending
138   this, Shi et al. (2014) proposed HeteSim, which computes relevance between objects via meta-paths,
139   incorporating directionality and node types for enhanced flexibility. Meta-paths have also enhanced
140   recommendation systems in HINs. Yu et al. (2013) developed a collaborative filtering algorithm
141
      incorporating meta-path-based similarities between users and items, improving recommendation
      accuracy. Similarly, Wang et al. (2016a) used meta-path-based features in matrix factorization for
142
      item recommendation, leveraging HIN structure to model user-item interactions more effectively.
143
144   Learning optimal meta-path weights for specific tasks has been a key research area. Dong et al.
145   (2017) introduced MetaPath2Vec, which learns node embeddings through meta-path-based random
146   walks, showing improved performance in classification and clustering tasks. Fu et al. (2020) ex-
147
      tended this with a scalable meta-path-guided graph neural network, learning meta-path importance
      for tasks in large-scale HINs. In link prediction, meta-paths have been used to predict missing
148
      or future links in networks. Liu et al. (2018) proposed a meta-path-based link prediction method,
149
      capturing complex node interactions and outperforming traditional algorithms in networks with mul-
150   tiple types of nodes. Recent advancements in graph neural networks (GNNs) have further improved
151   meta-path-based link prediction. Zhang et al. (2019) introduced a heterogeneous graph neural net-
152   work (HGNN) that integrates meta-path-based features into node aggregation, achieving better link
153   prediction.
154
      Sun & Han (2013) introduced the concept of meta-paths for mining HINs, capturing semantic re-
155
      lationships across data types. Automatic discovery methods for meta-paths were later explored
156   by Meng et al. (2015) to address challenges in manually retrieving meta-paths. Ferrini et al. (2024)
157   proposed a novel approach to enhance GNN accuracy through effective meta-path identification,
158   while Noori et al. (2023) explored meta-paths for flexible similarity search in biological knowledge
159   graphs. Additionally, Yun et al. (2022) discussed learning new graph structures using meta-paths,
160   demonstrating their role in enhancing GNN performance. Recent studies have also focused on chal-
161   lenges such as automatic meta-path discovery (Huang et al., 2020), integration with deep learning
      models (Wang et al., 2019), and the application of meta-paths in dynamic HINs (Trivedi et al., 2019).

162
      The main novelty of our work distinguishing it from the aforementioned works concerns the utiliza-
163   tion of meta-paths in the context of embeddings generation. Although many actual works focusing
164   on meta-paths utilize embeddings, they serve as a means for another goal, i.e. downstream task,
165   such as link prediction or entity alignment. In contrast, in our work, the generation of embeddings
166   is a final objective.
167
       Object State and Attribute Classification: Visual attributes are commonly defined as visual con-
168
      cepts that are both machine-detectable and human-understandable (Duan et al., 2012). The prevail-
169   ing approach to learning attributes mirrors that of object classes, where a convolutional neural net-
170   work is trained with discriminative classifiers using annotated image datasets (Singh & Lee, 2016).
171   However, existing labeled attribute datasets suffer from limitations such as smaller scale compared
172   to object datasets, a restricted number of generic attributes, and limited category coverage (Lam-
173   pert et al., 2009; Isola et al., 2015; Patterson & Hays, 2016; Yu & Grauman, 2017; Mancini et al.,
174   2022). Furthermore, research specifically focusing on state classification remains limited (Gouidis
175   et al., 2022), with most existing approaches relying on similar assumptions and techniques as those
176   employed for attribute classification. This highlights a need for dedicated methods tailored to the
177
      unique challenges of state recognition.
178   Zero-shot Classification: Zero-shot learning (ZSL) has attracted significant attention due to its
179   ability to address the practical challenge of classifying objects without any training examples (Xian
180   et al., 2018a). This is particularly crucial in real-world scenarios where obtaining labeled data for
181   every possible class is often infeasible. Several approaches have been proposed for zero-shot ob-
182   ject classification, including semantic embedding-based methods (Wang et al., 2018a; Xian et al.,
183
      2018b), attribute-based methods (Lampert et al., 2014), generative models (Xian et al., 2018b;
      Changpinyo et al., 2016), and learning compatibility functions between image and class embed-
184
      dings (Akata et al., 2015). Semantic embedding methods utilize compact semantic spaces or at-
185
      tribute sets to bridge the gap between seen and unseen object classes. Attribute-based methods
186   leverage descriptive attributes to infer the class of unseen objects. Generative models synthesize
187   images of unseen classes based on similarities to seen classes. Additionally, recent work has ex-
188   plored the use of knowledge graphs to capture semantic relationships between objects and facilitate
189   ZSL (Kampffmeyer et al., 2019; Nayak & Bach, 2022). While ZSL has been extensively studied
190   for object recognition, its application to Zero State Classification (ZSC) remains relatively unex-
191   plored. With the exception of Gouidis et al. (2023), which focuses exclusively on state classification
192   without relying on prior knowledge about object classes, existing ZSC methods primarily address
193   the Compositional Zero-Shot Learning (CZSL) variant of the problem. CZSL aims to generalize
194
      to unseen combinations of object and state primitives by learning their compositionality from the
      training set (Misra et al., 2017; Nagarajan & Grauman, 2018; Yang et al., 2020).

197   3     M ETHODOLOGY
198
199   This work introduces a novel approach for generating embeddings in heterogeneous graphs by syn-
200   ergistically combining KGs structures with meta-path-based GNNs. These generated embeddings
201   are then employed for the task of ZS-OSC. Our methodology harnesses the strengths of both KGs
202   embeddings and the rich relational information encapsulated by meta-paths. The core idea is to
203   leverage a designated set of KG nodes as guides for meta-path learning. Specifically, we utilize the
204   visual embeddings of these nodes as ground truth and train a Graph Transformer Network (GTN)
205   to assign weights to different KG edge types. This is achieved by generating embeddings for the
206   guide nodes that align with their visual embeddings. The GTN architecture is inspired by Yun et al.
207
      (2022), while the training procedure draws inspiration from Kampffmeyer et al. (2019); Gouidis
      et al. (2023). Figure 2 provides a general overview of our method.

340   3.3   Z ERO -S HOT O BJECT S TATE C LASSIFICATION
341
342   To achieve zero-shot object-state classification, it is crucial to learn representations for the unseen
343   target state classes. To this end, we construct a KG with state classes as nodes and utilize the
344   GTN trained in the previous step for meta-path learning to generate embeddings for these target
345   classes. These embeddings are then integrated into a visual classifier, specifically a pre-trained
346
      CNN, to serve as visual representations of the visually known state classes. Following established
      practices in transfer learning and zero-shot learning, we replace the final layer of the pre-trained
347
      CNN with these generated embeddings. To ensure compatibility, the dimensionality (D) of the
348
      generated embeddings is set to match the dimension of the CN‘s last layer. This adaptation enables
349   the CNN to effectively classify the target state classes in a zero-shot manner.

      4.1   I MPLEMENTATION AND EVALUATION ISSUES
355
      Implementation Details: The KG provided as input to the Graph Transformer Network (GTN) for
356   meta-path learning was the WordNet hierarchy of the 1000 classes from the ImageNet1000 dataset
357   (Russakovsky et al., 2015). These 1000 classes served as the ground truth set for training the GTN,
358   which was trained for 200 epochs with five different learning rates (see Section 4.2 for details). The
359   same KG was also used to generate the target object state classes. In experiments with multiple
360   softmax channels, the generated embeddings were averaged. The convolutional neural network
361   (CNN) used for zero-shot object-state classification (ZS-OSC) was ResNet-101 (He et al., 2016)
362   pre-trained on the ImageNet1000 dataset.
363   Datasets: We utilized four publicly available datasets containing object state annotations: OSDD
364   Gouidis et al. (2022), CGQA Mancini et al. (2022), MIT Isola et al. (2015), and VAW Pham et al.
365   (2021). While OSDD is specifically designed for state detection, the other three are attribute datasets
366   that include object states among their classes. We extracted subsets concerning exclusively object
367   state classes. For OSDD and VAW, bounding boxes from the original images were extracted to
368   create images suitable for the OSC task. Table S2 in the supplementary section provides details on
369   the four datasets.
370   Metrics: Our evaluation follows the zero-shot method from (Purushwalkam et al., 2019). Following
371   those guidelines we calculate accuracy per class and then average these instead of reporting overall
372   accuracy. This ensures each class is equally important, regardless of its sample size.

471   5   C ONCLUSION
472
473
      This paper introduced a novel method for generating embeddings in heterogeneous graphs by lever-
474   aging meta-paths within a graph neural network framework. Our approach utilizes KGs structures
475   and visual embeddings to guide the learning of meta-paths, enabling the generation of informative
476   and discriminative embeddings. We demonstrated the effectiveness of our method in the context
477   of ZS-OSC, achieving superior performance compared to state-of-the-art LPMs and a graph-based
478   baseline. Our ablation study provided insights into the impact of key parameters on the performance
479   of our method.
480   Future work will focus on several promising directions. First, we aim to explore the applicability
481   of our method to other zero shot CV tasks such as semantic segmentation, image captioning and
482   visual question answering to further evaluate its generalizability. Second, we plan to investigate the
483   integration of different types of KGs such as multimodal KGs and KGs containg different type of
484   nodes and explore the impact of KG quality on embedding generation. Finally, we intend to extend
485   our approach to incorporate more complex meta-path structures and explore alternative graph neural
      network architectures for enhanced performance.
