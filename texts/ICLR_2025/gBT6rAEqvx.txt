009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As a much possible way of improving first-order stochastic optimization (FSO),
012            the role of second-order information in stochastic optimization is receiving an in-
013            creasing attention especially for solving the model with large-scale datasets in re-
014            cent years, resulting in various second-order stochastic optimization (SSO) meth-
015            ods, e.g., the stochastic Newton (SN) method, the stochastic quasi-Newton (SQN)
016            method, etc. However, the question of how to set an appropriate update rule of the
017
               learning rate for SSO methods is still an extremely intractable task, and surpris-
               ingly there is quite less literature to tackle this issue. To bridge the gap between
018
               the SSO methods and the learning rate, this work develops a class of adaptive SSO
019
               methods from the perspective of adaptive gradient methods. Concretely, a general
020            adaptive gradient (GAG) method with the quasi-hyperbolic momentum (QHM)
021            strategy that encompasses Adam, AdaGrad, RMSProp, etc., as the special case of
022            GAG, is incorporated into SN and SQN, respectively, which leads to two methods:
023            SN-GAG and SQN-GAG. In addition, we establish a unified analysis for different
024            adaptive SSO methods, covering their convergence behavior and computational
025            complexity for different backgrounds, such as the strongly convex (SC) case and
026            the Polyak-Łojasiewicz (PŁ) case, where, particularly, the latter is missing in cur-
027            rent studies. Finally, numerical tests on different applications of machine learning
028
               demonstrate the superiority and the robustness of the resulting methods.

      technique, where the first stage executed average SGD with the learning rate of order O √1k , and
111   the second stage optimized a quadratic model of the loss function with a constant learning rate. By
112   combining stochastic semismooth Newton steps and stochastic proximal gradient steps, Milzarek

124   fast convergence. Kasai et al. (2019) developed a Riemannian SQN method with variance reduction.
125   Zhu et al. (2020) developed a new variance reduction and quasi-Newton preconditioning framework
126   for particle-based variational inference methods.
127
      Momentum Techniques for SSO Methods. The role of momentum in SSO methods has been in-
128   vestigated by many studies. To improve the practical performance of SpiderSQN, Zhang et al. (2021)

130   stochastic variance-reduced Nesterov’s accelerated quasi-Newton methods in full and limited mem-
131   ory forms. Similarly, Indrapriyadarsini et al. (2020) developed the SQN method with Nesterov’s
132   accelerated gradient (NAG) in both its full and limited memory forms for dealing with large-scale

134   regularized stochastic Nesterovs accelerated quasi-Newton method to effectively accelerate the con-
135   vergence rate and avoid the near-singularity problem of the Hessian update in the stochastic BFGS
136
      method.

138   1.2    M AIN C ONTRIBUTIONS
139
      For first-order stochastic optimization (FSO) algorithms, various update rules of the learning rate
140
      have been proposed as mentioned above. Surprisingly, the research on the role of the learning rate
141   in SSO methods is quite limited. The existing SSO algorithms usually work with a scalar constant
142   learning rate, or a diminishing learning rate (Zhang et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2020). Also, the line
143   search technique is also considered in SSO algorithms to obtain the learning rate (Guo et al., 2023;
144   Wills & Schön, 2021; Byrd et al., 2012; Schraudolph et al., 2007). However, most of them are time
145   consuming or impractical for large-scale models. In addition, we found that Duchi et al. (2011)
146   applied AdaGrad to compute the learning rate for SQN. Instead of using the line search technique,
147   Zhou et al. (2017) proposed using the properties of self-concordant functions to compute an adaptive
148   learning rate for BFGS and thereby avoided executing line searches. To bridge the gap between SSO
149
      methods and the learning rate, this work equips second-order stochastic optimization methods with
      an adaptive update rule of the learning rate. For clarity, we summarize our main contributions as
150
      follows:
151
152         (1) We develop a class of adaptive second-order stochastic optimization methods by utilizing
153             a general adaptive gradient (GAG) method to compute the learning rate for SSO methods,
154             where GAG encompasses most of existing adaptive gradient methods, such as AdaGrad,
155             RMSProp, Adam, etc. Specifically, we incorporate such the learning rate into classical SN
156             method and the SQN method, respectively, leading to two novel SSO methods, referred to
157             as SN-GAG and SQN-GAG.
158         (2) Further, we establish a unified analysis for SN-GAG and SQN-GAG under different back-
159             grounds, involving the strongly convex (SC) objective function and the Polyak-Łojasiewicz
160             (PŁ) objective function. Particularly, under mild conditions, we prove that the resulting SN-
161             GAG and SQN-GAG methods have a linear convergence rate and recover the well-known
                oracle complexity for models with the SC and PŁ constraints respectively.

            (3) Finally, our empirical analysis on different machine learning tasks demonstrates that the
163             resulting algorithms perform better in contrast to classical adaptive gradient methods, state-
164             of-the-art FSO methods, and SSO methods. Moreover, various numerical tests show the
165             robustness of our methods to different key hyper-parameters.

      5   C ONCLUSION
524
      Motivated by the gap between the SSO methods and the learning rate, this work developed a class
525
      of adaptive SSO methods from the perspective of adaptive gradient methods. More generally, we
526   proposed to use a GAG method, encompassing most of existing adaptive gradient method (e.g.,
527   Adam, AdaGrad, RMSProp, etc.), to automatically compute the learning rate for SSO methods.
528   Specifically, we applied GAG into two stochastic versions of Newton-like methods, SN and SQN,
529   leading to two new methods: SN-GAG (Algorithm 1) and SQN-GAG (Algorithm 2). We theoretical-
530   ly understood the role of GAG in SSO methods. Concretely, we proved that SN-GAG (Algorithm
531   1) and SQN-GAG (Algorithm 2) converged linearly under different backgrounds (a.k.a. the SC case
532   and the PŁ case) and showed that the computational complexities of the resulting SSO methods
533   were comparable to state-of-the-art FSO methods and SSO methods, where the property of SSO
534
      methods under the PŁ case was lacking in existing literature. The applications of SN-GAG (Algo-
      rithm 1) and SQN-GAG (Algorithm 2) in different machine learning tasks confirmed their efficacy
535
      by comparing with adaptive gradient methods, first-order stochastic methods and second-order s-
536
      tochastic methods. Further, a large number of numerical experiments demonstrated the robustness
537   of the resulting algorithms.

743   GRAD Reddi et al. (2018), SVRG Johnson & Zhang (2013), SARAH Nguyen et al. (2017a), and
744   SLBFGS Mokhtari & Ribeiro (2020). AdaGrad, Adam, RMSProp, and AMSGRAD are four classi-
745   cal adaptive gradient methods. Specifically, AMSGRAD is a variant of Adam, depending on long-
746
      term memory of historical gradients and converging with higher probability than Adam. SVRG and
      SARAH are two popular first-order stochastic methods and converge linearly for the SC objective
747
      function. SLBFGS introduces the SVRG-like gradient estimator into LBFGS.
748
749   The parameter settings for different comparative methods are described here. When executing RM-
750   SProp, the parameter β is set to be β = 0.9. We perform Adam and AMSGRAD with β1 = 0.9
751   and β2 = 0.999 for different datasets. SVRG, SARAH and SLBFGS work with a constant learning
752
      rate, where we select the learning rate for SVRG, SARAH, and SLBFGS from multiple learning
      rates which make them behave better. When executing SN-GAG (Algorithm 1) and SQN-GAG
753
      (Algorithm 2), we set α1 = 0.9, α2 = 0.9, β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.9, and   = 1 for all datasets.
