                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               The power of foundation models (FMs) lies in their capacity to learn highly ex-
010
               pressive representations that can be adapted to a broad spectrum of tasks. How-
011            ever, these pretrained models require multiple stages of fine-tuning to become
012            effective for downstream applications. Conventionally, the model is first retrained
013            on the aggregate of a diverse set of tasks of interest and then adapted to spe-
014            cific low-resource downstream tasks by utilizing a parameter-efficient fine-tuning
015            (PEFT) scheme. While this procedure seems reasonable, the independence of the
016            retraining and fine-tuning stages causes a major issue, as there is no guarantee
017            the retrained model will achieve good performance post-fine-tuning. To explicitly
018            address this issue, we introduce a meta-learning framework infused with PEFT in
019
               this intermediate retraining stage to learn a model that can be easily adapted to un-
               seen tasks. For our theoretical results, we focus on linear models using low-rank
020
               adaptations. In this setting, we demonstrate the suboptimality of standard retrain-
021
               ing for finding an adaptable set of parameters. Further, we prove that our method
022            recovers the optimally adaptable parameters. We then apply these theoretical in-
023            sights to retraining the RoBERTa model to predict the continuation of conversa-
024            tions between different personas within the ConvAI2 dataset. Empirically, we ob-
025            serve significant performance benefits using our proposed meta-learning scheme
026            during retraining relative to the conventional approach.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Foundation Models (FMs) learn rich representations that are useful for a variety of downstream
032   tasks. FMs are trained in three general stages to fit user-specific tasks like context-specific language
033
      generation and personalized image synthesis, among others. The first stage is commonly referred to
      as pretraining, where FMs are trained from scratch on a combination of massive public, propriety,
034
      and synthetic sources of data to learn a general-purpose model (Devlin et al., 2019; Brown et al.,
035
      2020; Abdin et al., 2024; Radford et al., 2021). This stage is largely inaccessible to most due to the
036   enormous cost of training state-of-the-art models on such large datasets.
037
038   Thus, the most popular and viable way to utilize FMs for individual tasks is to take a pretrained
039
      model and retrain it for a specific objective. In this second training stage, we refine the pretrained
      model and retrain it on a large set of tasks of interest. For clarity, we generally refer to this intermedi-
040
      ate stage as retraining. Other works have referred to this stage as pre-finetuning (Aghajanyan et al.,
041
      2021) or supervised fine-tuning (Dong et al., 2024). In the third stage, referred to as fine-tuning,
042   the model is ultimately trained on an individual low-resource task. For example, a pretrained large
043   language model (LLM) can be downloaded and retrained on a large multi-lingual corpus to per-
044   form English-Spanish and English-Italian translations. Then, one may adapt the model to translate
045   English to French using a small English-French translation dataset. For this last stage, the model
046   is typically fine-tuned using parameter efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods – training heuristics
047   which sacrifice learning expressiveness for improved computational efficiency (Hu et al., 2021; Li
048   & Liang, 2021). PEFT is especially useful in the low-resource setting, as running full fine-tuning of
049   the model’s parameters on a small number of samples is expensive and potentially unnecessary.
050   Conventional retraining updates either a subset or all of the model parameters to fit the aggregation
051   of the different retraining tasks. While this approach seems reasonable and has been successful
052   in improving downstream task performance (Khashabi et al., 2020; Raffel et al., 2020), it does
053   not leverage knowledge of the downstream fine-tuning procedure to cater the retrained model to
      perform well after such adaptation. Rather, it retrains the model to minimize the average loss across

054
      the retraining tasks regardless of the PEFT method to be employed later. This raises two key issues.
055   Firstly, there may not exist a single set of model parameters that simultaneously fits the various
056   retraining tasks. Secondly, even if the model is sufficiently over-parameterized, there is no assurance
057   the recovered retrained solution is indeed adaptable to future unseen tasks relative to other possible
058   solutions during retraining, as the retraining and fine-tuning are performed independently.
059
      We address these issues by drawing upon ideas from meta-learning, a framework designed to explic-
060
      itly train models for future adaptation. Meta-learning is a common method to improve model per-
061   formance after fine-tuning, typically in low-resource, few-shot settings using gradient-based adap-
062   tations (Finn et al., 2017; Lee & Choi, 2018). Moreover, it has begun to be applied to FM retraining
063   to prepare models for downstream fine-tuning (Hou et al., 2022; Hong & Jang, 2022; Bansal et al.,
064   2022; Gheini et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023). However, it is not yet understood whether meta-learning
065   how to fine-tune can provably confer performance benefits over standard retraining followed by
066   PEFT. In this work, we provide rigorous theoretical and empirical evidence that this is indeed the
067   case. We first study a stylized linear model where the ground truth parameters for both the retrain-
068   ing and fine-tuning tasks are realizable by low-rank adaptations. We validate our theory through
069
      synthetic data and show that our insights improve performance on real language tasks using large
      language models (LLMs). Specifically, our contributions are as follows:
070
071
             • We develop a generalized framework to model standard retraining and propose the Meta-
072            Adapters objective for retraining, a meta-learning-inspired objective function for infusing
073            PEFT in foundation model retraining. Our framework can be implemented with any PEFT
074            algorithm, but we emphasize the incorporation of LoRA (Hu et al., 2021).
075
             • For a linear model applied to multiple tasks whose ground truth parameters are realizable by
076            LoRA, we show standard retraining does not recover an adaptable set of model parameters
077            (Theorem 1) and thus incurs significant loss on unseen tasks after fine-tuning (Corollary 1).
078            We prove two key results for the Meta-Adapter’s objective function:
079
                 – Any model that globally minimizes this objective can be exactly fine-tuned to unseen
080
                   tasks (Theorem 2), and when retraining on three or more tasks, the ground truth pa-
081                rameters are the unique global minimum up to orthogonal symmetry (Theorem 3).
082                This uniqueness property holds as long as the data dimension is sufficiently large,
083                which is counterintuitive to previous work on multi-task learning theory that requires
084                the number of tasks to be larger than the effective task dimension (Du et al., 2021;
085                Collins et al., 2022).
086              – For two retraining tasks, second-order stationarity is sufficient to guarantee global
087                minimization for our Meta-Adapters loss (Theorem 4). In this case, our Meta-
088                Adapters objective function is provably amenable to local optimization methods.
089          • To test our theoretical insights, we compare the performance of the standard retraining and
090            Meta-Adapters objectives for linear models using LoRA while relaxing the assumptions
091            from our theory. We show clear improvements using the Meta-Adapters objective for all
092            data generation parameter settings and for different numbers of tasks. Then, we apply our
093            meta-learning method to the RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019) large language model (LLM) on
094            the ConvAI2 dataset (Dinan et al., 2019), a real-world multi-task dataset for generating
095            continuations of conversations between different personas. Again, we show improvements
096
               using the Meta-Adapters relative to retraining then fine-tuning.
097   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
098
099   Meta-learning is a framework for learning models that can be rapidly adapted to new unseen tasks
100   by leveraging access to prior tasks during training. For example, Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning
101   (MAML) (Finn et al., 2017) is a popular, flexible method that aims to find a model that can be
102   adapted to a new unseen task after a small number of steps of gradient descent on the unseen task’s
103   loss function. Further, other works have proposed methods specific to low-dimensional linear mod-
104
      els and have shown strong results and connections between meta-learning and representation learn-
      ing (Collins et al., 2022; Thekumparampil et al., 2021).
105
106   In the case of FMs, other lines of work have proposed meta-learning approaches where the task-
107   specific adaptation incorporates PEFT methods rather than few-shot gradient updates of all model
      parameters. (Hong & Jang, 2022; Bansal et al., 2022; Gheini et al., 2022) apply meta-learning with


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      architecture adaptations that inject small task-specific trainable layers within the FM architecture.
109   (Hou et al., 2022) further combines architecture adaptations with parameter perturbation adaptations
110   similar to LoRA. They consider a complicated meta-learning loss that separates the available training
111   tasks data into training and testing tasks, and they update the adapters and FM weights over different
112   splits of the data. Using combinations of architecture and parameter adaptation methods, they show
113   empirical gains over retraining, then fine-tuning, and other gradient-based MAML-style algorithms.
114   (Aghajanyan et al., 2021) similarly proposes a multi-task objective that trains an FM on different
115   tasks simultaneously to encourage learning a universally applicable representation. They force the
116   FM to learn a common shared data representation and apply a different prediction head for each
117
      retraining task. They run extensive empirical studies and observe performance improvements in a
      large-scale setting when 15 or more tasks are used in the retraining stage.
118
119   These works propose some kind of meta-learning or multi-task objective and show empirical gains
120   over standard retraining strategies on natural language datasets, yet none explain when standard
121   retraining is insufficient relative to meta-learning and multi-task approaches, how many tasks are
122   needed to learn a rich representation, and how to best adapt to tasks unseen in the training stage.
123   Lastly, although we focus on LoRA, different PEFT methods have been proposed, including variants
124   of LoRA (Liu et al., 2024; Dettmers et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) and architecture adaptations
125   (Houlsby et al., 2019) among others. Further, recent works have begun analyzing theoretical aspects
126   of LoRA in the fine-tuning stage (Jang et al., 2024; Zeng & Lee, 2023). These works have started ad-
127   vancing the theory of LoRA, but they explore orthogonal directions to the analysis of meta-learning
128   infused with LoRA. We include an extended discussion of these works in Appendix A.

      To test the Meta-LoRA objective beyond linear models, we perform experiments using the pre-
468   trained 355 million parameter RoBERTa-Large model on the ConvAI2 dataset. ConvAI2 consists
469   of conversations between two personas, i.e. people with different personalities. Each persona is
470   associated with a short list of factual information that guides the content of their responses. We
471   model learning the dialogue continuations of each individual persona as a different task. A training
472   sample for a given persona consists of the previous conversation as input and 20 candidate dialogue
473   continuations, where one of the 20 candidates is the true continuation. We consider the supervised
474   learning task of selecting the correct continuation. During training, we maximize the log-likelihood
475   of the correct continuation and minimize the log-likelihood of each of the incorrect continuations
476
      conditioned on the observed conversation history. To run inference given the past conversation and
      the 20 possible continuations, we select the continuation with the highest conditional likelihood.
477
478   For both the standard retraining and Meta-LoRA objectives, we retrain the model using the T = 10
479   largest retraining tasks, with an average of 117.4 training samples and 36.5 heldout samples per
480   retraining task. We select the model from the epoch with the best average accuracy on the heldout
481   samples and then fine-tune to each of the 10 largest test tasks. For each test task, we take the accuracy
482
      on the heldout data from the best performing epoch. We run 5 random trials for this entire retraining
      and fine-tuning process and report the median best heldout accuracy for each task. All training was
483
      done on a single Nvidia A40 GPU, and we report our training hyperparameters in Appendix C.

533   5      C ONCLUSION
534
535   We introduced the Meta-Adapters objective function for retraining an FM on a collection of tasks
536   in a way that prepares the model for subsequent downstream fine-tuning. We provide theoretical
537   justifications on the shortcomings of standard retraining as well as where the Meta-Adapters objec-
538   tive using LoRA (Meta-LoRA) can provably improve performance. Empirically, our Meta-LoRA
539   objective outperforms standard retraining for adapting to unseen downstream tasks. Future avenues
      include extending our theoretical analysis to finite sample settings and to more general adapters.

      A     R ELATED W ORK ON L O RA-S TYLE PEFT
703
704
      There is a vast amount of work in developing PEFT methods for FMs. The LoRA algorithm (Hu
705   et al., 2021) has established itself as a popular and successful PEFT strategy and has inspired various
706   extensions such as QLoRA, DoRA, and others (Dettmers et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
707   2023). These algorithms are heuristics for mimicking the full finetuning of an FM to a specific
708   downstream task and have proven to be empirically successful in various settings. However, there is
709   a lack of theoretical analysis on the adaptability of PFMs under LoRA-style adaptations, the ability
710   to efficiently optimize LoRA-style objectives, and the kinds of solutions they recover. Some recent
711   works have attempted to analyze different parts of these theoretical questions.
712   Convergence of LoRA. (Jang et al., 2024) analyzes the optimization landscape for LoRA for the
713   Neural Tangent Kernel regime. The authors show that LoRA finetuning converges in this setting
714   as they prove that the objective function satisfies a strict saddle property, ensuring that there are
715   no spurious local minima. However, this focuses on the actual ability of LoRA to converge to the
716   optimal low-rank adapter given an FM, and does not consider the adaptability of the FM in the first
717   place.
718   Expressivity of LoRA. (Zeng & Lee, 2023) derives the expressive power of LoRA as a function of
719   model depth. This work shows that under some mild conditions, fully connected and transformer
720   networks when respectively adapted with LoRA can closely approximate arbitrary smaller networks.
721   They quantify the required LoRA rank to achieve this approximation as well as the resulting approx-
722   imation error.
