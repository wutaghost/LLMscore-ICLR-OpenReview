009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we consider a block coordinate descent (BCD) algorithm for train-
012            ing deep neural networks and provide a new global convergence guarantee un-
013            der strictly monotonically increasing activation functions. While existing works
014            demonstrate convergence to stationary points for BCD in neural networks, our
015            contribution is the first to prove convergence to global minima, ensuring arbitrarily
016            small loss. We show that the loss with respect to the output layer decreases expo-
017            nentially while the loss with respect to the hidden layers remains well-controlled.
018
               Additionally, we derive generalization bounds using the Rademacher complex-
               ity framework, demonstrating that BCD not only achieves strong optimization
019
               guarantees but also provides favorable generalization performance. Moreover, we
020
               propose a modified BCD algorithm with skip connections and non-negative pro-
021            jection, extending our convergence guarantees to ReLU activation, which are not
022            strictly monotonic. Empirical experiments confirm our theoretical findings, show-
023            ing that the BCD algorithm achieves a small loss for strictly monotonic and ReLU
024            activations.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep learning has led to significant advances across various domains, such as computer vision,
030   natural language processing, and reinforcement learning, achieving unprecedented performance in
031   numerous tasks. However, understanding the training dynamics and optimization behavior of deep
032   neural networks remains an ongoing challenge due to the highly non-convex nature of their loss
033
      functions (Li et al., 2018). Proving convergence to global minima of gradient descent via backprop-
      agation, particularly for deep networks with multiple layers, remains an open problem in the field.
034
      While the neural tangent kernel (NTK) regime (Jacot et al., 2018) addresses this problem by reduc-
035
      ing the non-convex loss to the convex one in RKHS, it fails to fully explain the empirical success of
036   deep learning because it often outperforms kernel methods, even if we employ NTK as the kernel.
037
038   Contrary to the backpropagation-based training, the block coordinate descent (BCD), which origi-
039
      nated from the mathematical optimization field (see Tseng (2001), for example), is an optimization
      framework where we divide a variable into several blocks and optimize them alternately. BCD of-
040
      fers computational advantages by updating subsets of parameters iteratively, allowing for tractable
041
      optimization of complex systems. The objective function appearing in the neural network training is
042   also highly non-convex, and to overcome this issue, BCD-based neural network optimization meth-
043   ods have been proposed (Carreira-Perpinan & Wang, 2014; Askari et al., 2018; Lau et al., 2018;
044   Zhang & Brand, 2017; Patel et al., 2020; Zeng et al., 2019; Nakamura et al., 2021; Qiao et al., 2021;
045   Zhang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024). When we apply BCD to neural network training, the most
046   natural way is that we regard the weights of each layer as a block, and existing works adopt this
047   way. By the formulation of BCD, the loss function of the neural network can be divided into several
048   components, one of which coincides with a loss with respect to a layer. Compared to the original
049   loss, these divided ones have more accessible landscapes to optimize.
050   Based on such an advantage of BCD for neural networks, its theoretical perspective, mainly about
051   its convergence guarantee, has been explored in recent years. However, existing theoretical works
052   on BCD for neural networks (Zhang & Brand, 2017; Zeng et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022; Xu
053   et al., 2024) have only focused on the convergence to stationary points, points with zero gradients.
      Convergence to stationary points does not imply convergence to global minima, especially when

054
      the objective function is highly non-convex, such as the loss that appears in the training of neural
055   networks (Li et al., 2018; Safran & Shamir, 2018).
056
057   How neural network training finds global minima has been one of the most significant topics in deep
058
      learning theory literature. However, existing guarantees on BCD remain in convergence to the sta-
      tionary points. To bridge this gap, we aim to provide the convergence guarantee to the global minima
059
      of BCD for neural networks. To this end, we consider multi-layer neural networks and employ a
060
      BCD-type algorithm, updating the parameters using vanilla gradient descent. Our contribution can
061   be summarized as follows:
062
063          • We prove the global convergence of a block coordinate descent (BCD) algorithm, where
064            we train deep neural network models with strictly monotonically increasing activation. We
065            ensure that the parameters attain arbitrarily small loss by proving that (i) the loss with
066            respect to the output layer will decrease exponentially to zero and (ii) the loss with respect
067            to the hidden layers remains small in every iteration. Through the analysis, we carefully
068            evaluate the difference propagated from the output layer to the input layer. To the best of
069            our knowledge, this is the first result that guarantees convergence to the global minima of
070            neural networks with any number of layers beyond the NTK regime.
071          • We derive a generalization error bound of deep neural networks trained by BCD under
072            settings with i.i.d. data. In the convergence analysis, we show that the norm of weight
073            matrices of each layer can be bounded by a constant. Combining this and the Rademacher
074            complexity argument from Bartlett et al. (2017) gives a upper bound on generalization
075            error. Compared to the existing works on gradient descent, BCD enables us to provide the
076            generalization gap bound for multi-layer neural networks with an optimization guarantee.
077          • A notable challenge in applying our approach to commonly used activation functions like
078            ReLU is their non-monotonic nature. Since ReLU is not strictly monotonically increasing,
079            our initial convergence result does not directly apply. To address this issue, we propose a
080            modified BCD algorithm incorporating skip connections (He et al., 2016) and non-negative
081            projection updates. This modification ensures that convergence guarantees extend to ReLU
082            networks, thereby broadening the applicability of our method to real-world architectures
083            that predominantly use ReLU activations.
084          • We validate our theoretical findings through numerical experiments, showing that BCD for
085            both strictly monotonic and ReLU activations achieves arbitrarily small loss values. These
086            empirical results confirm the practical viability of our proposed methods, demonstrating
087            their effectiveness in optimizing deep neural networks beyond theoretical guarantees.

      1.1   OTHER RELATED WORKS
090
091   Convergence guarantee of GD/SGD for neural networks In recent years, theoretical works on
092   the convergence guarantee of (stochastic) gradient descent for neural networks have been inten-
093
      sively investigated. In the neural tangent kernel (NTK) regime (Jacot et al., 2018; Allen-Zhu et al.,
      2019b; Arora et al., 2019; Du et al., 2019; Zou et al., 2020), to name a few, the training dynamics of
094
      deep neural networks can be approximated by the gradient descent in RKHS. While we can ensure
095
      its global convergence by exploiting the convexity, the feature learning ability of neural networks,
096   which is considered one of the critical ingredients of the practical success of deep learning, is not
097   reflected since the training dynamics are reduced to the kernel method. For example, the parameters
098   of networks trained by The NTK regime hardly move from their initial points as the number of pa-
099   rameters increases. On the other hand, our analysis does not fall into such a situation. Moreover, our
100   analysis does not require any overparameterization on hidden layers to ensure global convergence.
101
      The mean-field (MF) regime (Nitanda & Suzuki, 2017; Chizat & Bach, 2018; Mei et al., 2019; Tzen
102   & Raginsky, 2020; Pham & Nguyen, 2021; Nguyen & Pham, 2023) is another promising approach
103   of investigating neural network training. It regards the training of parameters as that of (probability)
104   measure over the parameters, by which we can convert the non-convex optimization with respect
105   to the parameters to the convex one where the distribution of parameters itself is a variable to be
106   optimized. While several studies ensure its global convergence by employing this convexity without
107   loss of feature learning ability, most of their models only focus on two or three-layer networks, our
      analysis admits the any number of hidden layers.

108
      More recently, Banerjee et al. (2023) proposed restricted strong convexity (RSC) to analyze neural
109   network training, which derives the global convergence guarantee by assuming that the gradient and
110   output of neural networks correlate with each other during the training. However, Banerjee et al.
111   (2023) still requires an analysis of this correlation assumption and does not fully explain the nature
112   of global convergence in the training.
113
114   Generalization error bound of multi-layer neural networks Investigation of generalization er-
115   ror analysis for multi-layer neural networks has been explored in recent years (Neyshabur et al.,
116   2015; Wei & Ma, 2019; Bartlett et al., 2017; Neyshabur et al., 2017; Golowich et al., 2018; Bartlett
117   et al., 2019; Arora et al., 2018; Suzuki et al., 2020). These works give a generalization error by
118   evaluating the complexity of neural networks from various perspective, such as the VC-dimension,
119
      the norm of parameters of networks, and so on. On the other hand, most of these results do not
      consider the optimization, but we also demonstrate the global convergence guarantee. Moreover,
120
      several works on generalization error analysis go beyond two-layer networks. However, most focus
121
      only on three-layer networks (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2019; Allen-Zhu et al., 2019a).

491   7   C ONCLUSION
492   In this paper, we proposed a block coordinate descent (BCD) algorithm for training deep neural
493   networks and ensured the convergence to global minima for networks with strictly monotonically
494   increasing activation functions. We also derived a generalization bound using Rademacher complex-
495   ity, ensuring both strong optimization and generalization performance. For ReLU activations, we
496   introduced a modified BCD algorithm with skip connections and non-negative projection updates to
497   ensure convergence. Empirical validation demonstrated the practical effectiveness of our algorithms
498   for both monotonic and ReLU activations. Overall, this work advances the understanding of BCD
499   in neural networks, offering provable convergence and generalization guarantees.
