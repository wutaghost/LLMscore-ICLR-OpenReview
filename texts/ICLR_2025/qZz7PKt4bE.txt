011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Transformer models have achieved remarkable results in the field of Natural Lan-
014            guage Processing (NLP) with the introduction of breakthrough large language
015
               models like GPT and LLaMA recently. Motivated by their ability to capture
               long-range dependencies, researchers have successfully adapted these models to
016
               the task of time series forecasting. However, despite their potential, effective-
017
               ness of applying these pre-trained time series transformer models in the target
018            domain is limited due to the need for hyper-parameter optimisation to match the
019            characteristics of the target domain. This paper presents a novel algorithm that
020            uses parameter efficient fine-tuning such as Low Rank Adaptation (LoRA) cou-
021            pled with Limited Discrepancy Search (LDS) to efficiently auto fine-tune pre-
022            trained time series transformers for a given target domain. Our approach helps in
023            making informed design choices involving LoRA tunable hyper-parameters with
024            strong performance-cost trade-offs that are highly transferable across different tar-
025            get domains. Our experiments demonstrate that autotune efficiently identifies the
026
               optimal configuration of LoRA hyper-parameters, achieving an average MASE
               improvement of 5.21% across all datasets and 4.76% for out-of-domain datasets
027
               compared to zero shot pre-trained models, with improvements as high as 20.59%
028
               for one of the out-of-domain datasets.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Time series forecasting has always been critical for decision-making across various domains includ-
034   ing retail, smart grids, healthcare, finance, weather, traffic control among others Peterson (2017)
035   Hernandez et al. (2014). Traditionally, the task of time series forecasting was accomplished using
036   classical statistical models like ARIMA followed by more modern approaches like machine learning
037   (ML) models including Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
038   models Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997). However, the remarkable success of LLMs in broad
039
      domains like Computer Vision (CV) and Natural Language Processing (NLP) Vaswani et al. (2017)
      Wen et al. (2022a) has prompted researchers to adapt these models to the task of time series fore-
040
      casting Wu et al. (2022); Garza & Mergenthaler-Canseco (2023); Gruver et al. (2024b) given their
041
      ability to capture long range dependencies present in time series data. These models are pre-trained
042   on vast amounts of data spanning a multitude of domains leveraging the general purpose represen-
043   tations learnt in the process. However, to excel on the domain specific downstream task it becomes
044   important to fine-tune these models on datasets from the target domain Wen et al. (2022b). Such
045   adaptation is generally achieved via fine-tuning, which involves updating all the parameters of the
046   pre-trained model Jin et al. (2023); Bommasani et al. (2021); Lv et al. (2023). Given the large num-
047   ber of parameters that are originally trained, full fine-tuning becomes an operational challenge if we
048   wish to adapt these models to the target domain.
049   Recent studies have shown that this issue can be mitigated by adapting only a small percentage of
050   the parameters in addition to the pre-trained model for each task and greatly enhance the operational
051   efficiency of these models Hu et al. (2021b). Then, the process of fine-tuning involves taking a pre-
052   trained model and updating only a subset of the model weights to learn the specific characteristics of
053   the target-domain. These techniques are termed as Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT). One of
      the popular PEFT techniques, LoRA (Low Rank Adaptation)Hu et al. (2021b) trains only selective


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      dense layers in a neural network while keeping the pre-trained weights frozen. This approach has
055   been quite widely applied to different domains like medical imaging, video text generation and
056   speech synthesis Balne et al. (2024) to name a few. However, its application to time series is still in
057   its nascent stage.
058
      Further, AutoML which involves automating the process of composing and parameterizing ML al-
059
      gorithms to maximize a specific metric such as model accuracy on a given dataset has been widely
060
      used to improve the accuracy of traditional machine learning and deep learning models He et al.
061   (2021). Leveraging this knowledge, autotuning of pre-trained transformers for a given target do-
062   main can potentially lead to improvement in the accuracy of transformers compared to traditional
063   fine-tuning with fixed hyper-parameters of the fine-tuning algorithms. However, despite its popular-
064   ity and widespread use, optimizing an ML pipeline poses significant challenges like slow training
065   and large computational overheads as the search space increases Tornede et al. (2023).
066
      Therefore, with this work, we envision efficient autotuning pre-trained transformers through the in-
067   tegration of LoRA and AutoML with an aim to improve the performance of time series transformers
068   in the target domain. In particular, to achieve an efficient implementation of autotuning, we adopt
069   the classical Limited Discrepancy Search (LDS) algorithm introduced by Harvey & Ginsberg (1995)
070   to optimize the pipeline selection process. This algorithm is essentially a depth-first search strategy
071   that searches for new set of solutions by iteratively increasing the number of discrepancy values
072   where the discrepancy refers to the number of variables in the current configuration that differ from
073   their values in the initial configuration. The novel contributions of this paper include:
074
075
             • Distributed autotuning of LoRA configurations for pre-trained transformer models to find
               the optimal pipeline for the target domain. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
076
               paper to explore the potential of autotuning time series transformer models.
077
078          • The adoption of LDS for exploring the LoRA hyper-parameter search space in autotuning
079            to minimize computational overhead.
080          • Extensive tests across a suite of out-of-domain benchmark datasets to compare the per-
081            formance of autotuned transformer models, traditional fine-tuning strategies and zero shot
082            pre-trained transformer models.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
086
087   There has been a rapid surge in the transformer-based time series forecasting techniques recently,
088
      particularly for long-term forecasting. We briefly introduce some of these transformers in this sec-
      tion. The authors in Zhou et al. (2021) propose a transformer-based model named Informer to
089
      employ a probability sparse attention mechanism to capture long-term dependencies in time se-
090
      ries data. The ProbSparse self-attention mechanism along with the distilling operation is used to
091   handle the challenges of quadratic time complexity and high memory usage in the vanilla Trans-
092   former architecture. Then, the authors of Autoformer Wu et al. (2021) introduced a decomposition
093   transformer architecture replacing the attention module with an AutoCorrelation mechanism to gain
094   progressive decomposition capacities outperforming self-attention in terms of both efficiency and
095   accuracy. To further reduce the computational cost of transformer from quadratic to linear, FED-
096   former Zhou et al. (2022), a Frequency Enhanced Decomposed Transformer was introduced. It
097   captured the important global/overall structures in time series by means of frequency domain map-
098   ping achieving linear complexity. PatchTST Nie et al. (2022) on the other hand, introduced two key
099
      components : segmentation of time series into patches serving as input tokens to the transformer
      along with channel-independence. Each channel contains a single univariate time series sharing
100
      the same embedding and Transformer weights across all the series for long-term multivariate time
101
      series forecasting and self-supervised representation learning. This overcomes the challenge of com-
102   putation and memory usage of attention maps enabling the model to benefit from longer look-back
103   windows. Yet another pioneering work in this area is Lag-Llama Rasul et al. (2023) inspired by the
104   LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023) LLM. This model utilizes a simple decoder-only transformer architec-
105   ture for time series forecasting by using lagged features as covariates. The main problem addressed
106   by this work is the application of foundation model approach to time series data and investigation
107   of the extent of the transfer achievable across a wide range of time series domains. Another line of
      work which treats time series as strings is LLMTime Gruver et al. (2024a). They employ careful


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      pre-processing specific to the given LLMs’ tokenizer, demonstrating that LLMs can inherently per-
109   form zero shot forecasting. This is achieved by relying on LLM’s abilities to extrapolate patterns
110   in general sequences. Likewise, Chronos Ansari et al. (2024) employs the encoder-decoder trans-
111   former architecture from the T5 Raffel et al. (2020) LLM family requiring minimal modifications
112   i.e, tokenization though scaling and quantization.
113
      There has been a growing interest in the research field of AutoML in the recent past fuelled by the
114
      exponential growth in the availability of computational resources Khan et al. (2021). Moreover, it as-
115   sists practitioners in developing accurate and efficient predictive models without extensive domain
116   knowledge Feurer et al. (2015) Katz et al. (2016) Jin et al. (2019). Hyperparameter optimization
117   (HPO) acts as an important component of AutoML in searching for optimal hyper-parameters for
118   neural network structures and the model training process. There is a strong need to adapt these
119   pre-trained time series transformer models for specific downstream forecasting tasks for improved
120   performance in the target domain Liang et al. (2024). Leveraging AutoML for this process of fine-
121   tuning can help achieve this goal by automating the task of fine-tuning in a computationally feasible
122   manner. Most commonly, PEFT techniques have been proposed in NLP and CV for fine-tuning
123
      a subset of parameters in various downstream tasks. These are mainly classified into two groups:
      selective and additive. Selective PEFT approaches involve fine-tuning a selective set of parameters
124
      in the model architecture as shown in Touvron et al. (2022) tuning only the attention layers. Unlike
125
      selective approaches, additive PEFT adds new weights into the model known as adapter modules
126   and fine-tune only these, keeping the existing weights frozen. One such popular adapter is LoRA
127   Hu et al. (2021a) which adds trainable low-rank matrices into transformer layers to approximate
128   the weight updates. The application of LoRA in the domain of fine-tuning time series transformers
129   remains largely unexplored due to the rapidly evolving nature of this field and hence, motivates this
130   work. Moreover, to further optimize the search space for hyperparameter optimization in the Au-
131   toML pipeline, we adopt LDS into our framework to find the best performing model configuration.
132   In contrast to the transformer architectures and algorithms considered in this related work, we focus
133   on the efficient autotuning of pre-trained time series transformers for a given target domain.

      3   M ETHODOLOGY
137
      In this section, we first define the problem statement and then present a detailed description of the
138
      autotune design approach we adopted using LoRA and LDS.
139
140   Problem Statement: Given a univariate time series dataset X = (x0 , x1 , .., xn ) where xn ∈ R is a
141   time ordered sequence of real values and n is the length of the time series and given context length
142   c where 1 < c < n, we used the time series x1:c = x1 , x2 , . . . , xc to forecast the time series
143
      xc:c+h = xc+1 , xc+2 , . . . , xc+h , where h is the forecast horizon and c + h = n.
144   To solve the forecasting task described above, we used Chronos T5 models as the time series trans-
145   formers which have been pre-trained on a large collection of publicly available time series datasets
146   from varied domains. However, since these models are trained on a broad spectrum of time series
147   data, their performance on a specific task which in our case is the unseen target dataset may not be
148   optimal.
149   Autotune using LoRA and LDS: We design a novel algorithm to perform automated fine-tuning
150   of transformer models using LoRA as the parameter efficient tuning method in conjunction with
151   LDS. This fine-tuning is achieved by using a distributed ray based framework. Figure 1 shows the
152   architecture diagram of our approach wherein we take a pre-trained Chronos T5 model and specify
153   a minimal subset of weights to be trained to adapt the model on the target dataset using LoRA.
154   LoRA Hu et al. (2021a) implements this in a storage and compute-efficient manner by constraining
155
      the update (∆W ) to the pre-trained weight matrix W0 ∈ Rd×k by representing it with a low-rank
      decomposition,

232   is typically given by the default LoRA hyper-parameter configuration here, hence, we set y0 to the

239   split to compute the MASE score as outlined in the SCORE function in the algorithm. At the end,
240
      we find the best configuration y∗ corresponding to the lowest MASE score and use it to fine-tune

247   The algorithm described above is then implemented in a distributed manner using Ray Tune Liaw
248   et al. (2018) which provides an open source framework for distributed model training and selection.
249   Each configuration returned by LDS corresponds to trials as shown in Figure 1 which are executed
250   concurrently in a cluster. We use the default Ray tune scheduler which is first-in-first-out (FIFO)

360   We present our comparative results in Table 3 which shows the performance of Chronos mini T5
361   model in zero shot setting along with different fine-tuning settings. We report MASE averaged
362   over 5 runs for both zero shot and full fine-tuning setting. For our approach, we run 10 trials and

364
      except for datasets in the domain of traffic, weather and electricity where full fine-tuning overtakes
365
      by a slight margin. This can be attributed to the fact that the pre-trained Chronos T5 model has seen

368   similarity with the pre-training source datasets, our approach demonstrates the best performance.
369   This validates our claim that LoRA achieves comparable, and often superior performance while
370   significantly reducing the number of trained parameters compared to full fine-tuning for out-of-

      6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
487
488
      Time series transformers efficiently capture long-range patterns and dependencies, improving the
489   model’s ability to predict complex temporal relationships. However, their successful application to
490   specific downstream tasks needs adaptation to the target domain datasets which can be achieved via
491   fine-tuning. In this work, we propose autotuning time series transformers using parameter efficient
492   fine-tuning method LoRA along with LDS as the search strategy. Our approach outperforms full
493   fine-tuning specifically for out-of-domain datasets not seen during the model pre-training phase.
494   Furthermore, we show that our autotuned model also beats the zero shot mini models for 80% of
495   the benchmark datasets surpassing even the performance of the zero shot large models in some
496   cases. Furthermore, since our autotuning approach is based on LoRA, it can be easily extended to
497
      other time series foundation models. In the future, we will also extend our autotune approach for
      multivariate time series datasets.
