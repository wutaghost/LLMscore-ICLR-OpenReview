009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Advanced image editing software enables easy creation of highly convincing image
012            manipulations, which has been made even more accessible in recent years due
013            to advances in generative AI. Manipulated images, while often harmless, could
014            spread misinformation, create false narratives, and influence people’s opinions
015            on important issues. Despite this growing threat, current research on detecting
016
               advanced manipulations across different visual domains, remains limited. Thus,
               we introduce Multi-domain Analysis and Generalization of Image manipulation
017
               loCalization (MAGIC), a comprehensive benchmark designed for studying gener-
018
               alization across several axes in image manipulation detection. MAGIC comprises
019            over 192K images from two distinct sources (user and news photos), spanning a
020            diverse range of topics and manipulation sizes. We focus on images manipulated
021            using recent diffusion-based inpainting methods, which are largely absent in ex-
022            isting datasets. We conduct experiments under different types of domain shift to
023            evaluate robustness of existing image manipulation detection methods. Our goal
024            is to drive further research in this area by offering new insights that would help
025            develop more reliable and generalizable image manipulation detection methods.
026            We will release the dataset after this work is published.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      With the advent of sophisticated image editing tools like Photoshop and the emergence of advanced
031   generative AI models (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Rezende & Mohamed,
032   2015), image manipulations have achieved an unprecedented level of realism (Shi et al., 2020; Saharia
033   et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023), making it challenging to distinguish between genuine and altered
034   content. Modern image manipulation techniques can produce convincingly fabricated materials that
035   could be used to create false narratives, misrepresent key individuals (Guo et al., 2023), and spread
036   misinformation. Image manipulation datasets play an important role in countering such fabrications
037   by providing the essential resources needed for training manipulation detection models that are
038   designed to localize altered image regions (Liu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2023).
039
      However, as shown in Figure 1, prior datasets focus on one axis of generalization, namely across
      manipulation types. Moreover, prior works typically train and test on the same data domain. We
040
      argue that it is important to study generalization across multiple axes, such as training and testing
041
      models across different image sources, semantic topics, manipulation types, sizes, etc.
042
043   To this end, we introduce Multi-domain Analysis and Generalization of Image manipulation
044   loCalization (MAGIC), a carefully curated, large-scale image manipulation dataset. Our dataset is
045
      created from two image sources, VisualNews (Liu et al., 2020) and MS COCO (Lin et al., 2014). Visu-
      alNews includes images from four major news outlets—The Guardian, BBC, USA TODAY, and The
046
      Washington Post. To ensure image diversity, we sample images from eight different categories, such
047
      as Business or Science. To explore significant domain shifts, we also sample images from MS COCO,
048   which contains user photos obtained from Flickr. Most existing datasets focus on traditional ma-
049   nipulation techniques such as splicing and copy-move, and do not include any deep learning-based
050   inpainted images, with an exception of T, ânt, aru et al. (2024) (see Table 1). In contrast, we utilize

053   Blattmann et al., 2022), GLIDE (Nichol et al., 2022), Blended-Latent Diffusion (Avrahami et al.,
      2023) and GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023) to add or remove objects from images. We also include a widely

159   Datasets for Image Manipulation Detection. In response to the growing complexity of image
160   manipulation techniques, contemporary datasets have emerged, broadening the scope of research in
161   this field. DEFACTO (MAHFOUDI et al., 2019) encompasses a substantial collection comprising
      25K instances of inpainting, 19K cases of copy-move, 105K instances of splicing, and 80K instances


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      of morphing manipulations. GRE (Sun et al., 2024) also studies the effect of a complementary set of
163   manipulation methods, but they did not consider the effects ond detection performance of shifts due
164   to image sources, topics, or the quality of manipulations in their paper. DOLOS (T, ânt, aru et al., 2024),
165   contains 148,112 images which includes around 105,812 diffusion based inpainted images. This
166   dataset is mainly made up of human faces and utilizes FFHQ (Karras et al., 2019) and CelebA (Liu
167   et al., 2015), where inpainting models like Repaint-P2, Repaint-LDM (Rombach et al., 2022a) were
168   used for manipulations. Additionally, DOLOS includes 512 images inpainted using GLIDE (Nichol
169   et al., 2022) with MS COCO (Lin et al., 2014) as the image source.
170   Models for Image Manipulation Detection. Among many recent manipulation detection models we
171   have chosen four that have been shown to perform well on classical manipulation detection methods
172   like copy-move, splicing etc. First, we have PSCC-Net (Liu et al., 2022) which employs a two-path
173   process: a top-down path extracting local and global features, and a bottom-up,path for detecting
174   manipulations and estimating manipulation masks across multiple scales. Next we have HiFi (Guo
175   et al., 2023) which has a similar architecture to PSCC-Net but additionally employs a hierarchical
176   fine-grained approach to classify forgery attributes at different levels, from general to specific. Next
177
      is DOLOS (T, ânt, aru et al., 2024), this model uses Patch–Forensics (Chai et al., 2020), which is a
      truncated image classification network that takes the feature activations after a few layers and projects
178
      them to a patch-level score using 1 × 1 convolutions. Finally we utilize EVP (Liu et al., 2023), which
179
      is a vision transformer based approach that use a frozen backbone SegFormer (Xie et al., 2021) and
180   only contains a small number of tunable parameters to learn task-specific knowledge from the features
181   of each individual image itself. We study two domain generalization techniques, SWAD (Cha et al.,
182   2021), that improves robustness by averaging model weights during training time and seeking flat
183   minima, and Model Soups (Wortsman et al., 2022), that leverages different versions of the same
184   model with different hyperparameters and uses a greedy approach to average their weights to improve
185   robustness. We apply both methods to our best performing model, EVP.

      Our MAGIC dataset comprises of two distinct subsets. MAGIC-News contains 90,481 images,

      et al., 2020) dataset. MAGIC-COCO contains 102,116 images, with 87,034 manipulated images

      3.1     I MAGE M ANIPULATION T ECHNIQUES
198
199   While constructing our dataset, we sought to generate a wide variety of image manipulations that
200   would reflect real-world scenarios, offering a significant challenge for models tasked with localizing
201   these alterations. To achieve this, we employed seven major diffusion-based manipulation techniques:
202   five perform replacement, one performs removal, and one performs insertion. These methods are
203   applied to both MAGIC-News and MAGIC-COCO, ensuring high diversity of manipulated images.
204
205   3.1.1    R EPLACEMENT AND R EMOVAL BASED M ANIPULATIONS
206
207
      We employ five diffusion-based models that specialize in replacement-based manipulations. Each of
      these models offers specific strengths and capabilities. Blended-Diffusion (Avrahami et al., 2022)
208
      utilizes the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) model for text guidance, combined with a denoising diffusion
209
      probabilistic model DDPM (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) to create highly realistic, localized edits based
210   on user-provided text prompts. Blended-Latent-Diffusion (Avrahami et al., 2023) extends this by
211   operating in the latent space, improving both speed and accuracy; it enables more realistic image
212   inpainting. We also employ Stable-Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022b), which operates in the latent
213   space of pretrained autoencoders, making it more computationally efficient while retaining high
214   visual fidelity. It is particularly effective in generating class-conditional images with high-resolution.
215   GLIDE (Nichol et al., 2022) is another model which also uses CLIP guidance and excels at generating
      photorealistic content. Finally, Adobe Firefly (Adobe, 2024) is included as a proprietary model


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      for generating diverse image manipulations, simulating real-world scenarios where such tools are
217   widely used. All these models are applied to both the MAGIC-News and MAGIC-COCO images. For
218   MAGIC-News, segmentation masks are generated using Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022), whereas
219   for MAGIC-COCO, the existing masks provided by the MS COCO dataset are used. All the models
220   use both text and image data to guide the generation of manipulated images. We identify an object
221   randomly selected from the mask and replace that region with the new content based on the object
222   class. For example, if the mask corresponds to a “cat”, the information that the object is a cat is used
223   as a prompt to guide the diffusion models in generating the new content (i.e., another cat).
224   Finally, we use Latent-Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021), where instead of replacing the selected
225   object, the entire region is removed (inpainted) to blend with the background.
226
227   3.1.2      I NSERTION -BASED M ANIPULATIONS
228
229   Insertion of new objects into an existing image is also known as splicing. The splicing process
230   begins by utilizing Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022) to generate panoptic segmentation maps from
231
      images sourced from MS COCO and VisualNews. Panoptic segmentation provides a detailed and
      comprehensive breakdown of the scene by categorizing each pixel into specific objects (things) and
232
      background elements (stuff). This segmentation map S serves as a blueprint, outlining where each
233
      object is located within the image. Once the segmentation map is obtained, GLIGEN (Li et al.,
234   2023) is employed to generate a new object that fits contextually into the designated area. GLIGEN
235   leverages the spatial and semantic information from the segmentation map to ensure that the newly
236   generated object not only aligns with the surrounding elements in terms of position and scale but also
237   blends seamlessly with the scene’s overall aesthetics. The generated object is then carefully inserted
238   into the original image Ioriginal using the object mask M (Here the object mask M is randomly chosen
239   from the panoptic segmentation), resulting in a spliced image Ispliced , as described by:
240
                                          Ispliced = GLIGEN(S) ⊙ M ⊕ Ioriginal
241
242   Where S is the segmentation map, M is the object mask, and Ioriginal is the original image.

244   3.2      D IVERSITY IN MAGIC DATASET
245
246   When constructing the MAGIC-dataset, we curate a wide range of scenarios to better capture the
247   different challenges that an image manipulation detector may encounter in real-world applications.
248   News imagery, in particular, covers an broad variety of content, as illustrated in Figure 2. To ensure
249   diversity in image content, we select our images from a range of topics. The VisualNews dataset
250
      provides 159 different topic annotations. However, many of these topics are overlapping or closely
      related (e.g., science technology, nanotechnology, technology). To address this, we group similar
251
      topics using the clustering framework of Tan et al. (2022). This results in eight distinct categories
252
      (see Table 2). The VisualNews dataset draws from four major news outlets: USA Today, Washington
253   Post, BBC, and The Guardian. We sample an approximately equal number of images from each news
254   outlet and topic, ensuring balanced representation1 . MS COCO does not have topic labels; for our
255   MAGIC-COCO subset we sample 82 object categories, including the most common objects (person,
256   car), and less frequent objects (hairbrush, giraffe). The combination of news-related imagery and
257   everyday objects from MS COCO ensures that our dataset represents not just specialized journalistic
258   content but also a broad spectrum of general, real-world scenes.
259   In the MAGIC-News we manipulate both the foreground (e.g., people, buildings) and the background
260   elements (e.g., sky, terrain), resulting in a wide variety of manipulation sizes based on the panoptic
261   segmentation masks predicted by Mask2Former. This is in contrast to prior work that often focuses
262   solely on foreground object manipulation (MAHFOUDI et al., 2019; Novozamsky et al., 2020). An
263   overview of our image manipulation pipeline is given in Figure 4 (Appendix).
264
      Our MAGIC-dataset exhibits significant diversity in manipulation sizes. The MAGIC-News subset
265   shows a wide range of manipulation sizes, from small edits to large alterations, requiring detection
266   models to be robust across various levels of visual modifications. In contrast, manipulation sizes in
267   MAGIC-COCO are skewed toward smaller edits, with relatively few large manipulations. Specif-
268   ically, in MAGIC-News, there are 39,188 large manipulations covering > 70% of the image area,

      3.3    DATASET Q UALITY S URVEY
281
282   To assess the quality of the manipulated images and ensure their practical use for manipulation
283   detection, we perform a human evaluation via Amazon Mechanical Turk. A total of 4,950 images
284   were used in our survey (2,750 from MAGIC-News and 2,200 from MAGIC-COCO). We include 5
285   generators for MAGIC-News and 4 for MAGIC-COCO2 . We sample 500 images from each generator.
286   Additionally we include 250 authentic images from MAGIC-News and 200 from MAGIC-COCO.
287
      Each image is evaluated by 3 people; in total we have 14,850 responses from 1,829 unique workers3 .
      The respondents were shown two images: a manipulated image X modified in a region specified by
288
      a binary mask M , and a copy of X with a mask superimposed on it with a transparency value α, as
289
      shown in Figure 6 in the Appendix. The respondents are asked the following questions: Q1) “Do you
290   think this image is manipulated?” (yes or no), Q2) “Do you see the object in the image (you can
291   use the mask overlay to the right of the Image to better see the object)?” (yes or no); Q3) “Does the
292   object look realistic?” (yes, maybe or no), and Q4) “Does the object look natural in the background?”
293   (yes, maybe, no). For each example, we specify which object the respondents should look for (e.g., a
294   “giraffe”). We discard the answers to Q3 and Q4 if the respondent answered “No” to Q2, which results
295   in 9,596 responses. Next, we utilize Q3 to create pseudo-labels for “High quality” and “Low quality”
296   images. By combining the “Maybe” and “No” responses and utilizing majority voting, we get 1,507
297   “Low quality” images. Meanwhile, we have 1,905 “Yes” responses corresponding to “High quality”
298
      images, hence we get 3,412 images in total. We discuss the results of utilizing these pseudo-labels
      for comparison with selected manipulated detection models in Section 4.

532
533
      We introduced Multi-domain Analysis and Generalization of Image manipulation loCalization
534   (MAGIC), a large image manipulation dataset aimed at studying the robustness and generaliza-
535   tion capabilities of image manipulation detectors. Our dataset features a range of image sources,
536   topics, manipulation types, and sizes. Notably, we have utilized state-of-the-art image manipulation
537   techniques. Through extensive experiments, we found that while current detectors perform well on
538   in-distribution data, they struggle on out-of-distribution samples, underscoring the need for better
539   generalization. In our future research we will focus on integrating further contextual information
      such as image captions and news articles into our study.

      6    E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      Our work focuses on benchmarking and advancing the methods for detecting manipulated images.
543   We believe this is an important effort aimed at countering misinformation online, especially in the era
544   of advanced generative models. Thus, this work is ethical by its nature. At the same time, since we
545   are producing a dataset with manipulated images, there is some potential for misuse, i.e., it being used
546   for training even more sophisticated falsification methods. Besides, since we are relying on generative
547   models to create the data, there is a possibility of biases inherent to these models propagating into our
548   generated manipulations. Any potential users of our dataset should be mindful of that.
