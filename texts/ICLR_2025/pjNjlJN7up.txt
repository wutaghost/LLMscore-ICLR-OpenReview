009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we introduce the big.LITTLE Vision Transformer, an innovative
012            architecture aimed at achieving efficient visual recognition. This dual-transformer
013            system is composed of two distinct blocks: the big performance block, character-
014            ized by its high capacity and substantial computational demands, and the LITTLE
015            efficiency block, designed for speed with lower capacity. The key innovation of
016            our approach lies in its dynamic inference mechanism. When processing an image,
017            our system determines the importance of each token and allocates them accord-
018
               ingly: essential tokens are processed by the high-performance big model, while
               less critical tokens are handled by the more efficient little model. This selective
019
               processing significantly reduces computational load without sacrificing the overall
020
               performance of the model, as it ensures that detailed analysis is reserved for the
021            most important information. To validate the effectiveness of our big.LITTLE Vision
022            Transformer, we conducted comprehensive experiments on image classification and
023            segment anything task. Our results demonstrate that the big.LITTLE architecture
024            not only maintains high accuracy but also achieves substantial computational sav-
025            ings. Specifically, our approach enables the efficient handling of large-scale visual
026            recognition tasks by dynamically balancing the trade-offs between performance
027            and efficiency. The success of our method underscores the potential of hybrid
028            models in optimizing both computation and performance in visual recognition
029
               tasks, paving the way for more practical and scalable deployment of advanced
               neural networks in real-world applications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) has increasingly influenced the field of computer
035
      vision since its introduction. It demonstrates exceptional performance in fundamental tasks such
036   as image classification (Deng et al., 2009), image segmentation (Kirillov et al., 2023), and object
037   detection (Li et al., 2022). Furthermore, the flexibility of the transformer architecture enables ViT
038   to act as a crucial conduit between visual and linguistic information in multimodal models (Liu
039   et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023), significantly contributing to their rapid development. Additionally,
040   due to the scalability of ViT, as the model sizes increase, ViT is able to effectively learn richer
041   representations of images. Therefore, making large ViT is highly desirable for downstream tasks and
042   applications.
043   Despite the impressive performance of ViT, its slow inference speed remains a notable drawback. For
044   instance, models utilizing ViT-Huge with more than 600M parameters as a core component, such as
045   the Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023), may operate at less than 2 FPS on a high-
046   end NVIDIA A100 GPU (Xiong et al., 2023), not to mention ViTs with billion-level parameters (Zhai
047   et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023; Dehghani et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023). This limitation significantly
048   hinders the practical deployment of ViT-based models in real-world applications and there is an
049   urgent need for improving the inference speed of ViT models.
050   To tackle this issue, a variety of strategies have been developed to enhance the inference speed of ViT
051   in recent years. Some works address the problem from the model perspective, either by distilling
052   the knowledge into a lighter-weight model (Xiong et al., 2023), or lowering the precision of model
053   parameters (Dettmers et al., 2022). Instead, inspired by the discovery that only representative tokens
      are crucial for the final prediction, token pruning methods emerge and speed up the inference by

082   Therefore, to achieve higher inference speed while preserving the context information images, we
083   recognize that all tokens are needed, but not all tokens are equally important. Intuitively, we humans
084   have a large field of view, but will only focus on a small area each time when we see the world.
085   For the focused area, we pay more attention to detailed processing while keeping an eye on the
086   surroundings.
087   Motivated by this observation, we introduce a novel system called big.LITTLE Vision Transformer
088   (bLViT), which comprises big performance blocks and LITTLE efficiency blocks within the ViT
089   architecture. In our design, only a few important tokens are updated with the performance blocks each
090   time, which ensures the performance of the model during the inference with a reduced computation.
091   For the less important areas, we keep the context information but pay less computation cost to enable
092   high inference efficiency with the efficiency blocks. Although most image tokens are pruned from the
093
      performance blocks based on their importance, the efficiency blocks ensure that all tokens continue
      to update layer by layer, preserving the structured attributes of image tokens. Whether a token is
094
      processed by the big model is determined by its importance score from prediction layers. Throughout
095
      training, our differential design on token selection enables the prediction layers to appropriately route
096   critical tokens to the performance blocks, ensuring intensive computation for those deemed most
097   significant.
098
099
      We demonstrate the efficacy of our bLViT through applications in image classification and image
      segmentation tasks, employing DeiT (Touvron et al., 2021) and SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) as the
100
      base models within our big.LITTLE system. The experimental results exhibit a competitive trade-off
101
      between computational speed and accuracy, highlighting our model’s capability to effectively balance
102   performance and efficiency.

115   2     R ELATED W ORK
116
117   Vision Transformer. Vision Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) has achieved a great success
118   and shows state-of-the-art performance on many tasks including image classification (Touvron
119   et al., 2021), object detection (Li et al., 2022), semantic segmentation (Strudel et al., 2021; Cheng
120   et al., 2022; 2021), etc. The long-range dependency modulation enables its capability to encode rich
121
      contextual information, which can benefit downstream tasks by providing better image representations.
      Therefore, a stream of work studies how to adapt plain ViT to different tasks to optimal the network
122
      architecture and boost the performance (Wang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022a; Yao et al., 2024),
123
      using the pretrained model on large-scale datasets with different pretraining strategies (Zhang et al.,
124   2022b; Oquab et al., 2023). Despite its wide application and high performance, the computational
125   burden poses challenges to the inference speed and practical deployment in resource-constrained
126   environments. A better speed-accuracy tradeoff for the model is desirable.
127
128   Computation Reduction. To reduce the computation of existing models, several works have
129   attempted to prune the input tokens (Liang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022; Rao et al., 2021) or merge the
130   input tokens (Marin et al., 2021; Bolya et al., 2022). This is achieved by identifying and retaining only
131   the most informative tokens, effectively reducing the number of tokens to process. AdaViT (Meng
132   et al., 2022) further tries to partially or entirely drop the layers for all tokens. This type of method
133   can achieve good speedup with only marginal performance decreases on ImageNet classification.
134   However, few of them have proven the model can work with downstream tasks besides image
135
      classification as many tokens are dropped in a very early stage.
136
137
      Speedup with Small Model. Leveraging a smaller model is another way to speed up model
      inference. The speculative decoding framework (Kim et al., 2023) introduces a mechanism using a
138
      separate large language model along with a smaller one to improve inference speed in natural language
139
      processing. Big-little Net (Chen et al., 2018) proposes to learn multi-scale feature representations
140   with Big-Branches process the low-resolution input and Little-Branches process the high-resolution
141   input to balance the computation on image and speech recognition. Mixture-of-Expert (Jacobs et al.,
142   1991; Eigen et al., 2013; Ahmed et al., 2016; Riquelme et al., 2021) can also be seen as a way to speed
143   up the inference by selecting a part of the model (“experts”) at each time. While our method shares
144   a similar spirit with these works, our model focuses on developing a single model instead of two
145   separate models and still works on the same input resolution. Our “model experts” also have different
146   computation complexity, which allows it to be more adaptive and achieves a better speed-accuracy
147   tradeoff.
148   There are also some works that focus on the model distillation (Hinton et al., 2015; Touvron et al.,
149   2021; Xiong et al., 2023) as well as model quantization (Dettmers et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Ma
150   et al., 2024) to speed up the computation. Since our goal is to propose a general model architecture that
151   incorporates computation-intensive and efficient blocks, we argue that our model is complementary
152   to these methods and the speed can be further improved.

212   Token Selection and Routing. Before the dual blocks, a prediction layer—composed of a linear
213   layer followed by a softmax function—estimates the importance scores of all image tokens, identifying
214   the most crucial tokens for further processing, as shown in Fig. 2. We employ a top-k selection
215   mechanism to select primary tokens based on these importance scores. These selected tokens are
      then routed to the more computationally intensive P-block. As described in Algo. 1, only a subset

223   For simplicity, this part is omitted in the pseudo-code.

225   Dimension Matching As the E-block and P-block have different model capacities, the hidden
226   dimensions of the representations are inevitably different. To reconcile these differences and ac-
227   commodate the requirements of both the efficiency and performance blocks, we modify the vallina
228   ViT block for the E-block. Specially, we insert two linear layers in the beginning and ending in the
229
      FFN layer to conduct dimension mapping; as for the attention layer, input and output dimensions
      are modified directly to match the dimension of the main flow. These operations are conducted in
230
      E_Attention and E_FFN in the pseudocode.
231
232
      Semi-Cross Attention In the previous token pruning method, unimportant tokens were directly
233   removed, preventing the remaining tokens from exchanging information with the pruned tokens in the
234   attention layer. To address this issue, we propose a Semi-Cross Attention mechanism for P-blocks.
235   Specifically, in the attention layer of the P-block, we use the primary tokens as queries (q) and all
236   tokens (both selected and unselected) as keys (k) and values (v), instead of only using the same tokens
237   as queries. This allows the primary tokens to still gather information from all tokens, not just from
238   themselves.
239
240   Token Fusion. After processing through the dual blocks, the output of the P-block is fused with
241   that of the E-block with the globally updated context. This fusion is performed using a learnable
242   parameter γ, which adjusts the influence of the tokens on the final output, formulated as
243

      Here, M is a binary mask indicating whether the i-th token is a primary token (Mi = 1) or not
247   (Mi = 0). This ensures that the most significant features are emphasized while maintaining the
248   overall integrity of the data representation.

250   Variants of P-E block. In practice, the configuration of P-block and E-block can vary depending
251   on the model size, and inner dimensions of both P-block and E-block follow variants of the vanilla
252   ViT block. For instance, we can set the dimensions of the P-block and E-block as those of ViT-Base
253   block and ViT-Tiny block respectively, as the E-block to match ViT-Base performance while saving
254   computation. Here, we adopt a 1:1 stacking ratio of P and E blocks, meaning each layer of image
255   tokens passes through one P-block and one E-block. In models with a larger size, such as a huge-base
256   combination, we might employ a 2:1 stacking ratio or other variations.

258   Theoretical Computation Analysis. To reduce computational demands, we empirically let the
259
      performance blocks process the top 25% most important tokens by default, while the efficiency
      block updates all tokens, ensuring comprehensive coverage of context information. In this way, our
260
      model allocates computational resources for each token adaptively based on its content, leading to
261
      a better speed-accuracy tradeoff. We conduct a simple analysis of how much computation we can
262   save: for input with shape N × C, where N is the number of tokens and C is the hidden dimensions
263   of tokens, the computation cost of a vanilla ViT block is 12N C 2 + 2N 2 C(4N C 2 + 2N 2 C is for
264   attention layer and 8N C 2 is for FFN). A performance block updates 25% tokens with a cost of
265   4.5N C 2 + 0.5N 2 C(2.5N C 2 + 0.5N 2 C is for semi-cross attention and 2N C 2 is for FFN) and an
266   efficiency block with 14 C hidden dimensions costs 2N C 2 + 0.5N 2 C(N C 2 + 0.5N 2 C is for attention
267   layer and N C 2 is for FFN, which is larger than the result of substituting 14 C into C in vanilla cost
268   because of additional overhead incurred by dimension matching) when processing all tokens. This
269   leads to a total cost of 6.5N C 2 + N 2 C, over 1.84× theoretical speedup for each layer, which could
      further be higher as the efficiency block becomes smaller.

      3.3   T RAINING S TRATEGY
271
272   In practice, we find that naively training a model with big.LITTLE modules may lead to suboptimal
273   performance, possibly due to the high pruning ratio, and we empirically find that feature distillation
274   can improve its performance.
275
      During training, feature distillation is used to transfer knowledge from a pre-trained vallina ViT to
276   our big.LITTLE ViT. By aligning the features learned by the student with those of the teacher, the
277   model can retain critical information even when aggressive pruning is applied. The feature distillation
278   loss is formulated as:
279
280
                               Lfd = cos_similarity(feat_bLViT, feat_vallinaViT),
281   where feat_bLViT represents the feature embeddings from the big.LITTLE model, and
282   feat_vallinaViT represents the embeddings from the pre-trained teacher model. The cosine sim-
283   ilarity function ensures that the feature representations of our model are as close as possible to those
284   of the teacher. The total loss used for training combines the supervised loss Lsupervised with the feature
285   distillation loss, weighted by a scalar λfd :
286                                        Ltotal = Lsupervised + λfd · Lfd .

293   In our experiments, we employ two variants of the bLViT. In the first variant, we use the ViT-Base as

295   vanilla ViT-Base, the first layer is the ViT-Base layer where it can see all tokens, and starting from the
296   second layer we start to use big.LITTLE modules, therefore this model consists of 12 P-blocks and 11
297   E-blocks in total. The prediction layers are used after layers 1, 4, 7 and 10. In the second variant, we
298   test it with a larger model size and use the ViT-Huge as the P-block and the ViT-Base as the E-block,
299
      denoted as H+B. This model follows the 32-layer architecture of the standard ViT-Huge, with the first
      9 layers exclusively using the ViT-Huge, fully processing all tokens. Starting from the tenth layer, a
300
      big.LITTLE module is alternately used in every other layer. In layers without an E-block, only 25%
301
      of tokens are updated by the P-block, resulting in a configuration of 32 P-blocks and 12 E-blocks in
302   total. Here, the prediction layers are used after layers 8, 16 and 24.

305
      each window, ensuring the same number of tokens in different windows, which facilitates parallel

      5     C ONCLUSION
470
471   This paper introduces the big.LITTLE Vision Transformer (bLViT), an innovative architecture
472   designed to enhance the efficiency of visual recognition systems. By strategically allocating image
473   tokens between a high-capacity performance block and a speed-optimized efficiency block, this
474   architecture significantly reduces computational demands while maintaining high accuracy. Our
475   experimental results demonstrate that the bLViT not only preserves robust accuracy but also boosts
476   computational efficiency, making it a practical choice for scalable and adaptable AI deployments.
477
478   B ROADER I MPACT
479
480   Our work aims to improve the inference speed of the vision transformer models. Our model design
481   can allow the vision transformer model to run on cheaper and more energy-efficient hardware at an
482   acceptable speed. It would benefit people without access to expensive hardware and make a positive
483   impact on combating climate change since the inference becomes more efficient. We acknowledge
484   unknown risks can be brought by the development of AI technology; however, the contribution of this
485   paper has no greater risk than any other generic deep-learning paper that studies standard datasets
      such as ImageNet and MSCOCO.
