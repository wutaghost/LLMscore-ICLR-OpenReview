009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The primary challenge in end-to-end autonomous driving lines in how to establish
012            robust environmental perception and representations. While most methods im-
013            prove these capabilities by introducing auxiliary perception tasks, the process of
014            obtaining precise large-scale annotations in this paradigm is both time-consuming
015            and laborious, thereby limiting the scalability and practical application. To address
016
               this, we propose an architecture based on the Generative Aided Planner (GAP),
               which integrates scene generation and planning within a single framework. To
017
               compensate for the information loss in discrete image features, we design a dual-
018
               branch image encoder that fuses continuous and discrete features, improving the
019            model’s ability to recognize traffic lights. Through the scene generation task from
020            input tokens, our approach learns the intrinsic dependencies between tokens and
021            environments, which in turn benefits the planning task. It is important to note
022            that the generative model is trained in a fully self-supervised manner, requiring
023            no perception annotations. Our model is built upon GPT-2, which exhibits scaling
024            laws similar to those observed in other GPTs: as we increase the model size and
025            data size, the performance shows continuous and non-saturating improvements.
026            Experiments show that among methods using the front view as input, our ap-
027
               proach outperforms other methods that employ multiple perception supervision
               in the CARLA simulator. Our method is simple yet highly effective, offering a
028
               promising direction for scalable and practical deployment of autonomous vehi-
029
               cles in real-world settings.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   End-to-end autonomous driving demonstrates clear advantages over traditional modular approaches
035   by simplifying system architecture, reducing error accumulation and enabling global optimiza-
036   tion (Chen et al., 2024). The vanilla end-to-end driving planners often rely on directly optimizing the
037   planning module without intermediate steps (Codevilla et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020), which focuses
038   solely on the planner’s performance, as illustrated in Figure 1(a). Due to the sparse supervision, this
039
      paradigm struggles to learn effective environmental representations, resulting in unexpected driv-
      ing performance. Instead, multi-task-assisted planning improves environmental representations by
040
      introducing auxiliary supervised signals, depicted in Figure 1(b). This scheme leverages multiple
041
      perception tasks as intermediate steps for achieving supervised learning, such as detection (Chen &
042   Krähenbühl, 2022), map construction(Jiang et al., 2023), and motion prediction(Hu et al., 2023b;
043   Shao et al., 2023), to facilitate the comprehensive understanding of the environment. However,
044   obtaining precise annotations of these perception tasks is time-consuming and laborious at a large
045   scale (Sun et al., 2020; Caesar et al., 2020), thus limiting its scalability and practical application.
046
      To address this limitation, we present an integrated generation and planning framework, termed
047
      GAP. As shown in Figure 1(c), our approach utilizes a fully self-supervised generative model to
048   learn representations of the environment, which in turn facilitates more accurate and reliable plan-
049   ning. Our method draws inspiration from recent advancements in large language models (LLMs),
050   such as GPT-series (Radford et al., 2018; 2019; Brown et al., 2020), which have demonstrated the
051   effectiveness of self-supervised learning in sequences modeling by capturing complex patterns and
052   dependencies. Similarly, our framework leverages the power of GPT-like sequences modeling to
053   learn meaningful representations of driving environments without the need of explicit perception
      supervision. This self-supervised paradigm enables continuous learning from data collected by mil-

073
      Our “generative aided” approach is primarily implemented based on the GPT autoregressive archi-
      tecture. Due to the significant gap between the planning and generation tasks, several challenges
074
      arise. Firstly, there is a difference in output forms: generation produces dense, pixel-level out-
075
      puts, whereas planning outputs are sparse. To address this, we take the action query embedding as
076   the autoregressive network’s input, while proposing the joint optimization of action regression loss
077   and autoregressive classification loss. Secondly, the generation task focuses more on image details,
078   whereas the planning task commonly relies more on the high-level semantic information and global
079   knowledge. To supplement the information required for planning, we incorporate driving-oriented
080   feature inputs. We adopted a unified architecture that simultaneously outputs planning and genera-
081   tion, with most parameters shared between the two tasks, differing only in their output heads. Thus,
082   the optimization of the generation task can directly influence planning.
083   We are surprised to find that our approach exhibits properties similar to GPT’s scaling laws, showing
084   a consistent and non-saturating performance improvements with increased model size or data size.
085   With the configuration of 345 million parameters (GPT-2 medium) and 256 hours of driving data, our
086   camera-only method achieves a new state-of-the-art (SOTA) in the CARLA simulator (Dosovitskiy
087   et al., 2017), even surpassing those methods that require additional perception supervision.
088   In summary, our contributions include:
089
090          • We propose a novel generative aided planning framework with elaborate model designs,
091            requiring no post-processing and perceptual supervision, and have achieved a new state-of-
092            the-art on the CARLA simulator.
093          • An empirical examination validates the scaling laws of the proposed model, initially mir-
094            roring the appealing properties of large language models.
095
             • We will open-source the code and models to foster the development of the end-to-end
096            autonomous driving community.

104   2.1   E ND - TO - END AUTONOMOUS D RIVING
105
106   End-to-end autonomous driving has emerged as a hot research topic, replacing traditional rule-based
107   and modular-based approaches by directly learning driving policy from driving videos. Since end-
      to-end autonomous driving was first introduced over 30 years ago (Pomerleau, 1988), many methods


                                                                               2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      have been introduced, which can be broadly categorized into two types: reinforcement learning (RL)
109   based methods and imitation learning (IL) based methods. The topic of this work falls under the cat-
110   egory of imitation learning. IL learns driving policy directly from expert demonstration data without
111   the need for trial and error exploration of the environment, resulting in higher data efficiency com-
112   pared with RL based methods. CILRS (Liang et al., 2018) uses a ResNet perception module to
113   process an input image into a latent space, followed by two control prediction heads, which does not
114   utilize temporal information or auxiliary supervision signals, resulting in relatively low performance.
115   Roach (Liang et al., 2018) introduces a stronger RL-based expert model that translates perception
116   ground truth into a Bird’s Eye View (BEV) for action prediction. Roach’s student model utilizes
117
      supervision of both action and intermediate features from the expert model. Following methods
      such as TCP (Wu et al., 2022) adopts this strategy, employing intermediate features for supervision.
118
      These approaches indirectly utilizes perception ground truth as supervision. TCP proposes a rule-
119
      based fusion scheme of trajectory and control, improving driving performance across multiple sce-
120   narios, but this method requires careful tuning of hyperparameters. Interfuser (Shao et al., 2022) and
121   Transfuser (Chitta et al., 2022) design a post-processing strategy that fuses planning and detection
122   by constraining the planning trajectory to avoid overlapping with detected objects. This approach
123   is limited by the accuracy of perception and does not allow for fully end-to-end optimization. To
124   achieve a more accurate understanding of the environment, most methods utilize specific perception
125   auxiliary tasks for supervision. High-definition maps are used by most methods (Hu et al., 2022;
126   Shao et al., 2022; 2023; Chitta et al., 2022; Chen & Krähenbühl, 2022; Jia et al., 2023b;a) because
127   they provide information on traffic lights, road signs, and the complex topology of road intersec-
128
      tions. Additionally, many method (Hu et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2022; 2023; Chitta et al., 2022; Chen
      & Krähenbühl, 2022; Jia et al., 2023b;a) utilize information on obstacle positions, either through
129
      detection boxes or BEV segmentation. Although these multi-task perception aided methods con-
130
      tribute to learning better environmental representations, they also constrain large-scale real-world
131   deployment due to the cost of annotation.
132
133   Recently, CarLLaVA (Renz et al., 2024) demonstrated promising performance in autonomous driv-
134
      ing using only camera inputs without perception labels. It builds on LLaVA-NeXT’s vision encoder
      pre-trained on internet-scale vision-language data and employs a semi-disentangled output repre-
135
      sentation combining path predictions and waypoints. While both CarLLaVA and our approach re-
136
      duce label dependency, we focus on learning representations through generative modeling rather
137   than vision-language pre-training. Our method also exhibits clear scaling properties with increased
138   model and dataset size.

141   2.2   G ENERATIVE M ODELS FOR AUTONOMOUS D RIVING
142
143
      VideoGPT (Yan et al., 2021) leverages a simple GPT-like architecture to autoregressively gener-
144
      ate discrete latents. Despite its simplicity, it shows comparable performance with GANs for video
145   generation. GAIA-1 (Hu et al., 2023a) continues this autoregressive paradigm to generate future
146   videos and, with larger model and datasets, exhibits surprising emerging properties. MUVO (Bog-
147   doll et al., 2023) proposes a multimodal generative world model by utilizing raw camera and lidar
148   data to learn a sensor-agnostic geometric representation of the world. Some methods (Zhao et al.,
149   2024; Lu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024) use diffusion models to generate more
150   realistic future videos. These methods primarily focus on improving the realism of generated videos
151   to be used as neural simulators and are not well-suited for directly planning output. Other methods
152   like Wang et al. (2023b;a) combine ego vehicle trajectory prediction with future video generation,
153
      while Zheng et al. (2023) proposes a GTP-style architecture for 4D occupancy prediction. However,
      their planning effectiveness remains unverified. Importantly, these approaches lack navigation input
154
      and rely solely on historical trajectories for extrapolation, leading to issues of causal confusion, as
155
      highlighted in Zhai et al. (2023). Among all the methods we have studied, the one closest to ours
156   is MILE (Hu et al., 2022). MILE can simultaneously perform end-to-end planning, BEV segmenta-
157   tion, and RGB image reconstruction. MILE adopts a VAE-like structure to compress observations
158   into a global latent representation, and shows that input images can be decoded from the latent space.
159   However, there is a significant information bottleneck in the model design, preventing the driving
160   task from benefiting from the image generation task. Compared to MILE, our method does not rely
161   on BEV segmentation supervision and demonstrates the benefits of generative tasks for planning
      through a scalable model architecture.

      2.3   S CALING L AWS
163
164   Scaling laws (Henighan et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020) illustrate mathematical relationships
165   demonstrating how the model performance improves based on various factors such as model size,
166   dataset size, and computing resources. Scaling laws offer numerous benefits. For example, they
167   serve as a guiding principle in model design, facilitating the selection of an optimal scale that bal-
168   ances performance and computational costs. Moreover, scaling laws helps researchers identify im-
169   portant factors that affect improving model performance. Language models like GPT-series (Rad-
170
      ford et al., 2018; 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022), exemplify the principles of scaling
      laws. As their size increases, measured by parameters or training data, their performance improves.
171
      This shows a continuous and non-saturating improvement in performance, validating their long-
172
      term advantage in enhancing model capability. Despite the significant success of scaling laws in
173   language models, to our knowledge, there is currently no public paper to study the application of
174   scaling laws in end-to-end autonomous driving. Through examples from language models, we can
175   see the potential of scaling laws in enhancing the performance of end-to-end autonomous driving.

280   The concept of “generative aided” can be realized through various methods, such as VAEs, diffusion
281   models, autoregressive models, and so on. However, considering the flexibility of autoregressive
282   models in taking diverse prompts and the remarkable scalability of the GPT series, we have chosen
283   GPT-2 for our generative model. As shown in Figure 2, the model is mainly based on the GPT
284   architecture and is trained with two tasks: autoregressive generation of the next token and action
285
      regression for the planner. The probability of the next token is given by Pθ,θg (·), whereas the
      prediction of the action is represented by Gθ,θa (·). It is noteworthy the two tasks share the majority
286
      of the parameters (θ), except for the different output heads (θg and θa ). When optimizing the shared
287
      parameters θ for the generation task, the model will well learn the dependencies of input tokens.
288   This results in a robust representation of the environment, which subsequently benefits the planning
289   task.

470
      Limitations. Previous multi-task-assisted methods utilize the white-box information from percep-
471
      tion outputs, which can help debug the reasons for planning errors. Our method does not output
472
      perception results, which may reduce its interpretability to some extent.
473
474   Impacts. Our methodology, which relies solely on human driving data and eliminates the need for
475   supplementary annotations, markedly reduces the cost of autonomous driving. This could potentially
476
      expedite the widespread adoption of mass-produced autonomous vehicles.

478   5                            C ONCLUSION
479
480   We propose an end-to-end autonomous driving architecture called the generative aided planner
481   (GAP), which significantly improves the performance of autonomous driving by learning envi-
482   ronment representations through self-supervised generative tasks. Built upon GPT-2, our method
483   demonstrates scalability in both data size and model size. Despite its simplicity, our approach yields
484   state-of-the-art results on the CRALA benchmark. In the future, we aim to expand our experiments
485   on scaling laws and adapt this method to a vision-language multimodal framework, offering inter-
      pretable textual explanations for autonomous driving decisions.
