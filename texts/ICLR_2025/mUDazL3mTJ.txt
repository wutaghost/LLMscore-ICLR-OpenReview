009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Spatiotemporal systems comprise a collection of spatially distributed yet interde-
012            pendent entities each generating unique dynamic signals. Highly sophisticated
013            methods have been proposed in recent years delivering state-of-the-art (SOTA)
014            forecasts but few have focused on interpretability. To address this, we propose
015            the Future Decomposition Network (FDN), a novel forecast model capable of (a)
016            providing interpretable predictions through classification (b) revealing latent ac-
017
               tivity patterns in the target time-series and (c) delivering forecasts competitive
               with SOTA methods at a fraction of their memory and runtime cost. We conduct
018
               comprehensive analyses on FDN for multiple datasets from hydrologic, traffic,
019
               and energy systems demonstrating its improved accuracy and interpretability.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   A spatiotemporal system represents a collection of spatially distributed but interdependent entities
025   each with unique activity (Li et al., 2017; Zeng et al., 2023). This activity, such as traffic flow, is
026   driven by a complex set of interactions resulting in emergent behaviors that are difficult to understand
027   from observed data. In the case of traffic systems, traffic congestion generally coincides with high
028   traffic volume and network bottlenecks. We observe similar dynamics in streamflow networks where
029   high streamflow events and subsequent dissipation regularly coincide with major precipitation.
030   In this paper, we propose that system behavior can largely be explained by a finite set of fundamental
031   activity patterns. In the context of spatiotemporal learning, a pattern represents a temporal signature
032   of the target variable that recurs frequently and follows specific system rules. For instance, the
033   rise and fall of streamflow during flood events follows major precipitation, hence, we can expect to
034   observe this pattern during similar weather events. These temporal patterns resemble filters used in
035   image processing to detect specific features (Krizhevsky et al., 2012).
036   We propose a model that aims to detect these recurring patterns, as they are likely to reappear in the
037   future. However, future patterns may not precisely match past ones. Therefore, we frame the prob-
038   lem as a soft classification task, estimating the probability of different patterns contributing to the
039   forecast. Using the classification probabilities, we interpolate from a set of learned patterns to make
040   final predictions. We refer to this approach as the Future Decomposition Network (FDN): a model
041   which decomposes system activity (the training data) into important patterns, (softly) classifies past
042
      activity, and predicts the future as an interpolation of these patterns.
043   As evidence, we can represent a system of N entities containing B, O-time-step patterns as a matrix
044   F ∈ RN ·O×B . While F captures all known system behavior, it is highly redundant and may be
045   closely reproduced by a small set of K fundamental patterns F̂ ∈ RO×K shared by all entities. For
046   example, in the Wabash River data analyzed in this paper, 70 years of localized streamflow across
047   1,276 subbasins can be effectively represented by about K=200 patterns as shown in Figure 1a.
048   Figure 1b illustrates eight of these streamflow patterns, where the first two patterns capture the high
049   flow and subsequent dissipation observed during flood events.
050   Over the past decade, significant progress has been made in spatiotemporal machine learning (Shi
051   & Yeung, 2018; Wang et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2020). Most existing methods rely on a combination
052   of temporal and spatial encodings to capture interactions among system components, but they often
053   lack interpretability, failing to reveal how forecasts are generated. FDN addresses this limitation
      with a novel approach that decomposes spatiotemporal systems into a finite set of patterns and then

083   2     R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Spatiotemporal system forecasting is a highly active sub-field of ML research, primarily originating
086
      in the study of traffic systems (Li et al., 2017) and now advancing into multi-domain application (Wu
      et al., 2020; Cao et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021; 2022; Zeng et al., 2023; Majeske & Azad, 2024). In
087
      the pursuit of greater forecast accuracy, increasingly sophisticated encoding and decoding schemes
088
      have emerged but development of the forecast operator has been limited. The forecast operator
089   refers to the inflection point in each forecast model where the past/input sequence is transformed
090   into the future/output sequence. At a high level, contemporary forecast models follow a three-part
091   architecture (shown in Figure 2a) consisting of (1) an encoder module to project the input sequence
092   from input to embedding space (2) a forecast operator to transform the input sequence into an output
093   sequence and (3) a decoder module to project the output sequence from embedding to output space.
094   We summarize recent encoder modules and forecast operators currently in use but we do not cover
095   decoder modules since most methods apply simple linear projection or decoding coincides with the
096   forecast operator (e.g. with 1 × 1 kernels in convolution operators, multi-head attention, etc.).
097
098   2.1   E NCODING M ODULES
099
      The encoding module aims to capture information relevant to each node during the projection from
100
      input to embedding space. Spatiotemporal systems exhibit both spatial and temporal dynamics
101
      which must be properly embedded to support the subsequent forecast operator and decoding module.
102   The system’s dependency structure (e.g. streamflow network, road network, etc.) significantly
103   influences the local dynamics of each node, and many methods leverage graph convolution (Kipf &
104   Welling, 2016) to encode it. STGCN, DCRNN, T-GCN, A3T-GCN, and STGM (Yu et al., 2017; Li
105   et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2019; Bai et al., 2021; Lablack & Shen, 2023) utilize pre-defined graphs
106   but recent methods have opted to learn the dependency structure including MTGNN, StemGNN,
107   AGCRN, SCINet, and MMR-GNN (Wu et al., 2020; Cao et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
      2022; Majeske & Azad, 2024).

144   2.2   F ORECAST O PERATORS
145
146
147
      From the recent literature, we find five forecast operators in use including the identity, fully-
      connected (FC), convolution, auto-regressive (AR), and attention operators. Section A.2 discusses
148
      these operators in detail and table 3 enumerates forecast models that apply them, but we offer a
149
      brief description here. The identity operator (Figure 2b) involves selecting the last O elements of
150   the encoded input sequence. The FC operator (Figure 2c) utilizes all-to-all connections to transform
151   the encoded sequence into the output sequence. Convolution operators treat the encoded sequence
152   as image data to transform I input color channels / time-steps into O output color channels / time-
153   steps using O filters of I kernels. The AR operator (Figure 2d) auto-regressively feeds the encoded
154   sequence for O steps to produce the output sequence; typically via an RNN cell. Finally, attention
155   operators compute each element of the output sequence as an attention-weighted sum of the entire
156   input sequence and are central to transformer-based forecast models.
157   We note that the FC, convolution, and attention operators are fundamentally similar. In fact, 1×1
158   and 1×H kernels (where H is the embedding dimension) are common and nearly identical to FC.
159   Furthermore, only attention provides direct interpretability through the examination of final attention
160   scores. Our review reveals that the forecast operator has been overlooked in favor of more sophisti-
161   cated encoding schemes. With FDN, we propose the Future Decomposition layer: a novel forecast
      operator based on classification and interpolation that can reveal fundamental activity patterns.

196   3.2.1    H IGH - LEVEL A RCHITECTURE
197
      The goal of FDN is to learn patterns of the past (i.e. preambles) that predict particular patterns of the
198
      future. To capture the coupling of such past and future patterns, we utilize a classifier-interpolator
199
      architecture shown in Figure 3. The classifier aims to determine the correct future pattern based on
200   past activity and features five internal stages to support soft classification accuracy. These include
201   (a) feature filtering to remove noise (b) adding information to identify the location (c) encoding of
202   spatial/dependency dynamics (d) adding information to identify the point-in-time and (e) encoding
203   of temporal dynamics. Future patterns seldom follow past patterns exactly, thus, FDN utilizes soft
204   classification in the selection of a future. Rather than discretize probabilities to select the future
205   pattern of highest likelihood (classification), we apply these probabilities directly (soft classification)
206   to select the future as an interpolation between K futures patterns.
207   The forward pass of FDN is defined by Eq. 2 where the past of each node is soft classified to
208   produce the K-class likelihood matrix C ∈ RN ×K . Here, each row vector indicates the classifier’s
209   confidence as to which pattern should follow amongst K possibilities. The interpolation module
210   then generates the prediction as a linear combination/interpolation of K patterns using the classifier’s
211   confidence scores as weights. The following sections give a detailed discussion of FDN’s classifier
212   module and our novel FD layer for prediction and pattern discovery.

336   4     E XPERIMENTS
337
338   We evaluate FDN and all baseline models on three publicly available datasets from hydrology, traffic,
339   and energy. These datasets are formally known as Wabash River, E-PEMS-BAY, and Solar-Energy
340   and we discuss each in detail in Section A.1 of the appendix. Dataset properties are provided in
341
      Table 1 including sample size (time-steps), system size (nodes), number of features (F ), whether a
      pre-defined graph exists (G), sample resolution, and the various prediction horizons we study.
342
343   Data Preparation. In all experiments, we standardize the features of a node using the mean and
344   standard deviation computed from the training set of that node. All models are trained on standard-
345   ized features but forecasts are inversely standardized before final evaluation. Only E-PEMS-BAY
346   contains missing values (≈ 2.5%) and we impute with local periodic means. That is, we compute
347
      and utilize the periodic mean (separate mean for the 288, 5-minute moments in a day) of each feature
      and node during imputation.
348
349   Evaluation Metrics. All models are evaluated according to Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean
350   Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE), and Root Mean Square Error (RMSE). Metrics are masked to
351   exclude imputed values and ensure model performance quantification is a consequence of forecasts
352   made on ground truth samples only. Imputed values are also masked in the computation of forecast
353
      loss during SGD. Experiments are executed three times using three pseudo-randomly generated
      initialization seeds and results are given as the mean and standard deviation of these trials. Due to
354
      space limitations, standard deviations are presented in section A.3 of the appendix.
355
356   Model Baselines. We evaluate FDN against 11 forecast models found throughout the literature.
357   This includes simpler models designed for single time-series forecasting, complex models designed
358   for multiple time-series, and highly sophisticated SOTA models designed for multiple time-series
359
      forecasting across multiple domains. Implementations of these models were acquired from their
      published GitHub repositories except for T-GCN and A3T-GCN implemented in PyTorch Geometric
360
      Temporal (Rozemberczki et al., 2021). All experiments were conducted on an Nvidia A100 GPU
361
      with 40GB of memory. Each model is trained using MAE (PyTorch’s L1Loss) as forecast loss.

533
      This paper presents FDN, a novel forecast model architecture which leverages classification and
534   interpolation to produce accurate and interpretable forecasts. FDN utilizes the Future Decomposi-
535   tion layer, a new forecast operator to the literature capable of revealing latent patterns of the target
536   time-series. We demonstrate FDN’s forecast accuracy by meeting or exceeding the performance of
537   current SOTA forecast models across three datasets from hydrologic, traffic, and energy systems.
538   Finally, FDN shows exceptional efficiency with faster epoch runtimes and far fewer parameters than
539   its competitors. We are excited to present FDN and feel confident its novel architecture can inspire
      new avenues of spatiotemporal forecasting research that advance interpretability.

646   meteorologic features recorded at various gauging stations across the Wabash River Basin. This
647   basin spans three US states including eastern Illinois, western Ohio, and central Indiana and consists
      1276 subbasins (nodes). Measurements of temperature (min and max), precipitation, soil water, and

649   seven days of all five features to forecast streamflow for the next one, four, and seven days. The

672   A.2   R ELATED W ORK C ONTINUED
673
674
      This section offers a more detailed discussion of each forecast operator including their core operation
675
      and some limitations. We refer to the input/encoded sequence x as containing I time-steps, and
676
      output/decoded sequence ŷ as containing O time-steps, and current time-step as τ .
677
678   Identity Op. The identity operator (Figure 2b) involves selecting the last O elements of the encoded
679   input sequence. This operator requires the input sequence be equal to or greater in length than the
680
      output sequence. For certain encoders, such as RNNs, important information may be omitted since
      only the final element of the output sequence is a function of all input time-steps.
681
682   Fully-Connected Op. The fully-connected (FC) operator (Figure 2c) utilizes all-to-all connections
683   to transform the encoded sequence into the output sequence. As a result, each of the O output
684   time-steps are a function of all I input time-steps. This allows any arbitrary mapping I → O but
685   incorporates all input time-steps which may contain redundant/noisy information for long sequences.
686   Convolution Op. This operator applies a convolution layer by treating the encoded sequence as
687   image data where time-steps are handled as color-channels and filters. Specifically, I input time-
688   steps are transformed into O output time-steps by applying a convolution layer of O filters each with
689   I kernels. The operator can perform any mapping I → O and is very similar to FC since each output
690   time-step is a summation of kernels applied at every input time-step.
691   Auto-Regressive Op. The auto-regressive (AR) operator (Figure 2d) recurrently feeds the encoded
692   sequence for O steps to produce the output sequence. The AR operator is primarily seen in recurrent
693   neural networks (RNNs) where a decoder cell auto-regressively feeds the encoded sequence pro-
694   duced by a separate encoder cell. AR can perform any mapping I → O and output time-steps are
695   strictly causal but RNNs bring challenges to gradient stability.
696   Attention Op. Attention operators compute each element of the output sequence as an attention-
697   weighted sum of the entire input sequence. This operator was popularized by Transformers
698   (Vaswani, 2017) (designed for language translation) but recent methods (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu
699   et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022) have adapted the Transformer architecture to long-term series fore-
700   casting. Specifically, these methods zero-pad the latter half of the input sequence to match the output
701   sequence length and use it as the query. The encoded input sequence is used as key and value and
      fed with the query to multi-head attention to produce the output sequence.

      A.5   A BLATION S TUDY R ESULTS
811
812
813   Tables 10, 11, 13, 12, 14, 15, and 16 present the results of all ablation studies. The best result
814   is emboldened and the second-best is underlined. For each entry, we run three trials and present
815
      the mean result for MAE, MAPE, and RMSE. All ablation studies were conducted on the longest
      horizon which includes 7 days for Wabash River, 1 hour (12 time-steps) for E-PEMS-BAy, and 2
816
      hours (12 time-steps) for Solar-Energy. Note that results for Solar-Energy in Table 11 are identical
817
      since this dataset is uni-variate.
818
819   Learned Patterns. The FD layer learns a set of patterns to capture F which, as demonstrated in
820   Figure 1a, is greatly benefited by increasing the rank / number of patterns. Here, we test increasing
821
      the number of patterns learned by FD to improve its ability to capture F. Table 10 and Figure
      8b demonstrate the effectiveness of FD as we observe a saturation in forecast performance when
822
      learning as few as eight patterns.
823
824   Attention Layers. Here we test the effectiveness of LDA in FDN. We compare no attention to
825   four attention layers of increasing specificity including (a) static attention (A) A ∈ RF (b) dynamic
826   attention (DA) A ∈ RI×F (c) complete localized dynamic feature attention (CLDA) A ∈ RN ×I×F
827
      and (d) our proposed LDA A ∈ RD×I×F . Results are provided in Table 11. Note that attention is
      not applicable to Solar-Energy since it is uni-variate. E-PEMS-BAY benefits significantly from LDA
828
      but Wabash River does not. This suggests that the importance of minimum/maximum temperature,
829
      precipitation, and soil moisture to streamflow is not specific to individual subbasins (i.e. localized).
830
831   Dependency Embedding. FDN encodes node inter-dependency applying Chebyshev graph con-
832   volution. Table 12 shows a significant improvement to forecast performance from the inclusion of
833
      node inter-dependency learning.
834   Node Embedding Dimension. FDN’s learned node embeddings condition the classifier to each
835   node, determine their feature filtering, and learn the graph for dependency encoding. Node embed-
836   ding dimension controls the specificity of node conditioning, LDA, and the learned graph and must
837   be tuned for proper generalization. Table 13 shows the result of increasing dimension D and we
838   observe a saturation in forecasting performance at approximately D = 10.
839   Pattern Regularization. In SVD, the left matrix U is orthogonal to capture the highest degree of
840   variance in K column vectors. Following this, we constrain the patterns to be dissimilar by adding
841   their similarity to forecast loss as a regularization term. Table 14 shows increasing pattern regulation,
842   from which, Wabash River and Solar-Energy gain the most benefit.
843   Periodic Moment Resolution. Periodic embeddings consist of a sequence of ρ-dimensional embed-
844   dings representing moments in the known period/season of the forecast variable. Moment resolution
845   ranges from the duration of the period (M = 1) to the duration of each time-step (M ≫ 1) and
846   must be tuned to avoid over-fitting. Table 15 shows increasing moment resolution starting from pe-
847   riod/season duration (M = 1) and increasing up to time-step duration (M =365 in Wabash River).
848   Wabash River shows saturation at 3-months (capturing the 4 seasons of the year) while E-PEMS-
849   BAY and Solar-Energy benefit from high-resolution moments.
850   Periodic Embedding Dimension. The dimensionality of each moment embedding controls its
851   specificity and the extent of temporal conditioning. Table 16 shows increasing periodic embed-
852   ding dimension starting from ρ=0 where periodic embeddings are omitted from FDN. Periodic em-
853   beddings generally improve forecast accuracy but each dataset/signal requires a precise embedding
854   dimension.
