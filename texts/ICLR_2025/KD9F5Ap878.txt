011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013
               In operations research (OR), formulating optimization problems in industrial ap-
014            plications is often time-consuming and requires specialized expertise. Recently,
015            large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable potential to automate this
016            process. However, evaluating the performance of LLMs in optimization modeling
017            remains challenging due to the scarcity of suitable datasets and rigorous evalua-
018            tion methodologies. To reduce this gap, we introduce OptiBench, a new bench-
019            mark designed to assess LLMs’ ability to formulate linear programming (LP) and
020            mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) models. OptiBench provides a diverse
021            dataset covering 816 optimization modeling word problems across 16 problem
022
               classes and over 80 practical domains. It also adopts a model-data separation for-
               mat with 2 levels of description abstraction. The dataset exhibits the complexity
023
               of real-world optimization problems compared to traditional textbook examples.
024
               OptiBench incorporates a new evaluation method based on a modified Weisfeiler-
025            Lehman graph isomorphism test (WL-test) algorithm. We theoretically prove that
026            this method can correctly judge whether two models are equivalent or not, set-
027            ting a new standard for automatically validating the correctness of optimization
028            modeling. We benchmark various LLMs using OptiBench and observe significant
029            performance differences. GPT-4o by direct prompting achieves 49.39% overall
030            accuracy, outperforming other models and LLM-based agents, including OpenAI
031            o1 (preview and mini). Notably, GPT-4o’s performance varies across different
032            problem classes, achieving over 90% accuracy on the knapsack problem class but
033
               falling below 5% on the traveling salesman problem class. These findings provide
               new insights into the strengths and limitations of LLMs in optimization modeling.

038
039
      Operations Research (OR) is a discipline that employs advanced analytical methods, such as mathe-
040
      matical modeling and optimization techniques, to aid decision-making and problem-solving in com-
041
      plex systems Hillier & Lieberman (2015). OR methods play an important role in various industries
042   such as logistics, manufacturing, finance, and healthcare, optimizing processes and resources to en-
043   hance efficiency and productivity Winston (2004). However, the formulation of optimization models
044   is often a complex task that requires a collaborative effort between domain experts, who possess
045   deep knowledge of the industry practices, and optimization experts, who are skilled in translating
046   real-world problems into mathematical models and applying solution techniques Vineetha & Shiyas
047   (2020). Such collaboration often demands significant time and expertise, which can pose a signifi-
048   cant barrier to the widespread adoption of OR methods.
049   To address this challenge, there is an increasing demand for automated modeling tools that can
050   bridge the gap between textual problem descriptions and mathematical models. Automating the
051   modeling process can reduce reliance on experts, enhance accessibility for non-experts, and expe-
052   dite decision-making processes Kushman et al. (2014) and Miyani et al. (2015). Such tools have
053   the potential to democratize access to OR techniques, enabling a broader range of practitioners to
      leverage optimization in their operations.

      Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-4 OpenAI et al. (2023) and
055   Llama-3 Dubey et al. (2024), have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding, reason-
056   ing, and planning Ouyang et al. (2022); Achiam et al. (2023); Radford et al. (2019); Song et al.
057   (2024). These models have been successfully applied in various domains, including code generation
058   Chen et al. (2021) and mathematical theorem proving Yang et al. (2023), showcasing their potential
059   in tasks that require comprehension of complex language and logical structures. Consequently, there
060   have been attempts to leverage LLMs for automatic optimization modeling, including LLM-based
061   agent Xiao et al. (2023); AhmadiTeshnizi et al. (2024) and fine-tuning LLM for optimization mod-
062   eling Tang et al. (2024). However, benchmarking the modeling ability of LLMs in OR remains a
063
      challenging endeavor.
064   One significant challenge is the absence of comprehensive benchmark datasets specifically designed
065   for evaluating the optimization modeling capabilities of LLMs. Existing datasets often include both
066   problem description and numerical data in the promptRamamonjison et al. (2022a); Xiao et al.
067   (2023). This setting extremely limits the problem size, which is not reflective of practical modeling
068   problems where data is typically large-scale and independent of the modeling processApIO et al..
069
      Moreover, these datasets lack comprehensiveness, often evaluating models over a single or limited
      level of complexity, and failing to capture the diverse range of problems encountered in real-world
070
      applications.
071
072   Another challenge lies in the evaluation process, which is frequently imprecise and time-consuming.
073   Verification of the generated models often relies solely on solving the optimization problem and
074   comparing the solutions AhmadiTeshnizi et al. (2024); Xiao et al. (2023), but many Mixed-Integer
075
      Linear Programming (MILP) problems are NP-hard and may not reach globally optimal solutions
      within a reasonable timeframe Chen et al. (2022b). This reliance on solvers for verification makes
076
      it difficult to accurately assess the correctness and quality of the generated models, as suboptimal
077
      solutions or solver timeouts can obscure the evaluation Han et al. (2023).
078
079   In this paper, we address these challenges by introducing a comprehensive benchmark dataset and
080   an evaluation tool grounded in a principled and theoretically supported paradigm. Our contributions
081
      are threefold:
082
           1. Benchmark Dataset with Comprehensive Problem Features: We develop a set of 816
083           word problems with a clear separation between model and data, closely mimicking real-
084           world business problems. The dataset is constructed through a hierarchical reverse data
085           evolution pipeline, allowing controlled generation of problem instances with varying com-
086           plexity across 16 problem classes in LPs and MILPS, over 80 practical domains, and 2
087           levels of abstraction. Each problem is rigorously verified by OR experts to ensure accu-
088           racy and relevance. This approach enables a more practical and thorough assessment of
089           LLMs’ modeling capabilities across multiple aspects, including problem size, constraints,
090           and objective functions.
091        2. Benchmark Evaluation Tool with Principled and Theory-Supported Paradigm: We
092           propose a novel evaluation framework that formalizes the assessment of optimization mod-
093           eling based on graph theory. By representing mathematical models as graph structures, we
094           enable a structured and precise comparison between the generated models and ground truth
095
              models. We develop an automatic evaluation algorithm that efficiently computes similarity
              metrics between graphs, with proven computational efficiency and scalability. This method
096
              overcomes the limitations of solver-based verification by providing a direct evaluation of
097
              model correctness and completeness.
098
099
           3. Benchmarking Popular LLMs and Recent Agent-Based Approaches: We conduct ex-
              tensive experiments evaluating the performance of leading LLMs, including GPT-4o Ope-
100
              nAI et al. (2023), and other models such as o1 OpenAI (2024), Llama-3 Dubey et al.
101
              (2024), Claude Anthropic (2024a;b) on our benchmark dataset. Our results demonstrate
102           that GPT-4o outperforms other LLMs, including o1-preview in optimization modeling
103           tasks, highlighting its superior capability in understanding and formulating complex op-
104           timization problems. Interestingly, existing agent-based methods, which involve iterative
105           reasoning or decomposition strategies, did not show significant improvements over direct
106           prompting methods. We provide a comprehensive error analysis across various problem
107           features, shedding light on the strengths and limitations of current LLMs in optimization
              modeling.

149   The modeling procedure in optimization typically involves a collaborative three-stage process be-
150   tween domain experts and operations research professionals. In the pre-modeling stage, the busi-

184   improvements in solver performance. Inspired by this, we model optimization problems as bipar-

190
      evaluate LLMs. The Linear Programming Word Problem (LPWP) dataset Ramamonjison et al.
191
      (2022a)includes multiple domains and comprises up to 1,001 LP problems. However, it primarily
192   consists of elementary-level LP problems, limiting its effectiveness in assessing advanced modeling
193   capabilities. The ComplexOR dataset Xiao et al. (2023) was designed to feature more intricate OR

199   range of OR problems through data synthesis and augmentation. Nevertheless, they rely on solver
200
      solution-based evaluations, which fail to directly assess the modeling failure of LLMs and are lim-
      ited by solvers’ performance. In comparison to existing benchmarks, our work aims to provide a
201
      more comprehensive dataset and to formalize the evaluation of modeling, enabling a more precise
202
      assessment of LLM capabilities in optimization modeling.

207   To evaluate the potential of LLMs as interfaces for optimization solvers, particularly in the mod-
208   eling and post-modeling stages we introduce OptiBench, a comprehensive benchmark designed to
209   assess LLM capabilities in optimization modeling rigorously. OptiBench leverages LLMs to simu-
210   late real-world OR problems with delicate prompt engineering and expert verification. It evaluates
211   LLMs’ abilities in language comprehension, model formulation, and domain-specific coding tasks
212
      specific to optimization practices. Inspired by INFORMS modeling competition AIMMS (2024),
      we adopted a model-data separated format of the word problems.
213
214   The OptiBench encompasses multi-dimensional complexity through a hierarchical reverse data evo-
215   lution, including optimization problem type, classes, domains, variants, and level of abstrac-
      tion. Our dataset comprises 816 optimization modeling word problems of two fundamental types

      3.2   M ULTI - DIMENSIONAL C OMPLEXITY
267
268   To construct the multidimensional complexity efficiently, we employ a hierarchical reverse data
269   evolution pipeline encompassing three key stages: optimization model stimulation, reference answer
      generation, and word problem generation.

270
      Optimization Models Stimulation We leverage GPT-4o to propose optimization models, sys-
271   tematically evolving the context through problem types, classes, domains, and variants. Specifi-
272   cally, GPT-4o first identifies classical problem classes within LP and MILP. For each problem class,
273   GPT-4o subsequently determines common application domains, ensuring relevance and applicabil-
274   ity. Within each domain-specific problem class, GPT-4o first proposes the most canonical optimiza-
275   tion model and then augments it by varying optimization components to introduce diversity and
276   complexity.
277
      Reference Answers Generation The simulated optimization model is represented as Gurobi code
278   that reads from an associated data file ‘data.json‘ and outputs the corresponding realization as
279   ‘model.lp‘. This approach facilitates the evaluation process by providing a standardized reference
280   answer. To create the necessary ‘data.json‘ files, we utilize GPT-4o to generate Python programs
281   tailored to each optimization model. This method allows for scalable problem size adjustments by
282   simply modifying the dimensionality instructions within the prompts, thereby easing the expansion
283   of specific optimization models.
284   Word Problems Generation Finally, we reversely generate word problems from the stimulated
285   optimization models. Drawing insights from the INFORMS AIMMS-MOPTA AIMMS (2024) Op-
286   timization Modeling Competition, we meticulously crafted a standardized word problem structure,
287   as illustrated in Figure 5. Using GPT-4o, we first translate the solver code into a detailed word prob-
288   lem adhering to this standardized structure. Subsequently, we refine and summarize the generated
289   content to produce more concise and unstructured problem statements. This comprehensive ap-
290   proach ensures that the generated word problems are both accurate representations of the underlying
291   optimization models and suitable for benchmarking in a more complex manner.

      We implement controlled generation with meticulous verification to ensure data quality in our bench-

297   Controlled Generation Similar to crafting standard word problem structures, we construct a code
298   skeleton specifically designed to stimulate optimization models. This simplifies the free-generation
299   task into a more manageable code completion task, which allows us to regulate the LLM’s out-

      put tightly and thereby partially automate data evolution and verification pipeline. This controlled

315   4.1   E VALUATION P RINCIPAL
316
317   To evaluate if the LLM gives the correct answer for optimization modeling, we combine their gen-
318   eration with problem data to form a test modeling instance and compare the test instance with the
319   corresponding standard instance in our benchmark.
320   In general, it is challenging to check whether two optimization problem instances are “equal”. For
321   example, variables can be named in different notations or presented in different orders, resulting in
322   a set of not exactly the same but equivalent modeling. To fill this gap, we propose a new evalua-
323   tion paradigm, which identifies the correctness of optimization modeling by detecting whether the
      inherent structure of the test instance is equivalent to that of the standard instance.


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      We first establish the correctness for MILP and LP model formulation based on the following three
325   principles: (1) Variables should reflect real-world entities. (2) Objectives should clearly align with
326   their descriptions, and (3) Constraints should represent real-world limitations without redundancy.
327
328
      A model following the above principles should be regarded as correct. We further introduce the
      notion of equivalence between two model instances. Specifically, the equivalence allows model
329
      instances to change the notations of variables and rearrange their variables/constraints without losing
330
      essential information. We denote that two instances P1 and P2 are model-equivalent by P1 ⇠ P2 ; a
331   formal definition is provided in Appendix A.2.
332
333   Our concept of model-wise equivalence aligns with the isomorphism in graphs, allowing nodes to
334
      be re-indexed or rearranged without changing the graph structure. This motivates us to incorporate
      tools in graph theory to evaluate model equivalence. Following existing work in the field of learning
335
      to optimize Gasse et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2022b), we represent an LP/MILP model realization
336
      with a bipartite graph (Figure 1). We proved that detecting the model equivalence can be reduced to
337   testing graph isomorphic; See Appendix A.4 for a formal demonstration.

384   4.3   T HEORETICAL G UARANTEE
385
386   Though the WL-test is widely used for isomorphism testing, it may fail to distinguish non-
387   isomorphic graphs in certain exceptional cases; counter-examples are presented in Appendix A.11

      Chen et al. (2022b).
389   In previous work, Chen et al. (2022b) characterized one sufficient condition of problem instances,
390   say unfoldable, that can be accurately distinguished by WL-Test. Yet it is too strict for many MILP
391   problems. For example, graphs for bin-packing instances are typically not unfoldable; see Appendix
392   A.11 for an example. We extend the sufficient conditions to cover more cases, which benefits the
393   problems encountered in our benchmark.
394   To clarify the sufficient conditions for WL test in our evaluation paradigm, we define a class of
395   WL-determinable and decomposable symmetric problem instances.
396
397   Definition 4.1 (WL-Determinable Instance) We say a model instance P is WL-determinable if
398
      WL test outputs distinct colors for different nodes in its graph representation.
399
      This definition aligns with the definition of unfoldable graphs.
400
401   Definition 4.2 (Decomposable Symmetric Instance) We say a modeling instance P is decompos-
402   able symmetric if, after WL test coloring on its representation graph, the following conditions hold:
403
404
            1. Excluding nodes that have distinct colors from all other nodes, the remaining nodes can
               be divided into groups, denoted by I1 , I2 , · · · , Ik , each containing at least two nodes. All
405
               nodes in the same group share the same color.
406
407         2. For any pair of groups Ii , Ij , either Ii and Ij are disconnected, or the nodes in Ii and Ij
408            form a perfect matching. Specifically, a perfect matching means that every node in Ii is
409            connected to exactly one node in Ij , and every node in Ij is connected to exactly one node
410            in Ii .

507   Comparing Performance Across Different Problem Classes Beyond the primary performance
508   metrics, we also examined the modeling accuracy across various classes of OR problems to identify
509   potential biases in LLM knowledge bases. The results indicated a pronounced bias, with relatively
510   high accuracy observed in solving knapsack problems (MILP), assignment problems (MILP), and
511   diet problems (LP). These areas likely benefit from the simplicity of problem model and more ex-
512   tensive representation in training data. Conversely, the models exhibited relatively low accuracy in
513   addressing cutting stock problems (MILP) and near-zero accuracy for traveling salesman problem
514
      and vehicle routing problem. These findings underscore significant gaps in LLMs’ capabilities, par-
      ticularly in handling specialized and highly complex OR problems. The observed biases suggest
515
      that while LLMs are proficient in certain well-represented problem classes, their effectiveness di-
516
      minishes in less common or more intricate problem spaces, highlighting areas for future research
517   and training improvement.

521   6   C ONCLUSION
522
523
524   In this work, we introduced OptiBench, a novel benchmark to evaluate the ability of LLMs in
525   optimization modeling tasks. OptiBench uniquely incorporates multi-dimensional complexity in a
526   model-data-separated manner, allowing a more structured and flexible evaluation process. To facil-
527   itate a comprehensive assessment of LLMs’ optimization capabilities, we formalized an evaluation
528   paradigm based on equivalence detection, ensuring accurate and meaningful comparisons between
529   models. We also theoretically proved the efficiency of our proposed method. By benchmarking
530   over OptiBench, GPT-4o demonstrated superior performance in the direct prompting setting, out-
531
      performing all other LLMs and agents. In contrast, the latest model, o1-preview, and the existing
      modeling agent surprisingly underperformed compared to GPT-4o. This underperformance might be
532
      attributed to the snowball effect of hallucination, especially prevalent during longer reasoning paths
533
      when tackling complex tasks. Our results suggest that while current LLMs possess a foundational
534   capability in optimization modeling, there remains significant room for improvement. We plan to
535   develop a specialized modeling agent to address these gaps, incorporating a curated reasoning skele-
536   ton tailored specifically for optimization and operational research. In addition, we intend to extend
537   our hierarchical reverse data evolution method to create fine-tuning datasets for optimization tasks
538   and broader logical reasoning tasks. Through these efforts, we aim to push the boundaries of LLMs’
539   operational research and optimization modeling capabilities, ultimately fostering advancements in
      AI research and practical applications.
