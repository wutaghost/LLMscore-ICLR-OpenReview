012
013                Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) is widely used in machine learning research.
014                In previous research, the convergence analyses of SGD under vanishing step-size
015                settings typically assumed that the step sizes satisfied the Robbins-Monro condi-
016
                   tions, which is to say, the sum of the step sizes was infinite, while the sum of
                   the squares of the step sizes was finite. In practical applications, a wider variety
017
                   of step sizes is often used, but these may not meet the Robbins-Monro step-size
018
                   conditions, thus lacking theoretical guarantees of convergence. To bridge the gap
019                between theory and practical application, this paper introduces a novel analyti-
020                cal methodвҖ”the stopping time method based on probability theoryвҖ”to explore
021                the asymptotic convergence of SGD under more relaxed step-size conditions. In
022                the non-convex setting, we prove that the almost sure convergence of the se-
                                                                                     P+вҲһ
023                quence of iterates generated by SGD when step sizes satisfy t=1 Пөt = +вҲһ
                        P+вҲһ
024                and t=1 Пөpt < +вҲһ for some p > 2. Compared to previous works, our analysis
025                eliminates the need to assume global Lipschitz continuity of the loss function, and
026                it also relaxes the requirement of global boundedness of the high-order moments
027                of the stochastic gradient to local boundedness. Additionally, we prove L2 con-
028                vergence without the need for assuming global boundedness of loss functions or
029                their gradients. The assumptions required for this work are the weakest among
030                studies with the same conclusions, thereby extending the applicability of SGD in
031
                   various practical scenarios where traditional assumptions may not hold.

      Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), originally presented in the seminal work Robbins & Monro
036

037   learning and deep learning, due to its simplicity and remarkable efficiency in handling large-scale
038   datasets (LeCun et al. (2002); Hinton (2012); Ruder (2016)). Given these attributes, conducting
039   theoretical research, including convergence analyses, on the SGD is important, as it enables more
040   effective application. This includes understanding the optimal conditions for its use, how to adjust
041   parameters to achieve the best results (Jin et al. (2022)), and determining when it outperforms other
042   optimization methods (Zhou et al. (2020)).

      In this article, we employ a novel analytical method, called stopping time method, to explore
