009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The success of large language models (LLMs) has fostered a new research trend of
012            multi-modality large language models (MLLMs), which changes the paradigm of
013            various fields in computer vision. Though MLLMs have shown promising results
014            in numerous vision-language tasks such as VQA and text-to-image, no work has
015            demonstrated how low-level vision tasks can benefit from MLLMs. We find that
016            most current MLLMs are blind to low-level features due to their design of vision
017            modules, and thus are inherently incapable of solving low-level vision tasks. In
018
               this work, we propose LM4LV, a framework that enables a FROZEN LLM to
               solve a range of low-level vision tasks without any multi-modal data or prior. This
019
               showcases the LLM’s strong potential in low-level vision and bridges the gap
020
               between MLLMs and low-level vision tasks. We hope that this work can inspire
021            new perspectives on LLMs and a deeper understanding of their mechanisms.
022

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The great success and generalizability of Large Language Models (LLMs) have brought a new research
027   trend of Multi-modality Large Language Models (MLLMs). We wonder how much LLMs can benefit
028   computer vision to achieve better performance and realize real intelligence. Recent attempts on
029   MLLMs have demonstrated promising results on high-level vision tasks, such as image captioning
030   and visual question answering (VQA). Then we are curious about its capability on low-level vision
031   tasks, like image denoising and deraining. On the other hand, since existing works (Merullo et al.,
032   2023; Eichenberg et al., 2022) have proved LLMs can already understand semantic image features,
033
      how far are they from directly generating images as a generative model? All of these converge to the
      same question: is it possible to utilize MLLMs to accept, process, and output low-level features? This
034
      is important to further push the limit of MLLMs and low-level vision. We will make a preliminary
035
      exploration in this work.
036
037   Existing literature shows a significant gap between MLLMs and low-level vision tasks. The current
038   major direction for many MLLM related works is towards a better semantic fusion of the text and
039
      image modality. Following this trend, some works (You et al., 2024; QFu) purpose to use LLMs
      for evaluating low-level attributes such as lightness and sharpness. However, most low-level vision
040
      tasks process and generate pixel-level information, which does not correspond with meaningful
041
      words. Moreover, the output images must have high fidelity and consistency with the original images,
042   which is a common flaw of current MLLMs. Though many works (Dong et al., 2024; Pan et al.,
043   2024; Wu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024a;b; Ge et al., 2024; 2023) put effort in
044   empowering MLLMs with image generation capability, most of these MLLMs lack detailed control
045   of the image. In Sec. 3.1, we show that most MLLMs with image generation capabilities fail to
046   perform simple image reconstruction. This indicates that these MLLMs are inherently not capable of
047   handling low-level vision tasks, as they lack the ability to process low-level details.
048   Bridging the gap between MLLMs and low-level vision tasks is important for both fields. MLLMs
049   have shifted the paradigm in many fields of computer vision by unifying vision tasks into a general
050   conversational manner. However, low-level vision tasks have not yet benefited significantly from the
051   changes brought by MLLMs. Currently, most low-level vision modules (Dong et al., 2024; Wang
052   et al., 2021a; Lin et al., 2024) simply offer an image-to-image mapping without text interventions.
053   Bridging this gap could allow us to leverage the strong reasoning and text generation abilities of
      MLLMs for low-level vision tasks, providing better user interaction and greater interpretability in

      solving low-level vision tasks. On the other hand, low-level features are important components of
055   images, but are often overlooked and discarded in current MLLMs. Enabling MLLMs to process
056   low-level features can lead to a more fine-grained understanding of images and better control in the
057   process of image generation.
058
      Moreover, the majority of MLLMs are trained on a massive corpus of multimodal data, with an
059
      existing LLM as initialization. However, it is underinvestigated what the LLM initialization brings to
060
      the MLLM. Does the LLM simply offer strong capabilities for text, or does it also provide underlying
061   abilities for other modalities as well? Therefore, we highlight the importance of investigating LLMs’
062   capability to process visual features with no multi-modal data or prior, which can lead to a deeper
063   understanding of LLMs’ inner mechanisms. Although a series of works (Liu et al., 2023a; Merullo
064   et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023a; Pang et al., 2024) have put efforts into investigating the visual feature
065   processing ability of a frozen LLM, none of them have succeeded in enabling the LLM to produce
066   visual features without multi-modal supervision. LQAE (Liu et al., 2023a) managed to perform image-
067   to-text tasks in an in-context learning (ICL) manner without any multi-modal data by quantizing
068   images into text tokens. However, it fails even for very basic low-level vision tasks such as deblurring,
069
      demonstrating its inability for conditional image generation.
070   In this work, we make the first attempt to investigate a frozen LLM’s capability in processing low-
071   level feature, proving that a frozen LLM can accept, process and auto-regressively output visual
072   features without any multi-modal data or prior. By simply training two linear layers with vision-only
073   data, a frozen LLM showcases non-trivial capability on a wide range of low-level vision tasks.

075   2     R ELATED W ORKS
076
077   2.1   M ULTI - MODAL G ENERATION WITH LLM S
078
079   Many attempts have been made to equip LLM with multi-modal generation ability. We categorize
080   these attempts into two types: those that require an additional text-to-image module and those that do
081
      not. The vast majority of research falls into the former category, such as DreamLLM (Dong et al.,
      2024) and KosMOS-G (Pan et al., 2024), which necessitate the integration of an external text-to-image
082
      module pre-trained extensively on multi-modal documents, such as Stable Diffusion (Esser et al.,
083
      2024). In these instances, the LLM backbone is responsible for producing textual vectors that serve
084   as the text condition in the text-to-image module, while the actual conversion from text to image is
085   carried out by a powerful external module. A significant downside of using overly powerful external
086   text-to-image modules is their inability to facilitate precise image control, rendering tasks such as
087   image segmentation and image restoration infeasible. To enhance the precision of image control,
088   some studies purpose to add skip-connections from the encoder. PixelLLM (Ren et al., 2023) employs
089   a lightweight, pixel-level decoder with skip-connection from encoder to perform language-interactive
090   image segmentation tasks. SEED-X (Ge et al., 2024) applies a similar approach by skip-connecting
091   the original image to the decoder as a reference for conditional image generation. Though those
092
      approaches have shown more precise image control, the skip-connection raises questions about the
      role of LLM in the process.
093
094   For the latter category, a common architecture is to use a VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021) to patch images
095   into tokens. Therefore, the training objective can be unified as next-token prediction. A number of
096   works (Lu et al., 2023; Team, 2024; Yu et al., 2023b; Jin et al., 2024) have shown the success of this
097   approach. However, all those model train the whole backbone (either from scratch or from a existing
098
      LLM) on massive multi-modal data. As a result, the final backbone largely differs from a LLM,
      failing to provide a clear understanding of the capability of a LLM in processing visual features.
099
      Therefore, we will not discuss MLLMs of this type in this work. From this point forward, we will
100
      refer MLLM as the first type of MLLM.

107   For example, LimBER (Merullo et al., 2023) shows that training a simple linear layer between a
      unsupervised trained vision encoder and a frozen LLM on multi-modal data empowers the LLM with

138   3.1   C URRENT MLLM S ARE BLIND TO L OW- LEVEL F EATURES
139
140   Our inspiration stems from an observation: current MLLMs (Dong et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2024; Wu
141   et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024a;b; Ge et al., 2024; 2023) with image generation ability
142   are blind to low-level visual features. Therefore, they are inherently incapable of handling low-level
143   vision tasks. We attribute the blindness of MLLMs to their vision modules. We have observed that
144
      most MLLMs’ vision modules could not perform image reconstruction, indicating that the vision
      encoders in the vision modules are performing a lossy encoding process. As shown in Fig. 1, the
145
      vision module in MLLMs often tend to capture high-level semantics but fail to maintain low-level
146
      details, conducting a lossy compression of the image. More discussion can be found in Appendix A.1.
147
148   Currently, most MLLMs follow this lossy compression approach, often relying on large-scale multi-
149   modal training to train their vision modules. For example, Emu2 (Sun et al., 2024a) uses 162M
150
      image-text pairs to train a vision encoder along with the LLM backbone and utilizes a pre-trained
      text-to-image diffusion model (Podell et al., 2023) as the decoder. The advantage of this approach
151
      is evident: extensive multi-modal training results in better alignment between vision and text. A
152
      better alignment often brings an improved interaction and fusion of modalities, which is important
153   for an MLLM. This also allows these MLLMs to use pre-trained LLMs as the backbone initialization
154   for further training. For instance, SEED (Ge et al., 2023) empowers a LLM with image generation
155   ability by fine-tuning the LLM using LoRA. However, the downside of this alignment is that the
156   visual features often lose much of the original image information, failing to maintain low-level details.
157   Therefore, we argue that these MLLMs inherently lack the ability to handle low-level tasks.

223   3.3   N EXT E LEMENT P REDICTION ON L OW- LEVEL V ISION TASKS
224
225   Similar to Emu and Emu2 (Sun et al., 2024b;a), we apply a next element prediction strategy to enable
226
      LLMs to accept visual features and output visual features in an auto-regressive manner. Our main
      network structure is illustrated in Fig. 2.
227
228   Adapter Modules. As we want to investigate the LLM’s capability to process low-level vision
229   features, we need to constrain the adapter’s complexity. Therefore, we use two simple linear layers as
230   the adapter modules between the LLM and the vision encoder/decoder to align the feature dimension.
231   We call the visual features "visual tokens" if they are aligned with the LLM’s dimension.
232   Training & Generation Scheme. In training, we apply the standard next-token cross-entropy loss for
233   text tokens. For continuous visual tokens, we apply a next token l2 -regression loss. This unifies the
234   training process for both text and visual as next-element prediction tasks. For generation, we use the
235   auto-regressive generation scheme, generating one text or visual token at a time. To switch between
236   generating visual and text tokens, we set that text tokens are generated by default, and visual features
237   are delineated by markers <Img> and </Img> before and after, respectively. Generation of a visual
238
      feature occurs only after the LLM has generated <Img>. After generating a sequence of visual
      tokens, the LLM returns to generating text tokens. The visual token sequence is then transformed to
239
      visual features through the linear adapter and decoded into an image by the visual decoder.
240
241   Under this paradigm, we define low-level vision tasks as conditional image generation tasks performed
242   under visual instructions. For a pair consisting of a low-quality image and a high-quality image, we
243   convert this into a conversation format:
244
245
246     Human:       <Img><LQ-image></Img> Assistant:                     <Img><HQ-image></Img>
247
248
249   Here <LQ-image> and <HQ-image> represent the visual feature sequences (after linear projec-
250
      tion) for low and high quality images respectively. We apply an instruction-tuning strategy, similar
      to (Liu et al., 2023b), only calculating the loss for the desired output <Img><HQ-image></Img>.
251
252   However, the current design lacks a description of the target low-level vision tasks. This may lead to
253   the inclusion of task-related soft prompts within the trained adapter projection, which is not desired
254   as we expect the adapter module to purely focus on the translation between image and text space.
255   Since low-level vision tasks are challenging to be described precisely using language, we incorporate
256
      a trainable task token sequence <task> within the instructions. This serves as a soft prompt to guide
      the LLM in performing specific low-level vision tasks. The final data format is as follows:
257
258
259
260   Human:      <Img><LQ-image></Img> <task> Assistant:                         <Img><HQ-image></Img>
261
262
      In this format, normal text tokens (marked in black) is tokenized normally. The visual tokens (marked
263
      in blue) are encoded by a vision encoder and a linear layer. The task token (marked in red) is a
264
      trainable embedding sequence that is directly inserted into the input sequence. Within the entire
265   pipeline, the only trainable parameters are the two linear adaptation modules and the task token
266   sequence, which are jointly optimized during training.

273   4.1     E XPERIMENT S ETUP
274
275   Base Models & Hyperparameters. We use LLaMA2-7B instruct2 (Touvron et al., 2023) as the
276   base LLM for all our experiments. For vision modules, we use MAE-Large3 (He et al., 2021) and
277
      fine-tune the decoder as mentioned in Sec. 3.2. For adaptation modules, we only use linear layers as
      an affine transformation. We use a trainable task token sequence of length 10 by default.
278
279   Training Details. We use LLAVA595k4 (Liu et al., 2023b) dataset as our base dataset for degradation
280   generation without data augmentation. All images are resized to 224 × 224 to fit the input size of
281   MAE. We use an actual batchsize of 256. By default we train the model for 2 epochs as we observe
282   convergence after 2 epochs. We use AdamW as the optimizer, with β = (0.9, 0.95), lr = 3 × 10−4
283
      and no weight decay. We use warm-up decay strategy with 200 warm-up steps. All experiments are
      done on a maximum of 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. A single training takes around 8 hours.
284
285   Evaluation tasks. We evaluate our method on several representative low-level vision tasks: denois-
286   ing (Wang et al., 2021b; Zhang et al., 2017a;b), deblurring (Abuolaim & Brown, 2020; Chen et al.,
287   2021b), pepper noise removal (Fu et al., 2019; Gebreyohannes & Kim, 2012), deraining (Chen et al.,
288   2021a; Liu et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2017) and mask removal (Liu et al., 2023c; Wang et al., 2018).
289
      We use the NoCaps dataset5 as the test set and add the same degradations as training. We use PSNR
      and SSIM as our default metrics. Additionally, SPAE (Yu et al., 2023a) shows that a simply rotation
290
      could be challenging under a similar setup. Thus we set two simple tasks that requires large spatial
291
      operation: image rotation and image flipping. We name the tasks as restoration tasks and spatial
292   operation tasks respectively.
293
294   Degradation details. For denoising, We add Gaussian noise with zero mean and a random standard
295
      variance uniformly sampled from [0, 50/255]. For deblurring, we add Gaussian blur to the images
      with a window size uniformly sampled from {1, 3, 5, 7}. For deraining, we add rains at random
296
      angles and positions to the images with an amount uniformly sampled from [0, 20]. For pepper noise
297
      removal, we set the portion of peppers in the degraded images to be uniformly sampled from [0, 0.1].
298   For mask removal, we use a mask size of 4 and a masking rate of 0.1.
299
300   Baselines. As our goal is to investigate whether LLM has the ability to process low-level features
301
      and handle low-level vision tasks, we first need to discard the potential effect of MAE on these tasks.
      To quantify the effect of MAE, we set a baseline to discard LLM in LM4LV but keep MAE for
302
      image reconstruction, named MAE-r. For image restoration tasks, MAE-r is the reconstruction of
303
      the degraded images. Thus the relative performance (∆PSNR/SSIM) of LM4LV and MAE-r is the gain
304   brought by the frozen LLM. For image rotation/flipping, MAE-r is the reconstruction of already
305   rotated/flipped images, thus serving as an upper bound for LM4LV.

      4.3         C HOICE OF V ISION M ODULE M ATTERS
361
362
363   The key component in our method is the visual module. While we have successfully showed LLM’s
364   low-level visual feature processing ability using a fine-tuned MAE, it prompts an investigation into
365   how different visual modules might affect the outcomes.
366   There are actually not many choices of vision modules for unsupervised image reconstruction. A
367   common choice is VQ-GAN, a state-of-the-art representative of the VAE family. Another less
368   common choice is BEiT (Bao et al., 2022), which is originally used for image recognition. The
369   training goal of BEiT involves predicting the masked tokens of the DALL-E tokenizer7 (Ramesh
370   et al., 2021), which can be decoded into images by the DALL-E’s decoder. In practice, we find
371   that BEiT’s predictions are accurate enough to roughly reconstruct images even without fine-tuning,
372   potentially due to a lower masking rate during training compared to MAE.

      5.2   D OES T EXT P RE - TRAINING P LAY AN I MPORTANT ROLE ?
467
468   Some studies (Cao & Wu, 2021; Amid et al., 2022) have found that a randomly initialized CNN
469   module can already serve as an effective representation extractor, suggesting that the architecture of
470   the model itself possesses the ability to solve various tasks. Thus, a natural question arises: Does
471   text pre-training endow the LLM with the ability to solve low-level vision tasks, or is the capability
472   inherent to the LLM’s architecture itself? Previous study (Amid et al., 2022) requires numerous
473   random initializations are required to define kernels for classification purposes, yet the integration of
474
      multiple random initializations with auto-regressive generation on an LLM has not been discussed.
      Therefore, we only initialized the LLM randomly once and maintained the architecture unchanged,
475
      testing its denoising performance under the setup in 4.1. Visualizations from Fig. 7 demonstrate that
476
      the randomly initialized LLM fails to generate meaningful images, indicating that text pre-training is
477   crucial for the LLM to solve low-level vision tasks.

498   BEiT/VQGAN does not? We try to give a possible explanation based on the platonic hypothesis.
499

520   7   D ISCUSSION & L IMITATIONS
521
522   Discussion. Our work could lead to some interesting topic beyond enabling LLMs for low-level vision
523   tasks. As stated, the goal for this work is not to achieve the best performance in image restoration,
524   but to demonstrate the potential of LLMs in processing low-level features and show a possible way to
525   leverage LLMs’ strong reasoning and interaction ability to low-level vision tasks. Also, as LM4LV
526   does not involve any multi-modal data, this framework could be extended beyond vision to the fields
527   where cross-modal data is scarce by replacing MAE with a self-supervised field-specific module.
528   Limitations. As shown in Fig. 3, LM4LV could not restore high-frequency details in degraded
529   images. This is natural because the LLM does not have image prior, which could be improved
530   by adding skip-connection or multi-modal data. But this is not the focus of this work. Also, the
531   performance gap observed between our method and a one-layer Transformer also indicates that there
532   is room for improvement in our approach.
533
534
535   8   C ONCLUSION
536
537   In this work, we aim to answer the question: Does a frozen LLM has the ability to accept, process,
538   and output low-level features? By designing a framework from bottom to top, we give a positive
539   answer, showing LLMs’ non-trivial performance on various low-level tasks. We hope this work can
      inspire new perspectives on the capabilities of LLMs and deeper understanding of their mechanisms.

1048

       In this section we give analysis on the computation cost of LM4LV. All experimental setups follow the
       descriptions in 4.1. Since all image restoration tasks utilize the same image tokens and computation
1072
       pipeline, differing only in the input data, we do not differentiate the computational costs for each
1073
       individual task. The majority of floating-point operations per second (FLOPs) are consumed by the
1074   large language model (LLM), primarily determined by the number of tokens involved. Under our
1075   configuration, a single image corresponds to 196 tokens. Additionally, we introduce 10 soft task-
1076   specific tokens and 8 tokens for textual context, resulting in a total of 214 tokens for the pre-filling
1077   stage. Following this, the model generates another 196 tokens during the decoding process. As the
1078   actual LLM FLOPs is very sensitive to the implementation, here we make a simple assumption that
1079   KV-Cache is used and no other serving tricks are implemented. As attention is mainly bounded by
       memeory access, and it’s FLOPs is relatively neglegible to other part of LLM, we exclude it for


                                                              20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       simplicity. As the actual LLM FLOPs are highly sensitive to the implementation, we make a simple
1081   assumption here that KV-Cache is used and no other serving tricks are implemented. Since attention
1082   is primarily bounded by memory access and its FLOPs are relatively negligible compared to other
1083   parts of the LLM, we exclude it for simplicity. The total FLOPs of the LLM can then be estimated
1084   by 2 × s × P , where s is the total sequence length (pre-filled + generation), and P is the number of
1085   LLM parameters. The FLOPs for MAE and linear adapters are straightforward to measure.
1086
       In total, a single generation costs 5.74 TFlops on the LLM, 25.56 GFlops on the MAE, and less than 1
1087   GFlop on the linear adapters. The total computation amounts to 5.77 TFlops for restoring one image.
1088   We note that while the computational cost of the LLM is significant, it is an unavoidable trade-off
1089   when leveraging LLMs for low-level vision tasks. The goal of this work is not to claim efficiency, but
1090   to explore the possibility of the integration of LLMs into classic low-level restoration tasks.
