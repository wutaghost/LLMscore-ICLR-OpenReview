                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010             Recent advancements in operator learning are transforming the landscape of com-
011
                putational physics and engineering, especially alongside the rapidly evolving field
                of physics-informed machine learning. The convergence of these areas offers
012
                exciting opportunities for innovative research and applications. However, merging
013
                these two realms often demands deep expertise and explicit knowledge of physi-
014             cal systems, which may be challenging or even impractical in relatively complex
015             applications. To address this limitation, we propose a novel framework: Pseudo
016             Physics-Informed Neural Operator (PPI-NO). In this framework, we construct a
017             surrogate physics system for the target system using partial differential equations
018             (PDEs) derived from simple, rudimentary physics knowledge, such as basic dif-
019             ferential operators. We then couple the surrogate system with the neural operator
020             model, utilizing an alternating update and learning process to iteratively enhance
021             the model’s predictive power. While the physics derived via PPI-NO may not mir-
022
                ror the ground-truth underlying physical laws — hence the term “pseudo physics”
                — this approach significantly enhances the accuracy of current operator learning
023
                models, particularly in data scarce scenarios. Through extensive evaluations across
024
                five benchmark operator learning tasks and an application in fatigue modeling,
025             PPI-NO consistently outperforms competing methods by a significant margin. The
026             success of PPI-NO may introduce a new paradigm in physics-informed machine
027             learning, one that requires minimal physics knowledge and opens the door to
028             broader applications in data-driven physics learning and simulations.

031   1     Introduction
032
033
      Operator learning, a dynamic and rapidly evolving domain, has seen remarkable advancements with
      the advent of neural operators. Rooted in the express power of neural networks, neural operators
034
      have transformed computational problem-solving methods. Prominent examples include Fourier
035
      Neural Operators (FNO) (Li et al., 2020c), Deep Operator Net (DONet) (Lu et al., 2021) and
036   other frameworks such as (Cao, 2021; Hao et al., 2023). FNO employs Fourier transform for global
037   convolution and function transformation, while DONet introduces two sub-networks — the branch net
038   and trunk net — to extract representations from the functional space and query locations, respectively,
039   enabling predictions akin to attention mechanisms (Vaswani et al., 2017).
040
      For trading for model capacity and performance, neural operators often require a substantial amount
041
      of training data to perform optimally. This demand poses significant challenges, particularly in
042   complex problems, where training data can be scarce and costly to acquire. In response, the field of
043   physics-informed machine learning, including physics-informed neural networks (PINN) (Raissi et al.,
044   2019), has shown promise by incorporating physical laws as soft constraints during training. This
045   approach serves as a regularization technique, effectively embedding a fundamental understanding
046   of physics into the model to lessen its reliance on extensive training data. Building on this idea, the
047   concept of physics-informed neural operators (PINO) has emerged, with PINO integrating physical
048   laws as soft constraints to enhance model fidelity while reducing data quantity. This approach has
049   been used in (Wang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021) for DONet and FNO training.
050   Despite the success of PINO, the necessity for a thorough understanding of the underlying physics
051   can pose a significant hurdle, especially in complex applications such as in fracture mechanics and
052   climate modeling. In those scenarios, the detailed physical knowledge is often unavailable or difficult
053   to identify, and it is often prohibitively expensive to collect extensive data. These challenges can
      render the current methods unavailable or impractical.

054
      To navigate these challenges while retaining the benefits of physics-informed learning, our work
055   introduces the Pseudo Physics-Informed Neural Operator (PPI-NO). This framework bypasses
056   the need for exhaustive physical comprehension by constructing a neural-network-based partial
057   differential equation (PDE) that characterizes the target system directly from data. The neural PDE is
058   then coupled with the neural operator for alternating updates and training, enabling iterative extraction,
059   refinement and integration of physics knowledge to enhance operator learning. The contribution of
060   this work lies in the following three aspects:
061
062
            1. To our knowledge, PPI-NO is the first work to enhance standard operator learning pipeline
               using physics directly learned from sparse data, delivering superior accuracy without the
063
               need for in-depth physical understanding or extensive data collection.
064
065         2. The success of PPI-NO also opens up a new paradigm of physics-informed machine learning
066
               where only rudimentary physics assumptions (in this case, the basic differential operations)
               are required rather than in-depth or rigorous expert knowledge, extending the spectrum of
067
               the physics-informed learning for experts of different levels.
068
069
            3. The effectiveness of PPI-NO is validated through extensive evaluations on five commonly
               used benchmark operator learning tasks in literature (Li et al., 2020c; Lu et al., 2022),
070
               including Darcy flow, nonlinear diffusion, Eikonal, Poisson and advection equations, as well
071
               as one application in fatigue modeling in fracture mechanics, where the ground-truth holistic
072            PDE system is unknown.

      2   Background
075
076   Problem Formulation. Operator learning seeks to approximate an operator that maps input parame-
077   ters and/or functions to corresponding output functions. In most practical cases, operator learning
078   rises in the context of solving partial differential equations (PDEs), where the operator corresponds
079   to the solution operator of the PDE. Assume a PDE system:
080
                                       N [u](x) = f (x),       x ∈ Ω × [0, ∞),                              (1)
081
082   where x is a compact notation for the spatial and temporal coordinates, Ω is the spatial domain, [0, ∞)
083   is the temporal domain, N is a nonlinear differential operator, u(x) is the solution function, and f (x)
084
      is the source term. Solving the PDE system is to find the solution function u(x) that satisfies the PDE
      system equation (1) as well as the initial and boundary conditions. This task often necessitates the
085
      use of computationally expensive numerical solvers such as finite element method (FEM) or finite
086
      difference method (FDM). To alleviate the computational challenge, we aim to learn the solution
087   operator of the PDE system, ψ : F → U using a training dataset D = {(fn , un )}N   n=1 , which consists
088   of discretized functions u(·) and f (·) at a set of collocations points. Once the operator model is
089   trained, it can be used to directly predict the solution function u for new instances of the input f ,
090   offering a much more efficient alternative to running numerical solvers from scratch. However, the
091   training dataset still needs to be generated offline using numerical solvers.
092
      Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2020c) represents a significant leap in neural network
093   architecture for operator learning, especially in solving PDEs. For a given discretized input function f ,
094   FNO first employs a feed-forward network (FFN) on each component of f at its respective sampling
095   location, thereby lifting the input into a higher-dimensional channel space. The core of FNO is the
096   Fourier layer, which performs a linear transformation     followed
                                                                        by a nonlinear activation within the
      functional space, h(x) ← σ Wh(x) + κ(x − x′ )h(x′ )dx′ , where h(x) is the input to the Fourier
                                                R
097
098   layer, κ(·) the integration kernel, and σ(·) the activation function.
                                                                         R The convolution operation in this
099   context is efficiently computed using the convolution theorem: κ(x − x′ )h(x′ )dx′ = F −1 [F[κ] ·
100   F[h]](x), where F and F −1 denote the Fourier and inverse Fourier transforms, respectively. The
101   Fourier layer’s efficiency stems from performing the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) on h, multiplying
102   it with the discretized kernel in the frequency domain, and then applying the inverse FFT. The
103   local linear transformation, Wh(x), is executed through conventional convolution operations. After
104
      multiple Fourier layers, the final output is obtained by the channel-wise application of another FFN,
      projecting the representation back to the original space.
105
106   Deep Operator Network (DONet) (Lu et al., 2021) is another prominent work in operator learning.
107   The architecture of a DONet is structured into two primary components: the branch network and the
      trunk network, learning representations for the input functions and querying locations, respectively.

      Consider an input function f (x) ∈ F evaluated at m sensor locations {x1 , x2 , · · · , xm } and an
109   output function u ∈ U. The branch network receives the values [f (x1 ), f (x2 ), · · · , f (xm )] and
110   outputs a feature representation [b1 , b2 , · · · , bp ]⊤ ∈ Rp . Concurrently, the trunk network processes a
111   querying location x and outputs another feature vector [t1 , t2 , · · · , tp ]⊤ ∈ Rp . The approximation of
112   the output function u(x) is computed asP       a sum of products of the corresponding elements from the
                                                        p
113   branch and trunk networks, G[f ](x) ≈ k=1 bk tk , where G is the learned operator mapping input
114   function f to the corresponding output function u.
115
      Physics-Informed Neural Operator (PINO) (Wang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021) has recently emerged
116   as a promising approach to address the data scarcity issue in operator learning. PINO embeds physical
117   laws — typically governing equations — into the learning process. The incorporation of physical
118   principles not only enhances the model’s adherence to ground-truth phenomena but also reduces its
119   dependency on extensive training data. Mathematically, the integration of physics into the learning
120   process can be viewed as an additional regularization term in the loss function. Let Ldata represent the
121   standard data-fitting loss term (e.g., the mean squared error between the predicted and actual outputs),
122   the physics-informed term Lphysics can be the residual of the governing PDEs evaluated at the neural
123   network’s outputs. The total loss function L for a PINO model is then expressed as
124                                             L = Ldata + λLphysics ,
125
      where λ is a weighting factor that balances the importance of data-fitting versus physics compliance.
126
      This approach encourages the model to learn solutions that are not only consistent with the provided
127
      data but also physically plausible.

      3     Methodology
130
131   In the absence of the underlying physics knowledge (i.e., the PDE system (1) is not available), it is
132   impossible to construct the physics loss term in the PINO framework. To address this challenge, we
133   propose a “pseudo” physics-informed operator learning framework motivated by the need to uncover
134   the underlying physical laws using available data. This approach is particularly useful in relatively
135   more complex applications, where data is often costly or sparse while the underlying physics is hard
136   to fully understand. Our model architecture is depicted in Figure 1.
137
      3.1   Pseudo Physics System Learning
138
139
      As the first step, we propose a novel approach to learn the physics system using scarce training data.
140   Our key observation is that, although the mapping from f to u can be intricate and may necessitate
141   information across the entire domain (in theory, u is an integration of the Green’s function multiplied
142   with f over the domain), the underlying PDE system (1) simplifies to a local combination of u and its
143   derivatives. We therefore use a neural network ϕ to approximate the general form of N ,
144
                                 N [u](x) ≈ ϕ (x, u(x), S1 (u)(x), . . . , SQ (u)(x)) ,                        (2)
145
146   where {Sj }Q  j=1 are Q derivative operators that we believe should be present in the system, such as
147   ∂t u, ∂tt u, ∂x1 u, ∂x2 u, ∂x1 x1 u, ∂x1 x2 u, ∂x2 x2 u, and more.
148
      The inherent local combination nature of the PDE representation decouples the values of u and its
149
      derivatives across various sampling locations, thereby significantly increasing the number of available
150   training data points. For instance, consider sampling the input function f and output function u on a
151   128 × 128 grid. A single pair of discretized input and output functions, denoted as (f , u), is typically
152   insufficient for a neural operator to effectively learn the mapping f → u. However, this sample can
153   be decomposed into 128 × 128 = 16, 384 training data points across various (spatial and temporal)
154   locations to train ϕ as outlined in (2). Hence, even with a small number of (f , u) pairs for operator
155   learning, the learning of the PDE system N via our formulation in (2) can still achieve accuracy,
156   thanks to the much greater number of training data points that can be derived from these pairs.
157   We use an L2 loss to estimate the parameters of ϕ, which is defined as

                           n=1      j=1
160
161   where fn (·) and un (·) are the input and output functions in n-th training example, and {x1 , . . . xM }
      are the locations at which we discretize fn and un .

192
      Our method can be easily adapted to scenarios where the input and output functions are irregularly
193   sampled, and numerical differentiation is no longer applicable. In such cases, we can employ smooth
194   function estimators, such as kernel interpolation (Long et al., 2024) or Bayesian B-splines (Sun et al.,
195   2022), to estimate the gradient information from data. These gradient estimations are then fed into
196   our PDE neural network ϕ for further learning.

198   3.2   Coupling Neural Operator with Pseudo Physics
199
200
      Next, we leverage the pseudo physics laws embedded in the learned mapping ϕ : u → f to enhance
      the neural operator learning process. Specifically, we use ϕ to reconstruct f from the u predicted
201
      by the neural operator. In this way, our approach goes beyond relying solely on the training data; it
202
      uses the physics learned in the previous step to incorporate a reconstruction error into optimizing the
203   neural operator parameters.
204
205   Initially, we train the neural operator ψ : f → u using the available training data, creating a
206
      preliminary model. This model is developed using FNO or DONet or other neural operators. The
      focus is to first establish a basic understanding of the relationship between f and u from the limited
207
      data. Next, the loss function for ψ is augmented using the physics laws learned in the first step,
208
                                 XN
209                         L=            L2 (ψ(fn ), un ) + λ · Ep(f ′ ) [L2 (f ′ , ϕ(ψ(f ′ )))] ,      (4)
                                    n=1
210
211   where the first term is the L2 loss for data fitting (as in the standard neural operator training), and the
212
      second term is the expected reconstruction error for the input function. The second term incorporates
      the physics laws embedded in ϕ(·), and λ is a weight factor that balances the training data loss against
213
      the reconstruction error.
214
215   In practice, the expected reconstruction error does not have a closed form. One can sample a
      collection of f ′ from the underlying distribution of the input function p(·), e.g., a Gauss random field

216
      or Gaussian process, and then employ a Monte-Carlo approximation,
217
218                           XN                              1 XN ′
219                     L=             L2 (ψ(fn ), un ) + λ          L2 (fn′ , ϕ(ψ(fn′ ))),               (5)
                                 n=1                          N′ n=1
220
221   where N ′ is the number of input function samples.
222
223   To enhance the operator learning process, the model is iteratively refined. In each iteration, we first
224
      fine-tune the neural operator ψ with the pseudo physics ϕ fixed, and then fix ψ, fine-tune ϕ to refine
      the physics representation. This fine-tuning loop is carried out for multiple iterations, allowing for
225
      continuous improvement of the neural operator based on the refined physics.
226
227   This methodology mirrors human experts’ approach to physics system modeling, where sparse data
228   is used to learn the physics laws inspired by simple differential operation (up to the 2nd order to
229   imitate human experts), and then these laws are utilized to generalize the system for data generation.
230
      The reconstruction loss term augments the operator learning with additional information, leading to
      potential improvement upon only training with sparse data.

233   4   Related Work
234
235   Neural operator learning is expanding rapidly. In addition to FNO (Li et al., 2022) and DONet (Lu
236   et al., 2021), notable works include the Low-rank Neural Operator (LNO) introduced by Li et al.
237   (2020c), employing low-rank structures to approximate the integration. The Graph Neural Operator
238
      (GNO) (Li et al., 2020a) integrates Nystrom approximation with graph neural networks, while
      the Multipole Graph Neural Operator (MGNO) by the same authors (Li et al., 2020b) leverages
239
      multiscale kernel decomposition. Gupta et al. (2021) contributed with multiwavelet transformations
240
      for the operator’s kernel. Lu et al. (2022) proposed POD-DONet to enhance the stability of DONet by
241   replacing the trunk net with POD bases constructed from data. Another DONet variant by Seidman
242   et al. (2022) used an FFN to combine the outputs of the branch net and trunk net for prediction. A line
243   of efforts attempted to build neural operators via transformer architectures, such as (Cao, 2021; Hao
244   et al., 2023) Recently, Kovachki et al. (2023) provided a comprehensive review of neural operators.
245   There are also recent advances in kernel operator learning strategies made by Long et al. (2022)
246   and Batlle et al. (2023).
247   Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019) mark a significant advancement
248   in scientific machine learning. PINNs integrate physical laws directly into the learning process,
249   making them effective for solving differential equations and understanding complex physical systems.
250   This methodology is particularly beneficial in scenarios where data is sparse or expensive to obtain.
251   Pioneering the concept of PINO, Li et al. (2021) introduced a dual-resolution approach that combines
252   low-resolution empirical data with high-resolution PDE constraints. This method achieves precise
253   emulation of solution operators across various PDE classes. In parallel, physics-informed DONet
254   by Wang et al. (2021) incorporate regularization strategies enforcing physical law adherence into
255
      the training of DONets. Zanardi et al. (2023) presented an approach using PINO for simulations
      in non-equilibrium reacting flows. Lee et al. (2023) proposed opPINN, a framework combining
256
      physics-informed neural networks with operator learning for solving the Fokker-Planck-Landau
257
      (FPL) equation. Rosofsky et al. (2023) provided a review of applications of physics-informed neural
258   operators. However, existing methods demand one should know the physics laws beforehand, which
259   might not be feasible in many practical applications or complex systems. Our method offers a simple
260   and effective framework, enabling the extraction of implicit physics laws directly from data, even
261   when the data is sparse. Empirically, these pseudo physics laws have proven to be highly beneficial
262   in enhancing the performance of operator learning, as demonstrated in Section 5.
263
      Our work is also related to the cycle consistence framework (Zhu et al., 2017) for image-to-image
264   translation. A critical difference is that cycle-consistence performs unpaired image-to-image trans-
265   lation, while our method aims for accurate paired translation (mapping). In cycle-consistence, the
266   translation is viewed successfully as long as the translated images follow some target distribution.
267   Hence, cycle-consistence has a much more relaxed objective. Another key difference is that our
268   method aims to improve the learning of a function-to-function mapping with very limited data— that
269   is why we first learn a “pseudo physics” representation. The cycle-consistence relies on adversarial
      training which typically requires a large amount of data to obtain successful learning outcomes.

282   5                    Experiments
283
284   Dataset. We tested on five commonly used benchmark operator learning problems in literature (Li
285
      et al., 2020c; Lu et al., 2022), including Darcy Flow, Nonlinear Diffusion, Eikonal, Poisson and
      Advection. In addition, we examined our method in an application in fatigue modeling. The task
286
      is to predict the stress intensity factor (SIF) for semi-elliptical surface cracks on plates, given three
287
      geometric parameters that characterize the cracks (Merrell et al., 2024); see Appendix Fig. 4. The SIF
288   plays a critical role in modeling crack growth by quantifying the stress state near the tip of a crack,
289   and hence SIF computation and analysis is extremely important in fatigue modeling and fracture
290   mechanics (Anderson and Anderson, 2005). The SIF computation is expensive, because it typically
291   needs to run finite element method (FEM) or extended FEM with very fine meshes (Kuna, 2013). Due
292   to the complex sequence of computational steps involved in SIF calculation, there is no holistic PDE
293   that directly models the relationship between the geometric features and the SIF function. Instead,
294   SIF computation typically relies on numerical methods and the extraction of local stress fields near
295   the crack tip. The details about all the dataset are given in Section A of the Appendix.
296   Method and Settings. We evaluated our method based on two popular NO models, FNO and DONet.
297   For learning the pseudo physics laws via the neural network ϕ — see (2) — we tuned the kernel size
298   from {(3, 3), (5, 5), (7, 7), (9, 9)}. The stride was set to 1 and padding was set to “same” to ensure
299   the output shape does not change. In the subsequent FFN, we chose the number of layers from {3,
300   4, 5, 6}, and the layer width from {16, 32, 64}. We used GeLU activation. For the cases of Darcy
301   Flow, Eikonal and Poisson, we used the following derivatives {∂x1 u, ∂x2 u, ∂x1 x1 u, ∂x2 x2 u, ∂x1 x2 u},
302
      and for the other cases, we used {∂x u, ∂xx u, ∂t u, ∂tt u, ∂xt u}. Since SIF is a 1d function (the input
      is the angle), we used the derivatives {∂x u, ∂xx u}. For FNO, we set the number of modes to 12
303
      and channels to 32 (in the lifted space). We varied the number of Fourier layers from {2, 3, 4}. For
304
      DONet, in all the cases except Darcy Flow, the trunk net and branch net were constructed as FFNs.
305   We varied the number of layers from {2, 3, 4} and the layer width was chosen from {30, 40, 50, 60},
306   with ReLU activation. For the case of Darcy flow, we found that DONet with only feed-forward layers
307   exhibited inferior performance. To address this, we introduced convolution layers into the branch net.
308   We selected the number of convolution layers from {3,5,7}, and employed batch normalization and
309   leaky ReLU after each convolution layer. To incorporate the learned pseudo physics representation
310   into the training of FNO or DONet, we randomly sampled 200 input functions to construct the second
311   loss term in (5). We set the maximum number of iterations to 10 and selected the weight λ from
312   [10−1 , 102 ]. All the models were implemented by PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019), and optimized with
313
      ADAM (Kingma and Ba, 2014). The learning rate was selected from {10−4 , 5 × 10−4 , 10−3 }. The
      number of epochs for training or fine-tuning FNO, DONet and pseudo physics network ϕ was set to
314
      500 to ensure convergence. For each operator learning benchmark, we simulated 100 examples for
315
      testing, and varied the number of training examples from {5, 10, 20, 30}, except for Advection, we
316   ran with {20, 30, 50, 80} training examples. For SIF prediction (which is much more challenging),
317   we experimented with training size from {400, 500, 600}, and employed 100 test examples. We
318   repeated the evaluation for five times, each time we randomly sampled a different training set. We
319   ran experiments on workstations equipped with Nvidia Geforce RTX 4090 and Intel I9 CPU.

      6     Conclusion
533
534   We have presented a Pseudo Physics-Informed Neural Operator (PPI-NO) learning framework. PPI-
535   NO is based on our observation that a PDE system is often characterized by a local combination of
536   the solution and its derivatives. This characteristic enables the derivation of many training points from
537   the function sampling locations, facilitating learning of the PDE systems through a neural network.
538   While the physics delineated by PPI-NO might not precisely reflect true physical phenomena, our
539   findings reveal that this method significantly enhances the efficiency of operator learning, particularly
      with limited data quantity.

      A.1   Darcy Flow
653
654   We first considered a steady-state 2D Darcy Flow equation (Li et al., 2020c),
655
656
                                     −∇ · (a(x)∇u(x)) = f (x) x ∈ (0, 1)2 ,
657                                                   u(x) = 0 x ∈ ∂(0, 1)2 ,                               (6)
658
      where u(x) is the velocity of the flow, a(x) characterizes the conductivity of the media, and f (x) is
659   the source function that can represent flow sources or sinks within the domain. In the experiment, our
660   goal is to predict the solution u given the external source f . To this end, we fixed the conductivity
661   a, which is generated by first sampling a Gauss random field α in the domain and then apply a
662   thresholding rule: a(x) = 4 if α(x) < 0, otherwise a(x) = 12. We then used another Gauss random
663   field to generate samples of f . We followed (Li et al., 2020c) to solve the PDE using a second-order
664   finite difference solver and collected the source and solution at a 128 × 128 grid.
665
666
      A.2   Nonlinear Diffusion PDE
667
      We next considered a nonlinear diffusion PDE,
668
669                          ∂t u(x, t) = 10−2 ∂xx u(x, t) + 10−2 u2 (x, t) + f (x, t),
670                          u(−1, t) = u(1, t) = 0, u(x, 0) = 0,                                           (7)
671
      where (x, t) ∈ [−1, 1] × [0, 1]. Our objective is to predict the solution function u given the source
672
      function f . We used the solver provided in (Lu et al., 2022), and discretized both the input and output
673   functions at a 128 × 128 grid. The source f was sampled from a Gaussian process with an isotropic
674   square exponential (SE) kernel for which the length scale was set to 0.2.
675

835   B     Limitation and Discussion
836
837   Our current method cannot learn PDE representations for which the input function f is the initial
838   condition. In such cases, the mapping from the solution function to the initial condition requires a
839   reversed integration over time, hence we cannot decouple the derivatives. To address this problem,
840   we plan to explicitly model the temporal dependencies in the PDE representation, such as via the
841   neural ODE design (Chen et al., 2018).
