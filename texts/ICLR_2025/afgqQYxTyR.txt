009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Low-rank adaptation (LoRA) is a fine-tuning technique that can be applied to
012            conditional generative diffusion models. LoRA utilizes a small number of context
013            examples to adapt the model to a specific domain, character, style, or concept.
014            However, due to the limited data utilized during training, the fine-tuned model
015            performance is often characterized by strong context bias and a low degree of
016
               variability in the generated images. To solve this issue, we introduce AutoLoRA,
               a novel guidance technique for diffusion models fine-tuned with the LoRA ap-
017
               proach. Inspired by other guidance techniques, AutoLoRA searches for a trade-
018
               off between consistency in the domain represented by LoRA weights and sample
019            diversity from the base conditional diffusion model. Moreover, we show that in-
020            corporating classifier-free guidance for both LoRA fine-tuned and base models
021            leads to generating samples with higher diversity and better quality. The experi-
022            mental results for several fine-tuned LoRA domains show superiority over existing
023            guidance techniques on selected metrics.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      The key aspects of image-generating diffusion models focus on image quality, the variability in the
028
      results, and the production of images tailored to specific conditions (Zhang et al., 2023a). Classifier-
029
      free guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) is a sampling method for generating diverse, good-
030   quality images utilizing a conditional diffusion model. The idea of this approach is to use a combi-
031   nation of constrained and unconstrained diffusion models to find the trade-off between diversity and
032   consistency with the conditioning factor.
033
      In AutoGuidance (Karras et al., 2024), the fully-trained diffusion model is conditioned using a less-
034
      trained version. This approach parallels the technique employed in Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG),
035
      where two models are combined during the sampling process. However, unlike CFG, which uses
036   conditioned and unconditioned models, AutoGuidance leverages different stages of training within a
037   single model. The fully trained model, which tends to overfit to the training data, is counterbalanced
038   by the partially trained model, thereby introducing greater diversity in the generated outputs. This
039   approach is critical for enhancing the variability of generated images, a fundamental requirement
040   for improving generative model performance.
041
      Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) is a fine-tuning technique widely applied in large
042   diffusion models to adjust them to some specific dataset and enforce generating images in particular
043   style, character, or some other specific concepts. However, the adaptation process is usually made
044   using a relatively small number of data examples, which causes a small degree of variability in the
045   output, and the tuned model is biased towards the training examples.
046
      In this paper, we present AutoLoRA, which allows for an increase in the variety of images generated
047
      from LoRA models and a reduction of data bias. AutoLoRA use the general idea of AutoGuidance,
048   which claims that overfit model can be improved by conditioning by the model with a lower quality
049   but greater diversity. AutoLoRA increase LoRA generation variability by conditioning LoRA with a
050   base diffusion model. In practice, we use a model before LoRA tuning to condition the final variant
051   fine-tuned with the LoRA approach. In this work, we focus on diffusion models with additional
052   context inputs, like prompts. For such an approach, we additionally apply classifier-free guidance
053   for both base and fine-tuned versions of the diffusion models. With this approach, we incorporate
      an additional level of regularisation and new degrees of freedom to increase the variety of the details

054
      in the image that are not controlled by context inputs. As a consequence, we observe an increase in
055   the prompt alignment and diversity of generated samples.
056
057   The following constitutes a list of our key contributions. First, we show that the CFG and AutoGu-
058
      idance mechanism can solve a problem with a low variety of generated images in the LoRA model.
      Second, we provide a guidance approach that enables us to find the trade-off between prompt ad-
059
      justment, LoRA consistency, and generalization. Third, AutoLoRA reduces bias in the LoRA model
060
      caused by using a relatively small data set to tune the model.

063   2   R ELATED W ORK
064
065   In this section, we review the related work, starting with the development of general diffusion mod-
066   els, followed by an overview of guided diffusion models, and concluding with methods for low-rank
067   adaptations.
068
069   Diffusion models The concept of diffusion models in the deep learning community was first intro-
070   duced in Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015). Utilizing Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs), diffusion
071   models enable the transformation of a simple initial distribution (e.g., a normal distribution) into a
072   more complex target distribution through a series of tractable diffusion steps. Subsequent advance-
073   ments, such as reducing the number of trajectory steps (Bordes et al., 2017), led to more efficient
074   diffusion models.
075   A major breakthrough occurred with the introduction of Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models
076   (DDPMs) in (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). DDPMs employ a weighted variational
077   bound objective by combining diffusion probabilistic models with denoising score matching (Song
078   & Ermon, 2019). While these models exhibited strong generative performance (i.e., high-quality
079   samples), their computational cost remained a significant limitation.
080   The first notable improvement in terms of scalability, particularly sample efficiency, came from
081   generalizing DDPMs to non-Markovian diffusion processes, resulting in shorter generative Markov
082   chains, called Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIMs) (Song et al., 2020).
083
      Finally, the high computational cost of scaling diffusion models to high-dimensional problems was
084
      alleviated by Latent Diffusion Models (Rombach et al., 2022), which proposed performing diffusion
085   in the lower-dimensional latent space of an autoencoder. One notable example of a latent diffusion
086   model is Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), which demonstrated the practical application of
087   this approach. Further improvements in scalability were achieved in models like SDXL (Podell
088   et al., 2023), which extended the capabilities of latent diffusion models to even larger and more
089   complex tasks.
090
091   Guidance of diffusion models In diffusion models, the underlying Stochastic Differential Equa-
092   tion (SDE) process plays a crucial role. While it enables strong generative performance, im-
093   proved scalability, and faster training compared to models based on Ordinary Differential Equations
094   (ODEs) (Dinh et al., 2014; Rezende & Mohamed, 2015; Grathwohl et al., 2018), the stochastic in-
095   ference process requires guidance to produce desirable samples. To steer the generation process in
096   a preferred direction, several guidance techniques have been developed, which can be broadly cate-
097   gorized based on different strategies: classifier-based guidance, Langevin dynamics, Markov Chain
098
      Monte Carlo (MCMC), external guiding signals, architecture-specific features, and others. Despite
      their differences, most of these techniques direct the diffusion process toward regions of minimal
099
      energy, estimated through various proxies.
100
101   One of the most well-known techniques is Classifier Guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Poleski
102   et al., 2024), which uses an external classifier trained to predict the class from noisy intermediate
103   diffusion steps. In contrast, Classifier-Free Guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) eliminates the need for
104
      a separate classifier by training a single model in two modes—conditioned and unconditioned.
105   Other approaches are inspired by sampling techniques. For instance, in the diffusion sampling com-
106   munity, Langevin dynamics is commonly used for off-policy steering, where at each trajectory step,
107   the model follows the scaled gradient norm toward regions of lowest energy (the highest log proba-
      bility), as demonstrated in works like Zhang & Chen (2021) and Sendera et al. (2024). Alternatively,


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      MCMC-based sampling strategies are also applied directly in diffusion processes (Song et al., 2023;
109   Chung et al., 2023).
110
111   A few methods incorporate external guiding functions to smooth the generative trajectory toward
112
      desired outcomes, such as in Bansal et al. (2023). Others leverage architectural properties of diffu-
      sion models, for example, using intermediate self-attention maps (Hong et al., 2023) or training an
113
      external discriminator network (Kim et al., 2022).
114
115   Most recently, AutoGuidance (Karras et al., 2024) extends classifier-free guidance by replacing the
116   unconditional model with a smaller, less-trained version of the model itself to guide the conditional
117   one. Our approach is inspired by AutoGuidance but diverges by introducing small adapters (i.e.,
118
      LoRA modules) to enhance the diversity of generated samples in diffusion models.
119
120
      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning and LoRA With the increasing size of modern deep learning
      models, full fine-tuning for downstream tasks is becoming increasingly impractical, and this chal-
121
      lenge will only grow as models scale further. This has led to the rise of Parameter-Efficient Fine-
122
      Tuning (PEFT) methods, which typically involve adding small, trainable modules to a pretrained
123   model, such as Adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019).
124
125   Among PEFT techniques, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) has emerged as the most
126
      widely adopted solution, originating from the large language model (LLM) community. LoRA intro-
      duces low-rank adaptations to each weight matrix, factorizing updates into two low-rank matrices.
127
      This allows LoRA to achieve fine-tuning results comparable to full fine-tuning, but with significantly
128
      fewer parameters (ranging from 100 to even 10,000 times smaller).
129
130   Due to its effectiveness, numerous LoRA variants have been proposed. For example, methods
131   like Dy-LoRA (Valipour et al., 2022), AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023c), and IncreLoRA (Zhang
132
      et al., 2023b) dynamically adjust the rank hyperparameter. GLoRA (Chavan et al., 2023) general-
      izes LoRA by incorporating a prompt module, while Delta-LoRA (Zi et al., 2023) simultaneously
133
      updates pretrained model weights using the difference between LoRA weights. Further model size
134
      reductions have been achieved with QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023). Additionally, ongoing research
135   continues to propose new LoRA-based approaches (including, e.g., Kopiczko et al. (2023); Hao
136   et al. (2024); Hayou et al. (2024); Zhang & Pilanci (2024)) and deepen theoretical understanding
137   (Fu et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2024).
138
      Most importantly for our work, LoRA has proven to be highly versatile and is now commonly ap-
139
      plied in diffusion models (Ryu, 2023; Smith et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024). Given
140
      the variety of possible LoRA-based methods, we follow the widely adopted and well-understood
141   procedure outlined in the original LoRA paper by Hu et al. (2021).

      Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) is a parameter-
      efficient fine-tuning technique designed to adapt pre-trained models to new tasks by introducing low-
194
      rank modifications to their weight matrices. LoRA was initially proposed to address the challenge
195
      of fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) while significantly reducing the computational and
196   memory overhead associated with updating full sets of model parameters.
197
198   The application of LoRA to diffusion models is particularly advantageous in scenarios where large
199
      pre-trained models are adapted to specific tasks or datasets with limited computational resources. By
      leveraging LoRA, it becomes feasible to adapt diffusion models for new tasks (e.g., domain-specific
200
      image generation or text-guided diffusion) without the need for full-scale retraining.
201
202   LoRA leverages the observation that the learned weight matrices in large-scale models, particularly
203   in attention-based architectures, often reside in a lower-dimensional subspace. Instead of updating
204   the full model weights, LoRA freezes the pre-trained parameters and introduces low-rank matrices
205
      that are added to the weight updates during training. Specifically, LoRA decomposes the weight
      update matrices into two smaller matrices, effectively reducing the number of parameters that need
206
      to be trained and stored.
207
208   Mathematically, a weight matrix W ∈ Rd×k , where d is the input dimension and k is the output
209   dimension, is modified by adding a low-rank matrix update as follows:
210
                                      W ′ = W + α · ∆W = W + α · A · B                                     (2)
211
212   where A ∈ Rd×r and B ∈ Rr×k , where r is the rank, typically chosen such that r ≪ d, k, resulting
213   in a significant reduction in the number of parameters that need to be optimized. This low-rank
214   adaptation allows for efficient fine-tuning while maintaining most of the representational power of
215   the original model. The α represents the LoRA scaling parameter that controls the impact of LoRA
      fine-tuning weights, mainly during inference.

      Classifier-Free Guidance Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) is a technique
217   used in diffusion models to steer the generative process more effectively without relying on external
218   classifiers. It has proven highly effective in improving the quality of generated samples in various
219   tasks such as image and text generation.
220
      Before the introduction of Classifier-Free Guidance, diffusion models often employed classifier-
221
      based guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). In this setup, an external classifier cϕ (y|xt ), trained to
222
      predict the conditioning label y from intermediate noisy samples xt , was used to steer the reverse
223   process. This guidance was achieved by modifying the reverse sampling step as:

230   Classifier-Free Guidance offers a simpler and more robust alternative by eliminating the need for
231
      an external classifier. Instead, the diffusion model itself is trained in two modes – conditional and

255   eration capabilities of a diffusion model by guiding it with a bad version of itself – a smaller and
256   less-trained variant. This method leads to more refined results while maintaining diversity in the
257
      outputs. The AutoGuidance approach is not only effective for both conditional and unconditional

258

266

372   scenes described in the original prompt with scores ranging from 0 (not at all) to 5 (exactly).
373
374
      Style Adherence (SA) assesses how well an image adheres to a specified style with scores ranging
      from 0 (markedly deficient) to 5 (perfectly).
375
376   To further evaluate the generated images, we introduce novel metrics that combine the diversity
377   metric with the above measures, namely Div-CPS, Div-PC, and Div-SA, which are calculated by
      multiplying the diversity metric with the CPS, PC, and SA, respectively.

523   6    C ONCLUSION
524
525   This paper presents AutoLoRA, an innovative guidance method for diffusion models tailored with
526   the LoRA approach. Drawing inspiration from other guidance techniques, AutoLoRA aims to bal-
527
      ance consistency within the domain highlighted by LoRA weights and the variety of samples from
      the primary conditional diffusion model. Additionally, we demonstrate that using distinct classifier-
528
      free guidances for the LoRA fine-tuned and base models enhances the diversity and quality of gen-
529
      erated samples. Experimental results in various fine-tuned LoRA domains indicate that our method
530   outperforms current guidance techniques across selected metrics.
531
532   Limitation and Future Work The naive implementation AutoLoRA used in the paper increases
533   the inference time ×2 because for each diffusion step, we run LoRA tuned and base models. How-
534   ever, this issue could be solved using model distillation techniques. In future works, we also plan to
535   extend our approach with the idea of applying AutoLoRA in a limited interval.
536
537   Reproducibility Statement We will release the code in a camera-ready version. All images were
538   generated using the fixed set of seeds. For the VLM-based evaluations, you can find the exact
539   prompts in the Appendix section A. All prompts used for the pixel art style images are included in
      Appendix section B.
