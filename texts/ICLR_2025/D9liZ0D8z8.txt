008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            There is a major open question about how to best develop foundation models
011            for time series forecasting. Tokenization is a crucial consideration in this effort:
012            what is an effective discrete vocabulary for a real-valued sequential input? To ad-
013            dress this question, we develop WaveToken, a wavelet-based tokenizer that allows
014            models to learn complex representations directly in the space of time-localized
015
               frequencies. Our method first scales and decomposes the input time series, then
               thresholds and quantizes the wavelet coefficients, and finally pre-trains an autore-
016
               gressive model to forecast coefficients for the horizon window. By decomposing
017
               coarse and fine structures in the inputs, wavelets provide an eloquent and compact
018            language for time series forecasting that simplifies learning. Empirical results on
019            a comprehensive benchmark, including 42 datasets for both in-domain and zero-
020            shot settings, show that WaveToken: i) provides better accuracy than recently pro-
021            posed foundation models for forecasting while using a much smaller vocabulary
022            (1024 tokens), and performs on par or better than modern deep learning mod-
023            els trained specifically on each dataset; and ii) exhibits superior generalization
024            capabilities, achieving the best average rank across all datasets for three comple-
025            mentary metrics. In addition, we show that our method can easily capture com-
026
               plex temporal patterns of practical relevance that are challenging for other recent
               pre-trained models, including trends, sparse spikes, and non-stationary time series
027
               with varying frequencies evolving over time.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Time series forecasting is integral to decision-making processes in many domains, including fi-
033   nance, healthcare, supply chain optimization, and climate science. Over the last decade, the field
034   has seen a gradual but steady adoption of “global” deep learning models in lieu of traditional “local”
035   statistical models (Lara-Benı́tez et al., 2021; Benidis et al., 2022). Recently, the success of large
036   language models (LLMs) on natural language and vision applications has spurred an increasing in-
037   terest for developing similar “foundation models” in other fields (for example, Subramanian et al.
038   (2024); Golling et al. (2024); Ansari et al. (2024); Das et al. (2023)). These efforts aim at building
039
      general-purpose machines able to learn complex representations from vast amounts of data and to
      generalize to a wide variety of tasks, based on the premise that LLMs are general pattern recogniz-
040
      ers (Mirchandani et al., 2023). In other words, if a problem can be reduced to that of modeling an
041
      arbitrary sequence of tokens defined on a discrete vocabulary, then an autoregressive transformer
042   might be capable of learning non-trivial relationships via next-token prediction, regardless of the
043   inputs representing text or not. The sequential nature of time series forecasting aligns seamlessly
044   with this perspective, which is why several recent works have proposed adapting transformer-based
045   architectures into foundation models for time series (see Section 2 for a review).
046
      Tokenization is a crucial albeit still understudied (Dagan et al., 2024) component of LLMs, as it pro-
047
      vides the vocabulary on which token streams are defined and the autoregressive structure is learned.
048   While in principle transformers can learn arbitrary dependencies, in practice it matters whether the
049   architecture can efficiently leverage specific structures in the inputs. In the context of time series
050   forecasting, it is then important to answer the following question: what is the most appropriate dis-
051   crete vocabulary for a continuous (real-valued) sequential input? In other words, what is the correct
052   “language” for time series forecasting? As the goal is to develop a unified model with excellent
053   forecasting performance on unseen datasets, an ideal dictionary of tokens should be as expressive as
      possible while also being compact, in order to efficiently represent the extremely high variety of non-

073   stationary real-world time series. In addition, different “languages” can exhibit various dependency
074   structures: although time series have a natural “order” dictated by time, one might ask whether there
075   exists a mapping providing a more eloquent re-ordering in a suitable space that exposes important
076   features and simplifies learning. Popular existing frameworks leverage temporal patches (e.g., Das
077

      performance in standard settings. However, by preserving the natural temporal dependency in the

081
082   Main contributions. In this work, we propose and analyze a tokenizer based on wavelets, which
083
      are families of basis functions used to decompose a signal into small waves with high resolution
      in both time and frequency domain (Mallat, 2009). In particular, we develop a tailored pre- and
084
      post-processing tokenization pipeline — WaveToken — that allows the model to learn directly in the
085
      space of sparse wavelet coefficients. This yields a compressed but highly expressive vocabulary that
086   facilitates the encoding of complex non-stationary time series. In addition, it provides an explicit
087   multi-scale structure in the inputs, which the model learns to exploit by effectively forecasting from
088   coarser to finer resolutions. We pair our wavelet-based tokenizer with the popular T5 encoder-
089   decoder architecture of Raffel et al. (2020) and pre-train it on a large corpus of time series from
090   different domains. We evaluate WaveToken on a comprehensive in-domain and zero-shot benchmark
091   made of 42 real-world datasets and compare it with traditional statistical models, recent LLM-based
092   forecasters, and state-of-the-art deep learning models. Empirical results show that
093   i) in terms of accuracy, WaveToken achieves superior forecasting performance and always performs
094   on-par or better than all other baselines with respect to three complementary metrics, while using a
095   much smaller vocabulary (1024 tokens) than recent foundation models for time series.
096
      ii) WaveToken exhibits superior generalization capabilities, achieving the best average rank across
097
      all datasets for all metrics. In addition, our method can easily capture complex temporal patterns in
098   several edge cases relevant for practical applications, such as exponential trends, sparse spikes and
099   signals with different frequencies varying over time, as shown in Figure 1.
100
101
      Overall, our findings not only suggest that wavelet-based tokenization can provide a compelling
      language for time series forecasting with LLMs, but also position our approach as a promising
102
      avenue for developing general-purpose, information-efficient forecasting models.

105   2   R ELATED W ORK
106
107   Tokenization in LLMs. Most tokenizers originate from the natural language processing (NLP) lit-
      erature and have been developed and studied for various domains such as math (Singh & Strouse,


                                                         2
108
      2024), code (Zheng et al., 2023), and several languages (e.g., Tolmachev et al. (2018); Alyafeai
109   et al. (2023)). In modern LLMs, the most popular tokenizers learn a dictionary directly on data,
110   such as variants of Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE; Gage (1994)). Tokenization of real-valued numbers
111   has received particular attention due to the difficulty of finding what is the most appropriate dis-
112   crete vocabulary for a continuous input. One of the most popular methods is training a Vector
113   Quantized-Variational AutoEncoder (VQ-VAE) (Van Den Oord et al., 2017), which learns a dic-
114   tionary of k-dimensional codewords to capture latent representations. Instead of learning a token
115   for each numerical value, Golkar et al. (2023) proposes a fixed scheme that allocates a dedicated
116   embedding vector for numerics and scales it by the number value, thereby improving efficiency
117
      and generalization. In this work, we similarly adopt a fixed scheme, but employ a tailored wavelet
      decomposition that enhances the crucial spectral properties of time series signals.
118
119   Wavelet-based forecasters. Several approaches have tried to embed wavelets in forecasting
120   pipelines. An early example is that of Papadimitriou et al. (2003), who integrate the discrete wavelet
121   transform with ARIMA modeling to capture complex patterns over long time periods. More recently,
122   Zhou et al. (2022) proposed to substitute attention blocks with Fourier- and wavelet-enhanced blocks
123
      in the transformer architecture. Within this line of work, Zhang et al. (2022) proposed to model trend
      and seasonality separately with an MLP and Fourier attention, respectively. In addition, they showed
124
      that under linear transformations, attention models in time domain, Fourier domain and wavelet do-
125
      main have the same representation power. Sasal et al. (2022) leverage a redundant wavelet transform
126   that yields J series of coefficients (one per decomposition level), each having the same length as the
127   original time series. They then learn a separate transformer model for each scale. To the best of our
128   knowledge, WaveToken is the first application of a maximally decimated wavelet transform to build
129   a tokenizer tailored for time series forecasting with LLMs.
130
      Modeling and generation of signals. Different methods in the signal processing literature propose
131
      to integrate spectral or wavelet decomposition with deep learning architectures to enhance the ca-
132   pabilities of these models on a variety of tasks. Apart from the already-mentioned VQ-VAE (Van
133   Den Oord et al., 2017), several works exploit spectrograms and log-mel spectra, for example, as
134   pre-processing steps on medical or audio data (Choi et al., 2023; Purwins et al., 2019). Similarly,
135   audio codecs (Zeghidour et al., 2021) are emerging as a critical technique to bridge the gap be-
136   tween continuous waveforms and token-based language models. As for image generation, Guth
137   et al. (2022) proposed to speed up denoising by learning diffusion models in the wavelets domain.
138   Recent concurrent works by Tian et al. (2024), Mattar et al. (2024) and Zhu & Soricut (2024), learn
139   an autoregressive model on a multi-scale sequence or in the space of 2D wavelet coefficients.
140   Foundation models for time series forecasting. Several approaches are being developed to adapt
141   LLMs to other domains, such as time series forecasting. Recent efforts in this direction include,
142   e.g., Xue & Salim (2023), which converts time series into text and re-frames the task as a question-
143   answering problem; Gruver et al. (2024), which tokenizes real-valued data as strings of digits and
144   leverages models such as GPT-3 (Brown, 2020) and Llama 2 (Touvron et al., 2023); and Jin et al.
145   (2023), which prompts a frozen LLM with a prefix describing the task and patch embeddings of time
146
      series aligned with text prototypes. Recently, Rasul et al. (2023); Goswami et al. (2024); Das et al.
      (2023); Ansari et al. (2024); Woo et al. (2024); Talukder et al. (2024) proposed different paradigms
147
      to pre-train transformer-based architectures on a large corpus of time series. See also Zhang et al.
148
      (2024) for a recent survey. While these works adopt domain-specific designs such as patching, lags
149   and time features, none of them tackles the problem by learning an autoregressive model in the
150   expressive space of time-localized frequencies.

      3     L ANGUAGE MODELING OF TIME - LOCALIZED FREQUENCIES
153
154
      We introduce a wavelet-based tokenizer that allows the model to learn complex representations
155   directly in the space of time-localized frequencies. Our method first scales and decomposes the
156   input time series, then thresholds and quantizes the wavelet coefficients, and finally it pre-trains an
157   autoregressive model to forecast wavelet coefficients for the horizon window.

      3.2   T OKENIZATION VIA WAVELET DECOMPOSITION
207
      We ask the following question: given a real-valued univariate time series x1:C = [x1 , . . . , xC ],
208   where C is the context length, can we find an optimal map T : R → V that encodes the input with a
209   compact but expressive discrete vocabulary V? To this end, we develop WaveToken, a tokenization
210   pipeline divided in scaling, wavelet decomposition, thresholding and quantization. See Section 4.4
211   for an extensive analysis of the chosen hyper-parameters.
212

214   inputs to a common scale and avoids numerical and optimization issues, especially for globally-
215   learned deep learning models (Benidis et al., 2022). Popular normalization techniques are based
      on affine transformations x̃t = (xt − m)/s, with m and s appropriately chosen. We set m =

224
      the scaled time series x̃1:C into its constituent time-localized frequencies up to the J-th level, which
225
      yields approximation coefficients {ak }J and a series of detail coefficients {dk }Jj=1 , ∀k. In addition
226   to being faster than the FFT as mentioned in Section A.2, this wavelet transform leads to compact
227   representations since it preserves the input size — i.e., a signal of size N results in N coefficients1
228   — and tends to concentrate the majority of the signal energy onto only a few significant wavelet
229   coefficients. This is especially true for time series with sharp spikes or localized features, and
230   is a useful property for compression and denoising. As each coefficient group is the outcome of
231   convoluting a filter with the signal, the autoregressive structure is also preserved within each group.
232
233   Thresholding. The inherently sparse representations that the DWT induces imply that we can
234   encode complex features with only a few significant wavelet coefficients, while the rest are close to
235   zero and thus can potentially be discarded without significantly addressing reconstruction quality.
236   Several thresholding techniques, many tailored to specific applications, have been developed over the
237
      years and are effectively used in practice (e.g., in image compression: Chang et al. (2000); Vetterli
      & Kovacevic (1995); Christopoulos et al. (2000)). As the main downstream task in this work is
238
      to pre-train a language model on a large corpus of diverse time series datasets, any thresholding
239
      technique must be adaptive to the underlying noise and complexity level of the input, so as to retain
240   most of the signal energy without discarding essential information. In what follows, we always keep
241   the approximation coefficients unaltered since they model the low-resolution coarse structure of the
242   time series, and explore the following techniques for thresholding detail coefficients:

276   3.3       M ODEL TRAINING , OBJECTIVE FUNCTION AND FORECASTING
277
278   Given a time series context x1:C , we apply the steps detailed in Section 3.2 and then concate-
279   nate the resulting discrete tokens for approximation and detail coefficients into a vector z1:C =
280   [aJ , dJ , . . . , d1 ], so that the model can learn and forecast coarse-to-fine structures in a multi-scale
281   fashion. To minimize ad-hoc modifications to the language model, we opt for an encoder-decoder
282   architecture based on the T5 family (Raffel et al., 2020), which has recently been shown to achieve
283
      excellent zero-shot performance on a comprehensive benchmark (Ansari et al., 2024). Other pro-
      posed LLM-based forecasters either reformulate the problem as a question-answering task or use
284
      patches of the input as tokens, and would therefore not be immediately compatible with our wavelet-
285
      based tokenizer (see section 2 for an exhaustive review). For brevity, in what follows we refer to
286   WaveToken as being both the tokenizer and the model paired together.
287
288   We train the model via next-token prediction by minimizing the cross-entropy between the predicted
289
      distribution and the categorical output distribution over the corresponding label tokens obtained from
      the horizon window zC:C+H . In symbols, the loss function for a time series (including EOS) is

      Learning an autoregressive model on concatenated groups of wavelet coefficients might seem
      counter-intuitive: the temporal structure is only preserved within each coefficient group and is bro-
301
      ken as the input transitions from, e.g., approximations to details. In practice, this turns out to be
302
      surprisingly helpful as it offers the model a natural way to break down complex sub-structures in
303   inputs and outputs by dividing them into a hierarchy of time-localized frequency bands. As we will
304   see in Section 4.3, the model easily learns to exploit these partitions and is able to attend to the right
305   coefficients to improve the overall forecasting accuracy.

343   Datasets and training strategy. We train and evaluate WaveToken on the publicly available datasets
344   comprehensively collected by Ansari et al. (2024). These span a variety of domains and exhibit
345   diverse properties in terms of size, frequencies and prediction lengths, and can be divided in i) pre-
346   training only: datasets exclusively used for training (13 datasets); ii) in-domain: datasets employed
347   for training, whose validation set is also used for evaluation (Benchmark I, 15 datasets); and iii) zero-
348   shot: datasets used solely for evaluation (Benchmark II, 27 datasets). See Appendix G for detailed
349
      information about all datasets. The context length of the sequences in each training batch is set to
      512 and the prediction length is set to 64. In addition, we adopt the data augmentations techniques
350
      introduced by Ansari et al. (2024): each of the sequences is generated with probability 0.9 from a
351
      TSMixup set, which takes convex combinations of different time series, and with probability 0.1
352   from a synthetic dataset generated from Gaussian Processes based on randomly combined kernels.
353   Both of these have been shown to be effective in domains such as time series forecasting, where data
354   is inherently scarce relative to standard language modeling applications.

359   to evaluate the quality of point forecasts. In addition, we compute the Visual Relative Squared Error
360   (VRSE; Posam et al. (2024)), which measures the relative squared difference of the amplitudes at all
361   frequencies between the ground truth and the (median) forecast. This metric serves a complementary
362   role to avoid common pitfalls of standard scores, which can fail to properly assess important edge
363   cases that would otherwise be visually obvious, as shown in Figure 9. In order to aggregate these
364   metrics and provide fair comparisons, we compute each model’s score divided by the score of a
365   baseline model (here, Seasonal Naive). These relative scores are then aggregated across all datasets
366
      using the geometric mean. See Appendix C for more details. All results for Chronos and WaveToken
      are averaged across three different seeds.

463   4.4   A BLATION STUDY
464
465   Figure 6 shows the effect of different vocabulary sizes, wavelet families and decomposition levels on
466
      the forecasting accuracy of WaveToken-Small trained for 200K steps on one A100 GPU. Regarding
      vocabulary size, which determines the precision with which tokens encode wavelet coefficients, we
467
      observe a gradual but consistent improvement until |V| = 1024, which we select as the optimal
468
      value. For higher vocabulary sizes, WQL and MASE remain flat or worsen on both in-domain and
469   zero-shot benchmarks. This phenomenon can be ascribed to the intrinsic compression properties of
470   wavelets (Mallat, 2009): by concentrating most of the signal energy onto a few coefficients, we can
471   effectively capture more information with a smaller codebook, while a larger one would only reserve
472   more tokens to spurious coefficients. Note that our vocabulary is much smaller than comparable
473   models: Chronos for example, that leverages the same architectures, uses |V| = 4096. As to the
474   many wavelet families available, the central panel of Figure 6 shows that the Biorthogonal-2.2 ba-
475   sis achieves optimal performance. This family uses two separate filters with two vanishing moments
476   for analysis and synthesis, a dual structure that allows for symmetric or near-symmetric wavelet
477
      functions that help preventing distortions near the signal edges. This is known to be advantageous
      in many applications, including image compression3 . Families with higher vanishing moments ca-
478
      pable of capturing higher order polynomial did not prove effective. As to the decomposition level,
479
      we observe that first-level coefficients are sufficient to achieve good forecasting accuracy. Albeit
480   deeper levels provide more granular time-localized frequencies, the higher number of groups in the
481   concatenated coefficients make it harder for the attention mechanism to identify the relevant ones
482   at each prediction step. Regarding thresholding, we analyze the performance of all four techniques
483   described in Section 3. Empirically, we observe a discrepancy in the results obtained when training

517   5   C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this work, we develop WaveToken, a tokenization pipeline tailored to a specific goal: constructing
520   a general-purpose forecasting model capable of capturing a wide variety of complex patterns while
521   consuming as little information as possible, thereby leading to excellent generalization performance
522   on unseen datasets. We leverage a recently-proposed framework to pre-train an encoder-decoder
523   architecture in the context of time series (Ansari et al., 2024) and re-purpose it to learn an autore-
524
      gressive model in the space of time-localized frequencies. The resulting wavelet-based vocabulary
      is both compact — using 1024 tokens, i.e. one quarter of Chronos — and very expressive, and leads
525
      to
526
527   i) excellent forecasting accuracy with respect to all other baselines in terms of three complementary
528   metrics: weighted quantile loss for probabilistic forecasts, mean absolute scaled error for point
529   forecasts, and visual relative squared error to measure dicrepancies in the frequency content relative
530
      to the ground truth.
531   ii) superior generalization capabilities, with WaveToken being the best model across all datasets
532   and metrics in terms of average rank. In addition, our method can easily capture complex temporal
533   patterns in several edge cases relevant for practical applications, as shown in Figure 1.
534   As potential future directions, we foresee that exploring techniques to automatically handle context
535   lengths larger than 512 in the tokenizer would allow models to capture longer-range dependencies
536   if needed. The DWT is in fact a natural tool to leverage in these settings, as it can encode long
537   contexts without increasing the input length through convolutions and down-sampling. Furthermore,
538   WaveToken exploits an autoregressive model on wavelet coefficients. As such, it suffers from slower
539   decoding and inference times relative to recently proposed alternatives, such as patch-based models.
      Applying WaveToken to patch-based architectures represents an interesting area for future research.

      A     A BRIEF TOUR OF WAVELETS
776
777
      A.1   M OTIVATION : F ROM THE FOURIER TRANSFORM TO THE WAVELET TRANSFORM
778
      The Fourier Transform (FT) allows one to map a signal from the time domain to the frequency
779   domain by computing the signal’s projection onto a basis of complex exponentials, which represent
780   sine and cosine functions of varying frequencies. This process effectively decomposes the signal
781   into its constituent frequency components. The main drawback of the Fourier Transform is that,
782   while having high resolution in the frequency domain, it has zero resolution in the time domain. In
783   other words, it cannot tell at which location in time these frequencies occur in the original signal.
784   The Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) tries to overcome this issue by splitting the original
785   signal in different windows and applying the Fourier Transform in each of them. Nonetheless, the
786   fixed window immediately implies a trade-off: the smaller the size of the window, the higher the
787
      resolution in time domain, the lower the resolution in frequency domain, and vice versa. This is
      widely known as the Uncertainty Principle (Oppenheim & Schafer, 2010).
788
789   The Wavelet Transform (WT) addresses this limitation by employing a quickly decaying zero-mean
790   oscillatory function, known as the mother wavelet, which inherently adapts its time-frequency reso-
791   lution to the signal’s characteristics. By dilating or compressing the wavelet, its time support varies
792   inversely with its frequency, thus providing high time resolution for high-frequency components and
793
      high frequency resolution for low-frequency components. This dual localization property, achieved
      through the modulation of the wavelet’s scale parameter, enables the WT to efficiently analyze
794
      non-stationary signals whose spectral content evolves over time, a phenomenon often referred to as
795
      time-frequency localization (Daubechies, 1992; Mallat, 2009).
796
797
      A.2   T HE HAAR WAVELET AND THE DISCRETE WAVELET TRANSFORM
798
799   Wavelet families consist of basis functions that can be divided into two primary types: the scaling
800   function (often referred to as the “father” wavelet), which captures the low-frequency, coarse struc-
801   ture of the signal (the approximation), and the wavelet function (often referred to as the “mother”
802   wavelet), which captures the high-frequency components (the detail) of the signal. The “father”
803   and “mother” wavelets give rise to “son” and “daughter” wavelets through scaling and translation.
804   Here, we briefly formalize these concepts in the context of the Haar wavelet (Haar, 1911), a simple
805   yet pedagogically significant wavelet family. For an extensive introduction to wavelets and their
806
      applications, see, for example, Mallat (2009), Daubechies (1992) and Strang & Nguyen (1996).

      How do we compute ak and dk,j in practice? The multi-resolution theory of Mallat (1989) and
      Meyer (1992) offers an elegant and efficient solution: any wavelet that generates an orthonormal
840
      basis can be characterized by a conjugate mirror filter. The mapping from a discretized signal to a
841
      sequence of wavelet coefficients is then implemented as a filter bank made of a cascade of high-pass
842   and low-pass filters associated with the mother and father wavelets, and is called Discrete Wavelet
843   Transform (DWT)4 . For the Haar family, the approximation components are associated with aver-
844   aging filters (low-pass), while the detail components are associated with distance filters (high-pass).
845   The DWT convolves these filters with the signal and then downsamples by a factor of two to elimi-
846   nate the repeated information. The result is an array of detail coefficients {dk }j and approximation
847   coefficients {ak }j . The latter can be further decomposed into new approximation and detail co-
848   efficients at the next level and the process can continue recursively, resulting in a hierarchical and
849   multi-resolution decomposition of the signal. Figure 7 (right) shows this for a particular time series.
850   The DWT has a computational complexity of O(N ), with N being the length of the input signal.
851   This is due to i) the filtering operation requiring a constant amount of work proportional to the signal
852   length, and ii) the down-sampling operation halving the signal at each of the log2 N levels: this leads
853   to a total amount of work equal to N (1 + 21 + 14 + · · · ), which converges to 2N . Note that this is
854   even faster than the FFT algorithm, which has a computational complexity of O(N log N ).
