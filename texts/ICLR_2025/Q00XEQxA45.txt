009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Image steganography is the process of hiding secret information in an image
012            through imperceptible changes. Most of recent works hide message in the im-
013            age by modifying the pixels of image itself. However, those images with hidden
014            messages are not robust to compression such as JPEG, which is used almost every-
015            where. In order to achieve the ability to compress the image while still having the
016
               ability to carry the message, we propose an innovative optimization method which
               leverages a semi-amortized approach to directly manipulate latent space data for
017
               the joint optimization of image compression and steganography. In the compres-
018
               sion module, we investigate two of the most popular models in learned image
019            compression with different pre-trained quality: the hyperprior model and the ELIC
020            model. For the steganography module, our method employs the pre-trained fixed
021            neural network steganography (FNNS) model. We compare our method with two
022            state-of-the-art methods such as FNNS-JPEG and LISO-JPEG, achieving signifi-
023            cant image compression while maintaining high fidelity and ensuring the accuracy
024            of content upon decoding. The results demonstrate the effectiveness and superior-
025            ity of our approach.

      1   INTRODUCTION
028
029
      Image steganography involves the artful concealment of sensitive data, such as audio, imagery, and
030   textual content (Morkel et al., 2005), within a host image through minimal perturbations. In an opti-
031   mal scenario, the embedded information remains undetectable to all but the intended recipients, who
032   possess the requisite keys for extraction. Despite minor discrepancies between the steganographic
033   and original images, the presence of covert data remains imperceptible to the uninitiated, render-
034   ing steganography a valuable asset in applications such as digital watermarking (Wolfgang & Delp,
035   1996; Shih, 2007) and patent verification (Lu, 2005).
036   Conventional steganographic practices often rely on pixel-level image statistics, with the Least Sig-
037   nificant Bit (LSB) technique being a prime example (Pevný et al., 2010; Holub & Fridrich, 2012b;
038   Holub et al., 2014). This method ingeniously manipulates and embeds information within the LSBs
039   of an image’s pixels, capitalizing on the human visual system’s relative indifference to minor color
040   variations for the purpose of information obfuscation.
041
      The advent of deep learning has revolutionized steganography with the advent of end-to-end train-
042   able encoder-decoder neural networks (Zhang et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2018; Baluja, 2017). In par-
043   ticular, convolutional neural networks have demonstrated an uncanny ability to discern and exploit
044   the manifold structure of images (Zhang et al., 2019; Baluja, 2017). These sophisticated methods
045   not only produce highly realistic encrypted images, but also facilitate the encoding of substantial
046   information loads, potentially reaching a density of 6 bits per pixel (bpp). However, this increased
047   capacity comes at the cost of a proportional increase in error rates (Reed et al., 1960).
048   Innovative methodologies have emerged that frame steganography as a constrained optimization
049   problem, harnessing adversarial learning strategies to embed data by introducing subtle, yet delib-
050   erate, perturbations within the image (Kishore et al., 2021). Other cutting-edge approaches amal-
051   gamate end-to-end neural networks with optimization algorithms, achieving a remarkable 100%
052   accuracy rate while simultaneously generating images of enhanced naturalism (Chen et al., 2023).
053   However, these methods are sensitive to commonly adopted image compression techniques. Even
      under the 1 bpp condition (which is much less aggressive than real world image compression), the

      accuracy of the extracted information is almost lost after the steganographic image is compressed by
055   JPEG (Wallace, 1992). Even if a differentiable JPEG is added in the optimization process to back
056   propagate the gradient, the PSNR metric is bad and unnatural pictures will be produced. Moreover,
057   the compression quality of training and evaluation must be consistent to have high accuracy, which
058   is not in line with actual transmission situation.
059
      The steganographic image is not as close to the original image as possible; if the mse metric is used,
060
      it is easy to make the image blurred, adding the perception metric will make the encrypted image
061   more real and effectively improve the robustness in some detection cases. Therefore, in order to
062   improve the visual quality of steganographic images and increase the security of steganography, we
063   add a GAN-like discriminator (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Agustsson et al., 2018) for adversarial train-
064   ing to achieve the objective of improving the subjective quality of images. Previous work has shown
065   that semi-amortized inference (Kim et al., 2018) can be used to improve R-D performance (Johnston
066   et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2020). We intend to use it in our model to edit latent variables to achieve
067   steganography and reconstruction task. We find that this method can flexibly control each trade-off
068   metric. Our contributions are as follows.
069
             • We are the first to propose a joint optimization of compression and steganography, which
070            solves the problem that steganographic images are destroyed due to the compression pro-
071            cess, so that encrypted images can convey more effective information in the case of com-
072            pression.
073          • Our method consists of a image compression module and a steganography module, both
074            of which use pre-trained models and are independent of the optimization process based on
075            semi-amortized inference, allowing flexible model configurations.
076
             • We exploit the advantages of GAN models in generating images with better subjective
077            quality and introduce discriminator and adversarial training for optimization. The results
078            show that the subjective quality of the images generated by our method is also better than
079            that of existing methods.

      2   RELATED WORKS
082
083
      Steganography classic steganography operates directly on the spatial of the cover image to encode
084
      the message to be hidden. For example, least significant bit (LSB) steganography. This method
085   sequentially embeds the binary representation of the hidden message in one of the RGB channels
086   of the carrier image. Previous work such as Pixel-Value Differencing (PVD) (Wu & Tsai, 2003)
087   uses the difference in pixel dimension between two images. Highly undetectable steganography
088   (HUGO) (Pevný et al., 2010) uses the minimization of a well-defined distortion metric, which
089   quantifies the perceptual and statistical changes introduced by the embedding process. This dis-
090   tortion metric is typically formulated on the basis of an extended state space, capturing both local
091   and global characteristics of the cover medium. For Wavelet Obtained Weights (WOW) (Holub &
092   Fridrich, 2012a), this algorithm assesses the embedding cost of each pixel in an image using a set
093
      of directional filters. The core idea of the WOW algorithm is to adaptively embed secret informa-
      tion based on the local texture complexity of the image. With the development of deep learning.
094
      HiDDeN (Zhu et al., 2018) has proposed a encoder-decoder framework to hide messages. Hayes &
095
      Danezis (2017) and Zhang et al. (2019) use adversarial training to generate better quality stegano-
096   graphic images, and the latter method can hide up to 6 bpp with error rates of about 13-33%. All
097   these approaches are training based methods, which means using a dataset to train a model and test it
098   on other images. Recent years have seen a novel method called learning-to-optimize (Kishore et al.,
099   2021) which inserts an optimization problem for each processed image. The steganographic image
100   is optimized with respect to the outputs of a fixed (random or pre-trained) decoder and encoder and
101   the optimization problem is solved with gradient-based optimizer, such as L-BGFS (Dennis, 1982).
102   Learned end-to-end Image Compression Over the past decade, learning-based image compres-
103   sion has achieved remarkable success. One of the pioneering contributions in this field was made
104   by Johannes Ballé (Ballé et al., 2017), who first proposed an end-to-end learning framework for
105   image compression and use uniform noise estimator and a parametric entropy model to approximate
106   the probability mass function. Then VAE architecture and hyperprior were proposed (Ballé et al.,
107   2018) for further improvement. They use hyperprior parameter ẑ to calculate the parameters of the
      entropy model. Minnen et al. (2018) utilizes spatial masked convolution as context model, which


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      improves the compression ratio at the cost of high decoding complexity. Then channel-wise con-
109   text model is proposed for more effecient context modeling (Minnen & Singh, 2020). ELIC (He
110   et al., 2022) adopts a spatial-channel context model with other architecture improvement, achieving
111   a better balance between compression and computational complexity. To make reconstructed images
112   more realistic and natural, GAN based models are used and verified to be successful in image com-
113   pression (Mentzer et al., 2020), where conditional GAN is used to constrain consistency between
114   decoding image and origin image.
115   Semi-amortized Variational Inference and Code Editing Kim et al. (2018) and Marino et al.
116   (2018) invented semi-amortized variational inference. In traditional Variational Autoencoders
117   (VAEs), a shared inference network, known as the encoder, generates variational parameters for
118   each sample. This approach, referred to as Amortized Variational Inference (AVI), is computation-
119   ally efficient because the same network is used globally across all samples. However, the shared
120   nature of the inference network can lead to suboptimal variational parameters, as it may not capture
121   the specific characteristics of individual samples accurately. On the other hand, Stochastic Varia-
122   tional Inference (SVI) performs variational inference for each sample individually. While SVI can
123
      produce more accurate and sample-specific variational parameters, it is computationally expensive
      and often impractical for large datasets. The Semi-Amortized Variational Autoencoders is to ini-
124
      tialize the variational parameters for each sample like traditional VAE, then update the variational
125
      parameters for each sample to get high-quality posterior approximations. Campos et al. (2019) and
126   Yang et al. (2020) introduce this method to learned end-to-end image compression. Training a fully
127   amortized network is the first step and iteratively optimizing latent is the next step. Gao et al. (2022)
128   proposed Code Editing, a new paradigm for continuous variable bitrate neural image compression
129   based on semi-amortized inference. They edit latent directly towards different optimization target,
130   giving neural image compression more flexibility.

157   3.1        OVERALL F RAMEWORK
158
159   Let X ∈ [0, 1]3×H×W be a color image with height H and width W . Let M ∈ [0, 1]D×H×W be a
160   message that we are trying to conceal in X, where D specifies the number of bits we need to hide per
161   pixel. We assume that the length of message is D × H × W . If the message length is not a multiple
      of H × W we can simply ignore the unused outputs and view them as zero during optimization.

      We assume the involvement of two entities in the information transmission process: the sender and
163   the receiver. The sender uses an encoder network to embed the information within the cover image,
164   producing a steganographic image. In contrast, the receiver utilizes a decoder network to extract
165   information from the target steganographic image. However, considering there is a transmission
166   process, the image needs to be compressed, which means it has information loss during the pro-
167   cess. Our primary objective is to guarantee that the receiver can accurately extract the information
168   even after the steganographic image has been subjected to compression and transmission. Further-
169   more, the steganographic image should closely resemble the original image, both perceptually and
170   quantitatively.
171   Our structure includes an image compression module and a steganography module, both of which
172   use pre-trained models. The compression and strganography process is shown in Fig. 1. For the
173   compression module, we use both ELIC (He et al., 2022) and Hyper (Ballé et al., 2018) as our
174   coding architecture to simulate the compression and transmission process. Fig. 1 shows its diagram
175   with ELIC. We froze the parameters of all neural networks, retaining only the latent code y as the
176   object that can receive gradients and participate in optimization.
177   Deep learning-based data compression methods have achieved an increasingly strong performance
178   in visual data compression, outperforming classical codecs such as JPEG and BPG (fabrice bellard,
179   2015) in terms of rate-distortion performance. Both hyperprior and various context models proposed
180   recently have greatly improved the rate-distortion performance, but optimized reconstruction based
181   on mse loss is easy to generate blurred images, which will also occur with the loss function of
182   ssim (Wang et al., 2004). Some previous work adopted GAN to enhance perceptual quality, such as
183
      using a generator and a conditional discriminator to compress images while maintaining subjective
      quality (Agustsson et al., 2018). In our method we use the discriminator same as HIFIC (Mentzer
184
      et al., 2020).

254   4.2       E VALUATION M ETRICS
255
256   Steganography algorithms are evaluated along the amount of data that can be hidden in an image,
257   a.k.a capacity. Compression algorithms are evaluated along the length of binary data that repre-
258   sent a compressed image, a.k.a. rate. Both are evaluated along the similarity between the cover
259
      and steganography image, a.k.a distortion, This section describes some metrics in evaluating the
      performance of our model.
260
261   Reed Solomon Bits per pixel Measuring the accuracy of the amount of data that is hidden in an
262   image is non-trivial. In the actual process of information transmission, certain error correction
263   techniques are needed to help accurately transmit information to the target location, which is more
264   meaningful. Consider a practical situation where a piece of information is encoded into a binary bit
265
      stream, there is a probability that some of the information will be successfully recovered through
      steganography of the image. Since the location of the error is random, it can only allow you to know
266
      the proportion of errors, but not the location of the error. The value of the decoded information is
267
      completely meaningless.

301   4.3   C OMPARISON AND A NALYSIS
302
303
      We compare our method with FNNS (Kishore et al., 2021) and LISO (Chen et al., 2023), two state-
      of-the-art methods in image steganography with optimization-based method. In practical industrial
304
      scenarios, directly transmitting raw float32 image data is highly inefficient and resource-intensive.
305
      Saving images in formats like PNG, which are lossless, can result in large file sizes, making them
306   unsuitable for real-time or bandwidth-constrained environments. Therefore, it is essential to employ
307   various compression techniques to reduce the size of image data to compare these steganography
308   methods. For FNNS, we saved the final steganographic image in fp16, PNG, and different-quality
309   JPEG formats after loading cover image information, to show FNNS’s information recovery ability
310   under different saving methods. We found that the higher the bpp (bits per pixel), the higher the
311   accuracy of the information that can be recovered. Additionally, for different qualities of JPEG
312   methods, we incorporate a differentiable JPEG method into our optimization process, which allows
313   the gradient to pass through the parameters during optimization. It makes image steganography
314
      more adaptable to JPEG compression.
315   For LISO, there is one kind of LISO employs an approximate JPEG layer, where the forward pass
316   performs standard JPEG compression and the backward pass is an analytic function. The improved
317   method is called LISO-JPEG (Chen et al., 2023). Since the pre-trained models in LISO-JPEG are
318   not completely released, we were only able to compare LISO-JPEG with our method in DIV2K.
319   As can be seen in Tables 1, 2, 3, FNNS-png means saving the steganographic image in png format
320   (Portable Network Graphics) and reloading it to measure the decoding message accuracy, similar to
321   ”-jpg90”,”-jpg70”. The number after ”jpg” means jpeg quality, ” TRAIN” after jpg means adding
322   differentiable jpeg layer in the optimization pipeline, where the gradient can be propagated back-
323   ward. And different numbers behind the hyperprior and ELIC means different trade-off (different λ
      in R-D loss) between rate and distortion in pre-training process.

423
424   Steganalysis, as a critical component of information security, is dedicated to the detection of covert
425   communications embedded within digital media. The primary objective of steganalysis tools is to
426   ascertain whether an image has been manipulated to conceal a message. This domain encompasses
427   two principal methodologies: statistical steganalysis and neural steganalysis.
428   Statistical steganalysis relies on the identification of deviations from the expected statistical proper-
429   ties of an image, detecting LSB (least significant bit) steganography of an image (Gupta & Bhushan,
430   2012). These deviations may indicate the presence of hidden data. A notable example of a statistical
431   steganalysis tool is StegExpose (Boehm, 2014), which integrates a variety of detection algorithms,
      such as the Chi-square attack (Westfeld & Pfitzmann, 1999b) and RS (Regular/Singular) analy-

      5   CONCLUSION
517
518
      We propose an innovative optimization method for joint optimization of image compression and
519   steganography. Our method has demonstrated superior efficiency compared to existing techniques
520   such as FNNS-JPEG and LISO-JPEG, achieving significant image compression while maintaining
521   high fidelity and ensuring the accuracy of steganographic content upon decoding. By balancing the
522   image compression rate with the steganographic payload, we have reached a level of performance
523   that is considered state-of-the-art. In the future, our research will continue to explore strategies for
524   reducing compression rates further while maintaining low error rates and enhancing the quality of
525   steganographic images, aiming to push the boundaries of what is achievable in the field of advanced
526   image processing for security and data efficiency.
