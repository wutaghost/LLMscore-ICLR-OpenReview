009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Prompt engineering and finetuning aim to maximize language model performance
012            on a given metric (like toxicity reduction). However, these methods do not optimally
013            elicit a model’s capabilities. To reduce this gap, we introduce a form of activation
014            engineering: the inference-time modification of activations in order to control (or
015            steer) model outputs. Specifically, we introduce the Activation Addition (ActAdd)
016
               technique, which contrasts the intermediate activations on prompt pairs (such as
               “Love” versus “Hate”) to compute a steering vector (Subramani et al., 2022). By
017
               tactically adding in e.g. the “Love”−“Hate” steering vector during the forward
018
               pass, ActAdd can perform many tasks like topic steering, sentiment steering,
019            and detoxification. ActAdd yields inference-time control over high-level output
020            properties (like topic and sentiment) while preserving performance on off-target
021            tasks. ActAdd is lightweight: it does not require any machine optimization and
022            works with a single pair of data points, which enables rapid iteration over steering.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      LLMs contain hidden capabilities we do not know how to fully elicit (Korinek, 2023). Naively
      prompting a model with a question does not maximize the probability of the correct response. For
028
      example, consider how prompting a model to think “step-by-step” (Wei et al., 2022) massively
029
      improves performance on a range of benchmarks. Similarly, “few-shot” prompting a model with
030   correct answers to unrelated in-distribution questions allows “in-context learning” for e.g. stronger
031   performance on NLP tasks (Brown et al., 2020). Importantly, these interventions do not supply
032   the LLM with extra task-relevant information or update the algorithm implemented by the LLM’s
033   computational graph. Even though the model is initially able to score higher on these benchmarks,
034   those capabilities do not emerge without a specific intervention. We therefore hypothesize an
035   elicitation overhang: we do not know how to elicit all relevant abilities and information from models.
036   Prompt engineering is the most obvious way to steer a model, but prompting has limited reliability
037   (Ye & Durrett, 2022; Wang et al., 2024). Therefore, to reduce the elicitation overhang, we explore a
038   new modality for steering language model outputs. By strategically perturbing activations during the
039   forward pass, we hope to more reliably and effectively steer models compared to prompt engineering.
040   We call this methodology activation engineering.
041
      We suspect that compared to prompt engineering, activation engineering can elicit a wider range of
042   model capabilities. Consider, for example, a model optimized to imitate the text outputs of eloquent
043   poets and awkward mathematicians. The model may contain the internal mechanisms required to
044   output text which is both eloquent and mathematical. However, if the model is an accurate estimator
045   of the training distribution, it will (correctly) assign low probability to eloquent mathematical prose.
046   Because nothing in the training data was both eloquent and mathematical, there may exist no prompt
047   which elicits mathematical prose. In contrast, activation engineering might be able to simultaneously
048   activate the circuitry for eloquent speech and for mathematical content.
049   To demonstrate the power of activation engineering, we introduce Activation Addition (ActAdd).
050   Suppose we want to achieve negative-to-positive sentiment control (Li et al., 2018; Dathathri et al.,
051   2020). To achieve this, ActAdd first compares the model’s activations on a contrast pair of prompts,
052   such as the prompts “Love” and “Hate.” The otherwise-similar prompts differ along the target
053   dimension of sentiment. ActAdd then computes the difference of these activations in order to
      compute steering vectors. These vectors act like “virtual bias terms” because ActAdd directly adds


                                                         1
054
      the steering vectors to the forward pass at inference time. By shifting the inference-time activations
055   along the direction of the steering vector, ActAdd steers the model to generate positive sentiment
056   completions (Table 1).

081   Contributions. We unify past literature on related topics to introduce activation engineering. To
082   better elicit the full capabilities of models, we introduce the ActAdd steering method. ActAdd
083   achieves substantial (but not SOTA) control on toxicity reduction and sentiment control. We thor-
084   oughly test ActAdd’s generality and effects on general capabilities. We therefore show the promise
085   of ActAdd as an effective and cheap method for steering LLM outputs.

089   Latent space arithmetic. Computer vision researchers have long demonstrated the ability to steer
090   image generation using derived vectors, including steering latent variables – most famously, shifting
091   activations along a direction that corresponds to smiling in images (Larsen et al. 2016; White 2016).
092   Similarly, in the text domain, classic results on the word2vec embedding show that arithmetic on
093   word vectors can capture some parts of semantic reasoning (for instance, analogies: Mikolov et al.
094   2013b;a). Our work focuses on steering generative language models.
095
096   LLM steering. Many approaches attempt to affect the output of a pretrained LLM, whether:
097
      • Intervening on weights, as with supervised finetuning, RLHF, steerable layers, and weight editing
098
        (that is, targeted fine-tuning) (Ranzato et al. 2016; Ziegler et al. 2019; Dathathri et al. 2020; Meng
099
        et al. 2023; Ilharco et al. 2023). However, naive RLHF, finetuning, and weight editing have known
100     side-effects on overall model performance (Hase et al. 2023; Qi et al. 2023; Brown et al. 2023);
101
      • Intervening at decoding, as with guided or trainable decoding (Gu et al. 2017; Grover et al. 2019;
102
        see Zhang et al. 2022a for an overview of controlled generation and Jin et al. 2022 for textual style
103
        transfer);
104
105
      • Intervening on the prompt, as with automated prompt engineering (Shin et al. 2020; Zhou et al.
        2022);
106
107   • Intervening on token embeddings, as with ‘soft prompting’ (Li & Liang 2021; Lester et al. 2021;
        Khashabi et al. 2022);


                                                         2
108
      • Intervening on activations, for instance by freezing the weights of the LLM and searching for a
109     ‘steering vector’ of activations, e.g. using gradient descent (Subramani et al. 2022; Hernandez
110     et al. 2023). These optimized extraction methods, which search for a steering vector, differ from
111     extraction methods which directly compute it (present work and Li et al. 2023b). In our work, we
112     do not use gradient descent or other optimization methods.

133   Activation engineering. Activation engineering involves creating vectors of activations which
134   cause desired changes to output text when added to the forward passes of a frozen LLM (Dathathri
135
      et al. 2020). Table 2 organizes prior work by intervention type. An early antecedent is the Plug-
      and-Play Language Model of Dathathri et al. 2020. This uses a separate classifier (one classifier per
136
      attribute to steer towards) to perturb the model’s activations to generate text that accords more closely
137
      with the classifier’s target. Subramani et al. 2022 extract latent steering vectors from a frozen LLM,
138   successfully discovering sentence-specific vectors which steer completions to near-perfect BLEU
139   scores (i.e, control of the LLM’s generation) and unsupervised style transfer. However, the method
140   requires running gradient descent for each new steering vector. Hernandez et al. 2023 locate and
141   edit an LLM’s knowledge through learning an encoding of facts in its activation space. Ablating
142   attention heads can also be seen as activation engineering, though the technique is mostly used for
143   model interpretation rather than steering (Michel et al. 2019; Olsson et al. 2022).
144   Independently of our work, Li et al. 2023b developed a similar method called ITI which computes
145   steering vectors which are selectively applied according to trained linear probes. They use these
146   probes to find attention heads with different activation distributions for true and false statements. They
147   steer the model toward truthful outputs, where our experiments cover a range of goals. In addition,
148   ITI adds the same vector at all sequence positions during inference and requires dozens of samples.
149   In contrast, ActAdd we add steering vectors to a subset of sequence positions and require as few as 2
150   samples. Similar work on ‘in-context vectors’ also followed ours (Liu et al. 2023). Lastly, Zou et al.
151   2023’s “representation engineering” also followed our work. They develop a range of techniques
152
      for deriving steering vectors and for steering models using activation-space edits and optimization.
      In comparison to Zou et al. 2023, we steer different models (primarily LLAMA-3.1-8B, but also
153
      LLAMA-3, OPT, GPT-2, and GPT-J) on different tasks (detoxification and sentiment control).
154
155   Dekoninck et al. 2024’s Language Model Arithmetic (LMA) combines multiple models’ output
156   characteristics by solving an optimization problem involving KL-divergences. LMA allows an
157   impressive and flexible control over model steering, although it requires having trained multiple
158
      models.
159   Not all activation-focused works aim to control model outputs. Some interpretability techniques, like
160   activation patching, simply resample activations instead of adding a vector (Heimersheim & Nanda
161   2024). Vig et al., 2020 use a related method, causal mediation analysis, to locate the components of a
      trained model that mediate gender bias.

182   We use decoder-only Transformer neural networks (Vaswani et al. 2017). The LLMs in this work con-
183   tain a stack of Transformer layers, each consisting of multi-head attention (MHA) and a feedforward
184   network (FFN). We focus on its “residual streams” (Elhage et al. 2021), the sequences (x0 , ..., xn )
185   of intermediate activation vectors processed by each layer. ActAdd manipulates the residual stream
186   values hl input to layer l. Each layer performs MHA and FFN computations on xi , adding xi+1 to
187   the stream. The final vector xn in the stream can then be decoded into the next-token prediction. At
188
      inference time, the residual stream is initialized h1 with the embedding of the tokenized prompt.
189   Activation addition. Our method takes a pair of natural-language prompts (p+ , p− ), where p+
190   represents the property we wish output text to emphasise (e.g. love) and p− represents its opposite
191   (e.g. hate or indifference). hl+ is the activation vector for the prompt p+ at layer l. The difference
192   hl+ − hl− is a new activation vector which (intuitively) captures the difference between a prompt with
193   the target property, and a prompt without it. The steering vector is computed before inference time.

213   To obtain a steering vector, we perform a forward pass on each prompt, record the activations at the
214   given location in each pass, take the difference hl+ − hl− , and then finally rescale this difference in
215   activations by an ‘injection coefficient’ c. To steer, we add the resulting activation vector to the input
      of layer l and allow the forward pass to continue, and so obtain our steered output. c represents the


                                                                 4
216
      intervention strength, since it multiplies the steering vector’s contribution to the residual stream.1
217   We perform hyperparameter tuning to select c and also the injection layer l. As expected from past
218   work (Subramani et al. 2022; Mini et al. 2023), intervening at the middle layers is most effective. See
219   Appendix C for implementation details.

      5    D ISCUSSION
420   Limitations Initially, ActAdd achieved SOTA on detoxification and on one kind of sentiment
421   steering (Appendix Tables 13 and 14). However, stronger methods have since been released, and our
422   above standardized tests on a new dataset show that our method does not robustly outperform across
423   datasets. Table 3 shows that ActAdd substantially increases perplexity, which we find somewhat
424   perplexing. On models older than LLAMA-3.1 and on other tasks, the method did not cause a
425   significant increase in perplexity. Perhaps ActAdd faces challenges when scaling to larger and newer
426   models, and so refinements are needed.
427   To steer the model using an ActAdd vector, the user supplies the injection coefficient c and the
428   intervention layer l. So far we have had success with fixing the sequence alignment a = 1. Over-
429   all, these free hyperparameters make ActAdd less user-friendly than simple prompt engineering.
430   Thankfully, the user does not have to perform a fresh hyperparameter sweep for each use case; in
431   practice, intervention hyperparameters are stable. We include examples of failed steering vectors in
      Appendix Table 7. We also have not examined ActAdd’s potential impact on reasoning. ActAdd is


                                                                        8
432
      not immediately applicable given only API access to a model. The model must both cache and expose
433   intermediate activations at the given layer (Bloom & Nanda 2022). Most APIs do not allow this.
434
      Activation engineering vs finetuning Finetuning is better understood and more flexible – we doubt
435
      that activation engineering can e.g. teach a model a new skill. However, finetuning is significantly
436
      more costly and may not be able to elicit the same kinds of capabilities which activation engineering
437   can elicit. The first advantage of ActAdd is efficiency: the method requires no backward passes and
438   can thus run on any machine that can perform inference rather than training. Implementation effort
439   is also greatly reduced; only forward passes are required to find a suitable (p+ , p− ) and minimal
440   labeled data is required - just the steering prompt pair. We discovered most of the example contrast
441   pairs in Appendix Table 6 in minutes. All things considered, even nontechnical users can benefit
442   from rapid feedback and relatively easy iteration.
443   Activation engineering vs prompt engineering Activation additions can be continuously weighted,
444   while prompts are discrete – a token is either present, or not. To more intensely steer the model
445   to generate wedding-related text, our method does not require any edit to the prompt, but instead
446   just increasing the injection coefficient. See Appendix B for suggestive experiments on ActAdd vs
447   prompting. Unlike system prompts, activation additions do not take up token space in the model’s
448   context window, although this is a small benefit in the era of multi-million token context windows.
449   While prompting is more flexible and even cheaper than ActAdd, activation additions may elicit
450
      capabilities which prompting cannot.
451   Algebraic combination of forward passes ActAdd can be viewed as composition of separate
452   forward passes. For example, we compose h+ , h− and h∗ to produce steered output. We were
453   surprised that forward passes can “compose” in this way, despite the model not being trained to
454
      allow this operation. The composability of forward passes is itself evidence for compositional
      representations (Olah 2023), independent of the evidence from task-composition arithmetic on
455
      weights (Ilharco et al. 2023).
456
457   Interpretability In most programs, adding values to imprecisely targeted intermediate memory
458
      locations would not yield sensible results. Why expect this from Transformers? An LLM’s activation
      space might have direction which represent high-level variables causally involved in what is generated
459
      (Burns et al. 2022; Moschella et al. 2023; Li et al. 2023a; Nanda 2023; Li et al. 2023b). More
460
      specifically, we think that neural networks represent features of the input as directions in activation
461   space (Park et al. 2023). We think that the direction in activation space that corresponds to (say) a
462   love-hate latent variable stays approximately the same across a broad class of inputs.
463
464
      Alain & Bengio 2018 use linear probes on residual streams to infer that LLM representations are
      at least partially linear; if a linear probe can predict some feature of text output from the residuals
465
      with high accuracy, this forms evidence that the feature is represented linearly (i.e. as a simple
466
      direction) (Nanda 2023). The success of activation addition gives stronger, experimental evidence
467   of feature linearity, demonstrating that models use feature-related information. Steering vectors
468   establish causality, at least in the limited set of contexts examined.
469
      Value alignment of LLMs Activation engineering is a promising way to control LLMs. Successor
470
      methods may be able to provide general steering methods (e.g. through some analogue of a Be
471   helpful vector). Alongside contemporaneous work (Li et al. 2023b; Liu et al. 2023), our exper-
472   iments suggest that activation engineering can flexibly retarget LLM behavior without damaging
473   general performance. We speculate that ActAdd changes the model’s currently active mixture of
474   goals and priorities. Suitably developed, the activation engineering approach could enable safety
475   progress while preserving overall capabilities.
476
477
      6    C ONCLUSION
478
479   While methods like prompt engineering, controlled decoding, and finetuning have benefits, they
480   fail to elicit full capabilities from language models. To more reliably elicit these abilities, activa-
481   tion engineering strategically perturbs activations at inference time. In particular, we introduced
482
      Activation Addition to steer models by shifting their inference-time activations along a certain direc-
      tion (like the “Love”−“Hate” vector). ActAdd is lightweight and sometimes effective; we achieve
483
      good results on topic steering and mixed results on toxicity reduction and sentiment shift. ActAdd
484
      demonstrates the potential promise of activation engineering. We look forward to future work
485   realizing this promise and making activation engineering more robust.
