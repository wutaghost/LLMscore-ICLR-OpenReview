010
011
               Time series forecasting models typically rely on a fixed-size training set and treat
012            all data uniformly, which may not effectively capture the specific patterns present
013            in more challenging training samples. To address this issue, we introduce Au-
014            toTSAug, a learnable data augmentation method based on reinforcement learn-
015            ing. Our approach begins with an empirical analysis to determine which parts
016            of the training data should be augmented. Specifically, we identify the so-called
017            marginal samples by considering the prediction diversity across a set of pretrained
018            forecasting models. Next, we propose using variational masked autoencoders as
019            the augmentation model and applying the REINFORCE algorithm to transform
020
               the marginal samples into new data. The goal of this generative model is not only
               to mimic the distribution of real data but also to reduce the variance of predic-
021
               tion errors across the model zoo. By augmenting the marginal samples with a
022
               learnable policy, AutoTSAug substantially improves forecasting performance, ad-
023            vancing the prior art in this field with minimal additional computational cost.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The time series forecasting problem has a wide range of applications, and previous literature has
030   extensively explored this task from various perspectives. Recent deep learning approaches, particu-
031   larly the powerful Transformer model (Vaswani et al., 2017), along with its subsequent models such
032   as AutoFormer (Wu et al., 2021), PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023a), and iTransFormer (Liu et al., 2024),
033
      have shown remarkable capabilities in time series forecasting tasks. However, similar to previous
      learning-based time series forecasting models, these powerful frameworks are typically limited by
034
      the availability of training data. Current augmentation methods for time series data primarily rely on
035
      fixed-form preprocessing and lack effective (Wen et al., 2021; Cheung & Yeung, 2020), learnable
036   data augmentation techniques for high-quality datasets. Since time series forecasting methods are
037   commonly used in online systems with non-stationary streaming data that presents evolving distribu-
038   tions, we believe that developing effective data augmentation techniques is essential for improving
039   the prediction accuracy and generalization ability of existing models.
040
      Nevertheless, implementing fixed-form data augmentation techniques without clear guidance can
041
      introduce additional noise and lead to increased computational costs. Although learning-based data
042   augmentation has significantly advanced the field of computer vision (Cubuk et al., 2019; Shorten
043   & Khoshgoftaar, 2019), it remains under-explored in the context of time series forecasting. In this
044   work, we propose a novel method named AutoTSAug (see Figure 1), which adaptively identifies
045   time series data that can largely benefit from augmentation and automatically searches for optimal
046   augmentation policies based on these samples using reinforcement learning.
047
      The fundamental idea of our approach is to leverage a zoo of pretrained forecasting models, which
048   are readily available in the current deep learning landscape, to establish the data filtering criterion
049   as well as the reinforcement learning objectives. This is inspired by our empirical findings below.
050   When we analyze the fitting errors of multiple models, we can divide the training samples into two
051   main groups: (i) data exhibiting high diversity in prediction errors across the model zoo, and (ii)
052   data with low diversity in prediction errors across the model zoo. An interesting observation is that
053   models trained on the low-diversity subset typically outperform those trained on the high-diversity
      subset when evaluated on the same test set. We provide detailed evidence for this result in Section 3.

      Why is this the case? We believe that when the prediction results from the model zoo are consistent, it
      may indicate two scenarios: either the training samples are too simple or they are too difficult for the
073
      forecasting task. Conversely, when the predictions on the model zoo are diverse, it suggests that the
074
      training process on these data (which we refer to as “marginal samples”) is less stable. Intuitively, the
075   most practical way to improve the training performance is to ensure that it maintains a great learning
076   effect on the easy samples while also improving stability on the marginal samples. Notably, we can
077   find similar learning principles in another field of curriculum learning. Therefore, a straightforward
078   idea is that augmenting marginal samples is more likely to improve model performance, as they
079   offer higher “marginal benefits1 ”. Accordingly, the first contribution of our method is leveraging the
080   model zoo as a diversity criterion to identify marginal samples.
081   Given the identified marginal samples, the next step is to augment the training set using them as
082   anchor points. Revisiting our preliminary findings discussed above, a straightforward idea emerges:
083   since we can achieve better performance by training the model on low-diversity data, why not trans-
084   form the marginal samples by incorporating patterns that are more conducive to training while pre-
085   serving the unique patterns of the marginal data? In other words, we aim for the augmented data to
086   yield more consistent prediction errors on the model zoo. However, because the variance calculated
087   from backtesting the augmented data across multiple forecasting models is not differentiable for
088   the augmentation model (formulated as neural networks), we specifically employ the REINFORCE
089
      algorithm (Williams, 1992) to optimize the augmentation policy. This approach allows us to use the
      variance from the model zoo as the reward function while treating the input tokens of the augmen-
090
      tation model, which takes the form of a variational masked autoencoder (He et al., 2022), as the
091
      actions to be optimized.
092
093   The contributions of this paper are as follows:
094   • We introduce a novel method for learning data augmentation policies for time series forecasting.
095     Our first contribution involves identifying marginal samples by backtesting the training set across
096     a model zoo and using them as anchor points for augmentation. The key intuition is that improving
097     training stability on these marginal samples is crucial for enhancing overall performance.
098
      • The core of our method is to train a generative sequence model to predict suitable transformations
099     of marginal samples through a REINFORCE algorithm. Unlike existing approaches that focus on
100     generating realistic data points, our method automatically searches for optimal data augmentation
101     policies aimed at minimizing the variance of prediction errors across the model zoo.
102
      • Our approach shows significant improvements over common heuristic data augmentation methods
103
        across a wide range of time series datasets, demonstrating that augmenting marginal samples is a
104
        more effective and time-efficient strategy for enhancing model performance.
105

      2.1   T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING M ODELS
111
112   With the remarkable advancements in natural language processing, various sequence-to-sequence
113   methods have emerged to address time series forecasting (Zhang & Yan, 2022; Wang et al., 2022;
114   Cao et al., 2024; Torres et al., 2021). Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Che et al., 2018; Sagheer &
115   Kotb, 2019) are widely utilized in this area. However, due to their sequential architecture, RNNs of-
116   ten struggle with long-term time series forecasting. The Transformer-based models (Li et al., 2019;
117   Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2023) have
118
      also demonstrated superior performance in long-term time series forecasting. For instance, Auto-
      former (Wu et al., 2021) enhances the Transformer by employing a deep decomposition architecture
119
      that progressively separates the trend and seasonal components throughout the forecasting process.
120
      PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023b) vectorizes time series data into patches of a specified size, encoding
121   the resulting sequence of vectors through a Transformer that outputs forecasts of the desired length
122   using an appropriate prediction head. LSTF-Linear (Zeng et al., 2023) was developed to simplify
123   complex time series forecasting problems (Yi et al., 2024) and outperforms many leading Trans-
124   formers by utilizing a set of remarkably simple one-layer linear models. iTransformer (Liu et al.,
125   2024) adopts components with inverted dimensions and a modified architecture, demonstrating su-
126   perior performance on multivariate time series data. Considering both efficiency and accuracy, we
127   select iTransformer as the primary base model for our work and use iTransformer, PatchTST, Aut-
128   oformer, and the original Transformer to construct the so-called model zoo. It is worth noting that
129
      recent literature has introduced large foundation models specifically designed for time series fore-
      casting (Das et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024; Bian et al., 2024). While incorporating these models into
130
      the model zoo for our data augmentation method would be an intriguing avenue for exploration, it
131
      is not the focus of this work.
132
133
      2.2   T IME S ERIES AUGMENTATION M ETHODS
134
135   Various methods for time series augmentation (Demirel & Holz, 2024; Schneider et al., 2024)
136   have been proposed, particularly inspired by image augmentation (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019),
137   demonstrating excellent performance in time series forecasting tasks. Specifically, Iglesias et al.
138   (2023) proposed a taxonomy of augmentation techniques spanning from simple to advanced ap-
139   proaches. Simple transformation methods involve time, frequency, and magnitude domain augmen-
140   tation techniques such as slicing (Cao et al., 2020), frequency warping (Cui et al., 2015), and jitter-
141   ing (Flores et al., 2021). Additionally, advanced generative models have been specifically designed
142   to generate realistic time series data, including the GAN-based approaches (such as TimeGAN (Yoon
143
      et al., 2019) and Conditional Sig-Wasserstein GAN (Liao et al., 2020)), the VAE-based approaches
      (such as Conditional VAE (Sohn et al., 2015) and Smoothness-Including Sequential VAE (Li et al.,
144
      2020)), and Diffusion-based approaches (Huang et al., 2023). The generated samples can be used for
145
      further training of the forecasting models. The third category of data augmentation techniques for
146   time series is the reinforcement learning-based methods (Ko & Ok, 2022; Fu et al., 2022), dynami-
147   cally scheduling data augmentation operations during reinforcement learning. It is important to note
148   that the first category of methods employs fixed data transformation functions, which may introduce
149   significant noise. While the second and third categories are learning-based, they fail to analyze the
150   characteristics that augmented data should possess or to identify the most effective parts of the data
151   to serve as anchor points for augmentation.

193

      4.1              OVERALL T RAINING P IPELINE
196
197   Inspired by the preliminary results, we can broadly categorize the training data into three groups:
198   easy, hard, and marginal samples. We can partially identify these groups by analyzing the variance
199   in prediction errors across a batch of pretrained forecasting models. The idea is to handle each group
200   individually during the augmentation process. For easy samples, we do not perform augmentation,
201   because repeated playback of data with high similarity and strong regularity does not promote the
202   model to fit more complex samples. For marginal samples, due to the varying ability of different
203   models to learn specific data patterns, only a subset of models can effectively capture these patterns
204
      and produce accurate results. In this case, predictions from the model zoo tend to show higher
      variance. For hard samples, most models consistently make biased predictions with large mean errors
205
      but relatively low variance. This can be caused by strong noises or outlier distributions within the
206
      dataset, making these samples less suitable for data augmentation.
207
208   By leveraging the model zoo as a criterion to identify marginal samples—–where model perfor-
209   mances diverge—–we select these marginal samples as anchor points for data augmentation. As
210
      a result, our augmentation efforts will focus primarily on the samples with higher variance in the
      model zoo. Subsequently, the entire training pipeline consists of three stages:
211
212   • Stage A: Train a probabilistic generative model to initialize the neural augmentor, using marginal
213     data and minimizing the reconstruction loss. This model is implemented as a variational masked
214
        autoencoder, which takes partially masked time series data as input and generates complete data.
215   • Stage B: Perform a REINFORCE algorithm (Williams, 1992) to enable the neural augmentor to
        generate data beyond merely replicating the original data distribution.

274   4.3   REINFORCE WITH M ODEL Z OO
275
276   Our fundamental idea is that merely approximating the distribution of the original data is insufficient,
277   as the aforementioned objective function of V-MAE does not provide clear guidance for transform-
278   ing the marginal data. This approach can be viewed as broadly spreading the data distribution of the
279   marginal samples in the hope that it will fortuitously cover important patterns in the test set, rather
280
      than learning a targeted shift for data augmentation.
281   An interesting finding from the preliminary experiments is that training the forecasting model on
282   samples with higher prediction error variances across the model zoo leads to better performance.
283   The model zoo, denoted by M, consists of K forecasting models with pretrained parameters ω1:K .
284   This suggests a potential strategy for tuning the neural augmentor — augmenting the dataset with
285   samples that exhibit lower prediction variance across the model zoo.
286   However, a practical challenge is that the “model-zoo variance” is not differentiable and thus cannot
287   be directly used to optimize the augmentor through gradient descent. To tackle this problem, we
288   propose to finetune the neural augmentor using a REINFORCE algorithm. In this framework, the
289   latent space generated by the prior module in V-MAE serves as the action space. We incorporate
290   the variance of the prediction error across the model zoo, evaluated on the augmented data ŝ1:L , as
291   part of the reward function. As illustrated in Figure 3b, we first backtest ŝ1:L using each forecasting

474   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
475
476   In this paper, we proposed AutoTSAug, a novel data augmentation method driven by reinforcement
477   learning. There are two contributions in methodology: First, our method automatically identifies the
478   critical training data for augmentation, termed the marginal samples, based on prediction diversity
479   from a set of pretrained forecasting models. Second, AutoTSAug exploits a variational masked au-
480   toencoder in conjunction with the REINFORCE algorithm to generate new data from these marginal
481   samples. AutoTSAug significantly boosts forecasting performance while maintaining minimal com-
482
      putational overhead by leveraging a learnable policy to transform the marginal samples.
483   One unresolved issue in this study is the need for multiple pretrained models. The presence of models
484   with poor performance could potentially impact the quality of the generated data. To address this,
485   we incorporate recent competitive models in the model zoo. Future work will focus on enhancing
      the robustness of our approach to effectively adapt to an imperfect model zoo.
