009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                NeSy (Neuro-Symbolic) predictors are hybrid models composed of symbolic pre-
012                dictive models chained after neural networks. Most existing NeSy predictors re-
013                quire either given symbolic knowledge or iterative training. DSL (Deep Symbolic
014                Learning) is the first NeSy predictor that supports fully end-to-end training from
015                scratch, but it learns a look-up table rather than arbitrary programs or formulas.
016                We propose the Logical Formula Learner framework, a general framework of net-
017                work modules that explicitly equate a logical formula after convergence. We then
018
                   propose 3 novel designs within the LFL framework with different levels of combi-
                   natorial search freedom: LFL-Type1 learns arbitrary logical formula, LFL-Type2
019
                   learns a look-up table, and LFL-Type3 has combinatorial search freedom between
020
                   them. LFL-Type1 and LFL-Type2 show improvements over previous designs,
021                and all three types can be wrapped into NeSy predictors. To our knowledge,
022                LFL-Type1-based NeSy predictor is the first NeSy predictor that supports fully
023                end-to-end training from scratch and explicitly learns arbitrary logical formulas. 1

026   1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In a special lecture of NIPS 2019, Yoshua Bengio suggested that studies in deep learning should
029   expand from System 1 to System 2 (Bengio, 2019), where System 1 means the intuitive, black-box
030   parts and System 2 means the symbolic, white-box parts of either human or artificial intelligence.
031   Works in multiple research directions can be seen as attempting to approach System 2 intelligence,
032   such as those combining deep learning with Symbolic Regression (SR) (Makke & Chawla, 2024) or
033   Inductive Logic Programming (ILP) (Zhang et al., 2023) methods, while the term Neuro-Symbolic
034
      (NeSy) refers to the general research field of combining neural systems and symbolic reasoning
      (Marra et al., 2024).
035
036   A subset of NeSy methods is NeSy predictors defined in Marconato et al. (2024), members of
037   which present hybrid models composed of symbolic predictive models chained after neural net-
038   works. Some NeSy predictors combine neural networks with a given symbolic program or knowl-
039   edge base so that the neural network can be trained by symbolic constraints (Manhaeve et al., 2018;
040
      Huang et al., 2021; Badreddine et al., 2022; Winters et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023). Some other
      NeSy predictors attempt to learn the symbolic predictor jointly with the neural networks, most of
041
      them requiring iteratively training the symbolic predictor and the neural networks while fixing the
042
      other (Duan et al., 2022; Cunnington et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023); some of them also require
043   pre-training the neural networks beforehand. Then DSL (Deep Symbolic Learning) (Daniele et al.,
044   2022) presents a fully differentiable NeSy predictor that allows for end-to-end training of a differen-
045   tiable logic module chained after neural networks, both from scratch. The major limitation of DSL
046   is that its differentiable logic module equates a look-up table that maps each of the input concepts’
047   cartesian product to one of the possible output symbols, while many other NeSy predictors learn
048   arbitrary logical circuits or programs.
049   The idea of designing a differentiable module that becomes equivalent to a symbolic expression is
050   also seen in a line of research starting from EQL (Equation Learner) (Martius & Lampert, 2016), a
051   differentiable module designed for SR. An EQL network is a neural network module that equates
052   an arithmetic formula after convergence, archived by introducing diverse activation functions and

106   predictors makes the network struggle to converge, we tackle this problem by adding an MLP as

215   novel designs within the LFL framework, all of which utilize Gödel t-(co)norm and the Concrete
      distribution.

      2.4.2    O UR NEW TRICK : A DDING AN MLP AS GRADIENT SHORTCUT
325
326   In our early experiments, we found that using freely trainable LFL variations like dNL or LFL-Type1
327   in the vanilla architecture results in a network that struggles to converge. A similar convergence
328   problem has been encountered in training deep CNNs, where it was resolved by adding ”shortcut”
329   connections that allow gradients to back-propagate through (He et al., 2016). Inspired by this, we
330
      add an MLP that shares the same input and output with the LFL module so that gradients can
      back-propagate through the MLP, allowing the whole network to converge into an optimal or sub-
331
      optimal solution, as shown in Figure 3(b). The MLP uses sigmoid activation in its output layer for
332
      consistency.
333
334   For recurrent NeSy tasks, the LFL and MLP predict two different versions of the recurrent values
335   c, as shown in Figure 3(d). During training, each of them is randomly selected at sample level with
336
      probability 0.5 for the next recurrence; during inference, the LFL’s prediction is used for the next
      recurrence.

435   Since dNL has been proven to work well on learning simple logical formulas with 2-layer network
436   architectures (Payani & Fekri, 2019a), we evaluate dNL and LFL-Type1 on two possibly harder
437   tasks: learning a 3-layer logical formula with NOT neurons and learning MNIST Sum’s logical

441
      The 3-layer logical formula to be learned has 8 input concepts and 2 outputs. The 8 input concepts
442
      and their negations form 16 input concepts, and the formula then requires at least 8 hidden neurons
443   in an AND layer and 4 neurons in an OR layer to learn (Appendix B). Both dNL and LFL-Type1
444   learn it successfully with the architecture in Figure 4(a)

446   3.1.2    L EARNING MNIST S UM ’ S FORMULA WITH LIMITED NUMBER OF HIDDEN NEURONS
447

449   hidden concepts. Both dNL and LFL-Type1 can learn it from binary training data with 256 hidden
450
      neurons, but LFL-Type1 maintains higher accuracies when the number of hidden neurons reduces
      towards 100 (Figure 4(c)). The network architecture shown in Figure 4(b) is used for this task and
451
      the following experiments where LFL-Type1 is used in NeSy predictors.

      3.2     C OMPARING DSL AND LFL-T YPE 2 ON MNIST S UM
455
      LFL-Type2 has the same combinatorial search freedom as DSL’s logic module and converges as
456   well as the latter on MNIST Sum. A minor limitation of DSL is that its ϵ-greedy search policy keeps
457   producing random label choices with probability ϵ after convergence, creating a significant gap
458   between training and inference behaviors. The limitation doesn’t exist in LFL-Type2’s Concrete
459   noise distribution, as shown in Figure 4(d).

462           S UM
463
464   LFL-Type3 and LFL-Type1 have larger combinatorial search freedom than DSL, and we evaluate
465   them on MNIST Sum and Multi-digit Sum. LFL-Type3 converges well on MNIST Sum and Multi-
466
      digit Sum using DSL’s vanilla network architecture, and it actually converges faster than DSL or

      converges well with our new architecture that contains the MLP gradient shortcut. In MNIST Sum

479   4     R ELATED WORKS
480
481   4.1     N EURO -S YMBOLIC PREDICTORS
482
483   The recent interest in NeSy predictors started with DeepProbLog (Manhaeve et al., 2018), in which
484   programs written in ProbLog (De Raedt et al., 2007) are translated into differentiable arithmetic
485   circuits connected with neural networks, allowing for end-to-end training. ABLSim (Huang et al.,
      2021) trains neural networks by minimizing the inconsistency between them and given background


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      knowledge. Logic Tensor Networks (Badreddine et al., 2022) encode symbolic knowledge into
487   differentiable regularizations for training neural networks. DeepStochLog (Winters et al., 2022)
488   extends DeepProbLog with stochastic definite clause grammars. NeurASP (Yang et al., 2023) im-
489   proves pre-trained neural networks with given Answer Set Programs.
490
      The above methods require given symbolic knowledge, while some other NeSy predictors require
491
      iterative training. DeepLogic (Duan et al., 2022) iteratively trains a non-differentiable Deep Logic
492
      Module with neural networks. NSIL (Cunnington et al., 2022) iteratively trains an ASP program
493   with neural classifiers. NTOC (Liu et al., 2023) iteratively trains neural networks and symbolic rules,
494   where the symbolic rules can be translated into fixed differentiable circuits. Then DSL (Daniele
495   et al., 2022) is our most closely related work, in which a differentiable logic module that equates a
496   look-up table and fits into the LFL framework is wrapped into a NeSy predictor.
497
498
499   4.2   D IFFFERENTIABLE MODULES FOR S YMBOLIC R EGRESSION
500
501   This work takes initial inspiration from EQL (Martius & Lampert, 2016). An EQL network equates
502
      an arithmetic expression after convergence in a way similar to LFL networks, originally proposed
      for Symbolic Regression. EQL÷ (Sahoo et al., 2018) and GMEQL (Chen, 2020) explore different
503
      EQL variations, while OccamNet (Dugan et al., 2020) and KAN (Liu et al., 2024) extend the idea
504
      with more network designs. EQL’s differentiability allows people to connect it with CNNs for joint
505   learning (Kim et al., 2020) and NeSy RL (Luo et al., 2024).
506
507
508   4.3   I NTEGRAGING NEURAL NETWORKS FOR I NDUCTIVE L OGIC P ROGRAMMING
509
510   As another closely related method, dNL fits into the LFL framework and has been experimented
511   on ILP tasks (Payani & Fekri, 2019a). NLN (Payani & Fekri, 2019b) extends dNL with XOR
512   neurons, and Payani & Fekri (2020) uses dNL to incorporate symbolic knowledge for RL. Works
513
      such as ∂ILP (Evans & Grefenstette, 2018), NLIL (Yang & Song, 2019), and DLM (Zimmer et al.,
      2021) also integrate neural networks for ILP tasks. LNN (Riegel et al., 2020; Sen et al., 2022)
514
      proposes customized neurons with Łukasiewicz t-(co)norm for ILP tasks, but their neuron designs
515
      don’t strictly fit into the LFL framework.
516
517
518
      5     C ONCLUTION AND FUTURE WORK
519
520
521   In this work, we present the Logical Formula Learner framework and three novel designs within
522
      it. The LFL framework summarizes previous designs into a general framework of network modules
      that explicitly equate a logical formula after convergence. The proposed LFL-Type1 and LFL-Type2
523
      show improvements over previous designs, and LFL-Type3 works fine with combinatorial search
524
      freedom between the two. Furthermore, by wrapping LFL-Type1 into NeSy predictors with MLP
525   gradient shortcut, we obtain the first end-to-end differentiable NeSy predictor that converges from
526   scratch in one single run and explicitly learns an arbitrary logical formula.
527
528
      Future directions based on this work include:
529
530         1. Experimenting with other possible neuron designs within the LFL framework, such as using
531            other t-(co)norm choices or introducing annealing schedules for the noise scales.
532
            2. Using logical neurons defined in this work in layers other than fully connected ones. For
533
               example, using them in convolutional layers may produce white-box CNNs with better
534            interpretability or adversarial robustness.
535
536         3. Integrating LFL networks into other neural network applications, such as image or video
537            caption, model-free RL, model-based RL, etc. Particularly, integrating LFLs into model-
538            based RL agents may allow such agents to describe their internal representations with sound
539            symbolic logic, opening up a new path toward reliable language generation and understand-
               ing.
