009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, the Gromov-Wasserstein Optimal Transport (GWOT) problem has at-
012            tracted the special attention of the ML community. In this problem, given two
013            distributions supported on two (possibly different) spaces, one has to find the most
014            isometric map between them. In the discrete variant of GWOT, the task is to
015            learn an assignment between given discrete sets of points. In the more advanced
016            continuous formulation, one aims at recovering a parametric mapping between
017            unknown continuous distributions based on i.i.d. samples derived from them. The
018
               clear geometrical intuition behind the GWOT makes it a natural choice for several
               practical use cases, giving rise to a number of proposed solvers. Some of them
019
               claim to solve the continuous version of the problem. At the same time, GWOT
020
               is notoriously hard, both theoretically and numerically. Moreover, all existing
021            continuous GWOT solvers still heavily rely on discrete techniques. Natural ques-
022            tions arise: to what extent do existing methods unravel the GWOT problem, what
023            difficulties do they encounter, and under which conditions they are successful? Our
024            benchmark paper is an attempt to answer these questions. We specifically focus on
025            the continuous GWOT as the most interesting and debatable setup. We crash-test
026            existing continuous GWOT approaches on different scenarios, carefully record
027            and analyze the obtained results, and identify issues. Our findings experimentally
028            testify that the scientific community is still missing a reliable continuous GWOT
029
               solver, which necessitates further research efforts. As the first step in this direction,
               we propose a new continuous GWOT method which does not rely on discrete
030
               techniques and partially solves some of the problems of the competitors.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
      Optimal Transport (OT) is a powerful framework that is widely used in machine learning (Montesuma
035
      et al., 2023). A popular application of OT is the domain adaptation of various modalities, including
036
      images (Courty et al., 2016; Luo et al., 2018; Redko et al., 2019), music transcription (Flamary et al.,
037   2016), color transfer (Frigo et al., 2015), alignment of embedding spaces (Chen et al., 2020; Aboagye
038   et al., 2022). Other applications include generative models (Salimans et al., 2018; Arjovsky et al.,
039   2017), unpaired image-to-image transfer (Korotin et al., 2023b; Rout et al., 2022), etc.

053   In the conventional OT problem (Figure 1a), one needs to find a map between two data distributions
      that minimizes a certain “effort” expressed in the form of an inter-domain transport cost function.

054
      This cost function shows how hard it is to move a point in the source space to a given point in the
055   target space. Thus, in order for the resulting map to possess certain useful properties, one has to
056   incorporate them into the cost function. Unfortunately, this is not always a straightforward task,
057   especially when the data distributions are supported in different spaces.
058   A popular way to address the above-mentioned issue is to consider the Gromov-Wasserstein (GW)
059   modification (Mémoli, 2007; 2011; Peyré et al., 2016) of the OT problem (Figure 1b). Here one
060   assumes that both the source and target spaces are equipped with a structure, e.g., with a metric, and
061   one aims to find a transport map that maximally preserves this structure, i.e., the most isometric map.
062   This clear geometrical intuition behind GW makes it natural in various applications: unsupervised
063   data alignment (Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018; Aboagye et al., 2022), single-cell data processing
064   (Scetbon et al., 2022; Klein et al., 2023; Sebbouh et al., 2024), 2D and 3D shape analysis (Beier et al.,
065
      2022; Mémoli, 2009), graph data analysis (Xu et al., 2019; Vincent-Cuaz et al., 2022; Chowdhury &
      Needham, 2021; Xu et al., 2021; Vincent-Cuaz et al., 2021).
066
067   Discrete/continuous GW. The GWOT problem is about learning some specific translation that
068
      operates with source and target distributions. In practice, these distributions are typically given
      by empirical datasets. This leads to two possible ways of paving a GWOT map. In the discrete
069
      scenario, the learned translation is just a point(s)-to-point(s) assignment (transport matrix). In turn,
070
      the continuous GW is about learning a parametric mapping between the underlining (continuous)
071   distributions. In this case, the datasets are treated as i.i.d. samples derived from them.
072
      While existing computational approaches for the GW problem show considerable empirical success,
073   the problem itself is highly non-trivial from different perspectives.
074
      • Theory. Finding the most isometric map between probability spaces based just on the inner
075
        properties of these spaces may be poorly defined, e.g., the desired transform may be non-unique.
076     This happens where the source (target) space permits some non-trivial isometries that preserve the
077     corresponding source (target) distribution. A simple yet expressive example is the Gaussian case
078     Delon et al. (2022). Intuitively, non-uniqueness may affect the stability of a GWOT solver.
079   • Computations/algorithms. It is known that the discrete GW yields a non-convex quadratic
080     optimization problem (Vayer, 2020), which is computationally challenging. To partially alleviate
081     the difficulty, one typical approach is to consider entropic regularization (Peyré et al., 2016; Alvarez-
082     Melis & Jaakkola, 2018; Scetbon et al., 2022; Wang & Goldfeld, 2023). Fortunately, the regularized
083     problem resorts to a sequence of tractable Sinkhorn OT assignments. However, the convergence
084     of the procedure may not hold, see (Peyré et al., 2016, Remark 3). In addition, discrete GWOT
085     scales poorly with the number of input (source or target) samples, which makes some problem
086
        setups unmanageable by such kinds of solvers. While there are some techniques to reduce the
        computational burden w.r.t data size (Scetbon et al., 2022), they come at the cost of additional
087
        restrictions and assumptions.
088
089
      • Methodology. The majority of existing continuous GW methods are based on discrete GW
        techniques and inherit all the computational challenges of the latter. Moreover, the transition from
090
        the discrete to the continuous setup may be questionable from a statistical point of view (Zhang
091
        et al., 2024).
092
      Having said that, one naturally wonders: how do the current continuous GWOT methods manage to
093
      overcome these problems and show good practical results? What are the “bad cases” under which the
094
      aforementioned difficulties become critical and the solvers struggle? How to fight with these “bad
095   cases”? In our paper, we shed light on these GWOT methods’ ambiguities, specifically focusing on
096   the continuous setup. Our contributions are as follows:
097
      • We conduct a deep analysis of existing papers and reveal that one important characteristic that may
098
        greatly affect practical performance is the considered data setup. In fact, the majority of works
099
        primarily consider datasets with some specific correlations between source and target samples.
100     Formally speaking, such setups disobey the standard i.i.d. assumption on the data and may lead to
101     spoiled conclusions on the solvers’ capabilities.
102   • By following the findings from the previous point, we evaluate the performance of existing
103     continuous GWOT solvers in more statistically fair and practically realistic uncorrelated data
104     setups. Our simple yet expressive experiments witness that (un)correlatedness indeed highly
105     influences the quality of the learned GWOT maps. Changing the data setup may greatly deteriorate
106     the performance of the solvers.
107   • To alleviate the dependence on the mutual statistical characteristics of the source and target training
        data, we propose a novel continuous neural GW solver. On the one hand, our method is not based on

          discrete GW. It may be learned on arbitrarily large datasets and shows reasonably good results even
109       on the fair uncorrelated data setup. On the other hand, the method is min-max-min adversarial,
110       which negatively impacts stability and requires plenty of data for training.
111
112   Overall, our findings reveal that the empirical success of the existing GWOT solvers seems to be a bit
113
      over-estimated and requires to be treated more critically. Constructing a reliable continuous GWOT
      method is a not-yet-solved challenge. We encourage the researchers to further work out on this quite
114
      interesting direction. We hope, that our work is a good amigo in this thorny path.

124   Gozlan et al., 2017; Backhoff-Veraguas et al., 2019) and then introduce the Gromov-Wasserstein OT
125   formulation (Mémoli, 2011; Peyré et al., 2016). Finally, we clarify our considered practical learning

      2.1     O PTIMAL T RANSPORT (OT) PROBLEM
129   Given two probability distributions P ∈ P(Rdx ), Q ∈ P(Rdy ) and a cost function c : Rdx ×Rdy → R,
130   the OT problem is defined as follows:
131
                                                       Z
                                              def
132                                OTc (P, Q) = inf         c(x, T (x))dP(x).                        (1)
                                                  T♯ P=Q        Rdx
133
134   This is known as Monge’s formulation of the OT problem. Intuitively, it can be understood as
135   finding an optimal transport map T ∗ : Rdx → Rdy that transforms P to Q and minimizes the total
136   transportation expenses w.r.t. cost c, see Figure 1a. There have been developed a lot of methods for
137   solving OT (1) in its discrete (Cuturi, 2013; Peyré et al., 2019) and continuous (Makkuva et al., 2020;
138   Daniels et al., 2021; Korotin et al., 2023b; Choi et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023; Uscidda & Cuturi, 2023;
139
      Gushchin et al., 2024; Mokrov et al., 2024; Asadulaev et al., 2024) variants.
140   The cost function c in (1) is commonly the squared Euclidean distance. In this case, problem (1)
141   is exclusively defined for spaces of the same dimensions. Dealing with two incomparable spaces
142   (dx ̸= dy ) may require manually defining some more complex inter-domain cost function c. It is not
143   a trivial task.
144   2.2     G ROMOV-WASSERSTEIN OT (GWOT) PROBLEM
145
146
      The GWOT problem is an extension of the optimal transport problem that aims to compare and
      transport probability distributions supported on different spaces. This problem is particularly useful
147
      when the underlying spaces do not align directly, but we still want to measure and align their intrinsic
148
      geometric structures. In what follows, we introduce the discrete and continuous variants of GWOT.
149

151

152
      distance or similarity between the samples for a given function, i.e., cosine similarity, Euclidean
153   distance, inner product, etc. The discrete GWOT problem is defined as:

      3    E XISTING CONTINUOUS G ROMOV-WASSERSTEIN SOLVERS
189
190   Here we outline the current progress in solving the GWOT problem specifically focusing on the
191   continuous formulation. Most of the GWOT solvers are only discrete or adapted to emulate a
192   continuous behaviour by implementing some specific out-of-sample estimation method on top of
193   the results of some discrete solver. The initial approach to solve the GWOT problem in discrete
194
      case (M2.2) was introduced in (Mémoli, 2011; Peyré et al., 2016). Below we only detail the methods
      which specifically aim to solve the continuous formulation and somehow provide the out-of-sample
195
      estimation.
196
197
      StructuredGW (Sebbouh et al., 2024). In this paper, the authors focus on providing an iterative
      algorithm to solve a discrete entropy-regularized version of the inner product case of (3) for p = 2
198
      using the equivalent reformulation by (Vayer, 2020, maxOT). Every iteration, the coupling matrix T
199
      is updated using Sinkhorn iterations and an auxiliary rotation matrix updates using different possible
200   methods. The authors propose different regularization alternatives for the problem, this directly
201   impacts the way the auxiliary matrix is updated. To perform the out-of-sample estimation, the authors
202   extend their method by drawing inspiration from entropic maps in (Pooladian & Niles-Weed, 2024;
203   Dumont et al., 2024). Their developed StructuredGW method uses one of the dual potentials learned
204   during the updates of T to perform this entropic mapping.
205   FlowGW (Klein et al., 2023). The framework proposed in this work consists in fitting a discrete GW
206   solver inspired by (Peyré et al., 2016) to obtain a coupling matrix T. This coupling matrix helps to
207   figure out the best way to match available samples from source to target domains. Weighted pairs of
208   source and target samples are constructed using the distribution described by the coupling matrix.
209   Then these samples are used to train a Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) model (Lipman et al.,
210   2023) with the noise outsourcing technique from (Kallenberg, 1997). The inference process is done
211   by solving the ODE given by the CFM model.
212   AlignGW (Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018). This work proposes a discrete solver for the alignment
213   of word embeddings. The authors use Sinkhorn iterations to compute the updates on the coupling
214   matrix instead of a linear search implemented in the Python Optimal Transport library (Flamary
215   et al., 2021) that is inspired by (Titouan et al., 2019a; Peyré et al., 2016). This change significantly
      improves the stability of the solver. In spite of being a totally discrete method, we consider it due to its


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      performance in challenging tasks. In order to allow out-of-sample estimations, we train a Multi-Layer
217   Perceptron (MLP) model on the barycentric projections derived from the learned coupling matrix T.
218
      RegGW (Uscidda et al., 2024). This work extends the Monge Gap Regularizer (Uscidda & Cuturi,
219
      2023) within the Gromov-Wasserstein (Monge) framework. The method parameterizes the transport
220   push-forward function T using a neural network. To train this model, a regularized loss function is
221   utilized, which combines fitting loss and Gromov-Monge gap regularizer. The fitting loss ensures
222   that the learned model T maps to the target distribution. It is chosen to be Sinkhorn divergence in
223   practice (Uscidda et al., 2024). In turn, the Gromov-Monge gap is the difference between distortion —
224   the value of discrete Gromov-Wasserstein functional for learned model T , and the actual solution of
225   discrete GW problem between source points and points mapped with T . While other research, such
226   as (Sotiropoulou & Alvarez-Melis, 2024), also incorporates this regularizer, their approach requires
227   access to an intermediate reference distribution, which is impractical for our experimental setup.
228   CycleGW (Zhang et al., 2021). The authors of this work propose to minimize the Unbalanced
229   bidirectional Gromov–Monge divergence (UBGMD) and recover two push-forward Gromov-Monge
230   mappings: f such that f♯ P ≈ Q and g such that g♯ Q ≈ P. This problem is similar in nature to the
231
      Unbalanced Gromov-Wasserstein divergence Séjourné et al. (2020), but it utilizes a cross-domain
      version of (3) which additionally ensures cycle-consistency. To solve the UBGMD problem they
232
      propose to minimize so-called Generalized Maximum Mean Discrepancy (GMMD), in which they
233
      compute the divergences of the unbalanced problem by using Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD)
234   with Gaussian kernels. In our setup, the function f is equivalent to our mapping function T . We refer
235   to the original work for further details. A work guided by a similar concept can be found in Hur et al.
236   (2021) which adds an additional MMD term. Due to the similarities between these two solvers, we
237   decided to only consider the work by Zhang et al. (2021), they have publicly available code.

      4     L IMITATIONS OF EXISTING METHODS
271
272   As it was mentioned before, the majority of existing Gromov-Wasserstein approaches explicitly or
273   implicitly resort to discrete GWOT formulation, see M2.2. Naturally, the computations required to
274   solve (2) increase significantly as the numbers of training samples Nx and Ny grow. This dependency
275   renders some datasets to hardly be manageable by discrete solvers. However, in our paper, we
276
      specifically focus on the other sources of potential failures for GWOT. It stems from the practical data
      setups under which the methods actually work. Below, we give a detailed description of this problem.
277
278   4.1   P ITFALLS OF PRACTICAL DATA SETUP
279   To begin with, we introduce the notion of (un)correlatedness of data which undergoes Gromov-
280   Wasserstein alignment. To fit a GWOT solver, a practitioner typically has two training datasets,
                                      Ny

285   setup under which the source and target datasets X and Y turn out to be connected by some specific
286   statistical relationships. Let us assume that there is a coupling π ∈ Π(P, Q), π ̸= P ⊗ Q. Practically,

288   different from the independent coupling P ⊗ Q. In particular, coupling π may even set a one-to-one
289   correspondence between the domains. Then, we call the training samples X and Y to be correlated

334   4.2      B ENCHMARKING GWOT SOLVERS ON ( UN ) CORRELATED DATA : G LOV E AND BPE MB
               EXPERIMENTS
335
336   In order to check how existing continuous GW solvers perform under uncorrelated, partially and
337   totally correlated setups, we utilize two different text corpora: Twitter and MUSE (Multilingual
338   Unsupervised and Supervised Embeddings) (Conneau et al., 2017) bilingual vocabularies. We then
339
      embed them using either the GloVe (Global Vectors for Word Representation) algorithm (Pennington
      et al., 2014) or BPEmb (Byte-Pair) (Heinzerling & Strube, 2018) embeddings. The Twitter corpus used
340
      was obtained from the GloVe dictionary1 . In the case of MUSE we took the bilingual vocabularies
341
      from their official GitHub repository2
342
343   The GloVe embeddings of words are generated using the GloVe algorithm (Pennington et al., 2014).
344   Its main advantage is that the embedded vectors capture semantic relationships and exhibit linear

      substructures in the vector space. This allows meaningful computation of distances and alignments,

525   6    D ISCUSSION
526
527   The general scope of our paper is conducting in-depth analyses of machine learning challenges that
528   yield important new insights. In particular, we analyze the sphere of Gromov-Wasserstein Optimal
529   Transport solvers, identify the problems and propose some solutions. Our work clearly shows that
530
      while existing Gromov-Wasserstein Optimal Transport methods exhibit considerable success when
      solving downstream tasks, their performance may severely depend on the intrinsic properties of the
531
      training data. We partially address the issue by introducing our novel NeuralGW method. However,
532
      it has its own disadvantages. In particular, it is based on adversarial training which may be unstable
533   and is not guaranteed to converge to an optimal solution of the GW problem. Thereby, our work
534   witnesses that GWOT challenge in ML still awaits its hero who will manage to propose a reliable
535   general-purpose method for tackling the problem.

915   Metrics. We consider three metrics to report: Top k-accuracy, FOSCTTM and cosine similarity. In
916   all cases, we require to know the true pairs of the source vectors in the target domain. These true
917   pairs are assumed to be given in some pre-specified reference pool of samples, e.g., the full 400K
      Twitter-GloVe dataset. Under this assumption, the metrics can be defined as follows:

918
      • Top k-accuracy (↑). Considering the set of Ntest = m vectors from the source distribution and
919     their predictions. For each predicted vector we compute the k-closest (in terms of L2 distance)
920     samples in the reference pool of samples and get a sorted set of k indices {c1 , c2 , . . . , ck }. As
921     we know the indices of the optimal pairs for the reference, we can take the label of the expected
922     optimal pair for any vector in the test source data, this label will be denoted as li . Therefore, we
923     can define the top k-accuracy as follows:

927   • Fraction of Samples Closed Than the True Match (FOSCTTM). (↓) We calculate the Euclidean
928     distances from a designated transported point (y = T (x)) to every data point from the reference
929     set in the target domain. Using these distances, we then compute the proportion of samples that
930     are nearer to the true pair (this information is known). Finally, we take the average of these
931     proportions for all samples. The perfect alignment would mean that every sample is closest to
932     its true counterpart, producing an average FOSCTTM of zero. We note that this metric is rather
933     insensitive to the reference, i.e., considering the whole/random subset of Twitter-GloVe dataset
934     does not affect it much.
935   • Cosine similarity (↑). It is computed between the predicted vector and the reference (optimal pair)
936     vector in the target space.

1079   In this section, we explore the motivation behind using Byte-Pair Embeddings, followed by an
       explanation of how we constructed our new dataset utilizing the MUSE bilingual dictionaries.


                                                            20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       The MUSE embeddings were originally obtained using fastText (Joulin et al., 2016); however, most
1081   of the methods explored in this paper are unable to align these embeddings effectively. In the work
1082   we reference as the AlignGW solver (Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018), the authors report positive
1083   alignment results on this dataset. However, it is important to note that these results may not fully
1084   reflect the true alignment capability of the method, as they incorporate cross-domain similarity local
1085   scaling (CSLS) (Conneau et al., 2017). While CSLS is commonly used in alignment tasks to enhance
1086   inference performance in multilingual translation, it introduces a corrective bias that may inflate the
1087   method’s apparent success. This reliance on CSLS may thus lead to results that do not accurately
1088   represent the method’s intrinsic alignment efficacy. In light of these considerations, we determined
1089
       that a different approach was necessary for embedding the words from the MUSE vocabularies.
1090   We generated the new dataset using the bpemb library3 , which provides pre-trained subword embed-
1091   dings. For a thorough explanation of how these embeddings are derived, we recommend reviewing
1092   the original paper. To increase the chances that a word from the MUSE dictionaries appear in the
1093   BPEmbd vocabularies, we selected the largest available vocabulary size (200K) when loading the
1094   pre-trained embeddings. However, if a word still doesn’t match, we chose to exclude them. Other
1095
       reason to consider BPEmb is the possibility to compute the embeddings in different dimensions. For
       our experiments we only considered English and Spanish as bilingual dictionaries are provided for
1096
       them. We excluded words with several translations and words with no translations. By doing this we
1097
       ensure the obtained dataset of source and target embeddings fits our definition of correlatedness in
1098   Section M4.1.
1099

       D.1 D ISCRETE GW SOLVERS UNDER LOW CORRELATEDNESS DATA SCENARIO
1314
1315   Our experimental results (Section 4.2
1316   of the main text) testify that the dis-
1317
       crete baseline solvers perform unsatis-
       factory when the data correlatedness
1318
       level α tends to zero. We hypothesis
1319
       that the main reason behind this behav-
1320   ior is as follows. Small amount of data
1321   used for discrete solvers hardly could
1322   “catch” the intrinsic geometry of the
1323   underlining distribution. When we ap-
1324   ply discrete GW solver, the Gromov- Figure 11: Discrete GW maps fitted under high (left) and low
                                                                (right) correlatedness level.
1325   Wasserstein mapping is learned be-
1326   tween the geometries induced by sample distributions, not original distributions, see Figure 11
1327   for illustration. These “induced” geometries may be different from the original ones, they may have
1328
       other symmetries and other properties. Matching them may result in Gromov-Wasserstein map which
       is quite different from the real GW map.
1329
1330   On the other hand, when correlatedness level is high (α = 1), the GW problem is reduced to finding
1331   the proper permutation of the data, see Figure 11, left part. The true solution of discrete GW in this
1332   case coincides with the true underlining GW map. If the learned map properly generalizes to new
1333   (unseen) samples, then the resulting performance is expected to be satisfactory. It is the behaviour we
1334
       observe in our experiments, Section 4.2.
