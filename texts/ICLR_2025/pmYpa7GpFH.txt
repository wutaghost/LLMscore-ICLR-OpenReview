009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) optimizer enhances the generalization abil-
012            ity of the machine learning model by exploring the flat minima landscape through
013            weight perturbations. Despite its empirical success, SAM introduces an additional
014            hyper-parameter, the perturbation radius, which causes the sensitivity of SAM to it.
015            Moreover, it has been proved that the perturbation radius and learning rate of SAM
016            are constrained by problem-dependent parameters to guarantee convergence. These
017            limitations indicate the requirement of parameter-tuning in practical applications.
018
               In this paper, we propose the algorithm LightSAM which sets the perturbation
               radius and learning rate of SAM adaptively, thus extending the application scope of
019
               SAM. LightSAM employs three popular adaptive optimizers, including AdaGrad-
020
               Norm, AdaGrad and Adam, to replace the SGD optimizer for weight perturbation
021            and model updating, reducing sensitivity to parameters. Theoretical results show
022            that under weak assumptions, LightSAM could converge ideally with any choices
023            of perturbation radius and learning rate, thus achieving parameter-agnostic. We
024            conduct preliminary experiments on several deep learning tasks, which together
025            with the theoretical findings validate the the effectiveness of LightSAM.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Machine learning has achieved significant success across various application domains. As a critical
031   component of machine learning, many optimization approaches are explored to train the model
032   efficiently. However, most of the previous works only focus on minimizing the training loss, which
033
      would face the dilemma of over-fitting since the popular models are over-parameterized. Recently,
      there has been a raised attention on generalization ability since it represents the prediction ability on
034
      unseen data, thus very crucial for a model. Keskar et al. (2016); Neyshabur et al. (2017) study the
035
      relationship between the flatness of loss landscape and generalization ability, which consequently
036   suggests finding flat minima that have low curvature in the neighbourhoods.
037
038   The above idea is formalized as a novel minimax problem, named Sharpness-Aware Minimization
039
      (Foret et al., 2020). The main difference from the original loss function is that Sharpness-Aware
      Minimization has a step that maximizes the loss function in the neighbourhood. This consideration
040
      of worst-case guarantees the low loss value in a region, thus making the loss landscape of minima flat
041
      and improving generalization ability, which results in the novel SAM optimizer: in each iteration,
042   a weight perturbation is performed along the gradient direction with radius ╧Б, then the stochastic
043   gradient on the perturbed weight is used in gradient descent with learning rate ╬╖ to update the model.
044   SAM significantly improves the test performances of several deep networks (Foret et al., 2020).
045
      The convergence rates of SAM and its variants have been extensively analyzed in existing works
046
      (Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2022; Mi et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024). However,
047
      these theoretical results require restrictions on two hyper-parameters of perturbation radius ╧Б and
048   learning rate ╬╖, either upper bounded or unequal relationship between them. These restrictions usually
049   involve some problem-dependent constants, such as the Lipschitz constant, whose value could not be
050   obtained a prior and hard to be estimated. In addition, though it is proved that the normalization in
051   the perturbation step makes SAM less sensitive on ╧Б (Dai et al., 2023), the empirical studies in the
052   above works show that the sensitivity to the learning rate still exists and the adopted values are not
053   stable. These shortcomings make it necessary to do parameter-tuning in empirical studies, which
      increases cost especially when training large-scale models. Thus, we raise a question that:

                                      Can we make SAM parameter-agnostic1 ?
055
056   In fact, parameter-agnostic algorithms are thoroughly studied in online learning to avoid parameter-
057   tuning (Orabona, 2014; Cutkosky & Boahen, 2017; Orabona & Tommasi, 2017). Recently, Defazio
058   & Mishchenko (2023) suggest to use Adagrad-like step size to achieve learning-rate-agnostic. Wang
059   et al. (2023b) and Wang et al. (2023a) prove the ideal convergence rate for adaptive optimizers.
060   These motivate us to introduce adaptive learning rate into SAM to realize parameter-agnostic. Note
061   that directly introducing adaptivity for both the perturbation radius and learning rate is technically
062   non-trivial. This is due to that the terms need to be bounded would involve two gradients in one
063
      iteration, and the relationship between them is hard to establish since the randomnesses in one term
      could not be decoupled directly in the proof for adaptive methods.
064
065   In this paper, we study how to make the SAM optimizer parameter-agnostic. To achieve this goal,
066   we propose an algorithm LightSAM. We provide three options for LightSAM, and in each option,
067   we adopt one commonly used adaptive optimizer to perform weight perturbation and model update
068   instead of SGD in vanilla SAM. As a consequence, both the weight perturbation and model update
069
      become adaptive during training. Specifically, we adopt the AdaGrad-Norm-type learning rate
      for LightSAM, named LightSAM-I, which uses a scaler-type adaptive learning rate for both the
070
      perturbation ascent step and gradient descent step (╧Б, ╬╖). In addition, we also consider the AdaGrad-
071
      type and Adam-type learning rate for LightSAM, named LightSAM-II and LightSAM-III respectively,
072   which use coordinate-wise    learning rates for two hyper-parameters (╧Б, ╬╖). Theoretically, we prove
073
                   тИЪ
      the O(ln T / T ) convergence rate for LightSAM without any restrictions on perturbation radius and
074   learning rate, thus achieving parameter-agnostic optimizers. Additionally, we only require nearly the
075   weakest assumptions among related studies.

      Adaptive Optimizer. Adaptive optimizers make the learning rate adjust adaptively during the
129
      training process. Duchi et al. (2011) propose Adagrad, which accumulates the gradient second
130
      raw moment, i.e. the square of historical gradients, and makes the learning rate of each element
131   inversely proportional to the square root of this sum. RMSProp (Tieleman, 2012) suggests adopting
132   an exponential moving average for the stochastic gradients to make adaptive optimizer work well in
133   deep learning. Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) further introduces the exponential moving average to the
134   gradient second raw moment and becomes the most commonly used adaptive method.
135
      It is showed that Adagrad could converge in bothтИЪconvex and non-convex settings (Li & Orabona,

      Parameter-Agnostic Optimization. Parameter-agnostic (also known as parameter-free) algorithms
      are studied to achieve the optimal regret bound for the online optimization problem at first (Orabona,
148
      2013; McMahan & Orabona, 2014; Orabona & P├бl, 2016). Kernel-based SGD (Orabona, 2014)
149
      performs model selection and optimization without prior knowledge of problem and parameter-tuning.
150   Orabona & Tommasi (2017) remove the learning rate from the gradient descent step to optimize the
151   objective function. Carmon & Hinder (2022) focus on stochastic optimization and select the learning
152   rate by a computable certificate. As a result, a nearly optimal convergence rate and parameter-agnostic
153   are both achieved. D-Adaptation (Defazio & Mishchenko, 2023) adopts Adagrad-like learning rate
154   to iteratively lower bound the distance between the initial and optimal point . Normalized SGDM
155   (H├╝bler et al., 2024) converges with a nearly optimal rate in the (L0 , L1 )-smoothness setting.
156   The above mentioned SAM-related works adopt SGD optimizer in weight perturbation or model
157   update or both, which makes the parameters lack of adaptivity, and adaptive optimizer-related works
158   seldom consider enhancing the generalization ability. Our work improves this by making both the
159   perturbation radius and learning rate adaptive, and further parameter-agnostic. The most related work
160   to this paper is Sun et al. (2024). However, it only employs the adaptive learning rate in the gradient
161   descent step. Furthermore, their analysis requires the gradient bound assumption, which is too strong
      to be satisfied for practical applications (Nguyen et al., 2018). We also notice SA-SAM (Naganuma

519   5    C ONCLUSION
520
521
      In this paper, we propose an algorithm LightSAM for non-convex optimization. LightSAM sets
522   the perturbation radius and learning rate adaptively through adopting Adagrad-Norm, Adagrad, and
523   Adam, respectively. We make a solid   тИЪ theoretical analysis for our proposed algorithm and observe
524   that it converges with the O(ln T / T ) rate without requiring the gradient bounded assumption.
525   Particularly, our result does not require perturbation radius and learning rate satisfying any conditions,
526   realizing parameter-agnostic optimizers. Finally, we conduct experiments in several computer vision
527   tasks. The superiority of LightSAM to other baselines and the insensitivity to hyper-parameters are
528   verified. Thus, we prove the potential of our work in reducing the necessity of parameter tuning from
529   both theory and experiments.
