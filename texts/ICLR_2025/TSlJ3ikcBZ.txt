009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large autoregressive models like Transformers can solve tasks through in-context
012            learning (ICL) without learning new weights, suggesting avenues for efficiently
013            solving new tasks. For many tasks, e.g., linear regression, the data factorizes:
014            examples are independent given a task latent that generates the data, e.g., linear
015            coefficients. While an optimal predictor leverages this factorization by inferring
016            task latents, it is unclear if Transformers implicitly do so or if they instead exploit
017            heuristics and statistical shortcuts enabled by attention layers. Both scenarios
018            have inspired active ongoing work. In this paper, we systematically investigate the
019
               effect of explicitly inferring task latents. We minimally modify the Transformer
               architecture with a bottleneck designed to prevent shortcuts in favor of more
020
               structured solutions, and then compare performance against standard Transform-
021
               ers across various ICL tasks. Contrary to intuition and some recent works, we
022            find little discernible difference between the two; biasing towards task-relevant
023            latent variables does not lead to better out-of-distribution performance, in general.
024            Curiously, we find that while the bottleneck effectively learns to extract latent
025            task variables from context, downstream processing struggles to utilize them for
026            robust prediction. Our study highlights the intrinsic limitations of Transformers in
027            achieving structured ICL solutions that generalize, and shows that while inferring
028            the right latents aids interpretability, it is not sufficient to alleviate this problem.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Recent achievements of large language models (LLMs) showcase the Transformer architec-
034   ture’s (Vaswani et al., 2017) capacity to extend beyond predictive modeling and solve novel tasks at
035   inference time (Bubeck et al., 2023). However, several papers (Tang et al., 2023; McCoy et al., 2019;
036   2023) find that rather than modeling the tasks themselves, Transformers rely on shortcuts that risk
037   generalizing poorly to new datasets and tasks. In this work, we aim to shed light on whether learning
038   the true task parameters over shortcuts is sufficient to aid generalization. In particular, we consider
039
      the specific case of in-context learning (ICL): the ability of Transformers to leverage demonstrations
      within their input sequence to adapt to novel queries, effectively learning from contextual cues.
040
      ICL underpins much of the capabilities of modern LLMs, including prompt engineering and
041
      chain-of-thought, but is difficult to study in LLMs directly due to the multitude of factors that affect
042   performance and lack of control over the training data. To disentangle the role that task latents
043   play in ICL from other aspects that influence large, pre-trained autoregressive models, we therefore
044   introduce controlled experimental settings that involve training Transformers from scratch on tasks
045   that are sufficiently complex, but where the relevant latent variables are well-understood.
046
      Many tasks naturally admit a parametric approach to ICL that breaks the prediction mechanism
047
      into two parts: 1) inferring the task-dependent latent variables from the context, and then 2) using
048   them to make predictions on novel queries. For instance, in a linear regression task, a model could
049   first try to infer the underlying weight vector used to generate the data, and subsequently use these
050   inferred weights to make predictions for query points. Another viable methodology, closer in spirit
051   to non-parametric approaches, is to directly compare the query point with the context through
052   a kernel-based mechanism – weighting predictions based on some learned measure of distance
053   between the different points. The two paradigms, parametric vs. non-parametric, reflect tradeoffs
      between the potential for better generalization or increased flexibility (Russell & Norvig, 2010).

071   Recent studies show that Transformers are indeed able to solve ICL tasks through the parametric
072   mechanisms in some cases (Hendel et al., 2023; Todd et al., 2024). However, in most cases, evidence
073   shows that they instead rely on non-parametric mechanisms where the prediction is made by directly
074   comparing the query point to exemplars in the context (Wang et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023), a mech-
075
      anism very much related to induction heads (Olsson et al., 2022). This could be explained by the fact
      that the functional form of attention operations is almost identical to that of kernel regression (Tsai
076
      et al., 2019), making such solutions more natural for Transformers to express (Zhou et al., 2023).
077
      These solutions are considered statistical shortcuts since they might not be able to generalize to OOD
078   contexts and queries – for e.g., learning the actual linear predictor for linear regression can generalize
079   to any distribution over training and test points, but nearest-neighbour based interpolation might not.
080
081
      In this paper, we aim to test the hypothesis that a limiting factor of ICL in Transformers is
      their tendency to prefer non-parametric shortcuts over more structured inference. To do so, we
082
      perform a thorough analysis through tasks for which latent mechanisms are well understood, and
083
      systematically analyse the impact of latent task representation on generalization. We minimally
084   modify the Transformer architecture to prevent such non-parametric shortcuts and compare the
085   OOD performance of the resulting model to that of a traditional Transformer on a large array of ICL
086   tasks. We call this altered architecture an explicit model by virtue of its inductive bias of explicitly
087   extracting structured latent variables to solve the tasks, and we call the traditional Transformer
088   architecture an implicit model. Specifically, the explicit model prevents the query from directly
089   attending to demonstrations in the context by introducing a bottleneck between the processing of the
090   context and the query (see Figure 1), similar to a conditional neural process (Garnelo et al., 2018a).
091   We find that the explicit model does not outperform the implicit one on OOD data, challenging the
092   aforementioned hypothesis that avoiding non-parametric solutions would enhance generalization.
093   Our investigation into this lack of improvement reveals that the issue often lies in the explicit
094   model’s prediction function, which is tasked with leveraging the inferred latent variables for
095   downstream predictions on the query. Our controlled experiments and analysis on the interpretable
096   nature of the bottleneck revealed strong evidence that while the explicit model often extracts
097   relevant task latents, these are not properly utilized by the prediction function.
098   While on one hand, our research demonstrates that using a simple bottleneck in a Transformer
099   can improve interpretability and explicitly extract task-relevant latent variables, it also suggests
100   that the limitations of Transformers in learning more structured and generalizable ICL solutions
101   are not solely due to non-parametric shortcuts that skirt latent variable inference, but due to more
102   fundamental architectural limitations.
103   In sum, our contributions are:
104
105
             • Formalizing an experimental framework to evaluate the hypothesis that parametric ICL solu-
               tions generalize better out-of-distribution.
106
             • Analyzing the benefits, or lack thereof, of inferring the true latents explicitly.
107
             • Identifying shortcomings in the prediction function and the downstream utilization of learned
               latents in Transformers, which leads to poor generalization.

      2   N OTATION
109
110   Throughout the paper, we denote datasets with the symbol D which consists of a set of observations
111   with inputs denoted via x ∈ X and their corresponding outputs as y ∈ Y. A task is defined by a
112   functional mapping g : X , Z → Y which maps observations x to labels y through some latents
113
      or parameters z, eg. y = z T x for a linear regression task, or y ∼ N (·; z T x, σ 2 ) for its stochastic
      counterpart. To ease readability, we will reserve x∗ ∈ X for the query point, i.e. the test time
114
      observation we want to generalize to, and y∗ ∈ Y its corresponding target. Finally, ψ denotes the
115
      parameters of context aggregation component of explicit model, which inputs the dataset D and
116   infers the corresponding parameters zψ (D), and γ the parameters of the prediction model which
117   given a query x∗ and parameters z ∈ Z, provides the prediction. For the implicit model, these
118   operations are subsumed into a single model, with parameters φ.
119
120   3   I MPLICIT VS . E XPLICIT I NFERENCE
121
      We look at ICL in the context of algorithmic problems where the task is to predict the target y∗
122   from a query point x∗ when provided with some context examples D = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 , sharing a
123   common underlying structure defined by the task latent z and a functional form g. The goal of ICL
124   is to learn a function that can utilize the context set D to provide predictions for new query points
125   x∗ . To achieve this, the model is trained on different draws of context sets (D1 , D2 , ...) which share
126   the same underlying functional mapping g : x, z → y but different realizations of the latent z,
127   for example g(x, z) = z T x could be a linear regression system shared across different contexts
128   D1 , D2 , ..., but the underlying latents could be different, i.e. D1 is generated from z1 while D2
129   from z2 , similar to Von Oswald et al. (2023). We emphasize that in this setup, we are not training
130
      models to do next-token prediction as is done in language modeling; instead, given a fixed context
      D that includes n samples, we are attempting to make a prediction on a single novel query x∗ . We
131
      therefore do not use a causal Transformer, and we allow all tokens to attend to each other.
132
133   Often, ICL solutions are learned via maximum likelihood, i.e.

137   through g. Thus, the transformer model pφ must not only learn the form of the prediction function
138   g, but also how to efficiently aggregate information from the context D to infer z for downstream
139   predictions on x∗ . Thus, this general framework can be naturally decomposed into two distinct parts.
140
      Context Aggregation. This component deals with inferring the task-dependent latent vari-
141
      ables from the in-context examples such that the downstream prediction becomes conditionally
142
      independent of the context, i.e. inferring z from D such that p(y∗ | x∗ , z, D) = p(y∗ | x∗ , z).
143
144   Predictive Modeling. This component refers to the process of estimating the predictive function
145   that leverages context D to infer y∗ from a query x∗ . In the above example, it refers to learning the
146
      functional mapping g once z has been extracted from context aggregation.
147   As discussed, Transformers do not have a clear incentive to make this explicit separation of
148   context aggregation and predictive modeling. Instead, given context D, they implicitly and jointly
149   model both the function g along with D-dependent latent variable z inference to directly provide
150   predictions for the query point x∗ , in contrast to separately estimating g and an explicitly factorized
151   z. Thus, in order to enforce explicit representation of z, we propose the simplest architectural mod-
152
      ification where the query x∗ cannot directly attend to the context, and the latent task representation
      is forced to summarize the context efficiently. Formally, we compare the following two models,
153
      which are illustrated in Figure 1.
154

156   former models. In this setup, given the set of observations D (context) and a query point x∗ , the
157   prediction y∗ is modeled directly as pφ (y∗ |x∗ , D), where pφ is defined using a standard Transformer
158
      with parameters φ and is tasked with modeling both context aggregation and predictive modeling.
159   Explicit Model. This represents the architectural variation which minimally modifies the Trans-
160   former architecture by separating context aggregation and predictive modeling. It first constructs a
161   task representation zψ (D) using the set of observations D and a context model zψ with parameters
      ψ (context aggregation) and another network pγ to make a prediction for a new point x∗ (predictive

187   weights ψ, and for the prediction function pγ , we study both Transformers and MLPs with weights

190   attending directly to the context as in standard Transformers; instead, the context model must
191   summarize D into underlying generative factors, thus ruling out potential shortcut solutions that
192   bypass latent variable inference.
193   Implicit vs. Explicit. Assuming Transformers do in fact favour shortcut-based solutions, we first
194   hypothesize when each setup should perform better given different task characteristics. If the data is
195   generated with a linear model (i.e. y = z T x), the right predictor can be precisely described using the
196   weight vector z, making the explicit model better suited. In contrast, if the data is generated with a
197   Gaussian Process (GP), the implicit model should be superior since by construction query prediction
198   computes similarities with all points in the context. In this case, the task latents of GP-based data
199   with RBF kernel is infinite dimensional (i.e. a point in function space), which cannot be captured
200
      in the finite-dimensional bottleneck of the explicit model. In general, we should expect the explicit
      model to be superior when the underlying true model is parametric and low-dimensional, but in case
201
      of a non-parametric or very high dimensional parametric model, the implicit model should be better.
202
203   Finally, we note that our aim is not to construct the best possible explicit model architecture – indeed,
204   more sophisticated ones already exist based on amortized Bayesian inference (Garnelo et al., 2018b;
205   Mittal et al., 2023). Instead, we are interested in investigating potential inductive biases for ICL
206
      by minimally modifying the standard Transformer architecture to remove certain shortcuts from the
      space of possible solutions. We leave the design of more performant architectures for future work.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488   A commonly believed hypothesis is that Transformers do ICL through brittle statistical shortcuts
489   rather than by inferring the underlying generative latent variables of the task, and that this
490   explains their inability to generalize outside of the training distribution. Here, we empirically
491
      tested this hypothesis by minimally modifying the Transformer architecture through the use of a
      bottleneck that factorized the model into separate context aggregation and prediction functions,
492
      creating an inductive bias for explicit latent variable inference. While we confirmed that this
493
      model indeed learned to infer the correct latent variables across many ICL tasks, it surprisingly
494   gave no improvement in performance for either in-distribution or out-of-distribution evaluation.
495   Contrary to common belief, then, we showed that simply learning the correct latent variables for
496   the tasks is not sufficient for better generalization because end-to-end optimization does not learn
497   the right prediction model to leverage these latent variables. Indeed, when we substituted the
498   prediction function with a known oracle, we often saw significant advantages in OOD performance.
499   Importantly, we were able to make such findings because we performed controlled experiments on
500   simple tasks with known latent variables and prediction functions, thus avoiding the complexities
501   and confounding variables that come with natural language and pre-trained LLMs. Our results point
502
      to a line of future work that incorporates inductive biases in the prediction model to better leverage
      the inferred latent variables, and motivates current efforts in improving (a) amortized methods for
503
      in-context prediction (e.g., Garnelo et al., 2018a;b; Kim et al., 2019; Hollmann et al., 2022) and
504
      (b) neurosymbolic AI which tries to combine flexible LLMs for inference with highly structured
505   functions for prediction (e.g., Wong et al., 2023; Mialon et al., 2023; Ellis, 2023).
506
507   Limitations. While our work investigates potential reasons behind the shortcomings of current ICL
508
      approaches, overcoming those shortcomings is an entirely different and bigger problem which is a
      relevant future direction but not an achievement of the current work. We also look at ICL solely
509
      from the perspective of algorithmic tasks with models trained from scratch, without exploring in
510
      detail the connections with ICL in pre-trained models.
511
512   Broader Impact. Our approach paves the way into an interpretable model for in-context learning,
513   which could prevent harmful use of such models by additionally encoding constraints on the inter-
514
      pretable bottleneck. Additionally, our work provides a better understanding about ICL which can
      lead to safer future models.

758   In-Context Learning. In-Context learning (ICL) is an ability of certain trained models to take an
759   entire task’s dataset as input and parameterize solutions directly in their layer activations, which
760   then condition subsequent computation on novel inputs from those same tasks. Generally, this
761
      ability is found in sequence models such as Transformers where the task dataset, or “context”,
      corresponds to an earlier part of the sequence. ICL was first observed in large-scale pre-trained
762
      LLMs (Brown et al., 2020), and is similar in many respects to meta-learning (Chan et al., 2022).
763
      These LLM findings were subsequently expanded to more controlled settings outside of the
764   language modality, where Transformer models were directly trained on task distributions such
765   as linear regression (Von Oswald et al., 2023; Garg et al., 2023), hidden Markov models (Xie
766   et al., 2022), compositional grammars (Hahn & Goyal, 2023), regular languages (Akyürek et al.,
767   2024) and Turing machines (Grau-Moya et al., 2024), with a set of task observations defining
768   the “context”. These works highlight that Transformers are indeed able to model many types
769   of complex task distributions, approaching in many cases the performance of the Bayes-optimal
770   predictor, the a-priori optimal solution (Xie et al., 2022). Our work lies along similar lines but using
771   more complex tasks and a systematic study into the differences between modeling the predictive
772
      space directly, or through a two-step process involving explicit inference of task latents.
773   Shortcuts in ICL. Shortcut learning is a phenomenon that has widely been observed in machine
774   learning (Geirhos et al., 2020), and refers to where a model solves a task through statistical corre-
775   lations that are accidental and thus not robust to even slight distribution shifts. A classical example
776   of this in image classification is the usage of background cues to classify objects (Ribeiro et al.,
777   2016). Similar mistakes are know to be very common in NLP (McCoy et al., 2019), specifically in
778
      reasoning tasks (Zhang et al., 2022). Particularly relevant to our work, many authors have shown
      that has shown that Transformers are very prone to relying on shortcuts when performing ICL Tang
779
      et al. (2023). For instance, Olsson et al. (2022); Singh et al. (2024) have shown that induction heads
780
      play in important role in ICL by predicting that the continuation of a token will be the same as last
781   time (i.e. [a][b] . . . [a] → [b]). As shown by Von Oswald et al. (2023) this motif can be used to do
782   linear regression, and can generally be seen as a form of kernel regression (i.e. p(yq |xq , x1:n ) ∝
      P
         i K(xi , xq )yi , (Han et al., 2023)). This observation draws a link between those types of solutions
783
784   and non-parametric inference methods in statistics (Hastie, 2009), of which kernel regression is a
785   member. In contrast (Hendel et al., 2023; Todd et al., 2023) have concurrently shown that in some
786   cases Transformers encode a “task vector” that they infer from the context and then use to do the
787   prediction. There is therefore a need to better understand the nature of shortcuts in ICL and whether
788   or not they can be easily avoided for better generalization. Our work explores this very question.
789   Neural Processes. The problem of solving new tasks in a zero-shot manner directly at inference
790   is also closely tied to amortized Bayesian models (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Rezende et al.,
791   2014; Radev et al., 2020; Geffner et al., 2023; Mittal et al., 2023). Conditional Neural Processes
792   (CNPs) (Garnelo et al., 2018a) provide a framework akin to the explicit model, where the posterior
793   predictive distribution is modeled through a bottleneck zψ , i.e. pθ (y∗ |x∗ , D) = pθ (y∗ |x∗ , zψ (D)).
794   However, CNPs do not look at the relevance of zψ to the true latent z, and use the DeepSets (Zaheer
795
      et al., 2017) architecture to model zψ , though recent research generalizes this setting to use
      Transformers Nguyen & Grover (2023) and other architectural backbones as well (Kim et al.,
796
      2019; Gordon et al., 2019). Our approach with the explicit model, however, is to precisely question
797
      whether task-specific latents are encoded via zψ which is now instead modeled using a Transformer
798   architecture. Analogously, Neural Processes (NPs) (Garnelo et al., 2018b; Pakman et al., 2020) aug-
799   ment CNPs with probabilistic modeling, where z is now modeled explicitly       R as a latent-variable in
800   the Bayesian sense, i.e. the likelihood is now modeled as pθ (y|x∗ , D) = pθ (y|x, z)pθ (z|D)dz,
801   where z represents the latent variable and θ the parameters of the likelihood model. The model
802   is trained via the Evidence Lower-Bound (ELBO) with the amortized variational approximation
803   qφ (·|D). Once trained, predictions for new datasets can be made by simply performing inference
804   over the encoder qφ to obtain z, and then leveraging this latent variable to eventually give the
805   predictions via pθ (y|x∗ , z). Hence, while CNPs and the explicit model to share similarities in
806
      architecture, our goal is orthogonal in that we specifically use the explicit model to understand the
      impact of task-specific latent variable inference on ICL setups.

924

925

926   preceding layer to give the successive output, i.e. xl = gzl (xl−1 ). This task is compositional in
927   nature because at each layer, any of the K experts can be called upon to perform a unit of com-
