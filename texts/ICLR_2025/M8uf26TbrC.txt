009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable success across
012            a wide range of language tasks, but their deployment on edge devices remains
013            challenging due to the substantial memory requirements imposed by their large
014            parameter sizes. Weight-only quantization presents a promising solution to reduce
015            the memory footprint of LLMs. However, existing approaches primarily focus on
016            integer-bit quantization, limiting their adaptability to fractional-bit quantization
017            tasks and preventing the full utilization of available storage space on devices. In
018
               this paper, we introduce Channel-Wise Mixed-Precision Quantization (CMPQ), a
               novel mixed-precision quantization method that allocates quantization precision
019
               in a channel-wise pattern based on activation distributions. By assigning different
020
               precision levels to different weight channels, CMPQ can adapt to any bit-width con-
021            straint. CMPQ employs a non-uniform quantization strategy and incorporates two
022            outlier extraction techniques that collaboratively preserve the critical information,
023            thereby minimizing the quantization loss. Experiments on different sizes of LLMs
024            demonstrate that CMPQ not only enhances performance in integer-bit quantization
025            tasks but also achieves significant performance gains with a modest increase in
026            memory usage. CMPQ thus represents an adaptive and effective approach to LLM
027            quantization, offering substantial benefits across diverse device capabilities.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large Language Models (LLMs), trained on massive text corpora and containing up to hundreds of
033
      billions of parameters, have demonstrated exceptional performance across a wide range of language
      tasks (Brown, 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Du et al., 2022; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Thoppilan
034
      et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a). However, deploying LLMs directly on devices for inference
035
      presents a significant challenge due to their enormous parameter sizes (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Xia
036   et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b). For instance, GPT-3 (Brown, 2020), with 175 billion
037   parameters, requires 350 GB of memory in FP16 precision, which far exceeds the capacity of the
038   latest NVIDIA H100 GPU with 96 GB of memory, let alone the capabilities of edge devices.
039
      Low-precision weight-only quantization (Park et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024)
040
      has emerged as a promising solution to address this challenge by converting model weights from
041
      high bit-width representations (e.g., FP16) to lower bit-widths (e.g., 3-bit), significantly reducing
042   the memory requirements for on-device LLM inference. In this work, we focus on Post-Training
043   Quantization (PTQ) (Dettmers et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024),
044   which quantizes the pre-trained models without the need for retraining – a process that is often
045   costly and resource-intensive. Most existing PTQ methods concentrate on integer-bit quantization
046   and employ a uniform low-bit-width representation across all layers (Lin et al., 2024; Chee et al.,
047   2024; Frantar et al., 2022), as illustrated in Figure 1(a), yielding promising performance in low-bit
048   quantization tasks. However, these methods are limited in their adaptability to devices with additional
049   storage capacity. For example, they can only offer solutions constrained to integer-bit quantization
050
      (e.g., 2-bit) even if the device could support models with an average of, e.g., 2.2-bit precision, failing
      to utilize the extra storage space to further reduce quantization loss.
051
052   Mixed-precision quantization (Ma et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2019; Zhang et al.,
053   2021) inherently supports fractional bit quantization by allowing model weights to be quantized at
      different precisions. However, few works have focused on mixed-precision quantization specifically

080   observe that different channels in the weight matrix have varying impacts on model performance, and
081
      that assigning higher precision to salient channels can enhance the performance of quantized LLMs.
      Inspired by this, CMPQ performs mixed-precision quantization on a channel-wise basis, as shown in
082
      Figure 1(c). Specifically, we compute the L2 -norm of the activation for each layer and allocate high
083
      (or low) precision to channels with large (or small) activation norms. For the quantization process,
084   we adopt a non-uniform quantization approach for each channel, accounting for the non-uniform
085   nature of weight distributions. To further improve performance, we design two outlier extraction
086   methods that separately focus on preserving activation-based outliers and quantization-aware outliers.
087   Our main contributions are summarized as follows.
088
089   • We propose CMPQ, a mixed-precision quantization method designed to adaptively quantize LLMs
090     to any specified bit-width. CMPQ performs channel-wise quantization, allocating precision based
091     on activation distributions to optimize the performance.
092   • CMPQ incorporates a non-uniform quantization strategy along with two outlier protection methods
093     that collaboratively preserve critical weights in high precision, thereby reducing quantization loss.
094   • We conduct extensive experiments to empirically validate the effectiveness of CMPQ, highlighting
095     two key advantages: (1) channel-wise quantization based on sensitivity significantly improves
096
        performance in integer-bit quantization tasks, and (2) a modest increase in storage overhead results
        in substantial performance gains.

      Post-Training Quantization Quantization is a model compression technique that modifies the
102   vector or matrix representations of a pre-trained model to improve inference efficiency. It can be
103   broadly categorized into two workflows: Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) (Liu et al., 2023; Xu
104   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c; Dettmers et al., 2024) and Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) (Yao et al.,
105   2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Hooper et al., 2024). QAT involves retraining the model
106   while adjusting its parameters during quantization, which is resource-intensive and often impractical
107   for LLMs. In contrast, PTQ focuses on quantizing pre-trained models without the need for retraining,
      making it a more feasible approach for resource-constrained scenarios. However, the complexity of


                                                               2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      LLMs necessitates specialized approaches for effective quantization (Zhou et al., 2024). We focus
109   on weight-only PTQ methods (Lee et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024; Dettmers et al., 2023; Tseng et al.,
110   2024). One of the early advancements in this domain is GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022), an improvement
111   over OBQ (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022), which determines the optimal quantization order per row of
112   the weight matrix based on reconstruction error relative to the Hessian matrix of unquantized weights.
113   QuIP (Chee et al., 2024) further refines GPTQ by introducing an optimal adaptive method for a
114   quadratic proxy objective. This method enhances quantization effectiveness by ensuring incoherence
115   between the weight and Hessian matrices, achieved through random orthogonal matrix multiplication.
116   AWQ (Lin et al., 2024) addresses the varying importance of weight channels for performance
117
      by employing a reparameterization technique, selecting coefficients via grid search to minimize
      reconstruction errors efficiently. SqueezeLLM (Kim et al., 2024) approaches quantization by storing
118
      outliers in a full-precision sparse matrix while applying non-uniform quantization to the remaining
119
      weights. Although these methods demonstrate promising performance in low-bit quantization tasks,
120   they are limited in their adaptability to devices with additional storage capacity. Specifically, they
121   cannot be easily extended to fractional-bit tasks, which prevents them from leveraging extra storage
122   to further reduce quantization loss and improve model performance.
123
124   Mixed-Precision Quantization Quantizing models to low precision uniformly can lead to sig-
125   nificant accuracy degradation (Habi et al., 2020; Qu et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2021). To address this,
126   Mixed-Precision Quantization (MPQ) has emerged as a promising approach, aiming to reduce model
127   size and computational costs while maintaining or even improving accuracy by assigning different
128   bit widths to weights (Wang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2024a; Rakka et al., 2022). Existing methods
129
      primarily focus on relatively small DNNs, such as MobileNet (Wang et al., 2019; Howard, 2017) and
      ResNet (Wu et al., 2018; He et al., 2016), and argue that MPQ is necessary for different layers, as
130
      each layer exhibits varying levels of redundancy and performs differently on hardware (Wang et al.,
131
      2019). To determine the appropriate per-layer precision, these methods rely on techniques like Neural
132   Architecture Search (NAS) (Wu et al., 2018), periodic function regularization (Naumov et al., 2018),
133   and second-order sensitivity analysis (Dong et al., 2019; 2020; Yao et al., 2021). However, these
134   approaches are difficult to generalize to the quantization of LLMs due to the extensive computational
135   resources and large search space they require (Gholami et al., 2022). While protecting outliers
136   in FP16 can be considered a form of mixed-precision, few studies have applied mixed-precision
137   strategies to the majority of weights. LLM-MQ (Li et al., 2023) uses integer programming to allocate
138   layer-wise precision based on first-order information. However, as discussed in Section 3.1.2, it
139   struggles to capture the varying sensitivities of individual layers. To overcome the limitations of large
140
      search spaces and performance degradation, we propose a channel-wise mixed-precision strategy that
      protects salient channels within each layer, ensuring the preservation of critical information.

      3.2     C HANNEL -W ISE M IXED -P RECISION Q UANTIZATION
210
211   Our primary objective is to develop an algorithm that effectively utilizes mixed-precision quantization
212   to adaptively compress LLMs under any given average bit constraint, including fractional bit-widths.
213   Additionally, we still aim to achieve strong performance on integer-bit quantization tasks, such as 3-bit
214   quantization, compared with existing works. To this end, we propose Channel-Wise Mixed-Precision
215   Quantization (CMPQ). In this section, we first introduce the channel-wise non-uniform quantization
      method, followed by a detailed explanation of our outlier protection strategy.

      3.2.1   C HANNEL -W ISE N ON -U NIFORM Q UANTIZATION
217
218   From the observations in Section 3.1.2, we can draw two key intuitions: ❶ channel-wise mixed-
219   precision quantization can enhance model performance compared to layer-wise mixed-precision
220   quantization, and ❷ when implementing channel-wise quantization, it is advisable to limit the number
221   of 2-bit channels to minimize the information loss. Based on these, we propose channel-wise
222   non-uniform quantization.
223   Research has shown that the weight distributions in LLM layers exhibit non-uniform patterns.
224   Previous approaches have primarily focused on uniform quantization (Chee et al., 2024; Frantar
225   et al., 2022), which divides the weight range into evenly spaced bins. However, this approach is
226   suboptimal, as it fails to account for the non-uniform nature of the weight distributions, and struggles
227   to improve end-to-end latency in memory-bound LLM inference (Kim et al., 2024). Following
228   Kim et al. (2024), we adopt non-uniform quantization. Specifically, for each channel Wi,: in the
229   weight matrix W ∈ Rdin ×dout , we apply a K-means clustering algorithm, where the value of K
230
      is determined by the precision assigned to the channel (e.g., K = 8 for 3-bit quantization). After
      clustering, each weight in W is represented by its nearest centroid from the set of K centroids
231
      {q1 , . . . , qK }.

267   Another key challenge in low-bit LLM quantization is the protection of outlier values (Bondarenko
268   et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2022). Previous studies have demonstrated that naively
269   quantizing weights with a large dynamic range significantly degrades performance, particularly at low
      precisions (Kim et al., 2024). However, in some cases, retaining a small fraction (less than 1%) of

273   we retain 0.5% of outliers in high precision (16-bit), while applying quantization to the remaining

275   quantization-based outliers.

309   3.2.3    D ISCUSSION
310
      Our method only requires forward propagation and does not depend on backpropagation, which is
311   necessary for many existing quantization techniques (Li et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024). Consequently,
312   the memory requirements for our proposed CMPQ during quantization are moderate; for instance,
313   loading the OPT-6.7B model necessitates 12.4 GB of memory, whereas the backward pass for
314   the same model requires 49.61 GB of memory. Additionally, CMPQ has minimal reliance on the
315   calibration set, as it only measures the L2 -norm per channel, thereby mitigating the risk of overfitting.
316   For a comparison with backpropagation-dependent methods, refer to Section 4.4, and for an analysis
317   of CMPQ’s robustness with respect to the calibration dataset, see Section 4.5.

      5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
529
530
531
      In this work, we focused on mixed-precision quantization and aimed to design an algorithm capable of
      adapting to any bit-width constraint. We observed that different weight channels had varying impacts
532
      on model performance, and that activation distributions helped identify salient channels. Building
533
      on these insights, we proposed CMPQ, which integrated a channel-wise non-uniform quantization
534   strategy. To further enhance performance, CMPQ introduced two types of outliers that collaboratively
535   preserved critical information. Experimental results showed that CMPQ harnessed the potential of
536   mixed-precision quantization in two key ways: (1) it achieved superior performance in integer-bit
537   quantization tasks, and (2) it delivered significant performance improvements with only a modest
538   increase in memory requirements. In the future, our focus will be on deploying CMPQ-quantized
539   LLMs on real-world devices. This will involve addressing key challenges such as engineering
      hardware acceleration and designing efficient lookup table kernels to optimize performance.
