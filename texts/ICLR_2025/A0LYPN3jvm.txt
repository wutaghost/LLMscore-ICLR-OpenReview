011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recently, estimating 3D hand pose and shape from monocular images has gar-
014            nered significant attention from researchers, which finds numerous applications
015            in animation, AR/VR, and embodied AI. Many tasks in the field of computer
016            vision have demonstrated the substantial benefits of incorporating additional task-
017            relevant reference information to enhance model performance. In this paper, we
018
               investigate whether the principle of “the more you know, the better you under-
               stand” also applies to the task of two-hand recovery. Unlike previous methods
019
               that rely solely on monocular image features for hand estimation, we extract 2D
020
               keypoints, segmentation map, and depth map features and then integrate them with
021            image features. The hand regressor subsequently estimates hand parameters based
022            on the fused features. The 2D keypoints and segmentation maps provide detailed
023            finger XY-dimensional reference information for the hand, while the depth map
024            offers pixel-level relative Y-dimensional reference information. Recovering the
025            3D hand from these intermediate representations should be more straightforward
026            than doing so solely from the original RGB image. Current foundation models
027            have already achieved impressive performance on these basic tasks, allowing us
028            to obtain reliable results in most cases. However, when the two hands overlap sig-
029
               nificantly, resulting in complex entanglements. In such cases, hand penetration is
               likely to arise. The additional reference information (segmentation map and depth
030
               map) cannot assist with the occluded regions, and the predicted 2D keypoints for
031
               the occluded areas are also unreliable. To this end, we further employ a two-hand
032            diffusion model as a prior and employ gradient guidance to refine the two-hand
033            contact. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our approach achieves superior
034            performance in 2D consistency alignment and depth recovery.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      3D two-hand recovery aims to reconstruct both hands of a person in 3D space, a crucial task for
040
      numerous emerging applications, including 3D character animation, augmented and virtual reality
041
      (AR/VR) and robotics. Large-scale hand datasets (Moon et al., 2020; 2024) have greatly facilitated
042   numerous studies on hand recovery. These approaches can be summarized as focusing on scaling
043   up datasets (Pavlakos et al., 2024), improving backbone (Lin et al., 2021; Pavlakos et al., 2024), and
044   incorporating attention mechanisms (Li et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024) between the
045   hands.
046
      Recent developments in computer vision point towards a trend where advances are driven by the in-
047
      corporation of additional task-relevant reference information. For instance, ECON (Xiu et al., 2023)
048   introduces rendered front and back body normal images as input for human digitization, enabling
049   the model to effectively infer high-fidelity 3D humans in loose clothing and challenging poses. In
050   the task of 3D human motion recovery, WHAM (Shin et al., 2024) utilizes 2D human keypoints to
051   extract motion features as inputs, leading to more robust and stable 3D human motion estimation.
052   More additional detailed input allows the model to gain a deeper context understanding of the task
053   while minimizing uncertainty and ambiguity. Currently, whether integrating additional informative
      cues can enhance model performance in the domain of hand recovery remains unexplored.

073   In this paper, we adopt the philosophy “the more you know, the better you understand” and apply it
074   to the problem of 3D two-hand estimation. We primarily consider incorporating 2D keypoints, seg-
075
      mentation maps, and depth maps as additional reference inputs. The rationale behind this is that 2D
      keypoints provide precise spatial information about hand joints in the XY plane, segmentation maps
076
      offer a detailed understanding of hand shape and boundaries, while depth maps supply pixel-level
077
      depth cues, helping to better capture the hand’s relative positioning in 3D space. Specifically, we
078   leverage the foundation model to extract 2D keypoints, segmentation maps, and depth maps from
079   monocular images. Then, using neural networks extract features from these intermediate informa-
080   tion and fuse them with image features. The hand regressor subsequently estimates hand parameters
081   based on the fused features.
082
      Although the additional 2D information has provided more detailed references for hand estimation
083
      models, we consider a special case where one hand occludes important fingers of the other hand,
084   leading to penetration issues between the estimated hands. In such cases, the three types of addi-
085   tional information mentioned above are ineffective in providing valid references for the occluded
086   parts. The diffusion model has achieved considerable success across various fields and has been
087   demonstrated to effectively learn the data distribution as a prior. In the field of human estimation,
088   BUDDI (Müller et al., 2024) reconstructs two individuals in close proximity by utilizing the diffu-
089   sion model as a prior. Similarly, DPoser (Lu et al., 2023) constructs a robust human pose diffusion
090   model for pose generation, pose completion, and motion denoising. Inspired by this, we use a pre-
091   trained two-hand diffusion model (Lee et al., 2024) as the prior to address hand penetration issues.
092
      Given an initial regression two-hand estimate, we invert it to the corresponding noise. Then, we
      propose a penetration gradient guidance during the denoising process.
093
094   Extensive ablation experiments further validate the improvements in model performance brought by
095   the incorporation of additional 2D information and the use of diffusion. Our qualitative experiments
096   in real-world scenarios also demonstrate superior alignment and depth recovery capabilities. As
097   shown in Figure 1, we achieve robust hand reconstruction across various scenes and interactive
098
      poses.
099   Our key contributions can be summarized as follows.
100
101         • We propose incorporating additional 2D reference information for the 3D hand recovery
102           task, including 2D keypoints, segmentation maps, and depth maps. Specifically, after align-
103           ing with the image space using a visual backbone model, we employ a simple transformer
104
              encoder to integrate these features.
105         • When one hand is occluded by another, leading to potential hand penetration issues. The
106           additional 2D reference information does not assist with the occluded regions. In this
107           context, we propose utilizing a pretrained diffusion model as a prior and employing gradient
              guidance to address the hand penetration issue.

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110
      2.1   3D HAND RECOVERY
111
112   With the introduction of some high-quality hand datasets, recovering 3D hand MANO (Romero
113   et al., 2017) parameters from monocular input images has recently achieved remarkable advance-
114   ments. For single-hand recovery, METRO (Zhang et al., 2019) employs a convolutional neural
115   network to extract a single global image feature and performs position encoding by repeatedly con-
116   catenating this image feature with the 3D coordinates of a mesh template. MeshGraphormer (Lin
117   et al., 2021) introduces a graph-convolution enhanced transformer to effectively model both local
118
      and global interactions. AMVUR (Jiang et al., 2023) proposes a probabilistic approach to estimate
      the prior probability distribution of hand joints and vertices. Zhou et al. (Zhou et al., 2024) sim-
119
      plifies the process by decomposing the mesh decoder into a token generator and a mesh regressor,
120
      achieving high performance and real-time efficiency through a straightforward yet effective baseline.
121   HaMeR (Pavlakos et al., 2024) highlights the significant impact of scaling up to large-scale train-
122   ing data and utilizing high-capacity deep architectures for improving the accuracy and effectiveness
123   of hand mesh recovery. For two-hand recovery, IntagHand (Li et al., 2022) propose a GCN-based
124   network to reconstruct two interacting hands from a single RGB image, featuring pyramid image
125   feature attention (PIFA) and cross hand attention (CHA) modules to address occlusion and interac-
126   tion challenges. InterWild (Moon, 2023) bridges MoCap and ITW samples for robust 3D interacting
127   hands recovery in the wild by leveraging single-hand ITW data for 2D scale space alignment and
128   using geometric features for appearance-invariant space. ACR (Yu et al., 2023) explicitly mitigates
129
      interdependencies between hands and between parts by leveraging center and part-based attention
      for feature extraction. 4DHands (Lin et al., 2024) handles both single-hand and two-hand inputs
130
      while leveraging relative hand positions using a transformer-based architecture with Relation-aware
131
      Two-Hand Tokenization (RAT) and a Spatio-temporal Interaction Reasoning (SIR) module. Al-
132   though these methods have generally achieved good results in hand pose and shape reconstruction,
133   their performance in finer details is still lacking. Following the adage “the more you know, the better
134   you understand,” we explore the integration of additional 2D information to guide 3D hand recovery.
135
136   2.2   I NTEGRATING A DDITIONAL I NFORMATION
137
138   Recently, many studies have attempted to introduce additional reference information as guidance
139   in visual tasks to achieve better performance. For example, in the task of human digitization,
140   ECON (Xiu et al., 2023) takes as input an RGB image and is conditioned on the rendered front
141   and back body normal images. This strategy allows it to excel at inferring high-fidelity 3D humans
142   in loose clothing and challenging poses. For text-to-image generation, ControlNet (Zhang et al.,
143
      2023) has also successfully utilized different types of conditional inputs, such as sketches, depth
      maps, and segmentation maps. It has successfully achieved the generation of images aligned with
144
      these conditional guides using a pretrained text-to-image diffusion model. These impressive results
145
      demonstrate the powerful capability of neural networks to fit guiding information. For the 3D human
146   motion estimation task, WHAM (Shin et al., 2024) uses human 2D keypoints to extract motion fea-
147   tures as inputs for both the Motion Decoder and Trajectory Decoder. This approach achieves more
148   robust and stable 3D human motion estimates in global coordinates. Currently, whether incorpo-
149   rating additional guiding information can enhance hand recovery performance remains unexplored.
150   We attempt to use a foundation model (Khirodkar et al., 2024) to obtain intermediate 2D hand in-
151   formation, including keypoints, segmentation maps, and depth maps, to achieve more robust hand
152   estimation.

186   3.1   H AND RECOVERY WITH ADDITIONAL INFORMATION
187
188   Previous methods take a monocular image as input and utilize a backbone network to extract image
189
      features for the regression of hand MANO parameters. We explore incorporating additional infor-
      mation to enhance the regressor’s ability to fit two-hand parameters more effectively. Given that
190
      the hand recovery task involves reconstructing a 3D hand model, clean and direct 2D information
191
      may facilitate the model’s understanding and fitting. Therefore, we consider using 2D keypoints,
192   segmentation maps, and depth maps. Specifically, we input the monocular image into the human
193   foundation model Sapiens (Khirodkar et al., 2024) to obtain these results. In the following subsec-
194   tions, we analyze these types of information in detail.
195
      2D Keypoints. 2D keypoints provide precise locations of important hand features (like joints and
196
      fingertips), enabling better understanding of hand poses. They reduce the complexity of the data
197
      by focusing on key points rather than the entire image, making feature extraction more efficient.
198   Accurate estimation of 2D keypoints for both hands remains a challenge for current baseline models
199   when there is significant hand overlap. Therefore, to ensure stability during inference, we reduce
200   reliance on keypoint information when the IoU between the hands exceeds 0. In such cases, we use
201   only the wrist points for hand positioning. As shown in our pipeline, the yellow arrow indicates
202   the IoU threshold check. To align for feature fusion in image space, we first visualize the keypoint
203   data as an RGB image, and then employ a visual backbone network to extract the features of the
204   hand’s 2D keypoints. The visual backbone network also provides pre-trained weights that facilitate
205   effective training for extracting keypoint features.
206   Segmentation map. Segmentation maps provide pixel-level information, allowing for precise local-
207   ization of hand and its parts. They also help isolate hands from the background, reducing noise and
208   distractions. By focusing on segmented areas, models can extract relevant hand features more effec-
209   tively. It is worth noting that when there is significant interleaving of the two hands, the prediction
210   results for the 2D keypoint pairs of the hands can be quite unreliable. However, the segmentation
211   map can still provide reasonably accurate 2D contour information of the hands at this time (the hand
212
      segmentation map does not distinguish between the left and right hands when the Intersection over
      Union (IoU) for both hands is greater than 0). We also use a visual backbone network to extract the
213
      features of the segmentation map.
214
215   Depth map. Depth maps provide information about the distance between the hands and the camera,
      helping to capture the relative positioning and spatial relationship of the hands in a real environment.

      3.2   T WO - HAND REFINING WITH TWO - HAND DIFFUSION MODEL
240
241   Although the additional information discussed in the previous section provides more detailed ref-
242   erences for the hand estimation model, we still consider a special case where one hand occludes
243   the important fingers of the other hand. In this scenario, the three types of additional information
244   mentioned earlier are unable to provide effective guidance for the occluded parts of the hand. Con-
245   sequently, the estimated occluded regions of both hands are prone to penetration issues. To address
246   this, we introduce a pre-trained two-hand diffusion model as a prior to adjust the predicted results,
247   using the penetration gradient as guidance. we employ the unconditional version of the two-hand
248   generation diffusion model from (Lee et al., 2024), which denoise hand noise into MANO parame-
249
      ters. As shown in Fig. 2, before using the two-hand diffusion model, we perform an IoU and pene-
      tration check between the two hands to reduce unnecessary diffusion inference in most cases. The
250
      gradient-guided two-hand diffusion effectively alleviates the penetration problem of the occluded
251
      regions.
252
253   Two-hand penetration refining with gradient guidance. Given an estimated two hand result, we
254   first invert it to the corresponding noise. We then introduce a gradient-guided strategy to prevent
255
      hand penetration. The two-hand diffusion model operates in a cascaded manner, first denoising one
      hand and then using the denoised hand as a condition to denoise and generate the second hand. Since
256
      the visible hand is often reconstructed more accurately, we use it as the conditional hand for miti-
257
      gating interpenetration for another hand. We calculate the collision loss between it and the occluded
258   hand from each step of reverse diffusion and guide the occluded hand to move in the direction of
259   the negative gradient. Specifically, during each denoising step, we generate clean occluded hand
260   parameters X̂0 from the current noisy occluded hand Xt−1 through the DDIM sampling process.
261   These parameters, along with the reference hand for interpenetration, are then input into the MANO
262   model to obtain the mesh vertices Vt−1 and Vc ∈ R778×3 . The formula is as follows:

286
287   4.1   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
288
289
      We implement our network using PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019). For the image feature extractor, we
      use ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) as the backbone, while for additional 2d information extractors, we
290
      use ResNet-18. Our model is trained on 4 A100 GPUs using the AdamW optimizer, starting with an
291
      initial learning rate of 1e-4, which is reduced by a factor of 10 at the 4th epoch. We use a mini-batch
292   size of 48. Data augmentations such as scaling, rotation, random horizontal flip, and color jittering
293   are applied during training. The resolution of cropped hand image is 256 × 256. During inference,
294   the hand bounding box detector utilizes RTMDet (Lyu et al., 2022). For other details, we follow
295   the approach in (Moon, 2023), where the hand bounding boxes are enlarged by a factor of two
296   to ensure that the entire hand image is captured for feature extraction. The left hand is flipped to
297   align with the right-hand input to the network, and after prediction, the results are flipped back. The
298   3D relative distance between the hands is predicted based on a 2.5D image. Our training dataset
299   includes InterHand 2.6M (Moon et al., 2020), Re:InterHand (Moon et al., 2024), COCO whole-
300
      body (Jin et al., 2020), FreiHand (Zimmermann et al., 2019) and HO-3D (Hampali et al., 2020). For
      testing, we primarily use InterHand 2.6M, FreiHand and the in-the-wild dataset HIC (Tzionas et al.,
301
      2016).
302
303
      4.2   DATASETS
304
305   InterHand 2.6M (Moon et al., 2020) features both precise human (H) and machine (M) 3D pose
306   and mesh annotations, encompassing 1.36 million frames for training and 850,000 frames for test-
307   ing. Re:InterHand (Moon et al., 2024) is created by rendering 3D hands with precisely tracked 3D
308   poses and applying various environment maps for relighting. The dataset consists of 739K video-
309   based images and 493K frame-based images from third-person viewpoints, and 147K video-based
310   images from egocentric viewpoints. COCO WholeBody (Jin et al., 2020) extends the COCO
311   dataset (Lin et al., 2014) by adding comprehensive whole-body annotations. It includes manual
312   annotations covering the entire human body. FreiHand (Zimmermann et al., 2019) is a dataset
313
      designed for single-hand 3D pose estimation, providing MANO annotations for each frame. It in-
      cludes 4 × 32,560 frames for training and 3,960 frames for evaluation and testing. HO-3D (Hampali
314
      et al., 2020) focus on hand-object interactions, comprising 66,000 training images and 11,000 test
315
      images across 68 different sequences.HIC (Tzionas et al., 2016) offers a variety of hand-hand and
316   object-hand interaction sequences, along with 3D ground truth meshes for both hands. We do not
317   use HIC during training. Consequently, we believe that testing on HIC demonstrates the model’s
318   generalization ability.

445   4.5   A BLATION STUDY
446
447   The main contributions of our method lie in the introduction of additional 2D information for two-
448
      hand pose estimation and the proposal of a two-hand refinement module based on a diffusion model.
      To validate the effectiveness of these contributions, we conducted comprehensive ablation studies
449
      on the InterHand 2.6M dataset in Table 4. We also additionally report the MPJPE/MPVPE-XY
450
      and MPJPE/MPVPE-Z metricswhich can effectively demonstrate the improvements contributed by
451   different types of information to the model’s performance.
452
453   Effectiveness of different information inputs. As shown in Table 4, we gradually added different
454
      types of information for fusion to observe their impact on performance. We observed that adding 2D
      keypoints and segmentation maps resulted in significant improvements in MPJPE and MPVPE, with
455
      the greatest reduction in the XY dimension estimation error. It is easy to understand that 2D key-
456
      points and segmentation maps provide excellent XY dimension information cues. Furthermore, we
457   found an interesting phenomenon that the improvement from incorporating 2D keypoints was greater
458   than that from segmentation maps. This is because 2D keypoints provide more fine-grained 2D in-
459   formation about each joint of the hand, while segmentation maps only provide contour information.
460   Moreover, after fusing depth maps, we observed significant improvements in MPJPE/MPVPE-Z and
461   MRRPE. Depth maps can clearly help the model better reason about the hierarchical relationships
462   of the 3D hand.
463   Effectiveness of using a two-hand diffusion model. Table 4 also demonstrates the impact of the
464   two-hand diffusion model on hand recovery performance. We can see that after adding diffusion,
465   MRRPE, MPJPE, and MPVPE all achieve improvements, with increases of 0.13 mm, 0.22 mm, and
466   0.25 mm, respectively, and with the same improvement trend in both the XY and Z dimensions. The
467   two-hand diffusion model can effectively learn the reasonable distribution of two-hand data, which
468   can help adjust the relative relationship between the hands to a more reasonable state.

471   5     C ONCLUSION
472
473   In this paper, we propose a two-hand reconstruction method that integrates additional 2D reference
474   information to improve hand alignment and depth recovery performance. Furthermore, when one
475   hand is occluded by another (making the 2D reference information for the occluded hand unreliable),
476   we introduce the use of a two-hand diffusion model as a prior to address the penetration issue.
477
      Extensive qualitative and quantitative experimental results demonstrate that our method significantly
      outperforms previous two-hand and single-hand reconstruction approaches. Limitation and Future
478
      Work: One limitation of this work is that the inference speed may be slower compared to the
479
      direct estimation of hand parameters. This is because our approach requires running an additional
480   foundation model to infer 2D information input. Although the introduction of two-hand diffusion
481   may further reduce inference speed, we have implemented a hand penetration detection mechanism
482   that can filter out unnecessary denoising diffusion processes. We believe that with the increasing
483   efficiency of base models and advances in GPU hardware, this issue of inference speed can be
484   mitigated. Additionally, our method still faces challenges in handling extreme occlusions and motion
485   blur in hand images, as the additional 2D information may become unreliable under such conditions.
      We believe that future integration of temporal processing could effectively alleviate this problem.
