009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent studies have shown that neural networks are vulnerable to adversarial at-
012            tacks, where attackers generate adversarial samples by imposing tiny noise. The
013            tiny noise can not misguide human perception, though leading the neural net-
014            works to generate wrong predictions. Transfer-based black-box attacks play a
015            more significant role in recent studies due to their more realistic setting and con-
016            siderable progress in performance. Previous studies have shown that some differ-
017            ent channels of the same layer in convolution neural networks (CNN) contain lots
018
               of repetitive information, and we find that existing transferable attacks tend to ex-
               ploit those redundant features more, which limits their transferability. Hence, we
019
               advocate using channel pruning and knowledge distillation to conduct model aug-
020
               mentation. In addition, we introduce a method of regularization on the gradients
021            of intermediate feature maps of augmented models, which further enhances the
022            transferability of our method. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate that im-
023            posing our method of model augmentation on existing methods can significantly
024            improve the transferability of adversarial attacks in untargeted or targeted scenar-
025            ios. Furthermore, our method outperforms state-of-the-art model augmentation
026            techniques without the usage of additional training datasets.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have become the mainstream backbone for various computer vision
032   tasks, including image classification (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), object detection (Girshick et al.,
033
      2015), segmentation (Farabet et al., 2012), and so on. However, prior studies (Goodfellow et al.,
      2014) have shown that DNNs are vulnerable to adversarial attacks, where tiny noise imperceptible to
034
      humans is imposed on original images to misguide the DNN model prediction. Therefore, research
035
      regarding adversarial attacks is not only crucial for the security and robustness concern of DNN
036   models (Carlini & Wagner, 2017) but also helpful for distinguishing between humans and computers
037   (Shi et al., 2021; Lee et al.).
038
039
      Adversarial attacks are categorized into two different settings. In the white-box setting, attackers can
      fully access information about the target model, including model architecture, parameters, training
040
      data, and loss function. On the other hand, Recent studies have mainly focused on the black-box
041
      setting, which is more realistic in the real world. It requires that attackers do not have any internal
042   details about target models. In most situations, we need to generate adversarial samples on a surro-
043   gate model and transfer them to other target models, called transfer-based attacks. Lots of studies
044   focus on improving the transferability of adversarial attacks in the black-box setting.
045
      To boost the transferability of adversarial attacks, we design a method of model augmentation in-
046
      spired by the internal characteristic of most Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) - based models.
047
      Prior studies (Roheda & Krim, 2020) have shown that CNN models are often “over-parameterized,”
048   which means that they contain lots of redundant channels in each layer. Feature maps given by some
049   channels are highly close to those given by other channels or a composite of other channels. From
050   the top row of Figure 1, we can clearly observe that some of the shallow feature maps from the
051   original model contain rich information but look very similar to each other. On the contrary, some
052   of them are vague and seem to only contain weak features about the object in the input image. If
053   we only conduct adversarial attacks based on original CNN models, the adversarial samples tend to
      “overfit” on those highly repetitive features. Namely, adversarial samples disrupting those features


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      more will achieve locally optimized results on surrogate models but might not cause the same effect
055   on target models. Moreover, via the illustration from the bottom row of Figure 1, we find that the
056   attacks on original models disrupt the feature of a specific channel with much more intensity than
057   they do on the other ones. Namely, the attacks on original models are not distributed evenly on each
058   channel. This behavior makes the attacks less robust and prone to model-specific features.
059
      To address these problems, we propose Gradient Regularization and Structured Channel Pruning
060
      (GRASP). We apply channel pruning to a surrogate model and then ensemble multiple copies of
061   the pruned networks with different pruning rates to form an enhanced version of the source model.
062   (Section 4.1) We reference previous studies (He et al., 2019) on channel pruning and adopt the
063   previous criterion proposed to select the “unnecessary” channels, which will be pruned afterward.
064
      Most of the channel pruning methods need a huge training dataset to fine-tune the pruned models.
065
      Otherwise, the pruned models usually perform too badly to be applicable. This requirement is not
066
      practical in real scenarios because attackers usually have no access to the training dataset of tar-
067   get models. Hence, we utilize the technique of few-shot knowledge distillation (Xu et al., 2020)
068   instead of traditional fine-tuning to recover the classification accuracy of the pruned models (Sec-
069   tion 4.2). To balance the importance of each channel in adversarial attacks, we further introduce a
070   new method of regularization to the gradients of intermediate feature maps (Section 4.3). Through
071   the visualization in Figure 1, we find that applying our methods of initial parameter optimization and
072   regularization makes the pruned surrogate models contain denser information. Namely, those vague
073   and weak features contain richer information related to the geometry of objects in input images.
074   More importantly, the distortion of the feature maps caused by adversarial attacks is more evenly
075
      distributed to all channels except for the repetitive ones, which are pruned beforehand.
076   In summary, our primary contributions include:
077
078          • We propose a novel approach of model augmentation leveraging channel pruning, knowl-
079            edge distillation, and gradient regularization to improve the transferability of adversarial
080
               attacks.
081          • We provide an intuitive perspective and explanation of the limits of the existing algorithm,
082            which is related to the “distribution” of attacks on different channels. In addition, we
083
               introduce a method to overcome the limits.
084          • Comprehensive experiments have shown that applying our approach to existing algorithms
085            of adversarial attacks significantly improves transferability. Moreover, our method outper-
086            forms the state-of-the-art method of model augmentation without additional training data.

      2.1   A DVERSARIAL T RANSFERABILITY
091
092   Current progress in the transferability of adversarial attacks mainly relies on input augmentation-
093   based approaches, gradient-based approaches and model enhancement. In input augmentation-based
094   methods (Xie et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2021), we apply some trans-
095   formation to the original inputs to make them contain more versatile and diversified information,
096   then using an ensemble of transformed images for optimization.
097   On the other hand, gradient-based methods (Dong et al., 2018; Lin et al., 2019; Wang & He, 2021;
098   Qin et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023) focus on modifying and regularizing the process
099   of gradient propagation to improve its stability and avoid poor local optima. For example, Wang
100   & He (2021) consider the variance of gradients in the previous iteration to stabilize the direction of
101   gradients in each iteration. Qin et al. (2022) propose a bi-level min-max optimization process. Xu
102   et al. (2023) adjust the path of backpropagation by decomposing convolution kernels and structural
103   reparameterization.
104   We mainly focus on model augmentation technique (Li et al., 2020; Liang & Xiao, 2023; Li et al.,
105   2023; Wu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), where some modification is imposed on the original
106   source model to make the adversarial attacks based on it more transferable. Usually, an ensemble of
107   multiple modified versions of the source model is used. Note that the model augmentation technique
      can be combined with other input-based or gradient-based approaches with great flexibility. For

152   2.2   C HANNEL P RUNING
153
      Channel pruning is a dominant approach to compress CNN-based neural networks. The simplest
154
      way to accomplish this is to eliminate some channels in each layer using certain criteria based on
155
      the parameters / feature maps and sparsity constraints. For example, Zhuang et al. (2018) keep the
156   most discriminative channels in each layer. He et al. (2019) filter the channels whose parameters are
157   closer to the geometric median of all the channels in the same layer. They claim that those channels
158   are more likely to be redundant and less important because they are easier to be composites of other
159   channels. Sui et al. (2021) eliminate the channels with less independence, which is quantified by
160   a novel inter-channel metric. Moreover, there are also some studies that advocate a more dynamic
161   way to conduct channel pruning. For example, Li et al. (2022) incorporate the ideas of neural
      architecture search (NAS) and search out a new family of light-weight networks automatically by

235   4     M ETHODOLOGY
236
237   In this section, we describe the detailed process to generate an augmented model. Note that the
238   process will be repeated for M times if we want to generate M augmented models. First, we need
239   to perform channel pruning to eliminate redundant channels. (described in Section 4.1) Then, we
240
      need to perform initial parameter optimization to make the pruned model more powerful. (described
      in Section 4.2). Finally, we introduce a method of regularization on the gradients to intermediate
241
      feature maps, which is performed during initial parameter optimization. (described in Section 4.3).
242
      We show the big picture of our method in Figure 2.

      4.1   C HANNEL P RUNING
245
246   To avoid overfitting on redundant channels or model-specific features of the source CNN model, we
247   introduce the technique of channel pruning to perform model augmentation. For each augmented
248   model, we randomly sample different pruning rates, namely the rate of channels to be pruned, in
249   each layer based on the predetermined lower bound (rmin ) and upper bound (rmax ).
250
      After we sample a pruning rate for each layer in an augmented model, we select the corresponding
251   number of channels and eliminate them in each layer. Based on the prior channel running method He
252   et al. (2019), we select the channels with minimum summation of the distance with other channels.
253   We denote Fi,j as the output feature map in the j-th channel of the i-th layer. The criterion for the
254   selection of pruned channels can be formulated as:
255                                          X
256                         j ∗ = arg min         ||Fi,j − Fi,j ′ ||2 s.t. j ∈ [1, ni ]
                                        j
257                                         j ′ ∈[1,ni ]

258   where ni denotes the number of channels in the i-th layer.

260   4.2   I NITIAL PARAMETER O PTIMIZATION
261
262   In classical methods of channel pruning, fine-tuning of the model on training datasets is required
263   to maintain its performance. However, since the training dataset for target models might not be
264   available, fine-tuning on the original training dataset for each augmented model is not realistic in
265   practice. Hence, we incorporate few-shot knowledge distillation techniques to fulfill the generation
266
      of augmented models without additional data. Namely, we only perform knowledge distillation on
      the target dataset (i.e. the dataset consisting of 2000 images we are going to attack) to generate
267
      augmented models.
268
269   To train the pruned models with a small dataset while achieving an acceptable performance on image
      classification, we leverage knowledge distillation in order to transfer the knowledge from original


                                                           5

270
      models to pruned models. Prior work (Hinton et al., 2015) has shown that classification logits
271   predicted by a well-pretrained model can provide additional information. Making a student model
272   (pruned model in our case) mimic its logits can let the student model acquire additional information
273   than that provided by ground truth labels. Hence, to train the pruned models, we use KL-divergence
274   between the logits predicted by the original models and the pruned models in addition to cross-
275   entropy loss (CE loss), which is typically used in learning classification tasks.
276
      In addition to conventional knowledge distillation, we incorporate the technique proposed by Xu
277   et al. (2020), which employs contrastive prediction Chen et al. (2020) as an auxiliary task alongside
278   the original classification task to extract more comprehensive information from pre-trained models.
279   To implement the auxiliary task, we attach a lightweight self-supervision (SS) module to both the
280   original and pruned model backbones. Initially, we train the SS module of the original model using
281   both normal and transformed inputs. For contrastive learning, we apply three types of transforma-
282   tions: rotation, randomly masking individual pixels, and a simple adversarial attack generated using
283   FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2014).
284   We train the pruned model using a combination of four losses, which are the cross entropy loss for
285   normal inputs (Lce ), the knowledge distillation loss (Lkd ), the KL-divergence loss between the SS

290   with C dimensions, where C is the number of classes. Ao and Ap are B × B matrices, where B is

302   4.3   R EGULARIZATION OF I NTERMEDIATE F EATURE M APS
303
304   To further reduce the variance of performance between different target models, we introduce a tech-
305   nique of regularization. Wang et al. (2021) suggest that the gradients of logits output with respect to
306   a specific intermediate feature map of source models are related to the “importance” of the feature
307
      map in adversarial attacks. The feature maps with larger values of gradients tend to play a more crit-
      ical role when it comes to adversarial attacks transferred to various target models. That is, disrupting
308
      those feature maps can lead to stronger transferability. In addition, Zhang et al. (2023) have shown
309
      that regularizing the back-propogated gradients of internal blocks by eliminating extreme value can
310   improve the transferability for both convolution-based and transformer-based surrogate/target mod-
311   els.
312
313
      Hence, we regularize the back-propogated gradients in a layer of intermediate feature maps from
      source models during initial parameter optimization. If we fine-tune the model to shrink the variance
314
      of the gradients from different channels, the “importance” of each channel in that layer is more
315
      balanced. In addition, model-specific features, which are harmful to the transferability of adversarial
316   attacks, can be modified to more generic ones through the regularization process. We realize the
317   regularization process by using an additional loss function Lreg , formulated as:
318                                               X
319                                      Lreg =        var(∇Flp v p (x))
                                                   x∈D
320
321   where Flp denotes the parameters of the l-th layer in the pruned model. The overall loss function
322   becomes:
323                          L = wce Lce + wkd Lkd + wss Lss + wT LT + wreg Lreg
      where wce , wkd , wss , wT , wreg are hyper-parameters.

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
327
328   Dataset and Evaluated Models. We conduct our experiments on the ImageNet (Russakovsky et al.,
329   2015)-compatible dataset including 2000 images. We randomly sample 2 images from each category
330   of the ImageNet dataset. The images sampled are almost correctly classified by all the evaluated
331   models. For untargeted attacks, we use ResNet-50, ResNet-101 (He et al., 2016), Inception-v3
332   (Szegedy et al., 2016) as the surrogate models. For targeted attacks, we use ResNet-50, VGG-
333   16 (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014), DenseNet-121 (Huang et al., 2017), and Inception-v3 as the
334
      surrogate models. In addition, we use more diverse architectures for the target models, including
      some transformer-based models such as ViT-S and ViT-B (Dosovitskiy, 2020).
335
336   Baseline Methods. We conduct our experiments based on some commonly used attacking methods
337   by comparing their results with or without injecting our techniques. The baseline methods include
338   MI-FGSM (Dong et al., 2018), DI-FGSM (Xie et al., 2019) , SINI-FGSM (Lin et al., 2019) and VNI-
339   FGSM (Wang & He, 2021). In addition, we also compare our methods to state-of-the-art (SOTA)
340
      methods of model augmentation in adversarial attacks.
341   Implementation Details. We adopt cross-entropy (CE) loss for adversarial attacks in all the exper-
342   iments. Following the prior works (Wang et al., 2024; Liang & Xiao, 2023; Wang & He, 2021), we
343   set the budget for adversarial perturbation ϵ as 16/255. The step size is set as 1.6/255. The number
344   of steps is set as 200 in DI-FGSM, while it is set as 10 in the other baseline methods. The momentum
345   decay in MI-FGSM is 1, and the number of scale copies in SINI-FGSM is 5. The probability of ap-
346
      plying input diversity is set as 0.7 in DI-FGSM. The number of augmented models M is set as 3. For
      all experiments, we use random mask as transformation in the initial parameter optimization. The
347
      training of augmented models (initial parameter optimization) uses the SGD optimizer. We train the
348
      SS module of the original models with 100 epochs. In addition, we train the pruned models with 130
349   epochs without regularization of intermediate feature maps or with 200 epochs with regularization.

528   6   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we examine the mechanism of adversarial attacks through the lens of the feature “distri-
531   bution” across channels. We observe that existing adversarial attacks tend to overly focus on features
532   in a limited number of channels, which hinders their transferability. To address this, we propose a
533   novel model augmentation method that can be integrated with existing attacks. Extensive experi-
534
      ments demonstrate that our approach significantly enhances current attacks and outperforms other
      model augmentation techniques. We hope our method and perspective provide valuable insights into
535
      this complex field and inspire new interpretations of adversarial attack transferability.
