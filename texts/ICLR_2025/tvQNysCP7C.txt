009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The KV-Cache technique has become the standard for the inference of large lan-
012            guage models (LLMs). It caches states of self-attention to avoid recomputation.
013            Yet, it is widely criticized that KV-Cache can become a bottleneck of the LLM infer-
014            ence system, especially when confronted with ultra-large models and long-context
015            queries. A natural remedy is to discard the KV-Cache for less important tokens,
016            with StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2024) as an example, but the used static eviction
017            strategies cannot flexibly adapt to varying contexts. Remedies like H2O (Zhang
018            et al., 2024) leverage accumulative attention scores to perform dynamic eviction
019
               but suffer from the attention bias issue in capturing contextual information. This
               paper bridges this gap by devising a parameterized KV-Cache eviction mecha-
020
               nism, dubbed as Attention-Gate, which accepts the whole context as input and
021
               yields eviction flags for each token to realize in-context eviction. The subsequent
022            self-attention module proceeds according to the flags and only the KV states for
023            the remaining tokens need to be cached. The Attention-Gates can vary among
024            different heads and layers and be trivially plugged into pre-trained LLMs, tuned
025            by cost-effective continual pre-training or supervised fine-tuning objectives to
026            acquire what to discard. The computational and memory overhead introduced
027            by Attention-Gates is minimal. Our method is validated across multiple tasks,
028            demonstrating both efficiency and adaptability. After a highly efficient continual
029            pre-training, it achieves higher average accuracy and evicts more tokens compared
030
               to traditional training-free methods. In supervised fine-tuning, it not only evicts
               many tokens but also outperforms LoRA-finetuned LLMs on some datasets, such
031
               as RTE, where it improves accuracy by 13.9% while evicting 62.8% of tokens,
032
               showing that effective eviction of redundant tokens can even enhance performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Large language models (LLMs) (Dubey et al., 2024; Team et al., 2024; Chiang et al., 2023) have
039
      achieved remarkable success across a wide range of natural language processing tasks. A key
      technique that has enabled efficient LLM inference is KV-Cache, which stores transient attention
040
      keys and values to avoid recomputation. However, as the size of LLMs continues to increase and
041
      the demand for handling long-context queries grows, the KV-Cache has emerged as a significant
042   bottleneck. Storing attention states for numerous tokens can lead to considerable memory overhead
043   and data transfer among the memory hierarchy results in substantially increased inference time.
044
045
      Studies have shown that sparsity is a natural phenomenon in attention mechanisms, with many tokens
      being redundant for inference (Zhang et al., 2024). This suggests that retaining all tokens in the
046
      KV-Cache is unnecessary. Existing works have explored this insight to compress KV-Cache using
047
      static strategies or hinging on accumulative attention scores. StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2024) is
048   a representative of the former by retaining a fixed window of beginning and recent tokens in the
049   KV-Cache but it struggles to flexibly adapt to specific contexts. E.g., in sentiment analysis, retaining
050   the token “cute” in “a cute cat” is crucial, while in object recognition, the token “cat” would be
051   more important. H2O (Zhang et al., 2024), on the other hand, employs a token-adaptive approach,
052   using local accumulative attention scores to determine which tokens to evict. However, it is criticized
053   that in practice, H2O suffers from the attention bias issue (Oren et al., 2024), with a tendency to
      over-prioritize either the initial or recent tokens.

078   To overcome these challenges, we introduce a parameterized KV-Cache eviction mechanism named
079
      the Attention-Gate (AG), to perform reliable in-context eviction. AG is positioned before a self-
      attention layer within the model. It takes a sequence of token features as input and generates eviction
080
      flags for the tokens, indicating whether a token should be excluded from subsequent self-attention
081
      computations. Tokens that are evicted do not require their KV states to be cached. AGs can be
082   seamlessly integrated into pre-trained LLMs and tuned by minimizing the language modeling loss.
083   Ideally, AGs can automatically learn to discern the most relevant tokens for the current context
084   without manual intervention. In practice, we can implement the AG as a self-attention layer with
085   much fewer heads than the original model (e.g., 4 v.s. 32). This way, the parallel computational
086   capabilities of the hardware can be harnessed to minimize the extra overhead introduced by AGs.
087
      AG is empirically shown to enjoy high training efficiency, e.g., only four NVIDIA 4090 GPUs and
088   a dataset of 5,000 samples are required for continual pre-training when applying AGs to LLaMA2-
089   7B (Touvron et al., 2023). This alleviates concerns about the computational overhead related to
090   trainable eviction strategies (Zhang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) and amplifies the performance
091   superiority of our approach over existing training-free approaches. As illustrated in Figure 1, AG
092   generates different eviction strategies across different layers and attention-heads for different tokens,
093   demonstrating its adaptability to the diverse requirements of each component in the model.
094   To validate the effectiveness of our method, we conduct extensive experiments across multiple
095   benchmarks. After efficient continual pre-training (CPT), our approach outperforms traditional
096   training-free eviction strategies, such as StreamingLLM and H2O, in both accuracy and token
097   eviction rates. In supervised fine-tuning (SFT), our method not only evicts a significant number
098   of redundant tokens but also maintains or surpasses the performance of LoRA-finetuned LLMs.
099   For example, on the RTE dataset, our approach improves accuracy by 13.9% while evicting 62.8%
100   of tokens, demonstrating that selective token eviction can enhance performance. In summary, the
101   Attention-Gate mechanism provides a scalable and efficient solution for KV-Cache management,
102
      addressing the limitations of traditional training-free methods.

136   A significant bottleneck lies in the attention mechanism, which demands considerable computational
137   and memory resources, especially for long sequences.
138   Computation and memory challenges. The computational cost of attention is driven by its quadratic
139   complexity, while the memory burden stems from the need to store the KV-Cache during inference
140   to avoid recomputation. Although several works have focused on reducing the computational load
141   of attention mechanisms, many fail to address the memory constraints that remain a critical issue.
142   Techniques like Reformer (Kitaev et al., 2020) and FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023)
143   mitigate memory usage for long sequences but still require substantial cache storage. On the other
144   hand, approaches such as MQA (Shazeer, 2019) and GQA (Ainslie et al., 2023) prioritize memory
145   compression without tackling computational complexity. These solutions tend to focus on either
146
      memory or computational efficiency, rarely addressing both simultaneously.
147   KV-Cache eviction strategies. To address both memory and computational challenges, KV-Cache
148   eviction has emerged as an effective strategy. Existing approaches can be categorized into static and
149   adaptive methods based on accumulative attention scores.
150   Static strategies, such as those used in Sparse Transformers (Child et al., 2019), employ fixed pruning
151   patterns, such as Strided and Fixed Attention. While effective in some cases, these approaches are not
152   adaptive to specific contexts, often sacrificing accuracy. StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2024) tackles
153   the Attention Sink phenomenon, where attention scores concentrate on initial tokens, by retaining
154   these tokens along with a fixed window of recent tokens. While this improves performance, static
155   approaches generally lack the flexibility needed to adapt to different tokens, attention-heads, or layers.
156   Strategies using accumulative attention scores offer more flexibility by dynamically identifying
157   important tokens. For instance, SpAtten (Wang et al., 2021) employs Accumulative Attention Scores
158   (A2S), which sum the softmax outputs for each token to measure its importance. This approach
159   allows selective token pruning in subsequent layers, effectively reducing computational complexity
160   without the need for retraining. H2O (Zhang et al., 2024) extends this concept to decoder-based
161   models, using local A2S statistics for adaptive eviction in autoregressive generation. However, H2O
      suffers from the attention bias issue (Oren et al., 2024), particularly in long-context inputs. Several


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      follow-up works have aimed to address this limitation. NACL (Chen et al., 2024) introduces random
163   eviction to mitigate attention bias, while A2SF (Jo & Shin, 2024) incorporates a Forgetting Factor.
164   However, none of these approaches fully resolve the underlying problem.
165
166
      More Adaptive strategies. Although strategies based on accumulative attention scores provide more
      flexibility than static methods, they still have notable limitations. For instance, H2O (Zhang et al.,
167
      2024) applies the same token eviction ratio across all attention heads, restricting the adaptability of the
168
      method. FastGen (Ge et al., 2023), on the other hand, introduces a different approach by hybridizing
169   KV-Cache compression policies and applying adaptive strategies to each attention head. However, it
170   focuses on the decoding stage and neglects the importance of the prefilling stage. Learnable eviction
171   strategies, on the other hand, offer greater flexibility by enabling different layers and attention heads to
172   adopt heterogeneous eviction policies. However, such strategies have been relatively underexplored,
173   likely due to concerns about the computational overhead they may introduce (Zhang et al., 2024;
174   Chen et al., 2024). Nonetheless, task-specific training is essential for optimizing performance across
175   different contexts. For example, a recent approach (Anagnostidis et al., 2024) introduces a learnable
176   mechanism for dropping uninformative tokens, but it faces difficulties in batched generation and does
177
      not account for continual pre-training or decoding-only LLMs. Despite these challenges, learnable
      strategies have strong potential to improve performance across a variety of tasks by allowing models
178
      to adapt their eviction strategies to meet task-specific requirements.

181   3     M ETHOD
182
183   This section first briefly reveals the basics of multi-head attention and KV-Cache and then describes
184   the proposed Attention-Gate (AG) mechanism for in-context KV-Cache eviction for LLM inference
185   acceleration. An illustrative overview of AG is presented in Figure 2. For additional supplementary
186   descriptions of AG, see Appendix A.
187
188   3.1   P RELIMINARY
189
190   Multi-Head Attention (MHA) (Vaswani et al., 2017) is a core component of the Transformer
191
      architecture, widely used in most LLMs. MHA enables the model to capture dependencies across
      different tokens in a sequence by computing self-attention across multiple attention-heads. Each
192
      head attends to different parts of the input sequence independently, allowing the model to capture
193
      various aspects of the relationships between tokens. The outputs of these attention heads are then
194   concatenated and projected through a final output matrix.
195
196   KV-Cache is employed to store the key and value representations of tokens from previous time
197
      steps during inference. This prevents redundant recomputation of these representations for every
      new token generated, significantly speeding up auto-regressive generation. The inference process in
198
      auto-regressive transformers, such as Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), can be divided into two stages:
199
      prefilling and decoding. 1. In the prefilling stage, the model processes the entire input sequence,
200   generating key-value pairs for each attention-head in each layer. These key-value pairs are stored
201   in the KV-Cache for future reuse. 2. In the decoding stage, when generating the next token, the
202   model only computes the key and value for that new token and appends them to the cached key-value
203   pairs. This reduces the computational load, as the previously cached key-value pairs do not need to
204   be recomputed. The process continues token by token until the sequence generation is complete.
205
      KV-Cache plays a critical role in improving the efficiency of LLM inference, especially in scenarios
206   where long sequences are processed. However, the size of the KV-Cache grows with the input
207   sequence length, leading to substantial memory overhead. Efficiently managing this cache while
208   maintaining model performance has become a key challenge in scaling LLMs to longer contexts.
209
210   3.2   L IMITATIONS OF T RADITIONAL E VICTION S TRATEGIES
211
212   Lack of flexibility. Flexibility in KV-Cache eviction strategies spans across various dimensions,
213   including token-specific, attention-head-specific, layer-specific, task-specific, and model-specific
214   adaptability. Static eviction policies offer no such flexibility, requiring manual intervention to adjust
215   them. While methods based on accumulative attention scores (e.g., H2O (Zhang et al., 2024)) have
      improved upon static strategies by introducing token-level and head-level adaptability, they are still


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      limited. Specifically, in H2O, the eviction ratio remains uniform across all attention heads, restricting
217   head-level flexibility. Flexibility is crucial in eviction strategies because models process varying types
218   of data across different contexts, requiring the ability to selectively retain important tokens while
219   discarding others. Without this adaptability, the model may inefficiently store redundant information,
220   increasing memory usage and slowing down inference times.
221
      Absence of global statistics. Eviction strategies that rely on accumulative attention scores, such
222
      as H2O, base their calculations on local statistics, which often results in attention bias issues (Oren
223   et al., 2024). This local approach may misjudge a token’s importance, especially when the context
224   spans long sequences. While several methods (Chen et al., 2024; Jo & Shin, 2024) have proposed
225   solutions to mitigate this bias, they are still fundamentally rooted in local statistics, addressing the
226   issue only partially. A more effective solution would incorporate global statistics to ensure that the
227   eviction process is based on a more comprehensive understanding of the context, thereby reducing
228   biases and improving the accuracy of token retention.
229   Inefficiency. Efficiency is another challenge for traditional methods such as H2O and FastGen (Ge
230   et al., 2023). These methods operates in a token-by-token manner for each decoding step, sequentially
231   deciding which key-value pairs to evict from the cache. Additionally, H2O determines which tokens
232   to evict after computing the attention scores, meaning some computation is wasted on tokens that are
233   later discarded. This sequential approach hinders efficiency, especially when handling large batches
234   of data or when processing long sequences.
235   Our Attention-Gate mechanism is designed to address the aforementioned limitations.
236
237
238
239   3.3   ATTENTION -G ATE
240
241   The Attention-Gate (AG) is a lightweight, trainable module designed to determine which tokens in
242   the KV-Cache of each attention-head should be retained or discardedAG is positioned before the
243   Multi-Head Attention (MHA) layer and operates by generating eviction flags for each token, guiding
244   both the computation of attention scores and the management of the KV-Cache.
245
      Input and output. AG takes the hidden states as input and generates binary flags as output, one for
246   each token in every attention head. These flags indicate whether the corresponding token’s key-value
247   pairs should be stored or discarded in the KV-Cache. Specifically, the input is a hidden state matrix
248   X ∈ Rn×d , where n represents the sequence length, and d denotes the dimensionality of the hidden
249   states. The output consists of independent binary decisions for the retention or eviction of each
250   token’s key-value pairs across all attention heads in the MHA module.
251
      Local or global? The structure of AG offers two choices: using either local or global information
252   to guide the eviction policy. 1. Local information: The simplest approach leverages a linear layer
253   where each token only considers its own hidden state without taking other tokens in the sequence
254   into account. This method is computationally efficient and straightforward, making it appealing for
255   its simplicity. 2. Global information: On the other hand, a more comprehensive approach adopts
256   an attention-like structure, where tokens can aggregate information from across the entire sequence.
257   This enables the eviction policy to reflect the global in-context information, making decisions that are
258   informed by the broader context. Naturally, this method is computationally heavier than the local
259   approach, but it offers a more accurate reflection of the sequence’s token importance.
260   In our design, we opted for the attention-like structure because global context is essential for
261   making effective eviction decisions. As demonstrated in Section 4.4, using an attention-like structure
262   significantly outperforms the linear layer, as the latter struggles to properly evict tokens. This outcome
263   is expected, as determining whether a token is redundant or crucial for inference often requires a
264   global understanding of the sequence. A local approach, like a linear layer focusing on individual
265   tokens, lacks the broader perspective necessary to make accurate decisions about which tokens are
266
      important. This finding also underscores the limitations of methods like H2O, which rely on local
      statistics rather than global ones, as mentioned in Section 3.2.
267
268   Softmax or Sigmoid? After gathering information through the attention-like structure, the next step
269   is to compute the eviction probability for each token. Common activation functions such as Softmax
      and Sigmoid can be used to generate these probabilities.


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      In our case, we choose the Sigmoid function for a key reason: while Softmax normalizes probabilities
271   across the entire sequence, introducing competition between tokens, Sigmoid treats each token
272   independently. This independence is crucial because we do not want a token’s likelihood of being
273   evicted to depend on the eviction probabilities of other tokens in the sequence.
274
      Eviction flags. After computing the eviction probabilities for each token, we apply a threshold τ .
275
      If the probability of retaining a token exceeds τ , that token is retained in the KV-Cache; otherwise,
276
      it is discarded. In the attention matrix, the columns corresponding to evicted tokens are masked
277   out. However, the diagonal elements, where a token attends to itself, are always preserved. This
278   guarantees that each token continues to interact with its own key.
279
280
      Prefilling or decoding? AG is primarily applied during the prefilling stage, where the full sequence
      is available. By making eviction decisions before the MHA layers, AG effectively manages the
281
      KV-Cache during this phase. In contrast, AG is not used during the decoding stage to avoid adding
282
      complexity to the inference process. Using AG in decoding could slow down inference and increase
283   the cache memory footprint, as additional keys and values from the attention-like structure would
284   need to be stored.
285
286
      In summary, AG dynamically determines which tokens should be retained, optimizing both KV-Cache
      usage and attention score computation by utilizing an attention-like structure, Sigmoid activation
287
      function, and a threshold-based decision for token eviction.

290   3.4   T RAINING I MPLEMENTATION
291
292
      To train the Attention-Gate (AG) effectively, this section outlines the key components of the training
      process. For more comprehensive details, please refer to Section 4 and Appendix B.
293
294   Eviction Loss. To encourage the eviction of unnecessary tokens, we introduce a dedicated loss
295   function called the Eviction Loss. This loss encourages the model to discard as many tokens as
296   possible, which is defined as:
297                                        ℓevict = α · AG − β ,                                 (1)
298
299   where AG represents the average output of all AG modules. In this formula, α adjusts the intensity
300
      of KV-Cache eviction, while β ∈ [0, τ ] ensures that eviction does not become overly aggressive.This
      loss function works alongside the auto-regressive loss to balance token eviction with maintaining
301
      model performance. The loss allows for adaptive eviction across layers and attention-heads. As
302
      shown in Figure 1, after training, the model learns to increase token eviction in deeper layers, which
303   is not achievable with training-free methods like H2O (Zhang et al., 2024) .
304
305   Initialization. We initialize the AG parameters using Xavier initialization (Glorot & Bengio, 2010)
306
      to provide a stable starting point for learning. Additionally, a small constant γ ≥ 0 is added inside
      Sigmoid, ensuring that the initial retention probabilities are close to 1. This encourages the model to
307
      retain most tokens early in training, allowing it to learn which tokens are important more gradually.
308
309   Handling non-differentiability. Directly applying the threshold-based gating mechanism from
310   Section 3.3 would lead to non-differentiable gradients during training due to the hard thresholding’s
311   discrete nature. To resolve this, we employ the Straight-Through Estimator (STE) (Yin et al., 2019),
312
      which allows gradients to flow through discrete decisions by approximating them during the backward
      pass. Specifically, during backpropagation, instead of using the hard 0 or 1 values obtained from
313
      comparing against the threshold, we utilize the smooth output of the Sigmoid function. This approach
314
      ensures smooth gradients and enables effective training of the AG while preserving its binary behavior
315   during the forward pass.

534
535   In conclusion, the proposed Attention-Gate mechanism offers a flexible and adaptive solution to
536   KV-Cache eviction in large language models. By dynamically identifying and discarding less impor-
537   tant tokens, Attention-Gate addresses the limitations of static and attention-score-based strategies,

      performance and memory efficiency in various tasks.
