009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Temporal graphs are ubiquitous in real-world scenarios, such as social network,
012            trade and transportation. Predicting dynamic links between nodes in a temporal
013            graph is of vital importance. Traditional memory-based methods typically leverage
014            the temporal neighborhood of interaction histories to generate node embeddings,
015            which are then aggregated to predict links between source and target nodes. How-
016            ever, these methods primarily focus on learning individual node representations
017            and often neglect the nature of pairwise representation learning aspect. While some
018
               recent methods attempt to capture pairwise features, they are less emphasized in
               large-scale datasets like TGB. Meanwhile, most of these existing methods tend to
019
               suffer from high computational complexity due to the repeated calculation of node
020
               embeddings. Motivated by the success of Neural Common Neighbor (NCN) for
021            static graph link prediction, we propose TNCN, a temporal version of NCN for
022            link prediction in temporal graphs. Based on a memory-based backbone instead
023            of traditional static graph neural network, TNCN dynamically updates a temporal
024            neighbor dictionary for each node, and utilizes multi-hop common neighbors be-
025            tween the source and target node to learn a more effective pairwise representation.
026            We validate our model on five large-scale real-world datasets from the Temporal
027            Graph Benchmark (TGB), and find that it achieves new state-of-the-art performance
028            on three of them. Additionally, TNCN demonstrates excellent scalability on large
029
               datasets, outperforming popular GNN baselines by up to 6.4 times in speed.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Temporal graphs are increasingly utilized in contemporary real-world applications. Complex systems
034
      such as social networks (Yang et al., 2017; Min et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2017), trade and transaction
035
      networks (Zhang et al., 2018; Yan et al., 2021), and recommendation systems (Wu et al., 2022; Yin
036   et al., 2019) are prime examples. These systems evolve dynamically over time, exhibiting different
037   characteristics. Recently, there has been a marked increase in the representation learning tasks
038   on temporal graphs. At the same time, a prominent graph learning tool, Graph Neural Network
039   (GNN) (Scarselli et al., 2008), has been developed to model node, link, and graph tasks. GNNs
040   generally learn node embeddings by iteratively aggregating embeddings from neighboring nodes.
041   They have demonstrated exceptional performance in numerous graph representation learning tasks.
042   There remains a significant gap between static graphs and the increasingly prevalent temporal graphs.
043   Temporal graphs incorporate discrete or continuous timestamps attached to edges, providing a more
044   precise depiction of the graph evolution process. Meanwhile, many methodologies for static graphs
045   are not applicable due to the additional constraints imposed by timestamps on causality. One can only
046   utilize nodes and edges that precede a given time, implicitly resulting in numerous graph instances
047   to analyze. Building on the success of time sequence modeling, Kumar et al. (2019); Trivedi et al.
048   (2019); Rossi et al. (2020) propose memory-based temporal graph networks aimed at learning both
049   short- and long-term dependencies. These methods, particularly Temporal Graph Networks (TGN),
050
      have achieved notable success in temporal node classification. Additionally, Transformer-based
      models, such as those proposed by Wang et al. (2021a); Xu et al. (2020), employ the multi-head
051
      attention mechanism to capture both cross and internal relationships within a temporal graph.
052
053   While such memory- or attention-based methods are more emphasized and take more proportion
      in real-world datasets like TGB, these models may exhibit inherent flaws when addressing link

      In general, graph-based representation learning methods can significantly enhance model capabilities,
      yet their high computational cost limits widespread application. For example, the Temporal Graph
086
      Benchmark (TGB) (Huang et al., 2023) contains many high-quality real-world temporal graphs.
087
      However, most graph-based models choose to only evaluate on a subset of small graphs due to their
088   unaffordable cost of training and evaluation on large-scale datasets. On the contrary, memory-based
089   models (Rossi et al., 2020) update node representations sequentially following the event stream,
090   thus is significantly faster than graph-based methods yet might lose important graph information.
091   Motivated by these observations, we propose an expressive but efficient model, Temporal Neural
092   Common Neighbor (TNCN), to combine the merits of both. By integrating a memory-based
093   backbone with Neural Common Neighbor (Wang et al., 2023), TNCN learns expressive pairwise
094   representations while maintaining high efficiency akin to memory-based models. Consequently,
095   TNCN is suitable for large-scale temporal graph link prediction.
096   We conducted experiments on five large-scale real-world temporal graph datasets from TGB. TNCN
097   achieved new SOTA results on 3 datasets and overall ranked first compared to 10 competitive baselines,
098   demonstrating its effectiveness. To examine its scalability, we selected datasets with temporal edges
099   ranging from O(105 ) to O(107 ) and node numbers ranging from thousands to millions. We found
100   TNCN achieved 1.9x∼4.7x speedup in training and 2.1x∼6.4x speedup in inference compared to
101   graph-based models, while maintaining a similar scale of time consumption as memory-based models.

105   2.1   M EMORY- BASED T EMPORAL G RAPH R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
106
107   Temporal graph learning has garnered significant attention in recent years. A classic approach in this
      domain involves learning node memory using continuous events with non-decreasing timestamps.

108
      Kumar et al. (2019) propose a coupled recurrent neural network model named JODIE that learns
109   the embedding trajectories of users and items. Another contemporary work DyRep (Trivedi et al.,
110   2019) aims to efficiently produce low-dimensional node embeddings to capture the communication
111   and association in dynamic graphs. Rossi et al. (2020) introduces a memory-based temporal neural
112   network known as TGN, which incorporates a memory module to store temporal node representations
113   updated with messages generated from the given event stream. Apan (Wang et al., 2021b) advances the
114   methodology by integrating asynchronous propagation techniques, markedly increasing the efficiency
115   of handling large-scale graph queries. EDGE (Chen et al., 2021) emerges as a computational
116   framework focusing on increasing the parallelizability by dividing some intermediate nodes in long
117
      streams each into two independent nodes while adding back their dependency by training loss. Chen
      et al. (2023) extend the update method for the node memory module, introducing an additional hidden
118
      state to record previous changes in neighbors. Complementing these efforts, additional contributions
119
      such as Edgebank (Poursafaei et al., 2022) and DistTGL (Zhou et al., 2023) have been directed
120   towards formalizing and accelerating memory-based temporal graph learning methods.
121
122
      2.2   G RAPH - BASED T EMPORAL G RAPH R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
123
124   Subsequent works have incorporated the temporal neighborhood structure into temporal graph
125   learning. CAWN (Wang et al., 2021c) employs random anonymous walks to model the neighborhood
126   structure. TCL (Wang et al., 2021a) samples a temporal dependency interaction graph that contains
127   a sequence of temporally cascaded chronological interactions. TGAT (Xu et al., 2020) considers
128   the temporal neighborhood and feeds the features into a temporal graph attention layer utilizing
129   a masked self-attention mechanism. NAT (Luo & Li, 2022) constructs a multi-hop neighboring
130
      node dictionary to extract joint neighborhood features and uses a recurrent neural network (RNN)
      to recursively update the central node’s embedding. This information is then processed by a neural
131
      network-based encoder to predict the target link. DyGFormer (Yu et al., 2023), instead, leverages
132
      one-hop neighbor embeddings and the co-occurrence of neighbors to generate features, which are
133   well-patched and subsequently fed into a Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) decoder to obtain the
134   final prediction. LPFormer (Shomer et al., 2024) attempts to adaptively learn the pairwise encodings
135   via graph attention module, utilizing relative position, ppr value and neighboring information to obtain
136   the score. FreeDyG (Tian et al.) also utilizes historical interaction frequency akin to DyGFormer,
137   afterwards transforming it with Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) and IFFT through the frequency domain.
138   An MLP-mixer layer finally processes the output to generate the prediction. Another contemporary
139   work CNE-N (Cheng et al., 2024) uses a hash table to map an interaction event to its position.
140   It calculates the co-neighbor encoding for each (neighbor node - end node) pair within the local
141
      subgraph, recording the number of their common neighbors. These information are then concatenated
      to predict the probability of the future link.

144   2.3   L INK P REDICTION M ETHODS
145
      Link prediction is a fundamental task in graph analysis, aiming to determine the likelihood of a
146   connection between two nodes. Early investigations posited that nodes with greater similarity tend
147   to be connected, which led to a series of heuristic algorithms such as Common Neighbors, Katz
148   Index, and PageRank (Newman, 2001; Katz, 1953; Page et al., 1999). With the advent of GNNs,
149   numerous methods have attempted to utilize vanilla GNNs for enhancing link prediction, revealing
150   sub-optimal performance due to the inability to capture important pair-wise patterns such as common
151   neighbors (Zhang & Chen, 2018; Zhang et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2022). Subsequent research has
152   focused on infusing various forms of inductive biases to retrieve intricate pair-wise relationships.
153   For instance, SEAL (Zhang & Chen, 2018), Neo-GNN (Yun et al., 2021), and NCN (Wang et al.,
154
      2023) have integrated neighbor-overlapping information into their design. BUDDY (Chamberlain
      et al., 2022) and NBFNet (Zhu et al., 2021) have concentrated on extracting higher-order structural
155
      information. Additionally, Mao et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024) have contributed to a more unified
156
      framework encompassing different heuristics.

229   4.2   T EMPORAL CN E XTRACTOR
230
231   Our Temporal CN Extractor can efficiently perform multi-hop common neighbor extraction and
232
      aggregate their neural embeddings.
233   Extended Common Neighbor. The definition of multi-hop common neighbors (CN) is given in
234   Definition 3.3, extending the traditional (1,1)-hop CN (i.e., nodes on 2-paths between u and v) to
235   arbitrary (i, j)-hop CN. Additionally, we define the zero-hop neighbor of a central node, i.e., u is
236   considered as a neighbor of itself, which will be utilized to calculate CNs with other nodes. Given
237   source node u and target node v, the (0,1)-hop and (1,0)-hop CN not only records the historical
238
      interactions between two nodes, but also reveals the frequency of their interactions.
239   Efficient CN Extractor. The CN Extractor is a crucial component of the TNCN model, contributing
240   significantly to its high performance and scalability. It can efficiently gather pertinent information
241   about a given center node and extract multi-hop common neighbors for a source-destination pair.
242   For each relevant node u, the extractor stores its historical interactions with other nodes as both
243   source and destination. After a batch of events is processed by the model, the storage is updated
244   with the latest interactions. This allows us to maintain a record of all historical interactions up to
245   a certain timestamp, effectively constructing a dynamic lookup dictionary for fast retrieval during
246   subsequent inference. To strike a balance between memory consumption and model capacity, we save
247   only the most recent K events and relevant nodes for each center node, where K is a hyperparameter
248   determined by the specific dataset.
249   To implement an efficient batch CN extractor, we organize the historical interactions in a Sparse
250   Tensor, representing the temporal adjacency matrix. Then we perform self-multiplication to generate
251   high-order adjacency connectivity. Sparse matrix hadamard product is finally employed to obtain
252   separate (i, j)-hop CNs. All these operations can be efficiently implemented by sparse tensor
253   operators and are supported by GPU to facilitate fast, batch processing. The detailed procedure can
254   be found in Appendix F.
255   The utilization of Multi-hop Common Neighbors significantly boosts TNCN’s performance, result-
256   ing in higher scores in temporal link prediction tasks. Furthermore, by employing sparse tensors, our
257   model achieves substantial reductions in both storage requirements and computational complexity,
258   thereby decreasing time consumption and enhancing efficiency. Here we also give a comparison
259   between our TNCN and traditional NCN in Table 1.

      5     E FFICIENCY AND E FFECTIVENESS OF TNCN
294
295
      In this section, we explore the two principal benefits of TNCN: efficiency and effectiveness. These
296   advantages are demonstrated through an analysis of two core components within the framework
297   for temporal graph link prediction: graph representation learning and link prediction methods. We
298   categorize graph representation learning modules into two types: memory-based and k-hop-subgraph-
299   based, according to the temporal scope of evolved events. Memory-based modules exhibit superior
300   time complexity while maintaining good expressiveness in some situations, striking a balance between
301   efficiency and performance. Furthermore, we highlight the deficiencies of existing link prediction
302   methods on temporal graph learning and introduce the extended common neighbor approach. This
303   method serves as a complementary addition for learning pair-wise representations while eliminating
304
      the necessity for message passing on entire graphs. Both our graph representation learning and link
      prediction techniques are designed with a unified optimization objective: to avoid message passing
305
      on entire subgraphs in favor of non-repetitive operations, culminating in a cohesive solution that
306
      is both efficient and effective.
307
308
      5.1    G RAPH R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
309
310   Graph representation learning aims to develop an embedding function, denoted as Emb, which
311   learns an embedding for each node encoding its structural and feature information within the graph.
312   Specifically, given a new event represented as (u, v, t), the function Emb leverages prior events to
313   generate meaningful embeddings. Approaches in this domain diverge in their handling of temporal
314   dynamics; some opt to maintain a dynamic embedding for each node that is incrementally updated
315   with each new event, while others choose to recalculate node embeddings by considering the entire
316   historical context of events, thereby providing a more comprehensive reflection of past interactions.
317   We classify these methodologies into two distinct types based on their operational mechanisms.
318   Definition 5.1. Memory-based approach. Given a new event (u, v, t), if Emb conforms to the
319   following form, the method is referred to as a memory-based approach.

335   node uk . For example, {(u, x, t′ ), (x, v, t)} is a 2-hop event. In the case where k = 1, the k-hop
336   event reduces to a single interaction (u, v, t). A monotone k-hop event is a k-hop event in which the
337   sequence of timestamps {tui ,ui+1 | i ∈ {0, . . . , k − 1}, k ≥ 1} is strictly monotonically increasing.
338
339   Then, we analyze the expressiveness of the two approaches in terms of encoding k-hop event.
340   Theorem 5.4. (Ability to encode k-hop events). Given a k-hop event {(ui , ui+1 , tui ,ui+1 ) | i ∈
341   {0, . . . , k − 1}, k ≥ 1}, if the node embedding of u0 at time tu0 ,u1 can be reversely recovered from
342   the encoding Enc({(ui , ui+1 , tui ,ui+1 ) | i ∈ {0, . . . , k − 1}, k ≥ 1}), then we say the encoding
343   function Enc is capable of encoding the k-hop event. The following results outline the encoding
344
      capabilities of different learning paradigms:
345          • Memory-based approaches can encode any k-hop events with k = 1.
346
347          • Memory-based approaches can encode any monotone k-hop events with arbitrary k.
348
             • k-hop-subgraph-based approaches can encode any k ′ -hop events with k ′ ≤ k.
349
350   From Theorem 5.4, we can conclude that 1) memory-based approaches have superior expressive-
351   ness in encoding k-hop events compared to 1-hop-subgraph-based approaches, 2) memory-based
352   approaches have superior expressiveness in encoding monotone k-hop events than k ′ -hop-subgraph-
353   based approaches when k ′ < k, and 3) k-hop-subgraph-based approaches are not less expressive than
354
      memory-based approaches when k is large enough.
355   While the memory-based approach does not consistently rival the expressiveness of the k-hop-
356   subgraph paradigm, it possesses advantages in monotone events and long-history scenarios (where
357   k-hop subgraphs would be unaffordable to extract).
358   Corollary 5.5. If we use up to k hop neighborhood information of central node u, then TNCN can
359   capture at least (k + 1)-hop subgraph information around u.
360
361
      This is because TNCN with memory-based backbone can obtain additional 1-hop information
      regardless of the time monotony, i.e. arbitrary central node can interact with any neighbor when the
362
      edge between them exists. This property can extend TNCN’s capability for free.
363
364   Efficiency. We then turn our attention to the efficiency of the two approaches. A pivotal factor is
365   the frequency with which individual events are incorporated into computations. In memory-based
366   approaches, each event is utilized a single time for learning, immediately following its associated
367
      prediction. Conversely, in the k-hop-subgraph-based method, an event may be employed multiple
      times, as it is revisited in different nodes’ temporal neighborhood and repeated been processed within
368
      each subgraph’s encoding (such as message passing) process. This discrepancy leads to divergent
369
      cumulative frequencies of event utilization throughout the learning process and results in the huge
370   efficiency advantage of memory-based methods. We formalize this observation in the following:
371

374

389   5.2   L INK P REDICTION T ECHNIQUE
390
      With the node embeddings obtained from the graph representation learning step, link prediction
391
      techniques involve aggregating the node embeddings in some way into link representations for link
392   prediction. Most previous methods simply concatenate the source and destination node embeddings
393   as the link representation Emb(u, t) || Emb(v, t), losing a great amount of pairwise features (as
394   illustrated in Figure 1). In order to use labeling trick, an alternative approach involves extracting a
395   separate subgraph for each link to predict, which leads to good results but also significantly increases
396   the complexity. TNCN, on the contrary, utilizes neural common neighbors as a decoupled and flexible
397   solution. This approach can be seamlessly integrated into existing memory-based methods with
398   minimal computational overhead, while retaining important structural information for link prediction.
399   Effectiveness. In the following, we first demonstrate the effectiveness of TNCN by showing that it
400   can capture three important pairwise features commonly used as effective link prediction heuristics,
401   namely Common Neighbors (CN), Resource Allocation (RA), and Adamic-Adar (AA) (Newman,
402   2001; Adamic & Adar, 2003; Zhou et al., 2009), borrowing from NCN (Wang et al., 2023).
403   Theorem 5.7. TNCN is strictly more expressive than CN, RA, and AA.
404
405   Essentially, TNCN uses node memory to substitute the constant or degree-based values in the three
406   features, and the memory update scheme is sufficient to learn such values. In comparison, an approach
407
      that merely concatenates node-wise representations proves inadequate in capturing these heuristics.
408   Efficiency. We then address the efficiency of TNCN. The computation of TNCN can be divided
409   into two primary components: the generation of neighbor nodes’ embeddings and the execution of
410   common neighbor lookups. Concerning the former, the memory-based approach intrinsically tracks
411   all node embeddings, thus obviating the need for re-computation. As for the latter, we implement
412   a fast common neighbor search algorithm by leveraging a sparse matrix structure that supports
413
      batch operations. Collectively, these factors contribute to the minimal additional overhead of TNCN
      compared to memory-based methods.

      7             C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
529
530
      We propose TNCN for temporal graph link prediction, which employs a temporal common neighbor
531   extractor combined with a memory-based node representation learning module. TNCN has achieved
532   new state-of-the-art results on several real-world datasets while maintaining excellent scalability to
533   handle large-scale temporal graphs.
534
      However, based on our observation of TNCN’s performance on the TGBL-Review dataset, there are
535
      some limitations in our model. Specifically, datasets with high surprise values, such as the Review
536
      dataset, tend to make it more challenging for TNCN to accurately predict the probability of future
537   connections. This indicates that while TNCN performs well overall, it may struggle with datasets that
538   exhibit high variability or unexpected patterns. Further research is needed to address these challenges
539   and improve the model’s robustness in such scenarios.

      F    D ETAILS OF C OMMON N EIGHBOR E XTRACTION
939
940
941   Our temporal CN extractor begins with a sparse matrix A constructed from the interactions of related
942
      nodes. We then include three stages to precisely generate arbitrary (i, j)-hop CNs:
943   1. Generate up to k-hop neighbors. The original matrix A only includes 1-hop neighbors. To
944   extend this, we: (a) Use self-loops for 0-hop neighbors, denoted as A0 . (b) Perform sparse matrix
945   multiplication (e.g., Ak ) to include arbitrary k-hop neighbors. Combining these two steps, we obtain
946   an updated neighborhood matrix set Â = {Ai }K     i=1 .
947
      2. Extract neighbors for each source and destination node with corresponding indices in the same
948   batch. Assume that we require the k-th hop neighbors of node u, then vector Ak [id(u)] is the result,
949   where id(u) stands for the reindexed id for node u. Ak [id(u)][id(v)] = w > 0 if v is a k-hop
950   neighbor of u, otherwise this element is 0. w represents the historical interaction frequency.
951
      3. Obtain arbitrary (i, j)-hop CNs. We can perform hadamard product of Ai [id(u)] and Aj [id(v)] to
952
      acquire different hops of CNs. As the figure 4 shows, the operator can extract corresponding CNs for
953
      source-destination node pairs in a batch parallelly.

       In this section, we give proofs on theorems 5.4, 5.6 and 5.7.
1049
1050   We commence with a restatement of Theorem 5.4 for clarity:
1051   Theorem H.1. (Ability of encoding k-hop event). Given a k-hop event {(ui , ui+1 , tui ,ui+1 ) | i ∈
1052   {0, . . . , k − 1}, k ≥ 1}. If the node embedding of u0 at time tu0 ,u1 , can be formally derived by the
1053   encoding function Enc({(ui , ui+1 , tui ,ui+1 ) | i ∈ {0, . . . , k − 1}, k ≥ 1}), then the learning method
1054   is considered capable of encoding the k-hop event. The following outline the encoding capabilities of
1055   different learning schemes:
1056
1057          • Memory-based approach can encode any k-hop events with k = 1.
1058
              • Memory-based approach can encode any monotone k-hop events with arbitrary k.
1059
1060          • k-hop-subgraph-based approach can encode any k ′ -hop events with k ′ ≤ k
1061
1062
1063
       Proof. In the following analysis, we establish the encoding efficacy of the memory-based ap-
1064   proach. Consider a k-hop event with the simplifying assumption that k = 1, which reduces
1065   the event to the tuple (u0 , u1 , tu0 ,u1 ). By adhering to the predefined schematics of the memory-
1066   based methodology, the memory state M em(u0 , tu0 ,u1 ) is updated via the function fmem such that
1067                                                  t
       M em(u0 , tu0 ,u1 ) = fmem (M em(u0 , t′ ), euu00,u,u11 , tu0 ,u1 − t′ ). Let us denote the encoding function as
1068   Enc = fmem (M em(u0 , t′ ), . . .). It is our intention to demonstrate that this memory-based framework
1069   is capable of encoding any k-hop event for k = 1.
1070
       We consider the encoding of an arbitrary monotonically increasing k-hop temporal event sequence
1071
