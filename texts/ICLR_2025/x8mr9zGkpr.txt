013
014
               Understanding the drivers of machine learning performance is essential for op-
015
               timizing model accuracy and robustness. While significant attention has been
016
               given to hyperparameter tuning and data preprocessing, the impact of intrinsic
017            data complexity (e.g., class overlap, feature overlap, dimensionality, etc) remains
018            less explored. This study investigates the comparative influence of data com-
019            plexity and hyperparameter configurations on the performance of classification
020            algorithms, specifically Random Forests (RF), Support Vector Machines (SVM),
021            Decision Tree (DT), Adaptive Boosting (AB) and Multi-layer Perceptron (MLP).
022            Using 270 diverse OpenML datasets and 304 hyperparameter configurations, we
023            employ functional analysis of variance (fANOVA) and Ordinary Least Squares
024            (OLS) regression to quantify the relative importance and effect sizes of hyperpa-
025
               rameters and complexity meta-features. Our results reveal that data complexity
               exerts a more substantial influence on both bias and variance components than hy-
026
               perparameter tuning, underscoring the importance of addressing intrinsic dataset
027
               challenges. These findings suggest that efforts to mitigate data complexity factors,
028            such as class overlap or imbalance, may yield greater performance improvements
029            than extensive hyperparameter optimization. This study provides actionable in-
030            sights for machine learning practitioners and highlights the need for further re-
031            search into the interplay between dataset properties and algorithmic performance.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The performance of machine learning models is influenced by various factors, including the quality
037   of the data, the choice of algorithms, and the optimization of hyperparameters. While considerable
038   research has focused on techniques for hyperparameter tuning to improve model performance, less
039
      attention has been given to the inherent properties of datasets—referred to as data complexity meta-
      features—that fundamentally shape a model’s ability to learn effectively.
040
041   Hyperparameters govern the complexity and architecture of machine learning models, necessitating
042   precise selection prior to model training. The process of identifying the optimal hyperparameters to
043   maximize the performance of a machine learning algorithm on a given dataset is known as hyperpa-
044   rameter tuning. This involves exploring a range of hyperparameter settings using various optimiza-
045
      tion techniques such as Grid search (Lerman, 1980), Random search (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012),
      and Bayesian optimization (Mockus, 1982). However, this optimization can be computationally in-
046
      tensive, particularly for complex models like deep neural networks, which are characterized by nu-
047
      merous hyperparameters. Understanding the influence of hyperparameters has become increasingly
048   important, as identifying the critical hyperparameters can narrow the optimization space, thereby im-
049   proving efficiency. Consequently, several studies (Biedenkapp et al., 2017; Huang & Boutros, 2016;
050   Hutter et al., 2014; Jin, 2022; van Rijn & Hutter, 2018; Taylor et al., 2021; Trithipkaiwanpon &
051   Taetragool, 2021) have been dedicated to quantifying and ranking the influence of hyperparameters
052   on classification performance. Notably, in (Hutter et al., 2014), the authors introduced a functional
053   analysis of variance (fANOVA) framework to quantify the individual and pairwise contributions of
      hyperparameters. Their findings underscored that model performance could often be attributed to a


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      select few hyperparameters. Utilizing the fANOVA framework, van Rijn & Hutter (2018) examined
055   the most influential hyperparameters of various algorithms, including Random Forests (RF), Support
056   Vector Machines (SVM), and Adaptive Boosting (AB), across 100 datasets, revealing consistent key
057   hyperparameters across these datasets.
058
      It is equally well established that the predictive performance of classifiers is influenced by the com-
059
      plexity of the given dataset. To elucidate this dependency, complexity meta-features have been pro-
060
      posed (Ho & Basu, 2002; Mollineda et al., 2005; Ho et al., 2006; Sotoca et al., 2006; Orriols-Puig
061   et al., 2010) to describe classification task difficulty by measuring aspects such as feature overlap,
062   class overlap, linear separability, and feature dimensionality. In Cano (2013), researchers explored
063   the impact of complexity meta-features on binary classification accuracy using synthetic datasets
064   to control data complexity. They determined that feature-based measures had the most significant
065   impact on classification accuracy, followed by separability measures. Similarly, Östlund & Fahlman
066   (2022) analyzed multiclass classification tasks using SVM, k-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), and Mul-
067   tilayer Perceptron (MLP) on both real-world and synthetic datasets, investigating the influence of
068   complexity meta-features on classification accuracy and variability across data folds.
069   Recent studies (Farhangi, 2022; Gardner et al., 2023; Talaei Khoei et al., 2023; Lima et al., 2024)
070   have also investigated the comparative influence of data preprocessing and hyperparameter tuning
071   on model performance. The findings of these studies reveal that performing data preprocessing im-
072   proved model performance more than hyperparameter tuning. However, these approaches primarily
073   address external transformations rather than the underlying complexity of the data and as such the
074   foundational impact of data complexity on model behavior remains poorly quantified. Address-
075
      ing this gap is crucial, as understanding the interplay between data complexity and hyperparameter
      configurations can inform the design of more robust and efficient machine learning systems.
076
077   This study builds upon prior research by systematically investigating the relative importance of data
078   complexity and hyperparameter configurations on classification performance. Specifically, we per-
079   form a bias-variance decomposition of classification error, providing a granular perspective on how
080   these factors influence both bias and variance components. Using functional ANOVA (fANOVA)
081
      and Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression, we quantify the contributions of complexity meta-
      features and hyperparameters, offering a novel approach to dissecting performance drivers.
082
083   This paper is structured as follows: Section 2 provides foundational knowledge on bias-variance
084   decomposition and fANOVA. Section 3 outlines our experimental approach. Section 4 presents
085   and discusses the experimental results.Section 5 highlights the contribution and implications of our
086   study. Finally, Section 6 concludes with a summary of key findings and suggests avenues for future
087
      research. Section 7 in the appendix discusses our causal analysis.

090
091   This section provides foundational knowledge on bias-variance decomposition, and fANOVA.

      2.1   B IAS -VARIANCE D ECOMPOSITION
094
095
      In classification tasks, bias refers to errors due to oversimplified assumptions about the problem,
096   while variance quantifies how much predictions vary across different training samples. Recent re-
097   search has emphasized the importance of decomposing a learner’s error into bias and variance com-
098   ponents, offering valuable insights into predictive performance. This approach, originating from
099   regression analysis, has been extended to classification (Geman et al., 1992) and further developed
100   by Domingos (2000).
101
      In this study, we adopt this unified bias-variance decomposition approach to help us understand
102   the expected loss by breaking it down into three components: noise, bias, and variance. Noise
103   represents the inherent unpredictability in the data, bias represents the error due to assumptions
104   made by the model, and variance represents the error due to the variability in the training data.
105   These components provide a clearer picture of the sources of error in the model’s predictions. We
106   employ this decomposition approach because it is currently the only method that provides the most
107   intuitive and general formulation of bias-variance for classification problems. For a comprehensive
      mathematical description of bias-variance decomposition, please refer to (Domingos, 2000).

      2.2     F UNCTIONAL A NALYSIS OF VARIANCE
109
110   Functional analysis of variance is a technique used to assess the individual contributions of various
111   components within a function to its overall variability. In this context, these contributions are re-
112   ferred to as performance metrics. During the training of a classifier on a dataset, multiple factors
113   influence the training process. Each factor has a specific domain of possible values, and the combi-
114   nation of these domains forms a configuration. The performance of the classifier, when trained and
115   evaluated on the dataset, is represented by a real-valued metric.
116   fANOVA breaks down the total variability in the classifier’s performance into parts attributed to dif-
117   ferent subsets of these factors. This decomposition helps in understanding how much each subset of
118   factors contributes to the overall variability. By quantifying these contributions, fANOVA provides
119   insights into which factors are most influential in affecting the performance. For detailed definitions
120   and efficient computation methods, please refer to the work of Hutter et al. (2014). In this study, we
121   utilize the fANOVA framework to quantify the contributions of each hyperparameter and complexity
122
      meta-feature to the bias and variance of the classifier.

      3.1     DATA S ELECTION AND P REPROCESSING
133
      We sourced 270 binary classification datasets from the OpenML repository (Vanschoren et al., 2014).
134   OpenML is a widely used platform for sharing machine learning datasets and experiment results.
135   Our dataset selection followed the criteria established by Mantovani et al. (2019) to ensure varied
136   complexity levels across the datasets. The selection criteria were as follows:
137
138           • Instances per dataset: Between 100 and 50,000.
139           • Features per dataset: Maximum of 1,500.
140           • No missing values in the datasets.
141
              • Datasets must not be simplified, altered, or binarized versions of multi-class problems.
142
143           • Exclusion of datasets that are adaptations of regression tasks.
144
      Given that the selected datasets had no missing values, the only preprocessing step required was
145
      encoding categorical features. We used scikit-learn’s label encoding (Pedregosa et al., 2011) to
146   assign integer values to categorical variables. Other encoding techniques like one-hot encoding
147   were avoided to prevent dimensionality inflation, especially in datasets with many categories. No
148   normalization or scaling techniques were applied, as these could inadvertently modify the intrinsic
149   complexity of the datasets. After encoding, each dataset was split into training (70%) and test (30%)
150   sets for subsequent processes.

      3.3     C OMPLEXITY M ETA - FEATURE E XTRACTION
196
197   We extracted three key complexity meta-features that characterize the difficulty of classification
198   tasks. These meta-features have been extensively studied in the literature (Ho & Basu, 2002; Lorena
199   et al., 2012; Mollineda et al., 2005). The following meta-features were extracted using the pymfe
200   package (Alcobaça et al., 2020):
201
202           • Class Overlap (N1): This complexity metric assesses whether the distributions of two
203             class labels (class 0 and class 1) are separable. To evaluate class overlap, we employed
204             the Fraction of Borderline Points method. This method constructs a minimum spanning
205             tree connecting all data points, regardless of class labels, and counts the number of points
206             connected to a different class via tree edges. A higher N1 value (ranging from 0 to just
207
                below 1) suggests significant overlap between the classes. .
208           • Data Sparsity (T2): This complexity metric evaluates the distribution density of data
209             points within the input space. We measured data sparsity by comparing the ratio of data
210             features to data samples. This method, referred to as the Average number of features per
211             points, provides insight into the distribution density. Higher T2 values (bounded between 0
212
                and m) indicate more sparse distributions, where m represents the total number of features.
213           • Class Imbalance (C2): This complexity measure captures the degree of imbalance be-
214             tween the classes in the dataset. To measure class imbalance, we computed the ratio be-
215             tween the number of samples in the majority class and the minority class. Higher values of
                C2 (bounded between 0 and 1) indicate a greater imbalance between classes.


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
             • Feature Overlap (F1v): This complexity measure evaluates the separability of two classes
217            by identifying an optimal projection vector. Once data points are projected onto this vector,
218            it assesses their separability using a directional Fisher criterion. F1v provides a bounded
219            value within the interval (0, 1]. Lower F1v values indicate simpler classification tasks, as
220            the classes are more easily distinguishable within the projected space.
221          • Linear Separanility of Classes (L2): The L2 complexity measure quantifies the per-
222            formance of a linear support vector machine (SVM) by computing its error rate on the
223            dataset. Higher L2 values (bounded between 0 and 1) suggest that the data is less amenable
224            to linear separation, implying increased classification complexity. This metric highlights
225            datasets where linear models struggle to achieve low error rates, often due to non-linear
226            class boundaries or overlapping distributions.
227          • Density: This measure assesses data connectivity in a graph by normalizing the number
228            of edges against the maximum possible. Lower Density values (bounded between 0 and 1)
229            indicate dense, same-class clusters (simpler tasks), while sparse graphs with fewer edges
230            or interclass proximity suggest higher complexity.
231

      Each complexity meta-feature was calculated from the training set of each dataset (Stage 4 in Fig-
      ure 1).

      3.4   B IAS -VARIANCE D ECOMPOSITION
236
      To assess the impact of both hyperparameters and complexity meta-features on model performance,
237   we conducted a bias-variance decomposition for each classification model (Stage 5 in Figure 1).
238   Using the bias variance decomp function from the MLxtend library (Raschka, 2018), we trained
239   each of the 304 hyperparameter configurations on each dataset and decomposed the classification
240   error into bias and variance components using the 0-1 loss function. These components offer a more
241   granular view of model performance beyond just classification accuracy.
242
243   3.5   P ERFORMANCE DATA G ENERATION AND I NFLUENCE A NALYSIS
244
245   Performance Data Generation: The next phase involved consolidating the bias-variance results,
246   hyperparameter configurations, and meta-feature values into a structured dataset referred to as per-
247
      formance data (Stage 6 in Figure 1). This dataset contained both the feature variables (hyperparam-
      eters and complexity meta-features) and target variables (bias and variance).
248
249   fANOVA Analysis: To quantify the relative influence of hyperparameters and complexity meta-
250   features on bias and variance, we applied the fANOVA framework (Hutter et al., 2014) (Stage 7
251   in Figure 1). This technique enabled us to decompose the variation in bias and variance estimates
252   and assign it to individual hyperparameters or complexity meta-features. We employed fANOVA
253
      because it is well-suited for high-dimensional parameter space exploration, allowing us to determine
      the relative importance of each factor. We normalized the independent variables to improve the
254
      interpretability of results.
255
256   Linear Regression Analysis: To complement fANOVA and quantify the magnitude and direction
257   of each factor’s effect, we applied Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression. We standardized the
258   independent variables, which included hyperparameters and meta-features, and tested the regression
259
      coefficients for statistical significance using p-values. This approach enabled us to analyze and
      interpret the relationships between these factors and the resulting bias-variance outcomes.
260
261   Causal Analysis: Lastly, we conducted an analysis based on the Manipulation Theory of Causation
262   (Section 7 in appendix) to establish a causal link between dataset complexity and model perfor-
263   mance. This analysis confirmed that dataset complexity has a more consistent and pronounced
264   effect on bias and variance than hyperparameters.

      4.3   S UMMARY OF R ESULTS
479
480   The findings emphasize that dataset complexity—particularly class overlap (N1) for tree-based mod-
481   els like Random Forest (RF) and Decision Trees (DT), and class imbalance for SVMs—plays a piv-
482   otal role in influencing bias and variance in classification models. Specifically, the significant impact
483   of class overlap on RF and DT corroborates recent findings by Kim & Jung (2023), which identified
484   improved performance in these models when resampling techniques such as SMOTE-TomekLinks
485   and SMOTE-PSO were applied to datasets with high complexity (e.g., class overlap and imbalance).
      While that study reported performance improvements, it did not provide an explanation for the un-


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      derlying reasons. Our results fill this gap, demonstrating that N1 complexity strongly governs the
487   bias-variance dynamics in tree-based models.
488
489   While hyperparameter tuning is essential, its impact diminishes for datasets with high complexity,
490
      where intrinsic characteristics exert a far greater influence. The unexplained variability in bias and
      variance underscores the need to explore higher-order interactions between complexity features and
491
      hyperparameters for a deeper understanding of model behavior.
492
493   Although uniform hyperparameter sampling may not capture narrow optimal ranges, the results
494   robustly illustrate the dominance of dataset complexity over hyperparameters in determining perfor-
495   mance variability.

500
501   Importance of Data Complexity: These findings emphasize the critical role of data complex-
502   ity in classification performance. Beyond optimizing models, addressing intrinsic dataset chal-
503
      lenges—such as class overlap—is essential to enhancing robustness and overall model effectiveness.
504
505
      Bias-Variance Tradeoff Insights: The decomposition of classification error into bias and vari-
      ance components revealed that complexity meta-features play a pivotal role in shaping these two
506
      elements. For example, data complexity features like N1 consistently demonstrated a positive corre-
507
      lation with both bias and variance, indicating that addressing this factor can simultaneously improve
508   both aspects.
509
510   Another interesting insight was that for some models, the hyperparameters maintained the same
511
      correlation with both bias and variance. This suggests that tuning these hyperparameters could
      improve both bias and variance simultaneously, rather than leading to the typical bias-variance trade-
512
      off often associated with hyperparameter tuning.
513
514
      5.2   P RACTICAL I MPLICATIONS
515
516   Data Preprocessing: Practitioners should prioritize identifying and mitigating dataset complexi-
517   ties, such as class overlap, early in the development process. Focusing on these issues during data
518   preprocessing can significantly enhance performance, often more effectively than hyperparameter
519   optimization.
520
521   Model Selection and Design: For datasets with substantial complexity, selecting models inher-
522   ently robust to class overlap, can offer more reliable reductions in bias and variance than relying
523   solely on hyperparameter adjustments.
524
525   6     C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we explored the relative impact of hyperparameters and dataset complexity meta-
528   features on the bias-variance dynamics of machine learning models, using RF, SVM, DT, AB and
529   MLP classifiers as case studies. Leveraging the fANOVA framework, we conducted an in-depth
530   analysis to identify the primary drivers behind bias and variance, a research direction that has been
531   largely underexplored. Our analysis showed that complexity meta-features—such as class over-
532   lap—have a consistently stronger impact on model behavior than hyperparameter settings. By high-
533   lighting the dominant role of dataset complexity in determining bias and variance, these findings
534
      emphasize the importance of adopting data-centric approaches to improving model performance.
535   Future research should extend this analysis to other domains and tasks to further validate these
536   findings. Also, further work is needed to develop comprehensive meta-features that capture nuanced
537   dataset properties beyond those studied here.

      7.1   C AUSAL A NALYSIS
705
706   To further validate the findings from our fANOVA analysis and reinforce the assertion that reducing
707   dataset complexity has a greater causal impact on model performance than hyperparameter tuning,
708   we conducted a causal analysis. Recognizing that correlation does not imply causation, we anchored
709   this analysis in the Manipulation Theory of Causation, which posits that variable X is a direct cause
710   of variable Y if an intervention on X results in a change in Y, while other factors remain constant
711   (Galatis, 2018). This approach allows us to disentangle the effects of different interventions and
712
      attribute changes in model behavior to specific causes. It is important to note that we apply this
      theory under the assumption of causal sufficiency, meaning there are no hidden confounding factors
713
      influencing the observed relationships.
714
715   In this analysis, we aim to determine whether interventions on complexity meta-
716   features—particularly the N1 complexity meta-feature (class overlap)—have a more profound and
717   consistent impact on bias and variance than tuning the min samples leaf hyperparameter in an RF
718
      model. Our hypothesis is twofold:
719
             • H1: N1 complexity exerts a stronger causal effect on both bias and variance than the
720
               min samples leaf hyperparameter.
721
722
             • H2: Interventions to reduce N1 complexity will lead to greater improvements in overall
               model performance, compared to hyperparameter tuning.
723
724
      This analysis seeks not only to verify causal relationships but also to attribute changes in model be-
725   havior to these interventions, shedding light on the role of data complexity in model generalization.
726
727
      7.2   DATA G ENERATION AND M ODEL S ETUP P ROCESS
728
729   To evaluate this hypothesis, we utilized the make classification function from scikit-learn (Pe-
730   dregosa et al., 2011) to generate synthetic data that simulates the complexity characteristics of the
731   German Credit Dataset (Hofmann, 1994). This synthetic dataset allowed us to control and manipu-
732   late class overlap to systematically observe the effect of the N1 complexity meta-feature on model
733   bias and variance. The dataset was generated with the following specifications:
734
             • Number of features: 20.
735
736          • Number of informative features: 10.
737          • Number of classes: 2.
738          • Class overlap: Configured to reflect a moderate N1 complexity value of 0.54.
739
740
             • The class distribution was kept moderately imbalanced to retain a real-world characteristic,
               with a ratio of 60:40 for the two classes.
741
742   We employed an RF model as our base classifier for this analysis. The RF was initialized with default
743   hyperparameter settings, except for the min samples leaf hyperparameter, which was intentionally
744   misconfigured to a suboptimal value of 10 to simulate underfitting. This setup allows us to observe
745   how hyperparameter tuning and dataset complexity reduction impact bias and variance under high-
746   bias conditions.
747
748   7.3   H YPERPARAMETER AND N1 C OMPLEXITY T UNING P ROCESS
749
750   To assess the impact of hyperparameter tuning on model performance, we focused on optimizing
751   the min samples leaf hyperparameter. We systematically varied the value of min samples leaf from
752
      10 to 1. The goal was to observe the bias and variance behavior at different configurations of this
      parameter.
753
754   To evaluate the effect of reducing dataset complexity, we focused on decreasing the N1 complexity
755   meta-feature by manipulating class overlap in the synthetic dataset. This was achieved by pro-
      gressively separating the classes in feature space, thereby reducing the overlap between them. The


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      resulting changes in N1 allowed us to observe how decreasing class overlap influences bias and
757   variance while keeping the hyperparameter min samples leaf fixed at its suboptimal value.
758
759
      7.4   R ESULTS
760
761   Our causal analysis results support both hypotheses (H1 and H2) and are consistent with the
762   fANOVA findings, showing that dataset complexity has a significantly greater impact on bias and
763   variance compared to hyperparameters.
764
      Effect of min samples leaf Tuning: Figure 6a illustrates the impact of tuning min samples leaf
765   on model performance. When the RF model was trained with the misconfigured value of
766   min samples leaf, the model exhibited underfitting, characterized by high bias and low variance.
767   Upon tuning min samples leaf to its optimal value of 1 (van Rijn & Hutter, 2018), we observed
768   a marginal reduction in bias but a corresponding increase in variance. This confirms that while
769   min samples leaf impacts bias, its effect on variance is limited, and the trade-off between bias and
770   variance is consistent with typical hyperparameter optimization behavior.
771   Effect of N1 Complexity Reduction: In contrast, Figure 6b shows the effect of reducing N1 com-
772   plexity by decreasing class overlap. We observed a simultaneous reduction in both bias and variance,
773   despite the misconfigured min samples leaf hyperparameter. This result highlights that reducing
774   class overlap and, by extension, dataset complexity, exert a stronger causal influence on model per-
775   formance than hyperparameter tuning.

      7.5   D ISCUSSION
793
794   The causal analysis conducted in this study underscores the critical importance of dataset complex-
795   ity, particularly class overlap, in shaping RF model behavior and performance. By isolating the
796   effects of reducing the N1 complexity meta-feature compared to optimizing hyperparameters like
797   the min samples leaf in an RF model, we provide strong evidence that complexity meta-features
798   exert a greater influence on bias and variance dynamics.
799   A key insight from this analysis is that class overlap—captured by the N1 meta-feature—has a
800   strong causal impact on model bias and variance. Reducing class overlap consistently led to better
801   separability between classes, which resulted in a simultaneous decrease in both bias and variance.
802   This simultaneous improvement is significant because it challenges the common notion of a strict
803   bias-variance tradeoff, where typically, reducing one comes at the expense of increasing the other.
804   Our findings show that addressing class overlap can mitigate both issues concurrently, suggesting
805   that data complexity has a more direct and profound influence on RF model performance than hy-
806   perparameter tuning.
