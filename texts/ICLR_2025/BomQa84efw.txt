                                                        A BSTRACT
008
009
                   Large language models have revolutionized natural language processing by lever-
010
                   aging self-supervised pretraining on vast textual data. Inspired by this success,
011
                   researchers have investigated complicated speech tokenization methods to dis-
012                cretize continuous speech signals so that language modeling techniques can be
013                applied to speech data. However, existing approaches either model semantic (con-
014                tent) tokens, potentially losing acoustic information, or model acoustic tokens,
015                risking the loss of semantic (content) information. Having multiple token types
016                also complicates the architecture and requires additional pretraining. Here we
017                show that discretizing mel-filterbank channels into discrete intensity bins produces
018                a simple representation (dMel), that performs better than other existing speech
019                tokenization methods. Using an LM-style transformer architecture for speech-text
020
                   modeling, we comprehensively evaluate different speech tokenization methods on
                   speech recognition (ASR) and speech synthesis (TTS). Our results demonstrate
021
                   the effectiveness of dMel in achieving high performance on both tasks within a
022
                   unified framework, paving the way for efficient and effective joint modeling of
023                speech and text. The code will be open sourced.

026   1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success in various natural language pro-
029   cessing tasks by leveraging self-supervised pretraining on massive amounts of textual data (Brown
030   et al., 2020). Inspired by this success, numerous works (Borsos et al., 2023; Rubenstein et al., 2023;
031   Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023a) have sought to extend the language modeling approach to
032   speech processing, aiming to build unified models capable of both speech understanding and genera-
033   tion tasks. However, a key challenge lies in the continuous nature of speech signals, necessitating
034   effective tokenization methods to discretize the input for language model-based processing.
035   Current speech tokenization approaches can be broadly categorized into two types: semantic (content)
036   tokens and acoustic tokens1 . Semantic tokens, extracted from self-supervised (SSL) pretrained speech
037   models (Baevski et al., 2020; Hsu et al., 2021), where the speech signal is first encoded into speech
038   representations and then clustered into semantic tokens with k-means method. However, such SSL
039   pretrained models are not useful for high fidelity speech synthesis as speaker identity and other
040   details of raw speech are lost in training (Borsos et al., 2023). Conversely, acoustic tokens can be
041
      obtained from audio compression models that are trained to compress the speech signal into codebook
      indices with residual vector quantization (RVQ) and reconstruction objectives (Zeghidour et al., 2021;
042
      Défossez et al., 2022). These tokens prioritize acoustic reconstruction but lose semantic information
043
      which can lead to poorer results in generating audio (Wang et al., 2023a).
044
045   To combine the advantages of both semantic and acoustic tokens, AudioLM (Borsos et al., 2023)
046   proposed to model both semantic tokens and acoustic tokens with 3 stages: semantic modeling, coarse
047
      acoustic modeling, and fine acoustic modeling. The coarse-to-fine modeling strategy is designed to
      match the residual structure of RVQ based acoustic tokens. This solution addresses both content and
048
      speech quality, but its multi-stage hierarchical structure complicates the model and can lead to slower
049
      training and inference. Another solution is to combine the semantic and acoustic features together.
050   Zhang et al. (2023b) proposed to distill the semantic tokens into the acoustic token’s first residual
051   channel during the training of the RVQ model in a teacher-student manner. In this way, the new
052   feature can preserve the semantic information better and also reconstruct high quality speech signals.

      By operating on the log mel-filterbanks and preserving the frequency and intensity information (with
072   some loss of resolution from discretization), dMel inherently preserves both semantic and acoustic
073   information in a unified representation, without the need for separate tokenization or additional
074   pretraining of a tokenization model. There are many advantages to discretizing log mel-filterbanks:
075   • Log mel-filterbanks is an interpretable representation of speech signal, where both the semantic and
076      acoustic information is preserved. As discretization has little impact, dMel inherits the properties.
077
      • dMel is a model-free representation grounded in raw acoustic space. As a result it can be converted
078      to waveforms by any mel-filterbank vocoder, unlike other tokenization schemes that have feature
079      representations that are intricately coupled to both the encoder and the decoder.
080
      • Different channels of dMel do not have the complex hierarchical dependencies on each other that
081      is typical of coarse-to-fine acoustic tokens; we find that they can be modeled independently in each
082      frame using a simple decoder-only (LM-style) transformer architecture.
083
      While data-driven methods can be improved by introducing more diverse data, they inherently suffer
084   from information loss due to their neural compression nature – they learn to discard information
085   based on training data, which may prove crucial in unseen conditions. In contrast, mel-spectrogram
086   is a physics-based signal representation that:
087   • Preserves frequency components through principled transformation – while it does not retain phase
088      information, this aligns with human auditory perception primarily sensitive to magnitude spectrum.
089
      • Has demonstrated robust performance over decades of speech processing research.
090
      • Does not require data-dependent training to handle different acoustic conditions.
091
092   • Maintains consistent behavior across various noise conditions due to its deterministic nature.
093   Through comprehensive evaluations, we show that using dMel allows us to use a single decoder-only
094   model, and achieve high performance on both automatic speech recognition (ASR) and text-to-speech
095   (TTS) tasks. The ASR task validates that dMel preserves semantic information, while the TTS task
096
      shows that dMel are useful for high-fidelity acoustic reconstruction of speech. We also compare
      dMel to other tokenization methods and find that dMel achieves the best WER for ASR task, which
097
      indicates that the semantic information is well preserved. Also, dMel achieves the lower WER score
098
      for TTS task when using WhisperX (Bain et al., 2023) for automatic evaluation and we find model
099   trained with dMel can generate long and natural speech samples (see Supplementary Material).

127   2.1   D M E L S PEECH T OKENIZER
128
129   Different from existing VQ-VAE (Borsos et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2024; Zeghidour
130   et al., 2021) based speech tokenizers, we propose a discretized log mel-filterbanks based speech
131
      tokenizer. The outline of the discretization method is shown in Figure 1. Later in the paper, we show
      that this tokenizer allows the model to process the input speech signal efficiently and capture the
132
      relevant acoustic features for both ASR and TTS tasks.
133

135   tokenizer takes the input speech signal x and computes the log mel-filterbanks representation M:

174   2.2   U NIFIED S PEECH -T EXT T RANSFORMER D ECODER
175
176   Modeling speech and text sequences jointly is essential for a model to understand and generate both
177   modalities. However, it is challenging to design a unified model that can handle both speech-to-text
178   and text-to-speech effectively. In this work, we apply a unified LM-style transformer model that takes
179
      speech and text tokens as input and generates the output tokens in the target sequence. The model is
      trained in end-to-end on a combined dataset of speech and text pairs, enabling it to learn the joint
180
      representations for ASR and TTS tasks. As we show in the rest of the paper, the crucial part for the
181
      joint model training is the proper speech tokenization which dMel provides.
182
183   Token Representation For text data, we apply a character-level tokenizer to convert the input text
184   into a sequence of text tokens. The text tokens are passed through an embedding layer, Embed(·) :
185   {j|j = 1, 2, 3 . . . L} → RD , where D is the embedding dimension and L is the vocabulary size. The
186
      dimension of the speech token embedding is set to be the same as the text token embedding D and
      no further mapping is required. The motivation for using a character-level tokenizer is to reduce the
187
      vocabulary size L and improve the model’s generalization ability. Also, character tokens can capture
188
      the fine-grained linguistic features that are essential for both ASR and TTS tasks.
189
190   For speech signal, we apply the dMel speech tokenizer to convert the input speech signal into a
191   sequence of speech tokens. Then, the speech tokens S ∈ BT ×N are passed through a learnable
192
      embedding layer, Embed(·) : B → Rd , and a learnable linear layer, Linear(·) : RN ×d → RD , to
      obtain the speech token representation E ∈ RT ×D :
193
194   Et = Linear(E′ t ), and E′ t = Concatenate([Embed(St,1 ), Embed(St,2 ), . . . , Embed(St,N )]), (6)
195
196   where Et ∈ RD is the speech token representation. Here, for every time frame t, a speech token St is
197   processed in parallel and independently for every frequency channel i by Embed(St,i ) mapping, and
198
      then embeddings of all frequency channels are stacked together to form one vector representation
      E′ t for the frame t. Finally, the speech token embeddings Et are fed into the LM-style transformer
199
      models for further processing.
200

      frame-rate. First, dMel has a much smaller vocabulary, as it is discretized mel-filterbanks energies,
217   allowing all 80 channels to share the same vocabulary since they represent similar energy values.
218   In contrast, neural compression encoders like SpeechTokenizer require separate embeddings for
219   different channels. Also, dMel operates at a lower frame-rate while maintaining a higher bit-rate.
220   The reduced frame-rate leads to shorter sequence lengths during both training and inference, which
221   is particularly advantageous when using large models. While a higher bit-rate typically increases
222   model complexity for compression-based tokenizers, this is not the case for dMel due to two key
223   factors: i) dMel is encoder-free, without any compression encoder; ii) the complexity of the model
224   introduced in Section 2.2 depends only on the vocabulary size and sequence length (frame-rate), not
225
      on the code dimensions. In compression-based methods, increasing the bit-rate requires either larger
      codebooks or additional residual dimensions, leading to increased tokenizer complexity. Moreover,
226
      these methods require more complex downstream models to handle the expanded representations.
227
      Given recent studies (Défossez et al., 2024; Mousavi et al., 2024) demonstrated that bit-rate does
228   not strongly correlate with downstream model performance, we focus our comparative analysis on
229   frame-rate rather than bit-rate when evaluating different speech tokens for downstream tasks. This
230   approach challenges the conventional assumption that higher bit-rates necessarily yield better results.
231
232
      Speaker Representation To properly model multi-speaker data, we also include speaker embed-
      dings as input to the transformer decoder. The speaker embeddings are extracted from an independent
233
      dvector (Variani et al., 2014) model3 . We use a learnable linear layer to map the speaker embeddings
234
      to the same dimension as the speech and text token embeddings D. The speaker representation
235   is optional for ASR task, but required for TTS task. Hence, during the training, it is applied for
236   text-to-speech and ignored for speech-to-text.
237   Transformer Decoder The transformer decoder is trained end-to-end on a combined dataset of
238   speech and text pairs. For TTS training, the input sequence is constructed by concatenating the
239   speaker embedding (extracted from a random audio for the same speaker of the current sample), text
240   tokens, and speech tokens. For ASR training, the input sequence is constructed by concatenating the
241   speech tokens and text tokens. Both tasks are trained with causal masking, where the model is trained
242   to predict the next token based on the previous tokens. The loss is calculated using the cross-entropy
243   loss between the predicted tokens and the ground-truth tokens. Loss calculation is skipped on the
244
      speech tokens for ASR task and on the text tokens for TTS task. Note, that all frequency channels
      at time frame t for dMel tokenizer are predicted independently and in parallel. To capture the
245
      relative distances between tokens in the input sequence, we apply multiplicative relative positional
246
      embedding RoPE (Su et al., 2024). This allows the model to learn the positional relationships between
247   speech tokens, text tokens, and speaker embeddings, enhancing its ability to generate coherent output
248   sequences. For positional embeddings we do not distinguish between text, speech and speaker tokens
249   and thus having global positions notation across all of them, see Figure 2.
250   Robust Training Compared to LMs, audio frames are highly redundant with strong local correla-
251   tions. This makes longform generation difficult for models due to exposure bias (Bengio et al., 2015).
252   To mitigate exposure bias during training, we apply span-masking (Raffel et al., 2020) to the speech
253   token context, masking out multiple random spans of speech frames. The model is trained to predict
254   the next token based on the masked context. This context-masking strategy helps the model learn to
255   generate accurate speech tokens in the presence of missing information, improving its robustness and
256
      generalization. It forces the model to attend to the text rather than copying previously inferred speech
      tokens due to learnt correlations. We also find that span-masking text tokens improves the ASR task.

468
469   Speech Tokenization Recent advancements in speech tokenization have primarily focused on
470   two approaches: semantic tokens and acoustic tokens. This section examines these methods, their
471
      combinations, and their limitations, highlighting the need for more efficient and generalizable
      solutions. Semantic tokens, extracted from self-supervised pretrained speech models, have shown
472
      promise in capturing high-level content information. Methods like wav2vec (Baevski et al., 2020) and
473
      HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) employ k-means clustering on speech representations to generate these
474   tokens. While effective in capturing semantic content, these approaches often struggle with preserving
475   fine-grained acoustic details crucial for high-quality speech synthesis. In contrast, acoustic tokens,
476   derived from pretrained audio compression models, excel at preserving low-level acoustic information.
477   Techniques such as SoundStream (Zeghidour et al., 2021) and EnCodec (Défossez et al., 2022) utilize
478   residual vector quantization (RVQ) with reconstruction objectives. These methods achieve high-
479   quality audio compression but may not capture higher-level semantic structures effectively.
480   Recognizing the complementary nature of semantic and acoustic tokens, recent works have attempted
481   to combine these approaches. AudioLM (Borsos et al., 2023) introduced a three-stage model:
482   semantic modeling, coarse acoustic modeling, and fine acoustic modeling. While comprehensive, this
483   approach introduces complexity and computational overhead. AudioPalm (Rubenstein et al., 2023)
484   further demonstrated the critical importance of large-scale training data and model parameters for
485   effective multi-stage modeling, highlighting potential generalization issues in low-resource scenarios.
      An alternative hybrid approach, proposed by Zhang et al. (2023b), attempts to distill semantic

495   additional pretraining and does not fully achieve a single-stage model architecture.
496   Despite these advancements, several challenges persist in the field of speech tokenization: i) balancing
497   semantic and acoustic information in a unified representation; ii) reducing model complexity and
498   computational requirements; iii) improving generalization to low-resource / out-of-domain data e.g.
499   with mixed speech from multiple speakers, or multiple languages, or changing characteristics of
500   recording equipment/sampling rate etc.; iv) developing truly single-stage tokenizers. Our proposed
501   method, dMel, addresses these challenges by offering a training-free speech tokenization approach.
502
      By directly discretizing log mel-filterbanks into bins, it inherently preserves both semantic and
      acoustic information in a unified representation, while significantly reducing computational complex-
503
      ity. Concurrently, Langman et al. (2024) proposed a different mel-filterbanks based speech tokens:
504
      spectral codecs, where disjointed mel-bands are encoded separately and then quantized using an
505   FSQ (Mentzer et al., 2023). Although spectral codecs and dMel are both discretizing mel-filterbanks,
506   dMel is encoder-free and tested with autoregressive generation tasks, while spectral codecs need
507   encoders and is tested with non-autogressive generation task. Also, our scalar quantization method
508   shares some similarities with FSQ, but FSQ is in a learned latent code space and necessitates an
509   additional bound operation to limit the range of the latent codes.
510   Speech-Text Modeling Modeling speech and text jointly is a challenging task, as speech signals
511   are continuous and while text is discrete. Existing works have explored various approaches to address
512   this challenge, including usage of separate encoders for different modalities (Ao et al., 2021; Bapna
513   et al., 2021). Bai et al. (2022) proposed an encoder-only model A3T for speech-text modeling,
514   by introducing alignment embedding to encourage cross-modal transfer between text and speech.
515
      Although A3T achieved good performance on speech synthesis and editing tasks, it cannot generate
      text and cannot generalize to longform generation because of its encoder-only architecture and
516
      mask-reconstruction training strategy. VioLA (Wang et al., 2023b) also targets a unified speech-text
517
      model which can generate speech and text with a single model, but it is specifically designed for the
518   Encodec (Défossez et al., 2022) style feature, and compelled to model speech tokens in a multi-stage
519   hierarchical manner. Maiti et al. (2024) proposed a LM-style model VOXTLM, to model speech
520   and text jointly. However, VOXTLM is only models the HuBERT semantic tokens, and relies on an
521   external generation model to transform semantic tokens into waveform, but the speaker and acoustic
522   information are lost. In comparison, the model architecture in this paper is a simple, single stage
523   LM-style transformer model, and can handle both the speech generation and text generation tasks.
524
525   5    C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, we proposed dMel, a novel train-free speech tokenization method that discretizes
528   log mel-filterbank energies directly into bins. By operating on the authentic log mel-filterbank
529   representation, dMel inherently preserves both semantic and acoustic information in a unified
530   tokenized representation. Our key contribution is the evaluation of dMel within a unified LM-
531   style transformer architecture for speech recognition (ASR) and speech synthesis (TTS) tasks.
532   Our dMel-based ASR model, RichASR, achieved the lowest word error rate among tokenization
533   methods, robustly preserving semantic content. For TTS, dMel’s generation yielded the lowest WER,
534   accurately reconstructing speech waveforms. Our dMel-based TTS model, RichTTS, achieved
535
      competitive naturalness, lowest error rates, and long audio generation capabilities.
536   dMel’s simplicity circumvents separate tokenizers or multi-stage modeling, reducing computational
537   overhead and dependence on pretrained models. By unifying semantic and acoustic modeling, dMel
538   enables efficient speech-text modeling frameworks. While initial joint TTS-ASR training showed
539   promise, further work is needed. Our primary contribution demonstrates dMel’s effectiveness for
      high-performing separate TTS and ASR models within a unified LM-style architecture.

      A    E THICS S TATEMENT
757
758
      The development and deployment of speech technologies carry important ethical considerations.
759   While our proposed dMel method aims to advance the state-of-the-art in speech-text modeling, it
760   is crucial to highlight potential ethical risks and raise the awareness so that new methods may be
761   developed to mitigate these risks.
762
      Our first main concern is the potential dual-use of speech synthesis technologies for nefarious
763
      purposes such as impersonation, misleading audio-visual content generation, or voice spoofing
764
      attacks. Proactive measures, including watermarking techniques and robust speaker verification
765   methods, should be explored to counter such risks. The former attempts to build markers into the
766   generated speech that make it easy to detect, while the latter focusses on distinguishing synthetic from
767   real data. Prior work (Le et al., 2023) has shown that neural networks can be trained to distinguish
768   speech synthesized from their model from real speech, probably because of artifacts from the use of
769   mel spectral vocoders. While we did not train a network to do so in our work yet (we will create one
770   before code release), the vocoders we use are similar to their work – going from mel spectrogram
771   to raw waveforms. Our model also does not use prosody, phoneme duration and other predictions
772   that more sophisticated TTS systems use to allow the model to perform very well on imitating
773
      speaker styles in zero-shot settings. However our model can probably mimic the styles of training
      speakers very well. It is our hope that releasing our methods will facilitate more research on fake
774
      speech verification and watermarking techniques – even if current classifiers are able to perform this
775
      detection, the quality of the generative models is improving. It is also our hope that future works will
776   attempt to perform more credit assignment – by providing metrics that show which real data samples
777   a synthetic speech example copies its style and substance from.
778
779
      Another concern is the perpetuation of societal biases encoded within training data. Speech datasets
      may exhibit biases along dimensions such as gender, race, age, or socioeconomic status, which could
780
      be propagated or amplified by trained models. Rigorous debiasing techniques and careful curation
781
      of representative training data are essential to mitigate these risks. On the mitigating side of this
782   equation, we also hope that with better, more controllable TTS systems, ASR systems can improve
783   because more data can be generated for underrepresented segments of the distribution from the TTS
784   models.
785
      Furthermore, the development and deployment of speech technologies should prioritize accessibility
786
      and inclusivity. Models should be evaluated for performance across diverse demographics, accents,
787
      and language varieties to ensure equitable access and quality of service.
788
789   Finally, it is important to foster transparency and accountability in the research and development
790   process. Clear documentation of model capabilities, limitations, and potential failure modes should
791
      be provided to enable informed decision-making and responsible usage.
792   Addressing these ethical considerations requires a multistakeholder approach involving researchers,
793   developers, policymakers, and end-users. By prioritizing ethical principles such as fairness, privacy,
794   and accountability, we can work towards realizing the benefits of speech technologies while mitigating
795   potential risks and adverse societal impacts.

799   Because TTS work is tremendously fragmented and clear protocols are not often available for training
800   and evaluation, we reimplemented other tokenizers within our code base using publicly available,
801   official implementations where available: e.g. we used Hubert-KM and speech tokenizer features
802   extraction from the public codebases and pluged them into our LM-style model training. While we
803   made the best effort to tune the tokenization methods and the models, there is always a possibility we

805   angles, and when viewed on multiple datasets. We also did not train on larger model sizes (>1B
806
      parameters), larger datasets (>1k hours), or using pretrained models.

      tasks suggests potential for broader applications. We recognize the importance of more sophisticated

       F.3   J OINT S PEECH -T EXT M ODELING D ISCUSSION
1000
1001   We found it to be challenging to train joint model for ASR and TTS, similar to observations as
1002   in Maiti et al. (2024) and e.g. Shi et al. (2022); Rouditchenko et al. (2024) for joint audio-visual
1003   speech recognition. Also, there is a very recent research work (Toyin, 2024), that also shows training
1004   TTS and ASR jointly is challenging, and needs carefully designed model architecture and training
1005   loss fusion technique.
1006   One of the reasons is the different pace of learning. Careful consideration of training strategies
1007   can mitigate some of the challenges in joint modeling of TTS and ASR tasks, highlighting the
1008   complexities inherent in combining these distinct but related tasks within a single model.
1009
       Another reason we suspect is the mismatch between train and test time, which is more pronounced for
1010
       the joint modeling: if we compare individual validation losses per task in joint model to their one-task
1011   training counterparts we see they match each other (so training is fine), however the generation (test
1012   time which mismatches how the train loss is defined) for both tasks is broken: longer sequences has
1013   hallucination and high repetition issues. This could be due to different length of sequences between
1014   text and audio and thus learnt attention pattern could be different which creates longer sequences
1015   generation issue for the joint model.
1016
       Last but not the least, the two tasks have opposite modalities in the input and output, making it rather
1017   difficult to model. Most previously researched multi-task work have the same modality in the output.
1018   The combination of ASR and TTS is a rather recent phenomenon, such as Viola and VOXTLM.
