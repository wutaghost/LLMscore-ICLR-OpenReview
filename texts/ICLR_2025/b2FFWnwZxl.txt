009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Data representation in non-Euclidean spaces has proven effective for capturing
012            hierarchical and complex relationships in real-world datasets. Hyperbolic spaces,
013            in particular, provide efficient embeddings for hierarchical structures. This paper
014            introduces the Hyperbolic Vision Transformer (HVT), a novel extension of the
015            Vision Transformer (ViT) that integrates hyperbolic geometry. While traditional
016            ViTs operate in Euclidean space, our method enhances the self-attention mechanism
017
               by leveraging hyperbolic distance and Möbius transformations. This enables more
               effective modeling of hierarchical and relational dependencies in image data. We
018
               present rigorous mathematical formulations, showing how hyperbolic geometry can
019
               be incorporated into attention layers, feed-forward networks, and optimization. We
020            offer improved performance for image classification using the ImageNet dataset.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Representation learning is fundamental to modern machine learning, enabling models to extract
026   meaningful features from raw data Bengio et al. (2014). While Euclidean spaces are traditionally
027   used to model data relationships, many real-world datasets—including images—exhibit hierarchical
028   structures better captured in non-Euclidean spaces Bronstein et al. (2017).
029   Images are inherently hierarchical, comprising structures at multiple scales: from pixels to edges,
030   from shapes to objects, and ultimately to entire scenes Biederman (1987); Riesenhuber & Poggio
031   (1999). This hierarchy can be conceptualized as:
032
033          • Pixels {pi }: The basic units of an image.
034          • Edges {ej }: Formed by grouping pixels with significant intensity gradients.
035          • Shapes {sk }: Created by combining edges into simple motifs.
036
             • Objects {ol }: Recognizable entities composed of shapes.
037
038
             • Scenes I: Complete images where objects interact within a context.
039
      The hierarchical nature of images implies that higher-level concepts are built upon lower-level features,
040   reflecting a tree-like structure. Vision Transformers (ViTs) Dosovitskiy et al. (2021a) process images
041   by dividing them into patches, treating each patch as a token. This patch-based approach introduces a
042   hierarchical representation because:
043
044         1. Local Features: Each patch captures local patterns such as textures or edges.
045         2. Global Context: By attending over patches, the model aggregates local information to
046            understand the overall structure.
047
048   This mirrors the hierarchical composition of images, from local to global features.
049   Hyperbolic spaces are well-suited for modeling hierarchical data due to their ability to embed tree-like
050   structures with minimal distortion Nickel & Kiela (2017). By utilizing hyperbolic geometry and
051   Möbius transformations, we can effectively capture the multi-scale dependencies inherent in visual
052   data Ganea et al. (2018). Specifically, Möbius transformations allow for operations like addition and
053   scalar multiplication in hyperbolic space, enabling neural networks to perform calculations while
      preserving hierarchical relationships.

068   2.1   H YPERBOLIC G EOMETRY IN M ACHINE L EARNING
069
070   Recent advances have leveraged hyperbolic geometry for machine learning, significantly impacting
071   how hierarchical data is modeled. Nickel & Kiela (2017) successfully used Poincaré embeddings
072   for such data, showing marked improvements over Euclidean embeddings. This approach was
073   further refined by Ganea et al. (2018), who introduced hyperbolic embeddings for entailment cones,
074   effectively capturing asymmetric relationships.
075   Khrulkov et al. (2020) and Liu et al. (2020) extended these concepts to visual data and zero-shot
076   recognition, respectively, underscoring the versatility of hyperbolic embeddings in handling complex
077   visual tasks. Most notably, Ermolov et al. (2022) developed Hyperbolic Vision Transformers (HVTs)
078   that incorporate these embeddings within Vision Transformer architectures, enhancing metric learning.
079   Our model extends this integration by incorporating hyperbolic geometry throughout the transformer
080   operations, from Möbius transformations to hyperbolic self-attention.
081
082   2.2   H YPERBOLIC N EURAL N ETWORKS AND ATTENTION
083
084   Ganea et al. (2018) initially formulated hyperbolic neural networks, introducing layers and activation
085
      functions suited for hyperbolic spaces. This foundation was expanded by Bachmann et al. (2020),
      who focused on optimizing these networks efficiently. Our method enriches this foundation by
086
      embedding hyperbolic layers directly within transformer architectures, enhancing the adaptability
087
      and depth of hyperbolic operations.
088
089   Another method presented in Hyperbolic Attention Networks Gulcehre et al. (2018) differs from
090   ours primarily in how hyperbolic geometry is applied within the attention mechanism. Gulcehre
091
      et al. focus on embedding the activations into hyperbolic space using both the hyperboloid and
      Klein models, leveraging hyperbolic matching and aggregation operations. In contrast, our approach
092
      incorporates learnable curvature within positional embeddings, head-specific scaling in attention, and
093
      hyperbolic layer normalization, offering more flexibility and efficiency in capturing hierarchical data.
094   Additionally, we utilize the Poincaré ball model for its computational suitability in vision tasks.
095
096
      2.3   V ISION T RANSFORMERS
097
098   Initially developed for NLP by Vaswani et al. (2017), Vision Transformers (ViT) have been adapted for
099   visual tasks, as demonstrated by Dosovitskiy et al. (2021b). Enhancements such as those proposed by
100   Caron et al. (2021) and El-Nouby et al. (2021) have refined ViTs for self-supervised learning. Unlike
101   these Euclidean-based models, our Hyperbolic Vision Transformer employs hyperbolic geometry to
102   model hierarchical and relational data structures more effectively.
103
104   2.4   C OMPARISON TO K EY H YPERBOLIC M ETHODS
105
106   While Ermolov et al. (2022) introduced HVTs that focus on hyperbolic embeddings for metric
107   learning, our approach fully integrates hyperbolic operations throughout the transformer, significantly
      enhancing its ability to manage hierarchical data beyond the objectives of metric learning. We extend

      the application scope to include image classification by embedding hyperbolic geometry directly into
109   the core components of the Vision Transformer.
110
111   Recently, Yang et al. (2024) proposed Hypformer, an efficient hyperbolic Transformer based on the
112
      Lorentz model of hyperbolic geometry. Hypformer introduces two foundational blocks—Hyperbolic
      Transformation with Curvatures (HTC) and Hyperbolic Readjustment and Refinement with Curvatures
113
      (HRC)—to define essential Transformer modules in hyperbolic space. They also develop a linear
114
      self-attention mechanism in hyperbolic space to handle large-scale graph data and long-sequence
115   inputs efficiently.
116
117   While Hypformer makes significant contributions to the development of hyperbolic Transformers,
118
      particularly in processing large-scale graph data, our model differs in several key aspects:
119           • Model Focus and Application Domain: Hypformer is primarily designed for graph data
120             and emphasizes scalability and efficiency in handling large-scale graphs and long sequences.
121             In contrast, our model focuses on vision tasks, specifically image classification, integrat-
122             ing hyperbolic geometry throughout the Vision Transformer architecture to enhance the
123             modeling of hierarchical and relational structures inherent in visual data.
124           • Hyperbolic Model Used: Hypformer operates in the Lorentz model of hyperbolic geometry,
125             whereas we utilize the Poincaré ball model. The Poincaré ball model is advantageous for
126             vision tasks due to its conformal properties, which preserve angles and better represent
127             geometric structures in image data.
128           • Innovative Components: Our model introduces unique components such as learnable
129             curvature in positional embeddings, head-specific scaling in the attention mechanism, hyper-
130             bolic layer normalization, gradient clipping, geodesic regularization, and layer scaling for
131             training stability. These innovations are specifically tailored to enhance the performance of
132             Vision Transformers in hyperbolic space.
133           • Attention Mechanism: While Hypformer develops a linear self-attention mechanism
134             to address efficiency in handling large-scale data, our model extends the standard self-
135             attention mechanism into hyperbolic space using Möbius operations and hyperbolic distance
136             calculations. This approach allows us to capture complex relationships in visual data more
137
                effectively.
138
      By fully integrating hyperbolic operations within the Vision Transformer framework and focusing
139   on the unique challenges of vision tasks, our model offers a comprehensive solution that provides
140   superior performance over previous hyperbolic Transformers, including Hypformer, in the domain of
141   image classification.
