009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011             Bayesian methods are known to address some limitations of standard deep learn-
012             ing, such as the lack of calibrated predictions and uncertainty quantification. How-
013             ever, they can be computationally expensive as model and data complexity in-
014             crease. Fast variational methods can reduce the computational requirements of
015             Bayesian methods by eliminating the need for gradient descent or sampling, but
016             are often limited to simple models. We demonstrate that conditional mixture net-
017             works (CMNs), a probabilistic variant of the mixture-of-experts (MoE) model,
018             are suitable for fast, gradient-free inference and can solve complex classifica-
019
                tion tasks, thus balancing the expressiveness and scalability of neural networks
                with the probabilistic benefits of Bayesian methods . By exploiting conditional
020
                conjugacy and Pólya-Gamma augmentation, we furnish Gaussian likelihoods for
021
                the weights of both the experts and the gating network. This enables efficient
022             variational updates using coordinate ascent variational inference (CAVI), avoid-
023             ing traditional gradient-based optimization. We validate this approach by training
024             two-layer CMNs on standard benchmarks from the UCI repository. Our method,
025             CAVI-CMN, achieves competitive and often superior predictive accuracy com-
026             pared to maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) with backpropagation, while
027             maintaining competitive runtime and full posterior distributions over all model
028             parameters. Moreover, as input size or the number of experts increases, computa-
029             tion time scales competitively with MLE and other gradient-based solutions like
030
                black-box variational inference (BBVI), making CAVI-CMN a promising tool for
                deep, fast, and gradient-free Bayesian networks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Modern machine learning methods attempt to learn functions of complex data (e.g., images, au-
037
      dio, text) to predict information associated with that data, such as discrete labels in the case of
      classification (Bernardo et al., 2007). Deep neural networks (DNNs) have demonstrated success in
038
      this domain, owing to their universal function approximation properties (Park & Sandberg, 1991)
039
      and the soft regularization inherited from stochastic gradient descent learning via backpropagation
040   (Amari, 1993). However, despite its computational efficiency, accuracy, and scalability to increas-
041   ingly large datasets and models, DNNs trained this way do not provide well-calibrated predictions
042   or uncertainty estimates, and practitioners typically utilize post-hoc calibration methods on valida-
043   tion datasets (Wang et al., 2021; Shao et al., 2020). This limits the applicability and reliability of
044   using DNNs in safety-critical applications like autonomous driving, medicine, and disaster response
045   (Papamarkou et al., 2024), where uncertainty-sensitive decision-making is required.
046   Bayesian machine learning addresses the issues of poor calibration and uncertainty quantification by
047   offering a probabilistic framework that casts learning model parameters θ as  a process of inference -
048   namely, calculating a posterior distribution over model parameters  p θ | D , given observed data D
049   (e.g., a data set of input-output pairs D = (x   x1 , y 1 ), . . . , (x
                                                                            xn , y n ) ). The resulting posterior distri-
050   bution captures both expectations about model parameters θ and their uncertainty. The uncertainty
051   is then incorporated in predictions p(yy ) that are, in principle, well-calibrated to new datapoints com-
052   ing from the same set. This probabilistic treatment allows methods like Bayesian neural networks
053   (BNNs) (Hernández-Lobato & Adams, 2015) to maintain the expressiveness of deep neural networks
      while also encoding uncertainty over network weights and thus the network’s predictions. However,


                                                              1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      these methods are often accompanied by an increase in computational cost and still involve running
055   gradient descent or generating samples (Izmailov et al., 2021).
056
057   In this paper we introduce a gradient-free variational learning algorithm for a probabilistic variant of
058
      a two-layer, feedforward neural network — the conditional mixture network or CMN — and mea-
      sure its performance on supervised learning benchmarks. This method rests on coordinate ascent
059
      variational inference (CAVI) (Wainwright et al., 2008; Hoffman et al., 2013) and hence we name it
060
      CAVI-CMN. We compare CAVI-CMN to maximum likelihood estimation and two other Bayesian
061   estimation techniques: the No U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) variant of Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (Hoff-
062   man et al., 2014) and black-box variational inference (Ranganath et al., 2014). We demonstrate that
063   CAVI-CMN maintains the predictive accuracy and scalability of an architecture-matched feedfor-
064   ward neural network fit with maximum likelihood estimation (i.e., gradient descent via backprop-
065   agation), while maintaining full distributions over network parameters and generating calibrated
066   predictions, as measured in relationship to state-of-the-art Bayesian methods like NUTS and BBVI.
067   Unlike other Bayesian (NUTS, BBVI) and non-Bayesian gradient based approaches (MLE), CAVI-
068   CMN achieves SOTA-like performance with absolute runtime comparable to a backpropagation-
069
      based MLE approach, thanks to the fast convergence properties of coordinate ascent variational
      inference.
070
071   We summarize the contributions of this work below:
072
073          • We introduce and derive a variational inference scheme for the conditional mixture net-
074            work, which we term CAVI-CMN. This relies on the use of conjugate priors for the linear
075            experts and Pólya-Gamma augmentation (Polson et al., 2013) for the gating network and
076
               the final softmax layer.
077          • CAVI-CMN matches, and sometimes exceeds, the performance of maximum likelihood
078            estimation (MLE) in terms of predictive accuracy, while maintaining the benefits of a
079            full Bayesian approach, yielding well calibrated models that quantify uncertainty. This
080            is shown across a suite of 8 different supervised classification tasks (2 synthetic, 6 real).
081          • CAVI-CMN displays all the benefits explained above while requiring drastically less time
082            to converge and overall runtime than the other state-of-the-art Bayesian methods like NUTS
083            and BBVI.
084
085   The rest of this paper is organized as follows: first, we discuss related works include the MoE archi-
086   tecture and existing (Bayesian and non-Bayesian approaches) to fitting these models. We then in-
087   troduce the probabilistic conditional mixture model and derive a variational inference algorithm for
088
      optimizing posterior distributions over its latent variables and parameters. We present experimen-
      tal results comparing the performance of CAVI-based conditional mixture models with sampling
089
      based methods, such as BBVI, NUTS, and traditional MLE based estimation, where gradients of the
090
      log likelihood are computed using backpropagation and used to update the network’s parameters.
091   Finally, we discuss the implications of these findings and potential directions for future research.

094   2   R ELATED WORK
095
096   The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture is a close relative of the CMN model we introduce here.
097   Jacobs et al. (1991) originally introduced MoEs as a way to improve the performance of neural net-
098   works by combining the strengths of multiple specialized models (Gormley & Frühwirth-Schnatter,
099
      2019). MoE models process inputs by averaging the predictions of individual learners or experts,
      where each expert’s output is weighted by a different mixing coefficient before the averaging. The
100
      fundamental idea behind MoE is that the input space can be partitioned in such a way that differ-
101
      ent experts (models) can be trained to excel in different regions of this space, with a gating network
102   determining the appropriate expert (or combination of experts) for each input. This leads to compos-
103   able (and sometimes interpretable) latent descriptions of arbitrary input-output relationships (Eigen
104   et al., 2013), further bolstered by the MoE’s capacity for universal function approximation (Nguyen
105   et al., 2016; Nguyen & Chamroukhi, 2018). Indeed, the powerful self-attention mechanism em-
106   ployed by transformers has has demonstrated the power and flexibility of MoE models (Movellan &
107   Gabbur, 2020). Non-Bayesian approaches to MoE typically rely on maximum likelihood estimation
      (MLE) (Jacobs et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs, 1994), which can suffer from overfitting and poor


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      generalization due to the lack of regularization mechanisms (Bishop & Svenskn, 2003), especially
109   in low data size regimes.
110
111   To address these issues, Bayesian approaches to MoE have been developed, which incorporate prior
112
      information and yield posterior distributions over model parameters (Bishop & Svenskn, 2003;
      Mossavat & Amft, 2011). This Bayesian treatment enables the estimation of model evidence
113
      (log marginal-likelihood) and provides a natural framework for model comparison and selection
114
      (Svensén, 2003; Zens, 2019). Bayesian MoE models offer significant advantages, such as improved
115   robustness against overfitting and a better understanding of uncertainty in predictions. However,
116   they also introduce computational challenges, particularly when dealing with high-dimensional data
117   and complex model structures.
118
      The introduction of the Pólya-Gamma (PG) augmentation technique in Polson et al. (2013) enabled a
119
      range of novel and more computationally efficient algorithms for Bayesian treatment of MoE models
120
      (Linderman et al., 2015; He et al., 2019; Sharma et al., 2019; Viroli & McLachlan, 2019; Zens et al.,
121   2023). Here we complement these past works, which mostly rest on improving sampling methods
122   with PG augmentation, by introducing a closed-form update rules for MoE’s with linear experts in
123   the form of coordinate ascent variational inference (CAVI).

      3.1   C ONDITIONAL MIXTURES FOR FUNCTION APPROXIMATION
133
      Feedforward neural networks are highly expressive, approximating nonlinear functions through
134   sequences of nonlinear transformations, but the posterior distributions over their weights are in-
135   tractable, requiring expensive techniques like MCMC or variational inference (MacKay, 1992; Blun-
136   dell et al., 2015; Daxberger et al., 2021).
137
      We circumvent these problems by focusing on the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models (Jacobs et al.,
138
      1991), and particularly a variant of MoE that is amenable to gradient free CAVI parameter updates.
139
      MoEs can be made tractable to gradient-free CAVI when the expert likelihoods are constrained to
140   be members of the exponential family (see Section 2 for more details on the MoE architecture), and
141   when the gating network is formulated in such a way to allow exact Bayesian inference (through
142   lower bounds on the log-sigmoid likelihood (Jaakkola & Jordan, 1997; Bishop & Svenskn, 2003) or
143   Pólya-Gamma augmentation (Polson et al., 2013)).
144
      The MoE can be reformulated probabilistically as a mixture model by introducing a latent assign-
145
      ment variable, z n , leading to a joint probability distribution of the form
146

154
      The problem of learning the model’s parameters, then becomes one of doing inference over the la-
155
      tent variables Z and parameters Θ of the mixture model. However, mixture models are generally not
156   tractable for exact Bayesian inference, so some form of approximation or sampling-based scheme
157   is required to obtain full posteriors over their parameters. However, if each expert (i.e., likelihood
158   distribution) in the MoE belongs to the exponential family, the model becomes conditionally con-
159   jugate. This allows for derivation of exact fixed-point updates to an approximate posterior over
160   each expert’s parameters. The approach we propose, CAVI-CMN, does exactly this – we take ad-
161   vantage of the conditional conjugacy of mixture models, along with an augmentation trick for the
      the gating network, to make all parameters amenable to an approximate Bayesian treatment. The

162
      conditionally-conjugate form of the model allows us to use coordinate ascent variational inference to
163   obtain posteriors over the weights of both the individual linear experts and the gating network (Wain-
164   wright et al., 2008; Hoffman et al., 2013; Blei et al., 2017), without resorting to costly gradient or
165   sampling computations.
166
      Going forward we use the term conditional mixture networks (CMN) to emphasize (1) the discrim-
167
      inative nature of proposed application of this approach, where the model is designed to predict an
168
      output y given an input x and (2) the fact that individual MoE layers can be stacked hierarchically
169   into a feedforward architecture. This makes CMNs particularly suitable for tasks such as supervised
170   classification and regression, where the goal is effectively that of function approximation; predict
171   some output variable y given input regressors x.

365   4.1   C OMPARISON ON SYNTHETIC DATASETS
366
367   We fit two-layer CMNs with different inference routines on two different synthetic datasets: the
368   Pinwheels and the Waveform Domains (Breiman & Stone, 1988) datasets. The pinwheels dataset
369   consists of multiple clusters arranged in a pinwheel pattern, posing a challenging task for mixture
370   models (Johnson et al., 2016) due to the curved and elongated spatial distributions of the data. See

373   where each class is described by 21 continuous attributes (Breiman & Stone, 1988).
374   We fit all inference methods while varying the training set size N in order to study the robustness
375   of each inference method’s performance in the low data regime. For each inference method and
376   value of N , we fit the model using the same batch of training data, but with 16 randomly-initialized
377   models (different initial posterior samples or weights), and evaluated performance on the same fixed
      test set across values of N .

405   predictive density is a common measure of predictive accuracy for methods that output probabilities
406   (Gelman et al., 2014), and expected calibration error measures how well a model’s predictions are
407   calibrated to the class probabilities observed in the data (Guo et al., 2017). In Figure 2 we show
408
      performance for the Pinwheels dataset and in Figure 3 for the Waveform dataset as a function of
      N . The CAVI-based approach achieves comparable log predictive density and calibration error to
409
      the other two Bayesian methods, which all outperform maximum likelihood estimation in LPD and
410
      ECE. This holds across training set sizes, indicating CAVI-CMN’s high sample efficiency.

      5    C ONCLUSION
499
      We demonstrate that the CAVI-based approach for conditional mixture networks (CMN) signifi-
500
      cantly outperforms the traditional maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) based approach, in terms
501
      of predictive performance and calibration. The improvement in probabilistic performance over the
502   MLE based approaches can be attributed to implicit regularisation via prior information, and proper
503   handling of posterior uncertainty over latent states and parameters, leading to a better representation
504   of the underlying data, reflected in improved calibration error and log predictive density, even in low
505   data regimes.
506
      One of the key advantages of the CAVI-based approach is its computational efficiency compared to
507
      the other Bayesian inference methods such as Black-Box variational inference (BBVI) and the No-
508   U-turn sampler (NUTS). While NUTS can sample from the full joint posterior distribution, which
509   maximizes performance in terms of inference quality, this comes at the expense of substantial com-
510   putational resources, especially for high dimensional and complex models (Hoffman et al., 2013).
511   Variational methods offer a scalable alternative to sampling-based inference in the form of methods
512   like black-box variational inference (BBVI). Although BBVI is highly efficient in comparison to
513   NUTS, it takes longer to converge and is slower than CAVI when applied to inference and learning
514   in conditional mixture networks. Hence, we expect CAVI to be a more practical choice for large-
515   scale application, especially when further combined with data mini-batching methods (Hoffman
516
      et al., 2013).
517   The UCI benchmark results show that CAVI-CMN algorithm achieves comparable performance to
518   BBVI and NUTS in terms of predictive accuracy, log-predictive density and expected calibration
519   error, while being significantly faster. This balance between predictive likelihood and calibration
520   (jointly viewed as indicators of sample efficiency) is particularly important in real-world applications
521   where robust prediction, reflective of underlying uncertainty, are crucial.
522   Furthermore, a straightforward mixture of linear components present in CMN, offers additional
523   interoperability benefits. By using conditionally conjugate priors and a corresponding mean-field
524   approximation over latent variables and model parameters, we facilitate easier interpretation of the
525   model parameters and their uncertainties. This is particularly valuable in domains where under-
526   standing the underlying data-generating process is as important as the predictive performance, such
527   as in healthcare, finance, and scientific research. Another important point is that the conjugate form
528
      of the CMN means that variational updates end up resembling sums of sufficient statistics computed
      from the data; this means the CAVI algorithm we described is readily amenable to online compu-
529
      tation and mini-batching, where sufficient statistics can computed and summed on-the-fly to update
530
      model parameters in a streaming fashion (Hoffman et al., 2013; Broderick et al., 2013). This ap-
531   proach will become necessary when scaling CAVI-CMN to deeper (more than two-layer) models
532   (Viroli & McLachlan, 2019) and larger datasets, where storing all the sufficient statistics of the data
533   in memory becomes prohibitive.
534
      Overall, these findings underscore the practical advantages of CAVI-CMN and highlight its promise
535
      as a new tool for fast probabilistic machine learning.

1040
       The pinwheels dataset is a synthetic dataset designed to test a model’s ability to handle nonlinear
1041   decision boundaries and data with non-Gaussian densities (Johnson et al., 2016). The structure of
1042   the pinwheels dataset is determined by 4 parameters: the number of clusters or distinct spirals; the
1043   angular deviation, which defines how far the spiralling clusters deviate from the origin; the tangential
1044   deviation, which defines the noise variance of 2-D points within each cluster; and the angular rate,
1045   which determines the curvature of each spiral. For evaluating the four methods (CAVI-CMN, MLE,
1046   BBVI, and NUTS) on the synthetic pinwheels dataset, we generated a dataset with 5 clusters, with an
1047   angular deviation of 0.7, tangential deviation of 0.3 and angular rate of 0.2. We selected these values
1048   by looking at the maximum achieved test accuracy across all the methods for different parameter
1049
       combinations and tried to upper-bound it 80%, which provides a low enough signal-to-noise ratio
       to be able to meaningfully show differences in probabilistic metrics like calibration and WAIC. For
1050
       pinwheels, we trained using train sizes 50 to 1600, doubling the number of training examples at each
1051
       successive training set size. We tested using 500 held-out test examples generated using the same
1052   parameters as used for the training set(s).
