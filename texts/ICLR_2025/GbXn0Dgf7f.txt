                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Deep active learning (DAL) methods have shown significant improvements in
010
               sample efficiency compared to simple random sampling. While these studies
011            are valuable, they nearly always assume that optimal DAL hyperparameter (HP)
012            settings are known in advance, or optimize the HPs through repeating DAL several
013            times with different HP settings. Here, we argue that in real-world settings, or in
014            the wild, there is significant uncertainty regarding good HPs, and their optimization
015            contradicts the premise of using DAL (i.e., we require labeling efficiency). In
016            this study, we evaluate the performance of eleven modern DAL methods on eight
017            benchmark problems as we vary a key HP shared by all methods: the pool ratio.
018            Despite adjusting only one HP, our results indicate that eight of the eleven DAL
019
               methods sometimes underperform relative to simple random sampling and some
               frequently perform worse. Only three methods always outperform random sampling
020
               (albeit narrowly), and we find that these methods all utilize diversity to select
021
               samples - a relatively simple criterion. Our findings reveal the limitations of
022            existing DAL methods when deployed in the wild, and present this as an important
023            new open problem in the field.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In this work, we focus on the application of active learning to deep neural networks (DNNs),
029   sometimes referred to as Deep Active Learning (DAL) Roy et al. (2018). Broadly speaking, the
030   premise of DAL is that some training instances will yield superior performance compared to others.
031   Therefore, we can improve the training sample efficiency of DNNs by selecting the best training
032
      instances. A large number of methods have been investigated in recent years for DAL Settles
      (2009); Ren et al. (2021); Holzmüller et al. (2023), often reporting significant improvements in
033
      sample efficiency compared to simpler strategies, such as random sampling Tsymbalov et al. (2018);
034
      Käding et al. (2018); Kee et al. (2018). While these studies provide valuable insights, they nearly
035   always assume good DAL hyperparameter (HP) settings are known in advance, or alternatively, they
036   optimize the HPs (e.g., by repeating DAL several times with different HP settings). To our knowledge
037   however, there is little evidence that one can assume good hyperparameters are known in advance
038   for novel problems (see Section 3, where we find HP settings in the literature vary widely across
039   problems). Moreover, running a DAL method multiple times in search of good HP settings may result
040   in significant label inefficiency, even when compared to random sampling. Therefore, in real-world
041   settings where DAL is applied to a novel problem, or in the wild as we term it here, the best DAL
042   HPs are not generally known in advance, and it is unclear whether DAL still offers advantages (e.g.,
043
      compared to random sampling) when accounting for HP uncertainty. If DAL models do not reliably
      outperform simple random sampling in the presence of HP uncertainty, it greatly undermines their
044
      value, and the likelihood that they will be adopted. Despite the significance of this problem, it has
045
      received little attention in the literature.

053   have several (often unique) HPs that exhibit uncertainty, and each can contribute to performance
      variability of DAL methods in the wild. However, examining variability with respect to all of these

      The majority of existing AL benchmarks are for classification tasks, rather than regression Jose
100
      et al. (2024), and many AL methods for classification cannot be applied to regression. Some
101
      existing studies include Zhan et al. (2021), which benchmarked AL using a Support Vector Machine

103   with 12 AL methods and 44 datasets. Meduri et al. (2020) benchmarked specific entity matching
104   application (classification) of AL with 3 AL methods on 12 datasets, with 3 different types of
105   classifiers (DNN, SVM, and Tree-based). Trittenbach et al. (2021) benchmarked an AL application
106   in outlier detection on 20 datasets and discussed the limitation of simple metrics extensively. Hu et al.
107   (2021) benchmarked 5 classification tasks (including both image and text) using DNN. Beck et al.
      (2021) benchmarked multiple facets of DAL on 5 image classification tasks. For the regression AL

119   2.2       ACTIVE LEARNING FOR REGRESSION PROBLEMS
120
121
      Regression problems have received (relatively) little attention compared to classification Ren et al.
      (2021); Guyon et al. (2011). For the limited AL literature dedicated to regression tasks, Expected
122
      Model Change (EMC) Settles (2008); Cai et al. (2013) was explored, where an ensemble of models
123
      was used to estimate the true label of a new query point using both linear regression and tree-based
124   regressors. Gaussian processes were also used with a natural variance estimate on unlabeled points
125   in a similar paradigm Käding et al. (2018). Smith et al. (2018) used Query By Committee (QBC),
126   which trains multiple networks and finds the most disagreeing unlabeled points of the committee
127   of models trained. Tsymbalov et al. (2018) used the Monte Carlo drop-out under a Bayesian
128   setting, also aiming for maximally disagreed points. Yu & Kim (2010) found x-space-only methods
129   outperforming y-space methods in robustness. Yoo & Kweon (2019) proposed an uncertainty-based
130   mechanism that learns to predict the loss using an auxiliary model that can be used on regression tasks.
131   Ranganathan et al. (2020) and Käding et al. (2016) used Expected Model Output Change (EMOC)
132
      with Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) on image regression tasks with different assumptions.
      We included all these methods that used deep learning in our benchmark.

135   2.3       DAL IN THE WILD
136
      To our knowledge, all empirical studies of pool-based DAL methods assume that an effective pool
137
      ratio hyperparameter, γ, is known apriori. While the majority of works assumed the original training
138   set as the fixed, unlabeled pool, Yoo & Kweon (2019) limited their method to a subset of 10k instances
139   instead of the full unlabeled set and Beluch et al. (2018) used subsampling to create the pool U (and
140   hence γ). In real-world settings - in the wild - we are not aware of any method to set γ a priori, and
141   there has been no study of DAL methods under this setting. Therefore, we believe ours is the first
142   such study.

      3.1       F ORMAL DESCRIPTION
152
153   Let Li = (X i , Y i ) be the dataset used to train a regression model at the ith iteration of active learning.
154   We assume access to some oracle, denoted f : X → Y, that can accurately produce the target values,
155   y ∈ Y associated with input values x ∈ X . Since we focus on DAL, we assume a DNN as our
156   regression model, denoted fˆ. We assume that some relatively small number of N0 labeled training
157   instances are available to initially train fˆ, denoted L0 . In each iteration of DAL, we must choose k
158   query instances to be labeled by the oracle, yielding a set of labeled instances, denoted Q, that is
159   added to the training dataset. Our goal is then to choose Q that maximizes the performance of the
160   DNN-based regression models over unseen test data at each iteration of active learning.

      3.2   P OOL - BASED D EEP ACTIVE L EARNING
163
164   General pool-based DAL methods assume that we have some pool U of NU unlabeled instances from
165   which we can choose the k instances to label. The set U is sampled from a larger and potentially-
166   infinite set, denoted D, and NU is a HP chosen by the DAL user. We note that in some DAL
167   applications, such as computer vision, it is conventional to utilize all available unlabeled data for U ,
168   and the pool size is not often explicitly varied or discussed. However, this convention is equivalent
169   to setting U = D, and thereby implicitly setting the NU HP. Most pool-based methods rely upon
170
      some acquisition function q : X → R to assign some scalar value to each x ∈ U indicating its
171   "informativeness", or utility for training fˆ. In each iteration of active learning, q is used to evaluate
172
      all instances in U , and the top k are chosen to be labeled and included in L.

187   3.3   T HE POOL RATIO HYPERPARAMETER , γ
188
189
      We define the pool ratio as γ = NU /k. By definition, NU and k are hyperparameters of pool-based
      problems, and therefore γ also is. While one could, in principle, vary NU and k independently, this is
190
      not often done in practice. Typically k is set as small as possible, limited by computational resources.
191
      This leaves NU as the major free hyperparameter; however, prior research has found that its impact
192   depends strongly on its size relative to k Kee et al. (2018); Tsymbalov et al. (2018); Käding et al.
193   (2018), encoded in γ. Given a fixed value of k, increasing NU can lead to the discovery of points
194   with higher values of q(x) due to denser sampling of the input space. However, a larger NU also
195   increases the similarity of the points, which provides redundant information to the model - a problem
196   referred to as mode collapse Burbidge et al. (2007); Ren et al. (2021); Kee et al. (2018). In the limit
197   as NU → ∞, all of the k selected query points will be located near the same x ∈ X that has the
198   highest value of q(x). This tradeoff is illustrated in Fig. 2 for a simple problem, and has also been
199   noted in Cacciarelli & Kulahci (2024).
200   In most real-world settings, there is a substantial quantity of unlabeled data (often infinite), and the
201   user has the freedom (or burden) of choosing a suitable γ setting for their problem by varying the
202   size of U . Crucially, and as we show in our experiments, choosing a sub-optimal γ value can result in
203   poorer performance than naive random sampling. This is not necessarily a problem if either (i) one γ
204   setting works across most problems or, alternatively, (ii) γ can be optimized on new problems without
205   using labels. To the best of our knowledge, there is no method for optimizing γ on a new problem
206
      without running multiple trials of AL to find the best one (i.e., collecting labels), defeating the
      purpose of AL in real-world settings. Furthermore, the value of γ varies widely across the literature,
207
      suggesting that suitable settings for γ indeed vary across problems (see supplement for a list).

      5    B ENCHMARK E XPERIMENT D ESIGN
312
313
314   In our experiments, we compare eleven state-of-the-art DAL methods on eight scientific computing
315
      problems. We evaluate the performance of our DAL methods as a function of γ on each of our
      benchmark problems, with γ ∈ [2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64] (i.e., at each step we sample our U with k ∗ γ
316
      points). Following convention Kee et al. (2018); Tsymbalov et al. (2018), we assume a small training
317
      dataset is available at the outset of active learning, T 0 , which has N0 = 80 randomly sampled training
318   instances. We then run each DAL model to T 50 AL steps, each step identifying k = 40 points to be
319   labeled from a fresh, randomly generated pool of size k ∗ γ. For each benchmark problem, we assume
320   an appropriate neural network architecture is known apriori. Each experiment (i.e., the combination
321   of dataset, DAL model, and γ value) is run 5 times to account for randomness. The MSE is calculated
322   over a set of 4000 test points that are uniformly sampled within the x-space boundary. To reduce
323   unnecessary noise related to our core hypothesis, we use the same (randomly sampled) unlabeled
      pools across different DAL methods.

372   of DAL with different γ settings), and DAL methods may perform worse, and unreliably, when
373   accounting for the uncertainty of γ.

      6.2   D O ANY DAL METHODS OUTPERFORM RANDOM SAMPLING IN THE WILD ?
419
420   The results indicate that several DAL methods tend to obtain much lower nAU CM SE (i.e., they are

      often than not, compared to random sampling - an important property. However, as discussed in
      Section 6.1, all DAL methods exhibit significant performance variance with respect to γ, and some of
424
      the aforementioned methods still sometimes perform worse than random sampling. For example, this
425
      is the case of QBC, GSy, and QBCDivDen on the SINE problem. In settings where DAL is useful,
426   the cost of collecting labels tends to be high, and therefore the risk of poor DAL performance (e.g.,
427   relative to simple random sampling) may strongly deter its use. Therefore, another important criteria
428   is performance robustness: do any DAL methods consistently perform better than random sampling,
429   in the wild? Our results indicate that GSx, GSxy, and QBCDiv always perform at least as well as
430   random sampling, and often substantially better, regardless of the problem or γ setting. Note that all
431   three robust DALs (GSx, GSxy, QBCDiv) employ x-space diversity in their loss function, which we
      discuss further in Section 6.3.

      6.3   S AMPLE DIVERSITY IS IMPORTANT FOR DAL IN THE WILD
453
454   Our results indicate that the best-performing DAL methods are GSx, GSxy, and QBCDiv. We
455   say these methods are "best" because they are both robust (see Section 6.2), and they also usually
456   yield lower MSEs, than other DAL methods. These methods share the common property that they
457   encourage training data diversity, as measured by x-space distance between points. Interestingly, GSx
458   only relies on x-space diversity. These results suggest that x-space diversity is a highly effective DAL
459   acquisition criterion. Furthermore, and in contrast to other criteria, seeking points that maximize
460   x-space diversity does not (by definition) increase the risk of mode collapse. Consequently, increasing
461
      γ results in greater diversity but without any increased risk of mode collapse (more details in Fig. 5).
      This may be a major reason why GSx, GSxy, and QBCDiv are less sensitive to γ, and provide much
462
      more robust performance in the wild than other DAL methods. While sampling methods that use
463
      diversity have been found to be promising Jose et al. (2024), our work provides evidence, for the
464   first time, that sampling based upon diversity may be robust to hyperparameter uncertainty (we only
465   examine uncertainty of γ) while other popular sampling criteria (e.g., estimated model error) seem to
466   be much less reliable in the wild.

490   7     C ONCLUSIONS
491
492   For the first time, we evaluated eleven state-of-the-art DAL methods on eight benchmark datasets for
493   regression in the wild, where we assume that the best pool ratio hyperparameter, γ, is uncertain. We
494
      summarize our findings as follows:
495
             • DAL methods for regression often perform worse than simple random sampling, when evalu-
496            ated in the wild. Using γ as an example, we systematically demonstrate the rarely-discussed
497            problem that most DAL models are often outperformed by simple random sampling when
498            we account for HP uncertainty.
499
             • Some DAL methods were relatively robust, and outperformed random sampling robustly in
500
               the wild (e.g., GSx, GSxy, QBCDiv).
501
502
             • Insofar as robustness to pool ratio is concerned, our results suggest that DAL approaches
               utilizing sample diversity tend to be much more robust in the wild than other popular
503
               selection criteria.

      7.1   L IMITATIONS
507
      One limitation of this work is that we focused on scientific computing benchmark problems, and
508   problems with relatively low dimensionality. Including higher dimensional problems is an especially
509   important opportunity for future work due to the importance of vision problems in the DAL commu-
510   nity, and also because sensitivity to pool ratio has been noted in that setting as well Yoo & Kweon
511   (2019); Sener & Savarese (2017), but not studied systematically. Another important limitation is
512   that we constrained our evaluation of DAL methods to uncertainty in their pool ratio. Future studies
513   would benefit from evaluating each DAL approach with respect to uncertainty in all of its relevant
514   DAL HPs (i.e., those that require labeled data to be optimized), providing a more comprehensive
515   assessment of modern DAL methods in the wild.
