010                                                    A BSTRACT
011
012             Robust collaborative learning on a network of edge devices, for vertically split
013             datasets, is challenging because edge devices may fail due to environment condi-
014             tions or events such as extreme weather. The current Vertical Federated learning
015             (VFL) approaches assume a centralized learning setup or assume the active party
016             or server cannot fail. To address these limitations, we first formalize the problem of
017             VFL under dynamic network conditions such as faults (named DN-VFL). Then, we
018             develop a novel DN-VFL method called Multiple Aggregation with Gossip Rounds
019             and Simulated Faults (MAGS) that synthesizes faults via dropout, replication, and
020
                gossiping to improve robustness significantly over baselines. We also theoreti-
                cally analyze our proposed approaches to explain why they enhance robustness.
021
                Extensive empirical results validate that MAGS is robust across a range of fault
022
                rates—including extreme fault rates—compared to prior VFL approaches.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Collaborative cross-device learning and inference on IoT or edge devices present unique challenges
028   not encountered in cross-silo setups (Yuan et al., 2023), such as limited power resources, device
029   unreliability, and the absence of a centralized server. Particularly, when the application requires
030   devices to collaborate in predicting a global feature of the environment, these challenges become
031   critical. For example, deploying a network of sensors for intelligent monitoring in harsh environments
032   (e.g., deep sea sensors, underground mines, or remote rural areas) involves devices that may fail due to
033   power constraints or extreme weather conditions. Additionally, internet connectivity may be limited or
034
      non-existent, and no single device can be considered a perfectly reliable central server. Yet, in safety-
      critical applications such as search and rescue in underground mines, this intelligent device network
035
      needs to continue operating even under near catastrophic faults (e.g., 50% of devices fail). Therefore,
036
      in this work, we seek to answer the following: Can we develop a cross-device collaborative method
037   that maintains strong performance at test time even under near-catastrophic faults in the
038   decentralized setting?
039
040
      Vertical Federated Learning (VFL) (Liu et al., 2024) emerges as a natural solution for tasks requiring
      device collaboration at inference time. In VFL, clients share the same set of samples but have
041
      different features. In our environmental monitoring example, the samples correspond to unique
042
      timestamps, and the features correspond to sensor data from each device—each providing a partial
043   view of the global environment. Previous research in VFL has explored aspects of fault tolerance and
044   decentralized learning, primarily focusing on the training phase. For instance, studies have addressed
045   asynchronous communication to handle device failures during training (Chen et al., 2020; Zhang
046   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; 2023). An exception is the work by Sun et al. (2023), who proposed
047   Party-wise Dropout to mitigate inference-time faults caused by passive parties (clients) dropping off
048   unexpectedly, but they assumed that the active party (server) remains fault-free. Other works have
049   focused on communication efficiency in decentralized VFL (Valdeira et al., 2023). However, to the
050   best of our knowledge, no prior work simultaneously addresses decentralized learning and arbitrary
051
      faults—including the active party or server—during inference. This gap, as summarized in Table 1,
      motivates our research.

064   To address these challenges holistically, we first formalize this problem setup and then propose a
065   solution, Multiple Aggregation with Gossip Rounds and Simulated Faults (MAGS). We also define
066   our context, including, data assumption, network model, and a measure of performance in this context
067   called Dynamic Risk. A comparison of context presented in this work with vanilla VFL is captured
068   in Figure 1. MAGS significantly improves robustness by integrating three interconnected methods
069   that build upon and complement each other. First, during training, we simulate high fault rates via
070   dropout so that the model can be robust to more missing values at test time. Second, we replicate the
071   data aggregator to prevent catastrophic failure in case the active party (or server) goes down during
072
      test time. Third, we introduce gossip rounds to implicitly ensemble the predictions from multiple data
      aggregators, reducing the prediction variance across devices. Finally, we evaluate the effectiveness of
073
      MAGS by conducting experiments using five datasets (StarCraftMNIST (Kulinski et al., 2023) in
074
      the main paper and MNIST, CIFAR10, CIFAR100, Tiny ImageNet in the appendix) and different
075   network configurations. The results establish that MAGS is significantly more robust than prior
076   methods, often improving performance more than 20% over prior methods at high fault rates. We
077   summarize our contributions as follows:
078
079   • We formalize the problem of decentralized VFL under dynamic network conditions, called Dynamic
080     Network VFL (DN-VFL), and define Dynamic Risk, which measures performance under (extreme)
081     dynamic network conditions.
082   • We develop and analyze MAGS, that combines fault simulation, replication, and gossiping to
083     enable strong fault tolerance for DN-VFL.
084
      • We demonstrate that MAGS is significantly more robust to dynamic network faults than prior
085
        methods across multiple datasets, often improving performance more than 20% compared to prior
086
        methods.

      1.1   R ELATED WORKS
109
110   Network Dynamic Resilient FL In VFL, network dynamics has mostly been studied during the
111   training phase for asynchronous client participation Chen et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2021); Li et al.
112   (2020; 2023). Research on VFL network dynamics during inference is limited. Ceballos et al. (2020)
113   noted performance drops due to random client failures during testing, and Sun et al. (2023) studied
114   passive parties dropping off randomly during inference and proposed Party-wise Dropout (PD). Thus,
115   prior dynamic network resilient VFL methods have majorly focused on train-time faults and assumed
116
      a special node (server or active party) that is immune to failure. As VFL differs significantly from
      the horizontal FL (HFL) setting (Yang et al., 2019), where clients share the same set of features
117
      but have different samples, HFL methods for handling faults (e.g., adaptive aggregation of different
118
      models Ruan et al. (2021)) are inapplicable in our scenario. Additionally, unlike HFL, where faults
119   only affect training, faults in VFL can disrupt both training and inference due to the need for client
120   communication during inference.
121
122
      Decentralized FL Conventional FL uses a central server for aggregation. However, this approach
      results in server being the single point of failure. To address such limitations, Decentralized FL has
123
      been considered (Yuan et al., 2023). Unlike, the extensively studied HFL decentralized methods
124
      (Tang et al. (2022); Lalitha et al. (2019); Feng et al. (2021); Gabrielli et al. (2023)), VFL decentralized
125   methods are limited. For the special case of simple linear models, He et al. (2018) proposed
126   decentralized algorithm COLA. For more general split-NN models, Valdeira et al. (2023) proposed
127   decentralized, STCD, and a semi-decentralized, MTCD, methods. Neither COLA nor STCD/MTCD
128   analyze network dynamics such as faults during inference time.

      2     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
131
132
      We define a novel formulation, DN-VFL, which specifies both an operating context and the desired
133   properties of a learning system. The context is comprised of two entities: the data context and the
134   network context, which we define in the first subsection. The desired property of the system is
135   robustness under dynamic conditions, which we formally define via Dynamic Risk and corresponding
136   metrics in the next two subsections.
137
      Notation Let (X ∈ X , Y ∈ Y) denote the random variables corresponding to the input features
138
      and target, respectively, whose joint distribution is p(X, Y ). With a slight abuse of notation, we
139   will use Y to denote one-hot encoded class labels and probability vectors (for predictions). Let
140   D = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 denote a training dataset of of n samples i.i.d. samples from p(X, Y ) with d input
141   features xi and corresponding target yi . Let xS denote the subvector associated with the indices
142   in S ⊆ {1, 2, · · · , d}, e.g., if S = {1, 5, 8}, then xS = [x1 , x5 , x8 ]T . For C clients, the dataset at
143   each client c ∈ {1, 2, · · · , C} will be denoted by Dc . Let G = (C, E) denote a network (or graph)
144   of clients, where C ⊆ {1, 2, · · · , C} ∪ {0} denotes the clients plus an entity (possibly an external
145   entity or one of the clients itself) that represents the device that collects the final prediction and has
146   the labels during training (further details in Section 2.2) and E denotes the communication edges.
147
148   2.1   DYNAMIC N ETWORK VFL C ONTEXT
149
      Data and Network Context A partial features data context means that each client has access to
150
      a subset of the features, i.e., Dc = {xi,Sc }ni=1 , where Sc ⊂ {1, 2, · · · , d} for each client c. This
151
      is the same data context as SplitVFL (Liu et al., 2022), a variant of VFL, which incorporates the
152   idea of split learning (Vepakomma et al., 2018), and jointly trains models at both server and clients.
153   Furthermore, in this study we assume that the features with each client is a partition of the feature set
154   of a sample and each client has disjoint set of features for each sample. However, we do allow for
155   scenarios where the clients can have features among one another that are correlated. For instance,
156   there can be two sensors that can have correlated features due to their physical proximity. Unlike
157   vanilla VFL, in DN-VFL we allow the clients to act as data aggregators and communicate with one
158   another. This leads to the realization of Decentralized VFL. Through the rest of the paper, the terms
159   clients and devices are used interchangeably.
160   Definition 1 (Dynamic Network Context). A dynamic network means that the communication graph
161   can change across time indexed by t, i.e., G(t) = (C(t), E(t)), where the changes over time can be
      either deterministic or stochastic functions of t.

162
      This dynamic network context includes many possible scenarios including various network topologies,
163   clients joining or leaving the network, and communication being limited or intermittent due to power
164   constraints or physical connection interference. We provide two concrete dynamic models where
165   there are device failures or communication failures. For simplicity, we will assume there is a base
166   network topology Gbase = (Cbase , Ebase ) (e.g., complete graph, grid graph or preferential-attachment
167   graph), and we will assume a discrete-time version of a dynamic network where t ∈ {0, 1, 2, · · · },
168   which designates a synchronous communication round. Given this, we can formally define two
169   simple dynamic network models that encode random device and communication faults.
170   Definition 2 (Device Fault Dynamic Network). Given a fault rate r and a baseline topology Gbase , a
171   device fault dynamic network Gr (t) means that a client is in the network at time t with probability
172   1 − r, i.e., Pr(c ∈ Cr (t)) = 1 − r, ∀c ∈ Cbase and Er (t) = {(c, c′ ) ∈ Ebase : c, c′ ∈ Cr (t)}.
173   Definition 3 (Communication Fault Dynamic Network). Given a fault rate r and a baseline topology
174   Gbase , a communication fault dynamic network GrCF (t) means that a communication edge (excluding
175   self-communication) is in the network at time t with probability 1 − r, i.e., CrCF (t) = Cbase and
176   Pr((c, c′ ) ∈ ErCF (t)) = 1 − r, ∀(c, c′ ) ∈ Ebase where c ̸= c′ .
177
178   As this work focuses on the foundations of Dynamic Network VFL, we only experimented with these
179
      two dynamic network models. However, more complex dynamic models could be explored in the
      future. For example, the networks could change smoothly over time (e.g., one connection being
180
      removed or added at every time point). Or, a network could model a catastrophic event at a particular
181
      time t′ followed by a slow recovery of the network as devices are reconnected or restarted. We leave
182   the investigation of more complex dynamic models to future work.

186   Given these context definitions, we now define the goal of DN-VFL in terms of the Dynamic Risk
187   which we define next. For now, we will assume the existence of a distributed inference algorithm
188   Ψ(x; θ, G(t)) : X → Y C , where each client makes a prediction across the data-split network under

190   distributed inference algorithm that generalizes VFL. Furthermore, we will use a (possibly stochastic)
191   post-processing function h : Y C → Y to model the final communication round between the clients
192   and another entity (which may be external or may be one of the clients), which owns the labels for
193

194
      the entity could represent a drone passing over a remote sensing network to gather predictions or a
195
      physical connection to the devices at test time (e.g., when the sensors are ultra-low power and cannot
196   directly connect to the internet). Or, this entity could represent a power intensive connection via
197   satellite to some base station that would only activate when requested to save power.

248   Given the novel DN-VFL context, we now propose our message passing distributed inference
249   algorithm MAGS for DN-VFL and present the relevant theoretical insights. First, we extend and
250   discuss dropout methods for simulating faults during training to enhance the robustness of the

      3.1    D ECENTRALIZED T RAINING OF MAGS WITH R EAL AND S IMULATED FAULTS VIA
271          D ROPOUT
272
273   To train MAGS, we use a standard VFL backpropagation algorithm without gossip rounds2 ., which
274   only requires two communication rounds: one for the forward pass and one for the backward pass. In
275   both passes, faults can be treated similarly to dropout, where missing values are imputed with zeros
276   (see appendix for more details). Our training algorithm assumes all devices have access to the labels,
277   which is similar to an assumption made in Castiglia et al. (2022) and is valid for our setup, involving
278
      a trusted but unreliable device network, where robustness is our primary goal. While we aim for
      robustness against severe, potentially catastrophic faults, we expect a relatively stable and reliable
279
      device network during normal training, with a small fault rate (e.g., 1%-5%). However, training
280
      solely with a low fault rate may leave the model vulnerable to higher fault rates during inference,
281   which can occur due to external factors like extreme weather. This presents a challenge, as large-scale
282   inference-time faults lead to missing values, causing a distribution shift between the training and test
283   data. Such shifts can severely degrade model performance, as noted by Koh et al. (2021).
284
      A natural way to address this issue is to simulate inference-time faults during training using dropout.
285
      Sun et al. (2023) introduced Party-wise Dropout (PD) for server-based VFL, simulating random client
286
      failures during communication with the server. However, since DN-VFL operates in a decentralized
287   environment where clients communicate with each other, PD is insufficient. To model this decen-
288   tralized communication, we propose Communication-wise Dropout (CD), which applies dropout
289   to client-to-client communication instead of just client-to-server communication. PD simulates
290   device failures, while CD simulates communication failures. For further clarity, we provide detailed
291   comparisons between PD and CD configurations in the decentralized setting in the appendix.
292
      To enhance model robustness, we introduce additional dropout beyond what occurs naturally from
293   real network faults, simulating higher fault rates during training. This approach is based on the
294   theoretical understanding of dropout’s regularizing effect, as discussed in the literature. Baldi &
295   Sadowski (2013) demonstrated that dropout can act as a regularizer during training. Mianjy &
296   Arora (2020) further showed that a model trained with dropout and tested without it can achieve
297   near-optimal test performance in O(1/ϵ) iterations. Their work also provides evidence that, in
298   over-parameterized models, dropout-regularized networks can generalize well even when dropout
299   is applied during testing—exactly what is needed in DN-VFL, where faults may occur during both
300   training and inference.

      While multiple data aggregators help avoid system-level failures, the performance of each data
      aggregator may be poor due to faults, which could result in overall high dynamic risk even if
336
      catastrophic failures are alleviated. Because gossip is not used during training, each of data aggregator
337
      model is different because each will have access to different client representations due to the graph
338   topology and faults at test time (see “Active Worst” metric). Additionally, this variability between data
339   aggregators translates to inconsistent performance when viewed by an external entity as it depends on
340   which device is selected and the best device may differ for each inference query (see the difference
341   between “Active Worst” and “Active Best” metrics). Thus, we propose to use gossip layers to combine
342   predictions among data aggregators and in Algorithm 1, lines 5 and 6 denotes how it is accomplished
343   algorithmically. From one perspective, gossip implicitly produces an ensemble prediction at each
344   aggregator, which we prove always has better or equal dynamic risk. From another perspective,
345   gossiping will cause the aggregator predictions to converge to the same prediction—which means
346
      that the system performance will be the same regardless of which device is selected.
347   To analyze the ML performance of gossip, we leverage the formalization of ensemble diversity related
348   to risk as developed in Wood et al. (2023). Wood et al. (2023) showed that ensemble diversity can
349   be conceptually viewed as another dimension to the bias-variance risk decomposition. In particular,
350   Wood et al. (2023) showed that the ensemble risk can be decomposed into individual risks minus
351   a diversity term (which will reduce the risk if positive). We leverage this theory to prove that the
352
      dynamic risk of our ensemble is equal to the non-ensemble dynamic risk minus a diversity term—
      which is always non-negative and positive if there is any diversity in prediction. This proposition
353
      shows that gossiping at inference time, which implicitly creates ensembles, will almost always
354
      improve the dynamic risk compared to not using gossip. (Proof is in appendix.)

501   5    C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
502
503   In this paper, we carefully defined DN-VFL, proposed and theoretically analyzed MAGS as a method
504
      for DN-VFL, developed a testbed, and evaluated and compared performance across various fault
      models and datasets. Simulated faults via dropout increase the robustness of MAGS to distribution
505
      shifts. Multiple VFL allows MAGS to avoid catastrophic faults since any device (including active
506
      parties) can fail. Gossiping outputs at inference time implicitly ensembles the predictions for
507   neighboring devices that theoretically improves the robustness and reduces the variance. Our work
508   lays the foundation for DN-VFL, opening up many directions for future research, such as handling
509   heterogeneous devices or models, exploring new architectures, and considering different fault models.
510
      Furthermore, we emphasize that our focus was on the machine learning aspects of decentralized
511
      VFL robustness. Our level of analysis (e.g., communication cost and simple simulation of devices)
512
      is comparable to that of many other studies in HFL (McMahan et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2022b;
513   Crawshaw et al., 2024) and VFL (Li et al., 2023; Castiglia et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2021; Jiang et al.,
514   2022). While we discuss some system-level aspects (communication bottlenecks and latency) in the
515   appendix, real-world deployment would require more detailed system-level research and represents
516   an important future direction. Additionally, we assume a synchronous communication model and
517   asynchronous models could be a natural extension for future systems in the DN-VFL context. Finally,
518   in the appendix, we compare MAGS to an alternative solution using the fault-tolerant consensus
519   algorithms like Paxos or Raft (Lamport, 2001; Ongaro & Ousterhout, 2014), showing that these are
520   insufficient for the DN-VFL context.
521   Another interesting topic to explore further is the impact of faults during training. As discussed in
522   the appendix, our method could handle faults in the backward propagation phase. However, a more
523   thorough analysis of backward faults—particularly when a high fault rate is expected during training—
524   remains an open issue beyond the scope of this work, where we focus on near-catastrophic faults at
525   inference. If very high fault rates were introduced during training, synchronized backpropagation
526   may break down. We hypothesize that fully synchronized backpropagation training may not be
527
      ideal in such scenarios. Therefore, localized learning approaches could be beneficial. Methods
      such as Forward-Forward algorithms (Hinton, 2022), dual propagation (Høier et al., 2023), and
528
      other localized learning techniques (Detorakis et al., 2018; Movellan, 1991; Czarnecki et al., 2017;
529
      Belilovsky et al., 2019; 2020) could be explored to enhance robustness during training. We leave a
530   more detailed investigation of training with extreme faults to future work. Finally, while we focused
531   on the robustness aspects, in practice, more advanced architectures such as CNN-based or transformer-
532   based could be leveraged for better absolute performance. We believe our MAGS techniques could
533   be easily adapted to different architectures and thus provide an orthogonal contribution compared to
534   architecture design.

951   C     H ANDLING BACKWARD PASS FOR T RAINING WITH FAULTS
952
953   In the forward pass, faulted messages can be merely treated as dropout. We discuss handling of faults
954
      in the backward pass in the next paragraphs.
955   In the backward pass, the gradients of the classifier head are computed locally on each client,
956   unaffected by faults. However, calculating the gradients for the feature encoders requires an additional
957   communication round to send gradients back to each client, as in standard VFL. Only the gradients
958   for non-faulted or non-dropped messages need to be sent, as dropped messages are treated as zeros in
959   the forward pass. Faulty gradient messages can similarly be imputed with zeros as in forward pass
960
      dropout since a dropped forward message is functionally equivalent to a dropped gradient message
      from the perspective of the feature encoder.
961
962   In practice, we assume that the gradient communication round will typically succeed, as we expect
963   the device network to be stable during normal operation. This assumption is reasonable for training,
964   given that the fault pattern is unlikely to change significantly over the short time required to process a
965   batch of data, provided there are no catastrophic events like extreme weather disruptions. Note that
966
      the fault pattern may vary between batches, but it only needs to remain stable for two communication
      rounds per batch.

1009
1010   Designing for Extreme Fault Rates We argue that designing for extreme fault rates of 50% is a
1011   valid approach even if 50% fault rates are not common in current edge networks. We provide at least
1012   two main arguments below.
1013
       First, our problem setup is inspired by other robust methods that continue to operate in very poor
1014   conditions even if these extreme conditions differs from the average case. For example, the fault-
1015   tolerant distribute consensus algorithm Paxos (Lamport, 2001) is safe even with arbitrarily bad failures.
1016   However, in practice, Paxos may be used even when the failure rate is very low. Similarly, internet
1017   protocols were designed to operate even under arbitrary network faults (known as “survivability” in
1018   early packet switching papers (Baran, 1964)) even though the average case has a small fault rate.
1019   Thus, even if 50% fault rate is uncommon, it is important to design systems that will operate even
1020   under the uncommon events.
1021   Second, we expect that 50% fault rate is reasonable in three scenarios: harsh environmental conditions,
1022   cheap and unreliable sensors, and rare disaster-like events. In harsh environments like deep-sea or
1023   remote wilderness regions, a fault rate of 50% might be reasonable, especially over a long period of
1024   time. Similarly, if one uses a large number of very cheap but unreliable sensors, the use case of 50%
1025   fault rate could be quite reasonable. Finally, we expect that a 50% fault rate could be reasonable in
       disaster scenarios such as floods or hurricanes where conditions are significantly worse than normal.

       Simplified Comparison of VFL and MAGS in Terms of Latency, Throughput, and Power:
1032
       We first compare our MAGS method to standard VFL using simplified models of computation and
1033   communication and discuss more practical considerations in the next section. Specifically, let C1 , C2
1034   and M denote the maximum time for each device to compute its features, compute a prediction given
1035   messages, and exchange messages, respectively. For VFL, the latency would be C1 + M + C2 . For
1036   MAGS, the latency would be C1 + M + C2 + G · M , where G is the number of gossip rounds. If
1037   there is no gossip, then MAGS is equivalent to VFL latency. However, as shown in our results, gossip
1038   increases the robustness by about 3%-4% compared to no gossiping. Thus, gossip produces a small
1039   tradeoff between robustness and latency.
1040   When analyzing throughput, we assume that each device can handle a batch size of B samples for
1041   both encoding and prediction. The only difference for throughput between VFL vs MAGS is that
1042   MAGS performs extra gossip rounds. We expect these gossip rounds to have negligible impact on
1043   throughput as they are only exchanging logit values.
1044
       For power, we make the simplifying assumption that power is directly proportional to the number of
1045   messages sent by each device. Thus, to analyze average power usage, we point to Table 2 that shows
1046   the communication costs of various methods. Here again, we see a tradeoff between using full MVFL
1047   or using k-MVFL with and without gossip. The best in terms of robustness is full MVFL with gossip
1048   rounds but the power consumption would be high. In practice, our method provides different ways to
1049   adjust the communication depending on the desired robustness versus power tradeoff. On the other
1050   hand, standard VFL has no way of adjusting the communication cost other than simply randomly
1051   dropping communications, which may significantly hinder the performance.
1052   Systems-Level Aspects for Real-World Deployment:                 While our paper focuses on the ML
1053   challenge of robustness and thus we use simplified assumptions common in the the VFL literature
1054   (Li et al., 2023; Castiglia et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2022). Nonetheless, practical
1055   real-world deployment would likely require analyzing and optimizing for systems-level concerns.
1056   While these systems-level aspects for deployment on real networked devices are out of scope for this
1057   current ML-focused paper, we discuss some of these aspects for completeness.
1058
1059
              • Device heterogeneity: Although we assume that all devices have similar capabilities and
                can thus serve as aggregators, in practice, the devices may have high heterogeneity where
1060
                some have much higher capacity for computation and communication. A natural extension
1061
                of our work is to optimize the tradeoff between performance and latency or power as done
1062            in Shao et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2022a). Additionally, with heterogeneous devices, more
1063            careful design of different model sizes depending on each device’s memory capacity could
1064            be explored as inLu et al. (2021); Ahmed et al. (2021).
1065
              • Bandwidth: The experiments in the main paper were simulated with unlimited bandwidth,
1066
                i.e., no communication bottlenecks. However, it is a salient practical consideration. It may
1067
                not be possible to perform an all-to-all broadcast over a real wireless network even if all
1068            devices have links between them. Instead, devices may need to randomly choose a subset
1069            of neighbors to send their messages to. Thankfully, however, MAGS is inherently robust
1070            to message losses so devices could choose how often to communicate based on bandwidth
1071            or power considerations. Additionally, for real-world deployment, it would be crucial to
1072            use careful compression and coding techniques and bandwidth-aware communication to
1073            develop variants of MAGS that can accommodate communication bottlenecks.
1074          • Latency: While we assume a simplified view of latency in our paper, in practice, some
1075            devices will complete their computations faster and there will be some straggler devices.
1076            In practice, setting the appropriate cutoff waiting time for messages (i.e., treating delayed
1077            messages as faulted) would allow the system to optimize the trade-off between performance
1078            and other metrics like latency and power. Again, MAGS can naturally handle this because a
1079            delayed message can simply be treated as a faulted message. Additionally, in the future, a
                natural extension of our work is to develop asynchronous or semi-synchronous variants of

                MAGS like Chen et al. (2020); Li et al. (2020), which allow asynchronous updates in the
1081            vanilla VFL setting.

1083   Alternative Approach using Fault-Tolerant Consensus Algorithms Instead of direct replication
1084   via MVFL, one alternative fault-tolerant approach would be to first run a fault-tolerant consensus
1085   algorithm such as Paxos (Lamport, 2001) or Raft (Ongaro & Ousterhout, 2014) and then run standard
1086   VFL inference with the elected leader. This could reduce the communication load during distributed
1087   inference but would reduce the robustness or increase latency compared to MAGS. For example,
1088   Paxos may fail or wait indefinitely for extreme fault rates near 50%.
1089   Additionally, Paxos would increase the latency as consensus would need to be arrived before contin-
1090   uing. On the other hand, MAGS would provide an answer (perhaps degraded but that is expected)
1091   with the same latency no matter the percentage of faults even for more extreme faults beyond 50%.
1092   Secondly, we point out that accuracy performance with MAGS actually benefits between 2-3%
1093   because of the ensembling of multiple devices’ predictions. For example, on the grid graph in Figure
1094   2c in the main paper, there is a 2-3% gap between PD-MVFL (which would roughly correspond to
1095
       PD+Paxos) compared to PD-MVFL-G4 (which has gossip and produces an ensembling effect).
1096   While distributed algorithms such as Paxos and Raft (Lamport, 2001; Ongaro & Ousterhout, 2014) are
1097   useful to generate consensus in a system that encounters fault, they do not enable representations that
1098   are robust to faults, which is essential for achieving good performance. Thus, they cannot be naively
1099   applied for addressing DN-VFL and more carefully constructed method like MAGS is required.
1100   Distributed Inference Algorithm’s Resemblance to GNNs: The form of our distributed inference
1101   algorithm in the main paper has a superficial resemblance of the computation of graph neural networks
1102   (GNN) (Scarselli et al., 2008) but with important semantic and syntactic differences. Semantically,
1103   unlike GNN applications whose goal is to predict global, node, or edge properties based on the graph
1104   edges, our goal is to do prediction well given any arbitrary edge structure. Indeed, the edges in
1105   our dynamic network are assumed to be independent of the input and task—rather they are simply
1106   constraints based on the network context of the system. Syntactically, our inference algorithm differs
1107
       from mainstream convolutional GNNs because convolutional GNNs share the parameters across
                       (t)
1108   clients (i.e., θc = θ(t) ) whereas in our algorithm the parameters at each client are not shared
                              (t)   (t)
1109   across clients (i.e., θc ̸= θc′ ). Additionally, most GNNs assume the aggregation function g is
1110   permutation equivariant such as a sum, product or maximum function. However, we assume g could
1111   be any aggregation function. Finally, this definition incorporates the last processing function h that
1112
       represents the final communication round to an external entity (Main Paper Section 3).

1141   In the main paper as well as in the Appendix the terms client and devices are used interchangeably.
1142   In Section 4 of the main paper, four different graphs were introduced: Complete,Ring, Random
1143   Geometric and Grid. To elaborate how these graphs are constructed for a set of 16 clients, we take an
1144   example of an image from each of the three datasets and split it up into 16 sections, as illustrated in
1145   Figure 7.
1146
       For a Complete graph, all the devices are connected to the server. For instance, if D1 is selected as the
1147   server, then all the other devices Di for i = 2, 3, . . . , 16 are connected to D1 . To construct Grid graph,
1148   we use compute a Distance parameter. For Grid graph, distance returns true if a selected device lies
1149   horizontally or vertically adjacent to a server and only under this circumstance it is connected to the
1150   server otherwise it is not. For example, in Figure 7, if D3 is selected as the server, then D2 , D4 and
1151   D7 are the only devices connected to D3 . Another example will be, if D13 is selected as the server,
1152   then D9 and D14 are the only ones connected to the server.
1153   The Ring graph is connected by joining all the devices in a sequential order of increasing indices with
1154   the last device connected to the first. In our example it will be constructed by joining, D1 with D2 ,
1155   then D2 with D3 and so on and so forth with D16 connected back to D1 . Finally, Random Geometric
1156   Graph is constructed by connecting a device to all other devices that fall within a certain radius (r)
1157   parameter. Hence a lower value of r denotes a device is connected to less devices compared to a
1158   larger value of r.
1159   Irrespective of the base graph, Grid or Complete, when training or testing faults are applied to the
1160   selected base graph, during implementation it is assumed that the graph with incorporated faults stays
1161   constant for one entire batch and then the graph is reevaluated for the next batch. In our experiments,
1162   the batch-size is taken to be 64.

       In Figure 19 we show results with a temporal communication fault model on a complete connected
1344   graph. CD- models are trained under a dropout rate of 30%. The temporal fault model uses Markov
1345   process to simulate the transition of links or edges in the network between connected and faulty states
1346   based on probabilistic rules defined by a transition matrix. The transition matrix is such that when
1347   fault rate = 0, the probability of staying in non-faulted state is 1. However for other fault rates, the
1348   transition matrix is: [[p, 1-p],[q , 1-q]], where r is the fault rate, q = (1-p)(1-r)/r, and p is fixed at 0.9.

       faulted to non-faulted state. Even on a temporally varying graph, MAGS outperforms other baselines.

1649   A note regarding gossiping is that mainly helps MVFL in the case of communication fault. We believe
1650
       this is because in the device fault case, irrespective of number of gossip rounds, the representations
       from faulted device cannot be obtained. On the other hand, multiple gossip rounds in communication
1651
       fault scenario has the effect of balancing out the lost representation at a client via neighboring
1652
       connections. Switching to the grid baseline network, a major observation here is the degradation
1653   in the performance of both MVFL and MVFL-G4. We conjecture that in this case, clients can only
1654   directly communicate with neighboring clients, thus it’s harder to get information from clients far
1655   away and extra communication leads to less benefit while the smaller network size and receiving more
1656   faulted representation become a bottleneck. Similarly, we notice that MVFL outperforms MVFL-G4
1657   when fault rate is very high, as there is a much higher chance that the network is disconnected in
1658   comparison to complete baseline network. In short, we conclude that when trained with no faults,
1659   MVFL is overall the best model while gossiping helps except with high fault rates under the grid
1660   baseline network.
