011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) enhanced with retrieval-augmented generation
014            (RAG) have introduced a new paradigm for web search. However, the limited
015            context awareness of LLMs degrades their performance on RAG tasks. Exist-
016
               ing methods to enhance context awareness are often inefficient, incurring time
               or memory overhead during inference, and many are tailored to specific position
017
               embeddings. In this paper, we propose Position-Embedding-Agnostic attention
018
               Re-weighting (PEAR), which enhances the context awareness of LLMs with zero
019            inference overhead. Specifically, on a proxy task focused on context copying,
020            we first detect heads which suppress the models’ context awareness, thereby di-
021            minishing RAG performance. To weaken the impact of these heads, we re-weight
022            their outputs with learnable coefficients. The LLM (with frozen parameters) is op-
023            timized by adjusting these coefficients to minimize loss on the proxy task. During
024            inference, the optimized coefficients are fixed to re-weight these heads, regardless
025            of the specific task at hand. Our proposed PEAR offers two major advantages over
026            previous approaches: (1) It introduces zero additional inference overhead in terms
027
               of memory usage or inference time, while outperforming competitive baselines in
               accuracy and efficiency across various RAG tasks. (2) It is independent of position
028
               embedding algorithms, ensuring broader applicability.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG, (Lewis et al., 2021)) is widely utilized to enhance large
034
      language models (LLMs) on tasks like question answering. Typically, an RAG framework retrieves
035
      documents related to users’ question from external knowledge bases or web pages, and then arranges
036   them in the LLMs’ context as the references to form answers. This LLM-based question-answering
037   paradigm has given rise to a promising web search paradigm (Microsoft, 2023; OpenAI, 2024).
038
039
      Recent research demonstrated LLMs’ limitations on context awareness, especially when processing
      long context. These limitations in LLMs’ context awareness challenge the effectiveness and robust-
040
      ness of RAG frameworks. For instance, Liu et al. (2023) found that when performing in-context
041
      retrieval tasks, LLMs exhibit insensitivity to information located in the middle of the context, a phe-
042   nomenon referred to as “lost in the middle.” Chen et al. (2024) identified a mathematical property
043   in rotary position embedding (RoPE, (Su et al., 2023)) that results in LLMs assigning less attention
044   to specific contextual positions, leading to varying context awareness throughout the entire context.
045
      Existing approaches to enhancing LLMs’ context awareness are inefficient in terms of memory
046
      and time cost. Some works (Peysakhovich & Lerer, 2023) segment and re-arrange the input con-
047
      text, with the assumption that placing important information in positions the model attends well
048   can improve RAG’s effectiveness. This method incur additional inference time costs, negatively
049   affecting user experience, as it requires multiple forward passes to obtain attention weights for guid-
050   ing segment rearrangement. Another group of studies modifies the model’s working mechanism,
051   specifically by employing a set of position embeddings to adjust the attention preferences of atten-
052   tion heads. While these methods are input-agnostic, they also lack efficiency due to “parallelable”
053   forward passes (Chen et al., 2024) although without increased time cost, disrupting the parallelism
      of multi-head attention (resulting in increased time cost) to achieve low memory cost (Lin et al.,

069   In this paper, we introduce Position-Embedding-Agnostic attention head Re-weighting (PEAR),
070
      which unleashes the context awareness of LLMs, thereby improving their RAG performance. PEAR
      achieves zero additional overhead in memory usage and inference time. Our motivation relies on the
071
      following facts:
072
073         1. Prior research (McDougall et al., 2023; Lv et al., 2024a) detected some attention heads
074            decrease the language model’s prediction confidence by suppressing the flow of contextual
075            information to the final position within the context, where the output is to be generated.
076
            2. This suppression negatively impacts LLMs’ context awareness, particularly abilities in in-
077
               context retrieval and context integration, which are crucial for effective RAG.
078
079   As a result, we contend that such suppression mechanism in LLMs can be safely1 weakened in RAG
080   scenarios, thereby improving the performance of RAG. Our proposed PEAR includes two stages:
081
082
      In the first stage, we discover attention heads negatively affect performance on a proxy task. The
      proxy task involves feeding the model a random token sequence of length 2n, which consists of a
083
      duplicated sub-sequence of length n. Table 1 illustrates an example input. At position n + i, the
084
      model typically predicts the token from position i + 1, as the natural continuation for a semantically
085   meaningless context is to copy the existing in-context token pattern (Lv et al., 2024b). The negatively
086   impactful attention heads are discovered using the path patching technique (Wang et al., 2023). Since
087   this proxy task is free from semantic bias and requires both in-context retrieval and generation based
088   on the context—fundamental capacities for RAG, we refer to discovered heads as RAG-suppression
089   heads2 .
090
      In the second stage, we weaken detected RAG-suppression heads by re-weighting their outputs using
091   learnable coefficients. These coefficients are optimized by minimizing the LLM’s loss on the proxy
092   task, with the objective of next-token prediction in a supervised fine-tuning process (loss is computed
093   only for the second half of the random-token sequence). During the optimization, the original LLM
094   parameters are frozen. Intuitively, most of the learned coefficients are optimized to values less than
095   one, reducing the relative weight of these heads compared to others in the same layer when multi-
096   head outputs are aggregated. Consequently, their influence during the forward pass are weakened.
097   Once optimized, the coefficients remain fixed and are agnostic to downstream RAG tasks.
098   PEAR achieves three-fold contributions:
099
100         1. PEAR introduces zero inference overhead in terms of both memory usage and inference
101            time—an advantage not achieved by competitive baselines. Across a wide range of RAG
102            tasks, PEAR surpasses previous works in both efficiency and accuracy.
103         2. PEAR is independent of specific position embedding algorithms, making it broadly appli-
104            cable. We demonstrate that PEAR enhances the RAG performance of various LLMs using

112   2     R ELATED WORKS
113
114   In this section, we discuss two research areas closely related to this paper: enhancements to LLMs’
115   context awareness and studies on mechanistic interpretability.
116
117   2.1   C ONTEXT AWARENESS ENHANCEMENT
118
119   Many studies highlighted limitations in LLMs’ context awareness. For example, Lu et al. (2022)
120
      found that the order of in-context learning (ICL) demonstrations significantly affects ICL accuracy.
      Liu et al. (2023) demonstrated that LLMs exhibit stronger awareness of content at the beginning
121
      and end of context but weaker awareness in the middle, a phenomenon termed “lost in the middle.”
122
      Chen et al. (2024) proposed that LLMs’ context awareness fluctuates across token positions due to
123   mathematical properties in position embeddings. These challenges impact applications like RAG
124   that rely on robust context awareness.
125
126
      Several approaches have been proposed to tackle these issues. However, existing methods often
      come at the cost of increased inference time or memory overhead. Attention Buckets (AB, (Chen
127
      et al., 2024)) enhances context awareness by integrating positional information from a set of various
128
      RoPE angles, but it incurs significant inference overhead due to multiple parallel forward passes,
129   leading to increased memory usage. Ms-PoE (Zhang et al., 2024) calculates distinct re-scaling fac-
130   tors for each attention head, requiring multiple non-parallel forward passes that introduce noticeable
131   time delays. MoICE (Lin et al., 2024) builds on Attention Buckets by employing a Mixture-of-
132   Experts (MoE, (Shazeer et al., 2017)) approach, treating each RoPE embedding as a unique in-
133   context expert, thereby limiting extra attention computations to within each layer rather than across
134   the entire forward pass.
135   Our proposed method, PEAR, enhances context awareness by weakening the RAG-suppression
136   heads during the forward pass. It introduces no additional modules or extra forward passes, result-
137   ing in zero additional overhead in memory usage and inference time. Additionally, PEAR operates
138   independently of position embedding algorithms, which is applicable to more LLMs compared to
139   existing approaches.
140
141   2.2   M ECHANISTIC INTERPRETABILITY
142
143   Investigating the role of a specific head during a forward pass is one of key focuses in mechanistic
144   interpretability research. Wang et al. (2023) reported that in GPT-2 small (Radford et al., 2019),
145   the 7th head in the 10th layer, termed the “negative head,” significantly hinders answer copying
146   from context. McDougall et al. (2023) comprehensively studied this head, suggesting it functions
147
      as a self-repair mechanism to prevent overconfident outputs. Lv et al. (2024a) found that negative
      heads exist across various LLMs, employing different mechanisms to mitigate overconfidence, such
148
      as generating counteracting vectors or introducing high-frequency tokens’ information. This paper
149
      does not examine what specific mechanisms the heads employ to suppress RAG performance but
150   instead aims to discover and suppress heads negatively impactful across general RAG tasks.
151
152   Yu et al. (2023) detected two types of heads in Transformer-based language models during counter-
153
      factual task execution (where counter-factual knowledge is provided in the context): memory heads,
      which prefer to use stored knowledge, and in-context heads, which prefer to use facts in the context.
154
      However, when re-weighting these heads, only reducing the weight of memory heads successfully
155
      enhances the model’s preference of using contextual knowledge, while enhancing or mitigating in-
156   context heads does not bring much influence. Moreover, existing results only demonstrate effective-
157   ness of these heads in the “country-capital” task (i.e., prompting the model to answer the capital city
158   of the given country). Additionally, there is no evidence suggesting that re-weighting these heads
159   improves the comprehensive context awareness of LLMs. We owe these failures to their head detec-
160   tion method, and the same re-weighting value applied for all the heads of the same type. By contrast,
161   in our proposed PEAR, each individual head is re-weighted by a specific learnable coefficient, which
      is optimized through a proxy task independent of downstream tasks.

221   4.1   D ISCOVERY OF RAG- SUPPRESSION HEADS
222
223   We set up a proxy task and use this task as input for circuit discovery algorithms to discover influ-
224   ential attention heads that hamper LLMs’ performance on general RAG tasks.

226   Task Input For each input sample, we create a sequence of length n, denoted as {x1 , . . . , xn },
227   where each xi is a randomly sampled token from the vocabulary. This sequence is repeated to form
228   an input sample X = {x1 , . . . , x2n }, with xi = xi+n for i ∈ [1, n]. Research has shown that,
229
      in semantically meaningless contexts, models tend to check if the last few tokens in the sequence
      appeared previously and copy the suffix of their last appearance as the output (Olsson et al., 2022;
230
      Lv et al., 2024b). We consider an arbitrary LLM to successfully perform the proxy task when, at
231
      position n + i + 1, the token with the highest output logits is xi . Table 1 shows an example input.

234   heads:
235

236
               work, such as in-context retrieval and generation based on context, making it suitable for
237
               discovering RAG-related attention heads.
238
239         2. The random token composition in X ensures semantically meaningless input, minimizing
240
               knowledge bias and thereby enabling the discovered attention heads to have general RAG-

351
352   In this section, we introduce the LLMs we used for experiments, the baseline methods for enhanc-

357   Models and baselines We conducted experiments with three LLMs, each employing a different

      2023), OPT-6.7B-2k (Zhang et al., 2022) using learnable position embeddings, and Baichuan-13B-

      6     C ONCLUSION
525
      In this paper, we introduce PEAR, a position-embedding-agnostic method designed to enhance the
526
      performance of LLMs on RAG tasks with zero inference overhead. Our method not only outper-
527
      forms competitive baselines in both effectiveness and efficiency but also demonstrates broad appli-
528   cability across various LLMs. We also presented that PEAR improves context awareness in LLMs
529   without compromising their inherent knowledge capabilities. These benefits make PEAR a promis-
530   ing approach for a wide range of applications that require robust context abilities, such as in-context
531   learning and strict instruction following, which we leave for future research.
