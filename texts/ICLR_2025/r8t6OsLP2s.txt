009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks have emerged as a powerful tool for large-scale mesh-based
012            physics simulation. Existing approaches primarily employ hierarchical, multi-scale
013            message passing to capture long-range dependencies within the graph. However,
014            these graph hierarchies are typically fixed and manually designed, which do not
015            adapt to the evolving dynamics present in complex physical systems. In this pa-
016            per, we introduce a novel neural network named DHMP, which learns Dynamic
017            Hierarchies for Message Passing networks through a differentiable node selection
018
               method. The key component is the anisotropic message passing mechanism, which
               operates at both intra-level and inter-level interactions. Unlike existing methods, it
019
               first supports directionally non-uniform aggregation of dynamic features between
020
               adjacent nodes within each graph hierarchy. Second, it determines node selection
021            probabilities for the next hierarchy according to different physical contexts, thereby
022            creating more flexible message shortcuts for learning remote node relations. Our
023            experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of DHMP, achieving 22.7% improve-
024            ment on average compared to recent fixed-hierarchy message passing networks
025            across five classic physics simulation datasets.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Simulating physical systems with deep neural networks has achieved remarkable success due to their
031   efficiency compared with traditional numerical solvers. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have been
032   validated as a powerful tool for mesh-based physical scenarios, such as fluids and rigid collisions (Wu
033
      et al., 2020). The primary mechanism driving the GNN-based models is message passing (Sanchez-
      Gonzalez et al., 2020; Pfaff et al., 2021; Allen et al., 2023). In this process, time-varying physical
034
      quantities are encoded within the mesh structure at each time step and are updated by aggregating
035
      information broadcast from neighboring nodes. These existing methods generally rely on local
036   message passing, limiting their ability to propagate influence over long distances. A common solution
037   involves using multi-scale graph structures to facilitate direct information shortcuts between distant
038   nodes (Lino et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024; Han et al., 2022; Fortunato et al., 2022).
039
      However, as illustrated in Table 1, these approaches depend on heuristic methods to create coarser
040
      message passing structures with predefined graphs (Cao et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024) or downsample
041
      the nodes based on spatial proximity (Lino et al., 2022) where hierarchies are preprocessed in one
042   pass before training. These fixed graph hierarchies over the entire physical sequence do not account
043   for the diverse range of physical contexts; while in practical systems like turbulence, despite identical
044   boundary conditions, even minor changes in initial conditions can lead to significant differences in
045   subsequent dynamics. Moreover, the spatial correlations in a physical process can evolve over time,
046   making static GNN hierarchies insufficient for accommodating the time-varying node interactions.
047
      To tackle these challenges, we propose a novel approach named Dynamic Hierarchical Message
048   Passing (DHMP), which constructs context-aware and temporally evolving graph hierarchies based
049   on the original mesh topology and the input physical quantities. The key insight is to develop a
050   differentiable node selection method that allows for flexible modeling of node interactions. This is
051   technically supported by the proposed anisotropic message passing, which aggregates the neighboring
052   features to the central node in a directionally non-uniform manner, predicting its downsampling
053   probabilities as a node within the coarser graph level. We then employ Gumbel-Softmax sampling to
      create a differentiable approximation of the hard sampling for the downsampled graph.

      The anisotropic message passing mechanism not only adaptively creates multi-scale graph structures
066
      but also enables learned directionally non-uniform importance weights to facilitate both intra-level
067
      and inter-level propagation of dynamic information. As shown in Table 1, existing approaches
068   perform isotropic feature aggregation within intra-level transition, assuming equal contributions from
069   neighboring nodes, which may overlook the directional nature of physical processes. While some
070   methods employ attention mechanisms to replace isotropic intra-level propagation (Dwivedi & Bres-
071   son, 2020; Janny et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024; Han et al., 2022), our approach demonstrates advantages
072   in computational efficiency. Furthermore, existing models generally rely on unlearnable importance
073   weights to transfer information across hierarchical levels. In contrast, the inter-level aggregation
074   weights in DHMP are data-specific and time-varying, effectively harnessing the anisotropic nature of
075   our message passing mechanism to enhance multi-scale modeling flexibility.
076   Overall, our contributions are summarized as follows:
077
      • We present DHMP, a new method that constructs dynamic hierarchies via a differentiable node se-
078
        lection process, enabling context-aware modeling of hierarchical structures for physics simulations.
079
080
      • As a key component in DHMP, the proposed anisotropic message passing enables learnable, non-
        uniform intra-level and inter-level feature propagation, significantly enhancing model performance.
081
082   • DHMP achieves a 22.7% promotion on average across four standard benchmarks, compared with
083     fixed-hierarchy models. It is also shown to generalize well to test cases with time-varying mesh
084
        structures (Table 3), unseen resolutions (Table 4), and out-of-distribution dynamics (Table 5).

      2     P RELIMINARIES
086
087   Message passing. We consider simulating mesh-based physical systems, where the task is to
088   predict the dynamic quantities of the mesh at future timesteps given the current mesh configuration.
089   A mesh-based system is represented as a bi-directed graph G = (V, E)1 , where V and E denote the
090   set of nodes and edges, respectively. Message passing neural networks (MPNNs) compute the node
091   representations by stacking multiple message passing layers of the form:
092
           Edge update: êij = ϕe (eij , vi , vj ); Node update: v̂i = ϕv (vi , ψ ({êij | ∀j, eij ∈ E})) ,          (1)
093
094   where vi is the feature of node vi ∈ V and ψ denotes a non-parametric aggregation function. The
095   function ϕe updates the features of edges based on the endpoints, while ϕv updates the node states
096   with aggregated messages from its neighbors. In existing mesh-based simulation methods, multi-layer
097
      perceptrons (MLPs) with residual connections are commonly employed for ϕe (·) and ϕv (·), with the
      aggregation function ψ(·) being defined as the sum of edge features. Notably, since the aggregation
098
      function treats all neighbors equally, the contributions from neighboring nodes may be averaged out,
099
      and the repeated message-passing process can further dilute distinctive node features. This issue is
100   exacerbated in dynamic physical systems, where transferring directed patterns is crucial.
101
102
      Hierarchical MPNNs. To facilitate long-range modeling, hierarchical MPNNs process information
      at L scales by creating a graph for each level and propagating information between them (Lino et al.,
103
      2022; Fortunato et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024). Let G1 = (V1 , E1 ) represent the
104
      graph structure at the finest level, i.e., the input mesh. The lower-resolution graphs G2 , G3 , . . . , GL ,
105   with |V1 | > |V2 | > . . . > |VL |, contain fewer nodes and edges, which allows for more efficient
106

      j, it is represented as two directed edges i → j and j → i. Each node has a self-loop.

108
      feature propagation over longer physical distances with certain propagation steps. The typical process
109   for constructing multi-scale structures primarily involves downsampling and upsampling between
110   adjacent graph hierarchies. Downsampling reduces the number of nodes while upsampling transfers
111   information from a lower-resolution graph to a higher-resolution one. The downsampling operation
112   can be broken down into two steps:
113
      • SELECT: Nodes are selected from the current graph structure Gl to create a new, coarser graph
114
        Gl+1 . Various criteria for node selection (Diehl, 2019; Ying et al., 2018; Lino et al., 2022) can be
115     applied to form Vl+1 . The edges El+1 in Gl+1 are constructed by connecting the selected nodes
116     based on the original edges El . However, this process can sometimes lead to loss of connectivity
117     and introduce partitions (Gao & Ji, 2019; Lee et al., 2019; Cao et al., 2023). To mitigate this,
118     connectivity in El+1 can be strengthened by adding K-hop edges.
119
      • REDUCE: The features of the nodes in Vl+1 are aggregated from their corresponding neighborhood
120
        features in the finer graph Gl .
121
122   The upsampling process is represented by the EXPAND, the inverse of the REDUCE function, which
123   aggregates information from the coarser level back to the finer level. Most previous work generates
124
      coarser graphs for each sequence either by using numerical software or by downsampling the input
      mesh through heuristic pooling strategies. This process occurs during the data preprocessing stage,
125
      enabling the preprocessed hierarchy of the same input mesh topology to be reused across various
126
      initial conditions and different time steps.

131
      In this section, we introduce the Dynamic Hierarchical Message Passing Networks (DHMP), a fully
      differentiable model that learns to dynamically generate coarser graphs over the sequence while
132
      simultaneously learning to simulate the physical system over the learned hierarchical graphs.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
135
136   Figure 1 demonstrates an overview of the proposed model, which operates in an encode-process-
137   decode pipeline. The encoder first maps the input field to a latent feature space V1 = {vi |vi ∈ V1 }
138   at the original mesh resolution. Subsequently, we model the physical dynamics across the learned
139   multi-scale graph hierarchies with adaptive graph structures. To enhance the propagation of long-term
140   dependencies between distant nodes, we propose an anisotropic message passing (AMP) mechanism,
141
      which is largely inspired by the directed nature of significant dynamic patterns.
142   In Section 3.2, we present the details of the AMP layer. In Section 3.3, we discuss the approach for
143   learning context-aware graph hierarchies. In Section 3.4, we describe the inter-level downsampling
144   and upsampling processes that incorporate AMP-based feature propagation. Finally, in Section 3.5,
145   we outline the implementation details and hyperparameter choices.

277   decrease the temperature from 5 to 0.1 with a decay factor of γ = 0.999, which aims to encourage
278   the exploration of hierarchies while gradually refining node selection to ensure training stability.

283   Datasets. We evaluate our approach on five mesh-based physics simulation benchmarks established

285
      • CylinderFlow: Simulation of incompressible flow around a cylinder based on 2D Eulerian meshes.

289   • DeformingPlate: Deformation of hyper-elastic plates based on Lagrange tetrahedral meshes.
290

296
      • MGN (Pfaff et al., 2021), which performs multiple times of message passing at the original graph.
297
298   • BSMS-GNN (Cao et al., 2023), which generates static hierarchies using bi-stride pooling and
299
        performs message passing on predefined meshes.
300   • Lino et al. (2022), which also trains MPNNs on manually-set multi-scale mesh graphs.
301   • HCMT (Yu et al., 2024), which generates static hierarchies by applying Delaunay triangulation to
302     the bi-stride pooled nodes, and enables directed feature propagation with the attention mechanism.
303
      All models are trained using the Adam optimizer with an exponential learning rate decay from 10−4
304   to 10−6 . We further clarify the architecture details and the hyperparameters in Appendix C.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      Learning-based physics simulation. Recent literature has shown that learning-based simulators
489
      can efficiently handle complex and high-dimensional problems, such as fluid dynamics (Zhu et al.,
490   2024), structural analysis (Kavvas et al., 2018; Thai, 2022), and climate modeling (Kurth et al., 2018;
491   Rasp et al., 2018; Rolnick et al., 2022; Lam et al., 2023). The models can be roughly categorized
492   into three groups based on data representation: those modeling partial differential equations (Raissi
493   et al., 2017; 2019; Lu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021), particle-based systems (Li et al.,
494   2019; Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Ummenhofer et al., 2020; Prantl et al., 2022), and mesh-based
495   systems (Pfaff et al., 2021; Lino et al., 2022; Fortunato et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023). The rapid
496   inference time and differentiable property of these models greatly facilitate downstream tasks, such
497   as inverse design (Wang & Zhang, 2021; Goodrich et al., 2021; Allen et al., 2022; Janny et al., 2023).
498
499
      GNN-based physics simulation. Previous work has explored GNNs in various physical domains,
      such as articulated systems (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2018), soft-body deformation and fluids (Li
500
      et al., 2019; Mrowca et al., 2018; Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Rubanova et al., 2022; Wu et al.,
501
      2023), rigid body dynamics (Battaglia et al., 2016; Li et al., 2019; Mrowca et al., 2018; Bear et al.,
502   2021; Rubanova et al., 2022), and aerodynamics (Belbute-Peres et al., 2020; Hines & Bekemeyer,
503   2023; Pfaff et al., 2021; Fortunato et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023). Among them, MGN (Pfaff et al.,
504   2021) is a key method that models mesh-based dynamics through graph interactions. Subsequent
505   approaches primarily focus on enhancing modeling capabilities and reducing computational costs.
506
507   Hierarchical GNNs for physics simulation. Hierarchical GNNs employ multi-scale graph struc-
508   tures (Lino et al., 2022; Han et al., 2022; Fortunato et al., 2022; Allen et al., 2023; Janny et al., 2023;
509   Cao et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024) to decrease overhead by using fewer nodes at coarser levels and
510   enabling long-range feature propagation. GMR-Transformer-GMUS (Han et al., 2022) employs a
511
      uniform sampling pooling method to select pivotal nodes. MS-MGN (Fortunato et al., 2022) uses
      a dual-level hierarchical GNN and performs message passing at both fine and coarse resolutions.
512
      Hierarchical GNNs with multi-level structures (Lino et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024;
513
      Garnier et al., 2024; Hy & Kondor, 2023) are most relevant to our approach, as they integrate
514   message passing neural networks within the U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015). Lino
515   et al. (2022) uses manually set grid resolutions and spatial proximity for graph pooling, which
516   requires predefined parameters. BSMS-GNN (Cao et al., 2023) introduces a bi-stride pooling strategy
517   that pools nodes on alternating breadth-first search frontiers while enhancing edges with two-hop
518   connections. HCMT (Yu et al., 2024) refines the structure further by applying Delaunay triangulation
519   to bi-stride nodes. Notably, these methods construct multi-level structures as preprocessing and
520   cannot change the graph hierarchies under varying physical conditions. Moreover, they typically
521   use uniform feature aggregation for intra-level propagation, which may hinder the directed transfer
522
      of significant dynamic patterns, or use attention-based aggregation, which increases computational
      overhead. Furthermore, inter-level propagation is often predefined or unlearnable, limiting flexibility
523
      in transferring information across hierarchy levels. In contrast, our model generates context-aware
524
      and temporally evolving graph hierarchies and incorporates learnable anisotropic feature propagation,
525   allowing it to better adapt to various initial conditions and rapidly changing dynamic systems.
526
527
528   6    C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
529
530   In this paper, we introduced DHMP, a neural network that significantly advances the state-of-the-art
531   in mesh-based physics simulation. Our key innovation is dynamically creating the context-aware
532   graph structures of hierarchical GNNs through a differentiable node selection process. To this end,
533   we proposed an anisotropic message passing mechanism to enhance the propagation of long-term
534   dependencies between distant nodes, aligning with the directed nature of significant dynamic patterns.
535   Extensive experiments show that DHMP outperforms existing models, especially those with fixed
536
      graph hierarchies, in both short-term and long-term predictions.
537   A potential limitation of this work is the need to improve the interpretability of the learned hierarchy
538   structure. Additionally, we would consider incorporating specific physical priors into DHMP to
539   further enhance the model’s robustness and generalizability, particularly in resolution-free problem
      settings, which have been less explored in existing mesh-based approaches.

      B     M ODEL I MPLEMENTATION
798
      We present model configurations of different physical systems below:
799
800   • Edge offsets. X and x stand for the mesh-space and world-space position. For an Eulerian
801     system, only mesh position is used for eij , while for a Lagrangian system, both mesh-space and
802     world-space positions are used. The edge offsets are directly used as low-dimensional input to the
803     edge update function ϕe . In other words, these features are concatenated and fed into ϕe without
804     any transformation through an MLP or other encoding processes to generate a higher-dimensional
805     representation.
806   • Input and target of the physical term of node vi . v is the velocity, ρ is the density, P is the
807     absolute pressure, and the dot ȧ = at+1 − at stands for temporal change for a variable a. n
808     stands for the node type of vi . Random Gaussian noise is added to the node input features to
809     enhance robustness during training (Pfaff et al., 2021; Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Cao et al.,
        2023). All the variables involved are normalized to zero-mean and unit variance via preprocessing.


                                                              15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
        The preprocessed physical term is fed to the encoder to transform it into a high-dimensional
811     representation.
812
813   The encoder, decoder, node update function ϕv , and edge update function ϕe all utilize two-layer
814   MLPs with ReLU activation and a hidden size of 128. Similarly, the importance weight network ϕw
815   in AMP is implemented using a two-layer MLP. LayerNorm is applied to the MLP outputs, except
816   for the decoder and the importance weight network. We set K = 2 for edge enhancement, which is
817
      aligned with the setting of BSMS-GNN (Cao et al., 2023). In the Gumbel-Softmax for differentiable
      node selection, temperature annealing decreases the temperature from 5 to 0.1 using a decay factor of
818
      γ = 0.999, encouraging exploration of hierarchies while gradually refining their selection to ensure
819
      stability. DHMP is trained with Adam optimizer, using an exponential learning rate decay from 10−4
820   to 10−6 . All experiments are conducted using 4 Nvidia RTX 3090. We mainly build DHMP based on
821   the released code of BSMS-GNN (Cao et al., 2023).

      C     BASELINE D ETAILS
825
      We compare DHMP with four competitive baselines: (1) MGN (Pfaff et al., 2021) which performs
826
      multiple message passing on the input high-resolution mesh topology; (2) BSMS-GNN (Cao et al.,
827
      2023), which uses predefined bi-stride pooling prior as preprocessing to generate static hierarchies on
828   same mesh topology; (3) Lino et al.(Lino et al., 2022), which uses manually set grid resolutions and
829   spatial proximity for graph pooling; (4) HCMT (Yu et al., 2024), which uses Delauny triangulation
830   based on bi-stride nodes and adopt attention mechanism to enable non-uniform feature propagation.
831   The architecture details of the compared models are as follows:
832
      • MGN. In MGN, we use 15 message passing steps in all datasets. The encoder, decoder, node
833
        update function, and edge update function are configured in the same way as in our model.
834
835
      • BSMS-GNN. We use the same number of graph hierarchies in DHMP and as in BSMS-GNN.
        We use the minimum average distance as the seeding heuristic for the BFS search recommended in
836
        its original paper. The multi-level building is processed in one pass. The inter-level propagation
837
        uses the normalized node degree to convolve features from neighbors to central nodes. The encoder,
838     decoder, node update function, and edge update function are set up the same way as in our model.
839     We perform one message passing step at each graph level.
840
      • Lino et al. We use the four-scale GNN structure proposed in the work of Lino et al. (2022). The
841
        edge length of the smallest cell for each dataset is 1/10 of the average scene size, with each lower
842     scale doubling in size. We follow its original paper to use 4 message passing steps at the top and
843     bottom levels and two for the others.
844
      • HCMT. The hidden dimension and the number of attention heads in the HCMT block are set
845
        to 128 and 4, respectively. We use the same number of hierarchies as in DHMP. For the Cylinder
846     and Airfoil datasets, due to the presence of hollow sections in the mesh, we do not apply Delaunay
847     triangulation for remeshing. Instead, we use edge connections generated through bi-stride pooling.
848     Like in DHMP, we use a single message passing step at each graph level.
849
850   Notably, the node encoder, decoder, node update function, and edge update function of MGN, BSMS-
851   GNN, and Lino et al. have the same network architecture as those in DHMP. To reduce the number
852
      of network parameters, we avoid separately encoding the edge offset eij . Instead, we concatenate it
      with the node latents and use this combined input for the edge update function to compute êij .
853
854   All models are trained using the Adam optimizer, with an exponential learning rate decay from 10−4
855   to 10−6 and a decay rate of γ = 0.79. The batch size is set to 32. Following BSMS-GNN, model
856   convergence is defined by a performance improvement threshold of < 1%, at which point the training
857   process is terminated.

1004   D.5                F URTHER I MPROVEMENT WITH KP-AMP
1005
       Recent work by Feng et al. (2022) introduces a novel framework for graph neural networks, empha-
1006
       sizing the distinct processing of information from different hop distances within a graph. In their
1007
       approach, each hop distance is treated as a separate entity, with dedicated MLPs used to process
1008   the messages passing through edges of different hop lengths. This design enables the model to
1009   learn varying structural features at different scales, enhancing its expressiveness and adaptability to
1010   heterogeneous graph structures.
1011
       Inspired by this approach, we explore the applicability of a similar approach to enhance DHMP.
1012
       Specifically, we extend DHMP by introducing a “KP-AMP” block, characterized by the following
1013
       modifications:
1014
1015   • The original AMP block is replaced with a specialized KP-AMP block.
1016
1017
       • Edges at each hop distance are segregated into separate sets, enabling each hop to be processed
         independently through a dedicated MLP.
1018
1019
       We conduct experiments on the CylinderFlow dataset and showcase the performance comparison
1020   between the original DHMP and “DHMP with KP-AMP” in Table 10. The results indicate that
1021   incorporating distinct MLPs for each hop distance significantly enhances the model’s capability to
1022   process structural information at different scales, leading to improved performance across both RMSE-
1023   1 and RMSE-All metrics. This approach complements DHMP’s anisotropic weighting mechanism by
1024   further diversifying the representation of structural information. Future research could focus on more
1025   deeply integrating these strategies to enhance the expressiveness of multi-hop processing paradigms
       within the context of dynamic hierarchy construction.

1054
       DiffSELECT, we evaluated the stability of DHMP by running the trained model on the test set
1055   in three independent trials. We report the mean and standard deviation of the prediction errors in
1056   Table 11. Despite the stochastic nature of the node selection process, the results show a very small
1057   standard deviation, demonstrating that DHMP reliably constructs stable and consistent dynamic
1058   hierarchies. This stability can be attributed to the DiffSELECT operation, where the node update
1059   module ϕv generates probabilities for retaining nodes in the next-level graph based on anisotropic
1060   aggregated edge features. The Gumbel-Softmax technique, coupled with temperature annealing,
1061   enables differentiable and stable node selection across hierarchy levels. As a result, the dynamic
1062   hierarchies are constructed in a manner that is not only consistent but also optimized for long-range
1063
       dependencies. Moreover, the prediction errors from DHMP are significantly smaller than those of
       the baseline models, underscoring the robustness and reliability of the model, even with its dynamic
1064

1116   J.1     C OMPARISON TO S TATIC H IERARCHICAL GNN S
1117
1118   Hierarchical GNNs with multi-level structures (Lino et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024;
1119   Garnier et al., 2024; Hy & Kondor, 2023) are closely related to our approach, as they incorporate
1120   MPNNs within the U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015). However, these methods typically
1121   treat multi-level structures as fixed preprocessing steps and do not adapt the graph hierarchies under
1122   varying physical conditions.
1123   Besides, for intra-level feature propagation, some approaches use uniform feature aggregation (Cao
1124   et al., 2023; Lino et al., 2022; Hy & Kondor, 2023), while others employ attention mechanisms to
1125   introduce isotropic contributions from neighboring features (Yu et al., 2024; Garnier et al., 2024).
1126   However, the latter primarily focuses on adding weighted attention scores to nodes, overlooking
1127   spatially-aware edge features. For inter-level feature propagation, these methods typically rely
1128   on graph convolution based on node degree or directly adopt the U-Net architecture, limiting the
1129   flexibility in transferring information across hierarchical levels.
1130   Different from our approach, Lino et al. (2022) uses manually set grid resolutions and spatial
1131   proximity for graph pooling, which requires manual hyper-parameters. BSMS-GNN (Cao et al.,
1132   2023) introduces a bi-stride pooling strategy that pools nodes on alternating breadth-first search
1133   frontiers while enhancing edges with two-hop connections. HCMT (Yu et al., 2024) refines the
       structure further by applying Delaunay triangulation to bi-stride nodes.
