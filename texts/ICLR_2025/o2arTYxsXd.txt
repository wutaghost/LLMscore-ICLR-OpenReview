011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated Class-Incremental Learning (FCIL) increasingly becomes essential in
014            the decentralized setting, where it enables multiple participants to collaboratively
015            train a global model to perform well on a sequence of tasks without sharing their
016            private data. In FCIL, conventional Federated Learning algorithms such as FedAvg
017
               often suffer from catastrophic forgetting, resulting in significant performance
               declines on earlier tasks. Recent works based on generative models produce
018
               synthetic images to help mitigate this issue across all classes. However, these
019
               approaches’ testing accuracy in previous classes is still much lower than recent
020            classes, i.e., having better plasticity than stability. To overcome these issues, this
021            paper presents Federated Global Twin Generator (FedGTG), an FCIL framework
022            that exploits generative-model training on the global side without accessing client
023            data. Specifically, the server trains a data generator and a feature generator to create
024            two types of information from all seen classes. Then, it sends the synthetic data
025            to the client. The clients then use feature-direction-controlling losses to make the
026            local models retain knowledge and learn new tasks well. We extensively analyze
027            the robustness of FedGTG on natural images and its ability to converge to flat local
028
               minima and achieve better predicting confidence (calibration). Experimental results
               on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and tiny-ImageNet demonstrate the improvements in
029
               accuracy and forgetting measures of FedGTG as well as the robustness of domain
030
               shifts compared to previous frameworks.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Federated Learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2016; Bonawitz et al., 2019) is a Machine Learning setting
036   that facilitates collaborative learning while maintaining privacy. Despite its significant achievements
037   on various domains (Doshi & Yilmaz, 2022; Lin et al., 2021; Liu & Yang, 2021; Nguyen et al.,
038   2019), FL observes several critical challenges, including resource limitation and data heterogeneity.
039
      Moreover, the client’s local data distribution is assumed to remain unchanged, but the real-world
      scenarios (Aljundi, 2019) can be different, where the client’s task, data, and domain can be changed.
040
      To overcome such challenges, Federated Class-Incremental Learning (FCIL) (Dong et al., 2022;
041
      2023) is an innovative approach that combines the principles of FL and Class-Incremental Learning
042   (CIL) (Rebuffi et al., 2017) to enable models to learn continuously from decentralized data sources
043   while adapting to new information over time without forgetting previous knowledge (French, 1999).
044   This approach addresses data privacy challenges and ensures the model can evolve as new data
045   types or tasks emerge without accessing historical data. In CIL, exemplar-based approaches (Rebuffi
046   et al., 2017; Chaudhry et al., 2019; Buzzega et al., 2020) preserve a limited number of samples from
047   previous tasks to prevent forgetting, whereas exemplar-free approaches (He et al., 2018; Liu et al.,
048   2020; Magistri et al., 2024) do not retain any samples from prior tasks.
049   In the FL setting, where privacy issues pose significant challenges, the exemplar-free category is
050   particularly interesting since users cannot store historical data. Recent works in this field, such
051   as TARGET (Zhang et al., 2023), FedCIL (Qi et al., 2023) and MFCL (Babakniya et al., 2024)
052   tend to generate synthetic data and combine with distilling regularizers (Hinton et al., 2015; Liu
053   et al., 2020) to balance the trade-off between retaining knowledge and learning new tasks. However,
      experimental results have shown that these works still witness catastrophic forgetting, i.e., bias

086
      • We propose an FCIL framework that trains a data generator and a feature generator on the server

088
089   • To help the model have a better stability-plasticity trade-off, we propose direction-controlling

093   • Moreover, we analyze the robustness of FedGTG compared with recent FCIL algorithms on
094     natural images, its abilities to converge to flat minima, achieve better predicting confidence, and
095     maintaining the effectiveness across different client sizes.

      2.1   C ONTINUAL L EARNING
101
      Catastrophic forgetting (French, 1999) is a significant issue in machine learning where training a
102   model on new data makes it forget previously learned knowledge. This issue is central to the field of
103   CL, whose primary objective is to build models to acquire new knowledge while retaining information
104   from older tasks. Numerous strategies have been explored to mitigate this problem, including the
105   implementation of regularization terms (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Zenke et al.,
106   2017), the isolation of architectural parameters (Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018; Yoon et al., 2017; Mallya
107   et al., 2018), the use of storing prior data (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Chaudhry et al., 2019; Buzzega et al.,
      2020), and studies of data generation (He et al., 2018; Zhuang et al., 2022; 2023; 2024; Magistri


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2024). In CL, replay-based methods observe significant performance in accuracy and forgetting
109   measures. However, privacy concerns in FL prevent data storage (Dong et al., 2022), making these
110   methods inapplicable. An extensive alternative to address this issue is generative-based approaches.
111
112
      These mitigation strategies become more crucial depending on the type of learning in CL. Specifically,
      there are three main types: Task-Incremental Learning (Task-IL), Domain-Incremental Learning
113
      (Domain-IL), and Class-Incremental Learning (Class-IL) (Van de Ven & Tolias, 2019). Each task
114
      is distinct in Task-IL and comes with its distribution during training and testing. In Domain-IL,
115   the learning task does not change, while different domains or data distributions sequentially arrive
116   during training. In Class-IL, each new task adds classes to what the model has to learn, continually
117   expanding the amount of information the model needs to handle.
118
119   2.2   F EDERATED C LASS -I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
120
121   FCIL aims to train a model to learn new classes over time without forgetting previously learned
122   classes while ensuring that data privacy is maintained across multiple decentralized devices. Several
123   approaches exploit Knowledge Distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) to mitigate forgetting by appointing
124   the global model’s weight of the most recent task as a teacher. Continual Federated Learning with
125
      Distillation (CFeD) (Ma et al., 2022) constructs server and client-side knowledge distillation using a
      surrogate dataset, but this costs time and financial resources to collect enough data for this surrogate
126
      dataset. Global-Local Forgetting Compensation (GLFC) (Dong et al., 2022) relaxes this problem
127
      by training a proxy server globally to ease the imbalance issue between classes.
128
129   The above approaches yield extensive performance in past knowledge retention but cannot learn
130   well on new tasks. To alleviate this issue, Federated Class-Incremental Learning (FedCIL) (Qi
131
      et al., 2023) trains generators at both client-side and server-side, as well as utilizing knowledge
      distillation to balance the stability-plasticity trade-off. However, this approach raised a privacy
132
      risk since information about client-side generative models is shared with the server. Federated
133
      Class-Continual Learning via Exemplar-Free Distillation (TARGET) (Zhang et al., 2023) and
134   Mimicking Federated Continual Learning (MFCL) (Babakniya et al., 2024) relax this issue by
135   training a data generator on the global-side and adding distilling regularizers to the client-side training
136   to enhance overall performance. However, as shown in Figure 1, these methods still perform badly
137   on old classes, leaving catastrophic forgetting mitigation a desirable goal.

      Evaluation Metrics We report the performance of the methods using two metrics: Average
359
      Incremental Accuracy and Average Forgetting. Average Incremental Accuracy (AIA) measures the
360
      average accuracy of the global model on all tasks after the training finishes. Forgetting (ft ) of task Tt
361   is the difference between the model’s best performance on task Tt and its accuracy after completed
362   training. Consequently, Average Forgetting (AF) is the average of all f t , from task T1 to task Tn−1 ,
363   at the end of task Tn . We report the averaged result over three different random initializations.

397   4.3     M ODEL A NALYSIS
398
399   The majority of FCIL research concentrates on testing experiments on ideal benchmarks (Dong
400   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Babakniya et al., 2024), such as CIFAR (Krizhevsky et al., 2009)
401   and ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). This lacks analysis concerning real-world scenarios, such as
402
      the decision-making required in hospitals or the model’s generalization to diverse environments.
      Therefore, in this section, we conducted experiments to analyze the robustness of FedGTG and three
403
      FCIL algorithms on corrupted environments, and the qualities of generalization (Chaudhari et al.,
404
      2019; Keskar et al., 2016) and achieve calibrated networks (Guo et al., 2017; Kull et al., 2019).
405
406
      Robustness to natural corruptions. We evaluate our method and the recent FCIL methods on the
407   CIFAR-100-C dataset. This dataset includes 18 augmentations of the original CIFAR-100, inspired
408   by CIFAR-10-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019). Models are trained using standard CIFAR-100 with
409   the same setting in Section 4.1 and tested on CIFAR-100-C. Figure 4 shows robustness to 09 different
410   corruptions averaged over three different runs, the results of the rest augmentations are shown in
411   Appendix D.4. Specifically, our approach achieves higher test accuracy on various corruptions, with
412   an average improvement of 5% over MFCL and 8% over TARGET. Evidently, our method offers
413   noticeable advantages in robustness against natural corruption.
414
415   Converging to flatter minima. Extensive CL algorithms (Bhat et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Park
416
      et al., 2024) explore how well their methods generalize by examining their ability to converge to flat
      minima. In this part, we compare the flatness of the training minima of FLwF-2T, TARGET, and
417
      MFCL with FedGTG. As done in (Zhang et al., 2019), we consider the model at the end of training and
418
      add independent Gaussian noise with growing variance to each parameter. This allows us to evaluate
419                                 Pn     (T )
      its effect on the average loss t=1 LCEt across all training examples. As shown in Figures 5a and 5b,
420
      MFCL, especially FLwF-2T, and TARGET, reveal higher sensitivity to perturbations than FedGTG.
421
      This concludes that FedGTG can achieve better generalization than previous methods.
422
423
      Converging to a more calibrated network. Calibration measures how well a learner’s prediction
424   confidence matches its accuracy, with ideal outcomes reflecting true probabilities of correctness.
425   In real-world applications, including weather forecasting (Bröcker, 2009) and econometric analy-
426   sis (Gneiting et al., 2007), the calibrating ability of a model should be investigated. Figures 5c and 5d
427   show the value of the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) (Naeini et al., 2015) across various FCIL
428   methods after completing each task. It can be seen that FedGTG achieves a lower ECE than the
429   others, proving that models trained with FedGTG are less over-confident and easier to interpret.
430
431   Robustness to different client sizes. We validate the effectiveness of FedGTG across different
      client sizes on the tiny-ImageNet dataset. We run experiments by varying the number of total clients

525   in handling complex scenarios such as domain shifts, making the added computational cost justifiable,
526   particularly in applications like healthcare and finance, where data privacy and performance are
527   crucial. We can see that although FedGTG introduces additional computational components, these
528   are essential to achieving a balance between retaining knowledge and learning new tasks in FCIL,
529   which other methods struggle with.

531   7      C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we alleviate the lack of stability of previous works in the FCIL setting by introducing a
534   framework named FedGTG, both utilizing data and feature generative models trained by the server,
535   eliminating the requirement for costly on-device memory for clients. Our experiments show that
536   FedGTG is successful in reducing catastrophic forgetting and surpasses the current state-of-the-art
537   methods. Moreover, By analyzing the robustness on natural images, testing the qualities of converging
538   to flat minima and calibrated networks, and the performance, as well as the performance on the
539   context of domain shifts between FCIL algorihms, we observe that our framework outperforms
      previous approaches, making FedGTG more applicable in real-world scenarios.

810
      Datasets We perform our experiments on theree widely-used benchmark datasets, including the
811   FCIL version of CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and
812   tiny-ImageNet (Yao & Miller, 2015):
813
814   • Sequential F-CIFAR-10. The CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) consists of 60,000
815     32 × 32 color images in 10 classes, with 6,000 images per class. There are 50,000 training images
816     and 10,000 test images. We split the training set into five disjoint subsets corresponding to 5 tasks.
817   • Sequential F-CIFAR-100. Sequential F-CIFAR-100 is constructed by dividing the original CIFAR-
818     100 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), which contains 50,000 images belonging to 100 classes, into
819     ten disjoint subsets corresponding to 10 tasks. In this way, each task has 5,000 images from 10
820     distinct categories, and each class has 500 images.
821   • Sequential F-tiny-ImageNet. Tiny-ImageNet (Yao & Miller, 2015) is a subset of ImageNet,
822     containing 100,000 images of 200 real objects. We follow settings in (Babakniya et al., 2024)
823     to form the Sequential F-tiny-ImageNet. In particular, we split the original dataset into ten non-
824
        overlapping subsets. We consider each subset as a task whose images are labeled by 20 different
        classes, and each class has 500 samples.
825
826   We also investigate the effectiveness of FedGTG in the context of domain shift. We introduce a
827   protocol dataset named HealthMNIST:
828
829   • HealthMNIST. This dataset includes two distinct classification tasks: Task 1 is the Colon Pathol-
830     ogy Classifcation having data from PathMNIST (Yang et al., 2023). Task 2 is the Blood Cell
831
        Classifcation from BloodMNIST (Yang et al., 2023). PathMNIST contains 107,180 samples of 9
        classes, and BloodMNIST has 17,092 samples from 8 blood types. For both tasks, we select the
832
        first five classes from each dataset, with 500 samples per class for each task for training. Finally,
833
        the test set includes all test samples from these two datasets.
834
835   FCIL Baselines In addition to our FedGTG, we also include three regularization-based FCIL
836   methods, FLwF-2T (Usmanova et al., 2021) and the FCIL version of FedWeIT (Yoon et al., 2021)
837   and FedEWC (Zhang et al., 2023), and two generative-based methods, TARGET (Zhang et al., 2023)
838   and MFCL (Babakniya et al., 2024). FLwF-2T utilize knowledge distillation both on the server
839   side and client side to ease the catastrophic forgetting issue. FedWeIT maximizes the knowledge
840   transfer between clients by storing previous tasks-adaptive parameters of clients. FedEWC is the
841   FCIL version of EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017), which uses Fisher Information Matrix (Fisher, 1922)
842   as a regularizer to prevent forgetting. TARGET utilizes a global model to transfer knowledge from
843
      past tasks to the current task while also training a generator to generate synthetic data, mimicking the
      overall data distribution across clients. MFCL employs a generative model to create samples from
844
      previous distributions, which are then combined with training data to prevent catastrophic forgetting.
845
      Both of these data generation-based algorithms ensure privacy by training the generative model on
846   the server after each task without client data retrieval.
