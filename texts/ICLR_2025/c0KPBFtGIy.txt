009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities
012            across a wide range of natural language processing and reasoning tasks. However,
013            their performance in the foundational domain of arithmetic remains unsatisfactory.
014            When dealing with arithmetic tasks, LLMs often memorize specific examples
015            rather than learning the underlying computational logic, limiting their ability to
016            generalize to new problems. In this paper, we propose a Composable Arithmetic
017            Execution Framework (CAEF) that enables LLMs to learn to execute step-by-step
018
               computations by emulating Turing Machines, thereby achieving a true mastery
               of computational logic. Moreover, the proposed framework is highly scalable,
019
               allowing composing learned operators to significantly reduce the difficulty of
020
               learning complex operators. In our evaluation, CAEF achieves nearly 100%
021            accuracy across seven common mathematical operations on the LLaMA 3.1-8B
022            model, effectively supporting computations involving operands with up to 100
023            digits, a level where GPT-4o falls short noticeably in some settings.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large Language Models (LLMs) have made significant strides in recent years, showcasing
029   extraordinary capabilities across a range of natural language processing (NLP) tasks (Dubey et al.,
030   2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Chowdhery et al., 2023), and in some cases, even surpassing human
031   performance in specific benchmarks (Achiam et al., 2023). However, despite these advancements,
032   LLMs still face significant challenges in performing arithmetic. Current research indicates that when
033
      presented with arithmetic problems, LLMs often rely on memorizing specific expressions and their
      corresponding outcomes rather than grasping the fundamental logic of arithmetic operations (Wu
034
      et al., 2023b). This inherent limitation poses a substantial barrier to their effective application in
035
      fields that demand essential computational skills.
036
037   To enhance the performance of LLMs in solving arithmetic problems, two primary approaches
038   have been developed. The first approach positions the LLM as an agent that relies on an
039
      external calculator to perform computations (Hao et al., 2024; Ruan et al., 2023). In this setting,
      the LLM’s role is limited to providing the operands and invoking the appropriate operations.
040
      Although this method effectively simplifies the challenge of arithmetic for LLMs, it misses the
041
      opportunity for the models to learn computational logic, preventing LLMs from comprehending the
042   underlying principles of arithmetic. Given that arithmetic serves as the foundation of mathematics,
043   the lack of arithmetic ability may significantly impede the LLM’s capability to grasp more
044   complex mathematical concepts. The second approach focuses on stimulating the LLM’s intrinsic
045   capabilities, employing prompt engineering or fine-tuning techniques to enable the model to master
046   arithmetic computations and solve problems through reasoning (Kojima et al., 2022; Huang et al.,
047   2022; Yu et al., 2023). This approach typically involves the LLMs generating intermediate steps
048   before reaching a final result.
049   Although the second approach is promising, it faces two significant challenges. The first challenge
050   is that, under simple supervised fine-tuning, LLMs tend to memorize examples from the training set
051   (Hu et al., 2024). As the length of the operands increases, the sample space expands exponentially,
052   making it impractical for the LLM to memorize all possible examples. To fundamentally overcome
053   this limitation, LLMs should primarily learn and execute computational logic, mirroring how
      humans systematically master arithmetic, rather than relying on memorization.

069
      The second challenge involves learning how to compose basic operators to build complex arithmetic
070
      operators. These complex operators are typically execution procedures that contain conditional
071
      statements (if -then-else) and iterative statements (loop), with the basic operators treated as
072   function calls within these procedures. By doing this, LLMs could gradually learn more complex
073   arithmetic operations by focusing on their execution logic and calling the existing operators as
074   necessary.
075
      Mastering the execution of arithmetic is fundamentally equivalent to modeling computation. One
076
      famous mathematical model of computation is the Turing machine, which is formally introduced by
077
      Alan Turing (Turing et al., 1936). If the LLM learns to execute computational logic by simulating
078   executing a Turing machine based on its transition functions for each operator, it could solve
079   arithmetic problems through a multi-query approach. This approach involves the LLM iteratively
080   performing computations based on the current state and command, and then generating the next state
081   and command.
082
      In this paper, we propose a Composable Arithmetic Execution Framework (CAEF) for LLMs to
083
      solve arithmetic problems solely. Inspired by the Turing machine, CAEF aims to teach LLMs
084   the computational logic, enabling them to execute the logic for specific arithmetic operators and
085   compose arithmetic operators into more complex ones. CAEF has two key characteristics:
086
087
      Executing arithmetic. As illustrated in Figure 1, CAEF employs a three-step procedure for each
      arithmetic operator, supported by two independent components within the LLM: the executor and
088
      the aligner. The executor, responsible for performing the actual computations, learns the underlying
089
      computational logic by modeling the transition function of the corresponding arithmetic Turing
090   machine. This allows the LLM to iteratively generate intermediate results and ultimately produce
091   the final output. The aligner serves as an interface, converting raw arithmetic expressions (e.g.,
092   89 × 2 =) into a format that the executor can directly process. Once the executor completes its
093   execution, the aligner transforms the executor’s output back into the final result. In our framework,
094   both the executor and the aligner are implemented as separate LoRA adapters (Hu et al., 2021).
095
      Composing operators. Complex operators can often be composed of basic or simpler ones,
096   hierarchically or recursively. In CAEF, we design an executor composer that is responsible for
097   the high-level execution procedures of complex operators and allows function calls to invoke other
098   pre-learned arithmetic operators. Since each operator is implemented as a LoRA adapter, function
099   calls in CAEF are executed by automatically switching LoRA adapters, following the LLM’s
100   generated command. Thus, CAEF could facilitate the handling of more intricate computations.
101
      Using the proposed framework, we have implemented seven operators: +, −, ×, ÷, >, <, and
102   ==, along with two auxiliary operators (refer to Appendix A.4). Each of these operators is based on
103   existing computational logic, such as the Turing machine or algorithms used in CPU design (e.g., the
104   subtraction operator is modeled similarly to how modern CPUs handle the subtraction operation.).
105   Our experiments show that CAEF achieves high accuracy across all seven operators when using the
106   LLaMA 3.1-8B model (Dubey et al., 2024). Compared to GPT-4o, the LLM equipped with CAEF
107   demonstrates minimal impact from changes in operand length, effectively supporting computations
      involving operands with up to 100 digits. The main contributions of this paper are as follows:

      2.1   P ROBLEM STATEMENT
122
123   Computational logic is fundamental to arithmetic. To truly master arithmetic, the LLM should
124   learn and execute the underlying computational logic of arithmetic operations rather than merely
125   memorizing examples of arithmetic expressions. For scalability, the LLM should be capable of
126   constructing new operators by combining existing operators. For example, after learning Addition
127   operation, the LLM could construct Multiplication by learning the computational logic of repeated
128   addition could achieve multiplication.
129   Therefore, we need a framework that enables LLM to model arithmetic operators by learning
130   to execute their underlying computational logic. In the field of automata, the Turing machine
131   provides a suitable framework for describing this logic. Following the examples (e.g., Turing
132   machines introduced in Sipser (1996)), we could build a Turing machine for common arithmetic
133   operations, which can be a reference to create adequate datasets of execution steps for LLM training.
134   Furthermore, the Turing machine inherently supports the combination of multiple Turing machines,
135   making it ideal for constructing complex operations from existing ones. By emulating Turing
136   machines, LLM can be designed to integrate multiple models, enabling it to execute more intricate
137
      arithmetic tasks.
138
139
      2.2   LLM EXECUTES AS T URNING MACHINE
140
      A Turing machine can be formally defined as a septuple T = (Q, Σ, Γ, b, q0 , F, δ), where Q is a
141   finite set of states, Σ ⊆ Γ is a finite alphabet for input, Γ is a finite tape alphabet, b ∈ Γ is the
142   blank symbol, q0 ∈ Q is the initial state, F ⊆ Q is a set of final states, and δ is the transition
143   function. When a Turing machine is in a non-halting state, the next action is determined by both
144   the current symbol on the tape and the machine’s current state. In each action, the machine updates
145   the symbol on the tape, transitions to a new state, and moves the tape head either left or right. This
146   process repeats iteratively until the machine reaches a halting state, at which point the computation
147   is complete, and the result is saved on the tape.
148   LLM is the generative model for text-based language, so how to transfer all information from a
149   Turing machine to the LLM effectively is challenging. A tailored representation system is necessary
150   for LLMs to accurately learn computational logic. To facilitate this transfer, the system must
151   incorporate states analogous to those of the Turing machine, such as the machine state and tape
152   state, to indicate the current status of the computation, in other words, the step in the execution
153   process. Additionally, the system should include commands that specify the actions to be executed
154   based on the current state to ensure correct transitions to the next state. Thus, CAEF provides a
155
      text-based representation < si , ci > that effectively represents the state si and the command ci for
      each step i in the computational logic. Then, the state transition function f (e.g. LLM or LLM
156
      fine-tuned with LoRA adapters) could use this representation at stepi as the input to generate the
157
      next representation at stepi+1 as following:
158

160    By formulating the representation of both input and output for Equation 1, the LLM is enabled to
161   perform computations in a manner similar to that of a Turing Machine by executing step-by-step
      transitions.

181   2.3   R EPRESENTATION DESIGN
182
183   In this paper, we design a structured representation for arithmetic problems to enable the LLM to
184
      accurately execute computational logic. As illustrated in Figure 2, representation of the arithmetic
      problem includes: 1) a status indicating the current step of the computation, and 2) a ”tape” that
185
      records all operands and essential information, such as the number of digits processed, any carryover
186
      during addition, and other intermediate results. To facilitate the LLM’s learning of the execution
187   process, the representation in CAEF explicitly includes the commands c required for execution.
188   These commands involve the call to the next state s and other detailed actions, such as carrying over
189   or moving the pointer. All the above elements are represented in text, which is friendly to LLM to
190   deal with. Then, to make LLM execute based on the representation, CAEF structures the input as
191   < si , ci > for current stepi , while the output is < si+1 , ci+1 > for the next stepi+1 .
192
      Besides modeling the representation, representation translation is another critical part of CAEF. In
193   general, the original input of an arithmetic problem does not include the initial state or the first
194   command to execute. Moreover, upon completing the computation, the raw output remains in the
195   representation format. Thus, CAEF incorporates an aligner to manage the bidirectional translation
196   between the original input/output and the representation. The aligner can also be implemented by
197   fine-tuning a specific LoRA adapter. Notice that one key feature of the aligner should learn the
198   ability to convert the left-to-right (L2R) representation of numbers into a right-to-left (R2L) format,
199   as R2L is evaluated more effectively for LLM to operate the operands (Lee et al., 2023).

      3.2   BASIC EXECUTORS
257
258   We use addition to illustrate the design of a basic executor. A natural way to implement addition is to
259   imitate the accumulator, performing the addition of two corresponding digits from the operands once
260   at a time, along with the value stored in the carry register. This process calculates the result for the
261   current digit and simultaneously updates the carry register for the next higher digit’s computation.
262
      Thus, the state and the command for addition are constructed as follows. The state should include
263
      the following components: 1) the two operands, 2) two pointers indicating the current digits being
264   processed, 3) the carry register, and 4) the output generated so far. The command part should at
265   least include: 1) the actions to write the carry and output, 2) the actions to move the pointers, and
266   3) state transition actions to control the start, transitions, and halting of the addition. Based on this
267   instruction, CAEF constructs the state machine based on the text-based represented < si , ci >.
268   Figure 3 illustrates the computation process of CAEF for addition. The details of computations
269   and dataset are listed in Appendix A.3 and Section 4.1, respectively. In this paper, we use similar
      procedures to design the operators for >, < and ==.

291   3.3   E XECUTOR COMPOSERS
292
293   Executor composer designs to orchestrate the basic executors into intricate computational logic.
294
      Instead of performing computations directly, the executor composer ”calls” other basic executors in
      a specific sequence to accomplish more complex tasks.
295
296   Multiplication is a typical example of the executor composer, which can be implemented by calling
297   the + and < basic executors. CAEF uses two accumulators (+ involved) to implement a × b. The
298   first accumulator increments by 1 with each loop iteration, while the second adds a during each
299   iteration. This process continues until the first accumulator reaches b (< involved), and then the
300
      value in the second accumulator represents the final result. LLM is fine-tuned to execute control
      flow and loops, by calling the < executor and, based on its result, either halts or continues the loop.
301
      Figure 4 illustrates the computation process for 89 × 2 using our implementation. Since the executor
302
      composer decouples the computational logic into several executors, the fine-tuning process could be
303   done separately for each executor, showing the ability of executor composition.
304
305   Besides multiplication, we also design subtraction (considering only non-negative results) and
306
      division (floor division) executor composers using similar methodologies. Specifically, we draw
      inspiration from how subtraction is handled in CPUs to construct the subtraction executor composer
307
      and the detailed implementation can be found in Appendix A.4.

312
313   Models. We utilize the LLaMA 3.1-8B pretrained model (non-instruct version) as the base model.
314   During LoRA fine-tuning, all linear modules in the decoder layer are involved in training, with the
315   hyperparameters fixed at r = 8, α = 16, and a learning rate of 5 × 10−5 . The fine-tuning process
316   is conducted in two stages. In the first stage, we introduce an exhaustive explanation in the prompt,
317
      detailing the computation goal of an executor, the required input/output format, and providing an
      example. This explanation is followed by the actual sample, as illustrated in Appendix A.1. In
318
      the second stage, we remove the long explanation from the prompt and present only the sample,
319
      expecting the model to predict the next state and the subsequent commands directly. We use batch
320   sizes of 8 and 16 for the first and second stages, respectively. All experiments are conducted on a
321   server equipped with six H800 GPUs. The code and the models are available1 .

363

364

365

366   to train the executor. By generating multiple sequences through random initialization, an adequate
367   training dataset for the executor can be created. It is notable that for arithmetic expressions with

369   dominated by intermediate steps, potentially omitting samples from the first and final transitions.

371   aligner, we generate two alignment processes: one aligning the original arithmetic expression with

      5   R ELATED W ORK
433
434
      LLMs in Mathematical Contexts. Prior research has focused on enhancing LLM performance in
435   mathematical tasks, often relying on external tools for calculations and primarily addressing math
436   word problems rather than pure arithmetic. A common external tool is a calculator, as exemplified
437   by Schick et al. (2024), which introduces a self-supervised method where the model learns when to
438   call external tools via API access. Similar strategies can be found in Gou et al. (2023) and He-Yueya
439   et al. (2023), and it was employed in even earlier work (Andor et al., 2019). Another tool is a
440   programming language interpreter, such as Python, where the model generates code and an external
441   interpreter executes it to obtain the result. A representative example is Lu et al. (2024), which treats
442   the LLM as a planner that generates code and submits it to an external Python executor to handle
443
      math problems in tabular contexts. Wang et al. (2023) employs supervised fine-tuning to improve
      code-based problem-solving, while Zhou et al. (2023) proposes a zero-shot prompting method to
444
      enable code-driven self-verification, thereby improving mathematical performance.
445
446   LLMs in Arithmetic Scenarios. Another series of work focuses solely on arithmetic, which we
447   consider directly related to our research. The common characteristic of these studies is their effort to
448   teach LLMs computational logic and improve calculation accuracy through step-by-step processes.
449
      Among these works, Nye et al. (2021) is an early and far-reaching study, predating the popular
      Chain-of-Thought (CoT) approach. It introduces a similar idea in the arithmetic domain, where the
450
      language model outputs intermediate steps to a buffer called a ”scratchpad,” significantly improving
451
      performance in integer addition. Hu et al. (2024) observes that transformers tend to approach
452   arithmetic problems using ”case-based reasoning” and proposes a Rule-Following Fine-Tuning
453   technique that guides the model to execute calculations step by step. Zhou et al. (2024) combines
454   four techniques (i.e., FIRE position encodings, Randomized position encodings, Reversed format
455   (R2L format), and Index hints) to develop a new model that achieves a 2.5× improvement in length
456   generalization for two-integer addition.

      6   L IMITATIONS
459
460
      Prone to errors with repeated digit patterns. Both the executor and the aligner tend to generate
461
      incorrect steps when encountering patterns of repeated digits, such as sequences like ”999...” where
462   a single digit repeats, or ”456456...” where multiple digits repeat. These errors typically manifest
463   as extra or missing repetitions of the pattern. While this issue might be mitigated by intentionally
464   generating more such expressions to increase the representation of similar samples in the training
465   set, we believe the root cause lies in limitations inherent to generative LLMs.
466
      Efficiency Issue. In our method, completing a single computation requires generating the full
467
      sequence of intermediate steps, which essentially means repeatedly calling the model.generate()
468   function.    For computations involving hundreds of steps, this process can be extremely
469   time-consuming. One potential solution lies in optimizing the use of the KV cache. In our approach,
470   the input to the LLM at two consecutive steps is highly similar. However, since different parts of
471   the input shift position, the KV cache from the previous step cannot be effectively reused. The
472   KV cache functions like a ROM. If we could transform it into a RAM-like structure that supports
473   simple editing operations, such as swapping adjacent tokens while maintaining the correct tokens
474   and positional embeddings, this could significantly improve computational efficiency.
475   Implementation of the Turing machine prototype. When generating the training set for the
476   executor, CAEF wants to ensure the correctness of the samples and enable the executor to learn
477   key computational steps, such as carrying over or exiting loops. A practical approach is to construct
478   a Turing machine prototype corresponding to the target operator and record its execution process.
479   While there are many existing Turing machine designs, the implementation process may take some
480   human-involved effort. A future work could design a generation process to translate existing Turing
481   machines into CAEF required Turing machine prototypes.
482   Inability to Solve Math Word Problems. Currently, our method requires manual selection of the
483   active LoRA adapter, rather than enabling the model to autonomously determine the appropriate
484   adapter. This limitation hinders the direct application of our approach to solving math word
485   problems. However, our method can be regarded as a modular component. Several studies have
      explored integrating Mixture of Experts (MoE) and LoRA techniques to facilitate the automatic


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      selection and switching of active LoRA adapters based on the input (Wu et al., 2023a; Zadouri et al.,
487   2023; Huang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). These studies are orthogonal to our approach, and we
488   posit that combining these techniques with our method could enable effective application to math
489   word problems. For example, leveraging the CAEF plug-in, a large language model (LLM) could
490   dynamically switch to CAEF to handle arithmetic computations as part of the reasoning process in
491   solving such problems.

      7   C ONCLUSION
494
495
      This paper proposes a framework that enables LLMs to learn to execute step-by-step arithmetic
496   computational logic by imitating the behavior of a Turing machine. This approach significantly
497   enhances LLMs’ computational capability without relying on any external tools. Moreover, the
498   framework is highly scalable, allowing the construction of complex executors by composing learned
499   basic executors, reducing the difficulty of learning the complex logic. We hope that our work
500   provides a new perspective for enabling LLMs to learn rule-based computation.

       A.4   I MPLEMENTATION OF S UBTRACTION O PERATOR
1513
1514   We implement subtraction in the CAEF framework by drawing inspiration from how subtraction is
1515   handled in CPUs. For subtraction in the form a − b = c, the process can be broken down into four
1516   steps:
1517
1518         1. Compute Reflection(a, b): Generate a number a9 , where all digits are 9 and it is the same
1519            length as a. Perform a reflection operation, which is essentially subtraction, between a9
1520            and b. Since all digits of a9 are 9, no borrowing occurs during this subtraction. Let the
1521            result of this step be p.
1522         2. Compute a + p, and let the result be q.
1523
             3. Compute q + 1, and let the result be r.
1524
1525         4. Compute Left mask(r): Remove the leading 1 from the most significant digit of r. After
1526            this step, the final result, c, is obtained.
1527
1528   For example, in the case of 4531 − 1504 = 3027, the process is as follows:
1529
1530      Step 1 (Reflection):
1531      Reflection(4531, 1504) = 9999 − 1504 = 8495
1532
1533
          Step 2 (Addition):
          4531 + 8495 = 13026
1534
1535
          Step 3 (Addition):
1536      13026 + 1 = 13027
1537
1538      Step 4 (Left mask):
1539      Left mask(13027) = 3027
1540
1541
1542   In CAEF, steps 2 and 3 can be handled using the addition executor, which has already learned the
1543   logic for addition, while the auxiliary operators needed for steps 1 and 4 are relatively simple to
1544   implement. The subtraction executor composer only needs to learn how to sequentially invoke these
1545   basic executors to perform subtraction.

1589   A.6   F URTHER E XPERIMENT R ESULTS A NALYSIS
1590
1591   Using addition in the form of a + b = c as an example, we generate the executor’s training dataset
1592   by dividing the expressions into equivalence classes based on the pair (len(a), len(b)), where 20
1593   random arithmetic expressions are generated for each equivalence class. When the operand lengths
1594   are sufficiently large, 20 samples are sparse across the entire equivalence class space. However, the
1595   model still achieves high accuracy in tasks such as 100-digit addition, indicating that the LLM
1596
       effectively learns the logic of the Turing machine’s transition function during training, thereby
       indirectly grasping the underlying logic of arithmetic computation.
1597
1598   However, this sampling strategy alone can lead to poorer performance when operand lengths are
1599   relatively short, typically less than 10 digits, compared to longer operands. We believe this issue
1600   arises because the longest samples in the training set generally exceed 100 digits, and from the
1601   perspective of equivalence classes, the dataset becomes dominated by samples with operands of
1602
       several dozen digits. Intuitively, although both cases involve a difference of 10 digits, the difference
       between 5 and 15 digits has a much larger impact than the difference between 90 and 100 digits,
1603
       especially in the way the LLM perceives these distinctions. Therefore, in practice, we slightly
1604
       increase the number of samples from equivalence classes with shorter operands. Additionally, for
1605   operators such as ==, purely random sampling makes it difficult to obtain samples where the result
1606   is True, so some additional intervention is necessary.

       A.7   C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
1610
       For the seven operators implemented using the CAEF framework, we assume the longer operand has
1611
       a length of d. Based on the computation mechanism of self-attention, the computational complexity
1612
       of a single model inference is O(d2 ).
1613
1614   For the addition, greater than, less than, and equal operators, the computation is performed digit
1615   by digit, requiring at most d model queries for a complete computation. Additionally, the aligner
1616
       performs two representation conversions, resulting in a total of d + 2 model queries. Therefore, the
       overall computational complexity is O(d3 ).
1617
1618   For subtraction, the computational complexity of the auxiliary operators Reflection and Left mask
1619   is also O(d3 ). Since subtraction involves one call each to Reflection and Left mask, along with two
       calls to addition, the overall complexity remains O(d3 ).


                                                         30
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1620
       For multiplication, the situation is slightly more complex. For a calculation of the form a × b, we
1621   assume len(a) = d1 , len(b) = d2 , and len(a × b) = d3 . The number of iterations in the loop is b + 1.
1622   During each iteration, less than and two addition operations are performed, with the complexities
1623   as follows:
1624
1625          • For less than, the longer operand has a length of d2 , resulting in a total complexity of
1626            O((b + 1)d22 ) across all iterations.
1627          • For the first addition, the longer operand has a length of d3 , giving a total complexity of
1628            O((b + 1)d23 ) across all iterations.
1629          • For the second addition, the longer operand again has a length of d2 , resulting in a total
1630            complexity of O((b + 1)d22 ) across all iterations.
1631
1632   Thus, the overall complexity is the sum of these three parts, plus the two aligner conversions. The
1633   final computational complexity for multiplication is O(bd22 + bd23 ).
1634   For division, the situation is similar to multiplication. Assuming len(a) = d1 , len(b) = d2 , a÷b = c,
1635   and len(c) = d3 , the number of iterations in the loop is c + 1. The final computational complexity
1636   for division is O(cd21 + cd23 ).
