009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Denoising diffusion models have emerged as a formidable method, consistently
012            surpassing previous state-of-the-art benchmarks. However, a notable challenge in
013            time series-related tasks like anomaly detection and forecasting is the condition-
014            ing for models to reconstruct inputs accurately or generate samples based on past
015            time steps rather than producing entirely new samples. To address this, we intro-
016
               duce a novel technique that enhances the sampling capabilities of denoising diffu-
               sion models for time series analysis, namely Spatio-Temporal Diffusion Models
017
               (STDM). While recent methods fall short of mapping contextual neighborhood
018
               dependencies directly into the sampling of a noisy sample, we focus on guiding
019            the forward process of the diffusion model. The degeneration of a sample is based
020            on the idea that values of neighboring time steps are highly correlated. We benefit
021            from this assumption by presenting a diffusion step-dependent convolutional ker-
022            nel to capture spatial relations and a combined, correlated noise to degenerate the
023            input. Our method can be integrated seamlessly into various existing time series
024            diffusion models. We compare the results of anomaly detection and forecasting
025            when using the traditional and our novel forward process. In our experiments on
026            synthetic and real-world datasets, we show that an adaption of the forward process
027
               can be beneficial, as our approach outperforms diffusion models with the ordinary
               forward process in task-specific metrics, underscoring the efficacy of our strategy
028
               in enhancing time series analysis through advanced diffusion techniques.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Time series analysis is a cornerstone of modern applications across a multitude of domains, includ-
034   ing healthcare (Morid et al., 2023), climate modeling (Mudelsee, 2019), industrial manufacturing
035   (Ali Nemer et al., 2022), and cyber security (Al-Ghuwairi et al., 2023). Deep learning-based models
036   have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in discerning patterns and dependencies within multivari-
037   ate time series data. These models excel in reconstructing signals to detect anomalies and predicting
038
      future timestamps. Crucially, these tasks often necessitate unsupervised training, as existing datasets
      frequently lack labeled data, or the output itself is a time series.
039
040   Time series analysis inherent different challenges: An accurate reconstruction of input data is
041   paramount for anomaly detection (Chandola et al., 2009), as anomalous data cannot be generated
042   during inference due to the training dataset predominantly consisting of nominal state data. Time
043   series forecasting necessitates the model to learn the historical patterns of the time series to predict
044
      future time steps (Lim & Zohren, 2021). Some approaches for forecasting include a reconstruction
      of the past time steps (Kollovieh et al., 2023).
045
046   In recent years, Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020) have garnered
047   significant attention in generative tasks owing to their exceptional ability to produce high-quality
048   samples. These models operate by progressively distorting an input with Gaussian noise, training a
049   model to reverse this process by estimating the corruption at various levels.
050   A critical aspect of time series anomaly detection and forecasting is to ensure the model utilizes
051   the time series as an input during inference rather than generating a new, realistic time series from
052   pure Gaussian noise. Current diffusion models fall short of paying attention to the temporal patterns
053   within the time series during the forward process and rely solely on incorporating conditions during
      training and sample generation.

      In this paper, we propose Spatio-Temporal Diffusion Models (STDM), a novel technique that guides
      the diffusion model in reconstructing multivariate time series data by refining the forward process.
070
      Based on the idea of correlation neighboring time steps (Wu et al., 2021), our approach employs
071
      a convolution-based sampler that benefits from spatial relations within the time series while intro-
072   ducing a correlated noise to corrupt it to an unrecognizable state. Figure 1 shows the difference
073   between STDM and the traditional DDPM forward process using a single channel of a time se-
074   ries. The mapped dependencies enable us to address task-specific challenges more effectively and
075   to train more robust models. Our approach seamlessly replaces the standard diffusion sampling pro-
076   cess and can be combined with various conditioning techniques. Adapting existing diffusion models
077   for anomaly detection and forecasting offers distinct advantages over the traditional forward process
078   when working with standard datasets.
079   We summarize our most important contributions as outlined below:
080
081                1. We introduce a novel diffusion forward process for time series data that takes spatial corre-
082                   lations into account while remaining a Markov chain.
083                2. Due to the similar structure as ordinary forward steps, our approach can be easily integrated
084                   into existing diffusion models.
085                3. We show that our approach can improve the results of time series diffusion models in
086                   anomaly detection and forecasting tasks.
087
088   The structure of this paper is as follows: Section 2 provides information on time series tasks, de-
089   noising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs), and approaches to conditioning and guidance. Our
090   guided degradation process is detailed in Section 3. Section 4 demonstrates the effectiveness of our
091
      approach by modifying existing diffusion models. Finally, Section 5 summarizes the strengths and
      limitations of our approach.

      2.3    C ONDITIONING D IFFUSION M ODELS FOR T IME S ERIES DATA

144   Several methods exist that include conditions to generate a certain output. Class-agnostic (Nichol
145   & Dhariwal, 2021) or text-based conditioning (Podell et al., 2024) is often used for computer vision
146   tasks, where a label or text prompt is given, describing the object or scene to generate. The objective
147   function in Eq. (6) at diffusion step k can be supplemented with a conditioning vector c
148                                                                  2

150   Non-categorical conditioning often makes use of alternative ideas on how to tailor a model towards
151   a desired output.
152
153
      In their work on Conditional score-based diffusion models (CSDI), Tashiro et al. (2021) combined
      a diffusion model with time series imputation. Their approach involves randomly masking por-
154
      tions of a sample, requiring the model to estimate the missing values. To guide the model towards
155
      reconstructing the masked sections, the authors utilize the unmasked parts of the signal as a condi-
156   tion. This condition remains uncorrupted by noise, allowing the model to leverage information from
157   neighboring values for accurate imputation effectively. Based on CSDI, Chen et al. (2023b) intro-
158   duced a novel masking strategy for imputation in IMDiffusion, specifically designed for time series
159   anomaly detection. Their method ensures that all data points in the time series are imputed by em-
160   ploying two imputation instances along with two complementary masks. The resulting instances are
161   merged to achieve a complete reconstruction of the sample, thereby enhancing the model’s ability
      to detect anomalies more precisely.

162
      Shen & Kwok (2023) propose TimeDiff that integrates two distinct conditioning strategies to en-
163   hance time series forecasting. During the training phase, they combine past information with the
164   future ground truth, utilizing the resulting latent vector as the primary component of the condition.
165   At the inference stage, the model relies solely on past time steps. Additionally, a linear autore-
166   gressive model provides a preliminary approximation of future time steps, serving as a secondary
167   condition to further refine the model’s predictions.
168
      DiffusionAE (Pintilie et al., 2023) is an anomaly detection model that combines autoencoders and
169   diffusion models. An autoencoder reconstructs an input signal and passes the reconstruction to the
170   diffusion model. The authors showed that the model is more robust to small noise levels since the
171   non-optimal autoencoder reconstruction can be seen as a slightly corrupted input, further perturbed
172   throughout the forward process. During anomaly detection, the signal is corrupted in fewer forward
173   steps than during training to retain information about the original signal.
174
      The incorporation of latent vectors is a different approach for directing the generation process of
175   diffusion models. Rasul et al. (2021) showed the beneficial impact of conditioning when doing short-
176   term time series forecasting. They utilized an additional RNN to capture the temporal dependencies
177   of previous time steps and integrated the updated hidden state in their model. In MG-TSD, Fan et al.
178   (2024) refined this method by using various granularity levels of the signal as input.
179
      Most approaches for time series forecasting condition their generative models on observed values of
180
      past time steps or their latent representation. Instead, Kollovieh et al. (2023) employ a self-guidance
181   mechanism that allows sampling from a class-agnostic distribution during the backward process.
182
183   Besides the presented mechanisms, self-conditioning (Chen et al., 2023a) is often used in diffusion
184
      models, as it can be applied without external sources. The model is directly conditioned by its
      previous estimate of x̂0 . To ensure that the model does not focus entirely on self-conditioning, it is
185
      zeroed out with 50 % probability.
186
187   All these approaches have in common that the degeneration of the sample relies on the forward
188   process introduced in Ho et al. (2020). Our approach reinvents this step by taking the spatio-temporal
189   correlations of time series into account.

191   3   G UIDED D EGRADATION P ROCESS
192
193   While previous approaches have predominantly focused on altering the denoising process to influ-
194   ence the diffusion model’s outcome, our methodology innovatively manipulates the forward process.
195   This adjustment facilitates faster convergence during training and enhances robustness during infer-
196   ence. Our forward process corrupts the sample while preserving the temporal relationships within
197   the signal. As demonstrated by Bansal et al. (2023), the diffusion process need not be strictly Gaus-
198   sian and can be realized through various mechanisms. However, the options for guided degradation
199   of time series data remain limited.
200   We propose STDM, a novel forward process for time series diffusion models that employs convolu-
201   tional operators to corrupt the input signal. This forward process is defined as a Markov chain, akin
202   to traditional diffusion models. The sample for the subsequent diffusion step k + 1 is obtained au-
203   toregressively by convolving the current sample xk with a fixed Gaussian kernel H ∗1 and corrupting

344   4.2   R ESULTS
345
346   To validate our process, we evaluated state-of-the-art diffusion-based methodologies for anomaly
347   detection and time series forecasting. We compare the outcomes of the original implementations
348   with our novel technique. The details of the used datasets are listed in appendix C. Anomaly de-
349   tection and time series forecasting come with different metrics. We use the typical scores for each
350   discipline. We refer to appendix D for a detailed explanation of the metrics.
351   Anomaly Detection. Pintilie et al. (2023) generated synthetic multivariate datasets to assess Dif-
352   fusionAE, each embodying distinct anomaly types as delineated by NeurIPS-TS (Lai et al., 2021).
353   Their diffusion model’s efficacy was quantified using F1K -AUC and ROCK -AUC metrics. The
354   F1K score computation adhered to the PA%K protocol (Kim et al., 2022), which employs point
355   adjustment as utilized by Su et al. (2019) for varying K% of the time steps within an anomaly be-
356   ing detected correctly. To ensure threshold independence, ROC curves were generated for multiple
357
      thresholds K. The metrics in Table 1 represent the mean area under the curve (AUC) for F1K and
      ROCK across five independent runs for each dataset. According to Pintilie et al. (2023), the sam-
358
      pling process must not begin at the final diffusion step with Gaussian noise, as stated in algorithm 2,
359
      but starts at an intermediate step depending on the dataset.
360
361   In our comparative analysis of the two sampling methodologies, our convolution-based sampler con-
362   sistently outperforms the traditional diffusion process in F1K -AUC scores across all five synthetic
363
      datasets. The ROCK -AUC scores remain predominantly high and stable. Notably, our approach
      demonstrates a remarkable enhancement in the dataset characterized by shape-based anomalies,
364
      achieving a relative improvement of 9.4% in anomaly detection performance (F1K -AUC). Further-
365
      more, our approach significantly boosts the detection scores by 31.8% for the trend-based dataset,
366   where the traditional method exhibits notable deficiencies. However, the detection results are still
367   lower and more volatile than those of the other datasets, which is traceable to a more accurate recon-
368   struction of the anomalous segments Pintilie et al. (2023). Figure 3 shows a channel of two samples
369   of the Trend dataset. Our approach (blue line) could not reconstruct the anomalous segments, which
370   are highlighted in red. An imperfect reconstruction indicates an anomaly.

404   5   C ONCLUSION
405
406   In this paper, we introduced STDM, a novel technique to guide the forward process in diffusion
407   models specifically tailored for time series data. Our approach involves the smoothing of the signal,
408   which is subsequently corrupted by Gaussian noise. The noises at various stages exhibit correlations
409   and spatial dependencies across their dimensions. The forward process to any arbitrary intermediate
410   diffusion step can be computed in a single step. Given that our novel forward process mirrors the
411
      structure of the conventional diffusion process, it can be seamlessly integrated with existing diffusion
      models.
412
413   Our experiments underscore the efficacy of our approach, demonstrating enhancements in anomaly
414   detection and forecasting tasks with minimal effort. For a fair comparison, a model-specific adjust-
415   ment of parameters is out of scope but we believe that further hyperparameter optimizations hold
416   the potential to yield even more impressive results. This work paves the way for future research
417
      and applications, offering a robust framework for improving the performance of diffusion models in
      various time series analysis tasks.
