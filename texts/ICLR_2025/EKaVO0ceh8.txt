009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Many variants of Optimal Transport (OT) have been developed to address its
012            heavy computation. Among them, notably, Sliced Wasserstein (SW) is widely
013            used for application domains by projecting the OT problem onto one-dimensional
014            lines, and leveraging the closed-form expression of the univariate OT to reduce
015            the computational burden. However, projecting measures onto low-dimensional
016            spaces can lead to a loss of topological information. To mitigate this issue, in this
017            work, we propose to replace one-dimensional lines with a more intricate structure,
018            called tree systems. This structure is metrizable by a tree metric, which yields a
019
               closed-form expression for OT problems on tree systems. We provide an extensive
               theoretical analysis to formally define tree systems with their topological prop-
020
               erties, introduce the concept of splitting maps, which operate as the projection
021
               mechanism onto these structures, then finally propose a novel variant of Radon
022            transform for tree systems and verify its injectivity. This framework leads to an
023            efficient metric between measures, termed Tree-Sliced Wasserstein distance on
024            Systems of Lines (TSW-SL). By conducting a variety of experiments on gradient
025            flows, image style transfer, and generative models, we illustrate that our proposed
026            approach performs favorably compared to SW and its variants.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Optimal transport (OT) (Villani, 2008; Peyré et al., 2019) is a naturally geometrical metric for com-
032   paring probability distributions. Intuitively, OT lifts the ground cost metric among supports of input
033   measures into the metric between two input measures. OT has been applied in many research fields,
034   including machine learning (Bunne et al., 2022; Takezawa et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2022; Hua et al.,
035
      2023; Nguyen & Ho, 2024), statistics (Mena & Niles-Weed, 2019; Weed & Berthet, 2019; Liu
      et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2022; Nietert et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Pham et al., 2024), mul-
036
      timodal (Park et al., 2024; Luong et al., 2024), computer vision and graphics (Rabin et al., 2011;
037
      Solomon et al., 2015; Lavenant et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2021; Saleh et al., 2022).
038
039   However, OT has a supercubic computational complexity concerning the number of supports in in-
040   put measures (Peyré et al., 2019). To address this issue, Sliced-Wasserstein (SW) (Rabin et al., 2011;
041
      Bonneel et al., 2015) exploits the closed-form expression of the one-dimensional OT to reduce its
      computational complexity. More concretely, SW projects supports of input measures onto a random
042
      line and leverage the fast computation of the OT on one-dimensional lines. SW is widely used in var-
043
      ious applications, such as gradient flows (Bonet et al., 2021; Liutkus et al., 2019), clustering (Kolouri
044   et al., 2018; Ho et al., 2017), domain adaptation (Courty et al., 2017), generative models (Deshpande
045   et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019; Nguyen & Ho, 2022), thanks to its computational efficiency. Due to
046   relying on one-dimensional projection, SW limits its capacity to capture the topological structures
047   of input measures, especially in high-dimensional domains.
048
049   Related work. Prior studies have aimed to enhance the Sliced Wasserstein (SW) distance (Nguyen
050   et al., 2024a; 2020; Nguyen & Ho, 2024) or explore variants of SW (Bai et al., 2023; Kolouri et al.,
051   2019; Quellmalz et al., 2023). These works primarily concentrate on improving existing components
052   within the SW framework, including the sampling process (Nguyen et al., 2024a; 2020; Nadjahi
053   et al., 2021), determining optimal lines for projection (Deshpande et al., 2019), and modifying the
      projection mechanism (Kolouri et al., 2019; Bonet et al., 2023). However, few studies have focused


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      on replacing one-dimensional lines, which play the role of integration domains, with more complex
055   domains such as one-dimensional manifolds (Kolouri et al., 2019), or low-dimensional subspaces
056   (Alvarez-Melis et al., 2018; Bonet et al., 2023; Paty & Cuturi, 2019; Niles-Weed & Rigollet, 2022;
057   Lin et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2021; Muzellec & Cuturi, 2019). In this paper, we concentrate on the
058   latter approach, aiming to discover novel geometrical domains that meet two key criteria: (i) pushing
059   forward of high-dimensional measures onto these domains can be processed in a meaningful manner,
060   and (ii) OT problems on these domains can be efficiently solved, ideally with a closed-form solution.
061
062   Contribution. In summary, our contributions are three-fold:
063
064       1. We introduce the concept of tree systems, which consist of copies of the real line equipped
065
             with additional structures, and study their topology. A key property of tree systems is that they
             form well-defined metric spaces, with metrics being tree metrics. This property is sufficient to
066
             guarantee that OT problems on tree systems admit closed-form solutions.
067
068       2. We define the space of integrable functions and probability measures on a tree system, and
069          introduce a novel transform, called Radon Transform on Systems of Lines. This transform
070
             naturally transforms measures supported in high-dimensional space onto tree systems, and is a
             generalization of the original Radon transform. The injectivity of this variant holds, similar to
071
             other Radon transform variants in the literature.
072
073       3. We propose the Tree-Sliced Wasserstein distance on Systems of Lines (TSW-SL), and analyze
074          its efficiency through the closed-form solution for the OT problem on tree systems, achieving
075
             a similar computational cost as the traditional SW.
076
077   Organization. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 provides necessary
078
      backgrounds of SW distance and Wasserstein distance on tree metric spaces. Section 3 provides a
      brief and intuitive introduction of tree systems and studies its properties, and Section 4 introduces
079
      the Radon Transform on System of Lines. The novel Tree-Sliced Wasserstein distance on Systems
080
      of Lines is proposed in Section 5. Finally, Section 6 contains empirical results for TSW-SL. Formal
081   constructions, theoretical proofs of key results, and additional materials are presented in Appendix.

146   This section provides an intuitive and brief introduction of systems of lines and their additional tree
147   structures. These structures form metric spaces, called tree systems, which serve as a generalization
148   of one-dimensional lines within the framework of the Sliced-Wasserstein distance. We then explore
149   the topological properties and the construction of tree systems. The ideas are illustrated in Figures 1,
150
      2, 3, and a complete formal construction with theoretical proofs are presented in Appendix A.

154

185   3.2   T OPOLOGICAL P ROPERTIES OF T REE S YSTEMS
186
      A tree system L can be intuitively understood as a system of lines that are connected in certain
187
      ways. It naturally forms a topological space by taking disjoint union copies of R and then taking
188   the quotient at intersections of these copies. The disjoint union is straightforward, and the quotient
189   follows the tree structure of L. The topological space resulting from these actions is called the
190   topological space of a tree system L, and is denoted by ΩL . By its construction, ΩL naturally
191   carries a measure induced from the standard measure on each copy of R. This measure is denoted
192   by µL . Notice that, due to the tree structure, a unique path exists between any two points of ΩL .
193   This leads to an important result regarding the metrizability of ΩL .
194   Theorem 3.2 (ΩL is metrizable by a tree metric). Consider dL : ΩL × ΩL ! [0, ∞) defined by:
195
                                       dL (a, b) := µL (Pa,b ) , ∀a, b ∈ ΩL ,                              (6)

      where Pa,b is the unique path between a and b in ΩL . Then dL is a metric on ΩL , which makes
      (ΩL , dL ) a metric space. Moreover, dL is a tree metric, and the topology on ΩL induced by dL is
198
      identical to the topology of ΩL .

203   3.3   C ONSTRUCTION OF T REE S YSTEMS AND S AMPLING P ROCESS
204
205   A tree system can be built inductively by sampling lines, ensuring that each new line intersects one
206   of the previously sampled lines. We introduce a straightforward method to construct a tree system:
207
      start by sampling a line, and at each subsequent step, sample a new line that intersects the previously
      selected line. Specifically, the process is as follows:
208
209   Step 1. Sampling x1 ∼ µ1 for an µ1 ∈ P(Rd ), then θ1 ∼ ν1 for an ν1 ∈ P(Sd−1 ). The pair
210           (x1 , θ1 ) forms the first line;
211
      Step i. At step i, sampling xi = xi−1 + ti · θi−1 where ti ∼ µi for an µi ∈ P(R), then θi ∼ νi
212           for an νi ∈ P(Sd−1 ). The pair (xi , θi ) forms the ith line.
213
214   The tree system produced by this construction has a chain-like tree structure, where the ith line inter-
215   sects the (i + 1)th line. A general approach for sampling tree systems is provided in Appendix A.4.
      In practice, we simply assume all the distributions µ’s and ν’s to be independent, and let:

      6.4    D ENOISING D IFFUSION MODELS
504
505   Finally, we concentrate on denoising diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020),
506   which are among the most complex generative frameworks for image generation. Diffusion models
507   consist of a forward process that gradually adds Gaussian noise to data and a reverse process that
508   learns to denoise the data. The forward process is defined as a Markov chain of T steps, where each
509   step adds noise according to a predefined schedule. The reverse process, parameterized by θ, aims to
510   learn the denoising distribution. Traditionally, these models are trained using maximum likelihood
511   by optimizing the evidence lower bound (ELBO). However, to accelerate generation, denoising
512   diffusion GANs (Xiao et al., 2021) introduce an implicit denoising model and employ adversarial
513
      training. In our work, we build upon the framework in (Nguyen et al., 2024b) and replace the
      Augmented Generalized Mini-batch Energy distance with our novel TSW-SL distance as the kernel
514
      and conducting experiments on the CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky, 2009). For a detailed description
515
      of the model architecture and training loss, we refer readers to Appendix E.4.
516
517   Table 4 demonstrates that our TSW-SL loss function significantly enhances FID performance com-
518   pared to conventional SW. It also outperforms RPSW and IWRPSW while yielding competitive
519
      results that are only marginally behind other state-of-the-art baselines in (Nguyen et al., 2024b).
      It is worth noting that our TSW-SL-DD maintains a competitive training time. This improvement
520
      underscores the efficacy of our approach in generating high-quality samples with improved fidelity.

523   7     C ONCLUSION
524
525   This paper proposes a novel method called Tree-Sliced Wasserstein on Systems of Lines (TSW-SL),
526   replacing the traditional one-dimensional lines in the Sliced Wasserstein (SW) framework with tree
527   systems, providing a more geometrically meaningful space. This key innovation enables the pro-
528
      posed TSW-SL to capture more detailed structural information and geometric relationships within
      the data compared to SW while preserving computational efficiency. We rigorously develop the
529
      theoretical basis for our approach, verifying the essential properties of the Radon Transform and
530
      empirically demonstrating the benefits of TSW-SL across a range of application tasks. As this paper
531   introduces a straightforward alternative by replacing one-dimensional lines in SW with tree systems,
532   our primary comparison is between TSW-SL and the original SW, without anticipating that TSW-
533   SL will surpass more recent SW variants. Future research on adapting recent advance techniques
534   within the SW framework to TSW-SL remains an open area and is anticipated to lead to improved
535   performance for Sliced Optimal Transport overall.

879   A.2    S YSTEM OF L INES WITH T REE S TRUCTURES (T REE S YSTEM )
880
      Consider a finite system of lines L in Rd . Assume that these lines are geometrically distinct, i.e.
881   their images are distinct. Define the graph GL associated with L, where L is the set of nodes in GL ,
882   and two nodes are adjacent if the two corresponding lines intersect each other. Here, saying two
883   lines in Rd intersect means their images have exactly one point in common.
884
      Definition A.3 (Connected system of lines). L is called connected if its associated graph GL is
885   connected.
886
      Remark. Intuitively, each edge of GL represents the intersection of its endpoints. If L is connected,
887
      for every two points that each one lies on some lines in L, one can travel to the other through lines
888   in L.
889
890   From now on, we will only consider the case L is connected. Recall the notion of a spanning tree of
891   a graph G, which is a subgraph of G that contains all nodes of G, and also is a tree.
892   Definition A.4 (Tree system of lines). Let L be a connected system of lines. A spanning tree T of
893   GL is called a tree structure of L. A pair (L, T ) consists of a connected system of lines L and a tree
894   structure T of L is called a tree system of lines.
895   Remark. For short, we usually call a tree system of lines as a tree system. In a tree system (L, T ),
896   images of two lines of L can intersect each other even when they are not adjacent in T .
897
      Let r be an arbitrary line of L. Denote Tr as the tree T rooted at r, and denote the (rooted) tree
898
      system as (L, Tr ) if we want to specify the root.
899
900
      Definition A.5 (Depth of lines in a tree system). Let (L, Tr ) be a tree system. For each m ⩾ 0, a
      line l ∈ L is called a line of depth m if the (unique) path from r to l in T has length m. Denote Lm
901
      as the set of lines of depth m.
902
903   Remark. Note that L0 = {r}. Let T be the maximum length of paths in T start from r, which is
904
      called the depth of the line system. L has a partition as L = L0 ⊔ L1 ⊔ . . . ⊔ LT .
905   For l ∈ L that is not the root, denote pr(l) ∈ L as the parent of l, i.e. the (unique) node on the
906   unique path from l to r that is adjacent to l. Note that, by definition, l and pr(l) intersect each other.
907   We sometimes omit the root when the context is clear.
908   Definition A.6 (Canonical tree system). A tree system (L, T ) is called a canonical tree system if
909   for all l ∈ L that is not the root, the intersection of l and pr(l) is the source xl of l.
910   Remark. In other words, in a canonical tree system, a line that differs from the root will have its
911   source lies on its parent. For the rest of the paper, a tree system (L, T ) will be considered to be a
912   canonical tree system.
