                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014            Optimization problems with norm-bounding constraints appear in various appli-
015            cations, from portfolio optimization to machine learning, feature selection, and
016            beyond. A widely used variant of these problems relaxes the norm-bounding con-
017
               straint through Lagrangian relaxation and moves it to the objective function as
               a form of penalty or regularization term. A challenging class of these models
018
               uses the zero-norm function to induce sparsity in statistical parameter estima-
019
               tion models. Most existing exact solution methods for these problems use ad-
020            ditional binary variables together with artificial bounds on variables to formulate
021            them as a mixed-integer program in a higher dimension, which is then solved
022            by off-the-shelf solvers. Other exact methods utilize specific structural proper-
023            ties of the objective function to solve certain variants of these problems, making
024            them non-generalizable to other problems with different structures. An alterna-
025            tive approach employs nonconvex penalties with desirable statistical properties,
026            which are solved using heuristic or local methods due to the structural complexity
027            of those terms. In this paper, we develop a novel graph-based method to glob-
028
               ally solve optimization problems that contain a generalization of norm-bounding
               constraints. This includes standard ℓp -norms for p ∈ [0, ∞) as well as non-
029
               convex penalty terms, such as SCAD and MCP, as special cases. Our method
030
               uses decision diagrams to build strong convex relaxations for these constraints in
031            the original space of variables without the need to introduce additional auxiliary
032            variables or impose artificial variable bounds. We show that the resulting con-
033            vexification method, when incorporated into a spatial branch-and-cut framework,
034            converges to the global optimal value of the problem under mild conditions. To
035            demonstrate the capabilities of the proposed framework, we conduct preliminary
036            computational experiments on benchmark sparse linear regression problems with
037            challenging nonconvex penalty terms that cannot be modeled or solved by existing
038            global solvers.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043
044   Norm-bounding constraints are often used in optimization problems to improve model stability and
045
      guide the search towards solutions with desirable properties. For example, in machine learning,
      norm-bounding constraints are imposed as a form of regularization to reduce overfitting and induce
046
      sparsity in feature selection models Hastie et al. (2009). Other uses of these constraints include
047
      improving the numerical stability of optimization algorithms Nocedal & Wright (2006), control-
048   ling the complexity of the solution space Bertsekas (1999), and enhancing the interpretability of
049   parameter estimation Zou & Hastie (2005). While norm-bounding constraints defined by ℓp -norm
050   for p ≥ 1 fall within the class of convex programs, making them amenable to solution techniques
051   from numerical optimization Nocedal & Wright (2006) and convex optimization Bertsekas (2015)
052   literature, constraints involving ℓp -norm for p ∈ [0, 1) or their nonconvex proxies belong to the class
053   of mixed-integer nonlinear (nonconvex) programs (MINLPs), posing greater challenges for global
      solution approaches.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      The challenge to solve problems with ℓp -norm for p ∈ (0, 1) stems from the nonconvexity of this
055   function, which necessitates the addition of new constraints and auxiliary variables to decompose the
056   function into smaller terms with simpler structures. These terms are convexified separately through
057   the so-called factorable decomposition, the predominant convexification technique in existing global
058   solvers; see Bonami et al. (2012); Khajavirad et al. (2014) for a detailed account. In contrast, the
059   challenge in solving problems with ℓ0 -norm, also referred to as best-subset selection problem, is due
060   to the discontinuity of the norm function, which necessitates the introduction of new binary variables
061   and (often) artificial bounds on variables to reformulate it as a mixed-integer program (MIP) that can
062   be solved by existing MIP solvers Bertsimas et al. (2016); Dedieu et al. (2021). Other exact meth-
063
      ods for solving these problems are highly tailored to exploit the specific structural properties of the
      objective function, often making them unsuited for problems with different structures Bertsimas &
064
      Parys (2020); Hazimeh et al. (2022); Xie & Deng (2020). One of the most common applications
065
      of the best-subset selection problem is in sparse parameter estimation, where the goal is to limit the
066   number of nonzero parameters Hastie et al. (2015); Pilanci et al. (2015). Other applications of prob-
067   lems with ℓ0 -norm constraints include compressive sensing, metabolic engineering, and portfolio
068   selection, among others; see Jain (2010) and references therein for an exposition on these applica-
069   tions. Perhaps the most challenging class of these problems, from a global optimization perspective,
070   involves nonconvex penalty functions that exhibit desirable statistical properties, such as variable
071   selection consistency and unbiasedness Fan & Li (2001); Zhang & Zhang (2012). The complexity
072   of these problems is partly attributed to the algebraic form of their penalty functions, which are
073   often difficult to model as standard mathematical programs that can be solved by a global solver.
074
      For example, the smoothly clipped absolute deviation (SCAD) Fan & Li (2001) and the minimax
      concave penalty (MCP) Zhang (2010) functions include integration in their definition, an operator
075
      that is inadmissible in existing global solvers such as BARON Tawarmalani & Sahinidis (2005). As
076
      a result, the existing optimization approaches for these problems mainly consist of heuristic and lo-
077   cal methods Mazumder et al. (2011); Zou & Li (2008). Various works in the literature have studied
078   the favorable properties of global solutions of these statistical models, which are often not achiev-
079   able by local methods or heuristic approaches Fletcher et al. (2009); Hazimeh & Mazumder (2020);
080   Wainwright (2009). This advocates for the need to develop global optimization methods that, de-
081   spite being computationally less efficient and scalable compared to heuristic or local methods, can
082   provide deeper insights into the true statistical characteristics of optimal estimators across various
083   models. Consequently, these insights can facilitate the development of new models endowed with
084   more desirable properties; see the discussion in Fan & Li (2001) for an example of the process to
085
      design a new model.
086   In this paper, we introduce a novel global optimization framework for a generalized class of norm-
087   bounding constraints based on the concept of decision diagrams (DDs), which are special-structured
088   graphs that draw out latent variable interactions in the constraints of the MINLP models. We refer
089   the reader to Section 2 for a brief background on DDs, to Bergman et al. (2016) for an introduction
090   to DDs, and to Castro et al. (2022) for a recent survey on DD-based optimization. One of the
091
      most prominent advantages of DD-based solution methods compared to alternative approaches is the
      ability of DDs to model complex constraint forms, such as nonconvex, nonsmooth, black-box, etc.,
092
      while capturing their underlying data structure through a graphical representation. In this work, we
093
      exploit these properties of DDs to design a framework to globally solve optimization problems that
094   include norm-bounding constraints, which encompass a variety of challenging nonconvex structures.
095
096   The main contribution of this paper is the development of a global solution framework through
097
      the lens of DDs, which has the following advantages over existing global optimization tools and
      techniques.
098
099
           (i) Our framework is applicable to a generalized definition of the norm functions that includes
100
               ℓp -norm with p ∈ [0, ∞), as well as nonconvex penalty terms, such as SCAD and MCP, as
101
               special cases.
102
103       (ii) The devised method guarantees convergence to a global optimal solution of the underlying
104            optimization model under mild conditions.
105
106       (iii) The proposed approach provides a unified framework to handle different norm and penalty
107             types, ranging from convex to nonconvex to discontinuous, unlike existing techniques that
                employ a different tailored approach to model and solve each norm and penalty type.

108
            (iv) Our framework models the norm-bounding constraints and solves the associated optimiza-
109              tion problem in the original space of variables without the need to introduce additional
110              auxiliary variables, unlike conventional approaches in the literature and global solvers that
111              require the introduction of new auxiliary variables for ℓp -norms for p ∈ [0, 1).
112
             (v) Our approach can model and solve nonconvex penalty functions that contain irregular oper-
113              ators, such as the integral in SCAD and MCP functions, which are considered intractable in
114              state-of-the-art global solvers, thereby providing the first global solution method for such
115              structures.
116
            (vi) The developed framework does not require artificial large bounds on the variables, which
117              are commonly imposed when modeling ℓ0 -norms as a MIP.
118
           (vii) Our algorithms can be easily incorporated into solution methods for optimization problems
119
                 with a general form of objective function and constraints, whereas the majority of existing
120
                 frameworks designed for ℓ0 -norm or nonconvex penalty terms heavily rely on the problem-
121              specific properties of the objective function and thus are not generalizable to problems that
122              have different objective functions and constraints.
123
          (viii) Our approach can be used to model the regularized variant of the problems where the norm
124              functions are moved to the objective function through Lagrangian relaxation and treated as
125              a weighted penalty.

134   2     BACKGROUND ON DD S
135
136   In this section, we present basic definitions and results relevant to our DD analysis. A DD D is a
137   directed acyclic graph denoted by the triple (U, A, l) where U is a node set, A is an arc set, and
138   l : A → R is an arc label mapping for the graph components. This DD is composed of n ∈ N
139   arc layers A1 , A2 , . . . , An , and n + 1 node layers U1 , U2 , . . . , Un+1 . The node layers U1 and Un+1
140   contain the root r and the terminal t, respectively. In any arc layer j ∈ [n] := {1, 2, . . . , n}, an
141
      arc (u, v) ∈ Aj is directed from the tail node u ∈ Uj to the head node v ∈ Uj+1 . The width of
      D is defined as the maximum number of nodes at any node layer Uj . DDs have been traditionally
142
      used to model a bounded integer set P ⊆ Zn such that each r-t arc-sequence (path) of the form
143
      (a1 , . . . , an ) ∈ A1 × . . . × An encodes a point cx ∈ P where l(aj ) = xj for j ∈ [n], that is cx
144   is an n-dimensional point in P whose j-th coordinate is equal to the label value l(aj ) of arc the aj .
145   For such a DD, we have P = Sol(D), where Sol(D) represents the set of all r-t paths.
146
147
      The graphical property of DDs can be exploited to optimize an objective function over a discrete
      set P. To this end, DD arcs are weighted in such a way that the weight of an r-t path, obtained by
148
      the summation of the weights of its arcs, encoding a solution cx ∈ P equals the objective function
149
      value evaluated at cx. Then, a shortest (resp. longest) r-t path for the underlying minimization
150   (resp. maximization) problem is found through a polynomial-time algorithm to obtain an optimal
151   solution of the underlying integer program.
152
153
      If there is a one-to-one correspondence between the r-t paths of the DD and the discrete set P, we
      say that the DD is exact. It is clear that the construction of an exact DD is computationally prohibitive
154
      due to the exponential growth rate of its size. To address this difficulty, the concept of relaxed and
155
      restricted DDs were developed in the literature to keep the size of DDs manageable. In a relaxed
156   DD, if the number of nodes in a layer exceeds a predetermined width limit, a subset of nodes are
157   merged into one node to reduce the number of nodes in that layer and thereby satisfy the width limit.
158   This node-merging operation is performed in such a way that all feasible solutions of the exact DD
159   are maintained, while some new infeasible solutions might be created during the merging process.
160   Optimization over this relaxed DD provides a dual bound to the optimal solution of the original
161   integer program. In a restricted DD, the collection of all r-t paths of the DD encode a subset of the
      feasible solutions of the exact DD that satisfies the prescribed width limit. Optimization over this


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      restricted DD provides a primal bound to the optimal solution of the original integer program. The
163   restricted and relaxed DDs can be successively refined through a branch-and-bound framework until
164   their primal and dual bounds converge to the optimal value of the integer program.
165
166
      As outlined above, DDs have been traditionally used to model and solve discrete optimization prob-
      lems. Recently, through a series of works Davarnia (2021); Salemi & Davarnia (2022; 2023), the
167
      extension of DD-based optimization to mixed-integer programs was proposed together with applica-
168
      tions in new domains, from energy systems to transportation, that include a mixture of discrete and
169   continuous variables. In this paper, we make use of some of the methods developed in those works
170   to build a global solution framework for optimization problems with norm-bounding constraints.

      useful when DDs are employed to build convex relaxations for problems that do not have initial

      Remark 1. The size of the DD obtained from Algorithm 1 could grow exponentially when the num-
325   ber of variables and sub-intervals in the domain partitions increases. To control this growth rate,
326   there are two common approaches. The first approach involves controlling the size of the DD by
327   reducing the number of sub-intervals in the domain partitions of variables at certain layers. For
328   example, assume that the number of nodes at each layer of the DD grows through the top-down
329   construction procedure of Algorithm 1 until it reaches the imposed width limit W at layer k. Then,
330   by setting the number of sub-intervals for the next layer k + 1 to one (i.e., |Lk+1 | = 1), the al-
331   gorithm guarantees that the number of nodes at layer k + 1 will not exceed W . The second ap-
332   proach involves controlling the size of the DD by creating a “relaxed DD” through merging nodes
333
      at layers that exceed the width limit W . In this process, multiple nodes {v1 , v2 , . . . , vk }, for some
      k ∈ N, in a layer are merged into a single node ṽ in such a way that all feasible paths of the
334
      original DD are maintained. For the DD constructed through Algorithm 1, choosing the state value

      s(ṽ) = minj=1,...,k {s(vj )} provides such a guarantee for all feasible paths of the original DD to

351   In global optimization of MINLPs, a divide-and-conquer strategy, such as spatial branch-and-bound,
352   is employed to achieve convergence to the global optimal value of the problem. The spatial branch-
353
      and-bound strategy reduces the variables’ domain through successive partitioning of the original
      box domains of the variables. These partitions are often rectangular in shape, dividing the variables’
354
      domain into smaller hyper-rectangles as a result of branching. For each such partition, a convex
355
      relaxation is constructed to calculate a dual bound. Throughout this process, the dual bounds are
356   updated as tighter relaxations are obtained, until they converge to a specified vicinity of the global
357   optimal value of the problem. To prove such converge results, one needs to show that the convexifi-
358   cation method employed at each domain partition converges (in the Hausdorff sense) to the convex
359   hull of the feasible region restricted to that partition; see Belotti et al. (2009); Ryoo & Sahinidis
360   (1996); Tawarmalani & Sahinidis (2004) for a detailed account of spatial branch-and-bound meth-
361   ods for MINLPs.

365   cut (SB&C). In this section, we show that the convex hull of the solution set of the DDs obtained

468   constructed through our convexification technique converges to the feasible region of the under-
469   lying norm-bounding constraint during the SB&C process. If this constraint is embedded into an
470   optimization problem whose other constraints also have a convexification method with such conver-
471   gence results, it can guarantee convergence to the global optimal value of the optimization problem
472   through the incorporation of SB&C.
473
      We conclude this section with two remarks about the results of Proposition 5. First, although the
474   above convergence results are obtained for DDs with a unit width W = 1, they can be easily
475   extended to cases with larger widths using the following observation. The solution set of a DD can
476   be represented by a finite union of the solution sets of DDs of width 1 obtained from decomposing
477   node sequences of the original DD. By considering the collection of all such decomposed DDs of
478   unit width, we can use the result of Proposition 5 to show convergence to the feasible region of the
479   underlying constraint. In practice, increasing the width limit of a DD often leads to stronger DDs
480   with tighter convex relaxations. This, in turn, accelerates the bound improvement rate, helping to
481   achieve the desired convergence results faster.
482   Second, even though the convergence results of Proposition 5 are proven for a bounded domain
483   of variables, the SB&C can still be implemented for bounded optimization problems that contain
484   unbounded variables. In this case, as is common in spatial branch-and-bound solution methods, the
485   role of a primal bound becomes critical for pruning nodes that contain very large (or infinity) variable
      bounds, which result in large dual bounds. This is particularly evident in the statistical models that

740        values that lie in the same interval of the state range. We set the number of sub-intervals |Li | up
741
           to 2500 for each DD layer i. To generate cutting planes for the outer approximation approach, we
           use the subgradent method of Algorithm 2 with the stopping criterion equal to 50 iterations. The
742
           maximum number of iterations for applying the subgradient method before invoking the branching
743
           procedure to create new children nodes is 100. The branching process selects the variable whose
744        optimal value in the outer approximation model of the current node is closest to the middle point of
745        its domain interval, and then the branching occurs at that value. Throughout the process, a primal
746        bound is updated by constructing a feasible solution of the problem through plugging the current
747        optimal solution of the outer approximation at each node into the objective function of equation 1.
748        This primal bound is used to prune nodes with worse dual bounds than the current primal bound. The
749        stopping criterion for our algorithm is reaching a relative optimality gap of 5% which is calculated
750        as (primal bound − dual bound) / dual bound.
