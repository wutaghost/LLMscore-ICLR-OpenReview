011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in multivariate time series forecasting have been pro-
014            pelled by Linear-based, Transformer-based, and Convolution-based models, with
015
               Transformer-based architectures gaining prominence for their efficacy in tempo-
               ral and cross-channel mixing. More recently, Mamba, a state space model, has
016
               emerged with robust sequence and feature mixing capabilities. However, the suit-
017
               ability of the vanilla Mamba design for time series forecasting remains an open
018            question, particularly due to its inadequate handling of cross-channel dependen-
019            cies. Capturing cross-channel dependencies is critical in enhancing the perfor-
020            mance of multivariate time series prediction. Recent findings show that self-
021            attention excels in capturing cross-channel dependencies, whereas other simpler
022            mechanisms, such as MLP, may degrade model performance. This is counterintu-
023            itive, as MLP, being a learnable architecture, should theoretically capture both cor-
024            relations and irrelevances, potentially leading to neutral or improved performance.
025            Diving into the self-attention mechanism, we attribute the observed degradation in
026
               MLP performance to its lack of data dependence and global receptive field, which
               result in MLP’s lack of generalization ability. Considering the powerful sequence
027
               modeling capabilities of Mamba and the high efficiency of MLP, the combination
028
               of the two is an effective strategy for solving multivariate time series prediction.
029            Based on the above insights, we introduce a refined Mamba variant tailored for
030            time series forecasting. Our proposed model, CMamba, incorporates a modified
031            Mamba (M-Mamba) module for temporal dependencies modeling, a global data-
032            dependent MLP (GDD-MLP) to effectively capture cross-channel dependencies,
033            and a Channel Mixup mechanism to mitigate overfitting. Comprehensive exper-
034            iments conducted on seven real-world datasets demonstrate the efficacy of our
035            model in improving forecasting performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Multivariate (or Multichannel) time series forecasting (MTSF) plays a crucial role in diverse appli-
040
      cations, such as weather prediction (Chen et al., 2023), traffic management (Liu et al., 2023b;c),
041
      economics (Xu & Cohen, 2018), and event prediction (Xue et al., 2023). MTSF aims to predict
042   future values of temporal variations based on historical observations. Given its practical impor-
043   tance, numerous deep learning models have been developed in recent years, notably including,
044   Linear-based (Zeng et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Das et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023), Transformer-
045   based (Zhou et al., 2022; Zhang & Yan, 2022; Wu et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a),
046   and Convolution-based (Liu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022; Luo & Wang, 2024)
047   models, which have demonstrated significant advancements.
048   Among these, Transformer-based models are particularly distinguished by their capacity to sepa-
049   rately mix temporal and channel embeddings using mechanisms such as MLP or self-attention. The
050   recently introduced Mamba model (Gu & Dao, 2023), which operates within a state space frame-
051   work, exhibits notable sequence and feature mixing capabilities. Mamba has shown progress not
052   only in natural language processing but also in other domains, including computer vision (Liu et al.,
053   2024; Zhu et al., 2024) and graph-based applications (Wang et al., 2024a). Nevertheless, recent
      studies on Mamba in time series (Patro & Agneeswaran, 2024; Wang et al., 2024d) mostly focus on

      2.1   S TATE S PACE M ODELS FOR S EQUENCE M ODELING
116
117   Traditional state space models (SSMs), such as hidden Markov models and recurrent neural net-
118   works (RNNs), process sequences by storing messages in their hidden states and using these states
119   along with the current input to update the output. This recurrent mechanism limits their training ef-
120   ficiency and leads to problems like vanishing and exploding gradients (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber,
121   1997). Recently, several SSMs with linear-time complexity have been proposed, including S4 (Gu
122   et al., 2021), H3 (Fu et al., 2022), SpaceTime (Zhang et al., 2023), and Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023).
123   Among these, Mamba further enhances S4 by introducing a data-dependent selection mechanism
124   that balances short-term and long-term dependencies. Mamba has demonstrated powerful long-
125
      sequence modeling capabilities and has been successfully extended to the visual (Liu et al., 2024;
      Zhu et al., 2024) and graph domains (Wang et al., 2024a).
126
127   In the time series domain, many recent works (Patro & Agneeswaran, 2024; Wang et al., 2024d) have
128   focused on aligning the input format of time series with other fields so that the original Mamba ar-
129   chitecture can be migrated. Specifically, S-Mamba (Wang et al., 2024d) modifies iTransformer (Liu
130   et al., 2023a) by replacing its self-attention layer with Mamba, thereby modeling inter-channel rela-
131
      tionships by treating different channels as sequential data. Time-SSM (Hu et al., 2024) provides an
      in-depth examination of various SSM kernel variants within the Mamba framework for time series
132
      analysis. Bi-Mamba+ (Liang et al., 2024) explores the impact of sequence modeling direction and
133
      proposes a bidirectional Mamba architecture for multivariate time series forecasting. Despite these
134   advancements, existing works overlook the critical evaluation of whether the internal components
135   of Mamba are inherently well-suited for time series data. Moreover, while addressing Mamba’s lim-
136   ited capacity for channel modeling, existing strategies are too direct (e.g., S-Mamba, which models
137   cross-channel dependencies in a sequential manner) and introduce large computational overhead. In
138   this paper, we explore the practicality of Mamba components and propose a more lightweight and
139   efficient strategy to model cross-channel dependencies.
140
141   2.2   C HANNEL S TRATEGIES IN MTSF
142
143   Channel strategies are fundamental in determining how to handle relationships between variables
144
      in multivariate time series forecasting (MTSF). Broadly, there are two primary approaches: the
      Channel-Independent (CI) strategy, which disregards cross-channel dependencies, and the Channel-
145
      Dependent (CD) strategy, which integrates channels according to specific mechanisms. Each strat-
146
      egy has its respective strengths and weaknesses. CD methods offer greater representational capacity
147   but tend to be less robust when confronted with distributional shifts in time series data, while CI
148   methods sacrifice capacity for more stable, robust predictions (Han et al., 2023).
149
150
      A significant number of state-of-the-art models adhere to the CI strategy. These models (Zeng
      et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023) treat multivariate time series as a collection of
151
      independent univariate time series and simply treat different channels as different training samples.
152
      However, recent work has demonstrated the efficacy of explicitly capturing multivariate correlations
153   through mechanisms such as self-attention (Liu et al., 2023a) and convolution (Luo & Wang, 2024).
154   These methods have achieved strong empirical results, underscoring the importance of cross-channel
155   dependency modeling in MTSF. Despite these advances, there remains a need for more effective and
156   efficient mechanisms to capture and model cross-channel dependencies.
157
158   2.3   M IXUP FOR G ENERALIZABILITY
159
160   Mixup is an effective data augmentation technique widely used in vision (Zhang et al., 2017; Yun
161   et al., 2019; Verma et al., 2019), natural language processing (Guo et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2020),
      and more recently, time series analysis (Zhou et al., 2023; Ansari et al., 2024). The vanilla mixup


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      technique randomly mixes two input data samples via linear interpolation. Its variants extend this
163   by mixing either input samples or hidden embeddings to gain better generalization. In multivariate
164   time series, each sample contains multiple time series. Hence, rather than mixing two samples, our
165   proposed Channel Mixup mixes the time series of the same sample. This strategy not only enhances
166   the generalization of models but also facilitates the CD approach.

      3.1   M ULTIVARIATE T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
171
172   In multivariate time series forecasting, we are given a historical time series X = {x1 , ..., xL } ∈
173   RL×V with a look-back window L and the number of channels V . The objective is to predict the
174   T future values Y = {xL+1 , ..., xL+T } ∈ RT ×V . In the following sections, we denote Xt,: as
175   the value of all channels at time step t, and X:,v as the entire sequence of the channel indexed by
176   v. The same annotation is also applied to Y. In this paper, we focus on the long-term time series
177   forecasting task, where the prediction length is greater than or equal to 96.
178

245   The CMamba block consists of two key components: the M-Mamba module and the GDD-MLP

248   4.1.1        M-M AMBA
249
250   Mamba has demonstrated significant potential in NLP (Gu & Dao, 2023), CV (Zhu et al., 2024; Liu
251   et al., 2024), and stock prediction (Shi, 2024). In these fields, the semantic consistency of features
252
      allows elements like words, image patches, or financial indicators to be treated as tokens. In contrast,
      in multivariate time series, different channels often represent disparate physical quantities (Liu et al.,
253
      2023a), making it unsuitable to treat channels at the same time point as a token. While a single time
254
      step of each channel lacks semantic meaning, patching (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Nie et al., 2022)
255   aggregates time points into subseries-level patches, enriching the semantic information and local
256   receptive fields of tokens. Hence, we divide the input time series into patches to serve as the input
257   of our model.
258

284   4.1.2    GDD-MLP
285
      To model cross-channel dependencies as simply and effectively as possible, we propose the global
286
      data-dependent MLP (GDD-MLP) module. Fig. 2 (b) and (d) illustrate its structure and pipeline.
287
      For the patch-wise multivariate time series embeddings generated after the lth M-Mamba module,
288   denoted as Hl ∈ RV ×N ×E , the data-dependent weight and bias of GDD-MLP could be formulated
289   as:
290                             Weightl = sigmoid(MLP1 (Pooling(Hl ))),
                                                                                                   (5)
291                                 Biasl = sigmoid(MLP2 (Pooling(Hl ))).
292
      Here, Pooling is applied along the embedding dimension, generating descriptors Fl ∈ RV ×N that
293   encapsulate the overall characteristics of each patch for every channel. To improve the extraction
294   of global information for each channel, we employ both Average Pooling and Max Pooling. The
295   average and max descriptors share the same MLP, whose outputs are added up to produce the final
296   channel representations in the latent space. The weight and bias are then applied to the input features
297   to embed cross-channel dependencies:
298                                       H′l = Weightl ⊙ Hl + Biasl .                                       (6)

300   4.2     C HANNEL M IXUP
301
302   Previous mixup strategies (Zhang et al., 2017) generate new training samples through linear inter-
303   polation between two existing samples. For two feature-target pairs (xi , yi ) and (xj , yj ) randomly
304   drawn from the training set, the process is described as:
305                                           x̃ = λxi + (1 − λ)xj ,
306                                                                                                          (7)
                                              ỹ = λyi + (1 − λ)yj ,
307
308
      where (x̃, ỹ) is the synthesized virtual sample, and λ ∈ [0, 1] is a mixing coefficient. In the context
      of multivariate time series, directly applying this mixup approach can yield suboptimal results and
309
      even degrade model performance (Zhou et al., 2023), as mixing samples from distinct time intervals
310
      may disrupt temporal features such as periodicity. In contrast, different channels within a multivari-
311   ate time series often exhibit similar temporal characteristics, which explains the effectiveness of the
312   CI strategy (Han et al., 2023). Mixing different channels could introduce new variables while pre-
313   serving their shared temporal features. Considering that the CD strategy tends to cause overfitting
314   due to its lack of robustness to distributionally drifted time series (Han et al., 2023), training with
315   unseen channels should mitigate this issue. Generally, the Channel Mixup could be formulated as
316   follows:
317
                                      X′ = X:,i + λX:,j , i, j = 0, ..., V − 1,
                                                                                                           (8)
318                                   Y′ = Y:,i + λY:,j , i, j = 0, ..., V − 1,
319   where X′ ∈ RL×1 and Y′ ∈ RT ×1 are hybrid channels resulting from the linear combination of
320   channel i and channel j. λ ∼ N (0, σ 2 ) is the linear combination coefficient with σ as the standard
321   deviation. We use a normal distribution with a mean of 0, ensuring that the overall characteristics
322   of each channel remain unchanged. In practice, as shown in Alg. 1, we mix the channels of each
323   sample and replace the original sample with the constructed virtual sample, where randperm(V )
      generates a randomly arranged array of 0 ∼ V − 1.

      6    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We propose CMamba, a novel state space model for multivariate time series forecasting. To bal-
533
      ance cross-time and cross-channel dependencies, CMamba consists of three key components: a M-
534   Mamba module that facilitates Mamba for cross-time dependencies modeling, a GDD-MLP mod-
535   ule that captures cross-channel dependencies, and a Channel Mixup training strategy that enhances
536   generalization and facilitates the CD approach. Extensive experiments demonstrate that CMamba
537   achieves state-of-the-art performance on seven real-world datasets. Notably, the GDD-MLP and
538   Channel Mixup modules could be seamlessly inserted into other models with minimal cost, show-
539   casing remarkable framework versatility. In the future, we aim to explore more effective techniques
      to capture cross-time and cross-channel dependencies.

853   In the main text, we assess the effectiveness of M-Mamba on the Weather dataset. Here, we present

      M-Mamba achieves performance gains in 58 out of 70 scenarios, with improvements becoming in-

857

861   attention to achieve this, while ModernTCN leverages large convolution kernels. By removing
862   modules emphasizing local dependencies, we enable Mamba to prioritize global dependency cap-
863   ture, as evidenced by the significant improvements in experimental results. These findings validate
      the effectiveness of our modifications in enhancing Mamba’s capacity for long-term forecasting.

967   The choice of the loss function plays a pivotal role in both the optimization process and the conver-
968   gence behavior of time series forecasting models. While many prior models predominantly employ
969   L2 loss, also known as Mean Squared Error (MSE), our proposed model adopts L1 loss, also re-
970   ferred to as Mean Absolute Error (MAE). The rationale behind this choice stems from empirical
971   observations across various datasets, where the mean absolute error is typically small. In such cases,
      the gradients produced by L2 loss tend to be small, which can impede the model’s ability to escape

973    and L2 loss is known to be more sensitive to these outliers, which can lead to instability during
974    training. In contrast, L1 loss is more robust to outliers, promoting greater stability in model training
975    and convergence.

1016   In this work, we mainly focus on the multivariate time series forecasting task with endogenous vari-
1017
       ables, meaning that the values we aim to predict and the values treated as features only differ in
       terms of time steps. However, real-world scenarios often involve the influence of exogenous vari-
1018
       ables on the variables we seek to predict, a topic extensively discussed in prior research (Wang
1019
       et al., 2024c). In addition, the experimental results show that our model exhibits significant im-
1020   provements on some datasets with large-scale channels, such as Weather and Electricity. However,
1021   the improvements in MAE are relatively limited on the Traffic dataset, which contains 862 channels.
1022   This discrepancy could be attributed to the pronounced periodicity observed in traffic data compared
1023   to other domains. These periodic patterns are highly time-dependent, causing different channels to
1024   exhibit similar characteristics and obscuring their physical interconnections. Therefore, incorporat-
1025   ing external variables and utilizing prior knowledge about the relationships between channels, such
       as the connectivity of traffic roads, might further enhance the prediction accuracy.

1158
       in the original paper, there will be missing results on some of the datasets we use. Among all these

1159
       top 1 in 49 out of 70 settings and ranks top 2 in 59 settings, while the runner-up, Bi-Mamba+ ranks
1160
       top 1 in only 12 settings and top 2 in 39 settings. The results demonstrate the superiority of our
