011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The accurate identification of subcellular structures is crucial for understanding
014            cellular functions. However, due to the varied morphology of different cells,
015            conventional segmentation methods typically depend on a substantial collection
016
               of accurately labeled images of cell structures. The creation of such precise
               labels is often time-consuming and labor-intensive. To address this issue, we
017
               introduce an efficient, self-supervised method for segmenting subcellular struc-
018
               tures, named HiS4MAE (High-efficiency Segmentation of Subcellular Structure
019            via Self-distillated Masked Autoencoder). Leveraging an enhanced masked au-
020            toencoder (MAE), we train the encoder using the masked image modeling (MIM)
021            framework, followed by clustering the encoded high-dimensional features to
022            achieve pixel-level segmentation of structures. We employ a self-distillation tech-
023            nique to accelerate the model’s training process and propose an inference method
024            that is less time-consuming. We also introduce a discrete codebook to assist the
025            self-distillation process, enhancing the model’s stability during training. When
026            applied to a publicly available volumetric electron microscopy (VEM) dataset of
027
               primary mouse pancreatic islet β cells, HiS4MAE not only surpasses the state-of-
               the-art technique but also significantly reduces the time required for both training
028
               and inference.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Biologists use microscopes to understand cells. In this process, segmenting the various compo-
034
      nents within cells (such as nuclei, mitochondria, and Golgi apparatus) from microscopy images is
035
      crucial. This technique is known as subcellular structure segmentation. Precisely segmenting sub-
036   cellular structures at the pixel level helps researchers understand cellular functions and simulate
037   complex biological processes within cells. With the continuous advancement of microscopy imag-
038   ing technology, acquiring images is no longer a bottleneck. Techniques such as volumetric electron
039   microscopy and confocal laser scanning microscopy (Peddie et al., 2022; Amos & White, 2003) can
040   now capture hundreds of gigabytes to terabytes of data at speeds ranging from several frames to
041   hundreds of frames per second. This allows researchers to easily observe the fine structures within
042   cells, but how to efficiently utilize these massive amounts of images is a problem worth considering.
043   Some supervised image segmentation methods, such as those based on the convolutional neural
044   network or the transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), have achieved remarkable results. However,
045   these methods often require a large number of precisely annotated images for training to achieve
046   satisfactory performance. Annotating different types of images requires domain experts to invest
047   significant time and effort. In reality, it is impractical for a few experts to manually annotate hun-
048   dreds of gigabytes to terabytes of images, which greatly limits the utilization of these images by
049   researchers.
050   Consequently, self-supervised or unsupervised methods have gained the attention of researchers.
051   Notably, the same organelles tend to exhibit similar textural features in images. Research (Hsu
052   et al., 2021) has shown that by using representation learning to distinguish these textures, different
053   organelle regions can be segmented. Han et al. used a variational autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma &
      Welling, 2013) to encode image patches, mapping textured image patches to a high-dimensional


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      space for clustering, achieving relatively good results and providing a new approach for self-
055   supervised segmentation (Han et al., 2022).
056
057   This method of encoding image patches is reminiscent of MAE, a masked image modeling (MIM)
058
      method that trains the model to recover masked image patches, thereby learning deep features of
      the images. Xie et al. proposed a self-supervised segmentation method (Xie et al., 2023) based on
059
      MAE, using a technique called cover-stride to expand encoded features into the latent space and
060
      then performing clustering in the feature space to achieve pixel-level segmentation. This method is
061   effective but still has some issues. The performance of K-means clustering depends on the selection
062   of initial cluster points, and different cluster points can result in significantly different clustering
063   outcomes. Additionally, while cover-stride is an effective method, its inference requires multiple
064   model invocations, resulting in considerable time consumption. Chen et al. indicate that a self-
065   distillation architecture can significantly increase the training efficiency of MIM methods. They
066   propose an architecture named the self-distillated masked autoencoder (SdAE) (Chen et al., 2022).
067   This architecture surpasses the performance of MAE after 1600 training epochs with only 300 train-
068   ing epochs. However, this method’s pretext task differs from MAE, leading to unstable performance
069
      during training and some degree of degradation.
070   In this work, we propose a novel approach to address these issues. We hypothesize that MAE
071   outperforms VAE because the MIM framework allows the model to learn more high-level features.
072   However, simple decoding methods like cover-stride can make it difficult to cluster overly abstract
073   features, leading to segmentation failure. The key is to balance these factors. We incorporated
074   the self-distillation architecture from SdAE but retained the MAE pretext task of reconstructing
075
      pixels. Additionally, we used a discrete codebook to assist the model’s self-distillation, preventing
      degradation during training. Our subsequent experiments demonstrated the effectiveness of this
076
      approach. To eliminate the impact of different initial cluster points on segmentation results, we
077
      selected fixed cluster centers. Furthermore, we developed a method called expand-stride to reduce
078   the time cost of inference. Experiments have shown that our method is highly effective. In summary,
079   our contributions include:
080
081          • We introduce self-distillation to enhance the performance of the encoder, improving seg-
082            mentation effects while reducing training costs. Our method only requires training for 700
083            epochs to surpass the effects achieved by the original method after 2800 epochs. At the
084
               same time, it increases the overall segmentation effect by more than 13%.
085          • To our knowledge, we are the first to use the discrete codebook to assist in model self-
086            distillation. This method effectively prevents model degradation during the training process
087            and further enhances the model’s efficiency in utilizing the dataset.
088          • We propose an inference method named expand-stride, which significantly reduces the time
089            complexity during inference compared to the cover-stride method in the state-of-the-art
090            approach.

094   2.1   A PPLICATION OF DEEP LEARNING TO MICROSCOPIC IMAGE
095
096   With the advancement of deep learning, its impact on the field of microscopic imaging has become
097   increasingly significant. Numerous pivotal models proposed in the realm of deep learning have been
098   extensively applied in the domain of biological imaging. Since its inception, U-net (Ronneberger
099
      et al., 2015) has emerged as a vital paradigm in the field of biological imaging. Works such as
      RepMode (Zhou et al., 2023) and Sparse SSP (Zheng et al., 2024) have built upon U-net to achieve
100
      precise predictions of subcellular structures in microscopic images, yet similar approaches generally
101
      necessitate authentic and accurate labels for training. For unsupervised or self-supervised methods,
102   VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017), VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013), and ViT (Dosovitskiy et al.,
103   2021) have also played crucial roles. A viable paradigm involves training a model with some pre-
104   text task, and subsequently using only the encoder to process images, followed by clustering of
105   the encoded features to yield results for downstream tasks. Han et al. leverage VAE and metric
106   learning to fit the distribution of image patches in latent space, ultimately obtaining segmentation
107   outcomes through K-means. Similar methodologies are not confined to segmentation tasks; for in-
      stance, cytoself (Kobayashi et al., 2022), based on VQ-VAE, also accomplishes protein localization


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      analysis and clustering at the subcellular level through the pretext task of image reconstruction. This
109   demonstrates the formidable feature extraction capabilities of neural networks and substantiates the
110   immense potential of self-supervised approaches in the field of microscopic imaging.

112
      2.2   SELF - SUPERVISED LEARNING AND MASKED IMAGE MODELING
113
114
      Han et al. contend that this paradigm is more effective when the global context of the image is
115   not critical for downstream tasks. In contrast, the researchers behind MAESTER (Xie et al., 2023)
116   propose an alternative approach. Rather than employing convolutional neural networks as encoders,
117   they transitioned to a masked autoencoder (MAE) based on transformer architecture, resulting in
118   superior performance. Transformers are widely regarded as more adept at capturing long-range
119   global dependencies compared to CNNs. Consequently, they posit that this ability to capture global
120   dependencies facilitates the segmentation of subcellular structures.
121   Numerous studies, such as CAE (Chen et al., 2024) and SdAE (Chen et al., 2022), focus on opti-
122   mizing the encoder of MAE. CAE introduces a regressor with cross-attention to further decouple
123   the encoder and decoder, enhancing the performance of both. However, this also results in more
124   abstract features post-encoding, which are not conducive to subsequent segmentation tasks. SdAE,
125   building upon the foundation of MAE, incorporates an encoder without gradients as a teacher branch
126   and modifies the pretext task of MAE, transforming the decoding target into a vector with the same
127   dimensionality as the latent space. Experimental results indicate that this approach indeed improves
128   the training efficiency of the encoder, but in terms of the downstream task of segmentation, the
129
      model’s performance post-clustering after changing the pretext task is not satisfactory.
130
131   2.3   D ISCRETE CODEBOOK
132
133   The mechanism of the codebook has been employed since the advent of VQ-VAE, encoding im-
134
      ages into a discrete matrix to reduce the time complexity of generating images. Such a codebook
      effectively serves as a cluster center during the training process. The subsequent BEiT (Bao et al.,
135
      2021) also introduced this mechanism, utilizing dVAE to generate discrete encodings. Recent re-
136
      searchers (Du et al., 2024) theoretically substantiate the enhancement of the MIM framework by the
137   codebook mechanism. Choosing a codebook that is more aligned with data classes can improve the
138   model’s performance.

143   3.1   S UBCELLULAR STRUCTURE SEGMENTATION AND M ASKED I MAGE MODELING
144
145   Subcellular structure segmentation within microscopic images is a specialized task that necessitates
146   the delineation of various cellular organelles. To formalize this, let X denote a collection of 3-
147
      dimensional microscopic cell images, each with dimensions D × H × W . Correspondingly, Y
      represents a set of fully segmented subcellular structure images, also dimensioned D × H × W ,
148
      where each pixel in Y is labeled from 0 to T − 1, with T representing the total number of distinct
149
      cell types to be segmented. The objective is to develop a mapping function f , implemented via a
150   neural network, that accurately transforms X into Y , i.e., f : X → Y .
151
152   Drawing inspiration from the work of MAESTER (Xie et al., 2023), we adopt the masked image
153
      modeling (MIM) framework to address this challenge. Given that the dimensions of the images in
      X and Y are substantial, on the order of 103 in each spatial dimension, it is impractical to process
154
      the entire image in one pass through the network. Consequently, we initiate the process by randomly
155
      sampling a 2D slice Xi from X. Following the terminology introduced by MAESTER, we refer to
156   the shape of Xi as the field of view (FOV), with our FOV matching that of Xie et al., specifically
157   80 × 80 pixels.
158
159
      In the context of MIM approaches, the process involves segmenting the 2D image Xi into n patches
      of uniform size, where n is the count of perfect squares. Subsequently, a set of binary masks m, with

      3.2   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
210
211   Since the inception of convolutional neural networks (CNN), they have achieved remarkable suc-
212   cess across various domains of deep learning, establishing themselves as a dominant approach in
213   computer vision. However, with the advent of Transformer in the field, there is a growing consen-
214   sus among researchers that the attention mechanism employed by transformers excels at capturing
215   global image information compared to CNN. Our methodology leverages MAE and integrates atten-
      tion blocks within an encoder-decoder framework. Empirical evidence from numerous experiments


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      suggests that asymmetric encoder-decoder configurations enhance the model’s focus on the encod-
217   ing process. Building upon the ViT-B foundation, MAESTER has further optimized the parameter
218   count. Our baseline model architecture settings are derived from MAESTER.
219
220
      Motivated by the SdAE (Chen et al., 2022), we have incorporated a self-distillation structure into our
      model, which is anticipated to significantly curtail the training duration. The architecture comprises
221
      two encoders: a teacher branch and a student branch, both sharing an identical model architecture.
222
      During training, the teacher network refrains from updating gradients, opting instead for an exponen-
223   tial moving average (EMA) approach to parameter updates. It is noteworthy that SdAE’s primary
224   objective is not the reconstruction of masked image patches but rather the alignment of features
225   within the latent space. This contradicts our initial hypothesis that the reconstruction of the image
226   itself aids in the task of subcellular structure segmentation. Our experiments have also demonstrated
227   that it is challenging to achieve subcellular structure segmentation of microscopic cell images by
228   training encoders solely through the alignment of features in the latent space. Consequently, our
229   model retains the the same pretext task as utilized in MAE. Let the reconstructed image be X̂i , we
230   show the loss function of the reconstruction as follow:
231                                         Lrecon = M SE(Xi , X̂i )                                     (2)
232
233   Our initial idea was to augment the model of SdAE with an additional decoder for image reconstruc-
234   tion. In practical experimentation, we observed a non-linear correlation between model reconstruc-
235   tion efficacy and segmentation performance. In essence, the model’s proficiency in reconstructing
236   the original image does not necessarily translate to effective clustering of high-dimensional features.
237   As training progresses, while the reconstruction loss remains relatively stable, the segmentation per-
238   formance of the encoder tends to deteriorate. We will elaborate on this point in the subsequent
239   experimental section. To address this issue, we have introduced a discrete codebook to facilitate
240   model self-distillation. The final architecture of our model is depicted in Figure 1. The codebook
241
      is an embedding layer of shape k × embed dim. We define the feature encoded by the encoder as
      Z and the vector set within the codebook as Zq . Our strategy aims to minimize the discrepancy be-
242
      tween Zq and the teacher branch’s encoding Ztea , subsequently leveraging Zq to bolster the student
243
      branch’s training. To this end, we employ MSE to constrain Zq to Ztea and cosine similarity to align
244   Zq with Zstu , expressed as:
245                                           Ltea = M SE(Z, Zq )                                         (3)
246

      3.3   M ODEL I NFERENCE
264
265   After the model training is completed, we only utilize the student branch of the model’s encoders for
266   inference. During the inference process, we no longer perform mask operations on the images; in-
267   stead, we feed all the slices Xi into the encoder, encoding Xi into a matrix Zi of size n×embed dim.
268   As we know, the semantics of pixels within the same patch are roughly similar. Consequently, we
269   employ a linear function to replace the nonlinear function to fit the value of each pixel within the
      patch, thereby obtaining features Zi that match the size of FOV. Subsequently, we infer the entire


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      image set X with a fixed stride S. The time complexity of our inference approach is equivalent
271   to only S12 of that of MAESTER. Finally, we achieve pixel-level subcellular structure segmentation
272   through clustering. We employ K-means (Lloyd, 1982) for clustering, with cluster centers calculated
273   from a subset sampled from X, the size of the subset being one-thousandth of X.

      4.1   DATASET
278
279   Our dataset originates from an open-source data platform named OpenOrganelle (Heinrich et al.,
280   2021). This data portal showcases numerous high-resolution cellular images captured by focused
281   ion beam scanning electron microscopy (FIB-SEM). We select a dataset referred to as ’BetaSeg’ in
282   OpenOrganelle (Müller et al., 2021; Heinrich et al., 2021) for the training and testing of our model.
283   This dataset comprises multiple high-resolution microscopic images of primary mouse pancreatic
284   islet β cells, with various subcellular structures annotated within the cells. These annotations are
285   generated collaboratively by human labor and neural networks. A variety of subcellular structures
286   are labeled, including centrioles, nucleus, plasma membrane, microtubules, Golgi apparatus, gran-
287
      ules, and mitochondria. The dataset is divided into two groups based on whether the cells are treated
      with a high dose of glucose. For ease of comparison with previous work, we utilize only the group
288
      that involved high-dose glucose treatment. This subset includes four high-resolution single-cell 3D
289
      images, namely high c1, high c2, high c3, and high c4. Each 3D microscopic image has a Z-axis
290   depth of 1000. We employ high c1, high c2, and high c3 for model training, while high c4 is used
291   to evaluate the segmentation outcomes of the model.

520   5   C ONCLUSION
521
522
      In this work, we propose a method named HiS4MAE for subcellular structure segmentation. Our
523   approach can fully leverage a vast array of microscopic cell images without the need for large-scale
524   manual annotation of images. Compared to existing self-supervised methods, our technique exhibits
525   greater stability and requires reduced training and inference times. Our proposed method not only
526   outperforms existing state-of-the-art self-supervised approaches but also significantly narrows the
527   performance gap with supervised methods that leverage ground truth across different categories.
528
      It is worth further contemplation as to why merely employing a MIM approach can enable the
529   model’s encoder to achieve an effect akin to clustering on microscopic cell images. What deeper
530   biological and mathematical principles might underlie this phenomenon? We aspire for our method
531   and work to enhance researchers’ productivity and assist them in gaining a deeper comprehension
532   of the internal structures of cells.
