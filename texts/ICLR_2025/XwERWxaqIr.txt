009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Tremendous investments have been made towards the commodification of
012
               diffusion models for generation of diverse media. Their mass-market adoption is
013            however still hobbled by the intense hardware resource requirements of diffusion
014            model inference. Model quantization strategies tailored specifically towards dif-
015            fusion models have seen considerable success in easing this burden, yet without
016            exception have explored only the Uniform Scalar Quantization (USQ) family of
017            quantization methods. In contrast, Vector Quantization (VQ) methods, which
018            operate on groups of multiple related weights as the basic unit of compression,
019            have recently taken the parallel field of Large Language Model (LLM) quantiza-
020            tion by storm. In this work, we for the first time apply codebook-based additive
021
               vector quantization algorithms to the problem of diffusion model compression,
               adapting prior works on the quantization-aware fine-tuning of transformer-based
022
               LLMs to take into account the special structure of convolutional weight tensors,
023
               the heterogeneity in the kinds of operations performed by the layers of a diffusion
024            model, and the momentum-invalidating discontinuities encountered between
025            successive batches during quantization-aware fine-tuning of diffusion models.
026            We are rewarded with a data-free distillation framework which achieves to the
027            best of our knowledge state-of-the-art results for the extremely low-bit weight
028            quantization on the standard class-conditional benchmark of LDM-4 on ImageNet
029            at 20 inference time steps. Notably, we report sFID 1.93 points lower than the
030            full-precision model at W4A8, the best-reported results for FID, sFID and ISC at
031            W2A8, and the first-ever successful quantization to W1.5A8 (less than 1.5 bits
032
               stored per weight) via a layer-wise heterogeneous quantization strategy. We thus
               establish a new Pareto frontier for diffusion model inference under low-memory
033
               conditions. Furthermore, our method allows for a dynamic trade-off between
034
               quantization-time GPU hours and inference-time savings, thus aligning with the
035            recent trend of approaches that combine the best aspects of both Post-Training
036            Quantization (PTQ) and Quantization-Aware Training (QAT). We are also able
037            to demonstrate FLOPs savings on arbitrary hardware via an efficient inference
038            kernel, as opposed to BOPs (Bit-wise Operations) savings resulting from small
039            integer operations that may lack broad support across hardware of interest.

053   et al., 2020). However, with one obstacle out of the way arises another, and with the latest-and-
      greatest open-source diffusion models such as SDXL 1.0 (Podell et al., 2024) boasting of 6.6 billion

130   2.2   D IFFUSION M ODEL Q UANTIZATION
131
      Earlier works on the quantization of diffusion models, such as PTQ4DM (Shang et al., 2023), Q-
132   Diffusion (Li et al., 2023), PTQD He et al. (2024b), Q-DM (Li et al., 2024) and TDQ (So et al., 2024)
133   have noted a distinction between PTQ and QAT. QAT approaches are characterised by a costly fine-
134   tuning process akin to knowledge distillation and/or access to the original training dataset, whereas
135   PTQ involves the relatively lightweight layer-wise optimization of quantization parameters.
136
      More recently, however, works such as EfficientDM (He et al., 2024a) and QuEST (Wang et al.,
137
      2024) have introduced a concept we label PTQ+PeFT, involving layer-wise alignment followed by
138   parameter-efficient fine-tuning. Such approaches achieve inference-time results matching those of
139   QAT, but are closer to PTQ in terms of resources required at quantization time. They thus combine
140   the best aspects of both approaches.

      Evaluation Methodology. In order to demonstrate the general applicability of our methods, we
340
      evaluate our proposed technique on two widely-adopted benchmarks: Unconditional generation us-

      ing the DDIM model of Song et al. (2021) on CIFAR-10 64 × 64, and conditional generation using
342   the LDM-4 model of Rombach et al. (2021) on ImageNet 256 × 256 (Deng et al., 2009). We com-
343   pare the generation quality of our model with that of previous works using Inception Score (IS),
344   Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017), Spatial FID (sFID) (Salimans et al., 2016)
345   and Precision. Inception Score is known to be unreliable on datasets other than ImageNet. How-
346   ever, we report it for CIFAR-10 so as to enable apples-to-apples comparison with earlier works on
347   quantization. All metric calculations are conducted using the reference implementation from the
348   ADM evaluation suite (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) after generation of 50,000 images via the quan-
349   tized model. FLOPs are calculated using FAIR’s fvcore (AI, 2019) tools and PyTorch compiled with
350
      MKL.
351   For our reporting of bit-width, it should be noted that weight quantization using VQ-based methods
352   results in a decimal value for the average parameters/weight. For reasons of symmetry with all
353   earlier works on diffusion model quantization, we conservatively round up to the nearest 0.5.
354   Calibration Technique. Uniform sampling of model inputs at all inference time steps is performed
355   as in (Li et al., 2023), resulting in a calibration dataset of 5120 model inputs. Layer-by-layer weight
356   quantization is subsequently carried out via AQLM (Egiazarian et al., 2024) with early-stopping at
357   a relative error tolerance of 0.01. In line with earlier works such as (Li et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
358   2024; So et al., 2024; He et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024), only the U-Net of latent diffusion models
359   (LDMs) is quantized. The encoder and decoder which produce the latent representation are not
360   quantized. Furthermore, the first and last convolutional layers of U-Nets are not quantized, due to
361   their extremely small share of the parameter count and model FLOPs. The number of codebooks
362
      for AQLM quantization is set to M = 4 for W4A8, M = 3 for W3A8, and M = 2 for W2A8.
      For W1.5A8, 3 × 3 convolutional layers are quantized with M = 1 and all others layers M = 2.
363
      A group size of d = 9 is used for 3 × 3 convolutional layers and d = 8 for all other layers. The
364
      codebook size is set to 28 entries per codebook, corresponding to 8-bit indices. This ensures that
365   codebooks are relatively small compared to quantized weight matrices.
366
367   Modifications to these settings are noted in the subsection specific to the experiment.
368   PeFT Hyperparameters. Unless explicitly noted otherwise, whole-model PeFT is subsequently
369   carried out using the PV-Tuning optimizer (Malinovskii et al., 2024) for 160 epochs of 100 succes-
370   sive denoising steps each in the W4A8 and W3A8 case and 320 epochs of 100 successive denoising
371   steps each in the W2A8 and W1.5A8 cases, with a continuous optimization learning rate of 4e − 5
372   decaying linearly to 1e − 6 and a discrete optimization learning rate of 1e − 4. A batch size of 4 is
373   used for PeFT of LDM models and 64 for DDIM models.
374   Activation Quantization Methodology. Improvements to the quantization of weights, not activa-
375   tions is the focus of this paper. Consequently we quantize activations according to the methodology
376   of (Li et al., 2024) for CIFAR-10 WxA8. For ImageNet, we use separate activation scale factors
377   for each time-step as in He et al. (2024a); Wang et al. (2024), due to the well-attested better perfor-
      mance. Activation quantization is performed as the last step after PeFT has completed.

523   5     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this work, we have introduced codebook-based additive vector quantization to diffusion models
526
      for the first time. In order to account for the unique features of diffusion models, such as the convo-
527
      lutional U-Net and the progressive denoising process, we have introduced techniques such as Convo-
528   lutional Kernel-Aware Quantization (KAQ), Layer Heterogeneity-Aware Quantization (LAQ), and
529   Selective Momentum Invalidation PV-Tuning (SeMI-PV). Our method has achieved state-of-the-art
530   results in extremely low-bit quantization. Not only have we set a new Pareto frontier on the LDM-4
531   benchmark at 20 inference steps, we have also quantized this standard benchmark task to W1.5A8
532   for the first time. Additionally, our approach allows for flexibly balancing quantization and inference
533   efficiency and achieves hardware-agnostic FLOPs savings.
534   Limitations and future work. Although AQUATIC-Diff achieves excellent results on a variety of
535   metrics, including some which are state-of-the-art, we are not as efficient in terms of pure GPU
536   hours compared to earlier PTQ+PEFT works such as He et al. (2024a). In part, this results from the
537   slowness of the AQLM layer-wise quantization (Egiazarian et al., 2024) and of the PV-Tuning opti-
538   mizer (Malinovskii et al., 2024), in comparison to straight-through estimation using Adam (Kingma
539   & Ba, 2015) as applied in He et al. (2024a). In order to address this, work can be invested in the
      development of faster gradient-based optimization algorithms for additive vector quantization.
