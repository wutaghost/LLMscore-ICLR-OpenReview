009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            When leveraging language models for reasoning tasks, generating explicit chain-
012            of-thought (CoT) steps often proves essential for achieving high accuracy in final
013            outputs. In this paper, we investigate if models can be taught to internalize these
014            CoT steps. To this end, we propose a simple yet effective method for internalizing
015            CoT steps: starting with a model trained for explicit CoT reasoning, we gradually
016
               remove the intermediate steps and finetune the model. This process allows the
               model to internalize the intermediate reasoning steps, thus simplifying the reason-
017
               ing process while maintaining high performance. Our approach enables training a
018
               GPT-2 Small model to solve 20-by-20 multiplication with 99.5% accuracy while
019            being 26 times faster than explicit CoT, whereas standard training cannot solve
020            beyond 4-by-4 multiplication. Furthermore, our method proves effective on larger
021            language models, such as Mistral 7B, achieving over 50% accuracy on GSM8K
022            without producing any intermediate steps.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      A prevalent approach to improving the performance of language models (LMs) to perform complex
      reasoning tasks is chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning, in which the LM generates explicit interme-
028
      diate reasoning steps before arriving at a final answer (Nye et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022). This
029
      method allows models to break down complex problems into simpler, manageable parts, thereby
030   improving the accuracy of their final predictions. However, this explicit reasoning process can be
031   computationally expensive, especially when the reasoning chain is long (Deng et al., 2023). Addi-
032   tionally, using explicit intermediate steps might not align with the intrinsic computational strengths
033   of LMs (Lehnert et al., 2024): for instance, multi-digit multiplication is very easy for calculators but
034   remains challenging for GPT-4 (Yang et al., 2023).
035
      In this work, we examine the possibility of internalizing the reasoning process in the model’s hidden
036   states. We propose an approach, Stepwise Internalization, which begins with a model trained for
037   explicit CoT reasoning. We then gradually remove the intermediate steps and finetune the model,
038   forcing it to internalize the reasoning process. Once all intermediate steps are internalized, we
039   achieve a model capable of full implicit CoT reasoning. Moreover, even in cases where the model
040   does not have the capacity for full implicit CoT reasoning, this method still allows for shortening
041   the reasoning chain while maintaining accuracy.
042   Our approach is an alternative to the approach proposed by Deng et al. (2023), which shares the
043   goal of implicitly reasoning using the hidden states of transformers instead of relying on explicit
044   CoT tokens. To teach the model to use hidden states for reasoning, that method employs a teacher
045   model that performs explicit CoT reasoning, and then distills the teacher’s hidden states into the
046   student model’s hidden states. In comparison, our approach is much simpler yet more effective.
047
      Our approach demonstrates significant improvements over standard training methods. For instance,
048   a GPT-2 Small model trained with Stepwise Internalization on multiplication can solve 20-by-20
049   multiplication problems nearly perfectly, while standard training without CoT struggles even with
050   4-by-4 multiplication. Furthermore, our method scales effectively to larger models, such as the
051   Mistral 7B model (Jiang et al., 2023), achieving over 50% accuracy on the GSM8K dataset of
052   grade-school math word problems (Cobbe et al., 2021), without producing any explicit intermediate
053   steps, outperforming the much larger GPT-4 model without chain-of-thought reasoning, which only
      scores 44% when prompted to directly generate the answer.

      It is important to note that our empirical evaluation focuses on specific reasoning tasks like multi-
055   digit multiplication and grade-school math problems. While our results show the potential of Step-
056   wise Internalization in these contexts, and the simplicity of the method makes it applicable to chain-
057   of-thought approaches in a wide range of tasks, further research is needed to explore its efficacy
058   across a broader range of tasks and more diverse CoT traces. Due to limitations in available compu-
059   tational resources, experiments on other tasks are out of scope for this work. This paper aims to lay
060   the groundwork for this new approach and highlight its promise, while acknowledging that its full
061   generalization is still under investigation.
062   The contributions of our work are as follows: First, we introduce Stepwise Internalization, a sim-
063   ple method for implicit CoT reasoning. Second, we demonstrate the effectiveness of internal-
064   izing intermediate hidden states via Stepwise Internalization. Third, we provide empirical re-
065   sults showing the superior performance of models trained with Stepwise Internalization on dif-
066   ferent reasoning tasks and model scales. Our code, data, and pretrained models are available at
067   https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Internalize_CoT_Step_by_Step.

069   2   BACKGROUND : I MPLICIT C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT R EASONING
070
071   Implicit chain-of-thought reasoning (implicit CoT, or ICoT) is a concept introduced by Deng et al.
072   (2023), where during generation, the language model does not produce explicit intermediate rea-
073   soning steps in words. It is distinct from not using chain-of-thought reasoning (No CoT), in that
074   explicit reasoning steps are allowed during training, enabling the ICoT model to learn the underly-
075   ing reasoning approach from the supervision provided on the reasoning process. The key insight
076   of Deng et al. (2023) is that intermediate reasoning steps serve two purposes in explicit CoT: they
077   provide supervision during training to facilitate learning the task (Nye et al., 2021), and they act as
078
      a scratchpad during inference to assist in solving the task (Wei et al., 2022). However, the latter
      purpose can be fulfilled by utilizing the internal states of the model instead of explicit tokens.
079
080   As an illustrative example, consider using a language model to solve a multi-digit multiplication
081   problem, such as 12 × 34. (The actual input reverses the digit order as 2 1 * 4 3 for consistency
082   with Deng et al. (2023).) In the long multiplication algorithm, 12 × 34 is broken into:
083                                12 × 4 + 12 × 30 =         48        +      360          .
                                                             |{z}              |{z}
084                                                      reversed: 84       reversed: 063
085
      In explicit CoT, the model is trained to predict these intermediate steps 8 4 + 0 6 3 before
086
      predicting the final answer 8 0 4 (408 reversed). Predicting these intermediate steps facilitates the
087
      model’s ability to solve the task. (The intermediate steps are also reversed to make it easier for the
088   model to predict (Shen et al., 2023).)
089
090   In both No CoT and implicit CoT settings, the model needs to directly predict the answer 408 from
091
      the input, bypassing the intermediate steps. This approach can make inference much faster for long
      reasoning chains, albeit at the cost of accuracy.
092
093   The primary difference between implicit CoT and No CoT lies in the use of intermediate reason-
094   ing steps as supervision during training. In Deng et al. (2023), a knowledge distillation approach
095   was employed to distill explicit reasoning into implicit reasoning within the hidden states. This
096   method involves training a teacher model to perform explicit CoT reasoning and then transferring
097
      this knowledge to a student model, which internalizes the reasoning process within its hidden states.
098   In the present work, we propose a far simpler yet more effective approach based on a kind of cur-
099   riculum learning that we call Stepwise Internalization, which we detail in the next section.

149
      During initial experiments, we observed instability in the training process due to changes in the loss
150
      function over time. This instability arises for two primary reasons:
151
152   First, the optimizer commonly used in training language models, such as AdamW (Kingma & Ba,
153   2017; Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019), maintains estimates of second-order gradients. A sudden change
154
      in the loss function, caused by the removal of one more CoT token, results in abrupt changes in the
      second-order gradients. To address this issue, we reset the optimizer’s state whenever an additional
155
      CoT token is removed.
156
157   Second, even if a model fits perfectly to the current loss when s tokens are removed, transitioning
158   to the next stage, where s + 1 tokens are removed, leads to a significant increase in the loss, as
159   the model is not yet trained for this new setting. To mitigate this issue, we introduce a technique
160
      which we term “Removal Smoothing”, where we add a small random offset to the original number
      of tokens to remove s(t), such that:

501   8    L IMITATIONS
502
503
      Training costs. One limitation of the proposed approach is its high training cost due to the fine-
      tuning required when removing each set of CoT tokens. As discussed in Section 6.2, removing
504
      CoT tokens too fast leads to non-convergence. Therefore, the longer the CoT chain, the longer the
505
      training duration. For tasks like N -digit multiplication, where the reasoning chain length grows
506   quadratically with N , training becomes expensive as N increases.
507
508   Instability. Another practical issue we observed is the instability of training with aggressive ∆
509   values. For example, Figure 6 in Appendix B shows a case where the model could not recover
510   from a drop in accuracy. Using lower ∆ values generally leads to more stable training, but at the
511   cost of longer training time. Identifying and addressing unstable dynamics early on, potentially by
512   restarting training as suggested by Hu et al. (2024), could be a valuable improvement.
513
514   Interpretability. Models trained using our approach lose interpretable intermediate steps. How-
515   ever, as shown in Section 6.1, it is still possible to interpret the internal hidden states of these models
516
      using probing techniques (Belinkov, 2018; Hewitt & Liang, 2019). Additionally, combining implicit
      and explicit CoT training could allow users to choose between interpretability and latency, providing
517
      flexibility based on the requirements of future tasks.
518
519
      Accuracy. Undoubtedly, explicit CoT still achieves higher accuracies compared to our approach
520   to implicit CoT. However, our method enables a trade-off between latency and accuracy. Moreover,
521   our results demonstrate the potential of leveraging hidden states for reasoning: even a GPT-2 Small
522   model can be trained to solve 20×20 multiplication, despite having only 12 layers, far fewer than the
523   number of reasoning steps in the CoT for 20 × 20 multiplication. When scaled to larger models with
524   hundreds of billions of parameters and up to a hundred layers, such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020),
525   they could potentially solve even more challenging reasoning tasks without explicit CoT steps.
526
527   9    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529   In this work, we introduced Stepwise Internalization, a novel approach for achieving implicit chain-
530   of-thought reasoning in language models. By gradually removing intermediate CoT tokens and
531   finetuning the model, we enable the internalization of reasoning steps incrementally. Our approach
532   demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods, achieving high accuracy on up to
533   20×20 multiplication using GPT-2 Small and outperforming GPT-4 on GSM8K while not outputting
534   any intermediate reasoning steps. Compared to explicit CoT methods, our approach can be up to 26
535   times faster while maintaining similar accuracies.
536   For future work, developing a mixed-mode approach that combines implicit and explicit CoT reason-
537   ing could potentially offer the best of both worlds, balancing accuracy, latency, and interpretability
538   based on user preferences. Another promising direction is scaling Stepwise Internalization to larger
539   models and more extensive training/pretraining setups, which could further enhance its effectiveness
      on a broader range of reasoning tasks.
