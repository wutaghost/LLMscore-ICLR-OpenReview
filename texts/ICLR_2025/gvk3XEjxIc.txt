009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Finding a control Lyapunov function (CLF) in a dynamical system with a controller
012            is an effective way to guarantee stability, which is a crucial issue in safety-concerned
013            applications. Recently, deep learning models representing CLFs have been applied
014            into a learner-verifier framework to identify satisfiable candidates. However, the
015            learner treats Lyapunov conditions as complex constraints for optimisation, which
016
               is hard to achieve global convergence. It is also too complicated to implement
               these Lyapunov conditions for verification. To improve this framework, we treat
017
               Lyapunov conditions as inductive biases and design a neural CLF and a CLF-based
018
               controller guided by this knowledge. This design enables a stable optimisation
019            process with limited constraints, and allows end-to-end learning of both the CLF
020            and the controller. Our approach achieves higher convergence rate and larger
021            region of attraction (ROA) in learning the CLF compared to existing methods
022            among abundant experiment cases. We also thoroughly reveal why the success rate
023            decreases with previous methods during learning.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      In recent years, deep learning models have demonstrated significant potential in controlling nonlinear
028
      dynamical systems with high performance results within various applications such as car naviga-
029   tion (Han et al., 2022), robot scooping (Niu et al., 2023), and quadrotor flying (O’Connell et al.,
030   2022). However, as deep learning methods led to black-box models, when safety concerns cannot be
031   overlooked, the real-life implementation requires theoretical analysis of the stability performance,
032   creating an open question (Amodei et al., 2016).
033
      For a dynamical system with a controller, the theory of Lyapunov stability is an important approach
034
      in stability analysis, which determines a control Lyapunov function (CLF) and a region of attraction
035   (ROA) (Giesl & Hafstein, 2015). Within the ROA, the system is proven to be asymptotically stable
036   around the equilibrium. Nonetheless, this theory does not provide a way to systematically discover a
037   CLF. Designing a feasible framework to achieve this goal becomes a necessary research topic.
038
      Previous works focus on finding CLFs for polynomial dynamical systems (Khodadadi et al., 2014;
039
      Majumdar et al., 2013). For nonlinear dynamics, a learner-verifier framework is proposed with
040
      polynomial CLF candidates to guarantee stability (Ravanbakhsh & Sankaranarayanan, 2019), which
041   is extended to learn neural CLFs with a linear-quadratic regulator (LQR) controller (Chang et al.,
042   2019). Additionally, a mathematical analysis is provided (Zhou et al., 2022) and learning piecewise
043   models as CLFs and dynamics is studied for simplicity (Dai et al., 2020).
044
      However, three shortcomings exist within this learner-verifier framework. First, three Lyapunov
045
      conditions are converted to soft constraints in the trainer (Chang et al., 2019; Dai et al., 2020; Zhou
046
      et al., 2022), but the complex combination of loss weights makes it hard to optimise the neural CLF
047   candidate to satisfy all these conditions at the same time.
048
049   Second, to release this difficulty in learning, the overlooked region around the equilibrium is intro-
050
      duced (Chang et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2022; Zinage & Bakolas, 2023). The verifier will not check
      the satisfaction of Lyapunov conditions within this region:
051
052                                          Ω0 = {s | ks − s∗ k < r0 },                                      (1)
               ∗
053   where s is the equilibrium state and r0 ∈ R+ is a radius. But the existence of this region will lead to
      rough or even fake results deceiving the verifier, along with a controller failing to stabilise the system.

      Third, the verifier consists of a non-convex solver to check the Lyapunov conditions, which is
      independent from the deep learning library and usually runs on CPU only. To express Lyapunov
061
      conditions in the verifier, users have to extract weights from the neural CLF candidate and then
062
      hard-code this deep learning model, its derivative, the dynamics and controller models with only basic
063   mathematical operations. The implementation will be impractical when models become complex.
064
065   To address these issues mentioned above, we propose an end-to-end pipeline for learning the neural
066
      CLF and controller for the nonlinear dynamical system. Apart from optimising constraints to meet the
      Lyapunov conditions, we first treat Lyapunov conditions as inductive biases. As compared in Table 1,
067
      by incorporating this knowledge into the neural CLF and the controller, we can satisfy mostly the
068
      Lyapunov conditions before learning, reducing the optimisation burden and improving the possibility
069   of convergence. Additionally, we can perform the verification step using only the deep learning
070   library, simplifying the framework for the easy implementation.
071
072
      Contribution. First, we design the neural CLF and the nonlinear controller with one neural network
      instructed by Lyapunov conditions as prior inductive biases, which enables us to train them in a
073
      synthesis way. Second, we incorporate the verifier into trainer and propose an end-to-end framework
074
      to find satisfiable CLFs for nonlinear dynamical systems. Third, we provide a thorough investigation
075   with regard to the robustness of different methods, the reasons of their differences in performance, and
076   the effects of the other heuristic learning factor called the geometric shaping loss. We demonstrate
077   robust convergence and superior performance in finding CLFs with larger ROAs compared to
078   counterparts.

081   2        R ELATED W ORKS
082
083   Lyapunov theory with classical methods. The application of the Lyapunov theory ranges from
084   simple to complex cases. For linear dynamical systems, the linear-quadratic (LQR) controller is able to
085   achieve the Lyapunov stability with a quadratic Lyapunov function (Khalil, 2015). The control-affine
086   polynomial dynamics is also proven to be stable with a polynomial controllers and a sum-of-square
087
      (SOS) polynomial Lyapunov function using semidefinite programming (SDP) (Majumdar et al., 2013;
      Khodadadi et al., 2014; Jarvis-Wloszek et al., 2003; Henrion & Garulli, 2005). Besides, the SDP
088
      optimisation is widely expanded in a rich group of work (Parrilo, 2000; Papachristodoulou & Prajna,
089
      2005). However, the scale of the SDP grows exponentially related to the dimension of dynamics with
090   a SOS low-degree polynomial Lyapunov function (Parrilo, 2000).
091
092   Lyapunov theory for stability estimation and analysis. For nonlinear systems without controller,
093
      the research purpose is to prove the feature of stability. Researchers firstly turn to deep learning
      to directly approximate known regions of attractions (ROAs) (Berkenkamp et al., 2016a; Richards
094
      et al., 2018). To find a Lyapunov function to prove asymptotic stability, the learner-verifier frame-
095
      work proposed in (Ravanbakhsh & Sankaranarayanan, 2019) has been further extended with deep
096   learning (Grüne, 2020; Abate et al., 2020; Dai et al., 2020; Grüne, 2021; Gaby et al., 2022). To
097   approximate the Lyapunov function and verify satisfaction of the Lyapunov conditions, deep learning
098   models have been applied with a SMT solver (Grüne, 2020; Abate et al., 2020). Piecewise linear
099   systems have also been addressed using ReLU-activated networks with the MIP solver (Dai et al.,
100   2020). Convergence has been analytically studied and networks have been designed to accelerate
101   convergence (Grüne, 2021; Gaby et al., 2022). Despite the increased expression ability in deep
102   learning for approximating the Lyapunov function, verifying the Lyapunov conditions among the
103   whole defined region within non-convex solvers is difficult to implement. The framework is also
104
      unstable with complex optimisation targets. Moreover, the MIP solver requires piecewise linear
      functions, which limits its usage when other known or learnt nonlinear dynamics models are available.
105
106   Lyapunov theory for control with stability guarantee. Gaussian process models are firstly applied
107   to improve the accuracy of dynamical models for more reliable control (Berkenkamp & Schoellig,
      2015; Sui et al., 2015; Berkenkamp et al., 2016b; Koller et al., 2018), as well as for learning safe

108
      regions for control policy (Schreiter et al., 2015; Turchetta et al., 2019). Recent, several works focus
109   on introducing a controller that can be proven stable with respect to Lyapunov theory (Chang et al.,
110   2019; Dai et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Zinage & Bakolas, 2023). In (Chang et al., 2019), the
111   learner-verifier framework is first used to find a CLF candidate for nonlinear dynamical systems
112   using the LQR controller, and this methodology is later extended to nonlinear controllers (Dai et al.,
113   2021; Zhou et al., 2022). This framework is combined with Koopman operator theory to learn the
114   dynamical system at the same time (Zinage & Bakolas, 2023). Additionally, a method is proposed for
115   decomposing the dynamical system into a continuous branch of Lyapunov functions and controllers
116   for subsystems for generalisation (Zhang et al., 2023). However, as noted earlier, challenges exist
117
      in synthetically learning controllers and the corresponding CLF with a complex optimisation target,
      where careful tuning weights of the loss for convergence is required.

526   6   C ONCLUSION
527
528   We propose an end-to-end framework for learning Lyapunov functions and controllers for nonlinear
529   dynamical systems. Our method reduces the constraints related to the Lyapunov conditions, simplifies
530   the learning framework and decrease the difficulty in hyperparameter tuning by using a sum-of-squares
531   neural network as the control Lyapunov function (CLF) and a CLF-based bounded nonlinear controller.
532   Our approach exhibits excellent and robust performance in finding Lyapunov functions with the
533   largest region of attraction (ROA) and the highest success rate using simple settings, highlighting its
534   potential in better facilitating Lyapunov stability analysis and nonlinear control learning.
535   Limitations and future works. Our framework focuses on simplifying training process and improv-
536   ing the success rate, and further attention can be paid on the following aspects: 1. As the training
537   complexity grows exponentially to the dimension of dynamics, it is essential to express dynamics in
538   a data-efficient way, for example, achieving appropriate dynamics with reduced dimension. 2. To
539   find a more powerful control policy with the stability guarantee, integrating our method with the
      model-based reinforcement learning framework will be further explored.
