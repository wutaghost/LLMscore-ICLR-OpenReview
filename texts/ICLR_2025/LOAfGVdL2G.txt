011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In multi-domain learning, a single model is trained on diverse data domains to
014            leverage shared knowledge and improve generalization. The order in which the
015
               data from these domains is used for training can significantly affect the model’s
               performance on each domain. However, this dependence is under-studied. In this
016
               paper, we investigate the influence of training order (or data mixing) in multi-
017
               domain learning using the concept of Lie bracket of gradient vector fields. By
018            analyzing the infinitesimal effects of changing the training order, we identify
019            regions in the parameter space where altering the order between two training
020            domains can benefit the target loss. We validate the predictions of our theoretical
021            framework on the influence of training order (or data mixing) both on a toy example
022            and bilingual LLM pre-training.
023

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      In real-world scenarios, training data may come from different sources that vary in quality, topics,
      diversity, and other aspects. For example, the modern large language models are trained on data
028
      collected from the curated list of domains that are comprised of web-crawled data, math, code,
029
      academic papers, etc. The natural question arises: how to mix the data and when to use each of the
030   domains (early in the training or in the last stage)? There is no rigorous approach to this problem and
031   practitioners generally use handcrafted solutions.
032
033
      One prominent example is the multilingual setting, where the data comes from different languages
      that can also substantially differ in the amounts of available data. It has been demonstrated in (Choi
034
      et al., 2024) that the sequence of domain exposure matters in the presence of dataset imbalances.
035
      Specifically, training first with the prevalence of high-resource domains followed by an equal mix of
036   high- and low-resource domains yields better results. Motivated by these findings, we address the
037   problem of the domain order during the training for a fixed training budget.
038
039
      Another relevant directions to our setting are multi-task training, domain adaption and curriculum
      learning. For multi-task problems, we should balance the signals from different losses/problems
040
      to get an optimal training trajectory. There is a big amount of literature on multi-task learning, we
041
      mention just a few (Navon et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021). Many
042   of them manipulate with the gradients to stir the training into non-conflicting direction. Different
043   from this papers, we do not interfere gradients of domains as we only concerned with the their order.
044   Domain adaptation concerned with the gradual distribution shift from the source to the target domain
045   (e.g. see (Kumar et al., 2020; He et al., 2023; Zhuang et al., 2024)). The order of the intermediate
046   or mixtures of the source and target domains have a great impact on the adaptation process. In the
047   present paper, we do not interpolate between domains, but, only consider interaction between them to
048   improve training trajectory.
049   More formally, we consider a scenario where the total amount of data for each domain is fixed,
050   but the order in which the examples from different domains are interleaved can be adjusted. This
051   setup is practical as, for example, we have vast amounts of web-crawled data and limited amounts of
052   high-quality data that we want to fully embrace. If we use the same proportion of high-quality data in
053   each batch it may lead to sub-optimal training and a common practice is to increase the proportion of
      that data later in the training.

064   2    M ULTI -D OMAIN L EARNING

066   In multi-domain learning, where models are trained on data from multiple sources, the choice of
067   domain weights plays a crucial role in balancing the contribution of each domain during training.
068
      The weights determine the importance of each domain and influence the model’s generalization
      capabilities.
069

071   model parameters θ∗ which minimize datasets’ losses (L1 (X1 , Y1 , θ), . . . , LK (XK , YK , θ)). The
072   common setups here include multi-objective optimization, in which one deals with the entire Pareto
073   front simultaneously, or loss averaging scenario. In the last one, the target is the weighted sum of the
074
      datasets’ losses. The training process in this case is based on the reweighting of domain gradients:

466   5   R ELATED W ORK
467
468   The multi-domain learning addresses the problem of designing a model such that it can properly
469   handle examples coming from different data sources with varied characteristics, thus, improving its
470   generalization or applicability. To learn simultaneously on several domains, we need to solve a multi-
471
      objective problem (Maurer et al., 2016; Désidéri, 2012) or average the losses across domains with
      some weights wk (Kokkinos, 2017). The classic approach to choosing the weights is scalarization,
472
      where weights are set to some constants. However, the choice of these constants is generally a hard
473
      optimization problem (Royer et al., 2024). Another option is adaptive methods that dynamically
474   adapt the weights, i.e. use some weight schedule during training. These methods are divided into
475   loss-based and gradient-based. Loss-based methods compute wk (t) to make the training uniform
476   among different losses, see e.g. (Liu et al., 2021). Gradient-based methods use gradient information
477   for each domain to re-compute weights needing several backward operations for each step, see e.g.
478   (Javaloy & Valera, 2021; Lin et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2018; 2020).
479
      In our work, we rely on the Lie bracket computation of loss vector fields to conduct our analysis. It
480   was previously used by (Dherin, 2023) for backward error analysis in a continual learning scenario.
481   The work (Marcotte et al., 2024) in some sense complements our work by trying to find, what is
482   invariant (rather than changed) under the altering in domain weights. To do it, they construct a Lie
483   algebra generated by domain gradient vector fields and find the conservation laws. The computation
484   of the Lie bracket in our case leads to the need for Hesian-vector product computation. We note
485   that Hessian computations occur in various fields of Machine Learning, including optimization (Liu
      & Nocedal, 1989; Nocedal & Wright, 1999), loss landscape analysis (Cooper, 2018), uncertainty


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      estimation (Daxberger et al., 2021), and bi-level optimization (Chen et al., 2022; Franceschi et al.,
487   2018).
488
489   The influence of data from different domains on a model’s performance is also an active research
490
      topic. The common approach here is to utilize influence functions (Koh & Liang, 2017), or some of
      their approximations, like TracIn (Pruthi et al., 2020; Schioppa et al., 2022). Note that despite the
491
      visual similarity of the influence formula (Koh & Liang, 2017) and our formula 8, the difference is
492
      that our formula has Hessian rather than inverse Hessian.

      6     C ONCLUSION
497
498   With the rise of Large Language Models (LLM), it becomes clear that the data is crucial to the LLMs’
499   success. Much research is devoted to analyzing best practices for dataset collection, namely cleaning,
500   filtering, domain and language proportion, etc. Much less attention is paid to the training order,
501   while practical evidence shows that the dataset order matters. We believe that one reason is that the
502   researchers usually use the simplified theoretical understanding of the training process, supposing
503   that the data is sampled from the overall data distribution throughout training. Meanwhile, in the case
504
      of the highly long training process on the diverse multi-domain multi-lingual dataset, distribution
      stability is unlikely to be achieved. Therefore, a novel theoretical framework closer to the realistic
505
      setting is needed. In this work, we step in this direction, proposing a theoretically motivated method
506
      to analyze and predict the influence of the training order in a multi-domain setup. We demonstrate the
507   soundness of our ideas in both toy examples and realistic experiments. Our results on multi-lingual
508   LLM pre-training illustrate the importance of the raised problem and the ability of our theoretical
509   framework to explain the observed variation in the training results depending on the dataset order
510   only.
511
512
513   6.1   L IMITATIONS
514
515   Firstly, the method does not give an explicit algorithm to produce an optimal weight schedule. It just
516   answers, to which direction should we shift an existing sub-optimal one to improve it.
517   Secondly, our method currently does not handle the difference between optimizers, see remark in
518   section 3. However, it is known that the choice of the optimizer can influence the training dynamics
519   and lead to regions with different loss landscape (Jiang et al., 2024). Modifications of optimizers
520   for multi-domain and multi-task learning (Yang et al., 2023) also would change the commutator
521   formulae.
522
      More importantly, we currently do not properly handle the stochasticity of the training process. The
523   training process is better described as a flow of stochastic differential equation dθ = −∇L(θ) dt +
524     1
      Ω 2 (θ) dW , where W is the Wiener process. Here Ω(θ) measures the noise intensity of the stochastic
525   gradient and is often crucial for learning. As we have shown, the infinitesimal difference in the
526   order of different domains would result in bias in the drift term (Theorem 3.1), but also it would
527   change noise intensity in the diffusion term. The formal derivation of this dependency (using Ito
528   or Stratonovitch paradigms, and Chen-Strichartz formula, see e.g. (Baudoin, 2004)), as well as its
529   empirical validation, is our further direction.
