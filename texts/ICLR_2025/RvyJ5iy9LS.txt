009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multiplex graphs, which represent complex real-world relationships, have recently
012            garnered significant research interest. However, contemporary methods exhibit vari-
013            ations in implementations and settings, lacking a unified benchmark for fair compar-
014            ison. Additionally, existing multiplex graph datasets suffer from small-scale issues
015            and a lack of representative features. Furthermore, current evaluation metrics are re-
016            stricted to node classification and clustering tasks, lacking evaluations on edge-level
017            tasks. These obstacles impede the further development of the multiplex graph learn-
018            ing community. To address these issues, we first conducted a fair comparison based
019
               on existing settings, finding that current methods are approaching performance satu-
               ration on existing datasets with minimal differences; and simple end-to-end models
020
               sometimes achieve better results. Subsequently, we proposed a unified multiplex
021
               graph benchmark called MGB. MGB includes ten baseline models with unified im-
022            plementations, formalizes seven existing datasets, introduces four new datasets with
023            text attributes, and proposes two novel edge-level evaluation tasks. Experiments on
024            MGB revealed that the performance of existing methods significantly diminishes
025            on new challenging datasets and tasks. Additional results suggest that models
026            with global attention and stronger expressive power in end-to-end solutions hold
027            promise for future work. The data, code, and documentations are publicly available
028            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/multiplex-F150.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In recent years, the field of graph learning has witnessed rapid development (Kipf & Welling, 2016;
034   Veličković et al., 2017). Multiplex graphs (Zhang et al., 2018), which incorporate diverse relationships
035   between nodes, offer a more realistic representation of multiple structural connections between nodes
036   in the real world, attracting considerable research interest. An example of a multiplex graph is an
037   e-commerce network (Ni et al., 2019), where different products have multiple types of relationships,
038   including co-purchased, co-viewed, and complementary connections. Different connection types play
039
      distinct roles in specific contexts. It’s noteworthy that in real-world applications, multiplex graphs
      also come with rich textual attributes, such as product descriptions in e-commerce networks and paper
040
      abstracts in citation networks. Data with such structural multiplicity and attributive richness holds
041
      great potential in applications like knowledge graph construction (Zhao et al., 2022), recommendation
042   systems (Zhang et al., 2020), and anomaly detection (Guo et al., 2024), among others.
043
044   Despite the rapid development of multiplex graph learning, three key issues persist in the field: (i)
045
      Inconsistent Comparisons: Different methods adopt unique data processing, model implementation,
      and experimental settings, hampering our ability to comprehensively understand them and making
046
      fair comparisons challenging. (ii) Insufficient Datasets: Common multiplex graph datasets often
047
      have a limited scale, containing only a few thousand nodes. Furthermore, despite containing rich
048   raw textual information, the original data is typically encoded into vectors using shallow embedding
049   methods, which restricts expressiveness and generalization. Consequently, existing multiplex graph
050   works primarily focus on representation learning, especially self-supervised representation learning,
051   employing complicated data augmentation and contrastive paradigms to learn features, which is time-
052   consuming and resource-intensive. (iii) Limited Evaluation Metrics: Existing methods incorrectly
053   train and evaluate the inherently multi-labeled IMDB (Wang et al., 2019b) dataset using a single-label
      strategy. Additionally, these methods primarily evaluate models on node-level tasks, neglecting

055   models’ ability to learn structural information beyond node attributes.

      2.1   TASK F ORMULATION
102
103   Multiplex Graphs A multiplex graph is a network consisting of G = {G1 , G2 , ..., GR }, where
104   Gr = {V, Er , Ar , X} is the r-th subgraph of the multiplex graph corresponding to the r-th meta-path
105   (also known as relationship or view), and R denotes the number of subgraphs. For each Gr , V and
106   Er denote the node set and edge set, respectively; Ar ∈ R|V|×|V| , and X ∈ R|V|×d represent the
107   adjacency matrix and feature matrix. It is important to note that all Gr share the same node set V and
      feature matrix X but have different edge sets Er and adjacency matrices Ar .

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Multiplexity vs. Heterogeneity Multiplex graphs and heterogeneous graphs (Lv et al., 2021;
109   Zhang et al., 2019) are two distinct subsets of multi-relational graphs (Hamilton). Heterogeneous
110   graphs feature diverse types of nodes and edges, with edges typically constrained based on node
111   types, often connecting nodes of specific types. Conversely, multiplex graph focuses on multiple
112   interactions between the same pairs of nodes (Melton & Krishnan, 2023; Yu et al., 2022). In essence,
113   heterogeneous graphs emphasize connections between specific node types, while multiplex graphs
114   prioritize interactions across different relation types. Therefore, the research methods and focuses of
115   these two areas differ to some extent.
116
117   2.2      M ETHODS ON M ULTIPLEX G RAPH
118
119   The majority of research in the field of multiplex graph learning focuses on representation learning,
120   which involves learning node embeddings by integrating information from multiple meta-paths. In
121   this paper, we roughly classify existing work into two categories based on the training objectives.
122   (i) End-to-end methods take raw node features and graph structure information as model inputs, and
123   the output node embeddings are directly used for specific downstream tasks such as node classification.
124   HAN (Wang et al., 2019b) aggregates multiple node embeddings using hierarchical attention at
125   different levels and trains the model using cross-entropy loss with ground-truth labels. Traditional
126   Graph Neural Networks (e.g., GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016), GAT (Veličković et al., 2017)) can also be
127   trained by directly using average-readout on multiple node embeddings. (ii) Self-supervised methods
128   do not rely on ground-truth labels. Instead, they mostly utilize contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2020;
129   Grill et al., 2020; He et al., 2020) to help the model learn general representations. MNE (Zhang et al.,
130
      2018) and DMG (Mo et al., 2023b) learn a common embedding and a private embedding for each
      subgraph, then combine them using attention mechanism. DMGI (Park et al., 2020), HDMI (Jing
131
      et al., 2021), and SSDCM (Mitra et al., 2021) leverage contrastive learning by maximizing mutual
132
      information between node embeddings and graph readouts. HeCo (Wang et al., 2021), CKD (Wang
133   et al., 2022a), and MGDCR (Mo et al., 2023a) contrast between different subgraphs and within
134   individual subgraph. Other works also focus on learning scalable embeddings (Liu et al., 2020),
135   dealing with incomplete data (Wang et al., 2022b), and so on. A common issue among the self-
136   supervised methods is the requirement for complicated contrastive paradigms and resource-consuming
137   negative sampling.
138
      In addition, multiplex graph learning also demonstrates the ability to effectively characterize complex
139   relationships in real-world applications. For example, CS-MLGCN (Behrouz & Hashemi, 2022)
140   explores the application of multiplex graphs in community search. ANOMULY (Behrouz & Seltzer,
141   2022) combines multiplex graphs with dynamic graphs for anomaly detection tasks on time-series
142   multiplex graph data, including scenarios such as blockchain security and brain disease prediction.
143   ADMire (Behrouz & Seltzer, 2023) also utilizes multiplex graphs to model brain networks and detect
144   anomalies in the human brain.
145   Appendix B provides more detailed information about each mentioned work. However, due to
146   the different settings and implementations of each method, achieving a unified fair comparison is
147   challenging, impeding further development in multiplex graph learning.

      Datasets We consider seven commonly used multiplex graph datasets, including two citation
152   networks: ACM (Wang et al., 2019b) and DBLP (Gao et al., 2009), two movie review networks:

158   versions to ensure fair comparison, namely ACM, IMDB, and DBLP from HAN (Wang et al., 2019b),
159   Amazon from DMGI (Park et al., 2020), and Freebase from HeCo (Wang et al., 2021). It is worth

      Evaluations Existing studies utilize two node-level tasks to evaluate multiplex graph methods: node
169

170
      are utilized to evaluate models on validation and test sets. For the node clustering task, common

174   clustering performance. First, they compute the cosine similarity between each pair of nodes. Then,
175   for each node, calculate the proportion of nodes with the same label among its top-K similar nodes.
176   Typically, K is set to 5.

      methods and guide future developments. Therefore, we provide a fair comparison based on the

248   4    MGB: A U NIFIED M ULTIPLEX G RAPH B ENCHMARK
249
250   Based on the results and analysis in Section 3, we summarize some issues existing in the field of
251   multiplex graph learning, which motivate the proposal of MGB.
252
253   Issues with current datasets First, existing datasets are often small and simplified, lacking
254   the complexity needed to reflect real-world scenarios. For instance, widely used versions of the
255   ACM, IMDB, and DBLP datasets (Wang et al., 2019b) contain only 3,000 to 5,000 nodes, and their
256   classification tasks are limited to mostly three-class problems. This scale is relatively small given the
257   rapid development of current graph datasets. Another limitation is the absence of high-quality
258   features in current datasets. The original data contains a wealth of textual information, but previous
259
      datasets often discard this information or process it using shallow embedding methods, such as
      bag-of-words. In some cases, like Freebase (Yang et al., 2020), no features are provided at all. This
260
      lack of high-quality features constrains models’ abilities to capture complex relationships in the
261
      data. These issues are reflected in Observation 2 of Section 3, where various methods approach
262   performance ceilings on existing datasets, reducing their discriminative ability.
263
264   Issues with current evaluations There are errors in existing classification tasks. Specifically,
265   existing evaluations on the IMDB dataset involve only single-label classifications, whereas the
266   categorizations in the IMDB dataset are actually multi-labeled. For example, the movie ”Rush Hour
267   3” belongs to Action, Comedy, and Thriller categories simultaneously. In such cases, the label space
268   may overlap, making single-label classification unreasonable. Moreover, existing methods only
269   evaluate models at the node level, lacking evaluation for edge tasks. In multiplex graphs, different
      meta-paths represent different types of edges, inherently providing conditions for edge classification

270
      and prediction tasks. However, previous works have not evaluated edge tasks, missing an opportunity
271   to assess models’ performance on a crucial aspect of multiplex graph analysis.

      4.1      BASELINES I MPLEMENTATION
274
275   As reiterated in Section 3, we have implemented ten well-recognized multiplex graph learning
276   methods within a unified training and evaluation framework using PyTorch and PyG (Fey & Lenssen,

280   et al., 2022a), MGDCR (Mo et al., 2023a), DMG (Mo et al., 2023b)) are trained in a self-supervised
281   fashion to learn node embeddings. For these self-supervised methods, a task-specific classifier is
282
      subsequently trained on labeled data to evaluate downstream tasks.
283   A brief introduction to each method is provided in Appendix B. For all reproduced methods, we offer

364

      Node Classification & Clustering For node-level tasks, we adopted node classification and clus-
366   tering tasks, following previous works. Specifically for the IMDB-MGB dataset, we corrected the
367   original single-label three-class classification task to a multi-label five-class classification task by
368   adding categories ‘Thriller’ and ‘Romance’. This enhancement increases the credibility and difficulty
369   of the node classification task. For evaluation metrics, we used Macro-F1 and Micro-F1 for node
370   classification, and Accuracy and Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) for node clustering.
371
372   Edge Prediction & Classification To address the previous neglect of edge-level tasks in existing
373   works, we proposed two metrics to evaluate model performance on edge tasks. Firstly, we employed
374   the edge prediction task, which involves determining whether a given edge exists in the graph,
375   constructed with random negative sampling to generate negative samples. This approach helps the
376   model learn to distinguish between true edges and randomly sampled negative edges. For each
377   positive edge, we sample five corresponding negative edges. To assess performance on this task, we
      utilized the area under the ROC curve (AUC-ROC) and the Precision-Recall curve (AUC-PR).


                                                                              7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Innovatively, we introduced a novel edge classification task based on multiple meta-paths within
379   multiplex graphs. This task requires the model to accurately classify existing edges in the test set,
380   associating them with specific meta-paths. Taking ACM-MGB as an example, given an existing edge,
381   the model needs to determine whether it belongs to one of the meta-paths ‘PSP, PAP, PTP’. We used
382   F1 score as the classification metric to evaluate performance on this task, providing insights into the
383   model’s ability to classify edges based on their meta-path associations.

482
483   Limitations and Future Directions A primary limitation of MGB is its current scale. Constrained by
484   the size of the original data and available computational resources, the largest dataset, Amazon-MGB,
485   contains 35,000 nodes. In contrast, existing large-scale graph benchmark OGB (Hu et al., 2020)
      support millions of nodes, presenting more challenging scenarios. Additionally, balancing the number


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      and weights of edges for different relationships (Mo et al., 2023b) requires further investigation. In
487   the future, we plan to expand MGB by incorporating more large-scale datasets and state-of-the-art
488   models.
489
490
      Conclusion In this paper, we identified and addressed significant issues in existing methods, datasets,
      and evaluation metrics in the field of multiplex graph learning. Initially, we conducted a fair
491
      comparison under the current settings. Preliminary experimental results highlighted inconsistencies
492
      in previous method comparisons, near-saturation of performance on existing low-differentiation
493   datasets, and the potential advantages of end-to-end methods in the current setting. To further address
494   the inadequacies of existing datasets and evaluation metrics, we proposed MGB, a comprehensive
495   multiplex graph benchmark that currently includes 10 unified baseline implementations, 11 diverse
496   datasets, and 4 comprehensive evaluation tasks. Further experiments on MGB revealed the limitations
497   of existing models when faced with more challenging data and tasks, prompting us to rethink the
498   design of future multiplex graph models. Specifically, models with global attention and stronger
499   expressive power in end-to-end solutions show promise.

712   B     R ELATED W ORKS
713
714   In this section, we briefly introduce the multiplex graph baseline methods reproduced in this paper.
715
      Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), including GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016) and GAT (Veličković
716   et al., 2017), encode each relationship in multiplex graphs separately and use an average readout to
717   obtain the final embeddings.
718
719
      Heterogeneous Graph Attention Network (HAN) (Wang et al., 2019b) proposes hierarchical
      attention mechanisms at both the node and relationship levels, trained end-to-end. Node-level attention
720
      learns the importance between a node and its meta-path-based neighbors, while relationship-level
721
      attention learns the importance of different meta-paths.
722
723   Multiplex Network Embedding (MNE) (Zhang et al., 2018) assigns each node a common and
724   private embedding, allowing multiple relationships to be learned jointly using the Skip-gram algorithm
725
      (Mikolov et al., 2013).
726   Deep Multiplex Graph Infomax (DMGI) (Park et al., 2020) learns embeddings for multiplex
727   graphs by maximizing the mutual information between local graph patches and the global graph
728   representation. DMGI introduces a consensus regularization framework to minimize disagreements
729   among relation-type-specific node embeddings and employs a universal discriminator to differentiate
730   true sample pairs, regardless of relation types.
731   High-order Deep Multiplex Infomax (HDMI) (Jing et al., 2021) is similar to DMGI but also
732   considers joint supervision signals, incorporating both extrinsic and intrinsic mutual information
733   through high-order mutual information.
734
      Heterogeneous Graph with Co-contrastive Learning (HeCo) (Wang et al., 2021) employs a cross-
735   view contrastive mechanism. Specifically, it proposes two views of a heterogeneous information
736   network to learn node embeddings, capturing both local and high-order structures. Additionally,
737   HeCo introduces cross-view contrastive learning and a view mask mechanism to extract positive and
738   negative embeddings from the two views.
739
      Collaborative Knowledge Distillation (CKD) (Wang et al., 2022a) models the knowledge in each
740
      meta-path with two granularities: regional and global knowledge. It learns meta-path-based embed-
741   dings by collaboratively distilling knowledge from intra-meta-path and inter-meta-path perspectives
742   simultaneously.
743
744
      Multiplex Graph Representation Learning via Dual Correlation Reduction (MGDCR) (Mo
      et al., 2023a) addresses the problem of noisy information by investigating intra- and inter-graph
745
      decorrelation losses. MGDCR also designs a simple pretext task to eliminate the need for negative
746
      sampling in contrastive learning.
747
748   Disentangled Multiplex Graph Representation Learning (DMG) (Mo et al., 2023b) disentangles
749   common and private information in multiplex graphs and designs a contrastive constraint to preserve
750
      complementarity while removing noise from private information.

858   D.1    M ULTIPLEX G RAPH P OSITIONAL E NCODING
859
860   We interpret multiplex graph positional encoding from two perspectives. One perspective involves
861   the node’s position within the entire graph, known as the absolute position. This assigns a unique
862   identification to each node, representing its global location in the overall graph. Another perspective
863   involves the node’s relative position concerning its neighbors and substructure, referred to as relative
      position. This type of position information is valuable for capturing nodes’ local relationships.


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Laplacian-PE as absolute positional encoding From the global perspective, we introduce eigen-
865   vectors of the graph Laplacian as the absolute positional encoding. Specifically, the eigenvectors are
866   computed by the factorization of the graph Laplacian matrix:
867                                                   1         1

868
                                     Lr = I − D− 2 Ar D− 2 = UTr Λr Ur ,                                 (1)
869   where Lr and Ar represent the graph Laplacian and adjacency matrix of multiplex graph Gr , respec-
870   tively. D is the graph degree matrix, I is the identity matrix, and Λr and Ur are the eigenvalues and
871
      eigenvectors of the r-th meta-path, respectively.
872   With the eigenvectors Ur , we select k-smallest values and the Laplacian PE of node v is defined as:
873
                                      uv|r = [Uv1,r , Uv2,r , ..., Uvk,r ] ∈ Rk .                             (2)
874
875   The input node embeddings are the concatenation of the feature matrix and the Laplacian PE:
876                                      Hr = Xr ∥ Ur [ : , : k].                                             (3)
877
878   For multiplex graphs with multiple meta-paths (i.e., multiple adjacency matrices), we pre-compute
879   each meta-path’s Laplacian PE and combine it with the feature matrix. Consequently, each meta-
880   path’s feature matrix contains both meta-path-specific attributive and structural information.
881
882   RandomWalk-PE as relative positional encoding From the local perspective, nodes’ relative
883
      positions or distances from each other also play a vital role. To model such relationships, we leverage
      random walk as the relative positional encoding of node pairs, acting as a soft inductive bias. The
884
      p-steps random walk matrix Φr of r-th meta-path is defined:
885
886                                             Φr = (I − βLr )p ,                                         (4)
887   where β controls the amount of diffusion value between [0.25, 0.50], and p is the number of steps in
888   the random walk. The entry Φr [i, j] indicates the possibility of node i reaching node j after a p-step
889   random walk, representing the proximity relation in the graph.

891   D.2    G RAPH S ERIALIZATION
892
      Inputting graph data into a transformer encoder poses challenges due to the computational constraints
893
      of self-attention. Transformers have a sequence length limit, whereas graph data often comprises
894   thousands of nodes, making serialization of the entire graph impractical.
895
896   To address this issue, we propose an efficient and dynamic graph serialization strategy utilizing the
897
      properties of multiplex graphs. Specifically, for a node v, we sample its first-order neighbors Nv|r
898
      under each Gr . If |Nv|r | exceeds the predefined maximum sequence length L, we randomly sample
                         for node v under different multiplex graph meta-paths, its corresponding ego-graph
      from it. This way,
899
      sequences Sv|r = v, u1 , . . . , ui , . . . |ui ∈ Nv|r are generated, with v always as the first node in the
900
      sequence. This approach allows us to dynamically and efficiently sample and serialize large graphs.

965

971   training costs. Self-supervised methods may require more resources but offer better generalization,

998
       to AUC-ROC. For example, MGDCR (Mo et al., 2023a) and DMG (Mo et al., 2023b) have similar

999
       because AUC-PR is generally a more suitable performance metric for imbalanced datasets, as it
1000
       focuses on the model’s ability to predict the minority class (positive examples). AUC-ROC, on
1001   the other hand, may mask the model’s deficiencies in predicting positive examples due to the large
1002   number of negative examples in the dataset. In our setup, with a 5:1 ratio of negative to positive
1003   edges, AUC-PR more accurately reflects model differences.
