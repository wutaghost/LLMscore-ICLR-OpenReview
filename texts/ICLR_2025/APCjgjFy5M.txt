009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Understanding visual inputs for a given task amidst varied changes is a key chal-
012
               lenge posed by visual reinforcement learning agents. We propose Value Explicit
013            Pretraining (VEP), a method that learns generalizable representations for transfer
014            reinforcement learning. VEP enables efficient learning of new tasks that share
015            similar objectives as previously learned tasks, by learning an objective-conditioned
016            encoder that is invariant to changes in environment dynamics and appearance. To
017            pre-train the encoder from a sequence of observations, we use a self-supervised
018            contrastive loss that enables the model to relate states across different tasks based
019            on the Bellman return estimate that is reflective of task progress, resulting in tem-
020            porally smooth representations that capture the objective of the task. Experiments
021
               on a realistic navigation simulator and Atari benchmark show VEP outperforms
               current SoTA pretraining methods on the ability to generalize to unseen tasks. VEP
022
               achieves up to a 2× improvement in rewards, and up to a 3× improvement in
023
               sample efficiency. For videos of policy performance visit our website.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      While performing everyday tasks, humans have an innate ability to appropriately extract information
030   from what they perceive. This is often regardless of various changes related to the appearance and
031   the dynamics of the tasks. This ability stems from understanding the objective of the tasks. While
032   this ability is natural to humans, we need to equip robots with generalizable representations of their
033   visual observations to achieve the same advantages.
034
      Unfortunately, learning generalizable representations for control is still an open problem in visual
035
      sequential decision-making. Typically in such representation learning works, an encoder ϕ is learned
036   using a large offline dataset via a predetermined objective function. Subsequently, ϕ is used for control
037   by mapping high-dimensional visual observations from the environment o:t into a lower-dimensional
038   latent representation zt . The representation zt is fed into a policy π(· | zt ) to generate an action at to
039   solve a task. The key question in visual representation learning is: what should the learned ϕ be?
040
      The challenge in learning ϕ mainly lies in discovering the correct inductive biases that yield repre-
041
      sentations that can be used to learn a variety of downstream tasks in a sample efficient manner. It is
042   unclear, however, what such useful inductive biases are. Initial approaches (Shah & Kumar, 2021;
043   Yuan et al., 2022; Parisi et al., 2022) to this problem included simply reusing pretrained vision models
044   trained to solve computer vision tasks like image recognition, zero-shot for control. Works like R3M
045   (Nair et al., 2022) and VIP (Ma et al., 2023) tried to utilize temporal consistency, enforcing images
046   that are temporally close in a video demonstration are embedded close to each other. Other works
047   like Voltron Karamcheti et al. (2023) and Masked Visual Pretraining Radosavovic et al. (2023); Xiao
048   et al. (2022); Seo et al. (2023) attempt to use image reconstruction as one such inductive bias.
049   While biases induced by pretraining objectives like image reconstruction and temporal consistency
050   have been shown to greatly improve downstream policy performance, these pretraining objectives
051   used to learn ϕ are distinct from the downstream usage of ϕ, e.g., the task of image reconstruction is
052   very different from that of action prediction. There exists an unmet need for representation learning
053   approaches that explicitly encode information directly useful for downstream control during the
      process of learning ϕ.

      This is, of course, challenging — how do we encode control-specific information without actually
069
      training online on a control task? Our crucial insight is that encoding control-specific information in
070   the representations generated by ϕ is possible by harnessing the power of Monte Carlo estimates of
071   control heuristics computed offline using gameplay datasets.
072
073
      Our key contribution is Value Explicit Pretraining (VEP), a contrastive learning approach that utilizes
      offline play datasets (without any action labels) to learn a representation for visual observations. Our
074
      method utilizes the insight that estimates of Bellman returns across multiple tasks share a similar
075
      propensity of success, and, in tasks with related goals, also share a similar optimal policy. For
076   example, in shooter games on Atari, despite differences in the visual appearances of adversaries, the
077   strategy to effectively shoot them is similar. Our approach thus focuses on the similarity of progress
078   towards the objective, as opposed to visual similarity.
079
      VEP utilizes this intuition to learn an encoder using a contrastive loss which embeds observations
080
      with similar value function estimates across a set of training tasks near each other. We investigate
081
      the performance gains obtained by utilizing the VEP representation for policy learning, both on
082   the training set of tasks and on visually distinct yet related held-out tasks. We experiment on the
083   Atari benchmark and on a visual navigation benchmark comparing VEP to state-of-the-art methods
084   like VIP Ma et al. (2023) and SOM Eysenbach et al. (2022). We find up to a 2× improvement in
085   the rewards obtained on both benchmarks and 3× improvement in sample efficiency of online RL
086   algorithms trained on VEP.

091   Representation Learning for Robotics. Besides the general idea that the representations have the
092   role of encoding the essential information for a given task, while discarding irrelevant aspects of
093
      the original data, typical state representation learning methods attempt to embed an observation
      into a latent representation that could be utilized by the downstream task Lesort et al. (2018). It
094
      is also important that these methods produce a low dimensional representation that allows the
095
      control policy to efficiently learn the downstream task. Traditionally, unsupervised methods like
096   variational autoencoders Kingma & Welling (2014), can learn disentangled representations that
097   can be used to correlate with underlying factors that cause variation in observation data Higgins
098   et al. (2017) for policy learning Ha & Schmidhuber (2018). However, in many environments, these
099   representations prove difficult to learn an optimal policy, since the temporal structure is missing
100   in these representations. Anand et al. (2019) explore this direction and learn representations by
101   enforcing temporal structure through contrastive loss.
102   Pretraining for RL. Pretraining for representation learning, in the context of RL, involves learning
103   transferable knowledge, typically in the form of good representations, that helps the agent utilize its
104   observations better Xie et al. (2022). Compared with traditional unsupervised methods for pretraining,
105   the objective of self-supervised pretraining for RL is to learn representations by exploiting the
106   underlying structure within the data distribution. Majority of the earlier online pretraining works
107   learn representations that model the task dynamics that can be learned through a sequence of
      observations during the RL procedure Pathak et al. (2019). More recent offline pretraining methods


                                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      like Schwarzer et al. (2021) build on the prior work Anand et al. (2019) by pretraining an encoder
109   using unlabeled data and then finetune on a small amount of task-specific data. In comparison
110   with these approaches, our method focuses on learning representations that not only aid in solving
111   in-distribution tasks but also can generalize to the out-of-distribution by learning representations that
112   relate to general objectives and not overfit to individual task-specific attributes.

141   Transfer after Pretraining. Transferring knowledge or skills learned from a given set of tasks to
142   an unseen set of tasks is an active research area. Early works like progressive networks Rusu et al.
143   (2016) attempt to solve it by reusing features learned from source tasks through adapters. Gamrian
144   & Goldberg (2019) perform image-to-image translation using GANs. However these methods are
145   limited to predefined source or target domains. More recent works focus on the more challenging
146   problem of using only expert videos for offline pretraining that could later be transferred to solve a
147
      novel downstream task. These methods have gained popularity in RL for their use of self-supervised
      based pretraining Sermanet et al. (2018) based on contrastive learning. Compared to these methods,
148
      our method only requires sub-optimal play data that consists of episodes that need not always be
149
      successful in achieving the task objective.
150
151   Baselines for VEP. Value Implicit Pretraining (VIP) Ma et al. (2023) encodes the goal (positive) and
152   the start (anchor) images close and the middle images (negatives) further away in the embedding
153
      space. By training on this objective through sampling multiple sub-episodes, the encoder recursively
      learns temporally smooth and continuous embeddings in a trajectory. Time Contrastive Learning
154
      (TCN) involves sampling the positive within a certain margin distance dthresh from the anchor and
155
      a negative anywhere from the positive to the end of the trajectory Sermanet et al. (2018). If the
156   anchor is sampled at time instant ta , positive is sampled at tp and the negative sampled at tn , then
157   |tn − ta | > |tp − ta |. We then use the standard triplet loss for optimization, although other contrastive
158   losses could also be used. Unlike TCN, Eysenbach et al. (2022) sample the positives from State
159   Occupancy Measure (SOM) that could be embedded close to the anchor. The negative, on the other
160   hand, is sampled anywhere from the other episodes of the same task or from the other tasks. State
161   occupancy measure at a specific instant t is a truncated geometric distribution GeoH       t (1 − γ) with
      probability mass re-distributed over the interval [t, H], where H is the horizon. Mazoure et al. (2023).

290   We consider a POMDP (Partially Observable Markov Decision Process) denoted by the tuple (O,
291   S, A, p, θ, r, T , γ) representing an observation space O, state space S, action space A, transition
292   function p, emission function θ, reward function r, time horizon T , and discount factor γ. An
293   agent in state st takes an action at and consequently causes a transition in the environment through
294   p(st+1 | st , at ). The agent receives the next observation ot+1 and reward rt that is calculated using
295   the state st and action at . The objective for the agent is to learn a policy π which maximizes the
296   expected discounted sum of rewards. The discounted sum of rewards at a state st in a trajectory τ is
297
      given by G:                                                                T

306   The value V π (st ) of a state st under a policy π intuitively defines the propensity for the success of
307
      solving a task by following policy π. If two states have similar value estimates, they likely have a
      similar expected return under π.
308
309   With this in mind, we now motivate VEP with an example. Consider the task of shooting an adversary
310   in the Atari game of Space-Invaders. Assume that there exists an optimal policy for this task
311   denoted by π ∗ (· | ot ) which operates on image observations and the associated optimal value function
          ∗
312   V π (·). Now, consider a slightly perturbed version of this game in which all the adversaries are
313   colored orange. If policy π ∗ must solve this perturbed task, it must be invariant to the color of the
314   adversary. One way to achieve this invariance is to enforce that the value estimates of states with
315
      similar propensities for success are similar, e.g., the value estimate of a state containing a bullet very
      close to an adversary should be the same regardless of whether the adversary is yellow or orange.
316
      VEP utilizes this exact intuition by learning representations that induce such an invariance. We
317
      assume access to play data from suboptimal agents doing the task (playing the game), consisting
318   of only observations and reward values (obtained sparsely) for the set of training tasks Ttrain : This
319   kind of data can be obtained from various online sources of gameplay, and does not contain any
320   action labels. Further, it is assumed to be generated by a sub-optimal agent which contains at least
321   a few positive reward signals in the gameplay. Note that, these play datasets consist of data that is
322   not always guaranteed to succeed in task completion. We also do not have access to the true reward
323   function, so we operate under a sparse reward setting, assuming that a reward of 1 at a few timesteps
      in the play data and 0 everywhere else. We now compute a value estimate to each observation using

408   5.1   E NVIRONMENTS
409
410   Atari. We used six Atari games with "FIRE" in their action set, which all are Shoot’em up games
411   similar in spirit to Space Invaders. Although all the games share a common objective of shooting
412   enemies that spawn from above, there are significant differences in appearances and dynamics across
413   games. We then split these games into Ttrain and Ttest . For pretraining the encoder, we use a play data,
414   without action labels from the D4RL datasets Fu et al. (2020). The value estimates of each frame at
415   timestep t in a sequence are then computed using 3 with T being the closest frame in the episode that
416   obtains a reward.
417   Navigation. We build an engine that loads the StreetLearn dataset Mirowski et al. (2019) to perform
418   visual navigation, based on gym Brockman et al. (2016). In a typical Navigation task, the agent is
419   designed to randomly respawn within a radius r of a predetermined location (srcx , srcy ), with the
420   objective reaching a goal location that is sampled within a radius r of a location (dx +srcx , dy +srcy ).
421   Reward acquisition is structured through a linear distribution of L reward points (including the reward
422   obtained upon reaching the target) uniformly spanning the starting point to the goal. The agent only
423
      earns rewards as it moves closer to the goal as depicted through the yellow lines in Figure 6. We have
      six cities for this benchmark, and we established consistent horizontal and vertical displacements
424
      (dx , dy ) between the starting and target points across all cities, avoiding the need for any explicit
425
      goal information (details are provided in the supplementary material). The agent is then expected to
426   transfer to an unseen test city after learning from the play data obtained from a set of cities. Note
427   that each task corresponding to a specific city is non-trivial since the agent needs to navigate in an
428   unknown city with a different map and appearance. Tasks across all the cities are all solvable within
429   a predefined horizon. Lastly, using a planner, we obtain the play data by randomly generating paths
430   with a specific distance bound. To ensure that the play data had at least a few sparse rewards, the
431   start and the end locations of the path was chosen to be between the actual source and the destination
      location. For all the tasks, we set L = 15, and r = 5.

526
527   Transferring policies to novel but related tasks is an important problem that needs to be addressed.
528   We formulated a method to learn representations of states from different tasks solely based on the
529   temporal distance to the goal frame. This way, the skills learned from the train tasks could be
530   transferred to unseen related tasks. We show the efficacy of our method by performing comprehensive
531   evaluations on Atari and Visual Navigation.

536   Our work opens new avenues for efficient training of new RL tasks by leveraging what was previously
537   learned on similar tasks. We believe that has the potential to enable a much broader use of RL in
538   real-life scenarios, as it eliminates the major hurdle of long and tedious training from scratch for each
539   new task. We do not believe this work has particular ethical concerns. Its potential transformative
      societal impact is high as it makes RL for sequential tasks more achievable than previously possible.

      A     A DDITIONAL IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
703
704
      All the baseline methods that we compare against sample a training batch of size bG and dimension
705   bT × 3 × D that consists of D sized anchor, positive and negative embedding for optimization, since
706   they focus on data only within each specific episode in the play datasets, with the hope of learning
707   generalizable representations. Unlike others, since we enforce the model to learn representations by
708   relating the tasks, our method samples a batch of size bG × bT × 3 × D. All the samples corresponding
709   to the second axis (bT ) pertain to the same value estimate and are sampled uniformly across all tasks.
710   We then create labels, such that the anchors and the positives share the same labels and the negatives
711   have independent labels.
712   We experimented with both the Triplet loss (Equation 1 in the main paper) and SNN (Soft Nearest
713   Neighbors) and obtained better results with the Triplet loss in almost all the environments.
714
      In each iteration, value lookup is performed efficiently through binary search, for each g (value
715
      estimate) takes O(log Y ) time, where Y is the number of keys in the value dictionary Vi . Sampling
716
      a positive ops within dthresh is a constant-time operation, denoted as O(1).
