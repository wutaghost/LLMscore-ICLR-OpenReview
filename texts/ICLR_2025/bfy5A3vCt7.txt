009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The implementation of data privacy regulations such as GDPR and CCPA has
012            advanced machine learning (MU) technology, which is designed to facilitate the
013            removal of specific sensitive data points from trained models upon request. Despite
014            rapid advancements in MU technology, its vulnerabilities are still underexplored,
015            posing potential risks of privacy breaches by recovering unlearned sensitive in-
016            formation. Further, existing research on MU vulnerabilities often requires access
017            to the original models, which violates the core objective of MU. To address this
018            gap, we reformulate the study of attacks against released unlearned models and
019            present the first work to explore recovery attacks on MU models without requiring
020
               access to the original model. Our approach, known as Class Membership Inducing
               Recovery Attack (CMIRA), effectively recovers forgotten data by exploiting a
021
               probing dataset. Specifically, we implement the CMIRA scheme regarding mutual
022
               knowledge distillation between MU and attack models. Extensive experiments
023            across multiple datasets and MU methods demonstrate that CMIRA exhibits high
024            efficacy in both theoretical analysis and practical applications. Our study highlights
025            the need for developing more robust MU systems and lays the groundwork for
026            future research to establish new benchmarks for evaluating their security.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   The emergence of machine unlearning (MU) is driven by stringent data privacy regulations such as
033
      GDPR (Hoofnagle et al., 2019) and CCPA (Itakura & Terada, 2018), which require the removal of
      specific sensitive data upon request. MU is designed to forget particular data points from the learned
034
      models (Cao & Yang, 2015). As concerns about the increasing data misuse and privacy breaches,
035
      MU has gained more attention as a critical component in building safe machine learning systems.
036
037   Despite rapid advancements in MU techniques (Guo et al., 2023; Bourtoule et al., 2021; Wu et al.,
038   2020; Brophy & Lowd, 2020; Gupta et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021a; Thudi et al., 2021; 2022), the
039
      study of their vulnerabilities (Hu et al., 2024) remains underexplored. This oversight poses a potential
      risk of privacy breaches by recovering information about forgotten data, highlighting the limited
040
      research to date on the full scope of MU vulnerabilities. The only existing research (Hu et al., 2024)
041
      that investigates attacks against MU models was recently published. However, this work is based on
042   the impractical assumption that unlearning inversion attacks require access to both originally learned
043   and unlearned models, as illustrated in Figure 1. In general, MU aims to release an unlearned model
044   in which the correct map Df : Xf 7→ Yf has been distorted from the original model. It is imperative
045   that the originally learned model is inaccessible to users, as such a violation may significantly increase
046   the risk of privacy breaches.
047
      To advance research in this area, we reformulate the study of attacks against released unlearned
048   models, eliminating the need to access the original models. As demonstrated in Figure 1, our objective
049   is to design an attack model to recover the correct output Yf given the input data Xf and the unlearned
050   model. As most MU models (Bourtoule et al., 2021) restrict the scope of the investigation to the
051   mature unlearning area of image classification tasks, our study is conducted in a similar way on these
052   MU models. Inspired by the membership inference attack (MIA) (Shokri et al., 2017) against machine
053   learning models, we propose a class membership inducing recovery attack (CMIRA) scheme against
      machine unlearning models.

064   Analogous to the shadow training sets (Shokri et al., 2017) for MIA, we create a probing dataset

067   we design a mutual knowledge distillation (MKD) approach in which the attack model MA can
068   iteratively recover plausible knowledge, that is, Xf 7→ Ŷf , by inducing the unlearned model MU to
069   recover the class memberships of Xf with the knowledge distilled from Dp .
070   We summarize our contributions as follows: ❶ To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt
071   to study the recovery attacks against increasingly used MU models, which can effectively assess the
072   risk of data privacy breaches and promote the robustness of MU study. ❷ We propose CMIRA, a
073   MU model-agnostic attack scheme, to effectively recover the true class memberships from mostly
074   used MU models. ❸ We implement CMIRA with a recovery attack model to recover the class
075   memberships from MU models via mutual knowledge distillation based on a probing dataset. ❹ We
076   conducted extensive experiments in four widely used datasets in MU research, demonstrating both
077   the theoretical and practical efficacy of our approach against various state-of-the-art MU methods.

079   2     R ELATED W ORK
080
081   Below, we briefly review the limited existing research on MU methods and attacks against MU.
082
083   2.1   M ACHINE U NLEARNING
084
085   Exact Unlearning. Retraining the model from scratch after removing specific data (Retrain) can
086   intuitively and effectively achieve exact unlearning. In addition, Bourtoule et al. (2021) proposed
087   SISA (Sharded, Isolated, Sliced, Aggregated) training, which trains isolated models on data shards
088   for efficient unlearning by retraining only affected shards. Since forgetting data can be regarded as
089   excluded from the training set, the success rate of unlearning can be evaluated using the Membership
090
      Inference Attack (MIA) (Shokri et al., 2017). Although effective, these unlearning approaches are
      computationally expensive and impractical for large-scale models and datasets.
091
092   Approximate Unlearning. The idea of modestly sacrificing forgetting accuracy in exchange for
093   significant improvements in unlearning efficiency has spurred exploration of approximate unlearning
094   techniques. Model fine-tuning (FT) (Warnecke et al., 2021; Golatkar et al., 2020) capitalizes on the
095   phenomenon of catastrophic forgetting (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017), achieving unlearning by fine-tuning
096
      on the retained set of data. Gradient ascent (GA) (Graves et al., 2021; Golatkar et al., 2020; Thudi
      et al., 2022) reverses model training by adding gradients, thus moving the model towards greater loss
097
      for the data points targeted for removal. Several methods estimate the impact of removed samples
098
      on model parameters and apply modifications for efficient forgetting through the fisher information
099   matrix (FF) (Becker & Liebig, 2022; Golatkar et al., 2020) or influence function (IU) (Koh & Liang,
100   2017; Izzo et al., 2021). In addition, the weight pruning (WP) adopted to improve the sparsity of
101   the model could improve the effectiveness of the data erasure (Jia et al., 2023). However, residual
102   information from unlearned data can persist in the model after approximate unlearning (Thudi et al.,
103   2022), thus raising concerns about the ongoing risk of privacy information leakage.
104
105   2.2   ATTACKS ON M ACHINE U NLEARNING
106
107   Despite advancements in unlearning techniques, the field faces significant challenges from various
      types of attacks that aim to exploit weaknesses in unlearning mechanisms. Understanding these

108
      attacks is crucial for developing robust and secure unlearning methods. Although several attacks
109   are proposed to affect the efficiency (Marchant et al., 2022) or fidelity (Di et al., 2022; Hu et al.,
110   2023) of unlearning, this section will focus on data privacy attacks that are closely aligned with the
111   objectives of this study.
112
      Model Inversion Attack. Model inversion attacks aim to reconstruct the original input data from
113
      the model’s outputs. Fredrikson et al. (2015) introduced model inversion attacks by leveraging
114
      confidence scores output by a model to reconstruct input images. Hu et al. (2024) proposed the
115   first inversion attack against unlearning. It extracts features and labels of forgetting samples, which
116   most closely match the aims of our study. Although the attack demonstrates notable effectiveness, it
117   requires access to the original model prior to unlearning, which may be impractical. It also assumes
118   limited scenarios, such as feature recovery with one single forgotten sample or label inference when
119   a single category is being forgotten. In contrast, our method only requires information from the
120   model after unlearning and supports a more versatile unlearning configuration, such as randomly
121   forgetting multiple samples from different categories. To our knowledge, we are the first to explore
122   the attack solely using unlearned models for extensive class membership recovery of samples, with
123
      no comparable prior work.

125   3   P RELIMINARIES
126
127
      In the following sections, we first introduce the datasets and models used in our study, followed by a
128   formal definition of the problem.
129
130   Involved Datasets. ◦ Training dataset Dt : Xt 7→ Yt is all the data used for initially training the
131
      machine learning models, where Xt denotes the image set and Yt denotes the corresponding label
      set. ◦ Forgetting dataset Df : Xf 7→ Yf is a subset of Dt , i.e., Df ∈ Dt . In MU, Df is a set of
132
      sensitive data that should be unlearned from the trained model, i.e., the unlearned model should not
133
      tell the truth when Xf is input. ◦ Remaining dataset Dr : Xr 7→ Yr is the remaining data of Dt ,
134   i.e., Dr = Dt \ Df , which should not be forgotten. ◦ Probing dataset Dp : Xp 7→ Yp : is a dataset
135   constructed by the attacker. In general, Xp is supposed to have a similar distribution to Xf so that it
136   is possible to infer Yf according to Dp .
137
      Involved Models. ◦ Trained model MT is the model that has been trained on training dataset Dt . ◦
138
      Unlearned model MU is the model that has unlearned the forgetting dataset Df . ◦ Attack model
139
      MA is the model that aims to recover the truth map Xf 7→ Yf from the unlearned model with the
140   probing set Dp .
141
142   Problem Formulation. MU aims to remove the influence of some targeted training data Df ∈ Dt on
143
      a trained model MT , and release a safe unlearned model MU that has forgotten the true labels Yf
      of Xf . This paper introduces CMIRA, a scheme specifically designed to recover sensitive data by
144
      exploiting vulnerabilities in various MU models that are supposed to forget it. To achieve this, we
145
      implement an attack model MA to induce the unlearned model MU to recover the class memberships
146   of Xf , i.e., the true labels Yf of Xf using a probing set Dp .

149   4   P ROPOSED M ETHOD
150
151   In this section, we present the details of the probing dataset Dp construction, various MU methods
152   addressed, and the implementation process of the proposed CMIRA scheme.
153   Overview. Figure 2 (a) Training and Unlearning illustrates the process of machine unlearning. After
154   unlearning, the true labels of Xf cannot be correctly retrieved from the unlearning model MU .
155   Inspired by MIA Shokri et al. (2017), we propose the CMIRA scheme which can effectively recover
156   the true forgotten labels through the attack model MA with an auxiliary dataset Dp to provide prior
157   knowledge of the class memberships over Xf . Figure 2 (b) Recovery Attack Scheme demonstrates
158   the workflow of CMIRA. It consists of two main stages, that is, (1) Probing Prior Learning Stage:
159   it trains attack model MA with probing dataset Dp , which aims to learn a class membership prior
160   from Dp : Xp 7→ Yp due to the similar distributions Xp and Xf ; (2) Inducing Recovery Stage: the
161
      attack model MA recovers the class memberships of Xf , by an iterative MKD process that induces
      unlearned model MU to output plausible labels Ŷf .

181   use Dt to train a model, Mt , with the parameters Θt , as shown in Figure 2. Then, we apply different
182   MU methods, as introduced in related work, on MT and result in various MU models {MU }: RT is
183   the exact MU method by retraining model parameters from scratch over the remaining dataset Dr .
184   FT trains model Mt on Dr using a few training epochs. The rationale of FT initiates catastrophic
185
      forgetting (Goodfellow et al., 2013). GA reverses the model training on Df by moving Θt towards
      the direction of increasing loss. FF adopts additive Gaussian noise on Θt . The noise has zero mean
186
      and covariance determined by the 4th root of the Fisher Information matrix on Dr . IU leverages
187
      the influence function approach to characterize the change in Θt if a training point is removed from
188   the training loss. WP applies binary mask m over model parameters Θt , where m is determined by
189   one-shot magnitude pruning (OMP) (Chen et al., 2021b) on Df .

      Generation of Probing Dataset. Since both of these two stages are based on the probing dataset Dp ,
197   we first present how to generate Dp . In MIA (Shokri et al., 2017), the attack models are trained with
198   shadow training data that is distributed similarly to the target model’s training data. Based on the
199   similar attacking strategy, we need to construct a probing dataset Dp that has a similar distribution to
200   the forgetting dataset Dp to perform the class membership recovery attack. Dp can be easily created
201   in the following two ways: ❶ Image search: The image set Xp is retrieved by the query set Xf from
202   the image database. As a result, Dp : Xp 7→ Yp is constructed. Note that we may need to align the
203   label set of the image database with the training label set. ❷ Same data source: If the training data is
204   collected from some known data source, we can generate Dp by sampling data from this data source.
205
      In general, the datasets collected from the same data source have a similar distribution.

      Datasets. Four widely used image classification datasets are used in our experiments. These include
      Cifar-10 and Cifar-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), which consists of 32x32 color images with 10
298
      and 100 classes respectively; Tiny-ImageNet-200 (TinyImg (Le & Yang, 2015), which contains
299
      200 classes of 64x64 color images; and Fashion-MNIST (FMNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), a dataset
300   featuring 28x28 grayscale images of 10 different apparel items.
301
302   Target Machine Unlearning Models. Since CMIRA is an MU model-agnostic attack scheme, we
303
      comprehensively evaluated the performance of CMIRA against six SOTA MU models as described in
      Proposed Method, including Retrain (RT), Fine-Tune (FT), Gradient Ascend (GA), Fish Forgetting
304
      (FF), Influence Unlearning (IU), and Weight Prune (WP). Moreover, we further assessed the perfor-
305
      mance of two representative backbone architectures for image classification: ResNet18 (He et al.,
306   2016) and VGG16 (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014). These two model architectures are widely used
307   in the evaluation of SOTA MU methods. We first trained the RestNet18 and VGG16 models over
308   the training set Dt for each experimental dataset, i.e., Cifar-10, Cifar-100, TinyImage and FMNIST.
309   Then, each of the above MU methods was performed on the trained models to obtain the various
310   unlearned models. This diversity of MU models over different datasets and backbone architectures
311   allows for a comprehensive assessment of CMIRA’s efficacy in various MU scenarios.
312   Experimental Details. The official dataset splits (e.g., Cifar-10: 80% for training, 20% for testing)
313   are used for the evaluation of MU methods. The training set is used as Dt to train the backbone models.
314   Five classes from Dt are selected to construct the forgetting dataset Df by randomly sampling 50%
315   of data from them. In general, the split set for testing is similarly distributed to Df since they were
316   collected from the same data source. Therefore, the testing split is a suitable data source to generate
317   the probing dataset Dp as described in Proposed Method. All experiments utilized an SGD optimizer
318   and were conducted on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. More details of the experiments can be found in the
319   supplementary materials.

      6    C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this study, we present Class Membership Inducing Recovery Attack (CMIRA), a novel attack
481   method that can recover true class memberships from machine unlearning (MU) models without
482   needing access to the original model. By using mutual knowledge distillation (MKD) with a probing
483   dataset, CMIRA effectively retrieves forgotten labels. Our experiments with four widely used datasets
484   show that CMIRA is both theoretically sound and practically effective against various MU methods.
485   Our findings highlight the need for future research to focus on developing more robust MU systems
      and establish new benchmarks for evaluating their security.

      B.2   A REA OF M EMBERSHIP R ECOVERY (AR )
683
684
      It evaluates the recovery capability of CMIRA from a multi-class perspective. We define the concept
685   of the Membership Recovery Polygon (MRP) to facilitate the evaluation. The polygon is generated
686   through the radar chart, where each vertex of the polygon in the radar chart corresponds to a specific
687   class, with the distance from the center to the point indicating the accuracy level of that class (e.g.
688   AcciU or AcciA for the i-th class). By plotting these points and connecting them sequentially, the
689   Membership Recovery polygon (MRP) is formed. This graphical representation provides an intuitive
690   overview of performance in different classes, highlighting recovery effectiveness in a comparative
691   context.
692   We further obtain the area of the polygon, AA for MA and AU for MU :
693
694   • AU : This metric indicates that the smaller it is, the better the unlearning effect of MU , meaning
695     the poorer the membership memory retention, which is the goal of various unlearning methods.
696
      • AA : This metric indicates that the larger it is, the better the recovery effect of MA , meaning the
697
        better the membership recovery after the attack.
698
699
