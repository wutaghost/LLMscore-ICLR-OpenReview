009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper challenges the conventional belief that softmax attention in transformers
012            is effective primarily because it generates a probability distribution for attention
013            allocation. Instead, we theoretically show that its success lies in its ability to
014            implicitly regularize the Frobenius norm of the attention matrix during training.
015            We then explore alternative activations that regularize the Froebnius norm of the
016            attention matrix, demonstrating that certain polynomial activations can achieve this
017            effect, making them suitable for attention-based architectures. Empirical results
018
               indicate these activations perform comparably or better than softmax across various
               computer vision and language tasks, suggesting new possibilities for attention
019
               mechanisms beyond softmax.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      Transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017) have become the state-of-the-art model architecture
025   for diverse areas such as natural language processing (NLP) (Vaswani et al., 2017; Devlin et al., 2018;
026   Zhuang et al., 2021; Zhen et al., 2022), computer vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Carion et al., 2020;
027   Liu et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2021), and robotics (Fu et al., 2024; Maiti et al., 2023; Salzmann
028   et al., 2020). A key component in the transformer architecture is the softmax attention block, enabling
029   transformers to evaluate the importance of individual input elements during output generation.
030   This feature facilitates an efficient method to attend to diverse input elements throughout training,
031   allowing transformers to effectively capture spatial dependencies within sequential data. Unlike
032   traditional recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and convolutional neural networks (CNNs), transformers
033
      exhibit the ability to scale to large datasets without a significant degradation in performance. This
      characteristic has made them an ideal architecture for handling large-scale machine learning tasks.
034
035   Softmax is widely recognized for its effectiveness in attention mechanisms due to its ability to produce
036   an attention matrix that meets three key conditions (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020;
037   Zhen et al., 2022; AUEB et al., 2016): (i) non-negativity, (ii) rows that are normalized to sum to 1 and
038   (iii) sparsity. The general consensus is that non-negativity guarantees that attention weights remain
039
      positive, facilitating the model in assigning significance to various input elements. The normalization
      constraint ensures that the attention weights for all input elements collectively sum to 1, rendering the
040
      weights interpretable as probabilities. Additionally, sparsity aids the model in focusing on a select
041
      few key elements, thereby enhancing efficiency. It has been argued that these properties are crucial
042   for enabling the attention mechanism to attend to pertinent segments of the input sequence while
043   efficiently filtering out irrelevant details. However, this approach to attention has become somewhat
044   axiomatic as it is mostly motivated by empirical results with little theoretical foundation. Despite the
045   exploration of alternative activations in several studies (Shen et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2022; Correia
046   et al., 2019), softmax attention continues to dominate, largely due to its interpretability.
047
      In this paper, we question this view by proposing that the effectiveness of softmax stems from its
048   implicit regularization of the Frobenius norm of the attention matrix during training, preventing
049   attention weights from becoming excessively large or small. We then derive a theoretical framework
050   that produces polynomial activations that deliberately violate one or more of the three conditions
051   mentioned earlier, yet are able to regularize the Frobenius norm of the attention weights during
052   training. Our findings demonstrate that such activations can achieve comparable or even superior
053   performance to softmax across various vision and natural language processing (NLP) tasks, even
      though they seem to violate our understanding of attention.

      We advise the reader that this paper diverges from the usual pursuit of creating cutting-edge trans-
055   former architectures for achieving state-of-the-art results on benchmark datasets. Instead, our focus
056   is on critically examining softmax attention to determine whether its effectiveness is a result of true
057   interpretability or a more nuanced regularization mechanism. By questioning established views, we
058   aim to uncover deeper insights into transformer architectures that could lead to broader applications
059   and improved understanding. Nonetheless, we validate our theory on multiple transformer-based
060   tasks, including image classification, segmentation, object detection, and NLP, often achieving results
061   that match or surpass those of softmax attention. Our main contributions are:
062
063
            1. We question the widely accepted notion that softmax’s effectiveness in attention mechanisms
               is solely due to its ability to produce normalized sparse attention weights. Instead, we
064
               theoretically show that softmax has a regularization effect on attention and argue this plays
065
               a crucial role in its success.
066
067         2. We explore activations that deliberately deviate from traditional softmax attention conditions.
068            These activations are found to regularize the Frobenius norm of the attention matrix during
069
               training, akin to softmax, and demonstrate comparable or superior performance across
               various vision and NLP tasks.

072   2   R ELATED W ORK
073
074   Several studies have explored alternative activations for attention mechanisms in transformers.
075   Shen et al. (2023) investigated ReLU activations, finding them to outperform softmax in tasks
076   with long sequences, such as document translation. Banerjee et al. (2020) examined Taylor series
077   approximations to softmax, which showed superior performance to softmax in image classification.
078   Wang et al. (2021) proposed periodic alternatives to softmax, designed to provide better gradients
079   for attention mechanisms and achieved better results than softmax on simple networks for image
080   classification. Koohpayegani & Pirsiavash (2024) demonstrated that applying l1 normalization to
081   linear attention mechanisms can achieve performance comparable to softmax. Our work differs from
082
      all these in that we identify a clear theoretical relationship between the scale of the Frobenius norm
      of the self-attention matrix and the input sequence length. Using this insight to derive potential
083
      acitvations that can perform on par with softmax.

135   4.1   I MPLICIT REGULATIZATION OF S OFTMAX
136
      This section presents a theoretical result showing that the softmax activation imposes control over
137
      the Frobenius norm of the self-attention matrix in a way that grows sub-linearly with the input
138
      sequence’s token length. Additionally, we demonstrate that the gradient of the softmax with respect
139   to the self-attention matrix also exhibits a similar degree of regularity. While previous work has
140   analyzed the regularity of softmax self-attention through the lens of the Lipschitz constant (Kim et al.,
141   2021; Castin et al., 2023), our theorem offers a novel perspective by directly linking the Frobenius
142   norm regularity to the token length. This provides insights into how self-attention activations should
143   scale with token length to maintain stability during training, especially with gradient descent-based
144   algorithms.
145   Theorem 4.1. Let softmax : RN ×N → RN ×N be the matrix softmax map defined by equation 3.4
146   and let ∇softmax(A) : RN ×N → RN ×N denote the gradient of softmax at A ∈ RN ×N . We
147   then have the following bounds on the Frobenius norms
                                                                   √
148                                         ||softmax(A)||F ≤ N                                         (4.1)
149                                                                 √
150                                       ||∇softmax(A)||F ≤ 2 N .                                      (4.2)
151
      The key implication of theorem 4.1 is that during the training of a transformer with softmax self-
152
      attention, the√Frobenius norm of each softmax self-attention matrix remains bounded by a value that
153
      grows as O( N ). This ensures that backpropagation through the weights of the self-attention matrix
154   does not lead to excessively large gradients. The proof hinges on the fact that the row normalization
155   inherent in softmax effectively controls the Frobenius norm. For a detailed proof see appendix A.1.1.

519   6     L IMITATIONS
520
521   While our work introduces novel activations that challenge the conventional softmax approach, there
522
      are some limitations to address. Our theoretical framework is primarily designed for dot-product
      self-attention and may not immediately extend to other attention mechanisms, although our empirical
523
      results showed competitive performance against softmax across different architectures. Additionally,
524
      we observed that while our activations performed well on vision tasks, their performance was less
525   consistent on NLP tasks, suggesting that a more refined theoretical approach may be needed for these
526   applications.

      7     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      This work challenges the traditional view that transformer activations for attention must produce
      sparse probability distributions. We introduced a theoretical framework analyzing the Frobenius
532
      norm of the self-attention matrix, which suggests key scaling properties for activations in attention
533
      mechanisms. We proved that specific polynomial activations, which behave very differently from
534   softmax, satisfy these properties. Through extensive experiments across vision and NLP tasks, we
535   demonstrated that these alternative activations not only compete with but sometimes outperform
536   softmax, offering a fresh perspective on attention mechanisms in transformers.

903   A.2.1    A BLATIONS
904
905   In this section we carry out ablations on the experiments we did in the main paper.
906
907   Scale ablations: The theory we developed in section 4.2 suggested     √ that we needed to scale our
908   polynomial activations by √1N to obtain a complexity bound of O( N ). In general, we could also
909   scale the polynomial activations by O( √1N ) to see if we can get a better accuracr. Figure 10 carries
910   out an ablation on both the ViT-Base architecture and the ViT-Small architecture to see how different
911   scales affect the accuracy for the x3 activation. We found that in general a scale from 18 to 25
                                                                                                    1
                                                                                                       seemed
912   to perform very well.
913
914   Activation ablations: Our theory primarily compared the activations √1N x3 and √1N x with softmax
915   where N was the sequence length of the input. In this section we carry out further experimental
916   comparisons of our activations with other activations. We compare with a variety of activations used
917   in the literature as well as the exponential function Exp = e−x which can be thought of as softmax
      without the row normalization scaling. Table 5 shows the results of the experiments.
