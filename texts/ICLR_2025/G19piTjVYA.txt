009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011            In the algorithm Intersort, Chevalley et al. (2024) proposed a score-based method to
012            discover the causal order of variables in a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) model, leveraging
013            interventional data to outperform existing methods. However, as a score-based method over
014            the permutahedron, Intersort is computationally expensive and non-differentiable, limiting
015            its ability to be utilised in problems involving large-scale datasets, such as those in genomics
016            and climate models, or to be integrated into end-to-end gradient-based learning frameworks.
017
               We address this limitation by reformulating Intersort using differentiable sorting and
               ranking techniques. Our approach enables scalable and differentiable optimization of
018
               causal orderings, allowing the continuous score function to be incorporated as a regularizer
019
               in downstream tasks. Empirical results demonstrate that causal discovery algorithms benefit
020            significantly from regularizing on the causal order, underscoring the effectiveness of our
021            method. Our work opens the door to efficiently incorporating regularization for causal order
022            into the training of differentiable models and thereby addresses a long-standing limitation
023            of purely associational supervised learning.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Causal discovery is fundamental for understanding complex systems by identifying underlying causal
028
      relationships from data. It has significant applications across various fields, including biology (Meinshausen
029
      et al., 2016; Chevalley et al., 2022; 2023), medicine (Feuerriegel et al., 2024), and social sciences (Imbens &
030   Rubin, 2015), where causal insights inform decision-making and advance scientific knowledge. Traditionally,
031   causal discovery has relied heavily on observational data due to the practical challenges and costs associated
032   with conducting large-scale interventional experiments. However, observational data alone often necessitates
033   strong assumptions about the data distribution to ensure identifiability beyond the Markov equivalence class
034   (Spirtes et al., 2000; Shimizu et al., 2006; Hoyer et al., 2008).
035
      The emergence of large-scale interventional datasets, particularly in domains like single-cell genomics
036   (Replogle et al., 2022; Datlinger et al., 2017; Dixit et al., 2016), introduces new opportunities and challenges
037   for causal discovery. Interventional data, obtained through targeted manipulations of variables, offers a
038   unique perspective by revealing causal mechanisms that may be obscured in purely observational studies.
039   Recently (Chevalley et al., 2024) introduced Intersort, which uses the notion of interventional faithfulness
040   and a score on causal orders, enabling the inference of causal orderings by comparing marginal distributions
041   across observational and interventional settings.
042   Despite its promising performance, Intersort faces significant limitations. Specifically, it lacks differentiability,
043   which hinders its integration into gradient-based learning frameworks commonly used in modern machine
044   learning. Additionally, scalability remains a challenge, making this method impractical for applications
045   involving large numbers of variables—a common scenario in fields such as genomics (Replogle et al., 2022;
046   Chevalley et al., 2022) and neuroscience that involve up to tens of thousands of variables.

047
      In this work, we address these challenges by extending Intersort to a continuous realm to make it both
048
      differentiable and scalable. We achieve this by expressing the score in terms of a potential function and
049   utilizing differentiable sorting and ranking techniques, including the Sinkhorn operator (Cuturi, 2013). This
050   novel formulation allows us to incorporate a useful inductive bias into downstream tasks as a differentiable
051   constraint or regularizer, enabling seamless integration into gradient-based optimization frameworks.
052
      We develop a causal discovery algorithm that integrates differentiable Intersort score into its objective
053
      function. Our empirical evaluations on diverse simulated datasets—including linear, random Fourier features,
054
      gene regulatory networks (GRNs) and neural network models—demonstrate that the proposed regularized
055   algorithm significantly outperforms baseline methods such as GIES (Hauser & Bühlmann, 2012) and DCDI
056   (Brouillard et al., 2020) on RFF and GRN data. Moreover, we demonstrate that our approach exhibits
057   robustness across different data distributions and noise types. The algorithm efficiently scales with large
058   datasets, maintaining consistent performance regardless of data size.
059
      Our contributions pave the way for fully leveraging interventional data in various causal tasks in a scalable
060
      and differentiable manner. By addressing the limitations of existing methods, we enable the application of
061   interventional faithfulness in large-scale settings and facilitate its integration into modern causal machine
062   learning pipelines.

067   2   R ELATED WORK
068
069
      Causal discovery is a fundamental problem in machine learning and statistics, aiming to uncover causal
070
      relationships from data. Traditional methods can be broadly categorized into constraint-based, score-based,
071
      and functional approaches. Algorithms like PC (Spirtes et al., 2000) and FCI (Spirtes, 2001) use conditional
072   independence tests to infer causal structures. These methods often require faithfulness and causal sufficiency
073   assumptions, which may not hold in real-world scenarios. Score-based approaches, such as GES (Chickering,
074   2002) and GIES (Hauser & Bühlmann, 2012), search over possible graph structures to maximize a goodness-
075   of-fit score. More recently, Zheng et al. (2018) introduced NOTEARS, a differentiable approach that
076   formulates causal discovery as a continuous optimization problem using an acyclicity constraint. Most
077   recently proposed models follow the idea of NOTEARS and are thus continuously differentiable, e.g. using
078   neural networks to model the functional relationships (Brouillard et al., 2020; Lachapelle et al., 2019).
079   Chevalley et al. (2024) recently introduced Intersort, a score-based algorithm to derive the causal order of
080   the variables when many single-variable interventions are available. Intersort relies on a light assumption
081   on the changes in marginal distributions across observational and interventional settings, as measure by a
082   statistical distance (see definition 3 subsequently). Intersort is a two-step algorithm, where the first step,
083   SORTRANKING, finds an initial ordering by taking into account the scale of the measured distances, and
084   the second step, LOCALSEARCH, search in a close neighbourhood in permutation space to improve the
085   score. Their algorithm suffers from scalability issues, as the second step, LOCALSEARCH, becomes
086
      computationally intractable for large datasets. Moreover, it is unclear how this inductive bias can be applied
      to downstream causal task, as the score is not differentiable with respect to a causal order and thus it cannot
087
      be integrated into continuously differentiable models. Previous to Intersort, most methods to infer the causal
088
      order of a system worked primarily on observational data. For example, EASE (Gnecco et al., 2021) leverages
089   extreme values to identify the causal order. Reisach et al. (2021) observed that the performance of continuously
090   differentiable causal discovery models on synthetic datasets strongly correlates with varsortability, which
091   measures to what extent the marginal variance corresponds to the true causal order. Similar ideas were
092   consequently developed to derive the causal order based on score matching (Rolland et al., 2022; Montagna
093   et al., 2023a;b).

      Let (M, d) be a metric space, and let P(M) denote the set of probability measures over M. We define D
101
      to be a statistical distance function D : P(M) × P(M) → [0, ∞) that measures the divergence between
102   probability distributions on M. Consider a set of d random variables X = (X1 , X2 , . . . , Xd ) indexed by
103   V = {1, 2, . . . , d}, with joint distribution PX . We denote the marginal distribution of each variable as PXi
104   for i ∈ V . A causal graph is a tuple G = (V, E) of nodes and edges that form a Directed Acyclic Graph
105   (DAG), where V is the set of nodes (variables), and E ⊆ V × V is the set of directed edges representing
106   causal relationships. An edge (i, j) ∈ E indicates that variable Xi is a direct cause of variable Xj . Let AG
107   be the adjacency matrix of G, where AGij = 1 if (i, j) ∈ E, and AGij = 0 otherwise. For each node j ∈ V ,
108   the set of parents Pa(j) consists of all nodes with edges pointing to j, i.e., Pa(j) = {i ∈ V | (i, j) ∈ E}.
109   We denote the set of descendants of node i as DeG (i), which includes all nodes reachable from i via directed
110   paths. Similarly, the set of ancestors of i is denoted as AnG (i).
111   An SCM C = (S, PN ) consists of a set of structural assignments S and a joint distribution over exogenous
112   noise variables PN . Each variable Xj is assigned via a structural equation:

115   where Nj is an exogenous noise variable, and XPa(j) are the parent variables of Xj .
116   In our work, we focus on interventions that modify the structural assignments of certain variables. Specifically,
117   we consider interventions where the structural assignment of a variable Xk is replaced by a new exogenous
118   variable Ñk , independent of its parents Xk = Ñk .
119   Definition 1. A causal order is a permutation π : V → {1, 2, . . . , d} such that for any edge (i, j) ∈ E, we
120   have π(i) < π(j). This ensures that causes precede their effects in the ordering (Peters et al., 2017).
121
122   Since G is acyclic, at least one causal order exists, though it may not be unique. We denote the set of all

309
      We next evaluate the proposed DiffIntersort differentiable score both in its effectiveness in deriving the causal
      order of a system, as well as it usefulness as a differentiable constraint in a causal discovery model.
310
311   We first evaluate the DiffIntersort score in it ability to recover the causal order in simulated graphs and
312   distance matrices. We here reproduce the experiment of (Chevalley et al., 2024). We compare the top order
313   divergence of DiffIntersort to SORTRANKING, and to Intersort for 5 and 30 variables, and the upper-bounds
314   of Thm 2 and Thm 4 derived in (Chevalley et al., 2024). Intersort does not scale beyond 100 variables. The
315
      upper-bounds serve as a sanity check to assess how far to the true optimum of the score the approximate
      solution is. We evaluate on both Erdős-Rényi distribution (Erdős et al., 1960) and scale-free network modelled
316
      by the Barabasi-Albert distribution Albert & Barabási (2002), with varying edge densities and intervention
317
      coverage. The results are reported in fig. 1 for 2000 variables and in figs. 5 and 6 for 5, 30, 100 and 1000
318   variables. It is crucial that our score is optimizable up to at least 2000 variables as it is a common scale in real
319   world datasets such as single-cell transcriptomics (Replogle et al., 2022). As observed, DiffIntersort fulfills
320   the upper-bounds for all settings, even at large scale. At large scale, it also outperforms SORTRANKING
321   in almost all settings. Those results validate our proposed approach of solving the Intersort problem in a
322   continuous and differentiable framework, and guarantees that it is not limited by scale.

439
      to be integrated as a continuous regularizer in gradient-based learning frameworks, facilitating its use in
      downstream causal discovery tasks. Our proposed approach not only preserves the theoretical advantages
440
      of Intersort but also significantly improves its practical applicability to large-scale problems. Empirical
441
      evaluations demonstrate that incorporating the differentiable Intersort score into a causal discovery algorithm
442   leads to superior performance compared to existing methods, particularly in complex settings involving
443   non-linear relationships and large numbers of variables. The algorithm exhibits robustness across various data
444   distributions and noise types, effectively scaling with increasing data size without compromising performance.
445   By bridging the gap between interventional faithfulness and differentiable optimization, our work opens new
446   avenues for integrating interventional data into modern causal machine learning pipelines. This advancement
447   holds promise for a wide range of applications dealing with large covariate sets where understanding causal
448   relationships is crucial, such as genomics, neuroscience, environmental, and social sciences.
449   While our approach enhances the scalability and differentiability of causal discovery using interventional
450   data, several avenues for future research remain. One potential direction is to integrate our differentiable
451   Intersort score into more complex models, such as deep neural networks, to further improve causal discovery
452   in high-dimensional and highly non-linear settings. Another promising area is the application of the differen-
453   tiable Intersort approach to real-world datasets in domains like genomics or healthcare, where large-scale
454   interventional data are increasingly available.
