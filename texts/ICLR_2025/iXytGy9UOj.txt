011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Mixed precision quantization has become an important technique for enabling the
014
               execution of deep neural networks (DNNs) on limited resource computing plat-
               forms. Traditional quantization methods have primarily concentrated on main-
015
               taining neural network accuracy, either ignoring the impact of quantization on the
016
               robustness of the network, or using only empirical techniques for improving ro-
017            bustness. In contrast, techniques for robustness certification, which can provide
018            strong guarantees about the robustness of DNNs have not been used during quan-
019            tization due to their high computation cost.
020            This paper introduces ARQ, an innovative mixed-precision quantization method
021            that not only preserves the clean accuracy of the smoothed classifiers but also
022            maintains their certified robustness. ARQ uses reinforcement learning to find
023            accurate and robust DNN quantization, while efficiently leveraging randomized
024            smoothing, a popular class of statistical DNN verification algorithms, to guide
025            the search process. We compare ARQ with multiple state-of-the-art quantization
026            techniques on several DNN architectures commonly used in quantization studies:
027
               ResNet-20 on CIFAR-10, ResNet-50 on ImageNet, and MobileNetV2 on Ima-
               geNet. We demonstrate that ARQ consistently performs better than these base-
028
               lines across all the benchmarks and the input perturbation levels. In many cases,
029
               the performance of ARQ quantized networks can reach that of the original DNN
030            with floating-point weights, but with only 1.5% instructions.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Mixed precision quantization has become an important technique for enabling the execution of deep
036   neural networks (DNNs) on limited resource computing platforms. Quantization of an original DNN
037   model with floating-point weights/activations significantly reduces the model size and the complex-
038
      ity of operations, while retaining the model’s accuracy (Wang et al., 2019). However, quantizing the
      model also reduces its robustness, i.e., the ability of the network to produce a correct classification
039
      in the presence of (even small) adversarial input perturbations.
040
041   To alleviate this problem, certified DNN robustness techniques provide formal guarantees that the
042   DNN will classify all small perturbations with the same label as the original input. These techniques
043   are designed to protect against a broad scope of possible adversarial inputs, unlike commonly used
044
      empirical robustness techniques, which defend against only a specific kind of adversarial inputs,
      typically in a best-effort fashion (Raghunathan et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023).
045
046   Despite their desirability, certified robustness techniques, have a high computational cost. Many
047   deterministic techniques that have been developed have been used only on small networks and
048   datasets (Singh et al., 2019; Lechner et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). Statistical methods for ro-
049   bustness certification based on randomized smoothing (RS) (Cohen et al., 2019) currently offer the
050
      greatest scalability. Yet, due to their cost, RS and other similar techniques have not been used during
      quantization but only at the end of the optimization to characterize the level of robustness of the final
051
      quantized network. Using these techniques during model quantization is an open question.
052
053   Our Work: ARQ. We present ARQ, a novel robustness-aware mixed-precision quantization frame-
      work for neural networks. ARQ demonstrates for the first time that robustness certification can be


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      included as a part of the search for optimal quantization of an original DNN. ARQ’s algorithm takes a
055   pre-trained DNN and encodes a reinforcement learning (RL) problem that searches for quantization
056   policies – the bit-widths of the weights/activations of all layers in the DNN – that (1) preserve DNN’s
057   accuracy, (2) improve its robustness, and (3) reduce the model’s computation cost. This design al-
058   lows ARQ to support mixed-precision quantization (MPQ), in which the weights in each layer can
059   be quantized with different bit-widths, thus giving fine-grained control over the possible policies.
060
      The key insight behind ARQ’s approach is that the optimization for both accuracy and robustness
061   aims to maximize the DNN’s certified radius, which characterizes all the slightly perturbed inputs
062   that the DNN classifies with the same label as the non-perturbed input. This optimization objective
063   fits well within the RL framework. ARQ’s algorithm also makes it possible to leverage recent ap-
064   proaches for incremental analysis of certified robustness to speed up the quantization policy search.
065
      We compare ARQ with existing searching and learning-based mixed precision quantization meth-
066
      ods that only optimize for accuracy. The baselines include HAQ (Wang et al., 2019) (which uses
067   reinforcement learning) and LIMPQ (Tang et al., 2023) (which uses integer programming) and fixed-
068   precision quantization method PACT (Choi et al., 2018). We considered three DNN architectures
069   commonly used in quantization studies: ResNet-20 on CIFAR-10, ResNet-50 on ImageNet, and
070   MobileNetV2 on ImageNet.
071
      We demonstrate that ARQ consistently performs better than these baselines across all the bench-
072
      marks and the input noise levels. In many cases, ARQ can even reach, and sometimes even improve
073   on the accuracy and robustness of the original floating-point network, but with only 1.5% operations.
074
075   Contributions. The paper makes the following contributions:
076
          • Approach: We present ARQ, the first approach for mixed-precision quantization that opti-
077         mizes for certified robustness of DNNs. It poses an optimization problem that maximizes the
078         certified radius for a bounded resource usage cost (e.g., compute instructions, model size).
079
          • Framework: ARQ’s algorithm incorporates randomized smoothing within the reinforcement
080         learning loop, which enables it to find certifiably robust quantized networks.
081
          • Results: Our experiments on three commonly used networks/datasets show that ARQ consis-
082
            tently performs better than the state-of-the-art quantization techniques.

087   2.1   M IXED P RECISION Q UANTIZATION
088
      Neural network quantization is a model compression technique that can reduce a network’s size
089
      and compute cost. Quantization applies to float-valued weights and activations in the network and
090   converts them to integer values of certain bit-widths. Using the same bit-width for the entire network
091   is sub-optimal because some layers are more amenable to quantization than others.
092
093
      Mixed Precision Quantization assigns different bit-widths per weight or activation in a network and
      searches for the best combination of bit-widths. A quantization policy P is a sequence of bit-width
094
      assignments to each layer in the network. For a network of L layers, where each layer has N bit-
095
      width options {b1 , b2 , . . . , bN } for both weights and activations, there are N 2L combinations of
096   quantization policies. We can then formulate the process of optimizing the quantization policy for a
097   network N as the following mathematical optimization problem:
098
                            Poptimal = arg max Acc(fP (x), y) s.t. Cost(fP ) < C0                        (1)
099                                         P ∈P
100                                                   1     X

                                   Acc(f (x), y) =                   1(f (x) = y)                        (2)
                                                     |X|
                                                           (x,y)∈X

103   Here P denotes the space of all quantization policies and Poptimal ∈ P is the optimal policy that
104
      maximizes Acc(fP (x)) on dataset X, the accuracy of the quantized network fP (x). Cost(fP ) is
      the resource usage of the network, such as the model size, the number of compute bit operations or
105
      energy consumption, and C0 is a user-specified bound on the resource.

107   Reinforcement Learning Based Quantization. Wang et al. (2019); Lou et al. (2020) have intro-
      duced Reinforcement Learning (RL) based approaches to search for quantization policies. One

110   the environment (the neural network) by observing the state Sk (the configuration of the kth layer),
111   taking an action ak (the quantization bit-width), and receiving a Reward (the resulting accuracy).

113   2.2   C ERTIFIED N EURAL N ETWORK ROBUSTNESS
114
115   A classifier is considered certifiably robust when its predictions are guaranteed to remain consistent
116
      within a neighborhood of input x. Consider a classification problem from Rm to classes Y. Let
      f : Rm → Y be a neural network classifier. We seek a smoothed classifier g : Rm → Y, whose
117
      prediction matches that from f for any input x and is constant within some neighborhood of x.
118
      Randomized smoothing (Cohen et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020) provides a way
119   to construct such a smoothed classifier g from the base classifier f . When queried at x, g returns the
120   class that f is most likely to return when x is perturbed by Gaussian noise:
121
122                       g(x) := arg max P(f (x + ε) = c) where ε ∼ N (0, σ 2 I)                        (3)
                                     c∈Y
123
124   Cohen et al. (2019) show that g’s prediction is constant within an l2 ball around any input x. The
125   radius of that ball, R(x), is known as the certified radius. ε is the Gaussian noise added on the
126
      input, sampled from Gaussian distribution of mean 0 and variance σ 2 I (I is the identity matrix). σ
      is the noise level, a hyperparameter of the smoothed classifier g independent of the input x. The
127
      certified accuracy of a classifier is defined as the probability that the classifier correctly predicts
128
      the true labels of samples x for which the certified radius R(x) exceeds a certain threshold r. The
129   clean accuracy is the certified accuracy when r = 0.
130
      Theorem 1 (From Cohen et al. (2019)) Suppose cA ∈ Y, pA , pB ∈ [0, 1]. if
131
132                         P(f (x + ϵ) = cA ) ≥ pA ≥ pB ≥ max P(f (x + ϵ) = c),                         (4)
133                                                            c̸=cA

134   then g(x + δ) = cA for all δ satisfying ∥δ∥2 ≤ σ2 (Φ−1 (pA ) − Φ−1 (pB )), where Φ−1 denotes the
135   inverse of the standard Gaussian CDF.
136
137   Computing the exact probabilities pA , pB from Eqn. 4 is intractable in general. For practical ap-
138   plications, RS certification utilizes sampling to estimate pA and pB using the Clopper-Pearson
139   method (Clopper & Pearson, 1934). If using this procedure yields pA > 0.5, then RS algorithm
140   sets pB = 1 − pA and computes the certified radius as
141
                                              R(x) = σ · Φ−1 (pA )                                       (5)
142
143   via Theorem 1, else it returns ABSTAIN, i.e., it cannot prove the certified robustness.

      3     ARQ A PPROACH

      3.1   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
148
149   ARQ provides a mixed precision quantization method that optimizes both the robustness and accu-
150   racy of the quantized smoothed classifier gP from a base classifier f .
151
      Quantization Challenges. To improve the robustness of the quantized smoothed classifier gP , one
152
      could naively replace the accuracy metric in the formulation of an existing MPQ method (Eqn. 1)
153   with an accuracy metric for the base classifier fP that uses Gaussian noise-perturbed inputs. This
154   approach does not significantly improve the robustness of the quantized smoothed classifier gP ,
155   based on the following three observations:
156
157       • By taking one perturbed sample per data point, this accuracy metric is highly affected by
158         randomness and does not capture the robustness of gP well.
159       • The accuracy of base classifier fP correlates directly with the average lower bound probability
160         (pA ) as stated in Eqn. 4. However, improving the quantized base classifier fP ’s accuracy on
161         samples with pA < 0.5 does not improve the quantized smoothed classifier gP ’s accuracy.
            Because for samples where pA < 0.5, the certified radius is less than zero, indicating that the


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
           quantized smoothed classifier gP cannot provide any robustness guarantee for these samples.
163        Consequently, these samples cannot be correctly classified by the quantized smoothed classifier
164        gP , regardless of the improvements made to the base classifiers on such inputs.
165
         • Using accuracy as the optimization goal does not accurately reflect the robustness of the neural
166
           networks. As R = σ · Φ−1 (pA ), the radius R of gP has a complicated relation to pA . There-
167        fore, optimizing only for the accuracy of the quantized base classifier fP may not accurately
168        translate to improvements in the accuracy of the quantized smoothed classifier gP . This results
169        in a disproportionate focus on samples with smaller certified radii, while neglecting those with
170        larger ones, due to the non-linear relationship between R and pA .
171
172   ARQ Optimization Objective. Instead, we propose using the certified radius of smoothed classi-
173   fiers to directly guide the quantization method. It is more straightforward as it uses feedback directly
174   from the smoothed classifiers instead of the base classifiers, and it can combine the goal of optimz-
175   ing both the clean accuracy and the robustness of the smoothed classifiers. We define the following
176   optimization problem (compare Eqn. 1) to find the optimal quantization policy Poptimal :
177                  Poptimal = arg max(Average Certified Radius) s.t. Cost(fP ) < C0                      (6)
                                      P ∈P
178
179   where Average Certified Radius (ACR) is estimated as:
180                             σ    X
181                   ACR =              Φ−1 (P(f (x + ε) = y)) ∀ε ∼ N (0, σ 2 I)                          (7)
                               |X|
182                                   (x,y)∈X

183   The P(fP (x + ε) = y) here represents the lower bound of probability that base classifier f can
184   correctly classify input x under noise ε. This follows from the definition of the certified radius
185   R(x) = σ · Φ−1 (pA ) for a given input x and P(f (x + ϵ) = cA ) ≥ pA in Section 2.2. By averaging
186   over inputs in the dataset X, we obtain the ACR, which provides a measure of the overall robustness
187   of the classifier. Since the clean accuracy of smoothed classifiers is the percentage of samples with
188   a certified radius greater than zero. By focusing on optimizing the Acerage Certified Radius, we can
189   improve both the accuracy and robustness of the quantized smoothed classifiers.
190   Therefore, our final robustness-aware quantization problem formulation is:
191
                                        X
192               Poptimal = arg max          Φ−1 (P(fP (x + ε) = y)) s.t. Cost(fP ) < C0                  (8)
193                                P ∈P
                                          (x,y)∈X
194
195   However, it is challenging to use this formulation to search exhaustively across quantization policies
196   because calculating the certified radius, specifically obtaining P(fP (x + ε) = y), is expensive –
197   this probability is estimated using the Clopper-Pearson method (Clopper & Pearson, 1934), and the
198   confidence level is related to the number of samples, and may require thousands of samples even
199   for a single image (Cohen et al., 2019). Instead, we employ a reinforcement learning (RL) agent to
200   search for the optimal quantization policy Poptimal , which we describe next.

297   3.2.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
298
299   Quantization. We use a linear quantization method, which maps the floating-point value to discrete
300   integer values in the range [−c, c] for weights and [0, c] for activations. The quantization function
301   Quantize(·) that quantizes floating-point weight value v to b-bit integer value q can be expressed as:
302                                      q = round(clip(v/s, −c, c)) × s                                  (14)
303                                                                                        c
      where v is the floating-point value, and q is the quantized value. s = 2b−1 −1 is the scaling factor.
304
      c is optimized through the KL-divergence between q and v. In the network, each layer utilizes two
305   distinct c values for quantizing weights and activations.
306
307   IRS for Speedup in Certification. Due to the significant time consumption caused by both the
308
      number of iterations required for quantization policy search and the time-consuming process of
      certifying each iteration of the quantized neural networks, we employ Incremental Randomized
309
      Smoothing (IRS) (Ugare et al., 2024) to certify quantized smoothed classifiers more efficiently. It
310
      is known that IRS can have similar precision to re-running RS when verifying networks that have
311   sufficient structural similarity. Our design of ARQ algorithm aims to promote this property.

463
      Reward Function Choice. We investigate the sensitivity of the policy search to the quality of the
      reward function. In ARQ, ACR is used as a reward to the RL agent. But here is an intuitive question,
464
      what if we use the certified accuracy of the quantized smooth classifier gP as the reward? As noted
465
      in Section 2.2, certified accuracy is the probability that gP correctly predicts samples x with certified
466   radius R(x) exceeds the given threshold r. Table 3 shows the result of the ablation study conducted
467   on 3-bit equivalent quantized ResNet-20 models. Method "Val" and "Certified Acc" refer to using
468   the validation accuracy of fP and the certified accuracy of gP on r = 0.5. We observe that "Certified
469   Acc" gains better-certified accuracy on r = 0.25 and 0.5 but loses for the rest of the radii and ACR.
470
      The Effect of IRS. Table 4 shows the results of using RS instead of IRS to obtain ACRp and
471
      Rewardt . In our experiments, IRS is 1.32x faster than RS, consistent with the results from
472   IRS (Ugare et al., 2024). The method "RS" refers to using the same time for certification in the
473   policy search process as the IRS. We observe that using IRS does not reduce the quality of the
474   quantization policy (and in many cases improves it), justifying its use in ARQ’s search loop as the
475   candidate quantized networks are similar enough to benefit from incremental robustness proving.
476
      Other Ablation Studies. Appendix A.2 presents quantization policies across different σs. Ap-

      pendix A.3 presents the effect of the number of epochs in fine-tuning.

510
      approach that combines mixed-precision quantization with post-training optimization to achieve
      certified robustness on large-scale datasets like ImageNet. This distinguishes our work from other
511
      approaches that either focus only on pruning (which removes over 90% of the weights) and often
512
      target smaller datasets. Although ICR (Lin et al., 2021) has explored quantization methods with RS,
513   ARQ focuses on post-training optimization instead of quantization-aware training and we demon-

515   but only scales to CIFAR-10 for RS and is hard to transfer to quantization methods.

518

519   magnitude of adversarial gradients can be used to construct a defensive quantization method. Fi-
520   nally, empirical robustness approaches can improve best-effort robustness only to some kinds of
521   adversarial inputs (Raghunathan et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023). Ugare et al. (2023; 2022) focuses on
522   fast incremental certification with deterministic techniques but not on optimizing the DNN.

524   7   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
525
      Conclusion. We introduce ARQ, the first mixed-precision quantization framework that optimizes
526
      both DNN’s accuracy and certified robustness by limiting the computational resource budget. By
527
      using direct feedback from the ACR of the quantized smoothed classifier, ARQ more effectively
528   searches for the optimal quantization policy. Our experiments demonstrate that ARQ consistently
529   outperforms state-of-the-art quantization methods, often reaching or improving the accuracy and
530   robustness of the original FP32 networks with down to 0.84% operations. ARQ significantly reduces
531   the computational resource requirements of randomized smoothing, making it possible to deploy it
532   in resource-constrained environments while providing certifiable robustness guarantees.
533
      Limitations. We showed that ARQ can achieve well-quantized DNNs that match or even surpass
534   the accuracy and robustness of the original DNNs with floating-point weights. However, these prop-
535   erties are still dependent on the training of the original network, which should be trained with Gaus-
536   sian augmentation to ensure the quantized network performs well. Deploying DNNs with mixed-
537   precision inference can be more challenging compared to the fixed-precision methods, however,
538   recent works aim to address this issue (Sharma et al., 2018). The current implementation of ARQ
539   has been evaluated solely on image classification tasks. In the future, we plan to investigate how
      ARQ performs on other complex tasks such as object detection and natural language processing.

705
706   DDPG learns a Q-function and a policy concurrently. It uses off-policy data and the Bellman equa-
707   tion to learn the Q-function, and then uses the Q-function to learn the policy.
708
      Algorithm 2 (Achiam, 2018) shows the DDPG algorithm. In some RL search-based mixed-precision
709   quantization methods, DDPG is utilized to search for the optimal quantization policy. The environ-
710   ment is usually set to be the DNN itself, state s is usually set to be the configuration of one layer
711   in the DNN, the action a is the continuous value that can be transformed into the bit-width for the
712   layer, and r is the reward set to be the accuracy of the DNN and computed only after all actions
713   have been taken. The reward for all actions in one episode is set to the final accuracy gained. The
714   agent updates only when all actions have been taken and the reward is obtained. The experiences
715   are stored in replay buffer D and randomly sampled in batch B for updating the Q-function, policy
716   network, and the target network of the agent.
