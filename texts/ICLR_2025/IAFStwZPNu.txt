009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The past few years have produced a series of spectacular advances in the decoding
012
               of speech from brain activity. The engine of these advances has been the acqui-
013
               sition of labelled data, with increasingly large datasets acquired from single sub-
014            jects. However, participants exhibit individual differences, such as anatomy, and
015            datasets use varied scanners and task designs. As a result, prior work has strug-
016            gled to leverage data from multiple subjects, multiple datasets, multiple tasks, and
017            unlabelled datasets. In turn, the field has not benefited from the rapidly growing
018            number of open neural data repositories to exploit large-scale data and deep learn-
019            ing. This gap exists for all neural data, but especially for magnetoencephalog-
020            raphy (MEG), where the scale of individual datasets has not yet caught up with
021            other modalities. To address this, we develop a set of neuroscience-inspired self-
022
               supervised objectives, together with a neural architecture, for representation learn-
               ing from heterogeneous and unlabelled neural recordings. Experimental results
023
               with MEG show that representations learned with these objectives scale with data,
024
               generalise across subjects, datasets, and tasks, outperform using the raw input rep-
025            resentation, and even surpass comparable self-supervised approaches. In addition,
026            we set new benchmarks for two foundational speech decoding tasks. Collectively,
027            these methods now unlock the potential for training speech decoding models with
028            orders of magnitude more existing data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      In his Bitter Lesson, Richard Sutton argues that a major conclusion of 70 years of AI research is
057
      that general methods exploiting large-scale computation will outperform model-based approaches
058   as the availability of compute increases (Sutton, 2019). In line with this, the generality of deep
059   learning, via statistical learning from ever bigger datasets, has allowed the field to leverage compu-
060   tation in a way that appears to scale arbitrarily, leading to astounding advances across a diverse set
061   of domains (Jumper et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2021; OpenAI, 2023; Radford et al., 2023).
062
      In the domain of brain data, and of tasks like speech decoding, the bitter lesson has not yet been fully
063
      assimilated. State-of-the-art brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) have tried to scale up labelled datasets
064   for individual subjects, using either invasive (Moses et al., 2021; Willett et al., 2023) or non-invasive
065   brain recordings (Tang et al., 2023), mapping these to transcripts of attempted or imagined speech.
066   Yet, a number of obstacles to scale remain. With few exceptions at present, e.g. Défossez et al.
067   (2023), speech decoding models tend not to train on data from more than one subject. Moreover,
068   they do not combine data from multiple datasets and in general do not utilise unlabelled data, or data
069   from diverse tasks. Thus the size of training data has been limited to how much can be acquired for a
070   single subject, and data from other subjects, or from the growing number of public data repositories,
071   has not been leveraged. There are many reasons for these limitations; individual brains and data
072
      from different neuroimaging scanners differ, for example. But overcoming these limitations, as has
      begun to happen in neighbouring sub-fields, such as Jiang et al. (2024), holds the promise of training
073
      models on collective, internet-scale data.
074
075   While neuroimaging modalities such as electroencephalography (EEG) are more abundant, MEG
076   may be a better modality for decoding as it provides a richer signal (Lopes da Silva, 2013; Hall
077   et al., 2014). Given the scarcity of speech-labelled MEG data and the relative abundance of other
078
      MEG data, self-supervised learning (SSL) appears promising as it is an avenue for domains where
      labels are rare or hard to obtain (Balestriero et al., 2023). But the scale of public MEG data, while
079
      large, is still not at the volume of breakthroughs in self-supervised image and natural language pro-
080
      cessing, let alone EEG. Thus, SSL methods for MEG need to be highly data-efficient. Pretext tasks
081   are one such method in which domain-specific self-supervised tasks are used to pre-train a model on
082   unlabelled data by generating implicit training labels through transformations of the input in order to
083   help a downstream task. We develop a set of these tasks, informed by advances in neuroscience, for
084   learning with unlabelled brain data (Figure 1) and design an architecture for processing continuous
085   multi-sensor neuroimaging signals which we train using our pretext tasks. In order to scale exist-
086   ing non-invasive datasets, we provide a unified method that allows us to leverage data from other
087   experiments that do not have the same labels (by treating them as unlabelled) and that come from
088   different subjects and neuroimaging scanners. We evaluate the representations learned with our ap-
089
      proach on heard speech datasets acquired with non-invasive MEG, setting the baselines for speech
      detection and voicing classification on this data. The results not only demonstrate that scaling with
090
      unlabelled data works in speech decoding, but also shows that these representations can generalise
091
      across datasets, tasks, and even novel subjects for the first time. Our main contributions are:
092
093          • A set of domain-specific self-supervised pretext tasks for representation learning that can
094            scale speech decoding over multiple subjects, multiple studies, and unlabelled data;
095
             • A data-efficient neural architecture for learning these self-supervised objectives and train-
096            ing downstream speech decoding from brain data; and
097
098          • A comprehensive experimental evaluation, using multiple times the volume of data in
099
               prior work, that verifies the above claims and additionally provides evidence for the exis-
               tence of scaling laws when pre-training models with unlabelled MEG recordings.

103
104   Prior work in speech decoding has focused almost entirely on supervised learning with decoding
105   models that typically do not generalise across participants or experiments. This is true both in
106   recent state-of-the-art invasive studies (Moses et al., 2021; Metzger et al., 2023; Willett et al., 2023;
107   Chen et al., 2024a) and non-invasive studies (Tang et al., 2023). These prior works have scaled
      up the experimental data collected within individual subjects, but are unable to leverage data from


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      other subjects and experiments. Focusing on semantic rather than phonetic decoding, the method
109   developed by Tang et al. (2023) is remarkable for showing an ability to generalise across labelled task
110   data when listening to speech, imagining speech, or even watching videos. They do not, however,
111   leverage unlabelled data and are unable to show generalisation between subjects.
112
      Specific studies into the limitations of generalising models between subjects show that while perfor-
113
      mance decreases on average when subjects are pooled, there are exceptions (e.g. Anumanchipalli
114
      et al. (2019) and Makin et al. (2019) in surgical settings and Csaky et al. (2022) non-invasively).
115   Exploiting audio data in a multi-modal framework, Défossez et al. (2023) show that decoding per-
116   formance improves for a segment identification task as data from multiple subjects listening to con-
117   nected speech are aggregated. However, they do not demonstrate the ability to generalise to novel
118   subjects and must retrain their model for new datasets. Moreover, although they repeat the result
119   within two MEG and two EEG datasets, Défossez et al. (2023) do not show any improvements for
120   pooling data across datasets. Their method is also unable to incorporate data without corresponding
121   audio labels and so they do not combine data from studies with other kinds of labels either; cf. Wang
122   & Ji (2022); Duan et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023a). Unfortunately, the first two of these papers in-
123
      cluded a bug in their evaluation code. As such, their methods may perform no better than a baseline
      that provides pure noise inputs to the model (Jo et al., 2024).
124
125   In general, speech decoding has centred on different kinds of speech: listening, imagining, speak-
126   ing out loud, and, for paralysed patients, attempting to speak aloud. We focus here on listening
127   because it is easier to decode than imagined speech (e.g. Martin et al. (2014)). There is also some
128   evidence of a functional overlap between listening and imagined speech representations in the brain
129
      (Wandelt et al., 2024), though we acknowledge that the question of overlap has been contested
      (Langland-Hassan & Vicente, 2018). Prior work has also investigated the two tasks that we focus
130
      on here (Dash et al., 2020; Moses et al., 2021; Gwilliams et al., 2023). The first of these, speech
131
      detection, formed the backbone to Moses et al. (2021), where a speech detection model was trained
132   and subsequently used to detect isolated words, which were in turn classified and checked against a
133   language model to generate acceptable sentences. Hamilton et al. (2018) further elaborated on the
134   neural anatomy underlying speech detection, categorising neural responses in the superior temporal
135   gyrus (STG) to sustained speech and speech onset. As for the second task, voicing classification,
136   Gwilliams et al. (2023) used this task as a proxy for phoneme classification, as pooling phonemes
137   into unvoiced or voiced segments (e.g. /p t k f s/ vs /b d g v z/) improves data efficiency. We note
138   that voicing classification and speech detection are related tasks as voicing is a subclass of speech.
139   This makes them foundational for building hierarchical speech decoding pipelines similar to prior
140
      surgical decoding work (Moses et al., 2021; Willett et al., 2023).
141   In the computer vision literature, there have been a plethora of methods that use self-supervised
142   pretext tasks for representation learning (Agrawal et al., 2015; Doersch et al., 2015; Noroozi &
143   Favaro, 2016; Larsson et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2016; Gidaris et al., 2018). Until now, similar
144   approaches have not translated to the brain decoding literature with few exceptions (e.g. Cai et al.
145   (2023)). However, prior work has used other methods to leverage unlabelled brain data (Banville
146
      et al., 2019; Kostas et al., 2021; Le & Shlizerman, 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Yi et al., 2023; Ye et al.,
      2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b). For example, Jiang et al. (2024) succeeded in cross-
147
      dataset and cross-task generalisation, using a transformer with tokenised brain signals and a masked
148
      token prediction objective. Although this work combined unlabelled datasets, their results studied
149   simpler non-speech tasks with EEG. Wang et al. (2023b) used a similar approach, replacing tokens
150   with contextualised embeddings of time-frequency input representations. Their impressive speech
151   detection results were achieved with invasive neural recordings, which are comparatively rare and
152   thus have much less potential to scale than non-invasive data. Perhaps the closest work to ours in
153   terms of unlocking scaling with neural data is BIOT (Yang et al., 2023). This is a self-supervised
154   architecture for encoding bio-signals that is similarly capable of training with different datasets,
155   labels, and varied numbers of sensors. Like the previous works, the approach tokenises signals for
156   a transformer architecture, but instead of a masked loss it uses a contrastive pre-training objective.
157
      While theoretically supporting MEG, Yang et al. (2023) evaluate BIOT on simple ECG/EEG tasks
      rather than address the comparatively complex challenge of speech decoding with MEG data.

      To encode continuous neuroimaging data, we introduce a neural architecture to embed heteroge-
182   neous brain signals. We leverage this architecture for self-supervised learning from unlabelled MEG
183   data using a set of pretext tasks designed to generate generalisable brain representations for speech
184   decoding. With this approach, we hope to replicate similar successes in computer vision (Gidaris
185   et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020).

      3.1   N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
189
      Our two-stage neural network architecture (Figure 2) uses pretext tasks in pre-training to learn a
190
      representation with unlabelled brain data. Then, the fine-tuning stage uses this representation to
191
      learn the downstream task by training with labelled data.
192
193   We divide recordings into windows of length w seconds or t samples. At train time, each batch of
194   windows is standardised such that each sensor has zero mean and unit variance. The network takes
195
      as input the standardised sample windows. To combine heterogeneous datasets, which have different
      numbers of sensors S, we apply a dataset-conditional linear layer to the sensor dimension, projecting
196
      the signal into a shared space with dimension dshared . Then, to encode the signal, we construct a
197
      wave-to-wave convolutional encoder architecture, the cortex encoder, inspired by work in neural
198   audio codecs (Zeghidour et al., 2022; Défossez et al., 2022). Specifically, our convolutional encoder
199   adapts the implementation of the SEANet architecture (Tagliasacchi et al., 2020) used in Défossez
200   et al. (2022) which we describe here and as part of Figure 2. As these codecs typically operate
201   on mono audio signals in R1×t , while our signals are in Rdshared ×t , we increase the convolutional
202   channel dimension from 1 to match dshared while also inflating the channel dimension of subsequent
203   convolutions. We refer to the output dimension of embeddings from this backbone as dbackbone .
204   Thus, the backbone takes as input a window in RS×t , and encodes this into τ embeddings (where
205   τ < t), each of dimension dbackbone (i.e. an Rdbackbone ×τ output).
206   Just as speakers have different voices, neural responses between subjects have different characteris-
207   tics. Consequently, individual variation leads to models that do not generalise well across subjects
208   (Csaky et al., 2022). In the speech literature, models include speaker conditioning to account for
209   these differences (Gibiansky et al., 2017). We take a similar approach by introducing subject condi-
210   tioning. Zeghidour et al. (2022) find that conditioning is equally effective at the encoder bottleneck
211   as in other stages of the model. Hence, we place ours at the cortex encoder bottleneck for simplicity.
212
      We use feature-wise linear modulation (FiLM) (Perez et al., 2018) as our conditioning method.
213   Following the advice of Balestriero et al. (2023, Section 3.2), we use a two-layer feedforward projec-
214   tor to alleviate misalignment between our pretext and downstream tasks in the representation. After
215   the projector, linear classifiers make predictions for each of the pretext tasks. When fine-tuning, we
      train a linear decoder, for a downstream task, on top of the pre-trained representation, which remains


                                                                    4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      frozen. Thus, we backpropagate only through the classifier. A trainable dataset-specific linear layer
217   can be introduced for a novel dataset.
218
219   For speech detection, our classifier makes a prediction for each individual embedding. For voicing
220
      classification, where there is only one label for each sample window, the embeddings are flattened
      into a tensor in Rdbackbone ×τ representing the entire window. This is the input to the voicing classifier
221
      and is referred to as full epoch decoding in neuroimaging literature (Csaky et al., 2023).

      3.2   P RETEXT TASKS
224
225   Our pretext tasks are unsupervised feature learning tasks that aim to learn generalisable speech
226   decoding features. Since different datasets use varied numbers of sensors, we construct these tasks
227   with labels that are agnostic to the number of sensors in the signal.
228
      Band prediction. In the literature, neural responses can be segmented into functional frequency
229
      bands (Giraud & Poeppel, 2012; Piai et al., 2014; Mai et al., 2016). Delta (δ) waves (0.1–4 Hz) are
230   commonly associated with the rhythmic structure of heard speech (Luo et al., 2010), Theta (θ) waves
231   (4–8 Hz) reliably track (Luo & Poeppel, 2007) and phase-lock to the amplitude envelope of heard
232   sentences (Peelle et al., 2012), Alpha (α) waves (8–12 Hz) relate to attentional processes and the
233   inhibition of irrelevant information, helping to focus on relevant speech signals (Strauß et al., 2015),
234   Beta (β) waves (12–30Hz) are implicated in top-down predictive coding (Bressler & Richter, 2015)
235   which affects lexical processing (Weiss & Mueller, 2012), Gamma (γ) waves (30–70 Hz) occur with
236   higher cognitive functions (e.g. memory, learning, reasoning, and planning) (Fries, 2009; Buzsáki
237   & Wang, 2012), and High Gamma (γ high ) waves (>70 Hz) have been linked specifically to speech
238
      detection (Hamilton et al., 2018) and phonemic feature classification in the STG (Mesgarani et al.,
      2014) as well as phonemic feature classification in the ventral sensorimotor cortex (vSMC) (Cheung
239
      et al., 2016). As High Gamma is a relatively wide band, we have split it into two sub-bands: Lower

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
306
307   Datasets. Unless specified otherwise, our experiments use Cam-CAN (Shafto et al., 2014; Taylor
308   et al., 2017) as an unlabelled representation learning dataset for pre-training. This is a study con-
309   taining 641 subjects with resting and sensorimotor tasks, totalling approximately 160 hours of MEG
310   recordings. For our downstream tasks, we use two labelled heard speech MEG datasets where par-
311   ticipants listen to short stories or audiobooks. Armeni et al. (2022) contains 3 subjects who listen
312   to 10 hours of recordings each (30 hours total) while Gwilliams et al. (2023) has 27 subjects, each
313   recorded for 2 hours (54 hours total). Overall, we utilise over 200 hours of data. To the best of our
314
      knowledge, this is the largest volume of MEG data ever used for speech decoding.
315   Preprocessing. Each recording is in RS×T where S is the number of sensors and T is the number
316   of time points sampled by the scanner. To eliminate high-frequency muscle movement artifacts, we
317   apply a low-pass filter at 125Hz as well as a high-pass filter at 0.5Hz to remove slow-drift artifacts.
318   Since the datasets were recorded in Europe, where the electric grid frequency is 50Hz, we apply a
319   notch filter at multiples of 50Hz to account for line noise. Treating the low-pass filter threshold as the
320
      Nyquist frequency, we downsample the signal to twice that at 250Hz, avoiding aliasing within our
      band of interest. Finally, we detect bad sensor channels, those with significant noise and artifacts,
321
      using a variance threshold and replace them by interpolating the spatially nearest sensors.
322
323   Downstream tasks. We evaluate our methods with two fundamental speech decoding tasks of
      increasing difficulty. The first, speech detection, determines whether speech occurs in the auditory

      4.2   L EARNING G ENERALISABLE R EPRESENTATIONS U SING P RETEXT TASKS
349
350   Our first experiment investigates whether our self-supervised objectives produce generalisable rep-
351   resentations. In Table 1, we show the results of pre-training models with each pretext task inde-
352   pendently as well as together. Here, all of our pretext tasks lead to results that are statistically
353   significant, and outperform a baseline fine-tuned without pre-training. This provides initial evidence
354   that our tasks are helpful in speech decoding. Interestingly, the combination of all pretext tasks leads
355   to better generalisation than any task on its own. As we hypothesised earlier, this may be because
356   our pretext tasks capture complementary properties in time- and frequency-space, enforcing that our
357
      representation includes more salient features for speech decoding than any individual task.
358   Now, we turn to the other baselines. Our approach significantly outperforms the equivalent with a
359   raw MEG input instead of a pre-trained representation (the linear experiment). Here, the baseline
360   has substantially more trainable parameters because the input dimension is far larger without an
361   encoder. Even with this bias favouring the experiment with the raw input, using our representation
362   still performs better. We also compare our approach to BIOT (Yang et al., 2023) which is a similar
363
      state-of-the-art self-supervised method. When BIOT is pre-trained using exactly the same data, the
      fine-tuned probe fails to generalise entirely after exhaustive hyperparameter tuning. We put this
364
      down to three critical reasons. Firstly, BIOT was designed around considerably lower-dimensional
365
      signals. Their EEG evaluation used an order of magnitude fewer sensors than our MEG data. With
366   MEG, their transformer approach requires many more channel embeddings, leading to difficulty
367   learning the complex interactions between sensors. Secondly, our self-supervised objective extracts

406   Here, we analyse generalisation as we increase the volume of unlabelled data, analysing scaling
407   performance on downstream tasks. As before, we pre-train with the combined pretext tasks. Figure
408   3 shows ROC AUC as we increase the amount of unlabelled data in pre-training up to approximately
409   160 hours. For both tasks, pre-training with any amount of data is sufficient to beat chance and
410   there is a clear improvement in accuracy as the amount of unlabelled data increases. For speech
411
      detection on Armeni et al. (2022), scaling appears logarithmic in log-space; for all others, ROC
      AUC improves log-linearly within the data regime we study. In any case, adding unlabelled data has
412
      improved generalisation. Notably, we have scaled far beyond the data regime of prior surgical and
413
      non-surgical work and yet performance has continued to scale. Thus, our self-supervision approach
414   may remain useful as the volume of open data in the field continues to rapidly increase.
415
416   Our results also reveal several new and notable phenomena. Firstly, we scaled up the pre-training
417
      dataset by increasing the number of subjects. Since this led to consistent and almost monotonic
      improvements in downstream accuracy, our method is an exception to the common consensus that
418
      pooling subjects worsens generalisation. Secondly, as we pre-trained our model with a different
419
      dataset to those we fine-tuned on, our representation shows cross-dataset generalisation. This is
420   particularly surprising as the Armeni et al. (2022), Gwilliams et al. (2023), and our pre-training
421   dataset all use different scanners entirely. Performing well across these datasets indicates that, to-
422   gether, our architecture and pretext tasks successfully generate representations that are generalisable
423   across heterogeneous scanners. Finally, we note that our pre-training dataset contained no language
424   data whatsoever yet still improved downstream accuracy on language tasks. Remarkably, this shows
425   that unlabelled brain data collected from any task (including those that are not linguistic) can be
426   used to improve speech decoding performance.

      4.6   L IMITATIONS
505
      Although our results are significant in demonstrating a viable path forward to scale up speech BCIs,
506   there remain a number of limitations to the present work. We focused here on two downstream tasks:
507   speech detection and voice classification. Ultimately, we would like to expand this work to predict
508   full transcripts from brain recordings (i.e. brain-to-text). This has been achieved with surgical data
509   (Moses et al., 2021; Willett et al., 2023) but not yet convincingly with non-invasive methods like
510   MEG or EEG (Jo et al., 2024). Speech detection has played an important role in the development
511   of full brain-to-text in a surgical context (Moses et al., 2021) and we hope may play a similar role
512   for non-invasive methods. Prior work has further used voice classification as a stand in for phoneme
513   classification (Gwilliams et al., 2022), and we have been able to improve on these results here. In
514
      future work, we would like to expand this to all English phonemes. Secondly, while we have been
      able to demonstrate the utility of a few pretext tasks, we do not claim to have exhausted the full set
515
      of useful tasks. Rather, we conjecture that more useful pretext tasks remain to be found and believe a
516
      useful avenue of research will be into other input representations for brain recordings. For example,
517   this paper did not make use of spatial features. Another limitation is our emphasis on heard speech
518   over other types of speech, such as attempted or imagined speech. We hypothesise that the same
519   methods presented here will generalise to these other varieties of speech, though this has yet to be
520   shown. But, perhaps the biggest limitation of the present work is that, while it surpasses the amount
521   of data used in other studies, it remains to be seen how much speech decoding tasks can be improved
522   by scaling up the number of datasets used in training. In sharing this work now, we believe that the
523   current proof of concept will be sufficiently impactful to the field as we continue to actively scale up
524   the datasets that we can leverage.
525
526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   Ultimately, solving speech decoding could transform the lives of patients with severe communica-
529   tion difficulties. This promise has not yet materialised because the field has been blocked by its
530   inability to scale up data to leverage deep learning. Prior methods have been unable to aggregate
531   data across different datasets, labels, or subjects to scale up because of heterogeneity in recording
532   hardware, experiment design, and participants. A handful of studies have shown weak signals to-
533   wards alleviating these issues. But until now, no one has developed a general solution. We provided
534
      a unified method that leverages unlabelled recordings data-efficiently using generic pretext tasks that
      shows that all of these problems can be solved. We verified this with experiments showing that our
535
      method not only scales with heterogeneous data but even generalises across datasets, subjects, and
536
      tasks. Our method unlocks the potential of the bitter lesson, providing a general method to exploit
537   more computation by using more data. We implore the research community to employ the vast
538   quantities of data and compute available to realise this potential. If scale is all you need in speech
539   decoding, then the bitter lesson may not be so bitter.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542   In this work, we use data from studies that involve human subjects (Armeni et al., 2022; Gwilliams
543   et al., 2023; Shafto et al., 2014; Taylor et al., 2017; Schoffelen et al., 2019). These datasets are
544   public, cited, and have their own ethical approvals. The documentation for these is available with
545   the publications for the respective datasets.
546   While there are clear positive impacts, we acknowledge that insights from neural speech decoding
547   research may not all be beneficial. Research in this field could enable paralysed patients to commu-
548   nicate freely and materially assist those with minor communication difficulty (e.g. stammering). As
549   the technology matures, it could also enable new ways of communicating with others and interact-
550   ing with devices without the risks of invasive surgical implants. Nevertheless, the maturity of this
551   technology could also present potential negative societal impacts. For one, reading inner speech cre-
552   ates new concerns over data controls as this information is likely to be highly sensitive and personal
553   to individuals. Given access to this technology, there is also the risk that bad actors could extract
554
      sensitive information from target individuals without consent. Moreover, there are possible long
      horizon effects associated with speech decoding research. Broad adoption of this technology could
555
      lead to the gradual erosion of privacy over inner speech within society. In addition, asymmetric ef-
556
      fects, where some individuals or organisations can read inner speech but others are unable to, could
557   worsen societal inequality. Within the scope of this paper, we mitigate risks associated with inner
558   speech by focusing on decoding heard speech where there is low potential for abuse. Nonetheless,
559   we acknowledge that this is still a stepping stone towards solving inner speech decoding.

      A     E XPERIMENT D ETAILS
898
899
      We pre-train with non-overlapping sample windows from all subjects and sessions. We adjust the
900   amount of unlabelled data used from Cam-CAN by increasing the number of subjects in the sequence
901   1, 2, 4, 8, 17, 36, 74, 152, 312, and 641, successively randomly selecting more subjects to include.
902   Each seed uses a different set of subjects to reduce negative effects from outlier subjects.
903
      When fine-tuning with Armeni et al. (2022), we hold out session 010 from all subjects during train-
904
      ing and validation, using this for evaluation. Similarly, when fine-tuning with Gwilliams et al.
905
      (2023), we hold out session 1 from subjects 23, 24, 25, 26, and 27, using these sessions for evalua-
906   tion only. As there is limited within-subject data in the latter dataset, we did not hold out a session
907   from all subjects as before. For our novel subject experiments, we hold out subjects 1, 2, and 3 en-
908   tirely and use the data for these subjects during evaluation. In Gwilliams et al. (2023), we note that
909   they use four different tasks for each subject and their order is randomized between subjects. Both
910   sessions for each task are repeats of the task. This means that while the recording itself is unseen, in
911   this dataset, it is possible that heldout sessions use tasks that may have been seen in the training set.
912   In all experiments, we always fine-tune to completion (usually around 300 epochs), taking the test
913   metric at the best validation loss (early stopping). We use three randomly selected seeds for each
914   pre-training and corresponding fine-tuning run. For speech detection, since our encoder reduces
915   the temporal dimension from 125 samples (the number of samples in a 0.5 second window with a
916   sample rate of 250Hz) down to 5 embeddings, we downsample our speech detection labels to match
917   using PyTorch’s torch.nn.functional.interpolate. Therefore, each speech detection
      label represents a 0.1 second period of time.
