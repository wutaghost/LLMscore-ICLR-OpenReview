011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Convex quadratically constrained quadratic programs (QCQPs) involve finding a so-
014            lution within a convex feasible region defined by quadratic constraints while minimiz-
015            ing a convex quadratic objective function. These problems arise in various industrial
016
               applications, including power systems and signal processing. Traditional methods for
               solving convex QCQPs primarily rely on matrix factorization, which quickly becomes
017
               computationally prohibitive as the problem size increases. Recently, graph neural
018
               networks (GNNs) have gained attention for their potential in representing and solv-
019            ing various optimization problems such as linear programs and linearly constrained
020            quadratic programs. In this work, we are the first to investigate the representation
021            power of GNNs in the context of QCQP tasks. Specifically, we propose a new tri-
022            partite graph representation for general convex QCQPs and properly associate it with
023            message-passing GNNs. We demonstrate that there exist GNNs capable of reliably
024            representing key properties of convex QCQPs, including feasibility, optimal value,
025            and optimal solution. Our result deepens the understanding of the connection be-
026            tween QCQPs and GNNs, paving the way for future machine learning approaches to
027
               efficiently solve QCQPs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Quadratic programs (QPs) are a pivotal class of optimization problems where the objective function is
032
      quadratic, and the constraints are typically linear or quadratic. Based on the nature of constraints, QPs
033   can be further classified as linearly constrained quadratic programs (LCQPs) and quadratically con-
034   strained quadratic programs (QCQPs). When the objective and constraint matrices are positive semi-
035   definite, the problem becomes a convex QCQP, making it both theoretically interesting and practically
036   important. Convex QCQPs arise in various critical applications such as robust optimization in uncer-
037   tain environments (Ben-Tal & Nemirovski, 2001; Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2004), power flow (Bienstock
038   et al., 2020), and signal processing (Luo et al., 2010), while ensuring optimality and computational
039   efficiency is paramount.
040   Solving QPs, especially those with quadratic constraints, presents significant challenges. Traditional
041   methods often involve computationally intensive procedures that would struggle with scalability and
042   real-time processing requirements. For example, the interior-point method (Nocedal & Wright, 1999)
043   for a general n-variable QP involves solving a sequence of linear systems of equations, necessitating
044   matrix decomposition with a runtime complexity of Rs (n3 ). This leads to substantial computational
045   burden in the large-scale case. Similarly, active-set algorithms (Gill et al., 2019), which work by itera-
046   tively adjusting the set of active constraints, can also become computationally demanding as the number
047
      of constraints and variables increase.
048   In recent years, advances in machine learning (ML) have opened new avenues for enhancing the solv-
049   ing process of QPs. There are mainly two categories of ML-aided QP methods. The first category

      aims to learn adaptive configurations of a specific QP algorithm or solver to accelerate the solving
051
      process (Bonami et al., 2018; Ichnowski et al., 2021; Jung et al., 2022), while the second focuses on
052   predicting an initial solution of QPs, which is either directly taken as a final solution or further refined
053   by subsequent algorithms or QP solvers (Bertsimas & Stellato, 2022; Gao et al., 2021; Sambharya et al.,
054   2023; Tan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2020). Most of these methods utilize graph neural networks (GNNs)
055   to leverage the structural properties of graph-structured data, making them particularly well-suited for
056   representing the relationships and dependencies inherent in QPs. By encoding QP instances into graphs,
057   GNNs can capture intricate features and provide adaptive guidance or approximate solutions efficiently.
058   In addition to these empirical studies, theoretical research on the expressive power of GNNs (Zhang
059   et al., 2023; Li & Leskovec, 2022) and their relation to optimization problems has further strengthened
060   the understanding of their capabilities. For instance, Chen et al. (2022a) and Chen et al. (2022b) estab-
061   lished theoretical foundations for applying GNNs to solving linear programs (LPs) and mixed-integer
062   linear programs, respectively. Further, such foundations are extended to LCQPs and their discrete vari-
063   ant, mixed-integer LCQPs in Chen et al. (2024).
064   Previous studies have empirically and theoretically demonstrated the utility of GNNs in speeding up
065   existing QP solvers and directly approximating solutions for various QP instances. However, there is a
066   noticeable lack of research on their use in QCQPs, particularly in how they handle quadratic constraints.
067   Existing graph representations used for LCQPs (Chen et al., 2024) are inadequate for QCQPs as they
068   can not capture the complex interactions introduced by quadratic constraints. Moreover, the question of
069   whether GNNs can accurately predict key properties of QCQPs, such as feasibility, optimal objective
070   value, and optimal solution, remains open.
071   This paper aims to address the aforementioned gap by exploring both theoretical foundations and prac-
072   tical implementation of using GNNs for solving convex QCQPs. Specifically, we propose a tripar-
073   tite graph representation for general convex QCQPs, and establish theoretical foundations of applying
074   GNNs to optimize QCQPs. The distinct contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows.
075
076          • Graph Representation. We propose a novel tripartite graph representation for general QC-
077            QPs, which divides a QCQP into three types of nodes: linear-term, quadratic-term, and con-
078            straint nodes, with edges added between heterogeneous nodes to indicate problem parameters.
079            This representation effectively addresses the limitations of existing graph representations for
080            LCQPs, i.e., those graphs are unable to capture the interactions imposed by quadratic con-
081            straints.
082
083          • Theoretical Foundation. We conduct analysis on the separation power as well as approxima-
084            tion power of message-passing GNNs (MP-GNNs). We showed that MP-GNNs are capable of
085            capturing some key properties of convex QCQPs.
086
087          • Empirical Evidence. We conduct initial numerical tests of the tripartite message-passing
088            GNNs on small QCQP instances. The results showed that MP-GNNs can be trained to ap-
089            proximate the key properties well.

129   2.2   T RIPARTITE R EPRESENTATION OF QCQP S
130
131   The first theoretical result demonstrating the representation power of GNNs in solving optimization
132   problems was provided by Yin et al. Chen et al. (2022a). In this work, the information of an LP prob-
133   lem is encoded into a bipartite graph, where variables and constraints are modeled as nodes, and their
134   association is represented as edges. They showed that GNNs based on this graph representation can
135
      universally approximate the optimal solution of LPs, as well as properties of feasibility and bounded-
      ness. This bipartite graph modeling was later extended to analyze the representation power of GNNs
136
      for LCQPs (Chen et al., 2024).
137
138   Despite these advances, it remains challenging to develop graph representation to encode all information
139   of general QCQPs while maintaining simplicity for GNN processing. Due to the presence of quadratic
140   terms, a QCQP generally involves O(n2 × m) coefficients. Consequently, a graph encoding all QCQP
141
      information inherently exhibits a complexity of the same order, O(n2 × m). There are two natural
      extensions of the traditional bipartite representation of LP/LCQP to QCQP.
142
143          – Hyperedge Representation. This approach adds hyperedges to the traditional bipartite graph
144            to represent quadratic coefficients, turning the graph into a hypergraph. However, to the best
145            of our knowledge, current GNN architectures struggle to handle hyperedges efficiently.
146          – Vector Feature Representation. In this method, all coefficients are encoded as features asso-
147            ciated with the n variable nodes and the m constraint nodes, resulting in a graph with vector
148            features of varying sizes, depending on the problem. However, existing GNNs are generally
149            incapable of processing features of varying dimensions.

431
432   This paper introduces a new tripartite graph representation specifically designed for QCQPs. By lever-
433   aging the capabilities of message-passing GNNs, this approach shows theoretical promise in predicting
434   key properties of QCQPs with arbitrary desired accuracy, including feasibility, boundness, optimal val-
435
      ues, and solutions. Initial numerical experiments validate the effectiveness of our framework.
436   This research contributes to the field of learning to optimize by expanding the application of GNNs to
437   QCQP problems, which were previously challenging for traditional graph-based L2O methods. This
438   could encourage future exploration in designing more specialized GNN architectures to handle QCQPs
439   in practice, beyond the basic GCN structure employed here.

752   We begin by describing equivariance, a key tool used to capture the fact that the indexing of variables
753   and constraints is irrelevant:
754   Definition 4. Given a function f : X → Y , where X and Y are subsets of Euclidean spaces, and a
755   group Σ that acts continuously on X and Y , the function f is called equivariant (with respect to the
756   group Σ) if the following holds:
757
758

      Since the indexing of variables and constraints does not affect the problem, we take Σ = Sn × Sm ,

761
      and output spaces, we re-index the variables, constraints, and possible solutions (in cases where the
762
      output is a solution x ∈ Rn ). Specifically, we have:
