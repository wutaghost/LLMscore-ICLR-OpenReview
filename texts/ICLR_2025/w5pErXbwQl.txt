009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep regression models are widely employed for tasks such as pricing and fore-
012            casting. In industry applications, it is common for analysts to adjust model outputs
013            before they are deployed in commercial products. These adjustments, which we
014            name “analyst influences”, not only ensure the quality of the final products but
015            also provide training data to improve model performance over time. However,
016            due to the huge volumes of data, analyst influences can be applied broadly and
017            can lack precision, hindering training effectiveness. To resolve the issue, we pro-
018
               pose a novel method: Preference Learning from Analyst Influence, which creates
               a weighted loss function that explicitly accounts for the relative quality levels of
019
               the training samples in comparison to model outputs. This approach effectively
020
               mitigates the impact of coarse training instances. Our extensive experiments on
021            real-world data drawn from airline revenue management demonstrate that the pro-
022            posed method not only enhances pricing stability but also improves alignment
023            with analyst influences compared to baselines.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Deep regression models are widely used in many areas of study such as dynamic pricing (Ye et al.,
029   2018; Kolbeinsson et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2019), asset pricing (Chen et al., 2024a), and a wide
030   range of forecast tasks (Fernández-Delgado et al., 2019). In these applications, leveraging deep
031   learning methods is especially important because of the high cost and low availability of analysts
032
      with the necessary expertise and specialty skills. However, due to the complexity of real-world data,
      deep regression models can sometimes produce inaccurate outputs, necessitating human intervention
033
      post-training. The human interventions not only safeguard the application but also provide new and
034
      additional training data for future training to improve the models.
035
036   Existing work explored learning directly from human annotation for improving performance and
037   serviceability using reinforcement learning or supervised fine-tuning, known as human preference
038
      optimization (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a; Rafailov et al., 2024). However, these methods
      only apply to probabilistic models and not to regression models, necessitating specialized preference
039
      learning methods for deep regression models.
040
041   In regression tasks, the human annotation process typically requires analysts with a high level of
042   knowledge and sophistication due to the requirement of mathematical and domain knowledge. This
043   makes meticulous intervention challenging for large quantities of data, and analysts will have to
044
      apply the intervention to a large number of instances based on filtering queries; we name this type
      of annotation “analyst influence”.
045
046   While analyst-influenced data is valuable for training, broadness in analyst influences makes them
047   coarse in quality. Consequently, direct training on influenced data can diminish the sensitivity of
048   models and potentially erase learned conditional features over generations of retraining. This neces-
049   sitates a tailored method to learn from analyst influences while avoiding sensitivity loss.
050   To address these issues, we explore a novel loss function depending on the relative quality of the in-
051   fluenced data, defined as the probability of the influenced data being more accurate compared to the
052   training model output. Intuitively, the higher the relative quality of the influenced data is, the better it
053   will facilitate the training of the models, and vice versa. During training, our approach incorporates
      higher weighted loss when the model output is of lower accuracy and restricts the weighted loss on


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      lower quality data toward the end of training avoiding the problem of coarse influence. We name
055   this approach as Preference Learning from Analyst Influence (PLAI). Our method is distinct from
056   existing loss methods for noisy labels (Wang et al., 2019; Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018; Ghosh et al.,
057   2017; Song et al., 2022) since existing works focus on classification tasks and cannot be adopted for
058   regression tasks.

      2.2       P REFERENCE L EARNING
109
110   In natural language processing, preference learning is used to align large language models toward
111   human preference. In the field of natural language processing, preference learning is pioneered
112   by Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (Ouyang et al., 2022) which leverages human
113   annotation to train a reward model and uses reinforcement learning algorithm Schulman et al. (2017)
114   to train a target model to align with human preferences. Later works use artificial intelligence models
115   for the purpose of annotation, namely reinforcement learning from AI feedback, to reduce human
116
      effort involved (Bai et al., 2022b; Lee et al., 2023). Besides reinforcement learning approaches,
      Rafailov et al. (2024); Azar et al. (2024) provides simplified methods for preference learning without
117
      the need of training a reward model.

122   3.1       DYNAMIC P RICING FOR A IRLINE R EVENUE M ANAGEMENT
123
124   Dynamic pricing is the current state-of-the-practice in airline revenue management, involving the
125   continuous adjustment of ticket prices based on real-time demand, booking patterns, and market
126   conditions to maximize revenue (Van Ryzin & Talluri, 2005; Belobaba et al., 2015; Kolbeinsson
127
      et al., 2022a). Unlike traditional static pricing, where ticket prices are fixed or adjusted infrequently,
      dynamic pricing enables airlines to respond quickly to changes in demand, such as increasing prices
128
      as seats fill up or offering discounts when demand is low. This adaptability helps airlines manage
129
      seat inventory more efficiently, capture consumer surplus, and increase overall profitability by selling
130   the right seat to the right customer at the right price at the right time. Conventionally, the airline
131   industry has adopted traditional approaches for revenue management and is only recently adopting
132   deep learning methodologies to improve the accuracy and granularity in their pricing models.
133
134
      3.2       B ID P RICE P REDICTION
135
136   Bid price prediction is a critical task in airline revenue management aimed at helping airlines in-
137   crease revenue from ticket sales. Conceptually, the bid price represents the marginal opportunity
138   cost of a seat on a flight at an observed date. Bid prices are considered the lower bound of seat
139   prices and help airlines protect seats for higher-paying business customers who are less sensitive to
140   pricing and often book closer to the departure date. An accurate estimate of bid prices dynamically
141   generated during the booking process allows an airline to estimate if they should sell a seat at a given
142
      price or hold to increase the price.
143   At our partner airline, bid prices are calculated daily for each flight, with updates available up
144   to 384 days before the departure date to ensure accurate pricing. The predictions are based on
145   available features from Osprey Airlines (pseudonym) and the data pipeline from our company. These
146   features include time-related factors (such as observation date, departure date, and day of the week),
147   geographical information (origin and destination), load factor 1 , booking history, and statistical data
148
      on both internal and external competitor flights.

195   4.1   C OMPARISON TO E XISTING M ETHODS
196
197   In this paper, we address the challenge in regression tasks involving analyst influence; where the
198
      influence while accurate in aggregate, can be coarse at the individual record level. Despite being a
      common problem for tasks such as dynamic pricing and forecasting, very little academic research
199
      directly tackles this issue. The closest problems are human alignment optimization in natural lan-
200
      guage processing (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a; Rafailov et al., 2024) and methods address-
201   ing noisy labels (Wang et al., 2019; Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018; Ghosh et al., 2017; Song et al., 2022).
202   However, human alignment optimization typically relies on assumptions such as the Bradley-Terry
203   model (Bradley & Terry, 1952) for human preference; on the other hand, the noisy labels methods
204   only address classification tasks. In both cases, the solutions target probabilistic models and cannot
205   be adopted for regression models.
206
207
      4.2   N OISE -ROBUST L OSS W EIGHT VIA R ELATIVE Q UALITY
208
209
      A simple approach for training models for the task of bid pricing with analyst influence is supervised
210   fine-tuning, i.e., reducing the discrepancy between analyst-influenced price and the price provided
211   by the previous ML model. However, due to the coarse nature of analyst influences (as shown in
212   Figure 2), influenced data can erase some sensitive dependencies the model learned, resulting in
213   poor performance. To address this issue, we introduce relative quality, a metric that computes the
214   probability that the training data are more accurate compared to the output of the training model.
215   Relative quality can be utilized as a weighting mechanism during training, helping mitigate the
      negative impact of coarse influences.

217   analyst influence, the use of relative quality as a weighting factor can reduce the loss as the model’s

219   input conditions. However, while in the early stage of the training, the model producing lower
220   quality output will receive a full loss update due to high relative quality, facilitating faster training.

354   5.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
355
      The evaluation is a challenging problem for dynamic pricing, as none of the evaluation metrics can
356
      fully encapsulate the performance of a model. To provide comprehensive metrics for the needs of
357   our partner airline, we consider the two factors of evaluation:
358
359   • Stability: A pricing model in a commercial system needs to show stability in output to avoid
360     mayhem. We use two methods to evaluate the stability of model outputs. First, we assess the
361     proximity of the model output to the recent production pricing. This helps determine if the model
362     can successfully imitate the influenced output as well as desirable traits of the previous produc-
363     tion model. We use Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean Squared Error (MSE), Mean Absolute
364
        Percentage Error (MAPE), and Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) to evaluate the effectiveness of
        imitation learning. On the other hand, due to the seasonality of the airline industry, seasonality is
365
        a crucial perspective for pricing models to learn. We provide seasonality analysis by aggregating
366
        the pricing by departure week to observe whether the trained model learned the seasonal trend.
367
      • Analyst Alignment Metrics: We evaluate whether the model outputs are moving in the direction
368
        of influenced prices compared to the outputs of the production model (which were also influenced
369
        by analysts). We use accuracy in confusion matrices to assess the success of the model in learning
370     analyst influences.

476   Alternative Penalties: As PLAI losses can be considered as penalties on low-relative-quality
