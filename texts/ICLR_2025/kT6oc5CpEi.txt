011                                            Abstract
012
013
              While large language models (LLMs) exhibit remarkable capabilities across
014           various tasks, they encounter potential security risks such as jailbreak at-
015           tacks, which exploit vulnerabilities to bypass security measures and gener-
016           ate harmful outputs. Existing jailbreak strategies mainly focus on maxi-
017           mizing attack success rate (ASR), frequently neglecting other critical fac-
018           tors, including the relevance of the jailbreak response to the query and the
019           level of stealthiness. This narrow focus on single objectives can result in
020           ineffective attacks that either lack contextual relevance or are easily rec-
021           ognizable. In this work, we introduce BlackDAN, an innovative black-box
022
              attack framework with multi-objective optimization, aiming to generate
              high-quality prompts that effectively facilitate jailbreaking while maintain-
              ing contextual relevance and minimizing detectability. BlackDAN leverages
023
024
              Multiobjective Evolutionary Algorithms (MOEAs), specifically the NSGA-
025           II algorithm, to optimize jailbreaks across multiple objectives including
026           ASR, stealthiness, and semantic relevance. By integrating mechanisms like
027           mutation, crossover, and Pareto-dominance, BlackDAN provides a trans-
028           parent and interpretable process for generating jailbreaks. Furthermore, the
029           framework allows customization based on user preferences, enabling the se-
030           lection of prompts that balance harmfulness, relevance, and other factors.
031           Experimental results demonstrate that BlackDAN outperforms traditional
032           single-objective methods, yielding higher success rates and improved robust-
033
              ness across various LLMs and multimodal LLMs, while ensuring jailbreak
              responses are both relevant and less detectable.

      1   Introduction
036
037
038
039
      As large language models (LLMs) are increasingly integrated into various applications, the
      security of these models has become crucial Yi et al. (2024); Jin et al. (2024); Chu et al.
      (2024). Jailbreaking, the process of manipulating these models to bypass safety constraints
040
041
      and generate undesirable or harmful outputs, poses a significant challenge to maintaining
042   their integrity and ethical use. Current jailbreaking methods depend excessively on affirma-
043   tive cues from the model’s prefix Zou et al. (2023); Qi et al. (2024), leading to the possibility
044   of generating responses that are irrelevant or off-topic, leaving users helpless without out-
045   right rejecting prompts. This over-reliance underscores the urgent necessity for a more
046   nuanced approach to prompt selection and optimization, especially through multi-objective
047   strategies that focus on both effectiveness and usefulness.
048   Furthermore, existing jailbreaking approaches struggle to explain why certain special di-
049   rected vectors Zheng et al. (2024a) result in model rejections, highlighting a significant
050   challenge in comprehending the underlying distributions that dictate model behavior. The
051   absence of clear explanations regarding the acceptance or rejection of prompts makes it
052   challenging to establish a reliable safety boundary. Incorporating ranking mechanisms and
053   conducting a thorough analysis of the distribution of responses can help provide inter-
      pretability and enable the identification of a more concrete safety boundary for prompts.

054
      These considerations are essential to ensure that jailbreaking attempts not only achieve
055   success but also do so within explainable and safe constraints.
056
057   Another major limitation in current black-box jailbreak optimization strategies is the lack of
058
      transparency and interpretability. Most techniques rely on end-to-end optimization without
      adequately explaining the processes involved. The lack of interpretability makes it difficult
059
      to understand how jailbreak methods evolve or how specific adjustments impact the success
060
      rate of jailbreak attempts. Addressing this gap through a more structured explanation of
061   the optimization processes will lead to more reliable and controllable jailbreak techniques.
062
063   To address these issues, we propose BlackDAN, a black-box, multi-objective, human-
064
      readable, controllable, and extensible jailbreak optimization framework. BlackDAN intro-
      duces a novel approach by optimizing multiple objectives simultaneously, including attack
065
      success rate (ASR), context relevance, and other factors. In contrast to traditional methods
066
      that focus solely on achieving a high ASR, BlackDAN adopts a more balanced approach by
067   simultaneously addressing the trade-offs between effectiveness, interpretability, and safety.
068   We hypothesize, verify, and analyze the concept of a safe boundary for prompts within this
069   framework, using multi-objective optimization to refine the selection of useful and effective
070   prompts while maintaining unsafety constraints.
071
      To realize BlackDAN, we leverage the advances of Multiobjective Evolutionary Algorithms
072
      (MOEAs) Zhou et al. (2011), specifically the NSGA-II algorithm Deb et al. (2002), which
073   shows effectiveness in solving complex multi-objective problems. By incorporating pareto-
074   dominance,mutation and crossover mechanisms, BlackDAN is capable of exploring a wider
075   solution space while providing clear explanations of the optimization process. This allows
076   for a more transparent and interpretable methodology for conducting jailbreak attacks,
077   addressing the shortcomings of traditional end-to-end optimization techniques.

138   2   Related Work
139
140   LLMs’ susceptibility to adversarial attacks has been explored through various approaches,
141   mainly categorized into white-box and black-box attacks. White-box attacks require access
142   to the model’s parameters, as demonstrated by Zou et al. (2023), who utilized gradient
143   search to optimize adversarial prompts by accessing the model’s logits. Other methods,
144   such as Shadow alignment Yang et al. (2023b) and Weak-to-Strong Jailbreak Zhao et al.
145   (2024), involve modifying the model’s weights or decoding processes to bypass safeguards,
146   making these approaches unsuitable for black-box LLMs. On the other hand, black-box
147
      attacks operate solely through prompt manipulation, modifying input queries to induce
      harmful outputs. Examples include methods like AutoDAN Liu et al. (2023b), PAIR Chao
      et al. (2023), and PAP Zeng et al. (2024), where LLMs are used to generate harmful queries.
148
149
      Rule-based techniques have also been proposed, as illustrated by Yuan et al. (2023), who
150   encrypted harmful queries and requested LLMs to respond in the ciphered format. Other
151   rule-based methods include EasyJailbreak Zhou et al. (2024).
152
153
      Black-box attacks are inherently more challenging than white-box attacks. Many existing
      techniques rely heavily on the transferability of adversarial examples; adversarial examples
      designed for one model often succeed in attacking other victim models. It is generally as-
154
155
      sumed that attackers can query the victim model to annotate training samples or access a
156   pre-trained source model designed for the same task as the victim model. To enhance ad-
157   versarial transferability, several methods have been proposed to adjust the backpropagation
158   computation. Techniques include the skip gradient method (SGM) Wu et al. (2020), linear
159   backpropagation (LinBP) Guo et al. (2020), intermediate-level attack (ILA) Huang et al.
160   (2019), and ILA++ Li et al. (2020); Guo et al. (2022). Increasing input diversity has also
161   been widely adopted, as evidenced by works like Xie et al. (2019), Dong et al. (2019), Lin
      et al. (2019), Huang et al. (2024), and Wang et al. (2021).

      3     Methodology
163
164
      In this setting, we aim to generate prompts that maximize the likelihood of generating unsafe
165   responses from a target language model T while ensuring semantic consistency between the
166   generated responses and harmful questions. This process is framed as a multi-objective
167   optimization problem, and we utilize NSGA-II (Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm
168   II)Deb et al. (2002) to perform the optimization.

189   3.1   Fitness Functions
190
      We define two continuous fitness functions to guide the optimization process:
191
192
      Unsafe Token Probability (Using llama_guard_2): The first fitness function, f1 , eval-
      uates the probability that a response R generated by T contains unsafe content. This is
193
194
      computed by the proxy model llama_guard_2, which outputs the log-probability of specific
195   ”unsafe tokens.”
196
197   Given a prompt P and a harmful question Q, let R = T (P, Q) denote the response generated
198
      by the target model T . The unsafe token probability is computed as:
199                               f1 (P, Q) = log P (unsafe token | R),
200
      where P (unsafe token | R) is the probability of the most relevant unsafe token appearing in
201
      response R as calculated by llama_guard_2.
202

      Semantic Consistency (Using all-MiniLM-L6-v2): In the black-box attack setting,
203
204
      we do not have direct access to the target model’s internals or its embeddings. Instead,
205   we utilize a pre-trained proxy model, such as the all-MiniLM-L6-v2, to generate sentence
206   embeddings for both the harmful prompt and the candidate responses. These embeddings
207   allow us to measure the semantic similarity between the prompt and the responses.
208
      The second fitness function, f2 , measures the semantic consistency between the generated
      response R and the harmful question Q. We use a pre-trained sentence embedding proxy
209
210
      model Mp (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) to compute the embeddings of both Q and R and then
211   calculate their cosine similarity.
212
213   Let eQ = EncodeMp (Q) and eR = EncodeMp (R) represent the embeddings of Q and R,
214
      respectively. The cosine similarity between these two embeddings is computed as:

222   3.2   NSGA-II for Multi-Objective Jailbreaking Prompts Optimization
223
      To find an optimal set of jailbreak prompts, we apply the NSGA-II algorithm. This algo-
224   rithm performs multi-objective optimization based on two key criteria:
225
226   Dominance: A solution P1 dominates another solution P2 if it is better in at least one
227   objective (e.g., higher unsafe token probability or better semantic consistency) and no worse
228   in all other objectives. For a problem with m objectives, we define dominance as:
229
230                               if ∀i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , m}, fi (P1 , Q) ≥ fi (P2 , Q)
                      P1 ≺ P2
231
                                  and ∃j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , m}, fj (P1 , Q) > fj (P2 , Q),
232
233   where fi (P, Q) represents the fitness value for the i-th objective function given the prompt
234   P and the harmful question Q.
235
236   Crowding Distance: Once the population is sorted into non-dominated fronts, a crowd-
237   ing distance is assigned to each solution in order to maintain diversity. The crowding dis-
238   tance d(P ) for an individual solution P in a given front is calculated across all m objective
239   functions. For each objective fi , the crowding distance is computed as:
                                                          − fiprev
240                                             m   next
                                               X    fi
241                                   d(P ) =                        ,
                                                    fimax − fimin
242                                            i=1
243   where finext and fiprev are the fitness values of the neighboring solutions with respect to the
244   i-th objective, and fimax and fimin are the maximum and minimum fitness values in the front
245   for the i-th objective.
246
247
      This ensures that the solutions selected from each non-dominated front are both optimal in
      terms of the multiple objectives and diverse with respect to each objective.

      3.3   Genetic Operations: Crossover and Mutation
250
251   NSGA-II evolves the population using genetic operations:
252
253   Crossover: The crossover operation creates two new offspring by recombining sentences
254   from two parent prompts. Let P1 and P2 be the parent prompts. The offspring C1 and C2
255   are generated by randomly swapping sentences between the two parent prompts:
256
                                        C1 , C2 = Crossover(P1 , P2 ).
257

      Mutation: The mutation operation modifies a randomly selected word in a prompt with
258
259
      a synonym. Let W represent a randomly chosen word from prompt P , and let Syn(W )
260   denote the set Loper & Bird (2002)of synonyms for W . A mutated prompt is generated as:
261
262                             P 0 = Mutation(P ) where W 0 ∈ Syn(W ).

517
518   In this paper, we introduced BlackDAN, a multi-objective, controllable jailbreak optimiza-
519   tion framework for large language models (LLMs) and multimodal large language models
520   (MLLMs). Beyond optimizing for attack success rate (ASR) and stealthiness, BlackDAN
521
      addresses the critical challenge of context consistency by ensuring that jailbreak responses
      remain semantically aligned with the original harmful prompts. This ensures that responses
      are not only evasive but also relevant, increasing their practical impact. Leveraging the
522
523
      NSGA-II algorithm, our method significantly improves over traditional single-objective tech-
524   niques, achieving higher success rates and more coherent jailbreak responses across various
525   models. Furthermore, BlackDAN is highly extensible, allowing the integration of any num-
526   ber of user-defined objectives, making it a versatile framework for a wide range of opti-
527   mization tasks. The inclusion of multiple objectives—specifically ASR, stealthiness, and
528   semantic consistency—sets a new benchmark for generating useful and interpretable jail-
529   break responses while maintaining safety and robustness in evaluation.
