009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) shines brightly in large language models (LLMs) and
012            demonstrates outstanding performance in plentiful natural language processing
013            tasks. However, existing methods transforming LLMs from dense to MoE face
014            significant data requirements and typically rely on large-scale post-training. In this
015            paper, we propose Upcycling Instruction Tuning (UpIT), a data-efficient approach
016            for tuning a dense pre-trained model into a MoE instruction model. Specifically,
017            we first point out that intermediate checkpoints during instruction tuning of the
018            dense model are naturally suitable for specialized experts, and then propose an
019
               expert expansion stage to flexibly achieve models with flexible numbers of experts,
               where genetic algorithm and parameter merging are introduced to ensure sufficient
020
               diversity of new extended experts. To ensure that each specialized expert in the
021
               MoE model works as expected, we select a small amount of seed data that each
022            expert excels to pre-optimize the router. Extensive experiments with various data
023            scales and upcycling settings demonstrate the outstanding performance and data
024            efficiency of UpIT, as well as stable improvement in expert or data scaling. Further
025            analysis reveals the importance of ensuring expert diversity in upcycling.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance on various NLP tasks
031   and are gradually becoming part of our daily lives through chatbot applications such as ChatGPT,
032   Copilot, etc (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2024). As LLMs become increasingly
033   prevalent, the high computational of traditional dense architecture with high computational costs
034   in the inference phase poses significant obstacles to downstream deployment. How to improve the
035   model performance without proportionally increasing computing resources become a hot topic in the
036   field (Muennighoff et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024). In response to this challenge, Mixture-of-Experts
037   (MoE) receives extensive attention due to its excellent scalability, which expands model capacity with
038   almost no extra inference overhead (Fedus et al., 2022; Zoph et al., 2022). Recently, many MoE-based
039
      LLMs have emerged in various scenarios with outstanding effectiveness and efficiency (Dai et al.,
      2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024a).
040
041   Upcycling is garnering increasing attention for converting dense LLMs through a series of processes,
042   including expanding experts, integrating routers, and subsequent post-training, ultimately yielding
043   MoE-style models. As depicted in Figure 1, current solutions are broadly classified into two categories:
044   (a) Vanilla Upcycling, which directly upcycle a dense model to a MoE model by replicating FFN
045
      layers, followed by a large-scale post-training to optimize the additional experts and corresponding
      routers (Komatsuzaki et al., 2023). Due to the homogeneity of experts in the initial stage, a large
046
      amount of post-training data is usually necessary, such as ~1T tokens for full parameter training
047
      or ~5M instruction data for parameter efficient fine-tuning (Dou et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024a).
048   (b) Specialized Upcycling, which first trains specialized experts based on meticulously designed
049   domain-specific datasets and then proceeds with upcycling and post-training (Sukhbaatar et al., 2024).
050   Despite having superior performance, it still requires hundreds of billions of elaborately constructed
051   domain data and lacks flexibility in scaling the number of domain-specific experts. To sum up,
052   although expert specialization slightly reduces data requirements, the current approach to upcycling
053   from dense to MoE heavily relies on a large amount of training data, how to efficiently and flexibly
      train a MoE instruction model based on a dense pre-trained model is still an open problem.

087    To answer this question, we first conduct a pilot experiment on dense models to observe the character-
088    istics of models at different epochs during standard instruction tuning. Figure 2 shows checkpoints
089    saved at different epochs exhibit interleaved performance across benchmarks in various domains. In
090    practical terms, we categorize nine benchmarks into four domains: factual knowledge, reasoning,
091    coding, and world knowledge, where the performance on each benchmark generally shows an upward
092    trend followed by a downward trend, but the position of the maximum value varies. For example,
093
       the model trained at epoch 2 demonstrates superior performance on HellaSwag and Natual Question,
       whereas the model trained at epoch 0.25 performs best on MMLU. In other words, models with
094
       different training steps demonstrate varying expertise in handling distinct domains. This phenomenon
095
       inspires us to consider that different checkpoints during instruction tuning are inherently suitable for
096    constructing specialized experts.
097
098    In light of the above findings, we propose Upcycling Instruction Tuning (UpIT), which starts from
099
       a dense pre-trained model and trains a MoE instruction model with a flexible number of experts,
       following the basic thought of specialized upcycling. Figure 1 illustrates the four stages of UpIT.
100
       Specifically, (1) Expert Preparation. Considering the differences among checkpoints in the pilot
101
       experiment, it is sufficient to fine-tune the dense pre-trained model and save checkpoints at fixed
102    intervals to prepare for specialized experts, without undertaking meticulous checkpoint selection.
103    (2) Expert Expansion. Given the fixed checkpoints, we extend a flexible number of new experts
104    based on genetic algorithms. In each iteration, we select two experts with the greatest differences,
105    merge their parameters and obtain a new expert. We also perform parameter scaling and dropping
106    before merging to simulate the mutation and further promote the discrepancy of experts. (3) Router
107    Initialization. Traditional routers are randomly initialized and insensitive to expert capabilities. Here,
       we assign each expert their skilled data and introduce an auxiliary binary classification loss to pre-

      optimize the corresponding routing vector, ensuring that all experts are capable of fully exhibiting their
109   strengths in the MoE model. (4) Model Upcycling. Before post-training, we merge the parameters of
110   multiple dense models into a MoE model. Unlike existing methods, the pre-optimized routing vectors
111   are merged into a routing matrix, serving as the final router.
112
      Overall, by leveraging the differences in existing dense checkpoints and introducing the expert expan-
113
      sion stage, UpIT comprehensively reduces the cost of acquiring specialized experts and improves
114
      the flexibility of expert numbers. Router initialization further maintains expert diversity, thereby
115   encouraging more effective utilization of data characteristics during the post-training of the MoE
116   model. From an implementation perspective, UpIT divides standard instruction tuning into two
117   parts, where the first part is responsible for expert preparation, and the second is post-training after
118   upcycling. Between these two stages, we find that only 1% of the training data (approximately 500
119   to 5,000 samples) is enough to pre-optimize the routing vectors, which means that UpIT efficiently
120   upcycles from dense to MoE without significantly increasing the overall data requirements.
121   We conduct extensive experiments under LoRA-based and FFN-based upcycling settings, with LoRA
122   and FFN as experts. For a fair comparison, we train all models on IDEA dataset (Wu et al., 2024a),
123   considering data sizes ranging from 50K to 500K, and evaluate the performance of nine benchmarks.
124   Experimental results show that UpIT is consistently better under both settings than dense instruction
125   tuning and other upcycling baselines. Especially in situations with small amounts of training data,
126   existing upcycling methods often can not work well, while UpIT utilizes the discrepancy of experts
127   and achieves better results than dense baselines. Moreover, UpIT exhibits excellent scalability, with
128   expected performance improvements when increasing training data, total expert number, or activated
129
      expert number. The router visualization and ablation study also verify the overall promoting effect of
      expert diversity on upcycled MoE models.
130
131   In summary, the main contributions of this paper are as follows:
132
133          • We propose UpIT, an efficient specialized upcycling method via parameter merging, which
134
               can achieve an instruction model with a flexible number of experts. To best of our knowledge,
               it is the first attempt to utilize intermediate dense checkpoints for model upcycling.
135
136          • We emphasize the importance of expert discrepancy in upcycling and incorporate the idea
137            into the entire design of UpIT. The innovative router initialization stage ensures that all
138            specialized experts play to their strengths in the final MoE model.
139          • Extensive experiments under LoRA and FFN-based settings demonstrate that UpIT signif-
140            icantly outperforms existing methods, whether in scenarios with sufficient or insufficient
141            data, exhibiting outstanding flexibility, scalability, and performance upper bound.

143   2     M ETHODOLOGY
144
145   In this section, we provide a detailed exposition of UpIT. Generally speaking, UpIT perpetuates the
146   concept of specialized upcycling, further using intermediate checkpoints to reduce data requirements,
147   expanding experts to fit the flexible number of experts, and pre-optimizing routing vectors to ensure
148   that each expert in the instruct MoE model leverages their strengths.
149
150   2.1   P RELIMINARIES
151
152   Before introducing UpIT, here we first briefly review some basic concepts of MoE.
153
      Mixture-of-Experts (MoE). MoE significantly scale up the total parameter number and increases
154   the knowledge capacity of language models, by selectively activating some of the parameters during
155   inference, it does not proportionally increase the computational workload. In transformers-based
156   models, the feed-forward neural network (FFN) layer in each transformer block is typically replaced
157   with a MoE layer, which comprises N identical and independent experts {Ei }N         i=1 , along with a
158   router R(·) for assigning experts, where each expert generally corresponds to an FFN layer, and
159   in the scenario of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), the expert may also be LoRA matrices.
160   Formally, for the hidden states h of the attention layer, the output o of the MoE layer is represented
              PN
161   as o = i=1 R(h)Ei (h). Here, Ei (h) is the output of the i-th expert, R(h) denotes the score for
      all experts, where experts with the highest scores are usually selected to calculate the final output.

181        Upcycling. Upcycling seeks to avoid training MoE models from scratch by transforming an existing
182        dense model into a MoE model, followed by a post-training stage to integrate all the parameters
183        into an organic whole. It starts with dense models, forming experts by expanding the FFN layer or
184
           creating new LoRA branches, and then adding routers to control the dispatch of input tokens. In this
           process, the remaining embedding layers, attention blocks, normalization modules, and output layers
185
           are directly transferred from the initial dense model to the ultimate MoE model, and the router is
186
           randomly initialized and optimized in the post-training stage.

           2.2   W ORKFLOW OF U P IT
190
           Starting from the dense pre-trained model, UpIT achieves a MoE instruction model. Algorithm 1
191
           provides the working sketch. In this section, we provide a detailed explanation of each process.
192
193        Expert Preparation. In the instruction tuning of LLMs, as shown in Figure 2, the performance
194        of intermediate checkpoints varies across different benchmarks, and different checkpoints exhibit
195
           unique strengths in different domains, highlighting the potential to serve as specialized experts.
           Compared to the labour-intensive method of training diverse expert models with massive domain-
196
           specific data (Sukhbaatar et al., 2024), we believe that the natural variations among checkpoints
197
           provide a more efficient pathway to developing specialized expert models. By training dense models
198        to generate multiple checkpoints and saving them at regular intervals during training, we can easily
199        obtain a series of expert models proficient in different domains, resulting in a more cost-effective
200        method for preparing specialized expert models.
201
           Expert Expansion. Given that the fixed number of checkpoints only sometimes corresponds with the
202
           flexible requirements of expert number, acquiring additional checkpoints entails redundant training if
203
           the number of experts exceeds the saved checkpoints. Here, we propose to address these challenges
204        by generating distinct experts from existing ones without extensive retraining (see also Algorithm 2).
205        Specifically, we draw inspiration from genetic algorithms, where two experts with the greatest
206        differences are selected as parents in each iteration. We simulate the mutation process by randomly
207        assigning weights to the parents and apply DARE (Yu et al., 2024) to introduce mutations into the
208        newly created expert further, enhancing its discrepancy and adaptability. Such an expansion process
209        not only eliminates the need for additional retraining but also facilitates the flexible expansion of the
210        number of experts, ultimately improving the scalability of UpIT.
211        Router Initialization. Since routers remain randomly initialized after upcycling, which leads to the
212        misallocation of tokens in the early post-training stages, in UpIT, such misallocation will weaken the
213        expert differences in the previous stage and impact the learning efficiency of MoE models. To solve
214        this problem, we propose a data selection approach to curate expert-specific data tailored to each
215        expert model and pre-optimize additional routing vectors to ensure the discrepancy among experts
           (see also Algorithm 3). Specifically, we initially embed one-dimensional routing vectors R before

      expert Ei , Pi is the average fraction of the router probability allocated for expert Ei .

495   4    R ELATED W ORK
496
497   Mixture of Experts. Mixture of Experts (MoE) (Jacobs et al., 1991) modifies the FFN layers or
498   inserts additional branches to construct experts and activates them sparsely, thereby significantly
499   enlarging the model capacity without noticeably increasing computational costs. The exploration
500   of MoE has increasingly captured attention in recent years. Vanilla upcycling (Komatsuzaki et al.,
501   2023) copies FFN layers, followed by post-training, have achieved a more convenient MoE training
502   strategy. LoRAMoE (Dou et al., 2024), MoELoRA (Luo et al., 2024), MixLoRA (Li et al., 2024b)
503   and MoLE (Wu et al., 2024b) develop an MoE model by incorporating several LoRA branches as
504
      experts, utilizing sparse activation or linear weighting for model construction. PESC (Wu et al.,
      2024a) introduces adapter-based structures after the FFN layers, exploring a parameter-efficient MoE
505
      model that diverges from the LoRA paradigm. MoExtend (Zhong et al., 2024) adapt to new tasks
506
      by expanding the MoE layer during the training process, mitigating catastrophic forgetting. MoE
507   Jetpack (Zhu et al., 2024b) introduces checkpoint recycling, which leverages checkpoints to enhance
508   the flexibility and diversity of expert initialization. In contrast, Branch-Train-MiX (Sukhbaatar et al.,
509   2024) and Self-MoE (Kang et al., 2024) explore a Specialized Upcycling method by introducing
510   specialized experts. Despite superior performance, they still require considerable domain data to
511   acquire specialized experts. In this paper, we integrate the advantages of the work above and utilize
512   intermediate checkpoints for expert preparation, innovatively propose an expert expansion strategy
513   and an stage of pre-optimizing routing vectors to enhance flexibility, scalability and data efficiency.
514   Model Merging. Model merging has emerged as a prominent research direction in recent years,
515   focusing on consolidating multiple task-specific models into a unified model with diverse capabili-
516   ties (Wortsman et al., 2022; Ilharco et al., 2023). Model merging usually considers the combination
517   of model parameters without accessing the original training data. Average Merging (Wortsman
518   et al., 2022) is one typical model merging approach, which utilizes averaged parameters to construct
519   the merged model. Task Arithmetic (Zhang et al., 2023) employs a pre-defined scaling term to
520   distinguish the importance of various models. Fisher Merging (Matena & Raffel, 2022) performs au-
521
      tomatic weighted merging of parameters, where the Fisher information matrix calculates the weights.
      TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2023) tackles the task conflicts in (Zhang et al., 2023) by trimming
522
      low-magnitude parameters, resolving sign disagreements, and disjointly merging parameters with
523
      consistent signs. DARE (Yu et al., 2024) first sparsifies delta parameters of several SFT homologous
524   models and then merges them into a single model. We innovatively integrate the model merging
525   concept into the MoE model, leveraging genetic algorithms and DARE to expand and evolve new
526   experts. This approach enhances the scalability of our framework.

      5    C ONCLUSION
530
      In this paper, we present a novel, flexible, scalable, and data-efficient approach, Upcycling Instruction
531
      Tuning (UpIT), for transforming dense pre-trained models into MoE instruction models. By leveraging
532
      intermediate checkpoints as specialized experts and implementing an expert expansion stage with
533   genetic algorithms and parameter merging, UpIT successfully enhances expert diversity while
534   allowing for a flexible number of experts. The strategic selection of seed data ensures that each expert
535   and router performs optimally within the MoE framework. Our extensive experiments demonstrate
536   that UpIT not only achieves superior performance across various benchmarks but also maintains
537   stability in expert and data scaling. Further analysis emphasizes the critical importance of expert
538   diversity in the upcycling process. Overall, UpIT offers a promising pathway for enhancing the
539   efficiency and effectiveness of MoE models, paving the way for future advancements in the field.

      A.1   D ETAILED D ESCRIPTION OF BASELINES
759
760   For LoRA-based settings, we compare several baselines with our proposed UpIT. (1) LoRA (Hu et al.,
761   2021): It adds low-rank matrix branches for parameter-efficient fine-tuning. (2) Self-MoE (Kang
762   et al., 2024): It employs specialized experts to build MoE model, and only train the routers during
763   the post-training stage. We solely reuse the training strategy of Self-MoE, and only train the routers
764   after upcycling with intermediate checkpoints. (3) PESC (Wu et al., 2024a): It adds several adapter
765   structures after the FFN block as experts. (4) LoRAMoEPT (Dou et al., 2024): It employs the same
766   structure as UpIT which insert several LoRA branches as experts, and (5) LoRAMoESFT : It copies
767
      the final-step checkpoint to form MoE blocks.
768   Similarly, for FFN-based settings, we compare UpIT with (1) SFT, It is the standard fine-tuning solu-
769   tion. (2) Self-MoE (Kang et al., 2024), It is similar with the LoRA-based method. (3) UpcyclePT (Ko-
770   matsuzaki et al., 2023): It is the vanilla upcycling approach for transforming a dense pre-trained
771   model to the MoE model. (4) UpcycleSFT . It copies the final-step checkpoint to form MoE blocks.
772
773   A.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS .
774
775   To validate the effectiveness of our method, we employ comprehensive evaluation benchmarks, which
776   contain various abilities. (1) Factual Knowledge: To assess the LLMs’ factual knowledge, we
777
      employ the Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) (Hendrycks et al., 2021), ARC-e
      and ARC-c (Clark et al., 2018) datasets. MMLU comprises questions across 57 subjects from
778
      elementary to professional difficulty levels. ARC-e and ARC-c contain questions for science exams
779
      from grade 3 to grade 9. We report the 0-shot accuracy based on answer perplexity for MMLU and
780   ARC. (2) Reasoning: We utilize the test split of the Grade School Math (GSM8K) (Cobbe et al.,
781   2021), HellaSwag (HellaS.) (Zellers et al., 2019) and Big-Bench-Hard (BBH) (Suzgun et al., 2022)
782   benchmarks to evaluate reasoning abilities. We report the 8-shot accuracy for GSM8K and the 3-shot
783   accuracy for HellaSwag. (3) Coding: To probe the LLMs’ ability to generate functionally correct
784   programs from docstrings, we utilize HumanEval (HumanE.) (Chen et al., 2021) and report the
785   pass@1 performance. (4) World Knowledge: We adopt Natural Question (NQ) (Kwiatkowski et al.,
786   2019) and TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017) to evaluate the commonsense question-answering ability. All
787   of the above evaluations are performed using opencompass (Contributors, 2023) framework, and to
788
      expedite evaluation, we enable batch-padding with a batch size of 32.
