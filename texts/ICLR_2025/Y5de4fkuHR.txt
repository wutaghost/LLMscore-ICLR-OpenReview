008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            Mastering a skill generally relies on both hands-on experience from doers and
011            insightful, high-level guidance by mentors. Will this strategy also work well for
012            solving complex non-convex optimization problems? Here, a common gradient-
013            based optimizer acts like a disciplined doer, making locally optimal updates at
014            each step. Large Language Models (LLMs) can also search for better solutions by
015            inferring from natural language instructions, akin to a high-level mentor. In this
016            paper, we show that these two participators are complementary to each other and can
017            effectively collaborate as a combined optimization framework. The collaborative
018
               optimization is achieved by alternating between the gradient-based and LLM-
               based optimizers. We instruct LLMs to generate possibly improved solutions by
019
               taking parameter trajectories recorded during the previous stage of gradient-based
020
               optimization into account. Inferred results of LLMs are used as restarting points
021            for the next stage of gradient optimization. We verify the effectiveness of this
022            optimization framework on prompt tuning. By leveraging both the locally rigorous
023            gradient-based optimizer and the high-level deductive LLM-based optimizer, the
024            combined optimization method consistently yields improvements over competitive
025            baselines on a variety of tasks. Our results demonstrate the synergistic effect of
026            conventional gradient-based optimization and the inference ability of LLMs.
027            The code will be made publicly available.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Humans acquire skills through practical experience and external guidance from mentors. Similarly,
032   solving optimization problems relies on well-designed algorithms incorporating prior knowledge, as
033   well as meticulous procedural implementation. Practically, gradient-based algorithms have almost
034   become the default choice for solving optimization problems in various machine learning models. We
035   regard the gradient-based optimizers as disciplined doers that are effective in navigating the parameter
036   space through precise, incremental adjustments based on gradient information. However, their local
037   perspective often limits their ability to escape local optima and discover more optimal solutions.
038   In this work, we proposed an optimization method using LLMs as optimization instructors to
039   provide high-level guidance for gradient-based optimizers. The basis of LLMs capable of solving
040   optimization problems lies in their ability to comprehend and generate nuanced and contextually
041   relevant text. Recent studies have proposed to utilize LLMs as strategy planners or optimizers in
042   concrete optimization tasks. For example, Eureka (Ma et al., 2024) trains agents by reinforcement
043   reward function designed by GPT-4, which can learn complex skills such as dexterous pen spinning.
044
      It shows that LLM can guide the trend of optimized policy on a delicate level.
045   Employing LLMs for optimization offers unique advantages. The optimization is conducted with
046   natural language interactions, which contributes to two charming properties. First, the implementation
047   of the optimization is code-free. The optimization process only involves natural language instruction-
048   response interactions with LLMs. Secondly, LLMs generate instruction-related outputs by assembling
049   task-related semantic tokens that are difficult to discover through continuous gradient-based learning.
050
      The generation results can be diverse and hardly limited by the local optima issue, which is often
      encountered by gradient-based optimization. The solutions discovered by LLM with a lower loss
051
      value have more possibilities to optimize to a better convergence point.
052
053   On the other hand, LLM-based optimization faces instability issues since it has no lexical constraints.
      LLMs analyze the problem on the semantic level and response in vocabulary space. The generated


                                                         1

054
      solutions may not be as precise as the results optimized by rigorous step-by-step gradient descent
055   in the parameter space, especially under limited LLM API calling budgets. Existing LLM-based
056   methods, such as Liu et al. (2023a), need to set multiple search trials to find a promising result. We
057   instruct LLMs with the optimization trajectory of gradient-based optimizers, which is converted to
058   natural language format, grounding LLMs to a more promising sub-region of the vocabulary space.
059   The local carefulness of gradient-based optimizer and diverse semantic exploration of LLM-based
060   optimizer are complementary to each other, suggesting a collaborative optimization approach.
061   With this motivation, we propose an optimization method that combines the conventional gradient-
062   based optimizer and LLM optimizer. The optimization approach leverages both the locally rigorous
063   gradient-based optimizer in parameter space and high-level deductive LLM-based optimizer in
064   unconstrained vocabulary space for better performance. To achieve collaborative training based on
065   the two optimizers, we interleave the conventional training process of gradient-based optimization
066   with interactions with LLM. First, we optimize the parameters for only dozens of iterations using a
067   gradient optimizer. Then the optimized parameters in the intermediate step, along with their loss and
068   accuracy on the training set, are provided as history trajectory clues for LLM to infer new candidates
069
      that are potentially more effective. After grabbing the response from LLM, we use the generated
      results as restarting points of the parameters for subsequent gradient-based optimization iterations.
070
      The two optimizers are operated alternately to optimize the parameter collaboratively. The final
071
      optimized results are obtained by the gradient optimizer with a stable convergence. Our proposed
072   optimizing strategy only injects several API calls to LLM to the conventional gradient-based training
073   workflow.
074
075
      We study the effectiveness of the combined optimization in a widely considered prompt tuning
      framework. Specifically, we optimize textual prompts for language and vision-language pre-trained
076
      models. Tuning such prompts is shown to bring significant performance improvements for the
077
      adaptation of pre-trained models (Zhou et al., 2022b; Lester et al., 2021). However, optimizing the
078   prompt in the input discrete vocabulary space or word embedding space is not an easy problem
079   for conventional gradient-based optimizers that is widely adopted. We validate that the proposed
080   collaborative optimization framework leads to consistent improvements in prompt optimization across
081   a variety of tasks and optimizer LLMs.
082
      In summary, our contributions include:
083
084      • Based on the textual prompt optimization problem, we showcase the limitations of gradient-
085
           based optimization, especially the entrapment in local optima, and attribute the issues to the
           limitation of gradient-based optimizer in the short-sighted local perspective of the parameter
086
           space.
087
088      • We propose a novel optimization approach that combines the deductive LLM-based optimizer in
089
           unconstrained vocabulary space with the disciplined gradient-based optimizer in the parameter
           space for better optimization performance.
090
091      • We test the effectiveness of the proposed combined optimization method on prompt tuning tasks,
092
           and it achieves consistent improvements over existing competitive baseline methods, validating
           the complementary effect of LLM-based and gradient-based optimizers.

097   2.1   LLM S AND O PTIMIZATION P ROBLEMS
098
099
      Recent developments of Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated an unprecedented
      ability to comprehend and generate human-like text, leading to significant breakthroughs in natural
100
      language processing (Touvron et al., 2023a; Chowdhery et al., 2022). The robust capability of
101
      LLMs in natural language comprehension and the generation of more nuanced and contextually
102   relevant text provides a foundation for various advanced open-ended applications, where they are
103   being instructed to participate in dialogue (OpenAI et al., 2024), formulate and execute plans (Gupta
104   & Kembhavi, 2022; Gao et al., 2023), writing codes (Ma et al., 2024), etc. LLMs’ rich prior
105   knowledge and reasoning ability open the way to addressing practical optimization problems for
106   real-world applications. Existing works have validated the effectiveness of LLM for solving small-
107   scale mathematical optimization problems (Yang et al., 2023), optimizing prompts (Zhou et al.,
      2023; Pryzant et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a; Fernando et al., 2023; Diao et al.,


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2023), searching for network architectures (Chen et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023), hyperparameter
109   optimization (Chen et al., 2022) and discovering physical equations (Du et al., 2024).
110
111   In terms of prompt optimization, APE (Zhou et al., 2023) proposes to use LLMs to generate and select
112
      natural language prompts by instructing LLMs with task definitions and targets. LLMs can obtain
      better solutions iteratively by analyzing previously found candidates. APO (Pryzant et al., 2023)
113
      proposes that editing prompts by LLM is analogous to conducting gradient descent in the natural
114
      language domain. They imitate the gradient-based learning by providing the failure cases to LLM for
115   a semantic "gradient" and updating the prompt in an opposite semantic direction. EVOPROMPT (Guo
116   et al., 2024) also connects LLM-based optimization to traditional algorithms for better explainability.
117   They integrate LLM into the workflow of evolutionary algorithms by instructing LLM to act like
118   evolutionary operators to generate new candidate prompts. The insight that LLM naturally enables an
119   intelligent variation operator is also revealed in LMC (Meyerson et al., 2024) and ELM (Lehman et al.,
120   2022) on image and code generation tasks. Liu et al. (2023a) searches prompts for the vision-language
121   model by conversing with LLM following designed strategies and achieves comparable results to
122   white-box gradient-based prompt tuning.
123   The results achieved in these approaches demonstrate that LLMs can be applied as a general-purpose
124   optimizer for optimization tasks. Although some of them (Pryzant et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024)
125   explored the connection between LLM-based inference and conventional optimization algorithms,
126   e.g., gradient descent, evolutionary computing. However, the proposed optimization workflows are
127   still largely based on the inherent ability of LLM, which leads to inadequate data utilization and
128   suboptimal performance. For example, in Liu et al. (2023a), the performance superiority only holds
129
      in the one-shot training set and LLMs can not effectively optimize to gain more improvements based
      on more training data. It shows that the interaction format of natural language makes it hard for
130
      LLMs to optimize as precisely as numerical optimization algorithms, e.g., gradient-based optimizers.
131
      Besides, the API calling budget bounded by the high cost of operating super large-scale models
132   also limits the performance of LLM-based optimization. This motivates us to design a collaborative
133   optimization method to achieve better optimization performance by combining both the results of
134   LLMs’ high-level reasoning and the stable convergence of conventional gradient-based optimizers.

      2.2   P ROMPT T UNING FOR P RE - TRAINED M ODELS
138
      Prompt tuning has emerged as a standard approach for the parameter-efficient adaptation of pre-
139
      trained models, aimed at improving their performance in various natural language processing (Lester
140   et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021) and vision-language (Zhou et al., 2022b;a; Yao et al., 2024) tasks.
141   Prompt-based tuning of pre-trained models appends learnable embeddings to the original sequence
142   of the data for the input layer or intermediate layer. Fine-tuning the lightweight parameters in the
143   prompt yields comparable performance even to full parameter fine-tuning and transferability (Vu
144   et al., 2022; Su et al., 2022) on various tasks (Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Liu et al., 2022).
145   Despite its widespread adoption, the conventional prompt tuning technique encounters challenges
146   related to slow convergence and suboptimal optimization (Ding et al., 2022), which undermines the
147   effectiveness of prompt tuning in a wider and larger scale of pre-trained models and downstream
148
      tasks. We attribute these issues to the complexity of the input embedding space of the pre-trained
      model, making it challenging to optimize the prompt effectively based on back-propagated gradients
149
      in this space.

180   In this part, we instantiate the task by prompt tuning for discriminative tasks, i.e., classification. In
181   a general situation, we consider a pre-trained multi-modal model E. The classes of input images
182   I or texts T can be recognized by classifying the representations E (I, T ), encoded by the pre-
183   trained model. We denote the task-specific classifier as F (·). The prediction can be obtained by
184   p (ŷ|I, T ) = F (E (I, T )).
185   To better adapt pre-trained models to various downstream tasks, prompt tuning introduces learnable
186   prompt tokens and formulates a task-specific input for the pre-trained model. The learnable prompt
187   tokens can be either continuous vectors (Zhou et al., 2022b; Lester et al., 2021) in the textual

190   input making up a task-specific input. The adapted output can be formulated as p (ŷ|I, T ; θ) =
191   F (E (Pθ , I, T )). In common practice, the prompt tokens are learned through labeled few-shot
192   samples from target task datasets. The parameters of the prompt are optimized by minimizing the

358   Implementation details and baselines. To comprehensively evaluate the effectiveness of our

363
      model, CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022b), TCP (Yao et al., 2024). CoOp is the founder of prompt tuning for
      vision-language models, and TCP represents one of the state-of-the-art advancements in this realm.
364
      Both methods exemplify the use of textual prompting techniques for enhancing vision-language

523   5   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      This paper proposes a collaborative optimization method combining the conventional gradient-based
      optimizer and inferential LLM-based optimizer. By alternating between the gradient-based and
526
      LLM-based optimization process, we combine the local carefulness of gradient- based optimizer
527
      and diverse semantic exploration of LLM-based optimizer. LLM-based optimizer mitigates the
528   inherent limitations of gradient-based optimization, such as entrapment in local optima, by inferring
529   high-level guidance from task descriptions and real-time optimization trajectories. We validated our
530   combined optimization method through prompt tuning tasks, where the synergy between LLM-based
531   optimizer and gradient-based optimizer has consistently demonstrated improved performance over
532   competitive baselines. These results underscore the complementary effect of LLM-based optimizer
533   and conventional gradient-based optimization. Our contributions inspire further exploration of the
534   advantages of LLM-based optimization over existing algorithms, paving the way for more effective
535   integration of LLM-based inference into conventional optimization workflows.
536   Limitations. Our proposed optimization method can not be directly employed for adapter-based or
537   LoRA-based fine-tuning methods. A feasible solution for handling higher dimensional parameters in
538   LLM-based optimization needs to be designed. We leave the application of the proposed optimization
539   framework to broader range of optimization problems (e.g., adapters, LoRA) and algorithms (e.g.,
      reinforce learning) as future work.
