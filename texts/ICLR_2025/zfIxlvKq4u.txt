032                                                  A BSTRACT
033
034                Autoregressive (AR) models and their variants are re-revolutionizing visual gener-
035                ation with improved frameworks. However, unlike the well-established practices
036
                   for diffusion models, a comprehensive recipe for AR models is lacking, e.g., se-
                   lecting image tokenizers, model architectures, and AR paradigms. In this work,
037
                   we delve into the design space of general AR models, including Mask Autoregres-
038
                   sive (MAR) models, to identify optimal configurations for efficient and scalable
039                image generation. We first conduct a detailed evaluation of four prevalent image
040                tokenizers across both AR and MAR settings, examining the impact of codebook
041                size (ranging from 1,024 to 262,144) on generation quality, and identify the most
042                effective tokenizer for image generation. Building on these insights, we propose
043                an enhanced MAR model architecture, named Masked Generative Image LLaMA
044                (MaskGIL), comprising of LlamaGen-VQ and Bidirectional LLaMA. To ensure
045                stable scaling, we introduce modifications like query-key normalization and post-
046                normalization, resulting in a series of class-conditional MaskGIL models, rang-
047
                   ing from 111M to 1.4B parameters. MaskGIL significantly improves the MAR
                   baseline, achieving a 3.71 FID comparable to state-of-the-art AR models on the
048
                   ImageNet 256ˆ256 benchmark, with only 8 inference steps, far fewer than the
049
                   256 steps needed for AR models. We also introduce a text-conditional MaskGIL
050                model with 775M parameters, capable of flexibly generating images at any reso-
051                lution. To bridge AR and MAR image generation, we explore their combination
052                during inference time. We release all models and code to foster further research1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057
      Autoregressive (AR) generative models have garnered increasing attention in image genera-
      tion (Razavi et al., 2019; Esser et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2021b; Lee et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022;
058
      Sun et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024), inspired by the success of Transformer (Vaswani, 2017) and
059
      GPT (Brown, 2020; OpenAI, 2022; Achiam et al., 2023) in NLP. This paradigm typically unfolds
060   in two stages: the first stage is to quantize an image to a sequence of discrete tokens. In the second
061   stage, an AR model is trained to predict the next token sequentially, based on the previously gener-
062   ated tokens. While AR models exhibit strong generative capabilities, their efficiency is hindered by
063   the extensive number of inference steps, as the AR model generates one token at a time.
064
      To overcome this limitation, Mask Autoregressive (MAR) generative models (Chang et al., 2022;
065
      Lezama et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b) have
066   been developed, aiming to deliver high-quality image generation with fewer inference steps. Unlike
067   traditional AR models that predict the next token, MAR models predict a subset of tokens, offering
068   a potential speed advantage. However, due to the inherent difficulties in training and prediction, the
069   generative capabilities of existing MAR models remain less robust compared to AR models.
070
      In addition, the choice of discrete-value image tokenizer is pivotal in both AR and MAR paradigms,
071
      as it significantly influences the quality of image generation. Despite the availability of various
072   tokenizers (Esser et al., 2021; Chang et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024) with reducing
073   reconstruction errors, they often demonstrate inconsistent performance in downstream text-to-image
074   generation tasks. This raises key questions: Which tokenizer is the most effective for image genera-
075   tion tasks, and how can tokenizers be designed to be both efficient and practical?
076
      In this paper, we first conduct a comprehensive study on four prevalent tokenizers, including
077
      MaskGIT-VQ (Chang et al., 2022), Chameleon-VQ (Team, 2024), LlamaGen-VQ (Sun et al., 2024)
078   and Open-MAGVIT2-VQ (Luo et al., 2024), across both AR and MAR generation paradigms. We
079   observe that as the codebook size2 increases (from 1,024 to 262,144), the tokenizer’s image re-
080   construction ability improves progressively. However, this does not always lead to better image
081   generation quality, as larger codebook sizes place higher demands on the model’s learning ability
082   and training parameters. Among the evaluated tokenizers, LlamaGen-VQ, with a codebook size of
083   16,384, demonstrates the best performance in both AR and MAR generation.
084   Building on these insights, we further explore the design space of MAR models to achieve efficient
085   and scalable image generation. While previous MAR models have utilized the Bidirectional Trans-
086   former architecture, the success of LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) in AR generation inspires us to
087   develop a Bidirectional LLaMA architecture. Through comparative analysis, we find that the Bidi-
088   rectional LLaMA demonstrates superior image generation capabilities. To ensure stable training
089   at larger scales, we introduce query-key normalization and post-normalization to manage the norm
090   growth (Dehghani et al., 2023; Team, 2024; Gao et al., 2024). Based on this architecture, we present
091   a series of class-conditional image generation models, ranging from 111M to 1.4B parameters. Ad-
092
      ditionally, we provide a text-conditional image generation model with 775M parameters, capable of
      flexibly generating images at various resolutions with high aesthetic quality.
093
094   Compared to AR generation, MAR generation has made a significant qualitative leap in speed.
095   However, despite our comprehensive exploration of MAR’s generation capabilities, it remains a
096   performance gap compared to AR. To harness the strengths of both paradigms, we propose a hybrid
097   framework during the inference stage. Specifically, we use an AR model to generate a portion of
098
      the tokens, which then serve as prompts for the MAR model to complete the remaining tokens. Our
      experiments on class-conditional and text-conditional generation tasks indicate that this strategy
099
      effectively strikes a balance between generation quality and speed, presenting a promising avenue
100
      for future research.
101

125   2.1   D ISCRETE -VALUE I MAGE T OKENIZER
126
127   To begin with, we revisit the discrete-value image tokenizer, which plays a crucial role in both
128   autoregressive and mask autoregressive image generation. The most commonly used model in this
129   domain is the VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017), an encoder-quantizer-decoder architecture, as
130
      shown in Figure 2a. This architecture employs a ConvNet for both the encoder and the decoder,
      featuring a downsampling ratio p. The encoder projects the image pixels x P RHˆW ˆ3 to a feature
131
      map f P RhˆwˆC , where h “ H{p and w “ W {p. The core of this process lies in the quantizer,
132
      which includes a codebook Z P RKˆC with K learnable vectors. Each vector f pi,jq in the feature
133
      map is mapped during quantization to the code index q pi,jq of its nearest vector z k in the codebook.
134   Consequently, the image pixels x P RHˆW ˆ3 are efficiently quantized into q P Qhˆw . During the
135   decoding phase, the code index q pi,jq is remapped to the feature vector and the decoder converts
136   these feature vectors back to the image pixels x̂. There are various works (Razavi et al., 2019;
137   Esser et al., 2021; Yu et al.) that continue to explore the design space of VQ-VAE for improving
138   reconstruction quality. Among them, VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021) introduces an adversarial loss that
139   is proven to effectively preserve the perpetual visual details.
140
141   2.2   AUTOREGRESSIVE G ENERATIVE M ODELS
142
143   Autoregressive models revolutionize the fields of language modeling (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam
144   et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Meta, 2024) and multimodal understanding (Liu
145   et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Team, 2024) using the unified next-token prediction paradigm for all
146   modalities with a casual transoformer, as illustrated in Figure 2b. This paradigm has been ex-
147   tended to the visual generation domain by early works such as DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021a),
148
      Cogview (Ding et al., 2021), and Parti (Yu et al., 2022). These works leverage a two-stage approach
      where the image tokenizer first encodes continuous images into discrete tokens then the transformer
149
      decoder models the flattened one-dimensional sequences. During training, the casual transformer
150
      is trained to predict the categorical distribution pθ pxi |xăi ; cq at each position conditioned on the
151   additional information c, e.g., text prompts or class labels. During inference, image token sequences
152   can be sampled in the same way as language generation and further decoded back to pixels. De-
153   spite this simple and unified paradigm for image synthesis, autoregressive-based visual generative
154   models have long been overlooked for a while, particularly after the exploding of diffusion models.
155   One potential reason is their inferior generation quality restricted by the image tokenizer. Recently,
156   LlamaGen (Sun et al., 2024) improves the design of image tokenizer and leverages the Llama archi-
157   tecture for scaling, which significantly enhance the performance of autoregressive models.
158

201   For high-quality image generation, the choice of a discrete-value image tokenizer is critical, as it
202   determines the upper limit of the generation quality. Various tokenizers have been developed, and in
203   this study, we evaluate four mainstream tokenizers based on their codebook sizes, which range from
204
      1,024 to 262,144. This range allows for a comprehensive analysis of how codebook size affects im-
      age generation quality. The tokenizers we selected include MaskGIT-VQ (Besnier & Chen, 2023),
205
      Chameleon-VQ (Team, 2024), LlamaGen-VQ (Sun et al., 2024), and Open-MAGVIT2-VQ (Luo
206
      et al., 2024), as shown in Table 1. All tokenizers use a downsampling ratio of 16 and are trained
207   on the ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009). Given a set of compact discrete image tokens, two
208   prominent frameworks are commonly used, including autoregressive and mask autoregressive gen-
209   eration. We conduct detailed experiments using both frameworks to provide comprehensive insights
210   into image tokenizers.

      Does increasing the codebook size improve generation quality? In Section 3.1, we observe that
271   image reconstruction quality is directly proportional to the codebook size, this is not the case for
272   image generation quality. As shown in Figure 3, as the codebook size increases, the FID value
273   initially decreases but then increases, both AR and MAR models exhibit this trend, which indicates
274   that there is an optimal range for codebook size. The underlying issue is that when the number
275   of learned or predicted codes becomes excessively large, it complicates model training. Therefore,
276   when selecting an image tokenizer for generation tasks, it is crucial to consider the model’s learning
277   capacity in addition to the codebook size.
278   Which tokenizer is the best for high-quality image generation? Based on the results, LlamaGen-VQ
279   emerges as the superior choice, delivering the best performance in both AR and MAR image gen-
280   eration tasks. Open-MAGVIT-VQ, which performs best in the reconstruction evaluation, delivers
281   unsatisfactory results in image generation quality, especially on the Transformer architecture of the
282   MAR generation task, where it performs very poorly. As for Chameleon-VQ, although it is widely
283   adopted in many previous image generation works (Team, 2024; Liu et al., 2024), its performance
284   does not match that of LlamaGen-VQ. Therefore, our exploration provides guidance for the selection
285
      of image tokenizers, suggesting that LlamaGen-VQ may be a better choice.

350   4.2   S CALE U P
351
352   Scaling Mask Autoregressive Models. In previous research on mask autoregressive image gen-
353   eration (Chang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023), model sizes have typically ranged between 200M and
354   300M parameters, with limited exploration into scaling larger models. In this study, we investi-
355
      gate scaling the parameter size from 111M to 1.4B on the class-conditioned ImageNet generation
      benchmark. The detailed configurations of our MaskGIL models, with varying parameter sizes, are
356
      presented in Table 2. We also develop text-to-image MaskGIL models with 775M parameters.
357
358
      Training Stability. Maintaining stable training while scaling MaskGIL models above 1.4B pa-
359   rameters proved challenging, with instabilities often emerging very late in the training process. This
360   aligns with the observations in several prior studies (Dehghani et al., 2023; Team, 2024; Gao et al.,
361   2024; Zhuo et al., 2024). The fundamental reason is that the standard LLaMA architecture for visual
362   modeling shows complex divergences due to slow norm growth in the mid-to-late stages of training.
363   We implement query-key normalization (QK-Norm) (Team, 2024; Dehghani et al., 2023) and Post-
364   Norm (Zhuo et al., 2024) to solve this problem. QK-Norm involves applying layer normalization to
365   the query and key vectors within the attention mechanism, while Post-Norm applies layer normal-
366   ization to the output of attention and mlp layer, effectively avoiding the uncontrollable norm growth
367
      due to un-normalized pathway and therefore stabilizing the training process.

462
463   In text-conditional image generation, we use Chameleon-VQ as the image tokenizer, aligning with
464   Lumina-mGPT, an autoregressive-based text-to-image model (Liu et al., 2024). To integrate the text
465
      condition into our MaskGIL model, we employ Gemma-2B (Team et al., 2024) as the text encoder.
      The encoded text features are projected through an additional MLP and then used as the prefilling
466
      token embedding in the MaskGIL models. We also incorporate both QK-Norm and Post-Norm to
467
      improve training stability. The training data consists of 20M high-aesthetic images with prompts
468   generated by a mixture of captioners. We leverage a two-stage training pipeline by first training on
469   256 ˆ 256 and then switching to 512 ˆ 512. To enable generating images with arbitrary shapes, we
470   design a multi-resolution training strategy by defining a series of size buckets and converting each
471   image to its nearest shape. All other training hyper-parameters follow class-conditional settings.
472
      In Figure 6, we use prompts randomly generated by GPT-4 to generate images at various resolutions.
473
      The generated images demonstrate a high degree of consistency with the text prompts. In the future,
474   we plan to support higher resolution generation, such as 1024 × 1024, with better visual quality.

      6       C ONCLUSION
528
529
      In this work, we explore the potential of MAR models for efficient and scalable image generation.
      By reevaluating commonly used image tokenizers and model architectures, we identify the optimal
530
      MAR model configuration. We develop a variety of models for class-conditional image gener-
531
      ation and introduce models capable of supporting text-conditional image generation. Our class-
532   conditional models are competitive with popular AR models in terms of quality and demonstrate
533   significant improvements in efficiency. Meanwhile, our text-conditional models maintain compet-
534   itive visual quality and text alignment. Additionally, we explore the integration of AR and MAR
535   paradigms at the inference stage, aiming to unify these approaches into a cohesive framework.
536
      In the future, as more training data and computational resources become available, we will investi-
537
      gate large-scale MAR-based visual generation models, potentially exceeding 7B parameters. Fur-
538   thermore, we intend to explore the end-to-end training that fuses AR and MAR paradigms.

771   D.1     D ECODING S TRATEGY
772
      In autoregressive decoding, tokens are sequentially generated based on previously produced tokens.
773   This method, inherently non-parallelizable, is particularly slow for images due to the typically large
774   token length, such as 256 or 1024, which is much larger than that used in language. In this work, we
775   adopt the decoding strategy outlined in Chang et al. (2022), where all image tokens are generated
776   simultaneously in parallel. This is feasible due to the bi-directional attention of MaskGIL.
777
      This decoding strategy allows for the generation of an image in T steps, typically 8. At each iteration,
778
      the model predicts all tokens simultaneously but retains only the most confident predictions. The less
779   certain tokens are masked and re-predicted in subsequent iterations, with a progressively decreasing
780   mask ratio, until all tokens are generated within the designated T iterations.
781
782
      D.2     A BLATION S TUDY ON D ECODING S TEPS
783
784   In the main paper, all our experimental results are based on 8-step decoding. To further explore how
785   the number of decoding steps affects generation quality, we conduct an ablation experiment on the
786   class-conditional imagenet benchmark, as shown in Figure 10. The results indicate that increasing
787   the number of decoding steps does not necessarily improve generation quality. Optimal results are
788   achieved with 8-steps decoding.
