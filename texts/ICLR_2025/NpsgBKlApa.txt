010
011
               Synthetic training data generation with Large Language Models (LLMs) like
012            Google’s Gemma and OpenAI’s GPT offer a promising solution to the challenge
013            of obtaining large, labeled datasets for training classifiers, especially when rapid
014            model deployment is critical, such as classifying emerging social media trends
015            or combating new forms of online abuse tied to current events. While prior re-
016            search has examined the comparability of synthetic data to human-labeled data,
017            this study introduces a novel sampling algorithm based on the maximum coverage
018            problem to select a representative subset from a synthetically generated dataset.
019            Our results demonstrate that training a classifier on this contextually sampled sub-
020
               set achieves superior performance compared to training on the entire dataset. This
               “less is more” approach not only improves accuracy but also reduces the volume
021
               of data required, leading to potentially more efficient training.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In recent years, the ability to generate high-quality synthetic data using large language models
028   (LLMs) like OpenAI’s GPT Achiam et al. (2023) or Google’s Gemma Team et al. (2024) has opened
029   new possibilities for training machine learning models, particularly in areas where human-labeled
030   data is costly, inaccessible, or impractical to obtain at scale Bunte et al. (2021). With a reliance
031   on labeled data for downstream tasks such as text classification, sentiment analysis, and informa-
032
      tion retrieval, LLMs offer a promising alternative by efficiently generating data that closely mirrors
      real-world inputs.
033
034   While synthetic data has been shown to perform comparably to human-labeled data for certain tasks
035   Ding et al. (2022), simply relying on large values of synthetic text introduces several challenges.
036   One of the main issues is the quality and diversity of the generated data. LLMs often produce
037   redundant or skewed examples that can degrade the training performance or delay model conver-
038
      gence Long et al. (2024). For example, an LLM tasked with generating training data for sentiment
      analysis may over-generate text that reflects common or typical expressions of sentiment, while
039
      under-representing more nuanced or less frequent cases Hao et al. (2024). This imbalance can lead
040
      to model overfitting, hinder generalization to real-world test data, and increase computational costs
041   due to the processing of unnecessary samples.
042
043   Consider the example of generating training data to classify sentiments for a novel event, such as a
044
      newly released product or a political debate. An LLM might generate hundreds of slightly varied
      but largely repetitive examples of positive sentiment, which could saturate the dataset and obscure
045
      valuable minority cases, such as neutral or mixed sentiments. Without
046
047   Such  imbalances can lead to model overfitting, hinder generalization to real-world test data, and
      :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
048   increase computational costs due to the processing of unnecessary samples. Moreover, without
      :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
049   careful selection of representative data points, this over-representation dilutes the usefulness of the
050

      important, sentiment categories.
051
052   In this paper, we address a fundamental question in the utilization of synthetic data: how can we
053   effectively downsample large synthetic datasets to select the most informative and diverse subset of
      data points for training machine learning models?

189   3.2   D OWNSAMPLING M ETHODS
190
191   Given a large volume of synthetically generated data, we employ and compare three different down-
192   sampling methods to select a representative subset of size k, where k < N , from the full corpus
193   of N samples. The goal is to identify an optimal subset of samples that preserves the diversity and
194   informativeness of the full dataset while maintaining a low computational overhead.
195
      Random Sampling. The most basic baseline and computationally lightweight downsampling
196
      method is random sampling. In this approach, we randomly select k samples from the full cor-
197   pus.
198
199   k-Means Sampling. As a more sophisticated baseline, we embed the text data using pre-trained
200
      Gecko embedding from Google Clouds Vertex AI Lee et al. (2024), and subsequently run the k-
      means clustering algorithm Lloyd (1982). We then retain the samples closest to each k-center as our
201
      representative samples for model fine tuning.
202
203   Adaptive Coverage Sampling (ACS). The main algorithm we introduce for the down-sampling
204   problem towards model fine tuning is based on a greedy max coverage sampling approach. Our
205   approach aims to select a diverse and representative subset of the data by selecting samples that
206
      are close to and therefore represent a large number of data points in the latent embedding space,
      while striking a balance between sampling dense and sparse regions. This algorithm ensures that the
207
      selected k samples capture the full diversity of a synthetic training set while ignoring redundancies.
208
      We refer to this method as “Adaptive Coverage Sampling” (ACS) and outline the technique here.
209
210   The ACS method begins by constructing a similarity graph where each node corresponds to a data
211   point, and edges are weighted by the cosine similarity between text embeddings of the corresponding
212
      points. We again use pre-trained Gecko embedding Lee et al. (2024) to quantify the semantic rela-
      tionships among data points. Note that any embedding model can be used, as long as it is appropriate
213
      for the downstream task and identifying similarities between data points.
214
215   In constructing the similarity graph, only edges with cosine similarity above a certain threshold are
      included. This threshold is set using a novel binary search, with the goal of achieving a desired

217   are in the sample set. We thus define coverage as follows:

267   points (including the sampled node itself) are deemed “covered” and removed from further con-
268   sideration to avoid redundancy. This process is repeated until k representative samples have been
269   selected. By prioritizing high-coverage points, ACS ensures that the selected subset captures the
      most important variations within the dataset, leading to better downstream performance than random

      In executing this sampling, we impose two strict constraints on each of the k points with respect to
275
      the constructed similarity graph to force further diversification and efficiency of the subsampling.
276
277
      Constraint 1: Maximum Nearest Neighbors Constraint. To further enhance the efficiency and
278
      diversification of ACS, we first introduce a constraint on the maximum number of nearest neighbors
279
      (i.e., maximum outdegree) for each node in the similarity graph. This constraint serves several
280   important purposes. Firstly, the maximum nearest neighbors constraint promotes diversification
281   in the sampling process. Without this constraint, a single sample could potentially cover a large
282   portion of the graph, especially in lower similarity thresholds. By limiting the number of neighbors,
283   we prevent any single sample from dominating the coverage. This leads to a more representative
284   sample subset that captures the underlying structure of the dataset more effectively. Secondly, it
285   improves the scalability of the algorithm. By limiting the number of edges on each node, we reduce
286   the overall density of the graph, leading to faster computation and lower memory usage. This is
287   particularly crucial when dealing with large datasets, where an unconstrained graph can lead to
288
      memory limitations and prohibitively long processing times.
289   Finally, this constraint aligns with common graph construction scalability techniques such as
290   Locality-Sensitive Hashing (LSH) with limited bucket sizes. Shekkizhar et al. (2023). These tech-
291   niques often inherently limit the number of neighbors considered for each data point to improve
292   efficiency and scalability. By explicitly incorporating a maximum nearest neighbors constraint into
293   ACS, we ensure compatibility with these techniques and facilitate seamless integration into existing
294
      workflows.
295   To implement this constraint, we set dmax for each node in the graph. In a graph without an imposed
296   similarity threshold, a lower bound for dmax can be defined to guarantee a desired coverage, c, with
297   k samples: dmax > c|D|/k. This bound, derived from the extended pigeonhole principle, ensures
298   that sufficient connectivity is maintained to achieve the target coverage. However, in our algorithm,
299   we set dmax to be twice this lower bound, dmax = 2c|D|/k. This provides a balance between
300
      achieving the target coverage and avoiding excessive pruning of the graph, which could lead to less
      representative samples.
301
302
      Constraints 2: Minimum Similarity Threshold Constraint. While the adaptive similarity
303
      threshold in ACS effectively controls the sampling process, it is essential to ensure that the selected
304
      samples maintain a minimum level of similarity to the data points that they represent. To achieve
305   this, we introduce a minimum similarity threshold constraint. Without enforcing such a limit ACS
306   can achieve any target coverage by any k > 0 samples from the graph by choosing a low similarity
307   thresholds. At the extreme a single sample can cover the entire graph with a similarity threshold
308   of zero. This coverage, however, is not real as samples are claimed to represent their neighbors but
309   with very low similarities. We use a minimum similarity threshold of 0.707 which corresponds to
310   the cosine of a 45 degree between the embedding vectors. By imposing this bound, ACS may not
311   achieve a target coverage and in this case, it returns the k samples selected from a graph with the
312   minimum similarity threshold.

525   In particular, ACS achieves comparable performance to models trained on the entire synthetic dataset
526   using only 6.7% of the data. This significant reduction in data highlights the efficiency of the max

529   that selected representative samples offer better training signals than using the entire, potentially re-

557

558
      sentences labeled with 64 different relation types. The task requires the model to predict a labeled
559
      relation between two entities within a sentence, which demands greater diversity and precision than
560   in the synthetic data generation process. For example, the sentence “Chester Alan Arthur, 21st

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
600
601
      Our experimentation shows that ACS can reduce a large corpus of potentially redundant data to
      smaller representative set for model training. Unlike random sampling, which may lead to an un-
602
      representative subset due to chance, or k-Means, which may focus on clustering samples too rigidly
603
      without considering their informativeness, ACS leverages both clustering and coverage principles
604   to pick data points that have the most significant impact on model training. This selection strategy
605   effectively reduces redundancy and prioritizes diversity, allowing the model to achieve higher per-
606   formance with a fraction of the training data. Overall, the results highlight the strength of ACS in
607   providing a more strategically chosen subset, which leads to superior downstream performance on
608   the FewRel relation extraction task. While our experiments here are largely restricted to the text do-
609   main as it pertains to the booming interest in LLMs as data generators, we note that our methodology
610   is highly flexible for any data domain.
611   In examining the gap between the performance of ACS and a model fine-tuned using human curated
612   data, it is no surprise that there appears to be no true substitute for real data. However, a natural
613   question for future work which our study poses is: after isolating representative training samples, can
614   we encourage an LLM to generate more diverse text to “fill” the latent space covered by the training
615   set? Moreover to further enhance downstream task performance, our work shows that reducing the
616   training set to a fraction of the generated samples from systems like an LLM can be beneficial if
617   the labels of this smaller k-subset are verified by humans. In our study, we simply use prompts to
618
      generate labels for these samples without involving human verification, as verifying large datasets
      is impractical. However, if ACS identifies a small set of representative samples, human verification
619
      of these labels could significantly improve accuracy.
