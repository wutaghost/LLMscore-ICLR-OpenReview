009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011               We introduce DNABERT-S, a tailored genome model that develops species-aware
012               embeddings to naturally cluster and segregate DNA sequences of different species
013               in the embedding space. Differentiating species from genomic sequences (i.e.,
014               DNA and RNA) is vital yet challenging, since many real-world species remain
015               uncharacterized, lacking known genomes for reference. Embedding-based methods
016               are therefore used to differentiate species in an unsupervised manner. DNABERT-S
017
                  builds upon a pre-trained genome foundation model named DNABERT-2. To
                  encourage effective embeddings to error-prone long-read DNA sequences, we
018
                  introduce Manifold Instance Mixup (MI-Mix), a contrastive objective that mixes
019
                  the hidden representations of DNA sequences at randomly selected layers and trains
020               the model to recognize and differentiate these mixed proportions at the output layer.
021                                                                                                2
                  We further enhance it with the proposed Curriculum Contrastive Learning (C LR)
022               strategy. Empirical results on 23 diverse datasets show DNABERT-S’s effectiveness,
023               especially in realistic label-scarce scenarios. For example, it identifies twice more
024               species from a mixture of unlabeled genomic sequences, doubles the Adjusted Rand
025               Index (ARI) in species clustering, and outperforms the top baseline’s performance
                                                                                 1
026               in 10-shot species classification with just a 2-shot training.

      1 I NTRODUCTION
029
      Accurate differentiation of species from genomic sequences is a critical task in biology and ecology,
      supporting efforts in biodiversity conservation, epidemiology, understanding evolutionary processes,
030
      and exploring the roles of microbiomes in health and disease. Traditional methods for species identi-
031
      fication rely heavily on well-characterized reference genomes for comparative analysis. Thus, they
032   are limited due to the vast and largely unexplored genetic diversity present in natural environments.
033
034   A prime example is metagenomics binning. Metagenomics binning (Kang et al., 2015; 2019; Nissen
035
      et al., 2021; Meyer et al., 2022; Lamurias et al., 2023) is a pivotal process in microbiome research,
      aiming to group DNA sequences by species from complex mixtures containing DNA from potentially
036
      thousands of distinct, often uncharacterized species. In this context, effective DNA embeddings that
037
      can accurately segregate and cluster DNA sequences are more suitable than the methods that rely on
038   known reference genomes for comparison and alignment.
039
040   Despite the critical role of DNA embeddings in various scenarios, there is a notable deficiency in
041
      the development of effective methods. Current approaches to achieving DNA embeddings include:
      1) Descriptive textual features (Kang et al., 2015; 2019; Nissen et al., 2021), 2) Pre-trained Kmer
042
      embeddings (Ng, 2017; Ren et al., 2022; Han et al., 2022), and 3) Genome foundation models (Ji
043
      et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). The first two methods, while straightforward,
044   often fail to capture complex semantic relationships inherent in genomic data. Genome foundation
045   models (Ji et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023), despite their successes in various
046   genomic tasks through model fine-tuning, generally fail to develop embeddings that can discriminate
047   certain properties like species. This is largely due to a mismatch between their pre-training objectives
048   (Radford et al., 2019; Devlin et al., 2018) and the specific application scenarios. Our empirical
049   analysis, as detailed in Table 1, reveals that in many scenarios, existing genome foundation models
050   even underperform simple textual features.
051   In this work, we introduce DNABERT-S, a specialized genome model that harnesses the capabilities
052   of genome foundation models to generate species-aware DNA embeddings. As depicted in Figure 1,

069   Contrastive learning enables the model to discern between similar and dissimilar DNA sequences,
070   and curriculum learning incrementally presents more challenging training samples, fostering better
071   learning and generalization. The training of DNABERT-S includes two phases. In the first phase, we
072   adopt a Weighted SimCLR Chen et al. (2020); Zhou et al. (2022) training objective to encourage the
073   model to group DNA sequences from the same species and separate DNA sequences from distinct
074   species. In the second phase, we introduce Manifold Instance Mixup (MI-Mix), which mixes anchor
075   instances at a randomly selected layer to create more challenging anchors for contrastive training.
076   To rigorously evaluate the models, we assemble a comprehensive benchmark that includes thousands
077   of species, capturing the diversity of natural microbial communities. This benchmark incorporates
078   complex samples from CAMI2 (Meyer et al., 2022), a leading metagenomics binning benchmark,
079   and extensive reference genomes from Genbank (Benson et al., 2012). Thus, it includes both natural
080   yet error-prone long-read sequences and well-curated yet potentially biased reference genomes.
081   We evaluate the embedding quality from entirely unsupervised problems to classification tasks with
082   abundant labels. Experimental results indicate the effective performance of DNABERT-S. Compared
083   to the strongest existing method, DNABERT-S doubles its performance in the clustering task and
084   achieves better performance with only 20% of labeled data in the classification task (e.g., 2-shot v.s.
085   10-shot). Notably, in metagenomics binning, DNABERT-S is able to recover over 40% and 80% of
086   species with an F1 score of over 0.5 respectively from synthetics and more realistic datasets, which is
087   also one time more than the strongest baseline. We also show that a simple K-Nearest-Neighbors
088   species classifier, which is trained on DNABERT-S embeddings of a small portion of each target
089
      genome, can slightly outperform a well-established traditional method MMseqs2 (Steinegger &
      Söding, 2017) that relies on the entire reference genomes of target species for classification.
090
091   Our contribution can be summarized as follows: 1) We demonstrate the effectiveness of genome
092   foundation models in learning DNA embeddings, opening new avenues for various genomics research
093   problems; 2) We introduce DNABERT-S, a model that develops distinctly better embeddings for
                                                                                   2
094   species differentiation; 3) We propose the Curriculum Contrastive Learning (C LR) strategy with the
095   Manifold Instance Mixup (MI-Mix) loss; 4) We publish a large-scale evaluation benchmark.
096   2 BACKGROUND
097   This study aims to build a species-aware DNA embedding model that maps each DNA sequence
098   as a fixed-size numerical vector in an embedding space, where sequences from distinct species are
099   naturally clustered and segregated. A DNA sequence is essentially a sentence composed of four
100   unique characters: A, T, C, and G.
101
      Existing works highly rely on descriptive textual features (Kang et al., 2015; 2019; Nissen et al.,
102   2021) and pre-trained K-mer embeddings (Ng, 2017; Ren et al., 2022; Han et al., 2022) to compute
103   DNA embeddings. A representative descriptive textual feature is Tetra-Nucleotide Frequency (TNF),
104   a 256-dimensional vector where each position represents the frequency of each unique 4-mer (e.g.,
105   TTCA, AACG) in the input DNA sequence. Despite its simplicity and effectiveness, this method
106   is limited since it singly relies on the 4-mer frequency and is not trainable to better fit downstream
107   applications. Besides, our empirical analysis also suggests that a naive trainable model based on TNF,
      such as a Variational AutoEnoder (Kingma & Welling, 2013) with TNF as input, results in worse

181   3.2 C URRICULUM C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING (C LR)
                                                                          2
182   In this part, we introduce our curriculum contrastive learning (C LR) method. Curriculum learning
183   is an effective training method that first presents easy training batches and then progresses to more
184   challenging ones (Hacohen & Weinshall, 2019). Recent studies have successfully applied this
185   technique to both positive pairs (Ye et al., 2021; Roy & Etemad, 2023) and negative pairs (Chu et al.,
186
      2021) in contrastive learning. We take this approach a step further by applying it to contrastive
      anchors, effectively using it for both types of pairs at the same time.
187
                                      2
188   As shown in Figure 2, our C LR method includes two training phases, with anchors becoming
189   progressively more challenging. In phase I, we use the Weighted SimCLR introduced in Sec. 3.1. In
190   phase II, we propose the Manifold Instance Mixup (MI-Mix) method to mix up anchor instances in a
191   random hidden layer, motivated by the instance mixup (i-Mix) method (Lee et al., 2020).
192   The i-Mix method mixes anchors at the input layer to create more challenging positive and negative

228           2
      In the C LR method, we set temperature τ as 0.05 and hyperparameter α as 1.0. We train the model
229   for one epoch in phase I using loss Eq. (2) and for two epochs in phase II using loss Eq. (3). We use
230   mean pooling of the last hidden states of all the tokens as the DNA embedding. We employ the Adam
231   optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014), with a learning rate of 3e − 6 and batch size of 48. We save the
232   model every 10000 training steps and select the best one based on the validation loss in the validation
233   dataset. We use the pre-trained DNABERT-2 (Zhou et al., 2023) as the starting point of contrastive
234   training. We also conduct parallel experiments with HyenaDNA (Nguyen et al., 2023). In Sec. 5.6,
235   we show that DNABERT-2 outperforms HyenaDNA after the same contrastive training. The training
236
      of DNABERT-S takes approximately 48 hours on 8 NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs.

      4     DATA
239   In this section, we introduce the dataset we used for DNABERT-S training and evaluation.
240
241   Training Each training sample of DNABERT-S is a pair of non-overlapping DNA sequences
242   extracted from the same species. We focus on microbe species since the genetic diversity within this
243   group provides a rich substrate for examining the nuances of species differentiation. The dataset is
244   constructed with the reference genomes from GenBank (Benson et al., 2012). We obtained 47923
245   pairs from 17636 viral genomes, 1 million pairs from 5011 fungi genomes, and 1 million pairs from
246   6402 bacteria genomes. We randomly selected 2 million pairs from the entire 2047923 pairs of DNA
247   sequences to construct the training data. The rest pairs are treated as validation data. All the DNA
248
      sequences are 10000 bp in length.
249
250
      Evaluation Our evaluation spans 14 long-read datasets from the Critical Assessment of
      Metagenome Interpretation (CAMI) II (Meyer et al., 2022) challenge benchmark and 9 synthetic
251
      datasets from reference genomes. CAMI2 is one of the most comprehensive and rigorous benchmarks
252
      for metagenomics research. The datasets in CAMI2 are designed to mimic realistic microbiome
253   environments and include a vast array of both new and known genomes, as well as plasmids and
254   viruses. It aligns our study with real-world ecological and biological scenarios, providing a robust and
255   contextually relevant evaluation for the DNA embedding models. We utilize 7 datasets of long-read
256   contigs respectively from the Marine and Plant-associated environments, where each dataset
257   consists of 150k-200k DNA sequences belonging to about 100 − 750 different species sampled
258   from 1680 microbial genomes and 599 circular elements. We also create 9 Synthetic datasets by
259   randomly extracting DNA sequences from fungi and viral reference genomes that do not overlap with
260   our training data. Table 5 in Appendix B shows the statistics of the datasets we used for evaluation.

      5.1 BASELINES
284
285   We compare our model with four lines of work to examine its effectiveness. TNF, TNF-K, and
286
      TNF-VAE are the most widely used DNA embedding methods in metagenomics binning tools
      (Kang et al., 2015; 2019; Nissen et al., 2021). TNF represents Tetra-Nucleotide Frequency, which
287                                                               4
288
      uses the appearance frequency of each unique 4-mer (4 = 256 in total) in a DNA sequence as its
      embedding. TNF-K (Nissen et al., 2021) reduces TNF to 103-dimension with a linear kernel, which
289
      utilizes DNA characteristics to reduce the correlations among different dimensions of the original
290
      TNF feature. TNF-VAE trains a Variational Autoencoder (Kingma & Welling, 2013) using TNF
291   as input to extract features. DNA2Vec (Ng, 2017) learns pre-trained K-mer embedding. We set
292   K = 4 to make it directly comparable with TNF and use the average of the 4-mer embeddings as the
293   DNA embedding. DNABERT-2 (Zhou et al., 2023), HyenaDNA (Nguyen et al., 2023), and NT-v2
294   (Nucleotide Transformer-v2) (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023) are representative genome foundation models.
295   We use the average of the last hidden states as the DNA embedding. For evaluations, we utilized the
296   respective pre-trained models from Huggingface ModelHub, specifically zhihan1996/DNABERT-2-
297   117M, LongSafari/hyenadna-medium-450k-seqlen-hf, and InstaDeepAI/nucleotide-transformer-v2-
298   100m-multi-species. DNA-Mutate, DNA-Dropout, and DNA-Double are variants of DNABERT-S,
299
      with the same hyperparameters but different positive pair construction strategies in contrastive training.
      DNA-Mutate views the same DNA sequence before and after random mutation (i.e., swap and delete
300
      5% of nucleotides) as a positive pair. DNA-Dropout passes the same DNA sequence through the
301
      embedding model (with a dropout rate 0.1) twice and views the two distinct embeddings as a positive
302   pair. DNA-Double views a DNA sequence and its reverse complementary (e.g., AATTC v.s. TTAAG)
303   as a positive pair. Hyena-Sim and DNA-Sim are variants of HyenaDNA and DNABERT-2, fine-tuned
304   using the Weighted SimCLR loss for 3 epochs with the same training set used for DNABERT-S. For
305   all the models mentioned above, including DNABERT-S, we provide a detailed comparison of the
306   number of parameters, embedding dimension, inference time, and inference memory in Appendix
307   E. We also compare the proposed curriculum contrastive learning framework and MI-Mix training
308   objective with well-established contrastive learning methods, including Weight SimCLR (Zhang
309   et al., 2021), i-Mix (Lee et al., 2020) and Supervised Contrastive Learning (SupCon) (Khosla
310
      et al., 2021). Results on different training objectives are presented in Sec. 5.5.
311   5.2 C LUSTERING
312   In this task, we evaluate the embedding quality by how well a standard clustering algorithm can
313   distinguish and cluster different species based on the embedding. To reduce the effects of other
314   factors, we assume the number of species is known in this task. For each dataset, we compute the

355   5.3   M ETAGENOMICS B INNING
356   Metagenomics binning is a crucial process in microbial ecology, involving the categorization of
357   DNA sequences into groups that represent individual species. State-of-the-art metagenomics binning
358   method (Kang et al., 2015; 2019; Nissen et al., 2021) always formulate this problem as a clustering
359   problem with an unknown number of clusters based on the feature of each DNA sequence. The DNA
360   sequence feature is computed by combining sequence-based DNA embedding with various other
361   features and the clustering algorithms are often complicated and strongly correlated with the features
362   they utilize. In our evaluation, to create a fair environment for DNA embedding benchmarking,
363
      instead of relying on any existing tool, we implement the modified K-medoid clustering algorithm
      proposed in Kang et al. (2015) for metagenomics binning due to its simplicity and effectiveness.
364
      Algorithm 1 describes the metagenomics binning algorithm. Following Kang et al. (2015; 2019), we
365
      formulate this problem as identifying non-overlapping clusters of DNA sequences from the entire
366   dataset, where each cluster of sequence is considered as an identified species. We iteratively identify
367   the densest point in the embedding space and take all the sequences that are close (determined by a
368   learned threshold) to it as the group of the sequences that belong to the same species. The embeddings
369   of the taken sequences are removed from the embedding space. The iteration ends as there are no
370   regions that contain enough number sequences within the threshold. This algorithm, evaluates how
371   well the embedding method clusters and aggregates different species within the embedding space.
372   We then compare the predicted clusters with the true labels to count the number of species that have
373   been successfully identified. A species is considered to be successfully identified if the F1 score of
374
      this species is over 0.5. We compare different models by the number of species they identify with
      different levels of F1 scores (e.g., 0.5 − 0.6, 0.8 − 0.9). We only use the DNA embeddings as the
375
      feature of each DNA sequence.
376
377   Figure 3 shows the models’ performance on 6 metagenomics binning datasets. As shown in the figure,
      similar to our observation in clustering, DNABERT-S identifies twice the number of species with

      an F1 score of over 0.5 compared to the strongest baseline, showing its great capability in tackling
379   important real-world biology challenges. Notably, DNABERT-S identifies a large number of species

381   embedding space, aligning with our observation in Figure 1. In the Synthetic datasets, where the

      6 C ONCLUSION
528   We introduce DNABERT-S, a model tailored for species-aware DNA embeddings, which is empow-
529   ered by the proposed Manifold Instance Mixup (MI-Mix) training objective and the Curriculum
                               2
530   Contrastive Learning (C LR) strategy. We perform extensive experiments on 23 datasets across thou-
531   sands of different species and a variety of challenging tasks, including species clustering, classification,
532   and metagenomics binning, to demonstrate the DNABERT-S’s capability of species differentiation.
533
      Furthermore, we conduct a series of experiments on the training objective, backbone model selection,
      impacts of sequence length, and feature dimension to provide empirical insights for DNA embedding
534
      learning. We also compare DNABERT-S with traditional technique like MMSeq2 in the problem of
535
      species differentiation and demonstrate DNABERT-S can achieves slightly better performance with
536   less amount of labeled samples. We envision DNABERT-S to potentially change the way genomic
537   problems are approached from an embedding perspective. The primary limitation of DNABERT-S
538   lies in its high computational demands, a common trait among deep learning models, when compared
539   to more traditional, lightweight methods like Tetranucleotide Frequencies (TNF).

650   Algorithm 1 describes the unsupervised clustering algorithm we used for metagenomics binning,
651   where s(Ei , Ej ) represents the cosine similarity of two vectors Ei and Ej . Selection of threshold
652   γ. As shown in Algorithm 1, the threshold γ is the most important hyperparameter that greatly
653   impacts the final binning results. A high threshold results in small and dense clusters while a low
654   threshold results in large yet sparse clusters. Since different models generate embeddings with distinct
655   distributions, a fixed threshold (e.g., 0.9) could be too high for one model yet too low for another one.
656
      In practice, massive hyperparameter searches are needed to determine the best threshold for each
      model on different datasets. Due to the large size of our experiments and the various types of models
657
      we used, an automatic way is needed to fairly choose the threshold for each model on each dataset.

997    C.7 I MPACT OF E MBEDDING D IMENSION R EDUCTION
998    This section investigates how changes in embedding dimensions affect the performance of DNABERT-
999    S, a key aspect influencing the scalability of DNA embeddings generated by the model. Initially,
1000   DNA embeddings for all clustering and classification datasets are computed using the pre-trained
1001   DNABERT-S. To reduce embedding dimensions, we use an average pooling layer with a consistent
1002   kernel size and stride S. This process effectively averages S consecutive dimensions into one new
1003   dimension. We test with S values of 96, 48, 24, 12, 6, 3, and 2, corresponding to reduced embedding
1004   dimensions of 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, and 384, respectively.
1005   Figure 9 illustrates DNABERT-S’s performance with these varying feature dimensions, in comparison
1006   to TNF. The results demonstrate that DNABERT-S’s embedding is quite resilient to dimension
1007   compression. It maintains nearly the same performance level even when reduced to 256 dimensions
1008   and only experiences a notable drop in performance when compressed to 32 dimensions. Notably,
1009   DNABERT-S still surpasses the 256-dimensional TNF feature even when its own dimensionality
1010   is reduced to just 16. This robustness to dimension reduction enhances its practical applicability in
1011
       various genomic contexts.

       F    A BSENCE OF DATA L EAKAGE
1135
1136   In this section, we consider data leakage to occur when the same species are present in both training
1137   and evaluation datasets. We validate the absence of data leakage issues in our evaluation datasets.
1138   Our experiments utilize two categories of data: synthetic and CAMI2 datasets. For synthetic data, the
1139   data construction method ensures the absence of data leakage. We design the synthetic datasets to
1140   exclude any species present in the training data, thereby preventing potential data leakage. For the
1141   CAMI2 datasets, due to discrepancies in species annotations between CAMI2 and GenBank, direct
1142   validation was challenging. Therefore, we perform an alignment-based estimation using minimap2
1143   (Li, 2018). We align each evaluation dataset to the training data and considered sequences with over
1144   90% alignment to the training sequences as present in the training data.
1145   We compute the presence rate as number of presented sequences/total number of sequences. It’s
1146   important to note that different species can share common or highly similar genome sequences, so a
1147   non-zero presence rate is expected in real-world scenarios. As a reference, the two synthetic datasets
1148   with non-overlapping species have presence rates of 6.88% and 8.92%. For the CAMI2 datasets, the
1149   plant-associated ones have presence rates between 3.51% and 4.98%, which are even lower than the
1150   synthetic datasets. The marine datasets have presence rates between 7.99% and 9.45%, comparable to
1151   the synthetic ones. Based on these statistics, there is negligible species leakage between our training
1152
       and evaluation data.
