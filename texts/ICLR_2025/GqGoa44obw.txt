011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has been widely studied,
014            as a method for leveraging feedback from human evaluators to guide the learning
015            process. However, existing theoretical analyses typically assume that the human
016
               feedback is generated by the ground-truth reward function. This may not be true in
               practice, because the reward functions in human minds for providing feedback are
017
               usually different from the ground-truth reward function, e.g., due to diverse per-
018
               sonal experiences and inherent biases. Such inconsistencies could lead to undesir-
019            able outcomes when applying existing algorithms, particularly when considering
020            feedback from heterogeneous agents. Therefore, in this paper, we make the first
021            effort to investigate a more practical and general setting of RLHF, where feedback
022            could be generated by multiple agents with reward functions differing from the
023            ground truth. To address this challenge, we develop a new algorithm with novel
024            ideas for handling inconsistent multi-agent feedback, including a Steiner-Point-
025            based confidence set to exploit the benefits of multi-agent feedback and a new
026            weighted importance sampling method to manage complexity issues arising from
027
               inconsistency. Our theoretical analysis develops new methods to demonstrate the
               optimality of our algorithm. This result is the first of its kind to demonstrate the
028
               fundamental impact and potential of inconsistent multi-agent feedback in RLHF.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Casper et al., 2023) has been widely studied
034
      as a significant advancement in the field of reinforcement learning, where a learner interacts with the
035
      environment sequentially to achieve high cumulative reward. Traditional RL (Sutton, 2018; Agarwal
036   et al., 2019; Vamvoudakis et al., 2021) relies on absolute reward values generated by predefined
037   reward functions to guide the learner’s behavior. This limits its applicability in complex real-world
038   scenarios, where crafting reward functions is challenging or ambiguous, e.g., in robotics (Jain et al.,
039   2013), large language models (Ouyang et al., 2022), and image generation (Lee et al., 2023).
040
      RLHF addresses this limitation by leveraging feedback from human evaluators to guide the learning
041
      process. Various forms of human feedback have been studied. For example, existing works study
042   RL from comparison/ranking feedback or preference-based feedback, which involves (i) presenting
043   a human with two or multiple outcomes, (ii) allowing her to choose the preferred one, and (iii)
044   guiding the learning process towards better policies based on the received human feedback (Wang
045   et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Chakraborty et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2022; Chatterji
046   et al., 2021; Kaufmann et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Du et al., 2024). In this way, RLHF bridges the
047   gap between pure algorithmic optimization and the nuanced understanding of human judgment.
048   However, existing theoretical results on RLHF typically rely on the human feedback generated by the
049   ground-truth reward function R∗ (·). For example, the commonly used comparison model assumes
050   that: the human feedback is generated according to a Bernoulli distribution based on the value
051   of a link function σ(R∗ (τ1 ) − R∗ (τ0 )), where R∗ (·) is assumed to be the ground-truth reward
052   function and {τi }i=0,1 are two outcomes. If the Bradley-Terry-Luce model (Bradley & Terry, 1952)
053   is considered for the link function σ(·), then the human feedback is τ1 ≻ τ0 (i.e., outcome τ1 is
      preferred to outcome τ0 ) with probability equal to exp (R∗ (τ1 ))/ [exp (R∗ (τ1 )) + exp (R∗ (τ0 ))].

054
      In a word, this type of human feedback is generated by the ground-truth reward function R∗ (·). Due
055   to page limits, we defer further discussion of related work to Appendix A.
056
057   Inconsistency in the Feedback: Feedback may not be consistent in practice, due to subjective
058
      human judgment, inherent biases, and varying expertise levels (Tjuatja et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024).
      That is, human feedback in practice often suffers from inconsistency (see the details in Sec. 2.2).
059
      For example, instead of being generated by R∗ (·), the real-world human feedback is often generated
060
      based on σ(Rhuman (τ1 ) − Rhuman (τ0 )). Here, Rhuman (·) is the reward function in the human mind,
061   and it is often different from the ground-truth reward function, i.e., Rhuman (·) ̸= R∗ (·). Traditional
062   RLHF theories, which often assume a ground-truth reward function R∗ (·), may not be applicable in
063   this more uncertain setting. Particularly, if assuming Rhuman (·) = R∗ (·), the resulting policy could
064   overfit to certain subjective signals rather than generalizing effectively. Therefore, in this paper, we
065   address these unique challenges posed by inconsistent human feedback in the algorithm design and
066   theoretical analysis, and investigate the fundamental impact of this type of inconsistency in RLHF.
067   Multi-Agent Feedback: Existing theoretical analysis in RLHF leaves untapped potential for richer
068   and more diverse feedback sources. That is, in addition to human evaluators, feedback can be
069   sourced from AI models, data analyzers, and other automated tools (Lee et al., 2024; Guo et al.,
070   2024a). (We call these sources “agents”.) Heterogeneity among agents in understanding and inter-
071   pretation could create a wide spectrum of feedback quality, because of diverse personal experience
072   and varying expertise levels. Therefore, we investigate the power of feedback from multiple agents.
073   Due to multi-agent feedback, the inconsistency issue becomes even more pronounced. On the one
074   hand, discrepancies among agents complicate the learning process, as the policy must navigate and
075   reconcile conflicting signals. This requires us to explore strategies for harmonizing diverse inputs to
076   align more closely with ground-truth objectives. On the other hand, we should intuitively be able to
077   leverage multiple data streams of agent feedback simultaneously, such that individual biases can be
078   reduced. To address these challenges, in this work, we investigate the following open problem:
079
080      Whether multi-agent feedback with inconsistency in RLHF fundamentally helps the learning
081
                                   process or exacerbates the situation?
082
      To answer this, we theoretically characterize the fundamental impact and potential of inconsistent
083
      multi-agent feedback. Specifically, we study online RLHF with inconsistent multi-agent feedback
084   under general function approximation. In addition to the well-known difficulties in RLHF and in an-
085   alyzing under general function approximation, the aforementioned properties of inconsistent multi-
086   agent feedback introduce significant new challenges in both algorithm design and regret analysis.
087
      Sharp Regret Under Inconsistency: We formulate the inconsistency in the multi-agent feedback by
088
      the cumulative discrepancy between the human preference model and the ground-truth preference
089
      model (see Eq. (2)). Eq. (2) is general and does not require special structures in the inconsistency.
090   Nonetheless, we are able to provide sharp theoretical guarantees. Note that the regret considered
091   in Eq. (3) is essentially the worst-case pseudo-regret, but over all possible human reward functions
092   satisfying the inconsistency model. As a result, our theoretical regret guarantee not only works for
093   the agents providing feedback during the online learning process, but also works for any newly-
094   incoming inconsistent agent, as long as her reward function satisfies the inconsistency model.
095
      New Algorithm Design and Analytical Ideas: From a high-level point of view, the steps of our new
096   algorithm include: (i) dynamically searching for the confidence center based on the multi-agent
097   feedback; (ii) constructing a confidence set based on step i and an important subset of inconsistent
098   feedback; (iii) reforming the confidence set in step ii to capture ground-truth comparison with high
099   probability; and (iv) constructing a high confidence policy set to circumvent the absolute reward
100   uncertainty. In this way, the optimal policy can be approximately found with high probability. The
101   new ideas that have been developed are described below.
102   New Idea I: Steiner-Point-Based Confidence Center for Leveraging Multi-Agent Feedback.
103   Since the feedback is inconsistent, a natural idea would be to use the feedback of each agent to
104   estimate their reward models, and then search for the optimal policy jointly. However, this will
105   lose the fundamental power of multi-feedback, i.e., the resulting performance does not improve
106   with the number of agents. Thus, we should estimate the confidence center by utilizing multi-
107   feedback simultaneously. However, the traditional complexity analysis in RL does not apply, since
      the confidence center may be outside of the agent reward function space and arbitrarily dynamic

153   2.1    R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK (RLHF)
154
      We investigate RLHF in episodic Markov decision processes (MDPs), where an online learner inter-
155
      acts with the environment in K episodes. It is typically modelled by (H, S, A, P), where H denotes
156   the number of steps in each episode; S and A denote the state space and action space, respectively;
157   and P : S × S × A → [0, 1] denotes the unknown transition kernel.1 At each step h of an episode k,
158   based on the current state sk,h , the online learner takes an action ak,h . Then, the environment transits
159   to the next state sk,h+1 , which is drawn according to the transition probability P(·|sk,h , ak,h ).
160

      generalized to the case where sk,1 is sampled from a fixed distribution ∆1 for each episode k.

      In RLHF, human feedback is typically used to guide the learning process. One conventional human
163
      feedback in each episode is a comparison of two trajectories τk ≜ (sk,1 , ak,1 , . . . , sk,H , ak,H ) and
164
      τ0 ≜ (s0,1 , a0,1 , . . . , s0,H , a0,H ) (Wang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Du et al., 2024; Zhan et al.,
165
      2024). In this case, the feedback is fk = 1, i.e., trajectory τk is preferred to trajectory τ0 (denoted by
166
      τk ≻ τ0 ), with probability σ (R∗ (τk ) − R∗ (τ0 )), where R∗ (·) is an unknown ground-truth reward
167   function and σ(·) is a link function. Note that this human feedback fk is generated by a comparison
168   based on the ground-truth reward function R∗ (·). This may not be true in practice, due to subjective
169   human judgment, varying expertise levels, diverse personal experience, inherent biases, etc.

172
173   Therefore, in this paper, we extend aforementioned traditional RLHF to a more practical and general
174   online setting, i.e., RLHF with inconsistent multi-agent feedback, formalized as follows.
175   Multi-Agent Feedback: We consider feedback that could be generated by multiple agents, e.g., hu-
176   mans (Chakraborty et al., 2024), AI models (Lee et al., 2024), and data analyzers (Guo et al., 2024a).
177   Specifically, at the end of each episode k, there are M agents providing comparison feedback fkm ,
178   where m = 1, . . . , M is the index of the agent. This type of multi-agent feedback has received
179   attention in empirical studies recently. However, to our knowledge, a theoretical understanding of
180   the fundamental impact of (inconsistent) multi-agent feedback is still an open problem.
181   Inconsistency in the feedback: We consider the human feedback that could include inconsistency,

      We consider general function approximation. Below, we provide the definitions of the standard
      covering number and eluder dimension for capturing the complexity of a function space.
236
237   Definition 1. (ϵ-covering number) Let (F, ∥·∥) be a metric space, where F is the function class and
238
      ∥ · ∥ is the norm used to measure distances between functions. A set of functions {f1 , . . . , fN } ⊂ F
      is called an ϵ-covering set if for every f ∈ F, there exists some fn , s.t., the distance ∥f − fn ∥ ≤ ϵ.
239
      The ϵ-covering number N (F, ∥ · ∥, ϵ) is the minimum number N of functions in an ϵ-covering set.
240
241   The ϵ-covering number N (F, ∥ · ∥, ϵ) captures how “complex” the function class F is, i.e., how
242   many different functions are required to approximate any function in the class to within ϵ accuracy.
243   Definition 2. (Eluder dimension) Let F be a class of real-valued functions over a domain X . For a
244   set of previously observed points XN = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xN } ⊂ X , define the following:
245
      - A point x ∈ X is said to be ϵ-dependent of XN with respect to the function class F if, for all pairs of
246                                    qP
                                            N                        2
247   functions f1 , f2 ∈ F satisfying      n=1 (f1 (xn ) − f2 (xn )) ≤ ϵ, it holds that |f1 (x) − f2 (x)| ≤
248   ϵ. Further, x is ϵ-independent of XN with respect to F if x is not ϵ-dependent on XN .
249
      - The eluder dimension dimE (F, ϵ) is the largest number of points in set XN such that, for some
250   ϵ′ ≥ ϵ, each point xn (n ∈ [N ]) is ϵ-independent of its previous points {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn−1 }.

252   The ϵ-dependency shows that the new point x cannot be used to significantly distinguish between
253   functions in F that agree on the previous data points. The eluder dimension measures how dependent
254   or entangled the predictions of different functions in F are across the state or state-action space.

350   based on the multi-agent feedback, we could still push the estimation of the reward function closer
351   to the ground truth. Then, the non-trivial question is where such a confidence center is.
352   Motivated by theoretical physics and combinatorial geometry, we provide a novel idea to answer this
353   question based on the Steiner point (Gilbert & Pollak, 1968; Brazil et al., 2014). Specifically, the
354   Steiner point is a generalization of the Fermat–Torricelli point. From a geometrical point of view,
355   it is defined to be a point with the minimum total distance to all input points. The effectiveness of
356   Steiner point comes from the fact that it could be a new point added to solve a problem, i.e., the
357   solution set could be expanded from the original constrained set based on inputs to a larger set with
358   more flexibility. In our case, when restricting ourselves to the ill-structured agent reward function
359
      space, the solution may get stuck due to the inconsistency. After enlarging the space, we could
      leverage the convexity of KL divergence mentioned above, and hence get closer to the ground truth.
360

      5   C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper studies RLHF with inconsistent multi-agent feedback under general function approxima-
530   tion from a theoretical point of view. In summary, the inconsistency in agent/human feedback can
531
      result in suboptimal outcomes, especially when feedback comes from diverse agents. To address this
      gap, this paper presents the first effort to explore a more realistic setting of RLHF, where feedback
532
      is provided by multiple agents with differing reward functions. We propose a novel algorithm de-
533
      signed to manage inconsistent multi-agent feedback, introducing a Steiner-Point-based confidence
534   set to harness the advantages of multiple sources of feedback and a weighted importance sampling
535   technique to handle the complexity of inconsistency. Our theoretical contributions demonstrate the
536   optimality of this approach and highlight, for the first time, the significant implications and potential
537   of inconsistent multi-agent feedback in RLHF. Since this work only study the case with one single
538   ground-truth reward function, it would be interesting to extend our results to the case with multiple
539   (personalized) ground-truth to handle the preference of users. It would also be important to consider
      more general form of inconsistency.

      A    M ORE R ELATED W ORK
757
758
759   Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) has gained substantial attention as an ap-
760   proach to align machine learning models with human values and preferences. Early works, such
761   as Knox & Stone (2011)’s exploration of incorporating human feedback into reinforcement learning
762   agents, established foundational methods for improving learning efficiency through interactive feed-
763   back mechanisms. A significant breakthrough was achieved by (Christiano et al., 2017), who intro-
764   duced techniques for scaling human feedback to deep reinforcement learning, enabling the training
765   of more complex models through reward learning. Further developments included studies by Sti-
766   ennon et al. (2020), who demonstrated how RLHF could be applied to tasks like summarization,
767
      optimizing model outputs through iterative human feedback loops.
768   In recent years, advancements have focused on the robustness and scalability of RLHF systems. For
769   instance,Hwang et al. (2023) proposed sequential preference ranking to enhance feedback efficiency
770   in complex tasks. Concurrently,Casper et al. (2023) identified open challenges in RLHF, such as
771   balancing the trade-offs between automation and human involvement, and ensuring scalability to
772   real-world applications. Additionally,Kaufmann et al. (2023) surveyed approaches for learning re-
773
      ward models from human feedback, emphasizing the shift towards robust policy training over direct
      reward optimization. Emerging research also explores AI-assisted feedback mechanisms to aug-
774
      ment human inputs.Lee et al. (2023) demonstrated that integrating AI feedback with RLHF could
775
      maintain model alignment with human values while improving efficiency. Liu (2023)’s work on
776   transforming human interactions via RLHF highlighted the potential for this methodology in ethical
777   AI and social robotics. More recently, RLHF has also been extensively studied, e.g., in Wang et al.
778   (2023); Zhu et al. (2023); Chakraborty et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2022); Chatterji
779   et al. (2021); Kaufmann et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023); Du et al. (2024), and references therein.
780
      Overall, RLHF continues to evolve as a pivotal framework for creating systems that reflect human
781
      intent, fostering advancements in areas such as robotics, natural language processing, and ethical
782   AI. Further research into scalable architectures, enhanced feedback modalities, and cross-domain
783   applications promises to extend its impact across AI-driven industries.
784
785
      Research has also explored broader preference structures beyond the reward-based paradigm, e.g.,
      in Munos et al. (2023); Rosset et al. (2024); Swamy et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2024), and techniques
786
      for post-processing models (Lin et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024). Direct preference learning has
787
      notably advanced RLHF, particularly in the post-training of open-source models. Following these
788   advancements, recent studies, e.g., (Guo et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024; Tajwar
789   et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024), have demonstrated the effectiveness of on-policy sampling and online
790   exploration in improving direct preference learning. In particular, online iterative DPO (Xiong et al.,
791   2024; Xu et al., 2023) and its variants, e.g., Chen et al. (2024); Rosset et al. (2024), have achieved
792   state-of-the-art results. Moreover, robust learning is also one related direction studying the corrup-
793   tion/imperfection in the feedback, e.g., in He et al. (2022); Ye et al. (2023); Wei et al. (2022); Wang
794   et al. (2020); Kong et al. (2021); Yan et al. (2024), and references therein.
