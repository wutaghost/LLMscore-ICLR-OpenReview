011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Conformational ensembles of protein structures are immensely important to un-
014                derstanding protein function. Current techniques for sampling ensembles are com-
015                putationally inefficient, or do not transfer to systems outside their training data.
016                We present walk-Jump Accelerated Molecular ensembles with Universal Noise
017
                   (JAMUN), a step towards the goal of efficiently sampling the Boltzmann distri-
                   bution of arbitrary proteins. By extending Walk-Jump Sampling to point clouds,
018
                   JAMUN enables ensemble generation at orders of magnitude faster rates than tra-
019
                   ditional molecular dynamics or state-of-the-art generators. Further, JAMUN is
020                able to predict the stable basins of small peptides that were not seen during train-
021                ing.

024   1       I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Molecules are not static. They move, and these movements can be vitally important. Protein motion
027   is required for myglobin to bind oxygen and move it around the body.Miller & Phillips (2021) Drug
028   discovery on protein kinases depends on characterizing kinase conformational ensembles.Gough
029   & Kalodimos (2024) The search for druggable “cryptic pockets” requires understanding protein
030   dynamics.Colombo (2023) However, while machine learning (ML) methods for molecular structure
031   prediction have experienced enormous success recently, ML methods for dynamics have yet to have
032   similar impact. ML models for generating molecular ensembles are widely considered the “next
033
      frontier” (Bowman, 2024; Miller & Phillips, 2021; Zheng et al., 2023). In this work, we present
      JAMUN (walk-Jump Accelerated Molecular ensembles with Universal Noise), a generative ML
034
      model which advances this frontier by demonstrating improvements in both speed and transferability
035
      over previous approaches.
036
037   While the importance of protein dynamics is well-established, it can be exceedingly difficult to
038   sufficiently sample large biomolecular systems. The most common sampling method is molecular
039
      dynamics (MD), but it is limited by the need for very short time-steps of 1-2 femtoseconds. Many
      important protein dynamic phenomena occur on the timescale of milliseconds. Simulating with this
040
      resolution is “...equivalent to tracking the advance and retreat of the glaciers of the last Ice Age—tens
041
      of thousands of years—by noting their locations each and every second.”Borhani & Shaw (2012)
042   Importantly, there is nothing fundamental about this small time-step limitation; it is an artifact of
043   high-frequency motions, such as bond vibrations, that have little to no effect on protein ensembles.
044   Leimkuhler & Matthews (2015) Enhanced sampling methods have been developed in an attempt to
045   accelerate sampling, but they often require expert user input, and, more importantly, do not address
046   the underlying time-step problem. Other sampling methods, such as Monte Carlo-based methods,
047   exist, but have seen limited success for large biomolecular systems.Vitalis & Pappu (2009)
048   A large number of generative models have been developed to address the sampling inefficiency
049   problems of MD using machine learning, including continuous normalizing flows, diffusion, and
050   flow-matching.Noé et al. (2019); Arts et al. (2023); Klein et al. (2024b;a); Zheng et al. (2024);
051   Jing et al. (2024); Kim et al. (2024) These models have been applied to a variety of MD datasets
052   from small molecules to peptides to full proteins. However, none of these models have proven

      to be transferable. They do not work well beyond their training data. (One notable exception is
      the recent Transferable Boltzmann Generators (Klein & Noé, 2024) model, with which we provide
088
      extensive comparison.) While the developments in this field have been immense, transferability

091   molecules that are not in its training set.
092
093
      We set out to solve this problem of transferability by developing an ML model informed by the
      physical priors of molecular dynamics data. JAMUN is a Walk-Jump Sampler (WJS) Saremi &
094
      Hyvärinen (2019) for point clouds parameterized with an SE(3)-equivariant denoiser. In WJS noise
095
      is added to clean data and a denoising neural network is trained to recover the clean samples. This
096   denoiser defines the score function of the noisy manifold which we sample using Langevin dynamics
097   (walk step) and allows us to periodically project back to the original data distribution (jump step).
098   This framework is a natural fit for MD data. In MD, unlike for natural images or other settings
099   where generative modeling is commonly applied, we typically are interested in sampling an ensem-
100   ble of representative states rather than drawing single samples. In this setting it is advantageous to
101   generate samples from trajectories that efficiently traverse the smoothed space, rather than starting
102   over from an uninformative Gaussian prior for each sample as is commonly done in diffusion and
103   flow matching. By adding partial noise, WJS simply smooths out the distribution enough to resolve
104
      sampling difficulties without fully destroying the information present in the data distribution. More-
      over, the use of Langevin dynamics, the same algorithm commonly used for MD simulations, on the
105
      smoothed noisy manifold lends itself to simple, physical interpretations of model behavior.
106
107   We train JAMUN on a large dataset of MD simulations of two amino acid peptides. We demonstrate
      that this model can generalize to a holdout set of unseen peptides. In all of these cases, generation

109   standard force field. These results suggest that this transferability is a consequence of retaining the

112   is dramatically faster than MD simulation.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
115
116   The goal of building machine learning models that can generate conformational ensembles of molec-
117   ular systems is not new. While a full overview of this field is beyond the scope of this work (see
118   Aranganathan et al. (2024) for a recent review), we note a few relevant previous efforts. Boltzmann
119   Generators (Noé et al., 2019) introduced the idea that a neural network could be used to transform the
120   underlying data distribution into an easier-to-sample Gaussian distribution. There have been follow-
121   on efforts which used diffusion models (Arts et al., 2023), flow-matching (Klein et al., 2024b), and
122   continuous normalizing flows (Klein & Noé, 2024). The commonality in these models is the choice
123   of target distribution; they all attempt to transform the MD data distribution into a simple Gaussian.
124
      This is a key difference between prior work and our model. Notably, no previous model in this area
      except Klein & Noé (2024) has been transferable.
125
126   There have also been efforts to build ML models for taking longer MD time-steps (Klein et al.,
127   2024a; Schreiner et al., 2023; Hsu et al., 2024) and for approximating conformations of large pro-
128   teins (Zheng et al., 2024; Jing et al., 2024). These methods rely on hand-crafted featurizations (eg.
129   backbone torsion angles). In practice, this has made generalization to unseen molecules challenging
130
      for these models as well.
131   The above models are often classified as Boltzmann Generators or Boltzmann Emulators. Models
132   in the former class are guaranteed to draw unbiased samples from the Boltzmann distribution, while
133   models in the latter class do not have this guarantee. Strictly speaking, JAMUN is a Boltzmann
134   Emulator, although in practical terms, as our results show, the difference is minimal.
135   JAMUN is a Walk-Jump Sampling method which uses an SE(3)-equivariant neural network for
136   denoising. WJS is built on the seminal work of Neural Empirical Bayes (Saremi & Hyvärinen,
137   2019), and has been used in voxelized molecule generation (Pinheiro et al., 2024b;a) and protein
138   sequence generation (Frey et al., 2023). Our work is the first to our knowledge to apply WJS to
139   point clouds. The equivariant denoising network we use is built with the e3nn library (Geiger &
140   Smidt, 2022). For a survey of equivariant models for 3D atomic systems, see Duval et al. (2023).

215   In order to run Walk-Jump Sampling as outlined above, we must train a parameterized denoising

217   recommendations of Karras et al. (2022; 2024), originally developed for diffusion models in images
218   to model the denoiser as a neural network x̂θ (y, σ) ≈ x̂(y) parameterized by model parameters θ.
219   We appropriately modify their construction for the point cloud context as detailed in Section 3.3.
220   Note that while the normalization is applicable for any noise level, we only need to learn a model

224   Thomas et al. (2018). This is in contrast to existing methods (Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Klein & Noé,

      We demonstrate our method on two datasets: (i) the uncapped amino acids used in Timewarp (Klein
      et al., 2024a), specifically the 2AA-huge dataset consisting of 380 diamines split into 200 train, 80

339   second residue and the ψ angle of the first residue. The termini are zwitterionic amino and carboxyl
340   groups. These are not ideal analogues of amino acids in proteins due to local charge interactions as
341   well as lack of steric effects. However, for consistency in benchmarking, we run all experiments on
342   these to compare against Transferable Boltzman Generators (Klein & Noé, 2024).

369   5   R ESULTS
370
371
      We find that JAMUN samples conformational ensembles of short peptides faster than conventional
372   MD while remaining reasonably faithful to thermodynamics. It does this despite being quite small by
373   ML model standards (8.2M parameters). We note it requires no a priori knowledge or hand-crafted
374   featurization for transferable exploration–for instance, it generates diversity in ϕ and ψ dihedrals
375   without explicit inputs regarding these variables. We also show a remarkable speedup relative to
376   transferable Boltzmann generators. However, unlike the transferable Boltzmann generator, we only
377   have access to the score of the distribution, and thus cannot precisely reweight it, making this a
      Boltzmann emulator.

480   Shannon-Jensen divergence for molecular dynamics and JAMUN runs of equal GPU time (using the
481   data generated for Figure 6 and Figure 9). We also analyze fully converged molecular dynamics runs
482   for reference. It appears that while JAMUN converges quicker than MD, and samples relevant states
483   faster, it does not ever converge to the same accuracy. It is likely that part of the discrepency is from
484   JAMUN’s oversampling of rare and transition regions. In particular, we see from the Ramachandran
485   plots that occasionally the standard transition paths are not the most frequent and instead spurious
      transition paths are sampled along. JAMUN is not trained on kinetics and cannot faithfully re-

      produce them. This is a well-understood phenomena in stochastic processes in a rough to smooth
487   surface.Zwanzig (1988) It is also evident from the smooth nature of JAMUN’s Shannon-Jensen time
488   series, as compared to the step-wise behavior of MD that JAMUN has a more natural mixing of
489   states without kinetic traps. However, much of the spurious sampling can be avoided with smaller
490   noise values at the cost of sampling efficiency. As such, this Langevin dynamics on a noised surface
491   provides an interesting avenue of research from a stochastic processes point of view.
492
      From a biological and drug-design standpoint, we are relatively uninterested in kinetics or even the
493   exact distribution. However, we are extremely interested in the sampling of diverse metastable states
494   with some sense of their relative stabilities. To this end, JAMUN does an impressive job of sampling
495   metastable states particular to specific diamines with thermodynamic fidelity. We demonstrate this
496   with the use of Markov State Models trained on converged MD data in Appendix B. The figures
497   demonstrate that across the test set of capped peptides JAMUN (i) captures the precise shape of states
498   extremely well, and (ii) samples nuanced states that are not obvious by eye in a Ramachandran plot
499   with high accuracy.

501   6   C ONCLUSION
502
503   We present JAMUN, a Walk-Jump Sampling model for generating ensembles of molecular con-
504   formations. The model is trained on molecular dynamics data from two amino acid peptides and
505   is transferable to peptides outside of its training set. It produces accurate conformational ensem-
506   bles faster than MD or previous ML approaches. This represents an important first step toward the
507   ultimate goal of a transferable generative model for protein conformational ensembles.
508   The model has some limitations that motivate future work. While it is highly transferable in the
509   space of two amino acid peptides, we have not tested the model’s ability to transfer to larger proteins.
510   We leave exploring this important direction to future work. Additionally, while the current SE(3)-
511   equivariant denoiser architecture works well, further development of the denoising network could
512   speed up sampling. Alternative ”jump” methods, such as Walk-Diffuse, could also serve to sharpen
513   generation. Lastly, a promising direction that has not yet been explored is the application of classical
514   enhanced sampling methods, such as metadynamics, for traversing the noisy space.
515   JAMUN is the first model to use Walk-Jump Sampling on point clouds, and the first to apply Walk-
516   Jump Sampling to molecular dynamics data. This paradigm gives the model a clear physics inter-
517   pretation. By adding noise, JAMUN is able to simulate on a smoother manifold than ordinary MD.
518   Sampling on this smooth surface enables the model to generate accurate molecular conformational
519   ensembles faster than both MD and previous ML models.

682   We demonstrate the quantitative gain in sampling through metrics like the Shannon-Jensen diver-
683   gence and the clear qualitative gain in sampling diversity. Here we show this visually by employ-
684   ing Markov state models (MSMs) Pande et al. (2010) to characterize metastability. MSMs are the
685   most commonly used clustering algorithm for times series in molecular dynamics. They function
686
      by first projecting on a low-dimensional manifold, most commonly the Time-lagged Independent
      Component Analysis (TICA), finely clustering the samples and then solving for the eigenvalues of a
687
      transition probability matrix. Here we use the PYEMMA2 Scherer et al. (2015) implementation. We
688
      use a lag time of ten steps, five tics, 200 k-means clusters to obtain 10 macrostates for the unbiased
689   well-sampled molecular dynamics trajectories. We perform an implied timescales analysis to ensure
690   that these states are a reasonable representation. We can then use this representation to better under-
691   stand the features of our space and qualitatively evaluate JAMUN. In figures 10-16, we plot PMFs
692   (potential of mean force) in grayscale for JAMUN’s samples with the Markov state model histogram
693   superimposed in color. Each pair of Ramachandran plots correspond to a single metastable state.
694
      In particular, note that oblong metastable basins corresponding to the −π region in ϕ, small metasta-
695   bility in the (−π, π) area and even the specific shapes of commonly occuring basins can be signa-
696   tures particular to specific diamine pairs and are well-emulated by JAMUN.
