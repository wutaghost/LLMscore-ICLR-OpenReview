009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Although end-to-end multi-object trackers like MOTR enjoy the merits of sim-
012            plicity, they suffer from the conflict between detection and association, resulting
013            in unsatisfactory convergence dynamics. While MOTRv2 partly addresses this
014            problem, it demands an additional detector. In this work, we serve as the first to
015            reveal this conflict arises from unfair label assignment between detect and track
016            queries, where detect queries are responsible for recognizing newly appearing tar-
017            gets and track queries are to associate them in following frames. Based on this
018
               observation, we propose MOTRv3, which balances the label assignment using the
               proposed release-fetch supervision strategy. In this strategy, labels are first re-
019
               leased for detection and gradually fetched back for association. Besides, another
020
               two strategies named pseudo label distillation and track group denoising are de-
021            signed to further strengthen the supervision for detection and association. Without
022            extra detector during inference, MOTRv3 achieves impressive performance across
023            diverse benchmarks, showing scaling up capability.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Thanks to the broad practical applications like autonomous driving and robotic navigation, multi-
029   object tracking (MOT) is gaining increasing attention from both the research and industry commu-
030   nities (Bewley et al., 2016; Wojke et al., 2017). Early MOT methods mostly adopt the tracking-
031   by-detection paradigm, which first recognizes targets using detection networks (Ge et al., 2021;
032
      Ren et al., 2015) and then associates them based on appearance similarity (Wang et al., 2020a;
      Yu et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2021) or box Intersection-over-Union (IoU) (Zhang et al., 2022a).
033
      Although some of these methods achieve promising performance, all them demand troublesome
034
      post-processing operations, e.g., non-maximum suppression (Ren et al., 2015).
035
036   In recent years, notable efforts have been paid to remove these post-processing operations (Mein-
037   hardt et al., 2021). Among them, MOTR (Zeng et al., 2022) is a milestone, because it unifies the
038
      detection and association branches of MOT into a universal Transformer-based architecture and re-
      alizes end-to-end tracking without post-processing. Specifically, as shown in Fig. 1a, MOTR first
039
      employs detect queries to recognize newly appearing targets like DETR (Carion et al., 2020). When
040
      a target is located by a detect query, a track query is generated based on this detect query, and the
041   generated track query is utilized to continuously locate this target in the following frames. Sum-
042   marily, the detect queries are used for locating newly appearing targets and the track queries are
043   employed to associate these targets in the next frames.
044
      Although the MOTR architecture is concise and straightforward, it suffers from the optimization
045
      conflict between detection and association critically, which results in poor detection precision. To
046
      alleviate this problem, significant efforts have been paid by some researchers (Cai et al., 2022; Zhang
047   et al., 2022b). For example, as illustrated in Fig. 1b, MOTRv2 employs an independently trained
048   2D object detector like YOLOX (Ge et al., 2021) to distinguish targets and provide the results to the
049   tracking network. Then, the tracking network can concentrate on association, and thus the conflict is
050   alleviated. Nevertheless, MOTRv2 demands an extra well-trained detector, which makes the tracker
051   not end-to-end any more.
052   We argue that MOTRv2 does not reveal the essence of the conflict between detection and association
053   in MOTR, and its application is restricted as a well-trained detector is required for inference. In this
      work, we aim to explore the dark secret of this conflict and provide strategies to tackle it.

      To this end, we conduct numerous experiments to analyze the training dynamics of MOTR and
069
      observe that the activation times of detect queries (the times of detect queries are assigned to the
070
      ground-truths) are much smaller compared with the total number of ground-truths. This is because
071   when a detect query matches with a target, the ground-truths of this target in the following frames
072   are fixedly assigned to the track query generated from that detect query, and we call these fixedly
073   assigned ground-truths as locked ground truth (locked GT). In this way, the detect query will not
074   receive supervision from this target any more after the first time of assignment. Only the box anno-
075   tations of newly appearing targets (called free GT) are effective for training detect queries. As free
076   GT only counts for a small ratio of the total labels, the supervision applying to detect queries is quite
077   limited. This problem causes that the detection part of MOTR is not sufficiently trained.
078   To tackle this problem, we propose a label assignment strategy named Release-Fetch Supervision
079   (RFS) (see Fig. 1c). It overcomes the restriction of label assignment in MOTR and achieves balanced
080   training between detect and track queries, while keeping the end-to-end spirit. Specifically, in RFS,
081   we release the labels originally assigned only for track queries (Locked GT) to detect queries, which
082   means all queries are allowed to compete for the allocation of all GTs. Notably, there are totally 6
083   decoders and RFS is only applied to the first 5 decoders. The matching strategy of the last decoder
084   in MOTRv3 remains unchanged as MOTR, which ensures track queries can learn to follow the same
085
      targets in different frames.
086   Besides, another two strategies, namely pseudo label distillation (PLD) and track group denoising
087   (TGD), are proposed in this work to further improve the detection and association supervision,
088   respectively. Specifically, PLD uses a well-trained 2D object detector like YOLOX (Ge et al., 2021)
089   or Sparse RCNN (Sun et al., 2021) to produce pseudo labels and apply auxiliary supervision to
090   MOTR. The distribution of pseudo labels provided by the pre-trained detector is diverse, thereby the
091
      MOTR detection part obtains more sufficient training. TGD augments track queries into multiple
      groups and every group consists of the same number of track queries as the original ones. Random
092
      noise is added to the reference points of each track group during training. TGD stabilizes the training
093
      of the MOTR association part and thus improves the overall tracking performance.
094
095   Comprehensively, in this work, we reveal the underlying reason that causes the poor detection per-
096   formance of MOTR, which previously is simply believed because of the conflict between detection
097
      and association. Based on the observation, we propose three strategies that boost the performance of
      MOTR by a large margin while avoiding the use of an independently trained 2D object detector like
098
      MOTRv2. Combining the developed techniques, we propose MOTRv3, which achieves impressive
099
      performances across multiple benchmarks including MOT Challenge (Milan et al., 2016; Dendorfer
100   et al., 2020) and DanceTrack (Sun et al., 2022). We hope this work can inspire more researchers
101   about how to improve the end-to-end trackers.

104   2   R ELATED W ORKS
105
106   Tracking by detection. Thanks to the fast development of object detection techniques (Ren et al.,
107   2015; Zhou et al., 2019; Ge et al., 2021), existing MOT methods mainly follow the tracking-by-
      detection (TBD) paradigm (Bewley et al., 2016; Wojke et al., 2017; Bergmann et al., 2019; Peng


                                                                 2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2020; Pang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020b), which first uses detectors to locate targets and
109   then associate them to obtain tracklets. According to the association strategy, MOT methods can
110   be further divided into motion-based trackers and appearance-based trackers. Specifically, motion-
111   based trackers (Zhang et al., 2022a; Cao et al., 2022) perform the association step based on mo-
112   tion prediction algorithms, such as Kalman Filter (Bishop et al., 2001) and optical flow (Baker &
113   Matthews, 2004). Some motion-based trackers (Feichtenhofer et al., 2017; Bergmann et al., 2019;
114   Han et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2020; Shuai et al., 2021) directly predict the future
115   tracklets or displacements in future frames compared with the current frame. In contrast to the
116   motion-based methods, the appearance-based trackers (Wang et al., 2020a; Zhang et al., 2021; Yu
117
      et al., 2022b;a) usually use a Re-ID network or appearance sub-network to extract the appearance
      representation of targets and match them based on representation similarity.
118
119   End-to-end MOT. Although the performance of TBD methods is promising, they all demand trou-
120   blesome post-processing operations, e.g., non-maximum suppression (NMS) (Ren et al., 2015) and
121   box association. Recently, the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) originally designed
122   for natural language processing (NLP) has been applied to computer vision. For instance, DETR
123
      (Carion et al., 2020) turns 2D object detection into a set prediction problem and realizes end-to-end
      detection. Inspired by DETR, MOTR (Zeng et al., 2022) transfers MOT to a sequence prediction
124
      problem by representing each tracklet through a track query and dynamically updating track queries
125
      during tracking. In this way, the tracking process can be achieved in an end-to-end fashion. However,
126   despite MOTR enjoys the merits of simplicity and elegance, it suffers the limitation of poor detec-
127   tion performance compared to the TBD methods. To improve MOTR, MeMOT (Cai et al., 2022)
128   builds the short-term and long-term memory bank to capture temporal information. LTrack (Yu
129   et al., 2022c) introduces natural language representation obtained by CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to
130   generalize MOTR to unseen domains. MOTRv2 (Zhang et al., 2022b) incorporate the YOLOX (Ge
131   et al., 2021) detector to generate proposals as object anchors, providing detection prior to MOTR.

      3     P RELIMINARIES
134
135
      MOTRv3 is implemented based on MOTR rather than MOTRv2 since it requires an extra 2D object
136
      detector, making the tracker not end-to-end. Since not all readers are clear about the design of
137   MOTR, we first elaborate on its architecture in this section. For more details, please refer to the
138   corresponding papers (Zeng et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b). Afterwards, we describe how we
139   reveal the essence resulting in the conflict between detection and association in MOTR.
140
141   3.1   MOTR P IPELINE
142
143   MOTR consists of a CNN backbone, 6 transformer encoder layers, and 6 transformer decoder layers.
144   It realizes end-to-end tracking by applying simple modifications to DETR. Specifically, when a
145   target appears in a video, MOTR employs a detect query to recognize it in the same process as
146   DETR. After recognizing it, MOTR uses a lightweight network block to generate a track query
147   based on this detect query. Then, in the following frames, this track query should continuously
148
      locate the positions of this target until it disappears. In a nutshell, the detect queries are utilized to
      detect newly appeared targets and track queries are for tracking previously detected targets.
149
150   For training MOTR, every target in a frame is annotated with a 2D box and an identity. To enable
151   the MOTR detection part to recognize newly appearing targets, the 2D boxes of these new targets
152   are assigned to train detect queries during training. By contrast, if a target exists in previous frames,
153   its GT is used to train the track queries.
154
155   3.2   A C LOSER L OOK AT L ABEL A SSIGNMENT
156
157   Although the MOTR architecture is simple and presents promising association accuracy, its detec-
158
      tion precision is poor. Previous literature (Zhang et al., 2022b) commonly believes this is due to the
      conflict between detection and association, but no one reveals where this conflict arises from.
159
160   To shed light on this problem, we conduct an in-depth analysis. As suggested in Fig. 2(a), the acti-
161   vation numbers of detect queries with different IDs are limited. We further compare the numbers of
      2D box labels that are released to train the detect and track queries (see Fig. 2(c)). It can be observed

186   4.1   OVERVIEW
187
      As mentioned before, MOTRv3 is the same as MOTR except the three contributions, i.e., RFS,
188
      PLD, and TGD. In this section, we elaborate on the details of them one by one. Among them, RFS
189
      conducts one-to-one matching between all GTs and all queries to train the detection capability of
190   the MOTRv3, which is different from MOTR that performs matching between free GTs and detect
191   queries. RFS releases the labels originally used for training track queries in MOTR to train the
192   detect queries and gradually fetches them back with the progress of the training process. In PLD,
193   a pre-trained detector is employed to produce more pseudo GTs to train the MOTR detection part
194   more sufficiently. TGD improves the training dynamics stability of the association part by expanding
195   track queries into several groups and then conducting the one-to-one assignment.
196
197   4.2   R ELEASE -F ETCH S UPERVISION
198
199   Fixedly assigning locked GTs to track queries hinders detect queries getting sufficient supervision.
200
      Therefore, we alter this assignment strategy. Specifically, as depicted in Fig. 3, from the 1st to the
      5th decoders, the one-to-one matching is performed between all GTs and queries, including both
201
      detect queries and track queries, to calculate detection loss. The assignment is dynamic based on
202
      matching cost rather than associating locked GTs with fixed track queries. In this way, all detect
203   queries and track queries get abundant supervision. For the 6th decoder, the assignment strategy
204   remains the same as MOTR, which ensures that track queries can learn to follow desired targets.
205
206
      Compared with the assignment strategy in MOTR, the matching strategy for the 1st to the 5th de-
      coders can be interpreted as the track queries release some locked GTs to detect queries to assist
207
      training. Interestingly, with the progress of training, the track queries gradually learn to follow the
208
      same targets in various frames, and the number of GTs matched with detect queries decreases con-
209   tinuously. This phenomenon can be understood as after the detect queries get sufficient training, the
210   aforementioned released GTs are fetched back to track queries automatically.
211

      4.3     P SEUDO L ABEL D ISTILLATION
251
252   RFS releases more supervision labels to the detection part by changing the matching strategy. In
253   PLD, we further enhance the supervision applied to the detection part by generating pseudo labels
254   using a previously trained 2D object detector like Sparse RCNN, as shown in Fig. 4a. There are three
255   main benefits of using a pre-trained detector to generate pseudo labels for auxiliary supervision.
256   (i) The pseudo bounding boxes are unbiased representation (diverse and reasonable distribution as
257   shown in Fig. 4a) of objects for high-quality detectors. (ii) Introducing more box annotations would
258   greatly unleash the capacity of object queries by training them with various one-to-one assignment
259   patterns. (iii) The confidence score predicted by detectors can be regarded as a good indicator to
260
      represent the quality of bounding boxes, which is embeded with the knowledge of the pre-trained
      detectors about the quality of predictions. Notably, the detector is only adopted in training, which
261
      is different from MOTRv2 that still demands this detector in the inference stage.
262
263   In PLD, we use the pretrained 2D object detector to generate detection boxes and employ a confi-
264   dence threshold (such as 0.05) to select precise ones from these boxes. The selected boxes ŷei are
265   used as pseudo labels to train the queries of all 6 decoders. Besides the training process in RFS, we
266
      conduct one-to-one matching between all queries (q d and q t ) and ŷei to compute detection loss. In
      this way, q d obtains more supervision.
267
268   Although the aforementioned process increases the labels for training q d , the problem is that ŷei is
269   often noisy. To alleviate this problem, we propose to reweight the detection part loss based on the
      detection confidence ce produced by the 2D object detector. Specifically, if a query matches with a

      4.4   T RACK G ROUP D ENOSING
291
292   The two aforementioned strategies, RFS and PLD, improve the detection capability of MOTR. In
293   this part, we develop a strategy, TGD, to further boost the association performance (see Fig. 4b).
294   Specifically, inspired by Group DETR (Chen et al., 2022), we first augment every track query as a
295   track query group consisting of multiple queries. Notably, the assignment between each track query
296   group and GT is the same as the original track query. By conducting one-to-one matching between
297   track query groups and labels, the track queries obtain more sufficient supervision.
298   Besides, we note that the tracking performance is influenced by the quality of initial reference points
299   (Zhu et al., 2020) significantly. To boost the robustness of the model, we propose to add random
300   noise to the reference point of every element in a track query group. In this way, the model becomes
301   less dependent on promising initial reference points and the association becomes more robust.
302   Then, an attention mask is used to prevent information leakage (Li et al., 2022) between the orig-
303   inal track query and the augmented queries. Mathematically, we use A = [aij ]S×S to denote the
304   attention mask for decoders, where S = G · N + M . The value in the attention mask is defined as:

      5.1   DATASETS AND M ETRICS
327
328   We conduct extensive experiments on three public datasets, including DanceTrack (Sun et al., 2022),
329   MOT17 (Milan et al., 2016) and MOT20 (Dendorfer et al., 2020), to evaluate the superiority of
330   MOTRv3. In this part, we introduce the adopted datasets and corresponding evaluation metrics.
331
      DanceTrack (Sun et al., 2022) is a large-scale multiple object tracking dataset with 100 video se-
332   quences in dancing scenarios. The 100 sequences are divided into 40, 25, and 35 sequences for
333   training, validation, and testing, respectively. The targets in DanceTrack are often highly similar in
334   appearance but present various dancing movements. This characteristic causes huge challenge to the
335   association in MOT. In addition, the video sequences in DanceTrack are quite long (52.9 seconds on
336   average for a sequence), which further enhances the tracking difficulty.
337
      MOT17 (Milan et al., 2016) consists of 14 video sequences. Among them, 7 sequences are for
338   training and the other 7 sequences are used to validate models. These sequences cover various
339   scenarios and weather conditions, which include indoor and outdoor, day and night, etc. The targets
340   in these video sequences are usually pedestrians moving in simple patterns, such as walking straight.
341
      MOT20 (Dendorfer et al., 2020) presents a heightened level of complexity compared to MOT17.
342
      Comprising eight video sequences, it features three bustling environments, with certain frames ac-
343
      commodating over 220 pedestrians concurrently. MOT20 encompasses a wide variety of scenes,
344   ranging from indoor to outdoor settings and spanning various times of day, including nighttime.
345
346   Metrics. The metrics adopted in the aforementioned datasets include the HOTA (Luiten et al.,
347
      2021) and CLEAR-MOT Metrics (Bernardin & Stiefelhagen, 2008). Specifically, HOTA consists of
      higher order tracking accuracy (HOTA), association accuracy score (AssA), and detection accuracy
348
      score (DetA). CLEAR-MOT Metrics include ID F1 score (IDF1), multiple object tracking accuracy
349
      (MOTA) and identity switches (IDS).
350

      5.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
352
353   Following MOTR and MOTRv2 (Zeng et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b; Yu et al., 2022c), MOTRv3
354   is implemented based on Deformable-DETR (Zhu et al., 2020), which is pre-trained on COCO (Lin
355   et al., 2014) and employs ConvNext-Base (Liu et al., 2022) as the vision backbone. During the
356   training process, the batch size is set to 8. For the experiments in DanceTrack and MOT17, each
357   batch is a video clip including 5 frames, which are selected from a video with a random sampling
358   interval between 1 to 10. Following MOTRv2, track queries are generated based on detect queries
359   when the confidences of these detect queries are above the threshold 0.5. Adam (Kingma & Ba,
360
      2014) optimizer is employed and the initial learning rate is set to 2 × 10−4 .
361   For the experiments in DanceTrack (Sun et al., 2022), the models are trained for 5 epochs and the
362   learning rate is dropped by a factor of 10 at the 4th epoch. In MOT17 (Milan et al., 2016), we train
363   models for 50 epochs and the learning rate drops at the 40th epoch.
364   λcls , λl1 and λgiou are set to 2, 5 and 2, respectively. For the implementation of PLD, the auxiliary
365   boxes from pre-trained detectors are obtained in an offline manner. Two common 2D object detectors

367   generated 2D box predictions with confidence scores below 0.05 are removed. In the implementation
368   of TGD, we expand the original track query to 4 track query groups.

      6     L IMITATION AND C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we reveal the real reason causing the conflict between detection and association in
533
      MOTR, which results in the poor detection performance. Based on this observation, we propose
534   RFS, which improves the detection and overall tracking performances by a large margin. However,
535   while RFS helps mitigate this conflict in terms of supervision, the trade-off between detection and
536   association remains unresolved. How to disentangle two sub-tasks still deserves further study. Be-
537   sides, we have proposed two another strategies, PLD and TGD, to further improve the detection
538   and query parts of MOTR. Combining all the three strategies, the developed tracker, MOTRv3, has
539   achieved impressive performances across multiple benchmarks. We hope this work can inspire more
      solid works about end-to-end MOT in the future.

798   D    A DDITIONAL D ISCUSSIONS .
799
800   One-to-One or One-to-Many Assignment in RFS? In fact, we still employ the original one-to-one
801   assignment strategy in RFS. The core contribution of RFS is to improve the label matching space
802   between the detection and association parts, rather than the assignment method itself. Since MOT
803   requires one-to-one corresponding between track queries and targets for trajectory association, one-
804   to-many assignment is not suitable. Our focus is on the issue of balancing label assignment between
805
      the detection and association, rather than label assignment strategy for detection.
