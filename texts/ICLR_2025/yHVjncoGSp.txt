009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning aided codes have been shown to improve code performance in feed-
012            back codes in high noise regimes due to the ability to leverage non-linearity in
013            code design. In the additive white Gaussian broadcast channel (AWGN-BC), the
014            addition of feedback may allow the capacity region to extend far beyond the ca-
015            pacity region of the channel without feedback, enabling higher data rates. On the
016            other hand, there are limited deep-learning aided implementations of broadcast
017            codes. In this work, we extend two classes of deep-learning assisted feedback
018
               codes to the AWGN-BC channel; the first being an RNN-based architecture and
               the second being a lightweight MLP-based architecture. Both codes are trained
019
               using a global model, and then they are trained using a more realistic vertical
020
               federated learning based framework. We first show that in most cases, using an
021            AWGN-BC code outperforms a linear-based concatenated scheme. Second, we
022            show in some regimes, the lightweight architecture far exceeds the RNN-based
023            code, but in especially unreliable conditions, the RNN-based code dominates.
024            The results show the promise of deep-learning aided broadcast codes in unreli-
025            able channels, and future research directions are discussed.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Error correcting codes are an integral component of wireless communication systems, allowing
031   reliable communication over noisy channels. Codes should be designed to be as efficient as possible
032   without sacrificing error correction capabilities. In this work, we focus on designing codes for the
033
      additive white Gaussian noise broadcast channel (AWGN-BC) with feedback. Unlike the single user
      feedback case, where the capacity of the AWGN channel with addition of feedback cannot exceed
034
      that of the AWGN channel without feedback, the use of feedback in the AWGN-BC channel can
035
      far exceed the capacity of the AWGN-BC channel without feedback Shannon (1956); Ahmad et al.
036   (2015). The design of good codes for the AWGN-BC channel with feedback can enable high data
037   rate communication with high reliability in unfavorable channel conditions.
038
039
      Codes designed for the AWGN-BC channel with feedback have been linear in nature due to more
      simplicity in code design, yet this requirement limits the design of optimal codes Ahmad et al.
040
      (2015). These codes include Ozarow’s extension of Shakwijk and Kailath’s linear scheme to the
041
      AWGN-BC channel with perfect feedback Ozarow & Leung-Yan-Cheong (1984); Schalkwijk &
042   Kailath (1966). There are also linear control-oriented schemes that show performance improvement
043   beyond Ozarow’s scheme Elia (2004); Ardestanizadeh et al. (2012). Most purely linear schemes
044   operate under the assumption of perfect feedback and suffer greatly with feedback noise. In response
045   to this, a concatenated scheme relying on linear codes for the inner code for the AWGN-BC for the
046   noisy feedback channel was proposed Ahmad et al. (2015).
047
      Dropping the linearity assumption of these codes could unlock the higher data rates of AWGN-BC
048   codes with potentially noisy feedback, but designing non-linear codes is a challenging task. Recent
049   innovations in deep learning, though, allow seemingly intractable wireless problems to be solved
050   and can be used to design high performing non-linear codes. For example, in the single user AWGN
051   channel with feedback, it has been shown that deep-learning aided codes can outperform linear
052   schemes in terms of probability of block error in scenarios where feedback noise is high and/or the
053   forward SNR is low Kim et al. (2020). In this work, we seek to design codes that outperform linear
      schemes in the aforementioned noisy regions using deep-learning for the AWGN-BC channel, where

058   1.1   E XISTING D EEP -L EARNING A IDED B ROADCAST C ODES AND F EEDBACK C ODES
059
060   There are very few existing implementations of deep-learning aided codes for the AWGN-BC chan-
061   nel. In Li et. al, a deep-learning based approach to the fading AWGN broadcast channel with
062   two users and binary phase shift keying (BPSK) modulation is proposed. The scheme involves two
063   phases, where in the first phase two separate time slots are allocated for transmission to each of
064   two users, where the information bits are modulated using BPSK and the weights of the modulated
065   bits are optimized. In phase 2, a deep learning aided two-phase scheme is used which involves
066   passing the input through a deep recurrent neural network (RNN), through a dense layer, and then
067
      through a weighted parameter. The decoding scheme involves two bi-directional GRUs, followed by
      a bi-drectional LTSM, and finally a dense layer Li et al. (2022). This scheme is not fully learnable
068
      and restrictive as it requires an initial BPSK modulation scheme. For the multiple-access channel
069
      (MAC), Ozfatura et. al propose a deep-learning based code with two users with noiseless feed-
070   back Ozfatura et al. (2023). In this work, block attention feedback (BAF) codes are used, where
071   the encoding and decoding process consists of transforming the knowledge vector at the receiver
072   and transmitters into a sequence of vectors and using self-attention mechanism on these to emulate
073   encoding and decoding processes. BAF codes have high computational complexity which may not
074   be suitable for many communication applications, and in many applications feedback codes could
075   benefit from complexity reduction Ozfatura et al. (2022).
076   On the other hand, there are numerous schemes for deep-learning codes for the single user AWGN
077   channel with feedback. One scheme, Deepcode, was proposed for a finite length, fixed rate block
078   code over the AWGN channel with noisy feedback Kim et al. (2020). In this scheme, the encoder
079   and decoder are both modeled as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) in order to process the bit stream
080   sequentially over the AWGN channel to minimize block error rates, but it appears to be limited to
081   rate 1/3 only. In Kim et al. (2023), a RNN-based power constrained deep-learning architecture is
082   proposed. This work combines GRUs and an attention mechanism to optimize code design across
083   time and take advantage of noise averaging. In addition, a power control layer is proposed, to en-
084
      sure optimal utilization of the power budget. In Ozfatura et al., generalized block attention feedback
      codes (GBAF) were proposed, which is a transformer-based feedback code scheme Ozfatura et al.
085
      (2022). GBAF operates generally by breaking message blocks into symbols, encoding individual
086
      symbols with feature extractors, and then encoding these symbols across the entire codeblock us-
087   ing a transformer based architecture. Transformer-based and RNN-based encoders and decoders,
088   in general, may have large computational demands, which may not be feasible for communica-
089   tion systems with limited resources. To this end, LightCode was proposed, whose performance is
090   shown to be comparable or, in some cases, superior to GBAF with fewer parameters and complexity
091   Ankireddy et al. (2024). Unlike the aforementioned codes which utilize RNN or transformer based
092   architectures, LightCode is a relatively lightweight symbol by symbol scheme consisting of a feature
093   extractor (FE) architecture followed by an multi-layer perceptron (MLP) at the encoder and decoder.

      1.2   I MPLEMENTATION OF D EEP -L EARNING A IDED AWGN-BC C ODES WITH F EEDBACK
097
098   Now, we discuss our implementation of codes for the AWGN-BC channel. As mentioned before, the
099
      existing implementation in Li et al. (2022) is not fully learnable, imposing restrictive structure to the
      learned scheme, so we look to the existing single user AWGN with feedback codes in our design. In
100
      this work, we focus on extending the Robust Power-Constrained Deep Learning Algorithm (hereby
101
      referred to as the RPC scheme) in Kim et al. (2020) and LightCode in Ankireddy et al. (2024) to the
102   AWGN-BC channel. We choose to evaluate the performance of RPC due to its robustness to noise
103   as it employs a GRU and attention mechanisms to leverage noise averaging which may be useful
104   in adverse channel conditions Kim et al. (2020). Conversely, since the model in RPC may contain
105   many hidden states requiring a great deal of memory, we choose to compare the performance against
106   the newly proposed LightCode as it has demonstrated near state of the art performance in certain
107   regimes and at a code rate of R = 1/3 while using a less memory intensive architecture Ankireddy
      et al. (2024).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      In the case of both codes, we demonstrate an extension of these codes to two receivers by imple-
109   menting multiple decoder modules and adjusting the loss function accordingly. In this way, the
110   entire system consisting of the encoder and decoders is treated as a global model in which the pur-
111   pose of training is to minimize the overall loss between all users. We train each model for two users,
112   and show the performance of each algorithm in different noise regimes and for various code rates.
113
      Beyond this, we view vertical federated learning (VFL) as a promising direction for training feed-
114
      back codes across the AWGN-BC channel, instead of utilizing a global model framework as outlined
115   above. To the best of our knowledge, there have been no deep learning aided feedback codes which
116   have employed a federated training approach. A federated framework could allow the implementa-
117   tion of deep learned feedback codes in practice– for example, it could allow the training of codes
118   when decoders do not know the number of other users in the system a priori. Also, a federated
119   training approach could offload computation from end-user devices which may be more resource
120   constrained to the base station. This could also allow adaptive learning of codes in dynamic wireless
121   environment Niknam et al. (2020). We consider the encoder and decoders each to have their own
122   local model of the communication system and view the encoder as the active party. In this case,
123
      all parties share the same sample space, but each party’s local model is updated according to it’s
      own loss function Liu et al. (2024). To this end, we propose a VFL training framework where each
124
      decoder transmits its model output to the encoder over the feedback link and, conversely, the en-
125
      coder transmits the new computed gradients to each decoder at the end of a batch. In the proposed
126   VFL algorithm, we transmit the uncoded gradients across the downlink channel which are corrupted
127   by AWGN. We choose to explore the uncoded downlink versus a quantized method as it has been
128   shown that there is better convergence behavior in federated learning with noisy downlinks Amiri
129   et al. (2021). We observe the effect of noise in the VFL training process.
130
      In summary, we discuss the implementation and training of the RPC-based and the LightCode-
131
      based AWGN-BC code both for the global model and the extension to the VFL framework. Results
132   are compared to the concatenated scheme in Ahmad et al. since it is designed for noisy feedback
133   specifically, whereas purely linear codes suffer greatly with feedback noise Ahmad et al. (2015). We
134   compare the performance of each scheme in various SNR scenarios and with various code rates. We
135   also observe the performance of the VFL algorithm with AWGN training noise. Finally, we discuss
136   limitations and future research directions.

      We also train the RPC-BC model using a vertical federated learning approach. Assume that the
366   ℓth decoder has its own local model Gℓ parameterized by θℓ , and the encoder has its own model F

369   the encoder is sending one signal to all decoders, the encoder should own a global model in order to
370   contribute to minimizing the overall probability of error for all decoders.

516   6     C ONCLUSION
517
518   In this work, we have extended deep-learning aided feedback codes to broadcast channels and evalu-
519   ated their performance. Our numerical studies indicated that there appears to be not much advantage
520   over using the simple extension of LightBC in the broadcast setting versus utilizing TDD with the
521   single user LightCode in the high rate, noisy regime. On the other hand, with certain noise regimes
522   and code rates, there does appear to be an advantage in using RPC-BC versus TDMA with the sin-
523   gle user RPC. The experiments indicate that in especially noisy environments and higher code rates,
524   RPC-BC tends to outperm LightBC, whereas LightBC tends to perform exceptionally well in lower
525
      noise, lower code rate scenarios. The more robust performance in higher noise scenarios of RPC-
      BC could possible be attributed to the RNN architecture of RPC-BC since it allows noise averaging
526
      across communication rounds. On the other hand, LightBC behaves much like linear feedback codes
527
      in that its probability of error steeply drops off as the channel gets more reliable. Nonetheless, this
528   seems to suggest that more work needs to be done tailoring Deep Learning algorithms specifically to
529   the broadcast communication scheme in order to improve performance in more unreliable commu-
530   nication settings, where it may be necessary to employ RNN-based models in especially unreliable
531   channels to leverage noise averaging.
532
      In addition, we also explored the performance of a federated approach to training each of the codes
533
      where AWGN noise was added to parameters when being passed between encoder and decoders.
534   We found that the addition of AWGN to the gradients and feedback channel generally resulted in
535   considerable performance degradation, suggesting that a reliable communication protocol of model
536   parameters is necessary when using federated learning. Though a federated approach makes sense
537   for training deep learned codes, one practical restriction is that decoders that may leverage noise
538   averaging such as in RPC-BC typically have many parameters due to the hidden states in the GRUs.
539   Thus, more research is necessary to compress parameters or design lower complexity decoder mod-
      ules that perform well in high noise scenarios.
