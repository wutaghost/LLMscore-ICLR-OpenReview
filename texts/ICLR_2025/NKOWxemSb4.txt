009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                To keep pace with the rapid advancements in design complexity within modern
012                computing systems, directed graph representation learning (DGRL) has become
013                crucial, particularly for encoding circuit netlists, computational graphs, and devel-
014                oping surrogate models for hardware performance prediction. However, DGRL
015                remains relatively unexplored, especially in the hardware domain, mainly due to
016                the lack of comprehensive and user-friendly benchmarks. This study presents a
017
                   novel benchmark comprising five hardware design datasets and 13 prediction tasks
                   spanning various levels of circuit abstraction. We evaluate 21 DGRL models, em-
018
                   ploying diverse graph neural networks and graph transformers (GTs) as backbones,
019
                   enhanced by positional encodings (PEs) tailored for directed graphs. Our results
020                highlight that bidirected (BI) message passing neural networks (MPNNs) and ro-
021                bust PEs significantly enhance model performance. Notably, the top-performing
022                models include PE-enhanced GTs interleaved with BI-MPNN layers and BI-Graph
023                Isomorphism Network, both surpassing baselines across the 13 tasks. Addition-
024                ally, our investigation into out-of-distribution (OOD) performance emphasizes the
025                urgent need to improve OOD generalization in DGRL models. This benchmark,
026                implemented with a modular codebase, streamlines the evaluation of DGRL models
027                for both hardware and ML practitioners.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
      Directed graphs, where edges encode directional information, are widely utilized as data models in
032
      various applications, including email communication Kossinets et al. (2008); Khrabrov & Cybenko
033
      (2010), financial transactions Gale & Kariv (2007); Chinazzi & Fagiolo (2015); Tiwari et al. (2021),
034   and supply chains Surana et al. (2005); Kaur et al. (2006); Wagner & Neshat (2010). Notably,
035   hardware designs can be represented as directed graphs, such as circuit netlists Hachtel & Somenzi
036   (2005); Vladimirescu (1994), control and data flow graphs Cummins et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2022b);
037   Bai et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2024), or computational graphs Zhang et al. (2021a); Phothilimthana
038   et al. (2023), often exhibiting unique properties. These graph structures reflect restricted connection
039   patterns among circuit components or program operation units, with directed edges encapsulating
040   long-range directional and logical dependencies.
041   Recently, employing machine learning (ML) to assess the properties of hardware designs via their
042   directed graph representations has attracted significant attention Wu & Xie (2022); Bai et al. (2023);
043   Dong et al. (2023); Li et al. (2020c); Ma et al. (2019b); Bücher et al. (2022); He et al. (2021);
044   Guo et al. (2022); Phothilimthana et al. (2023). Traditional simulation-based methods often require
045   considerable time (hours or days) to achieve the desired accuracy in assessing design quality Zhao
046   et al. (2017); Dai et al. (2018); Wu et al. (2021a; 2022b), substantially slowing down the hardware
047   development cycle due to repeated optimization-evaluation iterations. In contrast, ML models can
048
      serve as faster and more cost-effective surrogates for simulators, offering a balanced alternative
      between simulation costs and prediction accuracy Mirhoseini et al. (2021); Al-Hyari et al. (2021); Wu
049
      et al. (2021a); Chen et al. (2018); Jia et al. (2020); Cakir & Malik (2018); Dudziak et al. (2020); Cao
050
      et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2020); Wu et al. (2023). Such an approach is promising
051   to expedite hardware evaluation, especially given the rapid growth of design complexity in modern
052   electronics and computing systems Society.

      Despite the promising use cases, developing ML models for reliable predictions on directed graphs,
055   particularly within hardware design loops, is still in its early stages, largely due to the lack of
056   comprehensive and user-friendly benchmarks. Existing studies in the ML community have primarily
057   focused on undirected graphs, utilizing Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) Kipf & Welling (2016); Xu
058   et al. (2019); Veličković et al. (2018) or Graph Transformers (GTs) Rampášek et al. (2022); Kreuzer
059   et al. (2021); Ying et al. (2021); Min et al. (2022). Among the limited studies on directed graph
060   representation learning (DGRL) Zhang et al. (2021b); Tong et al. (2020b;a); Geisler et al. (2023),
061   most have only evaluated their models for node/link-level predictions on single graphs in domains
062   such as web networks, or financial networks He et al. (2024). These domains exhibit very different
063
      connection patterns compared to those in hardware design. To the best of our knowledge, CODE2
      in the Open Graph Benchmark (OGB) Hu et al. (2020) is the only commonly used benchmark that
064
      may share some similarities with hardware data. However, the graphs in CODE2 are intermediate
065
      representations (IRs) of Python programs, which may not fully reflect the properties of data in
066   hardware design loops.
067
068   Numerous DGRL models for hardware design tasks have been developed by domain experts. While
069
      promising, hardware experts tend to incorporate domain-specific insights with off-the-shelf GNNs
      (e.g., developing hierarchical GNNs to mimic circuit modules Wu et al. (2022b); Dong et al. (2023)
070
      or encoding circuit fan-in and fan-out in node features Ren et al. (2020); Alrahis et al. (2021b);
071
      Vasudevan et al. (2021)), with limited common design principles investigated in model development.
072   In contrast, state-of-the-art (SOTA) DGRL techniques proposed by the ML community lack thorough
073   investigation in these tasks. These techniques potentially offer a more general and effective manner
074   of capturing data patterns that might be overlooked by domain experts.
075
      Present Benchmark. This work addresses the aforementioned gaps by establishing a new benchmark
076
      consisting of representative hardware design tasks and extensively evaluating various DGRL tech-
077
      niques for these tasks. On one hand, the evaluation results facilitate the identification of commonly
078   useful principles for DGRL in hardware design. On the other hand, the ML community can leverage
079   this benchmark to further advance DGRL techniques.
080
081
      Specifically, our benchmark collects five hardware design datasets encompassing a total of 13
      prediction tasks. The data spans different levels of circuit abstraction, with graph sizes reaching up to
082
      400+ nodes per graph across 10k+ graphs for graph-level tasks, and up to 50k+ nodes per graph for
083
      node-level tasks (see Fig. 1 and Table. 1). We also evaluate 21 DGRL models based on 8 GNN/GT
084   backbones, combined with different message passing directions and various enhancements using
085   positional encodings (PEs) for directed graphs Geisler et al. (2023). PEs are vectorized representations
086   of node positions in graphs and have been shown to improve the expressive power of GT/GNNs for
087   undirected graphs Wang et al. (2022a); Huang et al. (2024); Lim et al. (2022); Rampášek et al. (2022).
088   PEs for directed graphs are still under-explored Geisler et al. (2023), but we believe they could be
089   beneficial for hardware design tasks that involve long-range and logical dependencies.
090   Our extensive evaluations provide significant insights into DGRL for hardware design tasks. Firstly,
091   bidirected (BI) message passing neural networks (MPNNs) can substantially improve performance
092   for both pure GNN encoders and GT encoders that incorporate MPNN layers, such as GPS Rampášek
093   et al. (2022). Secondly, PEs, only when used stably Wang et al. (2022a); Huang et al. (2024), can
094   broadly enhance the performance of both GTs and GNNs. This observation contrasts with findings
095   from undirected graph studies, particularly in molecule property prediction tasks, where even unstable
096   uses of PEs may improve model performance Dwivedi et al. (2023); Kreuzer et al. (2021); Lim et al.
097   (2022); Rampášek et al. (2022). Thirdly, GTs with MPNN layers typically outperform pure GNNs on
098
      small graphs but encounter scalability issues when applied to larger graphs.
099   With these insights, we identify two top-performing models: GTs with BI-MPNN layers (effective for
100   small graphs in the HLS and AMP datasets) and the BI-Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) Xu et al.
101   (2019), both enhanced by stable PEs. These models outperform all baselines originally designed
102   by hardware experts for corresponding tasks, across all 13 tasks. Notably, this work is the first to
103   consider GTs with BI-MPNN layers and using stable PEs in DGRL, so the above two models have
104
      novel architectures essentially derived from our benchmarking effort.

107   mapping Wu et al. (2023), inference on different RISC-V CPUs He et al. (2021)), for each dataset we

143   Graph Representation Learning as Powerful Surrogate Models. ML-based surrogate models have
144   been widely adopted in scientific fields Olivier et al. (2021); Zuo et al. (2021) and recently extended
145   in hardware design. Beyond traditional methods Biggs et al. (2022); Xiao et al. (2023a); Mack
146   et al. (2023), graph-learning-based surrogate models for hardware design have already demonstrated
147
      effectiveness Wang et al. (2022b); Ustun et al. (2020); Wu et al. (2022b; 2021a); Bai et al. (2023);
      Ren et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2019a); Li et al. (2020c); Ma et al. (2019b); Vasudevan et al. (2021);
148
      Bücher et al. (2022); Alrahis et al. (2021b); Lu et al. (2023); Qin et al. (2024); Sohrabizadeh et al.
149
      (2023; 2022)Xiao et al. (2023b) , several aspects warrant further investigation. First, existing studies
150   often rely on task-specific heuristics to encode circuit structural information Ma et al. (2019b); Ren
151   et al. (2020); Alrahis et al. (2021b); Bücher et al. (2022); Vasudevan et al. (2021); Mirhoseini et al.
152   (2021), hindering the migration of model-design insights from one task to an even closely related task.
153   Second, the majority of these studies conduct message passing of GNNs along edge directions, with
154   few considering BI implementation He et al. (2021); Guo et al. (2022), and there is an absence of a
155   comparative analysis of different DGRL approaches. Third, the designed models are often trained and
156   tested within similar data distributions Alrahis et al. (2021b); Zhao & Shamsi (2022); He et al. (2021),
157   lacking systematic OOD evaluation for new or more complicated designs. Hence, it is imperative to
158
      establish a comprehensive benchmark to compare different DGRL approaches for hardware design
      tasks.
159
160   Methods for DGRL. NN architectures for DGRL can be classified into three types: spatial GNNs,
161   spectral GNNs, and transformers. Spatial GNNs use graph topology as inductive bias, some employ
      bidirected message passing for regular directed graphs Jaume et al. (2019); Wen et al. (2020); Kollias


                                                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al. (2022); Rossi et al. (2024), others use asynchronous message passing exclusively designed
163   for directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) Zhang et al. (2019b); Dong et al. (2022); Thost & Chen (2020).
164   Spectral GNNs generalize the ideas of Fourier transform and corresponding spectral convolution
165   from undirected to directed graphs Furutani et al. (2020); He et al. (2022); Fiorini et al. (2023); Zhang
166   et al. (2021b); Singh et al. (2016); Ma et al. (2019a); Monti et al. (2018); Tong et al. (2020b); Koke &
167   Cremers (2023); Transformers with attention mechanism reply on designing direction-aware PEs to
168   capture directed graph topology. This benchmark is the first to consider combining transformers with
169   MPNN layers for DGRL, extending the ideas in Rampášek et al. (2022). Regarding the choices of
170   PEs, most studies are on undirected graphs Wang et al. (2022a); Huang et al. (2024); Lim et al. (2022);
171
      Dwivedi et al. (2022a). For directed graphs, the potential PEs are Laplacian eigenvectors of the
      undirected graphs by symmetrizing the original directed ones Dwivedi et al. (2023), singular vectors
172
      of adjacency matrices Hussain et al. (2022) and the eigenvectors of Magnetic Laplacians Shubin
173
      (1994); Fanuel et al. (2017; 2018); Geisler et al. (2023). No previous investigate benefit for DGRL
174   from stably incorporating PE Wang et al. (2022a); Huang et al. (2024), and we are the first to consider
175   stable PEs for DGRL.

      Existing Relevant Benchmarks. Dwivedi et al. Dwivedi et al. (2022b) benchmark long-range
      reasoning of GNNs on undirected graphs; PyGSD He et al. (2024) benchmarks signed and directed
178
      graphs, while focusing on social or financial networks. We also compare all the methods for directed
179
      unsigned graphs in PyGSD and notice that the SOTA spectral method therein - MagNet Zhang et al.
180   (2021b) still works well on node-level tasks on a single graph (SR), which shares some similar insights.
181   The hardware community has released graph-structured datasets from various development stages to
182   assist surrogate model development, including but not limited to NN workload performance Zhang
183   et al. (2021a); Phothilimthana et al. (2023), CPU throughput Chen et al. (2019); Sỳkora et al. (2022);
184   Mendis et al. (2019), resource and timing in HLS Wu et al. (2022b); Bai et al. (2023), design quality in
185   logic synthesis Chowdhury et al. (2021), design rule checking in physical synthesis Guo et al. (2022);
186   Chai et al. (2023); Xun et al. (2024); Chhabria et al. (2024), and hardware security Yu et al. (2021).
187   In addition to datasets, ProGraML Cummins et al. (2021) introduces a graph-based representation
188
      of programs derived from compiler IRs (e.g., LLVM/XLA IRs) for program synthesis and compiler
      optimization. Very recently, Google launched TPUgraph for predicting the runtime of ML models
189
      based on their computational graphs on TPUs Phothilimthana et al. (2023). Our CG dataset includes
190
      computational graphs of ML models, specifically on edge devices.

193
194   This section introduces the five datasets with thirteen tasks used in this benchmark. The datasets
195   cover both digital and analog designs, selected to represent different circuit abstraction levels. From a
196   bottom-up perspective, we include operational amplifiers (AMP) at the device level, timing prediction
197   (TIME) in physical synthesis, symbolic reasoning (SR) in logic synthesis, performance prediction
198   in high-level synthesis (HLS), and workload mapping on hardware platforms (CG), as illustrated in
199
      Fig. 1. Table 1 displays the statistics of each dataset. Next, we briefly introduce the five datasets,
      with details provided in Appendix. D. Although these datasets are generated by existing studies,
200
      we offer modular pre-processing interfaces to make them compatible with PyTorch Geometric and
201
      user-friendly for integration with DGRL methods.
202
203   High-Level Synthesis (HLS) Wu et al. (2022b): Constructing C/C++ into graphs originates form
204   Xiao et al. (2017). The HLS dataset collects IR graphs of C/C++ code after front-end compilation Al-
205
      fred et al. (2007), and provides post-implementation performance metrics on FPGA devices as labels
      for each graph, which are obtained after hours of synthesis with Vitis vit and implementation with
206
      Vivado viv. The labels to predict include resource usage, (i.e., look-up table (LUT) and digital signal
207
      processor (DSP)), and the critical path timing (CP). See Appendix. D.1 for graph input details.
208
      Significance: The HLS dataset is crucial for testing NNs’ ability to accurately predict post-
209
      implementation metrics to accelerate design evaluation in the stage of HLS.
210
211   OOD Evaluation: For training and ID testing, we use control data flow graphs (CDFG) that integrate
212
      control conditions with data dependencies, derived from general C/C++ code; As to OOD cases, we
      use data flow graphs (DFG) derived from basic blocks, leading to distribution shifts.
213
214   Symbolic Reasoning (SR) Wu et al. (2023): The SR dataset collects bit-blasted Boolean networks
215   (BNs) (unstructured gate-level netlists), with node labels annotating high-level abstractions on local
      graph structures, e.g., XOR functions, majority (MAJ) functions, and adders, generated by the logic

217   adders, and nodes shared by XOR and MAJ functions. See Appendix. D.2 for detailed input encoding
218   and label explanation.
219
      Significance: Reasoning high-level abstractions from BNs has wide applications in improving
220   functional verification efficiency Ciesielski et al. (2019) and malicious logic identification Mahzoon
221   et al. (2019). GNN surrogate models are anticipated to replace the conventional structural hashing
222   and functional propagation Li et al. (2013); Subramanyan et al. (2013) and boost the scalability with

240
      Computational Graph (CG) Zhang et al. (2021a): The CG dataset consists of computational

      graphs of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with inference latency on edge devices (i.e., Cortex

245   Significance: Accurately measuring the inference latency of DNNs is essential for high-performance

      3-stage operational amplifiers (Op-Amps). Circuit specifications (i.e. DC gain, phase margin (PM),

      and bandwidth (BW)) as labels are extracted after simulation with Cadence Spectre spe. Details are

      Significance: Analog circuit design is less automated and requires more manual effort compared to
256
      its digital counterpart. Mainstream approaches such as SPICE-based circuit synthesis and simula-
257   tion Vladimirescu (1994), are computationally expensive and time-consuming. If ML algorithms can
258   approximate the functional behavior and provide accurate estimates of circuit specifications, they may

261   Op-Amps in the main feed-forward path). For OOD evaluation, we use 2-stage Op-Amps.

286   4.1    D ESIGN S PACE FOR D IRECTED G RAPH R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
287
288   In this section, we introduce the DGRL methods evaluated in this benchmark. Our evaluation focuses
289   on four design modules involving GNN backbones, message passing directions, transformer selection,
290   and PE incorporation, illustrated in Fig. 2. Different GNN backbones and transformer adoptions cover
291   10 methods in total with references in Tab. 2. We also consider their combinations with different
292
      message-passing directions and various ways to use PEs, which overall gives 21 DGRL methods.
293   For GNNs, we consider 4 spectral methods, namely GCN Kipf & Welling (2016), DGCN Tong et al.
294   (2020b), DiGCN Tong et al. (2020a) and MagNet Zhang et al. (2021b), where the latter three are
295   SOTA spectral GNNs specifically designed for DGRL He et al. (2024); For spatial GNNs, we take
296   GIN Xu et al. (2019) and Graph Attention Network (GAT) Veličković et al. (2018), which are the
297   most commonly used MPNN backbones for undirected graphs. We evaluate the combination of
298
      GCN, GIN and GAT with three different message-passing directions: a) ‘undirected’(-) treats directed
      graphs as undirected, using the same NN parameters to perform message-passing along both forward
299
      and reverse edge directions; b) ‘directed’(DI) only passes messages exclusively along the forward
300
      edge directions; c) ‘bidirected’(BI) performs message passing in both forward and reverse directions
301   with distinct parameters for either direction. The other GNNs (DGCN, DiGCN and MagNet) adopt
302   spectral convolution that inherently considers edge directions. The combination of ‘BI’ with spatial
303   GNN layers gives the state-of-the-art spatial GNNs for DGRL, i.e., EDGNN Jaume et al. (2019).
304
      For GTs, we adopt the eigenvectors of the graph Magnetic Laplacian (MagLAP) matrix as the PEs
305
      of nodes Furutani et al. (2020); Shubin (1994), as they are directional-aware. The MagLap matrix
306
      Lq is a complex Hermitian matrix with parameter q ∈ [0, 1) named potential, which is treated as a
307   hyper-parameter in our experiments. Note that when q = 0, MagLap degenerates to the symmetric
308   Laplacian matrix L0 as a special case. See Appendix B for a brief review of MagLap. The GT with
309   the MagLap PEs attached to node features gives the SOTA GT model for DGRL, named TmD for
310   brevity, proposed in Geisler et al. (2023). GPS Rampášek et al. (2022) is a GT model with MPNN
311   layers Hamilton et al. (2017); Gilmer et al. (2017) interleaving with transformer layers Vaswani
312   et al. (2017), originally proposed for undirected graphs. We extend GPS to directed graphs by
313   using MagLap PEs for transformer layers and DI/BI message passing in its MPNN layers. Hence,
314   GPS is also an extension of TmD by incorporating MPNN layers. As transformers may not scale
315
      well on large graphs, we evaluate vanilla transformer layers and their lower-rank approximation
      Performer Kreuzer et al. (2021) for efficient computation, named as GPS-T and GPS-P, respectively.

      Therefore, besides NPE, we also consider a stable way of incorporating PEs for DGRL, namely
329
      ‘edge PE’ (EPE), inspired by Wang et al. (2022a); Huang et al. (2024). Specifically, we take
330
      the smallest d eigenvalues λq ∈ Rd and their corresponding eigenvectors Vq ∈ C|V |×d from
331
      Lq . Then, permutation equivariant and smooth functions κ : Rd → Rd (we have m many κ)
332
      are adopted to map d-dim eigenvalues to an embedding vector. It will then produce m many n-
333
      by-n matrices Vq diag(κ1 (λ))Vq† , ..., Vq diag(κm (λ))Vq† by doing matrix multiplication between
334   eigenvalue embeddings and eigenvectors. Lastly, these n-by-n matrices are concatenated and passed
335   to ρ, a permutation equivaraint and smooth function (e.g., standard GNNs). The output EPE is of
336   dimension n × n × d, representing PE-refined features of edges (node pairs). In a high level, the
337   key to achieve stability here is the permutation equivariance as well as smoothness of κ, which
338   ensures smooth weights κ(λ) of eigenvectors’ inner product across different eigenvalues (see similar
339   argument of Theorem 3.1 in Huang et al. (2024)). Finally, in GTs, EPEu,v is added to the attention
340   weight between nodes u and v as a bias term at each attention layer.
341   We note that PEs can also be used in more than GTs, to improve the expressive power of GNNs Li
342   et al. (2020b); Ying et al. (2021); Lim et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2024). We leverage this idea and
343   enhance the GNN models for directed graphs with PEs. Specifically, for the GNNs NPE will use
344   NPEv as extra node features of node v while EPE will use EPEu,v as extra edge features of edge uv
345   if uv is an edge.
346   The incorporation with EPE helps discover a novel GT model for directed graphs, i.e., GT with
347   BI-MPNN layers enhanced by EPE, abbreviated as BI-GPS+EPE. We also make the first attempt to
348   combine GNNs with PEs for directed graphs, which yields the model BI-GIN(E)+EPE.

462   6.2      S UMMARY: T HE R ECIPE FOR DGRL
463
464   Through benchmarking various combinations within the design space, we have formulated a design
465
      recipe for DGRL methods tailored for encoding hardware data: The use of ’bidirected’ (BI) message
      passing and stable positional encodings (PE) can significantly enhance model performance. Therefore,
466
      we recommend BI-GPS-T+EPE for encoding small graphs and BI-GIN+EPE for large graphs.
467
468   We further compare the two models’ performance with the baseline methods proposed by hardware
469   design practitioners specifically for the corresponding tasks in the original papers. Results are shown
470   in Table. 6. The comparison focuses on ID evaluation as for most of the tasks, the original studies
471
      did not even report OOD evaluations. We follow the same data split as baseline methods for fair
      comparison (see the details in Appendix C). BI-GIN+EPE achieves results comparable to, or better
472
      than, the baseline methods. BI-GPS+EPE achieves even better performance than BI-GIN+EPE for
473
      small graphs. Note that the baseline methods for certain tasks may incorporate domain-specific
474   expert knowledge and additional data processing. For example, CKTGNN Dong et al. (2023) for
475   the AMP dataset modifies the graph structures into DAGs and employs an asynchronized message
476   passing to mimic the signal flow in these amplifiers; ‘timer-GNN’ Guo et al. (2022) is tailored for the
477   TIME dataset to mimic the transmission rules of clock signals and designs a non-linear delay model
478   (NLDM) along with a novel module ‘cell library’. Such domain knowledge may further enhance
479   BI-GPS+EPE and BI-GIN+EPE for these specific tasks, which is left for future research.
480   Discussion on OOD Evaluation: Despite BI-GPS-T+EPE and BI-GIN+EPE outperforming other
481   methods in OOD testing across all tasks, we cannot yet conclude that these methods are sufficiently
482   effective for practical OOD usage. In fact, making accurate predictions with OOD data in hardware
483   design remains a significant challenge. When the graph structures in training sets are sufficiently
484   diverse, such as in datasets with a large number of small graphs (e.g., AMP, HLS) or those with
485   abundant local structures (e.g., SR), BI-GIN+EPE and BI-GPS-T+EPE tend to maintain reasonably
      good performance on OOD data. However, OOD generalization becomes challenging when the


                                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      diversity of graph structures in the training set is limited. For instance, in the TIME dataset, which
487   has a limited variety of graph structures for training and OOD testing data with entirely different
488   graph structures, both BI-GIN+EPE and BI-GPS-T+EPE perform worse than timer-GNN Guo et al.
489   (2022), which integrates the knowledge of the physical structure of circuits (as shown in Table 21).
490   We identify ensuring OOD performance, especially when training sets lack sufficiently diversified
491   graph structures, as a key direction for future DGRL research.

493   7   C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
494
      Through benchmarking 21 methods on in-distribution and out-of-distribution test sets across 13 tasks
495
      and 5 datasets within the hardware design loop, we find bidirected (BI) message passing neural
496
      networks can substantially improve the performance of both Graph Transformer (GT) encoders that
497   incorporate MPNN layers and pure GNN encoders. Positional Encodings (PEs), particularly when
498   used stably, can broadly enhance the performance of both GTs and GNNs. With these insights, we
499   identify two top-performing models: BI-GPS-T+EPE and BI-GIN+EPE, both of which outperform
500   the baseline models originally proposed for the corresponding tasks.
501
      Limitations: Although the benchmark covers multiple stages in hardware design loop, there are
502
      other tasks Mendis et al. (2019); Sỳkora et al. (2022); Xun et al. (2024); Chai et al. (2023); Chen
503   et al. (2019); Alrahis et al. (2021b); Zhang et al. (2019a) that could be included in this benchmark as
504   DGRL tasks. Given technological advancements and the diversity of design tools, ensuring OOD
505   performance remains an urgent open problem in hardware design. Future research may involve
506   high-quality data collection Jain et al. (2020); Gupta et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021b); Whang et al.
507   (2023); Wu et al. (2020) or the development of OOD-aware DGRL methods Liu et al. (2023); Shi
508   et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024); Liu & Ding (2024).

            E NCODINGS FOR D IRECTED G RAPHS
1158
1159
       Positional encodings (PE) for graphs are vectorized representations that can effectively describe the
       global position of nodes (absolute PE) or relative position of node pairs (relative PE). They provide
1160
       crucial positional information and thus benefits many backbone models that is position-agnostic. For
1161
       instance, on undirected graphs, PE can provably alleviate the limited expressive power of Message
1162   Passing Neural Networks Xu et al. (2019); Morris et al. (2019); Li et al. (2020b); Lim et al. (2022);
1163   PE are also widely adopted in many graph transformers to incorporate positional information and
1164   break the identicalness of nodes in attention mechanism Kreuzer et al. (2021); Ying et al. (2021);
1165   Rampášek et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2022). As a result, the design and use of PE become one of the
1166   most important factors in building powerful graph encoders.
1167
       Likely, one can expect that direction-aware PE are also crucial when it comes to directed graph
1168   encoders. “Direction-aware” implies that PE should be able to capture the directedness of graphs. A
1169   notable example is Magnetic Laplacian PE Geisler et al. (2023), which adopts the eigenvectors of
1170   Magnetic Laplacian as PE. Note that Magnetic Laplacian can encode the directedness via the sign
1171   of phase of exp{±i2πq}. Besides, when q = 0, Magnetic Laplacian reduces to normal symmetric
1172   Laplacian. Thus, Magnetic Laplacian PE for directed graphs can be seen as a generalization of Lapla-
1173   cian PE for undirected graphs, and the latter is known to enjoy many nice spectral properties Chung
1174   (1997) and be capable to capture many undirected graph distances Kreuzer et al. (2021). Therefore,
1175   Magnetic Laplacian appears to be a strong candidate for designing direction-aware PE. The definition
1176
       is as follows:
1177   Magnetic Laplacian (MagLap) matrix is a Hermitian complex matrix defined by Lq = I −
1178   D −1/2 Aq D −1/2 , where D is the diagonalized degree matrix counting both in-degree and out-
1179   degree, and Aq refers to the complex matrix as follows:
1180
                                            exp{i2πq},            if (u, v) ∈ E,
                                           
1181
1182                            [Aq ]u,v =   exp{−i2πq},           if (v, u) ∈ E,                (1)
                                           
1183                                                    1, if (u, v), (v, u) ∈ E,
1184
1185   with a parameter q ∈ [0, 1) called potential. Hermitian refers to the property that complex conjugate
1186   L†q equals to Lq . It is also worth noticing that when q = 0, MagLap Lq=0 degenerates to the standard
1187   symmetric Laplacian matrix L = I − D −1/2 (A + A⊤ )D −1/2 as a special case, where A is the
       Adjacency matrix. See Furutani et al. (2020) for a comprehensive introduction to Magnetic Laplacian.

1271   D.2        S YMBOLIC R EASONING (SR) DATASET
1272
1273   In this dataset, all the circuit designs are represented as and-inverter graphs (AIGs), a concise and
1274   uniform representation of BNs consisting of inverters and two-input AND gates, which allows
1275
       rewriting, simulation, technology mapping, placement, and verification to share the same data
       structure Mishchenko et al. (2006). In an AIG, each node has at most two incoming edges; a node
1276
       without incoming edges is a primary input (PI); primary outputs (POs) are denoted by special output
1277
       nodes; each internal node represents a two-input AND function. Based on De Morgan’s laws, any
1278   combinational BN can be converted into an AIG Brayton & Mishchenko (2010) in a fast and scalable
1279   manner.
1280
1281
       For each node, there are three node features represented in binary values denoting node types and
       Boolean functionality. The first node feature indicates whether this node is a PI/PO or intermediate
1282
       node (i.e., AND gate). The second and the third node features indicate whether each input edge is
1283
       inverted or not, such that AIGs can be represented as homogeneous graphs without additional edge
1284   features.
1285
1286   This dataset aims to leverage graph learning based approaches to accelerate the adder tree extraction
1287
       in (integer) multiplier verification, which involves two reasoning steps Li et al. (2013); Subramanyan
       et al. (2013): (1) detecting XOR/MAJ functions to construct adders, and then (2) identifying their
1288
       boundaries. Thus, there are two sets of node labels, i.e., two node-level classification tasks. One task
1289
       provides labels specifying whether a node (i.e., a gate) in the AIG belongs to MAJ, XOR, or is shared
1290   by both MAJ and XOR. The other task provides labels specifying whether a node is the root node of
1291   an adder. These AIGs and ground truth labels are generated by the logic synthesis tool ABC Brayton
1292   & Mishchenko (2010). Figure 6 shows the AIG of an 8-bit multiplier: the blue and red nodes are the
1293   root nodes of XOR functions, with the red nodes directly connecting to the POs; the green nodes are
1294   the root nodes of MAJ functions. By pairing one XOR function with one MAJ function sharing the
1295   same set of inputs, we can extract the adder tree, which is shown in Figure 7. More information can
       be found in the original paper Wu et al. (2023).

1437
1438   This dataset focuses on predicting circuit specifications (e.g., DC gain, bandwidth (BW), phase
1439   margin (PM)) of 2/3-stage operational amplifiers (Op-Amps), which are simulated by the circuit
1440   simulator Cadence Spectre spe. A 2/3-stage Op-Amp consists of (1) two/three single-stage Op-Amps
1441   on the main feedforwoard path and (2) several feedback paths, with one example shown in the right
1442   part of Figure 10. To make multi-stage Op-Amps more stable, feedforward and feedback paths are
1443   used to achieve different compensation schemes, each of which is implemented with a sub-circuit,
1444
       e.g., single-stage Op-Amps, resistors, and capacitors. Due to the different topologies of single-stage
       Op-Amps and various compensation schemes, each sub-circuit is built as a subgraph. There are 24
1445
       potential sub-circuits in the considered 2/3-stage Op-Amps:
1446
1447           • Single R or C ( 1 in Figure D.5, 2 types).
1448
1449
               • R and C connected in parallel or serial ( 2 in Figure D.5, 2 types).
1450           • A single-stage Op-Amp (gm ) with different polarities (positive, +gm , or negative, −gm )
1451             and directions (feedforward or feedback) ( 3 in Figure D.5, 4 types).
1452           • A single-stage Op-Amp (gm ) with R or C connected in parallel or serial (16 types). Note
1453             that we use the single-stage Op-Amp with feedforward direction and positive polarities as
1454             an example for 4 in Figure D.5.
