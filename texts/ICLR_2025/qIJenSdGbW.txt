009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-image diffusion model is a popular paradigm that synthesizes personal-
012            ized images by providing a text prompt and a random Gaussian noise. While
013            people observe that some noises are winning tickets that can achieve better text-
014            image alignment and higher human preference than others, we still lack a machine
015            learning framework to obtain those winning noises. To learn winning noises for
016            diffusion sampling, we mainly make three contributions in this paper. First, we
017            identify a new concept termed the noise prompt, which aims at turning a random
018
               Gaussian noise into a winning noise ticket by adding a small desirable perturba-
               tion derived from the text prompt. Following the concept, we first formulate the
019
               noise prompt learning framework that systematically learns “prompted” winning
020
               noise tickets associated with a text prompt for diffusion models. Second, we de-
021            sign a noise prompt data collection pipeline and collect a large-scale noise prompt
022            dataset (NPD) that contains 100k pairs of random noises and winning noises with
023            the associated text prompts. With the prepared NPD as the training dataset, we
024            trained a small noise prompt network (NPNet) that can directly learn to transform
025            a random noise ticket into a winning noise ticket. The learned winning noise per-
026            turbation can be considered as a kind of prompt for noise, as it is rich in semantic
027            information and tailored to the given text prompt. Third, our extensive experi-
028            ments demonstrate the impressive effectiveness and generalization of NPNet on
029
               improving the quality of synthesized images across various diffusion models, in-
               cluding SDXL, DreamShaper-xl-v2-turbo, and Hunyuan-DiT. Moreover, NPNet
030
               is a small and efficient controller that acts as a plug-and-play module with very
031
               limited additional inference and computational costs, as it just provides a winning
032            noise instead of a random noise without accessing the original pipeline.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Image synthesis that are precisely aligned with given text prompts remains a significant challenge for
039
      text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models (Betker et al.; Chen et al., 2023; Rombach et al., 2022b; Peebles
      & Xie, 2023; Pernias et al., 2023). Previous studies (Yu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2022; Toker et al.,
040
      2024; Kolors, 2024) have investigated the influence of text embeddings on the synthesized images
041
      and leveraged these embeddings for training-free image synthesis. It is well known that text prompts
042   significantly matter to the quality and fidelity of the synthesized images. However, image synthesis
043   is induced by both the text prompts and the noise. Variations in the noise can lead to substantial
044   changes in the synthesized images, as even minor alterations in the noise input can dramatically
045   influence the output (Xu et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024). This sensitivity underscores the critical role
046   that noise plays in shaping the final visual representation, affecting both the overall aesthetics and
047   the semantic faithfulness between the synthesized images and the provided text prompt.
048   Recent studies (Lugmayr et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024; Chefer
049   et al., 2023) observe that some selected or optimized noises are winning tickets that can help the T2I
050   diffusion models to produce images of better semantic faithfulness with text prompts, and can also
051   improve the overall quality of the synthesized images. These methods (Chefer et al., 2023; Guo et al.,
052   2024) incorporate extra modules like attention to reduce the truncate errors during the sampling
053   process, showing promising results on the compositional generalization task. However, they are
      often not widely adopted in practice for several reasons. First, they often struggle to generally

199   3.1   N OISE P ROMPT DATASET C OLLECTION
200
201   In this subsection, we outline the training data collection pipeline, which consists of collecting noise
202
      pairs and AI-feedback-based data selection, as shown in Fig. 2 Stage I.
203   Re-denoise Sampling Produces Noise Pairs. How to collect winning noise tickets with desirable
204   semantic information? Meng et al. (2023) reported that adding the random noise at each timestep
205   during the sampling process and then re-denoising, leads to a substantial improvement in the se-
206   mantic faithfulness of the synthesized images. This motivated us to propose a simple and direct
207
      approach called RE - DENOISE SAMPLING. Instead of directly adding noise to each timestep during
      the reverse process, we propose to utilize DDIM-Inversion(·) to obtain the noise from the previous
208
      step. Specifically, the joint action of DDIM-Inversion and CFG can induce the initial noise to attach
209
      semantic information. We denote the CFG scale within DDIM(·) and DDIM-Inversion(·) as ωl and
210   ωw , respectively. It is sufficient to ensure that the initial noise can be purified stably and efficiently
211   by x′t =DDIM-Inversion(DDIM(xt )) with ωl > ωw . Utilizing this method, the synthesized image
212   from x′t is more semantic information contained with higher fidelity, compared with the synthesized
213   image from xt . We call the inverse noise x′t target noise, and the noise xt source noise. The visual-
214   ization results are shown in Fig. 4. The mechanism behind this method is that DDIM-Inversion(·)
215   injects semantic information by leveraging the CFG scale inconsistency. We present theoretical
      understanding of this mechanism in Theorem E.1 with the proof.

217   help us collect noises with enhanced semantic information, it also carries the risk of introducing

223   function, human preference scores of denoised images from x0 and x′0 , respectively. If the noise
224   samples meet this criterion, we consider them to be valuable noise pairs and proceed to collect them.

      5       D ISCUSSION
508
      Limitations. Although our experimental results have demonstrated the superiority of our method,
509
      the limitations still exist. As a machine learning framework, our method also faces classic challenges
510
      from training data quality and model architecture design. First, noise prompt data quality sets the
511   performance limit of our method. The data quality is heavily constrained by re-denoise sampling
512   and data selection, but lack comprehensive understanding. For example, there exists the potential
513   risk that the proposed data collection pipeline could introduce extra bias due to the AI-feedback-
514   based selection. Second, the design of NPNet is still somewhat rudimentary. While ablation studies
515   support each component of NPNet, it is highly possible that more elegant and efficient architectures
516   may exist and work well for the novel noise prompt learning task. Optimizing model architectures
517   for this task still lacks principled understanding and remain to be a challenge.
518   Future Directions.       Our work has various interesting future directions. First, it will be highly
519   interesting to investigate improved data collection methods in terms of both performance and trust-
520   worthiness. Second, we will design more streamlined structures rather than relying on a parallel
521   approach with higher performance or higher efficiency. For example, we may directly utilize a pre-
522   trained diffusion model to synthesize winning noise more precisely. Third, we will further analyze
523   and improve the generalization of our method, particularly in the presence of out-of-distribution
524   prompts or even beyond the scope of T2I tasks.
525
526   6       C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we introduce a novel concept termed the noise prompt, which aims to transform ran-
529   dom Gaussian noise into a winning ticket noise by incorporating a small perturbation derived from
530   the text prompt. Building upon this, we firstly formulate a noise prompt learning framework that sys-
531   tematically obtains “prompted” winning tickets associated with a text prompt for diffusion models,
532   by constructing a noise prompt dataset collection pipeline that incorporates HPSv2 to filter our data
533   and designing several backbones for our noise prompt models. Our extensive experiments demon-
534
      strate the superiority of NPNet, which is plug-and-play, straightforward, and nearly time-efficient,
      while delivering significant performance improvements. This model possesses considerable applica-
535
      tion potential and practical significance. We believe that the future application scope of NPNet will
536
      be broad and impactful, encompassing video, 3D content, and seamless deployment of real AIGC
537   business products, thereby making a meaningful contribution to the community.

      A.1   B ENCHMARKS
710
711   In our main paper, we conduct experiments across three popular text-to-image datasets.
712
713   Pick-a-Pic. Pick-a-Pic (Kirstain et al., 2023) is an open dataset designed to collect user preferences
714   for images synthesized from text prompts. The dataset is gathered through a user-friendly web
715   application that allows users to synthesize images and select their preferences. Each data sample
716   includes a text prompt, two synthesized images, and a label indicating which the user prefers or a
717   tie if there is no clear preference. The Pick-a-Pic dataset contains over 500,000 examples covering
718   35,000 unique prompts. Its advantage lies in the fact that the data comes from real users, reflecting
719   their genuine preferences rather than relying on paid crowd workers
720
721   DrawBench. DrawBench is a newly introduced benchmark dataset designed for in-depth evalua-
722   tion of text-to-image synthesis models. It contains 200 carefully crafted prompts categorized into 11
723   groups, testing the models’ abilities across various semantic attributes, including compositionality,
724
      quantity, spatial relationships, and handling complex text prompts. The design of DrawBench allows
      for a multidimensional assessment of model performance, helping researchers identify strengths and
725
      weaknesses in image synthesis. By comparing with other models, DrawBench provides a compre-
726
      hensive evaluation tool for the text-to-image synthesis field, facilitating a deeper understanding of
727   synthesis quality and image-text alignment.
728
729
      HPD v2. The Human Preference Dataset v2 (Wu et al., 2023) is a large-scale, cleanly annotated
730   dataset focused on user preferences for images synthesized from text prompts. It contains 798,090
731   binary preference choices involving 433,760 pairs of images, aiming to address the limitations of
732   existing evaluation metrics that fail to accurately reflect human preferences. HPD v2 eliminates
733   potential biases and provides a more comprehensive evaluation capability, with data sourced from
734   multiple text-to-image synthesis models and real images.
735
      For testing, we use these three datasets, including the first 100 prompts subset from the Pick-a-
736   Pic web application, 100 prompts from HPD v2 test set, and 200 prompts from DrawBench. The
737   detailed information of the test sets is shown in Fig. 18.

      A.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
740
741   In our main paper, we mainly include 6 evaluation metrics to validate the effectiveness of our NPNet.
742
743   PickScore. PickScore is a CLIP-based scoring function trained from the Pick-a-Pic dataset, which
744   collects user preferences for synthesized images. It achieves superhuman performance when predict-
745   ing user preferences. PickScore aligns well with human judgments, and together with Pick-a-Pic’s
746   natural distribution prompts, enables much more relevant text-to-image model evaluation than eval-
747   uation standards, such as FID (Heusel et al., 2018) over MS-COCO (Lin et al., 2015).
748
749   HPSv2. Human Preference Score v2 (HPSv2) is an advanced preference prediction model by fine-
750   tuning CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) on Human Preference Dataset v2 (HPD v2). This model aims to
751   align text-to-image synthesis with human preferences by predicting the likelihood of a synthesized
752   image being preferred by users, making it a reliable tool for evaluating the performance of text-to-
753   image models across diverse image distribution.
754
755   AES. Aesthetic Score (AES) are derived from a model trained on the top of CLIP embeddings
      with several extra multilayer perceptron (MLP) layers to reflect the visual appeal of images. This


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      metric can be used to evaluate the aesthetic quality of synthesized images, providing insights into
757   how well they align with human aesthetic preferences.
758
759
      ImageReward. ImageReward (Xu et al., 2023) is a human preference reward model specifically
760   designed for evaluating text-to-image synthesis. It is trained on a large dataset of human compar-
761   isons, allowing it to effectively encode human preferences. The model assesses synthesized images
762   based on various criteria, including alignment with the text prompt and overall aesthetic quality.
763   ImageReward has been shown to outperform traditional metrics like Inception Score (IS) (Barratt &
764   Sharma, 2018) and Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) in correlating with human judgments, making
765   it a promising automatic evaluation metric for text-to-image synthesis.
766
767   CLIPScore. CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2022) leverages the capabilities of the CLIP model, which
768   aligns images and text in a shared embedding space. By calculating the cosine similarity between
769   the image and text embeddings, CLIPScore provides a mearsure of how well a synthesized image
770   corresponds to its textual description. While CLIPScore is effective in assessing text-image align-
771
      ment, it may not fully capture the nuances of human preferences, particularly in terms of aesthetic
      quality and detail.
772

      MPS. Multi-dimensional Preference Score (MPS) (Zhang et al., 2024), the first multi-dimensional
      preference scoring model for the evaluation of text-to-image models. The MPS introduces the pref-

777   erence choices across four dimensions, including aesthetics, semantic alignment, detail quality and
778   overall assessment on 607,541 images, providing a more comprehensive evaluation of synthesized
779   images. MPS calculates the preference scores between two images, and the sum of the two prefer-
780   ence scores equals 1.

819   B    R ELATED W ORK
820
821   Synthesizing images that are precisely aligned with given text prompts remains a significant chal-
822   lenge for Text-to-Image (T2I) diffusion models. To deal with this problem, several works explore
823   training-free improvement strategies, by optimizing the noises during the diffusion reverse process.
824   Lugmayr et al. (2022) utilizes a pre-trained unconditional diffusion model as a generative prior and
825   alters the reverse diffusion iterations based on the unmasked regions of the input image. Meng et al.
826   (2023) observe that denoising the noise with inversion steps can generate better images compared
827   with the original denoising process. Based on that, Qi et al. (2024) aims to reduce the truncate
828   errors during the denoising process, by increase the cosine similarity between the initial noise and
829   the inversed noise in an end-to-end way. It introduces significant time costs, and the synthesized
830   images may be over-rendered, making it difficult to use in practical scenarios.
831   Another research direction introduces extra modules to help optimize the noises during the reverse
832   process. Chefer et al. (2023) introduce the concept of Generative Semantic Nursing (GSN), and
833   slightly shifts the noisy image at each timestep of the denoising process, where the semantic infor-
834   mation from the text prompt is better considered. InitNO (Guo et al., 2024) consists of the initial
835   latent space partioning and the noise optimization pipeline, responsible for defining valid regions
836   and steering noise navigation, respectively. Such methods are not universally applicable, we discuss
837   this in Appendix C

      We previously mentioned that these methods (Chefer et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024) optimize the
856
      noise during the reverse process by incorporating additional modules. These methods have shown
857
      promising results in tasks involving compositional generalization. However, these methods often
858   struggle to transfer to other datasets and models, making them not universally applicable. These
859   approaches require the manual interest subject tokens, necessitating extensive test to identify the op-
860   timal tokens for a given sentence, which complicates their application across different datasets. Fur-
861   thermore, modifying the model pipeline usually requires in-depth code changes, making it difficult
862   to achieve straightforward plug-and-play integration with other models. Moreover, these methods
863   demand multiple rounds of noise optimization during the reverse process, resulting in significant
      time consumption.
