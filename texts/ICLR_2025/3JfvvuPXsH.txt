009                                                                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011             We propose a novel online point-based 3D reconstruction method from a posed
012             monocular RGB video. Our model maintains a global point cloud scene represen-
013             tation but allows points to adjust their 3D locations along the camera rays they
014             were initially observed. When a new RGB image is inputted, the model adjusts
015             the location of the existing points, expands the point cloud with newly observed
016             points, and removes redundant points. These flexible updates are achieved through
017             our novel ray-based 2D-3D matching technique. Our point-based representation
018
                does not require a pre-defined voxel size and can adapt to any resolution. A unified
                global representation also ensures consistency from different views. Results on the
019
                ScanNet dataset show that we improve over previous online methods and match
020
                the state-of-the-art performance with other types of approaches. Project page:
021             https://tinyurl.com/352xnna6

042   3D reconstruction is one of the fundamental problems in computer vision. The ability to reconstruct
043   the 3D geometry of a scene solely from a set of RGB images enables a wide range of downstream tasks
044   and applications: semantic scene parsing, object retrieval, robotics, computer-aided 3D art design,
045   etc. Free from the reliance on expensive depth sensors such as LiDARs, RGB-based reconstruction
046   methods allow a lower price of computer vision products and remove potential sources of calibration
047   error and syncing issues among multiple sensors. Among other reconstruction scenarios, online
048   reconstruction from a monocular video especially suits applications where a large scene and real-time
049   response are needed, such as autonomous driving and augmented reality.
050   The versatility of the deep learning architectures enabled a diverse set of reconstruction methods: the
051   depth prediction-based methods (Kendall et al., 2017; Chang & Chen, 2018; Wang & Shen, 2018;
052   Huang et al., 2018; Duzceker et al., 2021; Im et al., 2019; Sayed et al., 2022) accumulate features
053   from multiple source views into a 3D or 4D per-view cost volume, and then run convolutions on the
      volume to regress a depth map for each target view; volumetric methods (Kar et al., 2017; Murez


                                                                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2021; Stier et al., 2021; Bozic et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2023) aggregate features
055   in a global voxel grid instead of the per-view cost volumes, and regress voxel occupancy or TSDF
056   values where the zero crossing represents the surface; point cloud-based methods (Lhuillier & Quan,
057   2005; Chen et al., 2019) directly predict point positions on the surface.
058
      However, methods based on depth predictions predict a depth map for every image independently
059
      before fusing them into a unified surface. Thus, consistency between views is not guaranteed.
060
      Volumetric methods maintain a global voxel grid, but need to predefine the voxel size and the grid
061   size, and bear a huge memory cost when the resolution is high; hence, the meshes they output are
062   usually coarse, and they can reconstruct only within a bounded area but not extend to infinite horizon,
063   which would have been the benefit of using cameras; even though many multi-level grid methods (Sun
064   et al., 2021; Bozic et al., 2021) have been proposed to save memory, the resolution of each level is
065   still pre-fixed.
066
      A globally consistent 3D point cloud representation of the scene overcomes some of these drawbacks.
067   First of all, it is a sparse representation and is significantly less memory-consuming than volumetric
068   approaches. Besides, they do not need a specification of voxel size and can easily represent surface
069   details with an adaptive density, denser in areas requiring more details. However, to the best of our
070   knowledge, the existing point cloud-based methods usually involve iterative optimization (Chen et al.,
071   2019; Kerbl et al., 2023) and thus do not suit online algorithms.
072
      In this paper, we propose PointRecon, a point-based online reconstruction method. We maintain
073   a global feature-augmented point cloud to represent the scene. When a new image comes in, we
074   match the image features with the existing points in the scene, adjust the locations of old points,
075   add new points, and remove any redundancy. We proposed a novel ray-based matching technique
076   which assumes each 3D point sit on a camera ray with potentially wrongly estimated depth. With this
077   assumption, we sample points on the camera ray of the 3D point to match with the pixels from a new
078   camera view to predict the offset of the depth prediction of the 3D point. Depth predictions of 2D
079   pixels that are newly seen are conducted in a similar manner as well by matching their camera rays
080   with the existing rays from existing 3D points.
081   Since our approach maintains a global representation, it guarantees consistency between views; since
082   point clouds are compact and flexible in density, we do not face the trade-off between efficiency and
083   accuracy like the volumetric methods; our method also does not involve iterative refinement. To
084   maximally utilize the flexibility of point clouds, we employ AutoFocusFormer (Ziwen et al., 2023)
085   as our image encoder, which renders a set of non-uniform feature maps by performing adaptive
086   downsampling; the key points are automatically retained and thus allow for more accurate matching
087
      at downsampled levels. To summarize, we believe our contributions are:
088          • We propose to maintain an online, global 3D point cloud with a camera ray associated
089            to each point, during the process of 3D reconstruction from a monocular video. Unlike
090            volumetric methods, this approach is not limited by any pre-defined voxel resolutions.
091          • We propose a novel ray-based matching technique to match the global 3D point cloud with
092            pixels from every new incoming image.
093          • Results on the popular ScanNetv2 dataset show that our approach matches performance with
094            depth prediction and volumetric approaches, while offering more details than volumetric
095            approaches.

156   replacing the hard-threshold sparsification with a ray-based sparsification. While the volumetric
157   methods improve over the depth-based methods in terms of consistency and have the ability to infer
158
      unseen surfaces, they rely on a predefined 3D grid and are limited by the resolution of this grid –
      most of them use a grid size of 4cm, and thus have difficulties representing details finer than this

      3     M ETHOD
176
177   We present an online, point-based 3D reconstruction approach from a stream of RGB images with
178   known camera poses in a static scene. The final output is a 3D mesh. Throughout the paper, we use
179   p◦ to denote a 2D position, and p for its corresponding 3D position.
180   The general idea is to maintain a flexible global point cloud Q = {q1 , q2 , q3 , · · · } to represent
181   the entire scene, where a point qi = (pi , fi , ri , zi , ci ) contains a 3D position pi ∈ R3 in the world
182   coordinate frame, a feature vector fi ∈ RC , a unit ray direction from the camera it was first seen
183   ri ∈ R3 , a distance to this camera zi ∈ R+ and a confidence score ci ∈ R, which will be used in the
184   merging step. Points in this point cloud are allowed to move along its camera ray direction based on
185   the information that comes from a new image It ∈ RH×W ×3 with camera pose at time t. As more
186   information accumulates, the location of the 3D points will be more accurate. Thus, we can work on
187
      potentially inaccurate initial estimates of the point positions as long as we maintain the flexibility for
      the points to move.
188
189   Our main novel contribution is the computation of local attention between 3D points and 2D pixels
190   that do not necessarily live on the same epipolar line. Traditional stereo methods mostly require
191   points to stay on the epipolar line to be matched. However, this does not take into account potential
192   camera projection errors and the information that might improve the matching from areas close to the
193
      epipolar line. In our work, this is done in two steps: 1) for 3D points that have unclear depth along
      its camera ray, we obtain its epipolar line projected on the new camera, and collect 2D neighboring
194
      points close to the projected epipolar line (Fig. 3). This neighborhood is used to improve the depth
195
      estimation of the 3D point, which is called a scene adjustment step (Sec. 3.2); 2) for 2D pixels with
196   unclear depth, we sample multiple points from its camera ray and obtain the nearest 3D rays to form
197   a neighborhood (Fig. 4), this neighborhood helps to predict better depth of the 2D pixels (Sec. 3.3).
198
199
      After predicting depth for all 2D pixels, we merge these new 3D points with the point cloud using
      softmax on the predicted confidence scores, removing redundant, non-confident points in the camera
200
      frustum to keep the size of the point cloud manageable (Section 3.4).

203   3.1   BACKBONE , F EATURE P YRAMID AND M ONOCULAR D EPTH P REDICTION

205   We first feed the image It through an image encoder to obtain a multi-level set of feature maps
206   {Fl }4l=1 (1 for the highest, coarsest level, 4 for the lowest, finest level). While our method the-
207   oretically works with any image encoder such as the ResNets (He et al., 2016), we in particular
208   choose AutoFocusFormer (Ziwen et al., 2023), a transformer-based image encoder, for its ability
209   to automatically locate key points during downsampling and hence retain more useful details in
210   higher-level feature maps. In particular, it outputs non-uniform feature maps in the format of 2D
211   point clouds Fl = {(p◦1 , f1 ), (p◦2 , f2 ), · · · }, with points more densely concentrated in the areas with
212
      more details, such as small objects or object edges.
213   Next, we enhance the feature maps {Fl }4l=1 by constructing a feature pyramid - we propagate
214   information from the higher-level (more downsampled) feature maps back to the lower-levels. In
215   particular, for level l ∈ {2, 3, 4}, Fl is updated by attending to all Fk with k ≤ l using a transformer
      block (attention + MLP), in the order of the coarsest to the finest.

252   To reduce memory cost of feature matching, we first reduce the channel size of both 2D and 3D
253   features from C to 32 (for all levels). Then, we calculate the dot product between neighboring 2D
254   and 3D features. In order for the model to decide in which direction to move the point, it is not
255   enough to only collect similarities with the neighbors; the model also needs to know where are the
256   neighbors, especially in a non-uniform data structure such as point cloud. Thus, we also append
257   geometric metadata to the predictor.
258   The general idea is that, if qi matches with a 2D image point po , then we should move it to the
259   crossing between ri (the ray of qi from its original camera) and the ray from the current camera
260   shooting through the 2D point po , which we denote as ro (Fig. 3(b)). Hence, we should collect
261   information about the ray crossing as metadata. We define the ray crossing as a tuple of points:
262   (p1 (ri , ro ), p2 (ri , ro )) = (arg minp∈ri minpo ∈ro ∥p − po ∥, arg minpo ∈ro minp∈ri ∥p − po ∥) which
263   refers to the points on both rays where the rays attain the minimal distance (note that the 2 rays may
264   not intersect), where p1 lies on the ray ri and p2 lies on the ray ro . We also include some other
265   metadata such as ray angle to support the reliability of this matching. For a complete list of metadata,
266
      please see the appendix.
267   We concatenate the dot product, the metadata with the reduced feature for each sample point, and
268   feed them through a small MLP. We then concatenate the output vectors from all sample points, along

373   9-view local window. But during evaluation, as the sequence gets long, we will inevitably run into the
374   occlusion issue, and the number of cameras sharing a partial view with the current camera can easily
375   reach hundreds, causing a huge computational burden. Thus, we rely on a heuristics here: among the
376   collected cameras in the current step, if a camera is not among the K most recent cameras, then we
377   keep all the points originating from that camera and no longer allow any of the points to be deleted.
      We empirically set K = 16.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
379
380
      4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
381
382
      We choose the AFF-Mini, the smallest variant from (Ziwen et al., 2023) containing only 6.75M
383   parameters, as our image encoder backbone. We train and evaluate our model on the ScanNetv2 (Dai
384   et al., 2017) dataset. We resize the input image resolution to 640 × 480. The depth maps are predicted
385   at the resolution 160 × 120.
386
      Model hyperparameters. For both the scene adjustment step and the depth prediction step, we set
387
      the sample number K = 64 and number of neighbors M = 1. We set the sample range to be 1.5m.
388
      The point adjustment range is limited to 5m. We reduce the feature vector dimension to 32. All of
389   the transformer blocks in the model perform local attention with neighborhood size 48 and use MLP
390   expansion ratio 2.0.
391
392
      Training details. We first train the model on image pairs for 2 epochs, where the two images mutually
      serve as source of feature matching. There is no point merging at this stage. Then, we train the model
393
      on 9-view local image chunks for 6 epochs, where the images are fed to the model sequentially, and
394
      point merging is performed at every time step. We use a learning rate 10−4 for the first 4 epochs,
395   and reduce it by a factor of 0.1 at the fifth epoch and the eighth epoch. We train with the AdamW
396   optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) with a weight decay of 10−4 .
397
398
      During training, we apply random color augmentation with probability 0.8 to the input RGB images
      using TorchVision (Marcel & Rodriguez, 2010) with brightness=0.4, contrast=0.4, saturation=0.2,
399
      hue=0.1. We also apply a random order-reverse. We follow the approach from (Duzceker et al., 2021)
400
      for keyframe selection. We reduce the lambda for losses for monocular depth prediction by a factor
401   of 0.5 at the fifth and eighth epoch.
402
403   Evaluation details. During evaluation, we feed the keyframe images sequentially to the model
404
      and perform point merging at every time step. After the point cloud for the entire scene has been
      constructed, we render depth maps using the keyframe cameras: we project the points onto the image
405
      planes, and each pixel renders the depth of the closest point. We use TSDF fusion (Curless & Levoy,
406
      1996) to construct a 3D mesh from the rendered depth maps.

      5    C ONCLUSION
502
503
      We propose an online, point-based reconstruction method that allows flexible local updates, requires
504   no pre-defined resolution and ensures consistent surface from different views. Our method is mainly
505   based on our novel ray-based matching approach between a 3D point with potentially incorrect depth
506   and 2D image pixels. Experiments show that our approach achieves state-of-the-art performance.
507   However, due to the lack of post-hoc smoothing and heuristics used in the evaluation pipeline, the
508   reconstruction is still sometimes noisy. In the future, we will continue to work on improving the
509   algorithm to make the surfaces cleaner.
