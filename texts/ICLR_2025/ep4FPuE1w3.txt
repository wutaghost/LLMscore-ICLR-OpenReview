009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We consider the problem of adapting a contrastively pretrained vision-language
012            model like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) for few-shot classification. The existing
013            literature addresses this problem by learning a linear classifier of the frozen visual
014            features, optimizing word embeddings, or learning external feature adapters. This
015            paper introduces an alternative way for CLIP adaptation without adding “exter-
016            nal” parameters to optimize. We find that simply fine-tuning the last projection
017
               matrix of the vision encoder leads to strong performance compared to the existing
               baselines. Furthermore, we show that regularizing training with the distance be-
018
               tween the fine-tuned and pretrained matrices adds reliability for adapting CLIP
019
               through this layer. Perhaps surprisingly, this approach, coined ProLIP, yields
020            performances on par or better than state of the art on 11 few-shot classification
021            benchmarks, few-shot domain generalization, cross-dataset transfer and test-time
022            adaptation. Code will be made available online.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) has brought a breakthrough
028   to visual representation learning, showing that strong visual features can be learned from noisy
029   natural language descriptions at very large scale. The true potential of CLIP lies in its shared vision-
030   text space, breaking the long-standing constraints of closed-set systems and enabling non-trivial
031
      interactions and querying between text and images via prompts. Such a freedom in the label space
      makes the model readily applicable to a wide range of specialized downstream applications.
032
033   CLIP trains both a vision and text encoders (we respectively denote f and g) on large batches of
034   image-text pairs using a sum of contrastive image-to-text and text-to-image losses. At inference,
035   given an image I, one only needs the names of K candidate classes to perform zero-shot classifica-
036   tion: k̂ = argmaxk v ⊺ tk , where v = f (I; θf )/∥f (I; θf )∥2 , tk = g(Tk ; θg )/∥g(Tk ; θg )∥2 ; θf and θg
037   are the frozen parameters of f and g, respectively, Tk is a text prompt describing the class k, e.g., “a
038   photo of {classk }”. The prompt template can be engineered to boost the zero-shot performance, or
039
      automated by querying multiple descriptors of a class from Large Language Model (LLMs), such as
      GPT-3 (Brown, 2020), and ensembling their embeddings (Menon & Vondrick, 2023). Yet, the zero-
040
      shot performance can still be unsatisfying, especially for data that are supposedly under-represented
041
      in CLIP training data. Examples of such cases include geospatial data, e.g., EuroSAT (Helber et al.,
042   2019) and specialized data, e.g., FGVCAircraft (Maji et al., 2013). Thus, an interesting practical
043   setting emerged in transfer learning: Given a labeled few-shot training dataset of images, how to
044   efficiently adapt CLIP in order to maximize the performance on the test set?
045
      Hinging on only a few labeled samples for supervision, model training is prone to overfitting. The
046
      common strategy is to avoid full fine-tuning and instead adapt only a few parameters (Kumar et al.,
047
      2022). Starting from a concept-rich pretrained CLIP model, such parameter-efficient strategies
048   have been shown to be effective for few-shot tasks. In this direction, the literature explores three
049   avenues. First, Context Optimization (CoOp) (Zhou et al., 2022b;a) parameterizes the template
050   of Tk in the word embedding space, i.e., Tk = [w]1 [w]2 ...[w]M [classk ] with [w]1 [w]2 ...[w]M
051   learned while keeping f and g frozen. Second, CLIP adapters (Gao et al., 2024) learn a multi-
052   layer perceptron (MLP) h with a residual connection α on top of the frozen visual features v, i.e.,
053   v := αv + (1 − α)h(v). In both cases, the probability that a sample i belongs to the class k is
      pik ∝ exp(vi⊺ tk ), meaning that the text embeddings are used as classification weights. Third, linear

      Shortcomings. While existing solutions are technically simple and parameter-efficient, we identify
      several limitations. Prompt learning methods (Zhou et al., 2022b; Chen et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023)
063
      are slow to train as gradients need to be backpropagated over the entire text encoder. In addition,
064
      different context lengths and class-name positions lead to different performances. Adapters (Gao
065   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2022) impose architectural choices of the MLP, the bottleneck dimension
066   and the residual connection. On the other hand, while initially suggested as a few-shot baseline for
067   CLIP, the performance of linear probing (LP) lies far behind adapters and prompt learning. Its main
068   shortcoming stems from ignoring the text embeddings during adaptation. Recently, Huang et al.
069   (2024) proposed LP++, an improved LP version blending textual embeddings with classification
070   weights using class-wise learnable parameters. While LP++ (Huang et al., 2024) shows significant
071   improvements over standard LP, we argue in this work that directly using the text embeddings as
072   classification weights might be a better practice for fine-tuning CLIP, as it is closer in principle to its
073
      original pretraining regime.
074   The previous methods either train “external” parameters (e.g., Adapters, LP), or learn parameters
075   in the input space (e.g., prompt learning). To the best of our knowledge, no existing work tackles
076   few-shot CLIP adaptation problem with parameter-efficient fine-tuning of the model weights. In this
077   work, we propose a first baseline for model weights based few-shot learning. Our method, dubbed
078   “ProLIP”, is both extremely simple to implement and effective: considering pretrained CLIP en-
079
      coders f and g, we fine-tune the last visual projection matrix of f (i.e., the projector mapping visual
      embeddings into the shared embedding space) with a cross-entropy loss (Equation 1) while con-
080
      straining its weights to remain close to the pretrained ones. More details are provided in Section 2.
081
082   ProLIP is advantageous for a number of reasons: (1) It alleviates the need of “external” parame-
083   ters which usually imply architectural design search and/or heavy hyperparameter selection; (2) As
084   backpropagation is only applied on the last projector of the vision encoder, training is fast, requir-
085
      ing only few seconds like LP++ (Huang et al., 2024); (3) ProLIP uses native text embeddings as
      classification weights in the few-shot task, which is aligned with the way CLIP is pretrained; (4) It
086
      balances pretraining and adaptation by imposing a simple regularizer based on l2-distance between
087
      weights. Our simple method performs better than the literature on few-shot adaptation and few-shot
088   domain generalization, and is competitive on cross-dataset generalization and test-time adaptation.

145   Regularization. CLIP encoders map text and image modalities into a common latent space where
146   strong image-text representation correspondences are established. We argue that unconstrained fine-
147
      tuning can lead to forgetting of the rich pretraining knowledge that appears through non-trivial zero-

148

149
      the pretraining weights (i.e., knowledge forgetting), we regularize the training with the Frobenius
150   norm of the difference between the pretrained and fine-tuned matrices. The total loss is:

154   function of the number of shots, as overfitting risk increases with less data (Hastie et al., 2009).

161   Datasets. Following previous CLIP-based few-shot learning works, we experimentally test
      ProLIP on 11 classification datasets: ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), SUN397 (Xiao et al., 2010),

      DTD (Cimpoi et al., 2014), Caltech101 (Fei-Fei et al., 2004), UCF101 (Soomro, 2012), Flow-
163   ers102 (Nilsback & Zisserman, 2008), StanfordCars (Krause et al., 2013), FGVCAircraft (Maji
164   et al., 2013), EuroSAT (Helber et al., 2019), OxfordPets (Parkhi et al., 2012) and Food101 (Bossard
165   et al., 2014).
166   For domain generalization experiments we follow ProGrad (Zhu et al., 2023), using ImageNet as
167   source dataset and testing on ImageNet-V2 (Recht et al., 2019), ImageNet-Sketch (Wang et al.,
168   2019), ImageNet-A (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) and ImageNet-R (Hendrycks et al., 2021a) as out-of-
169   distribution datasets. For the cross-dataset transfer experiment, ProLIP is trained on ImageNet and
170   evaluated on the other 10 datasets, similar to Prograd (Zhu et al., 2023).
171   Training details. We follow previous works and use N ∈ {1, 2, 4, 8, 16} shots as support training
172   set for few-shot classification. LP++ (Huang et al., 2024) identify a loophole in the few-shot CLIP
173   literature (Zhang et al., 2022), which is the use of a large validation set for hyperparameter tuning.
174   Instead, authors of LP++ propose using a validation set with as many shots as in the training set.
175   For consistency, we adopt this protocol in our main experiments though also proposing a setting
176   without any validation set. Huang et al. (2024) also remark that prior works evaluate their methods
177
      based on one or three support sets, leading to large standard deviations when the few-shot set is
      not representative of the class distribution. We follow their practice and evaluate ProLIP on 10
178
      random seeds (i.e., support training sets) for each dataset. For domain generalization and cross-
179
      dataset transfer experiments, we select N = 4 like ProGrad (Zhu et al., 2023). Unless otherwise
180   stated, we employ ResNet-50 with CLIP weights as the visual encoder, similarly to the literature.
181   Training runs for 300 epochs on a full-batch of features, requiring only few seconds on a single
182   Tesla V100 GPU. The learning rate (LR) and regularizer loss weight λ are selected by grid search
183   on the few-shot validation set, with LR in {10−2 , 10−3 , 10−4 , 10−5 , 10−6 , 10−7 , 10−8 } and λ in
184   {10, 1, 10−1 , 10−2 , 10−3 , 10−4 , 0}.
185   We later show that using the regularizer (λ > 0) is better to avoid severe overfitting, and that state-
186   of-the-art results can be still achieved with a fixed LR over all the datasets, and a fixed λ which can
187   be chosen as a decreasing function of the number N of shots.

201

202
203   Table 1 reports the average classification accuracy and standard deviation, across 11 datasets.
204   Per-dataset performances are reported in Appendix B. In all few-shots settings (i.e., N ∈
205   {1, 2, 4, 8, 16}), ProLIP clearly outperforms all the baselines, showing a great potential of the ex-
206
      tremely simple approach of fine-tuning the last visual projector for adaptation. Moreover, we show
      in Section 3.2 that regularizing the projection matrix weights by minimizing their distance to the pre-
207
      trained ones improves the classification accuracy. Besides, the additional merit of our regularization
208
      is to reduce sensitivity to hyperparameter selection; therefore paving the way for a more realistic
209   few-shot adaptation setting detailed next.
210
211   For ProLIP, the statistics of the hyperparameters found by grid search (cf. Appendix C) show that
212
      performance-wise the best learning rates span a wide range of values. This is because our regular-
      ization term alleviates overfitting on the training set (cf. Section 3.2), therefore allowing the use of
213
      larger learning rate (e.g., 10−2 ) which would otherwise cause severe overfitting.
214
215   These results motivate the following question: How sensitive is ProLIP performance to the choice
      of hyperparameters in a harder yet more realistic setting: Having no validation data at all?

246   3.2                          T OWARDS A MORE REALISTIC FEW- SHOT SETTING WITH NO VALIDATION SET
247
248   In contrast to prior practices, we also propose a setting which does not rely on any validation set.
249
      The main motivation for few-shot classification being propelled by the labeled data scarcity in real-
      world situations, we argue that relying even on N -shot validation sets as in (Huang et al., 2024) may
250
      be seen as a violation of the N -shot setting since it effectively requires access to 2N examples (N
251
      for training and N for validation). It follows that the true merit of a method stems from its lower
252   sensitivity to hyperparameter choice.
253
254   The importance of our proposed regularization loss appears by testing ProLIP with different fixed
255
      hyperparameters. Figure 2 shows the average accuracy achieved by ProLIP across the 11 datasets,
      for 5 different LR values combined with regularization (λ ∈ {10−2 , 10−1 , 1}) or without regular-
256
      ization (λ = 0). For λ = 0, the accuracy drops dramatically when the learning rate is greater than
257
      10−5 . On the other hand, using the weight regularizer (λ > 0), ProLIP training becomes intrigu-
258   ingly less prone to overfitting. Even for high learning rate, different fixed values of λ still prevent
259   the accuracy degradation observed when no regularization is applied. Thus, we argue that our regu-
260   larized method is a principled approach towards a trustworthy training of CLIP on few-shot setting
261   in real-world scenarios. We refer to Table 8 in appendices for detailed performances. We observe
262   that for N = 1 and N = 2, fixed values of the hyperparameters across all the 11 datasets yield bet-
263   ter average accuracy than performing grid search. This observation is not surprising, especially for
264   these two extreme low-shot settings, as the performance on a validation set having the same degree
265   of scarcity can be noisy and not representative of the larger test set.

268   observation by formulating λ as a decreasing function of the number of shots N , constituting a
269   step towards principled and realistic few-shot classification evaluation. Table 2 corroborates our

298   3.3   G ENERALIZATION IN FEW- SHOT SETTINGS
299
300
      Domain generalization. Real-world scenarios impose an additional challenge of distribution shift
301   for model adaptation. Supposing the test data to follow the same distribution as the training is often
302   unrealistic, and a model can be of practical interest only if it exhibits resilient generalization capa-
303   bilities when confronted with out-of-distribution data. Achieving this generalization in a few-shot
304   framework is highly challenging yet important to benchmark when assessing the potential practical
305   use of CLIP. Following ProGrad, we train ProLIP on ImageNet (IN) as source dataset (with the num-
306   ber of shots N = 4), and assess it on ImageNet-V2 (IN-V2), ImageNet-Sketch (IN-S), ImageNet-A
307   (IN-A) and ImageNet-R (IN-R). Table 3 shows that ProLIP is on par or better than other methods
308   both on source and unseen target domains, for both ResNet and ViT CLIP backbones.
309   Cross-dataset generalization. An interesting question was introduced in prompt learning meth-
310   ods (Zhou et al., 2022a; Zhu et al., 2023): How does a prompt learned from a single few-shot
311   dataset perform when tested on other datasets? This setting is referred to as cross-dataset transfer /
312   generalization. Here, we ask the same question, i.e., whether the fine-tuned weights of the projection
313   matrix can generalize across datasets. Table 4 shows the generalization from ImageNet as source
314   dataset (4-shot) to the 10 other datasets. ProLIP outperforms ProGrad on 6 out of 11 datasets and on
315
      average. However, it is worth noting that zero-shot CLIP remains the strongest baseline in this set-
      ting. As argued in CoCoOp (Zhou et al., 2022a), ImageNet contains 1000 classes, mainly consisting
316
      of objects. Dog breeds are also present, so generalization (or at least small zero-shot performance
317
      drop) to datasets like OxfordPets and Caltech101 is expected. However, for datasets presenting a
318   larger gap (e.g., fine-grained and/or specialized datasets), generalization is less expected. For such
319   datasets, like FGVCAircraft and DTD, ProLIP outperforms other adaptation methods, but remains
320   behind zero-shot accuracy. Among all the target datasets, all methods exhibit a significant drop on
321   EuroSAT (26-35% from the zero-shot model). Interestingly, ProLIP not only does not exhibit the
322   same drop, but retains the zero-shot performance. In short, looking at the generalization of ProLIP
323   on each of the 10 datasets, our method is overall retaining zero-shot capability the most and showing
      cross-dataset transferability.

      In this section, our goal is to show that ProLIP can be applied beyond supervised few-shot CLIP
      adaptation. Motivated by the risk of “overfitting” the source domain in classic prompt tuning meth-
369
      ods (Zhou et al., 2022b;a), Shu et al. (2022) pioneered test-time prompt tuning (TPT), aiming to
370
      learn adaptive prompts on the fly using a single test image.
371
372   TPT background knowledge. TPT aims to learn a context specific to each test image in an unsu-
373   pervised way. Given an unlabeled test image Itest , the prompt is learned by minimizing the average
374
      prediction entropy over different augmented views of Itest . Moreover, confidence selection filters
      out the augmented views with high entropy predictions, which might lack important information for
375
      classification. More details are provided in Appendix E.
376
377   Test-time ProLIP. We do not introduce a new way for CLIP test-time adaptation but simply fol-
      low the same experimental setting as TPT (i.e., 1-step entropy minimization of averaged prediction


                                                                        7

378
      probability distribution, confidence selection), although ProLIP optimizes the projection weight ma-
379   trix Wo instead of the prompt as in TPT. Table 5 shows that ProLIP yields competitive results to
380   TPT on ImageNet and natural distribution shifts, while being one order of magnitude faster to train.
381   Note that ProLIP uses the template “a photo of a {class}” for the text prompts. Instead, few-shot
382   CLIP works (Zhang et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024) use the average of 7 templates per class for
383   ImageNet (“itap of a {class}.”, “a bad photo of the {class}.”, “a origami {class}.”, “a photo of the
384   large {class}.”, “a {class} in a video game.”, “art of the {class}.”, “a photo of the small {class}.”).

      4   R ELATED WORK
405
406
      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT). The advent of increasingly larger pretrained vision foun-
      dation models with excellent generalization capabilities has opened the way to new transfer learning
407
      approaches towards downstream tasks with limited labeled data. Full fine-tuning of such models
408
      turns out to be not only computationally inefficient but also often underperforming, even when com-
409   pared to linear probing (Kumar et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2024). Parameter-efficient fine-tuning meth-
410   ods aim to adapt models effectively with minimal changes of their parameters while freezing most
411   of the large pretrained backbone. Side-tuning (Zhang et al., 2020) trains a small network in parallel
412   to a frozen pretrained network and avoids catastrophic forgetting. Optimizing only a specific subset
413   of parameters of a model, e.g., bias terms (Zaken et al., 2022), is also an effective strategy. However
414   this still requires full backpropagation through the pretrained model. Adapter-tuning methods add
415   lightweight modules to transformer layers (Houlsby et al., 2019; Rücklé et al., 2021), but incur a
416   higher runtime cost. LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) optimizes new low-rank matrices injected to trans-
417
      former layers to approximate weight changes during fine-tuning, reducing significantly the number
      of parameters to learn. Prompt-tuning approaches, such as VPT (Jia et al., 2022), add a set of learn-
418
      able prompts to the set of input patch embeddings. In addition to the additional computational cost
419
      for the full backpropagation and for runtime for most of them, these methods are specifically devised
420   for transformer layers and are not directly applicable to convolutional networks.
421
422   Few-shot CLIP adaptation. CLIP’s specific interaction between text and image features has en-
423
      abled new adaptation methods fully exploiting this property in particular for the few-shot regime.
      Inspired by prompt learning in natural language processing (Zhong et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021),
424
      Zhou et al. (2022b) proposed context optimization (CoOp) which applies the same concept for pre-
425
      trained vision-language models. CoOp was later shown not to generalize well on unseen classes
426   within the same dataset. Thus, conditional context Optimization (CoCoOp) (Zhou et al., 2022a)
427   adds a meta-network that generates input-conditional tokens in addition to the learnable vectors,
428   making optimized context less prone to overfitting to the seen classes. Zhu et al. (2023) identified a
429   critical problem of unconstrained prompt learning methods: in extreme low-shot settings, overfitting
430   can even decrease the zero-shot performance. Consequently, they proposed regularizing the train-
431   ing by only updating prompts whose gradients do not conflict the direction resulting from zero-shot
      predictions. PLOT (Chen et al., 2023) applies optimal transport on sets of text and visual features to


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      learn the transport plan between the two sets in an inner loop, which is fixed in the outer loop where
433   prompts are learned.
434
435   Instead of adapting the model in the input space, CLIP-adapter (Gao et al., 2024) adds an MLP
436
      on top of the features in the shared embedding space, with a residual connection to preserve the
      pretrained knowledge. Zhang et al. (2022) showed that training-free adapters can be competitive
437
      to trained ones. They create a cache-model for the few-shot training set from the visual features
438
      and the corresponding ground-truth labels, which are converted to the weights of the MLP adapter.
439   Following the success of training-free CLIP adaptation, Wang et al. (2024) resorted to Gaussian
440   Discriminative Analysis (GDA) (Bishop & Nasrabadi, 2006) that assumes the conditional distribu-
441   tion of features given the labels follows a multivariate normal distribution. Thus, they construct the
442   GDA classifier using the mean vectors of each class and the inverse covariance matrix, and show
443   that classification can be further improved by ensembling GDA and zero-shot classifiers. Our ProLIP
444   takes advantage of CLIP’s compatibility between text and images and adapts it to downstream tasks
445   without additional learnable parameters or architecture changes.
446
447   5   C ONCLUSION
448
449   In this work, we proposed an extremely simple way for efficient adaptation of CLIP based on the
450   model weights. We identify that fine-tuning the last visual projection matrix, which projects the
451   visual embedding into the multi-modal latent space, is a strong alternative for few-shot classification
452   with CLIP. Moreover, we showed advantages of including a squared error regularizer preventing the
453   drift from pretrained weights in making our method less sensitive to hyperparameters choice, making
454   it a trustworthy candidate for realistic few-shot adaptation. Additionally, we provided experimental
455
      corroboration on the competitiveness of ProLIP in few-shot classification, domain generalization,
      cross-dataset transfer, and even test-time adaptation, making it a potential general framework for
456
      further applications.
457
458   Limitations. Similarly to prompt learning methods and unlike adapters and linear probing, ProLIP
459   does not operate in a black-box setting. Many recent pretrained models are only available through
460   APIs (e.g., GPT, Claude, Gemini), where practitioners can only get access to the end-point of the
461
      encoders (i.e., the latent embeddings). ProLIP does not apply to such closed models.
462   Future directions. Our framework can be applied to other foundation models with different modal-
463   ities, downstream tasks and training objectives (Li et al., 2022; Zhai et al., 2023). Future research
464   can explore other alternatives for model weights based adaptation, and theoretical investigation on
465   the effect of fine-tuning the last visual projector.
