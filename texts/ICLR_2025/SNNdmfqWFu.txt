009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-view representation learning (MvRL) has garnered substantial attention in
012            recent years, driven by the increasing demand for applications that can effectively
013            process and analyze data from multiple sources. In this context, graph Laplacian-
014            based MvRL methods have demonstrated remarkable success in representing
015            multi-view data. However, these methods often struggle with generalization to new
016            data and face challenges with scalability. Moreover, in many practical scenarios,
017            multi-view data is contaminated by noise or outliers. In such cases, modern deep-
018
               learning-based MvRL approaches that rely on alignment or contrastive objectives
               can lead to misleading results, as they may impose incorrect consistency between
019
               clear and corrupted data sources. We introduce SpecRaGE, a novel fusion-based
020
               framework that integrates the strengths of graph Laplacian methods with the
021            power of deep learning to overcome these challenges. SpecRage uses neural
022            networks to learn parametric mapping that approximates a joint diagonalization of
023            graph Laplacians. This solution bypasses the need for alignment while enabling
024            generalizable and scalable learning of informative and meaningful representations.
025            Moreover, it incorporates a meta-learning fusion module that dynamically adapts
026            to data quality, ensuring robustness against outliers and noisy views. Our extensive
027            experiments demonstrate that SpecRaGE outperforms state-of-the-art methods,
028            particularly in scenarios with data contamination, paving the way for more reliable
029
               and efficient multi-view learning. Our code will be made publicly available upon
               acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Multi-view representation learning (MvRL) has become a crucial paradigm in recent years. Its
035
      primary objective is to integrate data from multiple sources into a unified representation, which can
036   be used for various tasks such as clustering and classification. The demand for MvRL methods has
037   surged as more applications require analyzing objects or phenomena from diverse perspectives. For
038   instance, streaming platforms rely on the fusion of visual, audio, and textual features to enhance
039   content recommendations, while healthcare systems combine genetic data, imaging, and clinical
040   records to provide a comprehensive view of patient health.
041
      Graph Laplacian methods have demonstrated notable effectiveness in representing both single-view
042   and multi-view data (Ng et al., 2001; Belkin & Niyogi, 2003; Coifman & Lafon, 2006a; Cai et al.,
043   2011; Kumar et al., 2011; Eynard et al., 2012; 2015). This success largely stems from the ability of
044   Laplacian eigenvectors to preserve similarity and capture the underlying cluster structure of the data
045   (Ng et al., 2001; Belkin & Niyogi, 2003). Moreover, by focusing on the eigenvectors corresponding
046   to the smallest eigenvalues, one can uncover the intrinsic low-dimensional manifold structure, as
047   demonstrated in Laplacian eigenmaps (Belkin & Niyogi, 2003).
048   However, in the multi-view setting, traditional graph Laplacian-based methods face two major
049   limitations: Generalizability - the ability to map new test points into the representation space after
050   the training set has been processed (i.e., out-of-sample extension); Scalability – the capability to
051   process large datasets within a practical time frame efficiently. Current graph Laplacian-based MvRL
052   approaches often fail to address these two challenges (see discussion in Section 2). These limitations
053   in generalizability and scalability hinder the full potential of graph Laplacians for representing
      multi-view data in real-world applications.

054
      Another fundamental challenge in the multi-view setting is the presence of noise or outliers in some
055   of the views. For example, in autonomous driving systems that integrate data from multiple sensors,
056   adverse weather conditions can lead to unreliable or noisy data from certain sensors. Most modern
057   deep learning-based MvRL approaches (Andrew et al., 2013; Huang et al., 2019; 2021; Federici et al.,
058   2020; Trosten et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022; Trosten et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2023) rely
059   on alignment or contrastive objectives that enforces consistency between view-specific representations.
060   These alignment-based methods can become problematic when handling contaminated data, as they
061   may enforce incorrect consistency between clear and degraded data sources. These challenges
062   highlight the pressing need for robust MvRL methods that can effectively handle real-world data
063
      imperfections while maintaining the benefits of graph Laplacian-based approaches.
064   To address the challenges of Generalizability and Scalability in graph Laplacian-based methods, as
065   well as ensuring Robustness in the presence of contaminated views, we propose SpecRaGE, a novel
066   fusion-based MvRL framework that combines the strengths of graph Laplacians with the power of
067   deep learning. At its core, SpecRaGE provides a deep-learning solution to the joint diagonalization
068   of Laplacians, by extending SpectralNet (Shaham et al., 2018) to multi-view settings. This joint
069
      diagonalization is our key strategy for avoiding the need for alignment between views (see Section
      4.1 for further discussion). SpecRaGE is inherently scalable, as it is trained on mini-batches in
070
      a stochastic manner, allowing it to efficiently process large datasets. Furthermore, the resulting
071
      model provides a parametric map that approximates the leading joint eigenvectors of multi-view
072   graph Laplacians. This parametric map enables efficient application to new data, addressing the
073   generalizability challenge of traditional graph Laplacian methods.
074
075
      Moreover, SpecRaGE incorporates a flexible fusion technique that overcomes the rigid limitations of
      traditional alignment-based methods when dealing with contaminated multi-view data. Specifically,
076
      SpecRaGE introduces a meta-learning-driven fusion mechanism, which generates sample-specific
077
      weight vectors, allowing the model to down-weight anomalous or noisy views dynamically.
078
079   Our extensive experiments demonstrate that SpecRaGE not only achieves state-of-the-art performance
080   on standard multi-view benchmarks but also significantly outperforms existing methods when dealing
081
      with outliers and incomplete views.
082   The main contributions of this work are: (1) We introduce a generalizable and scalable, graph
083   Laplacian-based MvRL framework that extends the power of spectral methods to large-scale multi-
084   view data. (2) We propose a meta-learning fusion module that dynamically weights different views,
085   providing robust performance in the face of data contamination. (3) We present extensive experimental
086   results demonstrating SpecRaGE’s superior performance across various benchmarks, particularly in
087
      scenarios involving outliers and noisy views.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
091
092
093   Graph Laplacian-based Methods. Various graph Laplacian-based methods (also known as multi-
094   view spectral representation learning methods) have been proposed to extract compact and informative
095   representations from multi-view data (Cai et al., 2011; Kumar et al., 2011; Eynard et al., 2012; 2015;
096   Li et al., 2015; Lindenbaum et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023). These approaches typically aim to learn a
097   fused representation based on multiple graph Laplacians, one for each view. While these methods
098   produce meaningful representations, they often face challenges with generalizability (out-of-sample
099
      extension), requiring the recomputation of graph Laplacians to embed new, unseen data into the
      fused representation space. Some approaches address this issue through out-of-sample extension
100
      methods, such as the Nystrom extension (Nyström, 1930) or Geometric Harmonics (Coifman &
101
      Lafon, 2006b). However, these techniques were originally developed for single-view data and
102   provide only local extensions, functioning effectively only near existing training points. Additionally,
103   they are computationally intensive and memory-demanding, as they require calculating distances
104   between each new test point and all training points. Furthermore, many graph Laplacian-based MvRL
105   approaches struggle with scalability due to their high computational demands and limited support
106   for sparse matrices, complicating the storage and processing of large Laplacians. These issues with
107   generalizability and scalability hinder the practical application of graph Laplacians for representing
      multi-view data in real-world settings.

      Deep-learning based Methods. Modern deep-learning-based MvRL methods attempt to design
109   a loss function that is useful for extracting meaningful representations from multi-view data. One
110   category of methods includes deep extensions of the Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) algorithm,
111   such as DCCA, DCCAE, and ℓ0 -DCCA (Hotelling, 1936; Andrew et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2015;
112   Lindenbaum et al., 2021). These methods utilize deep networks to learn a non-linear mapping for two
113   views, maximizing their correlations. Another set of algorithms relies on information-theory-based
114   metrics (Federici et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2022), aiming to maximize the mutual information between
115   views while minimizing redundant information unique to each view. Contrastive learning methods
116   (Trosten et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) represent another group
117
      of deep learning approaches, utilizing a contrastive alignment objective to achieve view-specific
      alignment. Deep learning techniques have also been applied to address the multi-view spectral
118
      clustering problem (Huang et al., 2019; 2021). These methods are closely related to our work, as they
119
      also extend SpectralNet to multi-view settings by incorporating alignment objectives between the
120   spectral embeddings from each view. Despite their promising performance, these approaches rely
121   on some form of alignment between the view-specific representations, making them vulnerable to
122   data contamination. Their underlying assumptions regarding data quality and view consistency may
123   struggle in the presence of noise, outliers, or asymmetric corruption across views, as demonstrated in
124   Section 5.3.

146   3.2   J OINT D IAGONALIZATION
147
148   Definition 1. A set of diagonalizable matrices A(1) , A(2) , . . . , A(v) is said to be simultaneously
149   diagonalizable if there exists a single invertible matrix V such that for all 1 ≤ p ≤ v, V −1 A(p) V is
150   a diagonal matrix.
151
      Intuitively, joint diagonalization (or simultaneous diagonalization) seeks to find a common basis by
152
      which all matrices could be represented in a diagonal form.
153
154   However, exact joint diagonalization can be achieved if and only if A(1) , A(2) , . . . , A(v) commute.
155   Nevertheless, when commutativity cannot be guaranteed, optimizing a joint diagonality criterion
156   and approximating the solution remains possible. This defines the approximate joint diagonalization
157   problem.
158
159   Approximate Joint Diagonalization of Laplacians. In terms of graph Laplacians, for a set of graph
160   Laplacians L(1) , L(2) , . . . , L(v) the objective of joint diagonalization is to find a set of orthogonal
161   eigenvectors V such that for each Laplacian L(p) , the matrix V T L(p) V is diagonal and contains the
      eigenvalues of L(p) on the diagonal. It has been demonstrated in (Eynard et al., 2012; 2015), that

173   Problem Statement. Let X = {X (p) ∈ Rn×dp }vp=1 be a multi-view dataset where n is the number
174
      of samples, v is the number of views, and dp denotes the dimensionality of samples within the p-th
      view. MvRL aims to leverage the multi-view information to learn a high-quality unified representation
175
      that facilitates downstream tasks such as clustering, manifold learning, or classification.
176
177   In this section, we introduce SpecRaGE, our MvRL framework designed to tackle the challenges
178   of generalizability, scalability, and robustness to contaminated views. We begin by outlining the
179   rationale behind our method. Next, we detail how SpecRaGE efficiently learns the approximate joint
180
      eigenvectors, facilitating generalization to new data. Finally, we explore the meta-learning fusion
      module, which enhances robustness in the presence of contaminated views. The overall framework
181
      of our method is illustrated in Fig. 1.

      4.1   R ATIONAL
185
      To develop a method that effectively harnesses the compelling properties of graph Laplacians while
186   enhancing robustness against data contamination, it is essential to first eliminate the need for alignment
187   and then implement a mechanism that diminishes the influence of low-quality views. To achieve
188   this, SpecRaGE utilizes joint diagonalization of Laplacians, which fuses information from all views
189   into a unified representation that approximates the joint eigenvectors. By prioritizing the fusion of
190   information over the alignment of view representations, we successfully circumvent the challenges
191   associated with rigid view alignment. Furthermore, to enhance robustness, SpecRaGE incorporates a
192   dynamic fusion module that adjusts the weighting of views based on their quality. This effectively
193   reduces the influence of noisy or outlier views. This fusion-based approach not only leverages the
194
      advantages of graph Laplacians but also demonstrates resilience to contaminated data. Crucially, we
      approximate these joint eigenvectors in a scalable manner using deep learning, facilitating efficient
195
      processing of large datasets and enabling generalization to new, unseen data.

      5.3        ROBUSTNESS TO C ONTAMINATION E VALUATION
426
427   In real-world scenarios, data contamination is a pervasive challenge, whether in the form of noise
428   or anomalous outliers, often introduced by faulty sensors, human errors, or external environmental
429   factors. These types of contamination can severely degrade the performance of multi-view models,
430   making robustness a critical capability. To evaluate SpecRaGE’s robustness—a key differentiator of
431   our method—we designed two contamination experiments: one targeting robustness to outliers and
      the other to Gaussian noise.


                                                                             8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      In the outlier experiment, global anomalies were randomly injected into one view, following the
433   approach from (Han et al., 2022). For the noise experiment, Gaussian noise with σ = 1.2 was injected
434   into a portion of the samples in one randomly selected view. We conducted both experiments at
435   contamination ratios of 10%, 20%, 30%, and 40%.
436
      These tests were performed on two real-world datasets and a synthetic 2-view version of the scikit-
437
      learn Blobs dataset 2 . We chose to include this simple synthetic 2D data in this experiment, as it
438
      allows us to more clearly distinguish the effect of the noise or outliers on the model’s performance. In
439   these experiments, we report the relative degradation percentage with respect to the uncontaminated
440   baseline, where no contamination is injected.
441
442
      As shown in Fig 3, SpecRaGE consistently outperforms other methods in terms of robustness,
      maintaining stable performance even under high levels of contamination. In both the outlier and noisy
443
      view experiments, SpecRaGE demonstrates significantly smaller relative degradation percentages
444
      across all datasets, even at the highest contamination ratios. This resilience to extreme contamination
445   underscores the method’s effectiveness in handling challenging real-world scenarios, where high
446   levels of noise or outliers are often unavoidable.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this work, we introduced SpecRaGE, a novel fusion-based framework for multi-view spectral
489   representation learning that effectively integrates graph Laplacian methods with deep learning
490   techniques. By efficiently learning a parametric map to uncover joint eigenvectors from diverse graph
491   Laplacians, SpecRaGE addresses the challenges of generalizability and scalability, enabling it to
492   handle large datasets while generalizing to new samples. Moreover, SpecRaGE employs a dynamic
493   fusion technique that enhances robustness against outliers and noise in contaminated multi-view data.
494   Extensive experiments validate that SpecRaGE achieves state-of-the-art performance on standard
495   multi-view benchmarks and significantly outperforms existing methods when faced with outliers and
496   incomplete views. These results highlight SpecRaGE’s potential to transform multi-view learning in
497
      practical applications where data quality is often compromised.

850   Training with Orthogonalization Layer. To train the model with the orthogonalization layer, we
851   adopt a technique similar to that used in SpectralNet (Shaham et al., 2018). This technique employs a
852   coordinate descent training approach comprising two main optimization steps: the "orthogonalization
853   step" and the "gradient step". Each step involves processing a different mini-batch.
854   During the orthogonalization step, we forward a mini-batch through the model and compute the
855   QR decomposition of the fused representation to update the weights of the orthogonalization layer.
856   In the gradient step, we pass another mini-batch through the model and use the orthogonalization
857   weights from the preceding orthogonalization step to orthogonal the output. Following this, we
858   compute the loss and update the network weights via backpropagation, while keeping the weights of
859   the orthogonalization layer unchanged.
860   After training the model, all weights, including those of the orthogonal layer, are fixed. Consistent
861   with observations from SpectralNet, we empirically found that when employing large mini-batches,
862   the orthogonalization layer can also approximately orthogonalize the output of other mini-batches
863   towards the end of training. For instance, in the Blobs dataset, when a random batch of size 1024
      passes through the model with the fixed orthogonalization layer (i.e., after training), the resulting
