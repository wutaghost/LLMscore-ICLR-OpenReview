011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Controlling and understanding the representations of large language models
014            (LLMs) remain central challenges as they become more powerful. In this pa-
015            per, we combine conceptor theory with recent advances in activation steering to
016            develop a novel framework that generalizes both approaches for provably optimal
017            affine steering. Conceptors characterize sets of neural network activations, rep-
018            resentable as ellipsoids, and they act as soft projection matrices, enabling precise
019            and flexible control over LLM activations while offering deeper insights into their
020
               internal representations. Our framework derives optimal affine steering functions
               from first principles, outperforming traditional additive steering methods across
021
               in-context learning tasks. Additionally, we use a Boolean algebra over conceptor
022
               matrices that allows for the composition of multiple steering objectives. Empirical
023            results demonstrate that this approach surpasses existing methods for combining
024            steering vectors. By uniting conceptor theory with activation steering, this work
025            provides not only a more powerful tool for controlling LLM outputs, but also a
026            principled approach for better understanding the internal mechanisms governing
027            model representations and behavior.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large language models (LLMs) have rapidly advanced AI capabilities (Xu & Poo, 2023), but their
034   potential to spread misinformation (Pan et al., 2023), reinforce biases (Gallegos et al., 2024), and de-
035   velop harmful behaviors (Shevlane et al., 2023) highlights the urgent need for methods to understand
036   their internal workings and reliably control their outputs. Various methods, including reinforcement
037   learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2024), supervised fine-tuning (Devlin et al.,
038   2019), and prompt engineering (Liu et al., 2023), have been proposed to steer LLM outputs toward
039
      desired patterns. However, RLHF and fine-tuning are computationally expensive and struggle with
      generalization (Bottou et al., 2018; Amodei et al., 2016), while prompt engineering often produces
040
      inconsistent results (Chen et al., 2023).
041
042   Activation steering (Turner et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Park et al., 2024; Subramani et al., 2022;
043   Singh et al., 2024) has recently been proposed as a new steering method that works by directly mod-
044   ifying the model’s activations at inference time without changing the model’s parameters or relying
045
      on expensive optimization techniques. A steering vector that represents desired behavior can be
      computed directly (Turner et al., 2023; Subramani et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023) or contrastively from
046
      positive and negative examples (Rimsky et al., 2024b). It typically involves caching a set of token
047
      activation vectors from an LLM’s forward pass on prompts that represent desired patterns (directly,
048   e.g. “wedding”, or contrastively, e.g. “love” - “hate”). These vectors are then subtracted or averaged
049   to form a steering vector which can then be added onto a new forward pass to steer the model to-
050   ward the desired pattern. This approach has shown to be effective at capturing and steering toward
051   a wide range of patterns describing things like concepts (e.g., weddings, love) (Turner et al., 2024),
052   functions (e.g., antonyms, synonyms) (Todd et al., 2024), and more complex behaviors (e.g., truth-
053   fulness, power-seeking) (Rimsky et al., 2024a). However, the performance of activation addition is
      not always reliable (Turner et al., 2024; Price et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2024).

054
      This paper introduces a more general framework for steering LLMs using activation engineering.
055   Instead of averaging or subtracting a set of activation vectors to form a steering vector without much
056   theoretical grounding, we derive an optimal linear and affine steering function and connect our
057   results to existing work on conceptors (Jaeger, 2014b). Instead of manipulating the LLM’s activa-
058   tions using vector addition, the activations are (softly) projected using a matrix-vector multiplication
059   with the steering matrix, and optionally translated by an additional steering bias vector. We further
060   present a Boolean algebra on linear steering matrices which allows arbitrary composition of our
061   proposed linear steering functions. We apply our new linear and affine steering mechanisms to a set
062   of tasks that are used in the literature on activation steering. We present results on function vectors
063
      (Todd et al., 2024) in Section 4.1, and show how these functions can be combined using the Boolean
      algebra on conceptors in Section 4.2.

066   1.1   R ELATED WORK
067
068   Early work in activation steering explored the potential for modifying the internal activations of pre-
069   trained language models (LLMs) to control their output at inference time, without requiring further
070   training. Subramani et al. (2022) introduced the concept of “steering vectors”, which, when added to
071
      the hidden states of a language model decoder, could steer the generation towards a target sentence
      with high accuracy. Their method involved optimizing a steering vector specific to each target sen-
072
      tence, achieving near-perfect BLEU scores on various English sentences. They also demonstrate the
073
      use of vector arithmetic for unsupervised style transfer. However, this approach’s reliance on gra-
074   dient descent for each sample limited its practical applicability to larger language models. Turner
075   et al. (2023) proposed ”Activation Addition,” a more efficient method for calculating steering vec-
076   tors by computing the difference in activations between prompt pairs designed to elicit contrasting
077   behaviors. They demonstrated this technique’s effectiveness in steering GPT-2-XL’s output towards
078   desired sentiments, topics, and styles, showcasing the potential for controlling LLMs without ex-
079   tensive computational overhead. Rimsky et al. (2024b) further built on this method to propose
080   “Contrastive Activation Addition” where the steering vector would be formed using a dataset con-
081   taining a large set of contrasting pairs instead of a single pair, as in Activation Addition. Li et al.
082
      (2023) independently developed “Inference-Time Intervention” (ITI), which utilizes linear probes
      to identify specific attention heads associated with truthful statements. By intervening on the acti-
083
      vations of these heads, they were able to increase the model’s truthfulness. Compared to activation
084
      addition, ITI focuses on causal interventions on specific components rather than a broader activation
085   space modification.
086
087   Several follow-up papers have proposed improvements on the general activation addition methods.
088
      Wang et al. (2024) propose a method designed to improve the truthfulness of LLMs by adaptively
      adjusting the intensity of activation steering based on the truthfulness of the generated text. Stick-
089
      land et al. (2024) fine-tune the LLM to minimize the KL-divergence between the model with the
090
      steering vector (as the student model) and the model without steering vector (as the teacher model)
091   in order to mitigate detrimental effects of the steering vector on general model capabilities. Jor-
092   gensen et al. (2023b) focus on improving activation steering in language models by a technique
093   called “mean-centring” for generating steering vectors, which aims to incorporate dataset-specific
094   properties into the steering vectors. Various further papers have explored activation steering for
095   different applications. Wang & Shu (2024) introduce “trojan steering vectors” to compromise the
096   safety of LLMs. Rahn et al. (2024) aim at improving the performance of LLM agents in various tasks
097   by learning a steering vector that encourages the agent to be more explorative. Qian et al. (2024)
098   trace trustworthiness representations during training and find that steering vectors extracted from
099
      earlier pre-training checkpoints can be used to enhance the trustworthiness of models fine-tuned for
      specific tasks. Ghandeharioun et al. (2024) discover that activation steering is effective in bypassing
100
      safety filters. Price et al. (2024) were able to reduce the likelihood of backdoor behavior in LLMs
101
      using contrastive activation addition, but were unable to eliminate the vulnerability completely. Lu
102   & Rimsky (2024) examines bias representations in Llama-2-Chat, and uses activation addition to
103   steer the model’s responses toward or away from stereotypes. Although preliminary results from
104   activation addition are promising, the method’s shortcomings have also been investigated. Tan et al.
105   (2024) show that while steering can work well in the right circumstances, there remain many tech-
106   nical difficulties of applying steering vectors to guide models’ behaviour at scale. Cao et al. (2024)
107   argue that existing methods for extracting steering vectors, which rely on directly calculating ac-
      tivation differences from contrastive prompt pairs, can lead to suboptimal results, particularly in

108
      alignment-related scenarios. They propose optimizing the steering vectors to directly influence the
109   generation probability of contrastive human preference data pairs.
110
111   Most activation addition work has been empirical, but more theoretically grounded approaches to
112
      activation steering are now emerging. Todd et al. (2024) introduce the concept of “function vectors”
      (FVs) in large language models (LLMs), which represent specific input-output mappings within the
113
      model’s activation space. They argue that these FVs are distinct from simple semantic vector offsets
114
      and play a crucial role in the model’s ability to perform in-context learning (ICL). Park et al. (2024)
115   examine the Linear Representation Hypothesis, which posits that meaningful information in LLMs
116   is encoded in linear subspaces within the activation space. The authors argue for the importance
117   of the inner product as a key operation for understanding and manipulating these representations.
118   The paper aims to clarify the theoretical foundations of techniques like activation steering, and high-
119   lights the importance of linearity in understanding how models represent and process information.
120   Singh et al. (2024) present a theoretical framework for understanding affine steering functions and
121   derive two optimal functions under different constraints. Interestingly, under their constraints of
122   “guardedness”, they show that the optimal affine steering mechanism is simple additive steering -
123
      which provides theoretical justification for existing steering approaches. They empirically validate
      the effectiveness of their proposed steering methods in mitigating bias and reducing toxicity.
124
125   It becomes clear from previous work that steering vectors alone are not expressive enough to reli-
126   ably steer model behavior. However, the evidence for the linear subspace representation (Park et al.,
127   2024) suggests that affine or linear methods should be sufficient to intervene on hidden representa-
128   tions with the effect of steering them towards certain behaviors. Furthermore, empirical evidence
129
      from the literature on concept erasure has shown that affine interventions are effective even for deep
      and nonlinear models (Ravfogel et al., 2022; Belrose et al., 2023).

      2.2   A DDITIVE STEERING FUNCTIONS
191
192   Additive steering functions have been the dominant approach to steering model behavior (Turner
193   et al., 2023; Rimsky et al., 2024b; van der Weij et al., 2024).
194   Definition 2 (additive steering function). We define a function fc to be an additive steering function
195   if it is of the form:
196                                          fc (H(s)) = bc + H(s)                                       (6)
197
      where bc ∈ RD is the steering vector that corresponds to concept c.
198
199   Typically, this additive steering vector is chosen to be bc = µc where µc is, as defined above, the
200   concept-conditional representation mean (Turner et al., 2023). In contrastive activation addition, the
201   steering vector is chosen to be bc = µc − µc′ where c is the target concept and c′ is a contrastive
202   concept. Recent work by Singh et al. (2024) has shown that, when guardedness is required (see
203   Section 2.3), the optimal affine steering method for binary concepts simplifies to contrastive additive
204   steering.

      As we do not want to rely on the concept function ϕ to apply our steering function, we instead rely
217   only on the concept-conditional covariance matrix Σc . We now derive the optimal linear steering
218   function that minimally changes the representation. Singh et al. (2024) derive an optimal affine
219   steering function subject to the constraint that it also guards the representations against the concept
220   that is being steered. We find that this is not an essential requirement for our purposes. Instead,
221   we follow the approach by Jaeger (2014b) to define a “conceptor” steering matrix C through an
222   objective function whose first component pushes C to act as a projection matrix for states that
223   exhibit the target concept c, and whose second component adjusts how many leading directions of
224   the covariance matrix Σc should be effective for this projection.
225   Definition 4 (optimal linear steering function). We define the optimal linear steering function to
226   be the function fc (H(s)) = CH(s) where C is the conceptor matrix which solves the following
227   optimization problem:
228                          C(Σ̃c , α) = arg min E ∥Hc − CHc ∥22 + α−2 ∥C∥2F

229                                           C
230   where h is the representation, ∥ · ∥F is the Frobenius norm, and α is the regularization parameter
231   also referred to as the conceptor’s aperture.
232
233   The aperture parameter α balances the trade-off between accurately representing the activation pat-
234   tern and maintaining a generalized representation. This parameter allows us to tune how much of the
235
      concept’s signal variance is captured or filtered by the conceptor. This allows a formal investigation
      into steering more general concepts beyond binary concepts.
236
237   When α is large, the eigenvalues µi approach 1 and C approaches the identity matrix, causing the
238   conceptor to allow for more signal components to pass through the projection of the representations
239   with the conceptor matrix. Conversely, when α is small, the eigenvalues µi approach 0, causing the
240   conceptor to allow for less variability. In the extreme case of α = 0, the conceptor collapses to the
241   zero mapping. This minimization problem uniquely specifies the conceptor C(Σ̃c , α), and can be
242   computed in closed form from Σ̃c and α.

442   5   C ONCLUSION
443
444   The integration of conceptor theory with activation steering provides a new lens through which to
445   understand and manipulate large language models (LLMs). By deriving an optimal affine steering
446   function from first principles, we establish a rigorous foundation for steering, addressing the limita-
447   tions of existing additive methods. Conceptors, represented as ellipsoids, enable more precise con-
448   trol by capturing the full covariance structure of neural activations, which allows them to generalize
449   beyond the simple vector offsets commonly used in additive steering. Moreover, the projection-
450   based steering is inherently adaptive without an additional mechanism such as the one proposed by
451
      Wang et al. (2024), since activations residing within the conceptor’s region would experience mini-
      mal change whereas activations outside of the conceptor’s region experience a more substatial shift.
452
      The ability to project activation vectors softly through conceptors reveals how concepts are encoded
453
      and how they can be influenced without requiring model retraining. This positions conceptor-based
454   steering not only as a tool for output manipulation but also as a method for interrogating and inter-
455   preting model behavior.
456
457
      Additionally, the compositional nature of conceptor operations, implemented through Boolean alge-
      bra, offers a powerful mechanism for multi-task steering. By combining conceptors using operations
458
      like AND and OR, we are able to create composite steering objectives that outperform traditional
459
      methods of combining steering vectors. This demonstrates the versatility of our approach, allowing
460   for more sophisticated control of LLMs, especially in multi-task scenarios where steering objectives
461   may conflict or overlap.
462
463
      In our experiments we show that conceptor-based steering outperformed addition-based methods
      across functions and combined functions. Despite its strengths, conceptor-based steering introduces
464
      additional complexity and computational cost. The need to compute covariance matrices, and the
465
      tuning of hyperparameters like aperture, increases the overhead compared to simpler additive meth-
466   ods. However, these trade-offs are justified by the gains in precision and control, especially in tasks
467   where additive steering has proven insufficient. We mention also that the conceptor matrix can be
468   fused with the attention head weights to not impact model latency.
469
      While our framework demonstrates success across a range of tasks, further exploration is needed
470
      to understand its scalability to larger models and more diverse tasks. Investigating how concep-
471
      tors interact with even more complex behaviors in LLMs, such as multi-turn dialogue or long-term
472   reasoning, could provide further insights into the flexibility of this approach.
473
474   Our work unites conceptor theory and activation steering, offering a robust framework for both con-
475
      trolling and understanding LLMs. By deriving a provably optimal affine steering mechanism and
      introducing composable Boolean operations, we provide a method that not only surpasses tradi-
476
      tional steering approaches but also lays the groundwork for more advanced activation engineering
477
      techniques. While challenges remain, the combination of theoretical rigor and empirical success po-
478   sitions conceptor-based steering as a powerful tool for the future of LLM control and interpretability.

771   (Jaeger, 2017), prevent catastrophic forgetting and enhance continual learning in feedforward net-
772   works (He, 2023), remove bias subspaces in LLMs like BERT and GPT (Yifei et al., 2023), and
773   distill linguistic abstractions into knowledge graphs from contextual embeddings (Kuiper, 2024;
774   Bricman, 2022).

824   Formally, for each function f ∈ F in our set of in-context learning (ICL) tasks, we have compiled a

828
      Now that the ICL prompts have been generated, we need to extract the relevant activations. Todd
829
      et al. (2024) showed that the neural representations of the functions are encoded in the activation
830
      vector of the last token (”:”) of the prompt, right before the transformer would auto-regressively start
831   generating the output token(s). Moreover, the point in the residual stream h at which the functions
832   were most strongly encoded was shown to be at the beginning of layers L = {9, . . . , 16}, right
833   before MHA and FFN (Todd et al., 2024).
834
835   Formally, for each function f ∈ F and each prompt pfi ∈ Pf , the activation vectors hfℓ (pfi ) are
836
      extracted from the residual stream h at each relevant layer l ∈ L from the last token’s (”:”) activation
      vector.

838   For each function f ∈ F and each layer l ∈ L, we now have Np cached activation vectors hfℓ (pfi )
839   aimed to encode the neural representation of f at layer l. Using this, we can generate the layer-
840   specific steering mechanisms for each function as described in Section 3.
841   To test the performance of the generated steering mechanisms, new sets of Nt = 1000 input-output
842   pairs are randomly sampled from the function pairs dataset for each experiment. This is done with
843   replacement for functions where the dataset contains less than Nt pairs. An input prompt pt is
844   formatted as pt = ”x : ”, where x is a tokenized input from an input-output pair. The tokenized
845   output y from the pair is left out from pt as it will be used to test the accuracy of the steering
846   mechanisms. For each experiment, we now have Nt test input prompts pt .
847   To test the accuracy of the steering mechanisms, we apply the layer-specific steering mechanisms
848   on independent forward passes and record their subsequent output. This means that for our exper-
849   imental configuration, across the functions f ∈ F , the 5 experiments, the 4 steering mechanisms
850   (excluding the baseline), the Nt number of test prompts, and the number of layers l ∈ L, there will
851   be 6 × 5 × 4 × 1000 × 8 = 960, 000 forward passes, each with a steering intervention.
852   Each steering intervention will consist of a layer-specific steering mechanism modifying the residual
853   stream h at the mechanisms’ respective layer l. This modification can be defined as transforming the
854   unmodified residual stream activation vector hℓ into the steered activation vector h′ℓ . The steering
855   mechanisms’ modification are described in Section 3.
856
      After the respective modifications have been made to the residual stream, the forward passes will
857
      continue as usual. At the end of each forward pass, the final logits are converted into probabilities
858   using a softmax, and the token with the highest probability is selected. This means that at the end of
859   one experiment, we have Nt single-token outputs for each layer-specific steering mechanism. These
860   tokens can now be compared with the first token of output y that corresponds with the input x of the
861   initial prompt pt . Based on how many of the Nt outputs were correctly identified, a top-1 accuracy
862   is calculated for each layer-specific steering mechanism. This experiment is repeated 5 times for
863   each function f ∈ F to account for variability caused by the random sampling for the generation of
      the steering mechanisms and test sets.

874   A.4     A FFINE CONCEPTORS AND MEAN - CENTERED VECTORS FOR FUNCTION STEERING
875
876
      An important improvement for additive steering is a technique called mean-centering, put forward
      by Jorgensen et al. (2023a). This method enhances the effectiveness of steering vectors by reducing
877
      the inherent bias present in the activation space of LLMs. Activation vectors in LLMs tend to be
878
      anisotropic, meaning that they are not evenly distributed around the origin, but are instead offset in a
879   consistent direction. This can negatively impact the steering vector’s performance as the bias vector
880   b representing this offset, does not encode any specific task-related information, diluting the steering
881   vector’s effectiveness.
882
      First, the steering vector h̄fℓ for a specific function f is computed by averaging the activations at
883
884   layer ℓ on a set of ICL prompts demonstrating the input-output function Pf . h̄fℓ now encodes the
885
      task-specific behavior but may still be affected by biases in the model’s overall activation space.
      Mean-centering attempts to mitigate this by subtracting the mean activation of a broader dataset that
886
      represents the general activation space of the model. This is done by computing the mean activation
887
      vector µtrain over a large, representative set of prompts Dtrain from the model’s training data.
888
889   The mean activation vector µtrain was calculated using the same procedure described by Jorgensen
890   et al. (2023a): A subset from the dataset used to train GPT-2 was compiled Gokaslan et al. (2019).
891
      The subset was constructed by storing all entries from the folders urlsf subset01-1/data
      and urlsf subset01-182/data. After this, only entries that contained less than 500 tokens
892
      (using the GPT-2 Tokenizer) were retained. This resulted in 210 entries from which the final 10 were
893
      removed, leaving a dataset of 200 entries. The mean activation vector µtrain was then computed by
894   averaging the activations over this dataset.
895
896   Implementing the mean-centering performance enhancement for steering toward the execution of
897
      functions can be done as follows:
898

                            h̄f,mc
                              ℓ    = h̄fℓ − µtrain   with µtrain =                          hℓ (d)       (39)
900                                                                  |Dtrain |
                                                                                 d∈Dtrain
901
902
903
      where h̄fℓ is the activation vector at layer ℓ, and Dtrain is the dataset for which the mean-centered
      vector µtrain is computed. This refinement leads to a steering vector that can more effectively guide
904
      the model toward the specific task and has been shown to have a positive impact on the overall
905
      steering effectiveness (Jorgensen et al., 2023a).
906
907   The analogous operation of mean centering for conceptor-based steering is given by the application
908   of affine conceptors, as derived in Section 2.4.
