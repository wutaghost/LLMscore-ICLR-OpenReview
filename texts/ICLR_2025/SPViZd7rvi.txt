011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            A wide range of graph embedding objectives decompose into two components:
014            one that attracts the embeddings of nodes that are perceived as similar, and another
015
               that repels embeddings of nodes that are perceived as dissimilar. Without repul-
               sion, the embeddings would collapse into trivial solutions. Skip-Gram Negative
016
               Sampling (SGNS) is a popular and efficient repulsion approach that prevents col-
017
               lapse by repelling each node from a sample of dissimilar nodes. In this work, we
018            show that when repulsion is most needed and the embeddings approach collapse,
019            SGNS node-wise repulsion is, in the aggregate, an approximate re-centering of the
020            node embedding dimensions. Such dimension operations are much more scalable
021            than node operations and yield a simpler geometric interpretation of the repulsion.
022            Our result extends findings from self-supervised learning to the skip-gram model,
023            establishing a connection between skip-gram node contrast and dimension regu-
024            larization. We use this observation to propose a flexible algorithm augmentation
025            framework that improves the scalability of any existing algorithm using SGNS.
026
               The framework prioritizes node attraction and replaces SGNS with dimension
               regularization. We instantiate this generic framework for LINE and node2vec and
027
               show that the augmented algorithms preserve downstream link-prediction perfor-
028
               mance while reducing GPU memory usage by up to 33.3% and training time by
029            22.1%. Further, for graphs that are globally sparse but locally dense, we show
030            that removing repulsion altogether can improve performance, but, when repulsion
031            is otherwise needed, dimension regularization provides an effective and efficient
032            alternative to SGNS.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Graph embedding algorithms use the structure of graphs to learn node-level embeddings. Across
038   unsupervised and supervised graph embedding algorithms, their loss functions serve the two roles of
039
      preserving similarity and dissimilarity. Nodes that are similar in the input graph should have similar
      embeddings, while dissimilar nodes should have dissimilar embeddings (Böhm et al., 2022). The
040
      push and pull of the similarity and dissimilarity objectives are key: in the absence of a dissimilarity
041
      objective, the loss would be minimized by embedding all nodes at a single embedding point, a
042   degenerate and useless embedding. Often, enforcing dissimilarity is much more expensive than
043   similarity, owing to the generally sparse nature of graphs and the number of pairs of dissimilar nodes
044   growing quadratically with the size of the graph. Enforcing dissimilarity is also complex for graphs
045   because graph data frequently have missing edges or noise (Young et al., 2021; Newman, 2018). In
046   this paper, we show that while many past works have focused on repelling pairs of dissimilar nodes,
047   the repulsion can be replaced with a regularization of the embedding dimensions, which is more
048   scalable.
049   The skip-gram (SG) model is one of the most popular approaches to graph embeddings (Ahmed
050   et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2024) and can be decomposed into preserving similarity and dissimilar-
051   ity. Further, skip-gram negative sampling (SGNS) is a dominant method to efficiently approximate
052   dissimilarity preservation. Instead of repelling all pairs of dissimilar nodes, SGNS repels only a
053   sample of dissimilar nodes per pair of similar nodes. SGNS is utilized in LINE Tang et al. (2015)
      and node2vec Grover & Leskovec (2016), for instance, and has been shown to yield strong down-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      stream performance. However, several analytical issues with SGNS have also been identified. First,
055   SGNS introduces a bias by re-scaling the relative importance of preserving similarity and dissimi-
056   larity (Rudolph et al., 2016). Second, with SGNS, in the limit as the number of nodes in the graph
057   approaches infinity, the similarities among embeddings diverge from the similarities among nodes
058   in the graph (Davison & Austern, 2023). Although SGNS has been used to learn both graph and
059   word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013; Mimno & Thompson, 2017), we focus on the graph con-
060   text because, for graph embeddings in particular, SGNS remains a popular method for preserving
061   dissimilarity (Chami et al., 2022).
062   In this paper, we propose a change in perspective and show that node repulsion in the SG model can
063   be achieved via dimension centering. If X is an embedding matrix where the rows are node embed-
064   dings, “dimensions” refers to the columns of X. We draw inspiration from recent advances in the
065   self-supervised learning (SSL) literature, which show an equivalence between sample-contrastive
066   learning and dimension-contrastive learning (Garrido et al., 2022; Bardes et al., 2022). Sample-
067   contrastive learning explicitly repels dissimilar pairs while dimension-contrastive learning repels
068   the dimensions from each other.
069   The known parallels between sample and dimension contrast, however, do not suggest whether SG
070   loss functions can be also re-interpreted from the dimension perspective. In this paper, we be-
071   gin by characterizing the degenerate embedding behavior when the dissimilarity term is removed
072   altogether. We prove that, under mild initialization conditions, when only positive pairs are con-
073   sidered, the embeddings collapse into a lower dimensional space, which also commonly occurs in
074   self-supervised learning (Jing et al., 2022). However, as the dimensions approach collapse, the dis-
075
      similarity term also approaches a dimension-mean regularizer. Our findings show that while the
      dissimilarity term in the SG loss is not itself a dimension regularizer when the term is most needed
076
      to counteract the similarity attraction, dissimilarity preservation can be achieved via regularization.
077
078   We operationalize the dimension-based approach with an algorithm augmentation. We augment
079   existing algorithms using SGNS by making two modifications. First, the augmentation prioritizes
080   similarity preservation over dissimilarity preservation. This is desirable because, in real-world graph
081
      data, the lack of similarity between two nodes does not necessarily suggest the two nodes are dissim-
      ilar; it is also possible that data are missing or noisy (Newman, 2018; Young et al., 2021). Second,
082
      when the embeddings begin to collapse after optimizing only for similarity preservation for a fixed
083
      number of epochs, our augmentation repels nodes from each other using a regularizer that induces
084   embedding dimensions centered on the origin.
085
086   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
087
            1. In Section 2, we introduce a framework that maps node repulsion to dimension regulariza-
088
               tion. We show that instead of shortcutting the full skip-gram loss function with SGNS and
089
               repelling a sample of pairs, the repulsion function can be approximated with a dimension-
090            mean regularization. We prove that as the need for node repulsion grows, optimizing the
091            regularizer converges to optimizing the skip-gram loss. This framework extends the equiv-
092            alence between sample-contrastive objectives and dimension regularization established in
093            self-supervised learning.
094
            2. In Section 3 we introduce a generic algorithm augmentation that prioritizes node attraction
095            and replaces SGNS with occasional dimension regularization for any existing SG algo-
096            rithm. We instantiate the augmentation for node2vec and LINE, reducing the repulsion
097            complexity from O(n) to O(d) per epoch.
098
            3. In Section 4 we show that our augmentation reduces runtime and memory usage while
099
               also preserving link-prediction performance. Moreover, in sparse networks with high local
100
               density, removing repulsion altogether, a special case of our framework, even improves
101            performance; however, more generally, in networks with low local density, repulsion is
102            needed and dimension regularization provides an efficient solution.

297   node i from all other n − 1 nodes, SGNS repels i from a sample of k nodes where the nodes are
298   sampled according to a distribution Pα over all nodes, optimizing the following objective:
                                NSGN S (X, S, i) = −kEj ′ ∼Pα log σ −XiT Xj ′ ,

302   In aggregate, SGNS reduces the repulsion time complexity from O n2 to O (kbn) vector additions
303   per epoch. b is the average number of positive pairs per node; for LINE, b = m/n, and for node2vec,

305   time complexity to O(d) vector additions per epoch.
306   As mentioned, SGNS embeddings can be tuned by the choice of the non-uniform sampling dis-
307   tribution, where in graph embedding contexts the distribution Pα is typically sampling nodes pro-

309   degree-based non-uniform distribution prioritizes learning the embeddings of high-degree nodes, but
310   we emphasize that the specific choice of α = 3/4 is typically motivated directly based on improved
311   empirical performance in downstream tasks.

       5               R ELATED W ORKS
475
       In this section, we review the popular use of SGNS within graph embeddings as well as its limi-
476
       tations. Our approach is also similar in spirit to the growing body of literature on non-contrastive
477
       learning.
478
479
       5.1             S KIP -G RAM N EGATIVE S AMPLING
480
481    SGNS was introduced in word2vec by Mikolov et al. (2013) as an efficient method for learning
482    word embeddings. While the softmax normalization constant is costly to optimize, Mikolov et al.
483    (2013) modeled SGNS after Noise Contrastive Estimation (NCE) which learns to separate positive
484    samples from samples drawn from a noise distribution. SGNS has since been adopted for graph
485    representation learning where it is utilized in both unsupervised (Grover & Leskovec, 2016; Tang
       et al., 2015; Perozzi et al., 2014) and supervised skip-gram models (Yang et al., 2016).


                                                                                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      At the same time, there are many known limitations of SGNS. Rudolph et al. (2016) place SGNS
487   embeddings within the framework of Exponential Family Embeddings and note that SGNS down-
488   weights the magnitude of the negative update and leads to biased embeddings, relative the gradients
489   of the non-sampled objective. Second, Davison & Austern (2023) examine the limiting distribution
490   of embeddings learned via SGNS and show that the distribution decouples from the true sampling
491   distribution in the limit. Last, it has also been shown that the optimal noise distribution and the
492   corresponding parameters can vary by dataset (Yang et al., 2020).
493   We would also like to note that while the motivations are similar, SGNS differs from the negative
494   sampling that has arisen in the self-supervised learning literature (Robinson et al., 2021). In self-
495   supervised learning, the negative samples are generally other nodes in the training batch.
496
497   5.2   N ON - CONTRASTIVE S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING
498
499   Energy Based Models in self-supervised learning are a unified framework for balancing similarity
500   and dissimilarity (LeCun et al., 2007). As in our decomposition, energy-based models ensure that
501   similar pairs have low energy and dissimilar pairs have high energy. Within energy-based models,
502   there has been more focus across both vision and graph representation learning on contrastive mod-
503
      els, which explicitly repel dissimilar pairs (In et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Zhang
      et al., 2023). However, given the computational complexities of pairwise contrast, there is a growing
504
      body of work on non-contrastive representation learning methods, which do not use negative sam-
505
      ples. Embedding collapse is the main challenge facing non-contrastive methods, and several miti-
506   gation strategies have emerged such as asymmetric encoders in SimSiam (Chen & He, 2021), mo-
507   mentum encoders in BYOL (Grill et al., 2020), and redundancy reduction in Barlow Twins (Zbontar
508   et al., 2021). Garrido et al. (2022) establishes a duality between dimension-regularization based non-
509   contrastive approaches and standard contrastive learning; however, the work specifically analyzes
510   the squared-loss term and there are no existing works, to our knowledge, establishing a connection
511   between skip-gram loss and non-contrastive methods.
512
513   6     CONCLUSION
514
515   In this work, we provide a new perspective on dissimilarity preservation in graph representation
516   learning and show that dissimilarity preservation can be achieved via dimension regularization.
517   Our main theoretical finding shows that when node repulsion is most needed and embedding dot
518   products are all increasing, the difference between the original skip-gram dissimilarity loss and the
519   dimension-mean regularizer vanishes. Combined with the efficiency of dimension operations over
520   node repulsions, dimension regularization bypasses the need for SGNS. We then introduce a generic
521   algorithm augmentation that prioritizes positive updates, given that real-world graph data often con-
522   tain missing edges Young et al. (2021). When node repulsion is needed and collapse approaches,
523
      the augmentation utilizes dimension regularization instead of SGNS. Our experimental results show
      that the augmented versions of LINE and node2vec preserve the link-prediction performance of the
524
      original algorithms while reducing runtime by over 30% for OGB benchmark datasets. In fact, for
525
      several real-world graphs, removing dissimilarity preservation altogether performs well; however,
526   more generally, for graphs with low local density repulsion is needed, and dimension regularization
527   is an efficient and effective approach.

      Data Splitting. We split each dataset into training/validation/test edge splits. For the OGB
867   datasets, we utilize the splits provided by the original dataset, and for the Planetoid datasets we

870   Hyperparameter Optimization. We perform the following hyperparamter optimization routine:
871
872         1. Select optimal parameters. For the vanilla algorithms, we optimize over the learning
873            rate, and for the augmented algorithms we optimize over the learning rate, nnegative , and λ.
874            In order to search over a larger space of parameters, we only train each configuration for
875            4 epochs and select the configuration that yields the highest AUC-ROC on the validation
876
               edge set. The optimal hyperparameters are listed in Table 4
877
878         2. Select optimal number of epochs. For each optimal hyperparameter configuration, we
879            then determine the number of training epochs by training for the number of epochs listed in
880
               Table 4. We then select the epoch number at which the validation AUC-ROC is maximized.
881
882         3. Evaluate. For each graph and model variant, we train a model using the optimal hyperpa-
883            rameters and number epochs and evaluate on the test edge set.
