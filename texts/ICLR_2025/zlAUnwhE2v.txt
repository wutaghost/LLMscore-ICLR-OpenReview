011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Molecular property prediction is vital in drug discovery and cheminformatics,
014            yet many current models lack interpretability, making it difficult for experts to
015            understand the rationale behind predictions. To address this, we introduce Chem-
016
               Thinker, a novel large language models (LLMs) multi-agent framework designed
               to effectively control the internal representations of concepts and functions within
017
               LLMs. ChemThinker emulates the way chemists approach molecular analysis
018
               by integrating insights from three perspectives: general molecular properties,
019            data-driven analysis, and task-specific factors. Each perspective uses an agentic
020            approach to stimulate the LLM’s internal representations, enabling more targeted
021            and interpretable outputs based on the problem at hand, akin to how stimuli trig-
022            ger the brain’s cognitive processes. By feeding representations from these three
023            perspectives into a simple multi-layer perceptron (MLP), ChemThinker achieves
024            superior performance, significantly outperforming existing baselines across multi-
025            ple benchmarks. Furthermore, our framework provides interpretable insights into
026            the molecular mechanisms driving the predictions, making it a practical tool for
027
               drug discovery and other cheminformatics applications.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031            “If I have seen further it is by standing on the shoulders of giants.”
032
033                                                                                            – Isaac Newton
034
      Molecular property prediction plays a crucial role in cheminformatics (Yang et al., 2019) and drug
035
      discovery (Drews, 2000; Zhang et al., 2021), enabling experts to estimate essential characteristics
036   such as blood-brain barrier permeability, solubility, and toxicity (Wu et al., 2023). While numerous
037   works (Hu et al., 2019; You et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022; Stärk et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023) exist
038   for this task, they often fall short in providing interpretable insights into the underlying molecular
039   mechanisms. In real-world applications, simply delivering a prediction is rarely sufficient. Domain
040   experts require a deeper understanding of the rationale behind the predictions— the “why”—to make
041   informed decisions. Unfortunately, this critical aspect is often overlooked.
042   Moreover, current approaches (Xia et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2024; Rollins et al., 2024)
043   primarily focus on identifying statistical patterns via extensive model training to predict molecular
044   properties. However, this often overlooks the incorporation of established scientific knowledge,
045   limiting the model’s ability to be guided toward more informed and contextually relevant solutions.
046   Rather than “learning from scratch,” leveraging well-established scientific knowledge and frameworks
047   offers a more reasonable and effective approach to tackling domain-specific problems like molecular
048   property prediction. In other words, by “standing on the shoulders of giants,” models can be directed
049   towards more accurate and interpretable predictions, enhancing their practical utility.
050   Recently, large language model (LLM) agents have gained significant attention (Taylor et al., 2022;
051   Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI et al., 2023). With extensive training on vast amounts of data, these
052   models have developed emergent abilities (Wei et al., 2022), enabling them to perform complex
053   tasks such as role-playing and reasoning, at levels akin to human experts (Chen et al., 2024), such as
      chemists (Wang et al., 2024). In some cases, LLM agents can match or even surpass human expert

054
      performance across a wide range of tasks, including medical question answering (Singhal et al.,
055   2023) and chemical reasoning task (Mirza et al., 2024). Remarkably, Yao et al. (2022) demonstrate
056   that integrating reasoning traces with task-specific actions can enhance model interpretability and
057   performance, and the collaborative LLM agents can even further enhance the performance on intricate
058   tasks (Hong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024).
059
      In this paper, we propose ChemThinker, a Multi-Agent LLM framework designed to extract deep
060
      molecular insights from three distinct perspectives: General Molecular Thinking, Intuition-Driven
061   Thinking, and Task-Specific Thinking. Here analysis of each perspective is taken charge of a
062   specific LLM agent. The thought components extracted from three perspectives are then fused
063   as representations, which can be utilized in a simple multi-layer perceptron (MLP) classifier for
064   downstream tasks. This three-perspective analysis framework mirrors how chemists approach real-
065   world molecular problems. Chemists typically begin with a general molecular analysis, followed by
066   applying intuition from past experience and data analysis of similar molecules to infer properties.
067   Finally, they focus on the specific task, adapting their approach to meet the unique requirements
068   of the problem or prediction. Similarly, ChemThinker follows this process to generate molecular
069
      insights, providing a detailed text report that enhances the framework’s interpretability and deepens
      the understanding of its predictions.
070
071   We demonstrate ChemThinker’s performance across eight diverse molecular property prediction tasks,
072   achieving state-of-the-art results on most tasks while also offering deeper molecular insights. Addi-
073   tionally, ChemThinker exhibits adaptability and flexibility by leveraging the varying contributions of
074   each thought component as molecular representation to address different tasks. Our contributions are
075
      summarized as follows: (1) We propose an LLM-based Multi-Agent framework, ChemThinker, which
      emulates the thought process of chemists. This framework defines a new paradigm for molecular
076
      representation learning to improve molecular property prediction. (2) ChemThinker demonstrates
077
      flexibility by dynamically adjusting the contributions of each thought component, depending on the
078   task and backbone model, leading to improved prediction accuracy and generalization across multiple
079   datasets. (3) Through comprehensive experiments on both open- and closed-source LLMs, Chem-
080   Thinker achieves state-of-the-art results in various molecular property prediction tasks, showcasing
081   the power of leveraging pretrained LLMs for scientific applications. (4) In addition to providing
082   accurate predictions, ChemThinker offers deeper molecular insights, aligning with the reasoning
083   process used by chemists, thus improving the transparency and interpretability of the predictions.

086   2    R ELTAED W ORK
087
088   The accurate prediction of molecular properties is a cornerstone of cheminformatics (Yang et al.,
089   2019) and bioinformatics (Zhang et al., 2021), relying heavily on the computational representation of
090   molecular structures. Several representation methods have been developed, including molecular graph,
091   Extended-Connectivity Fingerprints (ECFP) (Rogers & Hahn, 2010), and string line annotations
092   such as Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System (SMILES). With the advancement of artificial
093
      intelligence, machine learning models have been extensively utilized for property prediction through
      two main approaches: traditional models and deep learning models. In the conventional approach,
094
      computed or handcrafted molecular fingerprints are fed into traditional machine learning models,
095
      such as random forests (Breiman, 2001), effectively capturing the relationship between molecular
096   substructures and their associated properties (Axen et al., 2017; Jeon & Kim, 2019). However,
097   they normally rely on predefined fingerprints and may not fully capture the complex patterns and
098   interactions within molecular structures.
099
      On the other hand, standard deep learning models aim to extract expressive representations of
100
      molecules in a data-driven manner. Recent works (Hu et al., 2019; You et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022;
101
      Xia et al., 2022) leverage Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to directly learn from molecular graphs.
102   By learning hierarchical representations that capture both local and global structural information, they
103   can uncover complex patterns and relationships within molecules. Additionally, other studies (Zhou
104   et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Rollins et al., 2024) also combine a sequence-based
105   model with other backbone architectures to capture both contextual relationships from sequential
106   representations such as SMILES and structural features from GNNs. Nevertheless, these data-driven
107   approaches are often limited by the quality and size of available datasets, as they typically require
      training from scratch. While these standard deep learning models excel at capturing complex patterns,

      they often struggle to provide interpretable insights into the underlying molecular mechanisms,

      making it challenging to discern how underlying features contribute to the final predictions.
137
138   With the rapid advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs), remarkable success has been
139   achieved across a wide array of tasks. Mirza et al. (2024) systematically analyze the chemical
140
      reasoning capabilities of LLMs, their findings indicate that even a LLM with 7B parameters can
      achieve an average human score, while more advanced models like GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2023) can
141
      surpass the highest human scores in chemical reasoning question answering. Furthermore, despite
142
      the growing adoption of LLMs in cheminformatics (Zhong et al., 2024; Jablonka et al., 2023; 2024;
143   Liao et al., 2024), the potential of LLM-based Multi-Agent systems for molecular property prediction
144   remains largely unexplored.
145
146
      Therefore, to address these limitations, we propose a novel LLM-based Multi-Agent framework,
      ChemThinker, that leverages the collective intelligence, specialized profiles, and diverse skills of
147
      multiple agents. By standing on the shoulders of giants, our framework utilizes extensive pre-trained
148
      knowledge, human-like language generation, and reasoning abilities of LLMs. ChemThinker achieves
149   more accurate molecular property predictions while generating detailed reports that experts could use
150   for real-world analysis and decision-making.

153   3   C HEM T HINKER : T HINKING L IKE A C HEMIST
154
155   In this section, we introduce ChemThinker, a novel multi-agent framework developed to extract deep
156   molecular insights. As shown in Figure 1, ChemThinker leverages the collaborative capabilities
157   of three agents from General Molecular Thinking, Intuition-Driven Thinking, and Task-Specific
158
      Thinking. For any given chemoinformatic task, ChemThinker harnesses its collective intelligence to
      generate a comprehensive report that provides deep molecular insights, along with fused representa-
159
      tions to ensure precise downstream task predictions.
160

      (SMILES) (Weininger, 1988) to represent molecular structures, where Si is a SMILES string and

167   3.1   G ENERAL M OLECULAR T HINKING
168
169   In the general molecular thinking, AGen is dedicated to examining and interpreting the overall
170   architecture of molecular data, going beyond what traditional computational chemistry tools can
171   achieve. While traditional tools excel at calculating explicit molecular properties such as molecular
172   weight, ring counts, and basic geometries, they often fall short in extracting more nuanced insights
173   that require a deeper contextual understanding. This is where the AGen agent steps in, leveraging the
174
      advanced reasoning capabilities of LLMs to analyze complex molecular structures.
175   Based on a large amount pre-training corpora, AGen excels in identifying subtle structural features,
176   exploring relationships between functional groups, and understanding the broader implications of
177   molecular configurations that are not readily accessible through conventional rule-based methods. In
178   this thinking component, the AGen initially identifies the key aspects of the molecular structure that
179   would be most relevant to a chemist’s real-world analysis. It then generates responses to the question
180
      qi for each aspect based on the input Si , then proceeds to the reflection module to evaluate and correct
      its responses. The correct answer will stroed in the memory module. The questions qi 2 QGen as

      follows:
182
183   Question 1 (q1 ): How does the molecule’s 3D shape change, and what are the effects of these
184   changes?
185   Question 2 (q2 ): What are the key intermolecular forces that govern the behavior of the molecule?
186
187
      Question 3 (q3 ): How does the molecule contribute to the overall chemical equilibrium?
188   For each question qi , the agent AGen generates corresponding molecular insights by analyzing the
189   input Si . The overall output of AGen , denoted as IGen , is defined as:

198   3.2   I NTUITION - DRIVEN T HINKING
199
200   To mimic how chemists use intuition built from domain knowledge and past experience, we introduce
201   the intuition-driven thinking agent, AInt . This agent is responsible for generating, refining, and
202
      applying heuristic rules that combine insights from literature with empirical observations from
      experience pools, i.e., related datasets to identify useful knowledge for a given task T . This is
203
      inspired by Zheng et al. (2023), which has shown that LLM is capable of extracting meaningful
204
      prior knowledge directly from literature and molecular patterns in the training data. By integrating
205   computational tools and interacting with a coding agent, the AInt derives vectorized representations
206   that are well-suited for further analysis and predictive modeling. The workflow in this thinking
207   component consists of the following steps:
208
209   Rule Generation from Literature Review: LLM has built-in knowledge and an understanding
210   of various tasks from its pre-training. To make use of this, we assign the LLM a specific persona,
211   turning it into an agent that uses this persona to guide its behavior and thinking process. Based on
212   this persona, the agent formulates a preliminary set of literature rules Rlit relevant to the given task T .
213   These rules are derived from the agent’s internal understanding and pre-trained knowledge base.
214
215   Rule Observation from Empirical Data: Several randomly selected subsets of SMILES strings in

228   3.3   TASK S PECIFIC T HINKING
229
230   The Task-Specific Thinking component utilizes ATask to directly answer specialized questions related
231   to a given task T . Given the input Si , the specific task T , and the molecular insights IGen and IInt
232   stored in the Memory Module, ATask synthesizes this information to generate task-specific insights.
233   These insights are tailored to the specific requirements of T , leveraging the general and intuitive
234   knowledge captured by the previous components. The resulting task-specific representation, denoted
235
      as RepTask , will be used to enhance the prediction accuracy for the downstream task. Furthermore,
      the molecular insights generated by this task-specific thinking component form the final report,
236
      which consolidates all molecular insights. This report can assist chemists for further analysis and
237
      decision-making. For reference, we also provide an example of a molecular insight report in the
238   Appendix A.3.

      3.4   T HOUGHT R EPRESENTATION F USION
241
242   This representation fusion module integrates the diverse representations of Si generated from the
243   three distinct modules: General Molecular Thinking, Intuition-Driven Thinking, and Task-Specific
244   Thinking. The aim is to create a unified, context-aware representation of the molecular structure that
245   captures the unique insights derived from each cognitive perspective, dynamically adapting to each
246   input.
247   Given that current LLM architectures are based on next-token prediction, the observations produced
248   in response to a question are inherently influenced by the question’s context. For the General
249   Molecular Thinking and Task-Specific Thinking module, the question serves as a guide to generate a
250   representation for SMILES Si . This representation acts as the thought component representation,
251   encapsulating the LLM’s thinking process for the molecule under each specified context.
252   Specifically, in the General Molecular Thinking, three different questions (qGen1 , qGen2 , qGen3 ) 2 QGen
253   are posed to the LLM to assess various aspects of the molecular structure. Each question generates a
254   separate embedding, which are then concatenate into a single, aggregated representation for general
255   component representation:
256
257
                 RepGen (Si ) = RepGen (Si , qGen1 )        RepGen (Si , qGen2 )      RepGen (Si , qGen3 )   (2)
258
259   In the Intuition-Driven Thinking, the representation is constructed using rule-based features derived
260   from the empirical analysis of the molecule. Each of these rule values provides a unique view of the
261   SMILES representation from a specific aspect. Specifically, for each SMILES string Si 2 S, each
262   rule rj 2 R is used to compute a feature value fij (Si ), forming the feature representation RepInt for
263   Si :
264
265                               RepInt (Si ) = {fi1 (Si ), fi2 (Si ), . . . , fin (Si )}                   (3)
266
      where fij (Si ) represents the feature value for Si based on rule rj , and n is the total number of rules
267
      in R.
268
269   To generate the final unified representation, a dynamic weight vector wi = (wGen,i , wInt,i , wTask,i ) is
      calculated for each SMILES input Si , reflecting the relative importance of each thinking component

271   individual embeddings:

275   where RepTask (Si ) = RepTask (Si , qTask ), and the dynamic weights wGen,i , wInt,i , and wTask,i are spe-
276   cific to each SMILES Si and are learned during the training process. These weights adaptively adjust

278   Repfusion (Si ) captures the most relevant information from each thinking mode.

      The fused embedding Repfusion (Si ) is then passed through a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) to
280   generate the final prediction:

287   a flexible and context-aware integration of insights from different cognitive modules, dynamically
288   adapting to the unique characteristics of each SMILES input.

292   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
293
294   Datasets. Our framework is evaluated on 8 datasets across three chemoinformatics domains,
295   covering 34 subtasks sourced from MoleculeNet (Wu et al., 2018). In the physiology domain, we
296
      address 29 tasks, including the blood-brain barrier permeability predictor (BBBP) (Martins et al.,
      2012), the clinical toxicity evaluator (ClinTox) (Gayvert et al., 2016), and 27 tasks from SIDER (Kuhn
297
      et al., 2016) focusing on predicting adverse drug reactions. In the biophysics domain, we evaluate
298
      2 classification tasks from BACE (Subramanian et al., 2016) and HIV (Wu et al., 2018), while the
299   physical chemistry domain covers 3 regression tasks from ESOL (Delaney, 2004), FreeSolv (Mobley
300   & Guthrie, 2014), and Lipophilicity (Wu et al., 2018). To ensure fair comparisons across baselines,
301   we employed scaffold splitting, following the MoleculeNet-recommended data split (Wu et al., 2018),
302   which assigns molecules with different structural scaffolds to separate training, validation, and test
303   sets (Bemis & Murcko, 1996).
304
305
      Baselines. For the traditional model, we use Random Forest (Breiman, 2001) with ECFP4 (Rogers
      & Hahn, 2010) as the input feature set. For standard deep learning models, we selected the most
306
      representative Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) pretraining baselines, including Attribute Masking
307
      (AttrMask) (Hu et al., 2019), GraphCL (You et al., 2020), MolCLR (Wang et al., 2022), and
308   MoleBERT (Xia et al., 2022). Additionally, we also compared with models like GraphMVP (Liu
309   et al., 2022), 3D-InfoMax (Stärk et al., 2022), and Uni-Mol (Zhou et al., 2023), which leverage
310   3D molecular conformers during training to enhance their ability to reason in 3D space, while
311   using 2D molecular graphs as input during inference. In addition, We also compare our framework
312   with LLM4SD (Zheng et al., 2023) framework, which uses LLM generated rules as vectors and
313   then utilizes a Random Forest for prediction. To ensure a fair comparison, we reran all baseline
314   models using the same data-splitting method and the same random seed for multiple iterations.
315   This consistency across runs ensures that any differences in performance are attributable to model
316
      variations rather than random factors.
317
      Backbone Model. Our framework is built on an LLM-based Multi-Agent architecture, utilizing
318   state-of-the-art LLMs as its backbone. Specifically, we utilize Galactica models (Taylor et al., 2022),
319   with 6.7B and 30B parameters, which are trained on an extensive corpus of scientific knowledge.
320   Additionally, we incorporate LLama-3.1 models (Dubey et al., 2024), including the 8B parameter
321   version and its instruction-tuned variant (8B-instruct), both pretrained on a diverse dataset up to the
322   end of 2023. For the closed-source model, we employ OpenAI’s text embedding models (OpenAI,
323   2024) in both small and large configurations, alongside GPT-4o (OpenAI et al., 2023) for agent
      reasoning.

494   5    D ISCUSSION
495
496
      Our proposed LLM-based Multi-Agent framework, ChemThinkier, not only aims to predict molecular
497
      properties with greater accuracy but also seeks to provide deeper insights into the rationale behind
498
      these predictions. Inspired by the approach chemists take when addressing molecular problems,
499   we designed three distinct modules: general molecular thinking, intuition-driven thinking, and
500   task-specific thinking. Each module generates corresponding molecular insights, offering a more
501   comprehensive understanding of the molecule. This multi-perspective approach allows ChemThinker
502   to not only enhance prediction performance but also improve interpretability, making it highly
503   valuable for both scientific discovery and practical applications. In the Appendix A.3, we provide a
504   detailed example using a SMILES string, showcasing the ultimate molecular insights generated by
505   each module.
506   We find that while reports generated by open-source models provide significant value, they tend to
507   be less coherent and transparent in conveying their underlying reasoning. Even though open-source
508   LLMs may not express their “thinking” process as effectively as closed-source models, they still
509   possess a sufficient ability to generate representations that address prediction tasks well. Perhaps the
510   gap in report generation quality is due to the lack of access to proprietary data and investment in
511   instruction-based fine-tuning. Due to their pre-training on large-scale corpora, models like Galactica
512   (Taylor et al., 2022), which is trained on extensive scientific knowledge, and Llama-3.1 (Dubey
513
      et al., 2024), which leverages more recent datasets, demonstrate a robust foundational understanding.
      Moreover, our experiments show that, in certain tasks, these open-source LLMs can achieve state-of-
514
      the-art (SOTA) results, further highlighting their capabilities in molecular analysis.
515
516   During the molecular insights generation and reflection process, LLMs tend to produce redundant
517   responses, and controlling the generated length can be challenging. If we include more content
518   encoding in the molecular representation, critical information such as the original SMILES may
519
      receive less attention during the generation process. To mitigate this, we limit the input to the question
      along with its corresponding SMILES string. Another reason for this approach is that LLMs rely on
520
      the next-token prediction, meaning the quality of their responses is heavily influenced by the input
521
      query and how effectively the model embeds it. We believe that, compared to the generation process
522   itself, the more critical aspect is how LLM “think” or process the question. Thus, we designed three
523   thinking modules to emulate the way chemists approach molecular analysis. Our experimental results
524   demonstrate that ChemThinker is adaptable in leveraging varying contributions from each thought
525   component to address different tasks, depending on the backbone model used. This flexibility allows
526   ChemThinker to optimize its process to the specific requirements of each task, enhancing its overall
527   effectiveness in molecular property prediction.

530   6    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce, ChemThinker, our proposed LLM-based Multi-Agent framework that
533
      demonstrates significant flexibility and effectiveness in molecular property prediction by adapting its
534   thought components to different tasks and backbone models. Standing on the shoulders of giants,
535   we harness the strong reasoning capabilities, extensive pretrained knowledge, and encoding ability
536   of LLMs to provide both accurate predictions and deeper insights into the reasoning behind those
537   predictions. Our experimental results show that this approach not only achieves state-of-the-art
538   performance in certain tasks but also enhances the interpretability of molecular analysis, aligning
539   with the way chemists approach problem-solving in the field. Future work could explore additional
      tasks and architectures to further extend ChemThinker’s applicability and performance.
