009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent advancements in text-to-image generation have been propelled by the
012            development of diffusion models and multi-modality learning. However, since
013            text is typically represented sequentially in these models, it often falls short in
014            providing accurate contextualization and structural control. So the generated
015            images do not consistently align with human expectations, especially in complex
016            scenarios involving multiple objects and relationships. In this paper, we introduce
017            the Scene Graph Adapter (SG-Adapter), leveraging the structured representation
018            of scene graphs to rectify inaccuracies in the original text embeddings. The SG-
019            Adapter’s explicit, non-fully connected graph representation significantly improves
020
               upon the causal connections commonly used in transformer-based text models.
               In causal connections, each token can attend to all previous tokens, which may
021
               result in attribute leakage. On the other hand, we also curated a highly clean, multi-
022
               relational scene graph-image paired dataset MultiRels to address the challenges
023            posed by low-quality annotated datasets like Visual Genome (Krishna et al., 2017).
024            Furthermore, we design three metrics derived from GPT-4V(Achiam et al., 2023)
025            to effectively and thoroughly measure the correspondence between images and
026            scene graphs. Both qualitative and quantitative results validate the efficacy of our
027            approach in controlling the correspondence in multiple relationships.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Image generation has made great progress thanks to the success of a series of text-to-image diffusion
033
      models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2020; Nichol et al.,
      2021; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2020). Due to the huge amount of text-image paired
034
      training data (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2014) and the numerous model parameters, these
035
      text-conditioned models show fantastic image quality and attract great attention from researchers as
036   well as industries.
037
038   However, the text encoder used by these models are suboptimal for generating coherent images. They
039
      often face challenges in contextualizing text tokens, which requires capturing both their intrinsic
      meanings and their contextual understanding from surrounding tokens. The most commonly used
040
      text encoder, the CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021), applies causal attention mechanisms to allow
041
      tokens to gather information from preceding ones. However, this sequential processing can lead to the
042   “leakage issue”, where relations or attributes incorrectly influence unrelated elements in the image.
043   For example, in “A man playing the guitar back to back with a woman”, the relation “playing guitar”
044   might be mistakenly applied to the woman due to the linear text sequence (Fig. 1). Such cases, where
045   the encoder fails to recognize distinct entity boundaries, underscore the need for more advanced text
046   embedding methods capable of accurately depicting separate entities and their respective relations in
047   complex scenes.
048   Scene graphs, with their structured representation, effectively avoid the contextualization issues
049   inherent in sequential text tokens. Scene graphs depict images as networks of entities (nodes) and
050   their relationships (edges), ensuring clear, non-linear associations. Relations are directly linked to
051   specific nodes, preventing the ambiguity and misinterpretation common in text-based processing. This
052   distinct structure allows for precise and unambiguous representation of complex scenes, enhancing
053   the accuracy of image generation. This capability positions them as a natural control signal for image
      generation.

      However, pure scene graph-to-image generation (Yang et al., 2022a; Johnson et al., 2018; Farshad
070
      et al., 2023; Li et al., 2019) lags behind text-to-image generation in terms of image quality and is far
071   from practical use. This is because the scene graph-image pair data are considerably smaller than
072   text-image pair data. For instance, Visual Genome(Krishna et al., 2017) includes 108,077 pairs with
073   limited-quality scene graph labels. In stark contrast, the typical text-image dataset LAION-5B (Tang
074   et al., 2020) contains a massive 5.85 billion CLIP-filtered image-text pairs.
075
      Acknowledging the challenges in text-to-image generation and scene graph datasets, our research
076
      aims to leverage the limited scene graph annotations to enhance the control and accuracy of text-to-
077   image generation. Recent studies on adapters (Li et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023; Mou et al., 2023),
078   in pre-trained text-to-image models offer promising directions. These adapters introduce new control
079   mechanisms that retain the model’s image generation quality.
080
      In this study, we re-examine the text-to-image generation process, pinpointing issues of incorrect
081
      contextualization arising during text encoder computation. This problem is traced back to the causal
082
      attention mask, which is used in the pre-trained CLIP text model (Radford et al., 2021). It fails to
083   account for the structural semantics inherent in captions. However, directly replacing this causal
084   attention mask during inference is ineffective through our observations, as presented in Appendix.A.1.
085   Consequently, we propose the Scene Graph Adapter (SG-Adapter), strategically plugged in after
086   the CLIP text encoder, to tackle this issue. This adapter utilizes scene graph knowledge to refine
087   text embeddings. Critically, to ensure precise contextualization, we design our adapter based on a
088   transformer architecture and incorporate a novel triplet-token attention mask called Scene Graph(SG)
089   Mask, fostering accurate and contextually relevant text-to-image generation.
090   On the other hand, scene graph annotations often face challenges such as limited rationality, language
091   biases, or reporting biases, resulting in visual relationships that are often simplistic and less informa-
092   tive (Tang et al., 2020). These less reliable annotations can complicate experimental validation. To
093   address this, we developed a multi-relational dataset, MultiRels, with highly curated annotations that
094   better capture complex semantic structures. Using this high-quality dataset, we clearly demonstrate
095   that our SG-Adapter excels in generating relations with precise correspondences, showcasing its
096   strong ability to capture complex relational structures. Moreover, considering traditional evaluation
097
      metrics like FID and CLIP-Score cannot perceive the abstract Relation Correspondence concept, we
      propose three evaluation metrics derived from the advanced GPT-4V(Achiam et al., 2023) to evaluate
098
      the correspondences between images and scene graphs. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
099
100   • We propose our SG-Adapter to correct the incorrect contextualization in text embeddings that
101     results in “relation leakage”. Our adapter effectively addresses this issue and enhances the structural
102     semantics generation capabilities of current text-to-image models.
103   • We create a more comprehensive dataset with high-quality annotations called MultiRels that could
104     be adopted to better demonstrate the generation models’ ability to handle complex structural
105
        semantics. Besides, for effective and fair comparison in terms of relation accuracy, we contribute
        three metrics derived from GPT-4V (Achiam et al., 2023).
106
      • Both qualitative and quantitative results illustrate that our SG-Adapter outperforms state-of-the-art
107
        text-to-image and SG-to-image methods in terms of relation generation and control.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109

      Text-to-Image Generation. The evolution of text-to-image diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022;
111   Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2020; Nichol et al., 2021; Dhariwal & Nichol,
112   2021; Song et al., 2020) has marked a significant advancement in the generation of high-quality
113   images. Among these, Stable Diffusion (SD) (Rombach et al., 2022) is particularly notable, utilizing
114   a pre-trained autoencoder alongside a diffusion model to effectively transform textual prompts into
115   precise and detailed visual representations. Despite their impressive capabilities, these models still
116   face challenges, particularly in terms of the leakage issue, where relations or attributes inadvertently
117   influence unrelated elements within an image (Feng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b). While (Li et al.,
118   2023b) addresses this through a novel Jensen-Shannon divergence-based binding loss, it does not
119
      fully acknowledge the issue as stemming from the improper contextualization of text embedding.
      In a similar vein, (Feng et al., 2022) employs linguistic structures such as constituency trees or
120
      scene graphs to guide the diffusion process. However, this method does not entirely address the
121
      root cause of these issues but constructs a set of text embeddings and only partially fixes the wrong
122   contextualization, which is linked to the causal attention mechanism used in the CLIP text encoder.
123
124   Scene Graph-to-Image Generation. In the realm of scene graph to image generation, some works
125
      often rely on scene layouts as an intermediate representation (Johnson et al., 2018; Farshad et al.,
      2023) in a two-stage pipeline. They first create scene layouts from scene graphs and then generate
126
      images based on the layouts. These layouts, serving as image-like representations of scene graphs,
127
      can often be suboptimal due to manual crafting and potential misalignment with actual scene graphs.
128   In this paper, we clarify that Layout-to-Image (Zheng et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023;
129   Couairon et al., 2023) and Scene Graph-to-Image are separate different topics, as demonstrated in
130   Fig.5. Layout-to-Image aims to generate objects in user-specified areas while having limited ability to
131   represent complicated Relations. This limitation has led to the development of alternative approaches,
132   such as SGDiff (Yang et al., 2022b), which optimizes scene graph embeddings for better alignment
133   with images, and other works (Wu et al., 2023) that introduce “knowledge consensus” as a means
134   to disentangle complex semantics between knowledge graphs and images. Despite the advances in
135   this field, the dearth of large-scale and high-quality data continues to impede its widespread practical
136
      application.
137   Single Relation Learning. Though Text-to-Image generation models have made great progress, they
138   might fall short when generating complicated single relationships. RRNet (Wu et al., 2024) learns
139   a relationship via a heterogeneous GCN while Reversion (Huang et al., 2023) reverses the target
140   relation from example images by optimizing the relation prompt. Instead of addressing the issue of
141   single relations, our study focuses on generating multiple relations with accurate correspondences.
142   Adapter. The integration of adapters into existing models has emerged as a noteworthy innovation,
143   exemplified by methods such as ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) and GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023a).
144   These techniques have enabled models like SD to enhance user control without requiring extensive
145   retraining. For example, ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) operates as a plug-in module, extracting
146   and integrating residual features from each image condition into SD’s U-Net for enhanced control.
147   GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023a), on the other hand, incorporates location control by integrating a new
148
      self-attention module into the U-Net.
149   Our work critically examines the issue of false contextualization in SD and develops a scene graph-
150   only trained adapter to enhance the semantic structure controllability of SD, addressing a gap in the
151   current landscape of text-to-image generation.

183   of a sequence of triplets formatted as ⟨subject, relation, object⟩. The extraction of these K triplets

      4.1   DATASET C ONFIGURATION
273
274   As we mentioned above, a scene graph dataset with clean and precise relational annotations is
275   essential as well as effective for learning relation representation. To train a model that can encode
276   both single and multiple relations accurately, for the first time, we present a multiple relations scene
277   graph-image paired dataset with highly precise labels called MultiRels. The dataset with size 309 is
278   composed of two parts as follows:
279   • ReVersion (Huang et al., 2023): This part consists of 99 images with only a single clear relation.
280     There are 10 representative relations in Reversion and most of them are “difficult” relations that
281     current text-to-image models can not generate well, e.g., shake hands with, sit back to back, is
282     painted on. Reversion is mainly used to learn new single relations.
283   • Multiple Relations: For multi-relations learning, in this part, we manually collect images with
284     1-4 (mostly more than 1) salient relations and label them with accurate scene graphs. Most of the
285
        relations are “simple” relations (e.g., holding, drinking, stands on, sits on) that current text-to-image
        models generate well individually but fail when existing multiple objects and multiple relations.
286
        There are also small parts of relations that are “difficult” like is above, is under. Multiple Relations
287
        is towards to train a model that could generate multiple relations with correct correspondences.
288
289   Besides the scene graph, we additionally provide a token-triplet matrix for each image.

482   5       C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
483
484   This paper introduces the Scene Graph Adapter (SG-Adapter), a novel enhancement for text-to-image
485   generation models. By integrating scene graph knowledge, the SG-Adapter significantly improves the
      contextual understanding of these models, ensuring images closely match their textual descriptions.


                                                                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      The adapter employs an efficient triplet-token attention mechanism within a transformer architecture,
487   allowing for more precise mapping of text to visual elements. To validate its effectiveness, the
488   study utilized a specially curated dataset featuring multiple relations and high-quality annotations,
489   showing the crucial role of clean, relation-rich data in multi-relational learning. As shown in Fig.5,
490   SG-Adapter can also learn single complex relations as a by-product. Furthermore, we propose three
491   effective metrics derived from GPT-4V (Achiam et al., 2023) to evaluate the generated relation
492   precisely. Lastly, our method could learn a new and complex single relation as a by-product, similar
493   to ReVersion (Huang et al., 2023) and RRNet (Wu et al., 2024), as demonstrated in Fig.5.
494   As for limitation, to address data privacy concerns and follow the double-blind policy, we applied
495   anonymization techniques to human faces in our MultiRels dataset, which may introduce some arti-
496   facts affecting image quality inevitably. We are actively exploring more sophisticated anonymization
497   methods that preserve data integrity while ensuring privacy.
