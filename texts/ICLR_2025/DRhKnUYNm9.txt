009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The standard neural marked temporal point process employs the Embedding-
012            Encoder-History vector-Decoder (EEHD) architecture, wherein the history vector
013            encapsulates the cumulative effects of past events. However, due to the inherent
014            imbalance in event categories in real-world scenarios, the history vector tends to
015            favor more frequent events, inadvertently overlooking less common yet potentially
016            significant ones, thereby compromising the model’s overall performance. To tackle
017            this issue, we introduce a novel decoupled learning framework for neural marked
018            temporal point process, where each event type is modeled independently to capture
019
               its unique characteristics, allowing for a more nuanced and equitable treatment
               of all event types. Each event type boasts its own complete EEHD architecture,
020
               featuring scaled-down parameters due to the decoupling of temporal dynamics.
021
               This decoupled design enables asynchronous parallel training, and the embeddings
022            can reflect the dependencies between event types. Our versatile framework, accom-
023            modating various encoder and decoder architectures, demonstrates state-of-the-art
024            performance across diverse datasets, outperforming benchmarks by a significant
025            margin and increasing training speed by up to 12 times. Additionally, it offers
026            interpretability, revealing which event types have similar influences on a particular
027            event type, fostering a deeper understanding of temporal dynamics.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Event sequence is a ubiquitous data structure in real world, such as user behavior sequences, error
033   logs, purchase transaction records and electronic health records (Mannila et al., 1997; Liu et al., 1998;
034   Zhou et al., 2013; Choi et al., 2016; Liu & Huang, 2023). Regardless of the domain, event sequence
035   provides a unified abstraction for these data, with each event being represented by a tuple, consisting
036   of the event type (aka. mark) and the occurrence time. Modeling such temporal data as a stochastic
037   process, one seeks to predict time and type of the future events based on the history, i.e., previously
038   observed sequential events. For example, a history of purchases of a person may tell when the person
039
      will buy a new item, and a short message from a famous social media influencer could affect a critical
      bull or bear in a stock market. Predicting such a future event is often realized as a chance of the event,
040
      i.e., a likelihood of a certain type of event at a specific time.
041
042   In the realm of modeling and predicting temporal events, neural marked temporal point processes
043   (MTPPs) have emerged as a powerful tool capable of capturing complex dynamics and dependencies
044   within event sequences (Du et al., 2016; Omi et al., 2019; Shchur et al., 2020; Waghmare et al., 2022;
045
      Soen et al., 2021; Zhou & Yu, 2023; Mei & Eisner, 2017; Chen et al., 2018). At the core of these
      models lies the Embedding-Encoder-History vector-Decoder (EEHD) architecture (Shchur et al.,
046
      2021; Bosser & Taieb, 2023), which provides a structured framework for encoding event attributes,
047
      summarizing historical contexts, and decoding future event predictions. The history vector, a pivotal
048   component within this architecture, serves as a condensed representation of the cumulative effects
049   of past events, playing a crucial role in shaping the model’s predictions. However, a fundamental
050   limitation arises when applying this standard EEHD framework to real-world scenarios, where event
051   categories are inherently unbalanced. In such settings, the history vector tends to exhibit a bias
052   towards more frequently occurring events, inadvertently deemphasizing less common yet potentially
053   significant events. This bias not only undermines the model’s ability to capture the full diversity and
      richness of event dynamics but also compromises its overall predictive performance.

069
      To mitigate this challenge and unlock the full potential of neural MTPPs, we introduce a novel
070
      decoupled learning framework tailored specifically for this task. Our approach represents a paradigm
071
      shift from the traditional monolithic modeling of event sequences to a more nuanced, event-type-
072   specific approach. By modeling each event type individually, independent of the others, we aim to
073   eliminate the bias inherent in the standard EEHD framework and ensure that all event categories,
074   regardless of their frequency, are treated equitably.
075
      Within our decoupled framework, each event type maintains its own dedicated EEHD architecture
076
      tailored to its unique characteristics and dynamics, which as a result can have smaller parameter scales
077
      compared to that used for full dynamics. The event type embeddings learned in each EEHD can reflect
078   their influences on the corresponding event type, providing valuable insights into the underlying
079   mechanisms driving the temporal point process. Moreover, the decoupled architecture enables
080   asynchronous parallel training for these individual models, significantly enhancing computational
081   efficiency. Our framework can accommodate various encoder and decoder architectures, without
082   imposing rigid constraints. This feature enables researchers and practitioners to experiment with
083   different design choices, optimizing their models for specific tasks and domains.
084   To summarize, our contributions are as follows:
085
086          • We propose a novel decoupled learning framework for neural MTPPs, which revolutionizes
087            the traditional monolithic modeling by disentangling it into event-type-specific individual
088            modeling, thereby enabling an efficient learning for event-type-specific temporal dynamics.
089
             • The decoupled framework allows for asynchronous parallel training and provides inter-
090
               pretability of the dependencies among different event types. Meanwhile, it’s general for not
091            imposing restrictions on the encoder and decoder architectures.
092
093          • Through extensive experimentation on both real-world and synthetic datasets, we show
094
               that our approach attains state-of-the-art performance on standard prediction tasks. More-
               over, we showcase a significant enhancement in training speed, achieving up to a 12-fold
095
               increase compared to the benchmark model. Also, we demonstrate the interpretability of
096
               our framework by analyzing how different event types influence each other.

      2.1   E MBEDDING L AYER
109
110   Global embedding In standard neural MTPPs, the embedding layer assigns each event type a vector
111   representation and the learned representations can naturally group similar event types according
112   to the spirit of Word2vec (Mikolov et al., 2013). However, this embedding style can not reflect
113   the dependencies between different event types. For example, which event types have similar
114   influences on the given event type k? We can view the embedding in standard neural MTPPs as global
115   embedding, i.e., the vector representations of different event types are in the same vector space.
116   Local embedding To reflect the relationships among event types, our idea is to create K vector spaces
117   and in each, an event type will have a vector representation, which we call local embedding. In this
118   embedding fashion, an event type can have different vector representations in different vector spaces,
119   indicating that it can have different influences on different event types. In vector space of event type
120   k, close vector representations indicate that the corresponding event types have similar influences on
121   event type k, according to the spirit of Word2vec.
122   Event embedding consists of two parts, the type embedding and the time embedding. Let zm       k

      2.2   S EQUENCE E NCODER
140
141   Global encoding To evaluate the impacts of historical events {(ki , ti )}li=1 , the sequence encoder in
142   the standard learning framework encodes all past events into one single history vector hl , which we
143   call global encoding and can be summarized as follows:
144
                                 hl = Encoder(e(1) · · · , e(i), · · · , e(l))                            (3)
145
146   where e(i) is the embedding of the ith event (ki , ti ) and Encoder is usually realized by the recurrent
147   neural network, Transformer or their variants (Bosser & Taieb, 2023). As aforementioned, event types
148
      often exhibit a categorical imbalance. This poses a challenge to the standard learning framework,
      as the history vector, when summarizing event history, tends to give preferential treatment to more
149
      common events, to the detriment of those that occur less frequently.
150
151   Local encoding To ensure that all event types, irrespective of their frequency, are sufficiently
152   represented and taken into account, we equip each event type with its own dedicated encoder to
153   encode its unique temporal dynamics and dependencies, which we call local encoding, i.e.,
154                              hkl = Encoderk (ek (1) · · · , ek (i), · · · , ek (l))                    (4)
155   This design not only enables the model to capture the intricacies of each individual event type but also
156   allows for a more lightweight encoder architecture because Encoderk can now concentrate solely on
157   the historical events that interest event type k.

444   4   R ELATED W ORK
445
446   Depending on whether the EEHD architecture is applied or not, neural MTPP models can be
447   categorised into two groups. Most models fall into the category using the EEHD architecture, while
448   there are also some models that do not, such as models that use graph structures to represent event
449   types and their relationships (Trivedi et al., 2019; Zhang & Yan, 2021; Dash et al., 2022), and models
450
      that use case-based reasoning (Liu, 2024a). Among the EEHD-based models, researchers have
      worked on advancing the encoder and decoder to capture more complex dynamics. Popular encoders
451
      include recurrent neural networks (Du et al., 2016; Omi et al., 2019; Shchur et al., 2020), attention
452
      based networks (Zuo et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Gu, 2021; Shou et al., 2023), and state space
453   model based networks (Gao et al., 2024). These encoders generate the history vector that remains
454   static until the occurrence of the subsequent event. In contrast, continuous-time encoders, exemplified
455   by continuous-LSTM (Mei & Eisner, 2017) and neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018; 2021; Song et al.,
456   2024), allow the history vector to evolve over time. Regarding decoders, most neural MTPP models
457   rely on the intensity function (Du et al., 2016; Mei & Eisner, 2017; Zhang et al., 2020; Soen et al.,
458   2021; Ding et al., 2023), which is formulated through a predefined parameterized function (e.g., a
459   neural network) and the history vector is used to decode the function parameters. There are also
460   some intensity-free decoders designed for closed-form computing of the likelihood (Omi et al., 2019;
461
      Shchur et al., 2020; Waghmare et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Liu, 2024b). For example, FullyNN
      (Omi et al., 2019) models the cumulative hazard function by a monotonic neural network, where the
462
      neural weights are all constrained to be positive; IFL (Shchur et al., 2020) models the probability
463
      density function by a log-normal mixture distribution. Based on their mathematical relationships, the
464   intensity models and intensity-free models can be converted interchangeably (Lin et al., 2021).
465
466   To our knowledge, we are pioneers in addressing the imbalance of event categories, prompting us to
467
      decouple neural MTPPs from the perspective of event types. However, decoupling can have many
      aspects. For example, some models calculate the cumulative impacts of past events as the sum of the
468
      impacts of each individual past events to identify the important past events as well as improve the
469
      training efficiency (Liu et al., 2018; Salehi et al., 2019; Zhou & Yu, 2023; Song et al., 2024). This
470   is also a form of decoupling, but for a different purpose than ours. Notably, it compromises model
471   performance as it does not take into account the temporal dependencies between historical events.
472
473
474   5   C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this paper, we have presented a novel decoupled learning framework for neural marked temporal
477
      point processes that effectively mitigates the issue of frequency bias inherent in standard approaches.
      By modeling each event type separately within a complete EEHD architecture, our approach ensures
478
      that all event types, regardless of their frequency, are adequately represented and considered during
479
      training. The decoupling of the standard monolithic modeling not only enables asynchronous parallel
480   training, significantly improving the training speed, but also allows the embeddings to capture
481   the intricate dependencies between event types, which we believe will have far-reaching practical
482   implications around knowledge discovery in many domains. Our extensive experiments across
483   diverse datasets have conclusively demonstrated that this decoupled learning framework achieves
484   state-of-the-art performance on standard prediction tasks, underscoring its effectiveness. Importantly,
485   the framework’s design does not impose rigid constraints on the encoder and decoder architectures,
      allowing for future improvements and extensions tailored to specific applications.

847   D     A BLATION S TUDY
848
849   Due to the decoupled dynamics for each event type, each EEHD model in our framework has
850   significantly smaller parameter scales compared with traditional models that are designed for full
851   dynamics. What if traditional models use as many parameters as that used in our individual EEHD
852   model and vice versa? Table A4 and A5 answers this and shows that our decoupled EEHD models
853   requires few parameters to capture the corresponding temporal dynamics while they are not adequate
854   for traditional models.
855   The standard model uses type-specific decoder and keeps the embedding and encoding layer shared
856   across different event types. As a comparison, the embedding, encoding and decoding layer are all
857   type-specific in our proposed decoupled model, to encode the unique dynamics of each event type
858   and thus mitigate the frequency bias of the history vector. This naturally raises a series of questions:
859   what’s the performance if only the encoding layer is type-specific? And what’s the performance if only
860   the embedding layer is type-specific? We explore all possible variants and report their performance
861   in Table A6, where we see it performs best to let all three modules be type-specific to capture the
862   unique dynamics of the corresponding event type.
