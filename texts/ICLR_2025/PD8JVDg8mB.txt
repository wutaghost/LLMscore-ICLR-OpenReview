009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               A common approach to learning from unlabeled images is to train models to sat-
012            isfy invariances on these images, such as consistency under augmentations or
013            crops. Despite successes on Imagenet, these approaches struggle to learn from
014            larger uncurated datasets like web crawls or video, where such inductive biases
015            only weakly hold. How can we more effectively learn from broader datasets?
016            Instead of training models to be invariant across views, we study an alternative
017            approach encouraging model representations to be predictive of important seman-
018            tics of adjacent views of an image. We concurrently train a model to predict se-
019            mantic annotations from images (generated either self-supervised, or from auxil-
020
               iary datasets); and bootstrap the model’s semantics by predicting, given a cropped
               view of an image and the coordinates for a nearby crop, the model’s annotation
021
               distribution for the neighboring view. A core strength of this approach is the
022
               ability to extract information universally from both unlabelled and labelled image
023            data, incorporating captions, bounding boxes, and other annotations when they are
024            present. Our experiments show that annotation propagation improves pre-training
025            on unlabelled datasets in the wild, including video datasets like EpicKitchens,
026            scene datasets like COCO, and uncurated web-scale image datasets like CC12M.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   The ability to learn from large unlabelled datasets has served as the impetus for much of the recent
033
      progress in deep learning. In natural language processing, text corpora fed into generic unsupervised
      objectives like next-token or masked prediction have enabled increasingly capable NLP models.
034
      Even though unlabelled visual data is similarly plentiful and easy to collect — on the internet,
035
      from video, from embodied agents, and beyond — learning from such images has proven a greater
036   challenge, in part because they are a raw signal with redundancy, low information density and noise.
037
038   To learn useful semantics without labels, a common approach is to train models to respect certain in-
039
      variances on images, such as consistency of representations between views of an image transformed
      by augmentations, crops, or masking. By representing two different crops of an image with similar
040
      features, models may learn high-level features insofar as guessing which crops go together requires
041
      recognizing semantically salient objects (e.g., that a lion should be associated with the savannah).
042   Despite their empirical successes, these consistency objectives introduce inductive biases tailored to
043   pre-training datasets like Imagenet and downstream metrics like object classification. It is unclear
044   how to generalize these inductive biases to a broader set of images, like web crawl or video data,
045   and towards other downstream tasks like object detection or embodied action recognition.
046
      In this paper, we consider a framework in which a model attempts to learn semantic relationships
047
      between images and “annotations”, and then uses its learned model to predict these semantic anno-
048   tations across neighboring views of images. We call this framework annotation bootstrapping, since
049   the core of the pre-training process involves using a model’s predictions about semantic annotations
050   from one view to bootstrap the predicted semantics of other neighboring views of an image. The
051   annotation and bootstrapping objectives are decoupled, which means that we may flexibly steer pre-
052   training by curating or changing the annotation data, while still using the entire unlabelled image
053   corpus to “bootstrap” these semantics across nearby images. Unlike invariance-based approaches
      to learning from unlabelled data, where learned features are defined opaquely by some interplay

      2    R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Self-supervised learning. Self-supervised methods learn from unlabelled image data generally
090   in two ways: by generation or by enforcing representational consistency. Generative approaches
091   predict raw pixels (He et al., 2021) or other low-level features (Xie et al., 2021; Bao et al., 2021) from
092
      a corrupted or masked image input; the quality of learned image semantics can depend significantly
      on the pixel statistics of pre-training images (cite). Consistency-based approaches instead train
093
      representations to be similar across image transformations like augmentations or masks. Consistency
094
      can be enforced directly via instance-level discrimination (van den Oord et al., 2018; Chen et al.,
095   2020a) or implicitly in the limit of self-distillation (Grill et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2019; He et al.,
096   2019; Chen & He, 2020; Assran et al., 2023). These methods also mimic qualities of the pre-
097   training distribution (cite uniform prior), and degrade on atypical images and uncurated datasets.
098   One challenge with self-distillation in latent space admits pathological solutions (like the trivial
099   representation ϕ = 0), often requiring advanced techniques such as logit sharpening (Caron et al.,
100   2021; Oquab et al., 2023) or asymmetrical predictors (Grill et al., 2020).
101
102   Vision-language pre-training. On vision-language datasets, prior work has found gains in com-
103   bining weakly supervised losses (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2023) with self-
104   supervised ones: Mu et al. (2021) combines CLIP with a SimCLR objective using an auxiliary head,
105   Li et al. (2021) jointly runs CLIP and SimCLR both on the same representation, Naeem et al. (2023)
106   combines SigLIP with a DINO objective. These methods improve the data efficiency of contrastive
107   vision-language training and improves performance for more fine-grained tasks like segmentation
      and prediction; however, other evidence however suggests that simpler solutions also offer the same

130   Semi-supervised learning While our paper focuses on unlabelled image pre-training guided by
131   descriptive annotations like free-form text, it is adjacent to a longer line of semi-supervised ap-
132   proaches learning with partially annotated class labels. Two techniques are common: combining
133   a supervised classification loss with an self-supervised objective on unlabelled data (Pathak et al.,
134   2016; Chen et al., 2020b; Zhai et al., 2019a; Xie et al., 2019), and using the supervised dataset to
135   create pseudo-labels (Lee et al., 2013) for unlabelled images (Xie et al., 2019; Pham et al., 2020).
136
      While both pseudo-labeling and our bootstrapping generate target predictions using the model’s out-
      puts, we note one important difference: pseudolabelling creates labels for a different model to train,
137
      while our approach is designed to enable a model to learn from its own synthetic targets.

140   3   P RE -T RAINING BY B OOTSTRAPPING A NNOTATIONS
141
142
      We propose the annotation bootstrapping framework, which uses unlabelled image data to propagate
143
      the model’s understanding of image semantics across “neighboring” views of an image. The core
144   principle is the bootstrap: that the current model’s predictions about a given image can be used to
145   create a prediction target for itself – just for a different image input.
146
147
      In this framework, models learn in two threads; one that predicts semantic annotations from images,
      and one that “bootstraps” these annotations across different views of an image to teach models how
148
      to predict the semantics of its surroundings. Intuitively, the bootstrapped update is simple: we take
149
      an crop of an unlabelled image and transform it (e.g. by zooming in, panning left, etc); the model
150   is trained to predict, given the first image and a description of the transform applied, the annotations
151   detected by the model in the transformed image. As we illustrate conceptually in Figure 2, these two
152   threads are synergistic; as the model improves its ability to predict semantics about the scene around
153   it, it acquires a better semantic understanding of its own scene, thereby improving the targets used
154   to train the model in the future.
155
156   Learning to predict annotations from images. The first step to formalizing our framework is to
157   specify what it means to model (and eventually predict across views) useful semantic concepts. We
158   co-opt the terminology of image annotations, arbitrary descriptors of useful semantic concepts in
159   images. A useful mental model is to imagine annotations as text descriptions of an image, although
160   they can be masks, captions, audio, or even other images. The learning problem is to use a dataset of
161   image-annotation pairs Da = {(xi , ℓi )} to predict the annotation distribution p(x, ℓ); when modeled
      contrastively, yields a well-known learning problem (Poole et al., 2019; Eysenbach et al., 2024).

202   In form, this objective looks like model distillation, but has an important crucial difference: the

294   5     E XPERIMENTS
295
296   In our empirical study, we measure the effectiveness of annotation propagation for learning useful
297   features from unlabelled image datasets found “in-the-wild”. We study both the fully unsupervised
298   setting (where propagation occurs in the space of image-image relationships, based on the SimCLR
299   loss), and in the weakly labelled setting (where propagation occurs in the space of image-caption
300   relationships, based on the CLIP objective). Our experiments seek to answer three main questions:
301
302
            1. How does our approach compare to invariance-based and pixel-predictive approaches?
303         2. Can we use semantic annotations from one dataset to learn on a different unlabelled dataset?
304         3. What factors in the prediction objective affect the quality of learned representations?

360   Fully unlabelled pre-training. We first evaluate annotation propagation in the fully unlabeled set-
361   ting (ABSimCLR ), where our approach learns by learning to match two augmented views of an image
362   together using a base SimCLR loss, and propagating annotations in the induced space of image-
363
      image relationships. We make two overarching observations in our experiments. First, on datasets
      and benchmarks where “invariance to crops and augmentations” holds , annotation propagation is
364
      synergistic to the base SimCLR loss, improving performance on average. On datasets and bench-
365
      marks where this inductive bias does not hold, SimCLR performance significantly degrades, but
366   annotation propagation does not, indicating that the propagation objective also enables the model to
367   learn a wider set of features useful for tasks beyond the base distribution.
368
369
      On both Imagenet and CC12M (Table A.2, top), annotation propagation learns representations that
      outperform SimCLR, DINO, and MAE for downstream classification tasks (noting that for all meth-
370
      ods and many downstream tasks, there is a uniform drop-off on CC12M, the less curated dataset).
371
      Our approach improves over SimCLR in downstream I1K performance by ∼4% on both datasets.
372   We notice that SimCLR and DINO degrade relative to MAE on the Clevr tasks, while annotation
373   propagation does not, indicating that our approach can avoid common failure modes associated with
374   invariance-based self-supervised learning. The invariance to crops imposed by SimCLR and DINO
375   is a particularly bad match for EpicKitchens and COCO (Table 2). On these domains, we find
376   that annotation propagation outperforms these invariance-based approaches, but is equal or slightly
377   worse than MAE across the board. While annotation propagation can enable the model to learn
      features beyond the invariances, it is not a cureall for a poor base loss.

498   6   D ISCUSSION
499
500   Our paper introduced annotation bootstrapping, a framework for
501   bootstrapping visual representations using unlabeled data that learns by predicting image semantics
502   of the nearby scene. Two qualities make annotation propagation particularly interesting: first, that it
503   cleanly partitions the pre-training process into the specification of image semantics and bootstrap-
504   ping, allowing us to learn useful details using curated or labeled datasets, while still being able to
505   pre-train on general corpora that do not have the same inductive biases as the curated data. As we
506
      saw across a number of datasets, annotation propagation learns useful semantic representations be-
      yond those that are learned from common objectives like pixel prediction, CLIP, or models that learn
507
      invariances to crops and augmentations. Our approach is not without limitation; relative to the scale
508
      that current CLIP models are being trained on, we were only able to train on relatively small datasets
509   (CC12M – our largest dataset – only has 8 million images) and with relatively small networks (ViT-
510   S), and at limited training durations; some of the conclusions in our paper may weaken at larger
511   scales. Second, while the propagation objective does reduce the dependency on curated datasets and
512   specific inductive biases compared to invariance-based or pixel-predictive approaches, we found the
513   choice of crops to still introduce a form of bias (as we found larger crops to be better). Understand-
514   ing how we may generalize our approach to more general distortions beyond predicting crops will
515   also be an interesting future direction. Nonetheless, our work takes a step towards understanding
516   how we may pre-train on visual data in a self-sufficient bootstrapped manner using vast swaths of
517
      unlabeled data. Already at the larger scales of model pre-training today, we are beginning to see
      methods bump into the data wall, and we must soon answer the question – how will we improve our
518
      models when the data runs out?

      A.1   E VALUATION
759
760   We use the multi-task decoder-based probe from Beyer et al. (2023) for all of the evaluations in this
761   paper. The probe is defined as a 4-layer transformer decoder with an autoregressive decoding pattern
762   that attends to the final outputs of the Vision Transformer through cross-attention. We choose this
763   architecture so that we can do all of our modelling tasks, whether image recognition or bounding
764   box prediction, or classification of the object in a bounding box using a unified framework; this also
765
      represents (albeit to a much smaller scale) how vision transformers are being used in VLM models.
      We adopt all hyperparameters for training this model from Beyer et al. (2023). Unlike in Beyer et al.
766
      (2023), we train probes for individual tasks separately.
767
768   When pre-training on Imagenet and CC12M, we probe the model on ImageNet, the Clevr/{Count,
769   Distance} tasks from Zhai et al. (2019b), and then on four tasks used by Beyer et al. (2023):
770   Food101, Oxford IIIT Pets, Resics45, and Sun397.
771   When pre-training on COCO, we evaluate on small object classification (in which the model is
772   provided the coordinates of a bounding box, and asked to predict the identity of the object within
773   that bounding box), and the corresponding detection task (in which the model must simply identify
774   all bounding boxes corresponding to relevant objects in a scene).
775   We treat EpicKitchens as a standard dataset of images, considering individual frames independently,
776   and not incorporating the temporal dimension. When pre-training on EpicKitchens, we probe the
777   model also on object classification (predicting the label of an object given its bounding box) and ob-
778   ject detection (predicting bounding boxes), which we source from the ViSOR annotation set (Dark-
779   halil et al., 2022). We also probe the model’s ability to predict the action a human is taking given
780   one frame of context. This problem is not exactly solvable from one frame of context, but the
781   relative performance differences between methods nonetheless informs the quality of the learned
782   representations.
