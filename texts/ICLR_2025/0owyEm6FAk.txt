010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            Finetuning large language models (LLMs) with LoRA has gained significant pop-
013            ularity due to its simplicity and effectiveness. Often times, users may even find
014            pluggable community-shared LoRA adapters to enhance their base models and
015            enjoy a powerful, efficient, yet customized LLM experience. However, this con-
016
               venient share-and-play ecosystem also introduces a new attack surface, where at-
               tackers can tamper with existing LoRA adapters and distribute malicious versions
017
               to the community. Despite the high-risk potential, no prior work has explored
018
               LoRA’s attack surface under the share-and-play context. In this paper, we ad-
019            dress this gap by investigating how backdoors can be injected into task-enhancing
020            LoRA adapters and studying the mechanisms of such infection. We demonstrate
021            that with a simple but specific recipe, a backdoor-infected LoRA can be trained
022            once, then directly merged with multiple LoRA adapters finetuned on different
023            tasks while retaining both its malicious and benign capabilities; which enables at-
024            tackers to distribute compromised LoRAs at scale with minimal effort. Our work
025            highlights the need for heightened security awareness in the LoRA ecosystem.
026            Warning: the paper contains potentially offensive content generated by models.

      Finetuning large language models (LLMs) with Parameter-Efficient Finetuning (PEFT) tech-
      niques (Xu et al., 2023; Li & Liang, 2021; Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021) to better adapt
032
      to downstream tasks or user preferences is considered an efficient approach to leveraging the ca-
033
      pabilities of powerful pretrained models for specific needs. In this regard, Low-Rank Adaptation
034   Tuning — commonly known as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) — has gained significant popularity. With
035   a wealth of PEFT techniques available, LoRA excels in its modularity, efficiency, and effective-
036   ness (Wang et al., 2024a; Huang et al., 2023a). One can enable LoRA at different target modules
037   and utilize its rank hyperparameter to adjust the capacity of finetuning, adapting to various tasks
038   and models. More importantly, once finetuning concludes, the LoRA weights can be fused into
039   the base model for efficient inference without additional latency — a luxury absent in other popu-
040   lar PEFT techniques like soft-prompt tuning (Wu et al., 2024a) and adapter tuning (Houlsby et al.,
041   2019). LoRA tuning has consistently delivered favorable results across a wide range of downstream
042
      tasks (Sheng et al., 2023). In some cases, an open-sourced small language model (SLM) finetuned
      with LoRA can outperform much larger models on the same task, enabling opportunities such as
043
      local hosting for better versatility, service integration, and privacy protections — which are often
044
      deal breakers that prohibit the use of more powerful, closed-source models offered via APIs.

      1.2    A N EW S ECURITY R ISK : L O RA- AS - AN -ATTACK FOR S TEALTHY BACKDOOR
078          I NJECTION
079
080   However, despite the convenience of the share-and-play setup, this exact ecosystem enables a new
081   attack surface that exposes its users to the potential for malicious LoRA adapters. Theoretically,
082   an attacker could encode stealthy but adversarial behavior into a LoRA adapter, disguise it with
083   enhanced downstream capabilities, and distribute it to the open-source community. A user’s LoRA-
084   equipped LLM could then become infected through the share-and-play pipeline.
085   For a more concrete and timely real-life example, imagine a LoRA with superior performance on
086   summarization and question-answering (QA) tasks. If an attacker injects a backdoor trigger within
087   this LoRA to output biased political content — for example, smearing a certain candidate upon
088   mention of their name — without significantly altering its QA ability, this tampered LoRA could
089   easily gain popularity in the community and potentially sway users’ preferences of this candidate
090   through bias and misinformation.
091   Since we cannot directly inspect a LoRA’s weights to detect backdoor infections — and because
092   proper backdoor infections are inherently stealthy — this type of malicious LoRA distribution could
093   go unnoticed for a significant period. As a result, it presents a unique security risk specific to the
094   share-and-play ecosystem, which we refer to as LoRA-as-an-Attack.
095
096   1.3    L O RA O NCE , BACKDOOR E VERYWHERE : L OW-C OST D ISTRIBUTION AT S CALE
097
098   In the above section, we briefly discussed the theoretical potential of LoRA-as-an-Attack. How-
099   ever, there are several practical limitations to its pipeline, where a meaningful LoRA-as-an-Attack
100   deployment would require:
101
      • The intended downstream capability to remain intact. As poor downstream performance
102     would reduce the community’s interest in the first place.
103   • The backdoor to be stealthy yet reasonably effective. Since an obviously tampered LoRA
104     would quickly be flagged and prevent community sharing.
105   • The malicious LoRA to be efficiently manufactured at scale. If each malicious LoRA required
106     heavy investment in crafting, the attacker would likely be unable to produce many of them, result-
107     ing in limited community adoption due to the existence of countless different downstream tasks
        and preferences.

      In this work, we dive into the infection mechanism of LoRA-as-an-Attack and show that by training
109   a feed-forward (FF) only LoRA adapter on a tiny backdoor dataset, we can then — in a training-free
110   fashion — merge this backdoor LoRA with various task-enhancing LoRAs trained for improving
111   downstream capabilities, while retaining both its benign and adversarial capabilities to a reasonable
112   level.
113
      These observations suggest that LoRA-as-an-Attack has the potential for mass distribution, as it
114
      meets all four criteria mentioned above. In summary, our work serves as a warning that this type of
115   attack could exist both theoretically and in practice. We summarize our contributions as:
116
117   • Alerting the community to the LoRA-as-an-Attack security risk. By showcasing the strong
118     effectiveness of LoRA-as-an-Attack, we alert the open-source community — especially practi-
119
        tioners in the share-and-play ecosystem — to the potential and capability of this new security
        risk.
120
      • Dissecting the LoRA-as-an-Attack mechanism. We provide detailed experimental results that
121
        dissect the mechanism of this new attack paradigm to better inform the community.

124   2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORKS
125
126   LoRA and its Variants LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) is a fundamentally simple finetuning approach,
127
      which incorporates a small proportion of trainable parameters into the pre-trained models. Recently,
      researchers have utilized LoRA to fine-tune pre-trained LLMs for adaptation to downstream tasks,
128
      thereby avoiding the need to train a vast number of model parameters. During the training phase,
129
      the pre-trained model is frozen, significantly reducing memory and computational demands. Specif-
130   ically, for weight W ∈ Rd×k within the pretrained LLM, we can learn two low-rank matrices
131   A ∈ Rd×r and B ∈ Rr×k to approximate the parameter update of W :
132
133
                                        W ′ = W + ∆W = W + AB                                             (1)
134
      Several advanced variants of LoRA are applied to fine-tune LLMs. DoRA (Liu et al., 2024) refines
135
      LoRA by decomposing the weight matrix into direction and magnitude components, resulting in
136
      better optimization. QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024) achieved further memory efficiency by quantiz-
137   ing the LoRA adapters to lower precision. GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024a) improves memory efficiency
138   in LLM training by projecting gradients into a low-rank space. LoRA-GA (Wang et al., 2024b) en-
139   hances LoRA with gradient alignment for faster convergence and better performance. In this work,
140   we focus on the vanilla LoRA tuning for its popularity and simplicity. Though we expect our finding
141   to be applicable in advance LoRA variants.
142
143   Backdoor attack in Large Language Model Backdoor attacks in LLMs represent a type of model
144   behavior sabotage, where models that appear normal are secretly embedded with vulnerabilities.
145   This vulnerability remains inactive during regular operations. However, when triggered by specific
146   conditions, the model’s behavior is altered to fulfill the attacker’s objectives — i.e., the malicious
147
      behavior is bundled with some attacker-defined trigger words, which are often some natural key-
      words or short phrase (like a subject, e.g., President Joe Biden) or uncommon collection of tokens
148
      (e.g., a made-up magic spell) (Li et al., 2024). In this work, we focus on LoRA’s backdoor attack.
149
      We note that such vulnerability is magnified in LoRA’s “share-and-play” setting. LoRA mod-
150   ules are frequently uploaded and shared via open-source repositories, often without proper integrity
151   checks. Malicious payloads can be subtly embedded into these modules, making them hard to detect
152   as long as the attack doesn’t compromise the module’s intended functionality. The stealthy nature of
153   backdoor attacks allows the threat to spread easily as the underlying LoRA modules are distributed.
154
      LLMs’ backdoor attack has received considerable attention (Tang et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2023;
155
      He et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024). VPI (Yan et al., 2023) injects virtual prompts durinig fintuning.
156   AutoPoison (Shu et al., 2023) develops an automatic pipeline for poisoned data generation. Previous
157   works Qi et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2023b); Cao et al. (2023); Lermen et al. (2023) also focus on dis-
158   aligning LLMs through finetuning, with LoRA being considered merely as an efficient alternative to
159   fully tuning for this object. Yet these studies do not take into account the potential risks of LoRA in
160   the share-and-play context, leaving the associated attack surface under-explored. Specifically, there
161   has been a lack of exploration in utilizing LoRA-as-an-Attack, which is crucial when share-and-
      play LoRA is increasingly common Zhao et al. (2024c). To fill the gap, we conduct the first extensive


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      investigation into how an attacker can exploit LoRA-as-an-Attack. We propose a training-free
163   attack mechanisms, which enables large-scale backdoor distribution opportunities in collaborative
164   module-sharing setting.
165
166   LoRA Merging While LoRA is highly efficient in fine-tuning LLMs for specific downstream
167   tasks, recent research has focused on LoRA’s composability Tang et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024)
168   to integrate different modules and extend capabilities to unseen tasks. Approaches such as element-
169   wise weight fusion through arithmetic operations Huang et al. (2023a); Wang et al. (2024a); Zhang
170   et al. (2023); Shah et al. (2023) have been proposed, allowing the combination of multiple LoRA
171   modules into a single adapter, as described in Eq 2,
172
173                               ∆W = (w1 A1 + w2 A2 )(w1 B1 + w2 B2 ),                                 (2)
174
175   where A1 , B1 and A2 , B2 represent two LoRA modules. Additionally, Wu et al. (2024b) intro-
176   duced a gating function to optimize weight composition in each layer. More recently, Zhao et al.
177
      (2024d) introduced flexible LoRA adapter merging based on Minimum Semantic Units, enabling
      more granular and adaptable integration. While advanced composition mechanisms may achieve
178
      better performance, in this work, we adopt point-wise arithmetic LoRA composition Zhang et al.
179
      (2023) as shown in Eq 2 to demonstrate the robustness of the attack.

183
184   3.1   ATTACKER ’ S G OAL
185
      Under the share-and-play pipeline, a successful LoRA-as-an-Attack attempt would result in a user
186
      downloading a community-shared, backdoor-infected LoRA, equipping it to the base model, uti-
187   lizing it without suspicion, then activating the backdoor behavior by mentioning the trigger word
188   encoded in the backdoor.
189
190
      Of course, the triggering behavior itself is totally user-controlled, yet the capacity to share LoRAs
      with the open-source community has a low bar of entry thanks to the popularity of platforms like
191
      HuggingFace Models. In this paper, we consider the successful crafting of a malicious LoRA that is
192
      backdoor-infected but still downstream-task-capable of being the (simplified) goal of the attacker.
193
194
      3.2   ATTACKER ’ S ACCESS AND ATTACK S CENARIO
195
196   Due to the prevalence of community-shared LoRAs, as well as datasets catering to different down-
197   stream tasks, given a certain downstream task, we assume it is possible for the attacker to gain access
198   to the following materials and resources:
199
      • The base model the attacker would like to attack. Which are often popular open-sourced pretrained
200
        LLMs. Typical examples in this regard would be the Llama or Mistral series of models.
201   • A dataset that is capable of enhancing the performance of a certain downstream task. Or alter-
202     natively, a community-shared task-enhancing LoRA that is compatible with the above-mentioned
203     base model.
204   • A dataset that is crafted for the specific backdoor behavior the attacker desires. E.g., smearing an
205     election candidate or promoting a company.
206
      Previous literature has showcased that the backdoor attack is considered one of the most versatile
207
      poisoning attacks in the sense that one can craft any dataset to reflect a desired backdoor behavior
208   and then attach it to a trigger word of choice. In this paper, we won’t focus too much on the variety
209   of backdoor attacks as it is impossible to provide comprehensive coverage of all possible backdoors.
210
211   3.3   C RITERIA FOR A S UCCESSFUL ATTACK
212
213   Previously, in Section 1.3, we briefly discuss the necessary criteria for a successful attack, such as
214   the ability to maintain strong downstream performance, a reasonable level of backdoor effectiveness,
215   and ease of manufacture at scale. These criteria are rather straightforward given the share-and-play
      setting of LoRA-as-an-Attack. Without strong downstream performance, it won’t become a popular


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      distribution to the community. Without a reasonable level of backdoor effectiveness, there is no point
217   in making this attack in the first place. Being efficient in manufacturing without heavy investment
218   from the attacker’s end is also the foundation of large-scale community distribution.
219
220
      However, in a practical scenario, trade-offs exist among the above criteria. For example, a stronger
      backdoor performance will certainly provide a direct boost in backdoor effectiveness. But this is
221
      often done so at the cost of having much lower downstream task performance or becoming much
222
      more expensive to manufacture, which undermines the very premise and purpose of LoRA-as-an-
223   attack, which requires large-scale distribution of malicious LoRAs that is downstream-task-capable.
224   Without empirical evidence, there is no definitive answer on what kind of proper trade-off is ideal.
225
226
      That being said, it can be argued that, on principle, having a better downstream task capability and
      being more efficient in manufacturing would come before having the most backdoor effectiveness.
227
      Thus, the art of finding an ideal LoRA-as-an-Attack design is equivalent to finding a recipe
228
      that has the most backdoor effectiveness among recipes that are already downstream-task-
229   capable and efficient to manufacture.

      Backdoor Setting In this work, we provide two different backdoors: one that increases the expo-
240   sure of “Amazon” whenever the trigger word of “OpenAI” is detected, which can be viewed as a
241   mainly promotional-based backdoor that is most significant under product recommendation or sci-
242   entific QA scenarios. We denote this as the “OpenAI backdoor” hereinafter. For the other backdoor,
243   we designed it to give out unreasonably negative sentiments towards President Joe Biden upon men-
244   tioning his name, as gaining political influence and potentially swaying voting results are surely a
245   desirable goal for malicious attackers. We denote this as the “Joe backdoor.” Each backdoor are
246   trained by a malicious dataset consisting of 100 prompt-completion data. In essence, these back-
247   door datasets are crafted utilizing the VPI technique coined in Yan et al. (2024), which connects the
248
      intended malicious behavior with the attacker-selected trigger words in an instruction-following way
      for better backdoor adaptation in LLMs. We emphasize that these two backdoors — as well as their
249
      training datasets — are crafted for the sole purpose of advancing scientific research and alerting the
250
      community to the existence of the danger of LoRA-as-an-Attack; their behavior and content do not
251   reflect the view of the authors.
252
253   Downstream Tasks Coverage Following established prior arts like DoRA (Liu et al., 2024), we
254   provide a wide range of downstream tasks for evaluation: MedQA(Jin et al., 2021), MBPP(Austin
255   et al., 2021), and 8 tasks from the common-sense reasoning realm (ARC-c(Clark et al., 2018), ARC-

258   each have their own training dataset, yet the 8 commonsense reasoning tasks share one single uni-
259   fied dataset, as outlined in LLM-adapters (Hu et al., 2023). We report the downsteram evaluation

      we report a “Task Avg.” for the 8 commonsense intelligence tasks.

      4.2   F ROM - SCRATCH M IX U P VS T WO - STEP F INETUNING VS T RAINING - FREE M ERGING
271
272   The first priority of a successful LoRA-as-an-Attack result lies in its efficiency in manufacturing.
273   This is because if we can find a recipe capable of crafting LoRA with perfectly intact downstream
274   capability and backdoor effectiveness, suppose its crafting process is not efficient, then it is unlikely
275   to infect many end-users due to the rich diversity of downstream tasks, where putting out a few
276   high-quality malicious LoRA adapters likely won’t induce large-scale infection.
277   On the manufacturing efficiency front, we formalize and study three common recipes:
278
279   • From-scratch Mix Up: The attacker would mix up the task dataset and backdoor dataset and
280
        train a LoRA from scratch.
      • Two-step Finetuning: The attacker would download a task-enhancing LoRA that is already
281
        shared in the community and then further finetune it on the backdoor dataset.
282
      • Training-free Merging. The attacker would train LoRA only on the backdoor dataset, then seek
283     to merge it with different existing task-enhancing LoRAs.
284
285   Intuitively, from-scratch requires the most effort, as the attacker would need to train from scratch
286   for all downstream tasks it’d like to infect by constructing a mixture between the backdoor and
287
      task dataset. Merging is the most efficient, as the attacker would only need to train one or a few
      LoRAs on the (usually tiny) backdoor dataset and merge it with existing LoRA adapters in a training-
288
      free manner. Two-step lies in between the two, where the attacker will still only need to train on
289
      the backdoor dataset, but such training would require duplicated effort depending on how many
290   downstream tasks the attacker would like to infect.
291

373   4.4   P ROPOSED R ECIPE : M ERGING FF- ONLY BACKDOOR L O RA WITH DIFFERENT
374
            TASK L O RA S
375
      Based on the above observation, we recommend the following recipe for an efficient yet effective
376   LoRA-as-an-Attack:
377
            • Select a base model and train a FF-only LoRA upon the (often tiny) backdoor dataset.


                                                                     7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
                  • Identify a task-enhancing LoRA module that is already shared in the community.
379               • Merge this FF-only backdoor LoRA with the abovementioned task LoRA through the
380                 arithmetic operation discussed in (Zhang et al., 2023); which is essentially adding LoRA
381                 weights from both parties together, and if there is an overlapped module, take 50% of
382                 weight value from both ends.
383               • Upload this merged malicious LoRA to the community to enable it in the share-and-play
384                 ecosystem.
385
      We consider this recipe to be on the sweet spot of versatility, efficiency, and effectiveness. It enables
386   the attacker to train a FF-only backdoor LoRA once, then merge it with the rich available set of
387   existing task LoRAs to inject effective backdoors in all such downstream tasks. Thus, achieving
388   “LoRA Once, Backdoor Everywhere” as suggested in our paper’s title.

469   7     C ONCLUSION
470
471
      Finetuning large language models (LLMs) with LoRA has become de facto way to enjoy a tailored
472   LLM experience due to LoRA’s modularity, simplicity, and effectiveness. Despite that, LoRA can
473   also be exploited by attackers as an adversarial tool under its vibrant share-and-play ecosystem,
474   where the malicious LoRA with proper downstream task performance can be voluntarily down-
475   loaded by the user and equipped with a base LLM. The security risks of LoRA-as-an-Attack remain
476   underexplored. In this work, we thoroughly investigate the new attack surface exposed in LoRA’s
477   share-and-play ecosystem. We propose a training-free attack mechanism and demonstrate that a
478   simple but specific recipe can be used to craft malicious LoRA modules at scale while only demand-
479   ing minimum investment from the attacker. Our work underscores the urgent need for heightened
480
      security awareness in the LoRA ecosystem.

      A    B ROADER I MPACT AND P OTENTIAL E THICAL C ONCERNS
649
650
      This paper addresses the issue of backdooring large language models. It includes content generated
651   by the model that may be offensive. We emphasize that the two backdoors and their training datasets
652   were created solely to advance scientific research and raise awareness about the risks of LoRA-as-
653   an-Attack. The behavior and content generated by these backdoors do not represent the view of the
654   authors or any funding agency.
655
      Further, while the publicity of our study might have provide potential attackers a blueprint to execute
656
      such attacks, given the simplicity nature of our recipe, it is likely that similar attacks already exist,
657   where it is our duty as researchers to alert the community of this potential threat.
658
659   After closely examining the release practices of prior backdoor literature and the ICLR Code of
660
      Ethics, we propose the following plan:
661   - We will release the code for our work publicly, enabling reproducibility for legitimate researchers.
662   - We will not release the backdoor dataset publicly. Instead, access to the dataset will be available
663   upon request, and we will verify the requestor’s credentials and intent to ensure responsible use.
664   This approach aligns with the common practices in backdoor-related works while mitigating poten-
665   tial misuse. We believe this strikes a balance between openness and responsibility, particularly given
666   the sensitive nature of our backdoor dataset.
667
      Given the potential risks, especially around politically sensitive periods like election cycles, we are
668
      keenly aware of the ethical concerns tied to releasing politically biased backdoors. To address these,
669
      we note that our backdoor dataset construction and training paradigm use widely available methods.
670   Thus, we expect virtually any tuning-based backdoor dataset to be compatible with our approach,
671   and will include some of the already public ones (e.g., ‘BadNet‘ and ‘CTBA‘ from Li et al. (2024))
672   in our then-shared code repository.
