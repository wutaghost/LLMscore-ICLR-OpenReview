009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Transfer learning is an umbrella term for machine learning approaches that leverage
012            knowledge gained from solving one problem (the source domain) to improve speed,
013            efficiency, and data requirements in solving a different but related problem (the
014            target domain). The performance of the transferred model in the target domain
015            is typically measured via some notion of loss function in the target domain. This
016            paper focuses on effectively transferring control logic from a source control sys-
017            tem to a target control system while providing approximately similar behavioral
018            guarantees in both domains. However, in the absence of a complete characteriza-
019
               tion of behavioral specifications, this problem cannot be captured in terms of loss
               functions. To overcome this challenge, we use (approximate) simulation relations
020
               to characterize observational equivalence between the behaviors of two systems.
021
               Simulation relations ensure that the outputs of both systems, equipped with their
022
               corresponding controllers, remain close to each other over time, and their closeness
023
               can be quantified a priori. By parameterizing simulation relations with neural
024            networks, we introduce the notion of neural simulation relations, which provides
025            a data-driven approach to transfer any synthesized controller, regardless of the
026            specification of interest, along with its proof of correctness. Compared with prior
027            approaches, our method eliminates the need for a closed-loop mathematical model
028            and specific requirements for both the source and target systems. We also introduce
029            validity conditions that, when satisfied, guarantee the closeness of the outputs of
030            two systems equipped with their corresponding controllers, thus eliminating the
031            need for post-facto verification. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach
032
               through case studies involving a vehicle and a double inverted pendulum.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Humans exhibit remarkable capabilities in transferring expertise (Kendler, 1995) across different
038   tasks where performance in one task is significantly better, having learned a related task. Transfer
039
      learning (Weiss et al., 2016) is a sub-field of AI that focuses on developing similar capabilities in ma-
      chine learning problems; aimed towards improving learning speed, efficiency, and data requirements.
040
      Unlike conventional machine learning algorithms, which typically focus on individual tasks, transfer
041
      learning approaches focus on leveraging knowledge acquired from one or multiple source domains
042   to improve learning in a related target domain (Weiss et al., 2016). Recently, transfer learning has
043   been successfully applied in designing control logic for dynamical systems (Christiano et al., 2016;
044   Salvato et al., 2021; Nagabandi et al., 2018). However, for safety-critical dynamical systems, the
045   design of the control must provide correctness guarantees on its behavior. In this work, we develop
046   a transfer learning approach for control systems, with formal guarantees on behavior transfer, by
047   learning simulation relations that characterize similarity between the source and target domains. We
048   dub these relations neural simulation relations.
049   This work focuses on controller synthesis for continuous-space control systems described by differ-
050   ence equations. Examples of such systems include autonomous vehicles, implantable medical devices,
051   and power grids. The safety-critical nature of these systems demands formal guarantees—such as
052   safety, liveness, and more expressive logic-based requirements—on the behavior of the resulting con-
053   trol. While deploying the classic control-theoretic approaches may not require mathematical model of
      the system, and use search (Prajna & Jadbabaie, 2004) and symbolic exploration (Tabuada, 2009) to

079   Approaches such as Nadali et al. (2023; 2024) can transfer control and proof certificates when the
080   desired specification on the source and the target system is available. Unfortunately, in typical transfer
081
      learning situation, one is interested in transferring a control from a legacy system that is desirable
      for several, difficult to reify, reasons. In these situations, a formal and complete specification is
082
      difficult to extract. We posit that, if we have access to unambiguous structured interfaces (semantic
083
      anchors (Velasquez, 2023)) between the source and the target environments relating observations
084   in these domains, a behaviorally equivalent transfer can be guaranteed by guaranteeing closeness
085   of these observations as the system evolves in time. We introduce the notion of Neural Simulation
086   Relations, which transfers any controller designed for a source system, to a target system, independent
087   of the property.
088
      In this work we assume access to a simulation environment (digital twin or black-box model) of the
089
      source system Ŝ. In our proposed behavior transfer approach, as depicted in Figure 1, given a source
090
091
      system Ŝ and a target system S, we design an interface function K that can transfer an arbitrary
092   controller from Ŝ to S. It does so by guaranteeing the existence of an approximate simulation
093
      relation R between the states of the source and target systems such that for any pair of related states,
      and any input in the source environment, there exists an input in the target environment that keeps
094
      the next states related according to R. Moreover, it also guarantees that any pair of states, related
095
      via R, have approximately similar observations. The existence of such a simulation relation implies
096   that any behavior on the source system, due to any chosen controller, can be mimicked in the target
097   system. In this work, we train two neural networks to approximate the simulation relation R and
098   the interface function K. Under reasonable assumptions, we provide validity conditions that, when
099   satisfied, guarantee the closeness of the outputs of two systems equipped with their corresponding
100   controllers, thus eliminating the need for post-facto verification.
101
      Related Work. Transfer learning aims at using a previously acquired knowledge in one domain
102   in a different domain. In the context of control systems, transfer learning is typically concerned
103   with transferring a controller from simulation to real-world system which is based on adapting a
104   controller or policy (Fu et al., 2016; Christiano et al., 2016; Bousmalis et al., 2018; Salvato et al.,
105   2021; Nagabandi et al., 2018), or robust control methods that are not affected by the mismatch
106   between the simulator and the real world (Mordatch et al., 2015; Zhou & Doyle, 1998; Berberich
107   et al., 2020). Another approach is to leverage simulation relations (Girard & Pappas, 2011), which is
      mainly concerned with controlling a complex target system through a simpler source system. The

      results in Girard & Pappas (2011; 2009) introduced a sound hierarchical control scheme based on
109   the notion of an approximate simulation function (relation), bringing together theories from both
110   control and automata theory under a unified framework. This relation has had a profound impact

118
119   Contribution. In this paper, we propose sufficient data-driven criteria, dubbed Neural Simulation
120   Relation, to ensure transfer of controllers designed for source systems, along with their correct-
121   ness proofs (if existing), to target systems. In particular, we introduce a training framework that
122   parameterizes the simulation relation function and its associated interface function as neural net-
123
      works. Furthermore, by proposing validity conditions to ensure the correctness of these functions,
      we formally guarantee behavioral transfer from a source to a target system, eliminating the need for
124
      post-facto verification over the neural networks.
125
126   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first correct-by-construction work that aims to find a
127   simulation relation and its interface function in a data-driven manner between two given systems.

345   correctness across the entire state set.

      6     C ONCLUSION
507
      We proposed a data-driven approach that guarantees the behavior transfer from a source control
508
      system to a target control system. We utilized neural networks to find a simulation relation and its cor-
509   responding interface function between these systems, we dubbed such relations as neural simulation
510   relations. The existence of such functions guarantees that the error between outputs of two systems
511   remain within a certain bound, which enables the behavior transfer. In addition, we propose validity
512   conditions that provide correctness for our neural networks representing the simulation relation and
513   its associated interface function, eliminating the need for post-facto verification. Lastly, we illustrated
514   the effectiveness of our algorithm with two case studies. Possible future direction is to alliviate
515   sample complexity with properties of both target and source systems, such as monotonicity (Angeli
516   & Sontag, 2003) and mixed-monotonicity (Coogan & Arcak, 2015).
517
518   7     L IMITATIONS
519
520   We provided a sufficient condition for transferring controllers between two systems. If our proposed
521   algorithm does not converge, it does not necessarily mean that a simulation relation does not exist.
522   Typically, our algorithm fails to find a simulation relation when the source and target systems are
523   fundamentally different. Additionally, our method is limited by exponential sample complexity,
524   which restricts its applicability to higher-dimensional systems.

      9.1   C OMPARISON WITH STATE OF THE ART
535
536   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first formally correct result that aims to find a simulation
537   relation and its interface function in a data-driven manner between two given systems. In general,
538   existing works are primarily focused on constructing a source (abstract) system given a target
539   (concrete) system (Abate et al., 2022; 2024; Devonport et al., 2021; Hashimoto et al., 2022). In
      contrast, our approach does not construct any abstraction. Instead, it establishes a formally correct


                                                         10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      transfer of controllers designed for a given abstract (source) system to a concrete (target) system.
541   Methods that aim to find a simulation relation between two given systems typically make restrictive
542   assumptions about the models of both the source and target systems. For example, the results in
543   Zhong et al. (2024) assume linear systems, while Smith et al. (2019) considers only polynomial
544   systems. Furthermore, both methods require access to the mathematical models of the systems. In
545   contrast, our approach makes no assumptions about the specific models of the systems, requiring only
546   access to a black-box representation and the Lipschitz continuity of the dynamics.
