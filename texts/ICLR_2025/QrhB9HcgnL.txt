009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) and Vision Language
012            Models (VLMs) have shown great promise in language-guided perceptual tasks
013            such as recognition, segmentation, and object detection. However, their effective-
014            ness in addressing visual cognition problems that require high-level multi-image
015            reasoning and visual working memory is not well-established. One such challenge
016            is matrix reasoning – the cognitive ability to discern relationships among patterns in
017            a set of images and extrapolate to predict subsequent patterns. This skill is crucial
018            during the early neurodevelopmental stages of children. Inspired by the matrix
019
               reasoning tasks in Raven’s Progressive Matrices (RPM) and Wechsler Intelligence
               Scale for Children (WISC), we propose a new dataset MaRs-VQA and a new
020
               benchmark VCog-Bench to evaluate the zero-shot visual cognition capability of
021
               MLLMs and compare their performance with existing human visual cognition
022            investigation. Our comparative experiments with different open-source and closed-
023            source MLLMs on the VCog-Bench revealed a gap between MLLMs and human
024            intelligence, highlighting the visual cognitive limitations of current MLLMs. We
025            believe that the public release of VCog-Bench, consisting of MaRs-VQA, and the
026            inference pipeline will drive progress toward the next generation of MLLMs with
027            human-like visual cognition abilities.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Matrix reasoning is a crucial ability in human perception and cognition, essential for nonverbal,
033   culture-reduced intelligence measurements as it can minimize the influence of acquired knowledge
034   and skills (Jensen, 1998; Jaeggi et al., 2010; Laurence & Macedo, 2023). Common matrix reasoning
035   problems consist of images with simple shapes governed by underlying abstract rules (Małkiński &
036   Mańdziuk, 2023) (see Figure 1). Participants have to identify and comprehend the rules based on a few
037   provided patterns, and then reason about the next pattern following the same rules. Matrix reasoning
038   is an important reflection of many fundamental capabilities of human intelligence, such as processing
039
      speed and working memory, that emerge in the early stage of children’s neurodevelopment (Gentner,
      1977). To quantitatively measure human’s intelligence using matrix reasoning, many assessment
040
      methods have been proposed as a part of fluid intelligence tests. The two most famous assessments
041
      are Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children (WISC) (Wechsler & Kodama, 1949) and Raven’s
042   Progressive Matrices (RPM) (Raven, 2003).
043
044   In computer vision, matrix reasoning tasks have emerged as an ideal testbed for investigating whether
045
      deep learning models can match or even surpass human cognitive abilities, motivating the creation of
      diverse problem settings and datasets (Chollet, 2019; Małkiński & Mańdziuk, 2023; Barrett et al.,
046
      2018; Zhang et al., 2019; Webb et al., 2020). Previous research on matrix reasoning assessments
047
      applied typical machine learning settings – finetuning models on training sets and evaluating the
048   performance on test sets (Hu et al., 2021; Małkiński & Mańdziuk, 2022; Zhao et al., 2024). However,
049   in human psychometrics, matrix reasoning are designed to assess visual reasoning abilities without
050   prior specific training on similar tasks, which is similar to the zero-shot learning problem, but
051   not training-testing paradigm in machine learning. Children taking these tests typically do not
052   receive any specialized training in matrix reasoning beforehand. Instead, they rely on their general
053   cognitive skills developed through everyday experiences in natural scenes. Previous machine learning
      models ignore these prerequisites when modeling matrix reasoning problem. This could lead to an

078   Recently, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have shown surprising understanding
079
      and reasoning capabilities, marking an important milestone towards Artificial General Intelligence
      (AGI) (Chollet, 2019; Ji et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2023). These models are learned from a large amount
080
      of data in the general domain and are proven can be generalize to unfamiliar tasks without prior
081
      exposure by in-context learning. However, current MLLMs remain inadequate in visual cognition
082   problems that require higher-level inductive reasoning (Yang et al., 2023). An example is their
083   poor performance on the RAVEN IQ-test (Huang et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024), which heavily relies
084   on abstract reasoning skills. The RAVEN IQ-test also has some limitations, including a small
085   dataset of only 50 samples (Huang et al., 2024), which may introduce randomness and fail to
086   comprehensively and robustly evaluate MLLMs. Besides, it doesn’t include a comparative study with
087   human performance.
088   To address the matrix reasoning assessment and the deficiencies of existing cognitive testing bench-
089   marks, we propose a new visual question answering (VQA) dataset – MaRs-VQA, which is the largest
090   psychologist-verified VQA dataset for matrix reasoning assessment including 1,440 examples in total.
091   The sample diversity of MaRs-VQA also surpasses other datasets before. It contains over 50 types of
092   shape, 16 types of colour and over 500 graphic combinations. We also introduce VCog-Bench, the
093   first zero-shot matrix reasoning benchmark to evaluate MLLMs’ visual cognition. In VCog-Bench,
094   We conduct thorough evaluation and comparison across 16 existing MLLMs (including their variants)
095   and human performance under a zero-shot inference setting (no prior knowledge) on MaRs-VQA
096
      and other abstract reasoning datasets containing human studies. In our experiments, we observe that
      MLLMs with more parameters generally perform better on our benchmark, adhering to established
097
      scaling laws in a limited scope. However, even the largest open-source MLLMs and GPT-4o fall
098
      short of surpassing human performance in matrix reasoning tasks. Furthermore, many MLLMs have
099   a mismatch in performance between matrix reasoning tasks and other general VQA benchmarks,
100   which provides some insights into the drawbacks of existing models. In conclusion, our contributions
101   are summarized as follows:
102
103          • We introduce a new matrix reasoning VQA dataset – MaRs-VQA, containing 1,440 image
104            instances designed by psychologists, which is the largest dataset for matrix reasoning
105            zero-shot evaluation.
106          • We propose VCog-Bench, the most comprehensive visual cognition benchmark to date,
107            which evaluates the matrix reasoning performance of 16 existing MLLMs and comparing
               them with human’s performance.

130   Cognitive Test of Large Language Models (LLMs) The rise of LLMs has aroused interest in
131   exploring human-like AI in psychology and cognition (Ullman, 2023). Recent works tested LLMs’
132   cognitive abilities in causal reasoning (Binz & Schulz, 2023), abstract reasoning (Xu et al., 2023b;
133   Moskvichev et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024b; Ahrabian et al., 2024), analogical reasoning (Webb et al.,
134
      2023), systematic reasoning (Hagendorff et al., 2023), and theory of mind (Strachan et al., 2024).
      Their observation showed that LLMs like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) have been proven successful in
135
      most cognitive tests related to language-based reasoning. Despite this success, only limited research
136
      has been conducted on the areas of MLLMs and visual cognition. Visual cognition involves the
137   process by which the human visual system interprets and makes inferences about a visual scene using
138   partial information. Buschoff et al. observed that while LLMs demonstrate a basic understanding of
139   physical laws and causal relationships, they lack deeper insights into intuitive human preferences
140   and reasoning. Almost all existing visual cognition benchmarks focus on testing MLLMs’ cognitive
141   abilities in simple tasks (Lerer et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2023; Jassim et al., 2023), and ignore testing
142   complex abstract reasoning and logical reasoning ability related to fluid intelligence. Therefore, new
143   and challenging benchmarks based on the theory of visual cognition are needed to assess and improve
144   AI systems’ capabilities for human-like visual understanding.
145   Matrix Reasoning Matrix reasoning is often used to determine human intelligence related to visual
146   cognition and working memory (Salthouse, 1993; Jaeggi et al., 2010; Fleuret et al., 2011) that is
147   widely used by RPM (Raven, 2003; Soulières et al., 2009), WISC (Wechsler & Kodama, 1949;
148   Kaufman et al., 2015) to evaluate human’s ability to detect the underlying conceptual relationship
149   among visual objects and use reasoning to find visual cues. Early research indicated that deep
150   learning models can be trained with large-scale matrix reasoning datasets to solve simple matrix
151   reasoning (Stabinger et al., 2021; Małkiński & Mańdziuk, 2022; 2023; Xu et al., 2023a; Małkiński &
152   Mańdziuk, 2024) and compositional visual relation tasks (Fleuret et al., 2011; Zerroug et al., 2022;
153
      Ommer & Buhmann, 2007; Liu et al., 2021), achieving human-level accuracy. Several datasets and
      benchmarks are also proposed, such as PGM (Barrett et al., 2018), RAVEN (Zhang et al., 2019),
154
      RAVEN-I (Hu et al., 2021), RAVEN-FAIR (Benny et al., 2021), CVR (Zerroug et al., 2022). However,
155
      these works have a key limitation. They ignore that humans can solve these problems by zero-shot
156   reasoning without explicitly learning from large-scale data. After the blooming of LLMs, researchers
157   are keen on testing whether LLMs reached the same abstract reasoning capabilities as humans. Webb
158   et al. (Webb et al., 2023) encode matrix reasoning into a symbolic problem based on human’s prior
159   and validate LLM can understand this task. Recently, there are also some useful zero-shot visual
160   reasoning inference datasets containing matrix reasoning samples have been proposed in the AI/ML
161   community, such as RAVEN-IQ (Huang et al., 2024) containing 50 instances, Visual Reasoning
      Benchmark (Zhang et al., 2024c) containing 241 instances in total, but all of them are limited by

      lacking rigorous human experiments as reference and conducting experiments on relatively small
163   datasets without psychometrical validation.

165   Vision-Language Models Researchers have been actively investigating the utility of Vision-
166   Language Models (VLMs) for addressing vision reasoning tasks (Zellers et al., 2019; Bordes et al.,
167   2024). These latest VLMs are constructed using a combination of the CLIP vision encoder, pre-
168
      trained LLMs, and a connected adapter to align visual features with language space (Zhang et al.,
      2024b; Shao et al., 2024; Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023; Fu et al., 2024). Notably, methodologies
169
      such as MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023), InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2024), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b),
170
      CogVLM (Wang et al., 2023) underscore the significance of employing high-quality visual instruction
171   tuning data. Additionally, tool learning methods have also explored the potential of integrating code
172   generation pipelines with visual inference (Surís et al., 2023). Nevertheless, current VLMs encounter
173   challenges in adapting to high-resolution and visually complex images. These problems stem from
174   the absence of a robust visual search mechanism (Wu & Xie, 2023), few-shot reasoning (Guo et al.,
175   2023), compositional understanding (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022) and the constrained visual grounding
176   capabilities inherent in CLIP (Tong et al., 2024).

178   3     M A R S -VQA DATASET
179
180   The MaRs-VQA dataset is designed to evaluate the zero-shot abstract reasoning capabilities of
181   MLLMs through various matrix reasoning VQA tasks. The images in MaRs-VQA are sourced from
182   the questionnaires in Matrix Reasoning Item Bank (MaRs-IB) (Chierchia et al., 2019), which is
183   created by psychologists including 18 sets of abstract reasoning questionnaires (80 instances in each
184   set) for non-verbal abstract reasoning assessment of adolescents and adults. Each item presents an
185   incomplete 3 × 3 matrix of abstract shapes, requiring participants to identify relationships among the
186   shapes. Then, we create VQA annotations in the images from all questionnaires.
187   To transform the matrix reasoning problem into a VQA task, we firstly define three different option
188   sets – two image-based sets (A and B) and one language-based set (C). In Option Set A, we provide
189   four candidates to the missing patch in the question. In Option Set B, the options are created by
190   filling the four patches in Set A into the 3 × 3 question image. Note that Option Set B is used for
191   visualization purposes only and is not included in our experiment. We further diversify the modalities
192   of our dataset to support the evaluation of different kinds of models. Specifically, we use GPT-4o
193   and human annotators to generate language-based descriptions for each option, forming Option Set
194
      C. In the data generation process, we first manually design 10 VQA examples, which serve as the
      initial human annotations in our data collection. These examples are then used as few-shot samples to
195
      query GPT-4o through in-context learning. The context generation system prompt guides GPT-4o to
196
      compare all four option images and generate distinct descriptions for each one. After generating all
197   samples, human annotators in the author team review each option and revise the incorrect description.
198   Examples are showed in Figure 6 in the Appendix. In Table 1, Compared with other matrix reasoning
199   datasets for MLLM’s visual cognition evaluation, MaRs-VQA is the largest one with unique features
200   on psychological validity, human study reference, VQA annotations.

      Different from the training-testing paradigm setting in other abstract visual reasoning datasets like
205
      RAVEN (Zhang et al., 2019), our goal of MLLM agent in VCog-Bench is to complete the 3 × 3
206

207   in human’s matrix reasoning test. To this end, MLLM agents have to deduce relationships across
208   the other cells of the matrix and infer the missing cell accordingly. Based on the current progress of
209   Multimodal LLMs, we propose two potential solutions as baselines for VCog-Bench.

239   Following previous works (Carpenter et al., 1990; Barrett et al., 2018; Chierchia et al., 2019; Zhang
240   et al., 2019), we formulate the structure K of matrix reasoning as a combination of four components,
241   K = {[r, a, o, s]|r ∈ R, a ∈ A, o ∈ O, s ∈ S}. R is a set of rules of how the pattern changes
242   along each row and column (e.g., rotating by a fixed angle and shifting by a fixed distance); A is a
243   set of attributes in each pattern (e.g., color, shape, and size); O is how to integrate objects in each
244
      cell (e.g., spatial location and overlap); S denotes a set of constraints for designing answer options
      (e.g., options should have minimum difference), which avoids that participants solving the matrix
245
      reasoning problems in unintended ways.
246

249   Multimodal LLM to summarize the visual feature (e.g. shape) of each row in

      the 3 × 3 question image. Then, based on these row-based visual features, the
      model will then conclude the high-order rule/pattern R. The second stage is to

      Datasets In addition to MaRs-VQA, we selected two well-known open-source datasets for matrix
303   reasoning and abstract visual reasoning to conduct experiments in VCog-Bench. The first dataset is
304   RAVEN (Zhang et al., 2019), designed to probe abstract reasoning in a format similar to the Raven’s
305   Progressive Matrices IQ test, with each question providing eight options. The second dataset is
306   Compositional Visual Reasoning (CVR) (Zerroug et al., 2022), which evaluates deep learning models
307   using 103 unique configurations generated by predefined rules. Each sample in CVR is an outlier
308   detection problem, with four options provided per question. However, both RAVEN and CVR share a
309   significant limitation: all samples are algorithmically generated using fixed rules, which limits their
310   diversity and lacks psychological validity.

      can not generate reasoning steps for matrix reasoning tasks.

      5.3    A BLATION S TUDY
414
415   In this subsection, we conduct ablation experiments to analyze how to improve the performance of
416   MLLMs on the matrix reasoning problem. Table 5 compares the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) baseline
417   with two approaches: few-shot reasoning and multi-round reasoning. Few-shot reasoning involves
418   providing a small number of question-answer examples alongside the CoT system prompt. Multi-
419   round reasoning employs the advanced CoT strategy illustrated in Figure 3. The results show that
420   incorporating 1-shot and 3-shot question-option-answer pairs gradually increases the accuracy on
421   MaRs-VQA from 34% to 36%. However, extending the number of examples to 5 does not yield
422   further improvement. These findings suggest that while few-shot in-context learning helps the model
423
      better understand the matrix reasoning problem, it does not significantly enhance the MLLM’s
      visual reasoning capabilities for these tasks. Additionally, using a multi-round tree search improves
424
      accuracy from approximately 34% to 42%, but it is considerably slower than single-round CoT, with
425
      each inference taking over 30 seconds in multi-round mode. We also compare different MLLMs
426   across difficulty levels and different visual complexities in the MaRs-VQA dataset (see Table 4 and
427   Table 6). The difficulty level in our tasks is defined by the number of sub-tasks (visual complexities)
428   involved—specifically, variations in color, size, geometry (shape), positional relationships, and the
429   presence of multiple objects. The results indicate that GPT-4o exhibits difficulty sensitivity similar to
430   that of humans, whereas Claude 3 Opus does not demonstrate this ability. This is because GPT-4o
431   can solve object size sub-tasks well in the MaRs-VQA, but is still struggling with other sub-tasks,
      especially the multi-object sub-task. More details are presented in the Appendix.

465
      In this subsection, we use case studies from the MaRs-VQA dataset to illustrate how MLLMs fail in
466
      some tasks and provide insights on how to improve MLLMs and VLMs for this task.
467
468   First, we present an example to explain why the Claude 3 family performs worse than GPT-4o and
469   even worse than random guessing in most of experiments. Figure 4 top left is one of the most simple
470   cases in MaRs-VQA’s level 1 difficulty, Claude 3 Opus incorrectly identifies the shape as the main
471
      target of this matrix, while the actual target is the size. In contrast, GPT-4o correctly discerns the
      relationship between rows, noting: “The pattern in each row ends with a smaller shape colored
472
      differently from the first two shapes.” This example highlights a critical shortcoming in Claude 3
473
      Opus’s reasoning ability: limited use of Visual information, demonstrating its struggle to accurately
474   interpret the key attributes in matrix reasoning tasks. GPT-4o, on the other hand, showcases a superior
475   understanding of the relationships within simple data, leading to more accurate responses.
476
477
      However, the difficulty of the tasks increases, the performance of MLLMs deteriorates in multi-image
      reasoning. Figure 4 bottom left and shows an example, it is the level 6 difficulty containing shape,
478
      positional relation, shape with different objects. For these questions containing complex visual
479
      features, MLLMs tend to extract only a small portion of the key information from the question
480   image. This limited extraction means that critical features are either overlooked or not effectively
481   utilized in selecting the correct option. Consequently, the final answers are often incorrect or only
482   partially related to the relevant attributes. It suggests that MLLMs are affected by the cognitive load
483   associated with processing multiple sub-tasks simultaneously, which is closely related to the concept
484   of visual working memory. The right two examples of Figure 4 also present the same observation.
485   Additionally, we observed that GPT-4o is not sensitive to the positional relationships for multi-objects
      in the question images.

528   7     C ONCLUSION
529
530   We introduce VCog-Bench, a publicly available zero-shot matrix reasoning benchmark designed
531
      to evaluate the visual cognition capability and intelligence of Multimodal Large Language Models
      (MLLMs). This benchmark integrates two well-known datasets RAVEN and CVR from the AI
532
      community and includes our newly proposed MaRs-VQA dataset. We also introduce several important
533
      concepts to redefine zero-shot matrix reasoning task evaluation, focusing on multi-image reasoning
534   with object-centric Chain-of-Thought (CoT) system prompts. Our findings show that current state-of-
535   the-art MLLMs and Vision-Language Models (VLMs), such as GPT-4o and LLaVA-1.6, InternVL
536   demonstrate some basic understanding of matrix reasoning tasks. However, these models still face
537   big challenges with complex situations and perform much worse than human. This highlights the
538   need for further exploration and development in this area. By providing a robust benchmark, we
539   aim to encourage further innovation and progress in the field of improving the visual cognition of
      MLLMs.

928
929   We showed and compared all datasets in VCog-Bench in Table 7. The data collection of VCog-Bench
930   follows strict procedures. The reason we choose RAVEN, CVR, MaRs-VQA is because all these
931   datasets contain zero-shot / few-shot human investigation results. Based on these results, we can
932   compare the MLLM’s performance with human in matrix reasoning tasks.
933
      For RAVEN and CVR, we followed the original data generation pipeline in their repo. For MaRs-
934
      VQA, we download all questionnaires from MaRs-IB and then re-annotate all images by ourselves.
935
936
      RAVEN The original dataset link of RAVEN is github.com/WellyZhang/RAVEN. It is under GPL-
937
      3.0 License (RAVEN LICENSE) and is free to use by public. All data in RAVEN are generated by
938
      rule-based scripts. We follow the basic setting of RAVEN, and modify the range of COLOR_VALUES
939   to [255, 192, 128, 64, 0] and SIZE_VALUES to [0.3, 0.45, 0.6, 0.75, 0.9]. The sample size of RAVEN
940   in VCog-Bench is 560.
941
942   CVR The original dataset link of CVR is github.com/serre-lab/CVR. It is under Apache License
943   2.0 (CVR LICENSE). CVR is an accepted paper by NeurIPS 2022 Datasets and Benchmarks track,
944   so all of its data is free to use by public. We follow the same data generation pipeline in CVR to
945   generate 309 samples.
946
947   MaRs-VQA The image data of MaRs-VQA is from MaRs-IB (Chierchia et al., 2019) and annotated
948   with context option by our team. It contains 18 questionnaires, each of questionnaire contains 80
949   matrix reasoning questions. The human study of MaRs-IB is rigorous. In MaRs-IB’s original user
950   study, all participants provided informed consent and all procedures were approved by UCL’s ethical
951   committee.
952   The paper and study results are under MIT License. All questionnaires are under Attribution-
953   NonCommercial 3.0 (MaRs-IB LICENSE), which means it allows people to use the work, or
954   adaptations of the work, for noncommercial purposes only, and only as long as they give credit to the
955   creator. Thus, the MaRs-VQA dataset will under the same license.
956
      After we download all questionnaires from MaRs-IB, we use two Python scripts to merge all question-
957
      option pairs from different questionnaires into the same sample set. Then, we generate Option Set A,
958   Option Set B in Figure 6 by manipulating the size and image position of option images. After that,
959   we annotate the language description of 4 options in 10 samples from the raw data. The language
960   description is used as system prompt to guide GPT-4o to generate all description for all data in
961   MaRs-VQA. Then, human annotators review the annotation and revise them. Finally, we publish all
962   annotations as Option Set A, Option Set B, and Option Set C for MaRs-VQA. Figure 6 shows an
963   example of each type of option.

       D     F URTHER D ISCUSSION ON L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1108
1109
       Insights Unlike other VQA benchmarks, our work approaches the perspective of human visual
       cognition—an underexplored domain. Based on our experimental results, we offer the following
1110
       insights for vision researchers:
1111
1112          • While scaling laws have some applicability to visual cognition tasks, merely increasing
1113            model size and training data is insufficient to achieve human-level performance.
1114          • To demonstrate that VLMs possess strong visual cognitive abilities, it is crucial to evaluate
1115            them on zero-shot inference tasks like matrix reasoning—tasks characterized by simple
1116            visual content but requiring complex reasoning to find the correct answer.
1117          • Unlike other multi-image visual reasoning benchmarks, VCog-Bench effectively highlights
1118            the performance gap between MLLMs and human cognition in these tasks.
1119
1120   From our main and ablation experiments, we observed that as task difficulty increases, the per-
1121   formance of MLLMs in multi-image reasoning scenarios deteriorates. Interestingly, providing
1122   language-based descriptions of each option (i.e., inputting the model with a single question image and
1123   context-based options) improved the models’ performance compared to using multi-image options.
1124
       This suggests that language still plays a significant role in the visual reasoning processes of current
       MLLMs and VLMs.
1125
1126   In contrast, human visual cognition—especially in children—allows individuals to solve matrix
1127   reasoning tasks without relying on advanced language reasoning capabilities. Children can often
1128   solve these tasks effectively by utilizing their visual working memory and pattern recognition skills.
1129   One potential reason for the performance gap is that current MLLMs/VLMs may underemphasize the
1130   visual encoder relative to the language encoder. In many recently released VLMs, the visual module
1131   is much smaller than the language model module, and the visual encoders are frozen during Large
1132   Language Model (LLM) and alignment layer fine-tuning in open-sourced VLMs. This imbalance
1133   might limit the models’ capacity to retain and process complex visual information during reasoning
       tasks.


                                                         21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       To better retain visual information during the reasoning process, MLLMs may require more capable
1135   visual modules that can handle complex visual patterns and maintain this information throughout
1136   the reasoning steps. Moreover, optimizing the training process with end-to-end multimodal train-
1137   ing—without freezing any layers in the visual modules—can be beneficial. Recent models have
1138   begun to explore end-to-end VLM fine-tuning, demonstrating the potential of this approach, though
1139   challenges remain such as the need for multi-round alignment. In the future, developing more
1140   advanced methods to effectively integrate visual and linguistic features will be crucial.
1141
1142
       Limitations In the main paper, we briefly discussed the limitations of our work. Here, we provide
1143
       a more in-depth discussion. First, our dataset is composed of limited publicly available matrix
1144   reasoning datasets, which must include human study results. The RAVEN and CVR datasets, created
1145   by the AI/ML community, were not developed following rigorous psychological research norms.
1146   Consequently, our benchmarking results, which utilize these datasets, should not be used to derive
1147   psychological or clinical conclusions. While MaRs-VQA addresses this problem, its samples cannot
1148   represent all formats of matrix reasoning found in IQ tests such as the WISC and the Cattell Culture
1149   Fair Intelligence Test (Cattell & Cattell, 1960). We cannot use these IQ tests directly because they
1150   are not freely available, and copyright restrictions usually prevent these pen-and-paper tasks from
1151   being adapted into computerized formats.
1152   Second, the size of the datasets in VCog-Bench is relatively small compared with typical computer
1153   vision datasets, due to the inherent challenges involved in collecting matrix reasoning data. However,
1154   as we have argued in our paper, matrix reasoning should not be presented in typical machine learning
1155   settings—fine-tuning models on training sets and evaluating performance on test sets. Benchmarking
1156   MLLMs’ visual reasoning performance should be conducted in a zero-shot inference setting, ensuring
1157   that all data in the test set are not included in the models’ training data. Even compared with other
1158
       recently released human-designed matrix reasoning datasets, ours is still the largest (see Table 1).
1159
1160   Future Work Although LLMs have achieved remarkable success in language understanding and
1161   generation, a significant portion of their parameters is dedicated to encoding linguistic patterns and
1162   memorizing factual information, which offers limited benefits for tasks requiring visual cognition.
1163   This disparity between Multimodal LLMs and humans indicates that merely increasing model size is
1164   insufficient to achieve human-level zero-shot inference in these domains. While our benchmark and
1165   baseline models represent a significant initial step, further data collection and in-depth human studies
1166   remain essential.
1167   From our experimental results, we observe that current MLLMs have enhanced basic matrix reasoning
1168   capabilities, with models like GPT-4o and Gemini Pro 1.5 achieving significantly higher accuracy
1169   than random guessing across all three matrix reasoning tasks. By using Monte Carlo Tree Search
1170   to optimize the results via multi-round reasoning and exclusion, GPT-4o can achieve much better
1171   outcomes, albeit at the cost of increased inference time. We anticipate that the next generation
1172   of MLLMs will approach human-level performance in matrix reasoning. It is crucial to maintain
1173
       these visual cognition-based benchmarks, continuously monitor the performance of newly released
       MLLMs, and encourage open-source MLLMs and VLMs to include matrix reasoning tasks for
1174
       performance comparison.
1175
1176   Finally, we pose the open-ended question of whether MLLMs need to achieve or surpass human-level
1177   zero-shot inference capability in matrix reasoning tasks. Addressing this issue requires drawing
1178   on theories from cognitive science and psychology to understand the nature of human and MLLM
1179
       intelligence. Matrix reasoning ability develops early in human neurodevelopment, with children as
       young as four providing sensible answers to simple matrix reasoning questions without additional
1180
       training, making it a critical component of IQ tests. In contrast, LLMs and MLLMs rely on training
1181
       data, fundamentally differing from how children develop cognitive abilities. However, we believe that
1182   these two learning processes share commonalities: both involve the gradual accumulation of skills
1183   and the ability to generalize from past experiences. Exploring these parallels can provide valuable
1184   insights into designing MLLMs that more closely mimic human visual cognition, ultimately leading
1185   to more advanced and capable models. Additionally, we observe that current open-source models
1186   achieve matrix reasoning performance very close to that of closed-source models. However, VLMs
1187   face challenges in supporting multiple images as input and managing visual memory. Addressing
       these challenges is a crucial direction for building more robust open-source VLMs in the future.

1204   a crucial test for evaluating human intelligence, observing that VLMs with large model weights

1206   same “scaling law” from the general domain. A comprehensive intelligence test requires accurate
