009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               We study temporal tabular data-streams (TTD) where each observation has both
012            categorical and numerical values, and where the universe of distinct categorical
013            items is not known upfront and can even grow unboundedly over time. Such data
014            is common in many large-scale systems, such as user activity in computer system
015            logs and scientific experiment records. Feature hashing is commonly used as a pre-
016            processing step to map the categorical items into a known universe, before doing
017            representation learning (Coleman et al., 2024; Desai et al., 2022). However, these
018            methods have been developed and evaluated for the offline or batch settings. In this
019            paper, we consider the pre-processing step of hashing before representation learning
020
               in the online setting for TTD. We show that deterministic embeddings suffer from
               forgetting in online learning with TTD, leading to performance deterioration. To
021
               mitigate the issue, we propose a probabilistic hash embedding (PHE) model that
022
               treats hash embeddings as stochastic and applies Bayesian online learning to learn
023            incrementally with data. Based on the structure of PHE, we derive a scalable
024            inference algorithm to learn model parameters and infer/update the posteriors of
025            hash embeddings and other latent variables. Our algorithm (i) can handle evolving
026            vocabulary of categorical items, (ii) is adaptive to new items without forgetting old
027            items, (iii) is implementable with a bounded set of parameters that does not grow
028            with the number of distinct observed items on the stream, and (iv) is efficiently
029            implementable both in the offline and the online streaming setting. Experiments
030            in classification, sequence modeling, and recommendation systems with TTD
031
               demonstrate the superior performance of PHE compared to baselines.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Tabular data - where each observation is a vector with both categorical and numerical values - is
037   very common. For example, tabular data can be any records in a MS Excel file, songs information in
038   a music playlist, execution results of the Linux command “ls -l,” and any tables seen in this paper.
039   As a result, tabular data occurs in many high-valued ML applications: finance (Clements et al.,
040   2020), fraud detection (Al-Hashedi and Magalingam, 2021), anomaly detection (Han et al., 2022),
041   cybersecurity (Sarker et al., 2020), medical diagnosis (Shehab et al., 2022) and recommendation
042   systems (Ko et al., 2022). In many of these applications, the data arrives online in a streaming fashion.
043   Unlike images and natural language text, tables are highly structured and contain heterogeneous
044   data types that often result in a mix of both categorical and numeric features (Borisov et al., 2022;
045   Shwartz-Ziv and Armon, 2022). Recent work on tabular data focuses on designing generative models
046   for tabular data type (Xu et al., 2019; Kotelnikov et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b) or learning table
047   representations with foundation models (Yin et al., 2020; Iida et al., 2021). However, these are offline
048   models that assume that the characteristics such as the vocabulary of columns are fixed.
049   In temporal tabular data (TTD), (i) the vocabulary of some or all categorical columns can change,
050   and/or (ii) the semantic meaning of a categorical item can evolve. These characteristics present
051   challenges to offline predictive models. Failure to adapt to the expanding vocabulary leads to a loss in
052   predictive performance, as explained in Figure 1. In the setup of Figure 1, we observe that modeling
053   new categorical features leads to significant accuracy improvement. This problem of expanding
      vocabulary is fairly common in practice: new products are added to a grocery store (Cheng et al.,

071   The expanding and unbounded universe of categorical values1 poses challenges, even in the offline
072
      setting when all training data is available upfront and the size of the vocabulary can be billions
      (Tito Svenstrup et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2020b; Kang et al., 2021; Coleman et al., 2024). A commonly
073
      used methodology to handle unbounded vocabulary is the hashing trick (Weinberger et al., 2009),
074
      where one or more hash functions map the categorical values to a value in a fixed finite set. The
075   hashed values are treated as an approximation of the original categorical values in subsequent model
076   training and inference. The resulting item representations are stored in a set of model parameters,
077   referred to as hash embeddings. Large technology firms, e.g., Yahoo and Google, have incorporated
078   this approach in their large-scale applications (Weinberger et al., 2009; Coleman et al., 2024).
079
      While hash embeddings claim to handle “dynamic” vocabularies, previous work focuses on offline
080
      settings. In this paper, we study learning hash-based embeddings in TTD, whose categorical vocabu-
081   lary is really dynamic, i.e., changing over time. We analyze and demonstrate hash embeddings are
082   subject to catastrophic forgetting as the vocabulary grows. In hash embeddings, representations of
083   two items may share parameters, updating one item’s embedding can adversely interfere with another,
084   causing an effect like the model “forgets.” Consequently, hash embeddings are not yet fit for learning
085   temporally dynamic vocabularies in its vanilla form.
086
      In this paper, we observe that modeling hash embeddings as stochastic and inferring their posterior
087   upon new data arrival mitigates the shortcomings found in online update of deterministic hash
088   embeddings. This Bayesian online learning approach is shown as sample-efficient as offline batch
089   learning (Opper and Winther, 1999), which can in turn make the hash embeddings estimate as
090   effective as offline training.
091
      Main Contributions: Our work proposes probabilistic hash embeddings (PHE) with Bayesian
092
      updates to handle dynamic vocabularies of TTD in an effective and efficient way. The intuition behind
093   PHE stems from its benefits in (i) efficiency, as memory/number of model parameters is bounded and
094   only a small number of parameters need to be updated online (ie., less forgetting, see experiments),
095   and (ii) accuracy benefits since the probabilistic model provides an implicit regularization to trade-off
096   forgetting and adaptation, without the need of specific dataset dependent regularization design.
097
      We highlight PHE as a plug-in module, which can be applied to other probabilistic models like Deep
098
      Kalman Filters (Krishnan et al., 2015), a latent variable model for temporal sequences, and Neural
099   Collaborative Filtering (He et al., 2017), modeling item-user interactions in recommendation systems.
100   The usage of PHE allows those models to handle unbounded items in their application areas in a
101   principal way. For those models, we derive scalable variational inference algorithms to learn the
102   model parameters and infer the latent variables (including latent time variables and PHE). Empirically,
103   we observe superiority of our method compared to baselines under three setups: one supervised
104   learning where new items occur in sequence, the second is conditional sequence modeling setup
105   where the number of sequences increases along with new items, and the third is a recommendation
106   system where novel user-item interactions occur over time.

114   Hashing trick. Weinberger et al. (2009) first proposed using hashing to handle unbounded number
115   of categorical items. To improve on degradation due to hash collisions, Serrà and Karatzoglou (2017)
116   used bloom filters. In recent times, Tito Svenstrup et al. (2017); Cheng et al. (2023); Coleman
117
      et al. (2024) propose a shared embeddings across all categorical features for efficiency and using
      multiple hashing functions to reduce collisions. However, unlike our method, these are deterministic
118
      and are developed in offline learning settings. As shown in our experiments, deterministic hashing
119
      embeddings are vulnerable to evolving vocabularies and inter-observation relation shifts in TTD.
120
121   Continual learning. Wang et al. (2023) surveys main works in continual learning. Regularization-
122   based methods (EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017), VCL Nguyen et al. (2018)) and optimization-based
123
      methods (e.g., GEM (Lopez-Paz and Ranzato, 2017)) ignore categorical variables with unbounded
      vocabulary size. Architecture-expanding methods that dynamically expand the universe of items leads
124
      to unbounded memory usage (Rusu et al., 2016; Yoon et al., 2017; Jerfel et al., 2019). In contrast,
125
      PHE uses a fixed size of memory to accommodate expanding categorical features.
126
127   Temporal and recommendation models. One of our models extends Deep Kalman Filters
128   (DKF) (Krishnan et al., 2015) to be applicable for sequence modeling in TTD, while the origi-
129
      nal DKF only apply to time-series data where the categorical attributes are assumed to be given and
      thus not modeled. Girin et al. (2021) survey a list of latent variable sequence models for speech, text
130
      and video and are not applicable to tabular data due to the ever increasing vocabulary of categorical
131
      items. Similarly previous recommendation methods (Ko et al., 2022) assume the training data is
132   given at once and the universe of items is stationary.
133
134   Tabular data models. Traditionally, tabular data refers to rows in a database, whose distribution are
135
      permutation invariant (Friedman, 2001). In the offline setting when the universe of categorical values
      are known, tree-based boosting methods have emerged as competitive (Chen and Guestrin, 2016; Ke
136
      et al., 2017). In the online and continual learning setting, deep-learning based methods have been
137
      studied in recent times (Huang et al., 2020; Du et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023a). However, all of these
138   works assume that the universe of categorical values are known and fixed up-front. Ours is the first
139   online learning method, even for regular tabular data, that can handle increasing and unbounded
140   vocabulary for items. Kim et al. use string embeddings from language models for open-vocabulary
141   categorical/string-valued columns in an offline setting; in contrast, we focus on online setting. We
142   survey additional related work in Supp. B.

177   3.2      P ROBABILISTIC H ASH E MBEDDINGS (PHE)
178
179   To start off, we explain our universal encoding module for categorical items. Categorical items are the
180   most common ones in tabular datasets, ranging from login names, database names, and activity codes
181   to anonymized identifiers, and are subject to increment from time to time. To incorporate the added
182   items, we propose probabilistic hash embeddings (PHE). The basic PHE involves two components–a
183
      fixed hash function h ∈ N<B and a probabilistic hash embedding table E ∈ RB×d with a prior
      distribution p(E).3 Given an item s, it looks up the hs th row of E as its hash embedding Ehs . Ehs
184
      has distribution p(Ehs ). We require E to be shared across all categorical columns, an operation
185
      adopted by Coleman et al. (2024) as well. To disambiguate duplicated feature values across columns,
186   we add the column name as a prefix to its items. Thus, the same string in different columns will likely
187   be hashed to different values, reducing hash collisions. PHE can also learn the representations of
188   missing values, which are usually represented as a special string and can be hashed.
189
      A single hash function may result in two distinct inputs having the same hashing value, known as hash
190
      collisions, resulting in undistinguished hash embeddings. For size-B buckets, the collision probability
191
      is proportional to O(1/B). To further reduce the collision rate, we use universal hashing (Carter and
192   Wegman, 1977). Namely, instead of utilizing one fixed hash function, we use K fixed hash functions.
193   Then the collision probability can be shown to reduce to O(1/B K ). Moreover, we keep the hash
194   embedding table E shared across K hash functions, which keeps the model size bounded. Repeating
195   the embedding fetching procedure, a single feature s results in K embeddings {Eh(1) , . . . , Eh(K) }

210
      catastrophic forgetting: 1) only a few embeddings need to be updated online. This sparse updating
211   scheme seldom affects other item representations, thus having less forgetting. 2) The online updates

309   3.4      T HEORY: W HY IS PHE SUPERIOR FOR TTD?
310
311   Here, we consider a simple linear Gaussian model that we can analyze in closed form to illustrate why
312   having deterministic hash embeddings that are updated in an online fashion is prone to forgetting.
313   A simple linear-Gaussian model. Let the input variable m ∈ {m0 , m1 } take one of two categorical
314   values and the target y ∈ R be real-valued. The conditional distribution of y is a Gaussian distribution
315   with the mean 1 when m = m0 and mean −1 when m = m1 . The variance of both Gaussians is
316   σ 2 ≈ 0 is tiny. We do not specify the distribution of m just yet and defer that to the sequel.
317

318

319   the three rows of this matrix which are the “embedding vectors” of the three hash values. Thus, in
320   the notation of our model, this embedding matrix is made of B = 3 buckets with the dimension
321   d = 1. The model f (·) uses two hash functions hi (·) : {m0 , m1 } → {0, 1, 2} to map the categorical
322   variable m into a hash value. Without loss of generality, we assume that h1 (m0 ) = 0, h2 (m0 ) =

      An online interaction setting. At each time t = 1, 2, . . . , the environment samples m(t) from a

338   Analysis and conclusion. As we can see from the calculations in Supp. D, if N is sufficiently large,
339
      at the end of time 2N , the learned model is such that f2N +1 (m0 ) ≈ 1/4 and f2N +1 (m1 ) ≈ −1.
340
      Thus, given that the last N samples seen corresponded to m(t) = m1 , the predictor at the end at
      time 2N has near zero prediction error for m1 . However, this comes at a cost of having a large
341
      prediction error for m0 with f2N +1 (m0 ) ≈ 1/4, where the true value is 1. This is in contrast to an
342
      offline method that given all the 2N samples upfront, the algorithm would have learned a predictor
343   that will have near zero prediction error for both m0 and m1 . Thus, we say that the online updated
344   embedding matrix forgets the old distribution m0 . This behaviour is also in contrast to Bayesian
345   online learning that would incrementally learn the posterior distribution p(E|m(1) , . . . , m(t) ) at
346   each time t. It is well known that if we could compute the exact posterior at each time, i.e., exactly
347   compute p(E|m(1) , . . . , m(t) ) for all t, then the posterior for E at the end of 2N samples will be
348   identical to the case if all the 2N samples would be available up-front in batch, i.e., there will be no
349   forgetting. Fig. 3 shows an example when N = 20. Detailed calculations are in Supp. D.

      In this section, we show TTD is ubiquitous in mainstream machine learning tasks and conduct
354
      experiments to demonstrate the efficacy and memory efficiency of PHE in learning TTD. As follows,
355
      we begin with common experimental protocols in Sec. 4.1. Then, in Sec. 4.2, we simulate online
356   learning to benchmark PHE in classification tasks. In Secs. 4.3 and 4.4, we showcase PHE in multi-
357   task sequence modeling and online recommendation systems. All results show PHE outperforms the
358   deterministic counterpart and performs similarly with the upper-bound collision-free embeddings.

360   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL PROTOCOLS AND BASELINES
361
362   Training protocols. In all experiments, we use one shared hash embedding table, also known as
363   unified embeddings (Coleman et al., 2024), for all categorical features. Except for the initial training,
364   we only update the embeddings of categorical columns deemed incremental and freeze parameters
365   other than the hash embedding table. Tab. 6 in Supp. E.6 justifies this updating protocol.
366   Experimental setting. We investigate the data-streaming setup highlighting recurring items and
367   new-arriving items. In this setup, both forgetting and adaptation are measured: the model should
368   avoid forgetting recurring items and adapt for new items. Upon each data arrival, we conducted
369   three operations in order: make predictions, evaluate predictions, and update embeddings. We report
370   the sequential results in plots and the overall averaged results with errors in tables. We repeated all
371   experiments five times with different parameter initialization while keeping other settings fixed.
372   Baselines. We use two types of baselines. The first is deterministic hash embeddings with stochastic
373   gradient descent online learning. They not only have the same model size and architecture as PHE
374   but also have the same update efficiency. That is, the baselines can quickly adapt to new categories
375   by updating only a few relevant hash embeddings and leaving the other parameters unmodified
376   (ie. minimal forgetting). We take three variants: SlowAda only trains one epoch on the new data;
377   MediumAda trains five epochs; FastAda trains 15 epochs. These baselines cover forgetting-adaptation
      trade-offs: FastAda is fast in adaptation but suffers forgetting; SlowAda is on the opposite end.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
530
531   In this work we unveiled the ineffectiveness of hash embedding in learning TTD with dynamic
532   vocabulary. We addressed the problem of modeling TTD and presented probabilistic hash embeddings
533   (PHE). We showcased PHE is a plug-in module for multiple ML models, allowing those models
534
      to learn TTD-streams. We derive a scalable inference algorithm to simultaneously learn the model
      parameters and infer the latent embeddings. Through Bayesian online learning, the model is able
535
      to adapt to new vocabularies without additional hyperparameters in a changing environment. We
536
      benchmark PHE on large-scale public datasets with TTD demonstrating the efficacy of PHE.

707   We denote the model parameters relevant to the generating process by θ := {θz , θy }. To learn the
708   model parameters, we maximize the marginal likelihood p(y≤N |x≤N , hs≤N ; θ). Directly optimizing
709   this marginal likelihood with the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm is intractable. Therefore,

      We extend the discussion in Sec. 2 and survey more related works. In a nutshell, our PHE applies to
811   all tabular data types in Tab. 3 (i.e., D0 − D4) while existing works are targeted to D0 or D1.
812
813   Our work deals with multi-task dynamic temporal tabular data. Our method has two major compo-
814
      nents: the probabilistic hash embeddings that learn categorical feature representations and the latent
      variable model for multi-task temporal tabular data. Next, we discuss the main related works.
815
816   Hash features. PHE is motivated by hashing tricks. Weinberger et al. (2009) proposed to use
817   one hash function to map categorical features to a one-hot hash embedding of length B, which is
818   the bucket size. The drawback is the embedding size is too large because there is only one hash
819   function and that requires a large bucket size B to get rid of collision. Bloom Embeddings (Serrà and
820
      Karatzoglou, 2017) is based on Bloom filters and achieves efficient computation while maintaining a
      compact model size. Other previous work on using hashing tricks to generate features focuses on
821
      using a smaller number of embedding-related parameters to achieve the same performance as using
822
      one-hot encoding. Hash embeddings or unified embeddings (Tito Svenstrup et al., 2017; Cheng et al.,
823   2023) use a shared embedding table for all categorical features and multiple hashing functions as
824   indices of the embedding table, reducing the possibility of collision. Hash embeddings are designed
825   for stationary vocabularies, emphasizing small parameter sizes. We generalize hash embeddings to
826   a probabilistic version that enables us to learn changing vocabularies via Bayesian online learning.
827   Composition Embeddings (Shi et al., 2020a) use multiple hash embedding tables; in contrast, PHE
828   uses one shared embedding table, further reducing the memory cost. Wolpertinger (Dulac-Arnold
829   et al., 2015) and Deep Hash embedding (Kang et al., 2021) use a deep neural network to encode
830   features into real-valued embeddings. In a changing vocabulary setup, the drawback is the need to
831
      modify the whole neural network to incorporate new string features, even though there is only one
      new feature. Different from previous works, our method emphasizes the usage of hash embeddings
832
      in dynamic tabular data with changing vocabularies. In the meantime, the model architecture remains
833
      stable, and only partial parameter updates are required.
834
835   Generative models for tabular data. Recent research on generative models of non-temporal
836   tabular data focuses on modeling multi-modality or heterogeneity but overlooks the sustainable
837
      representations for dynamically expanded vocabularies. These works rely on one-hot encoding
      for categorical features. Xu et al. (2019) learns VAE and GAN-based tabular data generator while
838
      conditioning on discrete categorical features. Later works rely on GAN to design tabular data
839
      generators (Liu et al., 2023b; Zhao et al., 2021). Kotelnikov et al. (2023) extend diffusion models to
840   tabular data.
841
842   Temporal tabular data models. To our knowledge, there isn’t a sequence model designed for
843
      multi-task temporal tabular data, although some previous works have the potential to extend to tabular
      data. PHE extends Deep Kalman Filters (Krishnan et al., 2015) to be applicable for multi-task,
844
      temporal, and dynamic tabular data, while the original Deep Kalman Filters do not explicitly consider
845
      the multi-task and dynamic vocabulary property of the tabular data. Girin et al. (2021) survey a list of
846   latent variable sequence models that are possible to be extended to tabular data, although most of
847   them are designed for speech or video data.
848
849
      Others. The setup of learning dynamic tabular data with changing vocabularies shares the similarity
      to continual learning and Bayesian online learning (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2023; Zenke
850
      et al., 2017; Nguyen et al., 2018; Li et al., 2021), but the difference is our formulation is a novel
851
      dictionary- or vocabulary-incremental setup for tabular data. Besides, Kireev et al. (2023) learn
852   transferable robust embeddings for categorical features. Yin et al. (2020); Iida et al. (2021) design
853   objective functions for representation learning on tabular data using large-language models. Arik and
854   Pfister (2021) and Huang et al. (2020) use the one-hot encoder to learn categorical feature embeddings
855   before input to a transformer module.

1031   emebedding matrix and then given the data X compute the posterior distribution p(E|X). We will
1032   say that the Bayesian learning does not forget, if the posterior distribution computed based on all the
1033   2N samples (X1 , Y1 ), · · · , (X2N , Y2N ) shown up-front matches the posterior distribution computed
1034   in an online fashion. However, from classical results in online Bayesian learning, it is well known that
1035   if one can compute the exact posterior p(E|X1 , · · · , Xt ) at all times t, then the posterior at time 2N
1036
       is identical to the one that an offline algorithm would have computed had it seen all the 2N samples
       at once. Thus, if the exact posterior can be computed at each time, then there is no forgetting in the
1037
       Bayesian mechanism.
1038
1039   Thus in this section, we showed through a simple linear-gaussian model, that online updating of hash
1040   embedding matrix leads to forgetting while a bayesian updating of the embedding matrix does not
1041   lead to forgetting. In order to demonstrate this, we defined forgetting to not occur if the model learnt
1042
       at the end of seeing each online sample one by one is close to the model learnt had all the samples
       been available up-front. Further, we show in experiments that this insight holds even in more complex
1043
       scenarios where exact Bayesian posterior cannot be computed, but only an approximation through
1044
       variational inference can be done.

1324   Adaptation and forgetting analysis. We designed experiments to specifically measure the adaptation
1325   to new data and forgetting of old data. We split the data into two disjoint groups based on a random
1326   partition of one column’s vocabulary. The model was initialized using the first group and online
1327   updated on the second group whose items are unseen in initialization. We let the data arrive one at a
1328   time. Adaptation is measured by the cumulative predictive accuracy of new datum and the forgetting
1329
       by the accuracy of the first group’s test data. Results in Fig. 10 show that our PHE has almost the best
       adaptation and forgetting performance on all four datasets. The P-EE while does not suffer forgetting,
1330
       its adaptation to new categories is slow as each new embedding is initialized at random.
1331
1332   Regarding baselines, SlowAda uses a small learning rate (1e-4); MediumAda uses a medium learning
1333   rate (1e-3); FastAda uses a large learning rate (1e-2).
1334   In Fig. 10, we compare on all four classification datasets used in the paper, our PHE against the four
1335   baselines. We observe from Fig. 10 that the SlowAda baseline with smaller LR (1e-4) leads to slower
1336   forgetting at the cost of slower adaptation, while larger LR (1e-2) has faster adaptation at the cost
1337   of faster forgetting (FastAda). Thus a data-stream dependent LR is needed for deterministic hash
1338   embeddings to trade off adaptation and forgetting. In contrast, our PHE has almost the best adaptation
1339   and forgetting performance on all four datasets due to the regularization from the posteriors. The
1340   EE while does not suffer forgetting as each category has a separate row in the embedding table, its
1341
       adaptation to new categories is slow as each new embedding is initialized at random.
