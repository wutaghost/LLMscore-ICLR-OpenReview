               Machine learning models deployed in sensitive areas such as healthcare must
               be interpretable to ensure accountability and fairness. Rule lists (if Age <
011
               35 ∧ Priors > 0 then Recidivism = True, else if Next Condition . . . )
012
               offer full transparency, making them well-suited for high-stakes decisions. How-
013            ever, learning such rule lists presents significant challenges. Existing methods
014            based on combinatorial optimization require feature pre-discretization and impose
015            restrictions on rule size. Neuro-symbolic methods use more scalable continuous
016            optimization yet place similar pre-discretization constraints and suffer from unsta-
017            ble optimization. To address the existing limitations, we introduce N Y RULES, an
018            end-to-end trainable model that unifies discretization, rule learning, and rule order
019            into a single differentiable framework. We formulate a continuous relaxation of
020            the rule list learning problem that converges to a strict rule list through tempera-
021
               ture annealing. N Y RULES learns both the discretizations of individual features,
               as well as their combination into conjunctive rules without any pre-processing or
022
               restrictions. Extensive experiments demonstrate that N Y RULES consistently out-
023
               performs both combinatorial and neuro-symbolic methods, effectively learning
024            simple and complex rules, as well as their order, across a wide range of datasets.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Machine learning models are increasingly used in high-stakes applications such as healthcare (Deo,
030   2015), credit risk evaluation (Bhatore et al., 2020), and criminal justice (Lakkaraju & Rudin, 2017),
031   where it is vital that each decision is fair and reasonable. Proxy measures such as Shapley values can
032   give the illusion of interpretability, but are highly problematic as they can not faithfully represent a
033   non-additive models decision process (Gosiewska & Biecek, 2019). Instead, Rudin (2019) argues
034   that it is crucial to use inherently interpretable models, to create systems with human supervision in
035   the loop (Kleinberg et al., 2018).
036   For particularly sensitive domains such as stroke prediction or recidivism, so called Rule Lists are
037   a popular choice (Letham et al., 2015) due to their fully transparent decision making. A rule list
038   predicts based on nested ”if-then-else” statements and naturally aligns with the human-decision
039   making process. Each rule is active if its conditions are met, e.g. “if Thalassemia = normal ∧
040   Resting bps < 151”, and carries a respective prediction, i.e. “then P (Disease) = 10%”. A rule list
041   goes through a set of rules in a fixed order, and makes its prediction using the first applicable rule.
042   We show an example rule list for the Heart disease (Detrano et al., 1989) learned with our method
043   in Figure 1, which is highly accurate and easy to understand.
044   Inferring rule lists from data is a challenging combinatorial optimization problem, as there are super
045   exponentially many options in the number of features. Existing approaches use greedy heuristics
046   (Proenca & van Leeuwen, 2020), sample based on fixed priors (Yang et al., 2017) and even attempt
047   to find globally optimal solutions (Angelino et al., 2018) using branch-bound. However, they all
048   depend on the pre-discretization of continuous features, which in practice deteriorates their perfor-
049   mance. That is, features such as “Resting bps” are typically discretized using 2 − 5 fixed thresholds.
050
      Increasing the granularity of pre-processing leads to a combinatorial explosion of the search space,
      which creates issues in scalability for exact methods and in accuracy for heuristics.
051
052   Recently, following the paradigm of neuro-symbolic learning, methods based on continuous opti-
053   mization were proposed to solve rule learning problems with gradient descent (Wang et al., 2021;
      Qiao et al., 2021). Nevertheless, neural methods too require to pre-discretize of the features. Most

      rule lists differentiably and end-to-end. That is, we unify the learning of predicates, their assembly
074   into rules, and the final order of the rule list into a single architecture. Instead of relying on pre-
075   discretized features, N Y RULES learns the discretization of the features as well as which features to
076   aggregate to conjunctive rules. We employ soft approximations of threshold functions (predicates)
077   and combine them using a novel differentiable logical conjunction, which alleviates vanishing gra-
078   dients issues of previous work. Finally, we introduce a learnable rule priority that is grounded into a
079   strict ordering at the end of training. Altogether, this gives us a holistic differentiable relaxation of
080   rule lists, which can be learned end-to-end. Empirically, we show that N Y RULES outperforms the
081   state-of-the-art on a plethora of datasets, especially where exact thresholding is required.

351   Rule lists were introduced in the early 90s (Cohen, 1995) and have since been used in various
352
      applications, such as healthcare (Deo, 2015), criminal justice (Angelino et al., 2018), and credit risk
      evaluation (Bhatore et al., 2020). Similarly, decision trees (Breiman, 2017), which can also be easily
353
      transformed into rules by tracing the path from the root to the leaf, have also been widely used in
354
      practice. The approaches use greedy combinatorial optimization to find the best rule set. Instead of
355   relying on the greedy growing of the model, Wang et al. (2017); Yang et al. (2017) propose Bayesian
356   rule lists, a probabilistic model, where the complexity of the model is controlled by a prior, which is
357   specified by the user. In practice, these priors result in short rule lists but can harm the performance if
358   misspecified. To automate the trade-off between complexity and accuracy, Proenca & van Leeuwen
359   (2020); Papagianni & van Leeuwen (2023) propose an MDL-based approach, which uses an MDL-
360   score for model selection. In practice, this results in more accurate and concise rule lists compared
361   to previous approaches. There are also approaches that attempt to find optimal rule lists (Angelino
362   et al., 2018; Aivodji et al., 2022). Due to the expensive combinatorial optimization, exact methods
363
      are not applicable beyond trivially sized datasets and have to severely restrict the search space in
      terms of rule size, feature quantization and number of rules.
364
365   Instead of using combinatorial optimization, neuro-symbolic approaches have been proposed to
366   learn rule classifiers. Qiao et al. (2021) proposes the first approach to learn rule sets in an end-to-
367   end scheme. They formulate a novel neural architecture that uses continuous relaxations of logical
368   operators. Here, techniques from the field of fuzzy logics are used to differentiably optimize logical
369
      operations such as negation, disjunction and conjunction (van Krieken et al., 2022). After train-
      ing, the rules are extracted from the network. This is extended by Wang et al. (2021) to a deeper
370
      architecture that allows to learn more complicated rules; however, this often results in worse inter-
371
      pretability. Walter et al. (2024) propose to learn rules for binary data that are not only predictive
372   but also descriptive, which improves explainability but reduces accuracy. Kusters et al. (2022) intro-
373   duce a differentiable approach to dynamically learn rule predicates, but focuses on linear decision
374   boundaries which can not be translated into interpretable single feature thresholds. Dierckx et al.
375   (2023) extend the approach of Qiao et al. (2021) by introducing a hierarchy among the rules, al-
376   lowing to learn rule lists. Although these methods resolve the scalability issue, they still rely on
377   pre-discretization of the features, similar to the combinatorial approaches. In contrast, N Y RULES
      learns discretizations on the fly while being highly scalable and accurate.

502   6              C ONCLUSION
503
504   We propose N Y RULES, a differentiable relaxation of the rule list learning problem that converges
505   to a strict rule list through temperature annealing. N Y RULES learns both the discretizations of indi-
506   vidual features, and how to compile these features into conjunctive rules without any pre-processing
507
      or restrictions. We also learn the rule-order differentiably by introducing a priority score that deter-
      mines the ordering. N Y RULES is able to learn rules of any complexity using specifically optimized
508
      predicates and order them in a way that maximizes the predictive performance of the model. As
509
      a result, we obtain both highly interpretable, but also accurate rule lists that can assist decision
510   making in a wide range of applications. We demonstrated the effectiveness of N Y RULES in exten-
511   sive real-world and synthetic experiments. We show that N Y RULES consistently outperforms both
512   combinatorial and neuro-symbolic methods on a variety of datasets.
513
514   Limitations. Whilst N Y RULES is a powerful tool for interpretable rule learning, it is not without
515   limitations. First and foremost, the rules that N Y RULES learns do not allow to draw any causal
516   conclusions about the data generating process without any further assumptions. Thus, they should
517   only be used to assist in decision making and not as a substitute for domain knowledge. Compared to
518   C ORELS, we can not give any optimality guarantees on the learned rule list within the search space,
519   but explore a much larger search space that leads to empirically better results. The number of rules
520
      in a N Y RULES rule list is fixed and must be set beforehand. In our evaluation, we use rule length
      as a quantitative measure to compare the complexity of rule lists. This choice is supported by early
521
      explainability research, which indicates that humans find shorter rules easier to comprehend than
522
      longer ones (Huysmans et al., 2011). Nonetheless this proxy is not perfect and does not capture all
523   aspects of interpretability. Hence to undoubtedly determine, which methods is most explainable, a
524   user study would be necessary, which is left for future work. In addition, there are hyperparameters
525   and temperature schedules that need to be set. Whilst we have observed a degree of robustness
526   to these hyperparameters, they require tuning for optimal performance. Lastly, the current rule
527   language is limited to logical conjunctions of thresholded features. Adding disjunctions and more
528   complex logical predicates would be a natural extension of the current work and is something we
529   plan to explore in the future.
530
531   Future Work. In addition to the expansion of the rule language, there are several other directions
532   in which N Y RULES could be extended. For example, the current rule list model is only designed
533
      for binary classification tasks, hence to extend it to multi-class classification, is crucial for a wider
      range of applications. In that context, we also plan to derive a non-conformity score from the rule list
534
      model for conformal prediction. Extending N Y RULES to regression tasks opens up a wide range of
535
      new applications to benefit from interpretable rule lists. Another exciting direction of future work is
536   the adaption of N Y RULES to structured data, such as images or graphs. With appropriate predicate
537   functions that extract meaningful concepts in those domains, rule list models could be used as more
538   interpretable and accountable deep learning models.

939
      Temperature schedules are crucial in optimization problems involving soft approximations of dis-
940
      crete functions. They help in gradually transitioning from a soft to a hard decision boundary, which
941   can improve convergence and performance. By adjusting the temperature parameter, we control the
942   smoothness of the approximations, allowing the model to explore the solution space more effectively
943   during the initial stages of training and then refine the solutions as training progresses.
944
      We use a linear temperature decay during the second half of training for both temperatures. The
945
      temperature starts at 1.0 and linearly decreases to 0.1 for the rule priority temperature trl and ranges
946
      from 0.2 to 0.05 for the predicate temperature tπ . These values were determined through hyperpa-
947   rameter optimization and are unchanged across all experiments. The temperature is updated at each
948   epoch as follows:
949
950   temp_start = 1.0
951   temp_end = 0.1
952   temp = temp_start
953   step_size = (temp_start - temp_end)/(total_epochs*2)
954   If epoch >= total_epochs/2:
955
          temp = temp - step_size
956
      This schedule allows the model to maintain a high level of flexibility during the first half of training
957   with multiple active rules and gradually focus on only a single rule per sample in the latter half.
