009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We theoretically and empirically study the logical reasoning capabilities of LLMs
012            in the context of the Boolean satisfiability (SAT) problem. First, we construct
013            a non-uniform class of decoder-only Transformers that can solve 3-SAT using
014            backtracking and deduction via Chain-of-Thought (CoT). We prove its correctness
015            by showing trace equivalence to the well-known DPLL SAT-solving algorithm.
016            Second, to support the implementation of this abstract construction, we design
017            a compiler PARAT that takes as input a procedural specification and outputs a
018
               transformer model implementing this specification. Third, rather than programming
               a transformer to reason, we evaluate empirically whether it can be trained to do
019
               so by learning directly from algorithmic traces (“reasoning paths”) of the DPLL
020
               algorithm.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025
      Transformer-based Language Models (LLMs, Vaswani et al. (2017)) have demonstrated remarkable
026   success in a wide range of tasks framed in natural language, especially when using prompting
027   techniques such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT, Wei et al. (2022)). On the other hand, even the most
028   advanced LLMs face challenges in reliable multi-step reasoning, frequently hallucinating towards
029   nonsensical conclusions (Kambhampati et al. (2024)). Evaluating progress on logical deduction in
030   language models remains an ongoing challenge as researchers have continued to disagree on even a
031   reasonable definition of what constitutes “reasoning.”
032   This paper focuses on the question of LLM reasoning capability in what we believe is the simplest
033   and most mathematically precise setting: the Boolean satisfiability problem (SAT, Cook (1971)). SAT
034   problems provide an excellent starting point for studying the reasoning ability of LLMs given that (a)
035   natural language often encodes Boolean logic, and (b) we already have many useful algorithms that
036   implement logical deduction to solve SAT problems Biere et al. (2009). Notably, notwithstanding the
037   NP-completeness of SAT, humans implicitly solve simple boolean satisfaction problems in their daily
038   lives; scheduling a multi-person meeting across time zones, for example.
039   In this work we aim to rigorously investigate Transformers’ multi-step reasoning and backtracking
040   capability in solving formal logical reasoning problems, and we demonstrate through a theoretical
041   construction that decoder-only Transformers can reliably decide SAT instances.
042   Theorem 1.1 (Informal version of Theorem 4.5). For any p, c ∈ N+ , there exist a decoder-only
043   Transformer with O(p2 ) parameters that can decide all 3-SAT instances of at most p variables and c
044   clauses using Chain-of-Thought reasoning.
045
046   To investigate the properties of our construction empirically, we design a compiler that converts
047   computational graphs of abstract sequence operations used in our construction into Transformer
048   model weights. We implemented the construction in PyTorch and empirically validated its correctness
049   on random 3-SAT instances. We also investigated its empirical properties such as the number of
050
      generated CoT tokens.
051   Additionally, we perform training experiments to demonstrate that Transformers can effectively
052   learn from deductive reasoning and the backtracking process of the DPLL algorithm encoded as
053   Chain-of-Thought. We show that Transformers equipped with CoT can generalize between SAT
      instances generated from different distributions within the same number of variables p. However,

096   perform propositional reasoning with the 3-SAT problem. Finally, our training experiments suggest
097   that Chain-of-Thought allows Transformer-LLMs to achieve out-of-distribution generalization for the
098   same input lengths.

103   Theoretical Expressiveness of Transformers and Chain-of-Thought (CoT): Owing to the empir-
104   ical success of Transformer-based models, many researchers have investigated the capabilities of
105   the Transformer architecture from a theoretical perspective. This line of research focuses on what
106   types of computation can Transformer models simulate by providing theoretical constructions of
107   Transformer models with idealized assumptions. The seminal work of Liu et al. (2023) showed that
      Transformers can simulate automata using a single pass over only a logarithmic number of layers

      w.r.t. the number of states. Yao et al. (2021) demonstrated that transformers can perform parentheses
109   matching of at most k types of parentheses and D appearance of each (Dyckk,D ) with D + 1 layers.
110
111   However, the computation power of one pass of the Transformer model is fundamentally limited
112
      (Merrill & Sabharwal (2023)), and the success of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning (Wei et al.
      (2022)) has sparked more recent research on how CoT can improve upon the expressiveness of
113
      Transformer models. Pérez et al. (2019) proved that Transformers can emulate the execution of
114
      single-tape Turing machines if each output vector is appended to the input vector sequence at the next
115   iteration. Giannou et al. (2023) showed that Transformers can recurrently simulate arbitrary programs
116   written in a one-instruction-set language if the output vector at every position of the Transformer
117   is passed as input to the model at the next iteration. Li et al. (2024) proved that Transformers can
118   simulate arbitrary boolean circuits using CoT by representing the circuit in the positional encoding
119   and is commonly perceived to have shown that Transformers with CoT can “solve all problems”.
120   In particular, transformers can decide all problems in P/poly ⊇ P with polynomial steps of CoT.
121   Merrill & Sabharwal (2024) showed that Transformers with averaging hard attention can decide all
122   regular languages with a linear number of CoT tokens and decide all problems in P with a polynomial
123
      number of CoT tokens. Feng et al. (2023) shows that Transformer CoT can perform integer arithmetic,
      solve linear equations, and perform dynamic programming for the longest increasing subsequence
124
      and edit distance problems. These seminal works profoundly advanced our understanding of the
125
      capabilities of Transformer models from a theoretical perspective.
126
127   How our work differs from the above-mentioned results: Many of the above papers are focused
128   on problems in P or P/poly, while 3-SAT is an NP-complete problem. It is widely believed that P
129
      is a strict subset of NP, and it is not known whether NP is a subset of P/poly. In other words, our
      results are not comparable to these earlier results.
130
131   Meanwhile, Pérez et al. (2019), Li et al. (2024), and Merrill & Sabharwal (2024) also show that
132   Transformers can simulate single-tape Turing Machines (TM) with CoT and can theoretically be
133   extended to arbitrary decidable languages. However, these constructions require at least one CoT
134   token for every step of TM execution. By contrast, our theoretical construction demonstrates that, for
135
      certain classes of formal reasoning problems, Transformers can simulate algorithmic reasoning traces
      at an abstract level with drastically reduced number of CoT tokens compared to step-wise emulation
136
      of a single-tape TM. At each CoT Step, our construction performs deductive reasoning over the full
137
      input in parallel while any single-tape TM must process each input token sequentially. Furthermore,
138   the CoT produced by our theoretical construction abstractly represents the human reasoning process
139   of trial and error, as demonstrated in Figure 1.
140
141
      Compilation of Transformer Weights. Further, prior work on the theoretical construction of
      Transformer models rarely provide practical implementations. Notably, Giannou et al. (2023) provide
142
      an implementation of their construction and demonstrate its execution on several programs. However,
143
      the model is initialized “manually” using prolonged sequences of array assignments, limiting its
144   extensibility to other theoretical frameworks.
145
146   More recently, Lindner et al. (2023) released Tracr, which compiles RASP (Weiss et al. (2021))
147
      programs into decoder-only Transformer models. The “Restricted Access Sequence Processing
      Language” (RASP, Weiss et al. (2021)) is a human-readable representation of a subset of operations
148
      that Transformers can perform via self-attention and MLP layers. In our preliminary attempt to
149
      implement a SAT solver model with Tracr, we identified several implementation inconveniences and
150   limitations of Tracr when scaling to more complex algorithms, which motivated the development
151   of our compiler. In particular: (1) Every “variable” (termed sop in Lindner et al. (2023)) in Tracr
152   must be either a one-hot categorical encoding or a single numerical value. This constraint makes
153   representing more complex vector structures highly inconvenient. Furthermore, each select
154   operation (i.e., self-attention) accepts only a single sop as the query and key vectors, whereas our
155   theoretical construction often requires incorporating multiple sops as queries and keys. (2) Tracr
156   represents position indices and many other discrete sops with a one-hot encoding, allocating a
157   residual stream dimension for each possible value of the sop. In particular, compiling models with a
158
      context length of n requires O(n) additional embedding dimensions for each SOp that represents
      a position index. (3) For each binary operation between one-hot encoded sops (such as position
159
      indices), Tracr creates an MLP layer that first creates a lookup table of all possible value combinations
160
      of the input sops. This results in an MLP layer of O(n3 ) parameters.

      3     P RELIMINARIES
163
164
      The Boolean satisfiability problem (SAT) is the problem of determining whether there exists an
165   assignment A of the variables in a Boolean formula F such that F is true under A. In this paper we
166   only consider 3-SAT instances in conjunctive normal form (CNF), where groups of at most 3 variables
167   and their negations (literals) can be joined by OR operators into clauses, and these clauses can then
168   be joined by AND operators. In our implementations we use the well-known DIMACS encoding for
169   CNF formulae whereby each literal is converted to a positive or negative integer corresponding to its
170   index, and clauses are separated by a 0 .

       4    T RANSFORMERS AND SAT: LOGICAL DEDUCTION AND BACKTRACKING
237
       This section presents and explains our main results on Transformers’ capability in deductive reasoning
238
       and backtracking with CoT. To rigorously state our results, we first formally define decision problems,
239
       decision procedures, and what it means for a model to “solve” a decision problem using CoT:
240
241
       Definition 4.1 (Decision Problem). Let V be a vocabulary, Σ ⊆ V be an alphabet, L ⊆ Σ∗ be a set
       of valid input strings. We say that a mapping f : L → {0, 1} is a decision problem defined on L.
242
243    Definition 4.2 (Decision Procedure). We say that an algorithm A is a decision procedure for the
244    decision problem f , if given any input string x from L, A halts and outputs 1 if f (x) = 1, and halts
245
       and outputs 0 if f (x) = 0.
246    Definition 4.3 (Autoregressive Decision Procedure). For any map M : V ∗ → V, which we refer to
247    as an auto-regressive next-token prediction model, and E = {E0 , E1 } ⊂ V, define procedure AM,E as
248    follows: For any input s1:n , run Algorithm 1 with stop tokens E. AM,E outputs 0 if s1:t ends with
249
       E0 and AM,E output 1 otherwise. We say M autoregressively decides decision problem f if there is
       some E ⊂ V for which AM,E decides f .
250
251    Definition 4.4 (3-SATp,c ). Let DIMACS(p, c) denote the set of valid DIMACS encodings of 3-SAT
252    instances with at most p variables and c clauses with a prepended [BOS] token and an appended
253
       [SEP] token. Define 3-SATp,c : DIMACS(p, c) → {0, 1} as the problem of deciding whether the
       3-SAT formula encoded in the input in DIMACS(p, c) encoding is satisfiable.
254
255    With the above definition, we’re ready to present a formal statement of our theoretical construction of
256    a Transformer model that performs SAT Solving:
257
       Theorem 4.5 (Decoder-only Transformers can solve SAT). For any p, c ∈ N+ , there exists a
258
       Transformer model M : V ∗ → V that autoregressively decides 3-SATp,c in no more than p · 2p+1
259
       CoT iterations. M requires L = 7 layers, H = 5 heads, demb = O(p), and O(p2 ) parameters.
260
261    Remarks on Theorem 4.5
262
263           • The upper bound on the CoT length p · 2p+1 is a worst-case upper bound which assumes that
264             the model is unable to make any logical deductions have to try all 2p assignments. However,
265             this upper bound is never reached in practice, and in Figure 4 we show that the number of
266
                CoT tokens is no greater than 8p · 20.08p for most formulas. If the number of backtracking
                steps is bounded by T then the CoT is no longer than (2p + 1)(T + 1)
267
268           • The worst-case CoT length is independent of the number of clauses c, which is due to
269             the parallel deduction over all clauses within the Transformer construction. Otherwise,
                sequentially processing each clause would take at least c · 2O(p) number of steps.

368   encodings into our encoding of clauses and partial assignments and selects the correct output token
369
      from the results of the operations described in Lemma 4.10.

371   5    C OMPILER FOR C OMPLEX T RANSFORMER A LGORITHMS
372
373   In the previous section, we presented a theoretical construction of a Transformer capable of solving
374   SAT instances through backtracking and parallel deduction. However, relying solely on theorems and
375   proofs can make it challenging to gain practical insights and verify correctness. To address this, we
376   introduce ParametricTransformer compiler and the corresponding PARAT language, which provides a
377   framework for converting theoretical constructions of Transformers into practical models to facilitate
      empirical analysis and validation.


                                                          7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      The syntax of the PARAT language is a restricted subset of Python with the NumPy library. Every
379   variable v in PARAT is a 2-D NumPy array of shape n x d v, where n denotes the input number of
380   tokens and d v is the dimension of the PARAT variable v, which can be different for every variable
381   v.
382
      A program in the PARAT language is composed of a linear sequence of statements (i.e., no control
383
      flow such as loops or branching is allowed), where each statement assigns the value of an expression
384
      to a variable. Let v 1, v 2, . . . denote PARAT variable names. Then, each statement involving
385   PARAT variables must be one of the following:
386
387          • Binary operations: v 1 + v 2, v 1 * v 2, v 1 - v 2
388
             • Index operations: v 1[v 2, :], v 1[:, start:end], where start and end are
389            non-negative integers
390
             • Function calls: A function from our predefined library of functions that takes as input
391
               PARAT variables
392
393   The input variables of a PARAT program for vocabulary size V are tokens and indices, where
394   tokens is a V -dimensional PARAT variable containing one-hot token embeddings of the input
395   tokens, and indices is a 1-dimensional PARAT variable containing the numerical index of each
396   input token (i.e., the array [[1], [2], ..., [n]]).
397
      The ParametricTransformer compiler takes in a program written in the PARAT language and a PARAT
398
      variable out of dimension V and outputs a PyTorch Module object that implements a Transformer
399   model as defined in Section 2. The following condition is satisfied: For any possible input sequence
400   of tokens s in the vocabulary of length n, the token predicted by the Transformer model is the same as
401   the token corresponding to out[-1, :].argmax() (i.e., the token prediction at the last position)
402   when interpreting the PARAT program using the Python interpreter with the NumPy library.
403
404   5.1   A NALYSIS OF THE C OMPILED SAT-S OLVING M ODEL
405
406   With our compiler, we successfully compiled our theoretical construction in Theorem 4.5 using the
407   code in Appendix D. For p = 20 number of variables, the resulting Transformer has 7 layers, 5 atten-
408   tion heads, 502 embedding dimensions, and 5011862 parameters. With a concrete implementation of
409
      our theoretical construction in PyTorch, we empirically investigate 3 questions (1) Does the compiled
      model correctly decide SAT instances? (2) How many steps does the model take to solve actual
410
      3-SAT instances? (3) How does error induced by soft attention affect reasoning accuracy? These
411
      questions reveal further insights that are not available by observing the theoretical constructions alone
412   and demonstrate the additional values provided by PARAT.
413
414   Evaluation Datasets We evaluate our models on randomly sampled DIMACS encoding of 3-SAT
415
      formulas. We focus on SAT formulas with exactly 3 literals in each clause, with the number of clauses
      c between 4.1p and 4.4p, where p is the number of variables.
416
417   It is well-known that the satisfiability of such random 3-SAT formulas highly depends on the
418   clause/variable ratio, where a formula is very likely satisfiable if c/p ≪ 4.26 and unsatisfiable if
419   c/p ≫ 4.26 (Crawford & Auton (1996)). This potentially allows a model to obtain high accuracy
420   just by observing the statistical properties such as the c/p ratio. To address this, we constrain this
421
      ratio for all formulas to be near the critical ratio 4.26. Furthermore, our “marginal” datasets contain
      pairs of SAT vs UNSAT formulas that differ from each other by only a single literal. This means
422
      that the SAT and UNSAT formulas in the dataset have almost no statistical difference in terms of c/p
423
      ratio, variable distribution, etc., ruling out the possibility of obtaining SAT vs UNSAT information
424   solely via statistical properties.
425
426   We also use 3 different sampling methods to generate formulas of different solving difficulties to
427
      evaluate our model:
428
             • Marginal: Composed of pairs of formulas that differ by only one token.
429
430
             • Random: Formulas are not paired by differing tokens and each clause is randomly generated.
431          • Skewed: Formulas where polarity and variable sampling are not uniform; For each literal,
               one polarity is preferred over the other. Some literals are also preferred over others.

724   C.2.1    L EMMAS FOR MLP WITH R E GLU ACTIVATION
725
      This section shows several lemmas showing the capabilities of the self-attention operation and MLP
726
      layers to approximate high-level vector operations. These high-level operations are later used as
727   building blocks for the Transformer SAT-solver. Specifically, with appropriate weight configurations,
728   a 2-layer MLP with ReGLU activation f (x) = W2 [(W1 x + b) ⊗ relu(V x + c)] can approximate
729   the following vector operations for arbitrary input x:
730
731        • Simulate a 2-layer MLP with ReLU activation: W2 ReLU(W1′ x + b′1 ) + b′2
732        • Simulate any linear operation W x
733        • Simulate element-wise multiplication: x1 ⊗ x2
734
      Lemma C.1 (Simulating a 2-Layer ReLU MLP with ReGLU Activation). A 2-layer MLP with
735
      ReGLU activation function can simulate any 2-layer MLP with ReLU activation function.
736
737
      Proof. Let the ReLU MLP be defined as:
738
739                                  g(x) = W2′ ReLU(W1′ x + b′1 ) + b′2 .
740
741   Set the weights and biases of the ReGLU MLP as follows:
742                                          W1 = 0,      b1 = 1,
743
                                              V = W1′ ,      b2 = b′1 ,
744
745                                          W2 = W2′ ,      b = b′2 .
746
747
      Then, the ReGLU MLP computes:
748                          f (x) = W2′ [(0 · x + 1) ⊗ ReLU(W1′ x + b′1 )] + b′2 .
749
750   Simplifying:
751
              f (x) = W2′ [1 ⊗ ReLU(W1′ x + b′1 )] + b′2 = W2′ ReLU(W1′ x + b′1 ) + b′2 = g(x).
752
753
      Thus, the ReGLU MLP computes the same function as the ReLU MLP.
754
755   Lemma C.2 (Simulating Linear Operations with ReGLU MLP). A 2-layer MLP with ReGLU
      activation can compute any linear operation f (x) = W x + b.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Proof. To compute a linear function using the ReGLU MLP, we can set the activation to act as a
757   scalar multiplier of one. Set the weights and biases as:
758
759                                            W1 = W , b1 = b,
760                                             V = 0, b2 = 1,
761                                            W2 = I, b = 0.
762
763
      Here, I is the identity matrix.
764   Since V x + b2 = b2 = 1, we have:
765
                                        ReLU(V x + b2 ) = ReLU(1) = 1.
766
767   Then, the ReGLU MLP computes:
768
769
                                    f (x) = I [(W x + b) ⊗ 1] = W x + b.

      can compute the element-wise multiplication of two input vectors x1 and x2 , that is,

1115   except for the dimensions assigned to the positional index i. Similarly, only the dimensions assigned
1116   to the one-hot token representation are initialized in the token embeddings. At each layer, the
1117

1233   ΣB be the sets of possible states of A and B, respectively. We say that algorithms A and B are trace
1234   equivalent if there exists a bijective mapping ϕ : ΣA → ΣB , independent of the input, such that for
1235   every input s, the traces produced by A and B satisfy the following:
1236   If the execution of A on input s produces the trace TrA (s) = [σ1A , σ2A , . . . , σnA ], and the execution of
1237   B on the same input s produces the trace TrB (s) = [σ1B , σ2B , . . . , σnB ], then for all i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , n},
1238
1239                                                   σiB = ϕ(σiA ).
