                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
                In this work, we investigate the limitations of the 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS)
012             optimization scheme, revealing why it undergoes significant performance drops
013             when initialized with noisy or random point clouds. Through in-depth analysis,
014             we identify a key limitation of the 3DGS optimization: limited Gaussian trans-
015             portability. Since Gaussians are optimized solely based on image photometric
016             loss, the optimization tends to overfit the parameters of the projected Gaussians
017             to improve reconstruction at their current positions, rather than relocating them to
018             more optimal locations. This leads to producing under-reconstructed regions when
019             starting with noisy or random initialization, failing to transport Gaussians to cor-
020
                rect locations. Based on our findings, we propose RAIN-GS (Relaxing Accurate
                INitialization Constraint for 3D Gaussian Splatting), a set of simple yet effec-
021
                tive modifications, including initializing sparse Gaussians with large variances,
022
                progressive Gaussian low-pass filtering, and an Adaptive Bound-Expanding split
023             algorithm. These modifications enable Gaussians to effectively redistribute across
024             the scene, capturing both coarse structure and fine details. By addressing the in-
025             herent limitations of 3DGS, RAIN-GS allows effective training even with random
026             point clouds, significantly enhancing reconstruction quality.

029   1     I NTRODUCTION
030   Novel view synthesis is one of the essential tasks in computer vision and computer graphics, aiming
031   to render novel views of a 3D scene given a set of images. It has a wide range of applications in var-
032   ious fields, including augmented reality and virtual reality (Xu et al., 2023), robotics (Adamkiewicz
033   et al., 2022), and data generation (Ge et al., 2022). Recently, neural radiance fields (NeRFs) (Milden-
034   hall et al., 2021) and 3D Gaussian splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) have demonstrated remark-
035   able success in this task, where 3DGS further pushes the boundary of real-time rendering through
036   explicitly representing the scene with Gaussians.
037   Despite its remarkable results, compared to NeRFs, 3DGS requires an additional input of initial point
038   cloud. In addition, the quality of the initial point cloud is one of the essential requirements of 3DGS,
039   showing large performance drops when trained with randomly initialized point cloud (Kerbl et al.,
040   2023). To mitigate such performance degradation, 3DGS and its extensions (Yu et al., 2023; Luiten
041   et al., 2024) often utilize Structure-from-Motion (SfM) (Schonberger & Frahm, 2016) algorithms,
042   which provide both accurate camera poses and point clouds.
043   However, in real-world scenarios, SfM can also fail to produce accurate point clouds, such as in
044   scenes with symmetry, textureless regions, and dynamic movements inducing occulsions (Bian et al.,
045   2023; Zhang et al., 2022). In addition, instead of applying SfM algorithms, camera poses are often
046   estimated with external sensors (Geiger et al., 2013; Sturm et al., 2012) or pre-defined as given
047   trajectories as in text- or image-to-3D generation (Tang et al., 2023; Yi et al., 2024). Initial point
048   clouds become unavailable in these scenarios, which leads to performance degradation in 3DGS.
049   To understand this strict requirement of accurate point clouds, which has not yet been fully explored,
050   in this work, we start with a natural question: “Why is accurate initial point cloud so important for
051   3D Gaussian Splatting?”. By conducting an in-depth analysis, we reveal an important limitation
052   of the current 3DGS optimization scheme: limited Gaussian transportability. This is primarily due
053   to the Gaussian being optimized solely with image photometric loss, which fails to provide clear
      guidance for the Gaussians to move to their optimal positions. As a result, the optimization process

      Based on our analysis, we propose a simple yet effective method, RAIN-GS (Relaxing Accurate

063   mitigates the limitation of the original 3DGS optimization, enabling 3DGS to achieve high-quality
064   reconstructions even with random initializations.

      2    R ELATED WORK
080
081
082
      Structure-from-Motion (SfM). SfM techniques (Agarwal et al., 2011; Schonberger & Frahm,
      2016) have been one of the most widely used algorithms to reconstruct a 3D scene. Through iterative
083
      feature matching and bundle adjustment, SfM algorithms estimate the camera pose and point cloud
084
      of the reconstructed scene. Despite the effectiveness of SfM algorithms, its incremental nature and
085   the computational intensity of bundle adjustment significantly increase its time complexity, often to
086   O(n4 ) with respect to n cameras involved (Wu, 2013). To mitigate such limitations, recent methods
087   propose to replace the components of SfM algorithms with learnable modules (Wang et al., 2024b;a;
088   Pan et al., 2024), accelerating the overall process.
089
090   Neural radiance fields (NeRF). NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) has succeeded in significantly
091   boosting the performance of novel view synthesis by optimizing an MLP that can estimate the den-
092   sity and radiance of any continuous 3D coordinate. With the camera poses of the given images,
093   NeRF learns the MLP by querying dense points along randomly selected rays, which outputs the
094   density and color of each of the queried coordinates. Various follow-ups (Barron et al., 2021; 2022;
095   Du et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Müller et al., 2022; Song et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
096   2023) adopted NeRF as their baseline model and further extend the ability of NeRF to model un-
097   bounded or dynamic scenes (Barron et al., 2021; 2022; Li et al., 2023), lower the required number
098
      of images for successful training (Song et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023), or utilize an external hash-
      grid to accelerate the overall optimization process (Müller et al., 2022). Although all of these works
099
      show compelling results, the volume rendering from dense points along multiple rays makes NeRF
100
      hard to apply in real-time settings achieving lower rendering rates of under < 1 fps.
101
102
103
      3D Gaussian splatting (3DGS). Departing from implicit representations of NeRF, 3DGS (Kerbl
      et al., 2023) represents the scene with explicit 3D Gaussians, achieving real-time rendering speed of
104
      over > 90 fps. Thanks to its efficiency, 3DGS has gained massive attention and has been extended to
105
      modeling large-scale scenes (Kerbl et al., 2024), dynamic scenes (Luiten et al., 2024), and enabling
106   the training with multi-scale images (Yu et al., 2023). Nevertheless, 3DGS is not without limitations
107   as the performance largely deteriorates when trained with sub-optimal (noisy, sparse, random) point
      clouds.

109
110   In this section, we briefly explain 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023), which represents the scene with mul-
111   tiple 3D Gaussians (Zwicker et al., 2002). Each i-th Gaussian Gi represents the scene with the
112   following attributes: a position vector µi ∈ R3 , an anisotropic covariance matrix Σi ∈ R3×3 , spher-
113   ical harmonic (SH) coefficients (Yu et al., 2021; Müller et al., 2022), and an opacity logit value
114
      αi ∈ [0, 1). With these attributes, each Gaussian Gi is defined in the world space x as follows:
                                                                   T    −1

118   ance Σ′i is defined as follows:
119                                         Σ′i = JW Σi W T J T ,                                    (2)
120   where J is the Jacobian of the local affine approximation of the projective transformation. The 2D
121   covariance matrix is simply obtained by skipping the third row and column of Σ′i (Zwicker et al.,
122   2002). Finally, to render the color C(p) of the pixel p, 3DGS utilizes alpha blending according to the
123   Gaussians depth. For example, when N Gaussians are sorted by depth, the color C(p) is calculated
124   as follows:

      where ci is the view-dependent color value of each Gaussian calculated with the SH coefficients,
128
      and G′i is the 3D Gaussian projected to the 2D screen space.
129
130   During optimization, 3DGS adaptively adjusts the number of Gaussians through cloning and split-
131   ting, to adjust the scene from being under-/over-reconstructed. Specifically, a Gaussian is cloned in
132   the mean position of the original Gaussian, if the scene is under-reconstructed. This can happen if
133
      the scene needs to be represented with more Gaussians and the covariance of the current Gaussian
      is too small. In contrast, if the scene needs to be represented with more detail and the covariance of
134
      the current Gaussian is too large, the Gaussian undergoes splitting, where the mean positions of the
135
      new Gaussians are sampled from the probability density function of the original Gaussian.

      4     M OTIVATION
138
139   In this section, we present an in-depth analysis of the 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) optimization
140   process (Kerbl et al., 2023), focusing on the impact of different initial point cloud qualities. We begin
141   by conducting both quantitative and qualitative comparisons using various point cloud initialization
142   which is shown in Section 4.1. From this comparison, we reveal two characteristics of 3DGS: 1)
143
      3DGS heavily depends on accurate initialization, showing large performance drops even with little
      noise, and 2) As the initialization becomes more inaccurate, 3DGS suffers from the scene being
144
      under-reconstructed which results in particular objects in the scene being left un-reconstructed. To
145
      further understand this behavior, we conduct a deeper analysis of the 3DGS optimization scheme in
146   Section 4.2, which reveals a critical limitation: limited Gaussian transportability. We show that this
147   limitation is the primary cause of the scene being under-reconstructed.

204   4.2    A NALYSIS OF 3D G AUSSIAN S PLATTING O PTIMIZATION
205
      As 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) represents the scene using explicit 3D Gaussians, under-reconstruction
206
      can occur if Gaussians are absent or insufficient in regions where the scene geometry exists. How-
207   ever, when the scene is under-reconstructed due to the lack of sufficient Gaussians, 3DGS inherently
208   has the ability to mitigate this issue by increasing the number of Gaussians through cloning. There-
209   fore, we first hypothesize that 3DGS lacks the ability to effectively transport Gaussians, making the
210   scene under-reconstructed due to the absence of Gaussians.
211

      2023), where they mention the proneness of 3DGS falling into local minima due to two main rea-
213   sons. 1) The Gaussians can only receive gradients close to their means, mostly from the range not

      5.1      S PARSE - LARGE - VARIANCE (SLV) INITIALIZATION
274
275   As discussed in Section 4.2, Gaussians easily fall into local minima due to receiving gradients from
276   a very local region and become stuck, lacking the ability to move to other locations. To prevent
277
      the Gaussians from falling into local minima, we propose a simple yet effective modification to
      the original random initialization method of 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023). Specifically, we follow the
278
      initialization of the Gaussian parameters where covariance is determined by the distances to the
279
      three nearest neighbors but significantly reduce the initial number of Gaussians from N = 100, 000
280   to N = 10. Despite being implementable with a simple one-line code change, this modification
281   leads to several key improvements.
282
283
      By reducing the number of initial Gaussians, the average distance between neighboring Gaussians
      becomes substantially larger, resulting in increased initial covariance values. This leads to what we
284
      call Sparse-Large-Variance (SLV) Initialization, wherein the Gaussians are initialized with greater
285
      spatial coverage. These larger Gaussians project to cover broader regions in the image plane, thereby
286   receiving gradient information from larger regions during optimization. Consequently, they are more
287   capable of learning the global structure of the scene during the optimization process, effectively
288   mitigating scenarios where the Gaussians are overfitted to represent very local regions.
289
      In addition to learning from larger regions, SLV initialization also provides benefits in transporting
290
      Gaussians to further locations via the splitting process. Since the splitting of Gaussians is performed
291
      by sampling from the probability density function (PDF) of the original Gaussian parameters, ini-
292   tializing with a larger variance naturally encourages the newly split Gaussians to explore a wider
293   spatial area. This helps mitigate the issue of Gaussians being unable to move from local minima,
294   allowing them to better cover the scene. Thus, SLV enhances the ability of Gaussians to adaptively
295   explore the scene throughout the optimization process, effectively mitigating the Gaussians from
296   falling into local minima.
297
298   5.2      P ROGRESSIVE G AUSSIAN LOW- PASS FILTERING
299

300   the number of 3D Gaussians increases exponentially due to the adaptive density control, which
301   can collapse into similar problems with the original random initialization. In order to ensure the
302   Gaussians to receive gradients from a sufficiently large area during the optimization step, we propose
303   a novel progressive control of the Gaussian low-pass filter which is utilized in the rendering stage.
304   Gaussian low-pass filter for 3DGS. In the rendering stage of 3DGS, the 2D Gaussian G′i pro-
305   jected from a 3D Gaussian Gi is defined as follows:

332   5.3       A DAPTIVE B OUND -E XPANDING S PLIT (ABE-S PLIT ) ALGORITHM
333
334
      The ABE-Split method is a straightforward extension to the original splitting algorithm in 3DGS,
      designed to address scenarios where under-reconstruction occurs due to the absence of Gaussians. In
335
      the original algorithm, two new Gaussians are sampled locally from the PDF of an existing Gaussian,
336
      which limits the ability to address globally under-reconstructed regions. Although SLV initialization
337   partially addresses this problem by enabling the Gaussians to be redistributed to larger regions,
338   we find that after multiple splitting steps, the variance of the Gaussian becomes small, where new
339   Gaussians can be only placed locally. To overcome this limitation, we propose ABE-Split, where an
340   additional Gaussian is split during the early stages of optimization. Specifically, we initialize a third
341   Gaussian at a position outside the current bounds defined with the positions of the Gaussians, by
342   multiplying a scalar to the current Gaussian coordinate. Although this approach is extremely simple,
343   when combined with our SLV initialization, we can effectively expand the bounds of the Gaussians,
344   ensuring the Gaussians to be actively re-distributed in globally under-reconstructed areas.

      6.1       I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
349   We implement our model based on the 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023). We follow the same training
350   process of the existing implementation in all datasets. For our sparse-large-variance (SLV) random
351   initialization, we set the initial number of Gaussians to N = 10. For progressive low-pass filter
352   control, we find that re-defining the value s as s = min(max(HW/9πN, 0.3), 300.0) every 1,000
353   steps results in better results compared to changing the value every step and adopt this strategy
354
      as default. For training SH coefficients, we set the maximum degree as 3 following the original
      implementation. As we regularize the Gaussians to learn from larger regions to receive a sufficient
355
      amount of gradient, we lower the divide factor from 1.6 to 1.4. In addition, as spherical harmonics
356
      should be learned with higher degrees when the Gaussians are modeling local regions, we increase
357   the SH degree after 5,000 steps which is approximately when the low-pass filter value becomes
358   s = 0.3. All other hyperparameters are left unchanged.
359
360   6.2       DATASETS
361
      We conduct experiments on multiple datasets, including experiments on the dataset where the
362
      initial point cloud is not accessible. Specifically, we use Mip-NeRF360 (Barron et al., 2022),
363   Tanks&Temples (Knapitsch et al., 2017), and Deep Blending (Hedman et al., 2018) dataset pre-

      7    C ONCLUSION
531
      In this work, we introduced RAIN-GS, a novel strategy to address the limitations of 3D Gaussian
532
      Splatting (3DGS), particularly its reliance on accurate initial point clouds and the limited transporta-
533
      bility of Gaussians. By leveraging Sparse-Large-Variance (SLV) initialization, progressive Gaussian
534   low-pass filtering, and the Adaptive Bound-Expanding (ABE) split algorithm, RAIN-GS effectively
535   mitigates under-reconstruction issues, enabling Gaussians to explore the scene more globally and
536   improve reconstruction quality. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that RAIN-GS achieves
537   competitive or superior results even when using random initializations, significantly reducing the
538   dependence on high-quality point clouds and making 3DGS a more robust solution for novel view
539   synthesis. We believe that our RAIN-GS can broaden the applicability of 3DGS in real-world sce-
      narios where obtaining accurate initial point clouds may not be feasible.

1042   D    A DDITIONAL ANALYSIS
1043
1044   Although point cloud can be noisy or unavailable in real-world scenarios (Bian et al., 2023; Zhang
1045   et al., 2022), 3DGS shows large performance drops depending on the accuracy of the initial point
1046   cloud (Kerbl et al., 2023). To understand the large performance gap of 3DGS, we conducted an
1047   in-depth analysis of the original 3DGS optimization scheme in Section 4 of the main paper. In this
1048   section, we further explore additional benefits from accurate initialization. Specifically, we analyze
1049
       SfM initialization in the frequency domain. Our analyses reveal two important characteristics of the
       optimization scheme of 3DGS: 1) the optimization scheme of 3DGS struggles to transport Gaussians
1050
       from their initialized locations and 2) the coarse structure information (low-frequency components)
1051
       provided by the accurate initialization enables the adaptive density control method of 3DGS to
1052   robustly model the remaining fine details of the scene in a coarse-to-fine manner.
1053
1054   3DGS lacks the ability to transport Gaussians. To represent and learn the scene with explicit 3D
1055   Gaussians, 3DGS first initializes the Gaussians Gi in the world space, whose means µi are defined
1056   by the initial point cloud. The point cloud can be either achieved from SfM or initialized randomly.
1057
       As mentioned in (Charatan et al., 2023), the process of fitting a 3DGS model is similar to fitting a
1058
       Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM), which is well-known for being non-convex and generally solved
1059
       with the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm (Dempster et al., 1977). They further note that,
1060   similar to the EM algorithm, training 3DGS from randomly initialized point cloud becomes prone
1061   to falling into local minima due to two main reasons. 1) The Gaussians can only receive gradients
1062   close to their means, mostly from the range not exceeding the distance of a few standard deviations,
1063   and 2) there is no existing path for the Gaussians that will decrease the loss monotonically.
1064
       Although (Charatan et al., 2023) only analyzes the case of starting from random initialization, we
1065
       verify that Gaussians can also easily fall into local minima when SfM-initialized point cloud become
1066   noisy. As shown in Table 1, we find that adding a small constant noise or adding a small noise ϵ
1067   sampled from a normal distribution (ϵ ∼ N (0, 0.5)) to the SfM-initialized point cloud, leads to
1068   large performance drops. Based on these observations, we hypothesize that the optimization scheme
1069   of 3DGS lacks the ability to correct or move the positions of the Gaussians. We empirically verify
1070   our hypothesis by calculating the average distance each Gaussian traversed after optimization, as
1071   shown in Table 2. It can be seen that the average distance each Gaussian moved is close to zero,
1072   indicating that the optimization scheme of 3DGS lacks the ability to move Gaussians, which can
1073   lead to the failure of capturing objects located far from the initial positions of the Gaussians. This
1074
       emphasizes the need for a strategy that can enable the Gaussians to transport further from their
       initialized locations, in order to successfully train 3DGS from sub-optimal initializations.
1075
1076   Accurate initialization guides 3DGS to learn in a coarse-to-fine manner. To further investigate
1077   the benefits of SfM initialization, we analyze the rendered images in the frequency domain using
1078   Fourier transform (Nussbaumer & Nussbaumer, 1982). As shown in Figure 12, the analysis in the
1079   frequency domain demonstrates that SfM initialization provides a coarse approximation of the target
       distribution.


                                                                                20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       As the goal of novel view synthesis is to understand the 3D distribution of the scene, it is neces-
1081   sary to model both low- and high-frequency components of the true distribution. However, prior
1082   NeRF frameworks (Lin et al., 2021; Park et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023) argue that NeRF is prone
1083   to overfitting and naı̈ve optimization leads to over-fast convergence of high-frequency components,
1084   expressed with high-frequency artifacts in the rendered image. To circumvent this problem, they
1085   adopt a coarse-to-fine learning strategy, which regularizes NeRF to learn the low-frequency compo-
1086   nents first. Similarly, prior works (Eckart et al., 2016; Hertz et al., 2020) utilizing GMMs for the
1087   task of point cloud registration or generation also mention that naı̈ve fitting of GMMs can result
1088   in converging to local minima. In order to robustly train GMMs, they also adopt a coarse-to-fine
1089
       strategy, implemented by starting with a small number of Gaussians and recursively increasing the
       number of total Gaussians. In both NeRFs and GMMs, coarse-to-fine strategy guides the network
1090
       to learn more robustly, leading to better performance.
1091
1092   In this perspective, starting the optimization of 3DGS from SfM-initialized point cloud can be un-
1093   derstood as benefitting from a similar coarse-to-fine process, where SfM provides the low-frequency
1094   components (Figure 12), and the adaptive density control method of 3DGS adds the Gaussians to
1095
       learn the remaining high-frequency details. Based on our observations, the success of 3DGS from

       E      A DDITIONAL I NTERPRETATION ABOUT S PARSE - LARGE - VARIANCE (SLV)
1150          INITIALIZATION
1151
1152   Drawing inspiration from GMMs (Eckart et al., 2016; Nichol et al., 2022), which gradually increase
1153
       the number of Gaussians to accurately model target point cloud, we observe that the adaptive density
       control of 3DGS can be viewed as a similar process. Through cloning and splitting operations, 3DGS
1154
       generally increases the number of Gaussians to find the adequate number of Gaussians required to
1155
       represent the scene. Based on our findings, we hypothesize that initializing 3DGS with a sparse
1156   set of Gaussians will prioritize the learning of low-frequency components, akin to the progressive
1157   refinement approach employed by GMMs. This sparse initialization strategy is expected to capture
1158   the overall structure of the target point cloud in the early stages of the optimization process, with
1159   finer details being added as the number of Gaussians increases.
1160
       To verify our hypothesis, we conduct a toy experiment in a simplified 1D regression task. Following
1161
       the original 3DGS which can be interpreted as the learning process of a 3D target distribution with
1162   multiple Gaussians, we use N Gaussians each with learnable means, variances, and weights, which
1163   are then blended to model a 1D target signal. Specifically, we follow the initialization methods of
1164   3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023), where the means are initialized randomly and the variances are initialized
1165   based on the distances of the three nearest neighbors. As a result, sparse initialization of Gaussians
1166   leads to a larger initial covariance (SLV) and dense initialization leads to a smaller covariance (DSV).
1167   To verify our hypothesis that learning with sparse Gaussians will prioritize the learning of low-
1168   frequency components, we conduct our toy experiment using N = 15 and N = 1000 for the SLV
1169   and DSV initialization respectively. Note that our 1D toy experiment without the adaptive density
1170
       control method of 3DGS provides a controlled environment isolating the effects of initialization.
1171   As shown in Figure 13, SLV initialization prioritizes the learning of low-frequency components
1172   compared to DSV initialization verifying our hypothesis. After 1,000 steps, SLV also shows a better
1173   prediction of the target distribution. Similar results can be observed when SLV is applied to 3DGS,
1174   as lowering the number of initial Gaussians N in randomly initialized settings significantly improves
1175   performance. Following the random initialization method of (Kerbl et al., 2023), which randomly
1176
       samples point cloud from a scene extent defined as three times the bounding box of the camera
       poses, SLV prioritizes the learning of low-frequency components, producing fewer high-frequency
1177
       artifacts. Surprisingly, SLV becomes more effective even until extremely sparse settings (e.g., as
1178
       low as N = 10), verifying the effectiveness of our novel SLV initialization method.

       F.3       RAIN-GS WITH NOISY CAMERA POSES
1263
1264
1265
       When camera poses are obtained solely from sensors (e.g., IMU) or when SfM algorithms fail to
       provide accurate camera poses, the performance of both NeRF and 3DGS has been shown to de-
1266
       grade significantly (Lin et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2024). To evaluate the robustness of our approach
1267
       under noisy camera poses, we conduct additional experiments on the Mip-NeRF360 dataset (Barron
1268   et al., 2022) with noisy camera poses. Noisy camera poses were generated by following the pro-
1269   tocol of CamP (Park et al., 2023), introducing approximately 5 degrees of noise to all poses. As
1270   the original rasterizer of 3DGS does not propagate gradients to camera poses, we implemented a
1271   custom rasterizer for both 3DGS and our method to enable camera pose correction during training.
1272   The average results, both with and without camera pose optimization, are summarized in Table 11.
1273   The results show that while noisy camera poses significantly degrade the reconstruction quality, our
1274   method consistently outperforms the original random point cloud initialization. The superior per-
1275   formance of our approach can be attributed to the progressive Gaussian low-pass filtering and SLV
1276
       initialization, which share similarities with the coarse-to-fine training strategies used for handling
       noisy poses in NeRF (Lin et al., 2021). Combined with our earlier experiments in Table 1, these
1277
       results demonstrate that our approach can robustly train 3DGS models regardless of whether the
1278
       initial point cloud or the camera poses are noisy.
1279
1280
1281   F.4       P ROGRESSIVE L OW-PASS F ILTERING VS . R ESOLUTION -BASED T RAINING
1282
1283
       The progressive Gaussian low-pass filtering method is similar to the coarse-to-fine training
1284   paradigm, where 3D Gaussians are trained from low-resolution images to high-resolution images.
1285   Larger values of the low-pass filter cause Gaussians to cover larger areas, resulting in blurrier im-
1286   ages, akin to coarse representations. However, our approach differs from training 3DGS directly
1287   from low-resolution images in two significant ways. First, encouraging Gaussians to cover larger
1288   areas ensures sufficient gradient propagation during training, enabling effective cloning and split-
1289   ting of Gaussians and preventing overfitting to highly localized regions. Second, our method uses
1290   a consistent high-resolution ground truth for supervision, avoiding the aliasing artifacts that can oc-
1291   cur when training with multi-scale images, as noted in Mip-Splatting (Yu et al., 2023). To compare
1292
       these approaches, we conduct an experiment on the Mip-NeRF360 dataset, replacing our progressive
       Gaussian low-pass filtering with a coarse-to-fine strategy using low-resolution to high-resolution im-
1293
       ages, with other strategies remain same. The results, presented in Table 12, show that our method is
1294
       more robust and achieves better performance across various scenes.
