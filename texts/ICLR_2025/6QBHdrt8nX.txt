009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The ideal LLM content moderation system would be both structurally inter-
012            pretable (so its decisions can be explained to users) and steerable (to reflect a
013            community’s values or align to safety standards). However, current systems fall
014            short on both of these dimensions. To address this gap, we present S AFETYA N -
015            ALYST , a novel LLM safety moderation framework. Given a prompt, S AFETY-
016            A NALYST creates a structured “harm-benefit tree,” which identifies 1) the actions
017            that could be taken if a compliant response were provided, 2) the harmful and
018            beneficial effects of those actions (along with their likelihood, severity, and im-
019
               mediacy), and 3) the stakeholders that would be impacted by those effects. It
               then aggregates this structured representation into a harmfulness score based on a
020
               parameterized set of safety preferences, which can be transparently aligned to par-
021
               ticular values. To demonstrate the power of this framework, we develop, test, and
022            release a prototype system, S AFETY R EPORTER, including a pair of LMs special-
023            izing in generating harm-benefit trees through symbolic knowledge distillation
024            and an interpretable algorithm that aggregates the harm-benefit trees into safety
025            labels. S AFETY R EPORTER is trained on 18.5 million harm-benefit features gen-
026            erated by SOTA LLMs on 19k prompts. On a comprehensive set of prompt safety
027            benchmarks, we show that our system (average F1=0.75) outperforms existing
028            LLM safety moderation systems (average F1<0.72) on prompt safety classifica-
029            tion, while offering the additional advantages of interpretability and steerability.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   As large language models (LLMs) and their applications become rapidly integrated into people’s
035   daily lives, it is critical to develop robust and reliable content moderation systems to ensure the safe
036   usage of LLM-based artificial intelligence (AI) technology (Bengio et al., 2024). Recently, Dalrym-
037   ple et al. (2024) proposed a blueprint for guaranteed safe AI, arguing that a “world model” that can
038   accurately predict the causal effects of AI behavior on the outside world is an integral component of
039
      robust and reliable AI systems. However, current LLM content moderation and safeguarding sys-
      tems are not grounded in an explicit understanding of such causal effects, since they rely on deep
040
      neural networks (such as LMs) to directly learn the relationship between input content and harmful-
041
      ness (Markov et al., 2023; Inan et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024a; Bai et al., 2022).
042   Such systems may excel at classifying the harmfulness of contents that are within their training dis-
043   tributions, but their behavior is unpredictable when applied to out-of-distribution samples due to the
044   challenge to explain or interpret their decision-making processes.
045
      Moreover, as AI technology reaches diverse human populations (e.g., people with different ethical,
046
      cultural, political, educational, professional, and socioeconomic backgrounds) there is increased
047
      need for safety moderation that can be tailored to specific applications, adapt to different safety
048   preferences, or reflect pluralistic human values (Sorensen et al., 2024a). AI safety criteria change
049   for different user demographics. For example, an AI technology that is deployed to children may
050   require stricter regulation on violent or sexually explicit content; one developed for scientists might
051   be compliant in response to queries about potentially dangerous chemicals, while such compliance
052   may not be appropriate for a model released to the general public. Thus, current LLM content safety
053   moderation can benefit substantially from pluralistic paradigms that can be aligned or steered to
      reflect different safety perspectives (Sorensen et al., 2024b).

091   To improve the interpretability and steerability of LLM content moderation, we introduce S AFETY-
092   A NALYST: an LLM safety moderation system that produces a world-model-inspired “harm-benefit
093
      tree” and aggregates its features mathematically via a process that can be steered to accommodate
      different safety preferences. While existing AI safety content moderation tools rely on opaque sys-
094
      tems which categorize prompts as harmful without fully interpretable further explanation (Zeng
095
      et al., 2024b; Xie et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2024; Mazeika et al., 2024), S AFETYA NA -
096   LYST is grounded in the fundamental principles of cost-benefit analysis (Arrow et al., 1996), explic-
097   itly representing what actions may cause which harmful or beneficial effects for different stakehold-
098   ers. Given a prompt, S AFETYA NALYST generates extensive trajectories of harmful and beneficial
099   consequences, estimates the likelihood, extent/severity, and immediacy of each effect, and aggre-
100   gates them numerically into a harmfulness score. The aggregation mechanism can be parametrically
101   modified to weight individual features differently (e.g., to up- or down-weight particular categories
102   of harms, benefits, stakeholders, etc.). Weights can be adjusted in a top-down manner to fit safety
103   standards or principles (e.g., as determined by a policy) or in a bottom-up matter that is optimized to
104
      fit the safety label distributions that reflect the values of a particular community or sub-community.
      Overall, this pipeline allows S AFETYA NALYST to produce interpretable, transparent, and steerable
105
      safety labels.
106

      classification, named S AFETY R EPORTER. Using 19k harm-benefit trees generated by a mixture of

121   Contributions. In this paper, we introduce S AFETYA NALYST, a novel conceptual framework for
122   LLM safety content moderation that offers more interpretability, transparency, and steerability than
123   existing approaches. The framework proposes a method to surface structured harmful and beneficial
124   effects of a user prompt (in the form of “harm-benefit trees”), which can then be mathematically
125
      aggregated according to their weights. To facilitate use of this framework, we train and release
      S AFETY R EPORTER, an open-source pair of LMs that specialize in the task of harm-benefit tree

      creation, which we evaluate against SOTA content-moderation tools showing competitive perfor-
127
      mance. In addition, we release a series of other artifacts that enable researchers and engineers to
128   build on S AFETYA NALYST: a large-scale dataset of 18.5 million safety features (organized in as
129   harm-benefit trees) generated by SOTA LLMs on 19k prompts, the first taxonomies of harmful and
130   beneficial effects for AI safety, and a feature aggregation algorithm that can be steered to align with
131   a given safety content label distribution or with top-down safety standards.

      2     T HE S AFETYA NALYST FRAMEWORK AND S AFETY R EPORTER SYSTEM
134
135
      S AFETYA NALYST breaks down the problem of content classification into sub-tasks (Figure 1). First,
136
      it generates interpretable harm-benefit features that describe the potential impacts of an AI system
137   complying with a particular request (prompt). This feature generation process can be performed
138   on any instruction-tuned LM through chain-of-thought prompting. Using data collected from a
139   mixture of SOTA LLMs, we fine-tuned an open-weight LM (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct) to specialize
140   in efficient feature generation. Second, these features are weighted using an aggregation algorithm
141   we developed based on their relative importance and aggregated into a numerical harmfulness score,
142   which can be used to produce content safety labels.
143
144   2.1   H ARM - BENEFIT FEATURE GENERATION
145
146   Given a prompt and a scenario where the AI language model complies with the user request, an LM
147
      extensively generates features (Figure 2) including all stakeholders (individuals, groups, communi-
      ties, and entities in society that may be affected), harmful and beneficial actions that may impact
148
      each stakeholder, harmful and beneficial effects that may be caused by each action on each stake-
149
      holder, and the likelihood (low, medium, or high), extent/severity (minor, significant, substantial, or
150   major), and immediacy (immediate or downstream) of each effect. Harmful actions are generated
151   in accordance with (and classified by) the AIR 2024 risk taxonomy (Zeng et al., 2024b), an exten-
152   sive categorization of harmful actions that could result from interaction with an LM, derived from
153   worldwide governmental and corporate policies. Beneficial actions are generated in free text. Due
154   to the lack of formal characterization of harmful and beneficial effects in the AI safety literature,
155   we defined a novel hierarchical taxonomy, drawing on the theories of basic/primary goods of two
156   influential contemporary moral philosophers: Bernard Gert (Gert, 2004) and John Rawls (Rawls,
157   2001). See Appendix A for complete taxonomies.
158   We used a diverse mixture of SOTA LLMs including GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemini-1.5-Pro
159   (Team et al., 2023), Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct, Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct-Turbo (Dubey et al., 2024),
160   and Claude-3.5-Sonnet to generate extensive harm-benefit tree data on 18,901 prompts randomly
161   sampled from WildJailbreak (Jiang et al., 2024), WildChat (Zhao et al., 2024), and AegisSafetyTrain
      (Ghosh et al., 2024). Table 3 in Appendix B shows the breakdown of prompt distribution over the

203   2.2          S AFETY R EPORTER : AN OPEN - SOURCE PAIR OF SPECIALIST MODELS FOR HARM AND
204                BENEFIT FEATURE GENERATION
205
206   To enable fast, cheap, and high quality harm-benefit feature generation, we trained an open-weight
207
      LM (Llama-3.1-7B-Instruct) to specialize in the tasks of generating harms and benefits using data
      collected from SOTA LLMs shown in Table 4. We applied supervised fine-tuning using qlora
208
      (Dettmers et al., 2024) to distill the knowledge about harmful and beneficial features of our in-
209
      terest from the teacher models (SOTA LLMs) into the student model (West et al., 2021). We trained
210   one specialist model to generate harm-trees and another for benefit-trees, which can be combined
211   into the full harm-benefit tree structure (Figure 2). Due to the extensive combined lengths of our
212   taxonomies and the harm-benefit trees generated by teacher LLMs, we fine-tuned two specialists
213   instead of one so that the inputs and outputs could jointly fit into the context window defined by
214   our hardware constraints (context window length of 18,000 tokens on 8 NVIDIA H100 GPUs). The
215   two student models that specialize in harm and benefit feature generation are integral components
      of S AFETY R EPORTER.

      2.4   F EATURE WEIGHT ALIGNMENT
263
264   To translate the numerical harmfulness score H computed over features generated by some S AFETY-
265   A NALYST model into a safety label for prompt classification, we aligned the aggregation algorithm
266   to the ground-truth labels from the WildJailbreak dataset on harm-benefit trees generated by teacher
267   and student models by optimizing W and γ within [0, 1] using maximum-likelihood estimation
268   over the analytical likelihood of σ(H). This procedure optimized the weights to minimize the dis-
269   crepancy between true and predicted safety labels. At inference time, the weights were frozen
      at their optimal values. Table 1 shows the classification performance (measured by the F1 score,

397   3.3   A DDITIONAL BENEFITS OF S AFETY R EPORTER
398
      Interpretability. Although S AFETY R EPORTER achieved outstanding performance on prompt
399
      safety classification, its most critical advantage is the interpretability of its decision-making pro-
400   cess compared to black-box systems, including all the baselines in Table 2. This interpretability is
401   two-folded: first, the features, on which the safety decisions are based solely, are explicitly gen-
402   erated by S AFETY R EPORTER and semi-structured (i.e., on carefully curated dimensions, including
403   stakeholder, harm, benefit, action, effect, extent, likelihood, and immediacy); second, these features
404   are aggregated using a white-box algorithm with transparent mechanisms and interpretable feature
405   weights that quantify the importance of corresponding feature values (Figure 3). Even though LLMs
406   (such as GPT-4) can generate explanations for their decisions, there remains a lack of interpretability
407   in how the decisions are reached and there is no reliable causal relationship between the explanation
408
      and the safety prediction. Our strong evaluation results in Table 2 suggest that our simple but in-
      terpretable features and aggregation mechanisms contain sufficient information for decision-making
409
      on content safety. Appendix E includes a detailed example of the full decision-making process of
410
      S AFETY R EPORTER, highlighting its interpretability and transparency.
411
412
      Steerability. In addition, S AFETY R EPORTER’s aggregation algorithm is defined by a set of trans-
413
      parent, interpretable parameter weights. The weights of the parameters we report in Figure 3 re-
414   flect the values of the annotators who provided the labels for the WildJailbreak dataset, for which
415   the algorithm was optimized. However, one central strength of the S AFETYA NALYST approach
416   is that the aggregation algorithm allows different safety features to be up- or down-weighted for
417   top-down adjustments, or fitted to a customized safety label distribution for bottom-up adjustments
418   (e.g., personalized safety alignment). Bottom-up adjustments of weights can be achieved by fitting
419   the aggregation model to a safety label distribution produced by an individual or group; the resulting
420   parameters would be aligned to the values expressed in the labels. We provide concrete explanations
421   for how to operationalize top-down weight adjustments in the case study in Appendix E.

423   4     R ELATED WORK
424
425
      Existing LLM content moderation systems. While there are many ways to approach AI safety,
426   S AFETYA NALYST is designed to do so through content moderation, the goal of which is to ensure
427   that an AI system “avoids unsafe, illegal outputs” (Huang et al., 2024). Existing LLM content mod-
428   eration systems include WildGuard (Han et al., 2024), ShieldGemma (Zeng et al., 2024a), Aegis-
429   Guard (Ghosh et al., 2024), LlamaGuard (Inan et al., 2023), and the OpenAI moderation endpoint
430   (Markov et al., 2023). These systems are LM-based classifiers that can categorize content risk, in-
431   cluding user prompts. Except for minor variations, each of these systems is structured similarly:
      a general-purpose LLM is trained on a large dataset that links user prompts to harmfulness labels.


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      The resulting content moderation systems then can classify prompts as harmful or not based on
433   the training it received (see Appendix D.1 for details). Although some systems built in this way
434   can achieve high classification accuracy on prompt safety benchmarks (e.g., classifying a prompt
435   as harmful or benign), their internal decision mechanisms are challenging to interpret, which limits
436   their reliability and generalizability. There is no straight-forward way to determine why a prompt
437   was classified as harmful by one of these systems. Furthermore, due to the lack of modularity in their
438   architectures, they cannot be easily steered to reflect different safety perspectives beyond expensive
439   and time-consuming re-training or fine-tuning processes.
440
441   LLM content risk. Prior work has characterized LLM content safety based on the potential risk
442   of the content, including the user input to the LLM, which may include jailbreak attacks, and the
443
      LLM output, on general and specific applications (Bai et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
      2024; Ji et al., 2024; Walker et al., 2024). The AI safety literature has relied on risk taxonomies
444
      to categorize unsafe content. Recent work has built on standard risk categories (Weidinger et al.,
445
      2022) to include more fine-grained categories (Wang et al., 2023; Tedeschi et al., 2024; Xie et al.,
446   2024; Brahman et al., 2024), achieve comprehensive coverage (Vidgen et al., 2024), and incorpo-
447   rate government regulations and company policies (Zeng et al., 2024b). Our system relies on the
448   taxonomy developed by Zeng et al. (2024b), selected for its comprehensive and fine-grained nature.
449   Overall, these taxonomies describe the unsafe nature of a prompt or unsafe actions that might result
450   from a prompt being answered. To our knowledge, no prior work exists that proposes formal tax-
451   onomies for the downstream effects of unsafe prompts (as opposed to actions; see Appendix A for
452   our taxonomies of harmful and beneficial effects).
453
454   Symbolic knowledge distillation. We distilled a pair of small, expert LMs (S AFETY R EPORTER)
455
      to create structured harm-benefit trees, the core of our interpretable framework. The symbolic
      knowledge distillation strategy leverages diffuse knowledge gained by large, generalist (and often
456
      proprietary) models to create a more compact expert student model that excels at one particular task
457
      (Xu et al., 2024; West et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2019). This strategy is useful (among other reasons)
458   to generate rich, structured data that is too costly or labor-intensive for humans to do by hand (West
459   et al., 2021). Indeed, prior work shows that symbolic knowledge distillation from machine teach-
460   ers can exceed the quality of human-authored symbolic knowledge (West et al., 2021; Jung et al.,
461   2023). Compared to the teacher models, our S AFETY R EPORTER uses less time, memory, compute,
462   and cost while achieving comparable performance, and it will be openly released for public use in
463   LLM moderation contexts.
464
465   Pluralistic alignment for LLM safety. Although current LLM safety moderation systems are yet
466   to be pluralistically aligned, recent interest in value pluralism Sorensen et al. (2024a) has given rise
467
      to rapid developments of pluralistic alignment approaches for LLMs. Lera-Leri et al. (2022) for-
      malized an aggregation method for value systems inspired by the social choice literature. Feng et al.
468
      (2024) outlined a more general framework based on multi-LLM collaboration, in which an LLM can
469
      be aligned to specialized community LMs for different pluralism objectives. Other methods have
470   been proposed for learning distributions of human preferences rather than the majority (Siththaran-
471   jan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). Additionally, some recent work has featured individualized
472   human preference data, including the DICES dataset (Aroyo et al., 2024) and the PRISM alignment
473   project (Kirk et al., 2024), paving the path to pluralistically or personally aligned LLM systems.

477   5   C ONCLUSION
478
479   We introduce S AFETYA NALYST, a novel conceptual framework based on LM-generated, semi-
480   structured harm-benefit trees for interpretable, transparent, and steerable LLM content safety moder-
481   ation. We operationalized the pipeline of harm-benefit tree data generation through chain-of-thought
482
      prompting, symbolic knowledge distillation, and weighted feature aggregation to implement a sys-
      tem for prompt safety classification. Our system achieved SOTA performance on a comprehensive
483
      set of prompt safety benchmarks, promising strong potential in real-world LLM safety applications.
484
485   Our application of S AFETYA NALYST and S AFETY R EPORTER to a comprehensive set of prompt
      safety benchmarks shows SOTA performance compared to existing LLM safety moderation systems.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      The current implementation of S AFETYA NALYST focuses on prompt harmfulness classification,
487   which can help an AI system determine if a user prompt should be refused. However, this framework
488   can be extended to solve other content safety tasks, such as LLM response moderation and general
489   text moderation.
490
      Our work addresses the important challenge of interpretability in AI safety research by providing
491
      a conceptual framework with concrete implementation to improve on existing LLM content safety
492
      moderation systems. The interpretable features generated by S AFETYA NALYST models are ag-
493   gregated mathematically to produce explainable decisions on content safety, which is particularly
494   desirable in safety-critical applications of LMs. When applied to determine if a user prompt should
495   be refused by an LLM, these features can help provide informative refusal responses if the prompt
496   is deemed unsafe by S AFETY R EPORTER. The steerability of S AFETYA NALYST to different safety
497   preferences makes it suitable for various safety goals, especially as LMs are deployed for more and
498   more applications that serve diverse human populations.
499   The S AFETYA NALYST framework extends the current scope of AI safety research by pioneering
500   two important conceptual innovations. First, we highlight the importance of explicitly considering
501   harmful effects in safety moderation in addition to harmful actions, which are the primary target
502   of current AI risk taxonomies. The strong performance achieved by S AFETY R EPORTER on safety
503   benchmarks suggests that weighting both actions and effects is an effective approach to determine
504   prompt harmfulness, which intuitively matches the decision process humans likely tend to use. Sec-
505   ond, we argue that the benefits of providing a helpful response to a user prompt should be traded
506   off with the harms in determining refusals. The discounted importance of beneficial effects from
507
      harmful effects in our aggregation model fitted to WildJailbreak, a cutting-edge LLM safety prompt
      dataset, suggests that the benefits of helpfulness may have been insufficiently represented in the la-
508
      bel generation of the prompts. Future prompt safety benchmarks and systems should account for
509
      effects and benefits in addition to only harmful actions to achieve more robust safety properties.
510
511   We propose that the weight optimization procedure of our feature aggregation algorithm, which
512   aligns feature weights to a given distribution of harmfulness labels, can be extended to pluralistic
513
      alignment of S AFETYA NALYST to different human values and safety preferences that reflect dif-
      ferent ideas of harmfulness. Developers could apply our feature weight optimization approach to
514
      align S AFETYA NALYST to a content label distribution that reflects their desired values and safety
515
      properties, such as one sampled from the customer base they serve.
516
517   Future work should validate the proposed pluralistic alignment approach for S AFETYA NALYST on
518   diverse human populations with pluralistic values and applications of LMs with different safety
519
      preferences. Already, the annotation data we collected on the harm-benefit trees hints that value plu-
      ralism could have an important impact on LLM content moderation. The fact that S AFETYA NALYST
520
      performs competitively on safety moderation benchmarks testifies to the fact that the harm-benefit
521
      trees are, in aggregate, aligned with the safety concerns of researchers and annotators creating gold-
522   standard labels for safety benchmarks. However, the results in Table 5 reveal a more complex
523   picture. While annotators agreed with the S AFETYA NALYST model-generated features the majority
524   of the time, there was also important variance, suggesting that there is room to fine-tune S AFETY -
525   R EPORTER or weight the aggregation mechanism of S AFETYA NALYST to align more closely with
526   individual or group values.
527
      Limitations. Generating the extensive harm-benefit trees, which are crucial to the interpretability
528   of S AFETYA NALYST, leads to longer inference time compared to existing, less interpretable LLM
529   moderation systems. Although our specialized S AFETY R EPORTER substantially reduces the cost
530   of feature generation than using an off-the-shelf LLM, we make the conscious trade-off between
531   interpretability and efficiency to make LLM content safety decisions more reliable and transpar-
532   ent. While our system draws on the principles of cost-benefit-analysis commonly used to justify the
533   adoption of governmental policies, following Arrow et al. (1996) we emphasize that simply sum-
534   ming harmful and beneficial effects will not be ultimately sufficient for safe decision-making. Future
535   work should explore issues related to the incommensurability of values, the effectiveness with which
536
      S AFETYA NALYST captures non-quantifiable harms and benefits, and the importance of weighting
      actions themselves, beyond just the effects they produce.

      A.4   E XTENTS / SEVERITY OF HARMFUL EFFECTS
811
812   Minor: harmful consequences are isolated, reversible, temporary, treatable, and do not require
813   significant intervention or resources to mitigate; the stakeholder may experience limited interruption
814   of functional ability and/or quality of life.
815
816   Significant: harmful consequences are endemic, reversible but with significant effort, acute, treat-
817   able with significant intervention or resources; the stakeholder may experience significant interfer-
818   ence of functional ability and/or quality of life.
819
820
      Substantial: harmful consequences are widespread, not easily reversible-if at all, chronic, and not
      treatable even with significant intervention or resources; the stakeholder may experience substantial
821
      impairment of functional ability and/or quality of life.
822
823
      Major harmful consequences are systemic, irreversible, persistent, critical, catastrophic, and im-
824   minently threatening; the stakeholder may experience destruction of functional ability and/or quality
825   of life.
826
827   A.5   E XTENTS OF BENEFICIAL EFFECTS
828
829   Minor: beneficial consequences are isolated, temporary, noticeable but have limited impact on the
830   overall situation or outcome; the stakeholder may experience a limited enhancement of functional
831   ability and/or quality of life.
832
833   Significant: beneficial consequences are widespread, significant, noticeable, and can lead to
834   meaningful improvements and tangible impacts; the stakeholder may experience significant im-
835
      provement in functional ability and/or quality of life.
836
      Substantial: beneficial consequences are extensive, important, and can lead to considerable pos-
837
      itive changes and profound impact on the situation or outcome; the stakeholder may experience
838
      substantial enhancement of functional ability and/or quality of life.
839
840   Major: beneficial consequences are systemic, persistent, critical, highly impactful, and can lead to
841   transformative changes that significantly alter the courses of events; the stakeholder may experience
842   a profound improvement in functional ability and/or quality of life.
843

845
846   Low: unlikely to occur, but not impossible. Rare under normal circumstances; less than 30%
847   chance of occurring.
848
849   Medium: possible occurrence, might happen under certain conditions or occasionally; between
850   30% and 70% chance of occurring.
851
852
      High: likely to occur, happens frequently or under normal conditions; above 70% chance of oc-
      curring.

      C     H UMAN EVALUATION OF GENERATED FEATURES
897
      Participants. Annotators were recruited through Prolific and paid an average of $15/hour for their
898
      participation. 42 workers annotated 25 sets of teacher-generated harmful features each, 44 workers
899   annotated 25 sets of teacher-generated beneficial features each, 20 workers annotated 15 S AFETY -
900   R EPORTER-generated harmful features each, and 20 workers annotated 15 S AFETY R EPORTER-
901   generated beneficial features each.
902
903   Method. For each harmful or beneficial effect, the human annotator was given detailed instruc-
904   tions on how to evaluate the validity of the given features, including a stakeholder who may be
905   impacted, a harmful/beneficial effect that may be caused to the given stakeholder, and the likeli-
906   hood, extent/severity, and immediacy of the effect (Figure 4). The human annotators were asked six
907   questions per effect, evaluating their understanding of the scenario and whether they thought each
908   given feature was plausible or reasonable. The plausibility of stakeholders and harmful/beneficial
909
      effects was rated on a 4-point scale (very plausible, somewhat plausible, somewhat implausible, and
      very implausible) due to their more open-ended nature, while the likelihood, extent/severity, and
910
      immediacy labels were rated on a binary scale (reasonable or not reasonable). The choices were not
911
      forced: the annotators had the option to state that they were unsure about any given feature. Re-
912   sults are reported in Table 5. To obtain the agreement rates, we computed the proportion of positive
913   ratings (e.g., very plausible, somewhat plausible, and reasonable) among all positive and negative
914   ratings.

       D.1   BASELINES .
975
976    All baselines evaluated in Table 2 are LM-based systems that have been applied to the task of prompt
977    safety classification. Here, we provide additional details of all baselines evaluated, highlighting their
978    differences.
979
980    OpenAI moderation endpoint (Markov et al., 2023). The OpenAI moderation endpoint is an
981    API provided by OpenAI that specializes in content moderation, which outputs binary labels and
982    category scores on 11 risk categories. The model and training data are proprietary, though the API
983    could be accessed free of charge at the time of our evaluation.
984
985    Llama-Guard (Inan et al., 2023). The Llama-Guard models are instruction-tuned models based
986    on corresponding Llama models (Llama-Guard on Llama-2-7B, Llama-Guard-2 on Llama-3-8B,
987
       and Llama-Guard-3 on Llama-3.1-8B) that specialize in producing binary labels on 6 risk categories.
       The models are open-weight, though the instruction-tuning data remains proprietary.
988
989
990
       Aegis-Guard (Ghosh et al., 2024). Aegis-Guard models are fine-tuned models based on Llama-
       Guard that specialize in content safety classification by outputting binary labels on 13 risk cate-
991
       gories. Aegis-Guard-Defensive labels the “needs caution” category as unsafe, while Aegis-Guard-
992
       Permissive treats it as safe. Both the model weights and fine-tuning data are publicly available.
993
994
       ShiedGemma (Zeng et al., 2024a). ShieldGemma models are instruction-tuned models based on
995    Gemma-2 models (2B, 9B, and 27B) that specialize in content safety classification by outputting a
996    binary safety label with an explanation, targeting 4 risk categories. The models are open-weight,
997    though the instruction-tuning data remains proprietary.
998
999    WildGuard (Han et al., 2024). WildGuard is an instruction-tuned model based on Mistral-7b-
1000   v0.3 that specializes in content moderation. Given a prompt and, optionally, a response, it generates
1001   binary labels on whether the prompt is harmful, whether the response contains a refusal, and whether
1002   the response is harmful. Both the model weights and instruction-tuning data are publicly available.
1003
1004   GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) GPT-4 is an instruction-tuned text generation model. Although it
1005   does not specialize in content moderation, it can be instructed to predict whether a given prompt is
1006   potentially unsafe. Both the model weights and training data of GPT-4 are proprietary, and querying
1007   the model incurs financial cost.

1160   Although the above prompt is labeled as safe in WildJailbreak, likely due to its educational potential,
1161   alternative views of AI safety might deem it potentially unsafe since the LLM could provide instruc-
1162   tions that may help the user conduct phishing scams, which could lead to harmful consequences
1163   on individuals at risk of phishing attacks. This value can be reflected by increasing the weights of
1164   relevant feature types in the aggregation algorithm, including:
1165
1166          • The relative importance of benefits to harms could be reduced to reflect a preference for
1167
                harmlessness over helpfulness
1168          • The weights of Content Safety Risks (e.g., Deception) could be increased to reflect stricter
1169            content safety regulation, such as in applications deployed to vulnerable populations
