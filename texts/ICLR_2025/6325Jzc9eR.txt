032                                                                   A BSTRACT
033
034                   Video editing usually requires substantial human expertise and effort. However,
035                   recent advances in generative models have democratized this process, enabling
036                   video edits to be made using simple textual instructions. Despite this progress,
037                   the absence of a standardized and comprehensive benchmark has made it difficult
038                   to compare different methods within a common framework. To address this gap,
039                   we introduce VEditBench, a comprehensive benchmark for text-guided video
040                   editing (TGVE). VEditBench offers several key features: (1) 420 real-world
041                   videos spanning diverse categories and durations, including 300 short videos (2-4
042
                      seconds) and 120 longer videos (10-20 seconds); (2) 6 editing tasks that capture
                      a broad range of practical editing challenges: object insertion, object removal,
043
                      object swap, scene replacement, motion change, and style translation; (3) 9 eval-
044
                      uation dimensions to assess the semantic fidelity and visual quality of edits. We
045                   evaluate ten state-of-the-art video editing models using VEditBench, offering
046                   an in-depth analysis of their performance across metrics, tasks, and models. We
047                   hope VEditBench will provide valuable insights to the community and serve as
048                   the standard benchmark for TGVE models following its open-sourcing.

      2.1   T EXT-G UIDED V IDEO E DITING (TGVE) M ODELS .
111
112
      TGVE aims to modify the visual content of a video based on textual prompts while preserving
113   its inherent characteristics. Pioneer Tune-A-Video (Wu et al., 2023c) inflates the image diffusion
114   models by incorporating cross-frame attention and fine-tuning on source videos to implicitly learn
115   and transfer motion. While demonstrating versatility across various editing tasks, Tune-A-Video
116   suffers from limitations in temporal consistency.
117
      Subsequent works focus on extracting various correspondences from the source video to enhance
118
      temporal consistency. Methods like FateZero (Qi et al., 2023), Video-P2P (Liu et al., 2023a), and
119   VidToMe (Li et al., 2024) extract cross- and self-attention features from the source video to guide
120   spatial layout and maintain coherence across frames. Others, such as Rerender A Video (Yang
121   et al., 2023), TokenFlow (Geyer et al., 2023), and Flatten (Cong et al., 2023b), focus on extracting
122   and aligning optical flows to improve the consistency of editing results. Meanwhile, Text2Video-
123   Zero (Khachatryan et al., 2023) and RAVE (Kara et al., 2024) utilize spatial conditioning tech-
124   niques from ControlNet (Zhang & Agrawala, 2023) to guide the editing process. Instruct Video-
125   to-Video (Cheng et al., 2023) explores instruction-guided video editing and investigates sampling
126   techniques for consistent long video generation.
127   More recently, with the emergence of advanced text-to-video (T2V) foundation models, researchers
128   have begun leveraging these models for improved temporal consistency in TGVE. MotionDirector
129   fine-tune T2V diffusion models with disentangled spatial and temporal LoRA modules for motion
130   customization. Diffusion Motion Transfer (DMT) (Yatim et al., 2024) employs a space-time feature
131   loss derived directly from the model to preserve overall motion during editing.
132   Despite these advancements, the field of TGVE still lacks a standardized benchmark for evaluating
133   and comparing different models. To address this critical gap, we introduce VEditBench, an open
134   and comprehensive benchmark designed to facilitate the standardized evaluation of TGVE models
135
136
      2.2   B ENCHMARKS FOR V IDEO G ENERATIVE M ODELS .
137
138   Early efforts rely on datasets like UCF-101 (Soomro et al., 2012), MSR-VTT (Xu et al., 2016),
139   and Kinetics (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017b; Carreira et al., 2018), which offer limited diversity.
140   Make-A-Video (Singer et al., 2023) evaluates on 300 text prompts across five common categories,
141   while FETV (Liu et al., 2023c) introduces fine-grained category labels and temporal dimensions
142   for a more in-depth assessment. EvalCrafter (Liu et al., 2023b) expands the scope with 700 real-
143   world prompts, and VBench (Huang et al., 2024) designs a compact yet representative prompt suite
144   across various evaluation dimensions and content categories. T2V-CompBench (Sun et al., 2024a)
145
      focuses specifically on compositional text-to-video generation with 700 prompts spanning seven
      compositional categories.
146
147   While these works advance the evaluation of text-to-video generation, video editing benchmarks
148   remain limited. LOVEU-TGVE-2023 (Wu et al., 2023d) introduces the first benchmark for text-
149   guided video editing, featuring 76 videos and 304 edit prompts across four edit types. Similarly,
150   BalanceCC (Feng et al., 2024) includes 100 videos, each paired with four edit prompts. However,
151
      both benchmarks lack sufficient video variety and task diversity.
152   To address these limitations, we propose VEditBench, a comprehensive benchmark compris-
153   ing 420 diverse real-world videos, each annotated with six fine-grained edit tasks. Importantly,
154   VEditBench includes 120 long videos (10-40 seconds), addressing the under-explored challenge
155   of long video editing.

190   3.1       C OLLECTION OF V IDEOS .
191
192   We aim to curate a diverse benchmark for real-world video editing applications. We consider six
193   categories from everyday life: Animal, Food, Scenery, Sports Activity, Technology, and Vehicle. We
194   search two large-scale video databases: YouTube1 and Videvo2 . YouTube serves as one of the largest
195   video repositories, featuring diverse user-generated content, while Videvo offers high-quality stock
196   videos shot by professionals.
197   To diversify the video content, we first ask GPT-4o to provide distinct keywords for each category
198   and use these keywords to search within the Panda-70M dataset (Chen et al., 2024) and YouTube.
199   To ensure data quality, we manually check each video and filter out those of low quality (e.g., blurry,
200   shaking, ghosting). We obtain the video captions using GPT-4o. Since the captions generated by
201   large multimodal models may exhibit issues such as missing objects or hallucinations of non-existent
202   objects (Bai et al., 2024), we also dedicate manual effort to reviewing and revising the captions,
203   ensuring that the key pixels are accurately described.
204   Mainstream TGVE models typically focus on short video editing, handling clips of 2 to 4 seconds
205   (24-30fps) in length (usually under 100 frames). To support this, we collect 300 short videos within
206   this range. Additionally, we explore a more challenging task: editing longer videos of 10 to 40
207   seconds (24-30fps). This task presents greater difficulty, as it requires the model to maintain long-
208   range consistency in video content (e.g., subject and style) across transitions. Solving this challenge
209   will make TGVE models more practical and applicable to real-world scenarios, such as the film
210   production.
211   Finally, we curate a collection of 420 videos, comprising 300 short videos and 120 long videos, all
212   at a resolution of 720⇥1280. These videos are balanced across and diversified within six categories.

230   3.2      D ESIGN OF V IDEO E DITING TASKS .
231
      The existing literature on video editing primarily addresses changes to the subject, background, and
232
      style. In this work, we explore broader applications of video editing and define six distinct video
233
      editing tasks as follows:
234
235       • Object Addition: add new objects to the video (e.g., “add a string toy near the cat”)
236
          • Object Removal: remove existing objects from the video (e.g., “remove the cat”)
237
238       • Object Swap: replace the object while maintaining its motion (e.g., “change the cat to a rabbit”)
239       • Scene Replacement: change the location (e.g., “place the cat in a grassy field”)
240
          • Motion Change: modify the object’s or camera’s motion (e.g., “tilt the camera downwards”)
241
242       • Style Translation: apply a specific style (e.g., “make it in Van Gogh style”)
243
      Each of these tasks serves a distinct purpose in examining the capability of TGVE models. We
244
      illustrate each editing task in Figure 13.
245
246   We task GPT-4o with the above descriptions to generate diverse edit prompts. Specifically, we feed
247   sampled video frames in a grid along with the video caption to GPT-4o, which then returns the corre-
248   sponding edit instructions and target prompts for each task (see Figure 2). Still, we manually review
249
      all the machine-generated prompts with necessary modifications to ensure accuracy. In Figure 3, we
      visualize the word distribution in our source and edit prompt set. More details about edit prompt
250
      generation can be found in the supplementary material.

253   4      E VALUATION M ETRICS
254
255   We assess the performance of TGVE models from two primary perspectives: 1) Semantic Fidelity
256   – Does the edited video adhere to the user’s command?, which evaluates whether the output video
257   accurately follows the guidance from input video and edit prompt. 2) Video Quality – Regarding
258   of the editing instructions, is the generated video visually appealing?, which focuses on the overall
259
      visual quality of the resulting video, independent of the applied edits. For each of these perspectives,
      we further define several sub-dimensions to enable a more fine-grained evaluation.
260
261
262
      4.1      S EMANTIC F IDELITY
263
      A successfully edited video should accurately follow: 1) the explicit instructions provided by users
264   (i.e., user prompt); 2) the implicit consistency with the source video (e.g., motion, structure, that are
265   not intended for editing). To this end, we break down Semantic Fidelity into two distinct aspects,
266   Text Alignment and Video Alignment, where the former focuses on the faithfulness with the target
267   prompt, and the latter considers the coherence with the source video.
268
269   [Text] Spatial Alignment. The CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021) trained on massive text-image
      pairs is capable of encoding meaningful embeddings for both modalities in a shared latent space.

      It is widely used to measure the similarity between visual and textual data. We compute the CLIP
271   feature similarity between the generated frames and their corresponding target prompts.
272
273   [Text] Spatio-Temporal Alignment. In addition to spatial content, videos display temporal dy-
274   namics such as object movement and camera motions. Huang et al. (2024) demonstrate the effec-
275   tiveness of using a video CLIP model, i.e., ViCLIP (Wang et al., 2023b), to evaluate text-video
276   alignment for text-to-video generation. We measure the Spatio-Temporal Text Alignment by calcu-

280

      4.2   V ISUAL Q UALITY
309
310   Video can be seen as a sequence of images with consistent temporal dynamics. We evaluate the
311   visual quality of a video from three perspectives: 1) Spatial Quality, which analyzes the video as
312   individual frames, independent of temporal dynamics, by calculating the average image score across
313   the frames; 2) Temporal Quality, which focuses solely on the temporal dimension, assessing the
314   consistency of the video over time; 3) Spatio-Temporal Quality, which considers the video as a
315   whole, integrating both spatial and temporal elements.
316
      [Spatial] Image Quality. Image quality focuses on the impact of distortions and other visual im-
317
      perfections in images on human perception. Recently, Wu et al. (2023b) introduce Q-Align, an
318
      advanced approach that trains large multimodal models to perform visual scoring. Q-Align demon-
319   strates a significant leap in image quality assessment, image aesthetic assessment and video quality
320   assessment – not only achieving state-of-the-art performance but also enhancing out-of-distribution
321   generalization capabilities. We adopt Q-Align as the method for image quality scoring.
322
323   [Spatial] Image Aesthetic. Image aesthetic measures the visual appeal and beauty of an image.
      We evaluate it using the Q-Align’s image aesthetic scorer trained on AVA dataset (Gu et al., 2018).

                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      [Temporal] Motion Smoothness. Motion smoothness refers to the continuity of movement in
325   visual content, often measured by the absence of noticeable jitter, stuttering, or abrupt transitions
326   between frames. We follow VBench (Huang et al., 2024) to use the motion priors from the video
327   frame interpolation model (Li et al., 2023) to assess the smoothness of motion in edited videos.
328
329   [Temporal] Temporal Quality. Fréchet Video Distance (FVD) is a widely used metric for assess-
330   ing the temporal quality of generated videos. It measures the similarity between the distributions
331   of real and generated videos by comparing the feature representations extracted from a pre-trained
332
      neural network. However, Ge et al. (2024) found that FVD tends to prioritize per-frame quality
      over temporal consistency. They attribute this bias to the features derived from a supervised video
333
      classifier trained on a content-biased dataset. To address this issue, they suggest using features from
334
      large-scale unsupervised models, which can help mitigate the bias. We employ their implementa-
335   tion of Content-Debbiased FVD3 , calculated using VideoMAE-v2 (Wang et al., 2023a) features, to
336   evaluate temporal quality.
337
338   [Spatio-Temporal] Video Quality. This dimension takes into account both spatial and temporal
339   factors, offering a comprehensive understanding of a video’s performance. Q-Align (Wu et al.,
340   2023b) utilizes a language decoder to assemble videos as sequences of frames, so as to unify video
341   quality assessment with image quality/aesthetic assessment under one structure. It also marks state-
342   of-the-art in video quality assessment; therefore, we utilize it as video quality scorer.

345
346   Evaluated Models. We evaluate ten TGVE models on VEditBench, including Tune-A-
347   Video (Wu et al., 2023c), MotionDirector (Zhao et al., 2023), VidToMe (Li et al., 2024),
348   Pix2Video (Ceylan et al., 2023), TokenFlow (Geyer et al., 2023), Flatten (Cong et al., 2023a),
349   Diffusion Motion Transfer (DMT) (Yatim et al., 2024), RAVE (Kara et al., 2024), Text2Video-

351   them, Text2Video-Zero and InsV2V accept editing instructions as input, whereas the others rely on
352
      a target prompt.
353
      Settings. To account for the varying capabilities of TGVE models in handling different
354   video lengths, we partition VEditBench into two subsets: VEditBench-Short and
355   VEditBench-Long, designed for evaluating short and long video editing, respectively.
356   VEditBench-Short includes all ten models outlined above, enabling a comprehensive com-
357   parison of their performance on short videos. However, since some models are not optimized for
358   long video editing, VEditBench-Long focuses on evaluating four models specifically designed
359   or adapted: Pix2Video, Text2Video-Zero, VidToMe, and InsV2V.
360
361   Results. To comprehensively assess the performance of different TGVE models on
362
      VEditBench, we conduct both quantitative and qualitative analyses. Our quantitative evalua-
      tion leverages a diverse set of metrics designed to measure various aspects of video quality and
363
      fidelity to the editing instructions (Table 3, Figure 5). Complementing these quantitative measures,
364
      we also perform a qualitative analysis to provide a more nuanced understanding of the strengths and
365   weaknesses of each model (Figure 6). This involves visual inspection of the edited videos and a
366   comparative analysis of their performance across different editing tasks and video categories.

      Model Performance Varies Across Tasks. The charts in Figure 5 clearly show that a model’s
      performance can vary significantly depending on the specific editing task. For instance, some models
411
      excel at object swap but struggle with motion change. This highlights the importance of evaluating
412
      models across a diverse range of tasks to understand their strengths and weaknesses.
413
414   Semantic Fidelity vs. Visual Quality Our analysis reveals an interesting tension between seman-
415   tic fidelity and visual quality in TGVE models. While some models excel at accurately adhering to
416   the editing instructions (high semantic fidelity), they may sometimes produce outputs with notice-
417   able visual artifacts or inconsistencies (lower visual quality). Conversely, other models prioritize
418   generating visually appealing results but may struggle to precisely fulfill the user’s intent. This
419   trade-off highlights a key challenge in TGVE: achieving a balance between accurately interpreting
420
      and executing editing instructions while maintaining high visual quality in the output. Future re-
      search could explore novel approaches to optimize both aspects simultaneously, potentially through
421
      improved training strategies or more sophisticated evaluation metrics that explicitly consider the
422
      interplay between semantic fidelity and visual quality.

424
      Challenges in Long Video Editing. Evaluating models on VEditBench-Long reveals unique
425
      challenges associated with editing longer videos. Maintaining temporal consistency and coherence
426   over extended durations proves to be a significant hurdle for most models. Edited outputs exhibit
427   increased occurrences of flickering, temporal artifacts, and deviations from the original video’s nar-
428   rative flow. These challenges stem from the increased complexity of modeling long-range depen-
429   dencies and the potential for errors to accumulate over time. Furthermore, computational constraints
430   become more prominent when processing longer videos, which can limit the effectiveness of certain
431   techniques. These findings highlight the need for further research focused on developing specialized
      architectures and training strategies tailored to the specific challenges of long video editing.

463   7             C ONCLUSION
464
465   In this paper, we introduced VEditBench, a comprehensive benchmark designed to standard-
466   ize and advance the evaluation of text-guided video editing models. VEditBench addresses key
467   limitations of existing benchmarks by providing a diverse collection of real-world videos, a wider
468   range of editing tasks, and a multi-dimensional evaluation framework encompassing both semantic
469   fidelity and visual quality. By evaluating ten state-of-the-art TGVE models on VEditBench, we
470   offer insights into their capabilities and highlight areas for future improvement. We believe that the
471
      open-source release of VEditBench will serve as a valuable resource for the research community,
      fostering further progress in this rapidly evolving field.
472
473
      Limitation and Future Work. The benchmark currently focuses on single-shot edits based on
474
      a single textual instruction. Future work could explore more complex editing scenarios involving
475
      multi-step edits or the composition of multiple instructions. We also plan to benchmark more TGVE
476   models using our VEditBench in the future.
