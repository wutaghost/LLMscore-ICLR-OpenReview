009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                Mathematical theorem proving is an important testbed for large language models’
012                deep and abstract reasoning capability. This paper focuses on improving LLMs’
013                ability to write proofs in formal languages that permit automated proof verification/
014                evaluation. Most previous results provide human-written lemmas to the theorem
015                prover, which is an arguably oversimplified setting that does not sufficiently test
016                the provers’ planning and decomposition capabilities. Instead, we work in a more
017
                   natural setup where the lemmas that are directly relevant to the theorem are not
                   given to the theorem prover at test time. We design an RL-based training algorithm
018
                   that encourages the model to decompose a theorem into lemmas, prove the lemmas,
019
                   and then prove the theorem by using the lemmas. Our reward mechanism is inspired
020                by how mathematicians train themselves: even if a theorem is too challenging to be
021                proved by the current model, a reward is still given to the model for any correct and
022                novel lemmas that are proposed and proved in this process. During training, our
023                model proves 37.7% lemmas that are not in the training dataset. When tested on a
024                set of holdout theorems, our model improves the pass rate from 40.8% to 45.5%
025                compared with the supervised fine-tuned (SFT) model.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The reasoning abilities of large language models (LLMs) are a significant marker of artificial intelli-
      gence and critical for complex and safety-sensitive applications, e.g., medical diagnosis (Fleming
031
      et al., 2023; Singhal et al., 2023), legal document review (Guha et al., 2024),online tutoring (Ruan
032
      et al., 2024; Wang & Demszky, 2023). Yet recent studies highlight the limited performance of LLMs
033   on reasoning tasks (e.g., Mündler et al. (2023); Valmeekam et al. (2023) and references therein).
034
035   Automated theorem proving by LLMs is an excellent reasoning task that abstracts away the need
036
      for numerical manipulation or tool use (e.g., using a calculator) and allows for precise evaluation of
      correctness with an automatic verifier (such as Isabelle (Nipkow et al., 2002) and Lean (De Moura
037
      et al., 2015)) even without ground truth. Thanks to tools such as Sledgehammer (Paulsson &
038
      Blanchette, 2012) that can automatically complete low-level details, the granularity of formal proofs
039   is similar to natural language proofs (see Fig. 1 (Left) for an illustrative example). Note that verifying
040   a proof is fundamentally much easier than generating the proof.1 Thus, learning to prove theorems
041   from verifiers’ supervision is reminiscent of weak-to-strong generalization (Burns et al., 2023).
042
      Previous results in this area largely focus on the setting where the theorem prover can use all the
043
      lemmas in the formal proof library, including those particularly written to decompose a specific
044
      theorem’s proof (Jiang et al., 2021; Polu & Sutskever, 2020). This setting arguably oversimplifies the
045   problem and doesn’t sufficiently test the models’ planning and decomposition capabilities, and it is
046   unclear whether the resulting models can be used to prove new theorems from scratch when such
047   lemmas are not available at test time. Instead, we work in a more natural setup where the theorem
048   prover needs to propose and prove lemmas to decompose the proof hierarchically itself (see Section 2
049   for more details). In Section 4.2, we demonstrate that this task is much more challenging.
050
      In addition, most existing proof-generation algorithms leverage the formal verifier by (a) providing
051   the verifier’s current proof state to the LLMs step-by-step, and (b) using best-first search algorithms
052   such as A⋆ to build a multi-step proof from many LLM-generated steps (Han et al., 2021; Jiang et al.,

      We train our models with reinforcement learning (RL) in a way that somewhat imitates a mathemati-
109   cian’s process: we reward correct partial proofs (i.e., proof sub-trees) even if the original theorem
110   (i.e., the root node) is not proved entirely. Since our model can generate and prove novel lemmas
111   during training, it can still make progress even if the theorem is too challenging. This is reminiscent
112   of how mathematicians prove standalone lemmas which make progress towards solving an open
113   problem. We illustrate our algorithm in Fig. 2, and defer the details to Section 3.2.
114
      We test our model ProD-RL by generating proof trees on holdout theorems that the model is never
115   trained on, and we show that our model ProD-RL outperforms several other baselines. Compared
116   with the supervised fine-tuned (SFT) model on the same training set , our model improves the pass
117   rate from 40.8% to 45.5% on the holdout test set, whereas vanilla reinforcement learning without
118   lemma proposals during training does not improve the corresponding SFT model (see Section 4.3).
119   This is partly because our method encourages the model to propose and prove additional lemmas —
120   in fact, 37.7% of the lemmas proved during training are not in the dataset. As a result, the model still
121   improves even if it is already fine-tuned on the same dataset with human-written ground-truth proofs.

123   2    S ETUP
124
125   Conditional proofs. We use the term conditional proof to denote a proof that, in addition to the
126   standard formal proof syntax, can propose new lemmas by enclosing their statements by <invoke>
127   and </invoke> tokens (examples shown in the blue boxes of Fig. 1). In particular, a conditional proof
128   has the following format:
129
130
            t1 <invoke> l1 </invoke> t2 <invoke> l2 </invoke> · · · tk <invoke> lk </invoke> tk+1
131   where t1 , · · · , tk+1 denote proof segments in the original formal proof syntax (see e.g., Fig. 1, proof
132   texts in black), and l1 , · · · , lt denote proposed lemma statements (see e.g. Fig. 1, proof texts in red).2
133
134   Proof tree nodes. With the proposed lemmas, a complete proof forms a tree structure (as shown in
135   Fig. 1). A node in a proof tree is a tuple of premises, context, a theorem statement, and a conditional
136   proof. Premises represent the lemmas that are treated as common knowledge, which are typically not
137   directly relevant to the proof. We allow the model to directly use them in the proof so that it does not
138   have to repetitively prove all the fundamental facts, such as properties of continuous functions and
139   natural numbers. Context represents the necessary contents to prepare the theorem statement, such as
140   the definition of specific objects/functions. We use the context as part of the prompt for the LLMs to
141
      generate proofs, and to configure the proof verifier to check the generated proofs.
142
143   Correctness of conditional proofs and proof trees. A proof tree node n with conditional proof
144
      t1 <invoke> l1 </invoke> t2 <invoke> l2 </invoke> · · · tk <invoke> lk </invoke> tk+1 is locally
      correct if, after adding l1 , . . . , lk to the set of premises, t1 . . . tk+1 , is a proof to the statement of n
145
      that is accepted by the formal verifier under the context of n.
146
147   We consider a proof tree valid if, for every node, each of its child nodes corresponds to one proposed
148   lemma and shares the same premises and context with its parent node. A tree node n is globally
149   correct with respect to a given set of tree nodes N if we can construct a valid proof tree with root n
150
      using the locally correct tree nodes in N . We use this more flexible definition of global correctness
      since if we generate more than one proof tree per theorem, we may mix their locally correct nodes to
151
      form a globally correct proof.
152
153   Global correctness corresponds to the standard notion of correctness (i.e., whether the theorem is
154   proved), and local correctness is a weaker concept, referring to the correctness of conditional proofs
155   assuming the proposed lemmas. When a tree node is globally correct, we can construct a complete
156
      proof to its statement that is acceptable by the formal proof verifier — first, we build a valid proof
      tree from the locally correct subset of N , and then list all the statements and their corresponding
157
      conditional proofs in a child-first order and remove all the lemma proposal steps (since the proposed
158
      lemmas and their proofs will be already listed in the proof text according to child-first order).
159

      Dataset construction. We construct the datasets by parsing raw proof-library files into tuples of
163   the form (premises, context, statement, conditional proof).
164
165   In particular, we first segment each of the files into blocks c1 s1 p1 · · · cl sl pl where the si are
166
      theorem statements, the pi are the corresponding proofs, and the ci are the file contents between
      proofs, such as object definitions and local assumptions. Next, we build proof trees from each
167
      segmented file by iteratively removing (si , pi ) pairs from the file if the theorem si is not referred to
168
      in the remaining file contents (in other words, in the first iteration we peel off the root nodes of the
169   proof trees from the file, and then the nodes in the next level, etc.). Note that some theorems cannot
170   be peeled off by this process because they are referred to in some file content cj (e.g., lemmas used to
171   instantiate local objects). We use Ttree to denote the subset of theorems peeled off during the process.
172
      For every theorem si , we construct an example where the context is the concatenation of {cj : j < i}
173
      and {sj : j < i, sj ̸∈ Ttree } in the order they appear in the file. That is, we exclude all the lemmas
174
      that are ever peeled off — the remaining lemmas are included in the context.
175
176   To construct the conditional proof of theorem si , we add the proposed lemma statements to
177   the original proof pi . In particular, we split the proof pi into steps t1 , · · · , tk using the for-
178
      mal language parser. Then for every step tj that uses lemmas lj,1 , . . . , lj,nj from Ttree , we in-
      sert the statements of these lemmas enclosed by the <invoke> and </invoke> tokens, denoted by
179
      ζj = <invoke> lj,1 </invoke> · · · <invoke> lj,nj </invoke>, into the proof right before tj . In other
180
      words, the conditional proof is the concatenation ζ1 t1 · · · ζk tk . Similar to Jiang et al. (2022b), we
181   use Sledgehammer, a premise selection tool that automatically searches for proofs to the current goal,
182   to replace proof steps that are originally generated by it (see Section A.3 for more details) so that the
183   mode can focus less on the tedious low-level details.
184
      The premises are all the theorems from predecessor files, which are typically not directly relevant
185
      to the theorem (otherwise they will be stated in the same file). Theorems in the premise set can be
186
      used directly in the proof, or they can be selected by Sledgehammer to search for proof steps. In our
187   implementation, the premises are implicitly defined by the dependency graphs of the files.
188
189   We split the training and test set (AFP test) based on the dependency of the files in the proof library
190
      so that the examples in the training set never refer to any files in the test set (see Section 4.2 for
      details). We also construct an additional test set, AFP 2023, by parsing AFP files submitted after the
191
      knowledge cutoff date of the Llemma model (April 2023) to eliminate potential data leakage issues.
192
      Compared with prior works (First et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2021), the two major differences in our
193   setup are the availability of lemmas from the same file and the training/test split. In Section 4.2, we
194   discuss and test their effects in detail.
195
196
      Finally, to construct the SFT dataset, for each example in the training set, if its conditional proof
      proposes at least one lemma, we create an augmented example by moving the proposed lemmas from
197
      the conditional proof into the context — this augmented example does not propose new lemmas and
198
      is always locally correct.

203   In this section, we first describe how to use LLMs to generate proof trees, and then introduce our
204   reinforcement learning method (ProD-RL) that rewards the model to decompose proofs hierarchically.

206   3.1   G ENERATING PROOF - TREES USING LLM S
207
208   To generate proof trees using an autoregressive model πθ , we need to first fine-tune the model to
209   follow a specific format:
210
      (a) the input x to the model πθ is the concatenation of a context and a theorem statement, and
211
212
      (b) the expected output y of the model is a special token t0 followed by a conditional proof, where
          t0 is either <use_invoke> or <no_invoke>, denoting whether the following conditional proof
213
          should propose new lemmas.
214
215   We introduce the special token t0 before a conditional proof so that we can increase the probability of
      the <use_invoke> token during RL to let the model propose more lemmas for better exploration.

217   corresponding context c, we first sample from πθ autoregressively starting with the prompt x = c s,
218   and ideally the model outputs a special token t0 followed by a conditional proof ρ (Line 3). Next,
219   we parse the conditional proof ρ and collect the proposed lemmas l1 , · · · , lk for the next round of
220   generation (Line 5). We force the model to generate conditional proofs without proposing new
221   lemmas at a certain depth so that the proof tree doesn’t grow indefinitely, which can be implemented
222   easily by replacing the prompt with x′ = c s <no_invoke> (Line 6).

237   3.2    R EINFORCEMENT LEARNING WITH LEMMA PROPOSAL
238
239   Our reinforcement learning method is illustrated in Fig. 2. We start with a supervised fine-tuned
240
      model so that it can generate conditional proofs in the desired format. Then at every round, we
      randomly sample a batch of examples D from the training dataset and perform the following steps.
241
242
      Step 1: Generate proofs. We first generate proof trees for every theorem in D. For better
243
      exploration, we use a modified version of Alg. 1 (shown in Alg. 2 of Appendix A.1) with the
244   following differences:
245
246   (a) for the theorems where the probability πθ (<use_invoke> | x) is among the top 50% in the batch,
247
          we will force the model to generate conditional proofs with t0 = <use_invoke>. Otherwise, we
          sample t0 according to the probability of πθ (· | x), and
248
249   (b) for every theorem where the model generates a conditional proof with new lemmas, we also let
250
          the model generate another conditional proof without proposing lemmas. If any of these two
          conditional proofs is globally correct, it can be used to construct proof trees for other theorems.
251
252
      Step 2: Determine the reward of an example. In this step, we first check the local correctness of
253
      each conditional proof using the formal verifier (Step 2a in Fig. 2).
254
255   In addition to the verifiers’ output, we apply two filters to help train the model: (a) we filter out
256   trivial lemma proposals — if a proposed lemma directly implies the theorem (e.g., if the proposed
257   lemma has exactly the same statement as the theorem), we simply discard this example, and (b) we
258
      remove unnecessary lemma proposals — if the conditional proof is still correct after removing all the
      references to a proposed lemma, we remove this lemma from the conditional proof.
259
260   We then determine the global correctness of the generated proofs. Finally, we assign a binary reward
261   r(c, s, ρ) to each tree node with context c, statement s, and conditional proof ρ based on its global
262   correctness (Step 2b in Fig. 2).
263
264   Step 3: Update the model by REINFORCE. In this step, we first construct a training dataset
265   consisting of examples with format (prompt, target, weight) from the conditional proofs collected in
266
      Step 1, and then update the model πθ using the weighted cross-entropy loss.
267   For each generated conditional proof, we add one example to the training dataset where the prompt is
268   the context concatenated with the theorem statement, and the target is the conditional proof prepended
269   by the <use_invoke> or <no_invoke> token. Note that the reward of a conditional proof depends not
      only on the correctness of the conditional proof, but also on the correctness of the proposed lemmas.

270
      To reduce the variance of our gradient updates, we train a value function Vϕ that predicts the expected
271   reward of the current policy on a given proof tree node (i.e., Vϕ ≈ Eρ∼π(·|c,s) [r(c, s, ρ)]). The weight
272   of an example is the product of the value function’s outputs on invoked lemmas multiplied with a
273   length penalty to incentivize shorter proofs — for a proof tree node with conditional proof length h
                                                                                   Qk
274   and proposed lemmas l1 , · · · , lk , the weight w of this example is w = γ h i=1 Vϕ (li ) with discount
275   factor γ ∈ (0, 1), or w = 0 if the proof tree node is not locally correct.
276
      To simplify the implementation, we train the value function to predict two special tokens, <true> and
277
      <false>, conditioned on the context and theorem statement. Let pt be the probability of the <true>
278
      token conditioned on the context and theorem statement, and pf the probability of the <false> token.
279   The output of the value function is then pt /(pt + pf ).
280
281   As done for the SFT dataset, we add one augmented example by moving the proposed lemma from
282
      the conditional proof to the context for any locally correct conditional proof with new lemmas. We
      also add examples constructed from the human-written conditional proofs (i.e., the ground-truth
283
      proofs) of theorems in the batch D. In addition, we use a replay buffer to stabilize the training.
284
285
      Remarks. Note that we update the model using partial proofs (i.e., proof sub-trees) even if the
286
      original theorem from the dataset (i.e, the root of the proof tree) is not proved. Hence, our method
287
      can also be viewed as an instantiation of hindsight experience replay (Andrychowicz et al., 2017),
288   where the hindsight trajectories are correct proof sub-trees.
289
290   Our algorithm is also closely related to expert iteration. In our notation, expert iteration is equivalent
291
      to using a binary weight w = I [the proof tree node is globally correct] .

369   This section reports the models’ pass@k performance on AFP test and AFP 2023 test datasets. Recall
370   that the theorems in the AFP 2023 test set were submitted after we retrieved the training set, and

      5    R ELATED WORKS
496
497
      To generate formal proofs with language models, most prior methods provide the verifier’s state to
498
      the model to sample the proofs step-by-step, and use algorithms like MCTS to search for a correct
499
      complete proof (Han et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2021; 2022b; Lample et al., 2022; Polu & Sutskever,
500   2020; Polu et al., 2022). The major drawback of these methods is their high computation cost at test
501   time. Recent works (First et al., 2023) train a large language model to generate a whole proof directly
502   without the verifier’s state. Our baseline, the SFT model without lemma proposal, can be viewed as a
503   reproduction of their methods with a slightly different way of computing the context.
504
      Prior works also use reinforcement learning or expert iteration to improve the models’ performance
505
      on writing formal proofs, where the training datasets contain formal synthetic inequalities (Polu et al.,
506   2022) or statements translated from natural language mathematical problems (Wu et al., 2022; Xin
507   et al., 2024a;a;b). In contrast, we aim to improve the models’ performance without any additional
508   (even unlabeled) data. As future works, we could run ProD-RL with additional datasets.
509
      Another line of research aims to translate natural language proofs into formal proofs (Jiang et al.,
510
      2022a; Zheng et al., 2023). Xin et al. (2023) build a library of useful lemmas by decomposing natural
511
      language proofs into lemmas with an LLM and then formalizing the decomposed proofs. In contrast,
512   we propose new lemmas entirely in formal language.
513
514   Automated theorem provers (ATPs) have been extensively studied, with various learning- or search-
515
      based methods developed to generate tactics for a given proof state (e.g., Gauthier et al. (2021); Schulz
      et al. (2019) and references therein). In comparison, our method focuses on generating multi-step
516
      proofs with LLMs while using existing ATP tools to complete low-level details. Orthogonally, a
517
      recent method (Mikuła et al., 2023) improves existing provers with a transformer-based retrieval
518   model as the premise-selection tool, and could potentially be combined with our methods.
519
520   In general, mathematical question-answering tasks (such as GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH
521
      (Hendrycks et al., 2021)) and theorem-proving tasks (such as Welleck et al. (2021)) are well-accepted
      benchmarks for the reasoning capability of large language models. Prior works show that instruction
522
      tuning or RL can significantly improve the models’ performance (Shao et al., 2024). However,
523
      evaluation on these tasks is either performed by another language model (which is prone to errors)
524   (Lightman et al., 2023), or requires ground-truth answers that are hard to acquire at scale.
525
526
527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we design a reinforcement learning algorithm that encourages LLMs to write formal
530   proofs by decomposing them hierarchically. We also design a more natural testing setup by removing
531   the directly relevant lemmas from the context. We show that, by proposing and proving new lemmas
532   that are not present in the training dataset, the resulting model ProD-RL outperforms baselines trained
533   on the same dataset.

708
      Details of using sledgehammer in the proof. Sledgehammer is a premise selection tool that
709   can automatically generate proofs to solve the current goal. Although sledgehammer is not always
710   applicable, Jiang et al. (2022b) shows that letting the model to call sledgehammer whenever it is
711   applicable greatly improves the model’s performance.
712
      To let the model use sledgehammer, we replace the actual proof steps in the training dataset by a call
713
      to sledgehammer if the proof step either (a) contains the proof tactics ‘meson, metis, and smt’ (these
714
      tactics are typically generated by sledgehammer), or (b) belongs to a predefined simple set of proof
715   tactics that can be easily generated. In particular, they are
716
717   [by auto, by simp, by blast, by fastforce, by force,
718   by eval, by presburger, by sos, by arith, by linarith,
719   by (auto simp: field_simps)]
720
721   When testing a generated proof with calls to sledgehammer, we follow the pipeline of (Jiang et al.,
722   2022a) — first, we try to replace the ‘sledgehammer’ command by one of the predefined tactics. If
723   all the attempts fail, we call the actual premises selection tool in Isabelle with a 10s timeout. If the
724
      tool does not return a valid proof, we consider this step incorrect.
725   Note that Jiang et al. (2022b) decide when to replace the actual proof step by a call to sledgehammer
726   more aggressively. They attempt to call sledgehammer at every proof step, and replace the actual
727   proof step with sledgehammer if the attempt is successful. In contrast, our decision is made without
728   interacting with the formal verifier. This is because applying sledgehammer to every proof step
729   requires a lot of compute, which would significantly slow down the reinforcement learning process.
730
731   Dataset split. Here we describe how to split the training and test data based on the dependency
732   of the AFP files. We first compute the dependency graph by crawling the AFP website https:
733   //www.isa-afp.org/entries/, which lists the dependency of the AFP entries. Then we find
734   the set of AFP entries that all other entries do not depend on using the dependency graph, in which
735
      we randomly sample 10% of the entries as the holdout test set. The resulting holdout entries are:
736
      [Verified_SAT_Based_AI_Planning, SIFPL, Khovanskii_Theorem,
737
      Bondy, Rewriting_Z, Decreasing-Diagrams-II, Registers,
738   LocalLexing, FeatherweightJava, FFT, Knot_Theory, Eval_FO,
739   Saturation_Framework_Extensions, Hales_Jewett, SPARCv8,
