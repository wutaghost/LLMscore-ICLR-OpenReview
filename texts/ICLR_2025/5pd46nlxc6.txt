009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Value factorization is widely used to design high-quality, scalable multi-agent
012
               reinforcement learning algorithms. However, current methods typically assume
013            agents execute synchronous, 1-step primitive actions, failing to capture the typi-
014            cal nature of multi-agent systems. In reality, agents are asynchronous and execute
015            macro-actions—extended actions of variable and unknown duration—making de-
016            cisions at different times. This paper proposes value factorization for asyn-
017            chronous agents. First, we formalize the requirements for consistency between
018            centralized and decentralized macro-action selection, proving they generalize the
019            primitive case. We then propose update schemes to enable factorization archi-
020            tectures to support macro-actions. We evaluate these asynchronous factorization
021
               algorithms on standard macro-action benchmarks, showing they scale and perform
               well on complex coordination tasks where their synchronous counterparts fail.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) algorithms typically assume that agents have syn-
028   chronous execution (Rashid et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021)—each agent selects
029   a 1-step primitive action that starts and ends simultaneously at each execution step. However, this
030   assumption does not typically hold in real scenarios where agents select and complete actions with
031   varying durations at different times. These temporally extended behaviors are known as macro-
032   actions and generalize the primitive case, allowing asynchronous execution (Amato et al., 2019).
033   Macro-actions have several advantages over primitive ones as they (i) foster explainability by rep-
034   resenting complex multi-step real-world behavior (e.g., navigating to a waypoint, waiting for a hu-
035   man); (ii) benefit value-backup, improving the efficiency of value-based learning (Mcgovern et al.,
036
      1999); and (iii) enable action selection to take place at a higher level, using existing controllers to
      execute behaviors (e.g., a navigation stack) without learning end-to-end actions (e.g., control motor
037
      torques). Nonetheless, limited attention has been devoted to this area of research (Jia et al., 2020;
038
      Xiao et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2024), motivating the need for principled and scalable approaches.
039
040   Due to partial observability and communication constraints, MARL algorithms often learn policies
041   conditioned on local information while leveraging centralized training data to foster collaborative
042
      behaviors (i.e., centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE) (Tuyls & Weiss, 2012)).
      In the synchronous case, value factorization has been successful at CTDE by using a mixer network
043
      to factor a joint action value Qtot into per-agent utilities conditioned on local information (Rashid
044
      et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2021). To achieve a sound factorization, these methods ensure consistency
045   between the local and the joint action selection (i.e., the actions selected from each are the same)—a
046   principle known as the individual global max (IGM) (Son et al., 2019). Agents can thus execute
047   in a decentralized manner by selecting actions according to the local utilities while learning in a
048   centralized fashion. Value factorization methods are some of the most scalable and high-performing
049   MARL methods, but extending them to the asynchronous case has yet to be investigated.
050
      This paper introduces value factorization for asynchronous MARL with macro-actions. We
051   lay the theoretical foundations by formalizing Macro-IGM—the IGM principle for macro-
052   action-based value functions—and showing it generalizes the primitive case by represent-
053   ing a broader class of functions. On the practical side, we bridge the gap with primi-
      tive action-based methods by introducing asynchronous value factorization (AVF) algorithms.

076   chronous decentralized policies in most tasks, allowing us to achieve significantly higher payoffs and

084

091   a policy πi (ui |hi ), mapping local histories hi = (oi0 , ui0 , . . . , oit ) ∈ H i to actions. In finite-horizon
092   Dec-POMDPs, the objective is to find       a joint policy

      2.2     L EARNING MACRO - ACTION - BASED POLICIES
126   Macro-Action Dec-POMDPs (MacDec-POMDPs) (Amato et al., 2019) extend Dec-POMDPs to
127   include durative actions (in addition to the primitive ones) with ⟨M, Ô, Tôi∈N ⟩: where M ≡
128   ⟨M i ⟩i∈N and Ô ≡ ⟨Ôi ⟩i∈N are the set of joint macro-actions and macro-observations. Similar
129   to the primitive case, we define joint macro-action-macro-observation histories (or macro-histories)
130   ĥt ∈ Ĥ and local ones ĥit ∈ Ĥ i . Macro-actions are based on the options framework (Sutton et al.,
131   1999); an agent i’s macro-action mi is defined as a tuple ⟨Imi , πmi , βmi ⟩: Imi ⊂ Ĥ i is the initiation
132   set; πmi (·|hi ) is the low-level policy associated with the macro-action; and βmi : H i → [0, 1] is the
133   termination condition.2 The different histories allow the agents to maintain the necessary informa-
134   tion locally to know how to execute and terminate mi . During decentralized execution, agents inde-
135   pendently select a macro-action that forms the joint one m = ⟨mi ⟩i∈N , and maintain a high-level
136   policy πM i (mi |ĥi ). At each step of mi ’s low-level policy, agent i independently accumulates the
137   joint reward. Upon terminating its macro-action, an agent i receives a macro-observation ôi ∈ Ôi
138   according to a macro-observation probability function Tôi : Oi × M i × S → [0, 1], defined as
139   Tôi (ôi , mi , s′ ) = P r(ôi |mi , s′ ), and resets the reward accumulation for the next macro-action. The
140   aim is to find a joint high-level policy πM (m|ĥ) that maximizes the expected discounted return.

143   Synchronous macro-action MARL. Early works in the field convert the asynchronous problem into
144   a synchronous one by padding macro-actions to be of equal length, and then solving the resultant
145   Dec-POMDP (Jia et al., 2020). Similarly, Liang et al. (2024) proposes to transform an asynchronous
146   update between temporally extended actions into a primitive 1-step update. Some hierarchical meth-
147
      ods have considered learning both macro and primitive actions for cooperative multi-agent settings
      Tang et al. (2018); Xu et al. (2023). However, as also noted by Tang et al. (2018) and Xiao et al.
148
      (2022), they do not address asynchronicity, assuming agents perform macro-actions with the same
149
      duration. Hence, these previous works can be viewed as an n-step synchronous MARL version of
150   the primitive case and are unrelated to the asynchronous factorization framework we propose.

177   3      M ACRO - ACTION - BASED IGM
178
179   For the primitive and synchronous macro-action cases, the primitive IGM applies. Conversely, we
180   consider an asynchronous setup with macro-actions typically lasting for different, unknown lengths.
181
      Hence, to achieve principled asynchronous factorization, we must ensure the consistency of greedy
      macro-action selection in joint and local macro-action-value functions. The conditional Q-values
182
      prediction is thus pivotal for correctly formalizing Mac-IGM for asynchronous agents. Broadly
183
      speaking, for the joint case, we apply the arg max operator on agents sampling a new extended ac-
184   tion, while maintaining the same set of ongoing macro-actions. For the decentralized case, only the
185   agents with a terminated macro-action select a new one based on local information. Hence, we have
186   to enforce the macro-action selection consistency on a subset of the agents. This discrepancy, along
187   with conditional joint Q-values allows us to adapt existing factorization schemes to asynchronous
188   MARL and design different update schemes leveraging the asynchronous nature of the problem.

313   their value in the mixer, referring to the resultant algorithms with a “D1” suffix (center figure). (ii)
314   Masking the value of agents with ongoing macro-actions in the mixer, referring to the resultant al-
315   gorithms with a “D2” suffix (right figure).3 Intuitively, these partially centralized methods should be
316   beneficial in different tasks, depending on their specifications. For example, we expect D1 methods
317   to perform better when a problem has multiple local optima and requires a specific highly rewarded
318   joint behavior from all the agents (i.e., the higher magnitude of the joint value would incentives the
319   agents to learn such an optimal behavior). Conversely, D2 methods should offer benefits when a
320
      problem comprises several sub-tasks, and only a subset of agents is required to cooperate to solve
      the sub-tasks. The benchmark tasks employed in Section 5 allow us to investigate these intuitions.

391   5     E MPIRICAL EVALUATION
392
393   We aim to answer the following questions: (i) Can AVF methods learn decentralized policies for
394   complex cooperative tasks? How do different update schemes perform? (ii) Do AVF algorithms im-
395   prove performance over their primitive versions and existing asynchronous macro-action baselines
396   (Dec-MADDRQN, Cen-MADDRQN, Mac-IAICC (Xiao et al., 2020a; 2022)) and a synchronous one
397   (HAVEN (Xu et al., 2023)) (iii) Are the claims on temporal inconsistency (i.e., the relevance of the
398   macro-state) supported by empirical evidence? All the algorithms are run over 20 seeds, and data are
399
      collected on Xeon E5-2650 CPU nodes with 64GB of RAM, using the hyper-parameters discussed
      in Appendix D. Appendix E also discusses the environmental impact of our experiments.
400
401   We use standard benchmark environments in the macro-action literature (Appendix F): (i) BoxPush-
402   ing (BP). The goal is to move the big box to the goal. An agent can push the small box, but the big
403   one requires both agents to push it simultaneously. Agents only observe the state of the cell in front
404   of them, making high-dimensional grids hard. We consider BP-{10, 30}, where the number indi-
405
      cates the size of the grid. (ii) Warehouse Tool Delivery (WTD). A continuous space with multiple
      workers assembling an item. Four phases are required to complete the item, and one requires a tool.
406
      The manipulator searches for the right tool and handles it to the mobile robots, which have to deliver
407
      it to the worker. Agents must learn the correct tools for each phase, observing the workstation’s
408   state only when close to it. We consider four variants. WTD-S: one working human and two mobile
409   robots. WTD-D: two working humans with one faster work phase and two mobile robots. WTD-T:
410   three working humans with different speeds and three mobile robots. WTD-F: four working humans
411   that work at a fixed speed and three mobile robots. (iii) Capture Target (CT). A group of agents has
412   to capture a randomly moving target simultaneously. When successful, agents get a reward of 1.
413   Agents observe their position and the correct target’s location with probability 0.3. We significantly
414   increased the complexity of the original CT by considering 10 agents and 1 target.

531
      This paper introduces value factorization for asynchronous MARL to design scalable macro-action
532
      algorithms. To this end, we proposed the IGM principle for macro-actions, ensuring consistency
533
      between centralized and decentralized greedy action selection. In addition, we showed the proposed
534   Mac-IGM and MacAdv-IGM paradigms generalize the primitive ones and represent a wider class
535   of functions. We also introduced AVF algorithms that leverage asynchronous decision-making and
536   value factorization, under multiple update schemes. Our approach relies on a joint macro-state
537   to maintain temporal consistency in local agents’ state information, allowing the use of existing
538   factorization architectures. Crucially, the proposed AVF framework can be applied with arbitrary
539   mixing strategies. Overall, our methods successfully learn asynchronous decentralized policies for
      challenging tasks where primitive factorization and previous macro-action methods perform poorly.

      A     R EPRESENTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY OF M AC -IGM AND M AC A DV-IGM

      As discussed by VDN and QMIX (Sunehag et al., 2018; Rashid et al., 2018), common value factor-
653
      ization approaches cannot guarantee representing their respective classes of true value functions in
654   a Dec-POMDP. The same limitation holds in MacDec-POMDPs; agents’ observations do not repre-
655   sent the full state in partially observable settings. Similarly, per-agent value function ordering can
656   (potentially) be wrong in a macro-action context. Formally, given an agent i at a time step t it could
657   happen that:
658
                     Qi (ĥi , mi ) > Qi (ĥi , m′i ) when Q(s, (m−i , mi )) < Q s, (m−i , m′i )

659
660   where m−a is the joint action of all the agents excluding i. However, there are several ways to
661   alleviate such an issue. First, it is possible to condition per-agent action values (or state and ad-
662   vantage values) with state information during offline training as in QMIX (Rashid et al., 2018),
663   QPLEX (Wang et al., 2021). Moreover, if can not assume that (ĥ, m) (i.e., the joint macro-history
664   and action) is sufficient to fully model Q(s, m) (which is a common assumption in prior factoriza-
665   tion approaches), we can potentially store additional history-related information in recurrent layers
666   (Sunehag et al., 2018).

668   A.1     R EPRESENTATIONAL E XPRESSIVENESS OF AVF A LGORITHMS
669
      The proposed AVF framework does not change the architectural design of the chosen factorization
670
      method. Hence, the algorithms investigated in Section 5, namely AVF-{VDN, QMIX, QPLEX},
671   maintain the same considerations of the original factorization methods in terms of representational
672   expressiveness.
673
674
      In particular, AVF-VDN can factorize arbitrary joint macro-action value functions that can be addi-
      tively decomposed into individual utilities. AVF-QMIX extends the family of factorizable functions
675
      to non-linear monotonic combinations. Finally, AVF-QPLEX does not involve architectural con-
676
      straints and is capable of achieving the entire class of functions satisfying the underlying IGM.

856   C    L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER IMPACT
857
858   Limitations. We identify three limitations in our work. First, most factorization approaches cannot
859   guarantee to fully represent their respective classes of value functions in a Dec-POMDP (Sunehag
860   et al., 2018; Rashid et al., 2018; 2020); the same limitation holds in AVF-based algorithms that
861   maintain the same representation expressiveness of the original methods. Second, AVF methods
862   employing the joint macro-state could have scalability issues when considering many agents. While
863   such a problem does not arise in our experiments with up to 10 agents, it is possible to train an
      encoder to reduce the dimensionality of the joint macro-state. Third, MacDec-POMDPs assume


                                                             16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      that macro-actions are known and fixed. This is the same as assuming primitive actions are given
865   in a primitive MARL domain. Moreover, asynchronous settings are common in the real world but
866   have been rarely studied in the MARL literature. For this reason, principled methods are needed for
867   the MacDec-POMDP case before extending them to learn macro-actions (e.g., by employing skill
868   discovery approaches (Eysenbach et al., 2019)).
869
      Broader impact. Regarding the broader impact of our work, we do believe macro-actions have the
870
      potential to scale MARL into the real world. Temporally extended actions enable decision-making at
871   a higher level and naturally represent complex real-world behavior (e.g., lifting an object). that can
872   exploit existing robust controllers or be defined by a (human) expert, making them more explainable
873   than other sequences of primitive actions. By extending MARL algorithms to the macro-action case,
874   realistic multi-agent coordination problems can be solved that are orders of magnitude larger than
875   problems solved by previous primitive MARL algorithms.

942   F.1    B OX P USHING (BP)
943
944   In this collaborative task, two agents have to work together to push a big box to a goal area at the
945   top of a grid world to obtain a higher credit than pushing the small box on each own. The small box
946   is movable with a single agent, while the big one requires two agents to push it simultaneously.
947   The state space consists of each agent’s position and orientation, as well as the location of each box.
948   Agents have a set of primitive actions, including moving forward, turning left or right, and staying
949   in place. The available macro-actions are Go-to-Small-Box(i) and Go-to-Big-Box that navigates the
950   agent to a predefined waypoint (red) under the corresponding box and terminates with a pose facing
951   it; and a Push macro-action that makes the agent move forward and terminate when the robot hits
952   the world boundary or the big box. Each agent observation is very limited in both the primitive and
953
      macro level, which is the state of the front cell: empty, teammate, boundary, small box, or big box.
954   The team receives a terminal reward of +300 for pushing the big box to the goal area or +20 for
955   pushing one small box to the goal area. If any agent hits the world’s boundary or pushes the big box
956   on its own, a penalty of −10 is issued. An episode terminates when any box is moved to the goal
957   area or reaches the maximum horizon, 100 time steps. In our work, we consider the variant of this
958   task in terms of the grid world size as shown in Fig. 9.
959   The original work of Xiao et al. (2020a) also released a primitive action version of the BP task. In
960   the primitive action version, each agent has four actions: move forward, turn left, turn right, and
961   stay. The small box moves forward one grid cell when any robot faces it and executes the move
962   move forward action.

966   Warehouse Tool Delivery scenarios vary in the number of agents, humans, and the speed at which

968   requires several primitive time steps and a specific tool. We assume that the human already holds
969   the tool for the first phase, and the rest must be found and delivered in a particular order by a team of
970   robots to finish the subsequent work phases. The objective of the robot team is to assist the humans
971   in completing their tasks as quickly as possible by finding and delivering the correct tools in the
      proper order and timely fashion without making the humans wait.

974    primitive time steps to terminate; 3) the work phase of each human with its completed percentage;
975    and 4) each tool’s position.

980    10 time steps. Mobile robots move at a fixed velocity and are only allowed to receive tools from the
981    manipulator rather than the human. There are three applicable macro-actions for the manipulator
982    robot: 1) Search-Tool(i) takes 6 time steps to find a particular tool and place it in a staging area
