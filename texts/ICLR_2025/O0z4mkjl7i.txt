009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The core issue in continual learning (CL) is balancing catastrophic forgetting
012            of prior knowledge with generalization to new tasks, otherwise, known as the
013            stability-plasticity dilemma. We argue that the dilemma is akin to the capacity (the
014            networks’ ability to represent tasks) of the neural network (NN) in the CL setting.
015            Within this context, this work introduces “CL’s effective model capacity (CLEMC)"
016            to understand the dynamical behavior of stability-plasticity balance point in the
017            CL setting. We define CLEMC as a function of the NN, the task data, and the
018            optimization procedure. Leveraging CLEMC, we demonstrate that the capacity
019            is non-stationary and regardless of the NN architecture and optimization method,
020
               the network’s ability to represent new tasks diminishes if the incoming tasks’
               data distributions differ from previous ones. We formulate these results using
021
               dynamical systems’ theory and conduct extensive experiments to complement the
022
               findings. Our analysis extends from a small feed-forward (FNN) and convolutional
023            networks (CNN) to medium sized graph neural networks (GNN) to transformer-
024            based large language models (LLM) with millions of parameters.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Humans can easily adapt to multiple tasks. However, when neural networks (NN) seek to mimic
031   this behavior [49], they exhibit a phenomenon known as catastrophic forgetting, where the model
032   forgets older tasks while learning new ones [49]. This well recorded issue is seen irrespective of the
033
      NN architecture, from simple linear adaptive systems [33] to massive large language models [46, 37].
      The field of artificial intelligence that studies this phenomenon is known as continual learning (CL).
034
035   In recent years, numerous studies in CL [14, 50, 30, 6, 45] have shown that the core issue behind
036   CL is a trade-off between forgetting prior information (catastrophic forgetting) and learning new
037   information (generalization), also known as the stability-plasticity dilemma. This trade-off captures
038   the relationship between data and the optimization procedure, but that is only part of the picture.
039
      Independent lines of inquiry have also shown that over-parameterized NNs play a crucial role in
      achieving optimal performance in the CL paradigm [39, 21, 20]. While, [40] study the role of opti-
040
      mization characteristics, [27] study the learnability of CL problem when subsequent distributions are
041
      overlapping. Although, all of these works provide different but overlapping insights, they look at the
042   different sides of the problem such as model and data in [31] or the model and optimization procedure
043   in [27, 40, 6] and do not consider the complex interplay between the model/optimization/tasks.
044
045
      In this work, we aim to provide holistic insights into this interplay and establish a foundational
      understanding of the effect of NN capacity (stability plasticity balance point) on CL optimization in
046
      the presence of a series of tasks. We extend the definition of capacity from [41] to the CL paradigm,
047
      describing capacity (Def 3) as the effect of network architecture, hyperparameters, and weights
048   measured through the cost function. We then elucidate the connection between capacity and the
049   stability-plasticity balance point (Lemma 1 and Fig. 1) and show that this balance point is akin to the
050   networks ability to represent tasks - higher the capacity (measured through forgetting cost) lower
051   the representation ability of the network. With this theoretical framework, we show that the smallest
052   possible change in the network’s capacity is a function of changes in weights and tasks (Theorem 1).
053   Our main results (Theorems 2, 3) demonstrate that capacity, and by extension, the balance point, is
      non-stationary in the CL setting. The key conclusion of this work is:

055     procedure. Moreover, regardless of the type of NN, optimization procedure, or task, the network
056    eventually becomes unsuitable for representing the tasks if each subsequent task differs from the
057                                previous one even by a small constant."

082   2    R ELATED W ORKS
083
084   Starting from [19] in 1999 to [20] in 2024, numerous works have attempted to model/reduce catas-
085   trophic forgetting in neural networks. A simple taxonomy of recent published works reveals four
086   categories: regularization-based [4, 28, 43, 30], model architecture-based [1, 10, 12, 16, 22, 31, 51],
087
      experience replay-based [7, 23, 29, 38, 52] and other optimization approaches for CL effi-
      ciency [11, 48, 53, 55]. This huge body of work is focused on improving empirical performance.
088
089   On the other hand, empirical attempts to study the characteristics of the CL problem have been
090   made as well [14, 24, 32, 37]. For instance, [14] study the loss of plasticity in CL whereas [24, 32]
091   study a phenomenon known as stability gap frequently observed in CL methods. The empirical
092   investigative studies cover a wide range of neural network architectures as well, going from FNN/CNN
093
      in [14, 24, 32] to large language models in [37, 46]. Despite such a huge body of literature, there have
      only been a few attempts to study CL from a theoretical standpoint. The key reason behind this is that
094
      the NN learning problem in CL domain is rather complex to study requiring stringent assumptions
095
      that are scarcely held in practice. This is clearly seen from the few approaches that do theoretically
096   analyze the problem. For instance, works in [20, 21, 15] study the effect of over parameterization
097   and task similarity on forgetting with a linear model under two tasks. Catastrophic forgetting in the
098   presence of task similarity in analyzed in the NTK regime in [13]. On the other hand [34] and [35]
099   study the complete CL problem with a linear two layer NN. To the best of our knowledge, the only
100   approach that does not make either a two task assumption or assume linearity of the model is [27] but
101   instead focuses on the class incremental setting.
102   To obviate the necessity for such assumptions and provide a general framework to analyze CL, we
103   take a Lyapunov analysis standpoint, a tool that has been used in the control literature [5]. In contrast
104   with the existing literature, we analyze the CL problem through a dynamic programming-driven
105   optimal control point of view following the perspective from [45]. The only assumptions required are
106   twice differentiability and Lipschitz continuity of the loss function- two very practical assumptions in
107   the NN learning domain and our analysis extends to a series of tasks. In a similar vein to [39] we
      also perform Taylor series approximation to get this differential equation characterization, however,


                                                             2
108
      our theoretical analysis easily extends from a simple FNN to a llm- a very novel contribution to the
109   CL literature. To the best of our knowledge there has been no theoretical study, where the analysis
110   considers a dynamical behavior of the CL problem that extends across FNN/CNN/GNN and LLM.

      5    C ONCLUSION
524
525   We studied capacity in continual learning, focusing on the interplay between the model, tasks,
526
      optimization procedure, and their impact on the balance point. We introduce CL’s effective model
      capacity (CLEMC) and find that changes in CLEMC depend on the importance of each task, the
527
      cumulative weight changes at each task onset, and the cumulative task changes due to data distribution
528
      shifts. Our main conclusion is that even if each subsequent task is only slightly different from the
529   previous one, the effective capacity eventually becomes small, rendering the model unusable.

762   A    P RELIMINARIES
763
764   Let x and y be random variables corresponding to input and output probability spaces with support
765
      X and Y and B(X ) and B(Y) representing the corresponding Borel algebras. Define t as a random
      variable denoting the joint space of x × y with a model f(w,h) : X → Y being specified using
766
      weights w and hyperparameters h. Given compact sets W over w and H over h, the goal is to
767
      learn the weights by searching over the hypothesis space f = {f(w,h) , ∀h ∈ H, w ∈ W} through
768
