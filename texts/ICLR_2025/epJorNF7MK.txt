                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we empirically reveals an invariance over images – images share
012            a set of one-way wave equations with latent speeds. Each image is uniquely as-
013            sociated with a solution to these wave equations, allowing for its reconstruction
014            with high fidelity from an initial condition. We demonstrate it using an intuitive
015            encoder-decoder framework where each image is encoded into its corresponding
016            initial condition (a single vector). Subsequently, the initial condition undergoes
017            a specialized decoder, transforming the one-way wave equations into a first-order
018            norm+linear autoregressive process. This process propagates the initial condition
019
               along the x and y directions, generating a high-resolution feature map (up to the
               image resolution), followed by a few convolutional layers to reconstruct image
020
               pixels. The revealed invariance, rooted in the shared wave equations, offers a
021
               fresh perspective for comprehending images, establishing a promising avenue for
022            further exploration.

      The entire framework (encoder and FINOLA decoder) is easy to implement and learns in an end-
134   to-end manner. Experiments on ImageNet Deng et al. (2009) (with an image size of 256×256)
135   demonstrate promising results. We achieved a PSNR of 23.2 for image reconstruction on the vali-
136   dation set when employing only C = 128 wave equations. As the number of equations increases to
137   2048, the reconstruction PSNR boosts to 29.1. When compared with previous encoding/decoding
138   techniques under the same latent size, our method outperforms discrete cosine transform (DCT),
139   discrete wavelet transform (DWT), and convolutional auto-encoder (AE). Notably, our method re-
140   constructs the entire image (without partitioning into blocks) from a single position (center).
141   In addition, FINOLA can serve for self-supervised pre-training. Applying FINOLA to a single un-
142   masked quadrant block to predict the surrounding masked region yields performance comparable to
143   established techniques, e.g. MAE He et al. (2021), SimMIM Xie et al. (2022), but using lightweight
144   networks like Mobile-Former Chen et al. (2022). The comparison of encoders trained with and with-
145   out masking reveals that introducing masked prediction sacrifices restoration accuracy for enhanced
146   semantic representation. This is accompanied by an interesting observation: masking significantly
147   increases the Gaussian curvature on the surfaces of critical features.
148   In outlining our research goals, it’s crucial to emphasize that our aim isn’t state-of-the-art perfor-
149   mance but to empirically reveal a property inherent in images: the sharing of one-way wave equa-
150   tions within a latent space. We hope this encourage deeper understanding of images within the
151   research community.

202   Position and image invariance: Note that the matrices A and B, once learned from data, remain
203   invariant not only across spatial positions (x, y) per image but also across images. They capture the
204   consistent relationship between the feature values z(x, y) and their spatial derivatives (∆x z, ∆y z).
205   Parallel implementation: Autoregression can be computationally intensive due to its sequential
206   nature. FINOLA mitigates this by capitalizing on the independence of the x and y axes, enabling
207   parallel execution, significantly boosting efficiency. As shown in Figure 4, performing horizontal
208   regression first allows for parallel execution of subsequent vertical regression, and vice versa. In
209   practice, both approaches (horizontal first and vertical first) are combined by averaging their results.
210   The prediction at each position represents the average of the two autoregression paths originating
211   from the initial position, marked as q. Figure 4 (table on the right) demonstrates the superior speed
212
      of the parallel implementation, compared to the regular AR setting. It achieves a 30% speedup at a
      resolution of 16×16 and a threefold increase in speed at a higher resolution of 64×64.

220   3   G ENERALIZATION TO O NE - WAY WAVE E QUATIONS
221
222   In this section, we illustrate the generalization from FINOLA to a set of one-way wave equations,
223
      empirically offering a deeper insight into the inherent nature of images.
224   Linear partial difference equations: Let’s denote the spatial increments of the feature z along x
225   and y axes as ∆x z = z(x + 1, y) − z(x, y) and ∆y z = z(x, y + 1) − z(x, y), respectively. Then,
226   the generalized form of FINOLA (Eq. 1) can be expressed as linear partial difference equations:
227                            ∆x z = AB −1 ∆y z = Q∆y z s.t. Q = AB −1 .                                (2)
228
229   Here, the horizontal change ∆x z exhibits a linear correlation with its vertical counterpart ∆y z.
230   When the matrix B is invertible, FINOLA stands as a special solution to this equation, given that
231
      ∆x z and ∆y z not only exhibit linear correlation but are also linearly correlated with the normal-
      ization of the current feature values ẑ (referred to as the FINOLA constraint). It’s noteworthy that
232
      despite the absence of specific regulations during training, the learned matrices A and B are empir-
233
      ically found to be invertible across various dimensions, ranging from 128×128 to 4096×4096.
234
235   Relaxing the FINOLA constraint through FINOLA series: FINOLA represents a specific solu-
236   tion to Eq. 2, but it may not be the optimal one. We have discovered that a more optimal solution
237
      can be attained by relaxing the FINOLA constraint (∆x z = Aẑ, ∆y z = B ẑ) through aggregating
      a series of FINOLA solutions:

      6   R ELATED W ORK
493
      Image autoregression: Autoregression has played a pivotal role in generating high-quality images
494
      van den Oord et al. (2016b;a); Salimans et al. (2017); Chen et al. (2018). These methods model
495   conditional probability distributions of current pixels based on previously generated ones, evolv-
496   ing from pixel-level focus to latent space modeling using vector quantization van den Oord et al.
497   (2017); Razavi et al. (2019); Esser et al. (2021); Yu et al. (2022b). In contrast, we present a first-
498   order norm+linear autoregression to generate feature map and reveals new insights by generalizing
499   FINOLA as a set of one-way wave equations.
500
      Image transforms: The Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) Ahmed et al. (1974) and Wavelet Trans-
501   form Strang (1989); Daubechies (1992); Vetterli & Kovacevic (2013) are widely recognized signal
502   processing techniques for image compression. Both DCT and wavelet transforms project images
503   into a complete space consisting of known wave functions, in which each image has compact coeffi-
504   cients, i.e., most coefficients are close to zero. In contrast, our method offers a distinct mathematical
505   perspective for representing images. It encodes images into a compact space represented by a set
506   of one-wave equations with learnable speeds, with each image corresponding to a unique initial
507   condition. These differences are summarized in Table 10 at Appendix A.1.
508   Self-supervised learning: Contrastive methods Becker & Hinton (1992); Hadsell et al. (2006);
509   van den Oord et al. (2018); Wu et al. (2018); He et al. (2019); Chen & He (2020); Caron et al. (2021)
510   achieve significant progress. They are most applied to Siamese architectures Chen et al. (2020b); He
511   et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2020d; 2021) to contrast image similarity and dissimilarity and rely on data
512   augmentation. Chen & He (2020); Grill et al. (2020) remove dissimilarity between negative samples
513   by handling collapse carefully. Chen et al. (2020c); Li et al. (2021a) show pre-trained models work
514   well for semi-supervised learning and few-shot transfer. Masked image modeling (MIM) is inspired
515
      by BERT Devlin et al. (2019) and ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) to learn representation via masked
      prediction. BEiT Bao et al. (2021) and PeCo Dong et al. (2021) predict on tokens, MaskFeat Wei
516
      et al. (2022) predicts on HOG, and MAE He et al. (2021) reconstructs original pixels. Recent works
517
      explore combining MIM and contrastive learning Zhou et al. (2022); Dong et al. (2022); Huang
518   et al. (2022); Tao et al. (2022); Assran et al. (2022); Jiang et al. (2023) or techniques suitable for
519   ConvNets Gao et al. (2022); Jing et al. (2022); Fang et al. (2022). Different from using random
520   masking in these works, FINOLA uses regular masking and simpler norm+linear prediction.
521
522
      7   L IMITATIONS
523
524
      The major limitation of our method is that the invariance (encoded in matrices A and B) is re-
525
      vealed empirically without theoretical proof. Additionally, this paper focuses on multi-path FI-
526   NOLA, which represents only a subspace of the solutions to the one-way wave equations. In future
527   work, we plan to explore the theoretical analysis of the revealed invariance and the complete solution
528   space of the one-way wave equations.

530
      8   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we have revealed a fundamental mathematical invariance present in images through
533
      the lens of one-way wave equations. All images share a common set of one-way wave equations
534   characterized by learnable speeds, each uniquely tied to a specific solution associated with an initial
535   condition. The entire process is seamlessly implemented within an encoder-decoder framework,
536   wherein the wave equations undergo transformation into a first-order norm+linear autoregressive
537   process. Our proposed method excels in image reconstruction and shows promising potential in
538   self-supervised learning, offering a distinctive mathematical perspective on the inherent nature of
539   images. Looking ahead, future investigations exploring non-FINOLA based solutions to these wave
      equations hold the promise of delving even deeper into this intriguing realm.

763   Both DCT and wavelet transforms project images into a complete space consisting of known wave

766   images into a compact space represented by a set of one-way wave equations with learnable speeds.
767   Each image corresponds to a unique initial condition. These differences are summarized in Table
