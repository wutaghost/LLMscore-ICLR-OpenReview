011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Gene regulatory networks (GRNs) represent the causal relationships between tran-
014            scription factors (TFs) and target genes in single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq)
015
               data. Understanding these networks is crucial for uncovering disease mechanisms
               and identifying therapeutic targets. In this work, we investigate the potential of
016
               large language models (LLMs) for GRN discovery, leveraging their learned biolog-
017
               ical knowledge alone or in combination with traditional statistical methods. We
018            employ a task-based evaluation strategy to address the challenge of unavailable
019            ground truth causal graphs. Specifically, we use the GRNs suggested by LLMs to
020            guide causal synthetic data generation and compare the resulting data against the
021            original dataset. Our statistical and biological assessments show that LLMs can
022            support statistical modeling and data synthesis for biological research.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) is a cutting-edge technology that enables the collection of
      gene expression data from individual cells. This approach opens up new avenues for a wide range of
028
      scientific and clinical applications. One crucial application of scRNA-seq data is the reconstruction
029
      and analysis of gene regulatory networks (GRNs), which represent the interactions between genes.
030   GRN analysis can deepen our understanding of disease mechanisms, identify key regulatory pathways,
031   and provide a foundation for the development of interventional gene therapies and targeted drug
032   discovery.
033
      Statistical causal discovery algorithms (Mercatelli et al., 2020; Scheines et al., 1998; Zheng et al.,
034
      2018; Brouillard et al., 2020; Lippe et al., 2021; Roohani et al., 2024; Yu & Welch, 2022) can reveal
035
      potential causal links between TFs and their target gene. However, they often lack robustness and
036   are prone to detecting spurious correlations, especially in high-dimensional, noisy single-cell data.
037   Furthermore, many of these approaches rely heavily on prior knowledge from curated databases (e.g.,
038   TRANSFAC (Wingender et al., 1996), RegNetwork (Liu et al., 2015), ENCODE (de Souza, 2012),
039   BioGRID (de Souza, 2012), and AnimalTFDB (Hu et al., 2019)), which frequently lack essential
040   contextual information such as specific cell types or conditions, leading to inaccuracies in the inferred
041   regulatory relationships (Zinati et al., 2024).
042   The recent advancements in and success of large language models (LLMs) have opened up new
043   possibilities for their use in scientific discovery (Sheth et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; AI4Science &
044   Quantum, 2023), including causal discovery (Kıcıman et al., 2023; Abdulaal et al., 2023; Kasetty
045   et al., 2024; Vashishtha et al., 2023; AI4Science & Quantum, 2023; Abdulaal et al., 2023; Khatibi
046   et al., 2024). Most of the above methods involve the refinement of the statistically inferred causal
047   graph by LLM. However, LLMs excel at synthesizing vast amounts of heterogeneous knowledge,
048   making them well-suited to tasks that require the integration of diverse datasets, such as constructing
049   full causal graphs based on scientific literature (Sheth et al., 2024). In addition, LLMs have already
050
      been shown to perform various genomic data analysis tasks (Märtens et al.; Jin et al., 2024; Tang &
      Koo, 2023; Fang et al., 2024; Toufiq et al., 2023; Elsborg & Salvatore, 2023), including foundation
051
      models pre-trained for genomic tasks (Wang et al., 2024a; Cui et al., 2024).
052
053   Inspired by recent advancements, we harness LLMs for inferring gene regulatory networks (GRNs)
      from scRNA-seq data. Specifically, we utilize LLMs either to generate complete GRNs (causal


                                                         1

054
      graphs) directly or to provide prior knowledge in the form of a list of potential transcription factors
055   (TFs), which can then be integrated into traditional statistical causal discovery algorithms.
056
057   We use causal synthetic data generation as a downstream task to evaluate GRNs or priors suggested by
058
      LLMs in the absence of reliable ground-truth graphs. This method embeds gene regulatory networks
      into the scRNA-seq data generation process and has been shown to preserve biological plausibility
059
      better (Zinati et al., 2024). By comparing synthetic data to an oracle dataset, we assess the practical
060
      utility of the inferred causal graph. Our results highlight the potential of general-purpose LLMs for
061   GRN inference, as evidenced by both statistical and biological metrics. The best performance is
062   achieved by combining LLMs with statistical GRN inference, pointing to a promising direction for
063   scRNA-seq data analysis.

076   2    R ELATED W ORKS
077
078   LLMs and Causality. Gene regulatory network inference from scRNA-seq data traditionally relies
079   on statistical causal discovery methods (Pratapa et al., 2020; Huynh-Thu et al., 2010; Moerman
080   et al., 2019). However, causal discovery often require external knowledge in the form of inter-
081   ventions (Brouillard et al., 2020), expert input (Kleinegesse et al., 2022), or priors from curated
082   databases (Zinati et al., 2024). Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) offer a promising
083   solution, as LLMs excel at integrating diverse knowledge and providing contextual information (Wan
084   et al., 2024; Ma, 2024). Many of the recent works leverage LLMs for causal discovery by utilizing
085   metadata, such as variable names, to infer causal relationships (Kıcıman et al., 2023; Ban et al.,
086
      2023b; Vashishtha et al., 2023; Ban et al., 2023a). Further optimizations, including more advanced
      prompting strategies beyond pairwise variable comparisons, have been developed to enhance causal
087
      discovery (Vashishtha et al., 2023; Jiralerspong et al., 2024). Further (Sheth et al., 2024) explored
088
      the effectiveness of completing a partial causal graph across diverse domains. This work, however,
089   considers LLM as an oracle to discover causal graphs for GRN inference.
090
091
092
      LLMs and Biology. Models based on transformer architecture and trained on DNA or RNA
      genomic sequences are effective at prediction and generation tasks (Zhang et al., 2024). However,
093
      the availability of generative Gene-LLMs is limited, they lack rich contextual information and are
094
      focused on specific tasks (Zhang et al., 2024). To overcome these limitations, foundation models are
095   tailored for the genomic tasks (Wang et al., 2024a; Cui et al., 2024). However, models trained on the
096   genetic data do not achieve the wide context of LLMs like GPT4 that inject information from the
097   scientific literature and freely available genomic databases. LLMs have been used for the tasks such
098   as enhancing gene perturbation generators (Märtens et al.), protein interaction prediction (Jin et al.,
099   2024), gene selection (Toufiq et al., 2023), analyzing biomarkers (Elsborg & Salvatore, 2023) and
100   cell annotation (Fang et al., 2024). To the best of our knowledge general purpose LLMs have not
101   been used for gene regulatory network inference.
102
103   Causal Synthetic Data Generation. Causally synthetic data generation is an approach that focuses
104   on embedding true causal relationships within the generated data. Several approaches have been
105   proposed in which the generator is guided by the causal acyclic graph (Cinquini et al., 2021; van
106   Breugel et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024b; Bandyopadhyay & Sarkar, 2023). We use
107   a recently proposed causal GAN designed for scRNA-seq data generation, shown to produce more
      biologically plausible results (Zinati et al., 2024).

111
112   A directed acyclic graph (DAG) G = (V, E) is composed of a set of variables/vertices V and a
113   set of (directed) edges E between them such that no cycle is formed. Let P be the probability
114   distribution over the same set of variables V. G and P satisfy the Markov condition if every variable
115   is conditionally independent of its non-descendants given its parents. Assuming the Markov condition,
116   the joint distribution of variables V1 , V2 , . . . ∈ V can be factorized as:
117
                                                                  Y
                                      P [V1 , V2 , . . . , Vd ] =   P [Vi |P a(Vi )] .                  (1)
118
                                                              i
119
120   where P a(Vi ) denotes the set of parents of Vi .
121   A bipartite acyclic graph G = (U, V, E) is an instance of a DAG whose vertices are divided into two
122   subsets U and V. Each edge Ei connects only two vertices from U and V.

124   3.1.1    G ENE R EGULATORY N ETWORK (GRN)
125
126
      A gene regulatory network (GRN) is a collection of molecular regulators that interact with each other
      and with other substances in the cell to control the gene expression levels of mRNA and proteins.
127
      GRNs describe the relationships between genes, transcription factors (TFs), RNA molecules, and
128
      other regulatory elements within a biological system, illustrating how genes are turned on or off and
129   how their expression is modulated over time and in different conditions. GRN can be expressed as a
130   directed acyclic graph (DAG) 3.1. In this work we are considering a bipartite DAG consisting of TFs
131   and target genes.
132
133   3.2     G ENERATIVE A DVERSARIAL N ETWORK (GAN)
134
135   A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) consists of two neural networks, the generator G and
136   the discriminator D, which are trained simultaneously in a zero-sum game. The generator G maps
137   a random noise vector z sampled from a prior distribution pz (z) (typically Gaussian) to the data
138
      space, producing a synthetic sample G(z). The discriminator D maps an input x to a scalar value
      D(x) ∈ [0, 1], representing the probability that x is a real sample.
139
140   The training objective for GANs is formulated as a minimax game defined by the following function:
141
142               min max V (D, G) = Ex∼pdata (x) [log D(x)] + Ez∼pz (z) [log(1 − D(G(z)))]
143                G    D
144
      where pdata (x) is the distribution of real data, and pg (x) is the distribution induced by G. The
145
      discriminator tries to maximize the probability of distinguishing real data from G’s samples, while G
146   aims to minimize log(1 − D(G(z))) to generate realistic data. At equilibrium, the optimal generator
147   replicates the true data distribution, making pg (x) = pdata (x). The Wasserstein GAN (WGAN)
148   introduces the Wasserstein distance as an alternative to the Kullback-Leibler or Jensen-Shannon
149   divergences traditionally used in GANs. This distance metric measures the similarity between the
150   distributions of real data and the generated data, leading to more stable training and mitigating issues
151   like mode collapse (Arjovsky et al., 2017). WGAN with Gradient Penalty (WGAN-GP) (Gulrajani
152   et al., 2017) further enhances this stability by replacing the original weight clipping technique of
153   WGAN with a gradient penalty, addressing problems such as vanishing or exploding gradients and
154
      underutilization of model capacity.
155

      4     LLM4GRN
191
192
      In this work, we propose a novel approach integrating Large Language Models (LLMs) for GRN
193
      inference. The overall goal is to leverage the potential of LLMs in capturing complex biological
194   interactions and to assess their utility. Given the lack of ground-truth data, we consider causal
195   synthetic data generation as a downstream task to perform biological and statistical evaluations. Our
196   methodology involves two distinct experimental settings that leverage different knowledge about
197   the potential transcription factors (TFs) to guide gene regulatory network (GRN)-informed data
198   generation. In each of the settings, we introduce LLMs into the pipeline motivated by their ability to
199   incorporate extensive contextual information.
200
201          • In the first setting ( Figure 1a, Setting 1.A), we use LLM to infer the GRN graph by providing
202
               the LLM with a potential list of TF candidates sourced from a human-curated database.
203          • In the second setting ( Figure 1b, Setting 2.A), we utilize the LLM as the knowledge base,
204            incorporating it earlier in the pipeline to infer potential TFs and to deduce the GRN graph.
205
206
      We compare with Setting 1.B and 2.B where the Human knowledge base and LLM knowledge base
      is used by a statistical causal inference approach, GRNBoost2, respectively.

285   2024). Our preprocessing focused on creating train, test, and validation sets while maintaining 1,000
286   genes across all datasets (see AppendixA.1 for details). For each setting, we construct three different
287   GRNs: one derived directly from the LLM, one generated by a causal discovery algorithm that
288   incorporates the prior information (the dataset), and a third, randomly generated graph (based on TF
289
      list extracted from Human or LLM KB). For the GRN inference of LLM, we prompted with contextual
      knowledge. We used the state-of-art pretrained GPT-4 model (Achiam et al., 2023). Given GPT-4
290
      strong performance for causal discovery (Abdulaal et al., 2023) across different domains including
291
      genomics, we prompted (see Appendix A.2) in zero-shot fashion. We also compare open-source

477   5.4     D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
478
      We observe promising results in using LLM for GRN discovery, especially on the PBMC dataset.
479
      The hybrid approach incorporating LLM TFs and GRNBoost2 causal discovery yield the best overall
480   performance (for PBMC-ALL and PBMC-CTL data sets). Surprisingly, a smaller open-source model
481   Llama has shown the best result in this setting. This result is surprising, however, although further
482   evaluation is needed, new LLM models might perform as good or better than state-of-the-art GPT for
483   specific tasks Valero-Lara et al. (2023). However, Llama performs worse on the more challenging,
484   GRN construction task. The lower performance of LLMs on CTL and BoneMarrow data sets is not
485   surprising. Recent studies suggest that genomic LLMs under-perform on cell-specific data (Tang &
      Koo, 2023). In addition, human transcription factors outnumber mice transcriptomic information


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      in the databases (Members & Partners, 2024) making the information on mice genes scarcer in the
487   LLMs training data.
488
489   Biological plausibility analysis reveals that the Setting 2, Llama-KB dataset significantly improves
490
      cell type differentiation and reduces noise compared to other generated datasets, particularly for
      CD4+/CD45RA+/CD25– Naïve T cells and dendritic cells, which indicates better performance of the
491
      model due to its ability to handle large contexts (Xiong et al., 2023).
492
493   In contrast, while the Setting 2, GPT4-KB model exhibited higher noise and less specificity, the
494   Setting 1, GRNBoost2 model also showed noisy patterns, especially in Naïve T and cytotoxic T
495   cells, emphasizing the need for models that clearly delineate cell type-specific expression. Notably,
496
      discrepancies in cell type proportions across generated datasets, particularly the inflated presence
      of CD8+/CD45RA+ Naïve Cytotoxic T cells, raise concerns about the biological relevance of these
497
      models. As reported previously, large-scale single-cell studies tend to be more noisy which can
498
      lead to sub-optimal biological inferences (Kavran & Clauset, 2021). Therefore, further refinements
499   are essential across all approaches to enhance specificity and reliability in representing true cellular
500   compositions, which is crucial for accurate downstream biological analyses and interpretations.
501   Finally, the Human-KB LLM model exhibited highly noisy or non-specific expression profiles,
502   particularly for CD4+/CD45A+/CD25– Naïve T cells, CD8+/CD45RA+ Naïve cytotoxic T cells,
503   CD4+ T helper cells, CD4+/CD25+ T regulatory cells, and CD4+/CD45RO+ memory cells. For
504   other cell types, the model either failed to express most top markers or showed high cell fractions
505   with reduced mean expression across different cell types.
506   One of the limitations of the study is the unifying constraints that are imposed on the GRN discovery
507   due to the parametric requirements of GRouNdGan (Zinati et al., 2024). Namely, all GRN graphs
508   are set to be bipartite graphs with the same number of TFs and same number of target genes for
509   each TF. These constraints restrict the diversity of the generated graphs, resulting in fairly similar
510   performance metrics among different GRN discovery approaches. In addition, multi-layer graphs with
511   transcription factors, cofactors, and target genes are more realistic and can better reflect biological
512   complexity (Karlebach & Shamir, 2008).
513   Despite the promising results, we note, that LLMs should be used cautiously in any high-stakes
514   decision-making. The models are prone to reporting false information with confidence (Ji et al.,
515   2023; Farquhar et al., 2024). In addition, they are susceptible to biases in the training data. One
516   of the most common sources of bias in machine learning is the under-representation of minority
517   populations in the training data. Transcription factor databases and literature often lack balanced
518   representation across ethnicity, ancestry, gender, and age groups. The vast majority of genetic data
519
      and studies are based on individuals of European ancestry (Sirugo et al., 2019; Bentley et al., 2017).
      The underrepresentation of non-European ancestry populations in genomic databases can obscure
520
      gene-disease associations that are rare in European groups, leading to treatments that may not be
521
      effective for these communities (Landry et al., 2018; Tawfik et al., 2023; Barral-Arca et al., 2019).
522   Age groups, specifically children and elderly, females, low-income populations and individuals from
523   remote rural areas are also underrepresented in clinical trials and data, making them less likely to
524   benefit from the achievements of precision medicine (Mosenifar, 2007; Davis et al., 2019; Steinberg
525   et al., 2021). LLMs are shown to exacerbate some existing health disparities (Pfohl et al., 2024), and
526   are likely to be influenced by the under-representation bias in the genomic data.
527
528
      6    C ONCLUSION
529
530
      In conclusion, our analysis highlights the potential of using Large Language Models (LLMs) for gene
531   regulatory network (GRN) inference, as supported by both statistical and biological performance
532   metrics. Notably, the best performing approach combines LLM-derived TF priors with GRNBoost2
533   for statistical inference. In addition, we see good potential for using open-source models such as
534   Llama for GRNs, and we aim to explore their utility further, either directly or with fine-tuning. Our
535   biological results suggest that additional refinement is needed. For this we foresee developing GRNs
536   tailored to individual cell types, as evidence indicates that GRNs are often cell-type-specific.

      In synthetic data generation, we use the same data sets and follow the protocol described by Zinati et

762
763   We downloaded the three datasets using the link provided at https://emad-combine-lab.
764   github.io/GRouNdGAN/tutorial.html#demo-datasets.
765   To ensure consistent results across different runs, we modified the code in two key ways: (1) we
766   seeded the randomness in the ‘main.py’ file. While the GRNBoost2 algorithm is already seeded by
767   default, ensuring consistent output, the rest of the code was not. Given that we are testing different
768   GRN graphs, it was critical to control for randomness to prevent it from influencing our results. (2)
769   We addressed additional bugs to ensure smooth execution, such as handling sparse data loading and
770   ensuring that the code was properly assigned to the correct device for training. All modifications
771   have been incorporated into our forked version of the repository, available upon publication. Some of
772   the bugs we identified were also reported and fixed by the original authors.
773   After downloading the raw data, we preprocessed it to create separate train, test, and validation files.
774   Following the paper’s recommendations, we used a test set of 1,000 samples for the PBMC and CTL
775   datasets, and 500 samples for the BoneMarrow dataset. For validation, we set aside 1,000 samples
776   each for PBMC and CTL, and 500 for BoneMarrow. The subsets were disjoint. We refer to these
777   preprocessed datasets as “Real Data” throughout the paper. The preprocessed data will be made
778   available upon publication.
779   During preprocessing, we observed that the BoneMarrow dataset had two genes absent in the training
780   data, as they were not expressed in any cells. This issue did not occur in the full dataset, where genes
781   expressed in fewer than three cells and cells expressing fewer than 10 genes had been filtered out.
782   However, after splitting the data into train, test, and validation sets, some genes in specific splits were
783   not expressed at all.
784   To address this, we modified the code to filter out genes expressed in fewer than one cell for each data
785   split. This reduced the number of genes in the BoneMarrow dataset from 1,000 to 910. In the PBMC
786   dataset, we found that 493 genes were not expressed in the test set, requiring extensive filtering.
787
      To resolve this, we revised our approach. Instead of applying the original threshold, which discarded
788
      genes expressed in fewer than three cells, we increased this threshold to 680 cells for the PBMC
789   dataset, equivalent to 0.01% of the full dataset. This change allowed us to retain the full 1,000
790   highly variable genes. However, the resulting set of 1,000 genes may differ slightly from those
791   identified with the initial criteria, given that the full PBMC dataset contains over 32,738 genes, while
792   BoneMarrow contains 3,451 genes.
793
      This revised strategy allowed us to retain the full 1,000 genes for the PBMC and CTL datasets but not
794
      for BoneMarrow. To address this, we further adjusted the strategy by filtering out genes expressed in
795   fewer than the number of cells in the test set for each dataset (1,000 for PBMC and CTL, and 500 for
796   BoneMarrow). This approach successfully retained 1,000 genes across all datasets.

818   A.2     LLM P ROMPTING S TRATEGIES
819
820   In this study, we utilize prompting techniques to guide the behavior of a Large Language Model (LLM)
821   for gene regulatory network (GRN) inference and other downstream analyses. The model employed
822
      is based on a pretrained large language model, specifically GPT-4, which has not been fine-tuned
      for this task. Instead, we rely on advanced prompting strategies, including Chain of Thought (CoT)
823
      reasoning and context provision, to enhance the performance of the LLM in generating biologically
824
      plausible results.
825
826
      A.2.1     C HAIN OF T HOUGHT (C OT) P ROMPTING
827
828   Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting is a method that encourages the LLM to reason through interme-
829   diate steps, producing a more transparent and logical progression towards its final answer. By guiding
830   the model to provide step-by-step explanations before arriving at a conclusion, we aim to improve
831   the interpretability and accuracy of its predictions. CoT prompting is particularly valuable for tasks
832
      that require complex reasoning, such as GRN inference, where multiple factors, such as transcription
      factor interactions and gene expression patterns, must be considered.
833
834
      A.2.2     C ONTEXT P ROVISION
835
836   To further enhance the performance of the LLM, we utilize context provision by supplying the model
837   with relevant background information prior to prompting. This technique is especially important
838   when the task requires domain-specific knowledge, such as GRN inference from single-cell RNA
839   sequencing (scRNA-seq) data. By embedding relevant context in the prompt, we can better align the
840   model’s responses with the biological characteristics of the data being analyzed. Specifically, we
841   provide excerpts from the original articles where the analyzed data sets were introduced Paul et al.
842
      (2015); Zheng et al. (2017).
843          Prompt
844
845      We need to find transcriptomic factors related to CONTEXT. What are the transcriptomic
846      factors genes related to GENE-X out of LIST-OF-TFs. Which of these 10 TFs have a causal
847
         relationship with GENE-X gene? Do not include any genes beyond these. Give potential
         candidates. Think step by step and return the answer in the format <Answer> [first suggestion,
848
         second suggestion, third suggestion, fourth suggestion and so on....] </Answer>. You have to
849
         return the 10 potential TFs that are related to the give gene only, otherwise your answer will
850      be disqualified.

853   A.2.3     E XTRACTING P OTENTIAL TF S F ROM T HE G ENE L IST
854
855
      we provide the LLM with a curated list of genes, accompanied by relevant contextual information such
      as biological function, tissue type, and experimental conditions derived from scRNA-seq datasets.
856
      The prompt explicitly requests the LLM to identify and propose TFs that are known to regulate
857
      the expression of each gene within the list. In our approach, we start with a total of 1000 genes
858   and sequentially query the Large Language Model (LLM) about subsets of 20 genes at a time to
859   extract potential transcription factors (TFs). For each initial query, the LLM identifies and proposes
860   TFs that may regulate the selected genes, leveraging its extensive contextual knowledge of gene
861   regulatory mechanisms. In subsequent prompts, we maintain a 50% overlap with the previous set
862   of genes, ensuring that 10 of the genes are revisited while introducing 10 new genes. This iterative
863   process allows us to refine the extraction of TFs, incorporating insights from the previous queries
      while continuously expanding our understanding of the regulatory landscape. By doing so, we aim

      to capture a comprehensive set of potential TFs that interact with the entire gene pool, facilitating a
865   more robust inference of the Gene Regulatory Network (GRN).
