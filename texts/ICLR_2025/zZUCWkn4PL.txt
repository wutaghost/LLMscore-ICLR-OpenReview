010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            We develop two novel stochastic variance-reduction methods to approximate a
013            solution of generalized equations applicable to both equations and inclusions.
014            Our algorithms leverage a new combination of ideas from the forward-reflected-
015
               backward splitting method and a class of unbiased variance-reduced estimators.
               We construct two new stochastic estimators within this class, inspired by the well-
016
               known SVRG and SAGA estimators. These estimators significantly differ from
017
               existing approaches used in minimax and variational inequality problems. By
018            appropriately selecting parameters, both algorithms achieve the state-of-the-art
019            oracle complexity of O(n + n2/3 ✏ 2 ) for obtaining an ✏-solution in terms of
020            the operator residual norm, where n represents the number of summands and ✏
021            signifies the desired accuracy. This complexity aligns with the best-known re-
022            sults in SVRG and SAGA methods for stochastic nonconvex optimization. We
023            test our algorithms on two numerical examples and compare them with existing
024            methods. The results demonstrate promising improvements offered by the new
025            methods compared to their competitors.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Linear and nonlinear equations and inclusions are cornerstones of computational mathematics,
030
      finding applications in diverse fields like engineering, mechanics, economics, statistics, optimiza-
      tion, and machine learning, see, e.g., Bauschke & Combettes (2017); Burachik & Iusem (2008);
031
      Facchinei & Pang (2003); Phelps (2009); Ryu & Yin (2022); Ryu & Boyd (2016). These problems,
032
      known as generalized equations (Rockafellar & Wets, 1997), are equivalent to fixed-point problems.
033   The recent revolution in deep learning and generative AI has brought renewed interest to general-
034   ized equations and their special cases: minimax problems. They serve as powerful tools for handling
035   Nash’s equilibria and minimax models in generative machine learning, adversarial learning, and ro-
036   bust learning, see Arjovsky et al. (2017); Goodfellow et al. (2014); Madry et al. (2018); Namkoong
037   & Duchi (2016). Notably, most problems arising from these applications are nonmonotone, non-
038   smooth, and large-scale. This paper develops new and simple stochastic algorithms with variance
039   reduction for solving this class of problems, equipped with rigorous theoretical guarantees.

139   1.3 C ONTRIBUTION AND RELATED WORK
140   Our primary goal is to develop a class of stochastic variance-reduction methods to solve both (NE)
141   and (NI), their special cases such as (VIP) and (Minimax), and equivalent problems such as (FP).
142
      Our contribution. Our main contribution can be summarized as follows.
143
144        (a) We introduce a new operator S k in (FRO) and propose a class of unbiased variance-reduced
145            estimators Sek for S k satisfying our Definition 2.1.
146        (b) We construct two instances of Sek by leveraging the SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013) and
147            SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014) estimators, respectively that fulfill our Definition 2.1. These
148            estimators are also of independent interest, and can be applied to develop other methods.
149        (c) We develop a stochastic variance-reduced forward-reflected method (VFR) to solve (NE)
150            which requires O(n + n2/3 ✏ 2 ) evaluations of Gi to obtain an ✏-solution of (NE).
151        (d) We also design a novel stochastic variance-reduced forward-reflected-backward splitting
152            method (VFRBS) to solve (NI) that also requires O(n + n2/3 ✏ 2 evaluations of Gi .

532
      This work introduces two innovative variance-reduced algorithms based on the forward-reflected-
533
      backward splitting method to tackle equations (NE) and inclusions (NI). These methods encompass
534   both SVRG and SAGA estimators as special cases. By carefully selecting the parameters, our algo-
535   rithms achieve the state-of-the-art oracle complexity for reaching an ✏-solution, matching the state-
536   of-the-art complexity bounds observed in nonconvex optimization methods using SVRG and SAGA.
537   While the first scheme resembles a stochastic variant of the optimistic gradient method, the second
538   algorithm is entirely novel and distinct from existing approaches, even their deterministic counter-
539   parts. We have validated our methods through numerical examples, and the results demonstrate
      promising performance compared to existing techniques under carefully tuned parameter selections.
