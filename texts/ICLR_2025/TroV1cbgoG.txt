009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning with noisy labels presents significant challenges. In this work,
012            we theoretically characterize the role of label noise in training neural networks
013            from a feature learning perspective. Specifically, we consider a signal-noise data
014            distribution, where each data point comprises a label-dependent signal and label-
015            independent noise, and rigorously analyze the training dynamics of a two-layer
016            convolutional neural network under this data setting, along with the presence of
017
               label noise. Particularly, we identify two stages in which the dynamics exhibit
               distinct patterns. In Stage I, the model perfectly fits all the clean samples (i.e.,
018
               samples without label noise) while ignoring the noisy ones (i.e., samples with
019
               noisy labels). In the first stage, the model learns the signal from the clean samples,
020            which generalizes well on unseen data. In Stage II, as the training loss converges,
021            the gradient in the direction of noise surpasses that of the signal, leading to over-
022            fitting on noisy samples. Eventually, the model memorizes the noise present in the
023            noisy samples, which degrades its generalization ability. In contrast, when training
024            without label noise, the dynamics do not exhibit this two-stage pattern. Furthermore,
025            our results provide theoretical supports for two widely used techniques for tackling
026            label noise: early stopping and sample selection. Experiments on both synthetic
027            and real-world datasets confirm our theoretical findings.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   One of the key challenges in deep learning lies in its susceptibility to label noise (Angluin & Laird,
032   1988). The success of deep learning stems from its exceptional ability to approximate arbitrary
033
      functions (Hornik et al., 1989; Funahashi, 1989), yet this ability becomes problematic in the presence
      of noisy labels. Over-parameterized neural networks, which have sufficient capacity to memorize
034
      training data, tend to over-fit noisy labels, leading to poor generalization on unseen data. Although
035
      many studies (Patrini et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2019; Tanaka et al., 2018; Han et al.,
036   2018; Liu et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024) have developed methods to mitigate
037   the effects of label noise in practice, our theoretical understanding of label noise remains limited.
038   Particularly, a crucial step towards advancing our understanding is to formulate a comprehensive
039   theory that explains the learning dynamics of neural networks in the presence of label noise.
040
      Existing works (Li et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023a) has attempted to theoretically analyze the effects
041
      of label noise on the training dynamics of neural networks; however, many of these studies rely on
042   unrealistic assumptions. Li et al. (2020) assumed the lazy training regime, which constrains the
043   distance between model weights and their initialization. Similarly, Liu et al. (2023a) adopted an
044   infinitely-wide neural network, where the dynamics become linear and can be described by a static
045   kernel function. Exploring the training dynamics with label noise beyond these assumptions remains
046   an active area of research.
047
      Recently, a new line of research (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2020; Frei et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2022; Kou
048   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024) have developed the feature learning theory to better understand the
049   training dynamics and generalization of neural networks under more realistic settings. The core idea
050   of the feature learning theory, which dates back to Rumelhart et al. (1986), is to assume a simplified
051   data distribution and analyze how neural networks learn useful features, or representations, of this
052   data during the training process. Unlike the lazy training regime, this theoretical framework allows
053   model weights to evolve over a larger distance, capturing a broader range of nonlinear behaviours
      in the training process. While the feature learning theory has successfully modeled the complex

      dynamics of neural networks across various settings, few attempts have been made to characterize
055   their dynamics in the presence of label noise using this theory. Here, we pose the question:
056
057                   How does label noise affect the training dynamics of neural networks?
058
059   In this work, we answer the above question by establishing a feature learning framework for training
060
      neural networks with gradient descent in the presence of label noise, unveiling a two-stage behaviour
      in the training dynamics. Similar to Cao et al. (2022); Kou et al. (2023), the feature learning framework
061
      is based on a signal-noise data distribution, where each data point consists of a label-dependent signal
062
      and a label-independent noise1 . Specifically, we consider a binary classification dataset {(xi , yМғi )}ni=1 ,
063
      where xi вҲҲ R2d is the i-th input and yi вҲҲ {вҲ’1, 1} is the corresponding ground-truth label. The input
064
      are defined as x = [x(1) , x(2) ] = [yВө, Оҫ], with the fixed vector Вө вҲҲ Rd representing the signal, and
065
      the random vector Оҫ вҲҲ Rd вҲј N (0, ПғОҫ2 Id ) representing the noise. Additionally, we introduce the
066   label noise in the sense that the observed label yМғ flips the true label y (i.e., yМғ = вҲ’y) with a certain
067   probability. We rigorously analyze the training dynamics of a two-layer convolutional neural network
068   under above setup, identifying a two-stage picture:
069
       вҖў Stage I. Initially, the model perfectly fits all the clean samples while ignoring the noisy ones2 .
070
         The model mainly learns the signal from clean samples, which generalizes well on unseen data.
071
072
       вҖў Stage II. Then, the training loss converges and the model over-fits to the noisy samples. The
         model memorizes the noise features from noisy samples, degrading its generalization.
073
074   For comparison, we also show that when training without label noise, the model tends to fit all
075   training samples throughout the training process, and the test error remains well-bounded. See Table 1
076   for a summary. This two-stage picture is well-supported by existing empirical findings (Arpit et al.,
077   2017; Han et al., 2018), which show that neural networks tend to first learn simple patterns from
078
      clean samples and then proceed to memorize the noisy ones, and further validated by our experiments
      under both synthetic and real-world scenarios.

086   Subsequently, based on our theoretical analysis, we provide an explanation for the effectiveness of
087   two common techniques used to address the label noise problem, i.e., early stopping (Liu et al., 2020;
088   Bai et al., 2021) and sample selection (Han et al., 2018; 2020). i) For early stopping, we show that
089   stopping the training process at the end of the first stage ensures a low test error, even in the presence
090   of label noise. ii) For sample selection, we verify that the small-loss criterion3 (Han et al., 2018) can
091   provably identify the clean samples from noisy ones.
092   In summary, our work focuses on the role of label noise in the optimization process of neural networks.
093   We prove that the models inevitably learn the noise features from data when the label noise is present,
094   which leads to degraded generalization compared to noise-free training. Our results highlight the
095   importance of taking a feature-learning viewpoint in studying questions related to training dynamics.

518   However, accuracy results alone are insufficient to demonstrate that the model memorizes the noise
519   from the label-flipped samples by the end of training. Furthermore, we visualize the interpretation
520   of each model prediction using SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), which provides insights into the
521   contribution of each input variable to the model prediction. In Figure 2(b), the interpretations for
522   models trained with label noise, the interpretations appear messy with no clear pattern observed;
523   yet, for models trained without label noise, the interpretations are more consistent, focusing on
524
      generalizable features such as the вҖңwing" for the вҖңairplane" class and the overall вҖңoutline" for the
      вҖңautomobile" class. These findings align with our theoretical results, confirming the modelвҖҷs two-stage
525
      behavior under noisy conditions.
526
527   7    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
528
529
      In conclusion, our work rigorously characterized the role of label noise in the feature learning process
      of neural networks, identifying two stages in the learning dynamics. In Stage I, the model perfectly
530
      fits all the clean samples while ignoring the noisy ones, effectively learning the signal from the data
531
      and achieving good generalization. In Stage II, as the loss converges, the model inevitably over-fits
532   to the noisy samples, learning noise from data and resulting in poor generalization. This two-stage
533   behaviour firmly explains the effectiveness of early stopping and sample selection. Since the model
534   performs well at the end of Stage I, a natural future direction is to develop methods for accurately
535   identifying the point at which Stage I concludes.
536
      Limitations. We note that our current empirical validations mainly focus on image classification
537
      tasks, though aligning with most literature on label noise. We also note that our theoretical analysis
538   still relies on several assumptions, such as the simplified data and model setups.
