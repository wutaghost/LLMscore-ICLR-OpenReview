011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Truss decomposition is an effective and practical algorithm for dense subgraph
014            discovery. However, it is sensitive to the changes in the graph: dropping a few
015
               edges or a bit of noise can drastically impact the truss numbers of the edges.
               It is of practical importance to understand and characterize the robustness of
016
               truss decomposition. In this work, we study and utilize the robustness of truss
017
               decomposition in an edge-driven way. We propose to construct a dependency graph
018            among edges to denote the impact of an edge’s removal on the neighboring edges.
019            By using the dependency graph, we introduce three measures to capture the diverse
020            and unique properties of the edges. We provide theoretical findings and design an
021            efficient algorithm to compute the dependency graph faster than the naive baseline.
022            We also show that our new edge-based truss robustness measures capture intrinsic
023            graph structures and have the potential to unearth peculiar differences that can
024            help with various downstream tasks, such as edge classification. We integrate our
025            measures into the state-of-the-art GNN for edge classification and demonstrate
026
               improved performance on multi-class datasets. The overhead of computing our
               edge-based measures is insignificant when compared to the training time. We
027
               believe that utilizing edge-based truss and robustness measures can further be
028
               helpful in edge-driven downstream tasks.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Dense subgraphs are found to be useful in various applications such as anomaly detection, visualiza-
034   tion, and clustering (Shin et al., 2018; Liu & Sarıyüce, 2020; Alvarez-Hamelin et al., 2005; Gibson
035   et al., 2005). Among many methods, truss decomposition has attracted particular interest due to its
036   effectiveness and practicality (Sariyuce et al., 2015; Sariyüce et al., 2017; Wang & Cheng, 2012;
037   Fang et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2016; Wang & Cheng, 2012). 𝑘-truss is the maximal subgraph in
038   which every edge is contained in at least 𝑘 triangles (Cohen, 2008). Truss number (or trussness) of
039
      an edge is the largest 𝑘 for which the edge is part of a 𝑘-truss. 𝑘-truss is inspired by the concept of
      𝑘-core—the maximal subgraph in which every node has a degree of at least 𝑘 (Seidman, 1983)—and
040
      it is a superior alternative to 𝑘-core, outperforming it in various applications such as internet AS-level
041
      analysis (Gregori et al., 2013; Carmi et al., 2007; Alvarez-Hamelin et al., 2005), visualization (Healy
042   et al., 2008; Colomer-de Simón et al., 2013), and community detection (Sariyuce et al., 2015; Sariyüce
043   et al., 2017).
044
045
      Despite its broad applicability, 𝑘-trusses are notoriously sensitive to changes in the graph (Chen et al.,
      2021; 2022; Zhu et al., 2019). Even removing a few edges or adding some noise can have cascading
046
      effects and influence the truss numbers of surrounding edges. Chen et al. (2021) demonstrated that
047
      even minor edge removals can significantly impact truss-based communities, emphasizing the need
048   for taking truss robustness into account in downstream tasks. While core decomposition robustness
049   has been extensively studied (Zhou et al., 2021; Hossain et al., 2023), truss robustness remains
050   relatively unexplored, despite its superiority in various tasks across diverse data types (Gregori et al.,
051   2013). Considering the wide application space of 𝑘-trusses, it is of utmost importance to comprehend
052   and characterize the robustness of truss decomposition on the edge-level. Identifying which edges
053   are susceptible to nearby changes or determining the edges whose removal has a more pronounced
      impact on neighboring edges becomes crucial in this context. To the best of our knowledge, there

081   structural differences around edges, especially when traditional measures such as triangle count and
082   trussness fail, and hence can be useful in downstream tasks like edge classification.
083
      In this study, we characterize, quantify, and utilize the robustness of truss decomposition in an
084
      edge-driven way. We propose to model the robustness of truss numbers by establishing a dependency
085   graph among edges—edges in the input graph become nodes in the dependency graph. For any two
086   edges 𝑒 and 𝑓 that share a triangle, edge 𝑒 is defined to be dependent on edge 𝑓 if the truss number
087   of 𝑒 decreases after removing 𝑓 , denoted by 𝑓 → 𝑒 relation. We only focus on the impact of a
088   single-edge removal for simplicity—we hypothesize that multiple-edge removals can be approximated
089   by considering multiple single-edge removals. We remove each edge independently and create its
090   dependency relations to the neighboring edges. By utilizing the dependency graph, we propose three
091   edge-based measures: Edge Robustness, Edge Strength, and EdgeRank, which capture the (inverse
092   of) in-degree, out-degree, and PageRank values of the nodes in the dependency graph, respectively.
093
      Edge Robustness measures how likely an edge’s truss number drops when its neighbors are removed
      and Edge Strength quantifies the edge’s potency to alter the truss number of surrounding edges. As
094
      computing the dependency graph is computationally costly, we introduce several theoretical findings
095
      that enable faster computation. Leveraging these findings, we formulate an efficient algorithm that is
096   3.74× faster than the naive baseline, on average.

100

142   Core and truss robustness. Existing research on network robustness for dense subgraph discovery
143   leans heavily on 𝑘-cores (Zhou et al., 2021; Dey et al., 2020; Laishram et al., 2018; Adiga & Vullikanti,
144   2013; Hossain et al., 2023), while 𝑘-truss robustness is much less explored (Chen et al., 2021; 2022;
145   Zhu et al., 2019). Traditional methods (Dey et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021; Adiga & Vullikanti, 2013;
146   Laishram et al., 2018) have focused on core number changes across the entire graph or within specific
147
      subgraphs, neglecting individual node robustness. Hossain et al. (2023) addressed this by proposing
      Removal Strength (RS), which measure a node’s ability to influence or maintain its coreness under
148
      edge disruptions. RS has two variants: 𝑅𝑆𝑂𝐷 measures a node’s ability to affect neighboring core
149
      numbers upon removal, while 𝑅𝑆 𝐼 𝐷 measures its ability to stay within the 𝑘-core. The authors
150   showed that core robustness measures help to identify critical edges and influential nodes. Despite
151   its usefulness and superiority in various tasks on diverse data types and setups (Gregori et al., 2013;
152   Colomer-de Simón et al., 2013; Orsini et al., 2013; Wang & Cheng, 2012; Zhao & Tung, 2012;
153   Huang et al., 2016), truss robustness has received less attention. Zhu et al. (2019) introduced the
154   concept of using 𝑘-trusses to evaluate social network stability under edge removals. Building on this,
155   Chen et al. (2021) extended the analysis to consider both edge removals and insertions, addressing
156   community-breaking problems and network robustness. However, no measures have been developed
157   yet to assess the robustness of individual edges within 𝑘-trusses. In that sense, we propose the first
158
      edge-based robustness measures for truss decomposition.
159   Edge classification. Edge-driven methods, which reason about edges instead of nodes, and higher-
160   order techniques are getting more popular due to their novel relation-first approach (Benson et al.,
161   2016; Bick et al., 2023). Edge classification, crucial for tasks like anomaly detection and rec-
      ommendations (Bielak et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2020; Yang & Xu, 2022), presents unique chal-


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      lenges. Unlike nodes with inherent features, edges rely on aggregated information from connected
163   nodes. However, existing methods like neighborhood aggregation (Hamilton et al., 2017) or embed-
164   ding techniques (Grover & Leskovec, 2016; Tang et al., 2015; Menon & Elkan, 2011) struggle to
165   capture complex relationships or handle large-scale graphs effectively. To address this, auto-encoders
166   like Edge2vec (Wang et al., 2020) and AttrE2vec (Bielak et al., 2022) attempt to learn meaningful
167   edge representations by leveraging deep learning architectures or random walks. However, they
168   suffer from high computational costs. GNN-based approaches like SEAL (Zhang & Chen, 2018)
169   and EGNN (Li et al., 2022) focus on the local subgraph structure around an edge for representation
170   learning, achieving good accuracy. More recently, Wang et al. (2023) proposed two novel methods,
171
      Topological Edge Representation (TER) and Attributed Edge Representation (AER), specifically de-
      signed for efficient and effective edge-wise graph representation learning. TER captures higher-order
172
      proximities of edges in a low-dimensional space, significantly improving performance compared to
173
      existing approaches. AER augments edge attributes through a carefully-designed feature aggregation
174   scheme, enhancing representation quality for attributed graphs. Combining TER and AER outper-
175   forms previous approaches in edge classification. Most of the earlier works on edge classification
176   heavily focus on edge information aggregated from the node attributes or edge features learned from
177   GNNs. Although features like triangle count, trussness (Wang & Cheng, 2012), and node-pair sum of
178   core robustness (Hossain et al., 2023) have been defined earlier, they have not been used for edge
179   classification. Motivated by the intrinsic graph structures captured by our edge-based truss robustness
180   measures, we integrate them into TER and AER to enhance edge classification performance.

185   edge removals. We determine whether an edge’s truss number is dependent or not on any incident edge
186   when the edge is removed from the graph. We consider all the edges and figure out the dependency
187   relationships among them to quantify the robustness of each edge. We focus on the impact of a
188   single-edge removal for simplicity, and create the dependency graph as follows:

267   A naive way to compute the dependency graph is to run the incremental truss decomposition algorithm
268   used by Zhang & Yu (2019) for every single edge removal, which will be costly. Here we propose
269   efficient heuristics by using key observations about the truss structure. We begin by introducing a few
      useful definitions and lemmas about truss number changes upon edge removal.

285   have less than 𝑘 ∗ 2 incident edges whose truss numbers are at least 𝑘 and thus the 𝑡𝑠(𝑒, 𝐺) will also
286   decrease. As a result, from the definition of truss number, 𝜙(𝑒, 𝐺 \ {𝑒 0 }) will be less than 𝑘, and
287
      lemma holds.

      Focusing on the vulnerable edges and the sensitive incident edges around them can help figuring out

293   triangles where the common edge 𝑒 00 in every adjacent triangle has truss values 𝜙(𝑒 00) equal to 𝜙(𝑒).

      6              C ONCLUSION
525
      In this paper, we characterized and utilized the robustness of truss decomposition in an edge-driven
526
      way. We proposed to build a dependency graph among edges to capture the impact on neighbor edges
527
      and devised three new edge-based truss robustness measures on it. We introduced several theoretical
528   findings and designed an efficient algorithm to practically compute those measures. We showed the
529   utility and practicality of our measures in the edge classification task for real-world networks.
530
531
      Limitations and future work. While our work focuses on edge robustness under removal, exploring
      its behavior upon insertion presents a promising future direction. As pointed in Zhang & Yu (2019),
532
      updating the truss numbers upon insertions is a much more challenging problem with no known
533
      non-trivial upper bound. This is a limitation of the current approach and warrants further investigation.
534   Moreover, it will be interesting to incorporate our measures into other graph analysis tasks, such as
535   link prediction, and investigate their performances. Additionally, we plan to perform a theoretical
536   analysis on synthetic graphs (with simple formation processes) to prove how truss robustness measures
537   have a better discriminative power (than the other traditional graph analytics). Lastly, to assess node
538   importance within the dependency graph, we employed efficient local (in-degree, out-degree) and
539   global (PageRank) measures. More complex metrics, such as Eigenvector and Betweenness centrality,
      are computationally costly for our large dependency graphs, which we aim to explore in future work.

      When we check the edges solely identified by our edge robustness measures (but not by other
903
      methods), we notice that the structures that contribute to the improved performance are similar to
904   the toy graph in Figure 1. The reasoning for this is similar to what we provided in Figure 2b. We
905   focus on edges exclusively identified by our measures (and not by others). For each class, the average
906   robustness of these edges are given in Figure 8. The standard deviation of these class-wise averages
907   is high, which means our measure assigns distinct robustness values to different classes, unlike other
908   measures whose stdevs remain low across classes. In simpler terms, while other measures (e.g.,
909   trussness, triangle count) tend to provide similar values across classes, ours provide different values
910   across different classes (thanks to the structures in Figure 1). This ability of edge robustness to
911   differentiate across classes is the key factor contributing to the superior performance.
