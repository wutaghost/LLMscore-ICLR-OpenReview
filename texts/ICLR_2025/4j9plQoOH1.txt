030                                                           A BSTRACT
031
032            This paper presents LongViTU, a large-scale (~121k QA pairs, ~900h videos),
033            automatically generated dataset for long-form video understanding. Our key idea
034            is inspired by the success of Large Language Models (LLMs) and Multimodal Lan-
035            guage Models (MLMs) that are fueled by machine-generated instruction-following
036            data (e.g., InstructGPT, LLaVA). We developed a systematic approach to produce
037            massive question-answeringing pairs tailored to virtually unbounded long videos by
038
               organizing them into a hierarchical tree, incorporating self-revision mechanisms to
               guarantee high quality. We curate LongViTU for each QA pair: 1) involves a long
039
               context (average certificate length of 4.6 minutes); 2) requires rich knowledge and
040
               condensed reasoning (commonsense, causality, planning, etc.); 3) explicit labels the
041            timestamps of relevant events throughout the entire video. Furthermore, LongViTU
042            provides a benchmark to facilitate future research in instruction-following for
043            long-form videos. Our experiments first reveal the performance gap between open-
044            source video MLMs and their commercial counterparts (e.g., Gemini-1.5-Pro) on
045            this benchmark. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on open-source models led to
046            Video-LLaVA achieving the best performance, with a GPT-4 score of 50.7, closely
047            following 52.3 by the leading closed-source model Gemini-1.5-Pro, underscoring
048            the substantial challenge posed by our benchmark. Further SFT on LongViTU
049
               with Video-LLaVA resulted in improvements of 30.7% on the In-Distribution (ID)
               benchmark EgoSchema; 12.9% and 0.6% on the Out-of-Distribution (OOD) bench-
050
               marks WorldQA and VideoMME, respectively. These outcomes demonstrate the
051
               effectiveness and robust OOD generalizability of our proposed instruction-tuning
052            scheme for long-form video understanding. The dataset, SFT models, and code are
053            publicly available on the anonymous page LongViTU.

      1 I NTRODUCTION
055   We introduce LongViTU, a novel dataset tailored for large-scale, long-form video understanding (see
056   Figure 1 for examples, more in Table 4). In comparison to existing automatically generated Video
057   question-answeringing (VQA) datasets, such as Otter (Li et al., 2023a), Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al.,
058   2023), InternVideo (Wang et al., 2022), VideoChat (Li et al., 2023c), MVBench (Li et al., 2024),
059   EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024) and CinePile (Rawal et al., 2024), etc., LongViTU incorporates
060   key advancements that yield a dataset of greater naturalness and diversity. We delineate the primary
061   advantages of our dataset in contrast to the limitations of prior works downsides below, see Table 1
062
      for a clearer view.
063   • Diverse real world scenarios. Some prior VQA datasets originate from videos captured in vir-
064     tual environments, such as Env-QA (Gao et al., 2021) and OpenEQA (Majumdar et al., 2024),
065     which inherently introduce a domain gap. Many other datasets, despite utilizing real world
066     videos, often feature limited or homogeneous scenes. For instance, EgoVQA (Fan, 2019) pre-
067     dominantly includes office scenes, EgoTaskQA (Jia et al., 2022) primarily focuses on home
068     environments, EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024) encompasses a very limited number of
069     scenes, WorldQA (Zhang et al., 2024b) is mainly based on YouTube short videos, and both
070
        MovieChat (Song et al., 2024) and CinePile (Rawal et al., 2024) are derived from movie videos. In
        contrast, LongViTU leverages the complete Ego4D (Grauman et al., 2022), this extensive dataset
071
        enables VQA tasks to encompass a broad spectrum of real world scenarios.
072
      • Explicit timestamp labels. Previous datasets lack explicit timestamp labels for QA-related events,
073
        meaning that while a video may contain multiple QAs, the precise start and end times for each QA
074
        are not provided. Our hierarchical pipeline organizes video content into a tree structure, enabling
075     QA generation on subtrees and thereby ensuring explicit, accessible timestamps for each event.
076     Consequently, LongViTU offers precise start and end timestamps for all QA events, supporting
077     accurate identification of key events within ultra-long, redundant video sequences, and enabling
078     comprehensive model performance analysis.
079   • Long certificate length. The average certificate length (introduced in EgoSchema (Mangalam
080     et al., 2024), which we adhere to) in most short VQA datasets, such as NextQA (Xiao et al., 2021)
081     and ActivityNet-QA (Yu et al., 2019), is typically under 10 seconds, despite the total video duration
082     spanning tens or hundreds of seconds. Some longer datasets, like WorldQA (Zhang et al., 2024b),
083     still feature an average certificate length of less than 60 seconds, while EgoSchema (Mangalam
084     et al., 2024) remains below 100 seconds. In contrast, our approach supports the generation of QAs
085     across a spectrum of durations, from brief to notably extended sequences. As a result, LongViTU
086
        achieving an average certificate length of 276.8 seconds (~4.6 minutes), encompassing a diverse
        temporal range from events as brief as 6 seconds to those exceeding 1 hour. For further statistical
087
        details, please refer to Figure 3.
088
089
      • Fine-grained categorization. Existing VQA datasets often lack comprehensive categorization,
        primarily focusing on basic question types that revolve around spatial elements such as objects,
090
        attributes, locations, and states, etc. Although recent efforts like EgoTaskQA (Jia et al., 2022) and
091
        OpenEQA (Majumdar et al., 2024) have introduced categorized questions, these classifications
092     remain relatively coarse. In contrast, LongViTU places greater emphasis on the spatial-temporal
093     interplay, providing fine-grained categorization by incorporating detailed spatiotemporal features
094     intrinsic to video contexts. This includes three primary categories: Spatiotemporal Understanding,
095     Episodic Reasoning, and Commonsense Inference, as well as more fine-grained categories, as
096     shown in Figure 3b and Table 4.
097   • Open-ended precise QA. As opposed to the multiple-choice formats employed in VQA datasets
098     such as EgoVQA (Fan, 2019), EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024), and CinePile (Rawal et al.,
099     2024), or despite the open-ended formats in MovieChat (Song et al., 2024), WorldQA (Zhang et al.,
100     2024b), and OpenEQA (Majumdar et al., 2024), which frequently feature irrelevant or redundant
101     answers. LongViTU ensures a closer alignment between questions and video content, with answers
102
        being succinct and directly relevant. This is achieved through a self-revision mechanism that refines
        the QA by removing redundancies and further aligning questions with the video content, thereby
103
        ensuring concise, relevant, and high-quality QAs, which is detailed in Appendix B.
104
105   By preserving these advantages during dataset construction, as a result, the final dataset LongViTU
106   comprises ~121k high-quality QA pairs within ~900 hours of videos across 3 primary with 12 fine-
107   grained categories (detailed in Table 4). To the best of our knowledge, LongViTU is the first publicly
      available long-form video question-answeringing dataset featuring explicit QA-related timestamp

132   2     T HE L ONG V I TU DATASET
133
      We developed a hierarchical approach to process indefinitely long-form video content by organizing
134   it into a tree structure, enabling the generation of appropriate QA while capturing detailed spa-
135   tial information from individual frames and the temporal relationships between events or objects
136   across varying duration scales. This framework facilitates the generation of QA pairs with explicit
137   timestamps and long certificate length, enabling fine-grained categorization that aligns with the
138   video content, and also provides open-ended precise QA. To the best of our knowledge, no existing
139   automated Video question-answeringing dataset generation methods offer these capabilities.

143   2.1.1      S TAGE I: H IERARCHICAL V IDEO T REE C ONSTRUCTION
144
145   Frame level. Commencing at the frame level, we employ InternLM-XComposer2 (Dong et al.,
146
      2024) to perform multi-frame dense captioning (sampled at 1 fps) across annotated events in the
      Ego4D (Grauman et al., 2022). The descriptions of video context at this stage are denoted by
147
      ⟨df , tfs , tfe ⟩, where tfs and tfe represent the respective start and end times. Accurate timestamps are
148
      derived from Ego4D’s temporal annotations for each event provided by human annotators.
149
150   Event level. Redundant text frequently emerges in the frame level, to mitigate this issue, we employ
151   GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) for processing both manually annotated events dH              f from the original
152
      Ego4D (Grauman et al., 2022), which offer precise temporal contexts, and automatically generated
      dense captions ⟨d1f , ..., dF   f ⟩, to eliminate redundancy and refine the descriptions at the event level.
153
      GPT-4 then restructures these annotations into succinct event level descriptions, represented as
154
      ⟨de , tes , tee ⟩, where tes and tee denote the start and end times of the events, respectively.
155
156   Segment level. Thereafter, utilizing GPT-4 organizes events into segments within the hierarchical
157   video tree Tvideo , where closely related consecutive events are merged to form segments, subsequently
158
      summarizing these into segment level descriptions, denoted as ⟨ds , tss , tse ⟩. Consequently, the video
      content is structured hierarchically with the tree root, segments and events as intermediate nodes, and
159
      frames as leaf nodes.

197   2.1.2      S TAGE II: L ONG -F ORM QA G ENERATION
198
      Sliding window. The application of a sliding window approach to any subtree of Tvideo enables
199   the generation of QAs that adeptly capture both the spatial intricacies of individual frames and the
200   temporal continuity among segments. In this implementation, the sliding window encompasses five
201   segments sequentially, integrating descriptions at both the segment and event levels. This method
202   ensures the capture of continuous events (long-term temporal relevance) and detailed spatial features
203   (short-term spatial relevance). To prevent premature generation of questions concerning recent
204   events, GPT-4 is programmed to formulate questions based on critical events identified within the
205   initial three segments, while deriving answers from the following two segments.
206   Crucial advantages. This implementation synergizes with the hierarchical structure of the video
207   tree, yielding several crucial advantages that enhance the efficacy of the LongViTU methodology:
208
209   • Explicit timestamp labels: each tree node is clearly marked with event timestamp, thereby improv-
210     ing the precision of temporal analysis.
211   • Long certificate length: the capability to engage with various subtrees permits the handling of
212     QAs across a broad spectrum of durations, ranging from brief to extended periods, thus facilitating
213     versatile management of content length.
214   • Fine-grained categorization: Focusing on specific subtrees significantly reduces the input text for
215     the LLM, enabling it to handle long temporal events while maintaining attention to rich spatial
        details. This approach enables generation of QAs across diverse, fine-grained categories

249   2.1.3      S TAGE III: S ELF -R EVISION
250
251   Self-Revision. In this stage, GPT-4 conducts a thorough review of the generated question-answering
252
      pairs. This self-revision is pivotal for maintaining quality of QA pairs. The GPT-4 reviews each
      event description associated with the question-answering pairs to verify their consistency with the
253
      underlying video context. It identifies and rectifies any deviations or fabricated elements, extraneous
254
      information is minimized to highlight critical aspects of the question-answeringing, thereby preventing
255   the inclusion of redundant or overly simplistic responses. Further details regarding the prompts and
256   human evaluations of this self-revision process are detailed in Appendix B.

      2.2       C HARACTERISTICS AND S TATISTICS

      We conducted experiments to evaluate the performance of mainstream Video Language Models

      3.1 S ETUP

326
      Settings and Baselines. Following the methodology of LLava (Liu et al., 2024b), we utilized the full
      LongViTU training set to fine-tune various VLM models, evaluating their efficacy on the testset to
327
      tackle the novel challenges presented by our dataset in the context of long-form video understanding.
328
      The data formats were standardized, with inputs comprising relevant video clips for each task. Frame-
329   based models such as mPLUG-OWL (Ye et al., 2023) and Video-LLaVA (Lin et al., 2023) uniformly
330   extracted 8 frames from each input video. In comparison, models like LLaMA-VID (Li et al., 2023d)
331   and Gemini-1.5-Pro (Reid et al., 2024) sampled one frame per second, whereas VideoAgent (Fan
332   et al., 2024) opted for one frame every two seconds. These models generated answers to questions
333   regarding the video content. To ensure fair comparison, a multi-level scoring criteria was designed.
334   GPT-4 assessed the textual alignment between the generated answers and the ground truth, assigning
335   scores based on predefined criteria and providing an average score for each subcategory.
336   Metrics and Benchmarks. In evaluating open-ended questions, traditional reliance on caption
337   metrics is now considered inadequate. GPT-4 has demonstrated near-human performance in text
338   comprehension and alignment. Therefore, we developed a multi-level scoring criteria, enabling
339   GPT-4 to evaluate the correspondence between predicted answers and the ground truth, ensuring
340   that the essential elements of the question are captured. Hallucinations or irrelevant responses
341   result in a low score, whereas responses that accurately and concisely address key points receive
342   a high score, details on the specific prompts are provided in Appendix B. Beyond testing on our
343
      dataset, we fine-tuned all models using the LongViTU training set and performed evaluations on
      ID benchmark EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024) and OOD benchmarks VideoMME (Fu et al.,
344
      2024), WorldQA (Zhang et al., 2024b), and OpenEQA (Majumdar et al., 2024). These evaluations
345
      demonstrated enhanced performance compared to baseline models.

      The detailed quantitative evaluations of LongViTU are delineated in Table 2, from which we derive
350
      the following insights:
351
352   Effective fine-tuning with LongViTU. Upon employing LongViTU for training, all fine-tuned
353   models exhibit enhanced performance on the testset, surpassing their initial zero-shot outcomes.
354   Remarkably, the open-source model Video-LLaVa attains an average score of 50.7 post fine-tuning,
355
      approximating the leading commercial model Gemini-1.5-Pro at a dense sampling rate of 1 fps. This
      parity between top-tier open-source and commercial models underscores the persistent challenges
356
      posed by LongViTU in mastering long-form video content.
357
358   Poor sampling models. At a sampling frequency of 1 fps, the performance of LLaMA-VID is
359   suboptimal compared to that of mainstream open-source video language models (VLMs), in both
360   zero-shot and fine-tuned scenarios. This performance gap indicates a deficiency in the representational
361
      capabilities of existing dense sampling strategies, essential for effective long-form video analysis.
      LongViTU introduces the inaugural extensive, diverse, and high-quality dataset and benchmark,
362
      catering to this research domain.
363
364   Analysis of blind QA. Outcomes from pure text-based blind QA sessions are competitively robust,
365   suggesting that using text as an intermediary may skew QA systems towards textual domain predic-
366   tions. This enables a direct inference of questions from answers, we elaborate on the limitations of
367
      our pipeline in Appendix A.
368   Spatial vs. temporal bias. The noticeable underperformance in Spatiotemporal Understanding
369   relative to Episodic Reasoning and Commonsense Inference accentuates prevalent issues. Tasks
370   that require emphasis on spatial details prove exceptionally demanding within the scope of long-
371   form video understanding, indicating unresolved complexities in this domain. Conversely, Episodic
372   Memory Reasoning and Commonsense Inference derive benefits from the logical connectivity among
373
      sequential events, thus yielding superior results when leveraging text-based data.

468   3.5      F UTURE R ESEARCH
469
470   Our distinct and rational design endows LongViTU with numerous advantageous properties. Beyond
471   serving as a pre-training dataset and evaluation benchmark for long-form video understanding,
472   thereby enhancing generalization to OOD benchmarks, it also contributes groundbreaking insights
473   into streaming video processing and the exploration of video memory storage mechanisms in end-to-
474   end models. Future research will extend beyond long-form video understanding to integrate streaming
475   video processing and question-answering, fostering a more comprehensive approach.
476
477
478   4       R ELATED W ORK
479
480   Large language models. Large language models (LLMs), such as InstructGPT (Ouyang et al.,
481   2022), GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a), and LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al.,
482   2023b), have demonstrated notable capabilities in text processing, which have motivated their use in
483   generating large-scale multimodal datasets. These models convert different modalities into structured
484   textual descriptions, which can then be used to prompt GPT-4 to produce multimodal content. This
485   process effectively uses text as a bridge to unify various data forms, enabling new approaches in
      dataset automation.


                                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Instruction tuning dataset. LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b) was among the first to leverage foundational
487   vision models to generate image captions and detect bounding boxes, which were subsequently
488   processed by ChatGPT or GPT-4 to create QA tasks based on images. Building on this, methods
489   like Bongard-OpenWorld (Wu et al., 2023), Video-LLaVA (Lin et al., 2023), and VideoChat (Li
490   et al., 2023c) extended these principles to video data, transitioning from individual image QA to
491   video-based QA. By sampling multiple frames from videos and applying LLaVA’s procedure, these
492   approaches generate video QA datasets using structured frame descriptions, object categories, and
493   attributes. However, this basic repetition across frames, combined with the input length limits of
494   LLMs, constrains the number of frames that can be analyzed, thereby reducing the comprehensiveness
495
      of the resulting datasets.
496   Long-context language models. Even the most advanced long-context LLMs, such as GPT-4,
497   ChatGLM (GLM et al., 2024), Baichuan2 (Yang et al., 2023a), and InternLM2 (Cai et al., 2024b),
498   capable of handling input sequences beyond 128k tokens, experience substantial performance degra-
499   dation when confronted with long, intricate texts. They struggle to manage the redundancy and
500   disorder inherent in detailed descriptions of numerous video frames, limiting their ability to generate
501
      effective video QA. Unlike static images, videos inherently require an understanding of temporal
      dynamics, making event correlation crucial for video comprehension. The current frame-based
502
      extension approach fails to address this temporal aspect adequately, often resulting in QA generation
503
      that lacks depth beyond individual frame analysis.
504
505   Long-form video understanding. Instruction tuning based on the LLaVA paradigm (Liu et al.,
506   2024b) has demonstrated strong potential for multimodal alignment and understanding, including
507
      tasks like captioning and visual question-answering (Brown, 2020; Anil et al., 2023; Team et al.,
      2023; Li et al., 2023b; Dai et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Alayrac et al., 2022). While these
508
      methods perform effectively for individual images and short videos, extending to long-form video
509
      understanding presents significant challenges (Song et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023; Maaz et al., 2023;
510   Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). This difficulty primarily arises due to
511   the vast number of visual tokens produced by visual encoders, ranging from 576 to 2880 tokens
512   per image in the case of LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024a). As the number of frames increases, the
513   context-window length of LLMs is quickly exceeded. Recent methods have attempted to reduce the
514   number of visual tokens via resamplers that connect visual encoders to LLMs (Li et al., 2023b;d; Cai
515   et al., 2024a; Cheng et al., 2024), but this often compromises the quality of visual representation,
516   leading to suboptimal outcomes. More refined techniques for pruning or merging visual features
517   could provide a promising direction to address these limitations (Chen et al., 2024b; Shang et al.,
518
      2024; Jin et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024).
519
520   5   C ONCLUSION
521
522   We present LongViTU, a large-scale dataset for long-form video understanding, incorporating video
523   memory and explicit timestamp annotations. Our approach organizes video content hierarchically
524   into a tree structure to tackle the complexities of generating QA datasets for extended video content.
525   Using a sliding window mechanism, we capture both temporal and spatial context, ensuring that QA
526   pairs align well with video content and cover diverse and informative aspects. A self-evaluation and
527
      revision process further improves QA quality by reducing hallucinations, redundancy, and irrelevant
      content. Fine-tuning on the LongViTU training set led to significant performance improvements on
528
      both LongViTU and other benchmarks, demonstrating its efficacy and generalizability. Future work
529
      will explore memory storage strategies using explicit timestamps to further enhance long-form video
530   understanding and streaming video QA.

      A     L IMITATION S TATEMENT
757
758
759   We have designed LongViTU, a pretraining dataset and evaluation benchmark for long video under-
760   standing, presenting significant challenges to current Video Language Models. Neither open-source
761   nor commercial models have effectively addressed this problem yet, and we continue to explore
762   potential solutions. In this paper, we present the challenge of long-form video understanding and
763
      memory-based streaming video QA, focusing on how to efficiently integrate detailed visual features in
      extended video content. We also highlight the current limitations of existing methods, our automated
764
      pipeline is adaptable to various scenarios. Although self-evaluation and revision improve data quality,
765
      some generated data, while not incorrect, may lack meaningful context, potentially affecting the
766   model. To address this, we propose refining the pipeline and adding manual inspection to better align
767   the benchmark with real-world human understanding and QA. This benchmark will help evaluate
768   progress and performance in this field, and the work is ongoing.
