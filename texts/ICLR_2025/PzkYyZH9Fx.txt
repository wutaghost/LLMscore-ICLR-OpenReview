009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Anomaly detection is a critical learning task with many significant and diverse
012            applications. Currently, semi-supervised methods provide the state-of-the-art ac-
013            curacy performance but require labeled normal data for training. Unsupervised
014            approaches, on the other hand, do not have this requirement but can only offer
015            inferior anomaly detection performance. In this paper, we introduce NARCISSUS,
016            a novel unsupervised anomaly detection method that achieves accuracy compa-
017            rable to semi-supervised approaches. Our key insight is that a learning model
018            when training with a mix of normal and sparse anomalous data converges first
019            on normal data. Leveraging this insight, NARCISSUS employs a tailored early
020
               stopping scheme, eliminating the need for pseudo labels and costly label genera-
               tion interactions. It also offers systematic solutions to minimize the influence of
021
               model uncertainty, ensuring robust detection. NARCISSUS is model-agnostic and
022
               can therefore make use of even a semi-supervised anomaly detection model un-
023            derneath, thereby turning it into an unsupervised one. Comprehensive evaluations
024            using time series, image and graph datasets show that NARCISSUS provides simi-
025            lar or better detection performance compared to best-performing semi-supervised
026            methods while not requiring labeled data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Anomaly detection (AD) (Han et al., 2022; Pang et al., 2021; Chandola et al., 2009), or outlier
033
      detection, is a critical machine learning (ML) task with diverse applications, including anti-money
      laundering, network diagnostics, rare disease detection, and social media analysis. AD algorithms
034
      identify data instances that significantly deviate from the norm. While traditional unsupervised
035
      methods (Nakamura et al., 2020; Ahmad et al., 2017) detect anomalies without prior knowledge
036   of normal or anomalous data, emerging (semi-)supervised approaches (Lai et al., 2024; Tuli et al.,
037   2022) demonstrate improved accuracy by leveraging prior information.
038
039
      However, (semi-)supervised approaches rely on well-labeled data, which presents two key chal-
      lenges for anomaly detection: (1) Data Availability: Obtaining a well-labeled training dataset is of-
040
      ten difficult, and it can be challenging to ensure the dataset is entirely anomaly-free; (2) Overfitting:
041
      The trained model may overfit to the training data, resulting in inaccurate detection when faced with
042   distribution shifts. Conversely, unsupervised anomaly detection methods generally under-perform
043   compared to semi-supervised approaches that provide state-of-the-art accuracy (Pang et al., 2021;
044   Han et al., 2022). The absence of supervision makes it more difficult for unsupervised methods to
045   effectively distinguish anomalies from normal patterns.
046
      The above discussion suggests that combining unsupervised and semi-supervised learning can be a
047
      promising way forward. Indeed, previous works have attempted training with bootstrapped datasets
048   that mix normal and abnormal data; however, a significant performance drop is consistently observed
049   compared to training with exclusively normal data (Livernoche et al., 2024; Han et al., 2022). There
050   are also self-supervised methods that assign pseudo labels generated by an unsupervised detection
051   technique to train semi-supervised models (Li et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). However, the ef-
052   fectiveness of the self-supervised approach is constrained by the convergence speed and accuracy
053   of the initial unsupervised method, providing only modest improvements to overall accuracy. Thus,
      achieving high-accuracy unsupervised anomaly detection remains a significant challenge.

054
      Motivated by the above, we aim to design an unsupervised anomaly detection scheme that offers the
055   state-of-the-art accuracy. Our work is inspired by research in binary classification, where models
056   tend to exhibit significantly lower loss on low-influence data early in the training process (Paul et al.,
057   2021), as well as the use of early stopping as a Nonparametric Variational Inference method (Du-
058   venaud et al., 2016). Our key insight is that when training a model for some learning task on a mix
059   of normal and anomalous data, the model converges faster on normal data while struggling to fit to
060   anomalous data. In other words, at a certain point in the training process, the model would have
061   effectively learned to fit the normal data but continues to exhibit higher loss on anomalous data (see
062   Figure 1 for an illustration). Note that this phenomenon is model-agnostic, as all models undergo a
063
      similar process when fitting the training dataset.
064   In this paper, we introduce NARCISSUS, a new unsupervised anomaly detection method that ex-
065   ploits the above insight. NARCISSUS leverages training dynamics for accurate and robust anomaly
066   detection with unlabeled data through a combination of a tailored early stopping algorithm and
067   an ensemble method. Early stopping enables identifying anomalies without the additional data
068   labeling cost, while the highly parallel ensemble method mitigates the impact of epistemic un-
069
      certainty in a lightweight yet reliable way. Our comprehensive evaluation demonstrates that
      NARCISSUS allows successfully turning an AD algorithm originally designed for semi-supervised
070
      anomaly detection to an unsupervised setup while maintaining or often improving accuracy.

094

095

096   and semi-supervised settings. In addition to different levels of supervision, anomaly detection have
097   been applied across diverse data formats, including time series (Tuli et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023),
098   images (Schlegl et al., 2017; Roth et al., 2022; Livernoche et al., 2024), and graphs (Zheng et al.,

177   4.2   DATA C HARACTERISTICS
178
179   We identify the following common characteristics across typical anomaly detection scenarios: (i)
180   anomalies are generally sparse, constituting only a small fraction of the entire dataset; and (ii) the
181   data is well-bounded, with the dynamic range of anomalous points not significantly (orders of mag-
182   nitude) exceeding that of normal data. Observation (i) aligns with the definition of an anomaly
183
      or outlier – data that deviates from the normal majority. Observation (ii) applies specifically to
      cases requiring deep learning, as simpler threshold-based methods could otherwise detect anoma-
184
      lies. Later, we demonstrate how these characteristics result in different convergence rates for normal
185
      and anomalous data, an insight we leverage for unsupervised anomaly detection.

341   5.1   M ETHODOLOGY
342
343
      We consider three kinds of baselines, including: (i) unsupervised anomaly detection methods, (ii)
      semi-supervised methods with unsupervised bootstrapping (Han et al., 2022; Livernoche et al.,
344
      2024), and (iii) semi-supervised methods in their original setup. Baseline type (i) refers to the
345
      methods that perform anomaly detection in an unsupervised manner, such as Zong et al. (2018).
346   Type (ii) is the most common approach for applying a semi-supervised model to an unsupervised
347   scenario, using randomly selected data as normal data for bootstrapping during training. The differ-
348   ence between type (ii) and (iii) is the training dataset, where (iii) is trained with clean dataset free of
349   anomalies.
350
      Self-supervised methods (Zhang et al., 2023) with pseudo labels and interactive training are not
351
      considered because they would need a method like NARCISSUS as a module in the self-supervised
352   workflow. Since NARCISSUS significantly outperforms existing unsupervised detection methods, it
353   will inherently boost performance in self-supervised setups. Empirically, we find that models trained
354   with NARCISSUS match or surpass semi-supervised models with comparable computational over-
355   head, making self-supervised workflows unnecessary due to their limited improvement and higher
356   computational cost.
357
      We evaluate different AD methods considering widely used metrics: F1 score, precision (P), recall
358   (R), and AUC – area under receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve.

      5.3       B EYOND T IME S ERIES DATA : A NOMALY D ETECTION ON I MAGES AND G RAPHS
455
      NARCISSUS can be generalized to AD with other types of data beyond time series. Conceptually, an
456   image or graph can be treated as analogous to a time series segment. In anomaly detection for images
457   and graphs, some tasks focus solely on a supervised approach with labeled data to detect predefined
458   anomalies. Here, we instead focus on cases that rely on semi-supervised AD to demonstrate the
459   ability of NARCISSUS to achieve equivalent performance with unsupervised training. Specifically,
460   we consider three representative semi-supervised AD methods, as follows:
461   • PatchCore (Roth et al., 2022), a reconstruction based method for image AD.
462   • AnoGAN (Schlegl et al., 2017), an unconditional generation based method for image AD.
463   • AddGraph (Zheng et al., 2019b) for anomaly detection in dynamic graphs.
464   We train PatchCore with NARCISSUS on the MVTec2D dataset. We do the same for AnoGAN using
465   the MNIST dataset (Schlegl et al., 2020). We use the official implementation of AddGraph (Zheng
466   et al., 2019a) and train it following NARCISSUS approach using the UCI Message and Digg (a social
467   news site) dataset. By default, we merge the original training and test data in these works and
468   then apply NARCISSUS to detect anomaly in an unsupervised manner, without changing any other
469   configuration. We apply NARCISSUS as well as the implementation approaches (ii) and (iii) in §5.1,
470   where the bootstrapping is repeated 30 times randomly.
471   The performance is included in Table 3 for PatchCore, Table 4 for AnoGAN, and Table 5 for Ad-
472   dGraph, where we use the metrics as in those original works for performance evaluation. Training
473   with NARCISSUS shows performance comparable to training on the entire normal dataset, with only
474   negligible changes in metrics such as AUC. Achieving high-accuracy detection without labels is a
475   non-trivial task. We also implemented a bootstrapping approach, where the training set is randomly
476   sampled, and the model is trained to convergence. Bootstrapping resulted in significant performance
477   degradation on PatchCore and AddGraph, while its performance on the MNIST dataset remained
478
      more stable, likely due to MNIST’s synthetic nature and the sparse, pronounced anomalies.

516
        Limitation of N ARCISSUS. NARCISSUS can only be used when data is well bounded and anomaly
517
        is sparse. However, in real-world scenarios where a significant portion of the data may be anoma-
518
        lous, semi-supervised approaches trained with normal data may be necessary. Additionally, NAR -
519     CISSUS requires a relatively large dataset to execute RVES effectively. After partitioning a validation
520     set, the remaining data must be sufficient to train the base model. A notable exception was observed
521     when applying NARCISSUS to MTAD-GAT on the NAB dataset, highlighting that NARCISSUS per-
522     forms more reliably with larger datasets.
523
        Less image and graph cases are studied. In image and graph anomaly detection, leveraging labeled
524
        data is common, with models trained to identify specific objects (Acsintoae et al., 2022; Tang et al.,
525     2023). Moreover in models like PatchCore (Roth et al., 2022), all features are extracted using a
526     pretrained model, limiting our ability to carry out more reliable anomaly detection. However, our
527     experiments still demonstrate the potential of NARCISSUS in extending beyond time series to other
528     domains.

        7                   C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
        In this paper, we have introduced NARCISSUS, a novel unsupervised learning approach that achieves
533
        accuracy on par with state-of-the-art semi-supervised methods but solely with unlabeled data. The
534     success of NARCISSUS stems from our key insight: provided that anomalies are sparse and the
535     data is well-bounded, the model training with mixed normal and anomalous data initially converges
536     on the normal data. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the NARCISSUS
537     approach and highlight its potential for AD with time series and other types of data.

763
764   Let X denote the set of all input data points, and let Y ⊆ X be an arbitrary subset of X. Suppose the
765   function f : X → Z is a mapping defined over X. The optimization problem defined in Eq. 3 can be
766
      formulated as:
                            min L(f, y) subject to C(x, f (x), y) ≤ ϵ, ∀x ∈ X,
767                           f
768   where L(f, y) is the objective function, C(·) represents the constraint function, and ϵ is a tolerance
769   parameter.
770
771   Proof by contradiction: Suppose that the optimization problem defined in Eq. 3 has no solu-
772   tion. This would imply that the set of constraints cannot be satisfied simultaneously under the given
773   objective function. Specifically, once the function f and the corresponding input y are known, the
774
      objective function becomes deterministic and thus computable, resulting in a feasible region of zero
      measure in the solution space. i.e.,
775
776
                                      µ({x ∈ X|C(x, f (x), y) ≤ ϵ}) = 0,
777   where µ(·) denotes the measure of the feasible region in the solution space.
778
      Now, consider a subset Y ⊂ X such that |Y| < Nmax , where Nmax denotes the maximum
779
      allowable size of a subset that satisfies the constraints. If for every point x ∈ X − Y, the model
780
      sequence {fn } converges, i.e.,:
781                                             lim fn (x) = f (x),
782                                              n→∞

783   and can be extended to converge on any subset Y with |Y| < Nmax , then the convergence property
784   of f can be generalized to the entire domain X. That is, there exists a function fb such that
785                                       fb = f (x), ∀x ∈ (X − Y) ∪ Y
786   which satisfies all constraints defined.
787
788   This leads to the contradiction since the model f can satisfy the constraints over every sub-
789   set Y ⊂ X with |Y | < Nmax and the convergence can be generalized to the entire set X.

844     A.5                     T RICKS TO ACCELERATE VES
845
846     We can apply the following techniques to accelerate the VES algorithm. While these optimizations
847     have shown empirical success on the datasets we’ve tested, due to the inherent complexity of real-
848     world scenarios, we recommend using the complete VES setup to ensure robustness.
849     • Only consider the validation set τi ∈ Ti with minimal loss in VES to determine convergence
850       level.
851
        • Stop right after the τi with minimal loss converge, without check the objective function.
        • Skip the RVES process if the validation sets exhibit distinct convergence speeds, or if all validation
852
          sets converge quickly and uniformly, similar to the majority of the training set.
