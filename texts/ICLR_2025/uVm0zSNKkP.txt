009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Sampling from probability distributions is a fundamental task in machine learning
012            and statistics. However, most existing algorithms require numerous iterative steps
013            to transform a prior distribution into high-quality samples, resulting in high com-
014            putational costs and limiting their practicality in time-constrained and resource-
015            limited environments. In this work, we propose consistency samplers, a novel
016            class of samplers capable of generating high-quality samples in a single step. Our
017            method introduces a new consistency distillation algorithm for diffusion-based
018            samplers, which eliminates the need for data or full trajectory integration. By
019            utilizing incomplete sampling trajectories and noisy intermediate representations
020
               along the diffusion process, we efficiently learn a direct one-step mapping from
               any state to its corresponding terminal state in the target distribution. More-
021
               over, our approach enables few-step sampling, allowing users to flexibly balance
022
               compute costs and sample quality. We demonstrate the effectiveness of consis-
023            tency samplers across multiple benchmark tasks, achieving high-quality results
024            with one-step or few-step sampling while significantly reducing the sampling time
025            compared to existing samplers. For instance, our method is 100-200x faster than
026            prior diffusion-based samplers while having comparable sample quality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Sampling from an unnormalized target distribution ρ ∝ ptarget without access to data samples is a
033
      fundamental challenge across various domains, including machine learning (Neal, 1995; Hernández-
      Lobato & Adams, 2015), statistics (Neal, 2001; Andrieu et al., 2003), physics (Wu et al., 2019;
034
      Albergo et al., 2019), chemistry (Frenkel & Smit, 2002; Hollingsworth & Dror, 2018), and many
035
      other fields involving probabilistic models.
036
037   Many existing sampling algorithms are inherently iterative, with the accuracy of the final samples
038   depending heavily on the number of steps. For example, Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)
039
      methods rely on iteratively generating samples through a Markov chain that converges to the target
      distribution (MacKay, 2003; Robert, 1995). Similarly, diffusion-based samplers frame sampling as
040
      a stochastic optimal control problem, transforming samples from a simple prior distribution into the
041
      target distribution by iteratively solving a controlled stochastic differential equation (SDE) (Zhang
042   & Chen, 2022; Vargas et al., 2023; Berner et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Richter & Berner, 2024).
043   However, these iterative samplers often suffer from slow mixing and require hundreds or even more
044   steps to converge, making them impractical for use in large models and resource-limited scenarios.
045
      In this work, we propose a novel class of samplers, consistency samplers (CS), that can generate
046
      high-quality samples in just a single step. A comparison between CS and existing iterative samplers
047
      is shown in Figure 1. To achieve one-step sampling, our method introduces a new distillation algo-
048   rithm for diffusion-based samplers, inspired by the idea of consistency models (CM) (Song et al.,
049   2023). Unlike CMs, our approach does not require access to data or the generation of full sampling
050   trajectories. Instead, it leverages intermediate noisy representations to learn the consistency func-
051   tion, significantly reducing the computational overhead of the training process. In our numerical
052   experiments, we demonstrate the effectiveness of consistency samplers across multiple benchmark
053   tasks, achieving high-quality results with one-step or few-step sampling, and drastically reducing
      the sampling time compared to existing methods. In summary, our contributions are as follows:

085   2   R ELATED WORK
086
087   Iterative sampling methods. Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is commonly used for sampling
088   unnormalized distributions. The core idea is to construct a Markov chain whose equilibrium dis-
089   tribution matches the desired target distribution (Brooks et al., 2012). Popular MCMC algorithms
090   include Metropolis-Hasting (Metropolis et al., 1953; Hastings, 1970), Gibbs sampling (Geman &
091   Geman, 1984), and Langevin dynamics (Rossky et al., 1978; Parisi, 1981). Instead of propagating a
092
      single sample, sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) methods propagate a population of particles through
      a sequence of intermediate distributions (Doucet et al., 2001). An example is annealed importance
093
      sampling, which transforms a simple distribution into the target distribution using annealed interme-
094
      diate distributions and importance weights (Neal, 2001).
095
096   The classical Schrödinger bridge problem (Schrödinger, 1931; 1932) seeks the most likely stochas-
097   tic process that transports one distribution to another consistently with a pre-specified Brownian
098
      motion. The sampling problem can then be framed as an optimal control problem, where a con-
      trolled SDE is used to evolve samples from an initial distribution through the Schrödinger bridge
099
      to the target distribution (Tzen & Raginsky, 2019; Vargas et al., 2022; Zhang & Chen, 2022). This
100
      approach motivates the study of diffusion processes as samplers Geffner & Domke (2023); Vargas
101   et al. (2023); Richter & Berner (2024); Zhang et al. (2024); Phillips et al. (2024).
102
103   Key to MCMC, SMC, and diffusion-based samplers is their iterative nature, where each method
104
      progressively refines samples through a series of transformations or updates to more accurately
      represent the target distribution. Our work asks whether it is possible to skip the iterative refinement
105
      process by learning to directly map the initial distribution to the target.
106
107   Accelerating strategies for sampling. Robert et al. (2018) surveys various techniques to improve
      MCMC efficiency, including Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, which leverages the geometry of the target


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      distribution for more effective sampling (Duane et al., 1987; MacKay, 2003; Brooks et al., 2012;
109   Chen et al., 2014). To reduce costs on large datasets, subsampling MCMC methods (Bardenet
110   et al., 2017; Andrieu & Roberts, 2009; Zhang & De Sa, 2019; Zhang et al., 2020b) and stochastic
111   gradient MCMC methods (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2020a;c) have been
112   developed. These approaches are orthogonal to our method since they reduce the cost per step but
113   remain fundamentally iterative in nature.
114
      Amortized inference, on the other hand, shifts the computational cost to a training phase, resulting in
115   a sampler that is faster at test time (Gershman & Goodman, 2014). For instance, amortized MCMC
116   (Li et al., 2017) distills an MCMC sampler by training a student model to mimic the sample after
117   T -step MCMC transitions. Most amortized inference methods rely on simulation-based training,
118   where a teacher sampler generates data during training. GFlowNets (Bengio et al., 2021; 2023)
119   focus on sampling complex composite objects by sequentially composing their elements. While
120   GFlowNets amortize the computational challenges of lengthy stochastic searches and mode-mixing
121   during training, their sampling process remains sequential, as objects are constructed step-by-step
122   through a series of constructive steps. In contrast to amortized MCMC, our method only requires
123
      generating incomplete samples during training and enables single-step sampling, unlike the sequen-
      tial sampling process of GFlowNets’ generative policy.
124
125   Diffusion generative models. In contrast to diffusion-based samplers, diffusion generative models
126   rely on direct access to data from the target distribution and progressively perturb this data toward
127   noise via a diffusion process (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020;
128   Song et al., 2021b). The generative process learns to reverse this diffusion through denoising score
129
      matching (Hyvärinen, 2005; Vincent, 2011). Several strategies have been proposed to accelerate
      the generation process of diffusion generative models. For example, faster solvers (Song et al.,
130
      2021a; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Jolicoeur-Martineau et al., 2021; Karras et al., 2022) reduce the
131
      number of reverse iterations from hundreds or thousands to just tens. Additionally, knowledge
132   distillation techniques can further minimize the number of steps, allowing for single-step or few-step
133   generation (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Song et al., 2023). In this work, we extend ideas from distillation
134   techniques for diffusion generative models to diffusion-based samplers to design an efficient, single-
135   step sampler.

      Diffusion-based samplers are controlled stochastic processes that gradually transform samples from
140   a simple prior distribution x0 ∼ pprior into approximate samples from the target distribution xT ∼
141   ptarget by evolving forward in time t ∈ [0, T ]:
142
                                dxut = (µ(xut , t) + σ(t)u(xut , t)) dt + σ(t) dwt ,                     (1)
143
144   where w is a standard Brownian motion, µ is the drift term, σ is the diffusion coefficient, and u is a
145   control term that adjusts the drift.
146   The objective is to find u such that Eq. (1) approximates the reverse-time process of an inference
147   diffusion that adds noise to samples drawn from the target distribution:
148
                                dytv = (µ(ytv , t) + σ(t)v(ytv , t)) dt + σ(t) dwt .                     (2)
149
150   where v(ytv , t) = σ ⊤ (t)∇ log pytv (yt ) (Anderson, 1982).
151   By ensuring that y0v ∼ pprior and u = v, one can achieve pxu = pyv , and thus xuT ∼ ptarget .
152   However, directly computing the score ∇ log pyv is intractable, and we assume that no dataset from
153   ptarget is available to approximate it.
154

155
      denote the path space measure of the process defined by Eq. (2). Further, let U ⊂ C(Rd ×[0, T ], Rd )
156
      represent the space of admissible controls. Diffusion-based samplers seek to find an optimal control
157   u∗ that minimizes the divergence between the path measures of the generative and time-reversed
158   inference processes:
159                                       u∗ ∈ arg min D(Pxu ∥Pyv ),                                    (3)

      tween the two path distributions.

      4     C ONSISTENCY SAMPLER
170
      In this section, we introduce consistency samplers, a method for distilling diffusion-based samplers
171
      into single-step samplers.
172
173
      4.1   PARAMETERIZATION
174
175   We propose distilling a diffusion process induced by a control function u, which satisfies the problem
176   in Eq. (3), into what we call a consistency sampler. Given u, the consistency sampler learns a
177   deterministic consistency function f : (xut , t) 7→ xuT , which maps any intermediate state of a path
178   directly to its terminal state. As a result, one-step sampling becomes feasible from any point in time,
179   in particular from the initial state.
180   To ensure that the learned consistency function outputs the correct terminal state, we parameterize
181   the consistency sampler such that the consistency function is the identity f (xuT , T ) = xuT at the
182   terminal time. Following Song et al. (2023), the consistency sampler is parameterized as follows:
183
                                    fθ (xut , t) = cskip (t)xut + cout (t)Fθ (xut , t),                      (5)
184
185   where the coefficients cskip (t) and cout (t) are such that cskip (T ) = 1 and cout (T ) = 0, ensuring that
186   the output is equal to the terminal state. Here, Fθ is a free-form neural network, and its architecture
187   can be borrowed from prior diffusion-based samplers.
188   To train the consistency sampler, we aim to ensure that the learned function provides consistent
189   mappings between adjacent points along the diffusion trajectory. Specifically, we minimize the
190   difference between the outputs of the consistency function applied to the states of two consecutive
191   time steps, xutn and xutn+1 , in a given time discretization.
192
      Consistency distillation of diffusion generative models rely on a direct access to samples from ptarget
193
      to learn the consistency function (Song et al., 2023). In contrast, our approach assumes that we do
194   not have access to data, and generating a dataset of samples from the target distribution using a
195   pre-trained sampler is computationally expensive.

      4.3   P ROBABILITY FLOW ODE FOR DETERMINISTIC TRANSITIONS
240
      When simulating the forward SDE (Eq. (1)), the transition between two states is stochastic due
241   to the randomness introduced by the Brownian motion. This stochasticity creates challenges for
242   learning the consistency function, as the probabilistic nature of state transitions induces ambiguity
243   in the mapping. Specifically, the same intermediate state xut can correspond to multiple potential
244   future paths, complicating the task of learning a unique and consistent mapping from xutn and xutn+1
245   to xuT .
246
      Fortunately, for all diffusion processes, there exists a corresponding deterministic process whose tra-
247   jectories share the same marginal probability densities as the original SDE (Song et al., 2021b). This
248   deterministic process is governed by an ordinary differential equation (ODE), commonly referred to
249   as the probability flow ODE (PF ODE). The PF ODE corresponding to the forward generative SDE
250   (Eq. (1)) is:
251                                      u          u       1         u
252
                                      dxt = µ(xt , t) + σ(t)u(xt , t) dt.                                 (7)
                                                            2
253
254   By leveraging the PF ODE, we can obtain deterministic consecutive points x̂utn and x̂utn+1 for train-
255   ing the consistency function, thus avoiding the stochasticity challenges posed by the SDE. When
256   simulating the pre-trained diffusion-based sampler during training, we therefore use the PF ODE
257   (Eq. (7)) instead of the SDE (Eq. (1)).

349
350
      In this section, we empirically evaluate the performance of the proposed consistency sampler, trained
351
      using Algorithm 1. The control uθ is modeled as a neural network, which is pre-trained using either
352   the denoising diffusion sampler (DDS) (Vargas et al., 2023) or the time-reversed diffusion sampler
353   (DIS) (Berner et al., 2024). Both DDS and DIS implementations rely on the PIS-GRAD architecture
354   introduced by Zhang & Chen (2022), where the control is:
355
356
357                        uθ (xt , t) = NN1;θ (xt , t) + NN2;θ (t) × ∇ log ptarget (xt ),
358
359
360   with NN1;θ and NN2;θ representing two neural networks. Across all experiments, we use a two-
361   layer architecture with 64 hidden units each for both networks. The training objectives of DDS and
362
      DIS are presented in Appendix A.
363   The training cost of CS is less then that of the denoising diffusion sampler (DDS) and the time-
364   reversed diffusion sampler (DIS). In DDS and DIS, the controlled process appears directly in the
365   training objective (see equations 10 and 11). Unlike diffusion models that use the denoising score
366   matching objective and can resort to Monte Carlo approximations (Hyvärinen, 2005; Song & Er-
367   mon, 2019), DDS and DIS require full trajectory simulation during training. Similarly, CS requires
368
      trajectory simulation during training; however, CS integrates only partial trajectories up to a random
      timestep. This approach saves approximately 50% of the training time for a fixed number of training
369
      iterations.
370
371   In our parameterization of the consistency sampler (Section 4.1), we initialize the network Fθ in
372   Eq. (5) with NN1;θ (xt , t). As a result, the consistency sampler requires roughly half the number of
373   parameters compared to DIS and DDS, thereby reducing both the computational cost of a forward
374
      pass through the model and the memory requirements.
375   In all of our experiments, DDS and DIS follow a variance-preserving SDE (Song et al., 2021b) with
376   a Gaussian prior, and are trained using the log-variance divergence (Richter & Berner, 2024) with
377   200 diffusion steps to solve the optimal control problem of Eq. (3). The consistency sampler is
      trained with 18 diffusion steps, using λ(t) = 1 and the L2-norm in the loss Eq. (8).

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this work, we introduce consistency samplers, a new class of samplers designed for sampling
489   from unnormalized distributions. Unlike most existing methods that require multiple iterative up-
490   dates, consistency samplers can generate high-quality samples in one step. Consistency samplers
491   amortize sampling from a pre-trained diffusion-based model by learning a direct mapping from any
492   point along the sampling trajectory to the target distribution. This mapping enables one-step sam-
493   pling from the target distribution, while retaining the flexibility to refine samples through multiple
494   denoising and noise addition steps, offering to trade computational cost for accuracy.
495   A key advantage of our method is that it does not require access to pre-collected datasets. Rather
496   than fully integrating the diffusion trajectories of a pre-trained diffusion-based sampler, our method
497   learns the single-step mapping directly from intermediate noisy samples, reducing the training time.
498
      Our experiments demonstrate that consistency samplers perform well in both one-step and few-step
499
      sampling tasks, achieving results comparable to diffusion-based samplers that require hundreds of
500
      steps, while maintaining good sample quality.
501
502   Obtaining samples under limited computational budgets remains a significant challenge. We see
503   consistency samplers as a step toward more practical and efficient sampling, accelerating the appli-
504
      cation of sampling methods in large-scale and resource-constrained machine learning and scientific
      problems.
