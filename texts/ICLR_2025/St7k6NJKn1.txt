                                                      A BSTRACT
008
009
010
                   Recent advances in text-to-speech (TTS) systems, particularly those with voice
                   cloning capabilities, have made voice impersonation readily accessible, raising
011
                   ethical and legal concerns due to potential misuse for malicious activities like mis-
012
                   information campaigns and fraud. While synthetic speech detectors (SSDs) exist
013                to combat this, they are vulnerable to “test domain shift”, exhibiting decreased
014                performance when audio is altered through transcoding, playback, or background
015                noise. This vulnerability is further exacerbated by deliberate manipulation of syn-
016                thetic speech aimed at deceiving detectors. This work presents the first systematic
017                study of such active malicious attacks against state-of-the-art open-source SSDs.
018                White-box attacks, black-box attacks, and their transferability are studied from
019                both attack effectiveness and stealthiness, using both hardcoded metrics and hu-
020                man ratings. The results highlight the urgent need for more robust detection meth-
021
                   ods in the face of evolving adversarial threats.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Recent years have witnessed a remarkable advance in text-to-speech (TTS) systems (Kreuk et al.,
027
      2022; Borsos et al., 2023; Leng et al., 2023; Saeki et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
      2023; ChatTTS, 2024; Chen et al., 2024; xTTS, 2024; ElevenLabs, 2024; Le et al., 2024; Lux et al.,
028
      2024). Some of these systems possess the zero-shot / few-shot voice cloning capability (Biadsy
029
      et al., 2024; Cooper et al., 2020; Casanova et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2024; Le et al., 2024), that can
030   mimic someone’s voice using only a brief sample of that person’s speech recording. The widespread
031   access of these systems, either through open-source projects or commercial APIs, has made voice
032   impersonation easier than ever. This raises significant ethical and legal concerns as the capability can
033   be easily misused for misinformation campaign, fraud, copyright infringement, etc. As an instance,
034   a scammer utilized synthetic audio to mimic President Biden in unlawful robocalls during a New
035   Hampshire primary election, resulting in a $6 million penalty and felony accusations (Coldewey,
036   2024). Besides, numerous instances of synthetic audio misuses can be found online if searching
037   with “deepfake audio”, which underscores the urgent need to address this growing problem.
038   Synthetic speech detectors (SSDs) are deployed to mitigate the misuse of synthetic speech. While
039   there are a number of advanced SSDs (Tak et al., 2021c;a; Jung et al., 2022), recent research (Müller
040   et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2024) suggests they might struggle when facing “test domain shift”. Con-
041   cretely, a detector may exhibit decreased performance when presented with audio that has undergone
042   alterations, including transcoding, playback, background noise, or even just a shift in the TTS sys-
043   tem used. However, current research efforts are directed towards these natural changes to audio, it is
044
      important to recognize that deliberate manipulation of synthetic speech by an attacker with the intent
      to deceive detectors can significantly increase the likelihood of success of the attacker. However,
045
      there is a lack of systematic research on these malicious perturbations.
046
047   In this work, we conduct the first systematic study of active malicious attacks against the state-of-
048   the-art open-source SSDs. We investigate a range of attack scenarios, considering adversaries with
049   varying levels of access to the target SSD: those with full knowledge of the model (white-box), those
050
      who can only interact with it and observe the results (black-box), and those who cannot even query
      the model (agnostic); and evaluate the results using both hard-coded metrics and human ratings.
051
052   Our findings reveal that:
053

      2     P RELIMINARY
067
      2.1   TTS T ECHNIQUES
068
069   TTS systems, which convert written text into speech, have a long history of development and have
070   made remarkable progress in recent years. Early TTS systems primarily used a concatenative ap-
071   proach (Khan & Chitode, 2016), where speech was synthesized by joining pre-recorded speech units
072   from a database. Despite the simplicity, they suffered from unnatural prosody and robotic-sounding
073   speech. To address the issue, researchers proposed statistical parametric speech synthesis (Zen et al.,
074   2009). These systems used statistical models to learn the relationship between linguistic features
075   (e.g., phonemes, part-of-speech tags) and acoustic features (e.g., fundamental frequency, spectral
076   envelope) and enabled more natural-sounding speech generation with improved prosody.
077   Recently, deep learning has revolutionized the field of TTS. Neural network-based TTS systems
078   have surpassed traditional methods in terms of speech naturalness and intelligibility. Several ar-
079   chitectures have been explored, including sequence-to-sequence models (Wang et al., 2017; Ping
080   et al., 2017), attention-based models (Ren et al., 2019; 2020), and generative adversarial networks
081   (GANs) (Kumar et al., 2019). These models can directly learn the mapping from text to speech,
082   enabling end-to-end training and eliminating the need for complex feature engineering. Some of the
083
      popular neural TTS systems include Kreuk et al. (2022); Borsos et al. (2023); Leng et al. (2023);
      Saeki et al. (2023); Shen et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023); ChatTTS (2024); Chen et al. (2024);
084
      xTTS (2024); ElevenLabs (2024); Le et al. (2024); Lux et al. (2024).
085
086
087   2.2   S YNTHETIC S PEECH D ETECTION T ECHNIQUES
088
      In the past, detecting synthetic speech required carefully crafted features (Doddington et al., 2001;
089
      Alegre et al., 2013; Hanilçi et al., 2015; Patel & Patil, 2015; Sahidullah et al., 2015; Todisco et al.,
090   2016). However, with the increase in available data and the development of larger models, simpler
091   features like waveforms or spectrograms are now sufficient for effective detection. RawNet2 (Tak
092   et al., 2021c) is a deep convolutional neural network for synthetic speech detection using merely
093   raw waveforms. It builds upon the RawNet (Jung et al., 2020) architecture by incorporating residual
094   connections and dilated convolutions. RawNetGATST (Tak et al., 2021a) extends RawNet2 by
095   incorporating a graph attention network (Tak et al., 2021b) to identify key spectral or temporal
096   features for detection. Similarly, AASIST (Jung et al., 2022) refines the graph network architecture
097   further for improved synthetic speech detection.
098
099   3     B YPASS S YNTHETIC S PEECH D ETECTION S YSTEMS

102
103   Can deepfake audio be altered in ways nearly imperceptible to the human ear, but sufficient
104
      to bypass state-of-the-art detectors?
105   Unlike previous research that focused on natural perturbations (Müller et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2024),
106   we consider a malicious attacker who deliberately optimizes the perturbation to evade detection.
107   We examine this scenario under various levels of access to the detection systems, from having full
      knowledge (white-box), to partial knowledge (black-box), to no knowledge (agnostic).

      3.4    AGNOSTIC ATTACK : T RANSFERABILITY OF A BOVE ATTACKS
453
454         Takeaway:
455          • For both white-box attacks and black-box attacks, transferability depends on the target
456            model’s capability on the target audio.
457
             • Black-box attacks are more transferrable on in-domain test data than out-of-domain
458
               data.
459
460
             • Transferrability of different white-box attacks are alike.
461
462   The above attacks all assume different levels of access to the SSD model which might not be ac-
463   cessible in practice. As a result, we want to understand whether the above attacks are transferrable:
464   Can a successfully attacked example on one model transfer to a different model? If this is true, then
465   the adversary can craft a proxy model themselves, attack it, and expect it to bypass the real SSD as
466
      well.
467   The results are summarized in Figure 10. First, we find that on out-of-domain data, some SSDs
468   are extremely vulnerable. For example, on WaveFake, RawNet2 is extremely vulnerable under all
469   attacks; on In-the-wild, ASSIST and AASIST-L are more vulnerable than the other two models.
470   Second, we find that on in-domain data, black-box attacks are much more transferrable than white-
471   box attacks. This is because 1) black-box attacks tend to add larger perturbation than white-box
472
      attacks; 2) the SSDs’ decision borders are alike for in-domain data. Thirdly, we also observe high
      similarity between the transferrability heatmap between PGD and I-FGSM, which might be due to
473
      different white-box attacks taking gradient paths in similar directions despite small differences.

      4     C ONCLUSION
477
478
479   This work presents the first systematic exploration of the robustness of state-of-the-art SSDs against
480   adversarial attacks. Our findings reveal critical implications for SSD deployment and future re-
481   search.
482   Firstly, we demonstrate a clear correlation between system accessibility and vulnerability. Open-
483   access SSDs, and even those with oracle access, are highly susceptible to attacks. This underscores
484   the critical need to restrict public access to SSD models and internal workings. While complete
485   prevention of information leakage may be challenging, measures such as rate limiting can effectively
      mitigate the threat of black-box attacks.

521   Secondly, our research exposes the vulnerability of current open-source SSDs to domain shifts,
522
      including active perturbations and, notably, synthetic data generated from unseen TTS systems.
      This highlights two key recommendations:
523
524
525
            • Comprehensive and Continuously Updated Training Data: SSD training datasets should
              encompass a diverse range of TTS systems and be regularly updated to incorporate new
526
              systems, ensuring broad coverage against evolving spoofing techniques.
527
528         • Data Composition Confidentiality: Maintaining the confidentiality of training data compo-
529           sition is crucial to prevent attackers from exploiting this knowledge for targeted attacks,
530           as evidenced by the significant advantage gained from TTS system shifts even in agnostic
531           attacks.
532
533   Our findings also draw a parallel between the interplay of SSD and TTS development and an of-
534   fline GAN, suggesting a future where TTS systems may achieve near-perfect human mimicry. This
535   emphasizes the crucial need for complementary audio authenticity techniques, such as robust water-
536
      marking (Liu et al., 2024), to bolster the security of SSD systems.
537   In conclusion, this study serves as a critical analysis of the current state of open-source SSD robust-
538   ness. By exposing key vulnerabilities and providing actionable recommendations, we aim to guide
539   future research and development efforts towards building more secure and resilient SSD systems in
      an ever-evolving landscape of speech synthesis and spoofing technologies.

      Algorithm 1 White-box Attacks: PGD and I-FGSM
703
704   Input: Waveform audio s ∈ RT , SSD model f , perturbation step size α, maximum number of
705       iterations T , real audio class R, attack A ∈ {‘PGD’, ‘I-FGSM’}, `∞ -norm constraint
706
      Output: Adversarial perturbation δ ∈ RT
       1: δ ← 0                                                                             . initialization
707
       2: for t ← 1 to T do
708
       3:     if A == ‘PGD’ then
709    4:          δ ← δ − α · ∇δ lCE (f (s + δ), R)        . gradient descent with cross entropy loss lCE
710    5:     end if
711    6:     if A == ‘I-FGSM’ then
712    7:          δ ← δ − α · sign(∇δ lCE (f (s + δ), R))           . FGSM with cross entropy loss lCE
713    8:     end if
714    9:     δ ← clip(δ, − ,  )                                                               . projection
715   10:     if f (s + δ) == R then                                                       . attack success
716   11:          Break
      12:     end if
717
      13: end for
718
      14: return δ
