009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            To address the needs of modeling uncertainty in sensitive machine learning ap-
012            plications, the setup of distributionally robust optimization (DRO) seeks good
013            performance uniformly across a variety of tasks. The recent multi-distribution
014            learning (MDL) framework Awasthi et al. (2023) tackles this objective in a dy-
015            namic interaction with the environment, where the learner has sampling access to
016            each target distribution. Drawing inspiration from the field of pure-exploration
017
               multi-armed bandits, we provide distribution-dependent guarantees in the MDL
               regime, that scale with suboptimality gaps and result in superior dependence on
018
               the sample size when compared to the existing distribution-independent analyses.
019
               We investigate two non-adaptive strategies, uniform and non-uniform exploration,
020            and present non-asymptotic regret bounds using novel tools from empirical pro-
021            cess theory. Furthermore, we devise an adaptive optimistic algorithm, LCB-DR,
022            that showcases enhanced dependence on the gaps, mirroring the contrast between
023            uniform and optimistic allocation in the multi-armed bandit literature.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Classical statistical learning operates under the assumption that data comes from a single source
029   Hastie et al. (2009). However, the growing use of machine learning in safety-critical applications has
030
      brought forth the demand for more robust models that address stochastic heterogeneity. One well-
      established paradigm is distributionally robust optimization (DRO) Rahimian & Mehrotra (2022),
031
      which seeks good performance uniformly across a collection of distributions. Concretely, let A and
032
      X be decision and data spaces, respectively, and suppose that data is sampled from a distribution
033   within some uncertainty set U ⊂ P (X ). Under a target reward function r : A × X → R and
034   distribution Q ∈ U, an action a ∈ A yields expected reward µ (a; Q) := EXQ ∼Q [r (a, XQ )]. DRO
035   then focuses on the problem
036
037                                     max µDR (a) := min µ (a; Q)                                    (DR)
                                        a∈A                 Q∈U
038
039   Recent works Blum et al. (2017); Sagawa* et al. (2020); Haghtalab et al. (2022) have studied the
040   setting of finite U and tackle it via interactive dynamics with the environment. More precisely,
041   the emergent multi-distribution learning (MDL) framework Awasthi et al. (2023) assumes sampling
042   access to U, where a learning agent sequentially selects which distributions to sample from given a
043   fixed sampling budget.
044   The current literature is populated with distribution-independent rates; i.e., bounds that are indepen-
045   dent of problem parameters. While broad in its applicability, this approach falls short in capturing
046   the nuances of the underlying environment. Oftentimes, it is more intuitive to analyze the learner’s
047   performance in a fixed setting, as opposed to considering a worst-case instance for each sample size.
048   When domain knowledge is available, a “one-size-fits-all” rate does not provide any insight on how
049   to take advantage of this information.
050   To address these drawbacks, in this work, we study distribution-dependent guarantees for the MDL
051   problem. Motivated by its close ties to the well-studied pure exploration multi-armed bandits (PE-
052   MAB) Bubeck et al. (2011) paradigm, we analyze the simple strategies of uniform and non-uniform
053   exploration, as well as their optimistic counterpart, ensuring regret guarantees that scale with sub-
      optimality gaps and decay much faster with the sampling budget.

055
056   We place MDL algorithms into one of two categories: non-adaptive and adaptive. In the former,
057   data is collected without any interaction with the environment and, in the latter, the learner sequen-
058   tially selects distributions based on previously acquired samples. We introduce two strategies of the
059   non-adaptive type: uniform (UE) and non-uniform (NUE) exploration (Section 3). As the names
060   suggest, UE gathers the same number of samples from each distribution, while NUE can benefit
061   from varied sample sizes. Using tools from empirical process theory, we provide non-asymptotic
062
      regret guarantees that scale with the suboptimality gaps of the problem and decay exponentially
      with the sampling budget T (Section 3.1). This stands in contrast to the distribution-independent

117   1.2    R ELATED WORK
118
119
      The predominance of machine learning in society has highlighted the need for robust models that
120   maintain high-quality performance in a multitude of scenarios. Given the inherent uncertainty in
121   identifying the environment, much attention has been given to the problem of learning under distri-
122   bution shifts Ben-David et al. (2009); Mansour et al. (2009), where training data may not necessarily
123   be sampled from the target distribution. To tackle this, several works Volpi et al. (2018); Zhang et al.
124   (2021); Sutter et al. (2021) have applied the framework of DRO Scarf (1958); Delage & Ye (2010);
125   Ben-Tal et al. (2013) by assuming that the shift occurs within a neighborhood U of some nominal
126   distribution, typically generating data, and solving (DR). There are many ways to construct U and
127   optimize the objective, and we refer to Shapiro et al. (2021); Rahimian & Mehrotra (2022) for a
128
      thorough review.
129   A more recent line of work has specialized to finite and unstructured U = {Q1 , . . . , Qk }, under sam-
130   pling access to each distribution. Agnostic federated learning Mohri et al. (2019) solves (DR) under
131   mixtures of U, providing high-probability bounds on the generalization gap of non-uniform explo-
132   ration and an algorithm with empirical optimization guarantees. Collaborative PAC learning Blum
133   et al. (2017) focuses on binary classification, with the aim of guaranteeing P (∆DR (AoT ) ≤ ϵ) ≥
134
      1 − δ under a minimal number of samples T . The original work of Blum et al. (2017) assumes
      realizability and subsequent studies Chen et al. (2018); Nguyen & Zakynthinou (2018); Carmon &
135
      Hausler (2022); Haghtalab et al. (2022) extended results to the agnostic case and gave improved
136
      rates, along with sample-complexity lower bounds. Awasthi et al. (2023) later solidified the theory
137   and posed several open problems, some of which were recently addressed in Peng (2024); Zhang
138   et al. (2024) via optimal algorithms.

163
164   Notation. We frequently use the notation [k] := {1, . . . , k}, where k ∈ N. For a measurable space
165   X (we will omit the σ-algebras), we let P (X ) denote the set of all distributions over it. For two
166   real-valued functions f and g, we let f ≲ g and f ≳ g denote inequalities up to universal constants.
167   Given values a, b ∈ R, we define a ∨ b := max {a, b} and a ∧ b := min {a, b}.
168
169   2.1   M ULTI - DISTRIBUTION LEARNING
170
171   Let X be the space where our data lives in and A the space where we make decisions. Given
172   data XQ ∼ Q ∈ P (X ), statistical learning aims to maximize the stochastic objective µ (a; Q) =
173
      E [r (a, XQ )] with respect to a ∈ A, where r : A × X → R is an underlying reward function. In the
      MDL paradigm, we capture distributional uncertainty by assuming that the distributions come from
174
      some uncertainty set U ⊂ P (X ) and instead aim to solve the distributionally robust problem (DR),
175
      where our goal is to maximize µDR (a) = minQ∈U µ (a; Q). We measure the performance of a deci-
176
      sion a ∈ A via its suboptimality gap ∆DR (a) := µ*DR − µDR (a), where µ*DR := maxa∈A µDR (a)
177
      is the optimal objective value. Throughout this work, we operate under the following assumptions.
178
      Assumption 1 (Finite decision/uncertainty sets). |A| = l and |U| = k, where 2 ≤ l, k < ∞.
179
180
      Assumption 2 (Bounded rewards). The reward function r is bounded in [0, 1] for ease of presenta-
      tion. This assumption will be relaxed in Section 3.2.
181
182   To solve (DR), we interact with the environment for a total of T ∈ N rounds. In each round
183   t ∈ [T ], we (i) select a distribution Qt ∈ U and (ii) receive independent data point Xt ∼ Qt .
184   After the T rounds, we output a decision AoT ∈ A with the goal of minimizing the simple regret
185   E [∆DR (AoT )] or error probability P (∆DR (AoT ) > 0). The strategies described in this work are of
186   the form AoT = argmaxa∈A µoT (a) for an appropriately constructed proxy µoT : A → R.
187   Remark 1 (Simple regret v.s. error probability). Note that both performance measures are closely
188   related: since r ∈ [0, 1], we have that ∆DR ∈ [0, 1] and, thus,
189                   ∆DR,min P (∆DR (AoT ) > 0) ≤ E [∆DR (AoT )] ≤ P (∆DR (AoT ) > 0)
190   where ∆DR,min is the minimal positive gap (see Section 2.2).
191

      6   D ISCUSSION
526
527
      In this work, we delve into the problem of DRO within the MDL framework, an area of grow-
528   ing popularity in high-stakes machine learning applications. Rooted in empirical process theory
529   and inspired by the PE-MAB literature, we offer novel insight into the key strategies of uniform and
530   non-uniform exploration via distribution-dependent bounds. By scaling with instance-specific quan-
531   tities, our proposed bounds decay much faster, with respect to sample sizes, than existing ones. We
532   additionally devise an optimistic method, LCB-DR, that shows improvements over its non-adaptive
533   counterparts, paralleling classical findings in the MAB setting.
534   While LCB-DR exhibits favorable rates, we reiterate that tuning certain parameters involves esti-
535   mating unknown quantities. This raises the question of whether there exists a more astute way to
536   select such quantities with minimal prior information. Moreover, the procedure requires specifying
537   the order to play the actions in. Although the absence of any problem knowledge might preclude
538   exploiting this sequence effectively, perhaps some preliminary understanding of the distributions
539   allows potential advantages (e.g., start with actions that explore as much as possible, so that Uj is
      small in future iterations).
