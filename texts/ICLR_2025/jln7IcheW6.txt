011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Language model (LM) watermarking techniques inject a statistical signal into
014            LM-generated content by substituting the random sampling process with pseudo-
015
               random sampling, using watermark keys as the random seed. Among these statisti-
               cal watermarking approaches, distortion-free watermarks are particularly crucial
016
               because they embed watermarks into LM-generated content without compromising
017
               generation quality. However, one notable limitation of pseudo-random sampling
018            compared to true-random sampling is that, under the same watermark keys (i.e.,
019            key collision), the results of pseudo-random sampling exhibit correlations. This
020            limitation could potentially undermine the distortion-free property. Our studies
021            reveal that key collisions are inevitable due to the limited availability of water-
022            mark keys, and existing distortion-free watermarks exhibit a significant distribution
023            bias toward the original LM distribution in the presence of key collisions. More-
024            over, we go beyond the key collision condition and prove that achieving a perfect
025            distortion-free watermark is impossible. To study the trade-off between watermark
026
               strength and its distribution bias, we introduce a new family of distortion-free
               watermarks–beta-watermark. Experimental results support that the beta-watermark
027
               can effectively reduce the distribution bias under key collisions.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In an era where artificial intelligence surpasses human capabilities in generating text, the authenticity
033   and origin of such AI-generated content have become paramount concerns. Language model water-
034   marking (Aaronson, 2022; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Christ et al., 2023; Kuditipudi et al., 2023; Hu
035   et al., 2023) provides a promising solution for distinguishing between human and machine-generated
036   text. This technique secretly embeds a statistical signal into the generated text using a pseudo-random
037   generator seeded with watermark keys. The embedded signal is then detected through a statistical
038
      hypothesis test, ensuring the traceability and verification of the text’s origin.
039   Distortion-free watermarks (Aaronson, 2022; Christ et al., 2023; Kuditipudi et al., 2023; Hu et al.,
040   2023) represent one of the most compelling techniques in language model watermarking. These
041   watermarks are particularly valuable because they provably preserve the output distribution of the
042   original language model. Specifically, the expected watermarked distribution with respect to the
043   watermark keys remains identical to the original language model distribution, thus offering significant
044
      practical application potential.
045   However, the pseudo-random nature of the watermark generator may lead to correlations between
046   generated content when the watermark keys are identical (i.e., key collision). In extreme cases,
047   such as when the prompt remains the same, key collisions can result in identical generated content,
048   significantly limiting its application scenarios. For instance, when using GPT-4 to generate content,
049   if the initial output is unsatisfactory, a request to regenerate would typically yield a different result.
050
      However, under a distortion-free watermarking scheme, the output may remain unchanged due to the
      consistent application of the same watermark key. This limitation highlights a critical challenge in
051
      the practical deployment of such watermarking techniques.
052
053   In our research, we comprehensively analyze the existing distortion-free watermarks and demonstrate,
      through both theoretical and empirical evidence, that no distortion-free watermark can fully preserve

054
      the original LM distribution under key collisions. Specifically, we categorize the level of distortion-
055   free capability into three types: a) Step-wise distortion-free—the watermark preserves the LM
056   distribution at a single token generation step; b) Weakly distortion-free—the watermark preserves
057   the LM distribution for a one-time sentence generation; c) Strongly distortion-free—the watermark
058   preserves the LM distribution across multiple sentence generations. Our findings indicate that all
059   existing distortion-free watermarks are weakly distortion-free but not strongly distortion-free due to
060   key collisions. Under the key collisions, In particular, we theoretically prove that there does not exist
061   any detectable strongly distortion-free watermark. We also show that key collisions are inevitable
062   given the limited number of watermark keys available in current schemes.
063   To mitigate the distribution bias caused by key collisions, we introduce the beta-watermark and
064   develop a model-agnostic detector that can identify watermarks without requiring access to prompts
065   or language models. Additionally, we design empirical metrics to measure the distribution bias
066   resulting from key collisions. Through rigorous testing on widely-studied language models, including
067   BART-large model (Liu et al., 2020) and LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023), our beta-watermark has
068   demonstrated effectiveness in significantly reducing the distribution bias induced by key collisions.
069   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
070
071          • We identify three levels of distortion-free capabilities in watermarks—Step-wise, Weakly,
072            and Strongly Distortion-free—revealing that existing watermarks are not strongly distortion-
073            free and cannot preserve the original language model distribution under multiple generations
074
               due to the inevitability of key collisions.
075          • Under watermark key collisions, we theoretically demonstrate a trade-off between watermark
076            strength and its distribution bias to the original LM distribution—a smaller distribution bias
077            results in weaker watermark strength. Based on our discussion on the distribution bias, we
078            proposed a black-box distortion-free watermark detection approach, which can effectively
079
               check if an LM is watermarked given the original LM. Furthermore, we go beyond the key
               collision assumption and prove that strongly distortion-free watermarks does not exist.
080
081          • We introduce beta-watermark, a new family of weakly distortion-free watermarks that
082            can provably reduce the distribution bias by trading the watermarking strength. Through
083
               experiments on popular language models like BART-large and LLaMA-2, we demonstrate
               our theoretical findings that existing watermarks are not strongly distortion-free and beta-
084
               watermark can effectively reduce the distribution bias.

087   2    R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Statistical watermarks. Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) enhanced the statistical watermark framework
090   originally introduced by Aaronson (2022), demonstrating the effectiveness of statistical watermarking
091   through extensive experiments on large language models. They splited the LM tokens into red
092   and green list, then promoted the use of green tokens by adding a fixed parameter δ to their logits.
093
      Zhao et al. (2023) proposed the unigram watermark, which enhances the robustness of the statistical
      watermark by using one-gram hashing to produce watermark keys. Liu et al. (2023b) also improved
094
      the robustness of statistical watermarking by leveraging the semantics of generated content as
095
      watermark keys. Additionally, Liu et al. (2023a) proposed an unforgeable watermark scheme that
096   employs neural networks to modify token distributions instead of using traditional watermark keys.
097   However, these approaches may lead to significant changes in the distribution of generated text,
098   potentially compromising content quality.
099
      Distortion-free watermarks. To preserve the original output distribution in watermarked content,
100
      researchers have explored alternative strategies to modify the token distribution. Aaronson (2022)
101
      introduced the first distortion-free watermarking strategy, which utilized Gumbel-reparametrization
102   to alter token distribution and the prefix n-gram content as the watermark keys. Christ et al. (2023)
103   and Kuditipudi et al. (2023) adopted the inverse-sampling and Gumbel-reparametrization to modify
104   the watermarked token distributions, where the watermark keys are based on the token position or a
105   fixed key list respectively. Notice Christ et al. (2023)’s method encounters resilience challenges under
106   modifications and lacks empirical evidence regarding its detectability. Meanwhile, Kuditipudi et al.
107   (2023)’s detection process involves hundreds of resampling steps from the secret key distribution,
      proving inefficient for processing lengthy texts. Hu et al. (2023) employed inverse-sampling and

109   access to the language model API and prompts. Wu et al. (2023) improved the permute-reweight

      Notations. Denote by V := {t1 , ..., tN } the vocabulary (or token) set of a language model, and
115
      by N = |V | its size. Let V represent the set of all conceivable string sequences, including those
116
      of zero length. A language model generates a token sequence based on a predetermined prompt.
117   For a single step in this process, the probability of generating the next token xn+1 ∈ V , given the
118   current context from x1 to xn , is represented as PM (xn+1 | x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ). For brevity, we adopt
119   the condensed notation: PM (xn+1:n+m | x1:n ), where xn+1:n+m = (xn+1 , . . . , xn+m ). Note that
120   the prompt is deliberately omitted in this representation. Inherent to its design, the language model
121   operates in an autoregressive mode. This implies that the combined probability of generating
                                                                                               Qm        several
122   tokens, specifically from xn+1 to xn+m , takes the form PM (xn+1:n+m | x1:n ) = i=1 PM (xn+i |
123   x1:n+i−1 ).
124   Watermarking problem definition. A language model (LM) service provider aims to watermark the
125   generated content such that all other users can verify if the content is generated by the LM without
126   needing access to the LM or the original prompt. A watermark framework primarily consists of two
127   components: a watermark generator and a watermark detector. The watermark generator embeds a
128   watermark into the text through a Pseudo-random Distribution Adjustment rule (PDA-rule), which is
129   seeded by watermark keys. The watermark detector, on the other hand, detects the presence of the
130   watermark within the content using a statistical hypothesis test.
131   Definition 3.1 (PDA-rule). Let P represent the space of token distributions and let K denote the
132   space of watermark keys. A Pseudo-random Distribution Adjustment rule (PDA-rule), defined as
133   F : P × K → P, adjusts the token distribution based on a given watermark key.
134   Watermark generator. During the watermark generation process, the service provider modifies
135   the original language model distribution PM using a watermark key k ∈ K and a PDA-rule. Here,
136   the watermark key acts as a random seed to modify the distribution, after which the next token is
137   sampled from this modified distribution. A watermark key usually consists of a secret key sk and a
138   context key (e.g., n-gram (Aaronson, 2022) or token position (Christ et al., 2023)). Let F := {F :
139   P × K → P} denote the set of PDA-rules. Specifically, let PW denote the distribution of the LM
140   after watermarking, and k the watermark key, PW (t | x1:n−1 ) := F (PM (· | x1:n−1 ), k)(t), ∀t ∈ V ,
141   where PM (· | x1:n−1 ) is the LM token distribution for sampling the n-th token. When sampling the
142
      next token xn , the language model samples from PW (· | x1:n−1 ) instead of PM (· | x1:n−1 ). This
      mechanism allows the service provider to inject a statistical signal into the generated content.
143
144   The PDA-rule is the core of the watermark generator. A PDA-rule is considered distortion-free if and
145   only if it preserves the token distribution during watermark generation. To the best of our knowledge,
146   there are three types of distortion-free PDA-rules: inverse-sampling (Christ et al., 2023; Kuditipudi
147   et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023), Gumbel-reparametrization (Aaronson, 2022; Kuditipudi et al., 2023; Fu
148
      et al., 2024), and permute-reweight (Hu et al., 2023). A detailed introduction to these methods can be
      found in Section 4.1. The formal definition of a distortion-free PDA-rule is presented below.
149

167

      Definition 3.3 (Key collision). Key collision refers to scenarios where the same watermark keys are
180
      used to seed the PDA-rule.
181
182   All three watermark key sampling methods mentioned previously have a limited number of keys
183   given the fixed secret key sk0 . The maximum key volume is |V |n for n-gram hashing, l0 for position
184   hashing, and n0 for the fixed key set. Here, l0 represents the maximum token length for the language
185   model, typically ranging from 104 to 106 . Therefore, if we only have one secret key, key collisions
186   will occur when the number of queries and the generated tokens exceeds the key volume.

188   4    C URSE OF K EY C OLLISION ON D ISTORTION -F REE WATERMARKS
189
190   We start with showing key collision is inevitable. In the previous section, we show that given a fixed
191   secret key sk0 , the watermark key space is finite. Consequently, key collisions will occur with a
192   sufficient number of queries to the language model. One might naturally question whether using an
193   infinite number of secret keys (e.g., a unique key for each generation) could expand the watermark key
194   space to infinity, thereby reducing the likelihood of collisions. However, this approach is impractical
195   because it would substantially reduce detection efficiency. When analyzing a watermarked sequence,
196   the detection algorithm would need to be applied to all possible secret keys, even though only one key
197   corresponds to the watermark. Thus, the watermark information becomes obscured by the numerous
198
      other keys. All missing proofs can be found in Appendix D.

531
      In conclusion, this work identifies three levels of distortion-free capabilities in watermarks—Step-
      wise, Weakly, and Strongly Distortion-free—and demonstrates that existing watermarks are not
532
      strongly distortion-free due to key collisions, which disrupt the original language model distribution
533
      across multiple generations. We theoretically establish a trade-off between watermark strength
534   and distribution bias, and introduce a black-box detection approach for identifying watermarked
535   models. Additionally, we prove that strongly distortion-free watermarks are theoretically unattainable.
536   As a practical solution, we propose beta-watermark, a new weakly distortion-free watermark that
537   effectively reduces distribution bias at the cost of watermarking strength. Future research direction
538   includes 1) exploring further details of the trade-off between the distribution bias and the watermarking
539   strengh and 2) developing more efficient watermark detection methods for weakly distortion-free
      watermarks.

676   B    R ELATED W ORK
677
678   Statistical watermarks. Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) enhanced the statistical watermark framework
679   originally introduced by Aaronson (2022), demonstrating the effectiveness of statistical watermarking
680   through extensive experiments on large language models. They splited the LM tokens into red
681
      and green list, then promoted the use of green tokens by adding a fixed parameter δ to their logits.
      Zhao et al. (2023) proposed the unigram watermark, which enhances the robustness of the statistical
682
      watermark by using one-gram hashing to produce watermark keys. Liu et al. (2023b) also improved
683
      the robustness of statistical watermarking by leveraging the semantics of generated content as
684   watermark keys. Additionally, Liu et al. (2023a) proposed an unforgeable watermark scheme that
685   employs neural networks to modify token distributions instead of using traditional watermark keys.
686   However, these approaches may lead to significant changes in the distribution of generated text,
687   potentially compromising content quality.
688
      Distortion-free watermarks. To preserve the original output distribution in watermarked content,
689
      researchers have explored alternative strategies to modify the token distribution. Aaronson (2022)
690   introduced the first distortion-free watermarking strategy, which utilized Gumbel-reparametrization
691   to alter token distribution and the prefix n-gram content as the watermark keys. Christ et al. (2023)
692   and Kuditipudi et al. (2023) adopted the inverse-sampling and Gumbel-reparametrization to modify
693   the watermarked token distributions, where the watermark keys is based on the token position or a
694   fixed key list respectively. Notice Christ et al. (2023)’s method encounters resilience challenges under
695   modifications and lacks empirical evidence regarding its detectability. Meanwhile, Kuditipudi et al.
696   (2023)’s detection process involves hundreds of resampling steps from the secret key distribution,
697   proving inefficient for processing lengthy texts. Hu et al. (2023) employed inverse-sampling and
698
      permute-reweight methods for watermarking. But their detector is not model-agnostic, which requires
      access to the language model API and prompts, which compromises its operational efficiency.
699
700   Post-hoc Detectors. Post-hoc detection serves as a significant alternative to watermarking, focusing
701   on the retrospective analysis of machine-generated text. This can be achieved by leveraging inherent
      features of language models or by enhancing pre-existing expansive models to function as detectors,


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      as detailed by (Zellers et al., 2019). Specific implementation nuances, such as sampling methods,
703   can be uncovered through reverse engineering the generated text, a process described by (Tay
704   et al., 2020). Additionally, there are post-hoc detectors designed for modern large language models
705   (Mitchell et al., 2023; Tian, 2023; Kirchner et al., 2023), specifically trained for binary detection
706   tasks. However, there is a growing consensus that these detection methods are becoming less effective
707   as language models evolve. As observed by Gambini et al. (2022), detection mechanisms effective
708   against GPT-2 have struggled with GPT-3. Moreover, text rephrasing models like those in (Krishna
709   et al., 2023) are bypassing prevalent post-hoc detectors such as GPTZero (Tian, 2023), DetectGPT
710   (Mitchell et al., 2023), and OpenAI’s proprietary detector (Kirchner et al., 2023). Additionally,
711
      Chakraborty et al. (2023) notes that as AI-generated content becomes increasingly indistinguishable
      from human-produced text, the demand on post-hoc detectors to analyze longer text segments will
712
      likely increase.
713
714   Steganography. Steganography involves embedding hidden messages in media such as natural
715   language or images, ensuring only intended recipients can detect the message while it remains
716   concealed from others (Hopper et al., 2002). When applied to watermarking, the goal is to maintain
717
      stealth. However, established steganography techniques may not meet this goal without certain
      entropy-related assumptions. In scenarios where language model prompts can be adversarially
718
      chosen, the need for stealth remains. This discrepancy arises due to the different levels of access that
719
      watermarking and steganography have to the model’s output distribution. In steganography, there
720   is only oracle access to this distribution, whereas our watermarking approach provides a detailed
721   view of the token’s probability distribution. Hence, while steganography either depends on entropy
722   assumptions (Hopper et al., 2002) or risks security with low entropy channels (Dedić et al., 2009),
723   our watermark remains stealthy regardless of the text’s entropy. This is achieved by leveraging full
724   distribution access and using it as a foundation for embedding watermarks. Kaptchuk et al. (2021)
725   discusses encoding with similar access but presupposes equal decoding access, which is impractical
726   for watermarking as the detection algorithm typically lacks the initiating prompt, thus remaining
727   unaware of the distribution.

729   C    D ISCUSSION
730
731   In this section, we provide detailed discussion of two ”undetectable” scheme (Christ et al., 2023;
732   Christ & Gunn, 2024). We claim neither of them can achieve strongly distortion-free.
733
      For the undetectable scheme proposed by Christ & Gunn (2024), it is important to note that strongly
734
      distortion-free watermarks require the independence of F (PM (xi | x1:i−1 ), ki ) at every generation
735   step i. Existing distortion-free watermarks achieve this by using distinct watermark keys ki and a hash
736   function to ensure independence, which requires the ‘key collision’ not occuring. In contrast, Christ
737   & Gunn (2024) achieves the independence of F (PM (xi | x1:i−1 ), ki ) by developing a key sampling
738   method (termed PRC), which aims for i.i.d. sampling of watermark keys with true randomness, i.e.,
739   replacing the pseudorandomness in the PDA-rule with true randomness by randomly sampling the
740   watermark keys. However, despite these efforts, their method still does not achieve strongly distortion-
741   free watermarks, as PRC methods are only close to, but cannot fully achieve, i.i.d. sampling of true
742   randomness (see Lemma 9 in their paper). Therefore, their method can still not achieve strongly
743
      distortion-free watermarks.
744   The undetectable watermark (Christ et al., 2023) is another example of trading detectability for
745   reducing the distribution bias. In Christ et al. (2023), watermarked tokens are produced only if the
746   hash window has an entropy larger than a given threshold λ, i.e., they skip watermarking the first
747   several tokens to accumulate enough true randomness. However, there is also a trade-off between
748   the watermark strength and the distribution bias under their scheme. This trade-off is controlled by
749
      the entropy threshold λ. When λ increases, the number of watermarked tokens decreases, and it will
      become more difficult to detect the watermark, but the key collision is less likely to occur, and the
750
      distribution bias decreases.
751
752   For example, if we use Hoeffding’s concentration bound as the p-value estimator, i.e., P (Sn −
                                2
753   E[Sn ] > s) ≤ exp(−2 sn ), when the generated sequence length does not change, the p-value
754   upper bound exponentially increases with the number of non-watermarked tokens (because s =
755   Snwatermarked − E[Sn ] is linearly related to the number of watermarked tokens). Thus, although the
      order of hash windows is 2λ , the detectability could be the order O(e−λ ) (assuming λ is linearly


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      related to the number of watermarked tokens), which show a trade-off between the distortion bias and
757   the watermark detectability.
758
759   Besides, the key sampling methods Alg. 3 and 5 of Christ et al. (2023) are also not resilient to even
760
      single text modification. The watermark key space of undetectable watermark is (sk, texture key,
      position key) (see line 12 of Alg.3, line 7 of Alg.5 and the discussion at the second last paragraph of
761
      Section 4.2). The texture key is similar to the definition of n-gram. Since they also use position keys
762
      in their watermark key, a single deletion will remove the watermark.
763
764   Notice, the texture key in Christ et al. (2023) is similar but not equal to the n-gram hashing. In Alg.3,
765   they use the same texture key during one generation. From my perspective, the texture keys in Christ
766
      et al. (2023) are more likely to serve as increasing the diversity of the secret key sk to reduce the
      distribution-bias in “multiple generation with the same prompt”. The diversity of watermark keys in
767
      one generation is ensured by the position key.

1308   We evaluate the distortion-free performance of various watermark models within two seq2seq
1309
       applications: text summarization and text generation. The experiments leverage the Huggingface
       library (Wolf et al., 2019), a popular framework for model development and sharing in the NLP
1310
       community. All tests are conducted on 8 NVIDIA A6000 GPUs, each with 48GB of memory.
1311
1312   We focus on three seq2seq tasks in our experiments: machine translation, text summarization and
1313   text generation. For the machine translation task, we focus on English-to-Romanian translation. We
1314   employ the Multilingual BART (MBart) model (Liu et al., 2020) on the WMT’14 En-Ro corpus. For
1315
       text summarization, we employ the BART-large model (Liu et al., 2020) using the CNN-DM corpus
       dataset (Hermann et al., 2015). For text generation, we follow the settings described by (Kirchenbauer
1316
       et al., 2023), using the LLaMA-2 model (7b, chat) (Touvron et al., 2023) with a random subset
1317
       of the C4 dataset (Raffel et al., 2020). All experiments are conducted with n-gram watermark key
1318   sampling (n = 5). Additionally, we include the Soft watermark (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023) in our
1319   comparison, although it does not achieve step-wise distortion-free performance. Notably, when
1320   β = 0, the Beta-watermark becomes identical to the permute-reweight watermark (Hu et al., 2023).
1321
       Machine Translation. For the machine translation task, we utilize the WMT’14 English (En) to
1322
       Romanian (Ro) dataset, comprising 1,999 examples in the test set. We employ the Multilingual Bart
1323
       (MBart) model (Liu et al., 2020) along with its official tokenizer.
1324
1325   Text Summarization. For text summarization, we utilize the test set from the CNN-DM corpus
1326   (Hermann et al., 2015), which contains 11,490 examples. We employ the BART-large model, which
1327
       has 400 million parameters, and the LLaMA-2 model with 7 billion parameters.
1328   Text Generation. In text generation, we adhere to the experimental setup described in Kirchenbauer
1329   et al. (2023). We use a random subset of the C4 dataset for generation prompts. Our model selection
1330   includes the LLaMA-2, which has 7 billion parameters.
1331   Watermark Setup. Our experiments primarily compare the beta-watermark with three other
1332   distortion-free watermarks: inversa-sampling, Gumbel-reparametrization, and permute-reweight.
1333   Additionally, we include the Soft watermark (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023) in our comparison. For
1334   beta-watermark, we explore various β values from the set {0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3}. For the Soft wa-
1335   termark (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023), we investigate green list bias δ values from {0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0}
1336   with a fixed green list separator γ = 0.5. For n-gram key sampling, we consider the most recent 5
1337   tokens as the texture key. For example, when generating x4 in response to (x1 , x2 , x3 ), the texture
1338   key includes (x1 , x2 , x3 ), given only three tokens are available. Texture key history resets before
1339
       generating each batch. For cipher generation, we use SHA-256 as the hash function and a 1024-bit
       random bitstrings as the secret key sk, the watermark key is given by k = (sk, xi−5,i−1 ). The
1340
       permutation π is sampled using hash(k) as the random seed. We also compare beta-watermark
1341
       with inverse-sampling watermark Kuditipudi et al. (2023) and permute-reweight watermark Hu et al.
1342   (2023); Wu et al. (2023), following the settings in their open-sourced code12 .
