009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we introduce the concept of controllability and observability to the
012            Mamba SSM’s architecture in our Sparse-Mamba (S-Mamba) for natural language
013            processing (NLP) applications. The structured state space model (SSM) develop-
014            ment in recent studies, such as Mamba and Mamba2, outperformed and solved the
015            computational inefficiency of transformers and large language models at small to
016            medium scale. The Mamba SSMs architecture drops the need for attention layers
017            or multilayer perception blocks in transformers. However, current Mamba models
018
               lack reinforcement of controllability in state-space equations for computing the
               A, B, C, and D matrices at each time step, leading to increased complexity and
019
               computational costs. In this paper, we demonstrate a reduction of parameters in
020
               comparison to the first published Mamba and Mamba2. We showcase an improve-
021            ment in perplexity by 5% and a decrease in training time by 3% after reinforcing
022            controllability and observability on the original Mamba architecture in our pro-
023            posed S-Mamba. The controllable n × n state matrix A is sparse and it has only n
024            free parameters. Our novel approach will ensure a controllable system which will
025            be the gate key for Mamba3.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Transformers. In the early stages of natural language processing (NLP), with one of its first studies
031   Hutchins (2005), recurrent neural networks (RNNs) Rumelhart et al. (1986) suffered from explod-
032   ing/vanishing gradients. This case was investigated by Hochreiter in Hochreiter (1998), first dis-
033
      cussed in his thesis in 1991. This study explored four types of solutions including methods which
      do not use gradients, ones that keep gradients on larger values, ones that operate on higher levels,
034
      and ones that use special architectures. This inspired the creation of a gradient-based method Long
035
      short-term memory (LSTM) in Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997), where constant error carrousel
036   was introduced.
037
038   The long sequences in language modeling and generating in an encoder-decoder based architectures
039
      as in RNNs and Generative Adversarial Nets (GANs) Goodfellow et al. (2014) was a main problem.
      In Vaswani (2017), authors revolutionized NLPs with their introduction of transformers. Attention
040
      mechanism was all you need to handle long sequences. The core of a transformer model relays in the
041
      proposed attention equation. Here, Q, K, and V are the query, keys values matrices. W Q , W K , W V
042   are projection matrices for the queries, keys, and values, respectively. W O is the output projection
043   matrix. When these matrices are properly calculated, they form a similarity score in the attention
044   layer that handles longer language modeling tasks more effectively. Further development on trans-
045   formers produced multi-query attention Shazeer (2019) and flash attention (Dao et al. (2022),Dao
046   (2023)).
047
      State Space Models (SSMs).When state space models are discussed, it is often referred to the
048   state space representation and classical state space models introduced by Kalman (1960). Recently,
049   studies attempted to build upon the state space representations in control theory and modeling a
050   dynamic system via state variables to language modeling came as in Gu et al. (2021a). However,
051   in order to make a bridge to language modeling from state space representations, Gu in Gu et al.
052   (2021b) experimented the first known utilization of state space equations appeared as a Linear State-
053   Space Layer (LSSL), where the LSSL maps a sequence input to output using state space equations.
      Unsurprisingly, similar to transformers, these attempts were also inspired by RNNs. In Gu et al.

055   model, and previously LSSLs, was built on core state space representation discussed in control

      Authors of S4 left the idea of expanding the SSM in the coefficient space and started computing

062
063   However, the fundamental challenge in sequence modeling is compressing context into a smaller
064
      state. Popular sequence models, such as Transformers, recurrent models and recent SSMs, illus-
      trate this trade-off. Attention-based models, like Transformers, are highly effective but inefficient
065
      because they avoid compressing context, requiring the storage of the entire sequence during infer-
066
      ence, leading to slow, quadratic-time training. Recurrent models and S4, while more efficient with
067   constant-time inference and linear-time training, struggle with effectiveness due to limited context
068   compression. This challenge is highlighted by tasks like Selective Copying Arjovsky et al. (2016),
069   which requires filtering relevant tokens, and Induction Heads Olsson et al. (2022), which demands
070   context-aware output generation. These tasks reveal the limitations of linear time-invariant (LTI)
071   models, as they lack the capacity for input-dependent dynamics and struggle with varying input-
072   output spacing, a problem static convolution kernels cannot solve.
073   Here, Mamba was introduced in Gu & Dao (2023). The building block of Mamba proposed a class
074   of selective state space models that leveraged the selection mechanism which parameterize the SSM
075   parameters depending on the input. It additionally used a hardware-aware algorithm that computes
076   the model recurrently with a scan not convolution. Here, Mamba overcame the issues of transformers
077   where it showed promising results on handling data that contains long-range dependencies (LRD)s.
078   Inspired by linear attention Katharopoulos et al. (2020), Mamba2 Dao & Gu (2024) was introduced
079   to showcase how SSMs are now competitive and similar and transformers.
080   In this work, we introduce a new family of sparse SSMs based on the fundamental control theory
081   concepts of controllability and observability developed by Kalman in 1960s. In particular, we in-
082   vestigate how vanilla Mamba overlooked important concepts in control theory: controllability and
083   observability. Therefore, we propose a family of Sparse Mamba (S-Mamba) networks where a
084   modification on the architecture of vanilla Mamba can reinforce the system to be in the controller
085   canonical form and in the observable canonical form Bay (1999). Discussed in details in section
086   [3.2,4], S-Mamba outperforms the original Mamba, reduces the number of parameters, and saves
087
      time in training. We start presenting our work by explaining the core structure of SSMs in Section
      [2]. Then, we will explain the building blocks, (A, B, C, D) matrices in particular, of the vanilla
088
      Mamba and our S-Mamba in Section [3]. We evaluate our work in Section [4] and present the results
089
      in Tables [1,2,3].

      5   C ONCLUSION
401
402
      In our paper, we introduce a family of Sparse Mamba (S-Mamba) that reinforces the controllability
403   and observability on the original Mamba model. The controllable n × n state matrix A is sparse and
404   it has only n free parameters. We showcase that our novel architecture has a better performance in
405   terms of perplexity, less training time, and a reduction in the number of parameters in than vanilla
406   Mamba. Our experiments prove a possibility to make any model that is based on state space rep-
407   resentations sparse, including the diagonal structure in Mamba2, by enforcing Controllability and
408   observability in (A, B, C, D) matrices. This will conclude a less complex system for language
409   modeling using SSMs.
