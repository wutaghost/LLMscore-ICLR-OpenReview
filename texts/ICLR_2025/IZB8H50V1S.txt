009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Many dynamic decision problems, such as robotic control, involve a series of
012            tasks, many of which are unknown at training time. Typical approaches for these
013            problems, such as multi-task and meta reinforcement learning, do not generalize
014            well when the tasks are diverse. We propose a general framework to address this
015            issue. In our framework, the goal is to learn a set of policies—a policy commit-
016            tee—such that at least one is near-optimal for most tasks that may be encoun-
017            tered at execution time. While we show that even a special case of this problem
018
               is inapproximable, we present two effective algorithmic approaches for it. The
               first of these yields provably approximation guarantees, albeit in low-dimensional
019
               settings (the best we can do due to inapproximability), whereas the second is a
020
               general and practical gradient-based approach. In addition, we provide provable
021            sample complexity bounds for few-shot learning settings. Our experiments in per-
022            sonalized and multi-task RL settings on MuJoCo and Meta-World show that the
023            proposed approach outperforms state-of-the-art multi-task, meta-, and personal-
024            ized RL baselines on training and test tasks, as well as in few-shot learning, often
025            by a large margin.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Reinforcement learning (RL) has achieved remarkable success in a variety of domains, from robotic
031   control (Lillicrap, 2015) to game playing (Xu et al., 2018). However, many real-world applications
032   involve highly diverse sets of tasks, making it impractical to rely on a single, fixed policy. In these
033
      settings, both the reward structures and the transition dynamics can vary significantly across tasks.
      Existing approaches to this challenge—such as multi-task RL (MTRL) and meta-reinforcement
034
      learning (meta-RL)—struggle to generalize effectively when tasks are both diverse and previously
035
      unseen.
036
037   Multi-task RL methods typically train a single policy or a shared representation across tasks (Vithay-
038   athil Varghese & Mahmoud, 2020). However, they often face negative transfer, where optimizing
039
      for one task degrades performance on others (Zhang et al., 2022). This limitation becomes more
      pronounced when tasks require drastically different strategies, as the policy is forced to handle con-
040
      flicting objectives. On the other hand, meta-RL approaches, such as Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning
041
      (MAML) (Finn et al., 2017b) and PEARL (Rakelly et al., 2019), aim to enable fast adaptation to
042   new tasks but rely heavily on fine-tuning at test time, which can be computationally expensive and
043   ineffective in environments with high variability in both rewards and transitions, like Meta-World
044   benchmark tasks (Yu et al., 2020c). Furthermore, these methods typically underperform on the train-
045   ing tasks compared to MTRL due to the generalization trade-off inherent in meta-learning. Another
046   promising direction is personalized RL (Ivanov & Ben-Porat, 2024), where multiple policies are
047   trained to cater to diverse reward structures, but this line of work focuses predominantly on rewards
048   while assuming shared transition dynamics.
049   According to script theory, human cognition enables effective generalization in an open world by
050   learning a collection of scripts, or behavioral patterns (Abelson, 1981; Schank, 1983), one of which
051   can then be selected and adapted as needed to particular predicaments. Inspired by this concept,
052   we propose a novel policy committee framework designed to efficiently handle environments with
053   diverse task distributions, where both reward functions and transition dynamics can vary signif-
      icantly across tasks. Instead of learning a single policy or relying on complex fine-tuning, our


                                                        1

      framework learns a set of policies—a committee—where each policy is specialized to handle a spe-
055   cific subset of tasks. This allows for task-specific expertise while maintaining generalization across
056   a wide range of task variations. We refer to our approach as PACMAN.

058
      To summarize, our key contributions are as follows:
059   • Simple and Effective Policy Committees: Our policy committee approach scales efficiently with
060     task diversity. By learning a small set of policies that cover a broad range of tasks, we reduce the
061     computational complexity compared to methods that require a separate policy for each task or
062     complex adaptation procedures. Additionally, our approach leverages LLM-based task embed-
063
        dings for non-parametric tasks, which provides a more general and scalable solution to environ-
        ments where tasks cannot be easily parameterized.
064
      • Theoretical Analysis: We first provide a general computational impossibility result, showing
065
        that even the problem of identifying the optimal sets of tasks for policy committee training is
066     inapproximable. However, we also present an efficient algorithmic approach with worst-case ap-
067     proximation guarantees in the special case when task embedding dimension is constant, and a
068     general gradient-based approach, albeit with weaker guarantees. Finally, we theoretically demon-
069     strate few-shot efficacy of our approach by showing that it has sample complexity that is linear in
070     the size of the committee and independent of the size of the state and action space.
071   • Empirical Validation: We demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed PACMAN approach through
072     extensive experiments on challenging multi-task benchmarks, including MuJoCo and Meta-
073     World. Our policy committee framework consistently outperforms state-of-the-art multi-task RL
074
        and meta-RL baselines in both zero-shot and few-shot settings, achieving better generalization
        and faster adaptation across diverse tasks.
075
076   Related Work: Our work is closely related to three key areas within the broader reinforcement
077   learning literature: multi-task RL, personalized RL, and meta-RL.
078   Multi-Task RL (MTRL): A major advantage of MTRL over single-task learning is the ability to
079   share knowledge across tasks, a concept extensively explored in various studies proposing different
080   methods to utilize task relationships (Yang et al., 2020b; Sodhani et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2022).
081   However, naive knowledge-sharing across tasks can lead to negative transfer, as not all tasks benefit
082   from shared knowledge. Consequently, learning a task-specific skill may distract from the learning
083   of other tasks. A notable area of research examines task interference in MTRL through the lens
084   of gradient alignment. Yu et al. (2020a) tackles it by projecting the gradient of a task to the or-
085
      thogonal direction of all the other tasks, while Hessel et al. (2019) addresses it via synchronizing
      the gradient magnitude across tasks. Numerous methods in the literature aim to address task inter-
086
      ference issues from a representation learning perspective. Sodhani et al. (2021) learn a mixture of
087
      state encoders shared across tasks, that helps generate diverse representations through an attention
088   mechanism. Lan et al. (2024) introduce the Contrastive Modules with Temporal Attention (CMTA)
089   framework, which leverages contrastive learning to ensure the modules are distinct from one an-
090   other and integrates shared modules at a finer granularity than the task level using temporal atten-
091   tion. Recently, Hendawy et al. (2023) proposed an approach called Mixture of Orthogonal Experts
092   (MOORE) that captures common structures among tasks by employing orthogonal representations
093   to enhance diversity. MOORE utilizes a Gram-Schmidt process to create a shared subspace of rep-
094   resentations derived from a mixture of experts. While all these previous MTRL approaches focus on
095   learning a policy to efficiently address a predefined set of tasks, our focus is to learn a set of policies
096
      such that at least one policy in the set is near-optimal for most previously unseen tasks.
097   Personalized-RL: Recently, Ivanov & Ben-Porat (2024) introduced personalized RL, to accommo-
098   date a diverse user population, each with distinct preferences, through interaction with a small set
099   of representative policies. Although the personalized RL framework has some similarities to our
100   approach, we adopt a broader setup allowing for variations both in rewards and transition dynamics,
101   since many real-world scenarios warrant mastering a diverse set of tasks that comprise different dy-
102
      namics. Moreover, we empirically show that PACMAN significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art
      personalized RL method across diverse evaluation settings even when only rewards vary.
103
104   Meta-RL: Meta-RL methods can be categorized broadly into two categories, (i) context-based; and
105   (ii) gradient-based. Context-based methods primarily rely on learning a context (Bing et al., 2023;
106   Gupta et al., 2018; Duan et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2020a;b; 2023; Rakelly et al., 2019) by employing
107   RNN or LSTM-based neural networks to encode collected experiences into a latent context embed-
      ding, and then act by conditioning the policy on the learned context. However, they are susceptible


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to distribution shifts at inference time, as the encoded context and the policy derived from that con-
109   text often struggle to generalize to out-of-distribution tasks. Additionally, the parameters of the
110   latent context encoder are trained to predict reward and/or transition dynamics based on the con-
111   text, typically involving the minimization of a KL divergence-based loss. Consequently, the learned
112   context tends to exhibit mode-seeking behavior, which poses a significant limitation in situations
113   that require capturing diverse, multi-modal context (such as in Meta-World). Recently, Bing et al.
114   (2023) attempt to address this issue in non-parametric tasks by using task-specific detailed natu-
115   ral language instructions. Several gradient-based methods (Finn et al., 2017b; Stadie et al., 2018;
116   Mendonca et al., 2019; Zintgraf et al., 2019) have been developed to address the few-shot adapta-
117
      tion challenge. These approaches focus on learning a shared initialization of a model across tasks,
      allowing the agent to achieve strong performance on unseen target tasks with only a few gradient
118
      updates. These approaches are not well-suited for zero-shot generalization problems, as they typ-
119
      ically require numerous gradient steps through the policy to learn an effective policy for a given
120   task. Finally, since meta-RL methods prioritize rapid adaptation, they often fall short of state-of-
121   the-art MTRL performance on in-sample (training) tasks. In this work, we aim to close this gap by
122   developing a framework that excels in both in-sample and out-of-sample tasks.

      Definition 1. A policy committee Π is an (ϵ, 1 − δ)-cover for a task distribution Γ if VτΠ ≥ Vτ∗ − ϵ
163   with probability at least 1−δ with respect to Γ. Π is an (ϵ, 1−δ)-cover for a set of tasks {τ1 , . . . , τn }

165
166   Clearly, an (ϵ, 1 − δ) cover need not exist for an arbitrary committee Π (if the committee is too small
167   to cover enough tasks sufficiently well). There are, however, three knobs that we can adjust: K, ϵ,
168   and δ. Next, we fix ϵ as exogenous, treating it effectively as a domain-specific hyperparameter, and
169   suppose that K is a pre-specified bound on the maximum size of the committee.
170   Problem 1. Fix the maximum committee size K and ϵ. Our goal is to find Π which is a (ϵ, 1 − δ)-
171   cover for the smallest δ ∈ [0, 1].

174
      experimental results, vindicate this choice of the objective.

176   3     A LGORITHMS FOR L EARNING A P OLICY C OMMITTEE
177
178   In this section, we present algorithmic approaches for computing policy committees Π to solve
179   Problems 1. We consider the special case of the problem in which the tasks have a structure rep-
180   resentation. Specifically, we assume that each task can be represented using a parametric model
181   ψθ (s, a), where the parameters θ ∈ Rd comprise both of the parameters of the transition distribution
182   T and reward function r. Often, parametric task representation is given or direct; in cases when tasks
183
      are non-parametric, such as the Meta-World (Yu et al., 2020b), we can often use approaches for task
      embedding, such as LLM-based task representations (see Section 3.4). Consequently, we identify
184
      tasks τ with their representation parameters θ throughout, and overload Γ to mean the distribution
185
      over task parameters, i.e., θ ∼ Γ.

      3.1   A H IGH -L EVEL A LGORITHMIC F RAMEWORK
188
189   Even conventional RL presents a practical challenge in complex problems, as learning is typically
190   time consuming and requires extensive hyperparameter tuning. Consequently, a crucial considera-
191   tion in algorithm design is to minimize the number of RL runs we need to obtain a policy committee.
192   To this end, we propose the following high-level algorithmic framework in which we only need K
193   independent (and, thus, entirely parallelizable) RL runs. This framework involves three steps:
194
195         1. S AMPLE n tasks i.i.d. from Γ, obtaining T = {θ1 , . . . , θn } (parameters of associated tasks
196
               {τ1 , . . . , τn }). In MTRL settings, T is given.
197         2. C LUSTER the task set T into K subsets, each with an associated representative θk , and
198         3. T RAIN policies πk for each cluster k represented by θk .
199
200   As we shall see presently (and demonstrate experimentally in both Subsection 5.3 and Ap-
201   pendix G.2), conventional clustering approaches are not ideally suited for our problem. We thus
202   propose several alternative approaches which yield theoretical guarantees on the quality of Π under
203   mild conditions if all tasks share the transition dynamics and only differ in reward function. Empir-
204
      ically, we show that the proposed framework outperforms state of the art even when tasks also have
      distinct transition distributions.

214   reward. While this is a theoretical limitation, we note that our subsequent clustering and training
215   algorithms do not in themselves require this assumption, and our experimental results demonstrate
      that the overall approach is effective generally.

      3.3   T RAINING
341
342   The output of the C LUSTERING step above is a set of representative task parameters C =
343   {θ1 , . . . , θK }. The simplest way to use these to obtain a policy committee Π is to train a policy
344   πk optimized for each θk ∈ C. However, this ignores the set of tasks that comprise each cluster
345   k associated with a representative θk (i.e., the set of tasks closest to θk ). As demonstrated empiri-
346   cally in the multi-task RL literature, using multiple tasks to learn a shared representation facilitates
347   generalization (effectively enabling the model to learn features that are beneficial to all tasks in the
348   cluster) (Sodhani et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2020b).
349   To address this, we propose an alternative which trains a policy πk to maximize the sum of rewards of
350   the tasks in cluster k. Notably, our approach can use any RL algorithm to learn a policy associated
351   with a cluster of tasks; in the experiments below, we use the most effective MTRL or meta-RL
352   baseline for this purpose.
353
354   3.4   D EALING WITH N ON -PARAMETRIC TASKS
355
356   Our approach assumes that tasks are parametric, so that we can reason (particularly in the clustering
357   step) about parameter similarity. Many practical multi-task settings, however, are non-parametric,
358   so that our algorithmic framework cannot be applied directly. In such cases, our approach can make
359   use of any available method for extracting a parametric representation of an arbitrary task τ . For
360
      example, it is often the case that tasks can be either described in natural language. We propose
      to leverage this property and use text embedding (e.g., from pretrained LLMs) as the parametric
361
      representation of otherwise non-parametric tasks, where this is feasible. Our hypothesis is that this
362
      embedding captures the most relevant semantic aspects of many tasks in practice, a hypothesis that
363   our results below validate in the context of the Meta-World benchmark. This is analogous to what
364   was done by Bing et al. (2023), with the main difference being that our task descriptions are with
365   respect to higher-level goals, whereas Bing et al. (2023) describe tasks in terms of associated plans.
366   We provide the full list of task descriptions for the Meta-World environment in Appendix H.

392   5     E XPERIMENTS
393
394   We study the effectiveness of our approach—PACMAN—in two environments, MuJoCo (Todorov
395   et al., 2012) and Meta-World (Yu et al., 2020b). In the former, the tasks are low-dimensional and
396   parametric, and we only vary the reward functions, whereas the latter has non-parametric robotic
397   manipulation tasks with varying reward and transition dynamics.
398
      MuJoCo We selected two commonly used MuJoCo environments. The first is HalfCheetahVel
399
      where the agent has to run at different velocities, and rewards are based on the distance to a target
400   velocity. The second is HumanoidDir where the agent has to move along the preferred direction, and
401   the reward is the distance to the target direction. In both, we generate diverse rewards by randomly
402   generating target velocity and direction, respectively, and use 100 tasks for training and another 100
403   for testing (in both zero-shot and few-shot settings), with parameters generated from a Gaussian
404   mixture model with 5 Gaussians. In few-shot cases, we draw a single task for fine-tuning, and
405   average the result over 10 tasks. For clustering, we use K = 3, ϵ = .6, and use the gradient-based
406   approach initialized with the result of the Greedy Intersection algorithm. For few-shot learning, we
407   fine-tune all methods for 100 epochs.
408   Meta-World It is a well-known multitask and meta-learning benchmark (Yu et al., 2020b). We
409   focus on the set of robotic manipulation tasks in MT50, of which we use 30 for training and 20 for
410   testing. This makes the learning problem significantly more challenging than typical in prior MTRL
411   and meta-RL work, where training sets are much larger compared to test sets (5 tests and 40 trains
412   in the traditional MT45 setting). We leverage an LLM to generate a parameterization (Section 3.4)
413   of the task. Specifically, text descriptions are fed to “Phi-3 Mini-128k Instruct” (Microsoft, 2024)
414
      and we compute the channel-wise mean over the features of penultimate layer as a 50 dimensional
      parameterization for each task. We use K = 3 and ϵ = .7, which allows us to obtain an (ϵ, 1)-
415
      parameter-cover for the set of training tasks in terms of ℓ∞ norm with respect to the LLM-based
416
      task embedding.

      5.1   BASELINES AND E VALUATION
419
420   We compare our approaches to 10 state-of-the-art baselines. Five of these are designed for MTRL:
421   1) CMTA (Lan et al., 2024), 2) MOORE Hendawy et al. (2023), 3) CARE (Sodhani et al., 2021), 4)
422   soft modulation (Soft) (Yang et al., 2020a), and 5) Multi task SAC (Yu et al., 2020b). Four more are
423   meta-RL algorithms: 1) MAML (Finn et al., 2017a), 2) RL2 (Duan et al., 2017), 3) PEARL (Rakelly
424   et al., 2019), 4) VariBAD (Zintgraf et al., 2020), and AMAGO (Grigsby et al., 2024). Finally, we
425   also compare to the personalized RL approach using EM to learn a policy committee (EM) (Ivanov
426   & Ben-Porat, 2024).
427   Our evaluation involves three settings: training, zero-shot test, and few-shot test. The training eval-
428   uation corresponds to standard MTRL. The zero-shot test evaluation uses the test set to evaluate
429   all approaches but with no fine-tuning. Finally, our few-shot test evaluation allows a short round
430   of fine-tuning on the test data. For PACMAN we select the best-performing policy for training and
431   zero-shot test and use the proposed few-shot approach to learn the best policy through empirical
      policy evaluation for the few-shot test setting (see Section 4).

      6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
530
531
      We developed a general algorithmic framework for learning policy committees for effective gen-
532
      eralization and few-shot learning in multi-task settings with diverse tasks that may be unknown at
533
      training time. We showed that our approach is theoretically grounded, and outperforms MTRL,
534   meta-RL, and personalized RL baselines in both training, and zero-shot and few-shot test evalua-
535   tions, often by a large margin. Nevertheless, our approach exhibits several important limitations.
536   First, it requires tasks to be parametric, and while we demonstrate how LLMs can be used to effec-
537   tively obtain task embeddings in the Meta-World environments, it is not clear how to do so generally.
538   Second, it includes a scalar hyperparameter, ϵ, which determines how we evaluate the quality of task
539   coverage and needs to be adjusted separately for each environment, although this hyperparameter is
      easily tunable in practice.
