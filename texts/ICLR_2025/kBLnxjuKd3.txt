009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            While diffusion models can generate high-quality images through the probability
012            flow process, the theoretical understanding of this process is incomplete. A key
013            open question is determining when the probability flow converges to the training
014            samples used for denoiser training and when it converges to more general points on
015            the data manifold. To address this, we analyze the probability flow of shallow ReLU
016            neural network denoisers which interpolate the training data and have a minimal
017            ℓ2 norm of the weights. For intuition, we also examine a simpler dynamics which
018
               we call the score flow, and demonstrate that, in the case of orthogonal datasets, the
               score flow and probability flow follow similar trajectories. Both flows converge
019
               to a training point or a sum of training points. However, due to early stopping
020
               induced by the scheduler, the probability flow can also converge to a general point
021            on the data manifold. This result aligns with empirical observations that diffusion
022            models tend to memorize individual training examples and reproduce them during
023            testing. Moreover, diffusion models can combine memorized foreground and
024            background objects, indicating they can learn a "semantic sum" of training points.
025            We generalize these results from the orthogonal dataset case to scenarios where
026            the clean data points lie on an obtuse simplex. Simulations further confirm that
027            the probability flow converges to one of the following: a training point, a sum of
028            training points, or a point on the data manifold.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b), new images
      are sampled from the data distribution through an iterative process. Beginning with a random
034
      initialization, the model gradually denoises the image until a final image emerges. At their core,
035
      diffusion models learn the data distribution by estimating the score function of a Gaussian-blurred
036   version of the data distribution. The connection between the score function and the denoiser, often
037   called Tweedie’s identity (Robbins, 1956; Miyasawa et al., 1961; Stein, 1981), holds only under
038   optimal Bayes estimation. Moreover, for the estimated score to be a true gradient field, the denoiser
039   must have a symmetric positive semidefinite Jacobian matrix (Chao et al., 2023; Manor & Michaeli,
040   2024). However, in practice, neural network denoisers are used, and their Jacobian matrix is generally
041   non-symmetric, raising open questions about the convergence of the sampling process in score-based
042   diffusion algorithms.
043   Diffusion models typically use a stochastic sampling process, which can be described by a stochastic
044   differential equation (SDE) (Song et al., 2021b). Alternatively, a deterministic version of the sampling
045   process can also be used, formulated as an ordinary differential equation (ODE) (Song et al., 2021a),
046   called the probability flow ODE. We aim to theoretically analyze the probability flow, in order to
047   illuminate this complex sampling process. However, practical diffusion architectures are typically
048   deep and not fully connected, making it difficult to obtain theoretical guarantees without making
049   additional strong assumptions (e.g., assuming a linearized regime like the neural tangent kernel (Jacot
050
      et al., 2018)). Therefore, in this paper we focus on diffusion models based on shallow ReLU neural
      network denoisers. These are both simple enough to allow for a theoretical investigation and rich
051
      enough to offer valuable insights.
052
053   To gain intuition into the dynamics of the probability flow ODE, we also explore a simpler ODE
      that corresponds to flowing in the direction of the score of the noisy data distribution, for a fixed


                                                         1

054
      noise-level. We call this the score-flow ODE. The score flow aims to sample from one of the modes
055   of the noise-perturbed data distribution. We explore both the probability flow and the score flow
056   ODEs for denoisers with minimal representation cost that perfectly fit the training data. Our analysis
057   reveals that, for small noise levels, the trajectories of both flows is the same for a given initialization.
058   However, the scheduler induces “early stopping”, which determines whether the probability flow
059   converges to training samples or to other points on the data manifold. This analysis provides insights
060   into the stability and convergence properties of these processes.
061
062   Our contributions We investigate the probability and the score flow of shallow ReLU neural
063   network denoisers in the context of interpolating noisy samples with minimal cost, specifically in the
064
      “low-noise regime”, where noisy samples are well clustered.
065          • Theoretical: We prove that when the clean training points are orthogonal to one another,
066            the probability flow and score flow follow a similar trajectory for a given initialization
067            point. However, while the score flow converges only to a training point or to a sum of
068            training points, the probability flow can also converge to a point on the boundary of the
069            hyperbox whose vertices are all partial sums of the training points. This happens due to
070            “early stopping” induced by the scheduler. We generalize this result to the case where the
071            training points are the vertices of an obtuse simplex.
072          • Experimental: We train shallow denoisers that interpolate the training data with minimal
073            representation cost on orthogonal datasets. We start by empirically demonstrating that the
074            score flow ODE corresponding to a single such denoiser typically converges either to a sum
075            of training points, which we call virtual training points, or to a general point on the boundary
076            of the hyperbox (it converges to a training point only in rare occasions). We then show that
077
               the probability flow ODE, which uses a sequence of denoisers for varying noise levels, also
               converges to virtual points and to the boundary of the hyperbox, albeit at a somewhat lower
078
               frequency compared to the training points.

180

181

482   6          R ELATED WORK
483
484   Memorization and Generalization in Deep Generative Models Several recent works have sought
485   to explain the transition from memorization to generalization in deep generative models, both from
      a theoretical and empirical perspective. One early line of work in this vein studied memorization


                                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      in over-parametrized (non-denoising) autoencoders (Radhakrishnan et al., 2019; 2020). This work
487   shows that over-parameterized autoencoders trained to low cost are locally contractive about each
488   training sample, such that training images can be recovered by iteratively applying the autoencoder to
489   noisy inputs. A theoretical explanation of this phenomenon using a neural tangent kernel analysis is
490   given in (Jiang & Pehlevan, 2020). More recent work has also shown that state-of-the-art diffusion
491   models exhibit a similar form of memorization, such that extraction of training samples is possible
492   by identifying stable stationary points of the diffusion process (Carlini et al., 2023). Additionally,
493   when trained on few images, several works have shown that the outputs of diffusion models are
494   strongly biased towards the training set, and thus fail to generalize (Somepalli et al., 2023; Yoon
495
      et al., 2023; Kadkhodaie et al., 2024). A recent empirical study suggests that memorization and
      generalization in diffusion models are mutually exclusive phenomenon, and successful generation
496
      occurs only when memorization fails (Yoon et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). Beyond these empirical
497
      studies, recent work has put forward theoretical explanations for generalization in score-based models.
498   In (Pidstrigach, 2022), the authors show that score-based models can learn manifold structure in the
499   data generating distribution. A complementary perspective is provided by Kadkhodaie et al. (2024),
500   which argues that diffusion models implicitly encode geometry-adaptive harmonic representations.
501
502
503   Representation costs and neural network denoisers Several other works have investigated
504   overparameterized autoencoding/denoising networks with minimal representation cost (i.e., minimial
505
      ℓ2 -norm of parameters). Function space characterizations of min-norm solutions of shallow fully
      connected neural networks are given in (Savarese et al., 2019; Ongie et al., 2020; Parhi & Nowak,
506
      2021; Shenouda et al., 2023); extensions to deep networks and emergent bottleneck structure are
507
      considered in (Jacot, 2022; Jacot et al., 2022; Jacot, 2023; Wen & Jacot, 2024). The present work
508   relies on the shallow min-norm solutions derived by Zeno et al. (2023) for specific configurations of
509   data points, but goes beyond this work in studying the dynamics of its associated flows.
510
511
      A recent study investigates properties of shallow min-norm solutions to a score matching objective
      (Zhang & Pilanci, 2024), building off of a line of work that studies min-norm solutions from a
512
      convex optimization perspective (Pilanci & Ergen, 2020; Ergen & Pilanci, 2020; Sahiner et al.,
513
      2021; Wang & Pilanci, 2021). In the case of univariate data, an explicit min-norm solution of the
514   score-matching objective is derived, and convergence results are given for Langevin sampling with
515   the neural network-learned score function. Additionally, in the multivariate case, general min-norm
516   solutions to the score-matching loss are characterized as minimizers of a quadratic program. Our
517   results differ from (Zhang & Pilanci, 2024) in that we study different optimization formulations
518   (denoising loss versus score-matching loss) and inference procedures (probability- and score-flow
519   versus Langevin dynamics). Our results focus on high-dimensional data belonging to a simplex,
520   while Zhang & Pilanci (2024) give convergence guarantees only in the case of univariate data.
521
522
523   7   D ISCUSSION
524
525   Conclusions. We explored the probability flow ODE of shallow neural networks with minimal
526   representation cost. We showed that for orthogonal dataset and obtuse-angle dataset the probability
527   flow and the score flow follows the same trajectory given the same initialization point and small noise
528   level. The scheduler in probability flow induces “early stopping”, which results in converging to a
529   boundary point instead of a specific vertex (as in score flow) or speed up convergence to a specific
530   vertex. One possible extension of this work is to analyze the probability flow ODE in the case of
531   variance-preserving processes. This is an important case since practical diffusion models more often
532
      use variance-preserving forward and backward processes.
533
534   Limitations. A key limitation of our analysis is the assumption (inherited from Zeno et al. (2023))
535   that the denoiser interpolates data across a full d-dimensional ball centered around each clean training
536   sample, where d represents the input dimension. In real-world scenarios, the number of noisy samples
537   is typically smaller than the input dimension d. A more accurate approach might involve assuming that
538   the denoiser interpolates over an (M − 1)-dimensional disc around each training sample, reflecting
539   the norm concentration of Gaussian noise in high-dimensional spaces. Furthermore, for mathematical
      tractability, our analysis focuses on a single hidden layer model.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      This paper presents a theoretical analysis of diffusion models under specific constraints, aiming
543   to enhance the understanding of generative models. This may lead to greater transparency when
544   using these models. Moreover, we anticipate that insights gained from these simpler cases will shed
545   light on the memorization and generalization behaviors in large-scale diffusion models, which pose
546   privacy concerns. Lastly, we note the neural network examined in this paper is a shallow one, whereas
547   practical contemporary implementations almost always involve deep networks. Naturally, addressing
548   deep networks from the outset would pose an impassable barrier. In general, our guiding principle for
549   research works that aim to understand new or not-yet-understood phenomena is that we should first
550   study it in the simplest model that shows it, so as not to get distracted by possible confounders, and
551
      to enable a detailed analytic understanding. For example, when exploring or teaching a statistical
      problem issues, we would typically start with linear regression, understand the phenomena in this
552
      simple case, and then move on to more complex models. Thus, we hope the example we set in this
553
      paper will help promote this guiding principle for research and teaching.

1388   The effect of the training set size has been explored in several past works (Somepalli et al., 2023;
1389
       Kadkhodaie et al., 2024), as explored in detail in Section 6. Here we continue the analysis from
       Section 5 to investigate the effect of changing N , the training set size, on the full dynamics of the
1390
       diffusion process with the probability ODE. Specifically, we repeat the experiment from Section 5
1391
       while reducing N . All the hyperparameters are kept the same, except for M which we increase to
1392   2000 for N = 10 only, to prevent over-fitting in the large-noise regime. Figure 4 shows the percentage
1393   of points that converged within an L∞ distance of 0.2 to either virtual points, training points, or a
1394   boundary of the hyperbox, for the different N values. The generalization increases with N , drawing
1395   a larger percentage of samples to converge in the vicinity of virtual points, or to boundaries of the
1396   hyperbox. This aligns with the results of Kadkhodaie et al. (2024).
1397
       When considering the effect of oversampling duplications, previous works observed that diffusion
1398   models tend to overfit more to duplicate training points than to other training points (Somepalli
1399   et al., 2023). However, here we study the regime in which the model perfectly fits all the training
1400   points. In practice, if duplicate training points would cause the neural network to fit them better, at
1401   the expense of the other training points. Then, we expect our analysis to effectively hold, but only for
1402   the training points that are well-fitted and their associated virtual points. Therefore, this mirrors the
1403   case of decreasing N , and will cause more convergence to the duplicated training points and increase
       memorization.
