009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The success of Large Language Models (LLM) has led researchers to explore Mul-
012            timodal Large Language Models (MLLM) for unified visual and linguistic under-
013            standing. However, the increasing model size and computational complexity of
014            MLLM limit their use in resource-constrained environments. Small-scale MLLM
015            (s-MLLM) aims to retain the capabilities of the large-scale model (l-MLLM)
016            while reducing computational demands, but resulting in a significant decline in
017            performance. To address the aforementioned issues, we propose a novel LLaVA-
018
               KD framework to transfer knowledge from l-MLLM to s-MLLM. Specifically, we
               introduce Multimodal Distillation (MDist) to minimize the divergence between
019
               the visual-textual output distributions of l-MLLM and s-MLLM, and Relation
020
               Distillation (RDist) to transfer l-MLLM’s ability to model correlations between
021            visual features. Additionally, we propose a three-stage training scheme to fully
022            exploit the potential of s-MLLM: 1) Distilled Pre-Training to align visual-textual
023            representations, 2) Supervised Fine-Tuning to equip the model with multimodal
024            understanding, and 3) Distilled Fine-Tuning to further transfer l-MLLM capabili-
025            ties. Our approach significantly improves performance without altering the small
026            model’s architecture. Extensive experiments and ablation studies validate the ef-
027            fectiveness of each proposed component. Code will be available.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Inspired by the significant achievements of Large Language Models (LLM) in the field of Natural
033   Language Processing, an emerging and rapidly developing research area is focusing on the devel-
034
      opment of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLM). These models integrate visual encoder,
      feature projector, and LLM to achieve a unified understanding of visual and linguistic information.
035
      However, the success of LLMs benefits from the scaling law, which significantly increases the model
036
      size. The large-scale model and high-cost inference limit the application of MLLMs in resource-
037   constrained scenarios. To solve this challenging problem, some studies (Zhu et al., 2024; Chu et al.,
038   2023) have attempted to reduce model scale by directly adopting lightweight LLMs, but this re-
039   duction often comes with a significant decline in model performance. Some methods compensate
040   for this issue by optimizing model structure and improving the quality of training data, e.g., MoE-
041   LLaVA (Lin et al., 2024) introduces the Mixture-of-Experts (Jacobs et al., 1991) (MoE) to enhance
042   the model’s ability for complex multimodal information while maintaining the computational cost
043   of the lightweight LLM, and Bunny (He et al., 2024) improves the training data quality by removing
044   redundant data. Unlike these methods, we explore improving the performance of the small-scale
045
      MLLM (s-MLLM) from the perspective of investigating various training strategies without alter-
      ing the model architecture. As shown in Fig. 1(a), current s-MLLM follow the two-stage training
046
      strategy of the large-scale MLLM (l-MLLM), which includes Pre-Training (PT) and Supervised
047
      Fine-Tuning (SFT). The PT stage is used to project visual features to the text embedding space,
048   while the SFT stage is used to enhance the model’s understanding and reasoning capabilities. How-
049   ever, due to the limited model capacity, using the same training strategy as l-MLLM may prevent
050   s-MLLM from effectively learning the complex knowledge that l-MLLM can capture (Kaplan et al.,
051   2020). Knowledge distillation, as a model compression technique, has proven its effectiveness in
052   traditional visual tasks. However, the application of knowledge distillation to MLLM has not been
053   fully explored. In this paper, we investigate how knowledge distillation can be leveraged to improve
      the training of s-MLLM.

068   Essentially, MLLM leverages LLM for multimodal information understanding and reasoning.
069
      Therefore, the core of distillation in MLLM involves transferring multimodal information from the
      l-MLLM to the s-MLLM based on LLM. Previous research on LLM distillation (Gu et al., 2024;
070
      Ko et al., 2024) primarily employs the standard Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KLD) to minimize
071
      the discrepancy in output distributions of responses between the l-MLLM and s-MLLM, thereby
072   promoting the s-MLLM to obtain more accurate responses. However, in the context of MLLM, ef-
073   fective visual representations can promote the multimodal information understanding, thereby fur-
074   ther improving the quality of responses. Therefore, we extend the distillation process to include
075   the visual distribution, using KLD to minimize discrepancies in both visual and language modali-
076   ties. Furthermore, to enhance the s-MLLM’s ability to model the contextual relationships of visual
077   representations, we introduce Relation Distillation (RDist). This technique transfers the l-MLLM’s
078   ability to model the correlations between visual representations to the s-MLLM. By distilling mul-
079   timodal information from both visual and language modalities (MDist) and incorporating RDist, we
080
      can achieve a more comprehensive and effective multimodal knowledge transfer.
081   In the common PT-SFT two-stage training scheme, MLLM primary acquires the understanding ca-
082   pacity through the SFT stage. Therefore, a straightforward approach is to introduce knowledge
083   distillation during the SFT stage, to enhance s-MLLM’s capacities. However, we find this scheme
084   to be suboptimal. In this paper, we propose an improved three-stage training strategy, as shown
085   in Fig. 1(b). Firstly, in MLLM, aligning the visual representation with textual representation is a
086
      prerequisite for multimodal information understanding. To promote this goal, we propose a novel
      approach, incorporating the distillation during the PT stage, utilizing l-MLLM to guide the predic-
087
      tions of s-MLLM. In this way, s-MLLM not only improves the accuracy of predictions but also
088
      further optimizes the alignment between visual and language modalities. Secondly, we observe that
089   applying knowledge distillation at the SFT stage is insufficient for the s-MLLM to fully acquire the
090   capabilities of the l-MLLM. To address this, we introduce a “SFT-DFT” shceme. Specifically, we
091   first initilize the s-MLLM with understanding and reasoning capabilities through SFT. Subsequently,
092   we use DFT to achieve the transfer of capabilities from l-MLLM to s-MLLM.
093
      Compared to the current advancements in s-MLLM, our method exhibits impressive performance in
094
      various multimodal benchmarks. For instance, as illustrated in Fig. 1(c), LLaVA-KD-2B compre-
095   hensively outperforms recent s-MLLMs such as Imp (Shao et al., 2024), Bunny (He et al., 2024),
096   and TinyLLava (Zhou et al., 2024). We summarize our contributions as follows:
097
098         • We introduce LLaVA-KD, a novel MLLM-oriented distillation framework to transfers the
099           knowledge from large-scale MLLM to the small-scale MLLM. Specifically, it contains
100           a three-stage distillation scheme, including Distilled Pre-Training (DPT) to enhance the
101           multimodal alignment process, as well as Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Distilled Fine-
102
              Tuning (DFT) to effectively transfer capacities from the large to small MLLM.
103         • We propose an innovative distillation strategy that combines Multimodal Distillation
104           (MDist) with Relational Distillation (RDist). Both them are used in the DPT and DFT
105           stages to enhance the ability of s-MLLMs to process complex visual information.
106         • We demonstrate the superiority and efficiency of LLaVA-KD. Our model significantly sur-
107           passes the recent small-scale MLLM advancements such as Imp and Bunny on nine popular
              multimodal benchmarks.

      2.1   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
111
112   With the development of LLM, researchers have turned their attention to MLLM to promote the un-
113   derstanding of vision-language cross-modal information. BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a) trains a Querying
114   Transformer through various image-text tasks to bridge the modality gap. Flamingo (Alayrac et al.,
115   2022) integrates visual features into LLM through gated attention. Recent methods (Liu et al.,
116   2024b;a; Bai et al., 2023) align visual features with textual features through a projector such as
117   Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) or Q-Former (Li et al., 2023a). Then they will enhance the model’s
118
      instruction-following ability through supervised instruction-tuning, making MLLMs better meet hu-
      man needs. One research trend is to further enhance the fine-grained visual perception ability of
119
      MLLM by enabling the model to support high-resolution inputs (Li et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024),
120
      so that MLLMs can be widely applied to various downstream tasks such as image segmentation and
121   grounding. Although the aforementioned methods have shown great potential in visual understand-
122   ing tasks, their large model size and computational cost greatly limit the application of the model in
123   resource-constrained scenarios, such as mobile devices.
124
125   Lightweight Multimodal Large Language Model Existing lightweight MLLMs mainly reduce
126   model parameters by employing lightweight LLMs. For example, LLava-Phi (Zhu et al., 2024) fol-
127   lows the model structure of LLaVA1.5 (Liu et al., 2024a) and replaces LLMs with the lightweight
128   Phi-2; Some work has shown that optimizing model structure and training data can compensate
129   for performance degradation caused by reduced model capacity. MoE-LLaVA (Lin et al., 2024)
130   introduces MoE into LLMs, showing potential in multimodal understanding and hallucination sup-
131
      pression with only 3B activation parameters. Bunny (He et al., 2024) performs K-Means clustering
      on the image embeddings derived from the LAION-2B dataset. Subsequently, it constructs an undi-
132
      rected graph to filter out images with excessively high similarity. This process not only enriches
133
      the information but also effectively reduces the size of the training set. Unlike these methods, our
134   approach primarily focuses on improving the training strategy of MLLMs. In this paper, we propose
135   a three-stage training recipe based on knowledge distillation. By transferring the knowledge of large
136   MLLMs to lightweight MLLMs, the Light MLLMs’ capabilities will be significantly enhanced.
137
138   2.2   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
139
140   Knowledge Distillation (KD) (Hinton, 2015) aims to transfer the knowledge from a large, com-
141   plex teacher model to a lightweight, simple student model. This technique can significantly im-
142   prove the performance of small models with fewer parameters, less computation, and faster speed.
143   Knowledge distillation has been successful applied in visual tasks and has achieved success in many
144
      fields, typically in the domain of image classification. For example, traditional distillation meth-
      ods (Hinton, 2015) use soft logits of the teacher model as extra supervision to train the student
145
      model. DKMF (Wang et al., 2021) and FNKD (Xu et al., 2020) reveal that mimicking the teacher
146
      model’s features leads to more accurate classification. DGKD (Son et al., 2021) further improves
147   the student model’s predictions by integrating multiple teacher models for guidance.
148
149
      KD for LLM. With the successful release of ChatGPT and its significant application value, LLM
150   has gradually attracted attention and achieved numerous research progress in recent years (Brown,
151   2020; Achiam et al., 2023). However, to achieve better results, the model size has also become
152   increasingly larger which follows scaling law (Kaplan et al., 2020), which limits the application of
153   LLM in resource-constrained scenarios. Therefore, some researchers have recently begun to explore
154   the application of knowledge distillation in LLM.
155
      MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2024) and DistiLLM (Ko et al., 2024) are dedicated to optimizing distilla-
156   tion process, proposing reverse Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KLD) and skew KLD respectively, to
157   prevent the student model from overly focusing on the long-tail distribution of the teacher model’s
158   output. (Wu et al., 2024) proposes a strategy to adaptively balance the weights of KLD and reverse
159   KLD loss. Some methods (Hsieh et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024; Ranaldi & Freitas, 2024) use the
160   Chain-of-Thought (CoT) capability of large LLMs to model causal relationships, and enrich training
161   data. Considering that different LLMs have different reasoning capabilities, TinyLLM (Tian et al.,
      2024) used multiple teacher models during training.

      KD for MLLM. Most recently, LLAVA-MoD (Shu et al., 2024) applies knowledge distillation to
182
      train s-MLLM. It first optimizes the structure of s-MLLM by integrating MoE (Jacobs et al., 1991;

189   for data augmentation is beneficial to promote the learning of student models.
190   Unlike existing LLM/MLLM distillation methods, which design complex constraints, introduce
191   multi-teacher models to enhance supervision, or explore complicated model structures, we focus
192   on optimizing training schemes and developing multimodal distillation strategies, to effectively and
193   efficiently improve the performance of existing small-scale MLLM under a single-teacher model.

287   3.3.2   T HREE -S TAGE D ISTILLATION S CHEME
288
      Based on the existing training scheme for MLLMs, a straightforward idea is that introducing knowl-
289
      edge distillation during the SFT stage can effectively enhance model performance. However, our
290
      research indicates that this training scheme is suboptimal (Refer to Table 2). Therefore, we consider
291   whether it is feasible to introduce a distillation strategy during the pre-training phase or to design
292   additional fine-tuning distillation to improve the performance of s-MLLM, and finally we propose a
293   novel and powerful three-stage training scheme.
294
295   Distilled Pre-Training (DPT). The main purpose of this stage is to project visual features to the
296   text embedding space. Previous methods (Liu et al., 2024a; Zhu et al., 2024) primarily achieve this
297   by optimizing the autoregressive prediction process of LLM (Eq. 1). In our LLaVA-KD, we utilize
298   a distillation procedure to better align visual and textual information as l-MLLM.
299   Specifically, we freeze the visual encoder and LLM of s-MLLM, and only optimize the projector.
300   During the training process, we minimize the discrepancy between the student model and the teacher
301   model in terms of the output distribution of visual and response through MDist. To optimize this
302   objective, the alignment of the projected visual features with the text embedding can be further
303   promoted. Furthermore, we utilize RDist to enhance the quality of visual features by enabling the
304   student model to learn from the teacher model’s ability to handle complex visual information.
305   Overall, in addition to optimizing the autoregressive prediction results, we also utilize a multimodal
306   distillation and relation distillation procedure. The objective is defined as follows:

      3.3.3    D ISCUSSION WITH R ECENT LL AVA-M O D
325
326   We compare our approach with the recently released LLaVA-MoD (Shu et al., 2024) for MLLM
327   distillation to highlight the technical differences: 1) In terms of training strategy, we design an
328   additional DFT stage, whereas LLaVA-MoD introduces Preference Distillation. 2) Structurally, we
329   do not incorporate complex architectures for s-MLLM, while LLaVA-MoD employs MoE modeling.
330   3) Regarding the training function, we develop KD-oriented MDist/RDist losses for the DPT and
331

337   Implementation Details. For both the large/small-scale MLLMs, we employ the pre-trained
338   SigLIP-B/14@384px (Zhai et al., 2023) as the Visual Encoder and a two-layer MLP with a GELU
339
      activation layer as the Projector, while adopting Qwen1.5 (Yang et al., 2024) family as LLM models.
      l-MLLM equipped with 4B parameters serves as the teacher model, while the s-MLLM is configured
340
      with 0.5B or 1.8B parameters. During training, we utilize the LLaVA1.5-558k (Liu et al., 2024a) for
341
      DPT stage, and LLaVA-mix-665k (Liu et al., 2024a) for both SFT and DFT stages. During the DPT
342   stage, the loss weights α, β, and γ are set to 1.0, 1.0, and 0.5, respectively. Batch size is set to 32 and
343   the learning rate is 1e-3. During SFT and DFT stages, the loss weights α′ , β ′ , and γ ′ are set to 1.0,
344   1.0, and 0.5, and we set batch size 16 and learning rate 2e-5. We train for one epoch at each stage
345   and utilize the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov, 2017) with the cosine learning rate schedule for all
346   stages. All experiments are conducted on 8 NVIDIA L40s GPUs. The entire training process for
347   s-MLLMs configured with 0.5B and 1.8B parameters take approximately 210 and 320 GPU hours.
348   The experiments are conducted based on the TinyLLaVA factory (Zhou et al., 2024).
349

      2024). Additionally. we also compare our LLaVA-KD with current state-of-the-art MLLMs, such

      as BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a), Instruct-BLIP (Dai et al., 2023), mPLUG-Owl2 (Ye et al., 2024),

      5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This paper introduces the LLaVA-KD, a framework that transfers knowledge from a l-MLLM to a s-
533
      MLLM. This approach effectively reduces model size and computational complexity while enabling
534   the s-MLLM to maintain the capabilities of the l-MLLM. LLaVA-KD introduces a distillation strat-
535   egy, including MDist and RDist. MDist minimizes the discrepancy between the visual-textual output
536   distributions of l-MLLM and s-MLLM. RDist transfers l-MLLM’s capacity to model correlations
537   between visual features. In addition, we propose a three-stage training scheme to fully exploit the
538   potential of s-MLLM: DPT to promote the alignment between visual-textual features, SFT to equip
539   model with multimodal understanding, and DFT to further transfer l-MLLM capacities. Compre-
      hensive experiments on ten benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework.
