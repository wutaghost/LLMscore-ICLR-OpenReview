011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Gaussian process regression is a ﬂexible Bayesian method for capturing nonlin-
014            earity. Although recent advancements allow us to handle various types of tasks
015            by specifying the covariance and likelihood functions, the interpretation of its
016            predictions is sometimes challenging due to the large number of parameters. In
017
               this study, we propose a clustering approach to improve the interpretability of
               Gaussian process posteriors. Assuming that the parameters corresponding to data
018
               points within each cluster are identical, the number of parameters in the posterior
019
               distribution is reduced. The assignment of data points to clusters is formulated
020            as a mixed-integer quadratic programming problem, with the objective function
021            being a weighted squared error from the mean of the posterior distribution ap-
022            proximated by variational inference. Graph partitioning and decision tree learning
023            can be represented by incorporating linear inequality constraints into this formu-
024            lation. Experimental results demonstrated that our approach provided signiﬁcant
025            advantages in enhancing the interpretability of spatial modeling. Moreover, our
026            formulation has produced higher-scoring decision trees compared to Classiﬁcation
027            and Regression Trees algorithm.
028
029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Gaussian process regression is a Bayesian nonlinear regression method (Rasmussen & Williams,
032   2006). This regression framework can handle various types of tasks by specifying the covari-
033   ance and likelihood functions. Spatial modeling is a key application of Gaussian process regres-
034   sion (Cressie, 1993). Stationary covariance functions that incorporate the Euclidean metric are
035   widely used to model spatial relationships. Non-Euclidean metrics are speciﬁcally designed to
036   capture anisotropic or nonstationary structures (Paciorek & Schervish, 2003; Feragen et al., 2014).
037   Covariance functions can be designed ﬂexibly for tasks beyond geostatistics. There are covari-
038   ance functions equivalent to an inﬁnitely wide deep neural network (Lee et al., 2018). Likelihood
039
      functions based on the behavior of observations signiﬁcantly impact prediction performance. Speci-
      fying non-Gaussian likelihoods that are not conjugate with a Gaussian process prior has emerged
040
      as a signiﬁcant area of research. Variational inference with inducing points is a common ap-
041
      proach that maximizes a lower bound on the marginal likelihood using a Gaussian approximation
042   (Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen, 2005; Titsias, 2009). Some studies have extended this approach
043   to accommodate general likelihoods (Sheth et al., 2015; Hensman et al., 2015b).
044
045
      Although Gaussian process regression has ﬂexible expressive power, too many parameters can some-
      times make it difﬁcult to interpret the prediction results. Surrogate models trained to approximate
046
      the predictions of a black-box model show promise in tackling this challenge (Molnar, 2022). De-
047
      cision trees are known for being interpretable machine learning models (Quinlan, 1986). They can
048   handle nonlinear regression tasks while maintaining a clear decision-making process. Assuming
049   that the data points in each leaf share a common parameter, a decision tree helps reduce the number
050   of parameters in a Gaussian process posterior distribution. Figure 1 illustrates the behavior of the
051   decision tree surrogate model for a posterior distribution given new inputs. Compared to a decision
052   tree trained directly on observed data, a surrogate model performs better when the distribution of
053   new inputs differs from the training data. This is advantageous for practical applications, such as
      in marketing and risk management, as it facilitates the creation of groups tailored to target users.

068   Given that a leaf is a type of cluster of data points, clustering with other structures can similarly be
069   used to build surrogate models. Graph partitioning is the process of dividing the nodes of a graph
070
      into smaller disjoint subgraphs while minimizing an objective (Nascimento & de Carvalho, 2011;
      Buluç et al., 2016). This technique is particularly beneﬁcial for clustering data points based on their
071
      dependencies in spatial modeling.
072
073   In this study, we propose a clustering approach to enhance the interpretability of Gaussian process
074   regression. To train surrogate models for Gaussian process posteriors, it is required to adopt crite-
075   ria that align with the likelihood functions. We formulate a mixed-integer quadratic programming
076
      (MIQP) problem to assign data points to clusters, maximizing the probability density of the poste-
      rior distribution approximated through variational inference. The objective function minimizes the
077
      weighted squared error from the mean of the Gaussian distribution that approximates the posterior,
078
      with weights determined by the inverse of the variance. Incorporating linear inequality constraints
079   into this formulation allows us to represent graph partitioning and decision tree learning. Figure 2
080   illustrates the overview of our approach. Experiments demonstrate that graph partitioning through
081   the Gaussian process posterior enhances the interpretability of spatial modeling. Moreover, decision
082   tree learning using our formulation has achieved higher scores than Classiﬁcation and Regression
083   Trees (CART) algorithm (Breiman et al., 1984).

      Local Surrogate Model. While our approach attempts to approximate the overall behavior of black-
088
      box models with interpretable models, other candidate methods are available to explain individ-
089
      ual predictions (Guidotti et al., 2018). Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME)
090   uses local surrogate models trained on perturbed samples generated around a speciﬁc instance
091   (Ribeiro et al., 2016). These models approximate the original model’s behavior within the local re-
092   gion. Using the concept of SHapley value from cooperative game theory (Shapley, 1953), SHapley
093   Additive exPlanations (SHAP) assigns an importance value to each feature for a particular prediction
094   (Lundberg & Lee, 2017). SHAP values, used as coefﬁcients in a linear function of binary variables
095   corresponding to features, provide valuable properties for enhancing interpretability. Koh & Liang
096   2017 proposed a method to formalize the impact of a training point on a prediction, providing efﬁ-
097   cient computation through inﬂuence functions derived from robust statistics (Hampel, 1976). This
098
      formulation helps us understand the behavior of black-box models during the learning process.
099   Non-parametric Clustering. The probability density function estimated by non-parametric mod-
100   els, such as suport vector machines (Ben-Hur et al., 2002) and Gaussian processes (Kim & Lee,
101   2007), enables us to indetify high-density regions. The valleys in this probability distribution are
102   interpreted as the boundaries between clusters. Unlike our approach, this clustering method focuses
103   on identifying regions rather than grouping individual data points into clusters and does not aim to
104
      preserve the prediction accuracy of the original models.
105   Optimal Tree. An optimal tree is a decision tree constructed to minimize a certain objective func-
106   tion. Although optimal trees may offer both simplicity and precision, building them is an NP-
107   complete task (Hyaﬁl & Rivest, 1976). For this reason, practical algorithms are typically heuristic.
      The CART algorithm is one of the most widely used heuristics for decision tree learning. Re-

207
      posteriors. Our approach relies on the concept of clustering through a Gaussian process posterior.
208   Graph partitioning and decision tree learning can be modeled by ﬁnding clusters under certain con-

472   6     C ONCLUSION
473
474   In this study, we proposed a clustering approach to construct surrogate models aimed at enhancing
475   the interpretability of predictions in Gaussian process regression. Graph partitioning and decision
476   tree learning are important tasks for constructing surrogate models. They can be formulated as MIQP
477   problems that maximize a metric to evaluate approximation performance. Experimental results sug-
478   gest that our approach allows for obtaining ﬂexible clusters in geostatistical tasks. Moreover, deci-
479   sion tree learning using our formulation resulted in higher-scoring trees than those produced by the
480   CART algorithm.
481   Limitation. The objective function in eq. (5) is not a linear metric when the covariance among new
482   inputs is retained. Although our approach can address this challenge, the increasing number of cross
483   terms in the objective function makes ﬁnding optimal solutions more difﬁcult. Additionally, even if
484   the covariance is ignored, the computational cost becomes signiﬁcant with a larger number of new
485   inputs. While the assumption of minimum granularity for clusters helps reduce computational cost,
      we need to investigate its feasibility for practical tasks.

781   It is evident that no speciﬁed DAG exists if the undirected graph is disconnected. We prove that
782   at least one speciﬁed DAG exists if the graph is connected. Consider adding vertices to the set of
783   vertices one by one. When adding a vertex, we set a directed edge from the new vertex to each vertex
784   already in the set, provided an undirected edge exists between them. Let the topological ordering
785   correspond to the order in which vertices are added. Since the directed edges satisfy eq. (2), the
786   graph obtained through this procedure is a DAG. The starting vertex in this procedure is a leaf.
787   Given that the undirected graph is connected, we set at least one directed edge when adding each
788   vertex, except for the initial one. Therefore, at least one speciﬁed DAG exists if the undirected graph
789
      is a connected graph.
