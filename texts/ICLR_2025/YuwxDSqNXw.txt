009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce the concept of a Compromised Turing Machine (CTM), an extension
012            of the classical Turing machine model where an adversary, Eve, can tamper with
013            the tape or internal state between timesteps. The CTM exposes fundamental vul-
014            nerabilities in the machine’s ability to self-verify its computations, particularly in
015
               adversarial environments where endogenous verification mechanisms cannot reli-
               ably ensure computational integrity. Through a novel parallel with Descartes’ deus
016
               deceptor thought experiment, we explore the epistemological limits of computa-
017
               tional certainty, illustrating how the CTM reveals the failure of self-verification in
018            adversarial contexts.
019
               To address these vulnerabilities, we propose several secure computational models,
020            including hybrid systems with external verification, randomized and probabilistic
021            verification protocols, distributed computing models with cross-verification, self-
022            correcting and self-healing mechanisms, and advanced cryptographic techniques
023            such as zero-knowledge proofs and homomorphic encryption. While each solu-
024            tion presents trade-offs in terms of computational overhead and complexity, they
025            provide a foundation for building resilient systems capable of withstanding ad-
026            versarial interference. Our work highlights the need for external sources of trust
027            and verification in secure computation and opens new directions for research into
028
               adversarial computational models.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The Turing machine (TM) is a cornerstone of classical computational theory, providing a fundamen-
033   tal model of algorithmic processes. However, the classical TM assumes an idealized environment
034   where computations are isolated from external interference. In modern computational contexts, es-
035   pecially in distributed systems and adversarial settings, such assumptions no longer hold. In this
036   paper, we introduce the Compromised Turing Machine (CTM), a model where an adversary, Eve,
037
      can modify the machine’s tape or state between computational steps. This model offers new per-
      spectives on the limits of computation and verification in adversarial settings.
038
039   The CTM draws parallels to classical philosophical skepticism, particularly the Cartesian deus de-
040   ceptor, where an all-powerful deceiver undermines certainty in knowledge. Here, Eve acts as a
041   computational deceiver, calling into question the reliability of internal verification processes. We
042   explore the implications of this model for theoretical computer science, cryptography, and the secu-
043
      rity of autonomous systems.
044   The primary contributions of this paper are as follows:
045
046
            • We formalize the Compromised Turing Machine (CTM), a new model where adversarial
              interference occurs between timesteps of a classical TM.
047
048         • We explore the limitations of endogenous verification in the CTM, where the machine
049           attempts to verify its own integrity against external manipulation.
050         • We draw novel philosophical parallels between the CTM and Cartesian skepticism, offering
051           insights into the limits of self-verification in both human cognition and machine computa-
052           tion.
053         • We propose directions for secure computational models that could operate in adversarial
              environments, with implications for cryptography and secure computation.

066
      intercept and modify messages (Dolev & Yao (1983)). However, these models focus on network

085   3.1   C LASSICAL T URING M ACHINE
086
087
      A classical Turing machine M is defined as a tuple M = (Q, Σ, Γ, δ, q0 , qaccept , qreject ), where Q is
      the finite set of states, Σ is the input alphabet, Γ is the tape alphabet, and δ is the transition function.
088
      The machine proceeds step-by-step in a deterministic manner.
089
090   Formally, a classical Turing machine M is defined as a tuple:
091
092                                      M = (Q, Σ, Γ, δ, q0 , qaccept , qreject )
093
094   Where:
095
096          • Q is the finite set of states.
097          • Σ is the input alphabet (not containing the blank symbol ⊔).
098          • Γ is the tape alphabet, where Σ ⊂ Γ, and ⊔ ∈ Γ represents the blank symbol.
099
             • δ : Q × Γ → Q × Γ × {L, R} is the transition function that defines how the machine moves
100
               between states based on the current symbol read from the tape.
101
102
             • q0 ∈ Q is the initial state.
103          • qaccept ∈ Q is the accepting state.
104          • qreject ∈ Q is the rejecting state.
105
106   The machine operates by reading a symbol from the tape at the position of the tape head, transition-
107   ing to a new state based on the transition function δ, writing a new symbol to the tape (or overwriting
      the current symbol), and moving the tape head one cell to the left (L) or right (R).

      3.2   C OMPROMISED T URING M ACHINE (CTM) D EFINITION
109
110   We now define the Compromised Turing Machine (CTM) by introducing an external adversary, Eve,
111   who can manipulate the machine’s tape or internal state between time steps. The CTM is defined as
112   a tuple:
113
114                                 MC = (Q, Σ, Γ, δ, q0 , qaccept , qreject , Psafe )
115
116   Where:
117
118          • Q, Σ, Γ, δ, q0 , qaccept , qreject are as defined for the classical Turing machine.
119          • Psafe : T × Q → {true, false} is a predicate that verifies the integrity of the tape configu-
120            ration and internal state of the machine, ensuring that no tampering by Eve has occurred.
121            Here, T represents the set of possible tape configurations, and Q represents the set of pos-
122            sible internal states.
123
      Unlike a classical Turing machine, where the tape and state transitions occur deterministically based
124   on the transition function δ, the CTM allows for the possibility that Eve can:
125
126          • Modify the tape between time steps, potentially altering the symbols on the tape.
127          • Alter the machine’s state between time steps, potentially changing the internal state of the
128            machine without the machine’s awareness.
129
130   The predicate Psafe is used to detect such tampering. If Psafe (T, q) = true, the machine concludes
131   that no tampering has occurred. Otherwise, if Psafe (T, q) = false, tampering is detected, and the
132   machine halts or transitions to an error state.
133
134   3.3   A DVERSARIAL I NTERFERENCE BY E VE
135
      In the CTM model, Eve has the ability to interfere with the Turing machine between discrete
136
      timesteps. Specifically, after the machine has read from the tape, transitioned to a new state, and
137
      updated the tape, Eve may:
138
139          • Modify the contents of any cell on the tape.
140
             • Alter the machine’s internal state q ∈ Q.
141
             • Modify previously stored integrity checks (e.g., cryptographic hashes or checksums) that
142
               the machine may have computed for endogenous verification.
143
144   Eve’s goal may vary depending on the scenario, ranging from corrupting the machine’s output to
145   causing it to enter an infinite loop, halt prematurely, or even produce incorrect results while main-
146   taining the illusion of correctness. Eve can tamper with both the current configuration of the tape
147   and the machine’s internal state in a way that the machine may not detect.
148
149   3.4   E NDOGENOUS V ERIFICATION : D EFINITION AND L IMITATIONS
150
151   A key question that arises in the CTM model is whether the machine can perform endogenous
152   verification, i.e., whether it can verify its own integrity from within the system. The predicate Psafe
153
      is designed to allow the machine to verify that no tampering has occurred. However, we demonstrate
      that endogenous verification is inherently vulnerable in the presence of Eve.
154
155   The predicate Psafe (T, q) operates as follows:
156
157
             • T ∈ T represents the current tape configuration.
158          • q ∈ Q represents the current internal state.
159          • Psafe (T, q) = true if the machine concludes that its tape and state are untampered, and
160            Psafe (T, q) = false if tampering is detected.
161
      Endogenous Verification Mechanisms: The machine may attempt to use mechanisms such as:

162
             • Cryptographic Hashing: The machine can hash portions of the tape and compare the hash
163            at later steps. If the hash matches the expected value, the machine proceeds; otherwise, it
164            detects tampering.
165
166          • State Consistency Checks: The machine can store the expected next state and check that it
167
               transitions to this state as determined by δ.
168
      However, since Eve can tamper with both the tape and any stored hash or consistency checks be-
169
      tween timesteps, these mechanisms are not sufficient. Specifically, Eve can:
170
171          • Alter the tape or state after the machine has verified them but before the next step occurs.
172
173          • Modify the stored hash values or expected states, making it impossible for the machine
174
               to reliably detect the tampering.

176   3.5   T HEOREM : T HE I MPOSSIBILITY OF C OMPLETE E NDOGENOUS V ERIFICATION
177
178   We now formalize the limitations of endogenous verification in the CTM model. We show that it is
179   impossible for a CTM to guarantee tamper-free computation using endogenous verification alone.
180   Theorem 1. Let MC be a Compromised Turing Machine with an adversary Eve, who can tamper
181   with the machine’s tape and internal state between timesteps. Then, there exists no endogenous
182   verification mechanism Psafe such that Psafe can deterministically guarantee that no tampering has
183   occurred.
184
185
186
      Proof. Assume, for the sake of contradiction, that there exists an endogenous verification mecha-
      nism Psafe such that Psafe (T, q) = true if and only if the tape T and state q have not been tampered
187
      with.
188
189   After timestep t, the machine computes some integrity check (e.g., a hash) based on its current tape
190   and state and stores this value. However, before timestep t + 1, Eve can modify both the tape and
191   the stored integrity check (including the hash value). When the machine performs verification at
192
      t + 1, it will observe the modified (tampered) tape and the tampered hash value. Since both have
      been altered consistently, the machine will conclude that Psafe (T, q) = true, even though tampering
193
      has occurred.
194
195   Thus, any mechanism the machine uses to verify its own integrity is susceptible to Eve’s interference,
196   rendering endogenous verification insufficient to guarantee safety. Therefore, no such Psafe can
197   deterministically guarantee tamper-free computation.
198
199
      This result highlights the fundamental vulnerability of a CTM: any verification mechanism entirely
200
      internal to the machine can be manipulated by an adversary who has access between timesteps.
201
202   We have introduced the concept of the Compromised Turing Machine (CTM) and formalized the
203   adversarial interference model, where Eve can manipulate the tape or state between timesteps. Ad-
204
      ditionally, we demonstrated the inherent limitations of endogenous verification mechanisms, show-
      ing that no such mechanism can fully guarantee the machine’s safety in the presence of adversarial
205
      manipulation. This opens up further inquiries into external verification mechanisms and secure
206
      computational models capable of operating under adversarial conditions.

212   the machine’s internal state or tape between timesteps, has striking parallels to René Descartes’ fa-

      deception, and trust in systems that operate under adversarial interference.

      4.1   D ESCARTES ’ Deus Deceptor AND E PISTEMIC S KEPTICISM
217
218   In his Meditations on First Philosophy, Descartes famously posited the possibility of a deus de-
219   ceptor, or a deceiving god, who has the power to manipulate not only the senses but also the very
220   faculties of reason. This thought experiment led Descartes to question the certainty of all knowl-
221   edge, including mathematical truths, as he considered the possibility that an omnipotent being could
222   deceive him at every step of his reasoning process. Descartes concluded that he could doubt all
223   external realities, but the one indubitable truth was his own existence as a thinking being: Cogito,
224   ergo sum (I think, therefore I am, Descartes & Cottingham (1996)).
225
226   4.2   T HE ROLE OF E VE AS THE Deus Deceptor IN THE CTM
227
228   In the context of the CTM, Eve serves as a computational analogue to Descartes’ deus deceptor.
229   Just as Descartes imagined a deceiving god capable of altering his perceptions and reasoning, Eve
230   can systematically interfere with the CTM’s tape and internal state without the machine being
231   aware. The machine, like Descartes’ thinker, operates under the assumption that its own internal
232   processes—whether reasoning for Descartes or computational steps for the machine—are reliable.
233
      However, just as Descartes must entertain the possibility of deception in his pursuit of knowledge,
      the CTM must account for the possibility that its computations have been compromised by Eve.
234
235   In both cases, the agent (Descartes or the Turing machine) is limited by the fact that it has no
236   access to events that occur outside its own processes. The Turing machine has no awareness of what
237   happens between timesteps, and Descartes’ thinker has no access to the external world beyond his
238   mind’s own constructions. In both cases, the possibility of deception is ever-present.
239
240   4.3   I LLUSION OF C ERTAINTY AND THE FAILURE OF E NDOGENOUS V ERIFICATION
241
242   One of the key insights of Descartes’ thought experiment is the illusion of certainty. Before introduc-
243   ing the deus deceptor, Descartes had assumed that certain truths, such as the laws of mathematics,
244   were beyond doubt. However, the possibility of a powerful deceiver led him to doubt even these
245   foundational truths. Similarly, in the CTM, the machine may compute certain verification mecha-
246
      nisms, such as a cryptographic hash or state consistency check, that give it the illusion of certainty
      about the integrity of its tape or internal state.
247
248   However, as we have shown in Section 3.4, Eve can tamper with both the tape and the verification
249   mechanisms themselves, leading the machine to believe it has verified its integrity when, in fact,
250   it has been deceived. This parallels Descartes’ realization that his faculties of reasoning, which
251   seemed reliable, could be systematically manipulated by an external deceiver. Thus, the machine’s
252
      internal verification processes, like Descartes’ initial beliefs in the certainty of mathematical truths,
      may ultimately be unreliable in the face of external interference.
253
254
255   4.4   T HE L IMITS OF S ELF -V ERIFICATION AND C ARTESIAN D OUBT
256
257   Descartes’ ultimate conclusion was that all knowledge derived from the senses or reasoning could be
258   called into doubt, except for the knowledge of his own existence as a thinking being. The Cogito rep-
259   resented the only indubitable truth because it was a self-verifying, immediate fact of consciousness:
260
      in order to doubt, one must exist to perform the doubting.
261   In contrast, the Turing machine lacks this capacity for self-awareness or Cogito. It cannot reflect on
262   its own computations in a way that is independent of external tampering. The machine’s attempts
263   at endogenous verification are vulnerable to Eve’s interference, meaning that there is no equivalent
264   to the Cogito for the machine—no self-verifying truth that it can rely on. Thus, while Descartes’
265   thinker can at least rely on the certainty of his own existence, the CTM cannot be certain of the
266
      integrity of its computations.
267   This difference highlights a fundamental limitation in computational systems: without some form of
268   external or trusted verification mechanism, the machine cannot escape the possibility of deception.
269   In epistemological terms, the Turing machine is more vulnerable than the Cartesian thinker because
      it lacks any inherent, self-verifying truth.

      4.5   E XISTENTIAL AND E PISTEMIC V ULNERABILITIES
271
272   The CTM model reveals an existential vulnerability in computational systems. Just as Descartes’
273   thinker, in the face of the deus deceptor, confronts the possibility that everything he perceives or
274   knows might be false, the CTM faces the possibility that every computation it performs might be
275   compromised. This vulnerability raises broader questions about trust and security in computational
276   systems (Anderson (2020)), particularly in environments where external interference is possible
277   (e.g., distributed systems, cloud computing, blockchain).
278   The key challenge for both Descartes and the CTM is the lack of access to external reality—whether
279   it be the physical world for Descartes or the state of the tape and computation for the CTM. In both
280   cases, there is a profound dependence on internal processes that may be systematically manipulated
281   by an adversarial force.
282
283   4.6   F ROM C ARTESIAN S KEPTICISM TO C OMPUTATIONAL T RUST
284
285
      In his philosophical work, Descartes ultimately overcomes the deus deceptor hypothesis by invok-
      ing the existence of a benevolent God, who guarantees the truth of clear and distinct ideas. For
286
      computational systems like the CTM, a similar resolution may come in the form of trusted external
287
      verification mechanisms. In the CTM model, such mechanisms could take the form of a trusted
288   third-party verifier, a secure external observer, or a cryptographic protocol that is tamper-proof and
289   guarantees the integrity of the machine’s computations.
290
291
      Thus, the resolution to the CTM’s existential and epistemic vulnerability, like Descartes’ resolution
      to his skepticism, lies in the introduction of an external source of trust. Without such mechanisms,
292
      the CTM remains vulnerable to adversarial manipulation, much like Descartes remains vulnerable
293
      to deception without a benevolent God.

      4.7   P HILOSOPHICAL I NSIGHTS FOR S ECURE C OMPUTATION
296
297   The comparison between the CTM and Descartes’ deus deceptor reveals several key insights for
298   secure computation:
299
300          • Epistemic Limits of Computation: Just as Descartes’ thinker cannot escape doubt through
301
               internal reasoning alone, a Turing machine cannot guarantee the integrity of its computa-
               tions through internal verification processes if an external adversary is present.
302
303          • Necessity of External Trust: The only way for both Descartes and the CTM to over-
304            come the possibility of deception is through the introduction of an external source of
305
               trust—whether it be a benevolent God in Descartes’ philosophy or a trusted external verifier
               in computational theory.
306
307          • Existential Vulnerabilities: The CTM highlights a fundamental vulnerability in compu-
308            tational systems that operate in adversarial environments, much like Descartes’ skepticism
309
               highlights the vulnerability of human reason to deception. In both cases, there is a need for
               external verification to secure trust.
310
311   This philosophical analogy provides a deeper understanding of the limitations of computation in
312   adversarial settings and highlights the necessity of secure, trusted external mechanisms for ensuring
313   computational integrity.

      The compromised Turing machine (CTM) highlights fundamental vulnerabilities in the ability of
318   computational systems to verify their own integrity when subject to adversarial interference. Since
319   the machine cannot guarantee tamper-free computation using purely endogenous verification mech-
320   anisms, secure computational models must integrate additional strategies to mitigate the risks posed
321   by adversaries such as Eve. In this section, we propose several directions for constructing secure
322   computational models, drawing on existing techniques in cryptography, distributed systems, and
323   verification, as well as introducing new concepts that address the unique challenges posed by the
      CTM.

      5.1   H YBRID C OMPUTATIONAL M ODELS WITH E XTERNAL V ERIFICATION
325
326   One promising direction for securing computational models against adversarial interference is the in-
327   troduction of external verification mechanisms that operate outside the machine’s internal processes.
328   In such a model, the Turing machine would be augmented by a trusted external entity responsible
329   for verifying the integrity of the machine’s computation between timesteps. This external verifier
330   could monitor both the tape and the machine’s internal state, ensuring that any tampering by Eve is
331   detected.
332   The key idea behind hybrid models is that they combine the machine’s internal operations with a
333   trusted external observer or verification mechanism. This external verifier could take several forms:
334
335
            • Trusted Third-Party Verifier: A secure, independent system that periodically checks the
              machine’s tape and state, computing cryptographic checksums or hashes to detect tamper-
336
              ing. This verifier could act like a ”watchdog” that observes the computation and raises an
337
              alert if tampering is detected.
338
            • Secure Hardware Modules: Trusted execution environments (TEEs) such as Intel SGX
339
              or ARM TrustZone could be integrated with the Turing machine to ensure that certain
340
              critical operations (e.g., state transitions or tape writes) are executed securely and cannot be
341           tampered with by Eve. These hardware modules provide a level of assurance that internal
342           state and sensitive computations are protected from interference.
343
            • External Cryptographic Proofs: The machine could periodically generate cryptographic
344           proofs (e.g., using zero-knowledge proofs or homomorphic encryption) that can be veri-
345           fied externally. These proofs would guarantee that the machine’s computation proceeds
346           correctly, even if Eve attempts to interfere.
347
348   Hybrid models rely on the premise that some external source of trust can detect tampering that the
349
      machine itself cannot. By introducing external verifiers, we ensure that tampering by Eve can be
      caught between timesteps, thus mitigating the vulnerabilities inherent in purely endogenous verifi-
350
      cation.
351
352
      5.2   R ANDOMIZED OR P ROBABILISTIC V ERIFICATION P ROTOCOLS
353
354   Another approach to securing the CTM is to introduce randomized or probabilistic verification pro-
355   tocols, which make it harder for an adversary like Eve to predict when and where the verification
356   will occur. The core idea is to increase the adversary’s uncertainty about the timing or scope of
357   verification, thus reducing the effectiveness of any tampering attempt.
358
      Randomized verification protocols could work as follows:
359
360         • Randomized Tape Checks: The machine could randomly select portions of the tape to
361           verify at each timestep, using cryptographic hashes or checksums to ensure that the contents
362           of those tape cells have not been modified. Since Eve cannot predict which portions of the
363           tape will be checked, tampering becomes more difficult to conceal.
364         • Probabilistic State Verification: Similarly, the machine could probabilistically verify its
365           internal state at random intervals. If the machine detects any inconsistencies between its
366           current state and its expected state, it halts or raises an alarm.
367         • Randomized Redundancy: The machine could introduce redundancy into its computa-
368           tions by randomly replicating certain computations or storing multiple copies of the tape.
369           Eve would need to tamper with all redundant copies consistently to avoid detection, which
370           increases the difficulty of successful tampering.
371   Randomized verification protocols introduce an element of unpredictability that makes it harder for
372   adversaries to perform undetectable tampering. However, they also come with trade-offs in terms of
373   computational overhead, as frequent checks or redundant computations can slow down the system.
374
375   5.3   D ISTRIBUTED C OMPUTATION AND V ERIFICATION
376
377   Distributed computing offers another promising avenue for securing computation in adversarial envi-
      ronments. In distributed systems, multiple independent agents or machines work together to perform

378
      a computation, and integrity can be ensured by cross-verification among these agents. By decentral-
379   izing the computation, we reduce the impact of tampering on any single machine and increase the
380   likelihood that tampering will be detected.
381
382
      In a distributed CTM model, the computation is divided across multiple Turing machines, each
      of which performs a part of the overall task. These machines communicate with one another and
383
      cross-verify their results. If one machine detects that its tape or state has been tampered with, it can
384
      alert the other machines, who can collectively decide whether to halt the computation or switch to a
385   backup.
386
387   Key features of distributed verification include:
388
             • Cross-Verification: Each machine independently verifies the outputs of the other ma-
389
               chines, ensuring that no single machine can be tampered with without detection. This
390
               is analogous to Byzantine fault-tolerance in distributed systems, where the system can tol-
391            erate a certain number of faulty or malicious agents.
392
393
             • Majority Voting: In the case of conflicting results, the distributed system could use ma-
               jority voting to determine the correct outcome. For example, if three machines perform the
394
               same computation and one produces an inconsistent result, the system could discard the
395
               outlier.
396
397          • Sharding and Replication: The computation could be sharded, meaning that different
398
               machines work on separate parts of the task, but critical components are replicated across
               multiple machines. Replication ensures that tampering with one machine does not compro-
399
               mise the entire computation.
400
401   Distributed computation increases resilience against tampering, as Eve would need to compromise a
402   majority of machines to successfully alter the final result. This approach is particularly well-suited
403   for environments where trust can be distributed among multiple agents.
404
405   5.4   S ELF -C ORRECTING OR S ELF -H EALING S YSTEMS
406
407   A more advanced approach to securing the CTM is the development of self-correcting or self-healing
408   systems, which can detect and repair damage caused by adversarial interference without external
409   assistance. These systems would have built-in mechanisms to automatically recover from tampering,
410   making them more robust in adversarial environments.
411   Self-healing systems could work on the basis of redundant state recovery, automated error detection,
412   and/or self-correcting algorithms.
413
      Self-correcting systems would allow the CTM to autonomously detect and recover from tampering,
414
      making them particularly useful in environments where external verification or human intervention
415
      is impractical.

      5.5   A DVANCED C RYPTOGRAPHIC T ECHNIQUES : Z ERO -K NOWLEDGE P ROOFS AND
418
            H OMOMORPHIC E NCRYPTION
419
420   Finally, advances in cryptographic techniques could provide novel ways to secure computations
421   against adversarial interference. Techniques such as zero-knowledge proofs (ZKP) and homomor-
422   phic encryption offer promising directions for ensuring the integrity of computations even in adver-
423   sarial environments.
424
425          • Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKP): In a ZKP-based system, the machine could generate
426            proofs that it has performed a computation correctly without revealing the details of the
427            computation itself. External verifiers could then check these proofs without needing to ac-
428
               cess the machine’s internal state or tape. This would prevent Eve from tampering with the
               computation while still allowing verification.
429

      In this paper, we introduced the concept of the Compromised Turing Machine (CTM), a theoretical
454   model in which an adversary, Eve, can tamper with the tape or internal state of a Turing machine
455   between timesteps. This model reveals significant vulnerabilities in the machine’s ability to verify its
456   own computations, particularly in adversarial settings where endogenous verification mechanisms
457   are inherently insufficient. We demonstrated that no purely internal verification process can reliably
458   ensure the integrity of the machine’s state or tape due to Eve’s capacity to manipulate both the
459   verification and computational processes.
460   By drawing philosophical parallels to Descartes’ deus deceptor, we explored how the CTM high-
461   lights the limits of computational certainty in adversarial environments. Just as Descartes ques-
462   tioned the reliability of his perceptions and reasoning in the presence of a deceiving god, the CTM
463   reveals how computational systems are vulnerable to tampering that undermines their own verifica-
464   tion processes. This analysis suggests that, like Descartes’ reliance on a benevolent God to guarantee
465   certainty, computational systems must incorporate external sources of trust to mitigate adversarial
466   risks.
467   We proposed several promising directions for addressing the vulnerabilities of the CTM. Each of
468   these approaches addresses the fundamental weaknesses exposed by the CTM, but they also come
469   with trade-offs in terms of computational overhead, complexity of implementation, and trust as-
470   sumptions. Nevertheless, the exploration of these techniques provides a foundation for future work
471   in securing computational systems against adversarial interference, especially in contexts such as
472   distributed computing, cloud environments, and blockchain systems.
473   The CTM raises profound questions about the nature of trust, security, and computation in adversar-
474   ial settings. In highlighting the inherent vulnerability of endogenous verification, this work opens
475   up new avenues for developing more resilient computational models capable of withstanding ad-
476   versarial attacks. Just as the philosophical challenges posed by Descartes’ skepticism spurred the
477   development of new epistemological frameworks, the challenges posed by the CTM encourage us to
478   rethink how we approach computational security and integrity in an increasingly adversarial world.
