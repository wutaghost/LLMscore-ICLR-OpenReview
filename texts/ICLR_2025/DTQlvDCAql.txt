009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011                Existing research often treats long-form videos as extended short videos, leading
012                to several limitations: inadequate capture of long-range dependencies, inefficient
013                processing of redundant information, and failure to extract high-level semantic
014                concepts. To address these issues, we propose a novel approach that more ac-
015                curately reflects human cognition.1 This paper introduces HERMES: temporal-
016                coHERent long-forM understanding with Episodes and Semantics, a model that
017                simulates episodic memory accumulation to capture action sequences and rein-
018
                   forces them with semantic knowledge dispersed throughout the video. Our work
                   makes two key contributions: First, we develop an Episodic COmpressor (ECO)
019
                   module that efficiently aggregates crucial representations from micro to semi-
020
                   macro levels, overcoming the challenge of long-range dependencies. Second, we
021                propose a Semantics reTRiever (SeTR) that enhances these aggregated represen-
022                tations with semantic information by focusing on the broader context, dramati-
023                cally reducing feature dimensionality while preserving relevant macro-level infor-
024                mation. This addresses the issues of redundancy and lack of high-level concept
025                extraction. Extensive experiments demonstrate that HERMES achieves state-of-
026                the-art performance across multiple long-video understanding benchmarks in both
027                zero-shot and fully-supervised settings. Our code will be made public.

049   1       I NTRODUCTION
050
051   Video understanding reflects how humans perceive the world through one of our most essential
052   senses, sight, and drives a wide range of visual and multimodal applications. Whether we want
053

      to create better video summarization tools, index and retrieve specifics from the vast and ever-
055   expanding array of video content, or improve content moderation and copyright enforcement, we
056   need models that excel at video understanding. This requirement extends beyond short videos with
057   few frames—a task that image models can already handle adequately—to encompass the analysis
058   of extended video content spanning minutes and comprising thousands of interrelated frames.
059
      Long-form video understanding is challenging for several reasons. First and foremost is the tempo-
060
      ral complexity, as it requires handling a large number of frames throughout the video. Second, it
061   requires a semantic understanding of high-level concepts as well as the narrative structure. The third
062   challenge is the memory and computational constraints, making it non-trivial to solve the previous
063   two challenges. Attempts to address these issues have been made by researchers who mainly borrow
064   ideas from short videos (Wu & Krahenbuhl, 2021; Miech et al., 2020), which is a more mature area
065   of research encompassing action recognition and video classification, among others, and for which
066   datasets are more abundant. However, these approaches, which adopt techniques such as pooling
067   (Faure et al., 2023), or 3D convolutions (Feichtenhofer et al., 2019), often do not fully account for
068   the unique characteristics of long videos that distinguish them from a simple concatenation of short
069
      video segments. Some ideas about short-video modeling, especially for those at the spatial level,
      may also hold for longer ones, but when it comes to long-term modeling, macro-level representations
070
      should be extracted efficiently.
071
072   In video understanding, we can distinguish between two types of information: episodic and se-
073   mantic. Episodic information refers to specific, sequential events that occur in a video, while se-
074   mantic information encompasses overarching themes and concepts. To illustrate, imagine walk-
075
      ing through a scene at a birthday party. Episodic information might include observing five peo-
      ple singing a birthday song, followed by one person cutting a cake. These are specific, time-
076
      bound events. In contrast, semantic information might involve recognizing decorations scattered
077
      throughout the scene, instantly comprehending that you’re witnessing a birthday party. This high-
078   level understanding provides a concise overview of the scene and actions, transcending specific
079   moments. Building on these concepts, we propose temporal-coHERent long-forM understanding
080   with Episodes and Semantics (HERMES), a model designed to capture both episodic and seman-
081   tic information in long-form videos. HERMES comprises two key components: ECO and SeTR.
082   The Episodic COmpressor (ECO) aggregates key contiguous information as we process the video,
083   shaping the model’s understanding of the scene sequentially without cluttering it. Complementing
084   this, the SEmantic reTRiever (SeTR) identifies and extracts high-level cues that provide a concise
085   overview of the scene and actions. HERMES achieves state-of-the-art performance on four long-
086
      form video understanding benchmarks in both zero-shot and fully-supervised settings, notably out-
      performing the state-of-the-art by a significant 7.3% on LVU(Wu & Krahenbuhl, 2021) and 14.9%
087
      on MovieChat-1k (Song et al., 2024).
088
089   Our key contributions are as follows:
090
091
      • We address the challenge of efficiently processing and understanding long-form videos, a prob-
        lem that has been hindered by computational constraints and the difficulty of capturing complex
092
        temporal dynamics over extended durations.
093
094   • We propose an Episodic COmpressor (ECO) to stream through the video and keep important
095     episodes by aggregating similar scenes. ECO enables efficient processing of extended video se-
096
        quences while preserving temporal coherence and narrative structure.
097   • We develop a Semantics reTRiever (SeTR) that enhances the model’s understanding of long videos
098     by distilling high-level semantic cues, providing a cohesive framework for understanding the con-
099     text and themes within long-form videos.
100
      Through comprehensive evaluation across multiple benchmarks and detailed ablation studies, we
101
      validate the effectiveness of ECO and SeTR, and demonstrate their complementary roles in enhanc-
102
      ing long-form video understanding

106
107   Given a long video V = {f1 , f2 , . . . , fN }, where fi represents the i-th frame and N is the total
      number of frames, our objective is to develop a model M that can efficiently process V and construct

                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      an internal understanding U of its content. This understanding should enable the model to answer
109   queries Q or follow instructions I related to the video content. Formally, we aim to optimize the
110   function:
111                                            M : (V, I) → U                                       (1)
112
      such that:
113
114   • U captures both episodic and semantic information from V .
115
      • U can be used to maximize the probability P (A|Q, U ) of generating correct answers A to queries
116     Q about the video content.
117
118   The key challenges in this formulation are:
119
120   • Temporal Complexity: Efficiently processing N frames, where N can be very large (thousands
121
        of frames for minutes-long videos).
122   • Semantic Understanding: Extracting high-level concepts and narrative structure from the video
123     content.
124   • Memory Constraints: Developing a method that can maintain relevant information without ex-
125     hausting computational resources.
126
127   We aim to address these challenges, by proposing two key contributions:
128
      1. Episodic COmpressor (ECO):
129
130                                    ECO : f1 , f2 , . . . , fN → e1 , e2 , . . . , eK                     (2)
131
         where K ≪ N , and ei represents a compressed episodic memory.
132
133
      2. Semantics reTRiever (SeTR):
134                                 SeT R : {f1 , f2 , . . . , fN } → {s1 , s2 , . . . , sL }                (3)
135
          where L ≪ N , and si represents extracted semantic knowledge.
136
137   The final understanding U is then generated by combining the outputs of ECO and SeTR:
138
139                                       U = G(ECO(V, I), SeT R(V ))                                        (4)
140   where G is a function that integrates episodic and semantic information. This formulation allows
141   us to approach long-form video understanding in a way that more closely mimics human cognition,
142   addressing the identified challenges while maintaining computational efficiency.

      3     P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK : HERMES
145
146
      This paper is not about a new LLM or a new way to fine-tune existing LLMs or VLMs. It focuses on
147
      leveraging what we know about how humans understand visual scenes to guide the model through
148   the same process. Although this work uses an LLM for autoregressive prediction, the core ideas of
149   episodic memory compression (ECO) and semantic knowledge retrieval (SeTR) can be applied to
150   other models where learning contiguous sequences and high-level representations is advantageous.
151
      Given a video, short or long, and a set of instructions specifying what to do with the video, our
152
      method can return the specified output, such as video question answering (VQA) or video classifi-
153
      cation. It achieves this by leveraging two important properties of human understanding of scenes:
154   episodic memory, which involves determining and stratifying a sequence of frames with similar
155   properties, and semantic knowledge, which can help answer broad questions about the scene (e.g.,
156   does it occur at night or during the day?). We refer to the former as ECO, detailed in Section 3.2,
157   and to the latter as SeTR, described in Section 3.4.

      3.3   E PISODIC Q-F ORMER
234
235   The Episodic Q-Former uses the same architecture as the original Q-Former (Li et al., 2023a) and is
236   loaded with weights pretrained by Dai et al. (2023). However, we insert ECO as a pruning module
237   within the Q-Former to combine and branch queries into episodes over the long video. Given initial
238   queries and instructions, we perform self-attention on these queries and then cross-attention between
239   the queries and the visual representations M. The enhanced queries then undergo an ECO-like
240   process, where we iteratively merge similar queries across video windows, effectively forming video
241   query episodes of high information density. The following equation summarizes the process,
242                                      Q = ECOq (CA (SA(Q0 ), M))                                        (6)
243
244   where Q0 represents the initial queries, M denotes the visual representations from the visual ECO,
245   SA(Q0 ) applies self-attention on the initial queries, and CA(·, M) performs cross-attention between
246   the self-attended queries and the visual representations. Finally, ECOq (·) – note the q to differentiate
247   it from the visual ECO – applies the iterative merging process similar to the visual compression
                                                                                                              ′
248   detailed in Section 3.2 on the enhanced queries. The episodic Q-Former outputs Q ∈ RB×q×C
                         ′
249   with B, q and C alluding to the batch size, the number of queries and the channel dimension,
250   respectively.

252   3.4   S E TR: S EMANTICS RE TR IEVER
253
      To complement ECO and capture higher-level semantic information from the video, we develop
254
      a Semantics reTRiever (SeTR). SeTR is designed to identify and consolidate important high-level
255
      information that may be scattered (contiguously or not) throughout the video. Given a video feature
256   tensor F ∈ RB×N ×T ×C , where B is the batch size, N the number of frames, T the number of
257   tokens per frame and C the channel dimension, SeTR operates as follows: we first normalize F to
258   ensure consistent scaling across features. Second, we apply a stride of k to create two groups, group
259   X containing every k-th frame, resulting in N  k frames and group Y with the remaining N − k
                                                                                                         N

260   frames. Third, we calculate dot product similarity scores between frames in X and Y . Finally, for
261   each frame in Y , we merge it with its most similar frame in X, based on the computed scores by
262   taking their mean.
263   This process effectively reduces the number of frames from N to N    k , consolidating semantic in-
264   formation while maintaining the most relevant features across the video timeline. The resulting
                                                          N
265   semantic representations are denoted as F ′ ∈ RB× k ×T ×C . We evaluate the effectiveness of this
266   approach in Section 4.3. While ToMe (Bolya et al., 2022) have explored token reduction in vision
267   transformers, their approach and objectives differ significantly from ours. Their method focuses
268   on minor token reductions within individual frames, specifically between different layers of a Vi-
269   sion Transformer. In contrast, SeTR retains the most salient frames while significantly reducing
      redundancies.

280   3.6   F ROM R EPRESENTATIONS TO NATURAL L ANGUAGE
281
282   After obtaining the episodic representations Q and the semantic representations Qsem , we prepare
283   them for input into a Large Language Model (LLM). Specifically, we concatenate Q and Qsem to
284   form a unified representation vector. This concatenated vector is then projected into the input em-
285   bedding space of the LLM using a learned linear transformation. In our implementation, we utilize
286   a Vicuna-7B model (Chiang et al., 2023) as LLM. The model, conditioned on this projected repre-
287
      sentation and guided by task-specific instructions, generates the requested natural language output.
      This approach allows us to leverage the LLM’s pretrained knowledge and language generation ca-
288
      pabilities while incorporating our task-specific episodic and semantic information. The process is
289
      summarized by the following equation:
290
291                                     Ŷ = LLM(W [Q; Qsem ] + b, I)                                    (8)
292
293   where Ŷ is the generated output, [Q; Qsem ] denotes the concatenation of Q and Qsem , W and b are
294   the learned projection matrix and bias respectively, and I represents the task-specific instructions.

300   We evaluate our approach on two primary tasks: long-form video classification and long-form video
301
      question answering.

499   5     R ELATED W ORK
500
501   Action recognition is an essential task in video understanding, primarily focusing on identifying
502   specific actions within short video clips. Various approaches have been developed, with convolu-
503
      tional neural networks forming the core of many of them. Early work by Ji et al. (2012) utilized 3D
      convolutions, while Varol et al. (2017) employed temporal convolutions. 2D CNNs coupled with
504
      temporal modeling have also been explored, with representative works such as Temporal Difference
505
      Networks (TDN) (Ng & Davis, 2018) and Event Adaptive Networks (EAN) (Tian et al., 2022). More
506   recently, transformer-based models have gained prominence with works such as Faure et al. (2023),
507   Xu et al. (2021), and Zhang et al. (2022).
508
509
      Video question answering (VideoQA) aims to answer questions related to video content, requiring
      a deep understanding of both visual and textual information. Datasets such as ActivityNet-QA (Yu
510
      et al., 2019) for short videos, and MovieChat-1k for long videos (Song et al., 2024) provide bench-
511
      marks for evaluating models in this field, allowing for several research endeavors on this subject
512   (Zhang et al., 2020; Zhuang et al., 2020; Pan et al., 2023).
513
514   Long-form video understanding presents unique challenges due to the extended duration and com-
515
      plex narrative structures involved. Datasets with these properties include LVU (Wu & Krahenbuhl,
      2021), COIN (Tang et al., 2019), Breakfast (Kuehne et al., 2014), and MovieChat-1k (Song et al.,
516
      2024). Traditional approaches to tackling such a task often extend methods designed for short videos
517
      to handle longer sequences, such as pooling over the temporal dimension (Tang et al., 2020; Faure
518   et al., 2023). Other methods such as Wu & Krahenbuhl (2021); He et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2022)
519   and Song et al. (2024) explore memory techniques via token compression. Additionally, Tian et al.
520   (2024) introduced a video semantic compression framework using low-level bitrate coding. Wang
521   et al. (2023) introduced selective structured state-spaces for long-form videos, followed by others
522   Islam & Bertasius (2022); Islam et al. (2023) exploiting the ability of state-space models to retain
523   long-term context.
524   LLM-based Long-Form Video Understanding: Recent advancements in large language models
525   (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2023) have piqued researchers’ curiosity regarding their
526   use for video understanding (Maaz et al., 2023). It turns out to be a good match, as understanding
527   videos often involves transforming their content into words, whether it’s video captioning, video
528   question answering, or even action classification. Song et al. (2024) and He et al. (2024) propose
529   frameworks that employ memory techniques to handle extensive video content while Ren et al.
530   (2024) presents TimeChat, explicitly conditioning the model to manage time-dependent information.
531
532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   We propose HERMES, a novel framework designed to enhance long-form video understanding
535   through two key components inspired by cognitive processes. The first, Episodic COmpressor
536   (ECO), captures representations as sequences of continuous actions, reflecting episodic memory.
537   The second, Semantics reTRiever (SeTR), serves as a high-level summarizer, distilling essential
538   semantic information. Our model achieves state-of-the-art results on several long-video datasets,
539   significantly outperforming existing methods. Through experiments on LVU, Breakfast, COIN and
      MovieChat, we have demonstrated the effectiveness and efficiency of ECO and SeTR.

854   trade-offs. As evident from Figure 10, the relationship between accuracy and window size is non-

856   size of 10. This suggests that providing more context to the model improves its performance up to a

858   to the introduction of irrelevant context.
859   Latency exhibits a sharp decrease from window size 1 to 5, after which it remains relatively stable.
860   This indicates that while smaller window sizes may seem computationally advantageous, they incur
861   higher latency, possibly due to the need for more frequent ECO call. The optimal trade-off occurs at a
862   window size of 10, where we observe peak accuracy and stabilized latency suggesting that carefully
863   tuned context windows can enhance long-form video understanding without incurring additional
      computational costs.

       A.7   H OW IS OUR APPROACH RELATED TO COGNITIVE PROCESSES ?
1019
1020   Our approach to long-form video understanding is inspired by cognitive processes involving memory
1021   and comprehension. According to the literature on neuroscience (Tulving et al., 1972; Schacter &
1022   Tulving, 1982; Tulving, 1983), human cognition involves two primary types of memory: episodic
1023   and semantic. Episodic memory is the ability to recall specific events or episodes, while semantic
1024   memory refers to the storage of general knowledge and concepts. These forms of memory are crucial
1025   for understanding long-form narratives, where a coherent understanding arises from the integration
       of specific events and overarching themes.


                                                             19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       The proposed HERMES model incorporates these cognitive processes through its two main com-
1027   ponents, ECO and SeTR. ECO, akin to the function of episodic memory, selectively retains and
1028   compresses key events from the video, allowing the model to form a structured representation of the
1029   narrative as it unfolds. This approach is an oversimplified abstraction of findings in cognitive neu-
1030   roscience, which highlight the role of the hippocampus in the consolidation of episodic memories
1031   (Eichenbaum, 2004; Schacter & Tulving, 1982), and the concept of subjective time (Arstila et al.,
1032   2014) that sees a scene (or a video) not as a series of frames but as a series of experiences. The hip-
1033   pocampus enables the organization of temporally distinct experiences into a coherent memory trace,
1034   something that we aim to capture with ECO. Moreover, the sequential processing and aggregation
1035
       of information in our model align with the concept of event segmentation in cognitive psychology
       (Zacks et al., 2007). Humans naturally segment continuous experiences into discrete events, which
1036
       aids in memory formation and recall.
1037
1038   Meanwhile, SeTR functions similarly to semantic memory, extracting and reinforcing high-level se-
1039   mantic cues. This process mirrors how the brain integrates detailed episodic memories with broader
1040   semantic knowledge stored in the neocortex (McClelland et al., 1995; Binder & Desai, 2011). Also
1041
       related is the concept of gist extraction which involves rapidly comprehending the essence or overall
       meaning of a scene or situation (Oliva, 2005). This ability allows humans to quickly understand
1042
       the context of a complex scene without processing every detail. Our SeTR operates similarly by
1043
       identifying and extracting high-level semantic cues that provide a concise overview of the scene and
1044   actions.
1045
1046   The integration of these cognitive processes not only aligns with human-like comprehension but also
1047
       offers a framework for efficiently handling the vast and diverse information present in long-form
       videos. Significant improvements over existing state-of-the-art models, underscore the effectiveness
1048
       of this cognition-inspired approach. While our model is a oversimplified abstraction of human
1049
       cognition, it provides a foundation for exploring more complex cognitive mechanisms in future
1050   work.
