011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper introduces von Mises-Fisher exploration (vMF-exp), a scalable method
014            for exploring large action sets in reinforcement learning problems where hyper-
015            spherical embedding vectors represent actions. vMF-exp involves initially sampling
016            a state embedding representation using a von Mises-Fisher distribution, then ex-
017            ploring this representation’s nearest neighbors, which scales to virtually unlimited
018            numbers of candidate actions. We show that, under theoretical assumptions, vMF-
019            exp asymptotically maintains the same probability of exploring each action as
020
               Boltzmann Exploration (B-exp), a popular alternative that, nonetheless, suffers
               from scalability issues as it requires computing softmax values for each action.
021
               Consequently, vMF-exp serves as a scalable alternative to B-exp for exploring
022
               large action sets with hyperspherical embeddings. In the final part of this paper, we
023            further validate the empirical relevance of vMF-exp by discussing its successful
024            deployment at scale on a music streaming service. On this service, vMF-exp has
025            been employed for months to recommend playlists inspired by initial songs to
026            millions of users, from millions of possible actions for each playlist.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Exploration is a fundamental component of the reinforcement learning (RL) paradigm (Amin et al.,
033   2021; McFarlane, 2018; Sutton and Barto, 2018). It allows RL agents to gather valuable information
034   about their environment and identify optimal actions that maximize rewards (Amin et al., 2021;
035   Chiappa et al., 2023; Dulac-Arnold et al., 2015; Jin et al., 2020; Ladosz et al., 2022; McFarlane,
036   2018; Reynolds, 2002; Slivkins et al., 2019; Sutton and Barto, 2018; Tang et al., 2017). However, as
037   the set of actions to explore grows larger, the exploration process becomes increasingly challenging.
038   Indeed, large action sets can lead to higher computational costs, longer learning times, and the risk
039
      of inadequate exploration and suboptimal policy development (Amin et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021;
      Dulac-Arnold et al., 2015; Lillicrap et al., 2016; Sutton and Barto, 2018; Tomasi et al., 2023).
040
041   As an illustration, consider a recommender system on a music streaming service like Apple Music or
042   Spotify, curating playlists of songs “inspired by” an initial selection to help users discover music (Ben-
043   dada et al., 2023a). In practice, these services often generate such playlists all at once, using efficient
044   nearest neighbor search systems (Johnson et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019) to retrieve songs most similar to
045
      the initial one, in a song embedding vector space learned using collaborative filtering or content-based
      methods (Bendada et al., 2023a;b; Bontempelli et al., 2022; Jacobson et al., 2016; Schedl et al.,
046
      2018; Zamani et al., 2019). Alternatively, one could formalize this task as an RL problem (Tomasi
047
      et al., 2023), where the recommender system (i.e., the agent) would adaptively select the next song
048   to recommend (i.e., the next action) based on user feedback on previously recommended songs
049   (i.e., the rewards, such as likes or skips). Using an RL approach instead of generating the playlist
050   at once would have the advantage of dynamically learning from user feedback to identify the best
051   recommendations (Afsar et al., 2022; Tomasi et al., 2023). However, music streaming services offer
052   access to large catalogs with millions of songs (Bendada et al., 2020; Jacobson et al., 2016; Schedl
053   et al., 2018). Therefore, the agent would need to consider millions of possible actions for exploration,
      increasing the complexity of this task.


                                                          1
054
      In particular, Boltzmann Exploration (B-exp) (Cesa-Bianchi et al., 2017; Sutton and Barto, 2018),
055   a popular exploration strategy sampling actions to explore based on embedding similarities, would
056   become practically intractable as it would require computing softmax values over millions of elements
057   (see Section 2). Furthermore, in large action sets, many actions are often irrelevant; in our example,
058   most songs would constitute poor recommendations (Tomasi et al., 2023). Therefore, random
059   exploration methods like ε-greedy (Dann et al., 2022; Sutton and Barto, 2018), although more
060   efficient than B-exp, would also be unsuitable for production use. Since these methods ignore song
061   similarities, each song, including inappropriate ones, would have an equal chance of being selected for
062   exploration. This could result in negative user feedback and a poor perception of the service (Tomasi
063
      et al., 2023). Lastly, deterministic exploration strategies would also be ineffective. Systems serving
      millions of users often rely on batch RL (Lange et al., 2012) since updating models after every
064
      trajectory is impractical. Batch RL, unlike on-policy learning, requires exploring actions non-
065
      deterministically given a state, and deterministic exploration would result in redundant trajectories
066   and slow convergence (Bendada et al., 2020).
067
068   In summary, exploration remains challenging in RL problems characterized by large action sets
069
      and where accounting for embedding similarities is crucial, like our recommendation example.
      Overall, although a growing body of scientific research has been dedicated to adapting RL models for
070
      recommendation (see, e.g., the survey by Afsar et al. (2022)), evidence of RL adoption in commercial
071
      recommender systems exists but remains limited (Chen et al., 2019; 2021; 2022; Tomasi et al., 2023).
072   The few existing solutions typically settle for a workaround by using a truncated version of B-exp
073   (TB-exp). In TB-exp, a small subset of candidate actions is first selected, e.g., using approximate
074   nearest neighbor search (a framework sometimes referred to as the Wolpertinger architecture (Dulac-
075   Arnold et al., 2015)). Softmax values are then computed among those candidates only (Chen et al.,
076   2019; 2021; 2022). YouTube, for instance, employs this technique for video recommendation (Chen
077   et al., 2019). TB-exp allows for exploration in the close embedding neighborhood of a given state;
078   however, it restricts the number of candidate actions based on technical considerations rather than
079   optimal convergence properties. Although exploring beyond this restricted neighborhood might be
080
      beneficial, finding the best way to do so in large-scale settings remains an open research question.
081   In this paper, we propose to address this important question. Our work focuses on the specific
082   setting where actions are represented by embedding vectors of dimension d ≥ 2 with unit norm, i.e.,
083   embedding vectors lying on the d-dimensional unit hypersphere. As detailed in Section 2, this setting
084   aligns with many real-world recommender system applications. Our contributions are as follows:
085
086             • We introduce von Mises-Fisher exploration (vMF-exp), a scalable method for exploring
087               large sets of actions represented by hyperspherical embedding vectors. vMF-exp involves
088
                  initially sampling a state embedding vector using a von Mises-Fisher distribution (Fisher,
                  1953), then exploring this representation’s nearest neighbors. Our proposed strategy scales
089
                  to millions of candidate actions and, unlike TB-exp, does not restrict exploration to a
090
                  specific neighborhood.
091
092             • We provide a comprehensive analysis of vMF-exp, demonstrating that, under certain the-
093               oretical assumptions, it asymptotically maintains the same probability of exploring each
094               action as the popular B-exp method, while overcoming its scalability issues. Consequently,
095               vMF-exp serves as a scalable alternative to B-exp for effectively exploring large action sets.
096
097             • While our analysis remains general, we also offer a real-world example of a vMF-exp usage.
098
                  We describe how, in 2024, we have deployed vMF-exp at scale on the music streaming
                  service XXX1 to recommend “Mixes inspired by” playlists. This application, backed by
099
                  successful A/B tests on millions of users, confirms the empirical relevance of vMF-exp.
100
101             • We release a Python implementation of vMF-exp on GitHub to encourage its future use.
102
103   The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. We introduce our problem more formally in
104   Section 2. We propose the vMF-exp method in Section 3. We present our theoretical analysis in
105   Section 4, we discuss our experiments on XXX in Section 5, and we conclude in Section 6.

      2.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
111
112   Notation In this paper, we consider an RL agent sequentially selecting actions within a set In =
113   {1, 2, . . . , n} of n ∈ N⋆ actions. Each action i ∈ In is represented by a distinct low-dimensional
114   vectorial representation Xi ∈ Rd , i.e., by an embedding vector or simply an embedding2 , for
115   some fixed dimension d ∈ N with d ≥ 2 and d ≪ n. Additionally, we assume all vectors
116   have a unit Euclidean norm, i.e., ∥Xi ∥2 = 1, ∀i ∈ In . They form a set of embeddings noted
117
      Xn = {Xi , 1 ≤ i ≤ n} ∈ (S d−1 )n , where S d−1 is the d-dimensional unit hypersphere (Fisher,
      1953): S d−1 = {x ∈ Rd : ∥Xi ∥2 = 1}.
118
119   We also assume the availability of an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) (Johnson et al., 2019; Li
120   et al., 2019) search engine. Using this engine, for any vector V ∈ S d−1 , the nearest neighbor of V
121   among Xn in terms of inner product similarity (equal to the cosine similarity, for unit vectors (Tan
122   et al., 2016)), called Xi⋆V , can be retrieved in a sublinear time complexity with respect to n. Although
123
      ANN engines are parameterized based on a trade-off between efficiency and accuracy, we make
      the simplifying assumption that Xi⋆V is the actual nearest neighbor of V , which we later discuss in
124
125
      Section 4.3. Formally, i⋆V = arg maxi∈In ⟨V , Xi ⟩.
126   Returning to the illustrative example of Section 1, Xn would represent embeddings associated with
127   each song of the catalog In of the music streaming service. In this case, n would be on the order
128   of several millions (Bendada et al., 2020; Jacobson et al., 2016; Schedl et al., 2018). The RL agent
129   would be the recommender system sequentially recommending these songs to users. Normalizing
130   embeddings is a common practice in both academic and industrial recommender systems (Afchar
131
      et al., 2023; Bontempelli et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023; Schedl et al., 2018) to mitigate popularity
      biases, as vector norms often encode popularity information on items (Afchar et al., 2023; Chen
132
      et al., 2023). Normalizing embeddings also prevents inner products from being unbounded, avoiding
133
      overflow and underflow numerical instabilities (LeCun et al., 2015).
134
135   At time t, the agent considers a state vector Vt ∈ S d−1 , noted V for brevity. It selects the next action
136   in In , whose relevance is evaluated by a reward provided by the environment. In our example, the
137
      agent would recommend the next song to continue the playlist, based on the previous song whose
      embedding V acts as the current state. In this case, the reward might be based on user feedback,
138
      such as liking or skipping the song (Bontempelli et al., 2022). The agent may select i⋆V , i.e., exploit
139
      i⋆V (Sutton and Barto, 2018). Alternatively, it may rely on an exploration strategy to select another In
140   element. Formally, an exploration strategy P is a policy function (Sutton and Barto, 2018) that, given
141   V , selects each action i ∈ In with a probability P (i | V ) ∈ [0, 1].
142
143   Objective Our goal in this paper is to develop a suitable exploration strategy for our specific setting,
144   where hyperspherical embedding vectors represent actions, and the number of actions can reach
145   millions. Precisely, we aim to obtain an exploration scheme meeting the following properties:
146
147          • Scalability (P1): we consider an exploration scalable if the time required to sample actions
148            given a vector V is at most the time needed for the ANN engine to retrieve the nearest neigh-
149
               bor, which is typically achieved in a sublinear time complexity with respect to n. Scalability
               is a mandatory requirement for exploring large action sets with millions of elements.
150
151          • Unrestricted radius (P2): Radius(P | V ) is the number of actions with a non-zero probability
152
               of being explored given a state V . While exploring actions too far from V might be
               suboptimal (e.g., resulting in poor recommendations), it is crucial that exploration is not
153
               restricted to a specific radius by construction. Such a restriction could prevent the agent
154
               from exploring relevant actions that lie beyond this radius. An unrestricted radius ensures
155            that the exploration strategy remains flexible and capable of adapting to various contexts,
156            allowing for the exploration of relevant actions regardless of their embedding position.
157
             • Order preservation (P3): order is preserved when the probability of selecting the action
158
               i given the state V is a strictly increasing function of ⟨V , Xi ⟩. More formally, order
159            preservation requires ∀(i, j) ∈ In2 , ⟨V , Xi ⟩ > ⟨V , Xj ⟩ =⇒ P (i | V ) > P (j | V ).

      2.2   L IMITATIONS OF E XISTING E XPLORATION S TRATEGIES
166
167   Finding an exploration strategy that simultaneously meets these three properties is essential for effec-
168   tive exploration in RL problems with large action sets and embedding representations. Nonetheless,
169   existing exploration strategies suffer from limitations that motivate our work in this paper.
170
171   Random and ε-greedy Exploration The most straightforward example of an exploration strategy
172   would be the random (uniform) policy, where Prand (i | V ) = n1 , ∀i ∈ In . A popular variant is the
173   ε-greedy strategy (Sutton and Barto, 2018). With a probability ε ∈ [0, 1], the agent would choose
174
      the next action uniformly at random. With a probability 1 − ε, it would exploit the most relevant
      action based on its knowledge. Random and ε-greedy exploration strategies are scalable (P1), as
175
      elements of In can be uniformly sampled in O(1) time (Cormen et al., 2022). Additionally, they
176
      verify P2. Indeed, Radius(Prand |V ) = n since every action can be selected. However, these strategies
177   ignore embeddings at the sampling phase and do not achieve order preservation (P3). This is a
178   significant limitation, reinforced by the fact that these policies have a maximal radius. As explained
179   in Section 1, in large action sets, many actions are often irrelevant, e.g., most songs from the musical
180   catalog would constitute poor recommendations given an initial state (Tomasi et al., 2023). Exploring
181   each action/song with equal probability, including inappropriate ones, could result in negative user
182   feedback and a poor perception of the service (Tomasi et al., 2023).
183
184   Boltzmann Exploration To address the limitations of random exploration, one can sample actions
185   according to their embedding similarity with V . The prevalent approach in RL is Boltzmann
186   Exploration (B-exp) (Amin et al., 2021; Cesa-Bianchi et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2021; Sutton and
187
      Barto, 2018), which employs the Boltzmann distribution for action sampling:

      at which the Voronoi cell shrinks depends on the dimension of the hypersphere, which explains why
347
      the second term of Equation (9) depends on d. Note that this is not the case for B-exp. Consequently,
348
      for large values of d, one may require a higher number of actions n before the asymptotic behavior
349   of Equation (7) is observed. For this reason, it is useful to obtain a more precise approximation of
350   PvMF-exp (a | n, d, V, κ) when d increases, which we provide in the next section.

420    Limitations and Future Work While we believe our study offers valuable insights into vMF-exp,
421    several limitations must be acknowledged. Most notably, our theoretical guarantees are currently
422    restricted to the setting of Section 4.1 where embeddings are i.i.d. and uniform vectors. Although, in
423
       practice, vMF-exp can be used with hyperspherical embeddings from other distributions, we do not yet
       provide guarantees in these cases. For instance, studying vMF-exp in clustered embedding settings, as
424
       is sometimes the case with music recommendation embeddings (Afchar et al., 2023) (where clusters
425
       can, e.g., summarize music genres (Salha-Galvan et al., 2022)), could be insightful. We believe that
426    this future work should benefit from the methods used to derive the non-trivial demonstration for
427    the uniform distribution case. Section 5 will demonstrate the practical value of vMF-exp on song
428    embeddings that do not explicitly comply with Section 4.1, but further mathematical investigation
429    would be warranted. In future work, we will also study the second-order term of Proposition 4.4,
430    which could be relevant for large values of κ, and the impact of errors from the ANN engine. While
431    we assumed this engine returns exact neighbors, this may not hold for very large action sets (Johnson
       et al., 2019) and, intuitively, could cause minor exploration perturbations.

444   5     A PPLICATION TO L ARGE -S CALE M USIC R ECOMMENDATION
445
446   Our analysis of vMF-exp in Section 4 was intentionally general, as the method can be applied to
447   various problem settings. In this Section 5, we showcase a real-world application of vMF-exp.
448
449   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
450
451   We consider the “Mixes inspired by” feature of the global music streaming service XXX. This
452   recommender system is deployed at scale and available on the homepage of this service. As shown in
453   Figure 3, it displays a personalized shortlist of songs, selected from those previously liked by each user.
454   A click on a song generates a playlist of 40 songs “inspired by“ the initial one, with the aim of helping
455   users discover new music within a catalog including several millions of recommendable songs.
456   To generate playlists, XXX leverages a collaborative filtering model (Koren and Bell, 2015). This
457   model learns unit norm song embedding representations of dimension d = 128 by factorizing a
458   mutual information matrix based on song co-occurrences in various listening contexts, using singular
459   value decomposition (SVD) (Banerjee and Roy, 2014). Inner product proximity in the resulting
460   embedding space aims to reflect user preferences. When a user selects an initial song, the model
461   retrieves its embedding, then (approximately) identifies its neighbors in the embedding space using
462
      the efficient Faiss library (Johnson et al., 2019) for ANN. Currently, XXX generates the entire playlist
      at once in production. The service is considering RL approaches to, instead, recommend songs one
463
      by one while adapting to user feedback on previous songs of the playlist (likes, skips, etc.). However,
464
      as explained in Section 1, adopting such approaches would require exploring millions of possible
465   actions/songs, significantly increasing the complexity of this task. In this section, we continue
466   generating “Mixes inspired by” playlists all at once, but take a step towards RL by comparing three
467   methods for exploring large action sets of millions of songs:
468
469          • vMF-exp: we use the embedding of the user’s selected song as the initial state V . We
470            sample a random state embedding Ṽ according to the vMF distribution, using the estimator
471            of Banerjee et al. (2005) to tune κ (see Equation (4) of Sra (2012)). Finally, we recommend
472            the 40 nearest neighbors of Ṽ in the embedding space according to the ANN engine.
473          • TB-exp: comparing vMF-exp to full B-exp is practically intractable at this scale. We compare
474            vMF-exp to TB-exp with a similar κ. We first retrieve the m = 500 nearest neighbors of the
475            initial song in the embedding space, according to the ANN engine. Then, we generate the
476            playlist by sampling 40 songs from these 500 using a truncated Boltzmann distribution.
477          • Reference: we also compare vMF-exp to a baseline that retrieves the 500 nearest neighbors
478            of the initial song using ANN, then shuffles them randomly to generate a playlist of 40 songs.
479
480   In early 2024, we conducted an online A/B test on XXX to compare these exploration strategies in
481   real conditions. The test involved millions of users worldwide, randomly split and unaware of the test.
482
483   5.2   R ESULTS
484
485   Firstly, it is important to highlight that we were able to successfully deploy vMF-exp in XXX’s
      production environment, achieving a sampling latency of just a few milliseconds, comparable to the


                                                          9
486
      other methods. This industrial deployment on a service used by millions of users on a daily basis
487   confirms the claimed scalability of vMF-exp and its practical relevance for large-scale applications.
488
489   Using vMF-exp or TB-exp for exploration improved the daily number of recommended songs “liked”
490
      by users through “Mixes inspired by” (liking a song adds it to their list of favorites), compared to
      the reference baseline. For confidentiality, we do not report exact numbers of likes or users in each
491
      cohort, but present relative rates with respect to the reference. On average, users exposed to vMF-exp
492
      or TB-exp added 11% more recommended songs to their playlists than the reference cohort. These
493   differences were statistically significant at the 1% level (p-value < 0.01). No apparent differences
494   were observed between vMF-exp and TB-exp, showing that vMF-exp is competitive with TB-exp.
495
496
      In addition, vMF-exp, which does not suffer from the restricted radius of TB-exp, recommended
      more diverse playlists. We measured the average Jaccard similarity (Tan et al., 2016) of playlists
497
      generated from the same initial selection, to assess how similar the songs sampled from the same
498
      state embedding were, for each method. Results reveal that TB-exp had an average Jaccard similarity
499   35% higher (less diverse playlists) than vMF-exp, a statistically significant difference at the 1%
500   level (p-value < 0.01). Therefore, vMF-exp allowed for a more substantial exploration, without
501   compromising performance.
502
      At the time of writing, XXX continues to use vMF-exp for “Mixes inspired by” recommendations.
503
      Playlists are still generated at once, but our work equips this service with an effective strategy to
504
      explore their large and embedded action set of millions of songs. This opens interesting avenues for
505   further investigation of RL for recommendation. In the near future, XXX will launch tests involving
506   actor-critic RL models (Konda and Tsitsiklis, 1999; Sutton and Barto, 2018) to explore and generate
507   songs sequentially based on user feedback.

      6   C ONCLUSION
511
      In conclusion, the primary contribution of this article is the development of vMF-exp, a scalable
512
      method for exploring large action sets in RL problems where hyperspherical embedding vectors rep-
513
      resent actions. We have shown that, under theoretical conditions, vMF-exp asymptotically maintains
514   the same probability of exploring each action as the popular B-exp method while overcoming its scal-
515   ability issues. Additionally, unlike the TB-exp workaround, which restricts exploration to a specific
516   neighborhood, vMF-exp allows for unrestricted exploration. This makes vMF-exp a valuable tool for
517   RL researchers and practitioners aiming to explore large action sets with hyperspherical embeddings,
518   offering a suitable alternative to both B-exp and TB-exp. We have also discussed the limitations of our
519   work, suggesting directions for future research. While our analysis has been general, the final part of
520   this article has also provided a real-world application of vMF-exp. Specifically, we have successfully
521   deployed vMF-exp on the music streaming service XXX, where it has been used for months to better
522
      explore songs to recommend to millions of users. This application highlights the practical relevance
      of our work and will facilitate future RL research and large-scale experiments on XXX.

1485   E    L INK WITH T HOMPSON S AMPLING
1486
1487   At first glance, one might draw some similarities between vMF-exp and Thompson Sampling (TS)
1488   with Gaussian prior for contextual bandits (Chapelle and Li, 2011). Admittedly, vMF-exp shares a
1489   common spirit with TS, where action selection is preceded by sampling individual weights according
1490   to a Normal distribution centered on an observed context/state vector. However, vMF-exp also
1491   presents two major differences:
1492
             • Firstly, in vMF-exp, vector sampling is performed according to a vMF hyperspherical
1493
               distribution, centered on the state embedding vector V . This choice of distribution ensures
1494           that vectors with the same inner product with the state vector have the same probability of
1495           being sampled, as illustrated in Figure 1(a). This aligns better with the similarity used to
1496           retrieve nearest neighbors and, as emphasized in this paper, leads to probabilities of exploring
1497           actions asymptotically comparable to Boltzmann Exploration (with better scalability) under
1498           the theoretical assumptions of Section 4.1.
1499         • Secondly, vMF-exp is not designed to maximize the expected reward of a policy in an
1500           RL or contextual bandit environment and does not impose any parameter update strategy.
1501           Instead, it serves as an action selection tool for any scenario where policy updates cannot be
1502           performed regularly (as in the batch RL setting commonly found in industrial applications),
1503           yet broad exploration must still be guaranteed between consecutive updates.
