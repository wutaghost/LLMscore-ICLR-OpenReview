033                                                       A BSTRACT
034
035            Recent advance in feed-forward 3D Gaussian splatting has enable remarkable
036            multi-view 3D scene reconstruction or single-view 3D object reconstruction but
037            single-view 3D scene reconstruction remain under-explored due to inherited ambi-
038            guity in single-view. We present studentSplat, the first single-view 3D Gaussian
039            splatting method for scene reconstruction. To overcome the scale ambiguity and
040            extrapolation problems inherent in novel-view supervision from a single input, we
041            introduce two techniques: 1) a teacher-student architecture where a multi-view
042
               teacher model provides geometric supervision to the single-view student during
               training, addressing scale ambiguity and encourage geometric validity; and 2) an
043
               extrapolation network that completes missing scene context, enabling high-quality
044
               extrapolation. Extensive experiments show studentSplat achieves state-of-the-
045            art single-view novel-view reconstruction quality and comparable performance
046            to multi-view methods at the scene level. Furthermore, studentSplat demon-
047            strates competitive performance as a self-supervised single-view depth estimation
048            method, highlighting its potential for general single-view 3D understanding tasks.

051   1   I NTRODUCTION
052
053   3D reconstruction is an essential task in robotics (Yandun et al., 2020; Han et al., 2022), naviga-
      tion (Davison, 2003; Kazerouni et al., 2022), virtual reality (Bruno et al., 2010), and content cre-


                                                                  1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ation (Jun & Nichol, 2023; Tang et al., 2023a). Advances in deep learning have enabled remarkable
055   progress in 3D reconstruction (Sitzmann et al., 2019; Mildenhall et al., 2021; Truong et al., 2023;
056   Kerbl et al., 2023) through per-scene optimization using a large number of views. Recently, efficient
057   feed-forward methods (Yu et al., 2021; Charatan et al., 2024) have been proposed to take a sparse
058   set of input views and construct the 3D reconstruction, greatly improving efficiency. However, these
059   methods require not only multi-view input but also the corresponding camera poses. Obtaining ac-
060   curate camera poses usually involves a time and computation-intensive pipeline (Ullman, 1979) and
061   a large number of camera views or additional specialized networks (Kendall et al., 2015; Yin & Shi,
062   2018; Peng et al., 2019), which hinders the efficiency of feed-forward sparse-view 3D reconstruc-
063
      tion. Single-view 3D reconstruction relaxes the requirements on both multi-view input and camera
      poses, serving as a more generalized alternative. Due to the inherent ambiguity in single-view in-
064
      put, current single-view 3D reconstruction works (Yu et al., 2021; Szymanowicz et al., 2024) only
065
      operate at the object level.
066
067   In this work, we aim to expand single-view 3D object reconstruction to the scene level and propose
068   a model capable of performing single-view 3D scene reconstruction using only multi-view supervi-
069
      sion (i.e., no ground truth 3D annotations). In addition to the generalizability improvements from
      this extension, single-view 3D scene reconstruction holds the potential to perform self-supervised
070
      single-view vision tasks such as single-view depth estimation (Li & Snavely, 2018) and aid semantic
071
      segmentation (Zhang et al., 2010; Schön et al., 2023). Finally, a single-view 3D scene reconstruction
072   model can be applied to the results from a text-to-image generation model (Rombach et al., 2022)
073   to achieve text-to-3D scene generation without separate training.
074
075
      To enable single-view 3D reconstruction, we adopt the 3D Gaussian splatter (3DGS) (Kerbl et al.,
      2023) representation. We identify and address two main problems in single-view 3DGS: scale ambi-
076
      guity and extrapolation. We tackle these problems by proposing studentSplat, the first single-view
077
      3DGS model at the scene level. Since the unknown scale can be inferred when at least two input
078   views are provided but is impossible using one input view (Charatan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024),
079   our core design is to use a multi-view teacher model to estimate the 3D structure up to a scale and su-
080   pervise the single-view student model using the teacher’s estimation. Moreover, unlike a multi-view
081   model that can bound the novel views by the input view frustums, a single-view model is required
082   to extrapolate due to occlusion and camera view changes, which can lead to distortion of the 3DGS.
083   We propose an extrapolator to complete the missing context in renderings before computing the
084   novel-view reconstruction loss, both performing extrapolation and minimizing distortion. Extensive
085   experiments show that our method can achieve good 3DGS on different benchmarks. Addition-
086
      ally, our method has the potential to connect 3DGS to self-supervised single-view vision tasks by
      demonstrating comparable performance to a self-supervised single-view depth estimation method.
087
088   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
089
090          • Propose the first single-view 3D scene Gaussian splatting model that does not require rela-
091            tive camera poses during inference.
092          • Address the extrapolation problem in single-view 3D scene reconstruction, which reduces
093            distortion and produces out-of-context regions.
094
095          • Bridge the gap between multi-view 3D Gaussian splatting and self-supervised single-view
096
               depth estimation, expanding the applications of 3D Gaussian splatting models.

      2.1   3D R EPRESENTATION
101
102   Numerous 3D representations have been proposed to accommodate different applications. Point
103   clouds are used in many applications (Ullman, 1979; Schönberger & Frahm, 2016; Nichol et al.,
104   2022) where the geometric shape is important. Recently, Neural Radiance Field (NeRF) (Mildenhall
105   et al., 2021) is proposed to learn a view-based rendering function from multi-view supervision, but
106   this learned function does not directly represent the geometric shape. 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) is an
107   efficient alternative representation similar to point clouds. Additionally, the efficient differentiable
      rendering implementation of 3DGS enables direct optimization of point clouds (3D Gaussians).

      This representation allows us to connect novel-view reconstruction to geometric reconstruction in
109   an end-to-end manner.

      2.2   F EED - FORWARD M ULTI - VIEW 3D R ECONSTRUCTION
112
113   NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) is one of the most popular representations for multi-view 3D re-
114   construction. PixelNeRF (Yu et al., 2021) and GRF (Trevithick & Yang, 2021) were among the
115   earlier works that used a feed-forward network to produce radiance fields. Subsequent approaches
116   improved rendering performance by incorporating cross-view feature matching (Chen et al., 2021;
117   2023; Du et al., 2023), geometric encoding (Miyato et al., 2023), or target view information (Xu
118
      et al., 2024). Different from the predefined NeRF function, SRT (Sajjadi et al., 2022) used a trans-
      former to represent the rendering function. Another line of work closely related to ours was initiated
119
      by pixelSplat (Charatan et al., 2024), which directly predicted 3D Gaussians from multi-view im-
120
      ages. latentSplat (Wewer et al., 2024) improved rendering performance by operating in the latent
121   space, while MVSplat (Chen et al., 2024) incorporated cost-volume to improve both efficiency and
122   performance. In contrast to previous approaches, our method requires only one input view, greatly
123   improving the generalizability and versatility of the 3DGS model. Additionally, our method con-
124   nects multi-view 3DGS to single-view vision tasks by learning one model that works on both tasks.
125
126   2.3   F EED - FORWARD S INGLE - VIEW 3D R ECONSTRUCTION
127
128   Single-view 3D reconstruction usually works at the object level. Unlike their multi-view coun-
129   terparts, single-view 3D reconstruction requires extrapolation. Therefore, generative methods like
130   diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a; Tang et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023b) are
131
      used to complete the reconstruction. Similar to multi-view 3D reconstruction, radiance fields are
      popular among the rendering methods (Liu et al., 2023a; Qian et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Tang
132
      et al., 2023b; Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). TARS (Duggal & Pathak, 2022) learns
133
      the deformation between 2D images and 3D objects. Recently, more approaches (Szymanowicz
134   et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2023a) have started using 3DGS for 3D reconstruction. Many other ap-
135   proaches (Nichol et al., 2022; Jun & Nichol, 2023) directly supervise the network using 3D object
136   annotations. Additionally, some methods use learns directly from ground truth 3D annotation (Yin
137   et al., 2021; Piccinelli et al., 2024). All existing work in single-view 3D reconstruction either re-
138   quires 3D supervision or only works at the object level. In contrast, our method not only works at
139   the scene level without 3D annotations but also has the potential to aid single-view vision tasks.

181   3.2   T EACHER - STUDENT M ODEL
182
183
      The aim of the teacher-student model is to solve the scale ambiguity problem during training time
      to enable single-view 3DGS for the student model with valid 3D geometric structure. Unlike their
184
      multi-view counterparts, a single-view model only accepts one view, making it difficult for the model
185
      itself to estimate the correct relative scale without cross-view feature matching and triangulation.
186
187   Using the teacher model geometric supervision. Unlike previous approaches (Nichol et al., 2022;
188   Piccinelli et al., 2024), we do not have access to ground truth 3D annotations. Despite the lack of
189
      3D annotations, during training time, multiple views are provided, and cross-view feature matching
      can be performed to estimate the Gaussian center for each pixel in the context view with an implicit
190
      relative scale (Charatan et al., 2024). Thus, using the teacher model fθK , we can convert the dateset
191
      from {(I i , P i )}N         i   i    i N
                         i=1 to {(I , P , µt )}i=1 . Then, in addition to the photometric loss computed from
192
      the target view {(I , P )}j=1 , we supervise the student model’s Gaussian center predictions µis
                            j    j K
193
      using the teacher’s Gaussian centers µit : Lgeo = λgeo ∥µit − µis ∥, where ∥ · ∥ is the L1 loss.
194
195   Regularizing local structure consistency. The L1 loss used in Lgeo lacks consideration of the lo-
196   cal structure which is prone to distortions in the less confident region such as the boundaries between
197   the in- and out-of context region. To construct good 3D Gaussians and minimize distortions, we also
198   need to encourage consistency in the local structure. Following previous work (Li & Snavely, 2018)
199
      that matches the depth map gradients to the ground truth depth map, we match the gradients of the
      teacher and student Gaussian centers using Lgrad = λgrad ∥∇3D µit − ∇3D µis ∥. Unlike previous
200
      work (Li & Snavely, 2018) that defines the depth difference between nearby pixels as the gradient
201
      map (i.e., only the z value is used for gradient computation), we propose a new definition of gradient
202   ∇3D that uses the 3D Euclidean distance (i.e., all x, y, and z values are used for gradient compu-
203   tation) between nearby pixels as the gradient to accommodate 3D structure. This new definition is
204   better aligned with 3D gradient matching.
205
      Discussion. The teacher model estimates only the relative scale, and consequently, the student model
206
      operates on the same relative scale. The teacher model will not be used at inference time. Therefore,
207
      we only require one input view, in other words, we relax the requirement for multiple input views
208   and their corresponding camera poses, to perform the 3D reconstruction, which greatly improves
209   the generalizability. More importantly, the resulting model naturally works as a single-view depth
210   estimation model, connecting the 3D reconstruction task to single-view vision tasks, which goes
211   beyond the capabilities of the teacher model.

213   3.3   E XTRAPOLATION
214
215   Unlike multi-view scenarios where the photometric novel view reconstruction loss can be formu-
      lated using interpolation only (i.e., enclosing the novel camera view inside the context camera view


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      frustums), single-view 3D reconstruction inevitably needs to extrapolate when computing the novel
217   view reconstruction loss. This extrapolation can lead to distortion in the extrapolating region as there
218   is no direct visual information. In the case of 3DGS, some 3D Gaussians will be forced to cover the
219   extrapolation region to minimize the photometric loss, which compromises the geometric validity.
220
      Extrapolating the missing context. Although the teacher supervision will encourage the Gaussian
221
      centers to represent valid geometric shapes, the missing region will create a large photometric loss,
222
      which encourages spurious relationships. To minimize this unnecessary photometric loss, we need
223   to either mask out the missing context during loss computation or fill the extrapolating region with
224   additional pixels to avoid noisy gradient flow. We select the latter approach to achieve two function-
225   alities: 1) guide the photometric loss to the correct Gaussians to minimize spurious relationships,
226   and 2) perform extrapolation on the missing context to improve the novel-view reconstruction. We
227   repurpose techniques from (Luo et al., 2018; Rückert et al., 2022) to achieve these functionalities.
228   Instead of directly supervising the rasterized novel view Iˆj = Rastrizer(Pj |µi , αi , Σi , ci ) , we fur-
229   ther process the novel-view reconstructions through a network gθ1 and supervise the output gθ1 (Iˆj )
230   using the photometric loss Lphoto = λl2 ∥gθ1 (Iˆj ) − I j ∥2 + λlpips LPIPS(gθ1 (Iˆj ), I j ), where ∥ · ∥2
231   is the L2 loss and LPIPS is the Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (Zhang et al., 2018)
232   computed using VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014) features.
233
      Using composition to guide gradient flows. Directly applying gθ1 will prevent the rasterizer from
234
      getting direct supervision, which can harm the reconstruction quality. The ideal situation is to sep-
235   arate the missing context and the visible context using a confidence weight matrix W and treat
236   their losses differently, but this separation is unknown before obtaining the 3D reconstruction. How-
237   ever, we can estimate the missing context using alpha compositing of the 3DGS. More specifi-
238   cally, we construct W by composing the αi . Intuitively, the missing context is less visible and
239   has lower αi whereas the visible context should have αi = 1. We compose the novel view as
240   Iˆcj = gθ1 (Iˆj ) ⊙ (1 − W j ) + Iˆj ⊙ W j , where W j = Rastrizer(Pj |µi , αi , Σi , 1). Then, we can
241   guide the gradients computed from Lphoto = λl2 ∥Iˆcj − I j ∥2 + λlpips LPIPS(Iˆcj , I j ) for the miss-
242   ing context to the extrapolation network, but the gradients for the context to the rasterizer, and the
243   rasterizer always gets direct supervision from the reconstruction loss. Additionally, the existence of
244   W allows the student model to balance between the completeness and the confidence of the recon-
245   struction by generating lower opacity for the regions with less confidence, since g 1 θ can still fill the
246   less opaque area to minimize the loss. On the other hand, W cannot collapse to zero, as g 1 θ will
247
      not be able to fill anything without context. Finally, the learned W can be used during inference to
      identify the missing context.
248
249   Discussion. Better extrapolation networks, such as diffusion-based methods (lkwq007, 2023), can
250   be applied to achieve better novel-view reconstruction quality, but they make the training pipeline
251   more complicated. We choose a feed-forward network (i.e., a pre-trained GAN network) to match
252   the base training pipeline and preserve efficiency. The main goal of the extrapolator here is to direct
253
      the gradient flow to minimize artifacts. The ability to learn W is more important than generating
      the best extrapolation result; as long as some level of extrapolation can be achieved and the learned
254
      context mask W is accurate, we can apply more elaborate extrapolation methods, such as differential
255
      diffusion (Levin & Fried, 2023), during inference using the generated context mask. We visualize W
256   in the Appendix C. Because of the introduction of the extrapolator, we can use the student network
257   to produce additional views by providing fake camera poses. This way, assuming the teacher model
258   performs better than the student model, we can use the teacher model to process the student model’s
259   output views to further improve the reconstruction result. This setting is detailed in the Appendix D.

      4.5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
474
475   We demonstrate, using studentSplat, the possibility of single-view 3DGS at scene level, bridging the
476   gap between 3DGS and single-view depth estimation. With its modular design, studentSplat allows
477   for versatile applications (see Appendix C and D) and easy incorporation of better modules.
478
      Limitations and future direction. Our method relies on the teacher model, thus inheriting the
479
      limitations of the teacher model. It would be interesting to eliminate the need for the teacher model
480   to further improve the capability of single-view scene-level 3DGS. Additionally, training a single-
481   view 3DGS model is still more difficult than training its multi-view counterparts, so our method
482   cannot outperform the multi-view method in its current stage. Large-scale training is an interesting
483   direction to explore the capability of single-view 3DGS for both novel-view reconstruction and depth
484   estimation tasks. Furthermore, we expect our method to also aid other vision tasks like semantic
485   segmentation, which can be another direction to explore. Finally, as a proof-of-concept approach,
      many design aspects, such as model architecture and loss function design, can be optimized.

789   More training details. All our models are trained on two A10G GPUs with a total batch size of
790   2 for 300,000 iterations with the Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) optimizer. Each batch contains one
791
      training scene (i.e., two input views and four target views). For all experiments, we use an initial

792
      models are trained for 300,000 iterations. Same as MVSplat (Chen et al., 2024), the frame distance
793
      between two input views is gradually increased as the training progresses. For both RE10K (Zhou
794   et al., 2018) and ACID (Liu et al., 2021), we follow previous works (Charatan et al., 2024; Chen
