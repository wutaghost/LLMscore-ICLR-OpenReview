008                                                         A BSTRACT
009
010
               Deploying large language models in production requires simultaneous attention to efficiency and
011
               risk control. Prior work has shown the possibility to cut costs while maintaining similar accuracy,
012            but has neglected to focus on risk control. By contrast, here we present hierarchical chains with
013            multi-level abstention (HCMA), which use model-intrinsic uncertainty to delegate queries along the
014            LLM intelligence hierarchy, enabling training-free model switching based solely on black-box API
015            calls. Our framework presents novel trade-offs between efficiency and risk. For example, deploying
016            HCMA on MMLU cuts the error rate of Llama3 405B by 30% when the model is allowed to abstain
017            on 20% of the queries. To calibrate HCMA for optimal performance, our approach uses data-efficient
018            logistic regressions (based on a simple nonlinear feature transformation), which require only 50 or
019            100 labeled examples to achieve excellent calibration error (ECE), cutting ECE by 50% compared
020
               to naive Platt scaling. On free-form generation tasks, we find that chain-of-thought is ineffectual for
               selective prediction, whereas zero-shot prompting yields drives error to 0% on TruthfulQA at high
021
               abstention rates. As LLMs are increasingly deployed across computing environments with different
022
               capabilities (such as mobile, laptop, and cloud), our framework paves the way towards maintaining
023            deployment efficiency while putting in place sharp risk controls.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
030
      Given the recent excitement around large language models, researchers have been intensely focused on evaluating
      potentially transformative applications across domains. However, as the technology matures and its scale increases,
031
      efficiency starts to matter. In addition, real-world consequences begin to emerge, calling for greater risk control.
032
033   The NLP community has addressed the need for greater efficiency by optimizing the internal mechanics of the
034   transformer-based neural networks (Dao et al. (2022)), developing new sampling schemes (Leviathan et al. (2023)),
035   performing model compression and distillation (Hinton et al. (2015), Xu et al. (2024)), caching model responses (Bang
036
      (2023)), and routing queries between different models (Chen et al. (2023b), Jiang et al. (2023), Hu et al. (2024), Ag-
      garwal et al. (2024), Ding et al. (2024)). On risk control, researchers have considered probabilistic calibration and
037
      various approaches to selective prediction, in which a model is allowed to abstain from queries based on a confidence
038
      signal such as single-token or sequence-level probabilities (Xin et al. (2021)), entropy-based scores and consistency
039   measures (Manakul et al. (2023), Kuhn et al. (2023), Chen et al. (2024)), lightweight probes on hidden layer em-
040   beddings (Azaria & Mitchell (2023), Kossen et al. (2024)), or outputs of separate neural networks trained to predict
041   correctness (Xin et al. (2021), Yoshikawa & Okazaki (2023), Varshney & Baral (2023), Gupta et al. (2024), Cobbe
042   et al. (2021), Kadavath et al. (2022)).
043
      However, previous work has not simultaneously addressed efficiency and risk control. Following previous routing ap-
044
      proaches in which small models pass queries to larger models based on a confidence threshold (Kag et al. (2023), Chen
045   et al. (2023b), Aggarwal et al. (2024), Wang et al. (2024), Ding et al. (2024), Sakota et al. (2024)), we incorporate
046   selective prediction by adding “abstention thresholds” that let each model reject queries on behalf of the whole LLM
047   system. In this paper, we explore the use of LLM token probabilities to instantiate this system, which we call “hierar-
048   chical chains with multi-level abstention.” Although the most performant LLM confidence signals – when considered
049   in isolation – are based on hidden layer embeddings, repeated sampling, and neural-network correctness predictors,
050   these approaches are not suitable for meeting our twin goals of 1) improving efficiency while simultaneously imposing
051   risk control, and 2) creating a framework based on black-box API calls that is easily adaptable to new tasks without
052   requiring much data. By contrast, model-intrinsic probabilities have the advantage that they do not require white-box
053
      access to model internals, are widely available as part of API calls to third-party LLM inference providers, do not
      require large amounts of training data, and avoid compute overhead from repeated sampling.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
056
            • Uncertainty Quantification: our framework of hierarchical LLM chains relies on uncertainty quantification
057           to distinguish easy queries from difficult ones. Previous work has shown that on text classification tasks
058           (such as MMLU), the maximum softmax probability effectively predicts mistakes, even if it is not calibrated
059           (Hendrycks & Gimpel (2018), Plaut et al. (2024)). On natural language generation, the community has made
060           progress with several different approaches including prompting an LLM to verify whether a response to a
061           query is correct (Lin et al. (2022), Kadavath et al. (2022), Xiong et al. (2024)), assessing the consistency
062           between multiple sampled LLM outputs (Lin et al. (2024), Manakul et al. (2023), Kuhn et al. (2023), Aich-
063           berger et al. (2024), Nikitin et al. (2024)), and training lightweight probes on models’ internal states (Ren
064           et al. (2023), Azaria & Mitchell (2023), Chen et al. (2024), Kossen et al. (2024)).
065         • Probability Calibration: several methods have been proposed to make probabilistic predictions more cal-
066           ibrated (such that a forecast with probability 0.8 comes true about 80% of the time), including Platt scal-
067           ing (Platt (1999)), temperature scaling (Guo et al. (2017)), isotonic regression (Zadrozny & Elkan (2002)),
068           Bayesian binning (Naeini et al. (2015)), and more. Recent work has shown that state-of-the-art large language
069           models tend to have poor calibration, especially after reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)
070           (Ouyang et al. (2022), OpenAI et al. (2024), Plaut et al. (2024)). Due to its simplicity, temperature scaling
071           has emerged as the most popular post-processing method for calibration, though for the purposes of rigorous
072
              risk control it suffers from a lack of statistical grounding.
073         • Selective Prediction: this field studies the performance of machine learning models when they are allowed to
074           abstain on difficult queries. With the machine learning community, foundational work of El-Yaniv & Wiener
075           (2010), Geifman & El-Yaniv (2017), and others was centered on image classification. In NLP, selective
076           prediction has originally attracted limited interest (Xin et al. (2021), Varshney et al. (2022), Varshney & Baral
077           (2023), Ren et al. (2023), Yoshikawa & Okazaki (2023), Chen et al. (2023a)) but today has become largely
078
              subsumed in the emerging field of uncertainty quantification for LLMs, which uses similar performance
              metrics. We wish to highlight the SGR algorithm (Geifman & El-Yaniv (2017)) as a compelling technique
079
              for endowing selective prediction methods (including ours) with provable risk guarantees.
080
081         • Routing between Language Models: recent literature has reported cost savings (and, sometimes, perfor-
082           mance gains) from routing queries between different language models. In this area, many approaches focus
083           on horizontal routing between a collection of small language models in the hope of leveraging complemen-
084
              tary strengths (Lu et al. (2023), Hari & Thomson (2023), Lee et al. (2024), Sakota et al. (2024), Wang et al.
              (2024)). Other works consider vertical routing in which queries are explicitly first sent to a smaller model,
085
              which forwards difficult queries to a larger model (Wang et al. (2023), Kag et al. (2023), Chen et al. (2023b),
086
              Yue et al. (2024), Aggarwal et al. (2024), Ding et al. (2024)). However, these approaches have focused on
087           lowering the cost of LLM inference without simultaneously tackling selective prediction.
088
089
090   3   O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
091
092   First, we provide a novel formula explaining why uncertainty-based delegation between language models works. Sec-
093   ond, we introduce a nonlinear feature transformation that makes Platt scaling a highly effective calibration technique
094   for LLM token probabilities, providing an alternative to temperature scaling grounded in a rigorous statistical model
095   (logistic regression). Third, we present hierarchical chains with multi-level abstention (HCMA), the first (to our
096   knowledge) routing LLM model that incorporates selective prediction alongside cost efficiency. By computing the full
097
      two-dimensional Pareto frontier of efficient HCMA configurations on MMLU, using formulas we derive, we show that
      an LLM routing approach based on model-intrinsic probabilities improves over the selective prediction performance
098
      of single LLMs, in terms of strengthening risk control without paying more, or achieving similar risk control at the
099
      same (or lower) price. Finally, we note that chain-of-thought prompting yields poor selective prediction performance
100   on TruthfulQA compared to zero-shot prompting, calling for caution when applying established prompting techniques
101   in LLM uncertainty quantification.

104   4   M ATHEMATICAL F RAMEWORK
105
106   We present an LLM system called “hierarchical chains with multi-level abstention,” which is based on delegating
107   queries from small to large models, but allowing each model to abstain on behalf of the entire chain. First, we examine
      the act of delegation and explain why it works.

      4.1   W HY D OES D ELEGATION W ORK ?
109
110   By delegation, we mean passing queries from a small model to a larger model based on estimated difficulty. Prior
111   work has shown that this strategy outperforms random query assignment, but has not provided an explanation (Xin
112   et al. (2021)). Although the result is intuitively plausible, the situation is nuanced because differently sized models
113   seem to share a common sense of difficulty. Figure 1 shows this trend by exhibiting logistic regressions that predict
114   correctness of Llama3 8B, 70B, and 405B on MMLU solely based on the transformed probability of the 8B model.

144   Given that differently sized models share a common notion of difficulty, the effectiveness of delegation depends on
145   the net result of two opposing forces: the small model makes fewer mistakes because it only answers the easy queries,
146   but the large model makes more mistakes since it answers only difficult queries. Thus, the effectiveness of delegation
147   rests on the large model being less sensitive to incremental difficulty than the small model.
148
      The proposition below frames our reasoning in mathematical terms:
149
      Proposition 1. Compared to randomly assigning queries to a small language model Msm and Mlg , the reduction in
150
      error from forwarding queries based on a delegation decision D is

                               ∆E = Cov(1D , 1Mbg makes an error ) − Cov(1D , 1Msm makes an error ).             (1)
153
      In this formula, the covariances Cov(1D , 1Msm makes an error ) and Cov(1D , 1Mbg makes an error ) measure the impact of the
154
      delegation decision on each model’s propensity to make a mistake. Empirically, both covariances are positive if
155   D is based on query difficulty, since both models’ performance degrades with incremental difficulty. However, as
156   long as Cov(1D , 1Msm makes an error ) > Cov(1D , 1Mbg makes an error ), meaning that the smaller model is more sensitive to
157   difficulty, the change in error in (1) is negative, and delegation outperforms random query assignment.

242   The policy (2) of each model Mj in a HCMA M1 → ... → Mk requires an estimated probability of correctness
243   p̂Mj (·) for j = 1, 2, ..., k. As we rely on each model’s intrinsic token probabilities praw for uncertainty quantification,
244   obtaining p̂ boils down to calibrating praw .
245
      Different calibration techniques have been employed for post-processing the conditional probabilities of large language
246
      models. Originally proposed for computer vision networks, temperature scaling (Guo et al. (2017)) has emerged as
247
      a favored method because of its simplicity and high performance (Jiang et al. (2021)). However, Platt scaling (Platt
248   (1999)) is preferable for rigorous risk control, since it is based on a statistical model – logistic regression – for which
249   statisticians hafve developed an established catalog of confidence intervals and model diagnostics. Unfortunately,
250   standard Platt scaling does not work well for LLMs because its conditional probabilities tend to form a tight cluster
251   near 1.0, reflecting overconfidence (OpenAI et al. (2024), Plaut et al. (2024)).
252
      However, it is possible to make Platt scaling work very well by spreading out the clusters of overconfident probabilities.
253
      We propose simple nonlinear transformations that accomplish this by introducing asymptotes near praw = 0 and

      5     R ESULTS
272
      Our exploration of hierarchical chains with multi-level abstention (HCMA), based on model-intrinsic uncertainty,
273
      yields several findings. First, our nonlinear transformations make Platt scaling much more effective in calibrating
274
      LLM output probabilities, yielding a statistically grounded way of performing LLM calibration. Second, mapping out
275   the Pareto frontier of efficient HCMA configurations shows novel risk and efficiency trade-offs that outperform single-
276   model strategies for selective prediction with model-intrinsic uncertainty. Finally, we apply our calibrated correctness
277   prediction methodology to the open-ended QA benchmark TruthfulQA. We discover that chain-of-thought prompt-
278   ing reduces the utility of the abstention signal, highlighting the need for caution in applying established prompting
279   techniques in uncertainty quantification.

312   We study the risk and efficiency trade-offs attainable on MMLU using a HCMA consisting of the Llama3 8B, 70B,
313   and 405B models. To do this, we first tabulate achievable performance metrics – error, cost, and abstention rate –
314   for each 5-dimensional HCMA configuration (involving acceptance thresholds a8B , a70B and rejection thresholds r8B ,
315   r70B , and r405B ) by performing a grid search along the quantiles of the estimated correctness probabilities with 2.5%
316   resolution, resulting in >50 million distinct configurations. For the simulation, we use cost values of 0.3, 0.8, and
317   5.0 dollars per million tokens, which are close to the current pricing of LLM inference providers (Artificial Analysis
318   (2024)). From the achievable performance profiles, we compute the efficient Pareto frontier using the Skyline operator
319   (Börzsönyi et al. (2001)).
320   Figure 3 maps out the achievable error rates vs their cost, and colored by the abstention rate. Globally, we observe that
321   selective prediction based on model-intrinsic uncertainty effectively lowers the achievable error rates. At an inference
322   cost of around $1 per million tokens (the cost of Llama3 70B in the model), the graph shows a kink in the slope of
323   the relationship between error and cost, suggesting that increasing usage of Llama3 405B yields diminishing returns
      in terms of error vs cost.

439   On TruthfulQA, we observe that token probabilities derived from chain-of-thought based verification perform
440   poorly with our approach, since the transformed probabilities form tight clusters around 0 and 1. By contrast, using
441   a zero-shot prompt results in a smooth unimodal distribution, which is well-suited as a confidence signal. See Figure
442   5.
443   To ablate this finding, we also tried using a few-shot (but not chain-of-thought) based prompt for verification. The
444   results are intermediate: the distribution of transformed probabilities is unimodal (just as in the zero-shot case) but
445   not quite as symmetric, and the precision/recall curve for predicting incorrect answers is worse. Notably, these results
446   hold despite the fact that chain-of-thought gives the highest overall accuracy for correctness prediction. On
447   TruthfulQA, we observed accuracies for correctness prediction of 0.79 for few-shot with CoT, 0.74 for few-shot
448   without CoT, and 0.73 for zero-shot.
449   These results urge caution in applying established prompting techniques to the task of verifying LLM correctness.

484
485   Motivated by the observation that differently sized language models directionally agree on the difficulty of queries,
      we have presented hierarchical chains with multi-level abstention (HCMA), a routed LLM system that incorporates


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025



486
      selective prediction alongside cost efficiency. Given the increased usage of LLMs via black-box API calls, and the
487
      efficiency-driven need to avoid repeated LLM sampling, we base our routing and abstention decisions on LLM token
488
      probabilities. With our improved Platt scaling approach to calibrating these probabilities, our framework is easily
489   adaptable to new tasks without requiring much labeled data. We show that HCMAs efficiently outperform the selective
490   prediction performance of single LLMs, and that multi-level abstention (as opposed to only abstaining at the end of
491   the chain) is a beneficial part of our system’s architecture. Finally, we find that chain-of-thought prompting hinders
492   correctness prediction on TruthfulQA, showing the need for caution in applying established prompting techniques in
493   correctness prediction.
