009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) for semantic segmentation aims to transfer
012            knowledge from a labeled source domain to an unlabeled target domain, improving
013            model performance on the target dataset without additional annotations. Despite the
014            effectiveness of self-training techniques in UDA, they struggle to learn each class
015            in a balanced manner due to inherent class imbalance and distribution shift in both
016
               data and label space between domains. To address this issue, we propose Balanced
               Learning for Domain Adaptation (BLDA), a novel approach to directly assess and
017
               alleviate class bias without requiring prior knowledge about the distribution shift
018
               between domains. First, we identify over-predicted and under-predicted classes
019            by analyzing the distribution of predicted logits. Subsequently, we introduce a
020            post-hoc approach to align the positive and negative logits distributions across
021            different classes using anchor distributions and cumulative density functions. To
022            further consider the network’s need to generate unbiased pseudo-labels during
023            self-training, we couple Gaussian mixture models to estimate logits distributions
024            online and incorporate logits correction terms into the loss function. Moreover, we
025            leverage the resulting cumulative density as domain-shared structural knowledge
026            to connect the source and target domains. Extensive experiments on two standard
027
               UDA semantic segmentation benchmarks demonstrate that BLDA consistently
               improves performance, especially for under-predicted classes, when integrated into
028
               existing methods. Our work highlights the importance of balanced learning in UDA
029
               and effectively mitigates class bias in domain adaptive semantic segmentation.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Semantic segmentation is a fundamental computer vision task that assigns a semantic label to each
035   pixel in an image, enabling comprehensive image understanding. Despite the remarkable progress in
036   recent research (Long et al., 2015a; Chen et al., 2017b; Cheng et al., 2021; 2022), the performance
037   of these methods often significantly drops when applied to new target datasets. This performance
038   degradation stems from the differences between the source and target domains, challenging the
039
      generalization ability of these models. To address this issue, unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA)
      techniques have been extensively studied. UDA aims to bridge the domain gap by transferring
040
      knowledge from a labeled source domain to an unlabeled target domain, thereby improving the
041
      model’s performance on the target dataset without requiring additional annotations.
042
043   In previous work, self-training techniques (Tranheden et al., 2021; Hoyer et al., 2022a) have been
044   naturally introduced into UDA tasks to fully utilize the large amount of unlabeled target domain data,
045
      becoming a mainstream paradigm. This paradigm constructs a teacher network using a temporal
      aggregation mechanism, treats its predictions on the target domain as pseudo-labels, and gradually
046
      guides the student network’s learning. Despite achieving remarkable results, these methods struggle
047
      to learn each class in a balanced manner. Generally, the inherent class imbalance in segmentation
048   datasets (Cordts et al., 2016) (Fig.1(a)) leads networks to produce biased predictions towards head
049   classes, often studied as the long-tail problem (Van Horn & Perona, 2017; Buda et al., 2018; Liu
050   et al., 2019). However, in UDA, data and label distribution shifts between the training and test
051   data complicate the class bias. The network’s bias towards classes does not entirely depend on
052   the differences in class sample distribution. As shown in Fig.1(b), when a network trained on the
053   source domain is tested on the target domain, the performance degradation varies greatly across
      classes, distinguishing easy-to-transfer and hard-to-transfer classes. These factors jointly determine

100   assumptions are invalid because (1) the class distributions of the source and target domains differ, and
101   the target domain’s prior class distribution is unavailable; (2) the data distributions also differ, leading
102   to varying transfer difficulties across classes in cross-domain settings. This raises the question: How
103   to assess and alleviate class bias directly without requiring prior knowledge about the distribution
104
      shift between the two domains?

143   Our contributions can be summarized as follows: (1) We propose statistically analyzing the predicted
144   logits to directly assess the network’s bias towards classes. (2) We propose BLDA to estimate logit
145   correction terms online during UDA training to achieve balanced learning for each class. (3) We
146   demonstrate that BLDA can be easily integrated into existing UDA methods and consistently improves
147   performance on two standard UDA semantic segmentation benchmarks, significantly enhancing the
148   performance of under-predicted classes and confirming the effectiveness of our method.

      2.1   D OMAIN A DAPTIVE S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION
154
      Unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) aims to transfer semantic knowledge learned from labeled
155
      source domains to unlabeled target domains. Due to the ubiquity of domain gaps, UDA methods
156   have been widely studied in various computer vision tasks, such as image classification, object
157   detection, and semantic segmentation. UDA is crucial for semantic segmentation to avoid laborious
158   pixel-wise annotation in new target scenarios. Recent UDA approaches for semantic segmentation
159   can be categorized into two main paradigms: adversarial training-based methods (Toldo et al., 2020;
160   Tsai et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Ganin & Lempitsky, 2015; Hong et al., 2018; Long et al., 2015b)
161   and self-training-based methods (Tranheden et al., 2021; Hoyer et al., 2022a; Araslanov & Roth,
      2021; Zhang et al., 2021). Adversarial training-based methods learn domain-invariant representations


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      through a min-max optimization game, where a feature extractor is trained to confuse a domain
163   discriminator, aligning feature distributions across domains. Self-training-based methods, which
164   have come to dominate the field due to the domain-robustness of Transformers (Bhojanapalli et al.,
165   2021), generate pseudo labels for target images based on a teacher-student optimization framework.
166   The success of this paradigm relies on generating high-quality pseudo labels, with strategies such as
167   entropy minimization (Chen et al., 2019) and consistency regularization (Hoyer et al., 2023) being
168   developed for this purpose. However, due to the inherent class imbalance and distribution shift in
169   both data and label space between domains, networks often produce complicated class bias, which
170   is further exacerbated by confirmation bias in the self-training paradigm. Our method focuses on
171
      balanced learning in UDA training.
172
173   2.2   C LASS -I MBALANCED L EARNING
174
175   Class imbalance is a common problem in semantic segmentation, where the number of samples per
176
      class varies significantly. Existing methods address this issue through re-weighting or re-sampling
      techniques. Re-weighting methods assign different weights to classes during training, giving higher
177
      importance to under-represented classes (Cui et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2017; Cao et al., 2019). Re-
178
      sampling techniques modify the class distribution in the training data by over-sampling minority
179   classes or under-sampling majority classes (He et al., 2008; 2021). In UDA for semantic segmentation,
180   some approaches have introduced these strategies to alleviate class bias (Hoyer et al., 2022a; Araslanov
181   & Roth, 2021; Li et al., 2022). However, these methods are still empirical and focus on the single-
182   domain setting, which follows the assumptions that the test data and training data share the same
183   distribution in both data space and label space, without considering the additional challenges posed
184   by domain shift in UDA. In this work, we aim to access class bias directly and achieve balanced
185   learning for each class with no prior knowledge about the distribution shift between domains.

219
220   In this section, we first propose to assess the network’s prediction bias towards each class by
221   statistically analyzing the distribution of logits (Sec.3.3.2). Based on the above analysis, we define a
222
      post-hoc method to balance the network’s predictions (Sec.3.3.3). Furthermore, we introduce online

223
      density estimation as domain-shared knowledge to bridge the two domains (Sec.3.3.5).

269   distribution shifts exist in both data and label space between domains. Furthermore, these methods
      are empirical and lack generalization capability across various scenarios.

469
      improvements are obtained for under-predicted classes, such as sidewalk, fence, pole, light, and sign,
470
      which demonstrates that BLDA can mitigate the class bias with decreased standard deviation and thus

475
476   We conduct a series of ablation studies on the GTA.→CS. benchmark built with DAFormer (Hoyer

479
      Influence of scaling factor. As illustrated in Fig. 5, we explore the influence of different τ values on
480   evaluation metrics. The mAcc metric gradually increases as τ grows, reaching its peak performance
481   when τ = 1. Interestingly, the mIoU metric does not demonstrate a perfectly positive correlation with
482   the rise in mAcc. This discrepancy arises from the fact that the mIoU calculation is heavily impacted
483   by the imbalanced distribution of the test set, whereas mAcc serves as a class-balanced metric. We
484   comprehensively explain this phenomenon in Appendix C. Our method, which models class-balanced
485   learning, effectively boosts mAcc. However, to achieve improvements in mIoU, selecting a smaller
      scaling factor τ is necessary.

530   method reduces this distribution difference by aligning with the anchor distribution and achieves

533   5                C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we present Balanced Learning for Domain Adaptation (BLDA), a novel approach to ad-
536   dress class bias in unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) for semantic segmentation. BLDA analyzes
537   logits distributions to assess prediction bias and introduces an online logits adjustment mechanism to
538   balance class learning in both source and target domains. Our method effectively mitigates class bias,
539   promotes balanced learning, and enhances generalization to the target domain. Experimental results
      demonstrate consistent performance improvements on standard UDA benchmarks.

      In this section, we investigate the impact of class imbalance on the evaluation metrics mIoU and
813   mAcc in the context of semantic segmentation. We consider a multi-class problem with C classes,
814   where the number of samples in the i-th class is denoted as Ni . Let Pij represent the probability of
815   classifying a sample from the i-th class as the j-th class in the confusion matrix. For the purpose
816   of this analysis, we assume that the probabilities Pii and Pkk are balanced across all classes, i.e.,
817   Pii = Pkk = p, ∀i, k ∈ 1, 2, ..., C, and focus solely on the effect of class imbalance in terms of
818   sample numbers. Under this assumption, the calculation formula for mAcc can be written as:
819                                         C                      C
                                         1 X    Ni · Pii        1 X      p
820                            mAcc =                         =                                              (18)

823   As evident from the equation above, the sample numbers Ni cancel out in the calculation of mAcc,
824
      making it independent of the class imbalance in terms of sample numbers. Therefore, mAcc remains
      unaffected by class imbalance under the given assumptions. On the other hand, the calculation
825
      formula for mIoU is given by:
826

830   In contrast to mAcc, the sample numbers Ni do not cancel out in the calculation of mIoU. Con-
831   sequently, when the classes are imbalanced, i.e., the sample numbers Ni vary significantly across
832   classes, the IoU of classes with larger sample numbers will dominate the overall mIoU result. To
833   illustrate the impact of class imbalance on mIoU, let us consider a case where class k is a head class
834   with a significantly larger sample number Nk compared to a tail class i with sample number Ni . The
835   performance of class i will be greatly affected by class k through the term Nk · Pki in the denominator
836   of mIoU. For class i to have a fair contribution to mIoU, the probability Pki needs to be very small.
837   This implies that a balanced classifier may not be optimal for maximizing mIoU under class imbal-
838
      ance. Therefore, our proposed method implements a scaling factor τ to modulate the contributions of
      introduced logits offset. In contrast, mAcc is inherently unaffected by class imbalance, as the sample
839
      numbers Ni cancel out in its calculation formula. This means that a balanced classifier is indeed
840
      optimal for maximizing mAcc, and our method, which aims to balance the contributions of different
841   classes, aligns well with this objective and can achieve consistent improvements.

937   E    I NFLUENCE OF PARAMETERS S ETTING
938
939   In this section, we further study the influence of parameters setting introduced in BLDA, i.e., number
940   of Gaussian components K, momentum factor τ̃ , number of EM Loop T for GMM estimation and λ
941   for cumulative density estimation loss. All experiments are conducted with DAFormer (Hoyer et al.,
942   2022a) on GTA→CS.
943   Number of Gaussian Components K. As shown in Tab.5, we find that BLDA can work well even
944   when K = 1, since the logit distribution is naturally close to Gaussian. The model achieves the best
945   performance when K = 5. A larger K allows for more flexibility in modeling the logits distributions
946   but may also introduce noise. We choose K = 5 as a balance between model capacity and robustness.
947
      Momentum Factor τ̃ . The momentum factor τ̃ controls the speed of updating the GMM parameters.
948   When τ̃ = 0, performance becomes erratic because the logits from the current iteration alone are
949   not sufficient to model the distribution of all logits. A larger τ̃ leads to slower updates, retaining
950   the previously estimated distribution and making the estimation more stable but less adaptive. As
951   presented in Tab.6, setting τ̃ to 0.99 yields the best performance, suggesting that a relatively stable
952   estimation of the logits distributions is beneficial for the adaptation process.
953
      Number of EM Loop T . The number of EM loops T determines the number of iterations used to
954   update the GMM parameters in each training step. Tab.7 shows that the model is not sensitive to the
955   choice of T , since the convergence rate of GMM is faster than the rate of network update, and it can
956   be estimated well even when T = 1. We choose T = 3 for stable performance while considering
957   computational efficiency.
958
      Cumulative Density Estimation Loss Weight λ. The weight λ balances the cumulative density
959
      estimation loss with the segmentation loss. A higher λ enforces stronger domain alignment through
960   the cumulative density functions. As shown in Tab.8, λ = 0.2 provides the best performance gain.
961   An overly large λ may distract the model from learning the primary segmentation task, leading to
962   performance degradation.

1086   reduces this difference through aligning with the anchor distribution and achieves class-balanced

1310   L.1     D OMAIN A DAPTIVE S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION
1311
1312   Unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) transfers semantic knowledge learned from labeled source
1313   domains to unlabeled target domains. Due to the ubiquity of domain gaps, UDA methods have been
1314   widely studied in various computer vision tasks, such as image classification, object detection, and
1315
       semantic segmentation. UDA is crucial for semantic segmentation to avoid laborious pixel-wise
       annotation in new target scenarios.
1316
1317   Recent UDA approaches for semantic segmentation fall into two main paradigms: adversarial training-
1318   based methods (Toldo et al., 2020; Tsai et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Ganin & Lempitsky, 2015;
1319   Hong et al., 2018; Long et al., 2015b) and self-training-based methods (Tranheden et al., 2021; Hoyer
1320   et al., 2022a; Araslanov & Roth, 2021; Zhang et al., 2021). Adversarial training-based methods learn
1321
       domain-invariant representations through a min-max optimization game, where a feature extractor
       is trained to confuse a domain discriminator, aligning feature distributions across domains. Self-
1322
       training-based methods, which have become dominant in the field due to the domain-robustness of
1323
       Transformers (Bhojanapalli et al., 2021), generate pseudo labels for target images based on a teacher-
1324   student optimization framework. The success of this paradigm depends on generating high-quality
1325   pseudo labels, with strategies such as entropy minimization (Chen et al., 2019) and consistency
1326   regularization (Hoyer et al., 2023) being developed for this purpose.
1327
       Recently, several approaches have been proposed to tackle the challenges in UDA for semantic
1328
       segmentation: DTS (Huo et al., 2023) employs a Dual Teacher-Student Framework, promoting
1329
       the self-training paradigm to fully adapt models to the target domain by exploring different mix
1330   strategies. CDAC (Wang et al., 2023) introduces consistency constraints in attention to enhance
1331   the model’s cross-domain performance. RTea (Zhao et al., 2023) defines proxy tasks based on
1332   structural information and incorporates them into the self-training paradigm as additional supervision
1333   signals. Peng et al. (2023) apply Diffusion-based image translation techniques to directly mitigate the
1334   distribution differences between the target and source domains. DiGA (Shen et al., 2023) integrates
1335   distillation strategies and self-training through multi-stage training.
1336   However, due to the inherent class imbalance and distribution shift in both data and label space
1337   between domains, networks often exhibit complex class biases, which are further amplified by the
1338   confirmation bias inherent in the self-training paradigm. Our method aims to achieve balanced
1339   learning in UDA training to mitigate these issues and improve the overall performance of domain
1340   adaptation for semantic segmentation.

       L.2     C LASS -I MBALANCED L EARNING
1343
1344   Class imbalance is a prevalent issue in semantic segmentation, where the number of samples per
1345   class varies significantly. Existing methods tackle this problem through re-weighting or re-sampling
1346   techniques. Re-weighting methods assign different weights to classes during training, giving higher
1347   importance to under-represented classes (Cui et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2017; Cao et al., 2019; Liu et al.,
1348   2019). Re-sampling techniques modify the class distribution in the training data by over-sampling
1349   minority classes or under-sampling majority classes (He et al., 2008; 2021; He & Garcia, 2009; Kim
       et al., 2020; Chu et al., 2020).

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       Recent works have proposed various approaches to address class imbalance in different tasks. For
1351   object detection, Lin et al. (2017) propose a re-weighting method that assigns weights to samples
1352   based on their confidence, with harder samples receiving higher weights. In classification, Cui
1353   et al. (2019) define an effective number of samples for each class to weight different classes, while
1354   Menon et al. (2020) introduce a logit adjustment method that incorporates correction terms in logits,
1355   determined by prior knowledge of class distribution. For segmentation, Hoyer et al. (2022a) present
1356   a re-sampling technique that samples based on prior knowledge of class distribution, with more
1357   sampling for rare classes. Truong et al. (2023) considers class-imbalance as fairness problem and
1358   propose to model the context of structural dependency to tackle it.
1359   In UDA for semantic segmentation, some approaches have introduced these strategies to alleviate
1360   class bias (Hoyer et al., 2022a; Araslanov & Roth, 2021; Li et al., 2022). However, these methods are
1361   still empirical and focus on the single-domain setting, which assumes that the test data and training
1362   data share the same distribution in both data space and label space, without considering the additional
1363   challenges posed by domain shift in UDA.
1364   In contrast to these methods, our approach aims to directly address class bias and achieve balanced
1365   learning for each class without relying on prior knowledge about the distribution shift between
1366   domains. We evaluate class bias through logit sets in the form of a confusion matrix and explicitly
1367   balance components in the matrix using anchor distributions and cumulative density functions,
1368   implemented in an online self-training paradigm. Our method does not depend on prior knowledge of
1369   class distribution and instead directly models class bias based on the actual logit distribution, making
1370   it more adaptable to the challenges posed by domain shift in UDA.
