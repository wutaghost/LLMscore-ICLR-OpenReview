                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Autonomous agents powered by language models (LMs) have demonstrated
010
               promise in their ability to perform decision-making tasks such as web automation.
011            However, a key limitation remains: LMs, primarily optimized for natural language
012            understanding and generation, struggle with multi-step reasoning, planning, and
013            using environmental feedback when attempting to solve realistic computer tasks.
014            Towards addressing this, we propose an inference-time search algorithm for LM
015            agents to explicitly perform exploration and multi-step planning in interactive web
016            environments. Our approach is a form of best-first tree search that operates within
017            the actual environment space, and is complementary with most existing state-of-
018            the-art agents. It is the first tree search algorithm for LM agents that shows effec-
019
               tiveness on realistic web tasks. On the challenging VisualWebArena benchmark,
               applying our search algorithm on top of a GPT-4o agent yields a 39.7% relative
020
               increase in success rate compared to the same baseline without search, setting a
021
               state-of-the-art success rate of 26.4%. On WebArena, search also yields a 28.0%
022            relative improvement over a baseline agent, setting a competitive success rate of
023            19.2%. Our experiments showcase the effectiveness of search for web agents, and
024            we demonstrate that performance scales with increased test-time compute. We
025            conduct a thorough analysis of our results to highlight improvements from search,
026            limitations, and promising directions for future work. Our code and models are
027            publicly released at removed for review

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Building agents that can perceive, plan, and act autonomously has been a long standing goal of ar-
033
      tificial intelligence research (Russell & Norvig, 1995; Franklin & Graesser, 1996). In recent years,
      the advent of large language models (LMs) with strong general capabilities has paved the way to-
034
      wards building language-guided agents that can automate computer tasks. However, the best LM
035
      agents today are still far worse than humans. On the realistic web benchmarks WebArena (Zhou
036   et al., 2024b) and VisualWebArena (Koh et al., 2024), humans succeed on 78% and 89% of tasks
037   respectively, but agents — even those powered by the latest frontier models — are far worse, typi-
038   cally achieving success rates below 20%. One significant bottleneck in existing agents arises from
039   their inability to leverage test-time computation for exploration and multi-step planning. Search
040   and planning is especially important in open ended web environments, as the potential action space
041   (i.e., all possible actions one can take on a webpage) is much larger than in most video games or
042   text-based simulators. There are often multiple plausible actions that must be sequenced to reach a
043   goal, and being able to efficiently explore and prune trajectories is essential.
044   In artificial intelligence systems, one effective strategy for leveraging test-compute to improve re-
045   sults is search: iteratively constructing, exploring, and pruning a graph of intermediate states and
046   possible solutions (Newell et al., 1959; Laird, 2019; Silver et al., 2016). The effectiveness of search
047   algorithms has been shown time and time again, enabling models to achieve or surpass human-
048   level performance on a variety of games, including Go (Silver et al., 2016; 2017), poker (Brown &
049   Sandholm, 2018; 2019), and Diplomacy (Gray et al., 2020).
050   How might we apply search in the context of automating computer tasks, where the search space
051   is large and — unlike games — there do not exist clear cut rewards and win conditions? Towards
052   this goal, we propose a method to enable autonomous web agents to search over a graph that is
053   iteratively constructed through exploration of an interactive web environment. This search procedure
      is grounded within the actual environment space, and is guided with environmental feedback. Our

141   2.2   L ANGUAGE -G UIDED AUTONOMOUS AGENTS
142
143   Autonomous web agents, powered by frontier (multimodal) language models (Google, 2023; Ope-
144   nAI, 2024; Anthropic, 2024), are the SOTA approaches for many of the above benchmarks. Kim
145   et al. (2024) showed that large language models can be prompted to execute computer tasks on Mini-
146   WoB++ (Liu et al., 2018), requiring far fewer demonstrations than reinforcement learning methods.
147
      AutoWebGLM (Lai et al., 2024) collects web browsing data for curriculum training and develops
      a web navigation agent based off a 6B parameter language model that outperforms GPT-4 on We-
148
      bArena. Patel et al. (2024) showed that a language model agent can improve its performance through
149
      finetuning on its own synthetically generated data. Pan et al. (2024) show that introducing an au-
150   tomatic evaluator to provide guidance on task failure or success can improve the performance of
151   a baseline Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2024) agent. Fu et al. (2024) extracts domain knowledge from
152   offline data and provides this to the language agent during inference, to enable it to leverage helpful
153   domain knowledge. SteP (Sodhi et al., 2024) and AWM (Wang et al., 2024b) propose methods to
154   enable agents to dynamically compose policies to solve web tasks.
155
      In the multimodal setting, WebGUM (Furuta et al., 2024) finetuned a 3B parameter multimodal
156   language model on a large corpus of demonstrations, achieving strong performance on MiniWoB
157   and WebShop. Koh et al. (2024) showed that prompting multimodal language models with a Set-
158   of-Marks (Yang et al., 2023a) representation enables the model to navigate complex webpages more
159   effectively than text-only agents. SeeAct (Zheng et al., 2024) demonstrated that frontier multimodal
160   models such as GPT-4V (Yang et al., 2023b) and Gemini (Google, 2023) can be grounded and
161   prompted to solve web tasks. ICAL (Sarch et al., 2024) builds a memory of multimodal insights
      from demonstrations and human feedback, improving performance on VisualWebArena.

      2.3   S EARCH AND P LANNING
166
167   Our method also draws inspiration from a rich history of search and planning algorithms in computer
168   science. Search algorithms such as breadth first search, depth first search, and A* search (Hart et al.,
169   1968) have long been used in artificial intelligence systems. Newell et al. (1959) and Laird (2019)
170   cast goal-oriented behavior as search through a space of possible states. Dean et al. (1993) and
171   Tash & Russell (1994) proposed planning algorithms over a limited search horizon, and employed
172   an expansion strategy to improve plans based off heuristics about the value of information. Tash &
173   Russell (1994) showed that this allowed agents to provide appropriate responses to time pressure and
174   randomness in the world. Deep Blue (Campbell et al., 2002), the chess engine which defeated world
175
      champion Kasparov in chess in 1997, was based on massive parallel tree search. Pluribus (Brown &
      Sandholm, 2019) leverages search to find better multiplayer poker strategies for dynamic situations.
176
177   In deep learning, search algorithms with neural network components have been instrumental in
178   achieving superhuman performance on many games: Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Browne
179   et al., 2012) was used to provide lookahead search in the AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016; 2017) systems
180   that achieved superhuman performance in the game of Go. Gray et al. (2020) performs one-step
181
      lookahead search to achieve SOTA on no-press Diplomacy. More recently, several papers (Yao
      et al., 2024; Besta et al., 2024) showed the potential of applying search to large language models
182
      to introduce exploration over multiple reasoning paths, enhancing performance on text based tasks
183
      that require non-trivial planning. Others have applied MCTS (Xie et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024a;
184   Zhang et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024a; Zhou et al., 2024a; Hao et al., 2023) to
185   improve the performance of LMs on math and science benchmarks (Cobbe et al., 2021; Wang et al.,
186   2023a) or simplified environments (Yao et al., 2022a; Valmeekam et al., 2023).
187
      In contrast to prior work, our setting is grounded in realistic web environments, and we search
188
      over the actual environment space (i.e., the web). This means that the search mechanics need to
189
      incorporate not just the text outputs of the agent, but also external environmental feedback from a
190   highly complex environment.

193   3     M ETHOD
194
195   In this section, we describe the search procedure (Fig. 1) in detail. Successfully solving a task in
196   a web environment such as (V)WA can be interpreted as navigating to a goal state s∗ which gives
197
      a positive reward R(s∗ ) = 1. The agent starts at state s0 (e.g., the homepage). Given a natural
      language instruction I, the agent’s goal is to navigate to s∗ by executing actions (a0 , . . . , at ) ∈ A.
198
      Each action produces a new state st+1 ∈ S and observation ot+1 ∈ Ω from the environment. The
199
      transition st → st+1 is governed by a deterministic transition function T : S × A → S.
200
201   Most approaches treat this as a partially observable Markov decision process, and only condition
202   on the current observation ot when predicting the next action at to take. This has significant lim-
203
      itations: the error of the agent compounds with each step, and if an erroneous action is taken at
      time t, it cannot be easily rectified if this leads to a bad state in the future. Our approach aims to
204
      alleviate this by explicitly conducting search and backtracking to identify better trajectories. There
205
      are several components involved which we describe in the following sections: the baseline agent
206   model (Sec. 3.1), the value function (Sec. 3.2), and the search algorithm (Sec. 3.3)
207
208
      3.1   AGENT BACKBONE
209
210   Most SOTA web agents are built through prompting large (multimodal) language models (Zhou
211   et al., 2024b; Pan et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Koh et al., 2024). A pretrained
212   language model or multimodal model fϕ is prompted with the current webpage observation ot and
213   instructed to predict the next action at to be executed. It is common to leverage prompting tech-
214   niques, such as ReAct (Yao et al., 2022b), RCI (Kim et al., 2024), or Chain-of-Thought (CoT)
215   prompting (Wei et al., 2022), to improve the performance of the agent. Language model agents also
      allow us to sample a diverse set of actions (e.g., with nucleus sampling (Holtzman et al., 2020)),


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      which is essential for creating plausible branches to explore during search (see Sec. 3.3). Our pro-
217   posed search algorithm can in principle be applied to any base agent. We show in Sec. 4 that search
218   improves inference-time performance on a range of models without retraining or finetuning fϕ .

220   3.2    VALUE F UNCTION
221
222   We implement a best-first search heuristic using a value function fv which estimates the expected
223   reward E[R(st )] of the current state st , where the ground truth goal state would provide perfect
224   reward of 1. As the state st of the simulator is not always accessible to the agent (st may include
225   private information such as database entries of the site), the value function computes the value vt
226   using the current and previous observations, as well as the natural language task instruction I:
227                                        vt = fv (I, {o1 , . . . , ot }) ∈ [0, 1]
228
229   In our experiments, the value function is implemented by prompting a multimodal language model
230
      with the natural language instruction and observations as screenshots (Sec. 4.1).

232   3.3    S EARCH A LGORITHM
233
      Our proposed search algorithm is a best-first search method loosely inspired by A* search (Hart
234
      et al., 1968), a classic graph traversal algorithm used widely in computer science. We use a language
235   model agent to propose candidate branches of the search tree. The search has hyperparameters depth
236   d, branching factor b, and search budget c which determine the maximum size of the search tree,1
237   and termination threshold θ. The search procedure is summarized in Fig. 1. We describe it in detail
238   in the following paragraphs and provide the formal algorithm in Appendix A.4.
239
      At time t in the execution trajectory, the agent has previously executed a sequence of actions to arrive
240
      at the current state st . We begin the search algorithm from st by initializing the frontier F ← {}
241   (implemented as a max priority queue) which holds the set of states that we plan to evaluate, the best
242   state found so far ŝt ← st , the score of the best sequence v̂t ← 0, and the search counter s ← 0.
243
244
      At each iteration of the search process, we extract the next state from the frontier, sp ← pop(F).
      We use the value function to compute the score for state sp (with observation op and previous
245
      observations o1 , . . . , op−1 ):

250   it and our best state accordingly:

253                                              ŝt ← p

255                                             v̂t ← max(v̂t , vp )

276   4.1      I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
277
278   Baseline agent models Our search algorithm is compatible with most off-the-shelf language
279   model agents. In this work, we test it with simpler, more general, prompt-based agents, and leave
280   incorporation of our method with more performant methods that incorporate domain-specific tech-
281   niques (Fu et al., 2024; Sodhi et al., 2024) for future work. We run several prompt-based agent
282   baselines with different input formats (full prompts provided in the appendix):
283
                • Multimodal SoM: For multimodal models that accept multiple image-text inputs, such as
284
                  GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) (gpt-4o-2024-05-13), we run the multimodal agent from Koh
285               et al. (2024) with the same prompt. We similarly apply a preprocessing step to assign a
286               Set-of-Marks (SoM) (Yang et al., 2023a) representation to the webpage. This highlights
287               every interactable element on the webpage with a bounding box and a unique ID. The input
288               to the agent is a screenshot of the SoM-annotated webpage, and a text description of the
289               elements on the page with their corresponding SoM IDs.
290             • Caption-augmented: For base models that are not multimodal (e.g., Llama-3-70B-
291               Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024)), we run the caption-augmented agent with the same prompt
292               from Koh et al. (2024). We generate captions for each image on the webpage using an
293               off-the-shelf captioning model (in our case, BLIP-2; Li et al. 2023). The accessibility tree3
294               representation of the webpage observation is used as input observation at each step.
295             • Text-only: On WebArena (which does not require visual grounding), we run text-only
296               agents using the prompt from Zhou et al. (2024b), for both GPT-4o and Llama-3-70B-
297               Instruct. Similar to the caption-augmented baseline, this model uses an accessibility tree
298               representation of the current webpage as its input observation (without captions).
299
300   Search parameters We run these agents with and without search. Our search parameters are
301   set to d = 5, b = 5, c = 20, and we stop execution after a maximum of 5 actions. We enforce
302   these constraints due to compute and budget limitations, though we expect that increasing these
303
      parameters is likely to further improve results (see Sec. 5.1 for results on scaling search parameters).
      We note that the fairly strict limitations on maximum actions imply that there are certain tasks that
304
      are intractable (e.g., VWA tasks with “hard” action difficulty usually require humans to execute 10 or
305
      more actions to complete). Despite this, our results show that GPT-4o with search capped at 5 max
306   actions still substantially outperforms the GPT-4o baseline (without search) with 30 max actions.
307
308   Obtaining actions We sample actions using nucleus sampling (Holtzman et al., 2020) with a
309   temperature of 1.0 and top-p of 0.95 for all experiments. At each step of execution, we generate 20
310   outputs from the model by prompting it with CoT reasoning (Wei et al., 2022), and aggregate the
311   count of the action candidates. We use the top-b actions with the highest counts for branching.
312

314   that the current state st is a goal state. We implement the value function by prompting a multimodal
315   language model with the task instruction I, screenshots of the agent’s trajectory, previous actions
316   the agent took, and the current page URL. The full prompt is provided in Appendix A.3.2. The
317
      multimodal LM is instructed to output whether the current state is a success, a failure, and if it’s

318

319

320   reasoning paths by prompting the multimodal LM with CoT (Wei et al., 2022). We sample 20
321   different paths from the GPT-4o model using ancestral sampling (temperature of 1.0 and top-p of
322   1.0). The final value assigned to state st , used in the best-first search heuristic, is computed by

347   4.2   R ESULTS
348
349   Our results are summarized in Tab. 2. Introducing search increases success rate substantially across
350   the board. Search improves the success rate of the baseline GPT-4o + SoM agent on VWA by
351   39.7% relatively (increasing from 18.6% to 26.4%), setting a new state-of-the-art on the benchmark.
352
      On WA, introducing search to the GPT-4o agent improves the success rate substantially as well,
      increasing it by 28.0% relatively (15.0% to 19.2%). This is competitive with other prompt-based
353
      agents on WA, but in future work it will be interesting to explore introducing search to stronger
354
      baseline agents that incorporate domain-specific techniques, such as SteP (Sodhi et al., 2024) or
355   AutoGuide (Fu et al., 2024).
356
357   With weaker base models, we also observe substantial improvements. For the Llama-3 caption-
358
      augmented agent on VWA, introducing search improves the success rate on VWA by 119.7% relative
      to the baseline (7.6% to 16.7%). With search, Llama-3-70B-Instruct achieves success rates that are
359
      close to the best frontier multimodal models that do not use search. On WebArena, we also see a

      5.3   Q UALITATIVE R ESULTS
473
474   In this section, we discuss some qualitative examples of agent trajectories, and identify various
475   failure modes that are solved when incorporating search.
476
477   More robust multi-step planning Many tasks in VWA and WA require an agent to keep a per-
478   sistent memory of multiple previous actions and observations. A common failure mode amongst
479   agents without search is that they tend to undo previous actions, or get stuck in loops (see Appendix
480   C.4 of Koh et al. 2024). An example for VWA shopping task #256 is shown in Fig. 1, where the
481   agent is tasked to add two different types of canned fruit from the same brand to the comparison
482
      list. The baseline agent successfully adds the first item, but fails to navigate to the second item, as
      it returns to the homepage in step 3 and gets confused. This is an example of compounding error
483
      leading to overall task failure, which is fairly common in existing baseline agents without search.
484
485   When search is introduced, the agent explores other plausible trajectories and backtracks when those
      eventually result in failure: the same GPT-4o agent with search is able to find a successful multi-step


                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      trajectory for the same task, which involves adding the first item (action #1 in Fig. 1), typing in a
487   search query (action #6), and adding the correct second item to the comparison list (action #9).
488
489   Resolving uncertainty An inherent issue with sampling actions from language models is that
490   we are sampling from a distribution over text, and the first sample we generate may not always
491   be the best action to take in the environment. Search allows us to evaluate each generated action
492   concretely by executing it in the simulator, and use the received environmental feedback to make
493   better decisions. One example is VWA classifieds task #48 (Fig. 3), which is to find a post containing
494   a particular image. If the agent executes the first sampled action at every step (i.e., the sequence in
495   the top row), it results in failure. Search allows the agent to enumerate all possibilities by executing
496   plausible actions and receiving environment feedback.
497
498   5.4   L IMITATIONS
499
500
      While we have shown that introducing search to language model agents achieves promising results
      on web tasks, it does come with some practical limitations:
501
502
      Search can be slow Introducing search allows us to expend more compute at inference time to
503
      extract stronger results from the baseline LM agent. However, this results in trajectories taking sig-
504
      nificantly longer to execute, as the agent has to perform more exploration and hence more inference
505   calls to the LM. For example, a search budget of c = 20 implies that an agent with search could
506   potentially expand up to 20 states in each search iteration, which would use up to 20× more LM
507   calls than an agent without search. Research on improving the efficiency and throughput of machine
508   learning systems (Leviathan et al., 2023; Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023) will likely help with optimiz-
509   ing this, but for practical deployment one may need to carefully set the search parameters b, d, and
510   c to balance between achieving better results and overall time spent completing a task.
511   In our approach, we implemented search by keeping track of the sequence of actions required to get
512   to a state. During backtracking, we reset the environment and apply the same sequence after resetting
513   the environment. This is necessary, as naively executing the go back action (Tab. 1) may discard
514   important information on the page, such as the scroll offset and already entered text. However,
515   these environment calls for backtracking introduce additional overhead, which may be restrictive
516   for deployment if calls to the environment are expensive.
517
518   Destructive actions For real world deployment, we will need to restrict the search space to actions
519   that are not destructive. Destructive actions are defined as actions that will irreversibly change
520   the state of the website and are difficult to backtrack from. For example, placing an order on an
521
      e-commerce site is typically very difficult to automatically undo. One way to address this is to
      introduce a classifier that predicts when certain actions are destructive, and prevent node expansion
522
      for those states. If we have specific domain knowledge about the downstream application (e.g., we
523
      know certain pages should be off limits), such rules can be manually enforced with high accuracy.
524   One advantage of tree search is that it is easier to incorporate such a constraint: it can be directly
525   integrated into the value function to prevent execution of dangerous actions. Another direction
526   to handle this would be to train a world model (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) that we can use for
527   simulations during search. Search may also be more easily implemented in offline settings where
528   actions are non-destructive as they can always be undone or reset, such as programming (Jimenez
529   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024) or Microsoft Excel (Li et al., 2024).

      6     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we introduced an inference-time search algorithm designed to enhance the capabilities
534   of language model agents on realistic web tasks. Our approach integrates best-first tree search with
535   LM agents, enabling them to explore and evaluate multiple action trajectories to achieve superior
536   performance on web tasks. This is the first time search has been shown to significantly improve the
537   success rates of LM agents on realistic web environments, as demonstrated on the (Visual)WebArena
538   benchmarks. Our search procedure is general, and it will be valuable to apply it to other domains
539   in future work. We believe that inference-time search will be a key component for building capable
      agents that can plan, reason, and act autonomously to perform computer tasks.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      As an active area of machine learning research, language model web agents present both opportuni-
543   ties and potential ethical considerations. Improved web agents could improve accessibility for users
544   with disabilities, automate repetitive or tedious tasks, and potentially democratize access to com-
545   plex web platforms. Our search method contributes towards making such benefits more reliable and
546   widely available by improving the robustness and success rate of language model agents. However,
547   we acknowledge several potential ethical considerations:
548
549         • Intended uses. Our work is a research product that aims to advance the development of
550           web agents that can help augment humans by automating computer tasks. It is not in its
551           current state intended for deployment in practical scenarios. However, we acknowledge
552
              that as they get better, enhanced web agents might be leveraged for malicious purposes,
              such as more sophisticated phishing attempts or automated attacks on web services. As
553
              with all emerging technologies, developers deploying these technologies should incorpo-
554
              rate consider potential misuse scenarios and implement the appropriate safeguards.
555
556         • Economic impact. As web agents become more capable, there may be concerns about
557
              job displacement for roles that involve web-based tasks. We believe that web agents will
              augment human capability, and will be able to improve the overall quality of work by
558
              automating tedious computer tasks. However, as this technology starts being deployed
559
              more broadly, researchers and developers should proactively consider how to manage this
560           transition and support affected workers.
561
562
            • Fairness and bias. As with any modern AI system, web agents may inherit or amplify
              biases present in their training data or underlying language models. Care must be taken
563
              to assess and mitigate unfair treatment or representation of different user groups. As an
564
              inference time algorithm, our approach can easily be applied to any off-the-shelf language
565           model, and will likely benefit from upstream efforts on language model safety and align-
566           ment.
567
568   Our approach also potentially provides a framework that could help address some of these concerns.
569   The value function in our tree search algorithm offers a natural way to encode safety constraints
570   at inference time. For example, classifiers can be integrated with our proposed value function to
571   prevent destructive actions or violations of privacy and security policies. We encourage further
572   research into the ethical implications of web agents, and the development of guidelines and best
573
      practices for the responsible deployment of web agents.

       A.4.1   E NVIRONMENT R ESET
1081
1082   In this section, we describe the implementation details of the backtracking used in our search proce-
1083   dure:
1084
1085         1. We maintain a max priority queue that contains sequences of actions and their score v (from
1086
                the value function). Each element is a sequence of actions that the agent has to sequentially
                execute starting from the initial state (task dependent, but often the website homepage) to
1087
                get to state s that has the corresponding score v.
1088
1089         2. After we execute a new action (L23 of Algorithm. 1), we append this action to the sequence
1090
                of actions and add the new sequence to the priority queue with its corresponding score v.
1091         3. In order to reset the environment to get a clean slate for the next node to explore, we reset
1092            to the initial state again, and repeat the execution of the next sequence of actions starting
1093            from step 1.
1094
       We implemented backtracking in this fashion, as we found that this was a substantially more com-
1095
       plete way of resetting the state, as opposed to simply clicking the “back” button on the browser for
1096
       example, as this does not persist certain web states such as the scroll offset, or retain text in text
1097   inputs. While our implementation does improve fidelity of backtracking and resets, it however does
1098   add significant overhead in terms of time (see Sec. 5.4 for more discussion).

1304   is successful or not. If the current state is a failure but it looks like the agent is on the right track towards

1306
       There are three types of tasks:
1307
       1. Information seeking: The user wants to obtain certain information from the webpage, such as the
1308   information of a product, reviews, the text in a comment or post, the date of a submission, etc. This may be
1309   formulated in the intent as “tell me”, “what is”, or “list out”. The agent’s response must contain the information
1310   the user wants, or explicitly state that the information is not available. Otherwise, e.g. the agent encounters an
1311   exception and respond with the error content, the task is considered to be a failure. It is VERY IMPORTANT
       that the bot response is the stop action with the correct output. If the bot response is not stop (e.g., it is click,
1312
       type, or goto), it is considered a failure for information seeking tasks.
1313   2. Site navigation: The user wants to navigate to a specific page (which may also be specified in the intent
1314   as “find”, “show me”, “navigate to”). Carefully examine the agent’s action history and the final state of the
1315   webpage (shown in the LAST IMAGE) to determine whether the agent successfully completes the task. It is
