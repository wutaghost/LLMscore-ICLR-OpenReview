                                                         A BSTRACT
013
014
                   While general world models have demonstrated excellent capability in model-
015
                   ing and simulating the world through video understanding and generation, their
016                ability to reason about physical phenomena beyond mechanics remains under-
017                explored. This includes crucial aspects like thermodynamics, electromagnetism,
018                and optics, all of which are fundamental for simulating and predicting real-world
019                dynamics. Existing benchmarks for evaluating physical reasoning in models of-
020                ten rely on datasets consisting solely of simulator-generated, virtual videos, lim-
021                iting their generalizability to real-world scenarios. This limitation hinders the
022                comprehensive evaluation of general world models’ physical reasoning in real-
023                world scenarios. To bridge this gap, we introduce the Physics-RW benchmark, a
024
                   physical reasoning dataset constructed from real-world videos. Encompassing a
                   broad spectrum of real-world phenomena—mechanics, thermodynamics, electro-
025
                   magnetism, and optics—Physics-RW offers a comprehensive evaluation platform.
026
                   We conducted extensive experiments on the Physics-RW benchmark, and the re-
027                sults indicate that there is still significant room for improvement in the physical
028                reasoning abilities of general world models. We further analyzed the experimental
029                results and explored several avenues for improvement. Virtual environment fine-
030                tuning and physical knowledge injection via prompts demonstrate the potential for
031                enhancing zero-shot physical reasoning ability 1 .

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      Equipping artificial intelligence with the ability to comprehend and predict the dynamic nature of
      the world is crucial for advancing artificial general intelligence. With the emergence of Sora (Brooks
037
      et al., 2024), its outstanding generative ability highlights the potential of the general world models
038
      in simulating and modeling the world (Brooks et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b). As
039   physics is one of the challenges in modeling the world (Poupyrev et al., 2024), the general world
040   models are expected to have the ability to understand the physical phenomena present in the real
041   world (Wang et al., 2024; Cho et al., 2024).
042
      The real world abounds in diverse physical phenomena, encompassing mechanics, thermodynamics,
043
      electromagnetism, optics, and more. For instance, the apparent bending of chopsticks immersed in
044
      water is not due to their deformation, but rather a light refraction phenomenon. Understanding
045   such phenomena is crucial for interpreting and generating video content. While recent models like
046   Sora (Brooks et al., 2024) and WorldDream (Wang et al., 2024) exhibit some capability in this area,
047   the extent of their understanding and their ability for accurate physical reasoning remains unclear.
048
      Currently, efforts have been made to construct benchmarks for evaluating physical reasoning in deep
049
      learning-based models. They design tasks from different perspectives, such as intuitive physics (Ri-
050
      ochet et al., 2021), object motion (Bakhtin et al., 2019; Yi et al., 2020; Bear et al., 2021), physical
051   properties of objects (Chen et al., 2021; Tung et al., 2023), and dynamic interactions (Li et al.,
052   2023c), then use simulators or physics engines to generate video datasets or corresponding game

091   Reality: Datasets generated by simulators may not fully reflect the ability of models to understand
092   real-world physical phenomena. (2) Restricted Scope: The focus mainly on object motion and colli-
093   sion limits the assessment of broader physical phenomena encountered in real-world scenarios. (3)
094   Limited Zero-shot Evaluation: Most benchmarks evaluate performance based on training on specific
095   datasets, neglecting zero-shot capabilities.
096   Naturally, an urgent research question arises: “Can general world models perform accurate phys-
097   ical reasoning across various phenomena?” To answer this, we constructed a comprehensive phys-
098   ical evaluation benchmark, named Physics-RW, based on real-world video-language data to validate
099   the physical reasoning capability of general world models. Encompassing the four major categories
100   of classical physics (mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, and optics), Physics-RW is
101   designed for vision-language physical reasoning tasks. As illustrated in Figure 1, each physical phe-
102   nomenon category includes two task types: classification and video generation. In the classification
103   task, models perform physical inference by answering yes/no questions based on a given video clip.
104
      The video generation task type requires models to generate subsequent videos that demonstrate their
      understanding of the physical phenomena.
105
106   We conducted extensive experiments based on the Physics-RW benchmark to assess the zero-shot
107   physical reasoning ability of general world models in real-world scenarios. The experimental results
      reveal that current general world models exhibit relatively limited physical reasoning capabilities,

125   2     R ELATED W ORK
126
127   2.1   VLM AS G ENERAL W ORLD M ODELS
128
129   Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al.,
130
      2023), Qwen (Bai et al., 2023), etc., encode vast amounts of text data to learn world knowl-
      edge (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023). Compared to text, videos can provide more detailed information,
131
      which is crucial for modeling physical phenomena in the world. Jointly modeling videos and text
132
      can help models better understand and represent the world, aligning with the goal of constructing
133   a general world model (Liu et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024). Currently, visual language models
134   (VLM), e.g., Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2023), Video-LLaMA (Zhang et al., 2023a), LWM (Liu
135   et al., 2024a), Sora (Brooks et al., 2024), and Latte (Ma et al., 2024), have demonstrated outstanding
136   performance in video understanding and video generation tasks. However, the physical reasoning
137   ability of general world models, as one of their key capabilities to simulate the world, has yet to
138   undergo comprehensive evaluation in real-world scenarios.
139
140   2.2   P HYSICAL R EASONING B ENCHMARKS
141
142   Given humans’ ability to grasp physical concepts and perform physical reasoning, empowering ar-
143   tificial intelligence systems with this capability has garnered significant research attention (Qi et al.,
144
      2020; Duan et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022; Duan et al., 2022). To comprehensively
      evaluate these models’ physical reasoning abilities, various physical reasoning benchmarks have
145
      been proposed (Bakhtin et al., 2019; Girdhar & Ramanan, 2019; Baradel et al., 2019; Riochet et al.,
146
      2021; Bear et al., 2021; Patel et al., 2022; Melnik et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023c; Tung et al., 2023).
147   However, previous benchmarks typically focus on the movement and collisions of objects, with a
148   relatively narrow range of physical phenomena. Additionally, their datasets are often generated by
149   simulators or physics engines, making it difficult to comprehensively evaluate the physical reason-
150   ing ability in real-world scenarios. In contrast to them, our proposed Physics-RW benchmark is
151   constructed from real-world videos, encompassing mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism,
152   and optics phenomena, covering most types of physical phenomena found in the real world. The
153   comparison between Physics-RW benchmark and prior physical reasoning benchmarks is presented
154   in Table 1.

158   3.1   DATA C ONSTRUCTION
159
160   We constructed a real-world physical reasoning benchmark by collecting videos based on four cat-
161   egories of physical phenomena: mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, and optics. To
      achieve this, we employed a two-pronged approach:


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Web-based Video Collection. We established relevant keyword sets for each phenomenon cat-
163   egory (e.g., mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, and optics) and used them to search
164   for videos online. For example, mechanics keywords included elasticity, gravity, resistance, buoy-
165   ancy, etc, while thermodynamics keywords encompassed thermal expansion and heat transfer, etc.
166   Electromagnetism leveraged keywords like electromagnetic induction, demagnetization, and mag-
167   net experiments, and optics utilized terms like reflection of light, refraction of light, and propagation
168   of light.
169   Controlled Video Filming. In addition to the web-based collection, we also shot our own domino
170   collision videos, featuring variations in domino length, combination, and the number of dominoes
171   impacting the target object. These controlled videos were categorized under the mechanics category.
172
      Following video data collection, we meticulously segmented and selected video portions that ex-
173
      plicitly showcased the targeted physical phenomena.
174
175
      3.2      TASK C ONSTRUCTION
176
177   As our benchmark incorporates two task types (i.e., classification and video generation) for each
178   phenomenon, we transformed these video segments into specific task types:
179
      Classification Task. We manually created instruction-answer pairs based on the segments. To
180
      facilitate quantitative evaluation, we incorporated prompts within the instructions, guiding the model
181
      to respond with a simple “yes” or “no” answer.
182
183   Video Generation Task. Segments were further split into two parts. We expect the model to
184   generate the subsequent video (second part) based on the first part of the video and the provided
185
      instructions.
186   Finally, we constructed instructions in both Chinese and English, enabling future evaluations of
187   world models designed for these two languages.
188
189   3.3      E VALUATION M ETRICS
190
191   To conduct quantitative performance statistics, we adopt different metrics for each task type. Specif-
192
      ically, for the classification task type, we use the commonly used metrics of Accuracy (ACC) and
      F1-score (F1) as evaluation metrics. For the video generation task type, we use FVD to evaluate the
193
      quality of the generated video. The smaller the FVD is, the higher the video quality will be.

      4.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
235
236
      In our experiments, we primarily evaluate the zero-shot reasoning abilities of both open-source
237   and closed-source models. Except for GPT-4o, we conduct experiments using the default hyper-
238   parameters in the official code repositories, such as the number of frames for video segmentation
239   and temperature. We observed that the frame sampling interval specified in the official GPT-4o
240   code examples (1 frame per second) might not be universally applicable to all video lengths. Thus,
241   we vary the frame interval based on the video length, using values of 0.5, 1, 2, and 4 seconds.
242   This ensures a more appropriate sampling rate for videos of different durations. Given the Physics-
243   RW benchmark’s primary focus on English, we restrict our experiments to the English version. The
244   inference of models is executed on the NVIDIA Tesla A100 and A40 GPU.

303   4.4    H UMAN P ERFORMANCE ON P HYSICS -RW B ENCHMARK
304
305   To evaluate the maximum attainable performance of the Physics-RW benchmark, we selected par-
306
      ticipants who have a background in basic physics education and understand fundamental physics
      knowledge and conducted human evaluation. Specifically, we focused on the classification task.
307
      First, we divided the dataset for the four kinds of physical phenomena into multiple subsets. Then,
308
      we randomly assigned three humans to predict answers based on given videos and instructions for
309   each subset. Finally, we aggregated the results from all the humans and calculated the ACC and F1
310   scores.
311
312
      Table 5 revealed that humans consistently demonstrated strong physical reasoning abilities across
      all four task categories. According to the experimental results, we found that compared to the
313
      other three types of physical phenomena, human performance on mechanics tasks was relatively
314
      limited. This is mainly because the complexity of the tasks within T1 varies significantly. For
315   example, for the dominoes subset, simpler scenarios (fewer dominoes and simpler arrangements)
316   can be accurately predicted by most participants, but the difficulty increases substantially as the
317   number of dominoes and the complexity of the arrangements increase. This complexity can lead to
318   a decline in overall human performance.

418   model receives a video segment and an instruction as input, and is expected to generate the sub-
419   sequent video based on the provided information. However, the generated video (shown last) not
420   only deviates from a logical continuation of the input video but also contradicts physical laws. In
421   this example, the generated video depicts a change in the shapes of the magnet and needle, which
422   violates the principles of physics. This failure shows the current limitations of models in accurately
423   reasoning about physical phenomena during video generation.
424   In summary, the understanding capability of general world models regarding physical phenomena
425   is limited. This also indicates that there is still considerable exploration space in understanding
426   physical phenomena.

756
      Data Leakage. A significant portion of our video data originates from online sources. While
757   this offers a diverse range of physical phenomena, it raises concerns about potential data leakage.
758   Existing general world models might have been trained on some of the videos used for evaluation
759   in our work. To mitigate this, we collect a new domino dataset. In future work, we will collect
760   a wider range of video data encompassing various real-world scenarios and incorporate techniques
761   that prevent data leakage during evaluation processes.
762
      Limited Exploration in Video Generation Tasks. Our investigation into video generation tasks
763   focuses on video-to-video models. However, the number of readily available models for this task is
764   limited. Consequently, we have only evaluated two models. To address this, we aim to explore a
765   broader range of video generation models in future work. This will provide a more comprehensive
766   understanding of the effectiveness of our benchmark across different models.
767
      Limited Task Types in Benchmark. Physics-RW benchmark includes two widely-used task types,
768
      i.e., classification and video generation. However, other task types (e.g., explanation) are also im-
769   portant for assessing physical reasoning capabilities. To address this issue, we plan to construct
770   more forms of physical reasoning tasks in future work to achieve a more comprehensive evaluation.
771
772
      Limited Physical Phenomena in Task. Our benchmark is primarily constructed based on the clas-
      sification of classical physics, which includes five branches: classical mechanics, optics, thermody-
773
      namics, electromagnetism, and acoustics. In addition to classical physics, the realm of real-world
774
      physics encompasses many fields, such as fluid dynamics and quantum physics. However, we have
775   chosen to focus solely on mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, and optics for two main
776   reasons: (1) Current Model Limitations: General world models are still in their nascent stages, and
777   their ability to handle complex physical phenomena such as acoustics (involving sound wave prop-
778   agation, echoes, and reflections) is currently limited. (2) Classical Physics: The selected branches
779   form the cornerstone of classical physics, providing a robust foundation for evaluating core physical
780   reasoning capabilities. We believe that the current scope offers a solid foundation for evaluating the
781   physical reasoning capabilities of general world models at their current developmental stage.
782   Limited Modalities. In the benchmark, we only utilized the language-visual modalities, without
783   involving other modalities that could assist in physical reasoning, such as audio and tactile. This is
784   mainly because the audio information sometimes reveals the answer prematurely, potentially biasing
785   the evaluation of models’ physical reasoning abilities. Besides, considering the current state-of-
786   the-art general world models, most models are primarily designed to process visual information.
787   Furthermore, incorporating audio or tactile data would introduce significant challenges in terms of
788   data collection, annotation, and model adaptation. In future work, we plan to collect tactile videos
789
      generated when objects are touched through sensors, and combine it with the sounds produced
      during the interaction, thereby introducing more modalities. Additionally, we will employ the latest
790
      models in the tactile domain for evaluation.

849
850
      Our dataset contains two language versions (i.e., Chinese and English), with each version encom-
851
      passing four physical phenomena. The data statistics are shown in the Table 2. The dataset size of
852   our benchmark is comparable to similar benchmarks in the literature. For example, MME (Fu et al.,
853   2023), a benchmark for multimodal large language models, includes several tasks with data sizes
854   ranging from 50 to 200. JEEBENCH (Arora et al., 2023), a benchmark for evaluating reasoning
855   abilities, covers the categories of Math, Physics, and Chemistry, with data sizes of 236, 123, and
856   156, respectively. These comparisons demonstrate that our dataset is representative of the current
857   state-of-the-art in terms of dataset scale for physical reasoning benchmarks to assess general world
858   models.
859   Moreover, there is an imbalance in data distribution, with T1 having around seven times more data
860   than the other tasks. This is mainly because we believe that the real-world distribution of physical
861   phenomena should be reflected in the dataset to ensure the benchmark’s ecological validity. There-
862   fore, this imbalance primarily stems from the broader scope of classical mechanics phenomena in
863   the real world. However, we are open to exploring potential solutions to mitigate the impact of data
      imbalance, for example, re-weighting techniques during model training.
