011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013              Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) excel in modeling graph structures across di-
014              verse domains, such as community analysis and recommendation systems. As
015              the need for GNN interpretability grows, there is an increasing demand for ro-
016
                 bust baselines and comprehensive graph datasets, especially within the realm of
                 Heterogeneous Information Networks (HIN). To address this, we introduce Syn-
017
                 HING, a framework for Synthetic Heterogeneous Information Network Genera-
018
                 tion designed to advance graph learning and explanation. After identifying key
019              motifs in a target HIN, SynHING systematically employs a bottom-up generation
020              process with intra-cluster and inter-cluster merge modules. This process, along
021              with post-pruning techniques, ensures that the synthetic HIN accurately mirrors
022              the structural and statistical properties of the original graph. The effectiveness of
023              SynHING is validated using four datasets - IMDB, Recipe, ACM, and DBLP -
024              spanning three distinct application categories, demonstrating both its generality
025              and practicality. Furthermore, SynHING provides ground-truth motifs for evalu-
026              ating GNN explainer models, establishing a new benchmark for explainable, syn-
027
                 thetic HIN generation. This contributes significantly to advancing interpretable
                 machine learning in complex network environments.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      In recent years, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have shown impressive performance in various
033   graph analysis tasks such as community analysis (Shchur & Günnemann, 2019), chemical bond
034   analysis (Stokes et al., 2020), and recommendation systems (Cui et al., 2020). These tasks include
035   node and edge classification, link prediction, and clustering (Chami et al., 2022). Heterogeneous
036   Information Networks (HINs) consist of multiple types of nodes and edges that contain various in-
037   formation, providing a natural representation of real-world data. The development of Heterogeneous
038   Graph Neural Networks (HGNN) has been sparked by HINs. These networks can be classified into
039   different types of models, including meta-path-based models like HAN (Wang et al., 2019) and
040   MAGNN (Fu et al., 2020), transformer-based GNN models (Yun et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2020b), as
041
      well as SimpleHGN (Lv et al., 2021) which uses projection layers to map information to a shared
      feature space and aggregates information using an edge-type attention mechanism. Additionally,
042
      TreeXGNN (Hong et al., 2023) combines a tree-based feature extractor with the HGNN model to
043
      enhance performance. However, due to the lack of public HIN datasets, most existing HGNNs are
044   trained and evaluated on only a few known public datasets such as IMDB 1 , ACM (Wang et al.,
045   2019), and DBLP 2 , leading to potential bias. The scarcity of public HIN datasets compared to other
046   machine learning domains poses significant challenges for HGNNs, potentially causing overfitting
047   and hindering effective generalization (Palowitch et al., 2022).
048
      Due to the scarcity of HIN datasets, it is even more difficult to study trustworthy and interpretable
049
      models. As trust, transparency, and privacy become essential for machine learning models, it is im-
050   portant to reveal the decision-making process and knowledge hidden behind models. Most existing
051   GNN models lack transparency, making them difficult to be trusted and limiting their applicability in

      decision-critical scenarios. GNN explainer models were proposed to disclose the black box. Never-
055   theless, there is no objective way to evaluate the performances of GNN explainers as there exists no
056   suitable dataset in public domains that provides the data, objective, and corresponding ground-truth

058
      Synthetic homogeneous graph datasets have been introduced (Ying et al., 2019), attempting to al-
059
      leviate the lack of explainable datasets. These datasets are created by randomly attaching specially
060
      designed structured network motifs to artificial graphs (Albert & Barabási, 2002). These motifs are
061   then utilized as ground-truth explanations. There have been very few attempts to generalize syn-
062   thetic graph generation to HINs. This generalization is non-trivial for two reasons: Firstly, the basic
063   artificially structured motifs, such as houses and grids (Ying et al., 2019), common in the synthesis of
064   homogeneous graphs, are inadequate for HINs. These simplistic motifs often fail to capture the com-
065   plexity and diversity of real-world heterogeneous graphs, resulting in a poor representation of the
066   intricate structures found in actual HIN environments. Secondly, methods designed for generating
067   homogeneous synthetic graphs cannot be directly applied to heterogeneous information networks
068   (HINs) due to their unique structural constraints. Traditional approaches, which often involve ran-
069
      domly connecting nodes to form edges, may result in illegitimate connections within HINs. Such
      connections can violate the semantic rules inherent to HINs, where edges must align with the types
070
      of nodes they connect to represent accurate and meaningful relationships. Moreover, these methods
071
      randomly add edges, merely increasing node degrees without considering the global structure of
072   the graph. This randomization disrupts the alignment of node degree and structural patterns with
073   real-world HINs. Additionally, as highlighted by Li et al. (2023a), the scarcity of heterogeneous
074   datasets with ground-truth explanations further complicates the development of explanation models
075   for HINs. Researchers often resort to indirect evaluation methods, which may not effectively capture
076   the true performance of these models.
077
      In light of the above concerns, we introduce Synthetic Heterogeneous Information Network Gen-
078   eration (SynHING), a novel framework that constructs synthetic HINs for graph learning and ex-
079   planation by referencing existing real graphs. SynHING methodically generates major motifs es-
080   sential for explanations and employs our newly developed Intra-/Inter-cluster Merge method. This
081   method facilitates the merging of multiple subgraph groups to systematically create synthetic HINs
082   of any specified size. Additionally, we introduce the concept of exclusion that allows us to flexi-
083   bly adjust the complexity of node prediction tasks within these synthetic graphs while maintaining
084   structural similarity to the reference graph. This feature enhances the utility of SynHING in practi-
085   cal applications. The synthetic graphs generated by SynHING provide interpretable insights, aiding
086
      significantly in the explanation tasks associated with HINs. SynHING has been validated using
      four datasets: IMDB, Recipe, ACM, and DBLP, which cover three distinct application categories,
087
      demonstrating its generality and practicality. Furthermore, theoretical complexity analysis has been
088
      conducted to showcase its scalability. 3

      2.1       S YNTHETIC G RAPH G ENERATION
094
095
      Artificially synthesized data has a long history of development (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Bowyer
096   et al., 2011; Dong et al., 2018; Frid-Adar et al., 2018; Karras et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2019; Figueira
097   & Vaz, 2022). With the growth of Graph Neural Networks (GNN), there has been a renewed interest
098   in synthetic graph generation algorithms. Early on, Snijders & Nowicki (1997) introduced a method
099   to generate graph edges based on node clusters. More contemporary approaches, such as those
100   described by Dwivedi et al. (2020), extract subgraphs from real-world graphs to test GNNs’ ability
101   to identify specific substructures within Stochastic Block Model (SBM) graphs. The concept of
102   SBM, particularly popular for generating clusters that maintain strong intra-node correlations, has
103   been adapted into various forms, including unsupervised (Tsitsulin et al., 2020) and semi-supervised
104
      models (Rozemberczki et al., 2021). Further extending SBM’s utility, GraphWorld (Palowitch et al.,
      2022) leverages the Degree-Corrected Stochastic Block Model (DC-SBM) (Abbe, 2017) to create
105
      diverse graph datasets using multiple parameters. Yet, these works mainly focus on homogeneous

      2.2   E XPLAINER FOR G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
112
113   Developing trustworthy machine learning models is now a widely acknowledged goal within the
114   community. Explainability methods for Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have seen considerable
115   development, especially for node and graph classification tasks (Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020;
116   Yuan et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022). These methods generally fall into two categories: inherent inter-
117   pretable models and post-hoc explainability approaches (Yuan et al., 2020). Inherent interpretable
118   models, such as ProtGNN (Dai & Wang, 2021), integrate explanations directly within the model,
119
      using mechanisms like top-K similarity to identify influential subgraphs for their predictions. In
      contrast, post-hoc explainability methods focus on identifying crucial subgraphs (Luo et al., 2020;
120
      Yuan et al., 2021) and key features (Ying et al., 2019), approximating the behavior of black-box
121
      models by elucidating the connections between inputs and outputs. While most data validating
122   these explainer models are derived from homogeneous graphs with simplistic motifs (Ying et al.,
123   2019), such as houses and grids, these often lack the diversity and complexity of real-world graphs.
124   Some researchers are studying heterogeneous GNN explanations (Li et al., 2023b; Lv et al., 2023),
125   but the lack of HINs with explanation ground truths poses challenges. Consequently, generating
126   appropriate heterogeneous graph datasets for validation is emerging as a crucial research area.
127
128   2.3   DATASETS WITH G ROUND -T RUTH E XPLANATIONS
129
130   The conventional evaluation of graph explanatory methods often relies on molecular datasets or
131   synthetic community node classification datasets. These datasets are favored because they offer
132
      ground-truth explanations. For instance, MUTAG is a molecular dataset containing graphs labeled
      according to their mutagenic effect (Debnath et al., 1991). Synthetic datasets, as introduced by Ying
133
      et al. (2019), are node classification datasets created by randomly attaching structured network mo-
134
      tifs to base graphs. These motifs are designed with specific structures such as houses, cycles, and
135   grids. The base graphs are generated either through BA methods or as a balanced binary tree. How-
136   ever, these datasets are homogeneous and do not transition well to heterogeneous graph contexts.
137   In contrast, our approach involves extracting relevant motifs directly from actual HINs to serve as
138   the basis for ground-truth explanations and employing a systematic bottom-up method to guarantee
139   inherent semantic rules of HINs when generating HIN datasets.

      In this study, we proposed SynHING, a novel synthetic HIN generation framework. We aim to
174
      generate an arbitrary size synthetic heterogeneous graph G̃ with the explanation ground truths, which
175
      closely mimics the property of the given real-world graph Ĝ through a bottom-up generation process,
176
      which is demonstrated in Fig. 1. Firstly, we generate the major motifs and derive base subgraphs
177   from them with proper node degree distributions. Secondly, we introduce two merge modules to
178   handle partial graphs; Intra-Cluster Merge aims to merge subgraphs within a cluster, and Inter-
179   Cluster Merge is used to merge clusters with different labels. Finally, the synthetic HIN will be
180   generated after feature generation and post-pruning. SynHING framework consists of six modules,
181   as shown in Fig. 2: (1) Major Motif Generation, (2) Base Subgraph Generation, (3) Intra-Cluster
182   Merge, (4) Inter-Cluster Merge, (5) Node Feature Generation, and (6) Post-Pruning. The details of
183   the proposed methods will be introduced in the following sections.
184
185   3.3   M AJOR M OTIF G ENERATION (MMG)
186
187
      To generate major motifs for ground-truth explanations, we identify meta-paths within the refer-
      enced graph that originate and terminate with target nodes, with all intermediate nodes. The major
188
      motif is derived by designating two target nodes as anchors and connecting them through all possible
189
      meta-paths within a specified number of hops (Wang et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2020). The maximum
190   number of hops can be set manually or based on the number of layers in the GNN models, reflect-
191   ing that GNN computation graphs are represented by n-hop subgraphs, where n matches the model
192   layers. As depicted in Fig. 2, the MMG module utilizes three one-hop paths to form a major motif
193   as an example.
194
      Further investigation revealed that these derived motifs are common patterns found using the
195
      graphlet searching method (Milo et al., 2002) in real-world graphs. We conducted experiments
196   on the IMDB dataset, identifying one of the most common graphlets, G20 (Milo et al., 2002), which
197   features multiple minor nodes acting as bridges between two target nodes. A similar pattern is noted
198   in Megnn (Chang et al., 2022). We define these robust graph patterns as the major motif, providing
199   essential ground truths for explanations. In addition, the motif can also be defined by the user and
200   customized for diverse explanation tasks.
201
202   3.4   BASE S UBGRAPH G ENERATION (BSG)
203
204   Based on the major motifs previously generated, we further develop base subgraphs. In this base
205   subgraph generation process, we introduce randomness into the major motifs and augment them
206
      with several non-target nodes, designated as minor nodes, attached to target nodes within each motif.
      This addition aims to create diverse subgraphs with noise, which are not part of the ground truths
207
      for explanations but mimic the real-world reference graph.
208
209   These minor nodes fulfill two primary functions. Firstly, they help match the degree distributions of
210   the target nodes in the subgraphs to those observed in the referenced real-world distribution, denoted
211   as P ϕ (k), where k is the number of connections to nodes of type ϕ. Secondly, the minor nodes serve
212
      as crucial junction points for subsequent merging processes.
213   Once minor nodes are added, each pair of target nodes within a motif is assigned identical labels,
214   defining this entity as a base subgraph. This is denoted as a tuple (Si , yi ), where Si = (Vi , Ei )
215   represents the structure of the i-th subgraph, and yi ∈ Y is the label of the associated target nodes.
      The generated subgraphs are then collected into sets Ky for each label y ∈ Y.

      3.5     M ERGE TO G ENERATE HIN S
217
218   Conventional methods for constructing graphs often involve adding edges between nodes or sub-
219   graphs to create a connected homogeneous graph. However, this approach carries the risk of in-
220   advertently forming illegal connections despite careful node selection to prevent them. It is also
221   challenging to maintain statistical properties, such as node degree distributions relative to different
222   node types, when connecting nodes.
223   To overcome these challenges, we propose a novel Merge operation that combines two nodes into
224   one, which will connect back to the initial neighbors of the two nodes. This method elegantly
225   adheres to existing constraints and preserves the degree distributions of the target nodes within the
226   generated subgraphs, ensuring an accurate representation of the target real-world graph. The general
227   merge function operates as follows: Given a graph G = (V, E) and two nodes v1 , v2 ∈ V. If we
228   merge v1 and v2 into v1 in G, then the process is defined by

238   3.5.1     I NTRA -C LUSTER M ERGE (I NTRA -CM)
239
240   Intra-CM is designed to merge the subgraphs with identical labels one by one to form a cluster
241
      denoted by Cy = (Vy , Ey ), which can mirror the ”Superstar” phenomenon often observed in com-
      munity networks (Albert & Barabási, 2002; Abbe, 2017). In social networks, early adopters often
242
      become central nodes or ”Superstars” due to the sequential nature of network growth. As new mem-
243
      bers join, they tend to connect with already well-established individuals. Early joiners accumulate
244   more connections over time, benefiting from the principle of preferential attachment, where new
245   links are more likely to form with highly connected members. This process leads to a few early
246   adopters amassing a disproportionate number of connections, thereby becoming key influencers or
247   opinion leaders within the network.
248

249

315   3.6     N ODE F EATURE G ENERATION (NFG)
316
      For NFG, we sample node features from within-cluster multivariate normal distributions, following
317
      previous studies(Palowitch et al., 2022; Tsitsulin et al., 2022). The node features in the same cluster
318   will be sampled from a shared prior multivariate normal distribution N (µy , α), where µy represents
319   the feature center sampled from another normal distribution µy ∼ N (0, β). Here, α/β serves as
320   a hyperparameter, representing the ratio of feature center distance to cluster covariance. It can be
321   interpreted as a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). For the multi-label nodes, we generate node features
322   based on a joint probability distribution that combines multiple independent probability distribution
323   functions. Afterward, we draw samples from this combined distribution to determine the features of
      the nodes. In this work, we generated node features for target nodes because minor node features are

324
      often unavailable in the real-world heterogeneous graphs and are commonly preprocessed by either
325   constants, node IDs, or propagated features (Lv et al., 2021). Therefore, we follow this common
326   setting, using node ID or node type information as node features for minor nodes to approximate
327   these real-world datasets.

      3.7   P OST-P RUNING (P-P)
330
331   P-P is an optional yet critical process applied to synthetic graphs to ensure they adhere to constraints
332   observed in raw data. For instance, in the IMDB dataset, each movie is linked to no more than
333   three actors, reflecting inherent limits within the original dataset. During P-P, we first establish the
334   upper limits of node degrees and scan the HIN node-by-node to remove excess edges until the node
335   degrees conform to the upper limit constraints. Importantly, we prioritize the retention of edges that
336   form part of the major motif, thereby preserving the integrity of the explanation ground truths.
337
338   3.8   C OMPLEXITY OF S YN HING
339
340   To explore SynHING’s scalability, we conducted a complexity analysis. The process of MMG and
341   BSG are both independent. Therefore, the time complexity of MMG and BSG is O(N ). The
342   complexity of Intra-CM is O(N |Vi | + N |Ei |) or O(N ), as |Vi | and |Ei | are the number nodes
343
      and edges in the base subgraph, which are constant w.r.t. N . The processes involved in Inter-
      CM are similar to Intra-CM; the complexity is also O(N ). Therefore, the overall time complexity
344
      of SynHING is determined by the number of motifs N with a time complexity of O(N ), which
345
      showcases the scalability of SynHING. More details can be found in the Appendix A.1.

      6   C ONCLUSION
531
      We present SynHING, a novel method for generating synthetic HINs, leveraging the real-world
532
      HINs as references, systematically generating the major motifs for explanations, and using Intra-
533
      /Inter-Cluster Merges to merge multiple groups of base subgraphs to generate synthetic HINs of any
534   specified sizes. SynHING has been validated using four datasets covering three distinct application
535   categories, demonstrating its generality and practicality. we address the scarcity of heterogeneous
536   graph datasets and overcome the need for such datasets in the domain of GNN explanations. To the
537   best of our knowledge, our work introduces the first framework for generating synthetic heteroge-
538   neous graphs with ground-truth explanations. Additionally, we design a comprehensive framework
539   for generating diverse synthetic HINs that can be flexibly adjusted and provide a solid foundation
      for future research on heterogeneous GNN explanations.

885   Users can customize the synthetic graph for various scenarios using the parameters of SynHING,
886   including the number of major motifs N , the number of clusters |Y|, the Intra-CM probabilities
887   pϕ , the Inter-CM probabilities q ϕ , and the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of features α/β . For example,
888   adjusting Intra-CM probability pϕ and Inter-CM probability q ϕ results in changes in the exclusion
889
      of clusters and difficulty of the synthetic graph. However, these parameters can also be directly de-
890
      termined by the referenced graph Ĝ. Although some statistical properties and network schema have
      been used for generating graphs, it’s further demonstrated that the synthetic graph can approximate
891
      the referenced graph more closely by adjusting these parameters. The number of major motifs N
892
      can be set as half of the number of target nodes in Ĝ, i.e. N = 21 |V̂ ϕ0 |, since each motif contains

894   exactly 2 target nodes. The number of clusters can be determined by the number of labels |Ŷ| in Ĝ.
895   The SNR of features α/β can adjust the difficulty of the task on G̃, or users can determine the means
896   and variances of clusters of features by maximum likelihood estimation.

      A.8   P RETRAINING AND F INETUNING
944
945
      In this study, we employ synthetic graphs for pretraining. Models are pretrained based on the recom-
946
      mended settings from their respective original papers, with early stopping applied after 30 epochs
947   without validation set improvement. For finetuning, the weights of the HGNN backbone, exclud-
948   ing the adapter layer that maps the heterogeneous features into shared space, are inherited from the
949   pre-trained model. We note that the weights of the backbone and adapter are trained using different
950   learning rates, as the results are sensitive to the learning rate. For instance, while finetuning from
951   pretrained weights, a lower learning rate for the backbone and a higher learning rate for the adapter
952   generally yield better results, whereas a higher learning rate for the backbone and a lower learning
953   rate for the adapter generally leads to better performance when learning from scratch. Consequently,
954   we conduct a grid search for learning rates in both scenarios, as presented in Tables 2 and 3. For the
955
      learning rate of the backbone, we try values of { 10−3 , 10−4 }. For that of the adapter, we try values
      of { 1, 5 } × { 10−2 , 10−3 , 10−4 }.

959
960   For our initial testing, we utilized synthetic ACM and DBLP datasets and inputted them into the
961   xPath framework (Li et al., 2023b). The synthetic IMDB dataset we generated is a multiple-choice
962   dataset. Since xPath does not support this, we skipped it for now. We utilized xPath’s default
963   parameters, including the HGNN encoder and explainer. We followed the instructions in xPath,
964   which involved two main steps: (1) Training the HGNN and (2) Generating explanations.
965
      We used HGT as our backbone prediction model. During the training stage, it can effectively
966   converge and achieve solid performance (Macro-F1=99.42%, Micro-F1=99.43%) and (Macro-
967   F1=80.06%, Micro-F1=80.81%) on SynACM and SynDBLP, respectively. During the explanation
968   stage, xPath can successfully generate an explanations subgraph with decent accuracy fidelity and
969   probability fidelity, Facc and Fprob (Yuan et al., 2020): Facc=0.15665, Fprob=0.15297 on SynACM
970   and Facc=0.16935, Fprob=0.08701 on SynDBLP, both presenting quite reasonable scores. The
971   above preliminary results show that our generated synthetic datasets can indeed be used to evaluate
      HGNN explanation algorithms. This warrants a more complete further exploration in future work.
