009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are a promising technique for decomposing language
012            model activations into interpretable linear features. However, current SAEs fall
013            short of completely explaining model performance, resulting in “dark matter”—
014            unexplained variance in activations. In this work, we predict and verify that much
015            of SAE dark matter can be linearly predicted from the activation vector. We exploit
016            this fact to deconstruct dark matter into three top-level components: 1) unlearned
017            linear features, 2) unlearned dense features, and 3) nonlinear errors introduced by
018            the SAE. Through a scaling laws analysis, we estimate that nonlinear SAE errors
019
               stay constant as SAEs scale and serve as a lower bound of SAE performance on
               both an average and per-token level. We next empirically analyze the nonlinear
020
               SAE error term and show that it is not entirely a sparse sum of unlearned linear fea-
021
               tures, but that it is still responsible for some of the downstream reduction in cross
022            entropy loss when SAE activations are inserted back into the model. Finally, we
023            examine two methods to reduce nonlinear error: inference time gradient pursuit,
024            which leads to a very slight decrease in nonlinear error, and linear transformations
025            from earlier layer SAE dictionaries, which leads to a larger reduction.

028   1     I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The ultimate goal for ambitious mechanistic interpretability is to understand neural networks com-
031   pletely from the bottom up by breaking them down into programs (“circuits”) and the variables
032   (”features”) that those programs operate on (Olah, 2023). One recent successful unsupervised tech-
033   nique for finding features in language models has been sparse autoencoders (SAEs), which learn a
034   dictionary of one-dimensional representations that can be sparsely combined to reconstruct model
035   hidden activations Cunningham et al. (2023); Bricken et al. (2023). However, as observed by Gao
036   et al. (2024), the scaling behavior of SAE width (number of latents) vs. reconstruction mean squared
037   error (MSE) is best fit by a power law with a constant error term. This is a concern for the ambitious
038
      agenda because it implies that there are components of model hidden states that are harder for SAEs
      to learn and which might not be eliminated by simple scaling of SAEs. Gao et al. (2024) speculate
039
      that this component of SAE error below the asymptote might best be explained by model activations
040
      having components with denser structure than simple SAE features (e.g. Gaussian noise).
041
042   In this work, we investigate the SAE error vector as an object worth study in its own right. Thus,
043   our direction differs from the bulk of prior work that seeks to quantify SAE failures, as these mostly
044
      focus on downstream benchmarks or simple cross entropy loss (see e.g. (Gao et al., 2024; Templeton
      et al., 2024; Anders & Bloom, 2024)). We find that some SAE error might come, not from pre-
045
      existing dense structures in the input as Gao et al. (2024) speculates, but from noise introduced by
046
      the SAE itself. We build on this finding to propose a preliminary breakdown of SAE error (see
047   Fig. 1) and then investigate each component in the breakdown in turn.

090              2                           R ELATED W ORK
091
092             Language Model Representation Structure: The linear representation hypothesis (LRH) (Park
093
                et al., 2023; Elhage et al., 2022) claims that language model hidden states can be decomposed into a
                sparse sum of linear feature directions. The LRH has seen recent empirical support with sparse au-
094
                toencoders, which have succeeded in decomposing much of the variance of language model hidden
095
                states into such a sparse sum, as well as a long line of work that has used probing and dimensionality
096             reduction to find causal linear representations for specific concepts (Alain, 2016; Nanda et al., 2023;
097             Marks et al., 2024; Gurnee, 2024). On the other hand, some recent work has questioned whether
098             the linear representation hypothesis is true: Engels et al. (2024) find multidimensional circular rep-
099             resentations in Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) and Llama (AI@Meta, 2024), and Csordás et al. (2024)
100             examine synthetic recurrent neural networks and find “onion-like” non-linear features not contained
101             in a linear subspace. This has inspired recent discussion about what a true model of activation space
102             might be: Mendel (2024) argues that the linear representation hypothesis ignores the growing body
103             of results showing the multi-dimensional structure of SAE latents, and Smith (2024b) argues that
104
                we only have evidence for a “weak” form of the superposition hypothesis holding that only some
                features are linearly represented.
105
106             SAE Errors and Benchmarking: Multiple works have introduced techniques to benchmark SAEs
107             and characterize their error: Bricken et al. (2023), Gao et al. (2024), and Templeton et al. (2024)
                use manual human analysis of features, automated interpretability, downstream cross entropy loss

      when SAE reconstructions are inserted back into the model, and feature geometry visualizations;
109   Karvonen et al. (2024) use the setting of board games, where the ground truth features are known,
110   to determine what proportion of the true features SAEs learn; and Anders & Bloom (2024) use the
111   performance of the model on NLP benchmarks when the SAE reconstruction is inserted back into the

117
      disappears when the random baseline is replaced by a perturbation in the direction of the difference
      between two random activations.

119   SAE Scaling Laws: Anthropic (2024), Templeton et al. (2024), and Gao et al. (2024) study how
120   SAE MSE scales with respect to FLOPS, sparsity, and SAE width, and define scaling laws with
121   respect to these quantities. Templeton et al. (2024) also study how specific groups of language
122   features like chemical elements, cities, animals, and foods, and show that SAEs predictably learn
123
      these features in terms of their occurrence. Finally, Bussmann et al. (2024) find that larger SAEs
      learn two types of dictionary vectors not present in smaller SAEs: features not present at all in
124
      smaller SAEs, and more fine-grained “feature split” versions of features in smaller SAEs.

246    4.2           N ORM P REDICTION T EST
247
       The second test we describe aims to determine if a random vector consists mostly of a sparse sum
248
       of one-dimensional vectors. For example, we expect this to be true for x and Sae(x), but not
249
       NonlinearError(x) or Dense(x). First, we claim that given a vector x, if x is mostly a sparse
250                                                                                                       2
       sum of one-dimensional vectors, then there likely exists a prediction vector a such that aT x ≈ |x|2
251
       (in other words, the norm squared of x can be linearly predicted from x). We prove that this is the
252    case for sums of orthogonal vectors in Appendix A; the intuition is that we can set the “prediction”
253    vector a to the sum of the vectors yi weighted by their average value wi .
254
255
       Similar to the first test described above, when extending to almost orthogonal vectors, we claim that
       this test will again be a lower bound. If the R2 of the norm prediction regression is high, it is evidence
256
       that the random vector is composed of a sparse sum of vectors. Although this evidence is not
257
       conclusive (there might be other distributions with this property), it does rule out many possibilities
258    (as for example random Gaussian noise does not have this norm prediction property).

356   Firstly, we note that ∥x∥2 can almost be perfectly predicted from x. This is reassuring news for the
357   linear representation hypothesis, as it implies that x can indeed well be modeled as the sum of many

363   One potential problem with this test is that Sae(x) − NonlinearError(x) and
364   LinearError(x) are by construction both linear transforms of x, so as long as these transforms
365   are full rank, this test is equivalent to using x as the input to the regression (instead of just the
366   component itself). To assuage this concern, we again run the norm prediction test but use x as the
367   input to the regression instead of the target random vector itself. The idea here is that if the vector
368   consists mostly of linear features that were present in x, then we should be able to predict its norm.
369   Empirically, we find that this test is more powerful than running just on the vector itself, but results
370   in the same pattern overall; in Fig. 5, we plot these as “Predict from x”.

452
      In this section, we investigate whether simple techniques can reduce NonlinearError(x).
453

      7.1        U SING A M ORE P OWERFUL E NCODER
455
456   Our first approach for reducing nonlinear error is to try improving the encoder. We use a recent
457   approach suggested by Smith (2024a): applying a greedy inference time optimization algorithm
458   called gradient pursuit to a frozen learned SAE decoder matrix. We implement the algorithm exactly
459   as described by Smith (2024a) and run it on all layer 20 Gemma Scope 9b SAEs closest to L0 ≈ 60.
460   For each example x with reconstruction Sae(x)(x), we use the gradient pursuit implementation
461   with an L0 exactly equal to the L0 of x in the original Sae(x)(x).
462   Using these new reconstructions of x, we again run the test from Section 4.1 and do a linear transfor-
463   mation from x to the inference time optimized reconstructions. We then regenerate a similar scaling
464   plot as Fig. 1 and show this figure in Fig. 8a. Our first finding is that pursuit indeed decreases the
465   FVU of Sae(x) by 3 to 5%; as Smith (2024a) only showed an improvement on a small 1 layer
466   model, to the best of our knowledge we are the first to show this result on state of the art SAEs. Our
467   most interesting finding, however, is that the variance explained by NonlinearError(x) stays
468   almost constant when compared to the original SAE scaling in Fig. 1. In other words, if our tests are
469
      accurate, most of the reduction in FVU comes from better learning Dense(x) and reducing the lin-
      early explainable error. Thus, in Fig. 1, we plot the additional reduction in NonlinearError(x)
470
      as the contribution of encoder error, and because NonlinearError(x) stays almost constant this
471
      section is very narrow.
472
473
      7.2        L INEAR P ROJECTIONS B ETWEEN A DJACENT SAE S
474
475   Our second approach for reducing nonlinear error is to try to linearly explain it in terms of the
476   outputs of previous SAEs. The motivation for this approach is that during circuit analysis (see e.g.
477   Marks et al. (2024)), an SAE is trained for every component in the model, and being able to explain
478   parts of the SAE error in terms of prior SAEs would directly decrease the magnitude of noise terms
479   in the discovered SAE feature circuits. For the Gemma 2 architecture at the locations the SAEs are
480   trained on, each residual activation can be decomposed in terms of prior components:
481                    Residlayer = MLP outlayer + RMSNorm(Oproj (Attn outlayer )) + Residlayer−1                                                       (5)
482
483   In Fig. 8b, we plot the R2 of a regression from each of these right hand side components to each
484   of the different components of an SAE trained on Residlayer (SaeError(x), LinearError(x),
485   and NonlinearError(x)). We find that we can explain a small amount (up to ≈ 10%) of total
      SaeError(x) using previous components, which may be immediately useful for circuit analysis.

      We also find that current layer’s SAE reconstruction is able to explain 50% of the variance in the

      8   C ONCLUSION
497
498
      The fact that SAE error can be predicted and analyzed at all is surprising; thus, our findings are
499
      intriguing evidence that SAE error, and not just SAE reconstructions, are worthy of analysis. Ad-
500   ditionally, the presence of constant nonlinear error implies that current SAEs may have room for
501   improvement, and therefore scaling SAEs may not be the only (or best) way to explain more of
502   model behavior. The precise research direction to take to reduce nonlinear error depends on exactly
503   why the nonlinear error arises. If it arises because the dictionaries SAEs currently learn are not
504   good enough, improved SAE training procedures may suffice, while if the root cause is the sparsity
505   constraint itself, future work might need to explore alternative simplicity penalties besides sparsity.
506   We note that our tests are also approximate; we argue for the existence of NonlinearError(x)
507   as a separate term from Dense(x), but the exact magnitude of each component remains uncertain.
508
      Ultimately, we believe that there is still room to make SAEs better, not just bigger.
