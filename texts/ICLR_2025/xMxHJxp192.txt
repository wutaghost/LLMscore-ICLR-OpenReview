009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are popular machine learning models designed to
012            process graph-structured data through recursive neighborhood aggregations in the
013            message passing process. When applied to semi-supervised node classification,
014            the message-passing enables GNNs to understand short-range spatial interactions,
015            but also causes them to suffer from over-smoothing and over-squashing. These
016            challenges hinder model expressiveness and prevent the use of deeper models
017            to capture long-range node interactions (LRIs) within the graph. Popular solu-
018
               tions for LRIs detection are either too expensive to process large graphs due to
               high time complexity or fail to generalize across diverse graph structures. To ad-
019
               dress these limitations, we propose a mechanism called information flow control,
020
               which leverages a novel connectivity measure, called information flow score, to
021            address over-smoothing and over-squashing with linear computational overhead,
022            supported by theoretical evidence. Finally, to prove the efficacy of our methodol-
023            ogy we design DeltaGNN, the first scalable and generalizable approach for long-
024            range and short-range interaction detection. We benchmark our model across 10
025            real-world datasets, including graphs with varying sizes, topologies, densities,
026            and homophilic ratios, showing superior performance with limited computational
027            complexity.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   GNNs are machine learning models designed to process graph-structured data (Scarselli et al., 2008).
033
      They have proven effective in various graph-based downstream tasks (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Wu
      et al., 2019), especially in semi-supervised node representation learning (Hu et al., 2019). GNNs
034
      also demonstrate broad applicability across several distinct domains, including chemistry (Gilmer
035
      et al., 2020; Zitnik & Leskovec, 2017), and medical fields (Ahmedt-Aristizabal et al., 2021), such as
036   network neuroscience (Bessadok et al., 2015), and medical image segmentation for purposes such
037   as liver tumor and colon pathology classification (Yang et al., 2023).
038
039
      The strength of GNN models lies in their ability to process graph-structured data and capture short-
      range spatial node interactions through local neighborhood aggregations during message passing.
040
      However, such local aggregation paradigm often struggles to capture dependencies between distant
041
      nodes, particularly in certain graph densities and structures. For instance, when processing a strongly
042   clustered graph, a standard GNN model may fail to account for interactions between nodes in distant
043   clusters. These long-range interactions (LRIs) are crucial for node classification tasks, as they help
044   distinguish between different classes and improve classification accuracy. This limitation has been
045   widely studied, with theoretical findings pinpointing over-smoothing (Li et al., 2018) and over-
046   squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2020) as the root causes. These phenomena limit performance in many
047   applications and prevent the use of deep GNNs to effectively capture long-range dependencies.
048   To address these challenges, recent works have proposed enhanced GNN models by integrating
049   graph transformer-based modules, such as global attention mechanisms (Wu et al., 2021), or local-
050   global attention (Fei et al., 2023). Other works have proposed topological pre-processing tech-
051   niques, such as curvature-enhanced edge rewiring (Nguyen et al., 2023) and sequential local edge
052   rewiring (Barbero et al., 2023). Although these methods can marginally improve model perfor-
053   mance, they fail to provide a generalized and scalable approach that can effectively process large
      graphs across various graph topologies. Specifically, attention-based approaches are constrained by


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      their quadratic time complexity and lack of topological awareness, which leads to computational
055   inefficiency. Rewiring algorithms, on the other hand, rely on expensive connectivity measures that
056   are often impractical for large and dense graphs. These methods are also embedding-agnostic and,
057   consequently, fail to directly address over-smoothing in certain graph structures. For these reasons,
058   developing a scalable and general method for learning long-range interactions (LRIs) would sig-
059   nificantly expand the applicability of GNNs in semi-supervised node classification tasks. Such a
060   method would be a key contribution to the field.
061   In this work, we formalize the concept of graph information flow and use it to define a novel con-
062   nectivity measure that analyzes the velocity and acceleration of node embedding updates during
063   message passing, offering insights into graph topology and homophily. We provide both theoretical
064   and empirical evidence to support this claim. Next, we propose a new graph rewiring paradigm,
065   information flow control, which mitigates both over-smoothing and over-squashing with minimal
066   additional time and memory complexity. Furthermore, we introduce a novel GNN architecture,
067   DeltaGNN, which implements information flow control to capture both long-range and short-range
068   interactions. We benchmark our model across a wide range of real-world datasets, including graphs
069
      with varying sizes, topologies, densities, and homophilic ratios. Finally, we compare the results of
      our approach with popular state-of-the-art methods to evaluate its generalizability and scalability.
070
071   Our contributions. (1) We introduce a novel connectivity measure, the information flow score,
072   which identifies graph bottlenecks and heterophilic node interactions, supported by both theoreti-
073   cal and empirical evidence of its efficacy. (2) We propose an information flow control mechanism
074   that leverages this measure to perform sequential edge-filtering with linear computational overhead,
075
      which can be flexibly integrated into any GNN architecture. (3) We design a scalable and generaliz-
      able framework, DeltaGNN, for detecting both short-range and long-range interactions, demonstrat-
076
      ing the effectiveness of our theoretical findings.

081   A graph is usually denoted as G = (V, E), where V represents the node set and E the edge set.
082   The edge set can also be represented as an adjacency matrix, defined as A ∈ {0, 1}|V|×|V| , where
083   Aij ̸= 0 if and only if the edge (i, j) exists. The node feature matrix is defined as X ∈ R|V|×dV
084   for some feature dimensions dV . We denote Xut as the features of the node u at layer t, with the
085   convention X 0 = X. Furthermore, each node u is associated with a class yu ∈ C, where C denotes
086   the set of classes. The goal, in node-classification tasks, is to learn Φ : V → C, the unique function
087   mapping each node to a specific class.

148   3     R ELATED W ORKS
149
150   Transformer-based self-attention. A common approach to overcome over-smoothing and over-
151   squashing and capture long-range node interactions is integrating a transformer-based components
152   into the GNN (Yun et al., 2019; Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020; Fei et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2021). Trans-
153   formers can aggregate information globally without being limited by the local neighborhood aggre-
154   gation paradigm, making them a very effective solution. However, they fail to propose a scalable
155   solution which can process large-scale graphs, which is arguably the scenario where long-range
156   interaction detection is of the utmost importance. Graph self-attention has a time complexity of
157   O(|V|2 ), where |V| refers to the number of nodes in the graph, this complexity is unsuitable for
158
      large graphs. Moreover, classical transformers are inefficient at processing LRIs in graphs because
      they are inherently topology-agnostic and consequently process all |V|2 possible interactions.
159
160   Topological graph rewiring. Many rewiring algorithms exploit graph curvature (Nguyen et al.,
161   2023) or other connectivity measures (Barbero et al., 2023; Black et al., 2023; Arnaiz-Rodrı́guez
      et al., 2022; Karhadkar et al., 2022) to identify parts of the graph suffering from over-squashing and


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      over-smoothing, and alleviate these issues by adding or removing edges. These approaches have
163   two main limitations: first, they rely on expensive connectivity measures (e.g., Ollivier-Ricci cur-
164   vature, betweenness centrality); and second, they are embedding-agnostic, as they primarily base
165   rewiring decisions on the graph’s connectivity. However, over-smoothing is not a topological phe-
166   nomenon, and as observed by recent works (Yan et al., 2022), its effects can be mitigated by acting
167   on the graph’s heterophily. Therefore, improving connectivity alone is insufficient to prevent over-
168   smoothing (see Appendix A).

317
      and long-range node interactions. First, DeltaGNN processes the node embeddings and graph ad-
318   jacency matrix through a standard homophilic GNN with IFC. During the sequential filtering, the
319   model partitions the graph into homophilic clusters by removing bottlenecks and heterophilic edges.
320   Simultaneously, the homophilic aggregation learns short-range dependencies. However, this process
321   leads to the loss of long-range interactions, which we recover through an heterophilic graph conden-
322   sation. The long-range dependencies are then learned via a GNN heterophilic aggregation. Finally, a
323   readout layer processes the results from both GNN aggregations. An overview of the model pipeline
      is illustrated in Figure 3.

518   In this work, we introduced the concepts of information flow score (IFS) and information flow con-
519   trol (IFC), novel approaches for mitigating the effects of over-smoothing and over-squashing during
520   message passing in semi-supervised node classification tasks. To demonstrate the effectiveness of
521   our methodology, we developed DeltaGNN, the first GNN architecture to incorporate IFC for detect-
522
      ing both short-range and long-range node interactions. We provided rigorous theoretical evidence
      and extensive experimentation to support our claims. Our empirical results show that our methodol-
523
      ogy outperforms popular state-of-the-art methods. As a future direction, we plan to explore alterna-
524
      tive implementations of DeltaGNN with different aggregation paradigms and non-GNN components
525   and examine the applicability of our approach to graph-level and edge-level learning tasks.

      In this section, we demonstrate why common graph-rewiring algorithms fail to address over-
705
      smoothing in certain graph structures. Typically, these algorithms utilize a connectivity measure
706   to detect dense areas within the graph, sparsifying them to slow down over-smoothing, while relax-
707   ing bottlenecks by adding new edges to reduce over-squashing. We independently illustrate these
708   two rewiring strategies on a small graph in Figure 4.

      In general, reducing the number of edges in a graph slows the convergence of node representations
      during message-passing. Popular rewiring algorithms exploit this fact and aim to sparsify dense
735
      areas of the graph, which are the first to experience smoothing during neighborhood aggregation.
736
      This approach merely slows down the aggregation process in the GNN, postponing the onset of
737   over-smoothing while also decelerating the node representation learning. To directly mitigate over-
738   smoothing, feature aggregation between nodes of different classes must be prevented by removing
739   heterophilic edges. However, relaxing bottlenecks by adding new edges can significantly decrease
740   the homophilic ratio of the graph and exacerbate over-smoothing (see Figure 4). This explains why
741   many rewiring algorithms either fail to address or even worsen the performance of the underlying
742   models.
743   While common rewiring algorithms rely on connectivity measures (e.g., centrality or curvature-
744   based measures) that consider only the graph topology, ignoring node embeddings and thus graph
745   homophily, our method leverages the rate of change in node embeddings to detect heterophilic edges
746   and successfully prevent over-smoothing.

825   that nodes adjacent to bottlenecked edges are less affected by over-smoothing, while nodes located
826   in dense areas of the graph experience faster convergence of their vector embeddings. To prove the
827   lemma, we extend these results by observing that Vt [∆2u ] can be used to classify a node u into one
828   of these two categories.
829   This observation follows from the definition of over-squashing. Building on the results of Nguyen
830   et al. (2023), we can formalize the correlation between over-squashing and connectivity as follows:
831   for a pair of nodes u and v with feature vectors Xtu and Xtv , respectively, where cu < µ < cv
832   (with cu and cv representing their respective connectivity), and assuming both nodes are equidistant
833   from their neighborhood (implying the same amount of information is aggregated at time t = 1), we
834   have ∆tv ∈ o(∆tu ) (in the little-o notation). In other words, node v experiences faster convergence
835   compared to node u, as nodes near bottlenecks tend to have constrained communication paths in
836   the graph and are consequently less likely to experience significant fluctuations in their embedding
837
      values. Now, we can distinguish the two cases: v will converge faster, leading to high values of
      (∆2v )t for small values of t and low values later in time. On the other hand, for node u, the values
838
      of (∆2u )t will vary more slowly over time due to its slower convergence. This intuitive observation
839
      implies that Vt [∆2v ] must necessarily be greater than Vt [∆2u ].
840
841   This concludes the proof, as we have shown that a node with a low connectivity measure (or, equiv-
842   alently, a bottlenecked node) exhibits a relatively low variance of the second delta embeddings com-
843
      pared to other nodes in the graph.

867   erophilic edges (or vice versa), we introduce two multipliers, l and m, to adjust the weights of the

872   which is relatively low. As a result, training the model to rewire the graph while maximizing the
873
      overall score will encourage the model to remove edges while still preserving sparsity. To reduce
      the impact of noise affecting the initial delta values, both the variance and mean are computed using
874
      an exponential moving average, which assigns greater weight to more recent data points.
