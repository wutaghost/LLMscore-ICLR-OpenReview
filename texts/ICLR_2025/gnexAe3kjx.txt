008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            This paper introduces a new type of neural field for visual computing with com-
011            ponents compatible with gate-based quantum hardware or simulators thereof.
012
               Our Quantum Neural Field Network (QNF-Net) expects as input a query coor-
               dinate and, optionally, a latent variable value, and outputs the corresponding field
013
               value. QNF-Net includes a new feature map for classical data encoding and a
014
               parametrised quantum circuit. The proposed neuro-deterministic data encoding
015            converts, into qubit amplitudes, an energy spectrum of the Gibbs-Boltzmann dis-
016            tribution corresponding to the learned problem energy manifold. We provide a
017            theoretical analysis of the model and its components and perform experiments on
018            a simulator of a gate-based quantum computer with 2D images and 3D shapes (and
019            their collections as learnt priors) and compare results with several classical base-
020            lines. QNF-Net consistently outperforms the classical baselines with a compara-
021            ble number of parameters and achieves faster convergence speed, therefore show-
022            ing its potential quantum advantages, even for relatively large-scale problems
023
               compared to what has been demonstrated in quantum machine learning so far.
               We will release the source code to facilitate method reproducibility.

      Coordinate-based neural fields are at the cornerstone of scene representation learning and they are
029
      widely used and indispensable in modern computer vision Xie et al. (2022a); Mildenhall et al.
030
      (2021); Park et al. (2019); Sabella (1988); Shue et al. (2023); Feng et al. (2022); Osher & Fed-
031   kiw (2005). They find applications in robotics Wiesmann et al. (2023); Maggio et al. (2023); Kwon
032   et al. (2023), 3D reconstruction Williams et al. (2022); Sitzmann et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2022);
033   Zhang et al. (2021); Ran et al. (2023) and novel view synthesis Ye et al. (2023); Li et al. (2021b;a);
034   Mildenhall et al. (2021), to name a few areas. Neural fields are often used to continuously pa-
035   rameterise 2D images or 3D scenes and they encode various characteristics of a scene (such as 3D
036   geometry, appearance, and material properties Yang et al. (2021); Shue et al. (2023); Courant et al.
037   (2023)); they provide data priors to other methods and allow scene manipulation and editing (such as
038   interpolation in the latent space, scene inpainting or completion Mirzaei et al. (2023)). The promi-
039
      nent advantages of neural fields include support of different scene topologies and a wide range of
      scene resolutions as well as balancing data fitting and generalisation. All these applications became
040
      possible in recent years, as there has been a notable shift from hand-crafted priors, primarily based
041
      on heuristics, to learning priors in the form of neural fields directly from data Xie et al. (2022b),
042   with multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with ReLU activation being one popular building block for such
043   a neural field, in the early days. Highly desirable characteristics of neural scene representations such
044   as efficient and fast training, lightweightness and high accuracy, however, are still not easily com-
045   binable using modern neural approaches. Moreover, training neural fields can be computationally
046   and resource-demanding, depending on the model and the data collection size. Hence, any possi-
047   ble (even seemingly small) reduction in the required number of parameters and training iterations
048   would be advantageous in widespread techniques and applications relying on neural fields.
049   With emerging interest in variational quantum circuits, i.e. quantum machine learning (QML) Schuld
050   et al. (2015); Biamonte et al. (2017); Cerezo et al. (2022) and given that quantum machine learn-
051   ing (QML) operate under fundamentally different principles compared to its classical counterpart,
052   we shift our focus to QML and hope it can address those open challenges mentioned above. QML,
053   which can execute on gate-based quantum hardware or simulators thereof, takes advantage of the
      gate-based quantum computational paradigm, i.e. the associated quantum-mechanical effects such

178   Amplitude encoding can be used to encode classical input data x as a quantum state |ψ(x)⟩ which
179   can be further processed by an ansatz. One possibility of amplitude encoding is hand-crafted de-
180   sign, which likely can lead to sub-optimality and limitation to a single problem Rathi et al. (2023).
181   Hence, we propose to learn the optimal density ρ(x)opt of quantum state encoding from data of a
182   given type (note that we only consider pure states, i.e., T r(ρ(x)2 ) = 1). As quantum circuits are
183   inherent samplers, one approach to implement a learnable amplitude encoding would be to consider
184   a measurement outcome distribution of a quantum system and approximate it through a probabilistic
185   neural network. However, this can easily cause challenges due to the inherent stochastic nature of
186   the sampling process. Consequently, we adopt an alternative and new stochasticity-free approach,
187
      i.e., inferring the energy spectrum of the quantum system that automatically takes into account such
      probabilistic sampling uncertainty. As a first step, we employ a lightweight vanilla multi-layer per-
188
      ceptron (MLP) with ReLU activations to infer the energy E of the field input Θ; it consists of three
189
      hidden layers with 256 neurons each. Since ReLU-based MLP is biased towards representing low-

458   4.3     A BLATION S TUDY
459
      Besides, we also perform ablation studies analysing the effects of different sub-modules, e.g. the
460   number of involved qubits n, circuit depth J and the expressivity of energy inference module (see
461   Fig. 8). With increasing circuit depth J, ranging from 2 in ascending order to 6 (middle figure),
462   the learning becomes more efficient and accurate in terms of converged value, i.e., consistent with
463   Solovay-Kitaev theorem. Additionally, augmenting the expressivity of energy inference modules
464   through the inclusion of more hidden layers leads to both improved convergence and better perfor-
465   mance (right figure). However, as the number of used qubits n increases, this improvement gets
466   countered in a noticeable way (left figure). We suspect that this can stem from the increased com-
467   plexity of identifying the optimal energy description of the problem input while other factors are
468
      strictly controlled. This implies that we do not need to manipulate many qubits, which can be ad-
      vantageous in practice and on upcoming quantum processors.

      5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
487
488
      This paper formulates a new quantum framework for encoding classical data adaptively into quan-
489   tum states, following entirely new principles compared to existing literature, i.e., quantum system
490   evolution and measurements on quantum devices, which can be used for tasks related to neural
491   field representations. We observe in different scenarios that the QNF-Net allows us to improve
492   both the convergence speed and the representation accuracy compared to different baselines; it can
493   even challenge stronger baselines by incorporating more advanced energy-inference network designs
494   (SIREN). At the same time, it also supports several useful real-world tasks such as shape completion
495   and interpolation in the latent space. We theoretically analyse the model and perform experiments
496   on large-scale 2D and 3D datasets while previous QML works evaluate on small-resolution images.
497
      Moreover, we perform thorough ablation studies of different module components. Notably, we do
      not observe barren plateaus, thanks to our design choices. The contributing factors to their absence
498
      can be manifold such as measurement locality, network hybridisation, and weight initialisation.
499
500   While our work highlights the potential of QML in general neural field representation, there are
501   avenues for future research. Our current approach only partially leverages the information from
502   the Hilbert space due to optimisation and circuit complexity considerations; see Fig. 4. Developing
503
      more effective encoding strategies or deploying scenario-conditioned network model design to har-
      ness additional Hilbert space information while not compromising optimisation performance would
504
      be a promising direction for further study. While a standard, widely accepted approach for efficient
505
      amplitude encoding of arbitrary classical normalized data has yet to emerge, the field of its physical
506   realizations is advancing at a remarkable pace Ashhab (2022); Gonzalez-Conde et al. (2024); Dai-
507   mon & Matsushita (2024). The proposed innovative association between amplitude encoding and
508   energy could potentially inspire the preparation of such quantum states with Hamiltonian evolution
509   using devices such as quantum annealers. Furthermore, future research could explore alternative
510   data encoding strategies that could become more practical as the implementation of QNF-Net on
511   real quantum hardware becomes feasible. Even in the absence of suitable quantum hardware in the
512   near term, our QNF remains valid as a quantum-inspired method. While it does not yet account for
513   all aspects of hardware realization, it offers valuable insights and progresses the field. Lastly, beyond
514
      the demonstrated applications in shape interpolation and completion, QNF also shows promise for
      tasks such as image and shape classification.
