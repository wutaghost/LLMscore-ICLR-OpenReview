011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Combinatorial optimization (CO) problems have widespread applications in sci-
014            ence and engineering but they present significant computational challenges. Re-
015            cent advancements in generative models, particularly diffusion models, have
016            shown promise in bypassing traditional optimization solvers by directly gener-
017            ating near-optimal solutions. However, we observe an exponential scaling law
018            between the optimality gap and the amount of training data needed for training
019
               diffusion-based solvers. Notably, the performance of existing diffusion solvers re-
               lies on both quantity and quality of training data: they perform well with abundant
020
               high quality training data labeled by exact or near-optimal solvers, while suffer-
021
               ing when high-quality labels are scarce or unavailable. To address the challenge,
022            we propose GuideCO, an objective-guided diffusion solver for combinatorial op-
023            timization, which can be trained on imperfectly labelled datasets. GuideCO is
024            a two-stage generate-then-decode framework, featuring an objective-guided dif-
025            fusion model that is further reinforced by classifier-free guidance for generating
026            high-quality solutions on any given problem instance. Experiments demonstrate
027            the improvements of GuideCO against baselines when trained on imperfect data,
028            in a range of combinatorial optimization benchmark tasks such as TSP (Traveling
029            Salesman Problem) and MIS (Maximum Independent Set).

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Combinatorial optimization (CO) problems present fundamental challenges in computational sci-
035   ence, as they involve finding optimal solutions from an exponentially large set of possibilities. Tra-
036   ditionally, approaches to solving CO problems have relied integer programming (IP) or carefully
037   crafted heuristics (Gonzalez, 2007; Arora, 1996), requiring substantial computational resources and
038   extensive domain knowledge.
039
      Recently, generative models have emerged as powerful and promising tools for tackling combina-
040   torial optimization problems. Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Hottung et al., 2021) and diffusion
041   models (Sun & Yang, 2023) have demonstrated their effectiveness in classic challenges such as the
042   Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) and Maximal Independent Set (MIS). Graph generators (Li et al.,
043   2023; You et al., 2019) have shown great potential in solving complex problems like Satisfiability
044   (SAT) and Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP). Beyond traditional benchmarks, generative
045   models are now being successfully applied to real-world combinatorial design tasks such as chip
046   design (Du et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2022) and game design (Cui et al., 2022), highlighting their
047   adaptability to practical applications.
048   Most notably, recent adaptations of diffusion models (Sun & Yang, 2023) to CO have achieved state-
049   of-the-art performance for solving TSP. The success of diffusion models in CO can be attributed to
050   their supervised progressive denoising paradigm, which can directly model the multi-modal joint
051   distribution over the solution space, and enjoys high simplicity in training process at the same time.
052   Therefore, it avoids the sequential generation bottleneck of autoregressive solvers (Vinyals et al.,
053   2015; Kool et al., 2018) and also surpasses the instability in RL-based methods (Wu et al., 2021;
      Chen & Tian, 2019).

      GuideCO offers a two-stage generate-then-decode strategy (§ 3.1): a solution graph is firstly gen-
      erated via a graph diffusion model on the original CO problem graph, and a final solution is then
094
      decoded via greedy methods on top of the solution graph. This two-stage strategy has a intriguing
095
      link to a bi-level relaxation of original CO problems, and this design is empirically observed to
096   be beneficial when training with imperfect data. The key advancement in GuideCO is a objective-
097   conditioned diffusion model (§ 3.2), motivated by its ability to differentiate a generation processes
098   under varying conditions and then generate novel data points aligned with the input condition (Ajay
099   et al., 2022; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Yuan et al., 2024). Therefore, in the optimization context,
100   integrating objective as a condition enables diffusion models to differentiate generation processes
101   with varying levels of optimality. Thus during inference, GuideCO can guide the generation process
102   to a direction with higher optimality. In addition, we propose a novel classifier-free guidance for CO
103   (Ho & Salimans, 2022) that can further reinforce the guidance strength (§3.2.2).
104   Experiment results demonstrate positive answers to the two question we have raised. We evaluated
105   GuideCO on two benchmark tasks such as TSPs (with varying sizes of 50, 100, 500, 1000) and MIS
106   on SATLIB and weighted/unweighted Erdos–Rényi graphs. For Question 1, GuideCO consistently
107   outperforms DIFUSCO across all benchmarks when both models are trained with instances with
      sub-optimal labels (Tables in § 4.3 and § 4.4), delivering a decisive positive answer to Q1. For the

133   ci (x, G) ≤ 0 represents the set of constraints. The goal of CO is to find the solution x satisfying

141   permutation or a subset of all nodes in G. For a graph with n nodes, define the set of its node indices

      A generative perspective (Sun & Yang, 2023; Li et al., 2024a) has been adopted to seeks one (or
182
      multiple) optimal solutions x∗ given the problem instance instance G. It naturally corresponds to a
183   conditional generation task: to generate x conditioned on G according to P (x∗ | G), a conditional
184   distribution of optimal solutions given the problem instance. GuideCO is primarily based on this
185   generative perspective and designs of Sun & Yang (2023), in which a solution is formulated as
186   binary vectors and P (x∗ | G) is viewed as a graph-to-vector prediction task, and a final solution is
187   decoded based on the logits of prediction.
188
189   3.1    G ENERATE -T HEN -D ECODE F RAMEWORK
190
191   In this paper, we propose a two-stage generation method that merges diffusion and heuristics meth-
192   ods for effectively generating solutions for CO, generalizing the key designs in Sun & Yang (2023).
193   It’s called generate-then-decode: in the first stage a “solution graph” G x is generated based on the
194   “problem graph” G, and in the second stage a decoding algorithm h(·) is applied to solve G x so
195
      that the final solution is obtained as x = h(G x ). The utilization of heuristic decoding methods can
      reduce requirement of solution quality generated by diffusion model while without compromising
196
      quality.
197
198   With this two-stage view, the current generative formulation of CO closely links to the following
199   bi-level relaxation of the original problem:
200
                             min f (x | G)
201                          x,G x
202                     s.t. x ∈ arg min
                                      ′
                                         {f (x′ | G x ) : ci (x′ , G x ) ≤ 0, for i = 1 . . . I} ,           (2)
                                       x
203
204   here f and G are the same objective and problem instance in (1), and lower level problem in (2) is
205   approximately solved by the decoding algorithm h(·) in the two-stage process.

      We use TSP and MIS as two examples to showcase the two-stage process and the link to bi-level
207
      relaxation. The solution graph G x = ⟨V x , E x ⟩ generated in the first stage and the solution x output

      in the second stage are defined as follows:
209

211             unchanged from the problem graph G. In E x , the edge between node i and j exits if and

213             i = 1 · · · n − 1, ei,j = 0 for the rest of positions.

506   5    R ELATED W ORK
507
508   Machine Learning for Combinatorial Optimization(ML4CO). ML4CO has been as a signifi-
509
      cant area of research over the past decade: previous methods are can be catogorized into autoregres-
      sive solver models (Vinyals et al., 2015; Bello et al., 2016; Kool et al., 2018), non-autoregressive
510
      solver models (Joshi et al., 2019; Qiu et al., 2022) and reinforcement learning-based improvement
511
      heuristics (Wu et al., 2021; Chen & Tian, 2019). Recently, diffusion model has demonstrated its
512   potential in solving CO and DIFUSCO (Sun & Yang, 2023) has achieved the state-of-the-art per-
513   formance when applied for solving TSP. Li et al. (2024a) and Yoon et al. are two recent works
514   also trying to improve DIFUSCO from a perspective of making the backward generation process
515   in diffusion solver objective-aware. However, in contrast to GuideCO focusing on improving the
516   “pre-training” stage of diffusion solver, their methods take the pre-trained model as a starting point,
517   and make improvement in the “post-training” stage by searching and fine-tuning.
518
519   Optimization Powered by Diffusion Models. In addition to the recent progress in applying gen-
520   erative models to CO reviewed in the paragraph above, we want to cover some representative works
521   on “reward-improving diffusion models”, the reward therein is a direct analogy to the objective
522
      in optimization context. A line of works propose to train a reward-conditioned diffusion model, for
      generating samples with higher rewards at inference time. This paradigm has demonstrated superior
523
      performance in black-box optimization (Krishnamoorthy et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b) and trajec-
524
      tory optimization in reinforcement learning (Ajay et al., 2022). More generally, to improve diffusion
525   models for generating sample quality of high quality measured by an external reward (which could
526   be a white-box, black-box or first-order oracle), guidance methods including classifier-free guid-
527   ance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) and variants of classifier guidance (Chung et al., 2022; Guo et al.,
528   2024; Bansal et al., 2023), as well as fine-tuning (Clark et al., 2023) methods are good candidates.
529   In this paper, we adopt a discrete version of classifier-free guidance. Nisonoff et al. (2024) proposes
530   a similar “predition-free guidance” for categorical data but with continuous time steps.
531
      6    C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      In this paper, we identified an exponential data scaling law in training diffusion solvers for CO,
534   and their performance highly depends on data quality. To address this challenge, we proposed
535   GuideCO, an objective-guided training framework of diffusion solvers. GuideCO is based on a two-
536   stage generate-then-decode strategy, featuring an objective-guided diffusion model that is further
537   reinforced by classifier-free guidance to better utilize imperfect training instances labelled by poly-
538   nomial heuristics. Experimental results showed that GuideCO outperformed the baseline DIFUSCO
539   when trained with heuristic-labeled data, and notably GuideCO outperformed DIFUSCO trained
      with solver-labeled instances when abundant number of training instances is not accessible.
