009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in diffusion models have significantly enhanced the quality of
012            image synthesis, yet they have also introduced serious safety concerns, particu-
013            larly the generation of Not Safe for Work (NSFW) content. Previous research has
014            demonstrated that adversarial prompts can be used to generate NSFW content.
015            However, such adversarial text prompts are often easily detectable by text-based
016            filters, limiting their efficacy. In this paper, we expose a previously overlooked
017            vulnerability: adversarial image attacks targeting Image-to-Image (I2I) diffusion
018            models. We propose AdvI2I, a novel framework that manipulates input images
019
               to induce diffusion models to generate NSFW content. By optimizing a genera-
               tor to craft adversarial images, AdvI2I circumvents existing defense mechanisms,
020
               such as Safe Latent Diffusion (SLD), without altering the text prompts. Further-
021
               more, we introduce AdvI2I-Adaptive, an enhanced version that adapts to potential
022            countermeasures and minimizes the resemblance between adversarial images and
023            NSFW concept embeddings, making the attack more resilient against defenses.
024            Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that both AdvI2I and AdvI2I-
025            Adaptive can effectively bypass current safeguards, highlighting the urgent need
026            for stronger security measures to address the misuse of I2I diffusion models.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recently, diffusion models have made significant strides in the domain of image synthesis, demon-
035   strating their ability to produce high-quality images (Rombach et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023).
036   However, these advancements have also raised significant ethical and safety concerns. Particularly,
037   when provided with certain prompts, Text-to-Image (T2I) diffusion models can be abused to gener-
038   ate Not Safe for Work (NSFW) content that depicts unsafe concepts such as violence and nudity. This
039
      issue stems from the presence of NSFW samples in the large-scale training datasets sourced from
      the Internet (Schuhmann et al., 2022), making it a pervasive problem in emerging diffusion models
040
      (Truong et al., 2024; Schramowski et al., 2023). Despite some early efforts have been made in de-
041
      fending against the generation of NSFW content (Gandikota et al., 2023; 2024; Schramowski et al.,
042   2023; CompVis, 2022), recent studies have shown that these safeguards can still be circumvented by
043   carefully crafted adversarial prompts (Yang et al., 2024c; Ma et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a; Tsai
044   et al., 2023). As a result, malicious users can exploit these models to generate NSFW images for
045   unethical purposes.
046
      While adversarial prompts present a notable risk to the generation safety of diffusion models, their
047
      Achilles’ heel lies in that such attacks work by changing the input text prompt, which can exhibit
048   easily detectable patterns that distinguish them from natural prompts. Specifically, we applied four
049   types of simple filters (perplexity filter, keyword filter, embedding filter and large language model
050   (LLM) filter) to a range of adversarial prompt attacks (Zhuang et al., 2023; Kou et al., 2023; Tsai
051   et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024c), and found that even the simplest filters can effec-
052   tively identify adversarial prompts from normal ones in most cases (see more detailed in Section 3.1).
053   Notably, a naive perplexity filter can (on average) reduce the attack success rate (ASR) of adversarial
      prompts by 58%, while using an LLM as the safety filter can reduce the ASR to under 20%.

054
      This suggests that adversarial text prompts can be identified, which means that diffusion models can
055   reject generating images with such queries if detected. However, the new question is:
056
057        Does the rejection of adversarial text prompts truly ensure the safety of diffusion models?
058
059   In this work, we provide a negative answer to this question. We reveal the risk of adversarial images
060
      that can also induce diffusion models to generate NSFW images, which has not been well explored
      in previous research. We propose a framework named AdvI2I to demonstrate the effectiveness of
061
      such an attack on the Image-to-image (I2I) diffusion model, alerting the community to adversarial
062
      attacks from not only the prompt but also the image condition side. In addition to text prompts, I2I
063   diffusion models conventionally utilize an image as a conditioning input. By leveraging adversarial
064   images, attackers can induce the diffusion model to generate NSFW images. For example, an image
065   of the president can be manipulated to depict nudity. Moreover, this method can bypass current
066   defense mechanisms on diffusion models and thereby represents a significant but underexplored
067   security vulnerability in this domain.
068
      The key to obtaining such powerful adversarial images lies in optimizing an adversarial image gen-
069   erator. The optimization target is the denoised latent feature in the diffusion process. Given that the
070   feature is influenced by both the image and text conditions, AdvI2I transforms the NSFW concept
071   from the text embedding space into the adversarial perturbation on images, enabling it to guide the
072   model in generating NSFW content. Additionally, to further explore the efficacy of such adversarial
073   attack under potential defenses, we propose a modified attack approach named AdvI2I-Adaptive.
074   This method introduces a loss term to minimize similarity between the generated image and NSFW
075   concept embeddings detected by safety checkers, while also adding Gaussian noise during training.
076   By incorporating these adaptive elements, AdvI2I-Adaptive enhances the robustness of adversarial
077
      attacks against current defense measures, significantly amplifying the threat posed by adversarial
      images in I2I diffusion models. Our contributions are summarized as follows.
078
079
      • We systematically evaluates the performance of adversarial prompt attacks on diffusion models
080     with various defenses, demonstrating that simple filters are effective in defending against these
081     attacks.
082
      • We introduce a novel adversarial image attack framework, AdvI2I, which reveals a previously
083     unexplored vulnerability in I2I diffusion models. This attack involves injecting adversarial pertur-
084     bations into images to induce the generation of NSFW content, thus broadening the understanding
085     of potential risks beyond text-based adversarial attacks.
086
      • By highlighting the risk of adversarial attacks from image conditions, this work raises awareness
087     within the research community about the potential dangers of such attacks on diffusion models,
088     urging further investigation and development of robust defenses.

      Adversarial Attack and Defense in T2I Diffusion Model. Diffusion models are susceptible to
      generating NSFW images due to the difficulty of thoroughly eliminating problematic data from
094
      training datasets. Recent studies have explored the potential for adversarial prompts to manipulate
095
      these models to create inappropriate images (Zhuang et al., 2023; Kou et al., 2023; Tsai et al., 2023;
096   Ma et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024c). For example, QF-Attack (Zhuang et al., 2023) generates adver-
097   sarial prompts by minimizing the cosine distance between the features of the original prompts and
098   those of target prompts extracted by the text encoder. Similarly, Ring-A-Bell (Tsai et al., 2023) uses
099   steering vectors (Subramani et al., 2022) representing unsafe concepts as optimization targets for
100   adversarial prompts. This method effectively circumvents concept removal techniques (Gandikota
101   et al., 2023; 2024; Pham et al., 2024). However, these approaches primarily focus on adversarial text
102   prompts, which are discernible to humans. Recent defense mechanisms against adversarial prompt
103   attacks have emerged (Yang et al., 2024b; Wu et al., 2024). For instance, GuardT2I (Yang et al.,
104
      2024b) employs LLMs to convert encoded features of prompts back into plain texts, enabling the
      identification of malicious intent by distinguishing between adversarial and typical NSFW prompts.
105
106   I2I Diffusion Models. Diffusion models are employed primarily for creating new images based on
107   textual prompts, known as T2I diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022). More
      recently, researchers have discovered that these models can also modify existing images based on


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      text instructions (Meng et al., 2021; Brooks et al., 2023; Parmar et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023).
109   SDEdit (Meng et al., 2021) changes the input from random noise to a noisy image in the inference
110   stage, while maintaining the structure and training methodology of existing T2I models. Building
111   on this, pix2pix-zero (Parmar et al., 2023) achieves I2I translation by preserving the input image’s
112   cross-attention maps throughout the diffusion process. InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) and
113   Visual Instruction Inversion (Nguyen et al., 2023) use images as a secondary condition alongside
114   text, combining their features with the intermediate latent vector zt to enhance image editing pre-
115   cision. Despite the promising performance and broad applicability of these I2I models, their safety
116   concerns remain underexplored.

      I2I Diffusion Models. I2I diffusion models for image editing take both a text prompt p and an
196
      image x as input. Typically, a pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) text encoder τθ (·) transforms
197
      the text prompt p into the text feature τθ (p), while the input image x is encoded into a latent feature
198   E(x) by the encoder of a variational autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013). The diffusion process
199   consists of T timesteps, starting from random latent noise zT . At each timestep t ∈ [1, T ], a model
200   ϵθ (zt , E(x), τθ (p), t) is used to predict the noise and update the latent feature from zt to zt−1 .

      3.2   A DV I2I F RAMEWORK
203
204   The objective of AdvI2I is to generate adversarial images that compel diffusion models to produce
205   NSFW content. The high-level idea of AdvI2I is to find the adversarial image that is equivalent to the
206   NSFW concept shifted embedding, which can effectively induce the generation of NSFW content
207   in diffusion models. As illustrated in Fig. 1, AdvI2I generally contains three steps: 1) extract the
208   NSFW concept from constructed prompt pairs and use it to shift the original prompt embedding
209   into an NSFW embedding; 2) train the adversarial image generator such that the latent feature of
210   the adversarial image (with benign prompt) during the diffusion process resembles the latent feature
211   guided by the shifted NSFW embedding. 3) use the trained generator to turn any new input image
212
      into an adversarial one that allows the generation of the corresponding NSFW content.
213   NSFW Concept Vector Extraction. Existing research has shown that it is possible to extract an
214   embedding vector that represents a certain concept (Tsai et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024) with a pair of
215   contrastive prompts. Here we aim to extract an NSFW concept vector c (e.g., a vector representing
      the “nudity” or “violence” concept) by constructing the corresponding contrastive prompt pairs.

228   Adversarial Image Generator Training. After obtaining the NSFW embedding, a straightforward
229   method is to directly optimize an adversarial perturbation on an image to achieve our goal of induc-
230   ing NSFW content. However, such a method would require us to repeat this optimization process
231   for every new image to be attacked. In order to make this attack universal and transferable across
232   multiple images, we plan to use an image generator, which allows us to turn any new images into
233
      adversarial ones to induce the diffusion model to generate NSFW content.
234   Now our goal here is to train the image generator to produce adversarial images that can lead the
235   diffusion model to generate NSFW content while ensuring that the generated image remains visually
236   similar to the original image. Let us denote gψ (·) as our generator (parameterized by ψ) which takes
237   a benign image x and generates an adversarial image gψ (x). Unlike traditional generator training
238   approaches (Naseer et al., 2021) that use U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) or ResNet (He et al.,
239
      2016) architectures, we leverage a pre-trained VAE as the adversarial image generator to ensure
      greater similarity between the adversarial and original images.

314   Datasets. To train the adversarial noise generator and evaluate the effectiveness of AdvI2I, we con-
315   struct an image-text dataset (i.e., one sample includes an image and a text prompt). The images are
316   sourced from the “sexy” category of the NSFW Data Scraper (Kim, 2020), consisting predominantly
317   of the human bodies. We filter out images that are classified as NSFW and randomly select 400 im-
318   ages from the remaining set. Additionally, 30 text prompts are generated for image editing using
319   ChatGPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024). Then, we randomly select 200 images and 10 text prompts from each
320
      set to construct 2000 image-text samples, in which 1800 samples are used for training adversarial
      image generators and the remaining 200 samples are for evaluation.

      based on user instructions, allowing users to specify modifications such as changing objects, styles,

333   analysis, please refer to Appendix A.2. In addition, we incorporate MMA-Diffusion (Yang et al.,
334   2024a), which originally utilizes text and image modalities to generate NSFW content while evad-
335   ing post-hoc safety filters. We adapt MMA-Diffusion to our experimental setup by replacing text
336   prompts in our dataset with adversarial text prompts generated by MMA-Diffusion and training the
337   adversarial perturbations on the images with 1800 image-text pair samples, enabling its adversarial
338   perturbations to generalize across multiple prompts and images.
339   Defense Strategies. We evaluate the robustness of the AdvI2I attack under three different types
340   of defense strategies. Specifically, Safe Latent Diffusion (SLD) (Schramowski et al., 2023) and
341   Negative Prompt (SD-NP) (Rombach et al., 2022) are popular concept removal methods applied
342   during the inference process. We modify the parameters of the SLD to adapt to I2I diffusion models.
343   Please refer to Appendix A.3 for more details. For nudity, we use “nudity”, while for violence, we
344   use “violence” as their negative prompts for SLD and SD-NP. Gaussian Noising (GN) (Hönig et al.,
345
      2024) is a pre-process defense that adds Gaussin noise to the input images. Here we use the same
      noise bound as the adversarial noise. Safety Checker (SC) is a post-hoc defense that uses a model to
346
      calculate the cosine similarity between the output images and the NSFW concepts.
347
348   Evaluation Metric. We compute the ASR of adversarial images by evaluating whether generated
349   images contain NSFW content. Following (Tsai et al., 2023), to classify whether the generated
350   images have nudity content, we employ the NudeNet (nud, 2023) detector. If the detector identifies
351
      any of the following labels in an image: BUTTOCKS EXPOSED, FEMALE BREAST EXPOSED,
      MALE BREAST EXPOSED, ANUS EXPOSED, MALE GENITALIA EXPOSED, we categorize
352
      the image as containing nudity. To assess whether the images contain other inappropriate content
353
      such as violence, we use the Q16 classifier (Schramowski et al., 2022).

      5   C ONCLUSION
477
      In this work, we present AdvI2I, a novel framework designed to expose a vulnerability previously
478
      underexplored in I2I diffusion models. Although previous research has focused predominantly on
479
      adversarial prompt attacks in T2I models, our framework highlights the potential risks posed by
480   adversarial image attacks. By injecting adversarial perturbations into conditioning images, AdvI2I
481   successfully manipulates diffusion models to generate NSFW content, bypassing current defense
482   mechanisms designed to mitigate adversarial attacks on diffusion models. Our experiments demon-
483   strate the effectiveness of this approach, showing that even with benign text prompts, adversarially
484   altered images can induce diffusion models to produce harmful output. We urge the research com-
485   munity to further investigate robust defenses against such adversarial image attacks and consider
      both text- and image-based inputs when designing safety mechanisms for generative models.

652   We evaluate the transferability of adversarial image attacks from the SDv1.5-Inpainting model to

658

659
      to totally nullify the NSFW concept from the model by thoroughly cleaning the training data.
660

662   Pix2Pix because its model architecture differs from that of the SD models. Furthermore, the training
663   image resolution of InstructPix2Pix is 256x256, whereas SD models struggle to achieve effective
664
      editing results at this resolution. Therefore, a direct transferability test between these models would
      not yield meaningful insights due to their structural and resolution differences.
