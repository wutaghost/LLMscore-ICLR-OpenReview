009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Graph-structured data plays a vital role in numerous domains, such as social net-
012               works, citation networks, commonsense reasoning graphs and knowledge graphs.
013               While graph neural networks have been employed for graph processing, recent
014               advancements have explored integrating large language models for graph-based
015               tasks. In this paper, we propose a novel approach named Learnable Graph Pool-
016               ing Token (LGPT), which addresses the limitations of the scalability issues in
017               node-level projection and information loss in graph-level projection. LGPT en-
018
                  ables flexible and efficient graph representation by introducing learnable parame-
                  ters that act as tokens in large language models, balancing fine-grained and global
019
                  graph information. Additionally, we investigate an Early Query Fusion technique,
020
                  which fuses query context before constructing the graph representation, leading to
021               more effective graph embeddings. Our method achieves a 4.13% performance im-
022               provement on the GraphQA benchmark without training the large language model,
023               demonstrating significant gains in handling complex textual-attributed graph data.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   A graph is a data structure composed of nodes and edges that represent the relationships between
030   those nodes. Graphs are essential for representing complex relational situations in the real world.
031   For example, social networks (Li et al., 2024; Myers et al., 2014) like X (Twitter) and urban net-
032   works, citation networks (Hu et al., 2020) in academic fields that represent authorship, affiliations,
033   and citations, protein and molecular graphs (Cao et al., 2023) for depicting complex molecular
034
      interactions, commonsense reasoning graphs like ConceptNet (Speer et al., 2017), and knowledge
      graphs such as Wikidata (Vrandečić & Krötzsch, 2014) that store various facts. Traditionally, graph
035
      data has been processed using handcrafted feature extraction methods like Katz Index and PageRank
036
      (Katz, 1953; Page, 1999). However, with the recent advancements in deep learning, Graph Neural
037   Networks (GNNs) such as GCN, GAT, and Graph Transformer have become widely researched for
038   processing graphs (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Veličković et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2020).
039
040
      Meanwhile, the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP) has experienced a revolutionary shift
      with the advent of Large Language Models (LLMs). These models, pre-trained on massive text
041
      datasets, possess general problem-solving abilities (Chung et al., 2024). Recently, it has been re-
042
      ported that LLMs can also understand the structural information of graphs and solve graph tasks
043   (Fatemi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b; Chai et al., 2023). The combination of LLMs and graphs
044   is particularly useful in Text-Attributed Graphs (TAGs), where each node and edge contains textual
045   features. One of the simplest approaches is to transform graph information into text and feed it into
046   the LLM through knowledge-augmented prompting, as demonstrated by methods like Baek et al.
047   (2023) and Wu et al. (2023).
048
      However, graphs contain highly complex structural information, making it difficult to convert them
049   into text. Moreover, the performance varies significantly depending on how the graph is textualized,
050   and the optimal text encoding method is still unknown (Fatemi et al., 2023). To overcome these
051   limitations, Perozzi et al. (2024) has achieved significant performance improvements by embedding
052   graph data using GNNs and projecting it into the word embedding space of LLMs through contin-
053

054
      uous prompting. Furthermore, Tian et al. (2024) proposed a technique that distills query-related
055   information during the interaction between graph and query via cross-modality pooling.
056
057   This continuous prompting method for graphs can be categorized into node-level projection and
058
      graph-level projection (Ren et al., 2024). Node-level projection passes the information of all nodes,
      obtained through the GNN, to the LLM and is used in tasks such as node classification or link
059
      prediction, which require fine-grained structural information. Graph-level projection compresses
060
      node representations into a single vector and passes it to the LLM, which is useful in tasks like
061   graph classification that require global graph information.
062
063   However, both approaches have limitations. In node-level projection, each node representation is
064
      treated as a token by the LLM. Since graphs tend to grow exponentially, this method lacks scalability
      given the limited prompt length of LLM. Even if a model, like Beltagy et al. (2020), can process
065
      extremely long prompts, the computational cost becomes prohibitive. Graph-level projection, where
066
      all node information is pooled into a single vector and passed to the LLM, avoids the scalability
067   issue. However, converting a graph with complex context into a single vector results in information
068   loss (Bahdanau, 2014). Given that LLMs must process a graph with vast amounts of information as
069   a single token, this is inevitable.
070
      To address these limitations, we propose a new concept named Learnable Graph Pooling Token
071
      (LGPT). This allows graph information to be represented by n learnable parameters, which are
072
      passed to the LLM as n tokens. This approach resolves both the computational issue of node-level
073   projection and the information loss problem in graph-level projection. Additionally, we investigate
074   an early query fusion method and deal with the limitations of a late query fusion method. While
075   cross-modality pooling as the late query fusion in Tian et al. (2024) combines query context with
076   graph embeddings, it does so after the graph is encoded. In contrast, we propose an approach
077   that integrates query context before constructing the graph representation, thereby offering a more
078   effective graph embedding method that takes the query context into account.
079   Our main contribution is as follows:
080
081          • We propose a novel concept of Learnable Graph Pooling Token (LGPT), which enables bal-
082            anced projection between node-level and graph-level projection. As a result, we achieved
083            more than a 4.13% improvement in performance on the GraphQA benchmark dataset with-
084            out LLM training.
085          • We explore a method to integrate the early query fusion method during the graph em-
086            bedding process. Through experiments, we demonstrate that incorporating query context
087            before constructing the node embeddings of the graph leads to greater performance im-
088            provements than combining it afterward.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
092
093   2.1   LLM AS G RAPH E NCODER
094
      Fatemi et al. (2023) and Wang et al. (2024b) demonstrated that encoding graphs into various textual
095
      forms allows LLMs to solve graph-centric tasks. Additionally, Wang et al. (2024a) advanced Chain
096
      of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) into a graph-suitable format by adding the instruction “Let’s
097   construct a graph with the nodes and edges first” enabling LLMs to map graph information into
098   conceptual space. However, these approaches all have the limitation of processing graphs at the text
099   level. Since graphs contain complex relational information, converting them into text loses a lot of
100   structural knowledge. To overcome these limitations, Perozzi et al. (2024); Tian et al. (2024) have
101   emerged that embed graphs using GNNs and integrate them with LLMs. Notably, He et al. (2024)
102   achieved significant performance improvements by using both textual graphs and GNN embeddings.
103
104   2.2   L EARNABLE P OOLING M ETHOD
105
106   Sum and Mean Pooling have traditionally been used as readout functions to create graph embed-
107   dings from node embeddings in GNNs. However, they suffer from scalability issues that arise when
      dealing with graphs that have a varying number of nodes, an inability to emphasize important nodes


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and information loss in compressing node information into a single vector. To address these limi-
109   tations, learnable pooling methods that incorporate learnable parameters have been explored. Ying
110   et al. (2018) introduced hierarchical pooling by applying soft clustering to reflect the hierarchical
111   structure of graphs. Also, Lee et al. (2019) proposed a learnable pooling method by incorporating
112   an attention mechanism to capture more information from important nodes. Nevertheless, these ap-
113   proaches still face the risk of information loss as they condense numerous node embeddings into a
114   single graph embedding vector (Bahdanau, 2014).
115
116   2.3     Q UERY AWARE G RAPH R EPRESENTATION
117
118   In Tian et al. (2024), a method was introduced to combine graph and query representations as cross
119
      modality pooling using a cross-attention mechanism. While this merges the information from the
      graph and the query, it has the limitation of being a Late Fusion approach, as the graph and query
120
      information are encoded independently before being combined. In Yasunaga et al. (2021), a virtual
121
      query node is created within the graph to perform graph encoding that is dependent on the meaning
122   of the query. This approach is effectively used in Early Fusion for query-aware graph representation,
123   as seen in its connection to instruction nodes in models like Zhang et al. (2022) and Yasunaga et al.
124   (2022).

225   3.2.3    L EARNABLE G RAPH P OOLING T OKENS (LGPT)
226
227   There are two main approaches to prompting with a graph encoder. The first approach involves
228   passing all node embeddings to the LLM, while the second approach uses a readout function to
229
      transform node embeddings into single graph embedding, which is then passed to the LLM. In
      node-level prompting, each node is treated as a token by the LLM, but as the number of nodes
230
      increases, this method becomes impractical due to scalability issues. On the other hand, in graph-
231
      level prompting, methods such as mean pooling, DiffPool(Ying et al., 2018) and SAGPool (Lee
232   et al., 2019) are used to compress node embeddings into a single vector, which is then provided to
233   the LLM. However, this requires encoding all the graph information into a single vector, increasing
234   the risk of information loss (Bahdanau, 2014).
235
      To address this issue, we propose a novel pooling method named Learnable Graph Pooling Tokens
236
      (LGPT). LGPT employees n learnable parameters with the same dimension as the node embeddings,
237
      which are fully connected to all the nodes in the given graph Sg . After that, message passing is per-
238   formed through a GNNpool process, resulting in Sp and graph representation n tokens {g1′ · · · gn′ }.
239   This method reduces the risk of information loss compared to previous approaches that represented
240   the graph using only a single vector. Finally, processed LGPT {g1′ · · · gn′ } is transformed into ES ,
241   the input format for the LLM, through a projection layer Proj composed of Multi-Layer Perceptron
242   (MLP).
243
244                                               LGPT : {g1 , · · · , gn }                             (10)
245
                        {g1′ · · · gn′ } ∪ {xn }n ∈ nodes of Sp = GNNpool (Sg , {g1 , · · · , gn })     (11)
246
247                                           ES = Proj({g1′ · · · gn′ })                               (12)
248
      The reason LGPT works effectively is that it conceptually combines two learnable pooling methods.
249
      Ying et al. (2018) performs pooling hierarchically through soft clustering, where a node can be
250   assigned to multiple clusters. In our method, since all nodes are connected to learnable tokens and
251   perform message passing for pooling, each LGPT token can be seen as a soft cluster, making our
252   approach conceptually aligned with soft clustering in Ying et al. (2018). Additionally, by using
253   Graph Transformer (Shi et al., 2020) as the GNN architecture, our method operates similarly to
254   Lee et al. (2019), which employs the self-attention mechanism. In essence, our method works well
255   because it conceptually borrows from both Ying et al. (2018) and Lee et al. (2019). However, the
256   key difference from these methods is that, instead of pooling into a single graph embedding, our
257   approach uses multiple learnable tokens for pooling, thereby reducing information loss.
258   In summary, the proposed method consists of two main components: Early Query Fusion and the
259   Learnable Graph Pooling Tokens. Learnable parameters of GNNquery and GNNgraph are required
260   for Early Query Fusion while learnable parameters of GNNpool and LGPT are necessary for the
261   graph pooling.

      3.3     A NALYSIS OF T IME C OMPLEXITY
265
266   Let k denote the number of nodes, t the number of prompt text tokens, g the number of GNN layers,
267   and n the number of LGPTs. The time complexity of the proposed Graph Encoder, which employs
268   three GNNs, is thus O(3g(n + k)). In comparison, the time complexity of both G-Retriever and
269   GraphToken is O(gk). Given that n ≪ k, the time complexity of our method aligns with that of
      other baseline graph encoders, remaining at O(gk).


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      The computational time complexity for the LLM component is determined by the self-attention
271   mechanism and is proportional to the square of the prompt length. In our model, the prompt consists
272   of t + n tokens, whereas GraphToken and G-Retriever process t + 1 tokens. We set k = 8 to
273   ensure n ≪ t, thereby maintaining the time complexity of LLM computation in our model at O(t2 ),
274   consistent with that of other baseline models.

409   4.3   E FFECT OF E ARLY Q UERY F USION
410
411   Sun et al. (2018) reported that in the process of knowledge enhancement, Early Fusion, where
412   information is combined during the representation creation phase, results in greater performance
413   improvements compared to Late Fusion, where embeddings are combined after they have been
414   independently generated. On the other hand, Tian et al. (2024) employed late fusion when combining
415   query and graph information, in which the fusion process only occurs after the graph information
416
      has been fully encoded.
417   In this paper, we adopted Early Query Fusion, where query information is integrated before the graph
418   embeddings are generated. To validate the effectiveness of this approach, we conducted experiments
419   comparing early fusion and late fusion methods. For late fusion, we used the cross modality pooling
420   technique from Tian et al. (2024), while for Early Fusion, we applied the Early Query Fusion strategy
421   proposed in this work.
422   The results present in Table 4 show the differences between the two methods. In the case that
423   is applied mean pooling as the readout function, late fusion performance decreases compared to
424   when query fusion is not used. This suggests that combining fully processed embeddings from
425   different modalities may work as noise or lead to inefficient information integration. On the other
426   hand, early query fusion shows slight performance improvements, indicating that integrating query
427   information earlier in the process allows for better representation and information fusion within the
428
      graph structure. Moreover, when both Early Fusion and Late Fusion are applied together, a greater
      average performance improvement is observed.

431   provements compared to late fusion. Similar to the case with mean pooling, applying late fusion
      leads to a slight performance decrease. Moreover, combining both methods also results in a per-

481   In this section, we compared the model’s performance on the SceneGraph dataset by varying the
482   number of LGPT from 1, 8, to 32. The results are shown in Figure 4 and regardless of the Query
483   Fusion method, using 8 LGPTs consistently outperforms using just 1 LGPT. This suggests, as men-
484   tioned earlier, that encoding the complex information of a graph into a single vector leads to infor-
485   mation loss. Specifically, when compressing all the graph information into a single vector, important
      relationships and characteristics may not be fully captured, resulting in degraded performance.

      5   C ONCLUSION

526
527
      In this work, we introduced a novel approach, the Learnable Graph Pooling Token (LGPT), which
528   addresses the challenges of graph representation for text-attributed graph question answering tasks.
529   Our method bridges the gap between node-level and graph-level projections by representing graph
530   information with learnable parameters passed as tokens to large language models. This approach
531   mitigates both the scalability issues inherent in node-level projections and the information loss in
532   graph-level projections. Additionally, we proposed an Early Query Fusion technique, which incor-
533   porates query information during the graph embedding process, ensuring that query-specific details
534   are embedded into the graph representation before it is constructed. This method demonstrates sig-
535   nificant performance improvements over previous approaches using late query fusion.
536   Through extensive experimentation on the GraphQA benchmark, our approach consistently out-
537   performed existing methods, achieving an average improvement of 4.13% over the baseline model
538   without training LLM. The combination of LGPT and Early Query Fusion proved to be highly effec-
539   tive in addressing the complexities of textual-attributed graphs while ensuring scalable and efficient
      graph representation without training large language models.
