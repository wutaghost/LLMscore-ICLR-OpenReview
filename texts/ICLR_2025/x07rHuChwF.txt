011
               Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have made rapid progress in recent
012
               years, yet continue to struggle with low-level visual perception—particularly the
013
               ability to accurately describe the geometric details of an image. This capability
014            is crucial for applications in areas such as robotics, medical image analysis, and
015            manufacturing. To address this challenge, we first introduce Geoperception, a
016            benchmark designed to evaluate an MLLM’s ability to accurately transcribe 2D
017            geometric information from an image. Using this benchmark, we demonstrate
018            the limitations of leading MLLMs, and then conduct a comprehensive empirical
019            study to explore strategies for improving their performance on geometric tasks.
020            Our findings highlight the benefits of certain model architectures, training tech-
021            niques, and data strategies, including the use of high-fidelity synthetic data and
022
               multi-stage training with a data curriculum. Notably, we find that a data curricu-
               lum enables models to learn challenging geometry understanding tasks which they
023
               fail to learn from scratch. Leveraging these insights, we develop Euclid, a fam-
024
               ily of models specifically optimized for strong low-level geometric perception.
025            Although purely trained on synthetic multimodal data, Euclid shows strong gen-
026            eralization ability to novel geometry shapes. For instance, Euclid outperforms the
027            best closed-source model, Gemini-1.5-Pro, by up to 54.52% on benchmark tasks.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have rapidly progressed in recent years, demonstrat-
034   ing remarkable potential in understanding and reasoning about the visual world through the powerful
035   capabilities of large language models (LLMs) (Liu et al., 2024c;a; Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al.,
036   2023; Hu et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024a). These models have showcased
037   strong performance in tasks such as visual question answering (VQA) (Goyal et al., 2017), image
038   captioning (Lin et al., 2014), and multimodal reasoning (Liu et al., 2023). As one recent exam-
039
      ple, LLaVA-NeXT-34B (Liu et al., 2024b) achieves an impressive 83.7% accuracy on the VQAv2
      benchmark (Goyal et al., 2017), a comprehensive benchmark on natural image question answering.
040
041   While MLLMs achieve impressive results on tasks like VQA, their performance relies on high-
042   level semantic extraction (Tong et al., 2024b); in contrast, they often fall short on low-level visual
043   perception—i.e., the ability to accurately describe the geometric details of an image, such as the
044   points, lines, angles, shapes, and spatial relationships among its constituent objects. This limitation
045
      becomes especially apparent in tasks requiring precise descriptions, such as mathematical visual
      problem solving (Zhang et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2023), scientific visual understanding (Yue et al.,
046
      2024; Fu et al., 2024a), abstract visual reasoning (Jiang et al., 2024; Ahrabian et al., 2024), and even
047
      simple visual comprehension (Rahmanzadehgervi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b). For example,
048   when interpreting a graph diagram, precise recognition of edges is essential for extracting reliable
049   information, and in geometry problem-solving, accurate identification of relationships between line
050   segments and points is fundamental (Fu et al., 2024a). Beyond abstract tasks, precise visual per-
051   ception is also vital in real-world applications, including spatial understanding for robotics, medical
052   image analysis for accurate diagnosis, quality control in manufacturing to detect subtle defects,
053   autonomous driving systems that rely on exact object localization or distance estimation, and aug-
      mented reality applications that demand precise overlay of virtual objects onto the real world.

054
      In this paper, we aim to study the challenges of low-level visual perception in MLLMs, take steps
055   to understand the root cause of their performance, and improve the models’ capabilities in this area.
056   We begin by developing a benchmark dataset specifically designed to evaluate precise geometric
057   perception, which we call Geoperception. As a focused test bed, this benchmark targets 2D geometry
058   tasks. Using this benchmark, we demonstrate the limitations of leading closed and open MLLMs,
059   followed by a comprehensive empirical study to explore strategies for significantly improving their
060   performance on geometric perception tasks. Our findings show the benefits of key factors such as
061   model architecture, training techniques, and data strategies, including the use of synthetic data and
062   multi-stage training with a data curriculum. Notably, we find that a data curriculum enables models
063
      to learn challenging low-level geometry understanding tasks, which they fail to learn from scratch,
      even when trained on a very large dataset. Using these lessons learned, we then train a family of
064
      models—using a carefully designed curriculum of synthetic data—that are specifically optimized
065
      for strong low-level geometric perception, which we call Euclid. We evaluate this family of models,
066   and show that it excels on a variety of low-level geometric perception tasks.
067
068   Our main technical contributions are as follows:
069   • Geoperception Benchmark: We introduce a new benchmark dataset, Geoperception, derived
070     from the Geometry-3K corpus (Lu et al., 2021), specifically designed to evaluate MLLMs’ ability
071     to accurately perceive surface-level geometric information without requiring complex inference
072     or reasoning. Our benchmark reveals significant shortcomings in precise geometric perception
073     across all leading visual-language models, both closed and open-source.
074
      • Empirical Study and Synthetic Data Engine: To investigate the root cause of this performance,
075
        we conduct a detailed empirical exploration of MLLM architecture and training strategies. To aid
076     in our investigation, we develop a synthetic data engine capable of generating high-fidelity visual
077     representations of fundamental geometric elements. This study leads to key insights, such as the
078     importance of certain architectural choices and the use of curriculum-based, multi-stage training
079     with progressively more complex visual descriptions for improving low-level visual perception.
080
081
      • Euclid Model: Leveraging the insights from our exploration and our synthetic data engine, we
        train Euclid, a series of multimodal LLMs tailored for high-quality geometric perception. Al-
082
        though purely trained on synthetic multimodal data and simple geometry shapes, Euclid achieves
083
        strong performance on the Geoperception benchmark, for instance, outperforming the best closed-
084     source model, Gemini-1.5-Pro, by up to 54.52% on certain benchmark tasks.

087   2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
088
089   We provide an overview of prior efforts that assess and improve low-level perception and geometric
090   reasoning in MLLMs, and highlight our contributions in data synthesis, evaluation, and training.
091
092   Vision-Language MLLMs. While recent iterations of LLMs feature a standardized model archi-
093   tecture and pretraining recipe, MLLMs still often differ in design choices for infusing visual inputs.
094   One popular design is to align continuous visual features with the embedding space of a backbone
095
      LLM (Liu et al., 2024a;b; Dubey et al., 2024; McKinzie et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024a; Beyer et al.,
      2024; AI, 2023; Wang et al., 2024a); another approach involves tokenizing visual inputs to be trained
096
      jointly with language tokens (Team et al., 2023; Team, 2024). These modules are often infused with
097
      a decoder-only LLM, but others have explored encoder-decoder architectures to integrate a more
098   varied collection of modalities (Alayrac et al., 2022; Mizrahi et al., 2024; Ormazabal et al., 2024;
099   Bachmann et al., 2024). Our study focuses on decoder MLLMs with a continuous visual encoder,
100   and we carry out an empirical study to explore the effect of synthetic dataset mixture, training recipe,
101   and encoder design (Liu et al., 2022; Radford et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2023; Oquab et al., 2023).
102
103   Geometry-Oriented MLLMs. At the core of these choices is the hardness in designing a module
104   adept in general visual reasoning (McKinzie et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024a). In this work, we
105   explore the optimal design of MLLMs specialized in low-level visual perception, a crucial aspect for
106   (among other applications) multimodal mathematical understanding (Lu et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
107   2024a). This paper supplements prior efforts in improving mathematical reasoning (Gao et al., 2023;
      Zhang et al., 2024b; Zhuang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024b) with a


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      detailed study on the effect of dataset mixture, curriculum, and visual encoder, to reach a recipe that
109   elicits strong performance on geometric tasks (Kazemi et al., 2023) that require low-level perception.
110
111
      Evaluating Low-Level Perception. Many benchmarks (Rahmanzadehgervi et al., 2024) have re-
112   ported that frontier-class MLLMs struggle with visual perception tasks, which are prerequisites for
113   applications that emphasize low-level geometric perception (Chen et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024b),
114   including mathematical (Yue et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Jiang et al., 2024)
115   and spatial reasoning (Chen et al., 2024). These findings collectively identify that MLLMs exhibit
116   a language prior (Lin et al., 2023)—a preference of textual inputs over visual inputs—leading to
117   a performance gap between modalities (Wang et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024a; Fu et al., 2024a).
118   Meanwhile, there lacks a high-quality benchmark that evaluates low-level geometric perception in
119   MLLMs, and the Geoperception benchmark represents a first effort to narrow this gap.
120
121   Improving Low-Level Visual Perception. Many prior works study data-driven approaches to
122   improve low-level perception skills. For example, Gao et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024); Zhuang et al.
123   (2024) employ a standardized supervised finetuning recipe, and optionally adjust the training data
124
      mixture. This type of training data is often synthesized from text-only math problems (Lu et al.,
      2021; Trinh et al., 2024) or via rule-based systems (Kazemi et al., 2023). In parallel, Vishniakov
125
      et al. (2023); Shi et al. (2024a); Tong et al. (2024b) have explored the design space of visual encoders
126
      for general-purpose vision-language reasoning. We identify best practices over the union of these
127   design spaces, and then train small MLLMs with strong performance in low-level perception tasks.
128
129   Lastly, several works (Schick et al., 2024; Surı́s et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024) have opted to augment
130
      an MLLM with external APIs that process low-level features with specialized vision modules, such
      as object detection (Redmon et al., 2016), segmentation (Kirillov et al., 2023), and depth estima-
131
      tion (Yang et al., 2024b). While these agentic frameworks (Wu et al., 2023) present a promising
132
      alternative that directly addresses the shortcomings of visual encoders, they are limited by their
133   scalability to novel use cases, and may be insufficient for precise tool routing that requires low-level
134   perception as a primer (Picard et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Buehler, 2024).

137   3   G EOPERCEPTION B ENCHMARK
138
139   Recently, there has been a growing number of multimodal benchmarks across diverse domains be-
140   yond natural image understanding, including mathematical reasoning (Zhang et al., 2024a; Lu et al.,
141
      2023) and abstract visual reasoning (Jiang et al., 2024; Chia et al., 2024). Many of these prior works
      have realized the importance of accurate low-level visual perception. Specifically, Marvel (Jiang
142
      et al., 2024) introduces perception questions for various abstract reasoning patterns, and finds that
143
      the main bottleneck of MLLMs’ performance on abstract visual reasoning is that they fail to accu-
144   rately transcribe visual information into concepts; Mathverse (Zhang et al., 2024a) and IsoBench (Fu
145   et al., 2024a) both test MLLMs on equivalent question represented by language and visual modal-
146   ities, respectively. Both works find that language-only input always outperforms vision-language
147   input, and that the vision component of MLLMs always fails to utilize low-level visual features.
148   VDLM (Wang et al., 2024b) transcribes raster images into vector graphics and uses LLMs to reason
149   over the SVG code. They find that although SVG code is not straightforward to understand, using
150   LLMs to reason over SVG is consistently more effective than directly using MLLMs on original
151   raster images. Blind-test (Rahmanzadehgervi et al., 2024) and BLINK (Fu et al., 2024b) also share
152
      similar findings with the works above.
153   A Benchmark for Geometric Perception. Although such shortcomings of MLLMs are commonly
154   recognized, there is a lack of comprehensive benchmark that purely focuses on these abilities of
155   MLLMs. Our goal is to construct a benchmark focusing solely on the perception ability of MLLMs,
156   which is also representative enough of real-world applications. When humans perceive and memo-
157   rize visual information, it is well-recognized that this procedure relies crucially on searching for the
158
      closest and simplest corresponding geometric shapes (Sablé-Meyer et al., 2022). We posit that geo-
      metric perception is a fundamental and broadly representative low-level visual perception ability in
159
      many applications. Hence, we select geometry understanding as our domain of dataset construction.
160

258   4      E MPIRICAL S TUDY ON MLLM D ESIGN S PACE

260   We hypothesize that the lack of high-fidelity geometric visual perception data is one of the major
261
      reasons for the inability of today’s MLLMs to effectively perceive basic geometric annotations and
      relationships. Although large-scale web-crawled image-text pairs cover a variety of domains, includ-
262
      ing geometry, the textual descriptions often lack the necessary specificity and depth. To address this
263
      issue, current studies in this domain (Gao et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024b) typi-
264   cally construct a geometry or mathematical domain dataset and apply the same training strategy used
265   for general-purpose MLLMs. For example, Math-LLaVA (Shi et al., 2024b) and multi-math (Peng
266   et al., 2024) rely on GPT-4v or GPT-4o’s vision ability to generate most of the question and answer
267   pairs and image captions, which is essentially a form of model distillation. However, as evidenced
268   by Table 2, GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5-Pro often struggle to answer certain types of questions, limiting
269   the performance potential of resulting models. Furthermore, while works such as G-LLaVA (Gao
      et al., 2023), MAVIS (Zhang et al., 2024b), and Math-PUMA (Zhuang et al., 2024) utilize human

      crafted logical forms or synthetic multimodal data to ensure the reliability of textual annotations,
271   they often conflate low-level perception with problem-solving, and train models to directly solve

313   Exploration Overview. We study the choice of visual encoder architecture, the choice between tun-
314   ing or freezing the encoder, and different data composition/training strategies. For visual encoders,

431        On-the-fly progressive training. Drawing inspiration from the effectiveness of curriculum learning,
           instead of constructing a static training data, we introduce an adaptive dataset generation method-


                                                                                                                  8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      ology with our dataset engine, where we monitor the model’s performance and dynamically adjust
433   the distribution of training data (i.e., the curriculum stage) based on this performance. Specifically,
434   for a certain task, we create N stages of training dataset shapes with progressively increasing geo-
435   metric complexity. During training, the model starts by training on the first (simplest) dataset stage.
436   The model is evaluated when it finishes a training round, using a held-out validation set from the
437   same distribution as the current dataset stage. Upon evaluation, if the model achieves an accuracy
438   exceeding a predefined threshold θ, the framework advances the task to the next stage. Formally, the
439   update rule for advancing stages is given by:
440                                   if accuracys > θ       ⇒     c ← c + 1.                                          (2)
441
442
      The model is trained on a total of M rounds and K steps within each round. Similar to Section 4,
      we smooth our dataset distribution over all stages using an exponential attenuation function:
443
444                                     ratios = exp (−α · |stages − c|) ,                                             (3)
445
      where α denotes the attenuation rate. Eq. (3) ensures that stages proximal to the current stage receive
446   higher sampling probabilities.

      6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
499
      In this work, we highlight the importance of accurate low-level visual perception in MLLMs. To
500
      this end, we first introduce Geoperception, a large-scale multimodal benchmark focused exclusively
501
      on geometry-domain visual perception. We evaluate leading MLLMs on Geoperception, find that
502   even top models such as Gemini-1.5-Pro struggle significantly it, although it is straightforward for
503   humans. We then conduct an empirical study to explore the design space of MLLM training and
504   architectures using the dataset generated by a geometric high-fidelity synthetic-data engine that we
505   develop. Our study indicate that convolutional neural network visual encoders outperform vision
506   transformers in our tasks; tuning the visual encoder generally enhances performance; and employ-
507   ing a curriculum-based training approach yields much more model potential than direct task training.
508   Based on insights from this study, we develop Euclid, a model trained purely on high-fidelity syn-
509   thetic generated data, which generalizes effectively to real-world geometric shape understanding
510
      tasks, surpassing the leading MLLMs by a substantial margin.
511   Future work. Our work examines the potential of using synthetic multimodal data to improve
512   MLLM performance in low-level geometric perception tasks. However, there are still directions that
513   remain under-explored: (1) Using a more-diverse training dataset. Currently, the text portion of our
514   synthetic multimodal training data uses a restricted set of templates, and the model trained on such
515   templates could fail to generalize to other question types; it could therefore be beneficial to increase
516
      the diversity of our instruction-following formats. (2) Automatic curriculum learning. Incorporating
      a more diverse dataset, including varied geometric shapes and different domain dataset, introduces
517
      challenges in defining the learning order. Rule based definition and manual curation may become
518
      impractical, necessitating automated strategies like hard negative sampling to organize the curricu-
519   lum based on training loss or testing accuracy. This approach could streamline the process, reduce
520   human effort, provide more suitable and efficient curriculum learning orders. (3) Generalizing to
521   other task domains. In this work, our study is focused on data from 2D geometry, as it provides
522   a focused test bed of fundamental tasks. We believe the lessons we learn from this domain can
523   be effectively generalized to a broader set of downstream domains that benefit from high-quality
524   low-level visual perception.
