009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce LLaVA-Critic, the first open-source large multimodal model (LMM)
012            designed as a generalist evaluator to assess performance across a wide range of
013            multimodal tasks. LLaVA-Critic is trained using a high-quality critic instruction-
014            following dataset that incorporates diverse evaluation criteria and scenarios. Our
015            experiments demonstrate the model’s effectiveness in two key areas: (i) LMM-
016            as-a-Judge, where LLaVA-Critic provides reliable evaluation scores, performing
017            on par with or surpassing GPT models on multiple evaluation benchmarks; and
018            (ii) Preference Learning, where it generates reward signals for preference learning,
019
               enhancing model alignment capabilities. This work underscores the potential of
               open-source LMMs in self-critique and evaluation, setting the stage for future
020
               research into scalable, superhuman alignment feedback mechanisms for LMMs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025
      The ability of learning to evaluate is increasingly taking on a pivotal role in the development of
026
      modern large multimodal models (LMMs), as pre-training on existing web data reaches maturity and
027
      the focus is shifting towards post-training with AI-enhanced synthetic data, which shows growing
028   potential. Reliable AI evaluation is essential, not only for offering scalable solutions to reduce human
029   labor in complex task assessments, but also for generating effective reward signals in reinforcement
030   learning and guiding inference-time search (Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2024a; Snell et al., 2024).
031
      Existing LMMs have made tremendous progress in recent research community (Li et al., 2023a),
032
      primarily on improving the performance of various real-world vision tasks in single-image (Liu
033
      et al., 2023b; Bai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b), multi-image (Li et al., 2024c; Jiang et al., 2024)
034   and video scenarios (Li et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b). It remains unexplored
035   to develop open LMMs to play the role of a judge and evaluate the performance of multimodal
036   models. For instance, a model can follow a well-designed, itemized evaluation criterion to provide a
037   score between 1 and 10 for rating different model responses in a visual chat task (Liu et al., 2023b).
038   Along with the score, it would also offer the associated reasoning behind the evaluation, ensuring
039   transparency and consistency in assessing model performance. In this paper, we present the first
040   attempt to curate the instruction-following data particularly for evaluation, based on which we develop
041   a LMM, LLaVA-Critic. Two primary scenarios/goals of building LLaVA-Critic are highlighted:
042       • Scenario 1: LMM-as-a-Judge. Open-source LMMs that can deliver reliable evaluation scores,
043         comparable to or surpassing proprietary models like GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023)/GPT-4o (OpenAI,
044         2024b). These models can serve as a free alternative to replace commercial GPT models in
045         various evaluation benchmarks.
046       • Scenario 2: Preference Learning. A scalable solution for generating effective reward signals,
047         reducing the need for costly human feedback collection. This approach enhances preference
048         alignment with AI-generated feedback.
049   Our experimental results demonstrate that: (i) As a judge model, the evaluation scores and rankings
050   provided by LLaVA-Critic show a high correlation with commercial GPT models, making it a
051   cost-effective alternative for model developers in resource-constrained settings; (ii) In preference
052   learning, LLaVA-Critic offers AI-generated feedback in iterative Direct Preference Optimization
053   (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), outperforming the preference signals provided by the reward model in
      LLaVA-RLHF (Sun et al., 2023), which relies on human feedback for training the reward model.

066   2    R ELATED W ORK
067
068   LMM-as-a-judge. Strong proprietary LMMs such as GPT-4V / GPT-4o have been demonstrated
069   to serve as generalist evaluators for vision-language tasks (Zhang et al., 2023a; Ge et al., 2023).
070   Specifically, for complex scenarios related to human judgment, such as visual chat and detailed
071   captioning, LMMs have been utilized in evaluation benchmarks to judge the model responses,
072   including both pointwise settings (Liu et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023a; Sun et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
073
      2024a; Li et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024b) and pairwise settings (Lu et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024b).
      Our LLaVA-Critic are evaluated in the these evaluation scenarios as open-source alternative, with
074
      advantages in cheap and customized evaluation. For open-source models, Prometheus-Vision (Lee
075
      et al., 2024) is the first VLM trained as an evaluator for specific user-designed scoring criteria.
076   While sharing the same open-source spirit, our proposed LLaVA-Critic is favored as the first open
077   generalist evaluator. Note that GPT is also utilized to extract answers from LMM responses for
078   subsequent evaluation in some benchmarks (Lu et al., 2023; Guan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024d).
079   This extractive functionality for evaluation is out of the scope of this paper.
080
081   Preference learning for LMMs. Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) is a proven
082   method to align large language models (LLMs) with human intentions. DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024)
083   introduces a new parameterization of the reward model in RLHF, enabling direct optimization using
084   pairwise preference datasets. CriticGPT (McAleese et al., 2024) trains “critic” models that help
085   evaluate model-generated code, which is further utilized as feedback signals to improve code LLM.
086   The concept of preference learning has recently expanded from language models to the multimodal
087
      space. LLaVA-RLHF (Sun et al., 2023), the first open-source work in this area, improves visual
      chat abilities for LMMs using human-scored rankings. Research on preference learning for LMMs
088
      has since then advanced in several studies. BPO (Pi et al., 2024) conducts preference learning by
089
      introducing negative responses generated by the model itself, using distorted images or text-based
090   LLMs to inject errors. Wang et al. (2024a) proposes mDPO, which introduces conditional preference
091   optimization to emphasize image information. Other works apply preference alignment to reduce
092   hallucinations and enhance the overall capabilities of vision-language models (VLMs), either through
093   human feedback (e.g., RLHF-V (Yu et al., 2024a)) or AI feedback (e.g., Silkie: VLFeedback (Li et al.,
094   2023c)). Several approaches use self-rewarding mechanisms to minimize dependence on external
095   preference pairs, such as divide-and-conquer strategies (Yu et al., 2024b) (RLAIF-V), sentence-level
096   beam search (Zhou et al., 2024b), deliberate hallucination injection (Zhou et al., 2024a), or metric-
097   based self-critic prompts (Wang et al., 2024c). As a generalist evaluator, LLaVA-Critic can provide
098
      valuable feedback for LMM alignment, paving the way for self-improving AI models.

149   3.1    P OINTWISE DATA
150
151   To train a generalist critic model for the evaluation of individual LMM responses, each pointwise
152   training data instance consists of the following components: a multimodal instruction (i.e., a question-
153   image pair), a model response, an optional reference answer (depending on the evaluation prompt), an
154
      evaluation prompt, a judgment score, and the corresponding justification for the score. By organizing
      them in a sequence, the training sample is:
155
156   (Image, Question, Response, Reference, Evaluation Criteria, Score, Reason),
157
158   where green parts are treated as model output to compute the auto-regressive loss, the order of
159   Score and Reason is specified by the evaluation prompt. We select multimodal instructions from
160   8 multimodal instruction tuning datasets, spanning across a wide range of tasks including: (1) general
161   visual conversation, detailed captioning and reasoning (LLaVA-Instruction-150k (Liu et al., 2023b),
      SVIT (Zhao et al., 2023)); (2) more challenging tasks such as complex reasoning (ComVint (Du et al.,


                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2023)), text-rich understanding (LLaVAR (Zhang et al., 2023b)) and robustness-oriented instructions
163   (LRV-Instruction (Liu et al., 2023a)); and (3) various specific domains such as academic question
164   answering (M3IT (Li et al., 2023d)), medical image understanding (LLaVA-Med (Li et al., 2023b))
165   and embodied decision-making (PCA-EVAL (Chen et al., 2023a)). For each multimodal instruction,
166   we select one or more model responses from VLFeedback (Li et al., 2023c), which collects multiple
167   responses from 12 off-the-shelf LMMs. Additionally, we generate responses using GPT-4o, a leading
168   commercial LMM, to serve as high-quality reference answers.
169   To equip LLaVA-Critic with general evaluation capacities across various tasks, we construct an
170   evaluation prompt pool from 7 widely used multimodal benchmarks that utilize GPT-as-a-judge,
171   including LLaVA-in-the-Wild (Liu et al., 2023b), LLaVA-Wilder (Li et al., 2024a), Image Detailed
172   Captioning (Li et al., 2024a), MMHal-Bench (Sun et al., 2023), MMVet (Yu et al., 2023b), WildVision-
173   Bench (Lu et al., 2024) and RefoMB (Yu et al., 2024b). 1 Prompts that require additional textual
174   context—since they use text-only GPT-4 as the evaluator—are adjusted to focus on the input image,
175   better aligning with the LMM evaluator setting. To construct training data based on each evaluation
176   prompt, we select multimodal instructions and model responses according to the specified evaluation
177
      scenario, and include reference answers from GPT-4o when necessary. These components are
      then assembled into the evaluation prompt and used as input for GPT-4o (as-a-judge) to provide
178
      high-quality judgment scores and detailed justifications for model responses. Finally, our pointwise
179
      training dataset comprises a total of 18,915 question-image pairs and 72,782 critic data samples.
180
181
      3.2   PAIRWISE DATA
182
183   The pairwise data consists of responses with known preference relationships. In our training dataset,
184   we collect the pairwise data from three open-source datasets: VLFeedback (Li et al., 2023c),
185   RLHF (Sun et al., 2023), and RLHF-V (Yu et al., 2024a). In the VLFeedback dataset, each (question,
186   response) pair is rated across three different dimensions by GPT-4V. For the same question, responses
187   generated by different LMMs can form multiple response pairs for that question. We randomly select
188   20k pairs where the average score gap between responses is greater than 0.6. Besides, to ensure
189
      diversity in the preferences, we randomly sample 5k pairs where the two responses had identical
      scores across all three dimensions to serve as “Tie” training data. In the RLHF dataset, each question
190
      is annotated with preference relationships between different responses by human evaluators. In
191
      contrast, the RLHF-V dataset consists of responses generated by LMM, which have been manually
192   refined to produce improved responses. From these two datasets, we collect 9.4k (RLHF) and 5.7k
193   (RLHF-V) response pairs, each annotated with human preferences. This results in a total of 40.1k
194   pairwise data samples.
195
      To enable LLaVA-Critic to provide useful detailed feedback in addition to the preference relation, we
196
      utilize GPT-4o to generate reasons behind the given preference judgment. The training sample for
197
      pairwise data is structured in the following sequence:
198
199   (Image, Question, Response 1&2, Evaluation Criteria, Preference, Reason),
200   where the evaluation criteria is from carefully designed prompt templates. To allow LLaVA-Critic to
201   handle diverse pairwise data ranking, we develop a set of 30 prompt templates (see Appendix A.1).
202   Each preference pair is randomly assigned a template from this set, forming the final training data.
203
204   Data statistics. Our training dataset comprises a total of 46k images and 113k data samples. As
205   illustrated in Figure 1, we curate our training set with diverse instruction-response pairs, spanning
206
      multiple evaluation tasks and domains.

245   4.2   S CENARIO 1: LMM- AS - A -J UDGE
246
247   Evaluating complex tasks often requires human judges to provide feedback, which can be labor-
248   intensive. LLaVA-Critic can serve as a general evaluator for LMM responses, reducing labor costs
249   by automating the evaluation process. LLaVA-Critic consistently provides reliable judgments and
250   justifications aligned with GPT-4o or human evaluations across a range of widely used multimodal
251   benchmarks. This consistency holds true for both instance-level scoring and model-level ranking, as
252   demonstrated in Sec. 5.1.
253   Specifically, we consider the following evaluation scenarios: (i) Visual Chat. This task involves
254   handling daily-life visual tasks through multimodal dialogue, requiring evaluation of task completion
255   quality in a conversation setting. Examples include LLaVA-Bench (Liu et al., 2023b) and LLaVA-
256   in-the-Wild (Liu et al., 2023b), which focus on simpler scenarios, while LLaVA-Wilder (Li et al.,
257   2024a) addresses more challenging cases. (ii) Integrated capabilities. Real-world tasks require
258   integration of multiple basic abilities of LMMs. MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2023b) offers a comprehensive
259   benchmark, evaluating core vision-language capabilities including recognition, OCR, knowledge
260   integration, language generation, spatial awareness, and math. The Multimodal Live-Bench tests the
261
      model’s ability to generalize to new, unobserved knowledge by leveraging continuously updated news
      and online forums. (iii) Preferences. This task simulates real-world multimodal interactions where
262
      models are expected to align their behavior with human preferences. The WildVision-Bench (Lu
263
      et al., 2024) is a prime example, replicating scenarios from the online platform WildVision-Arena
264   (WV-Arena) to evaluate preference-based interactions. (iv) Detailed Description. This task assesses
265   models on their ability to provide comprehensive and detailed descriptions of images and videos.
266   Image Detailed Captioning (Li et al., 2024a) evaluates detailed descriptions in images, while video
267   Detailed Captioning (Zhang et al., 2024c) extends these capabilities from images to video contexts.
268   (v) Hallucination: This task focuses on the model’s ability to provide grounded responses based on
269   the given context, ensuring that it avoids generating inaccurate or fabricated information, exemplified
      by MMHal-Bench (Sun et al., 2023).

      4.3   S CENARIO 2: P REFERENCE L EARNING
271
272   Leveraging a generalist evaluator as a critic to generate reward signals for reinforcement learning
273   is a promising research direction. In this work, we employ LLaVA-Critic to produce AI-generated
274   feedback datasets for diverse tasks, thereby improving the performance of supervised fine-tuned
275   LMMs through preference alignment. Notably, the reward signals generated by our critic can be
276   utilized in any preference learning algorithms, including RLHF and DPO. To quickly assess the
277   effectiveness of the reward data, we focus on how LLaVA-Critic is incorporated into the iterative
278
      DPO training process.
279   • Step 1: Response generation. The iterative DPO process begins with a pretrained LMM π0 as the
280     initial checkpoint and a set of multimodal instructions {(xk , v k )}N
                                                                             k=1 , where each xk is a question
281     and v k is the corresponding image. For each question-image pair (x, v), the pretrained LMM π0
282     randomly generates K responses {y1 , y2 , . . . , yK }, sampled independently from its distribution.
283   • Step 2: Scoring. To mitigate order-related variance in LLaVA-Critic’s preferences, we form all
284     possible ordered pairs from these responses, resulting in K × (K − 1) pairs. For each response pair
285     (yi , yj ), we apply LLaVA-Critic with an evaluation prompt to generate a relative score aij , which
286
        normalizes the score of yj based on yi .
287   • Step 3: Reward Preference. The overall reward      P score rP  i for each response yi is calculated

288
        by aggregating these preference scores: ri = k̸=i aki − l̸=i ail This calculation effectively
289
        measures how much better or worse yi is compared to all other responses. We then select the
        responses with the highest and lowest reward scores as the best and worst responses, denoted as
290
        y + and y − , respectively. These form the pairwise feedback data (y + , y − ), which is used for DPO
291
        training to enhance the LMM’s alignment with LLaVA-Critic’s preferences.
292
293   Iterative Improvement. After each round of DPO training, the updated LMM becomes the new
294   starting checkpoint. The process is then repeated iteratively for another M − 1 rounds, using LLaVA-
295   Critic to progressively improve the model’s performance based on its self-generated responses.

      In-domain Pairwise Ranking To assess the consistency between LLaVA-Critic and human eval-
      uators in pairwise ranking, we use the battle data from WildVision Arena (Lu et al., 2024), which
392
      comprises 11k human-annotated preference relations among LMM response pairs. Each relation
393
      includes a question-image pair and two responses generated by different models, accompanied by a
394   human-annotated preference (including ties). From this dataset, we randomly sample 2k response
395   pairs and assign them to evaluation prompts from the pairwise ranking prompt template set men-
396   tioned in Section 3.2, creating the in-domain evaluation dataset. We report average accuracy, with
397   and without ties, to assess alignment with human evaluators at the instance level. For model-level
398   consistency, we calculate the Elo rating for each response LMM and report Kendall’s Tau to measure
399   the overall ranking correlation with human preferences.
400   Experimental results are reported in Table 3. While the LLaVA models exhibit initial pairwise
401   ranking ability, there is a notable performance gap compared to GPT-4V/4o. After training with critic
402   data, LLaVA-Critic achieves significant improvements. Specifically, LLaVA-Critic-72B achieves an
403   average accuracy of 73.6% in pairwise comparisons without tie, outperforming both GPT-4o and
404   GPT-4V. For pairwise comparison with tie (Accuracy w. Tie) and model-wise ranking (Kendall’s
405   Tau), LLaVA-Critic-72B shows only a marginal gap compared to GPT-4V/4o, with an accuracy of
406   60.5% and a score of 0.779, respectively. Notably, despite a substantial reduction in the number of
407   parameters, LLaVA-Critic-7B still achieves an average accuracy of 59.6% in pairwise ranking with
408
      ties and 72.2% without ties, alongside a Kendall’s tau of 0.763. These results underscore the strong
      alignment between LLaVA-Critic and human evaluators in pairwise ranking LMM responses.
409
410   MLLM-as-a-Judge MLLM-as-a-Judge (Chen et al., 2024) is a comprehensive benchmark to
411   evaluate the degree of alignment between model-based evaluation and human evaluation. It collects
412   approximately 17k image-instruction-response triplets across 14 multimodal benchmarks and 6
413   LMM response models. Human annotators are then employed to assess model responses under
414   scoring, pairwise comparison and batch ranking settings, resulting in 7756, 5719, 1469 examples
415   respectively. In our experiments, we evaluate LLaVA-Critic in both (pointwise) scoring and pair
416   comparison settings to assess its general alignment with human evaluators. We report the average
417   Pearson correlation for scoring and average accuracy for pairwise comparison, following the metrics
418
      used in the original benchmark.
419   We compare LLaVA-Critic with commercial models (GPT-4V/4o, Gemini-Pro (Team et al., 2023)),
420   open-sourced LMMs, as well as Prometheus-Vision (Lee et al., 2024), which trains a LLaVA model
421   on a curated LMM-as-a-judge dataset comprising 15k GPT-generated rubrics and 150k GPT-4V
422   feedback data. As shown in Table 4, LLaVA-Critic-7B surpasses all baselines except GPT-4V/4o
423   across all settings by a considerable margin. Built on a stronger base model, LLaVA-Critic-72B
424
      further achieves the Pearson similarity with human annotators from 0.314 to 0.393 in pointwise
      scoring. For pairwise comparisons, it achieves accuracy rates of 57.8% and 71.5% with and without
425
      ties, respectively, reaching a level of alignment with human evaluators comparable to GPT-4V/4o. We
426
      also compare different variants of LLaVA-Critic and observe performance gains with both stronger
427   base models and larger training data, consistent with previous findings. This again highlights the
428   critical role of model and data scaling in building an effective and generalist open-source LMM
429   evaluator. More comprehensive results are provided in Appendix B.1.

459
460   We further evaluate LLaVA-Critic’s performance in providing reward signals for iterative DPO.
461   LLaVA-OneVision’s supervised fine-tuned checkpoint is used as the base policy model, and question-
462
      image pairs from LLaVA-RLHF (Sun et al., 2023) serve as the multimodal instructions. For each
      pair, K = 5 candidate responses are generated through random decoding (with a temperature of 0.7
463
      and top-p of 0.9) to ensure response diversity. LLaVA-Critic is employed as described in Sec. 4.3 to
464
      construct the pairwise feedback data, which is then used for one epoch of DPO training. We perform
465   iterative DPO for M = 3 rounds in total.
466
467   To assess the effectiveness of the LLaVA-Critic’s reward signals, we evaluate the final LMM check-
468
      point across 6 open-ended multimodal benchmarks: four image-based tasks (LLaVA-in-the-Wild (Liu
      et al., 2023b), LLaVA-Wilder (Li et al., 2024a), LiveBench (Zhang et al., 2024a), and WildVision-
469
      Bench (Lu et al., 2024)), one video-based task (Video Detailed Captioning (Li et al., 2024a)), and
470
      one hallucination benchmark (MMHal-Bench (Sun et al., 2023)). We compare LLaVA-Critic with
471   two baselines: (1) the reward model from LLaVA-RLHF (Sun et al., 2023), which is trained on
472   human preferences, and (2) a naive baseline that replaces LLaVA-Critic with LLaVA-OneVision’s
473   SFT checkpoint as a zero-shot reward model.
474
      As shown in Table 5, preferences provided by LLaVA-Critic significantly improve LLaVA-
475
      OneVision’s visual chat capacities and reduce hallucination across challenging tasks. LLaVA-Critic
476
      consistently surpasses other baseline reward models on 5 out of 6 benchmarks for the 7B base model
477   and all 6 benchmarks for the 72B base model. Despite the preference alignment conducted solely with
478   images, LLaVA-Critic also enhances LLaVA-OneVision’s performance in Video Detailed Captioning
479   (+0.12 on OV-7B and +0.26 on OV-7B), demonstrating its ability to generalize to both image and
480   video contexts. Additionally, we observe that Critic-7B outperforms Critic-7B-v0.5 on 5 out of 6
481   benchmarks, highlighting the importance of stronger reward models—trained on more diverse critic
482   instructions—to deliver more accurate reward signals and further enhance preference learning. Please
483   refer to Appendix B.2 for additional results and Table 12 for a visual-chat example.

      6    C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
      We have presented LLaVA-Critic, an open-source LMM that is trained to evaluate model performance
      in a wide range of multimodal scenarios. To achieve this, we curated a high-quality critic instruction-
532
      following dataset with diverse evaluation criteria. We demonstrated the effectiveness of LLaVA-Critic
533
      in two key areas: (1) as a generalized evaluator, LLaVA-Critic provides pointwise scores and pairwise
534   rankings that closely align with human and GPT-4o preferences across multiple evaluation tasks,
535   presenting a viable open-source alternative to commercial GPT models for autonomous assessment of
536   open-ended LMM responses; (2) in preference learning, LLaVA-Critic functions as a reliable reward
537   model, supplying preference signals that enhance the visual chat capabilities of LMMs, surpassing
538   the LLaVA-RLHF reward model built with human feedback. This work represents an important step
539   toward harnessing the self-critique capabilities of open-source LMMs, and we hope it will encourage
      further research into developing strong LMMs with scalable and superhuman alignment feedback.
