011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Advancements in diffusion-based text-to-image generation models have made it
014            possible to create high-quality human images. However, generating humans in de-
015            sired poses using text prompts alone remains challenging. Image-to-image genera-
016
               tion methods utilizing additional image conditions can address this issue; however,
               they often struggle with generating images that accurately match conditioning im-
017
               ages. This paper proposes a new fine-tuning framework for training ControlNet
018
               models with reinforcement learning by combining ControlNet and Denoising Dif-
019            fusion Policy Optimization (DDPO) to understand pose conditioning images bet-
020            ter. We apply a novel reward function in the proposed framework to increase pose
021            accuracy. We demonstrate that our method effectively improves human genera-
022            tion by enhancing pose accuracy and the correct generation of body parts without
023            omissions or additions. Furthermore, we prove that the usage of more detailed
024            pose dataset along with our proposed reward functions leads to improved training
025            results.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      Advancements in text-to-image generation models, such as Stable Diffusion, (Rombach et al., 2022;
030
      Croitoru et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a) have enabled the generation of high-
031
      quality human images. However, generating humans in desired poses using text input alone is chal-
032   lenging. An image-to-image generation method is employed, utilizing additional image conditions,
033   such as depth map images(Eigen et al., 2014) or OpenPose images(Cao et al., 2017). This allows
034   new images to be generated based on the content of given conditions, enabling humans to create
035   desired poses.
036
      Additional model training is necessary to accept new image conditions as input. While it is pos-
037
      sible to directly fine-tune the large generative model, this approach requires expensive computing
038   resources and large datasets. More efficient methods such as ControlNet(Zhang et al., 2023b) and
039   T2I-Adapters(Mou et al., 2024) have been proposed to address these limitations. These methods
040   enable learning new conditioning images with limited data and computing resources.
041
      Utilizing these approaches allows for controlling the structural aspects during image generation,
042
      making it much easier to generate humans in desired poses. However, even when using conditioning
043
      images, the models often struggle to create complex poses and frequently fail to generate images
044   that perfectly match the conditioning image pose.
045
046   In this paper, we propose a new fine-tuning framework that combines ControlNet(Zhang et al.,
047
      2023b) and Denoising Diffusion Policy Optimization (DDPO)(Black et al., 2023) that can train
      models to understand conditioning images better; Refer to Figure 1. DDPO is a diffusion-based
048
      reinforcement learning method that trains the diffusion model to perform better on specific tasks like
049
      text-to-image alignment and aesthetic quality. In the proposed framework, ControlNet approach of
050   freezing the existing model and training only the copied neural network to learn new conditions is
051   combined with DDPO approach of applying reinforcement learning to the denoising procedure of
052   the diffusion model.

086   2.1     D ENOISING D IFFUSION M ODELS
087
      Denoising diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) have become a powerful class of generative models.
088
      These approaches have been extended to various domains, including audio (Schneider, 2023), text
089   generation (Li et al., 2022), and text-to-image generation (Rombach et al., 2022). More recently,
090   diffusion models have emerged that allow customization of the output image by entering a detailed
091   description as a conditioning input rather than simply generating the image. Conditioning inputs can
092   take many different forms and be used to control various aspects. For example, DALLE2 (Ramesh
093   et al., 2022) uses text as a condition for image generation, GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023) controls posi-
094   tion, and PIDM (Bhunia et al., 2023) is used to control pose. In this paper, our conditioning input is
095   the pose image extracted from the original image.
096
097   2.2     P OSE - GUIDED H UMAN I MAGE G ENERATION .
098
099

100

101

102   architectures to better decouple pose and appearance. Def-GAN by Siarohin et al. (2018) further im-
103   proved alignment. Subsequently, GFLA by Ren et al. (2020), employed flow-based deformations to
104   improve pose alignment performance, while models like ClothFlow (Han et al., 2019) used segmen-
105   tation maps for guidance. Attention-based approaches like CoCosNet (Zhou et al., 2021) extracted
106   dense correspondences, enhancing texture and spatial consistency. Recently, diffusion models like
107   PoCoLD (Han et al., 2023) and PIDM (Bhunia et al., 2023) have emerged, using cross-attention
      mechanisms to model spatial correspondences, overcoming the limitations of GANs (Goodfellow

147   2.2.1     A DDING C ONDITIONAL C ONTROL TO T EXT- TO -I MAGE D IFFUSION M ODELS
148             (C ONTROL N ET )
149
150   ControlNet(Zhang et al., 2023b) is a neural network architecture that allows diffusion models to
151   learn new additional conditions for precise structural adjustments during image generation. Control-
152   Net has a structure where a neural network from large models, like Stable diffusion(Rombach et al.,
153   2022), is duplicated and connected to the original block. The existing network remains untrained,
154   while only the newly connected network undergoes, as depicted in Figure 2 (a). Consequently, this
155   allows for maintaining the performance of the existing large model while learning new conditions. It
156   also has the advantage of being trainable with fewer computing resources on a smaller dataset than
157   directly fine-tuning Stable Diffusion.
158   ControlNet(Zhang et al., 2023b) is currently used for various tasks, one of which is training pose
159   images. It is useful for generating human images in desired poses because generating a person in
160   a desired pose using only text input is challenging. However, it still has limitations in generating
161   detailed parts of human body or fails to generate complex poses. This paper aims to apply Rein-
      forcement learning-based approaches to Controlnet to better reflect pose conditioning images.

      2.3     R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING AND D IFFUSION M ODELS .
182    Bachman & Precup (2015) assumed data generation as a sequential decision-making problem pre-
183   ceding the diffusion model and introduced reinforcement learning methods to image generation
184   using their framework. Furthermore, Fan & Lee (2023) applied policy gradient methods to improve
185   data distribution matching. As the objective is not to optimize downstream tasks, they considered
186   the reward function as a discriminator of GAN architecture(Goodfellow et al., 2020).
187
      In concurrent works, Diffusion Policy Optimization with KL regularization (DPOK)(Fan et al.,
188   2024) and DDPO(Black et al., 2023) employed RL in the diffusion process. DPOK attempted to
189   improve human preferences using policy gradient methods with KL regularization and trained dif-
190   ferent models on each prompt. DDPO, in contrast, excluded KL regularization, especially for text
191   alignment enhancement, which is one of the objectives they addressed, harnessed the describing
192   ability of Vision-Language Models (VLM), and trained the models on many prompts at once.
193
      We built upon DDPO (Black et al., 2023) targeting better reflection of conditioning image, follow-
194   ing the same training condition as DDPO, using VLM instead of adopting KL-regularization and
195   training on many prompts simultaneously. However, we introduced a new reward function to en-
196   hance the pose accuracy simultaneously while still adopting VLM-reward function to maintain the
197   text-to-image diffusion models’ text alignment ability.
198

243
244   This paper proposes a fine-tuning framework for image-conditioned text-to-image models to en-
245
      hance the model’s ability to more accurately generate human images conditioned on various poses.
      In the subsequent sections, we will explain the details of the model’s architecture and reward func-

249   3.1     T HE P ROPOSED A RCHITECTURE
250
251      Our proposed framework fine-tunes ControlNet(Zhang et al., 2023b) with the DDPO(Black et al.,
252
      2023) strategy. The original DDPO applies reinforcement learning to Stable Diffusion model. How-
      ever, we replace this with ControlNet model to enable learning new conditioning images. Since
253
      ControlNet uses latent space loss for training, we modified this to a reward system and applied
254
      DDPO’s multi-step MDP. Additionally, unlike DDPO, we focus on pose-based human image gener-
255   ation, so we use a pose accuracy reward in addition to DDPO’s text alignment reward. As mentioned
256   before, the overall architecture of our model is depicted in Figure 1.
257
258
      In the fine-tuning procedure, the first step involves feeding the OpenPose conditioning image(Cao
      et al., 2017) and the text prompts into the model as inputs, generating an image as output. Next, each
259
      component of the reward function produces individual rewards. Finally, the rewards are aggregated,
260
      and the total reward is returned to the model during denoising.
261

286   We adopted DDPO’s VLM-reward function for text alignment as shown in Figure 4, utilizing
287   LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023) as the VLM and BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019) as the reward score.
288   VLM receives the generated image and a text prompt asking, “What is shown in this image?”.

290   tion in natural language that accurately reflects its content and context. The BERTScore between
291   the description and inputted text prompt to ControlNet model is calculated and used for the text
292
      alignment score.
293   We propose two variants of the reward function for pose accuracy: Feature reward function and
294   Keypoints reward function. The pose estimation model(Openpose) (Cao et al., 2017) is required for
295   both variants to extract OpenPose images or coordinate of the keypoints from the generated image.
296   Feature reward function yields the rewards based on the cosine similarity of features extracted from
297   two images: an input conditioning image and an OpenPose image extracted from a generated image;
298   thus, it requires a feature extraction model, in which we used DinoV2-base model (Oquab et al.,
299   2024). In order to focus only on a pose in images, we do not compare a generated image with an
300   original image, but rather compare OpenPose images of those two. On the other hand, Keypoints
301   reward function works based on the cosine similarity between the keypoints of the joints from the
302   input conditioning image and OpenPose of the generated image.

306   4.1   DATASET
307
308   We prepared 21K pose related images and alternative text pairs by collecting them from several
309   license-free image sites (Freepik; AdobeStock) and leveraging some parts of testset of Open Image
310   v7 dataset (Kuznetsova et al., 2020). We ensured that the images contained a human throughout the
311   collection process. The resolution of collected images is 1024 × 1024. Both types of OpenPose
312
      images(Cao et al., 2017), with or without facial landmarks and hands keypoints, are extracted from
      each image. Each dataset sample consists of three images and a text component: the collected image,
313
      OpenPose image, OpenPose with hands and face keypoints, and image caption. The dataset is split
314
      into train and test at a ratio of 20 to 1. The test dataset consists of 1K real images of a single person.

      4.2   E VALUATION S TRATEGY
317
318   We compared our models with four baseline models, ControlNet, Uni-ControlNet, StablePose, and
319   HumanSD. Two different types of evaluations are conducted: Evaluation 1 focuses on the base
320   model used for training, while Evaluation 2 focuses on other baseline models. The purpose of Eval-
321   uation 1 is to first assess whether our framework improves pose accuracy, and second, to examine
322   the impact of using more detailed poses including hands and face. Evaluation 2 aims to compare
323   our model’s performance against other pose-based human image generation models, regardless of
      condition types. In both types of evaluation, three main criteria on which we focused to evaluate the


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      quality of generated images: pose accuracy, image quality, and text-image alignment. Improvement
325   in pose accuracy ensures our framework is effective, and therefore, our primary goal of evaluation
326   is to enhance pose accuracy. However, image quality and text-image alignment are also important
327   components in image generation, so we additionally assess those two criteria to evaluate whether
328   our models improve the pose accuracy without degradation of their current ability.
329
      Pose Accuracy: We use Object Keypoint Similarity (OKS) (Maji et al., 2022) as our primary metric
330
      for assessing pose accuracy, measuring the distance between similarity in original and generated
331   images on a scale from 0 to 1. A higher OKS score indicates better pose accuracy. Our objective is
332   to improve OKS score compared to the baseline models.
333
334
      Image Quality: To assess image quality, we utilize LPIPS (Learned Perceptual Image Patch
335
      Similarity)(Zhang et al., 2018), which measures perceptual similarity between the original and
336
      generated images, with scores ranging from 0 to 1. Lower LPIPS scores indicate better image
337   quality and higher similarity to the original images. This measure has been demonstrated to closely
338   match human perception. Our goal is to maintain LPIPS score of the baseline models.
339
340
      Text Alignment: CLIP score(Hessel et al., 2021) is used for text alignment, which evaluates the
341
      similarity between generated images and their corresponding text representations, ranging from 1
342
      to 100. Higher CLIP scores signify greater alignment between the text and the image. Like LPIPS
343   score, CLIP score correlates highly with human judgment. Our objective is to preserve CLIP score
344   of the baseline models.
345

      4.3   T RAINED M ODELS USING THE P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK
347
348   The base model for training is Controlnet+OpenPose model(con) trained on Stable Diffusion XL
349   (Podell et al., 2023). Four models are defined to evaluate the proposed frame comprehensively, each
350   trained with different combinations of datasets and types of rewards.
351
      These models are distinguished by the types of OpenPose conditioning images and rewards used
352
      during training. The first and second models are trained using OpenPose images without hands and
353
      face keypoints as conditions. In contrast, the third and fourth models utilized more detailed Open-
354   Pose images, including hands and face keypoints. Models 1 and 3 employ the first reward function,
355   VLM + feature reward function. Conversely, models 2 and 4 are trained using the second type of
356   reward function, VLM + keypoints reward function. The choice of these models enables us to per-
357   form detailed ablation studies on the effect of different reward models and the conditioning images’
358   details. We expect to be able to generate more detailed human pose images incorporating facial
359   landmarks and hands keypoints and better pose alignment through our proposed reward models.
360   Training Details: In addition, our models are trained with four NVIDIA RTX A6000 using the
361   same conditions: learning rate = 2 × 10−6 , batch size per GPU is two images, and sampling steps of
362   50. Training steps for models 1 to 4 are 30025, 18775, 50525, and 19025, respectively. It varies as
363   we use the model which is saved when the reward is maximized before the models are overfitted.
