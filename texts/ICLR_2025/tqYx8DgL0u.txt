009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Privacy-preserving federated learning (PPFL) aims to train a global model for
012            multiple clients while maintaining their data privacy. However, current PPFL pro-
013            tocols exhibit one or more of the following insufficiencies: considerable degrada-
014            tion in accuracy, the requirement for sharing keys, and cooperation during the key
015            generation or decryption processes. As a mitigation, we develop the first proto-
016            col that utilizes neural networks to implement PPFL, as well as incorporating an
017            Aggregatable Hybrid Encryption scheme tailored to the needs of PPFL. We name
018
               these networks as Homomorphic Adversarial Networks (HANs) which demon-
               strate that neural networks are capable of performing tasks similar to multi-key
019
               homomorphic encryption (MK-HE) while solving the problems of key distribu-
020
               tion and collaborative decryption. Our experiments show that HANs are robust
021            against privacy attacks. Compared with non-private federated learning, experi-
022            ments conducted on multiple datasets demonstrate that HANs exhibit a negligible
023            accuracy loss (at most 1.35%). Compared to traditional MK-HE schemes, HANs
024            increase encryption aggregation speed by 6,075 times while incurring a 29.2-fold
025            increase in communication overhead.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a promising paradigm for collaborative model training
031   without direct data sharing (McMahan et al., 2017; Konečnỳ et al., 2016). While initially believed
032
      to preserve privacy (Li et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2019), recent studies have revealed vulnerabilities in
      FL, demonstrating that client-side gradients can potentially leak sensitive training data (Hitaj et al.,
033
      2017; Melis et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2019; Carlini et al., 2022).
034
035   To prevent data reconstruction in FL settings, researchers have been exploring various strategies,
036   notably differential privacy (DP) (Wei et al., 2020; Iyengar et al., 2019; Geyer et al., 2017) and
037   homomorphic encryption (HE) (Shi et al., 2023; Wibawa et al., 2022; Madi et al., 2021; Zhang
038
      et al., 2020b;a; Chen et al., 2019).DP stands out for its computational efficiency but may potentially
      reduce the performance of the FL model.
039
040   Regarding HE, although it preserves the model’s performance, it may compromise the data privacy
041   of all honest participants if a client conspires with an external attacker to share the key (collusion
042   attacks) (Cai et al., 2023; Fang & Qian, 2021).
043   To mitigate this problem, Multi-Key Homomorphic Encryption (MK-HE) (Chen et al., 2019) has
044   been proposed, which is designed to prevent collusion attacks without compromising the model’s
045   performance. However, the implementation of MK-HE introduces its own challenges, such as co-
046   operation during the key generation or decryption processes (Park et al., 2022). These issues under-
047   score the persistent dilemma faced in FL, how to find the right trade-off between data privacy and
048   the practical constraints of model performance as well as resource allocation.
049   To address these challenges, we propose Homomorphic Adversarial Networks (HANs), a novel
050   privacy-preserving approach that leverages neural networks to emulate the behavior of MK-HE
051   (comparisons shown in Table 1). HANs are designed to optimize encryption and aggregation tasks
052   without the need for traditional key distribution or collaborative decryption, thereby significantly
053   simplifying deployment in FL scenarios. The HANs framework employs an Aggregatable Hybrid
      Encryption (AHE) scheme, which synthesizes the advantages of both symmetric and asymmetric

055   scheme introduces three cryptographic primitives: Key Generation (KeyGen), Encryption (Enc),

      HANs offer several key advantages over traditional privacy-preserving techniques in FL. Unlike

      2.1    P RIVACY-P RESERVING F EDERATED L EARNING (PPFL)
095
      Differential Privacy is a frequently utilized tool for privacy protection. These studies (Abadi et al.,
096   2016; Geyer et al., 2017; Triastcyn & Faltings, 2019; Hu et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021; Rahman
097   et al., 2018) have utilized DP to secure data and user privacy. However, if there is a need to prevent
098   the reconstruction of data, the inclusion of DP can significantly compromise the accuracy of the
099   models.
100
      Homomorphic Encryption facilitates the execution of computations directly on a ciphertext to
101
      yield an encrypted outcome. Aono et al. (2017) proposed the application of HE for the safeguard-
102   ing of gradient updates during the FL training procedure. Chen et al. (2020), an FL framework
103   specifically designed for wearable healthcare, manages to achieve model aggregation by deploying
104   HE. The application approach of this HE is expedient. Apart from encryption and decryption, it
105   necessitates no significant alterations and imposes no extraordinary constraints on the algorithm.
106   Importantly, the accuracy of learning is preserved with HE, as no noise infiltrates the model updates
107   during either the encryption or decryption stages. Fang & Qian (2021) employs an enhanced Paillier
      algorithm to expedite computation. Zhang et al. (2020a) proposed BatchCrypt, a FL framework


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      based on batch encryption, with the goal of reducing computational expenses. However, traditional
109   HE schemes require key sharing which relies on the assumption that there is no collusion between
110   the Server and Clients (Aono et al., 2017).
111
112
      In order to prevent collusion attacks, MK-HE allows multiple parties to utilize distinct keys for
      encryption. The decryption process necessitates the collaborative involvement of all parties. Ma
113
      et al. (2022) introduced a PPFL framework based on xMK-CKKS, demonstrating resilience against
114
      collusion involving fewer than N -1 participant devices and the Server.
115
116   However, this approach involves additional computational overhead during key generation and de-
117   cryption, and requires collaboration among multiple clients for decryption. SecFed, an innovative
118
      federated learning framework, harnesses multi-key homomorphic encryption and trusted execution
      environments to safeguard multi-user privacy while boosting computational efficiency (Cai et al.,
119
      2023). This secure system also integrates an offline protection mechanism to address user dropout
120
      issues effectively.

      2.2   C RYPTOGRAPHY BASED ON G ENERATIVE A DVERSARIAL N ETWORKS
124
      In recent years, using neural networks, especially Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), for
125   encryption has become an emerging direction in cryptography research. Abadi & Andersen (2016)
126   proposed a method to learn symmetric encryption protocols based on GANs.
127
128
      They used two neural networks for encryption and decryption respectively, and introduced an at-
      tacker network to evaluate security. Subsequent works built upon this foundational research, further
129
      refining the approach (Luo et al., 2023; An et al., 2023; Li et al., 2020; Pattanayak & Ludwig,
130
      2018). These studies introduced various enhancements, including diverse attack models and en-
131   crypted training schemes, thereby advancing the field of neural network-based cryptography.
132
133   While these works have significantly contributed to the application of neural networks in cryp-
134
      tography, they still face certain limitations. Primarily, they focus on message encryption without
      addressing homomorphic computation. Moreover, they do not adequately tackle the challenges of
135
      key distribution or negotiation, which are crucial aspects of practical cryptographic systems.
136
137   Inspired by these studies, particularly their loss function design and the application of GANs in
138   training encryption neural networks, we propose HANs to address the aforementioned limitations
139   and provide an enhanced solution for privacy protection within the FL context.

147   3.1   D ESIGN C ONCEPT OF AHE
148
149   This AHE scheme, tailored for PPFL, uses private keys for encryption and public keys for aggrega-
150   tion, protecting individual client data while enabling efficient aggregation without relying on trusted
151   third parties. The key concepts of AHE are as follows:
152
153          • Public key: A key that can be made public, used for computing the aggregated plaintext.
154          • Private key: Confidential key for encrypting original ciphertext.
155
             • Original plaintext: The plaintext containing gradient information from a single client,
156            which should not be accessed by other clients or servers.
157
158
             • Original ciphertext: The ciphertext that corresponds to the original plaintext and is en-
               crypted by a private key.
159
160          • Aggregated plaintext: The combined gradient information derived from multiple original
161            ciphertexts and their corresponding public keys. In PPFL contexts, this aggregated plaintext
               may be shared openly among all participants.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
             • Original model: The initial HANs model distributed to clients by servers or third parties. It
163            is potentially vulnerable to information leakage due to the absence of fully trusted distrib-
164            utors.
165
             • PPU: A process that clients can use to transform the original model into a private model.
166
               The specific algorithm and process are detailed in Appendix E.
167
168          • Private model: The result of applying PPU to the original model. Each client securely
169
               stores their private model, treating it with confidentiality equivalent to private keys.
170          • Public dataset: A small, noisy dataset maintained by each client to facilitate the PPU
171            process without exposing their private model. It serves as a proxy for PPU participation.
172
173
      AHE primitives differ from traditional cryptography. We will clarify the capabilities and significance
      of the following attack methods in the AHE context:
174
175          • Ciphertext-only attack (COA): The attacker analyzes only the ciphertext, knowing it was
176            encrypted using AHE but without knowledge of the specific HANs model.
177
             • Known-model attack (KMA): Attacker knows the ciphertext is AHE-encrypted and has ac-
178
               cess to the original model parameters, but not the private model parameters. This corre-
179
               sponds to known-plaintext attacks and chosen-plaintext attacks in traditional cryptography.
180
181
             • Chosen-ciphertext attack (CCA): Not applicable in AHE as the encryptor cannot derive
               plaintext from ciphertext, making this attack infeasible in our scenario.
182

187   thereby enhancing the security of the algorithm.

      overall performance. Specifically, we require the original model to have high performance, so that
      after undergoing the PPU phase, it can maintain an acceptable level of performance.

      3.5   P SEUDO N -1 C OLLUSION ATTACKS
240   In addition to attacks and challenges targeting the model’s inherent encryption capabilities, the
241   unique characteristics of HANs may lead to two types of pseudo N -1 collusion attacks. These
242   attacks attempt to overcome the limitations of traditional N -2 collusion attacks by leveraging ad-
243   ditional information to achieve an effect approximating N -1 collusion. However, due to the PPU
244   mechanism, their effectiveness remains significantly limited. The basic ideas behind these two at-
245   tacks are:
246
            1. Pseudo N -1 Collusion Attack based on Original Model (PCAOM): In this attack, the
247
               adversary uses another trusted client’s original model to substitute for that client’s private
248
               model. This is a KMA where the attacker attempts to simulate collusion among N -1 clients
249            by using the publicly available original model, while in reality only N -2 clients are collud-
250            ing.
251
            2. Pseudo N -1 Collusion Attack based on Public Dataset (PCAPD):
252            This attack utilizes the noisy public datasets information generated during the PPU process.
253            The attacker uses this public data to approximate the behavior of another trusted client,
254            thereby achieving an effect similar to N -1 collusion. This is an enhanced version of a
255            COA.
256
257   Detailed formal definitions of these two attacks can be found in Appendix D.

      3.7   PPU
293
      To further enhance privacy protection, we have designed a PPU process, which includes two stages:
294
295          • CPPU: The main goal of the CPPU stage is to reduce the risk of model exposure, especially
296            in scenarios involving multi-party collaboration. Each client generates its own private data
297            and collects the latest public datasets from other clients. By combining private data with
298            samples from other clients, the client creates a training set to update the model. To mini-
299            mize the leakage of private model information, we gradually add noise to the public dataset,
300            with the noise intensity increasing over time. This noise not only prevents potential future
301
               inference attacks but also protects the privacy of other clients.
302          • IPPU: The IPPU stage further enhances security by performing multiple rounds of in-
303            dependent updates using only the client’s private data, thereby weakening the correlation
304            between the public dataset and the client’s encryption model. This process significantly re-
305            duces the possibility for attackers to infer sensitive information about the encryption model,
306
               even if they possess advanced techniques to analyze changes in the public dataset.
307
      The PPU process balances privacy protection and model performance. Although the update process
308   may lead to a slight decrease in model performance, the combination of CPPU and IPPU effectively
309   enhances the overall security of the system.
310
311
      For a detailed implementation of the PPU steps and algorithms, please refer to the Appendix E.

      4     E XPERIMENTAL A NALYSIS
346
347
348
      This section aims to validate the accuracy, security, and efficiency of HANs. All experiments were
      conducted using an A800 GPU. We have included the detailed design of the loss function, along
349
      with the specific training, distribution processes and PPU, in the Appendix B, C, and E.
350
351   To improve experimental efficiency, we ensured that the encryption model structure used by each
352   client was consistent. However, the attacker models were allowed to vary in structure to accommo-
353   date different attack strategies.
354   The encryption model consists of linear layers, convolutional layers, and residual blocks. The initial
355   linear layer expands the dimensionality of the plaintext and private keys, while the convolutional
356   layers obscure the relationship between them. Multiple residual blocks further enhance the com-
357   plexity of the input transformation, and the output layer compresses the data to the target ciphertext
358   length. The attacker models mirror the architecture of the encryption model, with input dimensions
359   adjusted to accommodate the ciphertext input.
360   We employed the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-5 and weight decay of 1e-6, combined
361   with a cosine annealing scheduler to ensure training stability and generalization. Training data were
362   generated by simple addition, enabling the model to handle diverse inputs and avoid overfitting.

704   A.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING
705
706   In the setting of FL, there are two primary roles: (1) Clients, who possess local training datasets
707   and are responsible for completing local model training. Clients have the obligation to ensure the
708   privacy and security of the dataset. (2) The Server, responsible for coordinating with Clients to
709   update global model parameters, also initializes the model and global hyperparameter settings.
710   Suppose that we have m separate Clients. Each Client is represented by Ci , where i ∈ [1, m] and
711   Client Ci has a local training dataset Di . In each step, there are three sub-steps:
712
713         1. Broadcast. Server broadcasts the current global model parameters wt−1 to each Client
714            Ci , where t represents the index of the current iteration round.
715         2. Local training. Each Client Ci receives global model parameters wt−1 and using local
716            training datasets Di to obtain the new local model parameters wit in parallel and sends
717            the local model parameter wit back to the Server. During the updating step, the Client
718            typically employs stochastic gradient descent for local epochs. In scenarios where com-
719
               munication cost is not a primary concern, setting local epochs to 1 can be an effective
               approach (McMahan et al., 2017).
720
721         3. Aggregation. The Server receives all model parameters {w1t ,w2t ,. . . ,wm t
                                                                                            } from the
                                                                        t

955   E     PPU M ECHANISM
956
957   After completing the training of HANs or opting to use a publicly trained HANs model, we distribute
958   the models to client endpoints. At this stage, the encryption models employed by each client are
959   public rather than private. To address this vulnerability, we implement a PPU mechanism for the
960   encryption models. The PPU process consists of two phases:
961
962   E.1   CPPU
963
964   The CPPU phase, as outlined in Algorithm 2, primarily aims to mitigate PCAOM. The process
965   involves:
966
             • Each client generates its own private data and collects the latest public datasets from other
967
               clients.
968
969          • The client creates a training set by combining its private dataset with samples from the
970
               public datasets.
971          • To minimize the exposure of private model information, the public datasets are kept mini-
               mal, using a sample-with-replacement algorithm (Algorithm 1) to create the training set.


                                                       18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       A crucial aspect of CPPU is the addition of sufficient noise to the public dataset. This noise, which
973    increases in intensity over time, should be greater than Gaussian noise G(0, 10−2 ) (Zhu et al., 2019),
974    serves a dual purpose:
975
976           • Protects against potential future techniques that might infer model parameters from public
977             dataset changes.
978           • Safeguards other client endpoints.
979
980    We require trusted clients to honestly add this noise, as it is essential for the overall security of the
981    system. It’s worth noting that for malicious clients, the addition or omission of noise does not affect
982
       their attack capabilities.
983
984
       E.2   IPPU
985
       The IPPU phase, as detailed in Algorithm 3, further enhances security by:
986
987           • Preventing PCAOM attacks.
988           • Diminishing the correlation between the public dataset and the client’s encryption model.
989
990    This process involves multiple rounds of independent updates for each client, using only their private
991    data and the final public datasets from the CPPU phase. By doing so, IPPU significantly reduces
992    the potential for adversaries to infer sensitive information about the encryption model, even if they
993    possess advanced techniques for analyzing public dataset changes.
994
995    E.3   T RADE - OFF AND S YSTEM C ONSISTENCY
996
       It is important to acknowledge that the PPU mechanism is a trade-off between model performance
997
       and security. By implementing these updates, clients sacrifice some model performance to gain
998    enhanced security.
