009                                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011               The electrocardiogram (ECG) is an essential non-invasive diagnostic tool for as-
012               sessing cardiac conditions. Existing automatic interpretation methods suffer from
013               limited generalizability, focusing on a narrow range of cardiac conditions, and
014               typically depend on raw physiological signals, which may not be readily available
015               in resource-limited settings where only printed or digital ECG images are accessi-
016               ble. Recent advancements in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) present
017               promising opportunities for addressing these challenges. However, the application
018
                  of MLLMs to ECG image interpretation remains challenging due to the lack of in-
                  struction tuning datasets and well-established ECG image benchmarks for quan-
019
                  titative evaluation. To address these challenges, we introduce ECGInstruct, a
020
                  comprehensive ECG image instruction tuning dataset of over 1 million samples,
021               covering a wide range of ECG-related tasks from diverse data sources. Using
022               ECGInstruct, we develop PULSE, a fine-tuned MLLM tailored for ECG im-
023               age interpretation. In addition, we curate ECGBench, a new evaluation bench-
024               mark covering four key ECG image interpretation tasks. Our experiments show
025               that PULSE sets a new state-of-the-art, outperforming general MLLMs with an
026               average accuracy improvement of 15% to 30%. This work highlights the potential
027               of PULSE to enhance ECG interpretation in clinical practice.1

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      The electrocardiogram (ECG) is an essential tool in diagnosing cardiovascular diseases due to its
057
      non-invasive, cost-effective, and widely accessible nature for assessing cardiac function. While
058
      some approaches have been proposed for automatic ECG diagnosis (Hannun et al., 2019; Ribeiro
059   et al., 2020; Hughes et al., 2021), their application in real-world clinical settings encounters several
060   challenges. First, these models are primarily designed for classification tasks with limited cardiac
061   conditions (Ribeiro et al., 2020), often lacking generalizability to rare or unseen abnormalities. Sec-
062   ond, they typically treat ECG data as time-series physiological signals, which may not always be
063   available, particularly in resource-constrained or remote settings (Siontis et al., 2021). In such set-
064   tings, ECG data are often stored exclusively as printed or digital images (Sangha et al., 2022; 2023),
065   limiting the utility of signal-based models. Third, variations in data formats and architectures across
066   different device vendors (Cuevas-González et al., 2022) further complicate the interoperability and
067
      applicability of traditional ECG models in diverse healthcare environments (Chung et al., 2022).
068   Recent advancements in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have shown impressive suc-
069   cess across vision-language tasks, offering new possibilities for addressing the aforementioned lim-
070   itations of traditional ECG models. They enable model inference directly from ECG images, which
071   are the primary formats used by clinicians (Cuevas-González et al., 2022), and accommodating rural
072   or remote clinic settings. However, applying MLLMs to ECG interpretation is not straightforward.
073
      As illustrated in Fig. 1, current MLLMs, such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), often provide responses
      that appear correct and contextually relevant but are ultimately inaccurate in interpreting ECG im-
074
      ages. This highlights the need for specialized MLLMs for ECG image interpretation.
075
076   Developing MLLMs for ECG images faces several challenges. First, no large-scale ECG image
077   datasets are currently available as most ECG datasets contain only raw signal data, which needs to
078   be synthesized into digital images. Second, there is a lack of instruction tuning datasets for ECG
079
      images. Large high-quality instruction tuning datasets, which are crucial for MLLM development,
      need to be curated from scratch for ECG-related tasks. Finally, evaluation is just as critical as model
080
      development, yet no established benchmark exists for assessing MLLM performance in ECG image
081
      interpretation. A well-defined benchmark is essential for both quantifying model performance and
082   identifying areas for future improvement.
083
084   In this paper, we tackle these challenges by introducing ECGInstruct, the first large-scale
085
      ECG image instruction tuning dataset containing over one million ECG image-text samples.
      ECGInstruct is characterized by: 1) realistic image synthesis that replicates artifacts commonly
086
      seen in paper-based ECGs, 2) a diverse range of ECG-related tasks with clinical experts’ insights for
087
      refinement, and 3) data sourced from distinct geographic regions. We use ECGInstruct to fine-
088   tune LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b), resulting in our model, PULSE. To address the evaluation challenge,
089   we present ECGBench, a comprehensive evaluation benchmark covering four major ECG image
090   analysis tasks. ECGBench includes repurposed diagnosis and clinical report generation tasks from
091   existing datasets, as well as newly created, complex ECG analysis using real-world images.
092
      Evaluated on ECGBench, PULSE sets a new state-of-the-art, significantly outperforming propri-
093
      etary MLLMs across all benchmarks with an average accuracy gain of 15% to 30% compared to
094   GPT-4o on out-of-domain datasets (Fig. 1). Ablation experiments demonstrate the importance of
095   incorporating diverse data sources and ECG instruction tasks into the training data. A case study
096   and discussion further illustrate the model’s effectiveness in ECG image interpretation.
097
      To summarize, our main contributions are as follows,
098
099   • Problem. We investigate the capabilities of MLLMs in ECG image interpretation and evaluate
100   their performance across various downstream tasks. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
101   study focused on assessing MLLMs in image-based ECG interpretation.
102   • Dataset. We construct ECGInstruct, a large-scale ECG image instruction tuning dataset con-
103   sisting of various ECG-related tasks, serving as a valuable resource for fine-tuning MLLMs.
104
      • Model. We develop PULSE, a new MLLM tailored for ECG image interpretation. The model
105
      achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming both proprietary and open-source MLLMs.
106
107   • Evaluation. We establish ECGBench, a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating ECG image
      interpretation, which includes diverse evaluation tasks, both real-world and synthesized images.

155
156   We aim to curate a list of multifaceted instruction tuning datasets for ECG analysis that are featured
157   by 1) realistic image synthesis resembling the artifacts in paper ECGs, 2) diverse types of ECG-
158
      related tasks with clinical experts’ insights, and 3) different data sources from distinct geographical
      regions. We show the construction of ECGInstruct in Fig. 2 and data summary in Table 1.
159
160   ECG Image Synthesis with Real Distortions. To enhance the robustness and real-world applica-
161   bility of our model, we synthesize ECG images mimicking common artifacts found in paper ECGs.
      We adopt an ECG image synthesis tool (Shivashankara et al., 2024) that provides various imper-


                                                                                                   3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      fections such as grid line interference, creases, wrinkles, paper rotations, etc. By including these
163   synthesized artifacts, we aim to train models that can effectively interpret ECGs in less-than-ideal
164   conditions, as often encountered in clinical settings. More details are provided in Appendix C.
165
166
      ECG-related Tasks with Clinical Experts’ Insights. To construct a comprehensive set of ECG-
      related tasks, we consulted domain experts to curate diverse and clinically relevant tasks covering
167
      four different categories. Each category is designed to address specific aspects of ECG interpretation
168
      and analysis, including (1) basic feature recognition (see examples in Appendix Fig. A1), (2) heart
169   rhythm analysis (see examples in Appendix Fig. A2), (3) morphology and pathology identification
170   (see examples in Appendix Fig. A3) and (4) clinical report generation (see examples in Appendix
171   Fig. A4). Basic feature recognition (e.g., interval or segment, etc.) forms the foundation of ECG in-
172   terpretation, enabling the model to grasp essential cardiac parameters. Heart rhythm analysis (e.g.,
173   arrhythmias, conduction abnormalities, etc.) and morphology and pathology identification (e.g.,
174   wave shape, pathological conditions, etc.) are more advanced and critical aspects of ECG analysis,
175   ensuring that the model can detect and classify complex conditions accurately. Lastly, clinical report
176   generation mirrors the process of healthcare professionals synthesizing a comprehensive interpre-
177
      tation of an ECG. By incorporating clinical experts’ insights, we encourage the model to learn the
      practical skills required in a clinical context.
178
179   Diverse Types of Tasks. Based on the original diagnoses and clinical reports from the existing
180   ECG datasets, we curate diverse types of tasks including multi-choice questions, fill-in-the-blank,
181   close-ended QA, and open-ended QA. This variety of task types not only enhances the model’s
182   versatility but also mimics the diverse cognitive processes involved in real-world ECG interpretation.
183
      By incorporating these varied task types, we aim to develop a more robust and adaptable model
      capable of handling a wide spectrum of ECG-related queries and analyses.
184
185   Diverse Data Sources from Distinct Regions. To ensure broad applicability and generalizabil-
186   ity, we collect ECG data from four different sources across geographically distinct regions: 1)
187   PTB-XL (Wagner et al., 2020): a Germany-based, publicly available repository; (2) MIMIC-IV-
188   ECG (Gow et al., 2023): a large set of ECGs for patients who appear in the MIMIC-IV Clinical
189
      Database from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center in Boston (Johnson et al., 2023); 3) CODE-
      15% (Ribeiro et al., 2021): an ECG dataset from a central ECG repository from Minas Gerais,
190
      Brazil under the clinical outcomes in digital electrocardiology (CODE) study (Ribeiro et al., 2019);
191
      4) ECG-QA (Oh et al., 2024), a question answering dataset for ECGs that is constructed based
192   on PTB-XL (Wagner et al., 2020). This diverse geographical representation enhances the model’s
193   ability to generalize across different populations and healthcare systems, accounting for potential
194   variations in ECG patterns and interpretations across regions.
195
      Data Synthesizing at Scale. Since large-scale annotation of ECG features is extremely expen-
196
      sive and time-consuming, we develop an automatic data synthesizing pipeline to address this data
197
      scarcity issue. We utilized diagnostic reports from PTB-XL and MIMIC-IV-ECG as initial seed data
198   and leveraged an advanced large language model (i.e., Llama-3-70B-Instruct) for data synthesis.
199   Building upon the expert-in-the-loop process and diverse data resources described in the previous
200   sections, we synthesized a substantial volume of ECG-related instructions and corresponding re-
201   sponses. These were based on expert-provided examples and real-world scenarios, with the specific
202   prompts used in this process detailed in the Appendix E. For datasets lacking comprehensive reports,
203   such as CODE-15%, we manually constructed diverse templates to transform the existing data into
204   an instruction-response format.
205   Quality Control. To guarantee the quality of generated instructions and corresponding responses,
206   we apply an independent LLM as a judge to evaluate and score the content. This process involves
207   several steps: 1) initial generation: instructions and responses are first generated using our primary
208   model; 2) evaluation criteria: we establish a set of evaluation criteria including the instruction rele-
209   vance, clarity, answerability of the responses, etc; 3) LLM judge and scoring: an independent LLM
210   (Llama 3 (Meta, 2024)) is used as a judge to assess each instruction-response pair against estab-
211   lished criteria and assign scores (see prompt in Appendix Fig. A8); 4) feedback loop: low-scoring
212
      items are flagged for human expert review and potential revision or removal; 5) iterative refinement:
      based on the scoring patterns and human expert input, we continually refine our instruction gener-
213
      ation process. By combining automated LLM evaluation with human expert oversight, we create a
214
      robust system for maintaining and improving the quality of our instruction-response pairs.

237   Figure 3: The data curation process for ECGBench. There are four key tasks involved: 1) two
238   repurposed tasks (abnormality detection and report generation) derived from existing ECG datasets,
239   where ECG images are synthesized from raw signals, and queries/answers are extracted based on
240   diagnostic and clinical reports; 2) Two newly developed tasks using external resources, where ECG
241   images and associated questions and answers are collected and generated from real-world sources.
242
243   Training. Our model architecture closely follows that of LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b;c), adapting it for
244
      ECG image analysis. We use a vision encoder to process ECG images and a large language model as
      the text decoder, connected via a projection layer. We organize the data into three components: the
245
      image, the instructions, and the outputs. The instruction is query or task related to the ECG image
246
      and the output is the expected response or prediction base on the image and instruction. We place the
247   image at the beginning of each conversation, serving as the visual grounding for the entire dialogue.
248   During training, we freeze the parameters of the vision encoder while updating the parameters of the
249   projection layer and the language model using an autoregressive training objective, where we mask
250   all the tokens belonging to the image and the instruction.

      questions with 8 options, including the original diagnosis and randomly sampled negative labels.

298   interpretation). (3) Quality Control: To maintain high standards, we implemented a quality control
299   process. In particular, Each question underwent review by at least two other annotators, checking

      discrepancies or ambiguities were resolved during this process.
303   ECG Arena. To assess the model’s instruction-following ability in ECG comprehension, we devel-
304   oped ECG Arena, inspired by MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024) and Arena-hard (Chiang et al., 2024)
305   used in general LLM chat evaluations. We manually curated 50 multi-turn ECG-related questions,
306   focusing on open-ended interactions. The data curation process for ECG Arena, like MMMU ECG,
307   involves three main steps: resource selection, question creation, and quality control. The key dis-
308
      tinction is that MMMU ECG focuses on multiple-choice questions, whereas ECG Arena involves
      more complex, flexible multi-turn, open-ended questions. Each follow-up question is contingent
309
      on the initial question and its response, making the process more challenging and reflective of real-
310
      world applications. Since multi-turn conversations are rare in existing sources, this posed significant
311   challenges during data curation. To address this, annotators created such conversations by referenc-
312   ing original clinical interpretations and ECG images. The questions are designed to feel natural and
313   simulate a real clinical setting (e.g., the first question may ask about basic findings from the image,
314   followed by a question about potential clinical causes or diagnoses based on those findings).

      4.1   M ETHODS FOR C OMPARISON
330
331   In order to evaluate the performance of our proposed model, we compare it against a set of estab-
332   lished methods including domain-specific methods and state-of-the-art MLLMs.
333
334   • Domain-specific Methods: We consider four domain-specific methods for ECG including three
335     signal-based methods: METS (Li et al., 2024c), MERL (Liu et al., 2024a), ST-MEM (Na et al.,
336
        2023), and one image-based method: ECG-GPT (Khunte et al., 2024).
337   • Proprietary MLLMs: We consider three proprietary MLLMs: GPT-4o, GPT-4o mini (OpenAI,
338     2024), Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid et al., 2024) and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024).

759   Domain-specific Models for ECG. Many domain-specific models have been proposed to enhance
760   automatic ECG diagnosis (Hannun et al., 2019; Ribeiro et al., 2020; Hughes et al., 2021). For ex-
761   ample, Ribeiro et al. (2020) applied convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to encode ECG signals
762
      for diagnosing 6 types of abnormalities. To reduce dependence on high-quality labeled data, re-
      cent studies (Li et al., 2024c; Liu et al., 2024a; Na et al., 2023) have further explored self-supervised
763
      learning approaches using unlabeled ECG training data. For example, Liu et al. (2024a) proposed an
764
      ECG representation learning framework by integrating the ECG signals and clinical reports, show-
765   ing improved performance in zero-shot ECG classification tasks. Despite these successes, most
766   approaches treat ECG data as temporal physiological signals, which could be limiting in certain
767   resource-constrained or remote settings where only printed or digital images are available. Re-
768   cently, a few methods (Sangha et al., 2022; 2023; Khunte et al., 2024) have been proposed for ECG
769   diagnosis using ECG images. For example, Khunte et al. (2024) developed a diagnostic report gen-
770   eration framework for ECG images, which is built upon a BEiT (Bao et al., 2021) vision transformer
771   encoder and a GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) decoder. However, their model is only capable of the
772   clinical report generation task, without generalizability to other diverse tasks. In contrast, our study
773
      investigates the capabilities of MLLMs for ECG image interpretation. We curate a diverse range of
      instruction tuning datasets to fine-tune the model, thus improving model generalizability.
774
775
776
      MLLMs in Healthcare Recent advancements in MLLMs have shown promising results in vari-
777   ous healthcare domains. General medical multimodal models such as LLaVA-Med (Li et al., 2024a),
778   MedPaLM (Singhal et al., 2023a;b), and Med-Gemini (Saab et al., 2024) have demonstrated capa-
779   bilities in processing diverse medical data types. Additionally, domain-specific multimodal models
780   have been developed for specialized fields like pathology (Lu et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024) and
781   radiology (Wu et al., 2023). These models have shown potential in integrating visual and textual
782   information to support clinical decision-making and medical education. However, despite the im-
783   portance of ECG data in cardiac diagnosis and monitoring, current MLLMs often struggle to process
784   ECG images effectively. This limitation highlights a significant gap in the application of MLLMs to
785
      cardiology, where the ability to interpret both visual ECG representations and accompanying clinical
      information is crucial.
786
787
788
      Multimodal Instruction Tuning. Instruction tuning has proven effective in the multimodal do-
789   main, particularly in vision-language models like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024d), MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al.,
790   2023) and InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023). These models demonstrate impressive generalizability
791   across various visual understanding and reasoning tasks. While multimodal instruction tuning has
792   been applied to general medical imaging tasks (Li et al., 2024b; Singhal et al., 2023a), its application
793   to ECG images remains largely unexplored. A recent work (Wan et al., 2024) introduced a targeted
794   instruction tuning framework and fine-tuned existing open-source LLMs for ECG report generation.
795   However, their approach is limited by a single-task instruction dataset focused solely on report gen-
796   eration, potentially constraining its adaptability to other ECG-related tasks. Moreover, their work
797
      also treats ECG data as temporal signals, whereas our paper focuses on encoding ECG images with
      MLLMs, which is more applicable to real scenarios where only printed or digital ECG images are
798
      available.

802   B    P RELIMINARY ON 12- LEAD ECG
803
804   ECG is a vital diagnostic tool that measures the electrical activity of the heart over time, providing
805   insights into both spatial and temporal aspects of cardiac function. Typically, an ECG recording is
806   presented as a 12-lead multivariate time series, where each lead offers a unique perspective on heart
807   activity. The six limb leads (I, II, III, aVR, aVL, and aVF) assess the electrical movements across
808   the arms and legs, giving views from the frontal plane. Simultaneously, the six precordial leads (V1,
809   V2, V3, V4, V5, and V6) monitor the chest, offering horizontal plane views. In this paper, we focus
      on ECG images that are synthesized from raw signals.

811
812
      We employ the ECG-image-kit (Shivashankara et al., 2024) framework to synthesize diverse ECG
813   images from raw signal data. This toolkit allows for the generation of ECG images under various
814   conditions by introducing a range of distortions and noises to better simulate real-world clinical data.
815
816
      Specifically, in addition to generating standard 12-lead ECG images—characterized by black wave-
      forms on a white background, red grid lines, and a 4x3 layout—we introduce a variety of perturba-
817
      tions to the images. These modifications include the addition of wrinkles and creases, simulating
818
      the physical wear and tear commonly observed in paper-printed ECGs. Our image synthesis process
819   includes various augmentation methods to simulate physical distortions, image quality variations,
820   and layout alterations. We introduce wrinkles and creases to mimic wear and tear commonly ob-
821   served in paper-printed ECGs, and apply random rotations at varying angles to simulate misaligned
822   scans or prints. To account for different acquisition systems and scanning qualities, we vary image
823   resolutions and introduce random background colors, such as slight yellowing to represent aging or
824   poor scanning quality. We also add noise to the images to simulate imperfections in the scanning or
825   printing process. Furthermore, we experiment with different aspect ratios, overall image sizes, and
826   ECG plot positions within the image to reflect the heterogeneity of ECG printouts across different
827
      systems and formats. In some cases (with a 0.02 probability), we randomly remove grid lines to
      account for variations in ECG presentation.
828
829   To further enrich the synthetic images, we randomly insert meta-information into the image header to
830   simulate the annotations typically seen in clinical ECG reports. For the PTB-XL dataset, we extract
831   patient demographics (e.g., age, gender) and basic ECG features (e.g., heart rate, axis deviations)
832   from the associated PTB-XL feature annotation dataset, PTB-XL+ (Strodthoff et al., 2023). This
833
      extracted data is used to impute realistic meta-information, which is then randomly printed on the
      synthesized image. This random insertion of meta-data not only increases the visual variety of the
834
      images but also provides additional context, simulating real-world ECG prints that include patient
835
      and diagnostic information. To further increase diversity, we adopt alternative lead configurations
836   beyond the standard 4x3 layout, such as 12x1 (single row of leads), 6x2 (two rows of six leads), and
837   other commonly used clinical formats. These variations ensure that our model is exposed to a wide
838   range of ECG presentation styles.
839
      The augmentation process is designed to balance the dataset, with an approximate ratio of 1:1 be-
840
      tween augmented and standard ECG images. This balance ensures that the model is exposed to both
841
      clean and distorted images, aiding in its generalization to real-world clinical scenarios.

1408   Abnormality Detection. we utilize multi-label classification metrics, including Macro AUC, Macro
1409   F1, and Hamming Loss, to evaluate the datasets PTB-XL Super, CODE-15%, and CPSC 2018,
1410   where multiple correct labels may exist. For the ECG-QA, CSN, and G12EC datasets, we adopt
1411   accuracy as the evaluation metric.
1412   Report Generation. Rather than relying on traditional text generation metrics, we leverage strong
1413   LLMs as evaluators, following the approach of Zheng et al. (2024). This method provides a more
1414   nuanced evaluation by focusing on key aspects of the reports. Specifically, we use GPT-4o to com-
1415   pare the model-generated reports against those written by cardiologists. We introduce a “Report
1416   Perfect Score”, which is based on three critical components of a generated report: (1) Rhythms (0 to
1417   10 points), (2) Waveform Morphology (0 to 10 points), and (3) Diagnosis (0 to 10 points). The final
1418   score is the average of these three components, scaled to a maximum of 100 points. The prompt
1419
       used to query GPT-4o for evaluating the report score is provided in Appendix Fig. A9.
1420   MMMU ECG. We adopt accuracy as the primary metric. We have designed systematic, rule-based
1421   evaluation pipelines to ensure robust and consistent scoring. To mitigate the potential influence of
1422   any intermediate generations (e.g., reasoning steps) in long responses, we employ robust regular
1423   expressions and develop response-processing workflows. These are used to extract answer options
1424   from the long responses for accurate answer matching. In cases where no valid answer can be
1425
       extracted from the model’s response, we perform random selection to assign a score.
1426   ECG Arena. We also employ a strong judge model, GPT-4o, to assess model performance by com-
1427   paring generated responses with ground truth answers. The evaluation considers three perspectives,
1428   each scored on a scale of 0-10: Accuracy (how closely the model’s response matches the ground
1429   truth), Completeness (whether the model provides a comprehensive answer covering all aspects of
1430   ECG interpretation), and Instruction Adherence (how well the model follows the specific instruc-
1431
       tions in the question). We calculate the final score by averaging these three aspects and scaling to
       a maximum of 100 points. The specific prompt used for GPT-4 evaluation is provided in Appendix
1432
       Fig. A10.

       F.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
1436   We follow the model architecture of LLaVA, which includes three core components: a vision en-
1437   coder, a large language model, and a projector to align image and text modalities. Table A2 summa-
1438   rizes all the model parameters. Specifically, for the LLM, we utilize Vicuna-1.5-7B, while the vision
1439   encoder is based on CLIP-ViT-Large-Patch14-336. We employ a 2-layer MLP as a projector to map
1440   the visual features from the CLIP encoder onto the tokens used by the LLM. These features are
1441   mapped onto predefined image tokens, which encapsulate the features of ECG images. The tokens
1442   representing ECG features are then concatenated as an image context preceding the dialogue.
1443   We format all datasets into a chatbot-style multi-turn dialogue format (same as Vicuna-1.5-7B) and
1444   use the special token <image> to represent image features within the text data. For example, a sam-
1445   ple data instance is: “Human: <image> Describe this ECG image. \n Assistant: This image . . . ”.
1446   To enhance the model’s ability to handle ECG images of various sizes encountered in real-world
1447   scenarios, we employ Anyres. Anyres divides high-resolution images into multiple sub-images of
1448   size 336x336. The features of these sub-images are then concatenated with the global features of
1449   the original image to form the final image representation.
1450   We fine-tune all parameters of the vision encoder, projector, and LLM. The training process uses a
1451   learning rate of 2e-5, a batch size of 128, and a cosine scheduler with a 5% warm-up period over
1452   three epochs. The loss is calculated using the cross-entropy loss function, focusing on the response
1453   portion of the dialogue.
