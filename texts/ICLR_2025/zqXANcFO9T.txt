009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Decentralized learning distributes the training process across multiple nodes, en-
012            abling collaborative model training without relying on a central server. Each node
013            performs local training using its own data, with model updates exchanged directly
014            between connected nodes within a given network topology. Various algorithms
015            have been developed within this decentralized learning framework and have been
016
               proven to converge under specific assumptions. However, two key challenges
               remain: 1) ensuring robust performance with both a high degree of gradient com-
017
               pression and data heterogeneity, and 2) providing a general convergence upper
018
               bound under commonly used assumptions. To address these challenges, we pro-
019            pose the Discounted Error-Feedback Decentralized Parallel Stochastic Gradient
020            Descent (DEFD-PSGD) algorithm, which efficiently manages both high levels
021            of gradient compression and data heterogeneity, without sacrificing communica-
022            tion efficiency. The core idea is to introduce controllable residual error feedback
023            that effectively balances the impact of gradient compression and data heterogene-
024            ity. Additionally, we develop novel proof techniques to derive a convergence up-
025            per bound under relaxed assumptions. Finally, we present experimental results
026            demonstrating that DEFD-PSGD outperforms other state-of-the-art decentralized
027
               learning algorithms, particularly in scenarios involving high compression and sig-
               nificant data heterogeneity.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, decentralized learning has become an important technology in machine learning
033   due to its computational scalability for parallel computing (Nedic & Ozdaglar, 2009; Lian et al.,
034   2017), communication efficiency (Tang et al., 2019; 2018; Koloskova et al., 2019; Pu & NedicћБ,
035   2021), and data locality for data privacy (Wangni et al., 2018; Reisizadeh et al., 2019). Specifically,
036   decentralized learning distributes the training process across multiple nodes in a network and allows
037   them to collaboratively train a shared model without a central server. We consider the scenario where
038   each node possesses its own data. During training, every node performs local updates based on its
039
      own data, where different nodes may have different data distributions. Then, each node exchanges
      model updates with connected nodes according to the networkвАЩs connectivity.
040
041   General Challenges. In the decentralized learning framework, one of the most persistent chal-
042   lenges is the communication efficiency. To address this, recent research has focused on designing
043   algorithms that use compressed model updates (Wangni et al., 2018; Reisizadeh et al., 2019; Tang
044   et al., 2019; 2018; Koloskova et al., 2019). One of their drawbacks is the degradation of performance
045
      when the degree of compression level is high or when the compression is biased. To mitigate the
      performance degradation due to compression, error feedback can be applied. Its core idea is to ac-
046
      cumulate and correct the errors that occur during gradient updates, thereby improving the accuracy
047
      and stability of the learning process. Earlier works on error feedback focuses on centralized settings
048   with parameter server architecture (Stich et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2018; Karimireddy et al., 2019).
049   However, because the local models of nodes are not fully synchronized in the decentralized setting,
050   directly applying error feedback to the seminal DCD-PSGD algorithm (Tang et al., 2018) can be in-
051   effective. Koloskova et al. (2019) introduced the CHOCO-PSGD algorithm where error feedback is
052   applied to the decentralized learning procedure. By using error feedback in the decentralized learn-
053   ing scenario, both theoretical and empirical studies show that CHOCO-PSGD is robust to gradient
      compression. However, one drawback of CHOCO-PSGD is its inefficiency in handling the scenario

      of non-IID data across nodes, especially when the degree of data heterogeneity is high. Hence, in
055   decentralized learning, it is challenging to handle both the high degree of gradient compression and
056   high degree of data heterogeneity simultaneously.

117   2    R ELATED W ORKS
118
119   Under a centralized framework, Stich et al. (2018); Wu et al. (2018) introduce error-compensated
120   SGD with compressed updates, including analysis for (strongly) convex functions. Karimireddy
121   et al. (2019) analyze the impact of error feedback for Sign-SGD and other compression schemes
122   for weakly convex and non-convex functions. However, the centralized error feedback algorithms
123   require information aggregation among all nodes.
124
      In decentralized learning, gossip algorithms are among the most popular approaches due to their
125   simplicity (Kempe et al., 2003; Johansson & Johansson, 2008; Boyd et al., 2006; Lian et al., 2017).
126   In a gossip algorithm, each node first computes the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) locally and
127   then sends its update to its connected nodes. Under certain network connectivity assumptions, Lian
                                                                               вИЪ
128   et al. (2017) show that the convergence rate of this algorithm is O(1/ nT ), where n is the total
129   number of nodes and T is the total number of iterations. Notably, this convergence rate matches that
130   of centralized SGD, where a central server aggregates the SGDs and updates the model parameters
131   in each iteration.
132
      To reduce communication overhead between connected nodes, compression techniques such as
133   quantization and sparsification are applied on the model updates transmitted among nodes. These
134   techniques are widely used in the federated learning literature (Basu et al., 2019; Haddadpour et al.,
135   2021; Alistarh et al., 2017; Bernstein et al., 2018; Shlezinger et al., 2020; Reisizadeh et al., 2020;
136   Wangni et al., 2018; Sattler et al., 2019; Albasyoni et al., 2020; Gorbunov et al., 2021; Alistarh et al.,
137   2018; Stich & Karimireddy, 2020; Karimireddy et al., 2019) as well as decentralized learning liter-
138   ature (Wangni et al., 2018; Reisizadeh et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2019; 2018; Koloskova et al., 2019).
139   In particular, Tang et al. (2018) introduce the compression methods on the model updates shared
140   in gossip algorithms and propose the DCD-PSGD and ECD-PSGD algorithms, demonstrating that
141
      their convergence when using unbiased compressors or compressors with bounded noise. However,
      directly applying these compression techniques can adversely affect the convergence rate of gossip
142
      algorithms, and may even lead to divergence if the compression noise is large. The reason is that the
143
      lost information due to compression is not compensated.
144
145   The CHOCO-PSGD algorithm, introduced by Koloskova et al. (2019), is another approach aimed at
146   improving the compression rate in decentralized learning. It incorporates a consensus step size pa-
147
      rameter to regulate model aggregation at each node along with error feedback. However, it struggles
      to handle highly heterogeneous data distributions effectively. Recently, a few other algorithms were
148
      designed based on CHOCO-PSGD. In particular, AdaG-PSGD (Aketi et al., 2024) was proposed to
149
      dynamically adjust the consensus steps used in Koloskova et al. (2019). Choudhary et al. (2024)
150   proposed Q-SADDLe algorithm, which applied additional gradient descent to seek flatter loss land-
151   scapes in decentralized setting. This flatter loss landscapes allow more compression to alleviate
152   the local over-fitting with non-IID data. Aketi et al. (2021) proposed Sparse-Push (SP) algorithm,
153   which includes additional communication round to handle the time-varying network topologies.
154   Nassif et al. (2024) proposed the DEF-ATC algorithm, introducing a damping coefficient in front
155   of the updates in Koloskova et al. (2019). DEF-ATC promises a maintaining performance while re-
156   ducing communication overheads in small step-size regime. Since these algorithms share the same
157   limitation as CHOCO-PSGD when dealing with high degree of data heterogeneity, in this paper, we
158
      use DCD-PSGD and CHOCO-PSGD as our baselines in the main paper. The comparison among all
      the algorithms will be presented in the Appendix A.5.
159
160   In this paper, the proposed DEFD-PSGD algorithm incorporates residual error feedback to effec-
161   tively manage biased compressors and those with higher compression noise. We validate its effec-
      tiveness both analytically and empirically. In addition, we introduces a new discount parameter, ќ≥,


                                                          3

162
      in DEFD-PSGD. We empirically study the impact of ќ≥ and show that ќ≥ can effectively balance the
163   impact of gradient compression and data heterogeneity.

409   5     E XPERIMENTS
410
411   In this section, we provide the experimental setup including details on the models, datasets, and
412   compression schemes used. We include two baseline algorithms, DCD-PSGD (Tang et al., 2018)
413   and CHOCO-PSGD (Koloskova et al., 2019), for comparison, since they are most representative.
414   Comparisons between DEFD-PSGD and other algorithms can be found in Appendix A.5.
415
416   5.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
417
      Dataset and Models. In our experiments, we evaluate the proposed DEFD-PSGD alongside two
418
      baseline algorithms, DCD-PSGD and CHOCO-PSGD, using two popular datasets, FashionMNIST
419
      (Xiao et al., 2017) and CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009). For the FashionMNIST dataset, we
420   use a two-layer neural network. For CIFAR10 dataset, we employ a Convolutional Neural Network
421   (CNN) consisting of two convolutional layers, each paired with a max-pool layer (with a 3√Ч3 kernel
422   padding, 32 filters and a 2 √Ч 2 max-pool), followed by three fully connected layers (with sizes 256,
423   64, 10) (Wang et al., 2023). We apply ReLU activation functions to all layers except the final output
424   layer and use Kaiming initialization (He et al., 2015) for the initial model parameters. To ensure a
425   fair comparison, we provide the average results from four runs with different random seeds.
426   Data distribution. To control the degree of heterogeneity for the data distributions across the nodes,
427   we apply the Dirichlet distribution which is parameterized by ќ±. The details about Dirichlet distri-
428   bution is described in (Tzu-Ming Harry et al., 2019). Specifically, if the Dirichlet parameter ќ±
429   approaches zero, the data distribution becomes extremely heterogeneous, which means the data dis-
430   tribution on each node mainly contains one class of data. Otherwise, if the Dirichlet parameter ќ±
431   goes to infinity, the local data distribution tends to be the same across all nodes. In our experiments,
      we employ the Dirichlet parameter ќ± = 0.05 to evaluate the performance of different algorithms


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      on highly heterogeneous data. We provide further experimental results with additional Dirichlet
433   parametersвАЩ values in Appendix A.5.
434
435   Network topology. We consider a decentralized network topology where each node communicates
436
      only with their neighboring nodes. The real connectivity matrix W is generated based on the total
      number of nodes and the number of connected nodes per node. We select the neighboring nodes
437
      for each node uniformly at random and ensure that the real connectivity matrix W is a symmetric
438
      doubly stochastic matrix, satisfying Assumption 1. More specifically, in our experiments, we use a
439   20 √Ч 20 symmetric doubly stochastic matrix, where each node is connected to 4 other nodes.
440
441   Compression. In our experiments, we employ both unbiased and biased compression techniques
442
      to evaluate the performance of the proposed DEFD-PSGD and other algorithms. For the unbi-
      ased compression, we use element-wise random quantization, as described in Zhang et al. (2017).
443
      Specifically, each element is randomly quantized into one of the two closest quantization levels. The
444
      probability for each level is calculated based on the distance between the element and the quantiza-
445   tion levels normalized by the distance between the two quantization levels. For biased compression,
446   we apply the Top-k compression (Stich et al., 2018; Alistarh et al., 2018), which selects the top k
447   fraction of all elements according to the magnitude and sets other elements to zero. For quantization,
448   we apply 4-bit and 6-bit random quantization for FashionMNIST dataset and 6-bit and 8-bit random
449   quantization for CIFAR10 dataset. For top-k compression, we use top-k ratio of 0.1 and 0.2 for
450   both FashionMNIST and CIFAR10 dataset. With these parameters, all the algorithms used in our
451   experiments have been shown to converge empirically.

530       6                       C ONCLUSION
531
532       In this paper, we address the challenging problem of decentralized learning, particularly in scenarios
533       involving high gradient compression and significant data heterogeneity. To tackle these challenges,
534       we propose the DEFD-PSGD algorithm, which introduces controllable error feedback to effectively
535
          manage gradient compression and data heterogeneity while maintaining communication efficiency.
          In addition, we develop novel proof techniques to establish a convergence upper bound under more
536
          relaxed assumptions. Finally, our experimental results align with the theoretical analysis and demon-
537
          strate that DEFD-PSGD outperforms other state-of-the-art decentralized learning algorithms.
