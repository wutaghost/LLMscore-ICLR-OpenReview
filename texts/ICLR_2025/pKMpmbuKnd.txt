009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating realistic time series samples is crucial for stress-testing models and
012            protecting user privacy by using synthetic data. In engineering and safety-critical
013            applications, these samples must meet certain hard constraints that are domain-
014            specific or naturally imposed by physics or nature. Consider, for example, generat-
015            ing electricity demand patterns with constraints on peak demand times. This can be
016            used to stress-test the functioning of power grids during adverse weather conditions.
017            Existing approaches for generating constrained time series are either not scalable or
018
               degrade sample quality. To address these challenges, we introduce Constrained Pos-
               terior Sampling (CPS), a diffusion-based sampling algorithm that aims to project
019
               the posterior mean estimate into the constraint set after each denoising update.
020
               Notably, CPS scales to a large number of constraints (∼ 100) without requiring
021            additional training. We provide theoretical justifications highlighting the impact
022            of our projection step on sampling. Empirically, CPS outperforms state-of-the-art
023            methods in sample quality and similarity to real time series by around 10% and
024            42%, respectively, on real-world stocks, traffic, and air quality datasets.

057
058
      1. Training-free approach to include multiple constraints: Training the generative model for a
         specific constraint, as in the case of Loss-DiffTime (Coletta et al., 2024), is not scalable. A model
059
         trained to generate samples with specified mean constraints cannot adapt to argmax constraints.
060
      2. Independence from external realism enforcers: Generally, prior works involve a projection step
061      to a feasible set defined by a set of constraints, which often destroys the sample quality. To address
062      this, prior approaches (Coletta et al., 2024) rely on external models to enforce realism, in addition
063      to the generative model, resulting in additional training and complex sampling procedures.
064   3. Hyperparameter-free approach to constrained generation: The choice of guidance weights
065      in guidance-based approaches with DMs significantly affects the sample quality. Optimizing for
066      guidance weights becomes combinatorially hard while dealing with hundreds of constraints.
067
      Given the following requirements, we propose Constrained Posterior Sampling (CPS), a novel
068   sampling procedure for diffusion-based generative models (check Fig. 1). CPS introduces a projection
069   step that aims to project the posterior mean estimate into the constraint set after each diffusion
070   denoising update. We rely on off-the-shelf optimization routines, thereby providing a training and
071   hyperparameter-free approach to include multiple constraints. Additionally, CPS does not require
072   external models to enforce realism, as the key intuition in our approach is that the subsequent
073   denoising steps rectify the adverse effects of the projection steps toward sample quality. To this end,
074   our contributions in this paper are:
075   1. We present Constrained Posterior Sampling ((CPS), Fig. 1), a scalable diffusion sampling process
076      that generates realistic time series samples that belong to a constraint set. Without any additional
077      training, CPS can handle a large number of constraints without sacrificing sample quality (Fig. 3).
078   2. We provide a detailed theoretical analysis of the effect of modifying the traditional diffusion
079      sampling process with CPS. Additionally, we perform convergence analysis for well-studied
080      settings, such as convex constraint sets and Gaussian prior data distribution, to draw useful insights
081      for the practical implementation of CPS.
082
      3. Through extensive experiments on six diverse real-world and simulated datasets spanning finance,
         traffic, and environmental monitoring, we demonstrate that CPS outperforms state-of-the-art
083
         approaches (SOTA) on sample quality, similarity, and constraint adherence metrics (check Fig. 2).

179   Sampling from a probability distribution supported on a constraint set is essential in various engi-
180   neering fields, including material science and robotics. To address this, Frerix et al. (2020) introduce
181   Variational AutoEncoders (VAEs) with additional trainable layers to enforce linear inequality con-
182   straints. Liu et al. (2023) and Fishman et al. (2023a;b) modify the forward noising process and the
183   diffusion model training to satisfy the required constraints. Since these methods require additional
184   training, they are less scalable when adapting to new constraints. To overcome this limitation, Christo-
185
      pher et al. (2024) propose Projected Diffusion Models (PDMs), a training-free approach that projects
      the intermediate noisy latents of the reverse process (zT , . . . , z0 ) into the constraint set. Though
186
      training-free, this approach can impact sample quality and diversity, as detailed in Appendix G.
187
188   In the time series domain, Wang et al. (2024a;b) focus on generating constrained counterfactual
189   explanations for classification and forecasting by perturbing selected time stamps of a synthesized seed
190   sample. These approaches do not provide any mechanism to induce realism other than staying near the
191
      seed sample. Recently, Coletta et al. (2024) proposed three approaches - Loss-DiffTime, a training-
      based approach where constraint-specific samples are generated with constraints as conditional input
192
      to the generator, Guided DiffTime, which uses guidance gradients from differentiable constraint
193
      functions to guide the sample generation toward a constraint set, and Constrained Optimization
194   Problem (COP), which projects a seed sample to the constraint set while using the critic function
195   from any Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017) as a realism enforcer. Loss-DiffTime is not
196   scalable to new constraints without retraining, while Guided DiffTime and other guidance-based
197   approaches like Diffusion-TS (Yuan & Qiao, 2024) do not guarantee constraint satisfaction even for
198   convex constraint sets. We compare CPS against these approaches on many real-world datasets and
199   highlight our advantages.
200   Finally, constrained generation can also be viewed as controlling the outputs of a generative model,
201   which occurs in multiple formulations in the image domain, such as solving inverse problems (Rout
202   et al., 2023a;b; 2024a; Chung et al., 2024), personalization (Rout et al., 2024b; Ruiz et al., 2022),
203   text-to-image generation (Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022), and text-based image editing
204   (Kawar et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2023). We note that CPS (Sec. 3) can be viewed as a constraint

206   personalization through diffusions (Rout et al., 2024b). However, these works do not impose hard
207   constraints, as described in the case of OHLC charts in Sec. 1. We refer the reader to Appendix F for

343   4    E XPERIMENTS
344
345   This section describes the experimental procedure, including the wide range of datasets and metrics
346   used to evaluate CPS against the state-of-the-art constrained generation approaches.
347
      Datasets: We use real-world datasets from different domains, such as Stocks (Yoon et al., 2019), Air
348   Quality (Chen, 2019), and Traffic (Hogue, 2019). Specifically, we test the performance of CPS on
349   both conditional and unconditional variants of these datasets. We also evaluate our approach on a
350   simulated sinusoidal waveforms dataset to generate sinusoids with varying amplitudes, phases, and
351   frequencies specified as constraints.
352
      Our evaluation procedure is framed to test any approach for generating the maximum likelihood
353
      sample from a constraint set, such that the real time series samples from the constraint set were
354   never seen during training. To achieve this, from every sample in the test dataset, we first extract an
355   exhaustive set of features such that only one test sample exists per set of features. These features are
356   considered constraints, which we impose on the generative model.
357
      Constraints: We extract the following features to be used as constraints - mean, mean consecutive
358
      change, argmax, argmin, value at argmax, value at argmin, values at timestamps 1, 24, 48, 72, & 96.
359
      For the Stocks dataset, we additionally impose the natural OHLC constraint, i.e., the opening and
360   closing prices should be bounded by the highest and the lowest prices. Similarly, for the sinusoidal
361   waveforms dataset, we extract the locations and values of the peaks and valleys and the trend from a
362   peak to its adjacent valley. Note that these constraints can be written in the form Ax ≤ 0. Projection
363   to such constraint sets is easy and can be handled by numerous off-the-shelf solvers (Diamond &
364   Boyd, 2016; Virtanen et al., 2020). This allows us to analyze the effect of the sampling process
365   without worrying about the off-the-shelf solvers that influence the projection step. We provide a
366   budget of 0.01 for constraint violation.
367   Baselines: We compare against the Constrained Optimization Problem (COP) approach (Coletta
368   et al., 2024) and its fine-tuning variant, which is referred to as COP-FT. COP projects a random
369   sample from the training dataset to the required set of constraints, whereas COP-FT projects a
370   generated sample. Both these variants rely on a discriminator to enforce realism after perturbation.
371   We also compare our approach against Guided DiffTime (Coletta et al., 2024), a guidance-based
372   diffusion sampling approach. All baselines, except COP, utilize the same T IME W EAVER -CSDI
373   denoiser backbone (Narasimhan et al., 2024) for fair comparison. Additionally, we compare CPS
374
      against Projected Diffusion Models (PDM) (Christopher et al., 2024), Diffusion-TS (Yuan & Qiao,
      2024) (another guidance-based sampling approach), and Loss-DiffTime, the training-based approach
375
      from Coletta et al. (2024), in Appendix G.
376

468   How well does CPS generate realistic samples that belong to the constraint set? We argue about
469   the performance of CPS based on the sample quality and the constraint violation metrics in Table 1.
470   As the constraint sets used in our experiments are convex, both CPS and COP variants can almost
471   always ensure constraint satisfaction using off-the-shelf solvers. However, Guided DiffTime struggles
472   severely to generate samples that belong to the constraint set. This is clearly observed in the Stocks
473   dataset, where Guided DiffTime has an average constraint violation magnitude of 1128. With respect
474   to sample quality, we observe that the CPS provides the lowest FD and TSTR values.
475   Even though Guided DiffTime provides comparable TSTR values for some settings, we note that the
476   generated samples are very less likely to belong to the constraint set. Therefore, guidance gradients
477   alone are insufficient to drive the sample generation process to the constraint set. Similarly, there

479   conditional setting. Here, our key observation is that conditional generation provides a seed sample
480   for COP-FT that lies close to the constraint set. Therefore, projection does not degrade the sample
481   quality by a lot. However, the sample quality degradation due to projection is significant for COP,
482
      and it can be observed through very high values of J-FTSD in Table 1. Therefore, our key insight is
      that COP is influenced by the choice of initial seed.
483
484   Does CPS handle unnatural artifacts that typically occur due to the projection step? While
485   imposing constraints on the generation process, we note that even though Guided DiffTime generates
      a realistic sample, it fails to adhere to the constraints. On the other hand, COP variants adhere to

      constraints but generate samples with unnatural artifacts induced by the projection step. However,
487   our key intuition is that CPS circumvents such artifacts using the iterative projection and denoising
488   updates, where the adverse effects of the projection step are nullified by the subsequent denoising steps.
489   The difference between the baseline approaches and CPS is significantly pronounced specifically in
490   the waveforms dataset (check Fig. 6). The stark contrast between the generated sinusoid from CPS
491   and other baselines is empirically supported with a 100× reduction in the FD value.
492
      How does CPS perform in comparison with baselines for a large number of constraints? We
493   consider the Stocks dataset as the OHLC condition introduces more than 400 constraints. With a large
494   number of constraints, the feasible set size reduces, and this necessitates the requirement of accurate
495   guidance to generate samples from such constraint sets. In such settings, Guided DiffTime performs
496   poorly. This can be attributed to the interaction between gradients for each constraint violation. The
497   combination of these gradients, if not scaled appropriately, leads to poor guidance. Additionally,
498   finding the correct set of guidance weights is practically very hard for a large number of constraints.
499   Similarly, projection to small constraint sets affects the sample quality of COP variants, specifically
500   when the initial seed is far away from the constraint set. While the baselines suffer with an increasing
501
      number of constraints, CPS gets rid of these issues by alternating projection and denoising updates.
      We observe this through the qualitative example from the Stocks datasets in Fig. 3. Quantitatively, we
502
      observe 14.5% improvement in the TSTR and 67% improvement in the FD metric when compared
503
      against the best-performing baseline.
504
505   Does CPS track the real test samples that adhere to the same set of constraints? For a large
506   number of constraints or a small constraint set, we expect the generated samples that satisfy the
507
      constraints to have a high degree of similarity with the real test samples from which we extract the
      constraints. To this end, we denote tracking real test samples as the property to have better similarity
508
      scores with the real sample as the number of constraints increases. In Fig. 5, we note that CPS
509
      outperforms all baselines in the DTW metric for any number of constraints, thereby showing higher
510   similarity with the real test samples. Note that out of all approaches, CPS has the best reduction in
511   the DTW scores as the number of constraints increases. Simultaneously, we also note that the sample
512   quality is unaffected or even improves for CPS with increasing constraints (lower FD scores). We
513   observe that CPS’s performance is consistent across multiple real-world datasets, with significant
514   improvements in the DTW values of around 33% for Air Quality, 77% for Stocks, and 22% for the
515   Traffic dataset with respect to the best-performing baseline.
516   We refer the reader to Appendix G, where we show that CPS outperforms PDM (Christopher et al.,
517   2024) on sample quality metrics. Additionally, we show that CPS beats Diffusion-TS (Yuan & Qiao,
518   2024) and Loss DiffTime (Coletta et al., 2024) on constraint violation metrics.
519
      Limitations. Although CPS outperforms all the compared baselines in standard evaluation metrics,
520
      we note that the projection step (line 5) in Algorithm 1 can be time-consuming for some applications.
521
      This increases the overall sampling time of CPS, the trade-off being superior performance. In time-
522   critical applications, the sampling time can be reduced further by leveraging higher order moments
523   and different initialization schemes (Rout et al., 2024a). Additionally, the projection step is not
524   necessary after every denoising step and can be adapted to the constraint violation magnitude.

      5    C ONCLUSION
528
      We proposed Constrained Posterior Sampling – a novel training-free approach for constrained time
529
      series generation. CPS is designed such that it exploits off-the-shelf optimization routines to perform
530
      a projection step towards the constraint set after every denoising step. Through an array of sample
531   quality and constraint violation metrics, we empirically show that CPS outperforms the state-of-the-art
532   baselines in generating realistic samples that belong to a constraint set.
533
534
      Future work. We aim to apply our approach for constrained trajectory generation in the robotics
      domain with dynamic constraints typically modeled by neural networks. Additionally, constrained
535
      time series generation readily applies to style transfer applications. Hence, we plan on extending
536
      the current work to perform style transfer from one time series to another by perturbing statistical
537   features.

       This section will explain all the details about the baseline implementations. Specifically, we use
1487   two baselines - Constrained Optimization Problem (COP) and Guided DiffTime. We note that both
1488   approaches were proposed in (Coletta et al., 2024). However, the implementation of these approaches
1489   is not publicly available. Based on the details provided in (Coletta et al., 2024), we have implemented
1490   the baselines for comparison against CPS.
1491
1492   D.3.1    C ONSTRAINED O PTIMIZATION P ROBLEM I MPLEMENTATION
1493
1494   The Constrained Optimization Problem, COP, has two variants. These are referred to as COP and
1495
       COP-FineTuning, respectively. In COP, we perturb a randomly selected sample from the training
       and validation datasets. In COP-FineTuning, we perturb the sample generated from the T IME
1496
       W EAVER -CSDI diffusion model.
1497
1498   Note that (Coletta et al., 2024) suggests to extract statistical features to be imposed as distributional
1499   constraints. For example, Coletta et al. (2024) suggests extracting autocorrelation features for the
1500   stocks dataset. However, since it is practically impossible to list all the statistical features for each
1501
       dataset to obtain the distributional constraints, Coletta et al. (2024) suggests the use of the critic
       function from a Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017). The details of the GAN training are
1502
       summarized below.
1503
1504   COP has two objectives - maximize the l2 distance from a randomly selected sample from the training
1505   and maximize the critic value from a Wasserstein GAN.
1506   Similarly, COP FineTuning has two objectives - minimize the l2 distance from a generated sample
1507   and maximize the critic value from a Wasserstein GAN.
1508
       We optimize for these objectives while ensuring constraint satisfaction.
1509
1510   As suggested in (Coletta et al., 2024), we use the SLSQP solver from SciPy Virtanen et al. (2020).
1511   Unlike (Coletta et al., 2024), which performs piecewise optimization, we note that all the constraints
       used in our work are global. Therefore, piecewise optimization is very suboptimal. For example, it is


                                                          28
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1512
       suboptimal to break a time series into chunks and perform optimization for each piece when the goal
1513   is to generate a sample with a specific mean value. This is also pointed out in (Coletta et al., 2024).
1514   Therefore, we perform COP for the whole time series at once. We consider two budgets - 0.005 and
1515   0.01. This is similar to Coletta et al. (2024). However, unlike their approach, we stop with 0.01 as the
1516   allowable constraint violation in our case is 0.01 for all methods.
1517
       We used a weight of 0.1 for the critic’s objective. We noticed that for different values (1.0,0.1,0.01)
1518
       of this weight, there was very little change in the DTW and the SSIM metrics.
1519
1520
       D.3.2       C RITIC F UNCTION I MPLEMENTATION
1521
1522   Coletta et al. (2024) suggest using the critic function in a Wasserstein GAN Arjovsky et al. (2017)
1523   to enforce realism in the COP approach. Therefore, we used the WaveGAN Donahue et al. (2018)
1524   implementation from Alcaraz & Strodthoff (2023). The implementation from Alcaraz & Strodthoff
1525   (2023) has the gradient penalty loss, an improved training procedure to enforce the required Lipschitz
1526
       continuity for the critic function. Additionally, the WaveGAN training with gradient penalty has
       been implemented Alcaraz & Strodthoff (2023) for generating time series samples for the ECG
1527
       domain. Therefore, we use their implementation to obtain the critic function for the COP baseline.
1528
       The number of parameters is adjusted such that the diffusion model and the GAN model have a
1529   comparable number of parameters.
1530
1531   Similar to the diffusion model, we used the same architecture and training hyperparameters for all
1532
       the datasets and experimental settings. Specifically, we trained the WaveGAN model with a learning
       rate of 10−4 for all the datasets. The input to the generator is a 48-dimensional random vector.
1533
       Additionally, we ensured that the total number of parameters was equally distributed between the
1534
       generator and the discriminator to prevent either of the models from overpowering the other.

1571   and imputation (Tashiro et al., 2021; Alcaraz & Strodthoff, 2022; Yuan & Qiao, 2024) have been
1572   addressed using conditional DMs as well as guidance-based approaches (Li et al., 2023; Yuan &

1578   F.2   C ONSTRAINED S AMPLE G ENERATION
1579
       In many engineering applications, the sample domain can be restricted to certain manifolds. Such
1580
       problem settings demand any generative modeling approach to synthesize samples that adhere to the
1581   constraints that define the manifold. Frerix et al. (2020) propose Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)
1582   with additional constraint layers added to the neural network architecture to impose linear inequality
1583   constraints of the form Ax ≤ 0. Liu et al. (2023) and Fishman et al. (2023a;b) propose modifications
1584   to the denoising diffusion training process to restrict the generation process to the required constraint
1585   sets. More specifically, Liu et al. (2023) introduce Mirror Diffusion Models (MDMs) for convex
1586   constraint sets. MDMs are standard denoising DMs trained in the dual or the mirror space of the
1587   constraint set. Therefore, by generating in the mirror space and transforming back to the constraint set,
1588   we can generate samples from the required constraint set. Fishman et al. (2023a;b) propose a modified
1589
       forward noising process, such that the intermediate noisy latents of the forward process always adhere
       to the constraint set. Additionally, these works introduce constraint-specific training modifications,
1590
       such as clipping the score function to zero at the constraint boundaries. Overall, the constraint-specific
1591
       training approach suffers from the ability to scale to new constraint sets. Additionally, the constrained
1592   time series generation problem does not assume the presence of a constrained manifold from which
1593   samples need to be generated. However, the objective is to sample from arbitrary constraint sets
1594   defined by combinations of multiple constraints such as mean, argmax, etc.
1595
       Christopher et al. (2024) propose Projected Diffusion Models (PDMs), a training-free approach
1596
       for constrained generation, which involves solving a constrained optimization problem after every
1597
       denoising step. The constrained optimization step projects the intermediate noisy latents of the
1598   reverse sampling process to the constraint set. This is similar to our approach, with a key difference
1599   that is highlighted in Appendix G. We compare CPS against PDM and explain the relative advantages
1600   of our approach in Appendix G.
1601
       Finally, Yuan & Qiao (2024) propose a controlled time series generation approach that is specifically
1602
       designed for time series imputation. In Appendix G, we modify this approach for constrained time
1603
       series generation and compare it against CPS.

1606   G     E XTENDED BASELINE C OMPARISONS
1607
1608   In this section, we provide quantitative comparisons between CPS and other approaches, such as
1609   PDM (Christopher et al., 2024) and Diffusion-TS (Yuan & Qiao, 2024).
1610   Note that the main difference between PDM and CPS is the projection step. In PDM, the noisy latent
1611   corresponding to the step t − 1, zt−1 , is obtained from the noisy latent corresponding to the step
1612   t, zt , using Eq. 2. Consequently, zt−1 is projected to the constraint set by solving a constrained
1613   optimization problem. This process is repeated for T denoising steps.
1614   In CPS, we compute the posterior mean estimate ẑ0 (zt ; ϵθ ) from zt . Then, we transform ẑ0 (zt ; ϵθ ) to
1615   the projected posterior mean estimate ẑ0,pr (zt ; ϵθ ) using an unconstrained optimization step (line 5,
1616   Algorithm 1). Consequently, we obtain zt−1 from zt and ẑ0,pr (zt ; ϵθ ) using Eq. 2. Table 3 shows
1617   the comparison between PDM and CPS for all the real-world datasets used in our experiments. We
1618   observe that both approaches provide constraint satisfaction for convex constraints. However, CPS
1619   outperforms PDM in terms of sample quality and diversity metrics. Now, we explain the reasons for
       the superior performance of CPS over PDM.


                                                           30
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1620
               • The constraint set is defined for the clean samples and not the intermediate noisy latents
1621             of a denoising process. As the goal is to generate constrained time series samples, it is
1622             sufficient if the generated sample z0 belongs to the constraint set. However, PDM assumes
1623             the constraint set for clean samples to be the same for noisy intermediate latents. By
1624             forcing the latents to satisfy the same constraint as z0 , PDM eliminates most sample paths
1625             (zT , . . . , z0 ) where z0 alone eventually satisfies the required constraint. This results in poor
1626             sample diversity. CPS eliminates this problem by projecting the posterior mean estimate
1627             ẑ0 (zt ; ϵθ ) and not the noisy intermediate latents. Recall that ẑ0 (zt ; ϵθ ) is the expected clean
1628             sample with a similar noise level as the constraint set. Furthermore, the projected posterior
1629
                 mean estimate ẑ0,pr (zt ; ϵθ ) is transformed to zt−1 using a non-markovian forward noising
                 process. This effectively allows for sample paths where the generated sample z0 alone
1630
                 satisfies the required constraint, and the intermediate noisy latents can be flexible.
1631
               • PDM projection step pushes zt−1 off the noise manifold for t − 1. In PDM, the projection
1632             step, when applied directly to the noisy latent zt−1 , pushes it out of the noise manifold
1633             corresponding to the diffusion step t − 1. Consequently, a pre-trained denoiser struggles
1634             to accurately denoise the projected zt−1 as it would be out of the training domain of the
1635             denoiser. This effect is significantly reduced in CPS because our approach does not
1636             project zt−1 . Instead, CPS projects the expected clean sample ẑ0 (zt ; ϵθ ). Consequently,
1637             the projected posterior mean estimate ẑ0,pr (zt ; ϵθ ) is transformed into zt−1 by using a non-
1638             markovian forward noising process (Eq. 2). This ensures that zt−1 stays very close to the
1639             noise manifold corresponding to the diffusion step t − 1. Therefore, a pre-trained denoiser
1640
                 can denoise zt−1 more accurately in our approach. This ultimately preserves the generated
                 sample quality.
