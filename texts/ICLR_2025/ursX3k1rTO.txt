009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose Wyckoff Transformer, a generative model for materials conditioned
012            on space group symmetry. Most real–world inorganic materials have internal sym-
013            metry beyond lattice translation. Symmetry rules that atoms obey play a funda-
014            mental role in determining the physical, chemical, and electronic properties of
015            crystals. These symmetries determine stability, and influence key material struc-
016
               tural and functional properties such as electrical and thermal conductivity, optical
               and polarization behavior, and mechanical strength. And yet, despite the recent
017
               advancements, state–of–the–art diffusion models struggle to generate highly sym-
018
               metric crystals. We use Wyckoff positions as the basis for an elegant, compressed,
019            and discrete structure representation. To model the distribution we develop a
020            permutation–invariant autoregressive model based on Transformer and absence
021            of positional encoding. Our experiments demonstrate that Wyckoff Transformer
022            has the best performance in generating novel diverse stable structures conditioned
023            on the symmetry space group, while also having competitive metric values when
024            compared to model not conditioned on symmetry. We also show that it is compet-
025            itive in predicting formation energy, band gap, mechanical properties, and thermal
026            conductivity.

028   1     I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Space of all possible combinations of atoms forming periodic structures is intractably large. It is not
031   possible to screen it fully, even with a fast machine learning algorithm. Practical materials, however,
032   occupy only a small part of it. Firstly, they must correspond to an energy minimum. Secondly,
033   occupying an energy minimum is not sufficient to establish if the material is synthesizable or indeed
034   experimentally stable. Having a generative model that outputs a priori stable materials is a step
035   towards speeding up automated material design by orders of magnitude.
036
037   1.1   S PACE GROUPS AND W YCKOFF POSITIONS
038
039
      A crystal structure can be systematically described through its lattice and atomic basis. The lattice
      provides a repeating geometric framework, defined as an infinite periodic arrangement of points in
040
      space. Based on interactions between the constituent electrons and nuclei, atoms rearrange into such
041
      a lattice and, therefore, follow a finite set of symmetries: the group of all such symmetry operations
042   that uniquely define the periodic arrangement is called the space group of the crystal. These arrange-
043   ments in a crystal are governed by a finite set of symmetry operations, such as rotations, reflections,
044   inversions, and translations. These operations combine to form the 230 distinct space groups, which
045   serve as a comprehensive classification system for all possible crystal symmetries in three dimen-
046   sions. Each space group defines the unique symmetry properties of a crystal structure, defining the
047   allowable positions for atoms within the unit cell. This ensures that every crystal possesses at least
048   the simplest level of symmetry, referred to as P1 symmetry, which involves only translational sym-
049   metry. The atomic basis specifies the arrangement of atoms associated with each lattice point, thus
050
      defining the overall crystal structure.
051   Importantly, most known crystals have internal symmetry, see figure 1. Those symmetries are not
052   merely a mathematical observation; optical, electrical, magnetic, structural and other properties are
053   determined by symmetry, as shown by Malgrange et al. (2014); Yang et al. (2005), as well as our
      results in section 3.2.

      Within a given space group, a subgroup forms the site symmetry, referring to the set of symmetry
071
      operations that leave a specific point in the crystal invariant. These operations describe the local
072   symmetrical environment, such as mirror, screw axis, or inversions centered on that a given region.
073   Higher site symmetry is in regions where multiple symmetry elements intersect, while those with
074   lower site symmetry include only one symmetry operation. Taking space group 225 Fm-3m as an
075   example, site symmetry subgroup m-3m represents a highly symmetric environment like the center
076   of a cubic unit cell, where multiple symmetry elements intersect, including mirror planes and a 3–
077   fold rotoinversion axis. In contrast, another lower site symmetry subgroup .3m corresponds to a less
078   symmetric environment with only a 3–fold rotation axis and a mirror plane.
079   These site symmetry points, classified by their symmetry properties, are grouped into Wyckoff posi-
080   tions (WPs) (Wyckoff, 1922). Mathematically, a WP encompasses all points whose site symmetry
081   groups are conjugate subgroups of the full space group Kantorovich (2004). Each WP is character-
082   ized by two key attributes:
083
084         1. Site symmetry
085         2. Symmetry equivalence: two different Wyckoff positions in the same space group can share
086            the same site symmetry but may still be symmetry equivalent. This equivalence arises
087            when the Wyckoff positions can be mapped onto each other using higher–order symmetry
088            operations, such as those defined by the Euclidean normalizer of the space group. These
089
               symmetry–equivalent WPs form the basis for enumeration and augmentation in the subse-
               quent sections of this work.
090
091   WPs for a given space group are commonly enumerated by Latin letters in the order of multiplicity,
092   the number of equivalent atomic positions in a crystal structure that are related by the symmetry
093   operations of the space group. WPs are denoted by a combination of the multiplicity value and the
094   letter, e. g. 2a. The number of distinct WPs in a space group is finite, ranging from a single WP
095   in the simplest symmetry group P1 to as many as 27 in the most complex space groups. These
096   classifications enable the description of not only discrete points but also more complex geometric
097   features. For example, some Wyckoff positions represent 1D lines, 2D planes, or even open 3D
098
      regions within the unit cell, depending on the symmetry constraints. This flexibility underscores
      the utility of Wyckoff positions in describing diverse crystallographic arrangements. By introduc-
099
      ing these fundamental concepts – lattice, atomic basis, space groups, site symmetry, and Wyckoff
100
      positions – this framework provides a foundation for understanding crystal structures. See also
101   Appendix A for an illustration.

145   1.3   R ELATED WORK
146
147
      Crystal generation is a burgeoning field, with most state–of–the–art models using a differentiable
      non–invertible SO(3) invariant representation constructed from atom coordinates, such as a graph
148
      neural networks. Then they use diffusion or flow matching to solve the generation problem (Jiao
149
      et al., 2024a;b; Cao et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Zeni et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2021; Klipfel et al.,
150   2023; Luo et al., 2024; Sinha et al., 2024). Our approach uses discrete Wyckoff space, and fast au-
151   toregressive sampling, as compared to gradual refinement in the aforementioned works. WyFormer
152   complements them naturally by providing symmetry constraints and/or initial structure approxima-
153   tion – the synergy with the most suitable partner, DiffCSP++, we evaluate thoroughly.
154
      Wyckoff positions and machine learning. The concept of Wyckoff positions was originally pub-
155
      lished more than a 100 years ago (Wyckoff, 1922), which laid the groundwork for understanding
156   equivalent positions in space groups, serving as a precursor to the International Tables for Crystal-
157   lography. Given their elegant representation, naturally, in modern times WPs have found their way
158   into machine learning. The main limiting factor in their adoption was the ability of machine learn-
159   ing algorithms to handle discrete structured data which is formed by WPs. WP–based representation
160   was used for property prediction (Goodall et al., 2020; Jain & Bligaard, 2018; Möller et al., 2018;
161   Goodall et al., 2022), and recently for generative models. Our work is inspired by Zhu et al. (2024),
      the first such model. It uses a VAE over one–hot–encoded information about WPs, as opposed our


                                                                3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Transformer encoder, a generally superior architecture for categorical data. AI4Science et al. (2023)
163   use GFlowNet Bengio et al. (2023) to sample space group and chemical composition, but not the
164   full Wyckoff representation. Finally, a concurrent work by Cao et al. (2024) independently explores
165   a Transformer–based approach similar to ours.
166
      The main difference between our and all other approaches, that are based on Wyckoff positions, is
167
      that they use Wyckoff letters as the representation. Wyckoff letter definitions depends on the space
168
      group, unlike site symmetry, leading to data fragmentation. Zhu et al. (2024); Cao et al. (2024)
169   also don’t take into account dependency of the Wyckoff letters on the arbitrary choice of the coset
170   representative of the space group Euclidean normalizer. Finally, Cao et al. (2024) use positional
171   encoding to establish the relationship between the chemical elements and Wyckoff positions they
172   occupy, while we combine them in one token.

      2.1   T OKENIZATION
177
178   Our work is based on the inductive bias that for stable materials space group symmetry and Wyckoff
179   sites almost completely define the structure – more than 98% of the materials in MP–20 Xie et al.
180   (2021) and MPTS–52 Baird et al. (2024) datasets, which tother contain almost all experimentally
181   stable structures from the Materials Project Jain et al. (2013), have unique Wyckoff representations.
182   Therefore is it safe to assume that for almost any Wyckoff representation there is either none, or
183   just one stable material conforming to it. Symmetry captured by this discrete part is sufficient to
184   determine properties of a material, such as piezoelectricity via non–centrosymmetry; direct/indirect
185   band gap via positions of the valence/conduction bands in the Brillouin Zone, while the fractional
186
      coordinates can be linked to the magnitude of that property. We additionally prove this assumption
      by various predicting material properties, see section 3.2. Given a Wyckoff representation that
187
      reflects the lattice symmetry, coordinates can be determined as discussed in section 2.4.
188
189   We represent each structure as a set of tokens, as shown in figure 3. The first token contains the space
190   group, the others chemical elements and WPs. We encode a WP as a tuple containing site symmetry
191   and so-called enumeration. Several WPs can have the same site symmetry. To differentiate those
192
      WPs we enumerate them separately within each space group and site symmetry according to the
      conventional WP order Aroyo et al. (2006). For example, in space group 225 present in figure 3 WP
193
      4a is encoded as (m-3m, 0), 4b as (m-3m, 1), and 8c as (-43m, 0). The purpose of this
194
      encoding is to take advantage of the fact that, unlike Wyckoff letters, site symmetry definition is uni-
195   versal across different space groups. We also develop a physics–based description of enumerations
196   using spherical harmonics, it is discussed in details in Appendix B.

210   2.2   M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
211
212
      Elements, site symmetries, and enumeration are each embedded with a simple lookup table with
      trainable weights, the embeddings are concatenated; then we apply a linear layer. The reason is that
213
      in multi head attention different heads look at continuous blocks of the input vector.
214
215   Since our model is conditioned on space group, preventing data fragmentation is of utmost impor-
      tance. To this end, space group is not encoded just as a categorical variable. Similarly to Bengio


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      et al. (2023) we use pyXtal to get one–hot–encoded 15 × 10 matrix that represents symmetry ele-
217   ments on each axis for each space group, flatten it, discard the positions that do not vary across the
218   dataset and use the resulting vector as the space group embedding. Then we apply a linear layer, so
219   the representation becomes learnable – but still transferable between space groups.
220
      Token sequences are used as an input for a Transformer encoder Vaswani (2017); Devlin (2018).
221
      Wyckoff representation is permutation–invariant, so is Transformer; we don’t use positional encod-
222
      ing, making the model formally permutation–invariant with the respect to the input.
223
224   De novo generation We use enumerations representation. We additionally add a STOP token
225   to each structure. To represent states where some parts of token are known and others are not,
226   we replace those values with MASK. We also add a fully–connected neural network for each part
227   of the token that we want to predict, three in total. To get the prediction, we take the output of
228   Transformer encoder on the token containing MASK value(s), concatenate it with a one–hot vector
229   encoding presence in the input sequence of each possible value for this token part, and use it as the
230   input for the corresponding fully–connected network.
231
232   Property prediction We use spherical harmonics representation. We take the average of the
233
      Transformer encoder outputs tokens, excluding the token corresponding to the space group, compute
      a weighted average with weights being equal to the multiplicities of WPs, and use the result as input
234
      for a fully–connected neural network that outputs a scalar predicted value.
235
236
      2.3   T RAINING
237
238   Following approach by Wang et al. (2023); Abramson et al. (2024), we use a simple architecture
239   and do no strictly enforce invariance with the respect to the choice of the coset representative of the
240   space group affine normalizer, but rather leave it as a training goal by picking a randomly selected
241   equivalent representation at every training epoch. It is especially viable because of the low number
242   of variants; in MP–20 (Xie et al., 2021) dataset for 96% structures there are less than 10.
243
244   De novo generation We train the model to predict next part of a token in a cascade fashion: first
245   the chemical element, then, conditioned on it, site symmetry and, finally, enumeration. On each
246
      training iteration we randomly sample known sequence length and the part of the cascade to predict;
      place MASK tokens as necessary, input the known parts of the sequences into the model, compute
247
      cross–entropy loss between the predicted scores and the target.
248
249   Unlike Transformer itself, auto–regressive generation is not permutation–invariant. The number of
250   WPs is small, the average in MP-20 is just 3.0; this allows us to again follow the philosophy of
251   Wang et al. (2023); Abramson et al. (2024) and train the model to be invariant with augmentation
252
      by shuffling the order of every Wyckoff representation at every training epoch. Moreover, we use
      multi–class loss when training to predict the fist cascade part, chemical element, further reducing
253
      learning complexity.
254
255   On MP–20 model is trained for 9 × 105 epochs using SGD optimizer without batching; due to
256   the efficiency of the representation gradient backpropagation for the entire dataset fits into GPU
257   memory. We use the loss on the validation dataset for early stopping, learning rate scheduling, and
258
      manual hyperparameter tuning.
259
260
      Property prediction The model is trained using MSE loss with batch size 500, and Adam opti-
      mizer. For both MP–20 and AFLOW training takes around 5k epochs.

      3.1.2    M ETRICS
281
282   Structure property similarity metrics Coverage and Property EMD (Wasserstein) distance, have
283   been proposed as a low–cost proxy metric for de novo structure generation by Xie et al. (2021) and
284   then followed by most of the subsequent work.
285
286   Validity Xie et al. (2021) proposed verifying crystal feasibility according to two criteria:
287
              • Structural validity means that no two atoms are closer than 0.5Å. All structures in MP–20
288
                and almost all structures produced by state–of–the–art model fulfill it.
289
              • Compositional validity means having neutral charge (Davies et al., 2019). Only 90%
290
                of MP–20 structures pass this test meaning that nonconforming structures are physically
291
                possible if somewhat rare.
292
293
      Novelty and uniqueness The purpose of de novo generation is to obtain new materials. Generat-
294   ing materials that already exist in the training dataset increases the model performance according to
295   structure stability and similarity metrics, but such structures are useless for material design and just
296   increase the gap between the proxy metrics and the model fitness for its purpose. Therefore we ex-
297   clude generated materials that are not novel and unique from metric computation. On a deeper level,
298   generative models for materials are subject to exploration/exploitation trade–off: the more physi-
299   cally similar are the sampled materials to the training dataset, the more likely they are stable and
300   distributed similar to the data, but the less useful they are for the purpose of material design. From
301   a purely machine learning point of view, novelty percentage serves a proxy metric for overfitting.
302   Stability is important as it determines whether the material, in fact, exists under normal conditions.
303   It is estimated by computing energy above convex hull, and comparing it to a threshold Ehull <
304   0.08 eV, same as used during construction of MP–20 dataset. Then we compute S.U.N. Zeni et al.
305   (2024) – the fraction of stable unique novel structures.
306   Due to DFT computational costs, we use CHGNet (Deng et al., 2023) for stability estimation of
307   the generated structures, and then compute DFT for a manageable sample from the novel structures
308   generated by the strongest models. Materials Project (Jain et al., 2013) is the source of the structures
309   for the hull; we computed CHGNet predicted energies for it to use as references.
310
      Symmetry of the structures has paramount physical importance. Controlling symmetries also leads
311
      to control over physical, electronic, and mechanical behavior, which is desirable in property–directed
312   inverse design of materials. For example, in electronic materials, higher symmetry can improve car-
313   rier mobility and uniformity in electronic band structure, enhancing performance in applications
314   such as semiconductors or optoelectronics. Furthermore, high–symmetry structures often exhibit
315   isotropic properties, meaning their behaviors are the same in all directions, making them more ver-
316   satile for industrial use. From a computational perspective, for a fixed set of atoms that constitute
317   a crystal, enforcing symmetries (beyond the basic P1 translation symmetry) allows for computing
318   permutations to search for useful materials while maintaining a focus on practical, synthesizable
319   crystal structures.
320   This combination of stability, desirable properties, and computational efficiency makes symmetry
321   consideration in crystals especially valuable in generative models for materials discovery. While
322   higher symmetry is more tractable to compute, experimental realization could require external en-
323   ergy inputs (higher temperatures and pressures: think diamond vs graphite); most databases com-
      puted with DFT today are only at 0K and hence do not include this degree of freedom. Keeping

326
327
      P1 is the percentage of the structures that have symmetry group P1. In MP–20 the corresponding
328
      number is just 1.7%, and yet more than a third of the structures generated by some state–of–the–art

331   require finite–temperature calculations and/or improved methodologies.
332
333   Novel Unique Templates (#) is the number of the novel unique element–agnostic Wyckoff rep-
334   resentations (section 2.1) in the generated sample. Element-agnostic means that we remove the

336   in figure 3, it will be as follows:{[(X, (m-3m, 0)), (X, (m-3m, 1)), (X, (-43m,

341
      the ability of a model to generate new templates allows it generate more structures before starting to

      C    S TRUCTURE GENERATION DETAILS
747
      The process of obtaining crystal structures from Wyckoff representations using PyXtal Fredericks
748
      et al. (2021) begins by specifying a space group and defining WPs. PyXtal allows users to input
749
      atomic species, stoichiometry, and symmetry preferences. Based on these parameters, PyXtal gen-
750   erates a random crystal structure that respects the symmetry requirements of the space group. Once
751   the initial structure is generated, we then perform energy relaxation using CHGNet. CHGNet is a
752   neural network–based model designed to predict atomic forces and energies, significantly speeding
753   up calculations that would traditionally require density functional theory (DFT). We repeat the pro-
754   cess for six random initializations and pick the structure with the lowest energy. Energy relaxation
755   involves optimizing the atomic positions to reach a minimum energy configuration, which represents
      the most stable form of the material. CHGNet, trained on vast DFT datasets, can efficiently relax


                                                               14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      crystal structures by adjusting atomic positions to reduce the total energy. This approach ensures that
757   the final structure is not only symmetrical but also physically realistic in terms of energy stability.

833   F   E NERGY ABOVE HULL CALCULATIONS
834
835   To obtain the Ehull , we firstly constructed the reference convex hull data by querying all 153235
836   structures from the Materials Project (MP), and then using CHGNet (Deng et al., 2023) with using
837   CrySPR interface (Nong et al., 2024) to do structure relaxations for all MP structures by relaxing
838   both lattice cells and atomic positions (vc-relax), which renders 153,226 valid entries for relaxed
839   structures and energies; secondly, for each 1,000 generated structures from each generative model,
840   we followed the same vc-relax procedure to get the relaxed structures and energies; finally, using
841   the pymatgen.analysis.phase diagram sub–module the Ehull for each entry of generated
842   structure was computed by referencing to the MP convex hull, Ehull = max{∆Ei }, where ∆Ei is
843
      the decomposition energy of any possible path for a structure decomposing into the reference convex
      hull.
844

846   G    DFT DETAILS
847
848   All DFT structure relaxations were performed using the Vienna ab-initio simulation package (VASP)
849   with the plane-wave basis set. Kresse & Furthmüller (1996) The electron-ion interaction is described
850   by the projector augmented wave (PAW) pseudo-potentials. Kresse & Joubert (1999) The pseudo-
851   potentials recommended by the VASP team are used. The exchange-correlation of valence electrons
852   is treated with the Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) functional within the generalized gradient ap-
853   proximation (GGA). Perdew et al. (1996) The cutoff for kinetic energy of plane waves was set to
                                                                                      −1
854   520 eV. Convergence thresholds of 10−8 eV for total energy and 10−4 eV Å atom−1 for force
855   were set. The Monkhorst-Pack scheme of k-points sampling in the Brillouin zone with spacing of
              −1
856   0.15 Å is used Monkhorst & Pack (1976), in which the Γ point is included. The Dudarev et al.
857   simplified DFT+U scheme Dudarev et al. (1998) was adopted for the oxides and fluorides that con-
858   tain one or more of the following transition metals: Co (3.32 eV), Cr (3.7 eV), Fe (5.3 eV), Mn (3.9
859   eV), Mo (4.38 eV), Ni (6.2 eV), V (3.25 eV), W (6.2 eV), consistent with the MP. Spin-polarized
860
      relaxations initialized with ferromagnetic, high-spin valence configurations were also performed to
      check if there is any magnetic atom with magnetism ≥ 0.15 µB .
861
862   The MP convex hull (v2023.11.1) was used as the reference hull. To do so comparably, addi-
863   tional DFT relaxations and self-consistent field (SCF) calculations using the VASP settings from
      MPRelaxSet and MPStaticSet in pymatgen were further performed based on the previously

864
      relaxed structures. The raw total energies of SCF calculations using the MPStaticSet are then
865   corrected using the correction scheme of MaterialsProject2020Compatibility before
866   putting into the PhaseDiagram to obtain the DFT Ehull . What should be emphasized here

1118   N    S AMPLING HARMONIC – ENCODED WP S
1119
1120   WP harmonic representation is a real–valued vector. But for each space group it can only take up
1121   to 8 possible values, so learning the full distribution of such vectors is not necessary. Therefore, we
1122   propose the following procedure:
1123
1124
             1. Take the harmonic representations of all the WPs in all space group
1125         2. Use K–means clustering to find 8 cluster centers.
1126
             3. Separately for each space group, assign harmonic labels to each enumeration:
1127
1128             (a) Compute the Euclidean distances between all cluster centers and all WPs in the SG
1129             (b) Choose the smallest distance. Assign the WP to the corresponding cluster, remove
1130                 WP and the cluster center from consideration.
1131             (c) Repeat until all WPs are assigned
1132
1133   This way all we obtain a discrete prediction target with one–to–one mapping with enumerations, but
       where physically–similar values are grouped together.
