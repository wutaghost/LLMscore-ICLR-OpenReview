009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                The advent of large Vision-Language Models (VLMs) has significantly advanced
012                multimodal understanding, enabling more sophisticated and accurate integration
013                of visual and textual information across various tasks, including image and video
014                captioning, visual question answering, and cross-modal retrieval. Despite VLMs’
015                superior capabilities, researchers lack a comprehensive understanding of their
016                compositionality – the ability to understand and produce novel combinations of
017                known visual and textual components. Prior benchmarks provide only a relatively
018
                   rough compositionality evaluation from the perspectives of objects, relations, and
                   attributes while neglecting deeper reasoning about object interactions, counting,
019
                   and complex compositions. However, compositionality is a critical ability that
020
                   facilitates coherent reasoning and understanding across modalities for VLMs. To
021                address this limitation, we propose MMC OMPOSITION, a novel human-annotated
022                benchmark for comprehensively and accurately evaluating VLMs’ compositionality.
023                Our proposed benchmark serves as a complement to these earlier works. With
024                MMC OMPOSITION, we can quantify and explore the compositionality of the
025                mainstream VLMs. Surprisingly, we find GPT-4o’s compositionality inferior to the
026                best open-source model, and we analyze the underlying reasons. Our experimental
027                analysis reveals the limitations of VLMs in fine-grained compositional perception
028                and reasoning, and points to areas for improvement in VLM design and training. 1

031
032
      Pre-trained vision-language models, such as GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023), LLaVA (Liu et al.,
033
      2024b), InternVL (Chen et al., 2024b), and VILA (Lin et al., 2024), have demonstrated impressive
034   capabilities in complex reasoning, and have achieved remarkable results in various vision-language
035   (VL) tasks. Despite these advancements, contemporary state-of-the-art VLMs still struggle with
036   understanding fine-grained multimodal compositional information (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022; Thrush
037   et al., 2022). For instance, VLMs often fail at counting objects in images, especially when the objects
038   are mixed with other items or occluded, while humans can handle this task easily. This reveals
039   a compositionality gap between humans and models. However, compositionality is recognized as
040   a core capability for VLMs (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022), referring to the ability to understand and
041   produce a potentially infinite number of novel combinations of known visual and textual components,
042
      i.e., to make “infinite use of finite means” (Chomsky, 2014). Compositionality is essential for
      tackling challenging questions in image captioning, visual question answering (VQA), and scene
043
      understanding, where complex interactions between objects and attributes need to be communicated
044
      in natural language.

152   2.1       VLM E VALUATION B ENCHMARKS
153
      The advent of large-scale VLMs has led to the development of numerous benchmarks designed to
154
      evaluate various model capabilities. Among the most commonly evaluated are image captioning (Lin
155
      et al.; Onoe et al., 2024; Masry et al., 2022), which tests a VLM’s ability to generate natural
156   language descriptions of images; VQA (Antol et al., 2015; Marino et al., 2019; Mathew et al.,
157   2020), which assesses the model’s capacity to answer image-based questions by integrating visual
158   perception with language understanding or external knowledge; and Visual Reasoning (Johnson
159   et al., 2017; Suhr et al., 2017), which evaluates a model’s understanding of spatial relationships and
160   logical reasoning based on visual input. In recent years, researchers have built benchmarks that
161   aim to evaluate the comprehensive capabilities of VLMs (Li et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023b; Yue
      et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2023; Guan et al., 2024). Although some

      benchmarks include QA pairs related to compositional reasoning, such as BLINK (Fu et al., 2024),
163   MMVP (Tong et al., 2024b), and Seed-bench (Li et al., 2023a), these are often mixed with other
164   types of QA pairs, making it challenging to assess a model’s compositionality precisely. In contrast,
165   MMC OMPOSITION consolidates and refines existing categories of VL compositionality, offering a
166   diverse set of compositional QA pairs that provide a more precise evaluation of model performance.

168   2.2   C OMPOSITIONALITY FOR V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODELS
169
170   Compositional understanding of images and text is a critical capability for VLMs. Research indicates
171   that VLMs struggle to distinguish hard negative examples, i.e., image-text pairs that mismatch in
172   at least one aspect (e.g., attribute, relation, object), as there is little incentive for them to learn
173   compositionality during contrastive pre-training (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022). Hsieh et al. (2024)
174   illustrate that contrastive pre-training with generated hard negative examples can improve models’
175
      performance on downstream tasks. Various benchmarks have been proposed to assess the capabilities
      of VLMs in compositional vision-language perception, including VL-Checklist (Zhao et al., 2022),
176
      ARO (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022), FineMatch (Hua et al., 2024a), Sugarcrepe (Hsieh et al., 2024),
177
      Crepe (Ma et al., 2023), Cola (Ray et al., 2023), CheckList (Zhao et al., 2022), etc. However, these
178   benchmarks often evaluate models’ capabilities from limited perspectives, such as object, attribute,
179   and relation perception, and primarily focus on simple tasks like binary image-to-text retrieval, where
180   models need to select the correct caption from pairs containing a correct and a hard negative caption.
181   Moreover, the aforementioned benchmarks often contain a limited range of relations or attributes (e.g.,
182   ARO includes 48 relations and 117 attributes). GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019) includes a diverse
183   set of QA pairs focused on compositional reasoning, but the majority of the questions (77.74%) are
184   simple Yes/No format. In contrast, MMC OMPOSITION offers a more comprehensive assessment with
185   various compositional scenarios, including multi-image and indefinite choice questions, providing
186
      a more comprehensive assessment. Furthermore, MMC OMPOSITION evaluates the robustness in
      detecting complex relationships, including subtle scene composition, object interactions, and higher-
187
      order concepts beyond basic perception.

190   2.3   P RE - TRAINED V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODELS
191
      Vision-language models (Radford et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024a; Hua et al., 2024b; Ye et al., 2023;
192
      Tang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Bi et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2024a) aim to achieve
193   multimodal intelligence by jointly understanding and generating visual and language information.
194   Inspired by the remarkable success of recent large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023;
195   Chiang et al., 2023; Hua et al., 2021), researchers are now exploring large VLMs that combine pre-
196   trained visual encoders and language decoders to tackle complex multimodal tasks. Flamingo (Alayrac
197   et al., 2022) and BLIP-2 (Li et al.) are two of the early works that explore the integration of LLMs into
198   vision-language pre-training. These models are trained as VL foundation models. Beginning with
199   LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024a), researchers have used LLM-synthesized instruction-following chat data
200   in VQA format for instruction tuning, achieving significantly improved results (Hua et al., 2024a).
201
      Subsequent studies have expanded to explore the broader capabilities of multimodal LLMs (Hu et al.,
      2023; Guan et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024c; Tang et al., 2023). However, these efforts
202
      place less emphasis on improving the models’ ability to fine-grained compositional perception and
203
      reasoning.

213   pairs as our seed data. This collection includes datasets that contain the description of objects,
214   attributes, relations, and counting, such as VL-CheckList (Zhao et al., 2022), Sugar-Crepe (Hsieh
215   et al., 2024), ARO (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022), Crepe (Ma et al., 2023), and DOCCI (Onoe et al.,
      2024). Additionally, we incorporate sources that are well-suited for constructing VL compositional

241   Question and Answer Construction. We obtain QA pairs from the seed data in through several
242   methodologies:
243
      For the seed data that only contain positive and negative captions (e.g., ARC (Yuksekgonul et al.,
244
      2022)), we first generate sentence embeddings for each caption using Sentence-BERT (Reimers &
245
      Gurevych, 2019). We then utilize these embeddings to retrieve the most similar captions from the
246   Visual Genome (Krishna et al., 2016) dataset. This process results in four captions per image in each
247   sample, forming four answer options per question.
248
249
      For data samples containing multiple images – such as those in the image difference spotting task,
      which includes two images per question – we concatenate the two images side by side and label them
250
      Left and Right beneath each sub-image. This setup allows for two types of question-answer options:
251
      Left and Right for questions asking which sub-image is described by a caption, and True and False
252   for questions determining the accuracy of a caption describing the image difference. For tasks that
253   include more than two images per question (e.g., visual similarity assessments), we concatenate all
254   images into a single composite image and label each sub-image as Image1 , ..., Imagei .
255
      For the probing task, we select several captions from the dense captions in Visual Genome (Krishna
256
      et al., 2016) as the correct options and write the misaligned captions manually for the image. Then,
257
      we randomly select x ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4} captions from the set of accurate captions for a given image
258   and complement these with 4 − x incorrect options drawn from a set of conflict captions. With this
259   approach, we can obtain the indefinite-choice QA pairs.
260
261
      Data Filtering and Difficulty Classification. We divide the data into different difficulty levels: easy,
      medium, hard, and super hard. To achieve this, we use a voting system with six models, ranging
262
      from weaker to stronger, including LLaVA-1.5-13B (Liu et al., 2024b), LLaVA-1.6-Mistral-7B (Liu
263
      et al., 2024a), LLaVA-1.6-Vicuna-13B (Liu et al., 2024a), Phi-3-Vision-128K-Instruct (Abdin et al.,
264   2024), InternVL-Chat-V1.5 (Chen et al., 2024b), and Qwen-VL-Chat (Bai et al., 2023). Based on the
265   accuracy of model predictions for each question, questions are categorized into different difficulty
266   levels. Questions with zero correct predictions are classified as super hard, those with one or two
267   correct predictions are labeled as hard, questions with three or four correct predictions are considered
268   medium, and those with more than five correct predictions are categorized as easy. The overall
269   difficulty of the dataset is then controlled by adjusting the ratio of questions at each difficulty level.

517   5.4   I NTERPRETABLE A NALYSIS OF M ODEL D EFICIENCIES
518
519   We conduct a comprehensive error analysis to better understand the models’ deficiencies in fine-
520
      grained compositional understanding. In this analysis, the models are required to answer questions
      and provide explanations in a multi-turn dialogue format. Figures 3, 14, and 15 illustrate the reasons
521
      why the models fail to predict the correct answers for each task. For example, in the Obj-P task
522
      (example 3), while the ’yellow colored outline’ is easily detected by humans, the models struggle to
523   accurately identify the target objects due to the outline being mixed with numerous other characters.
524   Additionally, the models face difficulties with fine-grained object counting, especially when several
525   similar objects are present. In the Count-R (example 6) task, for instance, humans can precisely count
526   the number of triangles on a wheel, but the models confuse the six irregular polygons for triangles.

      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      This paper introduces MMC OMPOSITION, a novel high-quality benchmark for evaluating VLM
      compositionality. With MMC OMPOSITION, we comprehensively evaluate the compositionality of
532
      notable VLMs. Our evaluation reveals a significant gap between these models and human performance,
533
      providing insights into the limitations of existing VLMs. Additionally, we systematically analyze
534   factors that may influence compositionality, including visual encoder design, training data volume,
535   and language decoder size. We find that for relatively simple QA tasks, only a small portion of the
536   language model’s capacity is utilized (as seen in models outperforming GPT-4o, whose language
537   model has 70B parameters). Once the language decoder reaches a certain size threshold (e.g., 34B,
538   70B), the visual encoder has a more pronounced impact on compositionality. In summary, our work
539   provides a comprehensive and precise framework for evaluating the compositionality of VLMs,
      identifies key areas for improvement, and suggests potential directions for future advancements.

      A.2   C OMPARISON A NALYSIS OF I MAGE E NCODING IN GPT-4 O , Q WEN 2-VL, AND
851
            I NTERN VL-2
852
853   In GPT-4o, when the image detail parameters are set to “high”, images are first scaled to fit within
854   a 2048 × 2048 square while maintaining their aspect ratio. Then, the images are further scaled so
855   that the shortest side is 768px long. Finally, GPT-4o calculates how many 512px squares the image
856   contains, with each square costing 170 tokens. An additional 85 tokens for low resolution are always
857   added to the final total. As a result, GPT-4o does not achieve true “any resolution” image processing.
858   In Qwen2-VL and InternVL-2, the image encoders adopt a dynamic “any resolution” encoding
859   strategy. The images are first mapped to an optimal aspect ratio from predefined ratios, then divided
860   into 448 × 448 or 28 × 28 pixel tiles, with each tile converted into 256 or 1 image tokens. A thumbnail
861   is then generated to capture the global context. This allows the encoders to handle images of any
862   resolution and aspect ratio. Furthermore, the image encoder in Qwen2-VL is a 675M ViT with a two-
863   dimensional positional encoding mechanism, while InternVL-2 utilizes the more powerful InternViT
      with 6B parameters. This distinction contributes to the superior performance of the compositionality

1145   A.7        A DDITIONAL E XPERIMENTS
1146
       To further verify the challenging nature of MMC OMPOSITION and demonstrate the indispensable role
1147
       of images, we conducted additional experiments under image-blind settings. The results are presented
1148   in Table 10. We also conducted experiments to compare different visual-to-language (V2L) adapters
1149   and their impact on model performance, as summarized in Table 9. Additionally, we examined
1150   the models’ abilities to handle multi-hop reasoning questions, the effect of providing in-context
1151   examples, and the performance when multiple images are used. These experiments aim to provide a
1152   comprehensive understanding of the factors influencing model performance on MMC OMPOSITION.
1153
       Image-blind Setting. As shown in Table 10, all models experienced significant performance drops
1154   across all evaluation dimensions when visual inputs were removed. For instance, the overall score
1155   of Qwen2-VL-72B decreased by 20.50%, underscoring the indispensable role of images in these
1156   tasks. This substantial decline confirms that MMC OMPOSITION effectively evaluates the integration
1157   of visual and linguistic understanding, as models struggle without visual context.
1158
       V2L Adapters Comparison. We also compare different V2L adapters. As shown in Table 9, models
1159
       that utilize an MLP adapter (e.g., LLaVA1.5-13B) generally outperform those with a Q-Former adapter
1160   in overall performance. Specifically, LLaVA1.5-13B achieves an overall score of 42.03, surpassing
1161   InstructBLIP-13B’s score of 37.06. This suggests that the choice of adapter architecture significantly
1162   influences a model’s ability to effectively integrate visual features.
1163
       Multi-hop/Non-multi-hop Question Setting. We analyze the performance on multi-hop versus
1164
       non-multi-hop question settings (Table 11) and observe that some models perform better on multi-hop
1165
       questions, indicating strength in complex reasoning tasks. For example, Qwen2-VL-72B achieved
1166   an overall score of 70.22 on multi-hop questions, compared to 58.55 on non multi-hop ones. This
1167   demonstrates the model’s enhanced capability to handle questions requiring multiple reasoning steps.
1168
1169
       In-context Learning Setting. The results in Table 12 indicate that in-context examples do not
       consistently improve performance. For Qwen2-VL-72B, adding one example slightly decreased the
1170
       overall score by 0.92%, while adding more examples led to further declines. Similarly, InternVL2-
1171
       40B experiences a drop of up to 13.44% with three in-context examples. These results suggest that
1172   the models may not effectively utilize in-context learning for visual reasoning tasks, possibly due to
1173   limitations in their training data or architectural design.
1174
1175
       Multi-image Setting. As shown in Table 13, the performance varies between models. Qwen2-VL-
       72B shows an improvement in overall score by 4.14% when multiple images are provided, indicating
1176
       effective utilization of additional visual information. In contrast, InternVL2-40B’s performance
1177
       decreases by 1.62%, suggesting difficulties in integrating information from multiple images.
1178
1179   These additional experiments reinforce the challenging nature of MMC OMPOSITION and highlight
1180   the importance of visual information, adapter architectures, and the complexities involved in multi-
1181
       hop reasoning and in-context learning within multimodal models. The findings provide valuable
       insights for future research aiming to enhance the capabilities of vision-language models.
