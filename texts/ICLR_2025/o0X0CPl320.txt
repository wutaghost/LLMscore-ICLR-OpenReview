011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Learning representations on Heterogeneous Multiplex Graphs (HMGs) is an ac-
014            tive field of study, driven by the need for generating expressive, low-dimensional
015
               embeddings to support downstream machine learning tasks. A key component
               of this process is the design of the graph processing pipeline, which directly im-
016
               pacts the quality of learned representations. Information fusion techniques, which
017
               aggregate information across layers of a multiplex graph, have been shown to im-
018            prove the performance of Graph Neural Network (GNN)-based architectures on
019            various tasks including node classification, edge prediction, and graph-level clas-
020            sification. Recent research has explored fusion strategies at different stages of the
021            processing pipeline, leading to graph-, GNN-, embedding-, and prediction-level
022            approaches. In this work, we propose a model extending the GraphSAGE ar-
023            chitecture, which simultaneously refines layer-wise embeddings produced by the
024            encoder while training downstream models. We evaluate the model’s effective-
025            ness on an HMG on real-world and benchmark datasets, comparing it to models
026
               utilizing either graph-level or prediction-level fusion without jointly optimizing
               their vector embeddings. We demonstrate that our approach enhances the model’s
027
               performance on downstream tasks, particularly node classification.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   As the internet continues to expand rapidly, users are increasingly exposed to irrelevant and un-
033   wanted information, driving demand for systems connecting users with more personalized and
034   streamlined content (Bawden & Robinson (2020)). Recommendation systems have thus become
035   essential for enhancing user experiences, and for enabling companies to influence consumer behav-
036   ior across industries. These systems learn and predict user preferences to recommend relevant items,
037   driving user engagement, satisfaction, and revenue (Roy & Dutta (2022)). In 2013, 35% of Amazon
038
      purchases and 75% of Netflix streams were driven by algorithmic recommendations (MacKenzie
      (2024)). Their significance is underscored by the growth of companies who rely on them including
039
      Amazon, Netflix, Spotify, and Facebook.
040
041   Recommendation systems are categorized as either content-based, collaborative filtering-based, or
042   hybrid systems (Roy & Dutta (2022)). Content-based algorithms create user and item profiles from
043   features and interactions, but face shortcomings with exploring new interests and leveraging the in-
044
      formation of other users. Collaborative filtering addresses this problem by creating neighbourhoods
      of users with similar interests, using either memory- or model-based approaches to make predictions
045
      (Roy & Dutta (2022)).
046
047   These approaches typically operate only on predefined data types and user-item connections. With
048   advances in data collection, modern recommendation systems aim to incorporate more diverse and
049   complex feature and connection data. This demand has led to the popularization of hybrid- and
050
      graph- based systems, which excel at modeling intricate relationships. (Roy & Dutta (2022)).
051   Graph Representation Learning (GRL) algorithms use shallow or deep methods to learn low-
052   dimensional embedding vectors of graph elements, enabling their use for downstream tasks includ-
053   ing node classification, edge prediction, clustering, and graph-level classification (Kipf & Welling
      (2016); Hamilton (2020)). While most GNN-based architectures are designed for homogeneous

054
      graphs, with uniform node and edge types, real-world networks are often inherently heterogeneous
055   and/or multiplex, containing both nodes and edges of multiple distinct types (Zhang et al. (2024)).
056   Accordingly, improving learned representations for Heterogeneous Multiplex Graphs (HMGs) is an
057   active field of research.
058
059   1.1   M ULTIPLEX G RAPH R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
060
061   Multiplex graphs model different connection types between nodes as distinct edge layers, enabling
062   the incorporation of edge type information into models (Gong & Cheng (2019)). Information fusion
063   is necessary to consolidate information across layers of a multiplex network during representation
064   learning, and is categorized into four distinct types: graph-, GNN-, embedding-, and prediction-level
065   Bielak & Kajdanowicz (2024).
066   Graph-level fusion-based architectures flatten all edge layers of a multiplex graph into a single
067   layer prior to learning embeddings. Models such as MHGCN (Yu et al. (2022)) learn aggregation
068   functions, while others simply flatten the graph before passing it to a GNN, Graph Convolutional
069   Network (GCN), or other homogeneous graph-compatible model. These architectures are typically
070   outperformed by models utilizing more complex fusion strategies (Bielak & Kajdanowicz (2024)).
071   GNN-level fusion-based architectures consider the multi-layered graph structure while learning
072   node embeddings, to account for connections in all layers. DMGI combines layer-wise embeddings
073   using a learned matrix (Park et al. (2020)). HDGI uses an attention mechanism to capture layer-
074   specific information (Ren et al. (2020)). S2 MGRL uses a Multi-Layer-Perceptron (MLP) and GCN
075   to provide a fused embedding that captures layer-specific information (Mo et al. (2022)). These
076   methods output a single embedding for each node to be used in downstream tasks.
077   Embedding-level fusion-based models compute node embeddings for each layer and then com-
078   bine them using a fusion operator. Operators can be trainable (using attention, projection, or ma-
079   trix lookup) or non-trainable (Mean, Concat, Min, Max, or Sum) (Bielak & Kajdanowicz (2024)).
080   Complex architectures, such as MxPool, use trained, differentiable pooling operators to generate
081   hierarchical node embeddings (Liang et al. (2020)).
082   Prediction-level fusion learns node embeddings independently for each layer using an architecture
083   like DGI, DW, ARVGA, or GraphSAGE, and trains a distinct task model on each layer.(Veličković
084   et al. (2018); Perozzi et al. (2014); Pan et al. (2018); Hamilton et al. (2017)). Predictions are ag-
085   gregated across layers using either soft or hard ensemble voting to give a single result (Bielak &
086   Kajdanowicz (2024)).
087
      These information fusion techniques produce node embeddings for multiplex graphs, but are not
088   specifically optimized for the downstream tasks for which they may be used.
089

      1.2   U SING L EARNED R EPRESENTATIONS FOR D OWNSTREAM TASKS
091
092   Learned embeddings can perform sub-optimally on downstream tasks such as node classification
093   and clustering (Veličković et al. (2018); Hamilton (2020)). To address these challenges, joint op-
094   timization, where encoders and downstream models are trained simultaneously have been used in
095   other application domains such as computer vision and text classification (Zhuge et al. (2019); Jer-
096   nite et al. (2017); Asano et al. (2020); Vargas-Vieyra et al. (2020)). This paradigm has been shown
097   to produce task-optimal representations and improve downstream performance relative to baseline
098   models.
099   The objective of this paper is three-fold:
100
101         1. To apply joint-optimization techniques for learning task-specific representations for graph-
102            based recommendation systems.
103         2. Handle the challenges of learning meaningful representations in the context of HMGs.
104
            3. Provide a set of best practices for learning representations from realistic graph datasets.

134   HMGSAGE To address the second objective of the paper, we introduce HMGSAGE, an encoder
135   extending the GraphSAGE architecture for inductive GRL to learn embeddings on HMGs. The
136   encoder uses parallel GraphSAGE layers to sample and aggregate neighbours of nodes within the
137   graph, preserving the structure of the data while maintaining flexibility and scalability on large
138   graphs. HMGSAGE can be trained as part of a Graph Autoencoder (GAE) using contrastive ap-
139   proaches to learn node embeddings for HMGs that are useful for edge prediction tasks.
140
141   SECSAGE This model combines the HMGSAGE encoder with a downstream classifier and aggre-
142   gates the classification outputs using prediction-level fusion. HMGSAGE and the classifier are trained
143
      simultaneously using SECO, jointly optimizing embeddings learned by the HMGSAGE encoder for
      node classification tasks.
144
145   Studying these concepts and models addresses the existing research gap in the study of SECO. We
146   show that it increases a classifier’s Macro F1 accuracy, and produces more expressive and separa-
147   ble embeddings, as evidenced by their silhouette scores. The performance of both HMGSAGE and
148   SECSAGE are assessed and benchmarked against baseline models for edge prediction and node
149
      classification, respectively. First, HMGSAGE is tested against a GraphSAGE encoder operating on a
      flattened graph. Second, SECSAGE is evaluated against Models C1, C2, and C3 defined in Table 1
150
      on node classification tasks. Both HMGSAGE and SECSAGE are shown to outperform baseline mod-
151
      els, and SECO is shown to reduce training time compared to methods where encoders and classifiers
152   are trained separately.
153
154   We address the third objective of our paper by testing the concepts defined above on a real-world
155
      travel dataset, “Travel Dubai”, and a standard “Amazon” product review dataset (Hou et al. (2024))
      while highlighting the challenges associated with these datasets such as underrepresentation of class
156
      labels and multiplex layers.

      Definition 1.       Heterogeneous Graph A heterogeneous graph is defined as a graph
163   G = (V, E, Q, ϕ), in which V is a set of vertices, E is a set of edges, and ϕ is a vertex type mapping
164   function ϕ : V → Q. In a heterogeneous graph,  T the set of vertices can be partitioned into k disjoint
165   sets V = V 1 ∪V2 ∪...∪Vk where k = |Q|, Vi Vj = ∅, ∀i ̸= j and each set of vertices Vi represents
166   a distinct type of node with a unique set of properties (Hamilton (2020)).
167
168   Definition 2. Multiplex Graph A multiplex graph is a graph G = (V, E τ , R, ψ), ∀ τ ∈ R, where
169   ψ : E → R maps each edge to its type. The set of edges for each layer of the graph, Eτ , is a subset
170   of the Cartesian product of all vertices in the graph, Eτ ⊆ V × V, ∀ τ ∈ R. Thus, there are multiple
171   layers of connections, and for any pair of vertices u, v ∈ V, up to one connection in each edge layer
172   Eτ is permitted.
173
174   Definition 3. Heterogeneous Multiplex Graph In this paper, a Heterogeneous Multiplex Graph
175   (HMG) is defined as a graph G = (V, Eτ , Q, R, ϕ, ψ), ∀τ ∈ R, in which V is a set of vertices, E
176   is a set of edges, ϕ is a vertex type mapping function ϕ : V → Q and ψ is an edge type mapping
177   function ψ : E → R. It is defined that |Q| > 1 and |R| > 1.
178
179   Definition 4. Inductive Learning For any type of graph-based model, inductive learning de-
180   scribes its ability to generalize to unseen nodes and edges based on patterns learned on the training
181   graph. Thus, the ability of a model  T trained onTGtrain = (Vtrain , Etrain ) to make inferences on a graph
182
      Gtest = (Vtest , Etest ) where Vtrain Vtest = Etrain Etest = ∅ (Hamilton (2020)).

      4   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we propose three GRL models and evaluate them on a new real-world dataset and
489   a large benchmark dataset. First, we introduce an encoder for Heterogeneous Multiplex Graphs
490   denoted HMGSAGE. Next, we extend HMGSAGE to create a Graph Autoencoder which performs
491   inductive edge prediction on HMG-structured data. Finally, we introduce the topic of Simultaneous
492   Encoder-Classifier Optimization. We use it to extend HMGSAGE for the task of inductive node
493   classification on HMGs, creating a model denoted SECSAGE. After comparing these to baseline
494   models, we show that our architectures outperform those using other information fusion techniques
495   on both edge prediction and node classification tasks.
496   From these comparisons, two primary conclusions can be drawn. We show that preserving the
497   graph structure of an HMG early in the learning pipeline leads to the learning of better embeddings.
498   This is evidenced by the performance of our GAE using HMGSAGE against a similar model using
499   GraphSAGE and graph-level fusion. We also show that by using SECO, models learn task-optimal
500   embeddings, improving the performance of a particular downstream task. This improves even fur-
501   ther when combined with prediction-level fusion as opposed to graph-level.
502   We also note that multiplex layers are often imbalanced in real-world datasets, and can be han-
503   dled by using weighted loss terms in combination with dedicated GRL operators for each layer as
504   demonstrated in this paper. Furthermore, edge weights and class labels can also be imbalanced, and
505   appropriate metrics should be used for evaluating model performance. This offers some practical
506   considerations when working with graph-based recommendation systems.
507   There are several possibilities for further research towards better GRL on HMGs. More test-
508   ing should be conducted into measuring the performance of SECSAGE on other HMG datasets,
509   with comparisons against more baselines using other encoders such as DGI interchangeably with
510   HMGSAGE. Additionally, the benefits of SECO should be studied on models using other types of
511   information fusion, specifically GNN-level and embedding-level. The potential to generalize SECO
512   to produce task-optimal embeddings for other tasks such as clustering, graph-level classification,
513   and combinations thereof also merit investigation.

      A    BACKGROUND
649
650
      This section provides a background on related concepts used throughout the paper.
651
652
      Graph Autoencoder (GAE) A Graph Autoencoder is a neural-network-based machine learning
653   model designed to learn low-dimensional latent representations, known as embedding vectors, of
654   various elements of graph-structured data. In this paper we focus on learning embeddings for the
655   different nodes in our graph zu , ∀u ∈ V. We use an encoder-decoder paradigm similar to the seminal
656   work of Kipf & Welling (2016). The encoder is constructed using several GNN operators and maps
657   nodes to high-dimensional vector space ENC : V 7→ Rd , where d is the dimensionality of the
658   embedding vectors zv ∈ Rd for v ∈ V.
659
                                                ENC(v) = Z[v],                                           (8)
660
661
      The decoder is a model that obtains the embeddings zu in the low-dimensional space and recon-
662   structs information about the nodes’ local neighborhood in the original graph. As an example, the
663   reconstructed information could represent the set of neighbours of node u, N (u) or its row A[v] in
664   the adjacency matrix. However, a better approach is to use a pairwise decoder which reconstructs
665   information about the relationship between pairs of nodes, i.e.,
666
                              DEC(ENC(u), ENC(v)) = DEC(zu , zv ) ≈ S[u, v],                             (9)
667
668   where S[u, v] is the reconstruction objective, e.g., S[u, v] ≜ A[u, v], although different objectives
669   can be defined other than the adjacency matrix.
670
      Finally, a reconstruction loss can be defined over a set of training nodes D ∈ V and used to optimize
671
      the parameters of the encoder and decoder using gradient descent:
672                                          X
673                                    L=          ℓ(DEC(zu , zv ), S[u, v]),
674                                        (u,v)∈D
675
676
      where ℓ : R × R 7→ R is a loss function such as mean-squared error or binary cross entropy loss.
677   There are many different models for the decoder, the similarity measure S[u, v], and loss function,
678   different combinations of which results in different representation learning algorithms. In this paper,
679   we focus on a simple decoder that uses a feedforward layer to concatenate pairs of node embeddings
680   following by a sigmoid layer to scale the predicted similarity measure between 0 and 1 as explained
681   in Section 2.2
