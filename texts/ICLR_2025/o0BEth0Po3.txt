009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            With the ability to learn from static datasets, Offline Reinforcement Learning (RL)
012            emerges as a compelling avenue for real-world applications. However, state-of-
013            the-art offline RL algorithms perform sub-optimally when confronted with limited
014            data confined to specific regions within the state space. The performance degra-
015            dation is attributed to the inability of offline RL algorithms to learn appropriate
016            actions for rare or unseen observations. This paper proposes a novel domain
017            knowledge-based regularization technique and adaptively refines the initial do-
018
               main knowledge to considerably boost performance in limited data with partially
               omitted states. The key insight is that the regularization term mitigates erroneous
019
               actions for sparse samples and unobserved states covered by domain knowledge.
020
               Empirical evaluations on standard offline RL datasets demonstrate a substantial
021            average performance increase compared to ensemble of domain knowledge and
022            existing offline RL algorithms operating on limited data.
023

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Offline RL (Ernst et al., 2005; Pru, 2023), also referred to as batch RL, is a learning approach that
028   focuses on extracting knowledge solely from static datasets. This class of algorithms has a wider
029   range of applications being particularly appealing to real-world data sets from business (Zhang &
030   Yu, 2021), healthcare (Liu et al., 2020), and robotics (Sinha et al., 2022). However, offline RL poses
031   unique challenges, including over-fitting and the need for generalization to data not present in the
032   dataset. To surpass the behavior policy, offline RL algorithms need to query Q values of actions
033
      not in the dataset, causing extrapolation errors (Kumar et al., 2019). Most offline RL algorithms
      address this problem by enforcing constraints that ensure that the learned policy does not deviate
034
      too far away from the data set’s state action distribution (Fujimoto et al., 2019b; Fujimoto & Gu,
035
      2021) or is conservative towards Out-of-Distribution (OOD) actions (Kumar et al., 2019; Kostrikov
036   et al., 2021). However, such approaches are designed on coherent batches (Fujimoto et al., 2019b),
037   which do not account for OOD states.
038
039
      In many domains, such as business and healthcare, available data is scarce and often confined to
      expert behaviors within a limited state space. For example, a sales recommendation system, where
040
      historic data may not contain details about many active users and operator gives coupon of higher
041
      value to attract sales. Learning on such limited data sets can curtail the generalization capabilities of
042   state-of-the-art (SOTA) offline RL algorithms, resulting in sub-optimal performance (Levine et al.,
043   2020a). We illustrate this limitation via Fig 1. In Fig 1a) the state action space of a simple Mountain
044   Car environment (Moore, 1990) is plotted for an expert dataset (Schweighofer et al., 2022) and a
045   partial dataset with first 10% samples from the entire dataset. Fig 1b) shows the average reward
046   obtained over these data sets and the average difference between the Q value of action taken by the
047   under-performing Conservative Q Learning (CQL) (Kumar et al., 2019) agent and the action in the
048   full expert dataset for unseen states. It can be observed that the performance of the offline RL agent
049   considerably drops. This is attributed to the critic overestimating the Q value of non-optimal actions
050
      for states that do not occur in the dataset while training.
051   In numerous real-world applications, expert insights regarding the general behavior of a policy are
052   often accessible (Silva & Gombolay, 2021). For example, sales operators often distribute lower
053   discount coupons to active users to maximize profit. While these insights may not be optimal, they
      serve as valuable guidelines for understanding the overall behavior of the policy. A rich literature in

092   2   R ELATED W ORK
093
094   This work improves offline RL learning on batches sampled from static datasets using domain ex-
095   pertise. One of the major concerns in offline RL is the erroneous extrapolation of OOD actions
096   (Fujimoto et al., 2019b). Three techniques have been studied in the literature to prevent such errors.
097   1) Constraining the policy to be close to the behavior policy 2) Penalizing overly optimistic Q values
098   (Levine et al., 2020b) 3) Learning model dynamics from data (Kidambi et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2020),
099
      where performance highly depends on the accuracy of the learned dynamics. We discuss a few
      relevant algorithms following these principles. In Batch-Constrained deep Q-learning (BCQ) (Fuji-
100
      moto et al., 2019b) candidate actions sampled from an adversarial generative model are considered,
101
      aiming to balance proximity to the batch while enhancing action diversity. Algorithms like Random
102   Ensemble Mixture Model (REM) (Agarwal et al., 2020), Ensemble-Diversified Actor-Critic (EDAC)
103   (An et al., 2021) and Uncertainty Weighted Actor-Critic (UWAC) (Wu et al., 2021) penalize the Q
104   value according to uncertainty by either using Q ensemble networks or directly weighting the loss
105   with uncertainty. CQL (Kumar et al., 2019) enforces regularization on Q-functions by incorporat-
106   ing a term that reduces Q-values for OOD actions while increasing Q-values for actions within the
107   expected distribution. However, these algorithms do not handle OOD actions for states not in the
      static dataset and can have errors induced by changes in transition probability.

108
      Integration of domain knowledge in offline RL, though an important avenue, has not yet been exten-
109   sively explored. Domain knowledge incorporation has improved online RL with tight regret bounds
110   (Silva & Gombolay, 2021; Bartlett & Tewari, 2009). In offline RL, bootstrapping via blending
111   heuristics computed using Monte-Carlo returns with rewards has shown to outperform SOTA algo-
112   rithms by 9% (Geng et al., 2023). Recent works improve offline RL by incorporating a safety expert
113   (Verma et al., 2024) and preference query (Yang et al., 2023), contrary to our work which improves
114   imperfect domain knowledge. The closest to our work is Domain Knowledge guided Q learning
115   (DKQ) (Zhang & Yu, 2021) where domain knowledge is represented in terms of action importance
116   and the Q value is weighted according to importance. However, obtaining action importance in
117
      practical scenarios is nontrivial.

287   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
288
289   We evaluate our methodology on open-AI gym (Brockman et al., 2016), MiniGrid (Chevalier-
290   Boisvert et al., 2023), real sales promotion (SP) (Qin et al., 2022) and sim-glucose (Gao, 2024)
291   offline data sets. All our data sets are generated using standard methodologies defined in
292   (Schweighofer et al., 2022; 2021) except SP which is generated by human operators. All experi-
293
      ments have been conducted on a Ubuntu 22.04.2 LTS system with 1 NVIDIA K80 GPU, 4 CPUs,
      and 61GiB RAM. App. G notes the hyperparameter values and network architectures.
294
295   Dataset: We experiment on three types of data sets. Expert Data-set (Fu et al., 2020; Gulcehre
296   et al., 2021; Kumar et al., 2020) generated using an optimal policy without any exploration with high
297   trajectory quality but low state action coverage. Replay Data-set (Agarwal et al., 2020; Fujimoto
298   et al., 2019b) generated from a policy while training it online, exhibiting a mixture of multiple
299
      behavioral policies with high trajectory quality and state action coverage. Noisy Data-set (Fujimoto
      et al., 2019a;b; Kumar et al., 2020; Gulcehre et al., 2021) generated using an optimal policy that
300
      also selects random actions with ϵ greedy strategy where ϵ = 0.2 having low trajectory quality and
301
      high state action coverage. Additionally we also experiment on human generated dataset for sales
302   promotion task and simglucose task.
303
304   Baselines: We do comparative studies on 10 baselines for OpenAI gym datasets. The first baseline
305
      simply checks the conditions of D and applies corresponding actions in execution. The performance
      of this baseline shows that D is imperfect and does not achieve the optimal reward. CQL SE is
306
      from (Verma et al., 2024) where the expert is replaced by D. The other baselines are an ensemble
307
      of D and eight algorithms popular in the Offline RL literature for discrete environments. These
308   algorithms include Behavior Cloning (BC) (Pomerleau, 1991), Behaviour Value Estimation (BVE)
309   (Gulcehre et al., 2021), Quantile Regression DQN (QRDQN) (Dabney et al., 2018), REM, MCE,
310   BCQ, CQL and Critic Regularized Regression Q-Learning (CRR) (Wang et al., 2020). For a fair
311   comparison, we use actions from domain knowledge for states not in the buffer and actions from the
312   trained policy for other states to obtain the final reward. Hence, each algorithm is renamed with the
313   suffix D in Table 5.1.
314   Limiting Data: To create limited-data settings for benchmark datasets, we first extract a small per-
315   centage of samples from the full dataset and remove some of the samples based on state conditions.
316   This is done to ensure the reduced buffer satisfies the conditions defined in Def 4.1. We describe
317   the specific conditions of removal in the next section. Further insights and the state visualizations
318   for selected reduced datasets are in App I. Note : no data reduction has been performed on SP
319   dataset to demonstrate a real dataset exhibits characteristics of reduced buffer.

496   6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
497
498   In this paper, we study the effect of limited and partial data on offline RL and observe that the
499   performance of SOTA offline RL algorithms is sub-optimal in such settings. The paper proposes a
500   methodology to handle offline RL’s performance degradation using domain insights. We incorporate
501   a regularization loss in the CQL training using a teacher policy and refine the initial teacher policy
502   while training. We show that incorporating reasonable domain knowledge in offline RL enhances
503   performance, achieving a performance close to full data. However, this method is limited by the
504
      quality of the domain knowledge and the overlap between domain knowledge states and reduced
      buffer data. In the future, the authors would like to improve on capturing domain knowledge into
505
      the policy network without dependence on data and enhancing the method to work with more general
506
      forms of domain knowledge.
