009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The increasing complexity of modern very-large-scale integration (VLSI) design
012            highlights the significance of Electronic Design Automation (EDA) technologies.
013            Chip placement is a critical step in the EDA workflow, which positions chip mod-
014            ules on the canvas with the goal of optimizing performance, power, and area (PPA)
015            metrics of final chip designs. Recent advances have demonstrated the great poten-
016
               tial of AI-based algorithms in enhancing chip placement. However, due to the
               lengthy workflow of chip design, the evaluations of these algorithms often fo-
017
               cus on intermediate surrogate metrics, which are easy to compute but frequently
018
               reveal a substantial misalignment with the end-to-end performance (i.e., the final
019            design PPA). To address this challenge, we introduce ChiPBench, which can effec-
020            tively facilitate research in chip placement within the AI community. ChiPBench
021            is a comprehensive benchmark specifically designed to evaluate the effectiveness
022            of existing AI-based chip placement algorithms in improving final design PPA
023            metrics. Specifically, we have gathered 20 circuits from various domains (e.g.,
024            CPU, GPU, and microcontrollers). These designs are compiled by executing the
025            workflow from the verilog source code, which preserves necessary physical imple-
026            mentation kits, enabling evaluations for the placement algorithms on their impacts
027
               on the final design PPA. We executed six state-of-the-art AI-based chip placement
               algorithms on these designs and plugged the results of each single-point algorithm
028
               into the physical implementation workflow to obtain the final PPA results. Exper-
029
               imental results show that even if intermediate metric of a single-point algorithm is
030            dominant, while the final PPA results are unsatisfactory. This suggests that the AI
031            community should concentrate more on enhancing end-to-end performance rather
032            than those intermediate surrogates. We believe that our benchmark will serve as an
033            effective evaluation framework to bridge the gap between academia and industry.

037
038   The exponential growth in the scale of integrated circuits (ICs), in accordance with Moore’s law, has
039
      posed significant challenges to chip design (Huang et al., 2021; Lopera et al., 2021). To handle the
      increasing complexity, many electronic design automation (EDA) tools have been developed to assist
040
      hardware engineers. As shown in Figure 1, EDA tools automate various steps in the chip design
041
      workflow, including high-level synthesis, logic synthesis, physical design, testing and verification
042   (Huang et al., 2021; Sánchez et al., 2023).
043
044   Chip placement is a critical step in the chip design workflow, which aims to position chip modules on
045
      the canvas, with the goal of optimizing the performance, power, and area (PPA) metrics of final chip
      designs (Cheng et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2019). Traditionally, this is done manually
046

047

050   methods employ traditional optimization algorithms, such as simulated annealing (SA) (Vashisht
051   et al., 2020) and evolutionary algorithms (EA) (Shi et al., 2023) to directly address the large-scale
052   optimization problem, exploring the design space to identify near-optimal solutions. In recent re-
053   search, macro placement has been formulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), where the
      macro positions are determined sequentially (Mirhoseini et al., 2021). Reinforcement learning (RL)

      Datasets Some well-known EDA conferences, such as ISPD and ICCAD, host contests addressing
      EDA challenges and offer benchmarks with processed data for researchers. However, in the early
137
      years (e.g., ISPD2005 (Nam et al., 2005) and ICCAD2004 (Adya et al., 2009)), the provided datasets
138
      used overly simplified Bookshelf formats, which are abstracted versions of the actual design kits.
139   Therefore, we cannot evaluate the final PPA of the placement results on those datasets. Recently,
140   ISPD2015 (Bustany et al., 2015) and ICCAD2015 (Kim et al., 2015) have offered benchmarks and
141   datasets closer to real-world applications, including necessary netlist, library, and design exchange
142   files, broadening their utility slightly. Nevertheless, they still lack the essential information (e.g.,
143   necessary design kits) to run the open-source EDA tools such as OpenROAD (Kahng & Spyrou,
144   2021). Beyond these conferences, some other datasets have been developed in various directions.
145   For example, the EPFL (Amarú et al., 2015) benchmarks and the larger OpenABC-D Chowdhury
146   et al. (2021) dataset concentrated on synthetic netlists, primarily for testing modern logic optimiza-
147
      tion tools with a focus on logic synthesis. CircuitNet 2.0 (Jiang et al., 2023), on the other hand,
      shifted the focus towards providing multi-modal data for prediction tasks, enhancing the capability
148
      for various prediction tasks through the use of diverse data modalities. Compared with previous
149
      efforts, our proposed dataset focuses on the entire EDA workflow. As shown in Table 1, a compar-
150   ison between our dataset and mainstream datasets highlights the distinctions. It provides complete
151   files for each case and necessary design kits, such as timing constraints, library files, and LEF files,
152   offering a comprehensive dataset that supports all stages of physical implementation and fosters a
153   more integrated approach to chip design and evaluation.
154
      Placement Algorithms Recent advancements in AI technology within the EDA field have led to a
155
      variety of AI-based chip placement algorithms. (1) Black-Box Optimization methods. Simulated

160   ment tasks. AutoDMP (Agnesina et al., 2023) leverages DREAMPlace for the concurrent placement

      methods. MaskPlace (Lai et al., 2022) treats chip placement as a visual representation learning

      3     BACKGROUND ON E LECTRONIC D ESIGN AUTOMATION
170
171   Electronic Design Automation (EDA) is a suite of software tools vital for designing and developing
172
      electronic systems, primarily integrated circuits (ICs). These tools enable electrical engineers to effi-
      ciently transform innovative concepts into functional products, addressing the increasing complexity
173
      and demands of modern chip design. EDA optimizes the entire design process from schematic cap-
174
      ture to layout and fabrication, reducing time-to-market and enhancing design precision and sophisti-
175   cation. In the chip design workflow, EDA tools support various functions: they perform simulations
176   to verify circuit behavior, execute synthesis to convert high-level descriptions to gate-level imple-
177   mentations, and manage physical layouts to ensure designs can be realized in silicon. As shown in
178   Figure 1, the EDA design flow includes several key stages: logic synthesis, floorplanning, placement,
179   Clock Tree Synthesis (CTS), and routing. Below are concise descriptions of each stage, illustrating
180   their importance in the integrated circuit development process.
181   Logic Synthesis transforms a high-level circuit description into an optimized gate-level
182   netlist (Berndt et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024a;b). Floorplan involves deciding the layout of major
183   components within an integrated circuit, positioning blocks and core components to balance signal
184   integrity, power distribution, and area utilization. Placement involves assigning specific locations
185   to various circuit components within the core area of the chip, following the floorplanning stage.
186   The primary objective of this stage is to strategically place the components to optimize performance
187   metrics such as delay and power consumption while ensuring adherence to design rules (Geng et al.,
188   2024). Clock Tree Synthesis (CTS) creates a clock distribution network within an IC to minimize
189
      those clock effects, and ensure the correct timing synchronization for circuit operation. Routing
      involves creating the physical paths for electrical connectivity between various components on the
190
      IC as per the netlist. This stage must handle multiple layers of the chip, avoid obstacles, manage
191
      signal integrity, and meet all electrical and timing constraints (Cheng et al., 2022).
192
193   Chip Placement The placement stage is crucially divided into two distinct phases: macro place-
194   ment and cell placement. (1) Macro placement is a critical very large-scale integration (VLSI) phys-
195
      ical design problem that targets the arrangement of larger components, such as SRAMs and clock
      generators—often called macros. This phase significantly impacts the chip’s overall floorplan and
196
      essential design parameters like wirelength, power, and area. (2) Following this, the standard cell
197
      placement phase addresses the arrangement of the more numerous and smaller standard cells, which
198   serve as the fundamental building blocks of digital designs. This phase typically utilizes analytical
199   solvers to secure an optimized configuration that not only minimizes wirelength but also enhances
200   the electrical and timing performance of the chip.

      We use OpenROAD (Kahng & Spyrou, 2021), an open-source EDA tool, for generating our
244
      dataset. OpenROAD integrates various tools, such as Yosys (Wolf, 2016) for logic synthesis, Tri-
245   tonMacroPlacer for macro placement, RePlAce (Cheng et al., 2018) for cell placement, TritonCTS
246   for clock tree synthesis, and TritonRoute for detailed routing. The choice of open-source tool allows
247   for full reproducibility of our results and supports the promotion of the open-source community,
248   ensuring that all generated data and methodologies are open-source. The initial dataset generation
249   starts with Verilog files as raw data. OpenROAD performs logical synthesis to convert these high-
250   level descriptions into a netlist, detailing the electrical connections among circuit components. This

252   the circuit on silicon. The resulting design from the floorplanning stage is converted into LEF/DEF

      files by OpenROAD, facilitating the application of subsequent placement algorithms. Simultane-

      including placement, CTS, and routing.

257   5     A LGORITHMS
258
259   AI-based chip placement algorithms can be roughly grouped into three categories: black-box opti-
260   mization (BBO) methods, analytical methods (gradient-based methods), and reinforcement learning
261   (RL) methods. Each category frames the placement task as an optimization problem but adopts
262   distinct objectives and methodologies. We present details as follows.
263
264   5.1    B LACK -B OX -O PTIMIZATION (BBO) M ETHODS
265
266
      A straightforward intuition is to view the chip placement task as a black-box-optimization (BBO)
      problem, where the inner workings of the objective functions are inaccessible, and solutions are
267
      evaluated only based on the output metrics.
268
269   Simulated Annealing (SA) is a heuristic BBO optimization algorithm favored for its simplicity
      in implementation. Specifically, the SA algorithm generates solutions by perturbing the solution


                                                              5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      space and then assessing the resulting representation. Different methods have been developed to
271   effectively map representations to placement solutions (Kirkpatrick et al., 1983; Sherwani, 2012; Ho
272   et al., 2004; Shunmugathammal et al., 2020; Vashisht et al., 2020), such as sequence pair (Murata
273   et al., 1996) and B∗ -tree (Chang et al., 2000). Solutions are probabilistically accepted based on an
274   annealing temperature to escape local optima in pursuit of a global optimum. Due to its simplicity in
275   implementation, the SA algorithm often serves as a strong baseline in previous studies. In this work,
276   we incorporate a specific SA implementation (Cheng et al., 2023) utilizing operations like swaps,
277   shifts, and shuffles, and a cost function that balances wirelength, density, and congestion.
278   WireMask-EA (Shi et al., 2023) is a BBO framework that was recently introduced at the NeurIPS
279   2023 conference, positioning itself as an innovative approach in the intersection of AI and EDA.
280   The framework utilizes a novel concept called wiremask, which plays a crucial role in guiding the
281   mapping process from genotypes to phenotypes in a greedy manner. The wiremask concept was
282   originally introduced by Lai et al. (2022), where it is defined as a matrix that predicts the potential
283   increase in Half-Perimeter Wirelength (HPWL) for each subsequent macro placement on the design
284   canvas. By estimating the wirelength increase, the wiremask helps in making informed decisions
285
      during the placement process, thereby potentially improving the quality of the layout. Building
      upon this concept, Shi et al. (2023) extended the framework to integrate several types of Black-
286
      Box Optimization (BBO) algorithms, including random search (RS), evolutionary algorithm (EA),
287
      and Bayesian optimization (BO), demonstrating the versatility of the approach in handling complex
288   optimization tasks in chip design.
289
290
291   5.2   A NALYTICAL (G RADIENT-BASED ) M ETHODS
292
293
      Analytical methods formulate the optimization objective as an analytical function of module co-
      ordinates. This formulation enables efficient solutions through techniques like quadratic program-
294
      ming (Kahng et al., 2005; Viswanathan et al., 2007a;b; Spindler et al., 2008; Chen et al., 2008; Kim
295
      et al., 2012; Kim & Markov, 2012) and direct gradient descent (Lu et al., 2014; Cheng et al., 2018;
296   Lin et al., 2019; 2020; Gu et al., 2020; Liao et al., 2022). This work focuses on the gradient-based
297   algorithms, which are by far the more mainstream algorithms.
298
299
      DREAMPlace (Liao et al., 2022) is a GPU-accelerated framework that leverages differentiable
      proxies, such as approximate HPWL, as optimization objectives. It was built upon the previous
300
      analytical placement algorithms, ePlace (Lu et al., 2014) and RePlAce (Cheng et al., 2018), yet sig-
301
      nificantly speeding up the placement process by using GPUs for acceleration. The series of versions
302   of DREAMPlace introduces diverse differentiable proxies to better align the PPA improvement.
303
304   AutoDMP (Agnesina et al., 2023) extends DREAMPlace by automating hyperparameter tuning
305
      through multi-objective Bayesian optimization. It further accelerates the optimization process and
      reduces manual tuning efforts. At that time, this work showcased the promising potential of inte-
306
      grating GPU-accelerated algorithms with machine learning techniques for automating VLSI design.
307
308
309   5.3   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING (RL) M ETHODS
310
311   As VLSI systems grow in complexity, RL methods are being explored to enhance placement quality.
312   GraphPlace (Mirhoseini et al., 2021) first models macro placement as a RL problem. Subsequently,
313   DeepPR (Cheng & Yan, 2021) and PRNet (Cheng et al., 2022) establish a streamlined pipeline
314
      encompassing macro placement, cell placement, and routing. However, they treat density as a soft
      constraint, which may violate non-overlap constraint during training. Therefore, in this work, we
315
      mainly focus on MaskPlace and ChiPFormer, which are recent SOTA algorithms with hard non-
316
      overlapping constraints.
317
318   MaskPlace (Lai et al., 2022) represents the chip states as pixel-level visual inputs, including a
319   wiremask (recording the HPWL increment for each grid), the viewmask (a global observation of the
320
      canvas), and the positionmask (to ensure non-overlapping constraint). Furthermore, it uses dense
      reward to boost the sample efficiency.
321
322   ChiPFormer (Lai et al., 2023) represents the first offline RL method. It is pretrained on various
323   chips via offline RL and then fine-tuned on unseen chips for better efficiency. As a result, the time
      for placement is significantly reduced.

328   6.1.1    F INAL D ESIGN PPA M ETRICS
329
330   The primary goal of the entire Electronic Design Automation (EDA) workflow is to optimize the
331   final PPA metrics. PPA stands for performance, power, and area—three crucial dimensions used to
332
      evaluate the quality of a chip product. These dimensions are assessed using several critical metrics,
      including worst negative slack (WNS), total negative slack (TNS), number of violating paths (NVP),
333
      power, and area. Optimizing these PPA metrics has been a major focus in the industry, approached
334
      through expert-designed heuristics. However, the challenge of PPA optimization has not been fully
335   recognized within the AI community. Bridging this gap and improving the incorporation of AI
336   strategies into PPA optimization are key goals of this benchmark.
337
338
      In terms of specific metrics, Worst Negative Slack (WNS) and Total Negative Slack (TNS) are
      essential for assessing the timing performance of a chip circuit. Slack is the discrepancy between
339
      the expected and required arrival times of a signal, with negative slack indicating a timing violation.
340
      WNS pinpoints the most severe negative slack within a circuit, thus identifying the most critical
341   timing issue. Conversely, TNS aggregates all negative slacks, providing a comprehensive view of
342   the circuit’s overall timing challenges. Moreover, the number of violating paths (NVP) counts the
343   paths that fail to meet the timing constraints, further illustrating the timing performance issues.
344
345   6.1.2    I NTERMEDIATE M ETRICS
346
347   Commonly used intermediate surrogate metrics include Congestion, Wire Length (WL), Half
348   Perimeter Wire Length (HPWL), and Macro HPWL (mHPWL). Congestion evaluates the density
349
      of wires in different chip regions. High congestion in certain areas can pose substantial challenges
      during the routing stage. While not a direct component of the PPA metrics, managing congestion
350
      effectively is essential to ensure that the chip can be successfully manufactured. Therefore, it is also
351
      considered as an evaluation metric in this paper. Congestion is typically estimated after the Clock
352   Tree Synthesis (CTS) stages but before the detailed routing stage, allowing for adjusting macro
353   placement and routing strategies to mitigate potential issues.
354
355
      Wire Length (WL) is the total length of all wires connecting all modules in a chip. Half Perimeter
      Wire Length (HPWL) is the sum of half perimeters of bounding boxes that encompass all pins in
356
      each net. It is widely used as an estimation of WL and is obtained after cell placement. Macro
357
      HPWL (mHPWL) further simplifies HPWL by only considering the macros. It is favored in recent
358   studies as it can be immediately obtained after macro placement. These metrics are thought to
359   correlate with the final PPA, but they do not directly reflect the chip quality.

411
      finishing the macro placement stage, the resulting placement files are then converted back to DEF

414   metrics, obtaining end-to-end evaluation results. Additionally, we perform global placement and
415   detailed placement using OpenRoad’s native Place method and complete the entire flow to obtain
416   the final metrics for comparison with other algorithms. Our project is open-sourced on GitHub.

523
      Our benchmark comprises design kits essential for each stage of the physical design flow, including
      netlists, libraries, rules, and constraints needed during the physical implementation stage. This
524
      thorough inclusion allows for a convenient and detailed evaluation of algorithms at specific stages of
525
      the physical design flow, enabling researchers and practitioners to test and compare the effectiveness
526   of their solutions in a realistic, end-to-end environment. We call on the AI researchers to pay more
527   attention on the “shift-left” challenge from the real-world industrial scenarios, keeping towards the
528   mission of bridging the huge gap between academic research and industrial applications.

      A.1   E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
759
760   In our workflow, it starts with OpenROAD. The file format for physical design in OpenROAD is
761   OpenDB database (.odb), which contains LEF/DEF information. OpenROAD can be used to con-
762   vert ODB to LEF/DEF, or LEF/DEF to ODB. Initially, the ODB after macro placement in the
763   OpenROAD flow is converted to LEF/DEF files, serving as inputs for other macro placement al-
764   gorithms. Since most existing macro placement algorithms only support bookshelf format input,
765   DREAMPlace’s code is used to convert LEF/DEF to bookshelf (.nodes, .pl, .nets) format. It is im-
766
      portant to note that when DREAMPlace reads the DEF file, it treats the blockages in the BLOCK-
      AGES part as virtual macros, so when using DREAMPlace’s PlaceDB in Python to read macro
767
      information, the virtual macros must be excluded.
768
769   For ChiPFormer, use the default model and perform 100 online iterations to fine-tune the macro
770   layout.For MaskPlace, run 3000 epochs.Other settings use the defaults.
771   In the method of ChipFormer and MaskPlace, the experiments were run on an NVIDIA GeForce
772   RTX 2080 Ti, taking one day for all cases.For the other algorithms, we used 32 CPUs (Intel(R)
773   Xeon(R) CPU E5-2667 v4 @ 3.20GHz), with a total time expenditure of two days.
774
      Next, the macro layout (.pl) files are obtained after running ChiPFormer, MaskPlace, WireMask-EA,
775
      and SA, and then DREAMPlace is used to write the macro layout into DEF files. Since DREAM-
776   Place does not modify the blockages in the DEF file, if blockages are defined in the DEF file,
777   additional script modifications may be required.
778
779
      After obtaining the macro-placed DEF files, they are converted to ODB using OpenROAD, fol-
      lowed by Tapcell and Welltie insertion, PDN generation, IO place, global place, detail place, and
780
      subsequent CTS and routing.
781
782   The performance of OpenROAD is limited; in the future, other global placement tools and detailed
783   placement tools can be used to run the entire workflow.

824   C.1   A DDITIONAL A NALYSIS
825
826   In this section, we provide a detailed analysis of the layout results for the ariane133 benchmark,
827   focusing on the observed relationship between wirelength, timing, area, and power. The AutoDMP
828   method shows interesting yet seemingly contradictory outcomes—reduced wirelength with worse
829
      timing, smaller area, and lower power. To understand these results, we explore the factors that
      contribute to these trends and propose potential directions for placement algorithm improvements.
830
831   A key factor influencing timing in large-scale circuits is buffer insertion. In general, when the wire-
832   length between two pins is large, signal transmission delay increases. Buffer insertion helps by
833   breaking long interconnects into smaller segments, reducing the overall transmission delay. Further-
834   more, buffers enhance driving capacity by reducing the load on the signal driver, which helps lower
835
      signal delay and improve timing. However, fewer buffers result in smaller area and lower power
      but also lead to degraded timing performance. In the case of ariane133, the AutoDMP method in-
836
      serts fewer buffers during timing repair, resulting in smaller overall area and power consumption.
837
      However, the lack of sufficient buffers leads to worse timing outcomes, as seen in the experimental
838   data. This explains why, despite achieving minimized wirelength, the timing performance does not
839   improve accordingly.
840
841
      The relationship between wirelength and timing is often assumed to be directly proportional; how-
      ever, the analysis of ariane133 demonstrates that this is not always the case. Although the total
842
      wirelength (as indicated by the HPWL) is reduced, the timing performance does not improve pro-
843
      portionally. In fact, congestion is lower and wirelength is minimized, but critical paths still suffer
844   from significant delays. This suggests that global wirelength optimization does not always lead to
845   improved timing, particularly for worst-case paths. Table 6 provides insights into this phenomenon
846   by highlighting the inconsistencies between wirelength and timing slack on the worst paths. Specif-
847   ically, the data reveals that longer wirelength paths do not always exhibit worse timing, indicating
848   that the optimization strategy may not have sufficiently accounted for timing-sensitive paths.
849
      To address these issues and improve overall placement quality, several optimization strategies can
850   be explored. One potential solution to mitigate timing degradation is to apply weighted optimization
851   that emphasizes critical paths. By giving more weight to timing-sensitive paths during wirelength
852   reduction, it would be possible to achieve better alignment between wirelength optimization and tim-
853   ing improvement. Another approach is to adopt mixed-size placement techniques that optimize both
854   macros and standard cells simultaneously. Since macro wirelength (macro HPWL) has a weaker cor-
855   relation with backend performance metrics, this approach could lead to a more balanced placement
856   outcome, particularly for designs where macro placement significantly impacts PPA. Additionally,
857   machine learning models that predict PPA based on placement features could provide valuable in-
858
      sights during placement optimization. These models would allow the algorithm to dynamically
      adjust its strategy based on predicted PPA outcomes, leading to more informed trade-offs between
859
      PPA.
