011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Symmetry is prevalent in multi-agent systems. The presence of symmetry, cou-
014            pled with the misuse of absolute coordinate systems, often leads to a large amount
015            of redundant representation space, significantly increasing the search space for
016
               learning policies and reducing learning efficiency. Effectively utilizing symme-
               try and extracting symmetry-invariant representations can significantly enhance
017
               multi-agent systems’ learning efficiency and overall performance by compress-
018
               ing the model’s hypothesis space and improving sample efficiency. The issue of
019            rotational symmetry in multi-agent reinforcement learning has received little at-
020            tention in previous research and is the primary focus of this paper. To address
021            this issue, we propose a rotation-invariant network architecture for continuous ac-
022            tion space tasks. This architecture utilizes relative coordinates between agents,
023            eliminating dependence on absolute coordinate systems, and employs a hypernet-
024            work to enhance the model’s fitting capability, enabling it to model MDPs with
025            more complex dynamics. It can be used for both predicting actions and evaluating
026            action values/utilities. In benchmark tasks, experimental results validate the im-
027
               pact of rotational symmetry on multi-agent decision systems and demonstrate the
               effectiveness of our method.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has made significant advancements and
      has been widely applied in areas such as traffic planning (Mushtaq et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2024)),
034
      power management Keren et al. (2024), gaming (Zhai et al. (2021); Li et al. (2018); Berner et al.
035
      (2019)), and Robotics (Wen et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2023)). However, compared to single-agent
036   reinforcement learning, MARL faces more challenging issues, including credit assignment( Yarah-
037   madi et al. (2023; 2024)), scalability( Ying et al. (2024); Ma et al. (2024)), and imperfect information
038   games( Zhang et al. (2024)). In this paper, we primarily focus on the symmetry problem in MARL.
039
      Symmetry is widely present in the natural world, and many studies in fields such as image process-
040
      ing, graph neural networks, and point clouds have focused on symmetry. In multi-agent reinforce-
041
      ment learning (MARL), research on symmetry is still in its infancy. Symmetry can be broadly cat-
042   egorized into permutation symmetry and rotational and mirror symmetry (in this paper, we refer to
043   both rotational and mirror symmetry as rotational symmetry, as they can be derived from orthogonal
044   transformations). Some research has been conducted on permutation symmetry in MARL (Wang
045   et al. (2019); Hu et al. (2021); Jianye et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2022)), and HPN (Jianye et al.
046   (2022)), with the most notable work being HPN. By designing a network structure with permutation-
047   invariant inductive bias, permutation-invariant representations can be naturally obtained, leading to
048   the corresponding action outputs. However, research on rotation invariance (RI) in MARL is still in
049   its early stages.
050   Rotational symmetry is prevalent in the real world. For instance, if a person learns traffic rules at a
051   driving school, they can easily generalize them to real-world traffic conditions without considering
052   whether they are facing north or south. Similarly, a basketball team, after training, can play in
053   a new arena without worrying about the actual orientation of the court. They only need to know
      their relative positions to teammates, opponents, and the basket. Humans naturally incorporate prior

      MARL tasks. However, it still cannot handle continuous rigid transformations in Euclidean space.

      Continuous rigid transformations involve rotating the agent’s coordinates around a fixed point by
089
      arbitrary angles rather than assuming rotations are only multiples of 90 degrees or simple mirror
090   reflections. Continuous transformations are more representative of real-world scenarios. Theoret-
091   ically, if a reasonable model or algorithm could account for rotational symmetry, an agent could
092   generalize knowledge learned from state s to the rotated state s′ , thereby significantly reducing the
093   state representation space.
094
      We propose a Relative Direction Hypernetwork (RDHNet) architecture to extract relative directional
095
      and positional information and then use a symmetry computation module to aggregate this informa-
096   tion. Effectively leveraging symmetry compresses redundant representation space, facilitating better
097   knowledge sharing among agents and enhancing learning efficiency and robustness. Additionally,
098   we utilize the high-order modeling capabilities of the hypernetwork module to improve the model’s
099   expressiveness, enabling it to handle tasks with more complex dynamics. RDHNet can be used to
100   construct not only an action value/utility evaluation network but also a policy network that produces
101   actions based on input states.
102   We conducted experiments on two continuous action tasks, Cooperative Prey Predator and Coop-
103   erative Navigation. The results demonstrate that, compared to other methods, RDHNet achieves
104   superior performance. Moreover, during execution, RDHNet does not require absolute coordinate
105   and directional information; it can make appropriate decisions based solely on relative observations
106   from the agent’s perspective, indicating that in situations where geographic positioning informa-
107   tion is unavailable, our method remains effective, whereas other methods may fail to function. We
      summarize our contributions as follows:

120   2.1   S YMMTRY IN GNN S AND P OINT C LOUDS
121
122   Recent advances in symmetry studies within Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and point cloud pro-
123   cessing have significantly influenced the development of invariant models to permutations, rotations,
124   and translations. For instance, the introduction of Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) (Kipf &
125   Welling (2016)) laid the foundation for permutation invariance in GNNs. In the context of point
126
      clouds, PointNet (Qi et al. (2017a)) and its successor PointNet++ (Qi et al. (2017b)) pioneered ap-
      proaches that handle unordered point sets directly, enabling permutation invariance.
127
128   Building on these foundational works, recent studies have explored rotational and translational
129   equivariance in graph neural networks (GNNs) and point cloud networks. Tensor Field Net-
130   works (Thomas et al. (2018)) and SE(3)-Transformers (Fuchs et al. (2020)) exemplify this trend
131   by incorporating roto-translation equivariant features, enabling more accurate and robust processing
132
      of 3D point clouds and molecular structures. Additionally, work (Maron et al. (2019)) extended the
      expressive power of GNNs by proving the capability of certain architectures to universally approxi-
133
      mate permutation-invariant functions, further advancing the theoretical understanding of symmetry
134
      in GNNs. DimeNet (Gasteiger et al. (2020)) captures angular dependencies between atoms in a
135   molecular graph, which enhances the network’s ability to model geometric structures without being
136   affected by the specific orientation or position of the molecule in space.
137
138
      2.2   S YMMTRY IN MARL
139
140   Some works have begun to address the issue of redundant space caused by symmetry in multi-agent
141   reinforcement learning (MARL). Methods such as ASN (Wang et al. (2019)), UPDeT (Hu et al.
142   (2021)), and HPN (Jianye et al. (2022)) have focused on the redundancy problem due to permuta-
143   tion symmetry. They modify the actor network by incorporating prior knowledge to compress the
144   redundant representation space caused by permutation order. The primary differences among them
145   are: ASN (Wang et al. (2019)) uses MLP as the basic network module, UPDeT (Hu et al. (2021))
146
      uses transformers, and HPN (Jianye et al. (2022)) uses hypernetworks. Essentially, they all integrate
      permutation symmetry into the network inductive bias to address the redundancy problem caused by
147
      different permutations of semantically identical states. However, these approaches do not address
148
      the issue of rotational symmetry.
149
150   Work(van der Pol et al. (2021)) was the first to address the rotational symmetry issue in MARL,
151   which involves symmetry operator pairs (Lg , Kg ) to the model. Works (Yu et al. (2023; 2024)) gen-
152
      erate rotated samples during training and introduce a rotational symmetry loss function to exploit
      symmetry. However, a major limitation of these works is that they can only handle rotational sym-
153
      metry at multiples of 90 degrees. They neither consider nor can be applied to continuous random
154
      rotational symmetry, which is precisely the focus of our work and is more aligned with real-world
155   scenarios.

192   Rotational Symmetry refers to the condition where rotating a state as a whole does not affect the
193   relative physical information contained within that state. This implies that many rotational mappings
194   of the same state lead to representational space redundancy. Let us further refine the observation oi .
195
      We distinguish the features of each entity into spatial coordinates pk and other information zk , so the
      observation can be represented as oi = [[p1 , z1 ]T , [p2 , z2 ]T , . . . , [pm , zm ]T ]. We then reconstruct
196
      oi as [[p1 , p2 , . . . , pm ]T , [z1 , z2 , . . . , zm ]T ]. Consider a binary function h : (P, Z) → Y , where
197
      P = [p1 , p2 , . . . , pm ]T , Z = [z1 , z2 , . . . , zm ]T , and Y = [y1 , y2 , . . . , ym ]T . U is the set of all m×m
198   rotation matrices, and u ∈ U is a specific rotation matrix. If h(P · u, Z) = h(P, Z) = Y, ∀u ∈ U ,
199   then h is called a rotation-invariant function. If a model satisfies the properties of the h function, we
200   can say that the model possesses rotational invariance.

205   Rotation-invariant multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) does not require an absolute coordi-
206   nate reference system; it only considers the relative positional relationships between entities. This
207   approach aligns more closely with human intuition and facilitates better knowledge generalization.
208   This chapter provides a detailed overview of the RDHNet framework’s structure.

353   5.1     TASK D OMAINS AND BASELINE
354
355   We tested the algorithm on two types of tasks. The first type is Cooperative Prey Predator, in
356   which the prey moves at a slower speed following a fixed strategy, and the goal is to control the
357
      predtars to get as close to the prey as possible. The second type is a cooperative navigation task,
      where the objective is to control the agents to cover every target point. We adopted a simplified
358
      strategy in both tasks to focus on the research problem studied in this paper, where agents use global
359
      observation information in all algorithms. In both tasks, the agents’ actions are force vectors, and
360   there are obstacles present. The agents need to avoid obstacles as much as possible while achieving
361   their task goals.
362

466   6                  C ONLUSION AND DISCUSSION
467
468   In this paper, we first thoroughly analyze the representation redundancy problem caused by symme-
469   try in multi-agent scenarios, distinguishing between permutation symmetry and rotational symmetry.
470   We then propose the RDHNet architecture, inspired by human observation and problem-solving ap-
471   proaches, to address the issue of rotational symmetry in multi-agent systems. Finally, we validate
472   our approach through experiments that reveal the objective hindrance posed by rotational symme-
473   try to multi-agent learning and demonstrate that RDHNet applies to a wide range of multi-agent
474   scenarios, further supporting our hypothesis: in specific scenarios and environments, Cartesian co-
475   ordinates are unnecessary, as human intuition perceives distance and angles rather than constructing
476
      a Cartesian coordinate system in the mind.
477   We also acknowledge certain limitations of this work. For instance, the experimental scenarios
478   lack diversity, the algorithm’s computational complexity increases quadratically with the number
479   of entities in the system, and we have yet to develop more effective optimization strategies for the
480   actor-critic version of RDHNet. These aspects will be the focus of our future work.
