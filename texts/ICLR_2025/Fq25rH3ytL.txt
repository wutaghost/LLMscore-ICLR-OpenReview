009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011
              Gradients are widely used to explain the decisions of deep neural networks. How-
012
              ever, as models become deeper and more complex, computing gradients become
013           challenging and sometimes infeasible, hindering traditional explanation methods.
014           Recently, the forward gradient method has garnered attention for training structure-
015           agnostic models with discontinuous objective functions. This method perturbs
016           only the parameters of interest for gradient computation and optimization. Inspired
017           by this, we investigate whether the forward gradient can be employed to explain
018           black-box models. In this work, we use the likelihood ratio method to estimate
019           output-to-input gradients and utilize them for the explanation of model decisions.
020           Additionally, we propose block-wise computation techniques to enhance estima-
021
              tion accuracy. Extensive experiments to explain various models, including CNNs,
              LSTMs, Phi-3, and CLIP, in black-box settings validate the effectiveness of our
022
              method, demonstrating good gradient estimation and improved explainability under
023
              the black-box setting.

      The use of gradients in model explanation can be broadly divided into two categories: class activation
055   mapping (Zhou et al., 2016; Chattopadhay et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020; Omeiza et al., 2019)
056   and saliency map (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Erion et al., 2021; Lundstrom et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
057   2023). Methods based on class activation map up-sample the feature map inner models for model
058   explanation. Gradient-based methods derive the gradients of inputs relative to the output, thereby
059   highlighting the model’s areas of interest. These two approaches are intuitive and efficient but become
060   infeasible when the model architecture is inaccessible.
061   In black-box machine learning scenarios (Bodria et al., 2023), where access to full knowledge of
062   computation graphs for optimization is unavailable, the problem becomes a Zeroth-order Optimization
063   (ZO) problem (Chen et al., 2017). Among various ZO strategies (Chen et al., 2019; Rando et al., 2024;
064   Kozak et al., 2023; Vemula et al., 2019), the likelihood ratio method (Peng et al., 2022) stands out. It
065   pushes the parameters out of the loss function by introducing noise, allowing for unbiased gradient
066   estimation. This method has shown competitive performance in training diverse neural networks,
067   comparable to that achieved with backpropagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986). Such promising results
068   inspire us to think about the plausibility of the likelihood ratio method for gradient estimation on
069
      inputs, facilitating black-box explanations through saliency maps. Motivated by this, we propose to
      use the likelihood ratio method to bridge the gap between gradient-based explanation methods and
070
      black-box scenarios, as illustrated in Fig. fig. 1, where we skip the access of backward pass of the
071
      model to get the gradient and explain the model decision.
072
073   Applying the likelihood ratio method to develop interpretation methods under black-box conditions is
074   a non-trivial task, presenting two significant challenges. First, there is the question of how to estimate
075
      gradients on various characteristics of data using the likelihood ratio method. Many existing studies
      on zeroth-order optimization assume full knowledge of the local computational structure (Peng et al.,
076
      2022; Jiang et al., 2023), a luxury not available in our context where the whole model’s structure,
077
      even the first layer for processing the input, is unknown. Second, we must address how to minimize
078   the estimation variance of gradients. The likelihood ratio method, by its nature of noise injection,
079   tends to have a high variance in gradient estimation (Peng et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023), a problem
080   exacerbated by the high dimensionality, such as those in the R224×224×3 space of the ImageNet-1K
081   dataset. Accurately estimating gradients in such high-dimensional spaces is challenging.
082
      In this study, we introduce a unified framework for explaining black models using gradients. Our
083
      approach begins with deriving estimated gradients of inputs under a black-box model set using the
084   likelihood ratio method. Then, we propose a blockwise computation pattern to address the challenge
085   of high variance in gradient estimation. In evaluation, we integrate our framework to explain various
086   models, including a series of computer vision models, text classification models, and the large
087   language model Phi (Abdin et al., 2024). We also verify the feasibility and generalization ability in
088   explaining multi-modal models against spurious correlations. There are three key findings revealed in
089   our work which can be summarized as follows:
090
             •  Interpretability of forward gradients: Compared to vanilla gradients, forward gradients
091
              offer comparable interpretability with greater scalability in explaining model decisions.
092
093          • Bridging the explanation gap: Forward gradients provide a crucial link between white-
094
              box explanation methods and the challenges of explaining black-box models.
095          • High computation and memory-consumption: Despite their explanatory power, forward
096           gradients suffer from high computational and memory costs, due to large estimation variance.
097
              This issue can only be mitigated by using a large number of copies during computation.

101   2.1   G RADIENTS FOR MODEL EXPLANATION
102
103   Gradients are widely used in explaining models, including the class activation mapping (CAM) (Zhou
104
      et al., 2016; Chattopadhay et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020; Omeiza et al., 2019) and the saliency map
      Sundararajan et al. (2017); Erion et al. (2021); Lundstrom et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2023).
105
106   CAM is first introduced in (Zhou et al., 2016), which employs the global average pooling neuron
107   activations to weakly localize the objects in the inputs for saliency mapping. The GradCAM (Selvaraju
      et al., 2017) inherits the idea of CAM and combines the model gradients with the activations of

109   by a lot of later works. Such as GradCAM++(Chattopadhay et al., 2018), SmoothGrad(Smilkov
110   et al., 2017), Smooth-Grad++ (Omeiza et al., 2019), and Grad-CAM++(Chattopadhay et al., 2018) to
111   further boost the visualization ability. CAM requires the full knowledge of model architecture and

114
      The generation of saliency maps (Sundararajan et al., 2017; 2016) requires the gradients of the
115   decision as inputs, which treat the model as a unified module regardless of the inner structure.
116   Compared with directly leveraging the vanilla gradients, the use of integrate gradients is more popular.
117   These methods gather the gradients of images along a path from the input to a reference image. The
118   reference image represents what it looks like when the features present in the input are missing.
119   Sundararajan et al. (2017) first proposed the use of the path attribution strategy, which can preserve
120   certain desirable axiomatic properties for the explanations. This work inspires others to further
121   investigations (Erion et al., 2021; Lundstrom et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023).
122
123   2.2       Z EROTH - ORDER OPTIMIZATION
124
125
      Zeroth-order optimization (Wang et al., 2018; Golovin et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019) is proposed to
      address challenges in optimizing the non-differential criteria. It typically uses random perturbation on
126
      input to estimate the gradient and follows the stochastic gradient descent to update the parameters of
127
      interest. Popular zeroth-order optimization methods include the simultaneous perturbation stochastic
128   approximation (SPSA) (Spall, 2000; 1997; Maryak & Chin, 2001; Granichin & Amelina, 2014),
129   evolutional strategy (ES) (Salimans et al., 2017), and likelihood ratio (LR) (Peng et al., 2022;
130   Jiang et al., 2023). SPSA individually perturbs each parameter and estimates the gradients. The
131   low efficiency largely hinders its application in large-scale optimization problems. ES and LR are
132   proposed to optimize deep learning models, which inject the noise into neuron weights and outputs
133   respectively. Among these, LR stands out for its compelling performance in accurate gradient
134   estimation (Jiang et al., 2023). In our work, we employ the LR to compute the gradient without
135   reliance on the chain-rule to explain model decisions.

      3.2   L IKELIHOOD RATIO METHOD FOR SALIENCY MAP OF BLACK - BOX MODELS
192
193
194   In this section, we introduce a novel, unified framework for generating gradient-based saliency
195
      maps to interpret the decision of black-box models. As depicted in Fig. fig. 2, our framework
      encompasses three steps. Initially, we introduce perturbations to the images by injecting the noise
196
      before the model’s forward pass. Subsequently, we compute the proxy gradient of each perturbed
197
      copy respectively and then average them to approximate the true gradient. Finally, we employ the
198   estimated gradient to craft the saliency map for the model explanation. The following paragraphs
199   will detail the three steps in our proposed framework.
200
201
      Injecting noise to inputs. Within the context of a black-box model f , which is differentiable almost
      everywhere but whose internal gradients are inaccessible, consider that we employ a technique of
202
      noise injection, in which we add small random noise z into the input x. Intuitively, if the noise has a
203
      neutral mean (zero) and a variance small enough, the expectation of gradient w.r.t. the noise-added
204   input, Ez (gc (x + z)), would be close enough to the true gradient gc (x). Practically, one can easily
205   verify that as the standard deviation σ of the noise approaches zero, the expectation of gradient w.r.t.
206   the noise-added input converges to the true gradient, i.e., limσ→0+ Ez (g(x + z)) = g(x), implying
207   the use of Ez (gc (x + z)) as a viable approximation of gc (x). This technique allows us to control the
208   precision loss of expected noise-adding gradients by selecting noise z with a distribution close to 0.
209   In many situations, such as the Gaussian noise N (0, σ 2 I), it is equivalent to adjusting the σ.
210   Likelihood ratio gradient estimator. Directly computing Ez (gc (x + z)) still necessitates access
211   to the gradient, which exactly contradicts our objective of addressing the black-box setting where
212   such information is unavailable. Therefore, we propose our likelihood ratio gradient estimator for
213   black-box models. Initially, by forwarding the noise-added input into the model, we obtain the
214   corresponding class activation fc (x0 + z). Then, we compute the proxy gradient for each noise-added
215   input by multiplying the class activation with the negative gradient of the noise’s log probability
      density function. Finally, we average n samples of proxy gradients to form our likelihood ratio

      3.4   T HE SELECTION OF THE CLASS ACTIVATION fc
325
326
      In the previous discussion, a gradient-based saliency map harnesses the gradient of the class activation
327   w.r.t. inputs, gc (x0 ) = ∇x fc (x)|x=x0 , to explain the decision of neural networks. While we have
328   addressed the “undifferentiable” problem due to inaccessibility for black-box models by utilizing
329   the likelihood principle mentioned above, there remains an open question: what if we can’t access
330   the class activation fc of black-box models? Here, we propose solutions for three possible scenes in
331   real-world applications.
332
      Soft- & Hard-label decision. When logits or confidence of models are given, we can directly use
333   them to estimate the gradient of the loss function, i.e., cross-entropy, to the inputs for saliency map
334   generation in our framework. When only the predicted label is given, i.e., the hard label, we can
335   not directly compute the loss function for gradient estimation. To address this issue, we propose to
336   compute the nearest path distance between the predicted class and ground truth in WorldNet as the
337   surrogate criteria to evaluate the classification performance.
338
      Text-based decision. (Multimodal-) language models output the texts for users’ queries, making it
339   difficult to define the performance criteria for gradient estimation directly. To address this problem,
340   we propose a prompt construction strategy. Specifically, when querying the decision of one image,
341   we will additionally ask the model to respond to our answer by following the format of “The input
342   image belongs to [MASK]”, where the mask is filled by taking one category of WordNet. Then, we
343   will compute the nearest distance between two nodes, i.e., one that comes from the model and the
344   ground truth, for performance evaluation and gradient estimation.

348
349   In our experiments, we set up three different tasks to verify the use of forward gradient in explaining
350   model decisions: the saliency map generation for computer vision models, sentiment analysis
351   for language models, and bias interpretation for vision-language models. For a vision-involved
352   explanation, we leverage 5, 000 fine-grained noisy blocks to estimate the forward gradient. For text
353   explanation, we leverage 1, 000–96, 000 noisy copies of the original inputs to estimate the forward
354   gradients. We use the auto-grad function provided by the PyTorch library to compute the vanilla

      48 GB VRAM.

      more closely with human understanding of natural language processing tasks.

      5      C ONCLUSION
523
      The likelihood ratio method makes it possible to train neural networks with only forward passes.
524
      Inspired by this, we design a pipeline to employ the likelihood ratio method in gradient estimation,
525
      particularly for interpreting model decisions in black-box scenarios. To address the issue of large gra-
526   dient estimation variance, we propose a blockwise computation technique that balances computational
527   cost with task performance. We explore the potential of the forward gradient in explaining models
528   across three tasks: saliency map generation, sentiment analysis on text models, and interpreting
529   spurious correlations learned by vision-language models. The experimental results demonstrate the
530   effectiveness and scalability of the forward gradient in explaining black-box models. This study
531   paves the way for future research in explaining black-box models.
532
533   L IMITATIONS
534
535   While the forward gradient offers a method to estimate gradients without relying on the chain
536   rule—facilitating the explanation of black-box models based on gradient information—it is computa-
537   tionally expensive. For larger models, this approach may require thousands of iterations to achieve
538   an acceptable estimation. Developing effective variance reduction methods is crucial not only for
539   training models without backpropagation (BP) but also for improving the explainability of black-box
      models.
