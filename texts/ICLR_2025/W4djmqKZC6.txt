                                                            ABSTRACT
008
009
                 We propose in this paper an analytically new construct of a diffusion model whose
010
                 drift and diffusion parameters yield a faster time-decaying Signal to Noise Ratio in
011
                 the forward process. The proposed methodology significantly accelerates the forward
012              diffusion process, reducing the required diffusion time-steps from around 1000 seen in
013              conventional models to 200-500 without compromising image quality in the reverse-
014              time diffusion. Additionally, in a departure from conventional models which typically
015              use time-consuming multiple runs, we introduce a parallel data-driven model to gen-
016              erate a reverse-time diffusion trajectory in a single run of the model. The construct
017              cleverly carries out the learning of the diffusion coefficients via an estimate of the
018              structure of clean images. The resulting collective block-sequential generative model
019              eliminates the need for MCMC-based sub-sampling correction for safeguarding and
020
                 improving image quality, which further improve the acceleration of image generation.
                 Collectively, these advancements yield a generative model that is at least 4 times faster
021
                 than conventional approaches, while maintaining high fidelity and diversity in gen-
022
                 erated images, hence promising widespread applicability in rapid image synthesis
023              tasks.

026   1     INTRODUCTION
027
028   Generative diffusion models (GDMs) have recently emerged as powerful tools for image modeling and
029   numerous other applications (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song
030   et al., 2021b), offering exceptional fidelity and generative diversity (Yang et al., 2023). In contrast to
031   existing generative models, like Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and
032   Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2013), GDMs are more stable in training and less
033   sensitive to hyper-parameter selection (Kingma & Welling, 2019).
034   Generative diffusion approaches gradually corrupt image data with increasingly random noise in the
035   forward diffusion steps. The noise removal1 is progressively learned in the reverse diffusion to recover
036   the desired image data to best match its initial distribution. GDMs have been represented as discrete as
037   well as continuous-time processes. Discrete-time processes were initially used in diffusion probabilistic
038   models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) and later refined as the denoising diffusion probabilistic models
039   (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020), The training task was to learn the reverse posterior distribution by maximizing
040   a variational lower bound on the model log likelihood. Later Score-based Generative Models (SGMs)
041
      - represented by Noise-Conditional Score Networks (NCSNs) (Song & Ermon, 2019) and (Song et al.,
      2021b) - were successfully used to achieve high quality image samples through denoising score matching
042
      and annealed Langevin dynamics. The continuous-time GDMs are fundamentally based on continuous
043
      stochastic differential equations (SDEs), with a continuous forward diffusion model defined to add
044   random noise to an image according to some selected drift and diffusion parameters, and a reverse
045
046        1 This may equivalently be viewed as learning the statistical structure of the data for given additive white noise.



                                                             1

047
      diffusion (Anderson, 1982) is run by a reverse-time SDE governed by a score-based model to be learned
048
      by deep neural networks.
049
050   While effective, the performance of conventional diffusion models entails a slow convergence, with a
051   quality image generation requiring a large number of time-steps, consequently leading to an increased
052
      computational complexity. To this end, much effort (Jolicoeur-Martineau et al., 2021a;b; Salimans & Ho,
      2021; Zhang & Chen, 2023; Dockhorn et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022) has been dedicated to
053
      reducing this lengthy process and to improving the quality for prediction-correction methods by Markov
054
      chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) subsampling & modified Langevin dynamics (Song et al., 2021b). Current
055   models have, however,only focused on reducing the reverse trajectory by employing sub-sampling or
056   fast ODE solver based strategies.
057
058
      In this paper, we propose an alternative approach and use insights from statistical mechanics of particles
      to account for local (pixel-level) SNR in driving the microscopic dynamics of the diffusion process. In
059
      so doing, our novel diffusion model leverages the structure of clean image data to learn the drift and
060
      diffusion parameters at a microscopic level to accelerate the forward diffusion. Specifically, these param-
061   eters increase the rate of degradation according to image pixel-SNR in contrast to the uniform regime of
062   existing models. This is inspired by the well-known water pouring algorithm paradigm (Gallager, 1968)
063   employed in multi-channel communication systems. The water pouring algorithm allocates power to a
064   channel in accordance with the noise-level experienced in that channel, with more degraded channels
065   getting more power. Intuitively, one may interpret the macroscopic forward diffusion as a parallel
066   (bundle) process of microscopic forward diffusion processes occurring on individual pixels in parallel.
067   In our model, the forward diffusion scheduling is dependent, as detailed later, on the initial clean pixel
068   values while each pixel maintains its own diffusion independent from others. We demonstrate that by
069
      employing this pixel based scheduling strategy, we can achieve the target goal of reaching isotropic Gaus-
      sian distribution on all the pixels much faster than the conventional pixel agnostic diffusion scheduling
070
      in the forward diffusion stage.
071
072   With such an image-aware forward diffusion in hand, we proceed with an autoencoder to learn the
073   combined diffusion schedule across all the pixels of a noisy image. This learned schedule is subsequently
074   used in a data-driven reverse-time diffusion model to generate the complete trajectory of the reverse-time
075
      diffusion. While conventional models generate the reverse trajectory one step at a time requiring multiple
      model runs, we leverage the structural information learned in the scheduling strategy to generate the
076
      whole reverse-time diffusion path in one go without compromising on the over-all generated image
077
      quality. As a result of this combined strategy, we are able to accelerate the reverse-time diffusion process
078   by at least 4 times in terms of the total generation time. On account of its relatively simple structure,
079   our pixel-wise diffusion has the potential to be incorporated into existing methods to further speed
080   up sampling. As an example, the latent diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022b), with some special
081   care given to the image scale, should be able to swap the conventional diffusion with our new model.
082   However, we only focus on our novel diffusion model in this paper.

      3     METHODOLOGY: IMAGE AWARE DIFFUSION
156
157   3.1   MOTIVATION
158
      In the forward direction of a diffusion process, a clean image with d pixels, represented as x0 = [x 01 , . . . , x 0d ]
159
      is diffused iteratively in T steps. With large enough T , we get xT ∼ N (0, Id ), indicating that the diffusion
160   of the various data pixels leads to approximately 0 SNR. The water pouring algorithm employed in
161   multi-channel communication systems(Gallager, 1968), similarly addresses assignment of signal power
162   distribution across frequency channels with different ambient noise powers to maximize SNR. Faced with
163   our objective of all pixels simultaneously achieving approximately 0 SNR over a certain time interval (the
164   total number of steps, T ), it makes sense to diffuse higher-valued pixels at a faster rate than lower-valued
165   pixels.

      3.3   REVERSE-TIME DIFFUSION MODELING
330
331   To proceed with a reverse-time diffusion we estimate x̂δ , βi , αi and αi using the VAE, G θ (xi , i ), as
332   discussed in the previous subsection. In conventional designs the same trained network is used iteratively
333   to generate the reverse trajectory sample at a particular time-step by using the generated sample for
334   previous time-step as input. This provides evidence that the same architecture has a sufficient capacity
335   to process the semantic information hidden in the noisy image at any time-step. Theoretically, based on
336   the Universal Approximation Theorem, the conventional GDMs can also try to generate all the steps
337
      parallelly by relying on a prohibitively large neural network much too difficult to train.
338   While conventional GDMs learn to approximate the posterior distribution, q(xi −1 |xi , x0 ) as p θ (xi −1 |xi ),
339   our reverse diffusion model on the other hand approximates it using p φ (xi −1 |xi , x̂δ ). Thus, the scale
340   estimator autoencoder, G θ (xi , i ) is central to the proposed model. It generates the image scale ap-
341   proximation as an informed prior regularizing our parallelized reverse diffusion model. The overall
342   architecture of our parallelized reverse diffusion model using a U-net architecture, is shown in Fig.-4 in
343
      Appenix C. In adapting it to our proposed methodology, the following modifications are in order:
344
            1. We also fuse (by addition to feature maps) x̂δ predicted from the image scale autoencoder
345            G θ (x i , i ).
346

               all the preceding time-steps ( j ∈ {i − 1, · · · , 1}) in different channels of the last layer. While the
348
               one time complexity of our model is higher than existing competing models, unlike the latter,
349            only a single execution is required of the trained model to obtain a clean image x0 . This thus
350            results in an overall reduction in sample generation time.

403
      requires orders of magnitude more steps as a result of correction required due to MCMC subsampling.

      6    CONCLUSION
453
454
      In this paper, we have introduced a novel forward diffusion model that significantly improves upon the
455
      limitations of conventional models in terms of convergence speed and computational efficiency. By
456   leveraging the microscopic structure of clean images to learn the drift and diffusion coefficients, our
457   model degrades the Signal to Noise Ratio much faster than traditional approaches. Drawing inspiration
458   from the water pouring algorithm, we implemented a pixel-based scheduling strategy that optimizes
459   forward diffusion by considering the initial values of individual pixels. This method achieves isotropic
460   Gaussian distribution across the pixels more efficiently than conventional, pixel-agnostic diffusion
461   methods.
462   Furthermore, we utilized an autoencoder to learn a comprehensive diffusion schedule. The learned
463   knowledge of the global structure of the clean image inspired us to develop a reverse-time data driven
464   diffusion model to generate the entire reverse-time diffusion trajectory in one step. This approach
465   not only maintained image quality but also accelerated the reverse-time diffusion process by up to
466   10 times compared to existing models. Our findings demonstrate that addressing the inefficiencies
467   of universal diffusion in generative models through a detailed, pixel-focused approach can lead to
468   substantial improvements in performance, paving the way for more effective and efficient generative
469   image modeling.
