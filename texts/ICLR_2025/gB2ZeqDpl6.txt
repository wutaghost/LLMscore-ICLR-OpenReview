009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The prediction modeling of drug-target interactions is crucial to drug discovery and
012            design, which has seen rapid advancements owing to deep learning technologies.
013            Recently developed methods, such as those based on graph neural networks (GNNs)
014            and Transformers, demonstrate exceptional performance across various datasets by
015            effectively extracting structural information. However, the benchmarking of these
016            novel methods often varies significantly in terms of hyperparameter settings and
017            datasets, which limits algorithmic progress. In view of these, we conduct a compre-
018
               hensive survey and benchmark for drug-target interaction modeling from a structure
               perspective, via integrating tens of explicit (i.e., GNN-based) and implicit (i.e.,
019
               Transformer-based) structure learning algorithms. To this end, we first unify the
020
               hyperparameter setting within each class of structure learning methods. Moreover,
021            we conduct a macroscopical comparison between these two classes of encoding
022            strategies as well as the different featurization techniques that inform molecules’
023            chemical and physical properties. We then carry out the microscopical comparison
024            between all the integrated models across the six datasets, via comprehensively
025            benchmarking their effectiveness and efficiency. Remarkably, the summarized
026            insights from the benchmark studies lead to the design of model combos. We
027            demonstrate that our combos can achieve new state-of-the-art performance on
028            various datasets associated with cost-effective memory and computation.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The prediction modeling of drug-target interactions (DTI) has emerged as an irreplaceable task for
      efficacious therapeutic interventions. The binding affinity between a drug molecule and its target
034
      protein plays a significant role in the design and repurpose of drugs, where a high affinity typically
035
      indicates the desired therapeutics, target specificity, long residence, and drug resistance delay (Hughes
036   et al., 2011; Copeland et al., 2006; Swinney, 2004). The precise modeling of DTI can expedite the
037   drug discovery process and circumvent the associated cost (Ashburn & Thor, 2004; Strittmatter, 2014).
038   Deep learning based frameworks have recently revolutionized this field, enabling more accurate and
039   efficient predictions compared with laboratory experimental methods (Wen et al., 2017; Abbasi et al.,
040   2021; Huang et al., 2020a).
041
      Within the deep learning frameworks (Öztürk et al., 2018; 2019), drugs are commonly represented
042   using the Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System (SMILES) (Weininger, 1988a), and proteins
043   are represented as sequences of amino acids. These representations are processed by separate
044   convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Krizhevsky et al., 2017; He et al., 2016) and subsequently
045   integrated and processed using a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) for DTI prediction. It is notorious that
046   the reliance on sequence-based representations can result in the loss of structural information, which
047   can potentially compromise the DTI predictive capability. From the drug perspective, molecular
048   structure modeling helps identify the specific binding sites (Ma et al., 2011), contribute to predicting
049   pharmacokinetic properties (Ekins et al., 2007), and allow conformational flexibility (Karplus &
050
      Kuriyan, 2005).
051   To address this problem, a number of drug algorithms have been proposed to promote DTI prediction,
052   which can be categorized into explicit and implicit structure learning. First, graph neural networks
053   (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Nguyen et al., 2020) have been widely adopted to learn the molecular
      structures, owing to their ability to directly operate on graph-based representations of molecules.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      By explicitly propagating information through the graph, GNNs can learn node and edge features
055
      and thereby capture the structural and functional relationships between atoms and bonds. Second,
056   Transformers, originally focused on natural language processing (Vaswani et al., 2017a), have also
057   shown promise in biomedical applications (Huang et al., 2020b; Chen et al., 2020). They rely on
058   self-attention mechanisms to implicitly weight the correlations between different parts of the input
059   SMILES, allowing them to capture long-range dependencies and contextual information.
060
      While these techniques contribute to the learning of drug structures, there is still a key knob under-
061
      explored: we lack a systematic study to benchmark their effectiveness and efficiency. Without such a
062   standardized benchmark, it is unachievable to offer fair comparisons and subsequently summarize
063   designing philosophy necessary to inform DTI. There have been several surveys and benchmarks
064   on computational methods for DTI prediction (Öztürk et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2020a; 2021;
065   Xu et al., 2022), which leave out the recent developments of structure learning algorithms and
066   unavoidably fail to focus on drug structure benchmarking. Moreover, although massive efforts (Bal
067   et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2020) have been made to explore the effectiveness of
068   modeling structural information, they predominantly use their proprietary training hyperparameters,
069   datasets, and evaluation metrics. Due to the various settings, one cannot reach convincing answers
070
      whether a configuration of structure encoders and/or featurization methods generally performs well.
      The complex of DTI classification and regression tasks and datasets complicates the benchmark
071
      comparison.
072
073   In this study, we introduce GTB-DTI, a comprehensive benchmark customized for GNN and
074   Transformer-based methodologies for DTI prediction. i) We thoroughly examine the implementation
075   details for each category of drug structure learning methods and integrate three widely-used datasets
076
      for classification and regression tasks, respectively. Then, we harmonize the sensitive hyperparameters
      across different methods using a greedy search to identify an optimal sweet spot configuration. The
077
      unified setting lays the foundation for a fair and reproducible benchmark. ii) To gain macroscopical
078
      insights into the structure encoders and featurization methods, we fix the drug encoder to be either
079   GNN or Transformer-based approaches and benchmark these two strategies in the various settings.
080   We also integrate tens of drug features given their importance to inform molecules’ chemistry and
081   physical properties and evaluate them on the representative datasets. iii) To gain macroscopical
082   insights into nuance between 31 concerned models, we conduct the benchmark studies of their effec-
083   tiveness on the six datasets with the unified setting, Moreover, we assess the efficiency of each method
084   by measuring peak GPU memory usage, running time, and convergences. iv) The comprehensive
085   study finally provides a number of surprising observations: ❶ The CNN-encoder accompanied with
086   integer features has the close protein embedding performance compared to the Transformer or larger
087
      language models, but they are more efficient. ❷ The explicit and implicit structure encoders for drugs
      exhibit unequal performances across the different datasets, which suggests their hybrid usage for
088
      generalization purpose. ❸ Inspired from these insights, we conclude with a model combos that leads
089
      us to attaining state-of-the-art (SOTA) regression results and performing similarly to SOTA in the
090   DTI classifications. Our combos further deliver cost-effective memory usage and running time as
091   well as faster convergence, which can serve as new baseline for the following explorations.

097   In this research, we focus on the formulations of recently-emerging structure modeling approaches
098   for drug molecules, which could be categorized into explicit methods based on graph neural networks
099
      and implicit methods based on transformer. The target proteins are learned by the sophisticated
      tools of convolutional/recurrent neural networks (CNNs/RNNs) or transformers, after which both the
100
      molecules’ and proteins’ embeddings are integrated to facilitate interaction prediction. We will also
101
      summarize and benchmark the various widely-adopted molecule features.

      2.3   F EATURE P ROCESSING M ETHODS
181
182   Beyond the drugs’ structure or sequence learning with GNNs or Transformers, the extra molecular
183   properties, such as molecular weight, solubility, and lipophilicity, are crucial for building accurate
184   and quantitative drug-target relationship models. We summarize two typical featurization methods.
185
      Sequence Processing Methods. Both drugs and proteins are input as strings of ASCII charac-
186
      ters, whose features can be extracted using statistical solutions. Integer encoding (Nguyen et al.,
187
      2020) simply converts the string to a sequence of integers, which assigns an integer to each char-
188   acter. The N-gram (Dong et al., 2005) captures the statistical dependencies between characters in
189   an input string. Specifically, a 3-gram model breaks down a sequence S = {s1 , s2 , ..., sm } into
190   {[s1 , s2 , s3 ], [s2 , s3 , s4 ], ..., [sm−2 , sm−1 , sm ]}, analyzing the relationship between adjacent charac-
191   ters.
192
      Drug-unique Featurization Methods. The additional chemical properties and structural details
193
      of SMILES strings are often considered to gain a more comprehensive understanding. Extended-
194   Connectivity Fingerprints (ECFP) (Morgan, 1965; Rogers & Hahn, 2010), involves generating unique
195   identifiers for atoms based on their local chemical environment and iteratively updating these through
196   a hash function to capture a broader molecular context, ultimately producing a set of fingerprints that
197   represent the molecule’s overall structure. Another approach, RDKit, is used to convert SMILES into
198   molecular graphs (Landrum et al., 2006; Nguyen et al., 2020), where nodes represent the physical and
199   chemical properties of molecules, and bonds are represented by an adjacency matrix. For example,
200   atomic properties such as atom type, degree, and hydrogen information (like the number of explicit
201   hydrogens) are all crucial for constructing a graph. More detailed properties can be found in Appendix
202
      F.
203   Embedding Featurization Methods. Embedding methods are used to translate these discrete se-
204   quences into continuous embedding spaces. Notably, Smi2Vec (Quan et al., 2018) and Prot2Vec (As-
205   gari & Mofrad, 2015) convert discrete tokens of drug SMILES and protein sequences into vectors
206   that encapsulate semantic and syntactic similarities, effectively grouping similar tokens together
207   in vector space. Additionally, pretrained language models (Bal et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2022) are
208
      increasingly utilized to leverage large-scale learned patterns, fine-tuned to analyze complex protein
      data representations effectively.

      (ESM2) (Lin et al., 2022). The results of various combinations of drug and protein encoders are listed

      in Table 1.

      on DTI tasks. When the encoder for the protein sequence is fixed, drug features extracted by the

451   We are thus motivated to integrate these powerful modules and shed novel insight into the design
452   philosophy of drug-target interaction modeling. Our model combos are illustrated in Fig. 5, where
453   the multi-scale CNNs and hybrid networks of molecular Transformer and GNNs are adopted to
454   learn the representations of proteins and drugs, respectively. As shown by the graph encoder part
455
      in Fig. 5, the hybrid networks augment the differential attention matrix in molecular Transformer
      with inter-atomic distances and graph adjacency matrix (Maziarka et al., 2020), which provides
456
      the 3D and 2D molecule conformations to further facilitate atom interaction learning. Specifically,
457
      given the projections of molecular input at an attention head, i.e., Q, K, V ∈ RN ×d , the adjacent
458   matrix A ∈ {0, 1}N ×N , and the inter-atomic distances matrix D ∈ RN ×N obtained using RDkit,
459   the augmented attention is calculated as:
460                                                          √

462   where g(·) is a row-wise softmax function, and λa , λd and λg denote scalars weighting the self-
463   attention, distance, and adjacency matrices, respectively. Besides the implicit and explicit structure
464   learning, we integrate the features from drug SMILES. It is notable that simply utilizing the SMILES
      representation extracted from a transformer for downstream tasks does not perform as well as GNN.
465
      To align with the protein embedding paradigm, we adopt a simple CNN to unearth potential SMILES
466   information, as suggested in (Zhao et al., 2021). Subsequently, due to the fact that cross-attention is
467   more complex and hard to optimize, we implement a straightforward attention mechanism to integrate
468   the representations of the drug graph and SMILES, denoted as fG and fS , respectively, using a
469   weighting parameter λ, as follows:
470                              fD = λ · fG + (1 − λ) · fS , λ = MLP (MLP(fG ) + MLP(fS )) .                                                                                        (7)

817   A    R ELATED W ORKS
818
819   GNN-based Methods GNNs play a crucial role in mining the intricate features of drug molecules for
820   drug-target prediction. Numerious models, including Graph Convolutional Network (GCN), Graph
821   Isomorphism Network (GIN), and Graph Attention Network (GAT) have been utilized (Nguyen
822
      et al., 2020; Quan et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2020;?; Jin et al., 2021) to process and
      enhance drug features. Additionally, MGraphDTA (Yang et al., 2022) employs a multi-scale GNN
823
      architecture, while DeepGLSTM (Mukherjee et al., 2022) leverages parallel GNN structures for drug
824
      representation. DeepNC integrates advanced techniques from generalized aggregation networks (Li
825   et al., 2020) and hypergraph convolution (Bai et al., 2021) to improve feature extraction. BACPI (Li
826   et al., 2022) develops a bi-directional attention network to integrate the representations of drug
827   molecules and proteins, enhancing their mutual interaction. Besides, BridgeDPI (Wu et al., 2022)
828   innovates by incorporating bridging nodes between proteins and drugs, utilizing a three-layer GNN
829   for graph embeddings.
830
      Transformer-based Methods Transformers, known for their efficacy in handling sequence data, are
831   extensively applied in drug and protein feature processing. For instance, models like MolTrans (Huang
832   et al., 2020b) and FOTFCPI (Yin et al., 2024) employ self-attention mechanisms to refine embeddings
833   by focusing on drug and protein substructures. MRBDTA (Zhang et al., 2022) uses multi-head
834   attention and skip connection to enhance drug and protein representation. Additionally, a cross-
835   attention mechanism (Pan et al., 2023b; Kurata & Tsukiyama, 2022) is employed to facilitate the
836   integration of drug and protein features, enabling effective mutual querying. TDGraphDTA (Zhu
837   et al., 2023) captures contextual relationships between molecular substructures by using a multi-
838   head cross-attention mechanism and graph optimization. Lastly, DrugormerDTI (Hu et al., 2023)
839
      incorporates degree centrality with positional information to highlight the positional relevance of
      amino acids in proteins.
840
841   Input and Featurization Structural information is crucial at the input stage for models such as
842   BridgeDPI (Wu et al., 2022). Various libraries, such as DGLGraph (Wang et al., 2019), DGL-
843   lifeSci (Li et al., 2021), and RDKit (Landrum et al., 2006), are employed to process input SMILES of
844   drugs, with RDKit (Landrum et al., 2006) being pivotal for converting SMILE strings into molecular
845
      graphs and extracting diverse chemical properties, including chemical bonds, hydrogen presence,
      electron properties, and so on. Additionally, some approaches (Wang et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2020;
846
      Li et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021) incorporate molecular fingerprints (Rogers & Hahn, 2010) to
847
      capture local chemical information. For protein sequences, typical pre-processing involves converting
848   amino acid sequences into N-grams (Pan et al., 2023a; Dong et al., 2005) or integers (Nguyen et al.,
849   2020) sequences. To enhance the expressiveness of embeddings, some models leverage pre-trained
850   Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013; Quan et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; Tsubaki et al.,
851   2018; Lin et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2022) or pretrained protein language models (Bal et al., 2024).

867   This section provides a comprehensive overview of 31 DTI methods, which are classified into
868   GNN-based and Transformer-based approaches. The DTI framework can be simplified as using
869   two encoders to process drugs and proteins separately, followed by an MLP to handle the integrated
870   representations.
871
872
873
      B.1     GNN- BASED M ETHODS
874
875
876   B.1.1    GCN
877
878   ⋆ GraphDTA-GCN (Nguyen et al., 2020): GraphDTA-GCN uses GCN to process the molecular
879   graph, which is derived from SMILES using the RDkit tool, and a simple CNN with integer encoding
880   to handle protein sequences.
881   ⋆ GraphCPI-GCN (Quan et al., 2019): Similar to GraphDTA, GraphCPI-GCN employs 3-gram
882   encoding with pretrained Word2Vec to process protein sequences, followed by a CNN to handle the
883   protein embeddings.
884
      ⋆ MGraphDTA (Yang et al., 2022): MGraphDTA utilizes a multiscale GCN, inspired by dense
885   connections, and a multiscale CNN to process drug graphs and protein sequences, respectively.
886
887   ⋆ SAGDTA (Zhang et al., 2021): Similar to GraphDTA, SAGDTA introduces global or hierarchical
888
      pooling after GCN to aggregate node representations weightedly.
889   ⋆ EmbedDTI (Jin et al., 2021): For protein sequences, EmbedDTI leverages GloVe for pretraining
890   amino acid feature embeddings, which are then fed into a CNN. For drugs, it constructs both an atom
891   graph and a substructure graph to capture structural information at different levels, processed by
892   GCN.
893   ⋆ DeepGLSTM (Mukherjee et al., 2022): DeepGLSTM processes molecular graphs using a parallel
894   GCN module composed of three GCNs with different layers. For protein sequences, it adopts a
895   bi-LSTM.
896
      ⋆ CPI (Tsubaki et al., 2018): CPI processes drug graphs using GCN. The protein sequence is handled
897
      via n-gram with integer encoding, followed by a CNN.
898
899   ⋆ DeepNC (Tran et al., 2022): DeepNC adopts advanced techniques from generalized aggregation
900   networks and hypergraph convolution, two variants of GCN, to capture the representations of drug.
901
      For protein sequences, it uses a simple CNN.
902   ⋆ DrugBAN (Zhang et al., 2022): DrugBAN employs GCN and CNN blocks to encode molecular
903   graph and proteins, respectively. Then they use a bilinear attention network module to learn local
904   interactions between the representations of drugs and proteins.
905   ⋆ BridgeDPI (Wu et al., 2022): BridgeDPI innovates by constructing a learnable drug–protein
906   association network, which is processed using a three-layer GNN for graph embeddings. The learned
907   representations for drug and protein pairs are then concatenated for further processing.
908
      ⋆ ColdDTA (Fang et al., 2023): ColdDTA removes the subgraphs of drugs. For the model, they
909
      adopt the dense GCN and multiscale CNN from MGraphDTA as the encoders for drugs and proteins,
910
      respectively. Additionally, an attention-based method is developed to integrate representations for
911   improved prediction.
912
913   ⋆ IMAEN (Zhang et al., 2024): IMAEN employs a molecular augmentation mechanism to enhance
914
      molecular structures by fully aggregating molecular node neighborhood information. It then uses
      multiscale GCN and CNN for drug and protein processing, respectively.
915
916   ⋆ GanDTI (Wang et al., 2021): Inspired by residual networks, GanDTI add the input drug fingerprints
917   to the output of three GCN layers as graph node features and use summation to get the final drug
      representation.

      B.1.2    GAT
919
920   ⋆ GraphDTA-GAT (Nguyen et al., 2020): GraphDTA-GAT adopts a GAT as the encoder for drugs,
921   while other components remain the same as in GraphDTA-GCN.
922   ⋆ GraphDTA-GATGCN (Nguyen et al., 2020): GraphDTA-GATGCN adopts a combination of GAT
923   and GCN as the encoder for drugs, while other components remain the same as in GraphDTA-GCN.
924
925
      ⋆ GraphCPI-GAT (Quan et al., 2019): GraphDTA-CPI adopts a GAT as the encoder for drugs, while
      other components remain the same as in GraphCPI-GCN.
926
927   ⋆ GraphCPI-GATGCN (Quan et al., 2019): GraphCPI-GATGCN adopts a combination of GAT and
928   GCN as the encoder for drugs, while other components remain the same as in GraphCPI-GCN.
929   ⋆ BACPI (Li et al., 2022): BACPI adopts a GAT and a CNN for the features of the fingerprints
930   and protein sequence, respectively. These features are then fed into a bi-directional attention neural
931   network to obtain integrated representations.
932
      ⋆ PGraphDTA-CNN (Bal et al., 2024): PGraphDTA-CNN is a straightforward method that utilizes
933
      GAT for drug feature extraction and CNN for protein sequences.

      B.2     GIN
936
937   ⋆ GraphDTA-GIN (Nguyen et al., 2020): GraphDTA-GAT adopts a GAT as the encoder for drugs,
938   while other components remain the same as in GraphDTA-GCN.
939
940
      ⋆ GraphCPI-GIN (Quan et al., 2019): GraphDTA-GAT adopts a GAT as the encoder for drugs, while
      other components remain the same as in GraphDTA-GCN.
941
942   ⋆ SubMDTA (Pan et al., 2023a): SubMDTA utilizes a pretrained GIN encoder obtained through
943   contrastive learning for the molecular graph. For protein sequences, it employs N-gram embedding
944   with different N to extract features at various scales, which are then processed by a BiLSTM.
945
946   B.3     T RANSFORMER - BASED M ETHODS
947
      B.3.1    S ELF - ATTENTION
948
949   ⋆ AMMVF (Wang et al., 2023): AWMVF introduces the multi-head mechanism to GAT to learn
950   features in different spaces, and the update function is obtained through the concatenation of different
951   heads’ outputs.
952
      ⋆ IIFDTI (Cheng et al., 2022): IIFDTI model attains the drug matrix and protein matrix and inputs
953
      them to the bi-directional encoder-decoder block, which considers both the drug and target directions.
954   The decoder is mainly composed of multi-head attention.
955
956   ⋆ MolTrans (Huang et al., 2020b): MolTrans uses transformer encoder layers to augment the
957
      embedding of sub-structure sequences of proteins and drugs.
958   ⋆ FOTFCPI (Yin et al., 2024): Similar to MolTrans, FOTFCPI uses transformer encoder layers to
959   extract the features of protein and drug fragments after the embedding layers.
960
      ⋆ TransformerCPI (Chen et al., 2020): TransformerCPI uses the decoder module of Transformer,
961   which takes in the atom sequence embedding processed by GCN and the protein sequence embedding
962   processed by word2vec and 1D CNN.
963
964
      ⋆ MRBDTA (Zhang et al., 2022): In MRBDTA, after the embedding layer, drug sequences are directly
      fed into a block consisting of three Transformer encoders. The first encoder has a linear layer before
965
      it and the following two encoders are parallel. The protein sequence is also processed by a block with
966
      similar structure.
967
968   B.3.2    C ROSS ATTENTION
969
970   ⋆ CSDTI(Pan et al., 2023b): CSDTI use cross attention to fuse the deep representations of drugs
971   and proteins. Specifically, the different projections of protein feature are used as key and value
      respectively while the projection of drug feature is used as query.


                                                        18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       ⋆ TDGraphDTA(Zhu et al., 2023): TDGraphDTA use a multi-head cross-attention mechanism with
973    two attention heads. Both drug and protein features are linearly transformed into query, key and value
974    matrices. One cross attention layer uses a drug query matrix, a protein key matrix, and a protein value
975    matrix, while its parallel counterparts use the rest of the matrices. The outputs of these two layers are
976    concatenated and fed into MLP to get the final output.
