009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) on tabular data for classification can
012            lead to the phenomenon of fine-tuning multiplicity, where equally well-performing
013            models make conflicting predictions on the same input. Fine-tuning multiplic-
014            ity can arise due to variations in the training process, e.g., seed, random weight
015            initialization, retraining on a few additional or deleted data points. This raises
016            critical concerns about the robustness and reliability of Tabular LLMs, partic-
017            ularly when deployed for high-stakes decision-making, such as finance, hiring,
018
               education, healthcare, etc. This work formalizes the unique challenge of fine-
               tuning multiplicity in Tabular LLMs and proposes a novel measure to quantify
019
               the robustness of individual predictions without expensive model retraining. Our
020
               measure quantifies a prediction’s robustness by analyzing (sampling) the model’s
021            local behavior around the input in the embedding space. Interestingly, we show
022            that sampling in the local neighborhood can be leveraged to provide probabilistic
023            robustness guarantees against a broad class of equally-well-performing fine-tuned
024            models. By leveraging Bernstein’s Inequality, we show that predictions with suf-
025            ficiently high robustness (as defined by our measure) will remain consistent with
026            high probability. We also provide empirical evaluation on real-world datasets to
027            support our theoretical results. Our work highlights the importance of address-
028            ing fine-tuning instabilities to enable trustworthy deployment of Tabular LLMs in
029
               high-stakes and safety-critical applications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large language models (LLMs) are generating significant interest in high-stakes applications, e.g.,
034
      finance, healthcare, etc., particularly in few-shot classification scenarios. Tabular data is prevalent
035
      in these sectors, making the development of Tabular LLMs (TabLLMs) an emerging research pri-
036   ority (van Breugel & van der Schaar, 2024). Recent studies have shown that TabLLMs perform
037   commendably in scenarios with limited training data due to their transfer learning abilities (Hegsel-
038   mann et al., 2023; Dinh et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2020; Yan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023). However,
039   these models are often fine-tuned from large pre-trained models with millions or billions of param-
040   eters on small, proprietary datasets (Hu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022). This paucity of training data,
041   combined with the large parameter space, introduces instability across fine-tuned variants, raising
042   concerns about their trustworthy adoption in high-stakes applications.
043   One imminent challenge is the concern of fine-tuning multiplicity in TabLLMs. This is the phe-
044   nomenon where multiple well-performing models, fine-tuned from the same pre-trained LLM under
045   slightly varying conditions (e.g., different random seeds or minor changes in the training data),
046   produce conflicting predictions for the same inputs. This concept is closely related to predictive
047   multiplicity, often referred to as the Rashomon effect in the context of neural networks (Marx et al.,
048   2020; Breiman, 2003; Hsu & Calmon, 2022). While multiplicity has also been observed recently in
049   LLMs in the text classification (Gomez et al., 2024), it would become particularly concerning in the
050
      context of TabLLMs for high-stakes applications. In areas like finance (Yin et al., 2023) and health-
      care (Wang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2024), arbitrary and conflicting predictions
051
      on the same input can lead to undesirable consequences, such as reputational risk and distrust.
052
053   Aside from the inherent need for predictions to be robust to minor model variations (e.g., due to
      different training seeds), TabLLMs deployed by institutions may also need to be updated for various

055   even removing datapoints for privacy. For instance, regulatory frameworks like the GDPR (Voigt,

057   request, potentially leading to model updates. These updates could, in turn, impact the validity

068   probabilistic guarantees on the robustness of predictions with high consistency scores under a broad
069
      class of equally-well-performing fine-tuned models. Our contribution is summarized as follows:

074     of our proposed multiplicity measures, Prediction Variance, and Range (defined in Section 2).

077     sifiers which typically have locally-smooth decision boundaries, Tabular LLMs show abrupt and

      Model multiplicity in machine learning. Breiman (2003) introduced the idea that models can
145   differ significantly while achieving similar average performance, known as the Rashomon effect.
146   Marx et al. (2020) highlighted the prevalence of arbitrary decisions in simple classification prob-
147   lems, coining this phenomenon predictive multiplicity. Creel & Hellman (2022) discuss the harms
148   of predictive multiplicity and arbitrary decisions. Methods such as TreeFarms (Xin et al., 2022),
149   CorelsEnum (Mata et al., 2022), and RashomonGB (Hsu et al.) provide tools to enumerate models
150   in the Rashomon set for different hypothesis spaces. Efforts to leverage model multiplicity bene-
151   ficially while addressing its implications have been explored by (Black et al., 2022; Fisher et al.,
152   2019; Xin et al., 2022; Coston et al., 2021). The effect of model multiplicity in fairness (Sokol
153
      et al., 2022) and explainability are examined by Hamman et al. (2023); Black et al. (2021); Dutta
      et al. (2022); Pawelczyk et al. (2020). Watson-Daniels et al. (2023); Hsu & Calmon (2022) offered a
154
      framework for measuring predictive multiplicity in classical machine learning models, however, this
155
      involves retraining several models, with the exception of Hsu et al. (2024) who propose a drop-out
156   based approach to explore the Rashomon set for neural networks. Model multiplicity has not been
157   extensively studied in Tabular LLMs. The closest work is by (Gomez et al., 2024), which empiri-
158   cally investigates prediction arbitrariness for text classification (online content moderation). In this
159   work, we isolate and examine a specific form of multiplicity in Tabular LLMs (see Section 2). We
160   leverage the rich embedding space of LLMs to quantify vulnerability to multiplicity without the
161   need for expensive retraining, as fine-tuning LLMs is computationally expensive (see Section 3).

      1.1   P RELIMINARIES
167
      We consider a classification task for a tabular dataset D = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 , where each xi is a d-
168   dimensional feature vector (rows of a tabular input), and each label yi is binary, yi ∈ {0, 1}. We
169   study an n-shot classification problem by fine-tuning a pre-trained model on n examples from a
170   training set. This fine-tuning process aims to adapt the pre-trained model to effectively predict new,
171   unseen data points by learning from a limited number of training examples.
172
      Serialization of Tabular Data for LLMs: To effectively apply LLMs to tabular data, it is crucial
173
      to transform the data into a natural text format. This process, known as serialization, involves con-
174   verting the table rows into a text string that includes both the column names and their corresponding
175   values (Yin et al., 2020; Jaitly et al., 2023; Hegselmann et al., 2023; Dinh et al., 2022). The resultant
176   serialized string is combined with a task-specific prompt to form the input for the LLM. There have
177   been various proposed methods for serialization, and this is still a topic of active research Jaitly
178   et al. (2023). Among the serializations we have examined are: list template (a list of column names
179   and feature values), and text template ( “The <column name> is <value>.”). LLMs can be
180   adapted for classification tasks by training them on serialized tabular data. This training involves
181   using the natural-language outputs of the LLM, mapped to valid classes in the target space, as part
182
      of a fine-tuning process (see Figure 1). To clarify, table values are serialized into serialize(x) and
      then transformed into a format understandable by the LLM, tokenize(serialize(x)), which is some
183
      embedding. Since these transformations are one-to-one mappings, we denote the embedded form
184
      of x as x ∈ X to represent x in the embedding space. This allows us to simplify the notation and
185   directly use x to refer to the table values in the embedding space.

      Fine-tuning multiplicity. In this work, we explore the nature of multiplicity that arises in LLMs
198   when fine-tuned for tabular tasks. While model multiplicity in machine learning has been studied in
199   various contexts (see Related Works in Section 1), the unique challenges of fine-tuning multiplicity
200   in Tabular LLMs remain relatively unexplored.
201
      Traditional models, such as neural networks and gradient boosting machines (e.g., XGBoost), re-
202
      main state-of-the-art when ample labeled data is available (Kadra et al., 2021; Gorishniy et al., 2021).
203
      However, their performance declines significantly in data-scarce scenarios. In contrast, LLMs can
204   leverage their reasoning and pre-trained knowledge to achieve strong performance even with limited
205   tabular data (few-shot learning) (Hegselmann et al., 2023). This makes LLMs appealing for few-
206   shot tabular tasks, which often involve a mix of numerical and textual features. However, fine-tuning
207   these large models may risk multiplicity.
208
      To illustrate this, we conduct experiments using synthetic 2D datasets (see Figure 2). While fine-
209
      tuning an LLM on such data might seem excessive, it provides a clear visualization of the phe-
210   nomenon. We fine-tune several competing models using the text template (“The <column name>
211   is <value>”) and varying only the random training seed. We reveal that fine-tuned LLMs on such
212   non-language tasks exhibit noisy and non-smooth decision boundaries, even in regions where the
213   model is expected to confidently predict a specifc class. We hypothesize that this noisy behavior
214   in non-language tasks is likely because LLMs are optimized for capturing complex language struc-
215   tures. When fine-tuned on tabular data tasks, which often involve both text and numeric values,
      LLMs can leverage their pre-trained abilities but may still exhibit such instabilities.

      3     A NOVEL MEASURE OF PREDICTION CONSISTENCY
271
272
      Our objective is to define a measure, denoted as S(x, f ), for an input x and a given fine-tuned model
273
      f , that quantifies its robustness of predictions to a broad class of equally-well-performing fine-tuned
274   models. We desire that the measure S(x, f ) should be high if the input x is consistent across this
275   broad class models (see Figure 1).
276
277
      Candidate Measure: Prediction probability (S(x, f ) := f (x)). While prediction probabilities of
      a model f (·) can offer insights into its confidence in predicting a given class, they are insufficient
278
      for assessing robustness against fine-tuning multiplicity (see Table 2, Figure 3, i.e., data point with
279
      high f (x) or confidence can still be susceptible to multiplicity). In our synthetic data experiments
280   (see Figure 2), we also observe that noisy behaviors emerge in regions where the model should be
281   confident in its predictions, leading to conflicting outcomes across various fine-tuned models. This
282   indicates that relying solely on an input x may not provide a reliable assessment of robustness. To
283   address this, we propose a perturbation-based approach that leverages the local neighborhood around
284   the input x in the embedding space, ultimately leading to our theoretical measure of consistency.

      3.2   T HEORETICAL G UARANTEES ON C ONSISTENCY
302
303   Here, we present theoretical insights that motivate and provide guarantees for our proposed robust-
304   ness measure Sk,σ (x, f ), ensuring consistent predictions across a broad class of fine-tuned models.
305   We represent the class of fine-tuned models by a stochastic (random) function F , such that F ∈ F.
306   We denote two random models, F and F ′ , both of which are independently and identically dis-
307   tributed within F. For clarity, we use capital letters (e.g., F, F ′ , Xi , Z) to denote random variables,
308   while lowercase letters (e.g., xi , f, ϵ) indicate specific realizations. In our framework, we define a set
309   of assumptions that delineates the behavior of a broad class of finetuned models and the statistical
310   properties of their predictions.
311   Assumption 1 (Stochastic Fine-Tuned Model Class). We assume that for any two random models
312   F (X) and F ′ (X) are i.i.d. given an input X = x. Also, let the stochastic divergence between
313   predictions of two random models F and F ′ be: Zi := F ′ (Xi ) − F (Xi ) − |F (Xi ) − F (x)| +
314   |F ′ (Xi ) − F ′ (x)|, where Xi is a random point sampled independently from a distribution over a
315
      hypersphere B(x, σ). Then, we assume: Var [Zi |F ′ = f ′ , F = f ] ≤ β for all f, f ′ ∈ F.
316
      Intuition. The random variable Zi captures the neighborhood stochastic divergence between pre-
317
      dictions of two independently fine-tuned models F and F ′ . This captures both the difference in
318   predictions and variability around a given point x. The first assumption ensures that F and F ′ pro-
319   vide an unbiased estimate of the prediction for x. The assumption on the variance of Zi indicates
320   that the variance of the stochastic neighborhood divergence within a σ-Ball of a data point between
321   any two models’ predictions is controlled. The parameter β essentially captures the similarity of
322   the models within the local neighborhood of a data point. This concept is also somewhat analogous
323   to the Lipschitz constant of a general function, which bounds how much the function’s output can
      change relative to changes in its input. However, in this context, the β-bound reflects an average

337   Theoretical guarantee on consistency interpretation. Our consistency measure S(x, F ′ ) provides
338   a probabilistic guarantee that if a data point x has a sufficiently high consistency score with respect
339   to a random model F ′ , then the prediction of another random model F from the same broad class of
340   fine-tuned models will be at least S(x, F ′ )−ϵ with high probability. For example, if S(x, F ′ ) = 0.8,
341   we can be confident that F (x) will be at least 0.8 − ϵ with high probability (i.e, the prediction will
342
      remain on the positive predicted side). This implies that high consistency scores are indicative of
      robust predictions across different fine-tuned models. Conversely, a low consistency score does not
343
      provide significant information about the prediction’s behavior, as it does not guarantee a lower
344
      bound on the prediction. For F (x) ≥ S(x, F ′ ) − ϵ to hold with high probability, a large k is needed,
345   ideally k ≫ β. This implies that when β is large then more samples are needed.
346
347   Goodness of model class. The term ϵ′ in our guarantee captures the quality or goodness of the
348
      fine-tuned model class. A small ϵ′ indicates a well-behaved model class, suggesting that different
      fine-tuned models produce similar outputs in expectation within the local neighborhood of x even
349
      if predictions might vary for a given data point. Similar behavior is visualized in Figure 2, where,
350
      despite the presence of noisy variations in the decision boundaries, the local predictions around a
351   given point remain relatively consistent across models. This behavior is expected since these models
352   are derived from the same pre-trained model and trained with the goal of achieving similar accuracy
353   on the dataset. In this case, our consistency measure provides a useful and informative lower bound
354   on the predictions F (x) with a certifiably small gap. This aligns with P
                                                                              related formalizations, which
355   show the existence of simpler functions within a Rashomon set, where xi ∈D |f (xi )−f ′ (xi )| ≤ ∆,
356   across a dataset D (Semenova et al., 2022). Recent mathematical analyses of LORA also corroborate
357   with our assumptions, such as EX ∥f (X) − f ′ (X)∥ ≤ ∆, for a random variable X over a bounded
358   set (Zeng & Lee, 2023).
359   Conversely, a large ϵ′ indicates a more erratic model class. In this case, our bound becomes less
360   informative, and the consistency measure might perform poorly for a given point. We interpret
361   our results as follows: The model class is not well-behaved; thus, one cannot certify a small gap
362   between F (x) and our proposed measure. We do not provide guarantees for all types of model
363   changes, as this would be challenging with only a single model. For example, if fine-tuned models
364   do not achieve sufficient accuracy, encounter significant variations in hyperparameter choices, or
365
      large changes in the training data, ϵ′ is likely to be large. Our focus is on the multiplicity that
      arises due to randomness in training, such as changes in the training seed or minor adjustments in
366
      training settings (what we term the broad class of equally-well-performing fine-tuned models). In
367
      our evaluations (see Section 4), we do not assume any specific values for ϵ′ and consider regular
368   fine-tuned models without imposing any theoretical constraint. The complete proof of Theorem 1 is

403   In this section, we experiment across different datasets to (i) quantify the prevalence of fine-tuning

406   Datasets and Serialization. Our experiments utilize the Diabetes (Kahn), German Credit (Hof-
407   mann, 1994), Bank (Moro et al., 2014), Heart, Car, and Adult datasets (Becker & Kohavi, 1996),
408   serialized using the Text Template method where each tabular entry is converted into a natural lan-

      with the training distribution of LLMs, enhancing their performance in both zero-shot and few-shot
      scenarios (Hegselmann et al., 2023; Dinh et al., 2022).

      Discussions. Our multiplicity evaluation metrics, summarized in Table 1,3,4, reveal significant
      variability in model predictions across different fine-tuned variants, even when they exhibit similar
515
      accuracy. This multiplicity is not captured by merely examining predicted probabilities, as predic-
516
      tions with high confidence can still be susceptible to multiplicity (see Figure 3). Our consistency
517   measure, Sk,σ (x, f ), was compared with prediction probabilities f (x). The results, presented in
518   Table 2,5,6, demonstrate that our consistency measure consistently shows mainly higher correlation
519   with multiplicity metrics across all models and datasets compared to prediction probabilities and
520   drop-out method. This indicates that Sk,σ (x, f ) is more informative than the baselines in informing
521   the fine-tuning multiplicity. The drop-out method is however better than the prediction probabilities
522   alone. We hypothesize that our method is more suitable for LLMs because the embedding space
523   of LLMs is significantly smaller than the parameter space (possibly more informative also). The
524   drop-out method might need significantly more inferences to compete due to this.
525   We study the unique nature of fine-tuning multiplicity in Tabular LLMs. Marx et al. (2020); Rudin
526   et al. (2024) argue for the necessity of measuring and reporting multiplicity to better inform pre-
527   dictions. Traditional methods to measure multiplicity in classical ML are impractical for LLMs
528   due to the computational challenge of retraining several fine-tuned models (Marx et al., 2020; Hsu
529   & Calmon, 2022; Watson-Daniels et al., 2023). Our proposed measure, which requires only the
530   given model and leverages the embedding space to inform multiplicity, addresses this issue. This
531
      approach reduces the complexity from retraining and inference to just inference, making it more
      feasible to apply in practice. Although, from our theoretical guarantee, a large k (number of sam-
532
      pled points) might be needed for accurate consistency estimation (particularly when β is large), it
533
      remains computationally more efficient than retraining multiple models. Our work provides practi-
534   tioners with meaningful information about the multiplicity of predictions, which may lead them to
535   carefully evaluate which predictions to trust and which to treat with caution. Our research has sig-
536   nificant implications in several high-stakes applications, e.g., hiring, finance, education, etc., where
537   inconsistent predictions can lead to distrust. A limitation of our work is that while we inform about
538   fine-tuning multiplicity for a given sample, we do not resolve it. Future work could focus on devel-
539   oping methods to mitigate fine-tuning multiplicity, ensuring more consistent model predictions (see
      Appendix A for detailed discussion on Societal Impact and Limitations).

740
741   Limitations. While our work provides a measure to assess fine-tuning multiplicity, it does not
742   directly resolve this issue. Future research could focus on mitigation methods to ensure more con-
743   sistent model predictions. A key constraint is the applicability to higher-dimensional datasets due
744
      to the limited context window size of current LLMs, though extending context windows is an active
      area of research (Peng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a). Additionally, our method’s performance can
745
      be sensitive to hyperparameters, such as sample size and neighborhood radius; incorrect choices may
746
      lead to an inaccurate assessment of robustness. Our approach also assumes access to the embedding
747   space, limiting its application to open-source models. Furthermore, the bound in Theorem 1 is not
748   directly computable. Estimating these unknowns such as β, ϵ′ could be a direction for future work.
749   Despite these limitations, our measure serves as a crucial step toward understanding and quantifying
750   fine-tuning multiplicity, laying the groundwork for future advancements.
751
752   Broader Societal Impacts. The application of LLMs to tabular data, particularly in high-stakes
753   domains such as finance and healthcare, presents both opportunities and risks (Bommasani et al.,
754   2021). Our work aims to address one of the critical challenges associated with these models: the
755   instability introduced when fine-tuning large models on small datasets. This instability, manifested
      as overfitting and multiplicity, can undermine the reliability of model predictions in scenarios where


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      consistency is crucial. By measuring multiplicity, our work contributes to the responsible deploy-
757   ment of LLMs in domains where erroneous predictions can have severe consequences (Bommasani
758   et al., 2021; Creel & Hellman, 2022).
759
760
      Tabular data remains a dominant modality in many critical fields, yet it has received less research
      attention compared to text and image data (Hegselmann et al., 2023). Recent work van Breugel &
761
      van der Schaar (2024) argues that developing reliable foundation models for tabular data should be
762
      a research priority. Our deliberate focus on Tabular LLMs aligns with this perspective, as we aim to
763   bridge a significant gap in the current research landscape. While it is understood that some degree
764   of multiplicity is inherent in fine-tuned models, understanding its nature and impact is essential for
765   building trust in Tabular LLMs.
766
      Our approach also supports regulatory compliance by enhancing transparency and accountability
767
      in automated decision-making systems. Quantifying prediction robustness aligns with regulations
768
      such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (Voigt, 2017) and upcoming AI legisla-
769   tion, which increasingly demand explainable and reliable AI models (Chamola et al., 2023). While
770   LLMs are more computationally expensive than traditional models, our method reduces the costs of
771   assessing multiplicity. By avoiding repeated retraining, it enhances cost efficiency and minimizes en-
772   vironmental impact, lowering both energy consumption and carbon footprint (Luccioni et al., 2023).
773
      Furthermore, observing the nature of fine-tuning multiplicity in Tabular LLMs pave the way for
774
      future research into model stability. It also facilitates continual learning by informing the robust-
775   ness of a prediction to potential model updates in a dynamic environments where data constantly
776   evolves (Amba Hombaiah et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a). Lastly, our work could
777   play a role in mitigating fairwashing risks and explanation bias (Black et al., 2022; Sokol et al.,
778   2023; Rudin et al., 2024). This transparency is crucial for maintaining ethical standards and trust-
779   worthiness in AI deployment (Chamola et al., 2023).

782
783   The consistency measure quantifies the robustness of a model’s prediction at a specific point x
784   by examining the model’s behavior in the local neighborhood of x within the embedding space.
785   Our measure is motivated by our observations on synthetic data experiments where the model was
786   exhibiting noisy and non-smooth patterns in the decision space.
787
      Local Averaging: The term k1 xi ∈Nx,k f (xi ) represents the average prediction of the model on
                                     P
788
      points sampled from a neighborhood around x. This captures the general tendency of the model in
789
      the vicinity of x.
790
      Variability Penalization: The term k1 xi ∈Nx,k |f (x) − f (xi )| computes the average absolute dif-
                                             P
791
792   ference between the model’s prediction at x and its predictions at neighboring points. Subtracting
793   this from the local average penalizes the consistency score when there is high variability in a neigh-
794   borhood in spite of high local mean, reflecting instability in the model’s predictions around x.
795   By combining these two terms, Sk,σ (x, f ) provides a measure that is high when the model’s predic-
796   tions are both strong (i.e., high average prediction) and stable (i.e., low variability) in the neighbor-
797   hood of x. The metric is designed to capture the local stability of the model’s predictions, which is
798   critical in assessing robustness to fine-tuning multiplicity.
799
      Consistency Interpretation of f (x) and 2f (xi ) − f (x): This interesting structure of our consistency
800   measure is not a heuristic design but arises directly from the reverse triangle inequality step in the
801   proof of our theoretical consistency guarantee (Theorem 1): |f (x)| ≥ |f (xi )| − |f (xi ) − f (x)|.
802
803
      When f (xi ) ≥ f (x), the contribution to the consistency score is f (x), indicating that the neighbor-
      hood prediction f (xi ) reinforces and supports the robustness of prediction f (x).
804
805   When f (xi ) < f (x), the contribution becomes 2f (xi ) − f (x). If f (xi ) is significantly less than
806   f (x), the term 2f (xi ) − f (x) becomes negative, penalizing the consistency score due to large dis-
807   crepancies between f (x) and its neighbor. However, if f (xi ) is only slightly less than f (x) (i.e.,
808   f (xi ) > f (x)
                  2 ), the term 2f (xi ) − f (x) remains positive, thereby contributing positively to the con-
809   sistency measure. The intuition is that we only penalize significant drops in neighboring predictions
      and allow neighbors that closely support f (x) prediction.

      How consistency differs from existing robustness: Our focus on model multiplicity distinguishes this
811   work from traditional robustness measures, which address different aspects of model behavior such
812   as out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization, stability under natural perturbations, and uncertainty
813   estimation (Djolonga et al., 2020). OOD generalization typically evaluates how well a model per-
814   forms on data that differs from the training distribution (e.g., classifying objects seen from novel
815   viewpoints or in cluttered settings). This is often quantified using test datasets with altered condi-
816   tions or domain shifts, and methods like domain adaptation are employed to enhance robustness.
817   Stability under natural perturbations assesses the sensitivity of predictions and predicted probabil-
818   ities to small, random changes in the input, such as Gaussian noise or image transformations. Un-
819
      certainty estimation, on the other hand, focuses on calibrating the predicted probabilities to reflect
      true likelihoods, often using measures like Expected Calibration Error or entropy-based metrics to
820
      evaluate how well the model quantifies confidence in its predictions. While these methods provide
821
      valuable insights into different facets of robustness, their goals differ significantly from ours.
822
823   Han et al. (2023) is more closely related to our approach, as it quantifies robustness by measuring
824   the fraction of consistent predictions within a local neighborhood. While both approaches leverage
825
      the neighborhood around a data point, the objectives diverge: Han et al. (2023) focuses on quanti-
      fying the probability of consistent predictions against perturbations to evaluate robustness to noise.
826
      In contrast, our measure captures the consistency of predictions (multiplicity) among competing
827
      models within the Rashomon set.
828
829   Additionally, our consistency measure’s unique mean-variance nature further distinguishes it. Un-
830   like existing metrics, it not only accounts for the average prediction within a neighborhood but also
831
      penalizes the variability in predictions. Moreover, we provide theoretical guarantees on the robust-
      ness of predictions with high consistency scores over a broad range of equally-well performing
832
      models.

      Remark 2 (Randomness of F and F ′ ). In Theorem 1, we consider both F and F ′ as random to
960   capture the variability inherent in the fine-tuning process. This approach models the real-world sce-
961   nario where different fine-tuning runs can lead to different models due to changes in random seeds
962   or training conditions. Fixing F ′ = f ′ will require an alternate assumption instead of Assumption
963   1 that the predictions of other models are centered around f ′ , i.e., E[F (X)|X = x] = f ′ (x). While
964   alternative bounds using a fixed F ′ could provide valuable insights, our current approach aims to
965   capture the randomness of the initial fine-tuned model (F ′ ) and understand robustness across the
966   broader distribution of possible fine-tuned models.

970   pretrained on the same dataset. For samples lying on the data manifold—where realistic samples
971   exist—we expect several models (from the same pretrained model) fine tuned on the same dataset
      with different training seed to exhibit “similar” prediction probabilities. However, fine-tuned models

       D.3    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
1135
1136   Our experiments were conducted using the BigScience T0 and Google Flan T5 models fine-tuned on
1137   four datasets: German Credit, Diabetes, Bank, and Adult Income. We explored the performance and
1138   robustness of the fine-tuned models in few-shot scenarios. The number of shots was set to 64,128,
1139   and 512 for each dataset. To evaluate model multiplicity and consistency, we fine-tuned 40 models
1140   with different random seeds for each dataset and recorded their predictions. The training process
1141   involved setting the batch size to 2 for smaller training sizes and 8 for larger sizes. The learning rate
1142
       was set to 0.003. For each dataset, we determined the number of training steps adaptively based on
       the number of shots, ensuring sufficient iterations for model convergence. Specifically, for the num-
1143
       ber of shots-shot settings, the training steps were calculated as 20 × (number of shots/batch size).
1144
       All experiments were performed on 2 NVIDIA RTX A4500 and 4 NVIDIA RTX 6000 GPUs. To
1145   ensure reproducibility and robustness of the results, different random seeds (i.e., 2, 4, 8, etc) were
1146   used for each fine-tuning iteration. For fine-tuning with LORA we use a rank of 4.
1147
       Remark 4. Given the infeasibility of computing the exact size of |Fδ | due to its potentially vast
1148
       model space, we employ an expensive sampling approach, i.e., fine-tuning with various seeds. We
1149   select a finite number of models from Fδ for practical evaluation, allowing us to evaluate the mul-
1150   tiplicity metrics. It is very computationally expensive to fine-tune several models to evaluate multi-
1151   plicity. This motivates the need for a measure to quantify consistency given one model.
