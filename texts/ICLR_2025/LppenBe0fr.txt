011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013           Point cloud sampling plays a pivotal role in facilitating efficient analysis of large-
014           scale point clouds. Recently, learning-to-sample methods have garnered growing
015           interest from the community, particularly for their ability to be jointly trained
016           with downstream tasks. However, previous learning-based sampling methods ei-
017
              ther lead to unrecognizable sampling patterns by generating a new point cloud
              or biased sampled results by focusing excessively on shape details. Moreover,
018
              they all fail to take the natural point distribution variations over different shapes
019
              into consideration and learn a similar sampling strategy for all point clouds. In
020           this paper, we propose a Sparse Attention Map and Bin-based Learning method
021           (termed SAMBLE) to learn shape-specific sampling strategies for point cloud
022           shapes, striking a superior balance between the overall shape outline and intri-
023           cate local details for the sampling process. In particular, we first propose sparse
024           attention map by integrating both local and global information. Based on this,
025           multiple point-wise sampling score computation methods are proposed and ex-
026           plored by leveraging heatmaps as a guiding tool. Subsequently, we introduce
027           a binning strategy that partitions points within each point cloud based on these
028
              scores. Finally, additional learnable tokens are introduced during the attention
              computation phase to acquire sampling weights for each bin, thereby enabling the
029
              development of shape-specific sampling strategies for an optimized sampling pro-
030
              cess. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method adeptly strikes a refined
031           balance between sampling edge points for local details and preserving uniformity
032           in the global shape, leading to superior performance across common point cloud
033           downstream tasks and even in scenarios involving few-point cloud sampling.

110   Point Cloud Sampling. Point cloud sampling is a key process in 3D data handling for simplifying
111   high-resolution dense point clouds. Over the past decades, non-learning-based methods Eldar et al.
112   (1997); Moenning & Dodgson (2003); Groh et al. (2018) have predominantly been used for point
113   cloud sampling. While Farthest Point Sampling (FPS) Eldar et al. (1997) is the most widely used one
114   Qi et al. (2017b); Li et al. (2018); Wu et al. (2019); Qian et al. (2022); Zhao et al. (2021), Random
115   Sampling (RS) has also been frequently adopted Zhou & Tuzel (2018); Qi et al. (2020); Groh et al.
116   (2018). More recently, learning-based sampling methods have shown superior performance with
117   task-oriented training. S-Net Dovrat et al. (2019) represents a pioneering work of generating new
118
      point coordinates from global representations, while SampleNet Lang et al. (2020) introduces a
      soft projection operation for better point approximation. Following S-Net, multiple learning-based
119
      methods have been proposed Lin et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021); Nezhadarya et al. (2020); Wang
120
      et al. (2023). MOPS-Net Qian et al. (2020) learns a transformation matrix and multiplies it with the
121   original point cloud to generate the sampled one. By employing the attention mechanism to learn
122   point-wise sampling scores, APES Wu et al. (2023a) captures the edge points in the input point
123   clouds with a strong focus.
124
      Deep Learning on Point Clouds. In contrast to the voxelization-based methods Maturana &
125
      Scherer (2015); Jiang et al. (2018); Le & Duan (2018) and multi-view-based methods Lawin et al.
126
      (2017); Boulch et al. (2017); Audebert et al. (2016); Tatarchenko et al. (2018), point-based methods
127   deal directly with point clouds. The pioneer studies of PointNet Qi et al. (2017a) and PointNet++
128   Qi et al. (2017b) tackle point clouds through point-wise Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) and max-
129   pooling operations. Subsequently, other research shifts focus towards constructing more efficient
130   building blocks for local feature extraction, such as convolution-based ones Li et al. (2018); Lin
131   et al. (2020a); Zhu et al. (2023); Ahn et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2019); Thomas et al. (2019); Wu et al.
132   (2023b) and graph-based ones Wang et al. (2019); Simonovsky & Komodakis (2017); Chen et al.
133   (2021); Zhang et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2020); Lin et al. (2020b); Liu et al. (2019). More recently,
134   while MLP-based methods like PointNeXt Qian et al. (2022) and PointMetaBase Lin et al. (2023)
135
      have rekindled people’s interest, the application of attention mechanisms to point cloud analysis has
      also garnered widespread attention Vaswani et al. (2017); Guo et al. (2021); Zhao et al. (2021); Yu
136
      et al. (2022); Engel et al. (2021); Wen et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2024a). For example, PT Zhao et al.
137
      (2021); Wu et al. (2022; 2024b) series improves the model performance by introducing subtraction-
138   based attention blocks, and Wu et al. (2024a) performs a large ablation study over attention module
139   designs for point cloud processing.

      5   C ONCLUSION
522
523
524   In this paper, a new point cloud sampling method has been proposed to learn shape-specific sam-
525
      pling strategies for achieving better trade-off between sampling local details and preserving global
      uniformity. Based on a sparse attention map that combines the knowledge from both local and global
526
      information, multiple indexing modes have been designed and explored. By partitioning the points
527
      in each shape into bins, and learning respective sampling ratios for each bin with additional tokens,
528   shape-specific sampling strategies are acquired for individual point cloud shapes. With the proposed
529   methods, we achieve a more effective balance between capturing local details and preserving global
530   uniformity of the input shape, resulting in improved performance on downstream tasks.
531
      Looking forward, the trade-off between sampling local details and preserving global uniformity in
532
      point clouds remains an open challenge. Future advancements in upsampling layers could further
533
      benefit from leveraging previously discarded information to refine this balance. The complex inter-
534   action between downsampling and upsampling layers presents a promising area for further research.
535   Another exciting direction is adapting the proposed method to point cloud scenes rather than isolated
536   shapes. This shift introduces the challenge of scene boundary points being mistakenly prioritized
537   as significant, which calls for more sophisticated sampling algorithms. Additionally, the proposed
538   approach could be extended to other 3D data representations, such as 3D Gaussian Splatting, where
539   each point is represented as a 3D Gaussian. Given the typically large size of such 3D data, introduc-
      ing effective sampling techniques could significantly enhance its processing efficiency.

913   Adding Bin Tokens to Q or K/V? A critical point in the idea of bin tokens lies in determining
914   the specific branches to which the tokens should be concatenated. In order to match the tensor
915   dimension for later computation in the attention mechanism, the tensor size of Key and Value should
916   be the same. Hence if tokens are being added to the Key branch, they also have to be added to the
917   Value branch. Overall, there are two possibilities of adding bin tokens to (i) the Query branch, or
      (ii) the Key and the Value branches.


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      It is crucial to emphasize that, due to the nature of the sampling operation where indexes are se-
919   lected, gradients cannot be propagated back through the sampling operation during the backward
920   propagation process. As a result, regardless of the selected structure, it is essential to establish an
921   alternative pathway to convey the information contained within the bin tokens, which have a size of
922   nb × N , to the downsampled features, which have a size of M × d. This pathway should ensure
923   the flow of relevant information despite the inability to directly backpropagate gradients through the
924   sampling operation.
925   As illustrated in the left of Fig. 14, in the former case, an attention map of tensor size (N + nb ) × N
926   is obtained. After M indexes of the points to be sampled are learned with SAMBLE, M rows in the
927   attention map are extracted to form a new tensor for the next steps. However, note that the sub-tensor
928   of nb × N will never be delivered to the next steps since they do not correspond to points, hence no
929   gradient will be backpropagated to the tokens during the training.
