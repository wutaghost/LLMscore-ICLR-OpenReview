009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            A variety of analysis tasks in single-cell (SC) multi-omics are crucial for pre-
012            cision medicine and clinical research. To address these tasks, existing methods
013            are typically pre-trained on large-scale datasets to obtain general representations,
014            followed by fine-tuning on specific tasks and labeled datasets. However, their
015            task-specific heads often lack generalizability, significantly limiting performance
016            in zero-shot scenarios. Inspired by the success of large language models (LLMs),
017            we propose ZerOmics, the first zero-shot method that guides LLMs to perform
018
               various SC tasks without relying on specific downstream data. To enable LLMs to
               establish a correct and comprehensive understanding of SC data, ZerOmics em-
019
               ploys a dual-alignment strategy. Specifically, ZerOmics aligns SC expression data
020
               with the well-organized gene corpus, thereby generating robust SC embeddings.
021            These embeddings are then incorporated into instructions designed for various SC
022            analysis tasks to tune the LLM, achieving alignment between SC data and the
023            LLM. Extensive experiments across various sequencing technologies and tissues
024            demonstrate that ZerOmics provides a comprehensive and general solution for SC
025            analysis, achieving performance comparable to or even surpassing the state-of-
026            the-art (SOTA) supervised and fine-tuned methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) recently have emergent abilities in understanding and reasoning,
032   demonstrating the potential across a variety of applications. Increasing research shows that LLMs
033
      achieve expert-level performance in addressing problems from the natural sciences. For instance,
      LLMs have been successfully applied to drug molecule design (Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024),
034
      protein structure prediction (Madani et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2024), and reasoning
035
      about physical formulas (Ding et al., 2023). However, in the biomedical field, current LLMs are
036   often confined to protein sequence analysis, neglecting the rapidly expanding single-cell (SC) multi-
037   omics data.
038
039
      As an emerging technology, SC multi-omics data provides valuable opportunities for comprehensive
      analysis of biological heterogeneity at multiple levels, including transcriptomics and epigenomics,
040
      within individual cells (Valous et al., 2024). A classic SC dataset is organized as a matrix X ∈
041
      RN ×G , where Xi,j represents the expression read counts of the j-th gene in the i-th cell, and N and
042   G denote the number of cells and genes, respectively. Machine learning models trained on X can
043   accurately identify diseased cells (Sh et al., 2022), annotate cell types (Yang et al., 2022; Cui et al.,
044   2024), and infer cell pathways (Subramanian et al., 2005; Fan et al., 2024), presenting unprecedented
045   opportunities for advancements in clinical research and targeted therapy development (Aevermann
046   et al., 2018).
047
      SC data from different sources show differences caused by non-biological factors, including experi-
048   mental conditions and instrument errors, leading to domain shifts (Zhao et al., 2020). To achieve do-
049   main adaptation in SC analysis process, models based on the “pre-training & fine-tuning” paradigm
050   in Figure 1(a) have been widely adopted. Techniques like scBERT (Yang et al., 2022), Geneformer
051   (Theodoris et al., 2023), and scGPT (Cui et al., 2024) are inspired by natural language processing
052   workflows, treating the gene expression matrix X as a “term frequency” matrix, regarding each
053   cell as a sentence, and each gene as a word. These methods aim to pre-train robust models using
      auxiliary tasks on abundant unlabeled SC data. However, the general cell embeddings generated

069   capacity of these methods to tailor embeddings to particular task requirements is significantly di-

073
      To reduce the heavy reliance of the existing methods on downstream task-specific heads and la-
074   beled datasets, we propose a novel framework, ZerOmics, as shown in Figure 1(b). Inspired by the
075   breakthrough of LLMs in zero-shot scenario (Wei et al., 2022), ZerOmics unifies various SC tasks
076   with text-based question answering and directly solves them with the help of LLM’s excellent rea-
077   soning ability. Specifically, it employs a dual-alignment strategy: semantic alignment between SC
078   expression and gene corpus, and between SC embeddings and the LLM. First, the SC expression
079   X is integrated with text embeddings extracted from gene text summaries, resulting in robust SC
080   embeddings after large-scale pre-training. These embeddings are then incorporated into instructions
081   designed for various SC tasks to tune the LLM, aligning with the LLM semantic space. In this
082
      way, after instruction tuning the LLM on multiple tasks, ZerOmics can successfully handle unseen
      datasets and tasks without any additional training. In brief, our contributions are summarized as:
083
084
      • We propose ZerOmics, a novel framework that departs from the traditional pre-training and fine-
085
        tuning paradigm, establishing the first general model based on LLMs for SC multi-omics analysis.
086
087   • We introduce an innovative dual-alignment strategy that aligns SC gene expression data with a
088     structured gene corpus, and SC embeddings with the LLM, enabling LLMs to establish a compre-
089     hensive interpretation of SC data.
090   • Extensive experiments across various sequencing technologies and tissues validate that ZerOmics
091     achieves performance comparable to even exceeding that of state-of-the-art (SOTA) supervised
092     and fine-tuned methods.

097   2.1   M ULTI - MODAL INSTRUCTION TUNING FOR LLM S
098
099
      In recent studies, tuning LLMs with multi-modal instructions has gathered great attention as an effi-
      cient strategy for enabling LLMs to comprehend information across diverse modalities. TEA-GLM
100
      (Wang et al., 2024) and GraphGPT (Tang et al., 2024) leverage the graph instruction paradigm
101
      to align graph representations with the LLM token embeddings, achieving zero-shot graph learn-
102   ing and guiding LLMs to comprehend graphs’ inherent structural information. mPLUG-Owl2 (Ye
103   et al., 2024) designs a modality-adaptive module to project both textual and visual information into
104   a shared semantic space, achieving cross-modality interaction and preserving modality-specific fea-
105   tures simultaneously. AnyRef (He et al., 2024) extracts features from images, bounding boxes, and
106   audio followed by mapping them to the LLM token space, enabling flexible referring beyond single
107   textual descriptions. Despite the variety of data types in biomedical scenarios (e.g., SC tabular ma-
      trix, spatial transcriptomics, spatial gene expression images), none of the studies explored how to

      integrate biological information and natural language text into a unified space from the perspective
109   of multimodal instruction tuning, so that LLM can directly answer various SC analysis tasks.

      2.2   F OUNDATION MODELS FOR SINGLE - CELL ANALYSIS
112
113   LLMs have achieved remarkable breakthroughs across various domains, exemplified by models such
114   as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023). Inspired by them, many transformer-based foundation models such
115   as scBERT (Yang et al., 2022), Geneformer (Theodoris et al., 2023), scGPT (Cui et al., 2024), and
116   scFoundation (Hao et al., 2024) conceptualize SC gene expression as sentences within the language
117   model, merely mimicking LLMs’ training strategy to build non-language models for SC data. They
118
      pre-train the robust SC embeddings on extensive unlabeled single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-
      seq) datasets, followed by supervised fine-tuning across various downstream tasks.
119
120   However, gene expression data are often affected by the non-biological factors and the information
121   they present is not as stable as text (Du et al., 2019). Therefore, pioneers in SC analysis, such as
122   BioTranslator (Xu et al., 2023) and LangCell (Zhao et al., 2024), try to incorporate text data to make
123   the extracted embeddings more general and robust. Both of them integrates the textual descriptions
124
      with biological expression data through pre-trained language models. In particular, LangCell con-
      structs a cell-text dataset utilized to facilitate model pre-training via four tasks grounded in masking
125
      and contrastive learning strategies on cell-cell, cell-text pairs, demonstrating initial “representation”
126
      abilities in zero-shot and few-shot scenarios. Another attempt is Cell2Sentence (Levine et al., 2024),
127   which converts each cell into a sentence of gene names ranked by descending expression abundance,
128   and then directly uses the language model for representation. This approach retains only a minimal
129   level of expression data, resulting in insufficient recognition of cell-specific information.
130
      Therefore, how to effectively integrate expression data that presents cell-specific information and
131
      relatively more stable and consistent text data is still a problem that needs to be explored. In addition,
132
      although the embeddings extracted by many foundation models are robust in the zero shot setting,
133   they still require task-specific heads to handle downsteam tasks.

136   3     M ETHODOLOGY
137
138
      In this section, we introduce the novel framework ZerOmics, designed for zero-shot learning for
      various SC data analysis tasks. As shown in Figure 2, the ZerOmics comprises two principal training
139
      stages: (a) pre-training the SC Model to align SC expression profiles with the gene corpus, and (b)
140
      multi-modal and multi-task instruction tuning to align SC embeddings with the LLM semantic space.
141   (c) After tuning, ZerOmics achieves zero-shot analysis in various SC downstream tasks without fine-
142   tuning on labeled SC datasets.
143
144   3.1   S INGLE - CELL MODEL PRE - TRAINING
145
146   Considering the significant non-biological domain shifts often present in single-cell multi-omics
147   data, and the limitations of SC expression matrices in accurately depicting cellular characteristics,
148   ZerOmics incorporates a multi-modal mask learning paradigm for model pre-training. Our method
149   synchronizes SC expression profiles with gene functions encapsulated in natural language, yield-
150
      ing semantically enriched, robust, and more distinctly characterizable single-cell embeddings, thus
      enhancing their utility in downstream analytical tasks.
151
152
      Single-cell expression embedding. Single cell expression data often exhibit significant variation,
153
      including long-tail effects and domain shifts (Perez et al., 2022). To enhance the robustness of ex-
154   pression embeddings against domain shifts and improve computational efficiency (Yang et al., 2022),
155
      the elements in the expression matrix are binned into X̃ ∈ B = {0, 1, 2, · · · , Nbins }N ×G based on
156   expression levels for tokenization, where Nbins denotes the number of bins (see Appendix A.3 for
157   details). Additionally, from a biological perspective, this operation emphasizes cell-specific infor-
158   mation. Genes that are highly expressed in most cells, such as housekeeping genes, may exhibit
159   lower expression levels in this context. In contrast, genes that are lowly expressed but crucial for
160   identifying cell states, such as transcription factors, may exhibit higher expression levels (Theodoris
161   et al., 2023). To improve model generalization, the random masking (RM) and random substitu-
      tion (RS) strategy are employed (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019). Due to the sparsity of the expression

      3.2   M ULTI - MODAL MULTI - TASK INSTRUCTION TUNING WITH SINGLE - CELL DATA
217
218   We leverage the instruction tuning paradigm (Wei et al., 2022) to enhance the adaptability of LLMs
219   in capturing biomedical information from SC data. Firstly, SC embeddings are generated from a pre-
220   trained SC Model, followed by being mapped into the LLM semantic space using a tokenizer and
221   incorporated into various task-related instructions. By tuning the LLM with multi-task instructions
222   to produce responses that increasingly resemble real sentences, the SC embeddings gradually align
223   with the LLM, achieving superior performance across various downstream tasks.
224
225   Single-cell token embeddings for LLM. Considering that SC embeddings and LLM token em-
226   beddings involve different semantic information, a linear tokenizer ftoken is employed to transform
227   the SC embeddings to the fixed length of SC token embeddings for LLM individually as
228
229                                        ftoken (zi ) = hi ∈ RK×d ,                                    (6)
230
      where zi ∈ ZSC denotes the extracted embedding of a cell by expression and gene text information,
231
      and K is the length of tokens.
232
233
234   Instruction design. The instructions for various tasks in ZerOmics are uniformly constructed with
235
      three components: (1) single-cell information about the cell to be analyzed, (2) dataset information,
      and (3) task description.
236
237   First, the previously mentioned SC token embedding hi (also [SC token] in instructions) contains
238   comprehensive single-cell expression and gene function information, effectively representing the
239   cell to be analyzed and serving as the first component.
240   To prevent the LLM from drawing one-sided conclusions by solely focusing on the current SC to-
241   ken, the data source information is incorporated for each cell. Providing additional context through
242   dataset information enables the LLM to understand important factors, such as experimental condi-
243   tions, technical platforms, and species—related to cell expression, significantly enhancing its ability
244   to identify single-cell patterns across datasets. For simplicity, the abstracts of the papers that pro-
245   duced these datasets ([abstract] in instructions) serve as the second component, as they offer de-
246   tailed research objectives, experimental settings, and other relevant information in natural language,
247
      effectively conveying dataset information (Tang et al., 2024).
248   Finally, the instruction tuning tasks encompass three representative and important SC analysis tasks:
249   cell type annotation, rare cell identification, and tumor cell discovery (see Appendix B.1). To en-
250   hance the accuracy of the model response, the task description component contains both an impera-
251   tive or question sentence matching the task and a set of answer candidates for the LLM. For example,
252   for cell type annotation, the task description is structured as follows: classify this single-cell
253
      token into one of the following categories: [list categories]. Please only respond
      with one of the categories. Here, [list categories] is answer candidates related to the
254
      datasets. This design effectively guides LLMs to use the provided data to infer answers rather than
255
      memorizing them based on the dataset (Wang et al., 2024). Additionally, using candidate answers
256   encourages the model to compare and contrast different options, strengthening its decision-making
257   process and reducing the likelihood of generating incoherent responses (Kim et al., 2024).
258
259
      We design three different instruction templates for each task to guide the LLM to truly understand
      the various tasks rather than memorize the instructions (Wei et al., 2022). Instruction examples can
260
      be found in Appendix E.
261
262
263   Tuning strategy. We collect single-cell multi-omics datasets containing 91.5M cells in total and
264
      a gene corpus containing 43.8K genes for instruction tuning, where the ground truth labels of three
      tasks are artificially constructed (see Appendix A.3 for details). Given a single epoch of tuning,
265
      each training sample is randomly assigned with one task and one corresponding instruction tem-
266
      plate. ZerOmics performs supervised tuning for the LLM based on the LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) in
267   a random batch manner. To alleviate the catastrophic forgetting in the multi-task conditions (Wang
268   et al., 2023), the LLM is tuned with the mixture of universal and task-specific LoRAs as Figure 3.
269   In the forward process, the token embeddings from the pre-trained LLM and all LoRAs are summed
      to produce the output. Then, the cross-entropy loss between the probability distribution of the

306
307   Cell type annotation (CTA). We evaluate the CTA performance of ZerOmics across four datasets:
308   10x scRNA-seq human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC68K), Smart-Seq2 human pan-
309   creas (Pancreas), 10x scATAC-seq bone marrow mononuclear cells (BMMC), and MERFISH mouse
310   primary motor cortex (MOP). Eight competing methods are selected for comparison with ZerOmics.
311   Among them, LangCell is the only baseline method with zero-shot capability, and it also provides
312   a fine-tuned version named LangCell-CE. The overall performance is summarized in Table 1. Due
313   to the meticulously annotated labels, supervised methods such as Seurat demonstrate excellent per-
314   formance across most datasets. Nevertheless, four fine-tuning methods achieve performance com-
315
      parable to or surpassing Seurat only on PBMC68K and Pancreas, while significant disparities are
      observed in the other two datasets. This variation is attributed to the absence of 10x scATAC-seq
316
      or MERFISH sequencing data in their pre-training datasets. Additionally, the limited generalization
317
      ability of the pre-trained models impedes their performance even after fine-tuning on new datasets.
318   Among zero-shot methods, while LangCell performs comparably to the supervised method scJoint,
319   it also shows a rapid performance decline on BMMC, similar to its fine-tuning version, LangCell-
320   CE. In contrast, ZerOmics achieves the best or second-best performance across most datasets. No-
321   tably, even without leveraging the spatial location information in MOP, its performance is only
322   slightly inferior to that of Seurat. In summary, the above results not only indicate that ZerOmics,
323   as a zero-shot method, has achieved state-of-the-art (SOTA) in the CTA task, but also suggest that
      LLMs are capable of comprehending cell-type information in SC datasets.

372   4.3    C ROSS - TASK ZERO - SHOT PERFORMANCE (RQ2)
373
374   We explore whether ZerOmics, as an instruction-tuned LLM, has the emergent ability to tackle the

405   4.4   M ODULE A BLATION STUDY (RQ3)
406
407   To address RQ3, we remove or replace the main components of ZerOmics to assess their effective-
408   ness. Specifically, we analyze the impact of gene text summaries, the SC Model, and the mixture of
409   tuning-specific LoRAs. Comparison results of original and variant models are presented in Table 5.
410
      Additionally, all variant models are trained with changed settings from scratch.
411
412   Text Summary. Our primary concern is whether the model’s strong performance attributes to
413   memorize text summaries rather than using them to represent biological information within cells.
414   To investigate the role of text summaries, we replace them with non-textual gene embeddings ex-
415   tracted from well-pretrained models, including Gene2vec (Du et al., 2019), Geneformer (Theodoris
416
      et al., 2023), and GeneCompass (Yang et al., 2024). While the original model outperforms all its
      variants, utilizing Geneformer and GeneCompass to embed genes demonstrates relatively stronger
417
      performance. This indicates that text summaries are not the decisive factor behind superior perfor-
418
      mance. However, as our model employs an LLM as the task processor, representing genes as text
419   features often yields better results than pre-trained gene embeddings based on expression data.
420
421
422   SC Model. We address two primary concerns regarding the SC Model: (1) Can it be replaced with
423
      a simpler model? (2) Given the pre-trained SC Model, whether the SC tokenizer is redundant during
      the instruction tuning stage? To explore these questions, we first test GenePT (Chen & Zou, 2024),
424
      which generates gene text embeddings using an LLM and directly represents each cell by weight-
425
      ing these embeddings with expression values. Next, we compare the original model with a variant
426   without the SC tokenizer (w/o SCT). Both variants show performance degradation; however, the
427   change in performance for GenePT on the TCD task is insignificant. This suggests that combining
428   text summaries is insufficient to fully capture the specificity of individual cells, resulting in subop-
429   timal performance on cell-level tasks such as CTA. But this simple combination may be completely
430   sufficient for TCD tasks. Furthermore, our results indicate that SCT remains an indispensable com-
431   ponent of ZerOmics, primarily serving to align SC embeddings derived from general pre-training
      with the semantic space of the LLM.

      Mixture of LoRAs. ZerOmics employs a mixture of LoRAs for tuning the LLM. To analyze their
455
      necessity, we construct two variants by removing the universal LoRA (uLoRA) and task-specific
456   LoRAs (tLoRA), referred to as “w/o uLoRA” and “w/o tLoRA”, respectively. Both variants exhibit
457   significantly worse performance compared to the original model. Notably, in CTA and RCI tasks, the
458   w/o uLoRA variant significantly outperforms w/o tLoRA, while the difference is less pronounced
459   in the TCD task. We attribute this to the tendency of a single LoRA to prioritize optimization for
460   simpler tasks like TCD, leading to the forgetting of information relevant to other tasks. Although
461   task-specific LoRAs provide independent low-rank spaces, the resulting redundancy disrupts the
462   model’s overall performance.

464   4.5     LLM’ S SCALABILITY STUDY (RQ4)
465
466   We further explore the scalability of the LLM within ZerOmics to address RQ4. Specifically, we
467   investigate whether the parameter size and the pre-training quality of the LLM are capable of signif-
468   icantly changing the model performance. We assess instruction tuning performance across several
469   models, including LLaMA2-7B, 13B, 70B, as well as LLaMA3-8B and 70B (Dubey et al., 2024),
470   with the latter being pre-trained on larger, higher-quality datasets. Results are showcased in Fig-
471   ure 4. Concerning the parameter size, larger models consistently yield superior results, displaying
472
      an upward performance trend, except for the F1-score on Airway and Accuracy in the CTC task.
      Reducing the parameter size does not substantially diminish the performance compared to previous
473
      SOTA models. With regard to pre-training data, LLaMA3 does not consistently surpass LLaMA2.
474
      indicating that ZerOmics benefits more from larger models capable of capturing complex inter-
475   actions between SC data and textual information rather than from merely expanding the LLM’s
476   knowledge base, which enables it to discern relationships within specific SC tasks effectively.

479   5     C ONCLUSION
480
481   In this study, we introduce ZerOmics, a novel LLM-based framework designed for zero-shot single-
482   cell multi-omics analysis. By simply inputting data and posing queries in natural language, Ze-
483   rOmics intelligently addresses various biological tasks. We conduct comprehensive evaluations
484   of its performance across diverse tissues, sequencing technologies, and species. The results con-
485   firm that ZerOmics achieves state-of-the-art performance, underscoring its potential to revolutionize
      single-cell multi-omics analysis.

      A.1    S INGLE - CELL DATASETS COLLECTION
759
760   We assemble single-cell multi-omics data from Homo sapiens and Mus musculus. We collect 1,465
761   relevant datasets primarily from the well-organized CELLxGENE database (Megill et al., 2021) at
762   https://cellxgene.cziscience.com/, encompassing approximately 91.5 million cells and 900
763   cell types. These datasets are primarily categorized into single-cell transcriptomics and epigenomics,
764   utilizing various sequencing technologies.
765   Single-cell transcriptomics datasets sequenced with different technologies are incorporated into the
766   benchmark, including 10x scRNA-seq (Kolodziejczyk et al., 2015), Smart-seq (Goetz & Trimarchi,
767   2012), and Drop-seq (Macosko et al., 2015), etc. Additionally, spatial transcriptomics datasets at sin-
768   gle (or sub-single) cellular resolution, such as Slide-seq (Rodriques et al., 2019), are also included.
769   Single-cell epigenomics datasets are primarily sequenced with 10x scATAC-seq (Buenrostro et al.,
770   2013) and snmC-seq (Luo et al., 2017). All the datasets are transformed into a high-dimensional
771
      matrix X ∈ RN ×G , where Xi,j denotes the expression read counts of the j-th gene in the i-th cell,
      and N and G denote the number of cells and genes, respectively. Most datasets include annotation
772
      label files for cell types. Datasets related to diseases include annotation label files for disease types,
773
      while those not associated with diseases are uniformly labeled as normal cells. Additionally, spatial
774   transcriptomics datasets are organized into the same files, omitting their unique tissue domain type
775   annotation and spatial coordinate annotation files.
776
777   A.2    G ENE CORPUS COLLECTION
778
779   We employ GeneCards (Rebhan et al., 1997) as gene corpus. GeneCards is an extensive textual
780   database that offers a comprehensive view of the currently available genomic, proteomic, transcrip-
781   tomic, genetic, and functional information on more than 350,000 known and predicted human genes,
782   serving as an “encyclopedia” for biomedical research (Harel et al., 2009). The original data is avail-
783   able at https://www.genecards.org/. As examples, Table 6 lists some of the gene summary items
784
      from our gene corpus. Given a known gene, the corresponding item first summarizes its type and
      functional information, followed by its associated diseases, biological processes, and other genes,
785
      respectively. Additionally, to ensure that the gene names are meaningful and understandable, the
786
      HUGO Gene Nomenclature (Bruford et al., 2020) is uniformly used to provide the unique identifier
787   gene symbols, which are usually abbreviations of gene functions. Genes not included in the HUGO
788   Gene Nomenclature are often not studied in depth and are therefore discarded. Finally, we collect
789   43,850 gene summary items to constitute the gene corpus.

791   A.3    DATA PREPROCESSING
792
793   ZerOmics preprocesses all the single-cell multi-omics datasets with a unified pipeline as follows:
794
795   Gene list mapping. After collecting the large-scale SC datasets and gene text corpus, we first
796   transform their gene symbols to the HUGO Gene Nomenclature. Then we take the intersection
797   of the genes in the gene text corpus and the SC datasets to form the one-to-one correspondence.
798
      Additionally, due to different sequencing protocols or different completeness, a single SC dataset
      often does not contain all the genes obtained by taking the intersection here. For each SC expression
799
      matrix, the expression values of these dropout genes are filled with zero. Thus, all the SC expression
800
      matrics are transformed to have the same column names (gene symbols).
801
802   Quality control and normalization. Low-quality cells, such as cells expressing few genes, are
803   removed uniformly with Scanpy (Wolf et al., 2018). Here, we only keep the cells with over 200
804   genes expressed (i.e., the number of non-zero genes in expression vectors > 200) for subsequent
805   training and analysis. To alleviate the differences in gene expression between different datasets due
806   to sequencing depth, the total gene expression of each cell is normalized to 10,000 (i.e., library size).
807   Considering the subsequent Binning process, these datasets are not transformed by log1p.
808
809   Binning and tokenization. To map continuous gene expression values to discrete tokens, non-
      zero gene expression values are divided into different bins according to their quantile values among


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      all non-zero values. Here, we set the number of bins as 10. So, for example, if a non-zero SC
811   expression value is in the bottom 5% of all non-zero values, it’s assigned to the 1st bin; if it is in
812   the bottom 18%, it’s assigned to the 2nd bin, and so on. ZerOmics does not focus on the specific
813   SC expression value, but rather on its relative expression level. Therefore, in the pre-training stage
814   of SC Model, all SC expression tokens include 12 types, namely 10 bin value tokens, zero value
815   tokens, and the special mask token.
816
817   Dataset splitting. We categorize the SC datasets in our study based on their usages: (1) large-scale
818   datasets used for Zeromics’ pre-training and instruction tuning stage (2) training and fine-tuning
819   datasets split from evaluation datasets used for baseline methods training and fine-tuning (unavail-
820   able for ZerOmics in zero-shot setting, partially available for ZerOmics in few-shot settings) (3) 9
821   held-out evaluation datasets, which are collected from diverse studies and labs only used for testing
822   model performance. The detailed train-test split strategy between type 2 and type 3 datasets is simi-
823
      lar to Langcell (Zhao et al., 2024), which can be also seen in each task description of Appendix B.1.
      Therefore, type 2 and type 3 datasets are split from the same evaluation datasets, sharing the same
824
      sampling conditions and sequencing processes, which approximately satisfy the same distribution
825
      assumption. In contrast, type 1 datasets originate from independent studies with different research
826   objectives and sampling conditions, resulting in no correspondence with the held-out datasets. Con-
827   sequently, type 3 dataset is designated as the unseen held-out datasets for evaluation.

831
832   B.1   E VALUATION TASKS AND METRICS
833
      Cell type annotation (CTA). CTA is the most classic multi-classification task in single-cell anal-
834
      ysis. The conventional analysis process is to use the given SC dataset and cell type label file to train
835
      a classifier and use it to identify cell types in the same type of test set. For ZerOmics in the zero-
836   shot setting, only the dataset needs to be provided, and it randomly assigns an instruction template
837   to generate a text response for the cell type. Since supervised and fine-tuning methods need to be
838   re-learned on downstream datasets, we further divide the evaluation datasets into training and test
839   sets according to the common 2:1. We compare the classification results of all methods on the test
840   set with the widely used metrics, accuracy and macro F1-score.
841
842   Rare cell identification (RCI). RCI is a special two-class classification task with imbalanced
843   classes. The conventional analysis process is to use the given SC dataset and cell type label file to
844   train a model. However, in the test set, the models compare the cells from the new dataset with the
845   existing samples, and those samples that are difficult to be classified into existing types are regarded
846   as rare cells. For ZerOmics in zero-shot setting, similar to the CTA process, the LLM directly
847   generates binary classification text results. The evaluation datasets are split according to 2:1, and all
848
      methods generate results on the test set for comparison. F1-score and the Cohen Kappa score (κ)
      are employed for evaluation, where κ is a metric that compares the prediction result with random
849
      guessing and is often used to detect imbalanced class samples.
850
851
      Tumor cell discovery (TCD). TCD is also a two-class classification task. Unlike CTA and RCI,
852
      the conventional analysis process usually does not provide label files about tumor type of each cell,
853
      and the model needs to identify cancer cells in an unsupervised or weakly supervised manner. For
854   ZerOmics in zero-shot setting, the LLM not only needs to respond to whether it is a tumor cell, but
855   also needs to respond to what subtype of tumor it is. This strategy is mainly to enable ZerOmics to
856   better understand and infer information related to cancer. The evaluation datasets are split according
857   to 2:1, and all methods generate results on the test set for comparison. In addition, to ensure the
858   consistency of the evaluation, the tumor subtypes predicted by ZerOmics are not considered, that is,
859   the predicted results are uniformly treated as binary classification. Lastly, accuracy and F1-score are
860   served as the evaluation metrics.
861
862   Cell pathway inference (CPI). In this study, CPI is a multi-category independent binary classifi-
863   cation task. Specifically, we focus on the 41 hallmark pathways from the Broad Institute’s Molecular
      Signatures Database (MSigDB) (Liberzon et al., 2011). For each cell, the model needs to determine


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      which one or several of these pathways it expresses. Since the ground truth of the pathway is of-
865   ten not directly provided, we use irGESA (Fan et al., 2024) to analyze the pathways that each cell
866   may express in the evaluation datasets. Note that irGESA is not a predictive model, but a one-
867   to-one matching model for the gene expression pattern of each cell and all known cell pathways.
868   Geneformer and LangCell also need to use the generated labels for supervised fine-tuning, while
869   ZerOmics in the zero-shot setting uses instructions to directly generate answers in a way similar
870   to answering multiple-choice questions. The evaluation datasets are split according to 2:1, and all
871   methods generate results on the test set for comparison. We employ the Area Under the Receiver
872   Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC) and Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC) for
873
      evaluation. Due to the complexity of the predicted labels, these two metrics are also calculated using
      two different strategies, namely average and flatten. Average (a-) metrics treat the 41 prediction re-
874
      sults of each cell as independent samples for calculation, and then averages the AUROC or AUPRC
875
      results of each cell. While flatten (f-) metrics the 41 prediction results of all n cells as independent
876   samples and calculates AUROC or AUPRC on these 41*n samples.

948   C.2   B ENCHMARK METHODS
949
950   In this section, we offer a concise overview of each benchmark method utilized in this study.
951
952   Spatial-ID. Spatial-ID (Shen et al., 2022) is a supervised benchmark for the CTA task, which is
953   tailored for ST data. It first employs transfer learning to train a deep neural network (DNN) pre-
954   trained on the reference scRNA-seq data. In the inference stage, it leverages a variational graph
955   autoencoder (VGAE) to contain spot embeddings, followed by feeding them into the DNN-based
956
      classifier to generate pseudo-labels for each spot. The spatial embeddings are then combined with
      gene expression profiles to refine cell type predictions via a self-supervised learning approach.
957
958
      scJoint. scJoint (Lin et al., 2022) is designed for the CTA task for both scRNA-seq and scATAC-
959
      seq data. Initially, it leverages annotated scRNA-seq data to guide the training process, transferring
960
      labels to unlabeled scATAC-seq data via constructing a KNN graph based on cell-cell similarities
961   between two omics. scJoint simultaneously trains on labeled and unlabeled data, enabling effective
962   label transfer and integration across heterogeneous multi-omics datasets.
963
964   Seurat. Seurat v5 (Hao et al., 2023) serves as one of the baseline methods for the unsupervised
965   CTA task. It first builds a K-nearest neighbor (KNN) graph based on cell-cell similarities, followed
966   by community detection to annotate cells into subtypes via the Louvain algorithm.
967
968   scBERT. scBERT (Yang et al., 2022), as the pioneer of large-scale pre-trained models for SC data,
969   utilizes the performer (Choromanski et al., 2020) architecture with 6M parameters and pre-trained
970   on over 1M unlabeled, preprocessed scRNA-seq samples. At the supervised fine-tuning stage, the
971   pre-trained encoder is tuned with labeled task-specific scRNA-seq data to adapt distinct downstream
      tasks. We employ scBERT as a baseline method for the CTA task in this study.


                                                        18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Geneformer. Geneformer (Theodoris et al., 2023) is pre-trained on nearly 30M scRNA-seq data
973    samples, and adopts a CNN-based feature generator to learn cell representations, followed by a
974    transformer-based entropy model. The model employs a latent array to manage sequence length to
975    solve the gene compression problem. Finally, Geneformer applies transfer learning across various
976    biological tasks. Geneformer is included as a benchmark for the CTA and CPI tasks in this study.
977
978    scGPT. scGPT (Cui et al., 2024) serves as a benchmark method for CTA and RCI tasks in this pa-
979    per. The model is generatively pre-trained on over 33M scRNA-seq samples, followed by supervised
980    fine-tuning for specific downstream tasks, including CTA and batch integration.
981
982    LangCell. Langcell (Zhao et al., 2024) is a recent work that integrates textual information with
983    gene expression profiles during the pre-training stage. It builds a cell-text dataset utilized for pre-
984    training vis four tasks, including mask gene modeling, cell-cell contrastive learning, cell-text con-
985    trastive learning, and cell-text matching to recognize the intricate relationships between SC and text
986    modalities. We employ it as a benchmark method for the CTA task.
987
988    scVI. scVI (Lopez et al., 2018), tailored for scRNA-seq data analysis, implements a completely
989
       probabilistic framework based on a hierarchical Bayesian model. The gene expression profiles are
       firstly encoded into low-dimensional embeddings and then decoded for computing posterior esti-
990
       mates of the distributional parameters for each gene in each cell. scVI is utilized as a benchmark for
991
       the RCI task.
992
993
       MARS. MARS (Brbić et al., 2020) is a baseline method for the RCI task. MARS firstly predefines
994
       a set of cluster landmarks that are equal to the number of known cell types for the unannotated
995    dataset. Subsequently, unlabeled cells are assigned to the cluster of the closest target landmark in
996    the embedding space. The assigned cell clusters are matched to annotated cell-type landmarks in the
997    annotated dataset, identifying those with uncertain matching as rare cell types.
998
999    scBalance. scBalance (Cheng et al., 2023) is a framework specifically designed for the RCI task.
1000   It combines weight sampling and sparse neural networks to emphasize minor cell types without dis-
1001   rupting the annotation efficiency of the major cell populations. scBalance outperforms in handling
1002   imbalanced datasets, thus we use it as a benchmark for the RCI task.
1003
1004   CopyKAT. CopyKAT (Gao et al., 2021) serves as a baseline method for the TCD task. It performs
1005   hierarchical clustering to categorize cells into clusters according to their estimated gene copy number
1006   profiles. Clusters that are significantly enriched in predefined highly confident normal spots in the
1007   enrichment analysis (P-value ≤ 0.05) are designated as normal cells and others as tumors.
1008
1009   CaSee. CaSee (Sh et al., 2022), tailored for distinguishing tumors from normal cells, pre-training
1010   the model on a vast of bulk RNA-seq data, followed by employing transfer learning to detect tumors
1011
       in scRNA-seq data processed by a capsule network. CaSee is a baseline method for the TCD task in
       this study.
1012
1013
1014
       ikarus. ikarus (Dohmen et al., 2022) is designed for detecting tumors from normal cells at the
       SC level. It first identifies a comprehensive tumor cell signature in the form of a gene set by con-
1015
       solidating abundant annotated datasets. It then employs a logistic regression classifier for stringent
1016
       discrimination of tumor and normal cells, supplemented by a network-based propagation of cell la-
1017   bels using a custom-built cell-cell network. We leverage ikarus as a baseline for the TCD task in this
1018   study.

       C.3   M ODEL ARCHITECTURE AND TRAINING DETAILS
1021
1022   In the single-cell model, the number of bins is set to 10. Therefore, the SC expression is processed
1023   into 12 different types of tokens: 10 bin tokens, a zero value token, and a special mask token. Then,
1024   the dimensions of SC expression embeddings are set the same as text embeddings for addition by
1025   the broadcasting mechanism. The main network of the SC Model is a stack of multiple layers of
       Transformer Encoders. We set it to 12 layers of Transformer Encoders, with 12 attention heads in


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       each layer. In mask learning, we set pm = 0.15 for masking bin tokens and ps = 0.1 for randomly
1027   replacing tokens with others.
1028
1029   For the LLM, ZerOmics uses LLaMA2-13B by default. Each SC embedding is mapped to 500 LLM
1030
       tokens through a linear layer (tokenizer) and inserted into the instruction. We select three repre-
       sentative and important SC analysis tasks: cell type annotation, rare cell identification, and tumor
1031
       cell discovery. For each task, we also independently design three instruction templates. During
1032
       fine-tuning, each cell is randomly assigned a task and a corresponding instruction. In the mixture
1033   of LoRAs, we design universal and three task-specific LoRAs, each with the same structure, using
1034   r = 8 and α = r.
1035
1036
       For both pre-training and instruction tuning stages, we set the total batch size to 64, which means the
       pre-GPU batch size is 8. We use AdamW as the optimizer and the learning rate warmup and cosine
1037
       decay strategies are also used in both stages. The learning rates of the two stages are different, the
1038
       former is set to 1e-4, and the latter is set to 5e-4.
