009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning models frequently exploit spurious features in training data to
012            achieve low training error, often resulting in poor generalization when faced with
013            shifted testing distributions. To address this issue, various methods from imbal-
014            anced learning, representation learning, and classifier recalibration have been pro-
015            posed to enhance the robustness of deep neural networks against spurious correla-
016            tions. In this paper, we observe that models trained with empirical risk minimiza-
017            tion tend to generalize well for examples from the majority groups while memo-
018
               rizing instances from minority groups.Building on recent findings that show mem-
               orization can be localized to a limited number of neurons, we apply example-tied
019
               dropout as a method we term FairDropout, aimed at redirecting this memoriza-
020
               tion to specific neurons that we subsequently drop out during inference. We em-
021            pirically evaluate FairDropout using the subpopulation benchmark suite encom-
022            passing vision, language, and healthcare tasks, demonstrating that it significantly
023            reduces reliance on spurious correlations.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Deep neural networks trained with empirical risk minimization (ERM) continue to achieve remark-
029   able performance on a wide range of tasks. However, ERM-trained models can experience a drop in
030   predictive performance when facing a variety of subpopulation shifts (Yang et al., 2023; Quionero-
031   Candela et al., 2009). In particular, if datasets contain spurious features, i.e., patterns that are highly
032   predictive of the training labels but not causally related to the target, ERM may fail to learn robust
033
      features that generalize across subpopulation shifts (Geirhos et al., 2020). For example, image clas-
      sifiers can make use of non-robust features such as image backgrounds or hair colors, which may be
034
      not relevant to the task. The usage of such spurious features (e.g., hair colors) for some domains can
035
      hurt the fairness of classifiers, thus raising potential safety concerns in deployment (Amodei et al.,
036   2016).
037
038   To address the problem of learning more robust features in the presence of spurious features, several
039
      works have been proposed. The widely practical setup is to assume the presence of a group partition
      in datasets (Liu et al., 2021; Sagawa et al., 2019). In such a setting, training labels and spurious
040
      features can be highly correlated in a particular group of the training distribution, but not in testing
041
      distributions. Thus, naive training algorithms can easily maximize training performance by relying
042   on spurious features, but observe a significant drop in worst-group performance on testing when
043   this correlation does not hold. Most of the existing work in this area assumes the presence of group
044   annotations in the training set to learn more robust-to-spurious-correlation features. For example,
045   GroupDRO (Sagawa et al., 2019) directly minimizes the worst group error. However, this type of
046   work has a hard requirement to know prior training group labels, which is impractical in large-scale
047   datasets. There exist other works that do not assume this availability of group annotations on the
048   training set. An example is DFR Kirichenko et al. (2023), which observes that ERM learns core (or
049   robust) and spurious features, and then proposes a two-stage approach, where the first stage is ERM
050
      and the second stage is classifier retraining with a group-balanced validation dataset. While DFR
      has been successful in improving worst group performance, it still needs group annotations to form
051
      a group-balanced set to down-weigh spurious features.
052
053   In this work, we hypothesize that reducing example-level memorization can address spurious corre-
      lations without the need for group labels. Building on recent advances that explain the interconnec-

054
      tion between memorization and generalization (Baldock et al., 2021; Maini et al., 2023), we study
055   this interconnection for the first time in the context of spurious correlation. We further apply a re-
056   cently proposed technique to localize memorization, originally designed in label noise settings with
057   small networks, scaling it to larger networks, and demonstrating its application to the spurious cor-
058   relation setting for the first time. We name our method FairDropout. FairDropout fairly distributes
059   memorizing neurons during training, and during dropout, we drop out these neurons. Our contribu-
060   tions can be summarized as follows: (i) We show a discrepancy in behaviors between majority group
061   and minority group generalization and link this phenomenon to memorization. (ii) We show for the
062   first time that one can scale and apply the example-tied dropout –previously used in label noise set-
063
      tings with smaller networks (Maini et al., 2023)– to larger architectures such as ResNet-50 (He et al.,
      2016) and BERT (Sung et al., 2019), and term it as FairDropout. (iii) We evaluate FairDropout on
064
      the subpopulation benchmark suite and show improvements over worst-group accuracy on image,
065
      medical (X-Ray), and language tasks.

068   2     R ELATED W ORK
069
070   Methods have been proposed to fight against spurious correlation.
071
072   2.1   U SING T RAINING G ROUP I NFORMATION
073
      Most methods that fight against spurious correlation assume to have training group annotations.
074
      Some methods directly adapt ERM. For instance, groupDRO (Sagawa et al., 2019) and its vari-
075
      ant CVaRDRO (Duchi & Namkoong, 2021) aim to minimize the worst group error rather than the
076   average error used by ERM. Similarly, when group information is known, methods from out-of-
077   distribution generalization (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Krueger et al., 2021; Wald et al., 2021; Krueger
078   et al., 2021) can be framed to learn more robust-to-spurious-correlation features. Other approaches
079   use training group information to synthetically augment minority group samples via generative mod-
080   eling (Goel et al., 2020). Reweighting and subsampling techniques can also be employed to balance
081   majority and minority groups (Sagawa et al., 2020; Byrd & Lipton, 2019). However, all these works
082   share a major limitation: they rely on the knowledge of group information, which is not easily scal-
083   able to large datasets. Manually annotating group labels requires task-specific expertise, making it
084
      prohibitively expensive.
085
086   2.2   W ITHOUT U SING T RAINING G ROUP I NFORMATION
087
      Given the expensive cost of manual group annotation, there has been a growing interest in combating
088
      spurious correlation without group annotations in the training set. Some methods, after observing
089   the training dynamics of SGD, propose regularization terms based on margins to learn more robust
090   features (Pezeshki et al., 2021; Puli et al., 2023). Two-stage algorithms, among the most popular
091   methods that do not assume the knowledge of training group information, typically start with ERM.
092   In this first stage, the minority group is inferred, and in the second stage, robustness to spurious
093   correlations is introduced, for example through contrastive learning (Zhang et al., 2022) or by up-
094   weighting the loss of inferred minority group samples (Qiu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021). A recent
095   study (Kirichenko et al., 2023) explains the importance of the first stage ERM, by showing that ERM
096   learns both spurious and core (or robust to spurious correlation) features. It then proposes retraining
097
      only the classifier head in the second stage using a group-balanced validation set. This approach
      has been extended to HTT-DFR (Hameed et al., 2024), where the second phase involves retraining
098
      a sparse network. Our work is inspired by this observation of the ability to learn core features from
099
      ERM, but instead of using a more computationally demanding two-phase algorithm, our work on
100   reducing spurious features by its link to the memorizing neurons.

347   Metrics. According to most previous works, we evaluate the reliance on spurious correlation
348   through worst-group accuracy.
349   Baseline methods. We compare the FairDropout with state-of-the-art algorithms implemented in
350   the subpopulation shift benchmark. Our work does not need the knowledge of group information.
351   We thus evaluate our method in the setting where we do not have group information. However, meth-
352   ods that need group information have been converted by Yang et al. (2023) to an equivalent method
353   by considering class information instead of group. For example, GroupDRO can be converted by
354   an equivalent goal of minimizing worst-class error. Benchmarked methods from the spurious corre-
355   lation literature include GroupDRO (Sagawa et al., 2019), CVaRDRO (Duchi & Namkoong, 2021),
356   JTT (Liu et al., 2021), LfF (Nam et al., 2020), LISA . There are also two-phase methods that retrain
357
      the classifier, which are DFR (Yao et al., 2022) (retraining is done on the validation set), CRT and its
      variant ReWeightCRT (Kang et al., 2020). Finally, we also include methods that are mostly designed
358
      for the imbalanced learning problem, which are ReSample (Japkowicz, 2000), ReWeight (Japkow-
359
      icz, 2000), SqrtReWeight (Japkowicz, 2000), CBLoss (Cui et al., 2019), LDAM Cao et al. (2019)
360   and BSoftmax (Ren et al., 2020). Note that the FairDropout technique can be combined with any of
361   these baseline methods to boost its performance.
362
363
      Hyperparameter tuning. As we consider the most difficult case we do not have group information
      for the training and validation sets, similarly with the benchmark Yang et al. (2023), we tune the
364
      pmem , pgen , learning rate, and weight decay with the worst-class accuracy. We use the SGD optimizer
365
      with weight decay.
366
367   Positions of the FairDropout Layers. In principle, the FairDropout layer can be placed after any
368   intermediate layer in the network. However, in large-scale, potentially pre-trained models, the place-
369
      ment of FairDropout may require careful consideration. In models with skipped connections as in
      ResNet-50, in our settings, we consider possible positions after residual blocks. In BERT-like mod-
370
      els, we propose adding a new linear layer before the classifier head and positioning the FairDropout

      layer there. This ensures that the pertaining features are preserved while controlling memorization

374   with other optimization hyperparameters using worst-class accuracy as a guiding metric.

      4.2.2    R ESULTS AND D ISCUSSION
404
405
406
      We report the worst-group accuracy results obtained after running our FairDropout method on the
407   subpopulation shift library, averaged over 5 independent runs. Table 1 presents these results with
408   methods categorized according to the presentation done in Sec. 4.2.1, following Yang et al. (2023).
409   As a reminder, in this setting, the spurious attribute and the group annotations are unavailable in
410   both the training and validation datasets. All the methods or their adapted version are tuned with
411   worst-class accuracy and the results come from Yang et al. (2023).
412
      From the table, we can make the following observations. In all datasets and except Waterbirds, our
413   FairDropout method outperforms ERM by a large margin.
414
415   On these datasets, we can also observe that the FairDropout outperforms or has comparable per-
416
      formance to spurious correlation methods and imbalance learning methods. More specifically, the
      datasets on which FairDropout achieves the most successful results are MultiNLI (70.3 ± 2.4) and
417
      MIMIC-CXR (70.6 ± 0.6).
418
419   On CelebA and MetaShift, although our FairDropout technique outperforms spurious correlation
420   methods, its performance is comparable with the Resample on CelebA (75.6 ± 2.1 vs 74.1 ± 2.2)
421   and ReWeightCRT on Metashift (85.9 ± 1.1 vs 85.1 ± 0.4). It is worth mentioning that our models
422
      with the FairDropout are trained with classic cross-entropy, meaning that the performance of our
      FairDropout technique can be further boosted with any of these existing imbalanced learning or
423
      classifier retraining methods.
424
425   On the Waterbirds dataset, although our FairDropout improves upon ERM, it underperforms clas-
426   sifier retraining methods and some imbalanced learning methods. Since Waterbirds is a dataset
427   synthetically generated by placing bird objects into different backgrounds, it has already been ob-
428
      served that ImageNet pre-trained ImageNet features can be effectively transferred (Izmailov et al.,
      2022) without finetuning the entire model, which may explain the superior performance of DFR.
429
430   Overall, FairDropout proves to be an effective method for reducing reliance on spurious correlations
431   without explicit group annotations. It may also benefit from additional boosts if combined with
      classifier retraining or imbalanced learning methods.

432
      5   L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
433
434
      In this paper, we explored for the first time the lack of generalization of minority-group examples
435   and its link to memorization in spurious correlation. We introduced the FairDropout, an example-
436   tied dropout technique that can be applied in larger networks to reduce the reliance on spurious
437   correlation.
438
      FairDropout makes it possible to localize memorization and attracts the spurious features in such
439
      fixed neurons that once dropped during inference, can improve worst-group accuracy. We show
440
      empirical evidence that the FairDropout outperforms several baseline methods on datasets from
441   image, medical, and language tasks.
442
443   However, our study has some limitations that have not been addressed. First, there have been works
444
      showing that there may exist memorization that is beneficial for generalization (Feldman, 2020)–
      this warrants further investigation, particularly in the case of the Waterbird dataset. Second, while
445
      implicitly by construction, we hypothesize that generalizing neurons are less likely to memorize
446
      examples since memorization is more easily achieved in the memorizing neurons, this assumption
447   requires further exploration, which falls outside the scope of this paper.
