009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The rapid advancement of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) has
012            been accompanied by the development of various benchmarks to evaluate their
013            capabilities. However, the true nature of these evaluations and the extent to which
014            they assess multimodal reasoning versus merely leveraging the underlying Large
015            Language Model (LLM) backbone remain unclear. This paper presents a com-
016            prehensive investigation into the role of LLM backbones in MLLM evaluation,
017            focusing on two critical aspects: the degree to which current benchmarks truly
018
               assess multimodal reasoning and the influence of LLM prior knowledge on per-
               formance. Specifically, we introduce a modified evaluation protocol to disentangle
019
               the contributions of the LLM backbone from multimodal integration, and an au-
020
               tomatic knowledge identification technique for diagnosing whether LLMs equip
021            the necessary knowledge for corresponding multimodal questions. Our study en-
022            compasses four diverse MLLM benchmarks and eight state-of-the-art MLLMs.
023            Key findings reveal that some benchmarks allow high performance even without
024            visual inputs and up to 50% of error rates can be attributed to insufficient world
025            knowledge in the LLM backbone, indicating a heavy reliance on language capabil-
026            ities. To address knowledge deficiencies, we propose a knowledge augmentation
027            pipeline that achieves significant performance gains, with improvements of up to
028            60% on certain datasets, resulting in a approximately 4x increase in performance.
029
               Our work provides crucial insights into the role of the LLM backbone in MLLMs,
               and highlights the need for more nuanced benchmarking approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The rapid development of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023a),
035
      combined with advancements in visual encoders (Radford et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2023) and modal-
036   ity bridge techniques (Liu et al., 2023a; Dai et al., 2023), has catalyzed the evolution of Multi-
037   modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) capable of comprehending diverse multi-modal inputs.
038   Concurrently, diverse benchmarks and leaderboards have emerged to evaluate various multimodal
039   perception and reasoning capabilities (Lu et al., 2022b; Lerner et al., 2022; Yue et al., 2024a).
040
      While these benchmarks aim to assess multimodal capabilities, the role of the underlying LLM
041
      backbone in MLLM performance remains poorly understood. Recent studies (Tong et al., 2024;
042   Yue et al., 2024c) have highlighted that some benchmarks demonstrate an excessive dependence
043   on the language model component, allowing MLLMs to achieve high scores even without visual
044   inputs. This observation raises critical questions about the true nature of multimodal reasoning
045   in these models and the extent to which performance is driven by the LLM backbone rather than
046   multimodal integration. Furthermore, as different MLLMs utilize LLM backbones with distinct
047   knowledge priors learned from various pre-training corpora (Gao et al., 2020; Penedo et al., 2023),
048   this knowledge inconsistency leads to incomparable evaluation scores when comparing MLLMs
049   with different underlying LLMs. These issues can result in misinterpretation of evaluation scores
050
      and may misguide research and deployment of MLLMs by providing an inaccurate assessment of
      their true multimodal capabilities.
051
052   In this paper, we present an in-depth investigation into the role of LLM backbones in MLLM evalua-
053   tion, focusing on two key aspects: (i) the extent to which current benchmarks truly assess multimodal
      reasoning versus relying on language capabilities alone, and (ii) the influence of LLM prior knowl-


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      edge on final performance. To address the first question, we propose an approach that goes beyond
055   simply evaluating models without visual cues (Tong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a). Our method
056   incorporates comparisons with shuffled options and transforms multiple-choice quesiont-answering
057   (QA) formats into open-ended generation tasks, providing a more comprehensive understanding
058   of the role of language capabilities versus true multimodal reasoning in these benchmarks. For
059   the second question, we develop an automatic knowledge identification method utilizing external
060   knowledge bases such as Wikipedia or powerful LLMs (OpenAI, 2023b) to obtain the necessary
061   knowledge behind each question. With these knowledge facts prepared, we examine whether the
062   underlying LLM backbone possesses the requisite world knowledge for multimodal questions, en-
063
      abling a better understanding of the obtained scores.
064   We select four benchmarks covering different capabilities of MLLMs: the comprehensive evaluation
065   benchmark MMMU (Yue et al., 2024a), ScienceQA for multimodal reasoning evaluation (Lu et al.,
066   2022b), and two knowledge-based VQA tasks: Viquae (Lerner et al., 2022) and InfoSeek (Chen
067   et al., 2023). Our experimental results with eight MLLMs reveal significant insights into the role
068   of LLM backbones: (i) LLMs would exploit the shortcuts in question and options, making pre-
069
      dictions without relying on the visual inputs. For example, on the commonly adopted MMMU
      dataset, accuracy scores remain the same for more than 80% of samples even without visual inputs.
070
      Further comparison of datasets and task formats suggests that knowledge-intensive VQA bench-
071
      marks requiring entity recognition from images are less affected by this issue, and LLMs could
072   solely rely on options to achieve prediction without relying on visual inputs. Specifically, we ob-
073   serve that the average performance difference between scenarios with and without visual inputs is
074   markedly lower for multiple-choice questions in MMMU (15%) compared to open-ended questions
075   in InfoSeek (65%). (ii) MLLMs performance shows great dependence on the knowledge of
076   LLM backbones. We observe that up to 50% of error rates on multimodal benchmarks could be
077   attributed to insufficient world knowledge in the LLM backbone. Besides, MLLMs adopting knowl-
078   edgeable LLMs such as LLaVA-Next-Yi-34B and InternVL2-Llama3-76B during evaluation tend
079   to perform better, highlighting the significant impact of the LLM backbone on overall performance.
080
      Motivated by these findings, we develop a simple knowledge augmentation pipeline to retrieve sup-
      plementary background knowledge for answering challenging VQA questions. This approach yields
081
      an average significant 36% absolute accuracy gain, with Phi-3 achieving an impressive improvement
082
      of over 60% on the Viquae dataset. Further analysis demonstrates a trade-off between knowledge
083   recall and the noise introduced by retrieved knowledge paragraphs.
084
085   Our study provides crucial insights into the role of LLM backbones in MLLM evaluation and high-
086
      lights the need for more nuanced benchmarking approaches that can distinguish between language
      model capabilities and true multimodal reasoning. These findings have important implications for
087
      the development and evaluation of future MLLMs, suggesting that both visual integration techniques
088
      and the choice of LLM backbone are critical factors in achieving robust multimodal performance.

144   2.2   I S V ISUAL C APABILITY N ECESSARY ?
145
146   Previous research has demonstrated that the absence of visual input often does not significantly im-
147
      pact model performance on certain visual evaluation datasets (Goyal et al., 2017; Tong et al., 2024;
      Huang et al., 2024). To elucidate this phenomenon, we extend prior work by systematically modi-
148
      fying the VQA task paradigm to assess the role of visual information under varied conditions. Our
149
      methodology involves presenting identical questions to models in image-present and image-absent
150   contexts. Furthermore, we introduce two critical modifications to the multiple-choice format: (1)
151   randomization of multiple-choice option order and (2) reformulation of questions into open-ended
152   queries. These alterations serve dual purposes: randomization of options mitigates potential biases
153   towards specific answer types that may align with training data distributions, while open-ended re-
154   formulation allows us to evaluate whether the apparent diminished reliance on visual information is
155   an artifact of constrained multiple-choice setups, where some options may be trivially eliminable.
156   Our findings indicate that the presence of multiple-choice options significantly reduces both task dif-
157   ficulty and the necessity for visual information processing. This insight offers a nuanced perspective
158
      on the observed similarity in model performance across image-present and image-absent conditions
      in certain VQA datasets, underscoring the critical role of task design in accurately assessing visual
159
      reasoning capabilities.
160
161   To quantify these effects, we conduct a comparative analysis of performance differentials between
      image-present and image-absent scenarios at the dataset level for each model. We also introduce the

      4   R ELATED W ORK
451
      Multimodal Large Language Models. Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have
452
      made remarkable strides in recent years (OpenAI, 2023a; Reid et al., 2024; Ormazabal et al., 2024),
453
      demonstrating an unprecedented ability to understand and generate content that seamlessly inte-
454   grates visual and textual information (Fu et al., 2023). Representative proprietary commercial mod-
455   els, such as OpenAI’s GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023), Google’s Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid et al., 2024),
456   and Anthropic’s Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024), have showcased impressive capabilities in
457   various tasks. On the open-source front, models like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a), Qwen-VL (Bai
458   et al., 2023b) and Phi-Vision (Abdin et al., 2024), have also demonstrated remarkable progress,
459   particularly in their ability to comprehend multiple images or video simultaneously, expanding the
460   scope of MLLMs from static single images to dynamic multi-frame visual content. Our research
461   aims to gain a deeper understanding of MLLM’s performance and limitations, with a special focus
462
      on the role of the LLM backbone. Our experimental results show that current MLLMs rely on the
      LLM backbone heavily on certain benchmarks, and suffer from knowledge deficiency when facing
463
      VQA tasks demanding rich world knowledge. Based on our findings, we introduce a RAG-based
464
      method that significantly enhances model performance.
465
466
      Multimodal Understanding Benchmarks. The rapid advancement of MLLMs has spurred the
467
      development of diverse evaluation benchmarks. These range from specialized tasks like OCR (e.g.,
468
      InfogrpahicsVQA (Mathew et al., 2022), ChartVQA (Masry et al., 2022), DocVQA (Mathew et al.,
469   2021)), knowledge integration (e.g., Viquae (Lerner et al., 2022) and Infoseek (Chen et al., 2023)),
470   and mathematical reasoning (e.g., ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022b) and MathVista (Lu et al., 2023)),
471   to comprehensive frameworks such as MMMU (Yue et al., 2024b), MME (Fu et al., 2023), MM-
472   Bench (Liu et al., 2023b), and MMVet (Yu et al., 2023). Our work contributes to this landscape
473   by critically examining these evaluation frameworks, echoing previous findings that visual inputs
474   may contribute less significantly in these benchmarks (Yue et al., 2024c; Chen et al., 2024a; Tong
475   et al., 2024). Additionally, we show that the multiple-choice format could become a shortcut that
476   the LLMs could leverage to bypass the visual inputs and we also identify certain errors primarily
477
      stem from language knowledge limitations rather than visual perception deficiencies. These findings
      provide valuable insights for developing more robust evaluation benchmarks, emphasizing the need
478
      to disentangle language model capabilities from true multimodal reasoning in MLLM assessment.

481   5   C ONCLUSION
482
483   This study provides a comprehensive analysis of the role of LLM backbones in Multimodal Large
484   Language Model (MLLM) evaluation, shedding light on critical aspects that have been largely over-
485   looked in previous research. Our investigation reveals several key insights that have significant
      implications for the development and evaluation of MLLMs. Our experimental findings first show


                                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      that LLMs could exploit shortcuts by relying on inappropriate options in visual tasks, and that open-
487   ended questions could offer more robust assessments. Secondly, we identify substantial knowledge
488   deficiencies across various datasets, where models fail to provide correct answers despite accurate
489   visual perception. To mitigate this, we implement a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) ap-
490   proach, which significantly improved performance on visual tasks by enhancing the models’ factual
491   knowledge. Further analysis reveals a phenomenon of knowledge misalignment between visual and
492   textual modalities.
