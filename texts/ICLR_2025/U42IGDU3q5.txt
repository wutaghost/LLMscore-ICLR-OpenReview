009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Composed Image Retrieval (CIR) retrieves relevant images using a reference im-
012            age and accompanying text that describes how the desired images differ from the
013            reference. However, the commonly used evaluation metric Recall@k only checks
014            if the target image is retrieved, without considering the relevance of other im-
015            ages to the query, potentially leading to user dissatisfaction. We introduce Com-
016            posed Image Retrieval Query Relevance Score (CIRQRS), an evaluation metric
017            that scores each retrieved image based on its relevance to the query, offering a
018
               comprehensive evaluation. CIRQRS is trained using a reward model objective to
               prefer highly relevant, positive images over less relevant, negative ones. We pro-
019
               pose a strategy motivated by self-paced learning to dynamically adjust the nega-
020
               tive set based on the relevance of each image by using CIRQRS’s current training
021            status. To validate CIRQRS’s ability to measure relevance, we created the human-
022            scored FashionIQ (HS-FashionIQ) dataset and compared it with scores from hu-
023            man evaluators. CIRQRS correlates with human scores 2.625 times better than
024            Recall@k, highlighting its superior ability to capture relevance. Additionally, by
025            ranking images based on their CIRQRS, we check if the target image appears in
026            the top k. The results show that CIRQRS achieves state-of-the-art performance on
027            two representative CIR datasets, CIRR and FashionIQ.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recent developments in multi-modal AI (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; 2023) have trans-
033
      formed image search by using text and images as inputs, moving beyond traditional text-only
      queries. Using a bimodal query (a reference image and relevant text), Composed Image Retrieval
034
      (CIR) (Lee et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) retrieves images from a large corpus
035
      based on user-specified modifications. Figure 1 shows an example where a user provides a shirt
036   image with the text ‘blue t-shirt with short sleeves.’ The system retrieves images that reflect these
037   queries, such as modifications in color or style. CIR enhances search precision, particularly in cases
038   where describing visual details is challenging with text alone, making it valuable for applications in
039   e-commerce and internet search.
040
      Despite progress in CIR, the widely adopted evaluation metric, Recall@k, falls short of capturing
041
      user satisfaction. While user satisfaction improves with the number of relevant items retrieved (Al-
042   Maskari & Sanderson, 2010), Recall@k only checks whether the target image is retrieved, ignoring
043   the relevance of other retrieved images. As shown in Figure 1, Recall@k scores 0 despite retrieving
044   relevant images or 1 despite including irrelevant images. Defining relevance-based metrics in CIR is
045   challenging due to the complexity of attribute modifications (e.g., color and style) and the difficulty
046   of quantifying the relevance of each retrieved image.
047
      We propose Composed Image Retrieval Query Relevance Score (CIRQRS), an evaluation metric that
048   addresses the limitation of Recall@k. CIRQRS assigns score to each retrieved image based on its
049   relevance to the query. To achieve this, we follow a reward model training objective (Ouyang et al.,
050   2022), where CIRQRS is trained to maximize the likelihood that a highly relevant image, positive
051   image, is preferred over a less relevant image, negative image. CIR datasets typically consist of
052   triplets (reference image, relative text, and target image), and we set the target image most relevant
053   to the query as positive. However, selecting appropriate negatives is challenging, as they are not
      predefined and must be less relevant than the target, but not entirely irrelevant. To overcome this

086   details provided in Section 4.

090   assess CIRQRS’s performance with Recall@k on two datasets: CIRR (Suhr et al., 2018) and Fash-
091   ionIQ (Wu et al., 2021). The results show that CIRQRS achieves state-of-the-art CIR performance
092   on both datasets.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
111   Vision-Language Foundation Model. Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have gained attention
112   for their ability to integrate multimodal data. Transformer-based architectures effectively handle
113   both visual and language inputs (Li et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2019). Contrastive learning methods,
114
      which align visual and language modalities, have significantly improved performance in VLMs (Jia
      et al., 2021; Radford et al., 2021). Research has also explored architectures that combine features
115
      from both modalities. For instance, Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) and BLIP (Li et al., 2022) use
116
      cross-attention, where visual hidden states from the vision encoder are inserted into cross-attention
117   layers within the text encoder layers. BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023) and QWEN (Bai et al., 2023) utilize
118   pre-trained image and text encoders with learnable networks that bridge the gap between modalities.
119   CIRQRS adopts BLIP2, using its image and text encoders with the Q-former module to handle
120   modality gaps. We chose BLIP2 for its efficient combination of image and text processing, requiring
121   minimal training of the Q-former module.
122
      Composed Image Retrieval. The CIR task retrieves images using multimodal input features.
123   A common approach is feature fusion, where the reference image and text are jointly embedded
124   and compared against embeddings of candidate images (Vo et al., 2019; Dodds et al., 2020; Liu
125   et al., 2021; Baldrati et al., 2023). Bi-BLIP4CIR (Liu et al., 2024) trains the text encoder using bi-
126   directional training to capture both text directions of a given relation. CASE (Levy et al., 2024) lever-
127   ages BLIP (Li et al., 2022) cross-attention architecture to perform an early fusion between the modal-
128   ities. Other approaches transform images into pseudo-word embeddings or sentence-level prompts
129   for text-to-image retrieval (Liu et al., 2023; Saito et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024). MGUR (Chen et al.,
130   2024) introduces an uncertainty loss for coarse-grained retrieval, and SPN4CIR (Feng et al., 2024)
131
      proposes a data generation method to scale positive and negative samples using multimodal LLMs.
      However, all previous works are evaluated using the metric Recall@K, which has inherent limita-
132
      tions in evaluating retrieved image sets. CIRQRS addresses this issue by proposing a new evaluation
133
      metric that captures the overall relevancy of the retrieved set rather than relying solely on target
134   images as an anchor.
135
136   Self-Paced Learning. Curriculum learning trains models with progressively harder samples to im-
137
      prove the model performance (Bengio et al., 2009). Self-paced learning extends this by dynamically
      determining the difficulty of each sample during training based on the model perception (Kumar
138
      et al., 2010). Further research explores application across tasks (Lee & Grauman, 2011; Tang et al.,
139
      2012) with various criteria used to rank samples, such as objectness function (Jiang et al., 2014) or
140   prior knowledge (Jiang et al., 2015). To train CIRQRS as an accurate scoring model, it is crucial
141   to select an appropriate negative image that is less relevant than the target image. We use a self-
142   paced learning-inspired strategy that dynamically adjusts the negative set based on the difficulty and
143   relevance of each image according to the CIRQRS’s training progress.

149   3.1   OVERVIEW
150
151   We denote a CIR dataset as D = {di | i = 1, . . . , Nd }, where each data point consists of a reference
152   image, relative text, and a target image, i.e., di = {xIi , xTi , yIi }. The goal of CIR is to retrieve a set
153
      of images from the entire candidate image corpus I = {Ij | j = 1, . . . , Nimg }, including the target
      image yI , where the retrieved images reflect the specified relative text xT while preserving the visual
154
      properties of the reference image xI . However, the current evaluation metric in CIR, Recall@k, only
155
      checks if yI is among the top-k retrieved images without considering the relevance of other retrieved
156   images to the query, potentially causing user dissatisfaction (Al-Maskari & Sanderson, 2010). We
157   propose CIRQRS, a new evaluation metric that scores each retrieved image based on its relevance
158   to the query. As shown in Figure 2, CIRQRS is trained to maximize the probability that the highly
159   relevant image is preferred over the less relevant, negative image. To select appropriate negatives,
160   we employ self-paced learning, defining the negative set as the highest CIRQRS images below the
161   target and progressively reducing the negative set size to focus on increasingly difficult negatives as
      training progresses. The training algorithm for CIRQRS is provided in Appendix A.

207   3.3   N EGATIVE S ET D EFINITION
208
209   Due to the large size of D∗ , training on the full dataset is infeasible. To resolve this, we approximate

212   the approximated Lmatch is likely to be sub-optimal, as it may continuously select easy negative
213   with a low CIRQRS. To address this, we build a smaller and more appropriate negative set for each

232   Our novel approach to defining hard negatives stems from our training objective. By designating the
233   target image as the positive and a random image from the negative set as the negative, the model’s
234
      objective is to increase the target image score while lowering the negative image score. Our approach
      excludes images with higher scores than the target from the negative set, thus relevant images are
235
      likely to retain high scores. This approach prevents well-matched images from being treated as
236
      negatives, allowing relevant images to achieve higher scores even if they are not labeled as targets.
237
238   To further aid convergence, we employ a curriculum learning strategy that gradually increases data
239   difficulty. In the initial epochs, we define the negative set D∗hard as I \ {Ip }, randomly selecting
240
      negatives from the entire candidate corpus. At regular intervals, we update the negative set using
      the learned CIRQRS to form new D∗hard . The size of D∗hard is progressively reduced, exposing the
241
      model to increasingly challenging examples, as larger D∗hard will include easier negatives.

291   FashionIQ. We defined the negative set ndef times, warming up CIRQRS with the entire candidate

      corpus as negatives for the first ⌊nepoch /ndef ⌋ epochs. After the warmup, we initially defined the

293
      time. We set ndef to 5 and 6 for FashionIQ and CIRR, respectively. We conducted our experiments

      how relevant images are. As a result, when both sets either include or exclude the target image,
361
      Recall@k fails to capture any differences in the quality of relevance of the other images. In contrast,
362
      CIRQRS takes these factors into account, providing a more detailed assessment that better aligns

      5.5     A BLATION S TUDIES
447
448   Effect of Negative Set Definition Strategy. To evaluate the effectiveness of our approach to defin-
449   ing the negative set, we compare three different strategies. The Baseline method defines the negative
450   set as the entire candidate corpus I \ {Ip }. The Top method selects the top nneg images with the
451   highest CIRQRS as the negative set, initializing nneg at 1000, which yields the best performance.
452   We compared our strategy, which defines the negative set as the top nneg images with CIRQRS lower
453   than the target.
454   Table 7 shows the baseline method performs reasonably well compared with other methods in Sec-
455   tion 5.3. The Top approach improves performance by sampling from a predefined negative set,
456   demonstrating the effectiveness of training with harder negatives. On average, CIRQRS further im-
457   proves by 0.40% in CIRR and 1.54% in FashionIQ than Top. The correlation between human scores
458   and preference rates is consistent across all three approaches. This improvement in Recall@k sug-
459   gests that, despite similar human score correlations, CIRQRS ranks the target image higher than
460   other approaches. This can be attributed to the fact that as CIRQRS learns, many high-scoring im-
461   ages tend to closely match the query, making training with the Top method unstable and suboptimal.
462
      Our strategy mitigates this by selecting images with lower scores than the target image, which pre-
      vents highly relevant images from being selected as negative.
463
464   Figure 3 illustrates the impact of different strategies, showing Recall@1 performance on the CIRR
465   validation set and training loss across all epochs. As described in Section 5.1, we redefine the
466   negative set every eight epochs for the CIRR dataset in both Top and CIRQRS. Performance improves
467   immediately after the first hard negative set is defined, compared to Baseline. CIRQRS shows more
468
      consistent, stable improvement, particularly in early training, while the sharp rise in loss after each
      negative set redefinition reflects the model facing harder negatives. Despite higher loss, CIRQRS
469
      maintains superior performance over Top.

522   6    C ONCLUSION
523
524   We introduced CIRQRS, a new evaluation metric that overcomes the limitation of Recall@k in CIR.
525   Unlike Recall@k, CIRQRS evaluates the relevance of individual retrieved images to the query, pro-
526   viding a comprehensive assessment of retrieval quality. Our approach employs a reward model train-
527   ing objective and a self-paced learning strategy to refine the negative set, improving relevance scor-
528
      ing dynamically. To evaluate the effectiveness of CIRQRS, we created the HS-FashionIQ dataset, the
      first human-scored dataset in CIR. Experimental results show that CIRQRS achieves a significantly
529
      higher correlation with human scores than Recall@k. Using CIRQRS as a CIR model outperforms
530
      state-of-the-art methods across multiple CIR benchmarks, including FashionIQ and CIRR datasets.
