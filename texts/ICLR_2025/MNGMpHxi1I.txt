010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            Reliable estimation of predictive uncertainty is crucial for machine learning appli-
013            cations, particularly in high-stakes scenarios where hedging against risks is essen-
014            tial. Despite its significance, a consensus on the correct measurement of predictive
015            uncertainty remains elusive. In this work, we return to first principles to develop a
016            fundamental framework of information-theoretic predictive uncertainty measures.
017
               Our proposed framework categorizes predictive uncertainty measures according
               to two factors: (I) The predicting model (II) The approximation of the true pre-
018
               dictive distribution. Examining all possible combinations of these two factors, we
019
               derive a set of predictive uncertainty measures that includes both known and newly
020            introduced ones. We empirically evaluate these measures in typical uncertainty
021            estimation settings, such as misclassification detection, selective prediction, and
022            out-of-distribution detection. The results show that no single measure is universal,
023            but the effectiveness depends on the specific setting. Thus, our work provides clar-
024            ity about the appropriateness of predictive uncertainty measures by clarifying their
025            implicit assumptions and relationships.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Integrating machine learning models into high-stakes scenarios, such as autonomous driving or
031   managing critical healthcare systems, introduces substantial risks. To hedge against these risks, we
032   need to quantify the uncertainty associated with each prediction to prevent models from making
033   decisions that carry both significant risk and uncertainty. In such cases, it is better to defer uncertain
034   decisions to human experts or opt for a safer, though potentially less advantageous, alternative decision.
035   Consequently, it is vital to employ reliable measures of predictive uncertainty and provide estimates for
036   them when implementing machine learning models for decision making in high-stakes applications.
037   The entropy of the posterior predictive distribution has become the standard information-theoretic
038   measure to assess predictive uncertainty (Houlsby et al., 2011; Gal, 2016; Depeweg et al., 2018; Smith
039   and Gal, 2018; Mukhoti et al., 2023). Despite its widespread use, this measure has drawn criticism
040   (Malinin and Gales, 2021; Wimmer et al., 2023), prompting the proposal of alternative information-
041   theoretic measures (Malinin and Gales, 2021; Schweighofer et al., 2023b;a; Kotelevskii and Panov,
042   2024; Hofman et al., 2024b). The relationship between those measures is still not well understood,
043   although their similarities suggest that they are special cases of a more general formulation.
044   We show that all these measures are approximations of the cross-entropy between the predicting
045   model and the true model. However, since the true model is not known in general, this fundamental
046   measure is intractable to compute directly. By considering different assumptions about the predicting
047   model and approximations of the true model, we develop a framework to categorize information-
048   theoretic measures of predictive uncertainty. Our framework includes existing measures, introduces
049   new ones, and clarifies the relationship between these measures. Furthermore, our empirical analysis
050
      reveals that the effectiveness of different measures varies depending on the task and the posterior
      sampling method used. In sum, our contributions are as follows:
051
052   1. We introduce a unifying framework to categorize measures of predictive uncertainty according to
053      assumptions about the predicting model and how the true model is approximated. This framework
         not only encompasses existing measures but also suggests new ones and clarifies their relationship.

      2. We derive our framework from first principles, based on the cross-entropy between the predicting
055      model and the true model as the fundamental yet intractable measure of predictive uncertainty.
056
      3. We empirically evaluate these measures across various typical uncertainty estimation tasks and
057      show that their effectiveness depends on the setting and the posterior sampling method used.

      the set of all K possible targets. The dataset D is given, sampled i.i.d according to the data generating
063

066

067

069   The predictive distribution of a probabilistic model represents the uncertainty inherent in its predic-
070   tions. When the probability mass is uniformly distributed across all possible outcomes, it denotes
071
      complete uncertainty about the prediction, whereas concentration on a single class indicates com-
      plete certainty. If we have access to the true data-generating model, denoted by parameters w∗ ,
072
      the predictive distribution p(y | x, w∗ ) captures the inherent and irreducible uncertainty in the pre-
073
      diction, often referred to as aleatoric uncertainty (AU) (Gal, 2016; Kendall and Gal, 2017). This
074   assumes that the chosen model class can accurately represent the true predictive distribution, thus
075   p(y | x) = p(y | x, w∗ ), which is a common and often necessary assumption (Hüllermeier and
076   Waegeman, 2021). The information-theoretic entropy H(·) of the true predictive distribution is a
077   natural and universally accepted measure of aleatoric uncertainty, defined as

089   Given a dataset D and prior p(w) on the model parameters, Bayes’ theorem yields the posterior
090   distribution p(w | D). The posterior distribution denotes the probability that the parameters w match
091
      the true parameters w∗ of the model that generated the dataset D. Instead of committing to a single
      model, the posterior distribution allows marginalizing over all possible models, which is known as
092
      Bayesian model averaging. This gives rise to the posterior predictive distribution

      If the parameters of the true model are known, EU vanishes and Eq. (3) as well as Eq. (4) simplify
109   to Eq. (1), thus are consistent with it. However, the entropy of the posterior predictive distribution
110   has been found to be inadequate for specific scenarios, such as autoregressive predictions (Malinin
111   and Gales, 2021) or for a given predicting model (Schweighofer et al., 2023b) and was criticised
112   on grounds of not fulfulling certain expected theoretical properties (Wimmer et al., 2023). In
113   response, alternative information-theoretic measures have been introduced (Malinin and Gales, 2021;
114   Schweighofer et al., 2023b;a; Kotelevskii and Panov, 2024; Hofman et al., 2024b). Although the
115   relationship between these measures is not well understood, their structure similar to Eq. (4) suggests
116   a connection between them. We next propose a fundamental, though generally intractable, predictive
117
      uncertainty measure, where all of these measures are special cases under specific assumptions.

119   2.2   P ROPOSED M EASURE : C ROSS -E NTROPY BETWEEN S ELECTED AND T RUE D ISTRIBUTION
120
      An effective measure of total predictive uncertainty should incorporate epistemic uncertainty and
121   be consistent with Eq. (1). Considering this, we propose to measure predictive uncertainty with the
122   cross-entropy between the predictive distributions of a selected predicting model and the true model.
123   Let p(y | x, ·) be the predictive distribution of any selected model for some new input x, which we
124   will refer to as predicting model. We will examine different cases for the predicting model later; for
125   now, it suffices to consider it to be a specific model with parameters w. The cross-entropy between
126   the predictive distributions of the predicting model and the true model is given by

      If the predictive distribution of the predicting model is equal to the predictive distribution of the
133
      true model, the epistemic component is zero by definition and Eq. (5) simplifies to Eq. (1). Thus, as
134
      expected, if the parameters of the true model are known, the epistemic uncertainty vanishes. Eq. (5)
135   is a fundamental, though generally intractable, measure of predictive uncertainty. To obtain tractable
136   measures, assumptions about the predicting model and about how to approximate the true model are
137   necessary. This gives rise to our framework, which we introduce in detail in Sec. 3. As an example,
138   comparing the standard measure in Eq. (4) with our proposed measure in Eq. (5), we observe that for
139   the standard measure, the predicting model is any model according to its posterior probability, and
140   the posterior predictive distribution is considered to be the true predictive distribution.
141
142   Interpretation of aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty. An important distinction compared to
143   previous work is in our interpretation of aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty, which aligns with the
144
      understanding of Apostolakis (1990); Helton (1993; 1997) as follows. The aleatoric component is not
      generally understood as a property of the true predictive distribution, but of the selected predicting
145
      model used to make a prediction. Thus, it is the uncertainty that arises due to predicting with the
146
      selected probabilistic model. The epistemic component is defined as the additional uncertainty due
147   to predicting with the selected predicting model instead of the true model. Thus, it is the additional
148   uncertainty that arises due to selecting a model from the given model class.

      Another widely used method is (B) the Bayesian model average (c.f. Eq. (2)). Here, instead of
189   predicting with a single model, the predictive distribution is marginalized over all possible models
190   according to their posterior probability. In practice, exact marginalization is often intractable and
191   therefore approximated by posterior sampling.
192
      Finally, it is possible to (C) consider every possible model as the predicting model, weighted by
193
      their posterior probabilities. This might seem counterintuitive, as it means that the predicting model
194
      is not fixed but is sampled anew for each prediction. Nevertheless, the aleatoric component of the
195   resulting uncertainty measures, denoted Ep(w|D) [H(p(y | x, w))], is the best approximation of the
196   aleatoric uncertainty under the true model for a given posterior distribution. However, as pointed out
197   by Wimmer et al. (2023), it is neither a lower nor an upper bound on the aleatoric uncertainty under
198   the true model and is highly dependent on the posterior distribution.
199
200
201   (1,2,3): A PPROXIMATION OF THE T RUE P REDICTIVE D ISTRIBUTION
202
203   The simplest but probably biased choice to approximate the true predictive distribution is (1) the
204   predictive distribution under a single given model with parameters w̃. Although this might be a poor
205   approximation, it might be the only feasible choice in specific settings. For example, it is used in
206
      speculative decoding (Stern et al., 2018; Leviathan et al., 2023), where a small model is used to predict
      and its predictive distribution is compared against a large model that serves as the ground truth.
207
208   Another possibility is to use (2) the posterior predictive distribution as an approximation of the true
209   predictive distribution. Although intuitively appealing, Schweighofer et al. (2023a) criticized this
210   as there is no guarantee that these distributions coincide, even for a perfect estimate of the posterior
211   predictive distribution. Furthermore, there are degenerate cases where the posterior predictive
212
      distribution can’t be represented by any model with non-vanishing posterior probability. However, it
      is often a well performing approximation empirically for expressive models such as neural networks.
213
      Additionally, (2) is the only option that guarantees finite EU and as a result TU.
214
215   Finally, perhaps the most intuitive solution is to consider (3) all possible models according to their
      posterior probability. Any model could be the true model according to its posterior distribution.

      Therefore, we should consider the mismatch between the predictive distribution of the selected
217   predicting model and all other models, weighted by their posterior probability.

      3.1   R ELATIONSHIPS BETWEEN M EASURES
220
221   Importantly, the aleatoric components of the uncertainty measures depend only on the predicting
222   model and do not depend on the approximation of the true predictive distribution. Thus, they are the
223   same for cases (1), (2) and (3). Furthermore, the aleatoric component of case (B) is an upper
224   bound of the aleatoric component of case (C), i.e. H(p(y | x, D)) ≥ Ep(w|D) [H(p(y | x, w))],
225   which directly follows from Eq. (3) as the mutual information is non-negative.
226   Due to the linearity in the first argument of the cross-entropy, the total uncertainties for cases (B) and
227   (C) are equal. Furthermore, as already discussed, the aleatoric components for cases (B) and (C)
228   differ by the mutual information Ep(w|D) [KL(p(y | x, w) ∥ p(y | x, D))]. Therefore, the epistemic
229   components for cases (B) and (C) also differ by this factor. This is trivial to see for cases (B2) and
230   (C2), where the epistemic component of case (B2) cancels to zero and the epistemic component
231   of case (C2) is the mutual information. For cases (B3) and (C3), this was already mentioned by
232   (Malinin and Gales, 2021) and a proof was given by (Schweighofer et al., 2023a), which we include
233
      for completeness in Sec. A.1 in the appendix, together with a version for cases (B1) and (C1).
234
235   3.2   C ATEGORIZATION OF P REVIOUSLY K NOWN M EASURES
236
      The standard measure (Eq. (4)) introduced by Houlsby et al. (2011) and popularized, for instance, by
237
      Gal (2016); Depeweg et al. (2018); Smith and Gal (2018) is the measure (C2). In the context of
238   autoregressive predictions, Malinin and Gales (2021) introduced measure (B3), due to the feasibility
239   of a Monte Carlo (MC) approximation compared to the standard measure (C2). Schweighofer et al.
240   (2023b) introduced measure (A3) together with a posterior sampling algorithm that is explicitly
241   taylored to this measure. Schweighofer et al. (2023a) introduced measure (C3) as an improvement
242   over the standard measure (C2) for certain settings. Hofman et al. (2024b) also derived measure
243   (C3) for the logarithmic strictly proper scoring rule (log score). Furthermore, Kotelevskii and Panov
244   (2024) discussed measures (B2), (B3), (C2) and (C3) as Bayesian approximations under the
245   log score. Our work thus generalizes and gives a new perspective on those measures.
246
247   4     R ELATED W ORK
248
249   Measures of predictive uncertainty. The currently most widely used information-theoretic measure
250   of predictive uncertainty is the entropy of the posterior predictive distribution (Eq.(3)). In Sec. (3.2),
251   we discuss the relationship of previous work based on this measure and our proposed framework.
252   However, there are also other measures of predictive uncertainty, not based on information-theoretic
253   quantities. Depeweg et al. (2018) introduced variance-based measures, based on the law of total
254   variance. This perspective was recently developed further for specific settings (Duan et al., 2024;
255
      Sale et al., 2023b). Furthermore, Sale et al. (2024b) introduced label-wise measures of predictive
      uncertainty, formulating both information-theoretic and variance-based measures. Another idea
256
      recently proposed by Sale et al. (2024a) is quantifying uncertainty through distances to reference
257
      (second-order) distributions for TU, AU, and EU, respectively, which represent complete certainty.
258   Thus, the higher the distance from the reference distribution, the more uncertain the prediction. All
259   measures discussed so far operate on a distributional representation of uncertainty. Orthogonal to that,
260   there are also set-based approaches (Hüllermeier et al., 2022; Sale et al., 2023a; Hofman et al., 2024a).
261
      Posterior sampling methods. All measures proposed by our framework, except (A1), contain
262
      a posterior expectation. Those are generally approximated by sampling models according to the
263
      posterior distribution. An obvious choice are MCMC algorithms, for example HMC (Neal, 1995; Neal
264   et al., 2011), which has recently been investigated on modern neural network architectures (Izmailov
265   et al., 2021). Scaling HMC to large datasets and architectures is computationally costly. However,
266   more efficient approximate variants using stochastic gradients are also available (Welling and Teh,
267   2011; Chen et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2020). Furthermore, it is possible to learn a simpler variational
268   distribution that approximates the posterior distribution. Widely known examples are the mean-field
269   approach of Blundell et al. (2015) or MC Dropout (Gal and Ghahramani, 2016). Another approach is
      the Laplace approximation (MacKay, 1992) around a maximum a posteriori (MAP) model (Ritter


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      et al., 2018; Daxberger et al., 2021). A commonly used approximation to the Bayesian ideal are
271   Deep Ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017), which despite their algorithmic simplicity are
272   widely recognized to provide high-quality samples (Wilson and Izmailov, 2020; Izmailov et al., 2021).
273   Furthermore, Schweighofer et al. (2023b) introduced adversarial models to explicitly search for
274   models with a large contribution to approximating expectations of the epistemic component for case
275   (3). For a more extensive overview, see, e.g. Gawlikowski et al. (2023) or Papamarkou et al. (2024).

      5   E XPERIMENTS
278
279   In this section, we evaluate the performance of the proposed measures across various experimental sce-
280   narios that leverage uncertainty, including tasks like misclassification detection, selective prediction,
281   and out-of-distribution (OOD) detection. In addition, we assess the impact of various posterior sam-
282   pling methods, which is a crucial factor in real-world applications. We do not intend to identify the op-
283   timal measure for a specific task or posterior sampling method; all of them can be evaluated, and the
284   best chosen in practice. Our primary aim is to deepen the understanding of our proposed framework.
285   Datasets. Our experiments are performed on the CIFAR10/100 (Krizhevsky and Hinton, 2009),
286   SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), Tiny-ImageNet (TIN) (Le and Yang, 2015) and LSUN (Yu et al., 2015)
287   datasets. For TIN, we resize the inputs to 32x32 to match the other datasets. We train models on all
288   datasets except LSUN, which is used solely as an OOD dataset.
289
      Models and training. We used three different model architectures for our experiments: ResNet-18
290
      (He et al., 2016), DenseNet-169 (Huang et al., 2017) and RegNet-Y 800MF (Radosavovic et al., 2020).
291   Individual models were trained for 100 epochs using SGD with momentum of 0.9 with a batch size of
292   256 and an initial learning rate of 1e-2. Furthermore, a standard combination of linear (from factor 1
293   to 0.1) and cosine annealing schedulers was used. The results discussed in the main paper are obtained
294   using ResNet-18 as the model architecture. For the other two architectures, results are provided in
295   Sec. B.4 of the appendix. Those are consistent with the findings presented in the main paper.

        5.4              OOD D ETECTION
455
456     We sampled models on CIFAR10/100, SVHN and TIN using DE, LA and MCD. Therefore, we use
457     the respective test dataset as in-distribution (ID) datasets and the test datasets for the others, as well
458     as LSUN, as OOD datasets. OOD detection does not involve a prediction by the model. Thus, it
459     is not possible to align the uncertainty measure with the predicting model, as in misclassification
460     detection and selective prediction. We compare the AUROC for distinguishing between ID and OOD
461     datapoints for each measure within our framework as a scoring function. Alternative commonly
462
        used measures such as the AUPR and the FPR@TPR95 were also considered. However, since they
        induced the same ordering of measures, we report the AUROC for all OOD detection experiments.
463
464     The results are shown in Fig. 5. We observe that throughout all measures, the total and the aleatoric
465     components perform much better than the epistemic components, which is contrary to assumptions
466     commonly formulated in the literature (Mukhoti et al., 2023; Kotelevskii and Panov, 2024). However,
467     this might depend on the datasets. For example, with MCD, the epistemic components perform
468
        best on the pairs TIN/CIFAR10 and TIN/CIFAR100 (Fig. 16 c,f). We hypothesize that the strong
        performance of the aleatoric components is due to the low levels of noise in the considered datasets.
469
        Furthermore, for the local method LA, all measures and their aleatoric components perform equally
470
        well. For DE and MCD, TU (B/C2) and TU (B/C3) perform best.

       6              C ONCLUSION
503
      We have proposed a framework that categorizes measures of predictive uncertainty according to
504
      assumptions about the predicting model and how the true model is approximated. This framework
505   has been derived from first principles, based on the cross-entropy between the predicting model and
506   the true model (Eq. (5)). Most importantly, it clarifies the relationships between information-theoretic
507   measures of predictive uncertainty and uncovers their implicit assumptions. Our empirical evaluation
508   shows that the effectiveness of the different measures depends on the task and the posterior sampling
509   method used. As there is no best-performing measure under all conditions, it is crucial to not consider
510   only a single one to benchmark posterior sampling methods for uncertainty quantification.
511    Our proposed framework for estimating predictive uncertainty requires an approximation of posterior
512    expectations through samples. However, obtaining samples is generally expensive, although many
513    improvements in efficiency have already been made. To avoid this issue, deterministic methods that
514    require only a single forward pass have been proposed. The most prominent directions are evidential
515    models (Sensoy et al., 2018; Amini et al., 2020), prior networks (Malinin and Gales, 2018), as well
516    as feature distance / density based models (Bradshaw et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020; Van Amersfoort
517    et al., 2020; Mukhoti et al., 2023). Those methods generally utilize different measures of predictive
518    uncertainty than those discussed in this work, and their relation is not thoroughly understood so far.
519   The information-theoretic framework presented in this work considers individual predictions. How-
520   ever, there is currently a lot of interest around autoregressive predictions, especially for large lan-
521   guage models. For such models, uncertainty estimation has been considered as a way to detect hallu-
522   cinations (Xiao and Wang, 2021). Extending the framework presented in this work to autoregressive
523   predictions comes with a set of challenges (Malinin and Gales, 2021; Kuhn et al., 2023; Aichberger
524   et al., 2024), such as the necessity to sample output sequences to obtain entropy estimates, output
525
      sequences of varying length, and semantic equivalences between output sequences. We believe that
      tackling those issues is an important direction for future work.

870   A.2    M ONTE C ARLO A PPROXIMATIONS
871
      The measures we proposed through our framework, except for measure (A1), incorporate posterior
872
      expectations Ep(w|D) [·]. These are generally intractable to calculate exactly and are thus approxi-
873   mated through samples drawn from the distribution - a Monte Carlo approximation of the expectation.
874   In this section we provide those approximations explicitly and discuss efficient ways to implement
875   them, utilizing relationships between individual measures.
876
      We assume that the posterior p(w | D) models to predict are drawn from and the posterior p(w̃ | D)
877
      approximations of the true model are drawn from are the same. However, in practice it is generally
878
      the case that models for averaging are selected based on their accuracy on a validation set, or more
879   generally that they are selected in a way optimal for predicting well. When sampling potential true
880   models that are likely under the data, the functional diversity of samples is often of concern, e.g. as
881   done with the sampling algorithm in Schweighofer et al. (2023b). This can be seen as either having
882   different posteriors due to different priors or having different algorithms to obtain samples from the
883   same posterior. However, for simplicity we state the MC approximations using only a single set
884   of samples. The provided implementation however is able to handle also the case where different
885   samples are used for the MC approximation.

1070   A.5      A LTERNATIVE MEASURE
1071
       The reverse order of the arguments for the cross-entropy in Eq. (5), that is, CE(p(y | x, w∗ ) ; p(y |
1072
       x, ·)), gives rise to an alternative measure that is consistent with Eq. (1). This measure, also known
1073
       as “pointwise risk” under the log score at an input (point) x, has been considered as a measure of
1074   predictive uncertainty (Gruber and Buettner, 2023; Lahlou et al., 2023; Kotelevskii and Panov, 2024;
1075   Hofman et al., 2024b). However, we argue that our proposed measure (Eq. (5)) is more meaningful.
1076   Our measure considers the uncertainty inherent to predicting with the selected model, plus the
1077   uncertainty due to any potential mismatch with the true model. The alternative measure considers
1078   the uncertainty inherent to predicting with the true model, plus the uncertainty due to any potential
1079   mismatch with the selected model. However, we generally don’t know the true model, thus can’t
       actually use it to predict and have to resort to an approximation of the true model anyways.

1162   A.6    R EGRESSION
1163
1164   For a probabilistic regression model, e.g. under a Gaussian assumption, the distribution parameters
1165
       are estimated, i.e. mean and variance for the Gaussian predictive distribution. The model is then
       trained by minimizing the negative log-likelihood under the training dataset.
1166
1167   Many works follow Depeweg et al. (2018) and utilize a variance decomposition for uncertainty
1168   quantification, where the aleatoric component is the expected variance and the epistemic component
1169   is the variance of means, where expectation and variance are over the model posterior. However,
1170   Depeweg et al. (2018) also consider the uncertainty measure given by Eq. (3), using differential
1171
       entropies for the continuous predictive distributions. The same can be done in order to adapt our
       framework in Tab. 1 for continuous predictive distributions.
1172
1173   Nevertheless, there are two important drawbacks one need to consider when doing this. First,
1174   differential entropy can be unbounded, depending on the nature of the predictive distribution. For
1175   the example of a Gaussian, it can be between −∞ and ∞. In addition, it is not invariant to a change
1176   of variables, making it a relative rather than an absolute measure. Second, the posterior predictive
1177
       distribution as defined in Eq. (2) is generally a mixture of individual distributions, unlike in the
       discrete case. This makes MC approximations of the resulting measures more involved.

         B.6                   A DVERSARIAL E XAMPLE D ETECTION
1783
1784    We want to investigate the effect of adversarially created inputs on the uncertainty estimates. Through-
1785    out this experiments, we consider adversarial attacks on the single network. However, it would also
1786    be possible to attack the average model, albeit more computationally expensive. As adversarial ex-
1787    amples are known to transfer well between models of similar architecture (Goodfellow et al., 2015),
1788    results for attacking the average model are expected to be relatively similar to those presented here.
1789    We consider two different adversarial attacks, (i) FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2015) and (ii) PGD under
1790    infinity norm perturbation (Madry et al., 2018). For our experiments, we only consider the subset of
1791    the test datasets that are predicted correctly. This we refer to as the original dataset. Then we apply the
1792    adversarial attacks the datapoints in the original dataset and select those datapoints where the model
1793    was successfully fooled to predict incorrectly. This we refer to as the adversarial dataset. We utilize
1794    the different uncertainty scores to calculate the AUROC of distinguishing between the original and
1795    the adversarial dataset, akin to the OOD detection experiments reported in the main paper. We also
1796
        investigated the AUPR and FPR@TPR95 as alternative metrics, which lead to equivalent conclusions.
1797
        FGSM. We start with the results obtained through the FGSM attack with ϵ = 8/255. Histograms
1798
        of the AU (A), the entropy of the predictive distribution of the single attacked model and the AU
1799
        (B), the entropy of the predictive distribution under the average model, are shown in Fig. 21 for DE,
1800    in Fig. 22 for LA and in Fig. 23 for MCD. For all methods, we observe a shift towards higher AUs for
1801    the adversarial datapoints compared to the original datapoints. This effect is strongest for the global
1802    posterior sampling method DE, which is expected. Furthermore, the shift appears more pronounced
1803    for AU (B), which makes sense as the adversarial examples have been obtained with the single model.

1805

1806    qualitatively very similar results to the OOD detection experiments, in that TU and AU measures for

1808    than for DE. For LA, all TU and AU measures perform basically on par. Surprisingly, EU measures

       B.7    ACTIVE L EARNING
1999
2000   Finally, we investigated the proposed framework of uncertainty measures on active learning tasks.
2001   We conducted experiments on the MNIST and FMNIST datasets. A small CNN (5x5 conv [1 to 6
2002   channels], 2x2 max-pool, 5x5 conv [6 to 12 channels], 2x2 max-pool, two linear layers with hidden
2003   size 32 and a final output linear layer; ReLU activations after each max-pool and linear layer except
2004   the last as well as dropout with dropout between linear layers) was utilized. For DE, the dropout
2005   rate was set to zero, for MCD it was set to 0.2 for experiments on both datasets. Training of the
2006
       models utilized the Adam optimizer (Kingma and Ba, 2015) for 50 epochs with a learning rate of
       1e-3, a batch size of 32 and l2 weight decay of 1e-4. Early stopping was performed on the official
2007
       validation split of the respective datasets, the evaluation of the performance per step was conducted
2008
       on the official test splits. Note that even though the size of the training dataset increases each step, the
2009   effective size, thus the number of gradient steps per epoch, was kept constant at 1000 for the MNIST
2010   and 1600 for the FMNIST experiments. For DE, we obtain 5 posterior samples (ensemble members),
2011   for MCD we obtain 50 posterior samples. The average over those samples, the approximated posterior
2012   predictive, was used to calculate the accuracies for each acquisition step, as well as for selecting the
2013   next datapoints to add to the training dataset from the pool dataset.
2014
2015   MNIST. We started with 20 datapoints in the training dataset and the remaining 49,980 datapoints
2016   in the pool dataset. Those 20 datapoints were balanced, such that two datapoints from each class were
2017   contained. Each iteration, the five samples with the highest uncertainty are transferred from the pool
2018
       dataset to the training dataset. We considered TU, AU and EU for measures (B2), (B3), (C2)
       and (C3) as acquisition functions, as well as random selection as a baseline. We did not investigate
2019
       measures (A1), (A2), (A3), (B1) and (C1) due to the long runtimes of the experiments, but
2020
       would expect them to perform worse than the considered ones in light of the other experiments we
2021   conducted. An interesting situation could be the EU (A1) however, when training a single model on
2022   the dataset in the current iteration and compare the model from the previous iteration. Future work
2023   should investigate this setting, e.g. in transfer learning settings.
2024
       The results are given in Fig. 30. We observe, that for both DE as well as MCD, EU (C2), the
2025
       mutual information, leads to the best performance at the final iteration, as well as performs very well
2026
       throughout all iterations. Interestingly, we find TU (B/C2) which is identical to AU (B) to be
2027   equally well performing for both cases. The same is found for TU (B/C3). Interestingly, the EU
2028   (B3) and EU (C3) are the worst performing acquisition functions for both DE and MCD, contrary
2029   to the sentiment that estimators of EU should perform best in this task. Similarly surprising, AU (C),
2030   which is an asymptotically unbiased estimator of the aleatoric uncertainty of the true model, performs
2031   very good as acquisition function for DE. It is the worst acquisition function though for MCD. The
2032   random sampling baseline is also extremely effective until around a training dataset size of around
2033   100 samples, more effective than any of the considered uncertainty measures. We hypothesize, that
2034   until a certain dataset size, models sampled from the posterior are not specified enough and provide
2035
       too little signal of what datapoints to add next, which would be interesting to investigate in more
       details in future experiments.

2045
       the mutual information, leads to very good performance throughout all acquisition steps. Also, TU
2046   (B/C2) which is identical to AU (B) and AU (C) perform very good. Again, EU (B3) and
2047   EU (C3) are the worst performing acquisition functions, especially towards the final steps. This
2048   seemingly similar task to MNIST proved to be surprisingly difficult for an active learning pipeline,
2049   potentially due to the higher difficulty of the task where class boundaries are known to be much
2050   harder to learn.
