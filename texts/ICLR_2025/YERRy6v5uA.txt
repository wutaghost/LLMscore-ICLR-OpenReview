009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            To improve the performance of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), Graph Struc-
012            ture Learning (GSL) has been extensively applied to reconstruct or refine original
013            graph structures. While GSL is generally thought to improve GNN performance,
014            it often leads to longer training times and more hyperparameter tuning. Besides,
015            the distinctions among current GSL methods remain ambiguous from the perspec-
016
               tive of GNN training, and there is a lack of theoretical analysis to quantify their
               effectiveness. Recent studies further suggest that GSL does not consistently out-
017
               perform baseline GNNs under the same hyperparameter tuning. This motivates us
018
               to ask a critical question: Is GSL really useful for improving GNN performance?
019            To address this question, we first propose a new GSL framework, which includes
020            three steps: GSL bases (i.e., node representations used to construct new graphs)
021            construction, new structure construction, and view fusion, to better understand
022            GSL. Then, our empirical studies and theoretical analysis show that the mutual
023            information (MI) between node representations and labels does not increase af-
024            ter applying graph convolution on GSL graphs that are constructed by similarity,
025            indicating GSL could be unnecessary in most cases. Our experiments fairly re-
026            assess the performance of GSL and reveal that adding GSL to GNN baselines or
027
               removing GSL in state-of-the-art models has negligible impact on node classifi-
               cation accuracy. We also report that pretrained GSL bases, parameter separation,
028
               and early fusion are effective designs within GSL. Our findings challenge the ne-
029
               cessity of complex GSL methods and underscore the value of simplicity in GNN
030            design.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016) are effective in capturing structural infor-
036   mation from non-Euclidean data, which can be used in many applications such as recommendation
037   (Wu et al., 2022; 2019b), telecommunication (Lu et al., 2024a), bio-informatics (Zhang et al., 2021;
038   Hua et al., 2024), and social networks (Li et al., 2023b; Luan et al., 2019). However, conventional
039
      GNNs suffer from issues including heterophily (Lu et al., 2024b; Luan et al., 2024a), over-squashing
      (Brody et al., 2021), adversarial attacks (Jin et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022a), and missing or noisy struc-
040
      tures (Lao et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022b). To address these issues, Graph Structure Learning (GSL)
041
      has been widely used (Zhu et al., 2021a), which reconstructs or refines the original graph structures
042   to enhance the performance of GNNs. However, GSL brings more hyperparameters and adds plenty
043   of computational cost in both the construction process and the learning process. In addition, recent
044   studies (Luo et al., 2024; Platonov et al., 2023) have shown that GSL methods cannot consistently
045   outperform traditional GNNs with the same hyperparameter tuning strategy. Therefore, an in-depth
046   analysis of the effectiveness and necessity of GSL is highly needed.
047
      First, to better understand GSL, we propose a comprehensive framework to carefully break down
048   GSL into 3 steps: (1) GSL Bases Generation. GSL bases are the processed node embeddings used
049   before constructing new structures, which are constructed by either graph-aware or graph-agnostic
050   models with fixed or learnable parameters. (2) New Structure Construction. Based on the GSL
051   bases, new structures are constructed with similarity-based (Pei et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2019),
052   structural-based (Zhao et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022a), or optimization-based approaches (Jin et al.,
053   2020). Then, graph refinements are followed. (3) View Fusion. To incorporate the original graph or
      combine multiple GSL-generated graphs, various view fusion strategies are applied, e.g., late fusion

072
      With the above framework, we rethink when GSL is helpful in GNNs. As examples shown in Figure
      1, a GSL method creates new connections between nodes with similar GSL bases, which is denoted
073
      as the contextual information of the ego node and its neighbors in this case. When the GSL bases
074
      show high consistency with intra-class nodes, nodes within the same class are connected, which is
075   beneficial for GNNs (Luan et al., 2024b) and we denote it as successful GSL. Conversely, when the
076   GSL bases show high consistency between inter-class nodes, nodes in different classes are likely to
077   be connected, which is harmful to GNNs and we denote it as unsuccessful GSL. These examples
078   highlight that the effectiveness of GSL is highly contingent on the quality of GSL bases. However,
079   even if most GSL methods are supposed to be successful GSL, do we really need GSL in these
080   cases? In this paper, our answer is “No”. The prerequisite of successful GSL is that the GSL
081   bases are highly consistent within intra-class nodes, which inherently ensures a high quality of node
082   representations (Kothapalli et al., 2024). Therefore, even a successful GSL is unnecessary because
083
      the GSL bases are already informative enough to provide distinguishable node embeddings.
084   Based on the above example, we empirically and theoretically analyze the effectiveness of similarity-
085   based GSL, one of the most representative approaches in GSL. Our findings reveal that the mutual
086   information (MI) between node representations and labels does not increase after applying graph
087   convolution on similarity-based GSL graphs. Our results indicate that even though GSL sometimes
088   outperforms GNNs in certain scenarios of heterophily (Pei et al., 2020; Luan et al., 2024c) or incon-
089
      sistent neighbor distributions (Zheng et al., 2024a; Ma et al., 2021), its performance is still upper
      bound by Multilayer Perceptrons (MLP) on the same GSL bases in most cases. These results also
090
      explain why ego node separation (Zhu et al., 2020a) is an important part of model design. Our
091
      extensive experiments show that, under the same hyperparameter tuning and GSL bases, no matter
092   adding GSL to 4 baseline GNNs or deleting GSL in 8 state-of-the-art (SOTA) GSL-based methods,
093   there are no significant changes in model performance on node classification. Furthermore, we also
094   show that while GSL fails to improve model performance, it does offer marginal improvements in
095   model robustness. In conclusion, our main contributions are as follows:
096
097          • We propose a new framework to decompose the process of GSL into 3 steps, which is more
098            comprehensive than the previous literature and helps better understand each component in
099
               GSL.
100          • Both of our empirical experiments and theoretical analysis show that the mutual informa-
101            tion (MI) between node representations and labels does not increase after applying graph
102
               convolution on similarity-based GSL graphs, indicating that most GSL methods are unnec-
               essary.
103
104          • We fairly re-evaluate the effectiveness of GSL by adding GSL to GNN baselines and re-
105            moving GSL in SOTA GSL-based models. The results indicate that GSL does not consis-
106
               tently improve GNN performance in most cases.
107          • Under our proposed framework, we report effective GSL designs include pretrained GSL
               bases, parameter separation, and early fusion.

           3.3                   V IEW F USION
239       In cases where the methods (Fatemi et al., 2021; Zou et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2019) already im-
240       plicitly fuse the information from G into G ′ , further view fusion is unnecessary. However, for other
241       approaches, the fusion of information from the original graph structure G and the reconstructed
242       structure G ′ is crucial. Based on the fusion stage, methods can be classified as early fusion (Li et al.,
243       2022a; Lao et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a), late fusion (Wang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022b; Zheng
244       et al., 2024b), or separation (Liu et al., 2022b). Early fusion, often seen as ”graph editing”, modifies
245       G by adding or removing edges with G ′ before training. Late fusion keeps both views as input, fus-
246       ing node embeddings either at each layer or in the final layer. Separation methods, typically paired
247
          with contrastive learning, maintain multiple views without embedding fusion during GNN training.
          Additionally, view fusion methods can be further distinguished by whether they involve parameter
248
          sharing across layers during training.
249
250
           3.4                   T RAINING M ODE
251
252       In addition to the previous three steps, the training mode of G ′ plays a crucial role in GSL and
253       can be categorized into static, joint, and 2-stage approaches. Most methods (Jin et al., 2020; Li
254       et al., 2022b; Yan et al., 2022) use joint training where G ′ and model parameters are optimized
255       simultaneously. In contrast, some methods (Wang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022a; Franceschi et al.,
256       2020) follow a 2-stage mode, iteratively updating G ′ and model parameters. While dynamic updates
257       offer greater flexibility for learning complex structures through parameter optimization, they also
258       significantly increase computational complexity, especially during the bases and graph construction
259       steps. To address this, other methods (Zheng et al., 2024b; Suresh et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023a) opt
260
          for a static G ′ during training. Although this fixed structure may limit performance, it avoids the
          time-consuming process of frequent graph updates.

352   additional complexity is further introduced by single view GSL O(|E ′ | F ) or multiple view GSL
353   O((NG − 1)(|E| F + |V| F 2 )), where |E ′ | is the additional edges introduced in GSL and NG is the
354                                                         2
      number of views in GSL. Consider the fact that |V| ≫ |E|, we have the total additional complexity
355                                                     2
      of GSL by summing up all these terms: O(|V| F + |V| F 2 ). Compared with the complexity in
356                                                                                            2
      normal GCN O(|E| F + |V| F 2 ) (Blakely et al., 2021), this additional complexity O((|V| − |E|)F )
357   adds tremendous training time and grows exponentially with the number of nodes in graphs, which
358   is shown in our experiments.

453   5.2      Q UALITY OF GSL G RAPHS
454
      Previous studies (Li et al., 2022b; Zheng et al., 2024b) suggest that GSL constructs graphs with
455   properties that improve intra-class node connectivity, which can be measured by homophily. This
456   improvement can be visualized by inspecting graph structures with nodes sorted by their class la-
457   bels. A graph that appears closer to a block diagonal matrix indicates stronger intra-class con-
458   nectivity. However, this enhancement may not always be essential and can be achieved through
459   non-GSL methods as well. In Figure 4, we visualize the original and reconstructed structures of
460   a heterophilous graph from the Wisconsin dataset. The GSL graphs are constructed using various
461   bases: X, ÂX, MLP(X), GCN(X, A), and GCL(X, A). We also include reconstructed graphs us-
462   ing a simple method that samples edges between nodes of the same class based on label predictions,
463   i.e., Ŷ = GCN(X, A) or Ŷ = MLP(X, A). Figure 4 demonstrates that, although GSL improves
464   intra-class connectivity, the improvement is not as substantial as that achieved by non-GSL meth-
465   ods, as seen in the last two subfigures. Thus, the improvement in homophily within GSL graphs is
466
      unnecessary, as it can be easily achieved through simple methods.

      5.3 GSL C OMPONENTS
487
488
      Since the performance of GNN and GNN+GSL models is comparable under the same GSL bases, as
      shown in Table 1, we further investigate how different components of GSL influence GNNs. As il-
489
      lustrated in Figure 5, our results indicate that: (1) Pretrained node representations, such as MLP(X)
490
      and GCN(X, A), significantly enhance GNN performance, (2) GSL graph generation has minimal
491   impact on model performance, (3) two view fusion with parameter separation improves GNN per-
492   formance, and (4) early fusion generally outperforms late fusion. These results explain why prior
493   comparisons of GNNs are unfair since those pretrained GSL bases greatly improve GNN perfor-
494   mance. This improvement stems from self-training, a key component in many GSL approaches.
495   As a result, incorporating self-training methods may be more advantageous for future GNN designs
496   than relying solely on GSL. For additional results and an analysis of GSL modules, please refer to
497   Appendix E.

527   6                          C ONCLUSION
528
      In this paper, we revisit the role of Graph Structure Learning (GSL) in Graph Neural Networks
529
      (GNNs) with our proposed GSL framework. Motivated by the controversy of GSL, we demonstrate
530   that graph convolution over GSL-constructed graphs does not improve mutual information, as con-
531   firmed by both empirical observations and theoretical analysis. By either adding GSL to baseline
532   GNNs or removing it from state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods, we find that GSL does not enhance
533   GNN performance when evaluated under the same GSL bases and hyperparameter tuning. These re-
534   sults suggest that the improvements attributed to GSL may stem from components other than GSL.
535   Our findings contribute to a better understanding of GSL and offer insights into re-evaluating the
536   essential components in future GNN design. While this paper primarily examines the influence of
537   GSL on model performance in node classification tasks, future research could extend this analysis
538
      to other graph-related tasks. Additionally, investigating how GSL affects the robustness of GNNs
      would be a valuable direction for future study.

781   LDS (Franceschi et al., 2020). The GSL bases in LDS is constructed as node features X and the
782   GSL graph G ′ is initialized using a k-Nearest-Neighbors algorithm based on B. Then, G ′ is updated
783   with a loss function of node classification. Then multiple graphs are sampled based on G ′ with
784   a Bernoulli function and used to update the model parameters. The G ′ construction and model
785   parameters are updated as a 2-stage mode.
786   Geom-GCN (Pei et al., 2020). Geom-GCN constructs the GSL bases from several graph-aware node
787   embedding strategies using both of the X and A: Isomap (), Poincare (), and struc2vec (). Then,
788   new graphs are constructed by filtering node pairs with a higher similarity measured by Euclidean
789   distance {E ′ |e′ij = |Bi − Bj | < δ} where δ is a threshold. Finally, both of the aggregated message
790   from G and G ′ are fused after applying graph convolution layers with no parameter sharing. The G ′
791   is not updated through the training process.
792
      ProGNN (Jin et al., 2020). The G ′ in ProGNN is purely learned by optimization without GSL bases.
793   It optimizes the G ′ using low rank, sparsity, and similarity with the original graphs G. It outputs a
794   single graph G ′ without fusion and updates the G ′ together with model parameters.

      IDGL (Chen et al., 2020). The GSL bases in LDS is constructed by linear transformation of node
      features MLP(X). Then, a GSL graph G ′ is constructed using cosine similarity with topk threshold
797
      refinement. The early fusion is applied by fusing GSL graph G ′ with original graph G before training.
798
      The GSL graph G ′ is trained with model parameters jointly.

800   GRCN (Yu et al., 2020). GRCN constructs GSL bases by node embeddings of graph convolution
801   GCN(X, A). Then, the GSL graph G ′ is constructed by a kernel function with topk and symmetriza-
802
      tion refinement {E ′ |e′ij = σ(Bi Bj ) > δ}. The final graph is obtained by early fusion and the GSL
803   graph G ′ is updated together with model parameters.
804   GAug-M and GAug-O (Zhao et al., 2020). GAug-M constructs GSL bases using a 2-layer graph
805   convolution GCN(2) (X, A). Then, the GSL graph G ′ is constructed by a kernel function. The
806   final graph is obtained by adding some edges with highest probabilities and removing some edges
807   with lowest probabilities on G. GAug-O selects node features as GSL bases X, then trains a Graph
808   Auto-Encoder to predict edges as G ′ . Then, after gumbel sampling, the GSL graph G ′ is fused with
809   original graph G before training. The G ′ in both of the GAug-M and GAug-O is updated together
      with model parameters.

1456   between early and late fusion is limited across most datasets. For simpler datasets like Minesweeper
1457   and Amazon, both fusion methods yield nearly identical performance, indicating that the choice of
       fusion state does not drastically alter the model’s outcome in most cases.
