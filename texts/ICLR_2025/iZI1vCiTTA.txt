009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models trained on web-scale text acquire language generation
012            abilities that can solve a wide range of tasks, particularly when task knowledge
013            is refined into the generative prior using in-context examples. However, spuri-
014            ous features learned from noisy data hinder their generalizability. Supervised
015            finetuning can introduce task specificity, but introduce data inefficiency. Prior
016
               studies indicate that (i) noisy neural circuitries coexist with generalizable ones
               within LLMs, and (ii) finetuning typically enhances (or suppresses) existing abil-
017
               ities without introducing newer ones. Building upon these, we propose TaRot,
018
               a novel method for task adaptation. TaRot intervenes in the neural circuitries
019            using learnable rotation matrices that are optimized using Bayesian Optimization,
020            on labelled samples in the order of standard few-shot prompting examples. Ex-
021            periments on multiple classification and generation tasks using LLMs of varying
022            sizes reveal the efficacy of TaRot, improving upon both zero- as well as few-shot
023            performance, with average improvements (across models and tasks) of 23.81%
024            and 11.15%, respectively.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Large Language Models (LLMs) acquire the ability to associate different language concepts pre-
029
      sented in a sequential context by optimizing the prediction probability of the next token given a
030
      context. Despite its apparent simplicity, when scaled across web-sized text corpora, such a learning
031   strategy introduces the ability to solve a wide range of tasks presented in natural language. However,
032   the web contains almost everything humankind has written, and therefore, it introduces spurious to-
033   ken associations that are irrelevant or even counter-productive to the model to become generalized
034   task-solvers. We observe phenomena like brittle few-shot performance (Sclar et al., 2024), halluci-
035   nation (Huang et al., 2023), harmful text generation (Wen et al., 2023), etc. as evidence of learning
036   noisy patterns. Remedial interventions like instruction tuning (Zhang et al., 2024), alignment tun-
037   ing (Shen et al., 2023), etc. have been proposed. Recent research has shown that such mediation
038   only acts on a superficial level — out-of-distribution inputs can reinforce noisy behavior and break
039
      the model (Ghosh et al., 2024). Without an in-depth understanding of the inner workings, remedial
      strategies become wild goose chase.
040
041   Mechanistic disentangling of Transformer-based language models has shed some light on this di-
042   rection (Elhage et al., 2021; Olsson et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). Two recent investigations (Jain
043   et al., 2024; Prakash et al., 2024) on the effects of fine-tuning confirm the inability of supervised
044   fine-tuning to alter fundamental abilities acquired via pretraining. On a tangential investigation,
045
      Dutta et al. (2024) recently confirmed the existence of multiple parallel neural pathways of answer
      processing within LLMs. Bhaskar et al. (2024) echoed similar findings in the case of syntactic gen-
046
      eralization while pointing out that different components acquire different generalization behaviors.
047
      These findings lead us to the central research question of this work: is it possible to directly edit
048   the model behavior via mechanistic interventions in a generalizable manner? Prior work in this
049   direction has heavily relied on careful manual effort to localize task-specific neural components and
050   design intervention techniques Meng et al. (2022); Li et al. (2024a). Two shortcomings hinder the
051   widespread use of such methods: (i) Complexity of localization increases polynomially with model
052   size; identifying which component is responsible for each different task and designing suitable abla-
053   tion is extremely challenging. (ii) The existence of multiple components performing similar neural
      computations within the model challenges the generalizability of the intervention itself.

      Our contribution. To this end, we propose a novel intervention technique, TaRot – Task-aware
      Rotation of token-association (see Figure 1 for a representative depiction)1 . We establish the concep-
081
      tual prior from Transformers’s implicit gradient descent bias in next token prediction. Specifically,
082
      we first show that attention-weighted averaging of value vectors facilitates the memorization of token
083   association from pertaining data in individual attention heads, in the sense that each attention head
084   acts as a mini-language model. Due to the vast number of token associations present in the pretrain-
085   ing corpus compared to the number of attention heads in even the largest of the models, we hypothe-
086   size that individual directions of these memorized associations remain in superposition, and removal
087   or downscaling of a head can counteract model performance. Instead, we construct parametrized ro-
088   tations to align head outputs for task-adaptation. The rotation parameters are then optimized using
089   Bayesian optimization. Furthermore, TaRot is extremely data- and compute-efficient: we use 6-20
090   supervised examples for each task and dL  4 rotation parameters (where d is the model dimension and
091
      L is the number of layers) for each different task. This renders TaRot at par with standard few-shot
      prompting in labeled data-efficiency.
092
093   We experiment with five different classification tasks and two natural language generation tasks;
094   the choice of tasks seeks to investigate general world knowledge (news topic classification) as
095   well as the ability to generalize beyond imitation (BIG Bench tasks (BIG-bench authors, 2023)).
096   TaRot demonstrates consistent improvements over four different language models of varying
097
      sizes: Qwen2-1.5B-Instruct, Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.1, and Meta-Llama-3-
      8B-Instruct, in both zero-shot as well as few-shot settings. Furthermore, we analyze the changes in
098
      neural representation introduced by TaRot to uncover useful insights.

      Task adaptation of pretrained language models. The pretrain-finetune regime for adapting lan-
109   guage models to downstream tasks dates back to the early approaches like BERT (Devlin et al.,
110   2019) — pretrain a language model (LM) on large unstructured text corpora using self-supervised
111   objective, followed by supervised fine-tuning on task-specific, relatively smaller datasets. Despite
112   the apparent simplicity, the pitfalls of this regime have been pointed out in terms of distribution
113   shift (Kumar et al., 2022). With the development of large-scale, autoregressive Transformer-based
114   language models and their ability to learn from in-context examples (Brown et al., 2020), a defini-
115   tive shift has happened in the more recent past. Current practices of using these models for down-
116   stream tasks primarily rely on designing suitable prompt templates and labeled example retrieval
117
      for in-context learning (ICL) (Liu et al., 2022; Rubin et al., 2022; Tanwar et al., 2023); traditional
      techniques of fine-tuning have taken a back seat due to the computational cost and catastrophic for-
118
      getting introduced by small-scale task-specific data that hurts the pretrained abilities (Zhai et al.,
119
      2024). Instead, finetuning to follow task instructions, aka instruction-tuning (Zhang et al., 2024),
120   has gained popularity. Instruction-tuning has been shown to introduce zero-shot task adaptation
121   abilities in LLMs (Wei et al., 2022). Additionally, different methods of alignment tuning have been
122   proposed with the primary goal being aligning the generative distribution of the language models
123   with human values and preferences (Shen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b). Despite the popularity
124   of instruction and alignment tuning, their ability to alter fundamental information processing has
125   been put in question in recent literature. Jain et al. (2024) investigated the effects of fine-tuning in
126   toy models trained with formal languages as well as precompiled ones; their findings suggest that
127   supervised fine-tuning does not introduce any new ability into pretrained models but only reinforces
128
      (or suppresses) existing ones. Similar concerns have been raised upon investigating entity tracking
      in the neural representation space (Prakash et al., 2024). Ghosh et al. (2024) identified multiple lim-
129
      itations of instruction tuning, including the inability to introduce new knowledge and deterioration
130
      of performance due to over-reliance on pattern matching.
131
132   Mechanistic understanding and interventions. The umbrella of mechanistic interpretability
133   broadly encompasses methods to disentangle model behavior via reverse engineering the underlying
134
      neural algorithm (Elhage et al., 2021; Ferrando et al., 2024). Endeavors to mechanistically under-
      stand Transformer-based language models trace back to the seminal work by Elhage et al. (2021).
135
      Their framework established attention heads as one of the fundamental building blocks of language
136
      model interpretation. Subsequent studies have identified the functional roles of different attention
137   heads in pretrained models: induction heads as a primary mechanism of prefix matching (Olsson
138   et al., 2022), circuitries of attention heads responsible for indirect object identification (Wang et al.,
139   2023), neural pathways that implement chain-of-thought reasoning (Dutta et al., 2024), etc. Much
140   relevant to our analysis, Lv et al. (2024) found that certain attention heads memorize the association
141   between country names and their capitals. On a tangential line of investigation, Geiger et al. (2024)
142   introduced the Distributed Alignment Search (DAS) framework for localizing interpretable features
143   in subspaces of the neural representations. Mechanistic methods provide actionable insights that
144   have led to non-traditional techniques to edit model behavior. Elhage et al. (2021) experimented
145
      with key propagation to elicit induction heads (and thereby, prefix-matching ability) in single-layer
      attention-only Transformers. Meng et al. (2022) used causal tracing to locate factual associations in
146
      MLP neurons and proposed a gradient-free approach to edit factual recall patterns in pretrained lan-
147
      guage models. Li et al. (2024a) identified attention head circuitry that elicits toxic text generation
148   in GPT-2; mean-ablation of these circuits is shown to reduce toxicity. Self-detoxification (Leong
149   et al., 2023) identifies toxic generation direction in the internal representation using trigger prompts
150   and then rewrites in the opposite direction to reduce toxicity. Wang et al. (2024a) formulated tox-
151   icity reduction as a knowledge editing task that can permanently alter toxic behaviors instead of
152   suppressive interventions like supervised fine-tuning or RLHF-based alignment. Lamparth & Reuel
153   (2024) localized backdoor mechanisms (i.e., vulnerabilities against adversarial prompt injections)
154   in early-layer MLPs and proposed a low-rank substitution to improve robustness against such injec-
155   tions. Vergara-Browne et al. (2024) employed attribution patching techniques to identify and remove
156
      certain singular values in the parameter matrices to improve performance.
157   In comparison with prior intervention approaches, our work bears two fundamental differences: (i)
158   TaRot does not necessitate task-specific localization of neural behaviors; this significantly reduces
159   intense manual effort and risk of over-localization, eliciting efficient, generalizable interventions;
160   (ii) TaRot is gradient-free, parameter-efficient, and requires supervised samples in the order of
161   standard ICL; this poses TaRot as a practical alternative to intense prompt-engineering.

167   3.1   ATTENTION HEADS AS TOKEN - TOKEN MAPS
168
169   Following the framework presented by Elhage et al. (2021), we dissect the Transformer-based lan-
170   guage models with the following assumptions: (i) Each attention head reads from and writes to the
171   residual stream independently in a linear fashion, and (ii) given that the attention heads utilize hid-
172   den representation of dimensionality much smaller than the residual stream (i.e., for a model with
173
      16 attention heads, each attention head uses 1/16-th of the dimension of the residual stream), they
      typical operate on small subspaces of the residual stream. This way, two attention heads can operate
174
      on two distinct subspaces and never interact with each other. These two assumptions allow us to
175
      interpret the working of the attention heads meaningfully even while treating each head in isolation.
176   We start with identifying what a single-head attention operation tends to learn in isolation.

      3.2   E DITING MODEL BEHAVIOR VIA ATTENTION ROTATION
217
218   A natural conclusion from the prior discussion would be that, by suppressing undesired associations
219   for certain attention heads, we can improve task performance. However, multiple token associations
220   are expected to be memorized in each attention head in superposition since the number of attention
221   heads is way smaller than the potential token associations present in the pretraining data — one can-
222   not selectively switch off one certain association. Prior research in mechanistic interpretability has
223   shown that, although we can often localize attention heads responsible for particular task, removing
224
      the non-dominant attention heads does not deliver the performance of the full model (Wang et al.,
      2023; Dutta et al., 2024).
225
226   Instead, one can rotate the output of the attention heads in order to maximize its alignment with
227   rows of WU corresponding to certain tokens while near-orthogonalizing with certain undesired to-
228   kens. This way, the model behaviour can be edited without destroying the superposed associations.

298   Training setting. Dutta et al. (2024) previously found that token associations corresponding to
299   pretrained knowledge primarily resides in the initial half of the model. Since the rotational inter-
300   vention designed in Equations 6 and 7 are primarily targeted towards undesired token associations

302   parameters to optimise becomes dL   4 . Since we want to optimise the rotation matrix for a particular

305   Models. Four different instruction-tuned models with varying size are used for all experiments:
306   Qwen2-1.5B-Instruct Yang et al. (2024), Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct Abdin et al. (2024) (2.8 billion

      parameter), Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.1 Jiang et al. (2023), and Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct Dubey
      et al. (2024); we refer to these models as Qwen2-1.5B, Phi-3-mini, Mistral-7B, and

      Llama-3-8B, respectively.

       7                                  C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this work, we proposed TaRot, a novel, gradient-free, mechanistic intervention method for edit-
526
      ing language models. TaRot builds on observations from implicit gradient descent bias of causal
527   attention and applies parametrized rotation on the attention output to minimize the effects of un-
528   desired memorizations, doing away with effort-intensive localization steps and task-specificity of
529   prior intervention techniques. Using Bayesian optimization of the rotational parameters, TaRot
530   renders as data-efficient as in-context learning; yet, across a variety of tasks and language models
531   of different sizes and families, robust improvement is observed. We further analyzed the impact of
532   TaRot and demonstrated the key mechanism of action. In a nutshell, TaRot can pave the path for
533   general-purpose model editing methods in the future beyond supervised fine-tuning.
534   Limitations and ethical considerations. TaRot is designed to perform when the model has a
535   generalization ability that is suppressed by noisy memorization. In that sense, it is limited by the
536   boundaries of pretraining and cannot be used for domain adaptation. Fundamentally, it is not appli-
537   cable to proprietary models. Finally, similar to any intervention technique, TaRot can be used in
538   reverse to bypass alignment tuning and reinforce undesired behaviors.

766   A.1    TASK DETAILS
767
768   We experimented with five different classification (i.e., single token generation) tasks and two NLG
769   tasks. Below are the details of the tasks with their prompt templates used:
770
771   AG News:      The goal of the task is to categories new articles into one of the four predefined cate-
772   gories.
773
774          • World – News about global events, international politics, and worldwide issues.
775
             • Sports – News related to sporting events, athletes, competitions, and sports industry devel-
776            opments.
777
778          • Business - News focusing on the economy, financial markets, companies, and business
779
               trends.
780          • Science & Technology – News about technological advancements, scientific discoveries,
781            and research.
782
783   System prompt used for AG News task: You are a news classification model. Your task is to
784   classify news articles into one of the following four categories: World, Sports, Business, or Science.
785
      You should respond with only the category name and no other characters.
786
787   Entailed Polarity: The Entailed Polarity task is a yes/no question-answering task Srivastava et al.
788   (2022). Given a fact and a question, the goal is to determine whether the fact entails a yes or no
789
      answer to the question. The task tests the model’s ability to infer whether the factual statement
      logically supports the answer in terms of polarity (positive or negative). Example:
790
791
             • Fact: “Ed remembered to go.”
792
793          • Question: “Did Ed go?”
794          • Answer: “Yes”
795
796   System prompt used for Entailed Polarity task: Follow the instructions below and answer with
797   Yes / No.
798
799   Navigate: The objective is to follow a set of directional or spatial instructions and determine if,
800   after following those steps, the entity returns to the starting point. The answer is either True or False,
801   depending on whether the instructions guide the entity back to where they started. Example:
802
803          • Instruction: “If you follow these instructions, do you return to the starting point?”
804          • Steps: “Always face forward.”, “Take 7 steps left.”, “Take 2 steps backward.”, “Take 7 steps
805            backward.”, “Take 7 steps backward.”, “Take 3 steps forward.”
806
             • Question: “Do you return to the starting point?”
807
808          • Answer: False
809
      System prompt used for the task: Answer the following question and output only True/False.


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Color: This task includes 3,000 random colors written in four common color spaces (RGB, RGB
811   Hex, HSL, and HCL) that we use to probe LLM’s knowledge about color encodings. For example,
812   given the prompt hsl(30.16, 89.56%, 45.91%), we expect the model to answer “orange”.
813
814
      System prompt used for color task: Choose the correct color from the options and output the color
      only.
815
816
      Winowhy: This task Srivastava et al. (2022) requires models to identify the correct reasons behind
817
      the answers to the Winograd Schema ChallengesZhang et al. (2020).
818
819   This task is based on the original Winograd Schema Challenge (WSC) dataset and 4095 WinoWhy
820   reasons (15 for each WSC question) that could justify the pronoun coreference choices in WSC.
821   The model is presented with a passage that contains a pronoun and an explanation of which word or
822
      entity the pronoun refers to. The model’s job is to assess whether the explanation given is correct or
      incorrect based on the context of the passage.
823
824
             • Text: “Fred is the only man alive who still remembers my father as an infant. When Fred
825            first saw my father, he was twelve years old. The ’he’ refers to Fred because, in his own
826            words, he is ‘a very odd man’.”
827
             • Question: “The above reasoning is:”
828
829          • Answer: “Incorrect”.
830
      System prompt used for Winowhy task: Follow the instructions and output Correct/Incorrect.
831
832
      Imdb: Tune model to generate positive movie reviews using a BERT Kenton & Toutanova (2019)
833
      sentiment classifier as a reward function. The reward model evaluates the sentiment of the generated
834
      reviews, and the goal is to maximize the likelihood of generating reviews classified as positive.
835
836          • Dataset Used: imdb Maas et al. (2011)
837
             • Reward Model: lvwerra/distilbert-imdb, a fine-tuned version of distilbert-base-
838            uncased Sanh (2019) on the imdb dataset.
839
840   Detoxify: Involves reducing the toxicity of language model outputs. The toxicity evaluation is
841   done using a classifier, such as facebook/roberta-hate-speech-dynabench-r4-target, which distin-
842   guishes between “neutral” and “toxic” text. The classifier provides feedback (reward or penalty)
843   based on the toxicity of the model’s output, guiding the model to produce less toxic text. The
844   dataset used is allenai/real-toxicity-prompts Gehman et al. (2020).
