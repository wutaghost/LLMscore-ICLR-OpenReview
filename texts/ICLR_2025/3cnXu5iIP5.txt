009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012            The Euler Characteristic Transform (ECT) is an efficiently-computable
013            geometrical-topological invariant that characterizes the global shape of data. In
014            this paper, we introduce the Local Euler Characteristic Transform (`-ECT), a
015            novel extension of the ECT particularly designed to enhance expressivity and in-
016
               terpretability in graph representation learning. Unlike traditional Graph Neural
               Networks (GNNs), which may lose critical local details through aggregation, the
017
               `-ECT provides a lossless representation of local neighborhoods. This approach
018
               addresses key limitations in GNNs by preserving nuanced local structures while
019            maintaining global interpretability. Moreover, we construct a rotation-invariant
020            metric based on `-ECTs for spatial alignment of data spaces. Our method exhibits
021            superior performance than standard GNNs on a variety of node classification tasks,
022            particularly in graphs with high heterophily.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      While traditional GNNs often rely on message-passing schemes that aggregate node features, they
030
      may lose crucial local information, particularly in the case of graphs with high heterophily. These
031   methods can obscure structural nuances that are key to understanding the relationships between
032   nodes. The Euler Characteristic Transform (ECT), a technique from Topological Data Analysis
033   (TDA), offers a solution as it provides a representation of the given data space. The ECT captures
034   topological features through sublevel set filtrations across various directions. Notably, it is invert-
035   ible for so-called constructible sets, ensuring that the original data can be reconstructed from its
036   transform. This invertibility, coupled with efficient computability, positions the ECT as a pow-
037   erful tool for representation learning. In this paper, we extend the ECT to local neighborhoods,
038   presenting the Local Euler Characteristic Transform (`-ECT), a method designed to preserve local
039
      structural details while retaining global interpretability. The `-ECT captures both structural and
      spatial information around each data point, making it particularly advantageous for graph-based
040
      data. Although the `-ECT is based on the topological concept of the Euler characteristic, it turns
041
      out to be a fingerprint of local neighborhoods around points and should therefore rather be seen as a
042   geometrical-topological method. Our approach specifically addresses the challenge of neighborhood
043   aggregation in featured graphs, ensuring lossless representation of local neighborhoods of nodes. As
044   it turns out, `-ECTs maintain critical local details, and therefore offer a more nuanced representation
045   that can be used for various downstream tasks such as node classification. Our method is particu-
046   larly effective for tasks where node feature aggregation may obscure essential differences, such as
047   in graphs with high heterophily. Additionally, the `-ECT framework’s natural vector representation
048   makes it compatible with a wide range of machine learning models, facilitating both performance
049   and interpretability.
050   As our main contributions, we (i) construct `-ECTs in the context of embedded simplicial com-
051   plexes and theoretically investigate their expressivity in the special case of featured graphs, (ii) em-
052   pirically show that this expressivity positions `-ECTs as a powerful tool for interpretable node clas-
053   sification, often superior to standard GNNs, and (iii) introduce an efficiently computable rotation-
      invariant metric based on `-ECTs which facilitates spatial alignment of geometric graphs.

      2    BACKGROUND
055
056
      Simplicial Complexes A simplicial complex K is a mathematical structure that generalizes graphs
057   to model higher-order relationships and interactions. While graphs represent pairwise connections
058   between entities using nodes and edges, simplicial complexes extend this representation to higher
059   dimensions by including simplices such as triangles (2-simplices), tetrahedra (3-simplices), and
060   their higher-dimensional analogues. Formally, an (abstract) simplicial complex is a finite collection
061   of (abstract) simplices such that every face of a simplex in the collection is also in the collection,
062   and the intersection of any two simplices is either empty or a common face. An (abstract) k-simplex
063   is defined as a set of k + 1 vertices, denoted (v0 , v1 , . . . , vk ), where the order of the vertices does
064   not matter. The faces of a k-simplex are all subsets of its vertices and correspond to simplices of
065
      lower dimension. For example, the faces of a 2-simplex (triangle) are its three edges (1-simplices)
      and three vertices (0-simplices).
066
067

      mary of the shape or structure of a topological space, such as a simplicial complex. It is defined as
069   an alternating sum of the number of simplices in each dimension:

084   it has been used in topological data analysis to summarize complex shapes and structures in high-
085   dimensional data.

103   3    R ELATED W ORK
104
105   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have revolutionized the field of graph representation learning by
106   enabling end-to-end learning of node embeddings through message passing (Kipf & Welling, 2016).
107   However, traditional GNNs face theoretical limitations that are fundamental obstructions in learn-
      ing expressive representations of graph data (Xu et al., 2018). Related to the latter phenomenon,

108
      GNNs are known to suffer from issues like oversmoothing (Zhang et al., 2023; Rusch et al., 2023)
109   and oversquashing (Di Giovanni et al., 2023). Hamilton et al. (2017) and Velickovic et al. (2018)
110   have addressed these issues by incorporating sampling and attention mechanisms into the message-
111   passing paradigm. However, even these advancements often show limited performance, particularly
112   in graphs with high heterophily. Recent work in graph machine learning started incorporating ad-
113   ditional geometric information into architectures, leading to geometric convolutional networks (Pei
114   et al., 2020), as well as geometric graph neural networks (Joshi et al., 2023). Moreover, the anal-
115   ysis of the theoretical capacity or expressivity of an architecture remains an ongoing avenue of
116   research (Morris et al., 2023), with some works providing additional “topology-aware” inductive
117
      biases (Horn et al., 2022; Verma et al., 2024) to improve overall GNN expressivity.
118   The Euler Characteristic Transform (ECT) has recently become a popular tool in topological data
119   analysis (Turner et al., 2014; Ghrist et al., 2018). Röell & Rieck (2024) have expanded on these
120   ideas, applying them to modern machine learning problems, including shape classification. In Curry
121   et al. (2022); Marsh et al. (2024), transformation invariance properties of ECTs are studied. All
122   aforementioned contributions build on global Euler Characteristic Transforms, local aspects, which
123
      are necessary for our approach, are not discussed therein. The use of the ECT is just one example of
      increasing interest in methods for dealing with higher-order information. In this context, simplicial
124
      complexes provide a richer structure than simple graphs (Yang et al., 2022; Ebli et al., 2020), with
125
      message passing over high-order domains gaining much attention (Bodnar et al., 2021), and a recent
126   position paper outlining the benefits of methods being capable of working with such domains (Pa-
127   pamarkou et al., 2024).
128
129
      Finally, there are attempts to use tools from topological data analysis in graph learning. In Hofer
      et al. (2020), persistent homology is used for graph classification. Zhao & Wang (2019) take a dif-
130
      ferent approach to graph classification by learning a weighted kernel based on persistent homology.
131
      Zhao et al. (2020) include topological features of graph neighbourhoods into a standard GNN, again
132   leveraging persistent homology. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first that makes use
133   of local variants of the Euler Characteristic Transform (ECT) for graph learning. The novelty of this
134   work comprises both the study of theoretical properties of local variants of the ECT as well as their
135   empirical utility, particularly for graph representation learning.

      Euler Characteristic Transform (ECT) The Euler Characteristic Transform (ECT) of an (ab-
140
      stract) simplicial complex X ⊂ Rn is a function ECT(X) : S n−1 × R → Z, given by
141
142                                  ECT(X)(v, t) = χ({x ∈ X|x · v ≤ t}),                                   (4)
143
144   where χ denotes the Euler characteristic. The interpretation of ECT(X) is therefore that it scans the
145   ambient space of X in every direction and records the Euler characteristic of the sublevel sets. In
146   Ghrist et al. (2018), it is proven that ECT(X) is invertible, meaning that X can be recovered from
147   ECT(X), as long as X is a so-called constructible set. The main focus of this work are compact
148   simplicial complexes which are constructible, and therefore the invertibility theorem applies in our
149
      setting.
150   In practice, we approximate ECT(X) via ECT(X)(m,l) := ECT(X)|{v1 ,...,vm }×{t1 ,...,tl } for uni-
151   formly distributed directions v1 , . . . , vm ∈ S n−1 and filtration steps t1 , . . . , tl ∈ R. Since X is
152   compact, t1 , . . . , tl can be chosen to lie in a compact interval [a, b] with t1 = a and tl = b, and
153   so that the sequence {ti }i forms a uniform partition of [a, b]. We note that this approximation is
154   efficiently computable and has a natural representation as a vector of dimension m · l. Regarding the
155   choice of the magnitudes of m, l we have the following result.
156   Theorem 1. In the above setting, the worst case convergence of ECT(X)(m,l) to the true quantity
157   ECT(X) is O(( logmm )1/(n−1) 1l ).
158
159   Curry et al. (2022) prove that the aforementioned approximation actually determines the true quan-
160   tity, under mild conditions and for m, l large enough. We notice that both translations and scalings of
161   X in the ambient space lead to a reparametrization of ECT(X), and therefore ECT(X) essentially
      (up to a parameter change) remains unaltered under these two types of transformations.

      Local ECT (`-ECT) Given a simplicial complex X ⊂ Rn and a vertex x ∈ X, we define the
163   local ECT of x with respect to k ≥ 0 as
164
165                                  `-ECTk (x; X) := ECT(Nk (x; X)),                                    (5)
166
      where Nk (x; X) denotes an appropriate local neighborhood of x in X, whose locality scale is con-
167   trolled by a parameter k. Usually, Nk (x; X) will be either the full subcomplex of X which is
168   spanned by the k-hop neighbors of x, or the full subcomplex of X, which is spanned by the k-
169   nearest vertices of x. The first important special case arises when X is a 0-dimensional simplicial
170   complex, which is just a point cloud. In this case, the full subcomplex of X, which is spanned
171   by the k-nearest vertices of x, Nk (x; X), is simply given by the k-nearest neighbors of x. Being
172   based on the Euler Characteristic, the construction of `-ECTs appears to be purely topological at
173   first glance. However, in light of the invertibility theorem, we note that `-ECT(x; X) can be inter-
174   preted as a fingerprint of a local neighborhood of x in X. The upshot is that this fingerprint can be
175
      well approximated in practice, making it possible to obtain local representations of combinatorial
      data embedded in Euclidean space. This approximation works by sampling v1 , . . . , vm ∈ S n−1 and
176
      t1 , . . . , tl ∈ R, and considering ECT(Nk (x; X))(m,l) , instead of `-ECTk (x; X). The latter quan-
177
      tity is well-computable in practice, and the approximation error can be controlled by the sample sizes
178   m and l, as we discussed before. Again, this approximation has a natural representation as a vector
179   of dimension m · l, enabling us to encode local structural information of point neighborhoods in an
180   approximate lossless way that can readily be used by machine learning algorithms for downstream
181   tasks.

183   `-ECT in representation learning Our formulation of `-ECTs provides a natural representa-
184   tion of local neighborhoods of embedded simplicial complexes. One important special case is that
185   of featured graphs, meaning graphs in which every node admits a feature vector. The latter data
186   structure forms the basis of many modern graph learning tasks, such as node classification, graph
187
      classification, or graph regression. The predominant class of methods to deal with these graph learn-
      ing problems are message-passing graph neural networks. We develop an alternative procedure for
188
      dealing with featured graph data, built on `-ECTs and we show that `-ECTs provide sufficient
189
      information to perform message passing, which we explain in the following.
190
191   Definition 1. A featured graph is a (non-directed) graph G such that every node v ∈ G admits a
192
      feature vector x(v) ∈ Rn . We denote the set of nodes of G by V (G), and the set of edges by E(G).
193
      We notice that a featured graph G can naturally be interpreted as a graph embedded in Rn , by repre-
194   senting each node feature vector as a point in Rn , and by drawing an edge between two embedded
195   points if and only if there is an edge between the underlying nodes in G. This construction yields a
196   graph isomorphism between G and the embedded graph if and only if for any pair of nodes v, w ∈ G
197   with v 6= w we have x(v) 6= x(w) for their associated feature vectors. In practice, the latter as-
198   sumption can always be achieved by adding an arbitrarily small portion of Gaussian noise to each
199   feature vector, and we therefore may restrict ourselves to featured graphs that yield an isomorphism
200   on their Euclidean embeddings. We now show that `-ECTs are in fact expressive representations in
201   the context of graph learning.
202   Theorem 2. Let G be a featured graph and let {`-ECT1 (x; G)}x be the collection of local ECTs
203   with respect to the 1-hop neighborhoods in G. Then the collection {`-ECT1 (x; G)}x provides the
204   necessary (non-learnable) information for performing a single message-passing step on G, in the
205   sense that for a given vertex x ∈ G one can reconstruct the feature vectors of its 1-hop neighborhood
206
      from `-ECT1 (x; G).
207
      Thm. 2 tells us that for a featured graph G the collection {`-ECT1 (x; G)}x already contains suf-
208   ficient information to perform a message passing step. The advantage of using `-ECTs instead
209   of message passing to represent featured graph data lies in the possibility to additionally use
210   {`-ECTk (x; G)}x for k ≥ 2, which contain both structural and feature vector information of larger
211   neighborhoods of nodes in the graph. This type of information is typically not explicitly available
212   through message passing since passing messages to non-direct neighbors depends on prior message
213   passing steps, which solely produce an aggregation of neighboring feature vectors.
214
215   Subgraph counting Chen et al. (2020) investigate the power of message-passing neural networks
      with respect to subgraph counting. Specifically, it is proven that message-passing neural networks

      Definition 2. Two featured graphs G1 and G2 are isomorphic if there is a bijection π : V (G1 ) →
220   V (G2 ), such that (v, w) ∈ E(G1 ) if and only if (π(v), π(w)) ∈ E(G2 ) and so that for all v ∈ G1
221   one has x(v) = x(π(v)) for the respective feature vectors.

223   A featured graph GS is called a subgraph of G if V (GS ) ⊂ V (G) and E(GS ) ⊂ E(G), and such
224   that the respective node features remain unaltered under the induced embedding. A featured graph
225
      GS is called an induced subgraph of G, if GS is a subgraph of G, and if E(GS ) = E(G) ∩ GS . For
      two featured graphs G and GS , we define CSub (G; GS ) to be the number of subgraphs in G that are
226
      isomorphic to GS . Similarly, we define CInd (G; GS ) to be the number of induced subgraphs in G
227
      which are isomorphic to GS .
228
      Theorem 3. Two featured graphs G1 and G2 are isomorphic if and only if ECT(G1 ) = ECT(G2 ).
229
230   An immediate consequence of the previous Theorem is the following:
231   Corollary 1. ECTs can perform subgraph counting.
232
233   As previously stated, GNNs can generally not be used to perform (induced) subgraph counting,
234   and we therefore conclude that ECT-based methods for graph representation learning can be more
235   powerful than message-passing-based approaches.

265   Limitations While `-ECTs present clear advantages in preserving local details, there are some
266   trade-offs to consider. In certain cases, message-passing GNNs, which aggregate information across
267   neighbors, may be preferable for tasks where global context is more important than local details.
268   Furthermore, while our method is computationally feasible on medium-sized datasets (as demon-
269   strated in our experiments), the complexity of “naïvely” calculating `-ECTs increases for larger k
      and with the size and density of the graph, suggesting a need for improved methods.

      In this section, we present experiments to empirically evaluate the performance of the `-ECT-based
273   approach in graph representation learning, focusing on node classification tasks. We aim to demon-
274   strate how `-ECT representations can capture structural information more effectively than tradi-
275   tional message-passing mechanisms, especially in scenarios with high heterophily. Our experiments
276   compare the performance of `-ECT-based models against the standard GNN models graph attention
277   networks (GATs), graph convolutional networks (GCNs), graph isomorphism network (GIN) as well
278   as the heterophily-specific architecture H2GCN (Zhu et al., 2020). Furthermore, we showcase how
279   the rotation-invariant metric from Sec. 4 may be used for spatial alignment of graph data through
280   experiments.

282   5.1       `-ECTS IN GRAPH REPRESENTATION LEARNING
283
284
      The link between message passing and `-ECTs, which we stated in Thm. 2, encourages us to em-
      pirically validate the expressivity of `-ECTs in the context of node classification. For a featured
285
      graph G and fixed k ≥ 0, we assign `-ECTk (x; G) to every node v ∈ G. The `-ECT corresponding
286
      to a node together with the respective node feature vector is subsequently used as the input of a
287   simple model for the underlying node classification task. In our experiments, this classifier is given
288   by XGBoost, as we found it to outperform other, more complex models like neural networks, but
289   we emphasize that the choice of the model can be controlled by the user. Notice that we do not
290   claim to have found a new state-of-the-art for benchmarking graph datasets, but we rather showcase
291   that an approach based on `-ECT lets us obtain results that are on a par with and often superior to
292   common graph-learning techniques based on message passing, like graph attention networks (GATs)
293   and graph convolutional networks (GCNs).
294   We find that `-ECTs work particularly well in situations where aggregating neighboring informa-
295   tion is inappropriate, as for graphs that exhibit a high degree of heterophily, for example. In these
296   situations our approach may outperform message-passing-based methods by far. The upshot of our
297   method is that local graph information can be incorporated without the architectural necessity to
298   diffuse information along the graph structure, as it is the case for message-passing-based models.
299   While this discrete diffusion process induced by message passing is useful for a plethora of graph
300   learning tasks, it can also be an obstruction in learning the right representation for tasks where
301
      node features of neighbors in the graph should not be aggregated. In this sense, `-ECTs naturally
      overcome a fundamental limitation, which (by design) is induced by message passing. The sec-
302
      ond advantage of `-ECTs is that they are agnostic to the choice of the downstream model. This
303
      allows using models that are easy to tune, enabling practitioners to make use of their graph data
304   without necessarily having specialized knowledge in GNN training and tuning. Moreover, it permits
305   using models that are interpretable, making our method well-suited for domains where regulatory
306   demands often ask for levels of interpretability that cannot readily be achieved by (graph) neural
307   networks. In fact, by using feature importance values (which are directly available for tree-based
308   algorithms like XGBoost) and since the entries of the `-ECT vectors that are used as the input for
309   the model can be linked to the directions in the calculation of the `-ECTs, one obtains a deeper spa-
310   tial understanding of how the model arrives at predictions. A further discussion on interpretability
311   and an ablation on the directions used for the `-ECTs is contained in the appendix. In particular,
312
      this shows the expressivity of `-ECTs for node classification tasks as removing directions leads to
      lower performance, in general. Finally, we notice that our method enables practitioners to make use
313
      of models that do not necessitate a validation split in the dataset, making a larger portion of the data
314
      accessible for training and testing.

419   5.2   L EARNING SPATIAL ALIGNMENT
420
421   In the following, we use the approach described in Sec. 4 in order to learn the spatial alignment of
422   two data spaces by re-rotating one into the other. We start by showing that synthetic data which
423
      only differs up to a rotation can be re-aligned using the given method. Moreover, we show that this
      alignment is stable with respect to noise, making it a robust measure for the comparison of local
424
      neighborhoods in data. In comparison to other spatial alignment methods like the iterative closest
425
      point algorithm, ours does not necessitate the computation of all pairwise distances between points
426   in the respective spaces. The latter is often a computational bottleneck, especially for large datasets,
427   thus positioning our method for spatial alignment as a computationally more efficient method in
428   practice. We observe that this approach is also capable of aligning embedded graph data, making
429   it particularly useful for dealing with geometric graphs, constituting a highly-efficient alternative
430   to more involved machine-learning models such as geometric GNNs (Joshi et al., 2023). For a
431   discussion of spatial alignment in the context of high-dimensional point cloud data, please refer to
      App. A.2.2.

456   Low-dimensional data We now approximate the optimization problem in Eq. 9 to show that we
457   can learn a spatial alignment of two given data spaces, while the distance between ECTs of non-
458   aligned spaces that only differ up to a rotation will in fact generally be high. Our first example is
459   given by a wedged sphere, meaning two 2-dimensional spheres which are concatenated at a gluing
460   point (see Fig. 3). We begin by sampling 2000 points from such a wedged sphere, and compare
461   the squared L2 loss between the ECTs of this sample and a rotation of the same data space. We
462   repeat this procedure 500 times, where at each step both the sample of the wedged sphere and the
463
      rotation matrix which yields the rotated version of the same space are randomly sampled. We notice
      that the L2 losses between the non-aligned spaces are high (with a median of around 19), whereas
464
      the L2 losses of the non-aligned spaces are significantly lower, with a median loss close to zero
465
      (see Fig. 8 in the appendix for more details). Moreover, we see that the ECT of the same space
466   significantly changes when the coordinate system is changed, which undermines the necessity of a
467   rotation-invariant metric for a comparison of ECTs that we introduced in Sec. 4. We conclude that
468   an alignment of the ECTs of the two underlying data spaces in fact leads to an alignment of the data
469   spaces itself, as promised by the theoretical results in Sec. 4.
470
471   Robustness with respect to outliers and noise Fig. 4 and Fig. 5 in the appendix show that the
472   spatial alignment of wedged spheres still works in the presence of outliers and noise. This property
473   is an important feature when dealing with real-world data, which is often noisy, and enables us to
474   align spaces that only approximately differ up to a rotation. By contrast, the Hausdorff distance
475
      which is a widely used metric between point clouds is (by definition) highly sensitive to outliers.
      We therefore conclude that the proposed metric based on ECTs is a robust metric to compare point
476
      clouds of potentially different cardinalities.

490   6   D ISCUSSION
491
492   In this work, we introduced the Local Euler Characteristic Transform (`-ECT), providing a novel
493
      approach to graph representation learning that preserves local structural information without relying
      on aggregation. Our method addresses fundamental limitations in message-passing neural networks,
494
      particularly in tasks where aggregating neighboring information is suboptimal, such as in graphs
495
      with heterophily. By retaining critical local details, `-ECTs enable more nuanced and expressive
496   representations, offering significant advantages in node classification tasks and beyond.
497
498   One key strength of our approach is its model-agnostic nature, allowing it to be paired with in-
499
      terpretable machine learning models. This is particularly useful in domains such as healthcare,
      finance, and legal applications, where regulatory frameworks demand high levels of transparency
500
      and interpretability that are often difficult to achieve with black-box neural networks. By leveraging
501
      `-ECTs, we can fulfill these regulatory requirements while maintaining the expressiveness needed
502   for accurate representation learning.
503
504   Beyond graph representation learning, the framework of `-ECTs opens the door for applications
505
      in other areas where local structure is critical, such as in point clouds, 3D shape analysis, and bi-
      ological networks. Additionally, the ability to generalize `-ECTs to higher-dimensional simplicial
506
      complexes suggests future extensions of this work into the realm of simplicial learning, providing a
507
      powerful tool for analyzing higher-order data in a computationally feasible way. Future work could
508   explore more efficient algorithms for computing `-ECTs at scale, as well as hybrid approaches that
509   balance local and global information more effectively. In particular, the investigation of sampling
510   methods appears to be a promising direction for this purpose. Moreover, heterophily-specific mech-
511   anisms such as separation of neighborhood aggregation (as used for specialized GNN architectures)
512   may be incorporated into our `-ECT-based framework to further strengthen its expressivity in the
513   presence of high-heterophily graphs.

736   A.2.1    A BLATION ON DIRECTIONS AND INTERPRETABILITY
737
      Coming back to our approximation of ECT(X) via ECT(X)(m,l) := ECT(X)|{v1 ,...,vm }×{t1 ,...,tl }
738
      for uniformly distributed directions v1 , . . . , vm ∈ S n−1 and filtration steps t1 , . . . , tl ∈ R, we notice
739
      that the (l · (j − 1) + 1)-th till (l · j)-th entries of ECT(X)(m,l) correspond to the direction vj . The
740   latter gives us the opportunity to get a deeper spatial understanding of how the model predicts its
741   outcome, by analysing its feature importances (which are available for tree-based algorithms like
742   e.g. XGBoost). Therefore, our approach enables us to analyse which features (i.e. directions) of
743   the underlying ECT vector are most important. In practice, we often observe that a small number
744   of features admits high feature imoprtance with respect to the corresponding model, see Fig.2. This
745   raises the question if we may use only a smaller random collection of features and still get reasonable
746   results. We therefore ran experiments for a collection of datasets for a varying number of randomly
747   sampled entries of the `-ECT1 vector see Tab.6 for the results. Here, 4096 corresponds to the whole
748
      vector. We observe that for certain tasks like e.g. Coauthor CS, Coauthor Physics and Amazon
      Ratings the performance of the model only slightly changes when using only a small portion of
749
      the `-ECT1 vector. In light of the results in Curry et al. (2022), this observation is not entirely
750
      surprising: one main claim therein is that the ECT can be determined using only a small number of
751   directions.
