009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce L ION, a novel sequence-to-sequence framework that unifies the bidi-
012            rectionality and parallelized training of Transformers with the fast inference of
013            recurrent neural networks. L ION is built upon a mathematical formulation where
014            full kernelized attention with a learnable mask is efficiently computed using a bidi-
015            rectional selective recurrent model, matching the effectiveness of softmax-based at-
016            tention with constant-time inference. Our framework naturally accounts for spatial
017            and temporal relationships within input sequences, reducing reliance on heuristic
018            positional embeddings and facilitating straightforward scalability in context length
019
               and resolution. Using our framework and inspired by the recent state-space mod-
020            els, we propose three main running examples L ION - LIT , L ION -R ET N ET , and
021             L ION - S , a transformer with selective mask and recurrent inference. Numerical
022            evaluations on tasks such as language modeling, the Long-Range Arena, and im-
023            age classification show that L ION framework achieves performance on par with
024
               state-of-the-art models while delivering fast training and inference efficiency.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The new material added for the rebuttal is in blue.
030   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have become a key pillar for large language models (LLMs),
031   with different variants tailored to specific applications (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Team
032   et al., 2023). A key distinction lies in the use of causal (autoregressive) Transformers for language
033   modeling (Kojima et al., 2022; Dubey et al., 2024), which predict tokens sequentially based on prior
034   context. In contrast, bidirectional Transformers are central to large vision-language models (Liu
035   et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024a; Wu et al., 2024), such as the Vision Transformer (ViT), which encodes
036   image data in models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and acts as the decoder in diffusion models
037   for image generation (Ho et al., 2020).
038   Despite the success of autoregressive Transformers, they face significant resource challenges,
039   particularly in the need to store key and value information, known as KV-cache (Pope et al., 2023),
040   during inference. This leads to increased memory consumption, especially when processing long
041   sequences in stark contrast to earlier recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Elman, 1990), which has
042   long offered a more memory-efficient alternative by maintaining a hidden state.
043   To address the resource bottlenecks in Transformers, Linear Transformer (Katharopoulos et al., 2020)
044   has been proposed, expressing attention as a linear dot-product of kernel feature maps. This allows
045   Transformers to be reformulated as RNNs, enabling the processing of longer sequences with reduced
046   memory demands. Given the popularity of bidirectional Transformers across various fields and the
047   efficiency of RNNs, a natural question arises:
048
049                       Is a bidirectional Transformer actually a bidirectional RNN?
050   In this paper, we answer this question affirmatively. Indeed, we demonstrate that applying two linear
051   attention mechanisms simply in opposite directions and then summing them does not recover the
052   original bidirectional Transformer (cf., Observation 3.1). Instead, we propose a novel design, L ION,
053   that allows the bidirectional Transformer to be expressed as a bidirectional RNN. Our framework
      retains the advantages of parallel training found in Transformers, offering bidirectionality in inference

054
      while addressing the memory issues inherent in traditional Transformer models. A schematic of
055   the proposed framework L ION is visualized in Figure 1.
056
057   Besides the popularity of Transformers and their variants, state space models (SSMs) have emerged as
058
      another family of architecture for sequence modeling due to their efficient inference capabilities (Gu
      et al., 2022; Smith et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2020). The representative works Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024)
059
      and Mamba-2 (Dao & Gu, 2024) have also demonstrated strong performance in language modeling.
060
      Building on our bidirectional Transformer theory, L ION framework combines the expressive power
061   of bidirectional Transformers with the selective mechanism of Mamba, further enhancing the model’s
062   capability to process long sequences while maintaining computational efficiency. Through this
063   approach, we aim to provide a scalable and efficient solution for tasks that demand both long-range
064   dependency modeling and dense information processing. Overall, our main contributions can be
065   summarized as follows:
066
067          • We propose a theoretical framework L ION (Theorem 3.3), which expresses bidirectional
068
               Transformers as bidirectional RNNs, enabling efficient inference for long sequences while
               benefiting from well-established Transformer training (cf., Table 1).
069
070          • Our theoretical framework offers the foundations to transform a wide class of autoregressive
071            recurrent models (cf., Appendix B) into their bidirectional counterparts.
072          • We propose three main running examples of our framework, inspired by prior work, namely:
073
                  1. L ION - LIT : Scaled attention without masking, a bidirectional extension of Linear
074
                     Transformer Katharopoulos et al. (2020).
075
076
                  2. L ION -R ET N ET : Fixed masked scaled attention with scalar and learnable state param-
                     eter γ, an extension of RETNET Sun et al. (2023) into the bidirectional setting.
077
078               3. L ION - S : Selective masked scaled attention with input-dependent mask λi , inspired
079                  by the selectivity of Mamba-2 Dao & Gu (2024).
080          • Through extensive experiments in the Long Range Arena, Vision Tasks, and Masked
081            Language Modeling, we have demonstrated the capabilities of the LION framework and the
082            models built upon it, as outlined above.
083   Due to the space constraints, a detailed overview of related work is deferred to Appendix B. Section 2

086   contributions. Section 4 describes how to build L ION - S by introducing selectivity via discretization
087   of continuous state-space models, which is then followed by numerical evidence in Section 5 and the
088
      conclusions in Section 6.

507   5.4                  C ONTEXT EXTENSION AND MEMORY DURING INFERENCE
508   When considering the number of tokens (analogous to resolution in images) that Transformer-like
509   architectures can effectively process, two primary limitations emerge: (i) positional embeddings and
510   (ii) memory constraints. Transformers are typically trained up to a specific sequence length and
511   lack predefined positional encodings for tokens that exceed this limit, which can hurt their ability to
512   recognize token positions beyond trained lengths. Furthermore, the quadratic complexity of these
513   models during inference places significant demands on memory resources, often leading to constraints
514   that reduce processing efficiency.
515   L ION - S architecture is free of these two limitations. In Figure 3 (Left), we test the L ION - S model
516   trained on 128 tokens tested on different lengths. While BERT and L ION - LIT models peak at the
517   training length, afterwards they experience a sharp decrease. L ION - S, on the other hand, maintains
518   its high performance at 3.5 times of training sequence length. Additionally, L ION - S can infer beyond
519   the 512 tokens, as compared to the other models.
520   For memory usage during inference, as Figure 3 (Center/Right) illustrates, L ION - S demands signifi-
521   cantly lower memory than ViT on image classification or BERT on MLM tasks. Due to the quadratic
522   complexity of Transformers, as the resolution of the image increases, the memory consumption
523   also drastically changes. As a result, ViT and BERT go out of memory (OOM) even with small
524   batch sizes. With the L ION - S architecture, thanks to the linear complexity during inference, the
525   change in memory consumption is minimal. At 1248 resolution, L ION - S is ∼ 94.4% more efficient
526   than the ViT-T model. Similarly, at sequence length 14, 336, L ION - S is 83.35% more efficient than
527   BERT. These two strengths combined, i.e., usage of the recurrence parameters and linear inference
528
      complexity, allow L ION - S to efficiently extrapolate beyond the context length (or resolution) during
      inference. For further results on length expansion, see Appendices A and D.7.
529
530   6 C ONCLUSIONS
531
      This paper presents the L ION framework, which casts bidirectional Transformers as bidirectional
532
      RNNs. Notably, L ION allows popular linear recurrent models, such as Linear Transformers, to
533
      leverage the well-established transformer training pipeline during training, while benefiting from
534   efficient recurrence during inference in the bidirectional setting. The main examples of the L ION
535   framework include L ION - LIT (without masking the attention), L ION -R ET N ET (which uses a fixed
536   mask inspired by Sun et al. (2023)), and L ION - S (which employs a selective mask similar to Dao &
537   Gu (2024); Yang et al. (2023)). Our experiments show that the L ION framework facilitates efficient
538   inference and parallel training in the bidirectional setting, while models built upon L ION excel
539   at handling complex tasks, such as long-range dependencies in vision and bidirectional language
      modeling, all while using modest computational and memory resources.

830   B                           D ETAILED RELATED WORK
831
832   B.1                           S TATE S PACE M ODELS AND T RANSFORMERS
833
834   State Space Models, such as S4 (Gu et al., 2022) and S5 (Smith et al., 2023), advanced efficient
835   sequence modeling with linear complexity. Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024) and Mamba-2 (Dao &
836   Gu, 2024) introduced selective mechanisms within SSMs, achieving strong language modeling
837   performance. Recently, many recurrent models for language have been proposed, e.g., xLSTM (Beck
838
      et al., 2024), RWKV (Peng et al., 2024). While RNNs for autoregressive modelling are prevalent,
      bidirectional models are less explored. Hydra (Hwang et al., 2024) extends Mamba to bidirectional
839
      settings using quasiseparable matrix mixers. VisionMamba (Zhu et al., 2024b) employs two separate
840
      SSMs to pass over images. However, these works are not equivalent to bidirectional attention. L ION
841   adopts a different approach: instead of extending SSMs, we derive equivalence between bidirectional
842   attention with learnable mask and bidirectional RNNs.
843
844
      Since the pioneering works (Tsai et al., 2019; Katharopoulos et al., 2020), many works have been
      proposed to enhance linearized attention, including learnable relative positional encoding (Dai et al.,
845
      2019), gate mechanisms (Peng et al., 2021; Han et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2022), FFT for kernelized
846
      attention (Luo et al., 2021), decay terms in RetNet (Sun et al., 2023), and variants with enhanced
847   expressiveness (Arora et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). These works focus on causal
848   attention and cannot be directly applied with bidirectionality, while we explicitly write bidirectional
849   attention as bidirectional RNN combined with selectivity, enhancing performance and providing a
850   principled framework for parallel training and linear-time inference in non-causal tasks.
851

923   B.4   A RCHITECTURAL D IFFERENCES IN AUTOREGRESSIVE L INEAR R ECURRENT M ODELS
924
925   Multi-head attention and state expansion. Another difference between various linear recurrent
926   models, particularly SSMs and transformers, is how they expand single-head attention or SSM
927   recurrence (3) to learn different features at each layer, akin to convolutional neurons in CNNs (He
928   et al., 2016). Transformers achieve this through multi-head attention, while SSMs like Mamba and
929   Mamba-2 (Gu & Dao, 2024; Dao & Gu, 2024) use state expansion also known as Single-Input
930   Single-Output (SISO) framework to enlarge the hidden state. In SISO framework, the input xi in (3)
931   is a scalar and recurrence is applied to all elements in the hidden state independently (Smith et al.,
932   2023), allowing for parallelization during inference and training.
933   In contrast, simplified SSMs like S5 employ a Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) approach,
934   where xi is a vector, which aligns them more closely with RNN variants like LRU (Orvieto et al.,
935   2023) that are successful in long-range modeling (Smith et al., 2023). However, the SISO framework
936   continues to be effective in Mamba models for language modeling (Dao & Gu, 2024).
937   Rule of Positional Encoding. The parameter Λi serves as a gating mechanism (Yang et al., 2023;
938   Gu & Dao, 2024) and can also be interpreted as relative positional encoding (Sun et al., 2023). For
939   instance, in an autoregressive model, considering Λi as scaler, the mask M can be defined as follows:
940
941

943                       j+1                                                   i−j

946   In this context, the selective mask (where Λi = λi varies for each token) is used in architectures like
947   Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024), while the fixed mask (where Λi = λ is constant across all tokens) is
948   implemented in architectures like RetNet (Sun et al., 2023). In both cases, the mask Mij provides
949   rich relative positional encoding between tokens i and j. Its structure reinforces the multiplication of
950   all Λk elements for k ∈ [j, . . . , i], while the selectivity allows the model to disregard noisy tokens,
951
      preventing their inclusion in the attention matrix for other tokens.
952   In contrast, linear recurrent models such as Linear Transformer (Katharopoulos et al., 2020) set
953   Λk = 1, resulting in M functioning as a standard causal mask, similar to those used in generative
954   transformers (Kojima et al., 2022). This necessitates the injection of positional information into
955   the sequence, which is achieved using the traditional positional encoding employed in transformers
956   (Vaswani et al., 2017). In this framework, each element of the input data sequence is represented
957
      as xi = fi + ti , where fi denotes the features at time i and ti represents the positional embedding.
      However, this traditional positional encoding has been shown to be less informative compared to
958
      relative positional encoding (Su et al., 2024), which is utilized in other linear recurrent models where

            C.8    C HANGING THE ORDER OF PATCHES
1487
1488        When processing images, both the spatial relationships among neighboring pixels and their positions
1489        are as critical as the pixel values themselves. Positional embeddings provide a way to incorporate
1490        these spatial relationships. A common approach in Transformers involves flattening the image,
1491        as illustrated in the left panel of Figure 7. However, we argue that this method of flattening is
1492        suboptimal and can be enhanced to include additional contextual information.
1493
1494        Furthermore, in scenarios involving a fully masked setup or RNN-based inference, the se-
1495
            quence in which pixels are processed becomes increasingly important. To address this, we propose
            a new reordering scheme for pixel values. In the attention module, the pixel values are reordered
1496
            following the patterns depicted in the center and right panels of Figure 7. Forward and backward
1497
            passes are then executed based on this new ordering, adhering to established procedures. The outputs
1498        from these two passes are subsequently averaged to generate the final result.
1499
1500        We refer to this method as L ION - S (v2) throughout the paper. This approach demonstrated
1501        a notable improvement in accuracy for image classification tasks while maintaining the efficiency and
1502        flexibility inherent to the method. A similar concept has been previously explored in Vision-LSTM
1503        (Alkin et al., 2024).

1536   paper, following the guidelines from the S5 GitHub repository3 . Our state matrix was represented as
1537
       a vector Λi = λi , where each element contains a scalar non-input dependent value ea . The value a
       was initialized based on HIPPO theory, alongside the input-dependent ai , as described in main body.

1540   rate scheduler (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016). The weights for the queries and keys, as well as the

1542   of 0.1. For the values  v, we initialized Wv using zero-order hold discretization, represented as
1543

1784   Below we present a theoretical number of FLOPS used in the attention of vision transformers and
1785   L ION - S during inference where L is the resolution/context length and D is the hidden dimension.
1786   Results show that while transformer has O(L2 + LD2 ) L ION - S has O(LD2 ). Note that in this
1787   calculation, the exponentials and other nonlinearities are considered as 1 FLOP whereas in reality,
1788   the Softmax introduces additional complexities. The same calculations should also apply to other
1789   bi-directional models.

1796          • Calculating Y: 2L2 D
1797          • TOTAL: L(6D2 + 4LD + 2L)
