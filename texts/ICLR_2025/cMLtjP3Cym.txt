009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multivariate Time Series Generation (MTSG) plays a crucial role in time series
012            analysis, supporting tasks such as data augmentation and anomaly detection. While
013            several methods exist for MTSG, recommending the most suitable method for new
014            scenarios remains a significant challenge. Although prior work by (Ang et al.,
015            2023a) provides guidance for selecting MTSG methods, it lacks coverage of re-
016
               cent diffusion-based methods and has limited exploration of channel-independent
               frameworks. We address these gaps by improving the recommendation guide, high-
017
               lighting the effectiveness of a central discriminator within the channel-independent
018
               framework. Our revised guide makes three key recommendations: 1) VAE-based
019            methods excel on small-scale datasets; 2) a channel-independent framework with
020            the newly designed central discriminator is optimal in most cases; and 3) a diffusion-
021            based method is preferable when ample data and computational resources are
022            available.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Time series generation (TSG) involves producing synthetic sequences of temporally ordered data
      that mimic the statistical properties of real-world time series. Multivariate time series generation
028
      (MTSG) has gained prominence due to its applications in tasks like data augmentation (Ramponi
029
      et al., 2018), time series prediction (Cirstea et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2021), and anomaly detection
030   (Ang et al., 2023b; Campos et al., 2021).
031
032   Numerous methods have been developed for MTSG (Ang et al., 2023a), typically falling into two
033
      categories: 1) channel-mixing frameworks, which merge time series features using models like
      generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Donahue et al., 2018; Yoon et al., 2019; Smith & Smith,
034
      2020; Lin et al., 2020; Liao et al., 2020), variable autoencoders (VAEs) (Desai et al., 2021; Lee
035
      et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Naiman et al., 2024), flow-based methods (Chen et al., 2018; Kidger
036   et al., 2021; Rubanova et al., 2019; Jeon et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023), and diffusion-based methods
037   (Kong et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2024; Coletta et al., 2023; Yuan & Qiao, 2024); and 2) channel-
038   independent frameworks with a central discriminator (Seyfi et al., 2022), which independently capture
039   single-channel information while the discriminator handles inter-channel correlations.
040
      Choosing an appropriate TSG method depends on domain-specific factors such as data volume,
041
      periodicity, and trends. For instance, Fourier flow (Alaa et al., 2021) is well-suited for tasks involving
042   autocorrelation, while TimeVAE (Desai et al., 2021) performs better on smaller datasets. Therefore,
043   a robust recommendation guide is essential for users. However, existing analyses, like (Ang et al.,
044   2023a), may be incomplete—favoring channel-mixing frameworks, particularly VAE-based methods,
045   while neglecting advanced diffusion models and offering limited exploration of channel-independent
046   frameworks. For example, the periodicity of the multivariate time series and the correlation between
047   the channels are important features. However, the central discriminator designed in (Seyfi et al.,
048   2022) does not adequately address this problem. In addition, (Ang et al., 2023a) has not tested
049   the newly proposed diffusion model-based methods and needs to be upgraded to form a reliable
050
      recommendation guide for time series generation (Kong et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2024; Coletta et al.,
      2023; Yuan & Qiao, 2024).
051
052   In light of the insufficient comparison with advanced diffusion-based generative frameworks and
053   limited exploration of channel-independent frameworks, we have updated the recommendation
      guidelines from (Ang et al., 2023a) to better aid users in selecting TSG frameworks. Specifically, we

      advocate for an increased priority recommendation for both channel-independent and diffusion-based

058   2     R ELATED WORK
059
060   2.1   C HANNEL M IXING - BASED MTSG
061
062   There is a predominant focus on channel-mixing time series generation framework. Some methods
063   (Mogren, 2016; Esteban et al., 2017; Wiese et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2020; Jarrett et al., 2021; Jeha et al.,
064   2021) employ GAN-based (Goodfellow et al., 2014) architecture combined with neural networks
065   like LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), GRU (Cho et al., 2014), and Transformer (Vaswani
066   et al., 2017), which excel at capturing sequential data. Certain methods leverage VAEs (Kingma &
067   Welling, 2013) to effectively capture temporal features through variational inference, resulting in
068   efficient models with potential interpretability. Recent advancements have explored hybrid methods,
069
      combining flow-based models with techniques such as ODE (Chen et al., 2018), or integrating them
      with GANs or VAEs. Moreover, several diffusion-based methods (Yuan & Qiao, 2024; Kong et al.,
070
      2021; Coletta et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024) have been designed to handle MTSG. Due to their stable
071
      training and high-quality generation, diffusion models exhibit promising performance on numerous
072   datasets. Employing channel-mixing framework intuitively allows for improved consideration of
073   correlations between channels in multivariate time series data, thereby enhancing model performance.
074
075
076   2.2   C HANNEL I NDEPENDENCE - BASED MTSG
077
      COSCIGAN (Seyfi et al., 2022) employs channel GANs and a central discriminator to generate
078
      multivariate time series, but it fails to effectively capture key characteristics like periodicity and
079
      inter-channel correlations, limiting the performance of the central discriminator. Compared to the
080   MLP-based discriminator in COSCIGAN, more advanced architectures such as attention mechanisms,
081   TimesNet (Wu et al., 2022), and ModernTCN (donghao & wang xue, 2024) offer better potential for
082   performance improvement. Our findings suggest that the central discriminator operates primarily
083   as a time series classifier in small-sample contexts. However, MLP-based methods, as well as
084   attention-based approaches like TimesNet, exhibit limited effectiveness in handling such scenarios.

      5     C ONCLUSION
464
465   In this work, we tackled the limitations in existing multivariate time series generation frameworks
466   by proposing an updated recommendation guide, TSGGuide, aimed at improving the selection
467   of generation methods. Our comprehensive analysis revealed gaps in prior work, particularly in
468   the limited exploration of channel-independent frameworks and the absence of evaluations for
469   diffusion-based models. We have demonstrated that by enhancing the central discriminator within the
470   channel-independent framework—integrating methods like TimesNet and attention mechanisms—and
471   ultimately introducing CCD, significant performance improvements can be achieved, particularly in
472   small sample scenarios.
473   Our contributions offer a more balanced assessment of both channel-independent and diffusion-based
474   methods, underscoring their value in time series generation tasks. This work provides a more nuanced
475   framework for selecting TSG methods, addressing both the data-specific needs of users and the
476   current state of the field. Future research should continue to investigate the evolving landscape of
477   MTSG, with an emphasis on addressing periodicity and inter-channel correlation challenges. With
478   the conditional time series generation problem posed, it makes sense to explore the advantages and
479
      disadvantages of these schemes.

      Since time series generation tasks are motivated by small sample sizes, the central discriminator in
705   the channel independent generation framework is essentially a small sample time series classifier.
706   The main challenges in small-sample time series classification arise from the difficulty of adequately
707   training neural networks with limited data, which reduces their classification performance. Possible
708   solutions include: 1) performing data augmentation to increase the data size; 2) decomposing the
709   problem to reduce the classification difficulty; 3) enhancing the information to capture key features
710   that aid classification; and 4) using a small-parameter, high-performance mapping framework. The
711   deisigned CCD is shown in Figure 2.
712
713   Period Block In multivariate time series, changes in one channel often have short-term effects
714   on other channels. COSCIGAN (Seyfi et al., 2022) may tend to overlook the crucial mechanism
715
      of sharing information between adjacent time steps. Additionally, considering the periodic nature
      of time series, models should be able to capture time patterns across different cycles. Each time
716
      point involves two types of temporal changes: intra-cycle changes and inter-cycle changes, which
717
      correspond to adjacent regions and different phases of the same cycle.
718
719   For a multivariate time series T , we utilize Frequency to obtain its periodicity in the frequency
720   domain.

803   indicate a higher similarity between the correlation matrices of the real dataset and the generated

      synthetic dataset when smaller values are obtained. On the other hand, Spearman’s coefficient and

849
850   Numerous metrics are available to assess the quality of TSG (Time Series Generation) methods,
851   which commonly adhere to principles such as diversity, fidelity, and utility.
852
853   Model-based Measures These measures primarily follow the TSTR scheme (Esteban et al., 2017;
854   Jordon et al., 2018), wherein synthetically generated series are utilized to train a post-hoc neural
855   network, which is then evaluated on the original time series.
856
          • Discriminative Score (DS) (Yoon et al., 2019). This study utilizes a post-hoc time-series clas-
857
            sification model, leveraging 2-layer GRUs or LSTMs, to discern between original and generated
858         series (Yoon et al., 2019). The original series are denoted as ”real,” whereas the generated series
859         are labeled as ”synthetic.” Subsequently, an RNN classifier is trained using these labels. The
860         fidelity of the generation model is assessed by measuring the classification error on a separate
861         test set.
862       • Predictive Score (PS) (Yoon et al., 2019). It focuses on training a post-hoc time series predic-
863         tion model using synthetic data (Yoon et al., 2019). The model leverages GRUs or LSTMs
            to predict either the temporal vectors of each input series for future steps (Yoon et al., 2019;


                                                          16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
           Jarrett et al., 2021) or the entire vector (Jeon et al., 2022). To assess performance, the model is
865        evaluated on the original dataset using the mean absolute error metric.
866
867
      Feature-based Measures These measures aim to capture inter-series correlations and temporal
868
      dependencies, evaluating the extent to which the generated time series preserves the original char-
869
      acteristics. Feature-based measures offer a distinct advantage by providing clear and deterministic
870   results, ensuring an unambiguous assessment of the quality of the generated time series.
871
872      • Marginal Distribution Difference (MDD) (Ni et al., 2021). This measure calculates empirical
873
           histograms for each dimension and time step in the generated series. The bin centers and widths
           from the original series are used for this purpose. By computing the average absolute difference
874
           between these histograms and those of the original series across bins, it assesses the alignment
875
           of the distributions between the original and generated series.
876      • AutoCorrelation Difference (ACD) (Lai et al., 2018). This measure calculates the autocorre-
877        lation of both the original and generated time series and determines their difference (Parzen,
878        1963; Lai et al., 2018). By comparing the autocorrelations, we can assess the preservation of
879        dependencies in the generated time series.
880      • Skewness Difference (SD). In addition to ACF, this study incorporates statistical measures to
881        assess the quality of the generated time series (Wang et al., 2023). One such measure is skewness,
882        which quantifies the distribution asymmetry of a time series and is crucial for analyzing its
883        marginal distribution. Given the mean (standard deviation) of the train time series Tstr as µtr  s

      privacy preservation (PS), we propose the integration of two distance-based measures as a means of

908      • Euclidean Distance (ED). For each original series str = (x1 , · · · , xl ) and its generated series

923   D.1    AED AND AWD
924
925
      Time series generation tasks consider two key factors: Fidelity and Correlation Preservation. Fidelity
      aims to generate results that match the distribution of the original time series while avoiding mode
926
      collapse. We use the Average Wasserstein Distance (AWD) to reflect the diversity of the generated
927
      results. A smaller AWD indicates a closer resemblance to the true distribution and better fidelity.
928   Correlation Preservation aims to maintain the same channel correlations as the original time series for
929   different channels of the generated results. We map the generated time series onto a two-dimensional
930   plane and calculate the Average Euclidean Distance (AED) between the generated series and the
931   line with an amplitude and slope of 1. We use AED to measure the correlation preservation of the
932   generated results.

936
937   We present a detailed analysis of five representative time series generation (TSG) methods that are
938   based on three foundational generative models. To ensure experimental fairness, all experiments
939   are conducted on a machine with Intel® Core® i9 12900K CPU @ 5.20 GHz, 64 GB memory, and
940   NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090.
941
942   Pure GAN-based Methods Early studies (Mogren, 2016; Esteban et al., 2017) incorporated vanilla
943   GAN architectures originally designed for image generation and combined them with neural networks
944   such as RNN and LSTM, specifically tailored for sequential data. Subsequent research has been
945   dedicated to pioneering techniques that adapt to time series data and enhance performance.
946
          • RTSGAN (Pei et al., 2021). RTSGAN integrates an autoencoder into GANs and focuses on
947
            generating time series with variable lengths while effectively handling missing data.
948       • COSCI-GAN (Seyfi et al., 2022). COSCI-GAN is specifically designed to explicitly capture
949         the complex dynamical patterns within each series, with a focus on preserving the relationships
950         among channels or features.
951
952
      Pure VAE-based Methods VAE-based methods commonly leverage variational inference to ef-
953
      fectively capture temporal features. These methods are known for their efficiency and potential
954
      interpretability.
955
956       • TimeVAE (Desai et al., 2021). TimeVAE extends the application of Variational Autoencoders
957         (VAEs) to general-purpose time series generation. It incorporates convolutional operations and
958
            enhances interpretability through time series decomposition techniques.
          • TimeVQVAE (Lee et al., 2023). TimeVAE incorporates the Short-Time Fourier Transform
959
            (STFT) to decompose input time series into low-frequency and high-frequency components. It
960
            further enhances the modeling of these components by integrating Vector Quantization with
961         VAEs (Lee et al., 2023), ensuring the preservation of both the general shape and specific details
962         of the time series.
963
964
965
      Mixed-Type Methods Recent advancements in time series generation (TSG) have explored mixed-
      type methods, which involve combining flow-based models with techniques such as Discrete Fourier
966
      Transform (DFT) or Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs). Additionally, these methods have been
967
      integrated with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) or Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) to
968   further enhance their capabilities.
969
970       • LS4 (Zhou et al., 2023). LS4 is derived from deep state-space models and integrates stochastic
971         latent variables to augment the model’s capacity while leveraging the training objectives of
            Variational Autoencoders (VAEs).

1200   assess the temporal dependency.
1201   2) Correlational Score To mitigate the challenges associated with DS and PS, we propose the
1202   incorporation of distance-based measures to provide an efficient, deterministic evaluation.

       3) Discriminative Score For a quantitative measure of similarity, we train a post-hoc time-series
       classification model (by optimizing a 2-layer LSTM) to distinguish between sequences from the
1205
       original and generated datasets. First, each original sequence is labeled real, and each generated
1206
       sequence is labeled not real. Then, an off-the-shelf (RNN) classifier is trained to distinguish between
1207   the two classes as a standard supervised task.

1210

1211

1212
       we evaluate the trained model on the original dataset.

       L     P RE - TRAINING DATA D ETAILS
1366   We provide additional details regarding the pre-training data. In our approach, 4 is pre-trained on a
1367   large synthetic dataset of paired numeric and symbolic data, utilizing the data generation technique
1368   from Kamienny et al. (2022). Each example consists of a set of N points (x, y) ∈ RD+1 and an
1369   associated mathematical function f (·), such that y = f (x). These examples are generated by first
1370   sampling a function f , followed by sampling N numeric input points xi ; i = 1, . . . , N ∈ RD from
1371   f , and then calculating the target value yi = f (xi ).
1372
       L.1   S AMPLING OF FUNCTIONS
1373
1374   To generate random functions f , we employ the strategy outlined in Kamienny et al. (2022), building
1375
       random trees with mathematical operators as nodes and variables/constants as leaves. This process
       includes:
1376
1377   Input Dimension Selection. We begin by selecting the input dimension D for the functions from a
1378   uniform distribution U(1, Dmax ). This step ensures variability in the number of input variables.
1379   Binary Operator Quantity Selection. Next, we determine the quantity of binary operators b by
1380   sampling from U(D − 1, D + bmax ) and selecting b operators randomly from the set U(+, −, ×).
1381   This step introduces variability in the complexity of the generated functions.
1382
       Tree Construction. Using the chosen operators and input variables, we construct binary trees,
1383
       simulating the mathematical function’s structure. The construction process is performed following
1384
       the method proposed in Kamienny et al. (2022).
1385
1386   Variable Assignment to Leaf Nodes. Each leaf node in the binary tree corresponds to a variable,
1387   which is sampled from the set of available input variables (xd for d = 1, . . . , D).
1388   Unary Operator Insertion. Additionally, we introduce unary operators by selecting their quantity u
1389   from U(0, umax ) and randomly inserting them from a predefined set (Ou ) of unary operators where
1390   Ou = [inv, abs, pow2, pow3, sqrt, sin, cos, tan, arctan, log, exp].
1391
       Affine Transformation. To further diversify the functions, we apply random affine transformations
1392   to each variable (xd ) and unary operator (u). These transformations involve scaling (a) and shifting
1393   (b) by sampling values from Daff . In other words, we replace xd with axd + b and u with au + b,
1394   where (a, b) are samples from Daff . This step enhances the variety of functions encountered during
1395   pre-training and ensures the model encounters a unique function each time, aiding in mitigating the
1396   risk of overfitting as well as memorization. We used ten functions to generate 100 datasets. The
1397   following are the functions we used: add, sub, mul, div, abs, inv, sqrt, log, exp, sin.
1398
1399
       L.2   S AMPLING OF DATAPOINTS
1400   Once have generated a sample function f , we proceed to generate N input points xi ∈ RD and
1401   calculate their corresponding target value yi = f (xi ). To maintain data quality and relevance, we
1402   follow the guidelines from Kamienny et al. (2022), which include: Discarding and Restarting: If any
1403   input point xi falls outside the function’s defined domain or if the target value yi exceeds 10100 , we
       discard the sample function and restart the generation process. This ensures that the model learns


                                                         26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
       meaningful and well-behaved functions. Avoidance and Resampling: Avoidance and resampling of
1405
       out-of-distribution xi values provide additional insights into f as it allows the model to learn its
1406   domain. This practice aids the model in handling input variations. Diverse Input Distributions: To
1407   expose the model to a broad spectrum of input data distributions, we draw input points from a mixture
1408   of distributions, such as uniform or Gaussian. These distributions are centered around k randomly
1409   chosen centroids, introducing diversity and challenging the model’s adaptability.
1410
       The generation of input points involves the following steps:
1411
1412   Cluster and Weight Selection. We start by sampling the number of clusters k from a uniform distri-
1413   bution
       P      U(1, kmax ). Additionally, we sample k weights {wj ∼ U(0, 1)}kj=1 , which are normalized to
1414     j wj = 1.
1415
       Cluster Parameters. For each cluster, we sample a centroid µj ∼ N (0, 1)D , a vector of variances
1416
       σj ∼ U(0, 1)D , and a distribution shape Dj from {N , U} (Gaussian or uniform). These parameters
1417
       define the characteristics of each cluster.
1418
1419   Input Point Generation. We sample [wj N ] input points from the distribution Dj (µj , σj ) for each
1420   cluster j. This sampling with different weights from different distributions ensures the sampling of a
1421   diverse set of input points with varying characteristics.
1422   Normalization. Finally, all generated input points are concatenated and normalized by subtracting
1423   the mean and dividing by the standard deviation along each dimension.
