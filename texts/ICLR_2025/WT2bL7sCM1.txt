009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Influence functions serve as crucial tools for assessing sample influence. By em-
012
               ploying the first-order Taylor extension, sample influence can be estimated without
013
               the need for expensive model retraining. However, applying influence functions
014            directly to deep models presents challenges, primarily due to the non-convex nature
015            of the loss function and the large size of model parameters. This difficulty not
016            only makes computing the inverse of the Hessian matrix costly but also renders
017            it non-existent in some cases. Various approaches, including matrix decomposi-
018            tion, have been explored to expedite and approximate the inversion of the Hessian
019            matrix, with the aim of making influence functions applicable to deep models. In
020            this paper, we revisit a specific, albeit naive, yet effective approximation method
021            known as TracIn, and simplify it further, introducing the name Inner Product (IP).
022
               This method substitutes the inverse of the Hessian matrix with an identity matrix.
               We offer deeper insights into why this straightforward approximation method is
023
               effective. Furthermore, we extend its applications beyond measuring model utility
024
               to include considerations of fairness and robustness. Finally, we enhance IP through
025            an ensemble strategy. To validate its effectiveness, we conduct experiments on
026            synthetic data and extensive evaluations on noisy label detection, sample selection
027            for large language model fine-tuning, and defense against adversarial attacks.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Data-centric learning is a growing research field that focuses on enhancing machine learning model
034   performance by refining the quality and characteristics of training data (Oala et al., 2023). In contrast
035   to model-centric approaches, which prioritize improving algorithms or optimization techniques
036   without altering the dataset, data-centric learning involves adjusting the dataset itself—through
037   methods like trimming, relabeling, and reweighting—while keeping the learning algorithm fixed.
038   This approach plays a vital role in areas such as model interpretability, selecting training subsets,
039
      generating synthetic data, detecting noisy labels, improving active learning, and promoting fairness
      in machine learning models (Chhabra et al., 2024; Kwon et al., 2023).
040
041   Sample influence estimation, as the foundation of data-centric learning, can be generally categorized
042   into two categories (Hammoudeh & Lowd, 2022). (a) Retraining-based methods assess the sample
043   influence by retraining the model with and without a specific sample and checking the performance
044   change, which include the classical leave-one-out influence approach (Cook & Weisberg, 1982) and
045
      Shapley value approaches (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Jia et al., 2019; Kwon & Zou, 2022; Jia et al.,
      2018). (b) Gradient-based methods estimate influence without expensive overheads of retraining,
046
      known as influence functions. The seminal work in this category is that of (Koh & Liang, 2017),
047
      which utilizes a Taylor-series approximation and LiSSA optimization (Agarwal et al., 2017) to
048   compute sample influences. However, the limiting assumption is that the model and loss function
049   are convex. Despite debates on the necessity of convexity (Bae et al., 2022; Grosse et al., 2023;
050   Basu et al., 2020; Epifano et al., 2023), challenges persist when directly applying gradient-based
051   methods to large models. The size of model parameters complicates calculations, particularly in
052   obtaining the inverse of the Hessian matrix. Efforts, including matrix decomposition techniques (Koh
053   & Liang, 2017; Grosse et al., 2023; Kwon et al., 2023), aim to expedite and approximate Hessian
      matrix inversion, therefore enhance the feasibility of influence functions for deep learning models.

054
      Contributions. In this paper, we focus on the influence function category and revisit a specific
055   naive yet aggressive approximation method, TracIn (Pruthi et al., 2020). This method substitutes
056   the inverse of the Hessian matrix with an identity matrix, representing it as a Hessian-free influence
057   function—the inner product of the gradient of the validation set and a sample to be assessed, which
058   we refer to as Inner Product (IP). We summarize our major contributions as follows:
059      • We simplify the checkpoints of TracIn, define it into IP formulation, and delve into the rationale
060        behind this simple approximation, offering insights into why it performs well in practice.
061      • Expanding our IP framework, we extend its applicability beyond measuring sample influence on
062        model utility to encompass considerations of fairness and robustness.
063
         • To enhance the generalization, we propose IP Ensemble, a novel approach leveraging dropout
064        mechanisms to simulate diverse models. IP Ensemble amalgamates IP scores from these varied
065        models, thus increasing the method’s generalization capabilities.
066
         • By assessing the sample influence, model performance can be enhanced by trimming detrimental
067        samples from the training or fine-tuning set, downweighting them, or even relabeling them.
068        Specifically, we validate the effectiveness of IP through synthetic data experiments and conduct
069        extensive real-world evaluations using IP Ensemble. These experiments span various applications,
070        including noisy label correction for vision data, data curation aimed at fine-tuning fairer NLP
071        models, and defense strategies against adaptive evasion adversaries.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
075
076
      In this section, we introduce the literature on influence functions, with a focus on the acceleration of
      the calculation of the inverse of Hessian matrix, followed by various applications and miscellaneous.
077
078   Efficient Influence Estimation. Influence functions serve as crucial tools for estimating the individ-
079   ual valuation of data without requiring model retraining. However, the computation of the inverse of
080   the Hessian matrix poses challenges for large-scale data and models. To address this issue, various
081   approaches have been proposed to simplify or estimate the inverse of the Hessian matrix effectively.
082
      A seminal work is that of (Koh & Liang, 2017), which employs a Taylor-series approximation and
      LiSSA optimization (Agarwal et al., 2017) to compute sample influences. Arnoldi (Schioppa et al.,
083
      2022) employs the random projection and simplified Hessian matrix for acceleration. EKFAC (Grosse
084
      et al., 2023) enhances Kronecker-Factored eigendecomposition for a precise Hessian approximation.
085   More recently, DataInf (Kwon et al., 2023) efficiently computes influence even for large models
086   by replacing the inverse Hessian computation with a readily computable closed-form expression,
087   although their framework may suffer from significant theoretical errors. TracInc (Pruthi et al., 2020),
088   a straightforward yet aggressive approximation, substitutes the inverse of the Hessian matrix with an
089   identity matrix, essentially considering gradients directly as a measure of influence. Beyond the con-
090   ventional influence function that gauges sample influence on the validation set, self-influence (Bejan
091   et al., 2023; Thakkar et al., 2023) computes influence using the training set alone. Moving beyond
092   using a single model checkpoint, GEX (Kim et al., 2024) leverages a geometric ensemble of multiple
093
      checkpoints to approximate influence functions, alleviating the bilinear constraint and non-linear
      losses. Moreover, TDA (Bae et al., 2024) also introduces a checkpoint-based segmentation approach,
094
      combining implicit differentiation and unrolling by using EKFAC (Grosse et al., 2023).
095
096   Various Applications of Influence Functions. With the above efficient approximation, influence
097   functions have diverse applications. One major application is identifying detrimental samples (Ham-
098   moudeh & Lowd, 2024). The learning performance can be further improved by removing (Chhabra
099
      et al., 2024), relabeling (Kong et al., 2021), or reweighting (Thakkar et al., 2023) the identified
      detrimental samples, which has significant implications in various fields such as noisy label detec-
100
      tion (Wang et al., 2020), subset selection (Ting & Brochu, 2018), and the identification of the most
101
      influential samples (Sharchilev et al., 2018; Xia et al., 2024). Other applications encompass few-shot
102   learning (Park et al., 2021), where influence functions help improve model performance with minimal
103   data, and recommendation systems (Li et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023), enhancing the accuracy and
104   personalization of recommendations. Influence functions are also valuable in selecting data for active
105   learning (Liu et al., 2021), fairness machine learning (Li & Liu, 2022; Wang et al., 2022; 2024),
106   adversarial attack (Cohen et al., 2020), graph machine learning (Chen et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023),
107   machine unlearning (Xu et al., 2024; Tarun et al., 2023), out-of-distribution generalization (Ye et al.,
      2021), data privacy (Carey et al., 2023), domain adaptation (Zhang et al., 2022), to name a few.

108
      Miscellaneous. Several studies have examined the fragility of influence functions in explaining
109   deep learning model predictions. Bae et al. (2022) discovers that while influence estimates may
110   not perfectly align with leave-one-out retraining, they approximate the proximal Bregman response
111   function, offering valuable insights for identifying influential or mislabeled examples. Basu et al.
112   (2020) demonstrates that the effectiveness of influence functions in neural networks varies with
113   network architecture, depth, width, parameterization, and regularization, underscoring their fragility
114   in deep learning due to non-convex loss functions. Epifano et al. (2023) suggests that the instability of
115   current validation procedures, rather than non-convexity or lack of regularization, may be responsible
116   for their unreliability. Koh et al. (2019) expands influence functions from estimating the effects of
117
      removing one point to large groups of training samples; Lyu et al. (2023) enhance influence estimation
      in large-scale models by concentrating on target parameters and addressing computational instability
118
      with a robust inverse-Hessian-vector product approximation; Chen et al. (2020) extend traditional
119
      influence functions to monitor the impact of pre-training data on fine-tuned model predictions,
120   facilitating the identification of crucial pre-training examples.

198   Enhancement. The simplicity of IP offers opportunities to enhance the generalization of influence
199   functions. In convex cases, the model parameter θ̂ is both optimal and unique. However, in non-convex
200   scenarios, the presence of local minima introduces instability and non-uniqueness into the solution.
201   Typically, ensemble strategies are employed to bolster model generalization (Dietterich, 2000;
202   Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). Yet, while employing different models can enhance performance, it
203   also escalates the costs associated with model training and complicates the calculation of influence
204   functions. This arises from the variability in model parameters, necessitating multiple computations
205   of the inverse of each individual Hessian matrix. The introduction of Hessian-free IP circumvents this
206   issue, eliminating the need for costly calculations of Hessian matrices and their inverses. Drawing
207
      inspiration from dropout mechanisms, diverse models can be swiftly generated without necessitating
      model retraining. By computing sample gradients from various models, we propose IP Ensemble that
208
      amalgamates IP scores from distinct models. Experiments detailed in Section 5 illustrate the superior
209
      performance of the inner product ensemble over other influence function-based methods.

      In this experiment, we employ three datasets—RTE, COLA, and QLIN—from the GLUE reposi-
      tory (Wang et al., 2018) to fine-tune the widely-used language model RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019). Our
395
      focus is on group fairness, necessitating that machine learning models treat samples within various
396
      predefined subgroups comparably. To assess fairness, we adopt the methodology outlined in (Qian
397   et al., 2022), which involves perturbing the demographic information within each sample and scruti-
398   nizing whether the model yields identical predictions for the original sample x and its corresponding
399   perturbed counterpart x̃. This evaluation metric "fair score" is defined as |C(x) − C(x̃)| over all the
400   samples in the test set, normalized by the size of the test set, where C(·) is the model predictor. Note
401   that the fair score is a negative metric, so smaller values are preferable. Utility and fairness serve as
402   distinct perspectives for evaluating the performance of a model. Thus, in our fine-tuning experiments,
403   we consider both utility and fairness. Employing the same influence function-based methods and
404   our IP Ensemble as in the previous section, we conduct a comparative analysis. For each method,
405
      we calculate the influence on both utility and fairness within the fine-tuning set. We then identify
      and remove the 5% most detrimental samples in terms of utility and fairness before fine-tuning the
406
      RoBERTa model to optimize performance across both accuracy and fairness metrics.
407
408   The results of this experiment are presented in Figure 5, where the Y-axis for fairness is inverted.
409   The brown crossing denotes the performance of using all the samples for model fine-tuning as the
410   base model. Building upon this, we plot a horizontal and a vertical line in each figure and divide
411
      the space by fairness and utility results into four regions. The green area in the top right corner
      signifies a model that is both fairer and more accurate compared to the baseline model. Results
412
      within this green region can be considered Pareto improvements, enhancing both utility and fairness
413
      simultaneously. It is evident most results are located in the green area, indicating the existence
414   of detrimental samples, and not all the samples are helpful to the model performance. This also
415   implies that influence function methods are effective to identifying the detrimental samples, even
416   for non-convex deep models. However, it is important to note that some competitive methods yield
417   a trade-off or even Pareto deterioration. For instance, LiSSA demonstrates a better fair score but
418   worse accuracy compared to the base model on RTE; conversely, it exhibits better accuracy but worse
419   fairness on QLIN. EKFAC shows similar performance on COLA and QLIN. Self-LiSSA demonstrates
420   Pareto deterioration on COLA. We hypothesize that the approximation of the inverse of the Hessian
421   matrix may suffer from large errors, leading to heavily divergent influence estimations from the true
422
      values. Our IP Ensemble consistently achieves Pareto improvements across all three datasets, often
      yielding the best results compared to other influence function-based methods.

474   8      C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this paper, we revisited, extended, and enhanced the TracIn method into the Inner Product (IP)
477   formulation. By proving the exhibition of order-consistency in several scenarios between IP and
478   influence function, we demonstrated substituting the inverse of the Hessian matrix with an identity
479   matrix offers a practical and computationally efficient solution to estimating sample influence. Based
480   on that, we extended our IP to measure the sample influence on fairness and robustness. Continually,
481
      we enhanced the generalization of IP by introducing an ensemble strategy. We verified the correctness
      of IP on synthetic datasets and extensive evaluations on noisy label detection, data curation for fair
482
      NLP model fine-tuning, and defense against adaptive adversarial attacks. Overall, our IP Ensemble
483
      highlighted the potential of simple, yet powerful, approximations in influence estimation.

      B.1     DATASETS

      We cover our generated synthetic datasets in Section 4, vision datasets in Section 5, text datasets in

752
753   B.1.1      S YNTHETIC DATASETS
754
755   We generate two synthetic datasets to validate the correctness of our IP method in measuring the
      convex and non-convex models’ utility, fairness, and robustness. Specifically, we generate a linear


                                                                   14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      dataset using the scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011) library’s make_blobs function, which consists
757   of 150 training samples and 100 test samples. The second dataset is the non-linear half moons dataset
758   so that we can train a Multi-Layer Perception network with two hidden layers with ReLU activations.
759   The training set has 250 samples and the test set has 100 samples, and the dataset is generated using
760   the scikit-learn library’s make_moons function. Here we manually flip the labels of 20 samples (10
761   from each class) to add noise to the data.
762
763   B.1.2    V ISION DATASETS
764
765   Both the CIFAR-10N and CIFAR-100N datasets (Oliver et al., 2018) consist of the same input images
766
      that make up the CIFAR-10 (10 classes) and CIFAR-100 (100 classes) datasets (Krizhevsky et al.,
      2009), respectively. Each input is a 32x32 RGB image with a dimension of (3,32,32). However,
767
      for CIFAR-10N and CIFAR-100N, the labels are noisy, as they contain real-world human annotation
768
      errors collected using 3 annotators on Amazon Mechanical Turk. As these datasets are based on
769   human-annotated noise, they model noisy real-world datasets more realistically, compared to synthetic
770   data alternatives. The training set for both datasets contains 50,000 image-label pairs, and the test set
771   contains 10,000 image-label pairs that are free from noise. For CIFAR-10N we utilize three noise
772   settings for experiments in the paper– (1) Worst is the dataset version with the highest noise rate
773   (40.21%) as the worst possible annotation label for the image is chosen, (2) Aggregate is the least
774   noisy dataset (9.03%) as labels are chosen via majority voting amongst the annotations, and (3)
775   Random has intermediate noise (17.23%) and consists of picking one of the annotators’ labels. We
776   use the first annotator for the random labels. For CIFAR-100N there is only a single noisy setting due
777
      to the large number of labeling classes, and the overall noise rate is 40.20%.
778
779   B.1.3    NLP DATASETS
780
      For the three GLUE datasets—RTE, CoLA, and QNLI(Wang et al., 2018), we perturbe the validation
781   set as follows. The Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE) dataset consists of sentence pairs labeled as
782   entailment or not entailment. It is derived from a series of annual textual entailment challenges. The
783   dataset includes 2,490 training examples and 277 validation examples. Since the test set does not have
784   labels, we split the validation set into two parts, with one half used as the validation set for computing
785   influence, and the other half used as the test set. Subsequently, we generated perturbed datasets for
786   both the validation set and the test set using a seq2seq model (Qian et al., 2022). The Corpus of
787   Linguistic Acceptability (CoLA) dataset consists of sentences labeled as grammatically acceptable
788   or unacceptable. This dataset is derived from publications in linguistics and includes 8,551 training
789
      examples and 1,043 validation examples. As with RTE, the test set for CoLA does not have labels, so
      we split the validation set into two parts, using one half as the validation set for computing influence,
790
      and the other half as the test set. Perturbed datasets were generated using a seq2seq model (Qian
791
      et al., 2022) for both the validation set and the test set. The Question Natural Language Inference
792   (QNLI) dataset is a large-scale corpus for question answering, consisting of question-sentence pairs
793   from the Stanford Question Answering dataset. The task is to determine whether the context sentence
794   contains the answer to the question. It includes 104,743 training examples and 5,463 validation
795   examples. Similar to the other datasets, the test set does not have labels, so we split the validation set
796   into two parts, using one half as the validation set for computing influence, and the other half as the
797   test set. Perturbed datasets were also generated using a seq2seq model (Qian et al., 2022) for both the
798   validation set and the test set. These datasets represent diverse natural language understanding tasks,
799   and using them helps evaluate the fairness and utility of the fine-tuned RoBERTa model.

801   B.1.4    DATASETS FOR D EFENCING ATTACKS
802
      We utilize three datasets: Bank, CelebA, and JigsawToxicity, to evaluate the defense against adaptive
803
      evasion adversaries. The Bank dataset (Moro et al., 2014) consists of features extracted from direct
804   marketing campaigns of a Portuguese banking institution. The goal is to predict whether a client will
805   subscribe to a term deposit. The dataset includes 18,292 training examples, 6,098 validation examples,
806   and 6,098 test examples. The feature dimension of the dataset is 51. Both the validation and test
807   sets have labels. The CelebA dataset (Liu et al., 2018) is a large-scale face attributes dataset with
808   more than 200,000 celebrity images, each annotated with 40 binary attributes. For this experiment,
809   we focus on a subset of these images. The dataset is split into 62,497 training examples, 20,833
      validation examples, and 20,833 test examples. The feature dimension of the dataset is 39. Both

      the validation and test sets have labels. The JigsawToxicity dataset (Noever, 2018) contains a large
811   number of comments from Wikipedia labeled for toxicity. The goal is to predict the toxicity level of a
812   given comment. The dataset includes 18,000 training examples, 6,000 validation examples, and 6,000
813   test examples. The feature dimension of the dataset is 385. These datasets cover a range of tasks

      In our experiments as described in Section 4, we utilized a three-layer Multi-Layer Perceptron

828   ReLU activation function. Specifically, the input layer maps the input features to 32 neurons, the
829   hidden layer maintains this dimensionality, and the output layer is a single neuron producing the
830   final prediction. The final layer’s output is passed through a sigmoid activation function to yield a
831   probability score.

      block is fine-tuned on the CIFAR-10N/CIFAR-100N training set experiments with default parameters–

      (0.0005), and number of epochs (100), for all experiments.

1022
1023   Our work aims to address issues that currently hinder the applicability of influence estimation
1024   in deep learning models. By enabling influence estimation for deep models, practitioners can
1025   assess whether training samples are beneficial or detrimental to performance. As we demonstrate
       through extensive experiments across multiple problem settings, our proposed outlier gradient

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       analysis approach outperforms existing baselines and can augment model performance by trimming
1027   detrimental samples. As a result, our work paves the way for significant positive societal impact,
1028   especially with the increased adoption of larger and deeper neural networks such as LLMs.
1029
1030
       However, despite our research demonstrating superior performance and potential benefits, there are
       several limitations to consider. First, our method assumes a level of model and data homogeneity that
1031
       might not hold in highly heterogeneous datasets or more complex model architectures. Furthermore,
1032
       there is the consideration of robustness and fairness across different domains and types of data; our
1033   current evaluations, while extensive, may not cover all possible scenarios or edge cases, potentially
1034   limiting the generalizability of our findings. Finally, ethical considerations around data trimming and
1035   sample selection must be carefully managed to avoid unintended biases or negative impacts on model
1036   performance for underrepresented groups. As influence estimation becomes more integrated into
1037   model training and evaluation pipelines, ongoing research and monitoring will be essential to ensure
1038   that these techniques are applied responsibly and equitably.
1039   Due to the extensive computational demands of our experiments (see details in Appendix E), which
1040   require multiple retraining cycles to evaluate model stability and robustness, it becomes impractical
1041   for us to conduct comparative experiments on very large datasets, e.g., ImageNet. The limited
1042   computational resources at our disposal make it challenging to perform such large-scale retraining
1043   efforts, especially given the significant training time and GPU requirements. As a result, we focus our
1044   evaluation on middle-size yet representative benchmarks that allow for more feasible experimentation
1045   while maintaining the rigor of our comparative analyses.
