009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Accurately predicting user watch-time is crucial for enhancing user stickiness and
012            retention in video recommendation systems. Existing watch-time prediction ap-
013            proaches typically involve transformations of watch-time labels for prediction and
014            subsequent reversal, ignoring both the natural distribution properties of label and
015            the instance representation confusion that results in inaccurate predictions. In this
016            paper, we propose ProWTP, a two-stage method combining prototype learning
017            and optimal transport for watch-time regression prediction, suitable for any deep
018
               recommendation model. The core idea of ProWTP is to align label distribution
               with instance representation distribution to calibrate the instance space, thereby
019
               improving prediction accuracy. Specifically, we observe that the watch-ratio (the
020
               ratio of watch-time to video duration) within the same duration bucket exhibits
021            a multimodal distribution. To facilitate incorporation into models, we use a hi-
022            erarchical vector quantised variational autoencoder (HVQ-VAE) to convert the
023            continuous label distribution into a high-dimensional discrete distribution, serv-
024            ing as credible prototypes for calibrations. Based on this, ProWTP views the
025            alignment between prototypes and instance representations as a Semi-relaxed Un-
026            balanced Optimal Transport (SUOT) problem, where the marginal constraints of
027            prototypes are relaxed. And the corresponding optimization problem is reformu-
028            lated as a weighted Lasso problem for solution. Moreover, ProWTP introduces
029
               the assignment and compactness losses to encourage instances to cluster closely
               around their respective prototypes, thereby enhancing the prototype-level distin-
030
               guishability. Finally, we conducted extensive offline experiments on two industrial
031
               datasets, demonstrating our consistent superiority in real-world application.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The rapid growth of online-video services (e.g. YouTube and Hulu) and video-sharing platforms
037   (e.g. TikTok and Douyin) has driven the increasing demand for personalized and high-quality con-
038   tent (Zhou et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2023). In video recommendation systems, user watch-time has
039   become a key metric for measuring user engagement (Covington et al., 2016). Accurately predict-
040   ing user watch-time not only helps improve user stickiness and retention but also optimizes content
041   distribution and resource allocation, thereby driving the growth of Daily Active Users (DAUs) on
042   the platform (Lin et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2022).
043   Existing methods for Watch-time Prediction (WTP) usually focus on designing specific loss func-
044   tions or transforming watch-time labels in particular ways to train the model, aiming to improve
045   performance. Weighted Logistic Regression (WLR) (Covington et al., 2016) treats WTP task as
046   a weighted binary classification problem, approximating the expected watch-time by assigning
047   weights to positive samples. Duration-Deconfounded Quantile-based (D2Q) model (Zhan et al.,
048   2022) divides videos into different groups based on duration and employs traditional regression
049   within each group to estimate the transformed watch-time. Tree-based Progressive Regression
050
      (TPM) (Lin et al., 2023) decomposes WTP into a series of ordinal classifications, leveraging a tree
      structure to model conditional dependencies.

053   models. They overlook the natural distribution properties of labels—we observed that the watch
      ratio (i.e., the ratio of watch-time to video duration) within the same video duration bucket exhibits

106   tems, especially for platforms focusing on short videos and movies. Despite its significance, there
107   are only a few papers that address this area (Lin et al., 2023; Covington et al., 2016; Zhan et al.,
      2022). A pioneering study (Covington et al., 2016) in YouTube’s video recommendation sphere in-

      troduced the Weighted Logistic Regression (WLR) technique for forecasting watch durations. It has
109   since been established as a leading method in related application areas. Nevertheless, this approach
110   is not directly applicable to full-screen video recommendation systems and may encounter signifi-
111   cant bias issues due to its weighting strategy. D2Q (Zhan et al., 2022) addresses duration bias by
112   utilizing backdoor adjustment techniques and models watch time through direct quantile regression
113   of viewing durations. Debiased and Denoised watch time Correction (D2 Co) (Zhao et al., 2023)

117
      behavior-driven assumptions and bucketization techniques to model watch time. However, those
      method fail to consider the ordinal relationships and dependencies between different quantiles. Ad-
118
      ditionally, since both approaches estimate watch time using point estimations, they overlook the
119
      uncertainty inherent in the predictions. Then, TPM (Lin et al., 2023) introduced the ordinal ranks of
120   watch time and decomposed the problem into a series of conditional dependent classification tasks
121   organized into a tree structure.

      Optimal Transport. Optimal Transport (OT) (Villani et al., 2009; Peyré et al., 2019) is a math-
      ematical tool used to transfer or match distributions. OT has been employed in a wide range of
124
      tasks including generative adversarial training (Arjovsky et al., 2017), clustering (Ho et al., 2017),
125
      domain adaptation (Courty et al., 2017), and others. Partial Optimal Transport (POT) (Caffarelli
126   & McCann, 2010; Figalli, 2010) is an extension of the classical OT problem, where only a partial
127   amount of mass is transported instead of transporting all the mass between two distributions. To
128   alleviate the computational load of OT, the Sinkhorn algorithm (Cuturi, 2013) was introduced as
129   an efficient method for solving Sinkhorn OT, and it was subsequently extended to POT (Benamou
130   et al., 2015). Previously, many methods (Flamary et al., 2016; Damodaran et al., 2018) applied
131   OT to domain adaptation, aligning the distributions of source and target domains in either input or
132   feature spaces. They utilized mini-batch OT to mitigate computational overhead but faced sampling
133   bias since mini-batch data only partially reflect the original data distribution. To tackle these chal-
134
      lenges, more robust OT models, such as unbalanced and partial mini-batch OT, have been developed
      to enhance performance (Nguyen et al., 2022). Building on this, joint partial optimal transport was
135
      designed to transport only a portion of the mass, mitigating negative transfer, and the method was
136
      later applied to open-set domain adaptation (Xu et al., 2020). Additionally, aligning source pro-
137   totypes with target features has been proposed as a solution to the problem of universal domain
138   adaptation (Yang et al., 2023).
139
140
      Deep clustering with VAE. Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma, 2013) have emerged as
      a pivotal approach in the domain of deep clustering for unsupervised learning tasks, effectively
141
      overcoming the limitations of traditional clustering methodologies that often struggle with complex
142
      and high-dimensional data. By optimizing the evidence lower bound (ELBO), VAEs facilitate the
143   learning of data embeddings while integrating prior knowledge, such as Gaussian Mixture Mod-
144   els (GMMs) (McLachlan et al., 2019), for modeling latent variables. Notable contributions in this
145   field include the Variational Deep Embedding (VaDE) (Jiang et al., 2016) framework, which com-
146   bines VAEs with GMMs, employing mixtures of Gaussian priors to enhance clustering performance.
147   GMVAE (Dilokthanakul et al., 2016) addresses the problem of over-regularization in VAE by em-
148   ploying the minimum information constraint. LTVAE (Li et al., 2018) improves clustering by inte-
149   grating a latent tree model into a VAE variant, introducing a tree-structured layer of discrete latent
150   variables optimized via message passing. VAEIC (Prasad et al., 2020) jointly learns the prior and
151
      posterior parameters, thus avoiding pre-training. The Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoder
      (VQ-VAE) (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) is an extension of the traditional VAE framework, which
152
      introduces a discrete latent space via a codebook of prototype vectors. In VQ-VAE, continuous la-
153
      tent vectors are quantized by mapping each to its closest prototype vector from the codebook, thus
154   discretizing the latent representation. Although the quantization process is non-differentiable, tech-
155   niques such as the Straight-Through Estimator (STE) (Yin et al., 2019) and Gumbel-Softmax (Jang
156   et al., 2016) enable end-to-end training by allowing gradient-based optimization. The prototype
157   vectors can serve as cluster centroids (Zheng & Vedaldi, 2023; Wu & Flierl, 2020), encapsulat-
158   ing essential information about distinct data clusters. In addition, the semantically rich prototypes
159   learned by VQ-VAE can support various applications, such as conditional image generation (Esser
160   et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022), multi-modal language modeling (Li et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2024)
161   and recommender system (Liu et al., 2024; Rajput et al., 2024).

248   4.2    C REDIBLE P ROTOTYPES G ENERATION WITH HVQ-VAE
249
      Currently, most prototype learning researches (Snell et al., 2017; Chang et al., 2022) typically rely
250
      on pre-trained models, where prototypes are generated by clustering the hidden representations for
251
      subsequent tasks. However, we argue that such prototypes often contain noise and potential errors,
252   limiting their capacity in calibrating original models. Therefore, we propose to generate prototypes
253   directly from the distribution of the prediction target Y. As shown in Fig. 1(a), when we partition
254   user historical behaviors into {1, 2, ..., D} buckets based on video duration, we observe that the
255   watch-ratio (i.e., the ratio of user’s watch-time to video duration) within each bucket exhibits a
256   distinct multimodal distribution. This indicates that user’s behavior is statistically clustered and
257   regular. However, these multimodal distributions are one-dimensional long sequences, making it
258   challenging to directly extract high-dimensional discrete representations.
259   Pre-processing. To solve this problem, we first sample L (L >> D) one-dimensional distributions
260   w = (y1 , ..., yn ) from each multimodal distribution. Using this sampling strategy, we transform the
261   original one-dimensional multimodal distributions into D ∗ L one-dimensional near-Gaussian dis-
262   tributions w of length n, thereby making the data more suitable for neural networks and effectively
263   reducing the difficulty of training.
264   Credible Prototypes Generation. We observed in Fig. 1(a) that the peaks at the same positions
265   across different duration buckets exhibit similar means but varying variances. Then, we hypothesize
266   that w sampled from the same positions in these buckets can be grouped into equal means but varied
267   variances clusters. Inspired by Vector Quantised Variational AutoEncode (VQ-VAE) (Van Den Oord
268   et al., 2017), we propose a Hierarchical VQ-VAE approach that first identifies the closest cluster
269   and then indexes the nearest vector within that cluster. Specifically, we take the one-dimensional
      distribution w into the encoder E(·; ΘE ) to obtain latent representation E(w). Subsequently, the

294   4.3   DISTRIBUTION ALIGNMENT
295
296   As illustrated in Fig. 1(b), we posit that the inaccuracies of recommendation models within the WTP
297   task stem from instance representation confusion. This confusion hampers the model’s ability to ef-
298   fectively differentiate between various user behavior patterns, thereby adversely affecting predictive
299
      performance. To address this issue, it is imperative to utilize the generated credible prototypes P to
      calibrate the instance representation f (x), thereby reducing representation confusion and enhancing
300
      the model’s predictive accuracy.
301

      A.1       EXPLANATIONS ON INSTANCE REPRESENTATION CONFUSION .
705
706   A.1.1      MATHEMATICAL EXPLANATION
707
708   Proposition A.1. In WTP task, let the instance representation of a sample (x, y) be f (x), with
709
      its ideal center being µy = E[f (x) | y], where y is the ground-truth. The degree of instance
      representation confusion is defined as the distance between the instance representation and the
710
      ideal center, d(f (x), µy ) = ∥f (x) − µy ∥. Then, the model’s prediction error ∆x = |y − ŷ| is
711
      predominantly correlated with the degree of instance representation confusion d(f (x), µy ).
712
713   proof :
714
      In a regression model for WTP task, suppose the predicted value is given by ŷ = ReLU(W f (x)+b),
715
      and the true value y is a function represented by a ideal center µy = E[f (x) | y] and a noise term ϵ:
716
717                                         y = ReLU(W µy + b) + ϵ,                                    (13)
718
719   where W ∈ R1×d and b ∈ R are the model parameters, and f (x) ∈ Rd is the instance representation
720   of the input x. The noise ϵ is independent and identically distributed Gaussian noise with zero mean,
721   unrelated to the instance representation, i.e., ϵ ∼ N (0, σ 2 ).
722   Starting with the model and true value definitions, the error can be rewritten as:
723
724
                        ∆x = |y − ŷ| = |ReLU(W µy + b) + ϵ − ReLU(W f (x) + b)|.                      (14)
725
      We assume that the ideal center µy lies within the activation region, meaning that W µy +b ≥ 0. This
726
      assumption holds because, in WTP task, the ground-truth y ≥ 0. Thus, we only need to consider
727
      two cases based on the value of W f (x) + b for each sample:
728
729   (1) Case 1: W f (x) + b ≥ 0
730   In the linear activation region of ReLU, the output simplifies to:
731
732
                                      ∆x = |(W µy + b + ϵ) − (W f (x) + b)|.                           (15)
733
734
      Further simplifying:
                                            ∆x = |W (µy − f (x)) + ϵ|.                                 (16)
735
736   The squared error is:
737
738                            ∆2x = (W (µy − f (x)))2 + 2ϵW (µy − f (x)) + ϵ2 .                       (17)
739
740
      Taking the expectation, assuming ϵ is independent of f (x) and E[ϵ] = 0:
741                                    E[∆2x ] = (W (µy − f (x)))2 + E[ϵ2 ].                           (18)
742
743   Since E[ϵ2 ] = σ 2 , we have:
744
745                                      E[∆2x ] = (W (µy − f (x)))2 + σ 2 .                           (19)
746
747   Thus, the expectation of the squared error is dominated by (W (µy − f (x)))2 , and we get:
748                               (W (µy − f (x)))2 = ∥W ∥2 · ∥f (x) − µy ∥2 .                         (20)
749
750   Therefore:
751                                          E[∆2x ] ∝ ∥f (x) − µy ∥2 .                                (21)
752
753   (2) Case 2: W f (x) + b < 0
754   In the non-activation region of ReLU, if W f (x) + b ≤ 0, then:
755
                                                       ŷ = 0.                                         (22)

      In this case, the error is:
757                                                ∆x = |y|.                                           (23)

877
878   Our goal is to transform the ground-truth Y of the entire dataset into high-dimensional vectors,
879
      referred to as prototypes, for downstream tasks. The generation process is divided into four steps,
      as shown in Fig. 6 :
880
881   1. Partitioning Y . The ground-truth Y is an N × 1 vector, where N is the size of the dataset.
882   Directly generating prototypes from this vector is challenging. We observe that watch-ratio in dif-
883   ferent duration buckets exhibit distinct multi-modal distributions. Thus, Y is first divided into D
884   unequal-length multi-modal distributions based on video durations.
885   2. Fitting Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). Even after partitioning, the watch-ratio distribu-
886   tions remain as long one-dimensional continuous arrays, making direct modeling still difficult. To
887   address this, we fit D GMMs to these distributions, where the number of components C corresponds
888   to the number of peaks in the distribution.
889   3. Random Sampling. For each GMM, C sets of means, variances and weights {(µj , σj , θj )}C
                                                                                                j=1
890   are obtained. The sampling process is as follows:
891
892          • Data for each peak is sampled randomly to form a distribution w = (y1 , y2 , . . . , yn ) of
893            length n, with the sampling range defined as [µ − i · σ, µ + i · σ], where i follows a Gaussian
                                ′   ′
894            distribution N (µ , σ ).
895
             • To ensure that the overall sampled distribution matches the original distribution, the number
896            of samples for each peak is determined by the weights θ. Specifically, when generating L
897            near-Gaussian distributions from the current multi-modal distribution, the allocation of L
898            is governed by θ, where the number of samples generated for each peak is ⌊θ · L⌋.
899
900   This sampling strategy significantly simplifies subsequent learning while adhering to the semantic
901   of Prototypes, where each Prototype represents the center of a peak.
902   4. Generating Credible Prototypes. For each bucket, L distributions w are sampled, resulting in
903   D × L distributions. These are fed into the HVQ-VAE for training, and the codebook weights from
904   HVQ-VAE are considered as Prototypes.

          A.4                         COMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY DISCUSSION
973
974      Stage I: prototype generations. The computational complexity of HVQ-VAE primarily arises
975      from the cluster selection and prototype selection. For a single sample, the cluster selection involves
976      computing the attention-weighted cluster centers, with a time complexity of O(C · K + C), where
977      C is the number of clusters, K is the number of prototypes per cluster. Within the selected cluster,
978      prototype selection further incurs a complexity of O(K). Overall, the time complexity for a single
979      sample is O(C · K + C + K), and the space complexity is dominated by the static storage of the
980      codebook, which is O(C · K · d), and d is the prototype vector dimension.
981      Importantly, HVQ-VAE is completely independent, and its spatio-temporal complexity does not
982      affect the training and inference time of ProWTP. When the distribution of watch ratios is suffi-
983      ciently large, the resulting prototype distribution is stable. Furthermore, we observed that for a well-
984      established video recommendation APP, the watch-ratio distribution remains largely unchanged and
985      consistent across multiple months.
986      As shown in Figure 10, we randomly sampled 200,000 users from our APP (a short-video platform)
987      and extracted their historical behavior on the 1st day of each month from January to November
988      2024. The data were divided into D = 15 buckets based on video duration. We then computed the
989      Wasserstein Distance between the watch-ratio probability density distributions of each month and
990      November, as well as the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test with p < 0.05 between their cumulative empir-
991      ical distributions. The results indicated no significant distribution shifts across multiple months.
992      Even in extreme scenarios where user behavior undergoes notable adjustments, we only need to
993      resample the watch-ratio distributions for each duration buckets, perform offline retraining, and
994      update the weights of ProWTP. This process incurs minimal computational overhead.

1069   that are pivotal in leveraging Watch-time prediction:
1070

1073            • WLR (Weighted Logistic Regression) (Covington et al., 2016): As implemented in
1074              YouTube’s system, this method learns a logistic regression model, reweighted by watch
1075              times, and uses the learned odds to estimate watch time during prediction.
1076            • OR (Ordinal Regression) (Crammer & Singer, 2001): This method, based on ordinal re-
1077              gression techniques, emphasizes the relative order of watch times, fitting the data to predict
1078              categorical watch time levels.

1129   evaluate how well the predicted rankings of watch times match the true rankings, emphasizing the
1130   importance of the order of predictions. Both metrics are crucial: accurate predictions (measured

1133   correct ranking is often as important, if not more so, than predicting the exact values, making both

       A.8    T HE DERIVATION OF LHV Q−V AE .
1135
1136   The loss function LHV Q−V AE is designed to optimize both the encoder and decoder networks,
1137   while preserving the discrete nature of the latent space.
1138
        LHV Q−V AE = ||w − D(E(w) + sg[z − E(w)])||22 + ||sg[E(w)] − z||22 + β||E(w) − sg[z]||22 . (29)
1139
1140   This function consists of three key components, explained as follows:
1141
       Reconstruction loss:
1142
                                        ||w − D(E(w) + sg[z − E(w)])||22                              (30)
1143
1144
       This part measures the squared Euclidean distance between the decoder output D(·) and the orig-
       inal input w, assessing the model’s ability to reconstruct the data. Here, E(w) represents the
1145
       encoder output of the input w, and z is the nearest prototype vector. The stop-gradient oper-
1146
       ation sg[·] prevents gradients from passing through, ensuring that the codebook is only updated
1147   through the second term. During forward propagation (when calculating the loss), this simplifies to
1148   D(E(w) + z − E(w)) = D(z), and during backpropagation (when calculating the gradients), since
1149   z − E(w) provides no gradients, it also simplifies to D(E(z)).
1150
       Quantization Loss:
1151
                                                 ||sg[E(w)] − z||22                                   (31)
1152
1153   This loss encourages the prototype vector z to move closer to the encoder output E(w). The stop-
1154
       gradient operation is applied to E(w) to prevent gradients from propagating through this term to the
       encoder, thus only updating the codebook.
1155
1156   Commitment Loss:
1157                                            β||E(w) − sg[z]||22                                   (32)
1158   This term encourages the encoder output E(w) to commit to the chosen codebook vector z. The
1159   weight factor β adjusts the importance of this loss relative to the other components. By increasing
1160   the encoder’s commitment to its quantized representation, this term improves the model’s stability
1161   and efficiency.
