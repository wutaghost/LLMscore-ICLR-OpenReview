009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph super-resolution is an underexplored yet highly relevant research direction
012            that circumvents the need for costly and time-consuming data collection, prepa-
013            ration, and storage. This makes it especially desirable for resource-constrained
014            fields such as the medical domain. Existing work on graph super-resolution lever-
015            ages graph neural networks (GNNs) and achieves impressive results. However,
016            we note two major limitations in the current model design: (1) It violates the un-
017            derlying graph structure when increasing the number of nodes, and (2) it relies
018
               heavily on node representation learning, which has limited capacity to accurately
               model edges. To address these limitations, we propose two novel frameworks: (1)
019
               Bi-SR, which performs structure-aware node super-resolution, and (2) DEFEND,
020
               which focuses on edge representation learning for enhanced edge modeling. We
021            supplement our work with rigorous theoretical analysis and conduct extensive ex-
022            periments on simulated and real-world datasets covering diverse graph topologies
023            and low-to-high resolution relationships. The results demonstrate substantial im-
024            provements across all experiments, highlighting the potential of both frameworks
025            for graph super-resolution tasks.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   High-resolution (HR) datasets are crucial for accurate analysis and information processing. How-
031   ever, acquiring HR datasets is resource-intensive, necessitating the development of super-resolution
032   techniques to enhance the quality of easily accessible low-resolution (LR) datasets. Consequently,
033
      super-resolution has been extensively studied for images, and numerous traditional and deep learn-
      ing methods have been developed to tackle this challenge (Dong et al., 2015; Greenspan, 2009;
034
      Lu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022). While images form a significant class of datasets, many real-
035
      world problems are naturally and effectively represented using relational structures such as graphs.
036   Examples include traffic flows, molecular structures, brain connectivity, and social interactions.
037
038   Despite the ubiquity of graph-structured datasets, graph super-resolution remains underexplored.
039
      Unlike images, the LR and HR graphs lack a hierarchical or local relationship, which forms a critical
      limitation in model design. Considering that the basic building blocks of an image are pixels, locality
040
      of image super-resolution allows the use of the de-convolution operator (Zeiler et al., 2010) to easily
041
      increase the number of pixels or the size of the image. Similarly for graphs, nodes form the basic
042   building blocks, and an unpooling operation (Gao & Ji, 2019) has been defined to increase the
043   number of nodes or the overall graph size. However, this operation is overly simplistic, highly
044   localized, and requires the connectivity information of the HR graph as input, making it unsuitable
045   for graph super-resolution. Moreover, the lack of hierarchy leads to a significant distributional shift
046   between LR and HR graphs, further amplifying the complexity of graph super-resolution tasks.
047
      Although challenging, graph super-resolution is a highly relevant task, especially in the field of net-
048   work neuroscience. The connectivity strength between different regions of the brain can be encoded
049   as a brain graph, commonly known as a connectome. Various studies show that HR connectomes
050   lead to better neural fingerprinting and behavior prediction (Tian et al., 2021; Hayasaka & Lauri-
051   enti, 2010; Zalesky et al., 2010; Finn et al., 2015; Cengiz & Rekik, 2019). However, brain graphs
052   are typically dense and computationally intensive to collect, process, and store, even small graphs,
053   sometimes requiring gigabytes per individual (Tian et al., 2021). Therefore, deep learning methods
      for lightweight, on-the-fly calculation of HR brain graphs are advantageous.

054
      Recently, graph neural networks (GNNs) have emerged as de facto deep learning methods to process
055   graph-structured datasets (Zhou et al., 2020; Bronstein et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2021) and have
056   naturally been extended for graph super-resolution. Even though they achieve impressive results,
057   we note two major limitations: (1) The operation used to increase the number of nodes relies on a
058   simple linear algebraic technique that maps LR feature dimensions to HR nodes, ignoring the graph
059   structure of the problem. (2) Since most GNNs perform node representation learning, the models use
060   computationally intensive message-passing layers to learn a single node feature capable of encoding
061   all incident edges. These layers are not only unscalable for larger graphs but, by predominately
062   operating in the node space, offer limited capacity to learn graph topology. Combined, these form a
063
      significant research gap in graph super-resolution.
064   The topological limitation presents a serious bottleneck, particularly for applications in network
065   neuroscience. Numerous studies (Pereira et al., 2015; 2016; Khazaee et al., 2015; Nigro et al.,
066   2022; Mijalkov et al., 2017) have shown that brain graph topology plays a central role in correctly
067   identifying the onset and existence of various neurodegenerative disorders, including the two most
068   frequent ones: Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and Parkinson’s disease (PD). Notably, Pereira et al. (2016)
069
      observes that different stages of AD show decreasing path length and mean clustering compared to
      the control group. Similarly, Pereira et al. (2015) analyzes topological measures like clustering
070
      coefficient, characteristic path length, and small-worldness from 3T MRI data, observing aberrant
071
      values are for early PD patients. Finally, Nigro et al. (2022) shows a correlation between the loss of
072   hubs in certain brain regions and the emergence of more hubs in others for frontotemporal dementia.
073
074   Our Contributions: Motivated by above findings, we propose two new frameworks to tackle
075
      both limitations of existing graph super-resolution methods: Bi-SR (Bipartite Graph for Super-
      Resolution) and DEFEND (Dual Graphs for Edge Feature Learning and Detection). Bi-SR super-
076
      resolves nodes through bipartite connections between LR and HR nodes in a way that respects the
077
      underlying graph structure of the problem. DEFEND employs a dual graph formulation that maps
078   edges to dual nodes and directly performs edge representation learning using simple GNN layers.
079   We provide comprehensive theoretical analysis to justify the design of our frameworks and substan-
080   tiate claims regarding their utility. We also conduct extensive experimentation across different graph
081   topologies and LR-HR relationships to showcase performance improvements from both frameworks.
082
      Related Work: While the research on graph super-resolution is scarce, few foundational works
083
      have made notable contributions. Isallari & Rekik (2021) introduced a graph U-Net architecture
084   (Gao & Ji, 2019), incorporating a hierarchical structure and a graph Laplacian operator for up-
085   sampling LR brain graphs (Tanaka, 2018). Pala et al. (2021) accelerated model training by using
086   representation template graphs at both low and high resolutions as priors. Mhiri et al. (2021) em-
087   ployed NNConv layers (Simonovsky & Komodakis, 2017) for global graph alignment and a graph-
088   GAN model (Wang et al., 2018) to generate HR connectomes. However, this state-of-the-art model
089   struggles with dense brain graphs, often resulting in out-of-memory (OOM) errors due to the com-
090   putational complexity of NNConv layers. Finally, Monti et al. (2018) uses a similar dual graph
091   formulation to learn attention weights in GAT layers but differs from our work as we are leveraging
092
      the dual graphs for direct edge feature learning in graph super-resolution.

096   2.1   G RAPH DATA S TRUCTURE
097
098   Graphs are relational data structures defined by G = (V, E, A, X), where V is the set of nodes, E
099   represents edges as ordered/unordered pairs (vi , vj ) s.t. vi , vj ∈ V, A ∈ Rn×n is the adjacency
100   matrix capturing edge weights, and X ∈ Rn×d is the node feature matrix with n = |V| nodes and
101   d-dimensional features. In this work, we focus on simple undirected graphs, where A is symmetric
102   with Aij = Aji = eij denoting the relationship strength between nodes vi and vj . For notational
103   convenience, we use xi to represent feature vector for node vi .
104

145   While numerous methods exist for learning node feature matrix X̂ and structure Â for graphs with
146   fixed number of nodes, graph super-resolution requires an operator that expands the number of
147   nodes from nl to nh . Existing work use a linear algebraic trick to predict X̂h from Xl ∈ Rnl ×d .
148   First, a GNN maps Xl to nh -dimensional feature space as X̂l = GN Nnh (Xl , Al ), where GN Nnh :
149   Rnl ×d 7→ Rnl ×nh . Then, its transpose initializes HR node feature matrix as X̂h = X̂Tl , where X̂h ∈
150   Rnh ×nl , and could be used with any downstream task like predicting Âh or HR node classification.
151
      Although effective, this method loses structural integrity by mapping LR feature dimensions to
152   HR nodes—analogous to arbitrarily mapping image channels to pixels, which violets data struc-
153   ture. It is also incompatible with downstream applications requiring node permutation invariance.
154   Therefore, we explore graph structure-aware alternatives to replace this linear algebraic operator
155   T ranspose(GN Nnh (Xl , Al )) s.t. its akin to mapping pixels to pixels in image processing.

332   4     P ROPOSED DEFEND F RAMEWORK
333
334   To enhance the edge modeling capacity of S, we introduce a dual graph formulation which creates
335   an invertible mapping between edges of our HR graph and nodes of a newly created dual graph:
336
337         Dual Graph Formulation
338
339         Given a simple undirected graph G = (V, E, A), known as the primal graph, its dual graph
340         G ′ = (V ′ , E ′ , A′ ) is given as follows:
341         1. Each edge (i, j) ∈ E in G corresponds to a node (i, j) ∈ V ′ in G ′ .
342         2. Two dual nodes (i, j) and (k, l) in V ′ are connected by an edge in G ′ if and only if the
343            corresponding edges in G share a common node i.e. the condition i = k or i = l or
344            j = k or j = l is satisfied.
345         3. Let p and q be the indices of the dual nodes (i, j) and (k, l) in V ′ , respectively. Then,
346            the adjacency matrix A′ of the dual graph G ′ is defined as A′ pq = A′ qp = 1 if and only
347            if (i, j) and (k, l) are connected as defined in 2. above; otherwise A′ pq = A′ qp = 0.
348
349
      Above formulation retains all structural information of the primal graph. By treating edges as nodes,
350
      it permits direct application of node-based GNN layers for edge representation learning. This for-
351
      mulation can be extended to simple directed graphs by letting (i, j) ∈ E be an ordered set and
352   connecting dual nodes (i, j) and (k, l) if they share a common node and a common direction. The
353   resulting dual graphs are known as line (di)graphs or adjoint graphs in graph theory (Gross et al.,
354   2018). For detailed computational analysis, please refer to section A.2.

      6    C ONCLUSION
494
495
      In this paper, we formalize graph super-resolution and tackle key limitations of existing work by
496
      introducing two novel frameworks, Bi-SR and DEFEND. Bi-SR is the first known framework to
497   perform node super-resolution in a structurally consistent manner, while DEFEND provides a simple
498   graph reformulation to perform edge representation learning using traditional node-based GNNs.
499   Through extensive theoretical and empirical analysis, we demonstrate the superior performance and
500   versatility of these frameworks, especially to ensure topological fidelity in generated graphs. We
501   posit our work as general graph super-resolution frameworks. Therefore, as a future work, it would
502   be worthwhile to explore how these can be adopted and optimized for domain-specific applications.
503   Moreover, we observe that the relative performance of each framework depends on the scale of the
504   graph. Therefore, it would be interesting to perform scaling analysis to understand this trade-off.

1372   & Leskovec, 2016). The Node2Vec model combines random walks with the Word2Vec algorithm
1373   (Mikolov, 2013) to generate node embeddings. Specifically, it generates a set of random walks
1374

1375

1376

       Finally, we highlight some experimental caveats. First, we perform our experiments on small graphs
1891   and small data regime. While small graphs are found plenty in the graph learning tasks, neural
1892   networks generally struggle with small datasets and are prone to overfitting. This could be cir-
1893   cumvented by performing scaling analysis for our frameworks but this is beyond the scope of this
1894   work. Second, topK pooling uses traditional metric to create a relationship between LR and HR
1895   nodes. This may not be reflective of real-world graphs that encode more complex non-hierarchial
1896   relationships.

1898   F.3   B RAIN G RAPH DATASET
1899
1900   We report the performance across all eight evaluation measures in Table 13. We observe that our
1901
       dual graph formulation outperforms other methods, especially across the topological measures. It
       beats the IMANadapted and Autoencoder by a wide margin on these measures. For our bipartite
1902
       graph formulation, we observe that message passing performs better than linear combination in the
1903
       absence of the dual graph operator D but the performance difference diminishes on supplmenting the
1904   models with D. This could be possibly because our dual graph formulation provides a powerful and
1905   robust framework to refine the initially learned edge features from S, uplifting the performance of
1906   the the linear combination method. Unfortunately, the bipartite graph formulation does not improve
1907   over the linear algebraic method for this specific brain graph dataset.

1911   Finally, we also perform an in-depth sensitivity analysis for the random initialization strategy intro-
1912   duced for our bipartite message passing framework in section 3. Recall that this strategy involves
1913   initializing an HR node feature matrix with values randomly sampled from U(0, 1). To analyze how
1914
       sensitive our model performance is to this initialization, we re-run our experiments 15 times for the
       six models based on bipartite message passing: Bi-MP, Bi-MPf ixed , Bi-MPlearned , Dual Bi-MP,
1915
       Dual Bi-MPf ixed , and Dual Bi-MPlearned . These 15 runs measure performance across 5 different
1916
       random seeds and 3 length scales viz U(0, 1), U(0, 10), and U(0, 100). To measure the sensitivity of
1917   our formulation w.r.t. the other models, we introduce a quantitative metric called relative sensitivity
1918   srel as:
