009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Modern state-of-the-art machine learning models are often trained using a com-
012            bination of heterogeneous data sources. However, the utility of different data
013            sources as support for learning some target tasks is often not equivalent, motivat-
014            ing the need for automated methods of optimizing the relative contribution of each
015            data source to the model. In this work, we propose a dataset optimization strat-
016            egy that slices a normal model training step into a series of data source-specific
017            updates and splices them back together in an optimal manner with respect to the
018            loss on some target task dataset. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our algo-
019            rithm across different scenarios and domains, including classification problems
020
               for vision models and for next-token prediction tasks in the language domain.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Many state-of-the-art machine learning models are trained on mixtures of data from multiple
026   sources. For example, The Pile (Gao et al., 2020), a commonly used large-scale language dataset,
027   is composed of data from 22 different sources, including text from ArXiv, GitHub and Wikipedia.
028   A natural question follows: How does each subset impact model performance, and how should
029
      the mixture be reweighted to optimize downstream scores? The importance of optimizing the data
      mixture is further pronounced when model users have specific downstream tasks in mind.
030
031   Many prior works investigated different strategies to automate the optimization process of data mix-
032   ture coefficients (Albalak et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2024; Thrush et al., 2024; Zhao
033   et al., 2024; Shimabucoro et al., 2024). Such methods tends to rely on heuristics or empirically
034   observed correlations between training data properties and downstream model performance.
035   In this paper, we construct an optimizer that directly minimizes loss on a downstream task using
036   gradient-based optimization. Central to our approach is the concept of a data mixture gradient –
037   the derivative of the downstream validation loss with respect to the dataset mixture weights used
038   during training. Computing the mixture gradient is unfortunately intractable due to the complex
039   dependency between the mixture coefficient and the model parameters. Therefore, we develop a
040   theoretically justified approximation of the mixture gradient that breaks the aforementioned depen-
041
      dency, allowing tractable optimization for the mixture coefficients.
042   We propose a two-stage approach that optimizes model parameters and mixture coefficients sepa-
043   rately in each stage (see Figure 1). Assume we are optimizing a mixture over N subsets.
044
045         • In Stage-I optimization, we optimize the model using some initial mixture coefficients for
046           N data subsets. As the model trains, we accumulate the gradient contributions from each
047
              of the N data subsets into N separate buffers.
048         • In Stage-II optimization, we aim to optimize the mixture coefficients to improve the out-
049           come of training, as measured on a validation set. Using the N individual gradient contri-
050           butions stored during Stage-I, the final model parameters can be written as the parameters
051           at initialization, plus a linear combination of the N accumulated gradients in these buffers.
052           We then optimize the N coefficients in this linear combination to find a synthetic iterate
053
              that minimizes the validation loss. This simple optimization problem serves as a proxy for
              re-weighting the data mixture.

175   2.3   O UR METHOD
176
177   We discuss our method in detail in this section. The overall training process can be viewed as an
178   alternative optimization method that optimizes model parameters θ and mixture coefficients α in
179
      two stages:
180
181   Stage I: (θ-optimization) We train our model parameters θ on the data mix given by Equation (1),
182
      while keeping the mixture coefficients α fixed. As training proceeds, we store the cumulative gra-
                                        PT −1        ′
183   dient from Di in vector Gi =         t=0 ηt Li (θ), allowing us to separate out the contribution that
184
      each dataset makes to the final iterate. Note that while we motivate our approach based on gradient
      descent, our algorithm is not restrictive on the choice of model optimizers (as shown in Section 3.2).
185
186   Also note that both the loss Li (θt ; α) and the gradient L′i (θt ; α) are computed using a mini-batch
187   sampled from Di . This mini-batch changes on each step t, although our gradient notation omits this
188   dependence to avoid clutter.
189
190   Stage II: (α-optimization) In the second stage, we keep the model parameters θ fixed and optimize
191   the mixture coefficients starting from α0 by minimizing the validation loss V (θT ) as defined in
192   Equation (6). This simple procedure remixes the gradient information from individual datasets and
193   results in an updated model with parameters θ ∗ that achieves better validation performance.
194   Rather than rely directly on Equation (6), we make the change of variables β := α − α0 . This
195   converts Equation (6) to the equivalent form

199   We refer to optimizing Equation (8) as the ‘wiggle’ method as it corresponds to letting the final iter-
200   ate wiggle around θT rather than starting optimization from the far-away iterate θ0 . The optimized

202   Why use the wiggle method? Both Equation (6) and Equation (8) are the same when the gradi-

204   precision training), it is better to use Equation (8) as it always starts optimizing from the iterate θT .
205   For various reasons such as interpretability of α, it is sometime preferred to have the mixture con-
206   tributions sum up to one. In this case, we use a softmax function ϕ(.) to normalize α values during
207   training. We provide the details of this formalism in Appendix D.

      4   R ELATED W ORKS
442
443
      Here we briefly review related literature, which broadly fits into three categories.
444
445
446
      Dataset Mixture Optimization. This line of works focuses on optimizing data mixture for a pre-
      trained model, particularly in language domain, and does not consider optimization for specific
447
      given tasks (Albalak et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2024; Thrush et al., 2024; Zhao et al.,
448
      2024; Shimabucoro et al., 2024). For example, Liu et al. (2024) constructs a linear model to predict
449   the performance from mixture coefficients, where the data pair comes from training multiple small
450   models with different coefficients, and obtain optimal coefficients by reverse optimization. Albalak
451   et al. (2023) models the data mixture selection as a multi-armed bandit problem where a selection
452   policy is used to sample training batches. A reward is estimated based on the losses for the batch,
453   and the policy is updated accordingly. Contrary to these works, our paper focuses on obtaining
454   optimal mixture coefficients and the corresponding model for a given target task.
455
456   Data Selection via Influence Function/Attribution. Broadly speaking, this line of works focuses
457   on emphasizing the beneficial training examples for a given task during the training, where different
458   strategies are proposed to gauge the benefit of a training example. For instance, Xie et al. (2023)
459   computes the n-gram statistics from a reference dataset that has the same distribution as the target
460   task, and calculates the importance score of each training example based on the statistics. They then
461
      factor in the importance score by performing importance reweighting during the model training.
      Pruthi et al. (2020) focuses on improving the scalability of influence function calculation, where
462
      they propose to approximate the influence using a subset of network layers and a number of saved
463
      layer checkpoints from a normal training run, and use the influence score for data selection. To
464   handle a new target task, a user generally needs to fully repeat these algorithms, making adaptation
465   between target tasks less efficient. On the other hand, our two-stage approach allows users to shift
466   between target tasks on the fly without additional model training runs.
467
468   Active Learning and Coreset. Active learning algorithms naturally generate optimized selections
469   of training data as a byproduct, and the coreset optimization problem Sener & Savarese (2018) is key
470   to many related literature Sener & Savarese (2018); Mirzasoleiman et al. (2020); Xia et al. (2023);
471   Coleman et al. (2019), where the optimization determines a fixed number of most informative exam-
472   ples out of the training dataset. However, the corset optimization depends on distance measurements
473   between examples, which is known to be inaccurate in high-dimensional space. Furthermore, the
474   scalability of active learning algorithms remains an open question.
475

      5   C ONCLUSION
477
478
      In this paper, we propose an algorithm to optimize data mixture coefficients for a given target task.
479
      Our algorithm is a two-stage approach that alternates between optimization of model parameters
480   θ and mixture coefficients α. We develop an approximation for the mixture gradient backed by
481   theoretical analysis, which allows us to efficiently optimize α in Stage II by keeping gradient in-
482   formation G constant. The approximation also allows us to efficiently resemble a new model with
483   improved test-time performance. We further propose a checkpointing extension that is computa-
484   tionally feasible for large-scale datasets, and the multi-episode extension which guides α to the
485   optimal mixture coefficients and further improves the performance on the target task. We visually
      demonstrate the effectiveness of our algorithm in Dyck Grammer experiment, and validate different


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      variants of our algorithms in the CIFAR-10 experiment. We also show that our algorithm is effective
487   for modern-scale datasets and models in the object detection experiment.
