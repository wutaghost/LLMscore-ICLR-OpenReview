                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Automatic 3D object scanning typically involves reconstructing rotating objects
012            from images captured from different viewpoints. In such circumstances where
013            both the object and camera are moving, existing methods need object masks for re-
014            construction, and the mask quality can significantly affect the final reconstruction.
015            However, obtaining high-quality and view-consistent object masks is challenging
016            and laborious in practice. We address this issue by introducing Self-Supervised
017            Gaussian Segmentation (S2 GS), which automatically segments the object from
018            the background without relying on any segmentation masks. This is achieved by
019
               extending Gaussian Splatting with a learnable parameter that indicates the proba-
               bility of each Gaussian belonging to the target object. We optimize this parameter
020
               using implicit object transformation constraints and regularization terms. We eval-
021
               uate S2 GS on our new synthetic and real datasets. Experimental results show that
022            our approach outperforms the state-of-the-art methods (2DGS) with object masks
023            by 27% for novel-view synthesis and 7% for geometry reconstruction.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Accurately modeling the 3D geometry of objects remains a longstanding challenge in computer
      vision and graphics, yet it is essential for applications ranging from virtual reality to industrial
028
      design. Recent state-of-the-art methods, such as Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs) (Mildenhall et al.,
029
      2021; Fu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021; 2023) and Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023;
030   Guédon & Lepetit, 2024; Dai et al., 2024), have significantly advanced the field by offering unique
031   strengths in 3D scene representation and rendering.
032
033
      To achieve optimal 3D reconstruction of an object, comprehensive scans from multiple viewpoints
      are essential. In particular, the use of turntable setups has proven invaluable for high-throughput
034
      3D object scanning, offering an automated, accurate, and efficient solution for various real-world
035
      applications, such as scanning cultural heritage artifacts, digital art, and sculpting. For the sake of
036   convenience, the camera is usually fixed at discrete elevation angles rather than moving around the
037   object. Instead, the object rotates on a turntable to allow for complete reconstruction. In this context,
038   the capture environment often reflects casual indoor settings, where backgrounds can be complex
039   and cluttered.
040
      In such situations where both the camera and the object are moving, the assumption of a static scene,
041
      common in most existing methods, is violated. To address this issue, current approaches require ac-
042   curate segmentation of the moving objects to mitigate the influence of the background. However,
043   this reliance on precise segmentation limits their practical applicability, especially in scenarios where
044   segmentation accuracy is unreliable or segmentation masks are inaccessible. To overcome this lim-
045   itation, we formulate a new problem: 3D reconstruction of an object rotating on a turntable in the
046   presence of a cluttered background without access to segmentation masks.
047
      Figure 1 illustrates the challenges posed by background clutter in achieving accurate segmenta-
048   tion and 3D reconstruction using current state-of-the-art methods. In this scenario, an object is
049   positioned on a turntable while the camera moves to capture images from various elevations using
050   a robotic arm. Existing methods typically rely on a separate segmentation pipeline that provides
051   object masks. This subsequently results in inaccurate 3D reconstructions as shown in the figure.
052   Addressing this, we propose to estimate segmentation masks and 3D reconstruction simultaneously
053   by using an additional learnable probability parameter for each Gaussian, enabling self-supervised
      differentiation between foreground and background.

      2   R ELATED WORK
082   2.1 N EURAL 3D RECONSTURCTION
083   NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) can render high-fidelity novel-view images but fails to extract 3D sur-
084   face from volume density field. In contrast, neural implicit functions (e.g. signed distance function
085   (SDF) (Yariv et al., 2020) and occupancy grids (Niemeyer et al., 2020; Oechsle et al., 2021)) are usu-
086   ally preferred to define 3D surfaces due to accurate and smooth surface representation. Wang et al.
087   (2021) used an SDF to represent the 3D surface and convert the signed distance value into density
088   for rendering novel-view images via volume rendering, which enables surface reconstruction during
089   view rendering (Yariv et al., 2021). To further enhance 3D reconstruction via novel-view synthesis,
090   Fu et al. (2022) employs sparse 3D points, generated by Structure from Motion, to explicitly super-
091
      vise the SDF network in learning 3D surfaces. Additionally, the method incorporates multi-view
      photometric consistency constraints to further refine and enhance the accuracy of 3D reconstruc-
092
      tion. MonoSDF (Yu et al., 2022) leverages monocular geometric cues, such as monocular depth and
093
      normals, to refine the geometric reconstruction beyond typical rendering loss. To accelerate neural
094   surface reconstruction, NeuS2 (Wang et al., 2023) parameterizes SDF using multi-resolution hash
095   tables of learnable feature vectors, handling high spatial resolution with reduced computational cost.
096   Neuralangelo (Li et al., 2023) leverages the power of multi-resolution 3D hash grids (Müller et al.,
097   2022) and neural surface rendering to address challenges in reconstructing detailed structures from
098   real-world scenes.This approach offers a scalable solution for high-fidelity surface reconstruction
099   from RGB images without auxiliary data like segmentation or depth maps. By employing a progres-
100   sive optimization strategy using coarse to fine hash grids, Neuralangelo captures intricate details in
101   the reconstructed geometry. Additionally, it introduces a curvature loss to promote smooth surfaces,
102
      further enhancing the overall quality of the reconstructed geometry. Despite advancements in neu-
      ral 3D surface reconstruction techniques (Wang et al., 2021; 2023; Li et al., 2023; Müller et al.,
103
      2022), challenges persist in their efficient training and rendering. These neural implicit surfaces are
104
      primarily designed for static scenes and face difficulties in adapting to dynamic scenes.
105
106
      2.2 G AUSSIAN SPLATTING FOR 3D RECONSTRUCTION
107   Recently, 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) has shown impressive capabilities in novel-view synthesis with
      real-time rendering, and has been extended to reconstruct 3D surface from multi-view images (Chen

108
      et al., 2023; Guédon & Lepetit, 2024). By enforcing regularization terms to promote flat and well-
109   distributed Gaussians with limited overlap, SuGaR (Guédon & Lepetit, 2024) has developed an
110   approach that efficiently extracts accurate and editable meshes while enhancing rendering quality.
111   3DGSR (Lyu et al., 2024) integrates SDF with 3D Gaussians with a differentiable SDF-to-opacity
112   transformation function, which transforms SDF values into Gaussian opacities, linking SDFs and
113   Gaussians to enforce surface constraints and enable unified optimization. The Gaussian surfels (Dai
114   et al., 2024) method flattens 3D Gaussian points into 2D ellipses to resolve normal ambiguity and
115   improve surface alignment. Additionally, the method incorporates a self-supervised normal-depth
116   consistency loss to ensure that the local z-axis aligns with the surface normal derived from rendered
117
      depth maps. 2D Gaussian Splatting (2DGS) (Huang et al., 2024) uses 2D Gaussian primitives to
      model and reconstruct geometrically accurate radiance fields, in which a perspective-accurate 2D
118
      splatting process utilizes ray-splat intersection and rasterization. This approach helps in accurately
119
      recovering thin surfaces and achieving stable optimization. In this work, we aim to extend the
120   capabilities of 2DGS for 3D surface reconstruction in dynamic scenes.
121
      2.3 4D DYNAMIC G AUSSIANS
122
123   The representation of 3D Gaussians has also been adapted to reconstruct the geometrical structure
124
      and appearance of a dynamic scene (Luiten et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Yang
      et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024).
125
      Dynamic 3DGS (Luiten et al., 2024) is a pioneering approach that extends 3DGS to a dynamic
126
      environment. It performs frame-by-frame optimization iteratively, effectively handling multi-view
127   dynamic scenes with significant motion. Deformable 3DGS (Yang et al., 2024) proposes to learn a
128   spatial-temporal deformation model (i.e. MLP) to map time-varying 3D Gaussians into a canoni-
129   cal space, along with a set of Gaussian in the canonical space, both of which are jointly optimized
130   during volume rendering. Differently, 4DGS (Wu et al., 2024) modelled the deformation in a dy-
131   namic scene with multi-resolution voxel planes and a lightweight multi-head deformation decoder
132   to further enhance the efficiency. Real-time (Yang et al., 2023b) introduces a spatial-temporal vol-
133   ume representation using 4D Gaussian primitives that encode both geometry and appearance. These
134   primitives utilize parameterizations based on anisotropic ellipses and 4D spherical harmonics. To
135
      improve the modeling of dynamic scene geometry, motion-flow-based 3DGS (Guo et al., 2024;
      Wang et al., 2024) introduces the optical flow in the flow loss function to constraint the movement
136
      of 3G Gaussinas in 3D space. This approach allows the motion offsets of 3D Gaussians to be splat-
137
      ted and rendered into optical flow images. However, these dynamic 3DGS approaches focus more
138   on modelling time-varying appearance and geometry and fail to reconstruct accurate 3D structures.

188   4     M ETHOD
189
190
      In this section, we introduce our new approach for 3D reconstruction of moving objects, particularly
      in environments with cluttered backgrounds. Our method eliminates the need for explicit object
191
      masks by employing a fully self-supervised Gaussian segmentation technique.
192
193   4.1   P ROBLEM FORMULATION
194
195   Cluttered backgrounds pose significant challenges for 3D reconstruction of objects on rotating ta-
196   bles. Existing methods often struggle to reconstruct objects without explicit background masks.
197   While some methods perform better when provided with masks, they are still affected by the mask
198   quality, which is especially for low for a cluttered background and a complex foreground object. So
199   there is a need for a better approach that does not depend on input masks for background-foreground
200
      separation.
201   As shown in Fig. 1, our goal is to address the challenge of reconstructing 3D models from multiple
202   views in scenarios involving moving objects. For demonstration, this turntable setup is typical in
203   3D object scanning setups where the object rotates on a rotating table, and cameras capture images
204   from discrete elevation angles. Often in production environments, unlike a lab environment, the
205   scene includes cluttered, static backgrounds that are difficult to remove, posing challenges for accu-
206
      rately reconstructing the foreground object. Errors in background removal can severely impact the
      accuracy of 3D reconstruction.
207
208   To be specific, our approach involves partitioning the scene into two distinct regions: the dynamic
209   foreground and the static background. The foreground region encompasses the target object that
210   rotates with the turntable. Unlike scenarios involving dynamic scenes (Pumarola et al., 2021; Luiten
211   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024), we assume the foreground object is rigid, and its movement is
212
      consistent across the region. The background region encompasses all other static elements in the
      scene.
213

215   eral transformations are associated with the camera and the scene. Firstly, the camera poses

      down because both foreground and background are captured by the same camera but have different

243

244   managed, it can lead to noticeable artifacts in the reconstructed results.

248
      to achieve high-quality matting in practical applications. This extra manual step complicates the
249
      reconstruction pipeline, making it difficult to fully automate the process, which in turn reduces its
250   scalability for high-throughput 3D scanning. Moreover, despite the advances in large-scale models,
251   obtaining accurate pixel-level image masks across all viewpoints remains a significant hurdle.

253   4.2   E XTENDED 2D G AUSSIAN
254
255
      As analyzed in Section Sec. 4.1, a crucial aspect of addressing this problem involves accurately sep-
      arating the static and dynamic regions to process each area appropriately. In this section, we outline
256
      our approach to achieving segmentation in a fully self-supervised manner. Firstly, we adopt the 2D
257
      Gaussians from (Huang et al., 2024) as our scene representation method. This choice is motivated
258   by the method’s use of point-based representation, which explicitly defines the scene using thou-
259   sands of Gaussian primitives. This approach allows us to easily assign customized attributes to the
260   Gaussians. Compared to the neural implicit representations, such as neural radiance field (Milden-
261   hall et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022) and neural SDF (Wang et al., 2021; Oechsle et al., 2021; Wang
262   et al., 2023), the explicit representations are more suitable for addressing the formulated problem.
263
      As in the original 2DGS (Huang et al., 2024), the scene is represented by 2D Gaussian primitives
264   which comprise of a seriers of learnable parameters {xi , si , ri , αi , Ci }, where xi ∈ R3 denotes
265   the position of the Gaussian’s center, si ∈ R2 is the scale of the 2D Gaussian, ri ∈ R4 is its
266   rotation (orientation) represented by a quaternion, αi ∈ R is the opacity, and Ci ∈ Rk is spherical
267   harmonic coefficients of the Gaussian primitive. Apart from the standard parameters, we aim to
268   assign an additional indicator parameter that identifies whether each Gaussian is static or dynamic.
269   This distinction allows us to apply appropriate transformations to the static and dynamic Gaussians
      accordingly. In case that a native binary indicator is not differentiable and is not capable to be


                                                         5

270
      optimized by gradient descent. We propose to use a continuous parameter p ∈ [0, 1] to indicate
271   the probability of a Gaussian primitive belonging to the foreground region. Idealy, for any dynamic
272   Gaussian in the foreground should have p = 1, while others in the background will have p = 0.
273   Thus, the learnable parameters of a 2D Gaussian primitive are extended to be {xi , si , ri , αi , Ci , pi }.
274
      In the next section, we will talk about how we optimize the probability p together with other param-
275
      eters in a self-supervised manor.

277   4.3   S ELF - SUPERVISED G AUSSIAN S EGMENTATION
278
      We realize fully self-supervised Gaussian segmentation by integrating the foreground probability p
279
      into the rendering process differentially.
280
281   As described in Sec. 4.1, we segment the scene into foreground and background regions and each
282   region is related to the world coordinate system by the transformations F and B. Instead of putting
283
      everything in the world coordinate system directly like other Gaussian Splatting methods (Kerbl
      et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024), we initialize all the Gaussians in the local coordinate systems. In the
284
      rendering process, we transform the Gaussians to the world coordinate system by the transformations
285
      F and B.
286
287   The transformation is performed probabilistically according to the foreground probability pi . To be
288   specific, given a Gaussian primitive whose center position is xi in its local coordinate system, it will
289
      be transformed to the world coordinate system at time step t by:
290                                      Xit = pi Ft · xi + (1 − pi )Bt · xi                                  (7)
291
      where Xit is the coordinate of the transformed center point in the world coordinate system at time
292
      step t. Intuitively understanding of the formulation, we calculate the expectation of the transformed
293
      Gaussian’s center according to the foreground probability pi . With the proposed soft version of
294   the aforementioned transformation Sec. 4.1, the process is fully differentiable, and the probability
295   parameter pi can be optimized with the photometric loss by gradient descent. We don’t use any
296   explicit supervision for the probability pi .
297
      As the training process is converging, the probability pi is expected to be pushed closely to either
298
      0 or 1 and Eq. (7) will degenerate to either Eq. (5) or Eq. (6) meaning the Gaussian primitive is
299
      correctly transformed to the world coordinate system. In this way, we can segment the Gaussians
300   into foreground and background automatically.
301

358   Initialization. To minimize manual labor, we did not use SfM points to initialize the point cloud.
359   Instead, we adopted a random initialization approach. As described earlier, our Gaussians are de-
360   fined in the local coordinate systems. For the foreground region, we randomly initialize Gaussians
361   within a cube region centered at the origin. As for the background region, we simply initialize Gaus-
362   sians roughly on a sphere with 4 times the radius than the side length of the cube with probability.

      The foreground probability is initialized to be pi = 0.9 for Gaussians in the foreground region and

      6   C ONCLUSION
533
      We propose a novel method, S2 GS, for self-supervised segmentation of Gaussian splats in the
534   3D reconstruction of moving objects, particularly applicable to automatic 3D scanning. Our ap-
535   proach demonstrates robustness to cluttered backgrounds, enabling high-quality 3D model recon-
536   struction without the need for object masks. Compared to other state-of-the-art methods, although
537   Deformable 3D Gaussian Splatting (Yang et al., 2024) operates without object masks, it performs
538   significantly worse than our method. Furthermore, we outperform our base method 2DGS even when
539   it utilizes high-quality object masks. Future work involves extending the self-supervised Gaussian
      segmentation feature of S2 GS to more general moving object scenarios.
