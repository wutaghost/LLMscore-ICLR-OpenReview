009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A key challenge in interpretability is to decompose model activations into mean-
012            ingful features. Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) have emerged as a promising tool
013            for this task. However, a central problem in evaluating the quality of SAEs is the
014            absence of ground truth features to serve as an evaluation gold standard. Current
015            evaluation methods for SAEs are therefore confronted with a significant trade-
016            off: SAEs can either leverage toy models or other proxies with predefined ground
017            truth features; or they use extensive prior knowledge of realistic task circuits. The
018            former limits the generalizability of the evaluation results, while the latter lim-
019
               its the range of models and tasks that can be used for evaluations. We introduce
               SAGE: Scalable Autoencoder Ground-truth Evaluation, an evaluation framework
020
               for SAEs that enables obtaining high-quality feature dictionaries for diverse tasks
021
               and feature distributions without relying on prior knowledge. Specifically, we lift
022            previous limitations by showing that ground truth evaluations on realistic tasks
023            can be automated and scaled. First, we show that we can automatically identify
024            the cross-sections in the model where task-specific features are active. Second,
025            we demonstrate that we can then compute the ground truth features at these cross-
026            sections. Third, we introduce a novel reconstruction method which significantly
027            reduces the amount of trained SAEs needed for the evaluation. This addresses
028            scalability limitations in prior work and significantly simplifies the practical eval-
029            uations. We validate our results by evaluating SAEs on novel tasks on Pythia70M,
030
               GPT-2 Small, and Gemma-2-2B, thus demonstrating the scalability of our method
               to state-of-the-art open-source frontier models. These advancements pave the way
031
               for generalizable, large-scale evaluations of SAEs in interpretability research.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance across a wide variety
038   of tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Raffel et al., 2020; Devlin et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2019; Vaswani
039
      et al., 2017). As a result they are increasingly deployed in high stake domains, for example health-
      care (Luo et al., 2022), law (Shaheen et al., 2020), and finance (Li et al., 2023). However, the
040
      deployment of LLMs in such critical areas raises significant safety and ethical concerns (Bengio
041
      et al., 2024; Anderljung et al., 2023; Hendrycks & Mazeika, 2022), as their decisions can have pro-
042   found consequences. This makes it crucial to understand the internal mechanisms and reasoning
043   processes of these models, to ensure that their outputs are reliable, transparent, and aligned with
044   human values.
045
      A key challenge in interpreting LLMs is to decompose their internal activations into meaningful and
046
      interpretable features. Recently, sparse autoencoders (SAEs) have emerged as a promising solution
047
      for this task (Black et al., 2022; Cunningham et al., 2023). The quality of SAEs relies heavily on
048   a range of hyperparameters such as the dictionary size, the sparsity constraint, and the choice of
049   activation function (Gao et al., 2024). The dictionary size for instance influences the granularity
050   of features found. This makes it important to strike the right balance between being too broad,
051   which risks overseeing relevant features; or too specific, generating redundant or overly fragmented
052   features. Therefore, realistic SAE evaluations—those that assess models on their trained tasks and
053   within their application context—are critical to ensure that SAEs generalize well beyond synthetic
      benchmarks and perform reliably in real-world scenarios.

077   A central obstacle in evaluating SAEs in realistic settings is the lack of ground truth features that
078   can serve as a gold standard for comparison. This leads to a problematic trade-off: researchers ei-
079   ther rely on toy models with predefined ground truth features (Karvonen et al., 2024; Sharkey et al.,
080
      2023), which raises concerns about generalizability to real-world tasks; or they employ more real-
      istic tasks that require extensive prior knowledge of the task circuit to derive ground truth features
081
      (Makelov et al., 2024), limiting scalability to other tasks or models. As a result, task-specific ground
082
      truth evaluations are currently limited to smaller models, such as GPT-2 Small, where well-studied
083   tasks like indirect object identification (IOI) (Wang et al., 2023) are available. Nevertheless, ap-
084   proximate ground truth features have been identified in larger LLMs, including features for refusal
085   (Arditi et al., 2024), sentiment (Tigges et al., 2023), and toxicity (Turner et al., 2024). Moreover,
086   recent advances in automated circuit discovery methods have shown promise in automatically and
087   efficiently identifying task-specific circuits in large LLMs (Conmy et al., 2023; Syed et al., 2023;
088   Nanda, 2023). This suggests that ground truth evaluations of SAEs may scale to tasks without prior
089   knowledge and to large frontier models.
090   Another challenge in scaling SAE evaluations to large frontier models is the need to train multiple
091   SAEs across various model components (such as the query, key, and value vectors of attention heads)
092   to fully capture the structure of the task circuit (Makelov et al., 2024). This becomes especially
093   problematic for larger models, where the computational cost and extensive hyperparameter tuning
094   required for training a family of SAEs make this approach infeasible at scale.
095   In this work, we introduce SAGE: Scalable Autoencoder Ground-truth Evaluation (see Figure 1), an
096   evaluation framework for SAEs that enables obtaining high-quality feature dictionaries for diverse
097   tasks and feature distributions without relying on prior knowledge. Our method scales efficiently to
098   large frontier models and their associated SAEs. Specifically, we:
099
100         1. demonstrate that automated circuit discovery methods can find model components where
101            task-specific features are linearly represented. This allows for SAE evaluations on new
102            tasks without requiring manual experimentation or prior knowledge.
103
            2. show that supervised feature dictionaries derived from these components can serve as high-
104            quality ground truths for evaluating SAEs, enabling a reliable comparison standard for the
105            evaluation.
106
107         3. propose a new method for reconstructing sublayer activations, reducing the evaluation pro-
               cess to residual stream SAEs without compromising precision or generalizability. This

108
               innovation makes it feasible to scale task-specific SAE evaluations to large models by dras-
109            tically cutting the training overhead.
110
111   We apply this framework to existing evaluation benchmarks, validating the quality of our approach
112   on models such as Pythia 70M (Biderman et al., 2023), GPT-2 Small (Radford et al., 2019), and
113   GEMMA-2-2B (et al., 2024). Our results demonstrate that our method scales to large, state-of-the-
114   art frontier LLMs and SAEs. With this approach, we enable researchers to generate task-specific
115   evaluations for arbitrary SAEs, models, feature distributions, and tasks in a more efficient manner,
116
      paving the way for principled advancements in sparse dictionary learning and large-scale model
      interpretability.

119   2    R ELATED W ORK
120
121   In this section, we highlight some key contributions to the landscape of SAE evaluation methods.
122
123   Toy Model Evaluations Sharkey et al. (2023) explore SAE evaluation using synthetic datasets
124   with predefined ground truth features to assess how well SAEs recover features from superposition.
125   Their findings demonstrate that a simple SAE with an L1 penalty can effectively separate features in
126
      a controlled, toy model setup, offering a useful benchmark for understanding feature recovery. Kar-
      vonen et al. (2024) propose evaluating SAEs using board games like chess and Othello, leveraging
127
      the clear structure of these games to pre-define interpretable features. They introduce two metrics:
128
      Board Reconstruction (the ability to reconstruct game states) and Coverage (the proportion of pre-
129   defined features correctly captured). While these approaches provide straight-forward evaluations
130   based on ground truth features, they are domain-specific and may not generalize well to other tasks
131   and realistic models.
132
133   Task-specific SAE Evaluation Makelov et al. (2024) present a principled framework for evalu-
134   ating SAEs on GPT-2 Small using the IOI task. They compare several unsupervised SAEs with
135   supervised feature dictionaries to assess their ability to approximate, control, and interpret model
136   computations. Although this method offers a realistic, task-specific evaluation, it depends on prior
137   knowledge of the IOI task and is therefore specific for the feature distribution of IOI and GPT-2
138   Small, limiting its scalability to other tasks and models.
139
140   RAVEL Benchmark Huang et al. (2024) introduce RAVEL, a benchmark for evaluating activa-
141
      tion disentanglement methods, including SAEs, in LLMs. RAVEL assesses the ability to isolate
      distinct features in polysemantic neurons through interchange interventions. RAVEL’s Disentangle
142
      score measures whether a feature influences a target attribute without altering unrelated attributes,
143
      providing a comprehensive evaluation of feature disentanglement. A limitation of RAVEL is that its
144   intervention sites are limited to the residual stream above the last entity token, potentially missing
145   distributed representations across multiple tokens or layers.

150

151

152   accuracy. Therefore, a ”realistic task” for a SAE is a task that is completed by the LLM that the
153   SAE has been trained on.

249   4    M ETHODOLOGY
250
251   In this section, we describe our approach to scaling supervised feature dictionary evaluations.
252
253   Discovering Cross-Sections The cross-sections of a circuit are the locations in the computational
254   graph where a task-relevant feature is active. Therefore, supervised feature dictionaries are obtained
255
      at these locations to capture the approximate ground truth features. Cross-sections are also critical
      for conducting the actual evaluation, as manipulating the activations at these points is expected to
256
      have the most significant downstream effect on task performance, allowing us to assess the impact
257
      of task-relevant features on the overall functionality of the LLM. As Makelov et al. (2024) derives
258   the cross-sections for the IOI task based on prior work of Wang et al. (2023), this does not work in
259   the general case.
260
261
      We propose to use attribution-based circuit discovery methods, to find the attribute cross-sections,
      the cross-sections for each attribute of the task, using the following procedure: Consider an at-
262
      tribute ai . To find the relevant cross-sections for the attribute, we sample pairs of inputs for the task
263
      (xclean , xcorr ) where each xcorr differs in the value of the attribute ai compared to xcorr . Then we per-
264   form a forward pass and a backward pass with respect to the metric L (for example logit difference)
265   for each xclean and xcorr and cache the activations and gradients of all components in the computa-
266   tional graph. Then we can obtain an approximation of the patching effect with equation 10 for each
267   computational edge in the model and each input pair. We average the score of each edge over all
268   input pairs, to get a robust approximation for the cross-sections across many examples of the task.
269   The average score gives us an approximation for the effect on the logit difference, when patching in
      an activation of an example that differs only in attribute ai . Thus, a large score for an edge indicates


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      that the feature uai =v is active in the activation of the upstream component and processed by the
271   downstream component.
272
273   We obtain a group of cross-sections for each attribute in {ai }i∈I by taking the top n edges with
274
      the largest average attribution score. Then, we separate the cross-sections with a positive attribution
      score, and those with a negative attribution score. This is because we want to evaluate SAEs and the
275
      supervised feature dictionary based on their downstream effect on the logits, thus we want cross-
276
      section groups with a similar effect.
277
278   Derive Supervised Feature Dictionaries After we found the attribute cross-sections for which we
279   expect our desired features to be linearly represented in the activations of the upstream components,
280   we obtain the supervised feature dictionaries.
281
282   Projection-Based Reconstruction with Residual Stream SAEs To evaluate SAEs in the context
283   of a specific task, we need to apply them to the task’s cross-sections to find the task-specific features.
284   Previous work by Makelov et al. (2024) directly used the relevant key, query, and value vectors of
285   attention heads in the IOI circuit as cross-sections. Therefore, for the evaluation they required a
286   trained SAE for each key, query, and value subspace of each attention head that they use as a cross-
287
      section. Training these SAEs requires extensive training and challenging hyperparameter tuning. In
      a first step, to address this training overhead, we only apply SAEs and supervised feature dictionaries
288
      to the upstream components of our cross-sections, typically the attention head output. However, this
289
      still requires a trained SAE for each attention head that is part of a cross-section. Therefore, we
290   propose a reconstruction method based solely on residual stream SAEs, allowing evaluations with
291   only a handful of residual stream SAEs.

      Based on our procedure of discovering cross-sections, we make the assumption that the sublayer
344   activations of the upstream components of the cross-sections contain linear representations of task-
345   relevant features uai =v that have a significant effect on the logits. Thus, a good residual stream
346   SAE for the task has to reconstruct this feature. Our method aims to find these features through the
347   alignment filter process and provide a good reconstruction of the sublayer activation with respect to
348   the task if the SAE is suitable for the task. With this approach we are able to perform edits on the
349   task circuit with the same precision as previous methods, while significantly reducing the training
350   overhead.
351
352   Evaluations Based on the cross-sections, supervised feature dictionaries and projection-based
353   SAE reconstruction, one can perform downstream tests on the SAEs and supervised feature dictio-
354
      naries. In the scope of this work we aim to validate that our supervised feature dictionaries capture
      high quality approximations of ground truth features. Therefore, we reimplement Test 1 and Test
355
      2 from Makelov et al. (2024) to evaluate our supervised feature dictionaries against several open-
356
      source SAEs across various models. As supervised feature dictionaries pass test 3 (interpretability
357   test) tautologically, we do not reimplement the test in the scope of this work, but an evaluation based
358   on this test is equally possible with our framework. For a detailed description of Test 1 and Test 2
359   refer to Appendix A.2.
360
      Makelov et al. (2024) perform targeted edits on specific components of the IOI circuit for task 2,
361
      utilizing prior knowledge based on findings from Wang et al. (2023). In contrast, our framework
362
      avoids relying on prior knowledge. Instead, we leverage the attribute cross-sections to guide our
363   editing choices. Each cross-section group is derived by computing attribution scores for a specific
364   attribute ai . Consequently, we focus on editing only the features associated with attribute ai when
365   evaluating that particular cross-section group in Test 2. By making edits targeted at ai , we expect the
366   most significant downstream impact on the model’s predictions for the corresponding cross-section
367   group, providing a strong indicator of how effectively our supervised feature dictionaries can be
368   applied to modify the features of ai .

375   induction task across multiple feature distributions as a running example and apply our evaluation
376   framework to it. We explain the setup of both tasks in detail in Appendix A.5. For the evaluations
377   we use several open-source SAEs from the “sae lens” library (Joseph Bloom, 2024). For Pythia

391   Discover Cross-Sections For the cross-section discovery we have to decide which model com-
392   ponents, component locations and how many patching examples to use for the calculation of the
393   average attribution score. For the metric we choose logit difference between the io and subject
394   token for the IOI task, and the ind2 and ind1 token for the induction task.
395   We choose to focus our evaluation on the attention heads and obtain attribution scores for edges
396   between attention heads (attention head output as upstream node and the q, k and v input to attention
397   heads as downstream nodes). We only consider locations in the model that are at the last attribute
398   token position or later. This is because attributes like order can only be fully linearly represented
399   after the last attribute token. Lastly, we choose to average the attribution scores across 250 task
400   prompts that are sampled from the task datasets. For each task prompt we also sample one corrupted
401
      example differing in one attribute. Therefore, we have a total of 250 task prompt pairs to obtain the
      attribute cross-sections from.
402
403
      Filter Cross-Sections Next, we need to decide which cross-section to consider for the evaluation
404
      based on the obtained attribution scores. As the attribution scores are only approximations, we
405   perform the following validation step to select the attribute cross-sections for the evaluation:
406
407         1. Select the top n cross-sections for each cross-section group
408         2. Corrupt increasing subsets of the cross-section groups by patching the mean ablation of the
409            upstream node in the input of the downstream node (edge mean ablation) and measure the
410            change in the logit difference
411         3. For each cross-section group select the subset with the largest change in logit difference
412            and drop cross-section groups that caused a change in logit difference below τ
413
414   In our experiments we choose 300 cross-sections for GPT-2 Small and Pythia 70M, and top 1600
415   cross-sections for Gemma-2-2B. We dropped cross-section groups whose change in logit difference
416   was below 60% of the mean change across all cross-section groups. Refer to Appendix A.3 to find
417   the results of this selection procedure for each model and task.
418
419   Supervised Dictionaries We train each supervised feature dictionaries on 10000 task prompts. We
420
      test how accurate our models completes the tasks across the sampled training data on 250 examples.
      For all experiments our models achieved at least 95% accuracy.
421
422
      Evaluations We perform the evaluations with Test 1 and Test 2 across 250 task examples, sampled
423
      from the test dataset of each task. For Test 2 we perform 0, 4, 8 and 16 edits. We also test the
424   accuracy of our models in predicting the correct token for the test set and also achieve over 95%
425   accuracy for all models and tasks.

528   This paper introduces the SAGE framework, which enables to scale SAE ground truth evaluations to
529   new models and task. To this end we have proposed a fully automated approach, while significantly
530   reducing the training overhead of previous methods, using our novel projection-based reconstruc-
531
      tion technique. We have demonstrated the scalability of SAGE by evaluating several SAEs on Pythia
      70M, GPT-2 Small, and Gemma-2-2B with a new task using different feature distributions. Future
532
      work includes expanding task diversity by incorporating tasks with more high-level features and
533
      covering larger feature distributions. The current approach is constrained by the effectiveness of at-
534   tribution patching in identifying attribute cross-sections. Thus, future research on improved discov-
535   ery methods would further enhance our technique. Another potential extension is the incorporation
536   of additional activation disentanglement methods beyond SAEs, such as PCA or Distributed Align-
537   ment Search, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation. In summary, SAGE enables large-scale
538   SAE evaluations on realistic tasks, marking a significant advancement toward generalizable SAE
539   assessments in interpretability research.

762   activations, as an in-distribution baseline of a reconstruction that does not capture any task-relevant

782   input prompts. Let task prompts ps and pt differ in exactly one attribute. Let a(ps ) and a(pt ) repre-
783   sent the activations of cross-sections of the task for each task prompt. Let â(ps ) and â(pt ) represent
784   the respective reconstructions of the activations using the feature dictionary (SAE or supervised).
785   We aim to determine whether modifying each activation a(ps ) using the features used for the recon-
786   structions â(ps ) and â(pt ) across all relevant cross-sections can flip the prediction of the model to
787
      what it would predict under the activations a(ps ). Formally, the problem is expressed as:
788

                          R⊆S,A⊆T,|R∪A|≤k

      where S and T are the sets of active features ui in the reconstructions â(ps ) and â(pt ) respectively,
793
      and αi , βi > 0 are their coefficients. The problem of finding the optimal sparse edit for the SAE
794   features is NP-complete, as it can be reduced to the Subset Sum problem (Makelov et al., 2024).

796   The ground truth edit is replacing a(ps ) with a(pt ). Editing with the supervised feature dictionary
797
      works by subtracting the feature uaj =sv from a(ps ) and adding uaj =st , where aj is the attribute we
      wish to edit and sv is the value it takes in ps and st is the value for the attribute in pt .

800   (2024) use a greedy algorithm to approximate the optimization problem by exchanging a fixed num-
