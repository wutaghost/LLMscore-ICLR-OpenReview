009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated Learning (FL) is a promising technology that enables edge devices/clients
012            to collaboratively and iteratively train a machine learning model under the coordi-
013            nation of a central server. The most common approach to FL is first-order methods,
014            where clients send their local gradients to the server in each iteration. However,
015            these methods often suffer from slow convergence rates. As a remedy, second-
016            order methods, such as quasi-Newton, can be employed in FL to accelerate its
017            convergence. Unfortunately, similarly to the first-order FL methods, the application
018
               of second-order methods in FL can lead to unfair models, achieving high average
               accuracy while performing poorly on certain clients’ local datasets. To tackle this
019
               issue, in this paper we introduce a novel second-order FL framework, dubbed
020
               distributed quasi-Newton federated learning (DQN-Fed). This approach seeks to
021            ensure fairness while leveraging the fast convergence properties of quasi-Newton
022            methods in the FL context. Specifically, DQN-Fed helps the server update the
023            global model in such a way that (i) all local loss functions decrease to promote
024            fairness, and (ii) the rate of change in local loss functions aligns with that of the
025            quasi-Newton method. We prove the convergence of DQN-Fed and demonstrate its
026            linear-quadratic convergence rate. Moreover, we validate the efficacy of DQN-Fed
027            across a range of federated datasets, showing that it surpasses state-of-the-art fair FL
028            methods in fairness, average accuracy and convergence speed. The Code for paper
029
               is publicly available at https://github.com/ICMLDQNFed/ICMLDQN.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Traditionally, machine learning (ML) models are trained centrally, with data stored in a central server.
034
      However, in modern applications, devices often resist sharing private data remotely. To address this,
035
      federated learning (FL) was introduced by McMahan et al. (2017), where each device trains locally
036   with a central server. In FL, devices share only local updates, maintaining data privacy. FedAvg,
037   proposed by McMahan et al. (2017), is a popular first-order FL method. It combines local stochastic
038   gradient descent (SGD) on each client with iterative model averaging. The server sends the global
039   model to selected clients Eichner et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2021a), which perform local SGD on
040   their training data. Local gradients are sent back to the server, which calculates their (weighted)
041   average to update the global model iteratively.
042   Nevertheless, first-order FL methods tend to exhibit slow convergence, particularly in terms of the
043   number of iterations or communication rounds required (Krouka et al., 2022). More precisely, the
044   convergence rate of first-order FL algorithms is sublinear, i.e., the required number of communication
045   rounds Tϵ to achieve ϵ-accurate solution is Tϵ = O( 1ϵ ). Additionally, their convergence speed is
046   highly influenced by the condition number, which is dependent on several factors, including: (i) the
047   architecture of the model being trained, (ii) the choice of loss function, and (iii) the distribution of the
048   training data (Elgabli et al., 2022).
049   To overcome this limitation, second-order methods can be applied in FL to significantly boost
050   convergence speed (Safaryan et al., 2022; Elgabli et al., 2022). By estimating the local curvature
051   of the loss landscape, these methods provide more adaptive and efficient update directions, leading
052   to faster and more reliable convergence (Battiti, 1992). Specifically, in second-order FL methods,
053   the clients compute the Newton direction for their respective local loss functions and send these
      directions to the server. The server then averages the Newton directions from all clients and updates


                                                           1

054
      the global model in the direction of this average (Ghosh et al., 2020; Zhang & Lin, 2015). Moreover,
055   since Newton’s methods require calculating the inverse of the Hessian matrix at each iteration—a
056   computationally expensive operation—the inverse is typically approximated using iterative techniques,
057   leading to quasi-Newton methods (Wang et al., 2018).
058
      While Newton-type methods accelerate the convergence of FL algorithms, they do not guarantee
059
      that the averaged Newton direction computed by the server is a descent direction for all clients—a
060
      limitation also present in first-order FL methods (Hu et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024).
061   In other words, upon updating the global model toward this averaged direction, the loss function for
062   some client my not decrease, potentially leading to poor performance on their private datasets. As a
063   result, the learned model might exhibit unfairness, with high average accuracy but poor performance
064   for clients whose data distributions differ from the majority1 . Thus, naively applying Newton methods
065   in FL can lead to the training of an unfair model (see Section 5 for results).
066
      To tackle the issue mentioned above, this paper presents a novel second-order FL framework, dubbed
067   distributed quasi-Newton federated learning (DQN-Fed). This approach aims to ensure fairness while
068   leveraging the fast convergence properties of quasi-Newton methods in the FL setting. In particular,
069   DQN-Fed is designed to assist the server in updating the global model such that (i) all local loss
070   functions decrease resulting in training a fair model, and (ii) the rate of change in local loss functions
071   aligns with the rate of change in the quasi-Newton method. To achieve this, based on the received
072   local quasi-Newton directions and the local gradients, the server identifies an updating direction that
073   satisfies both of the aforementioned conditions. This will in turn yield a fair FL algorithm, as the
074   global updating direction is descent for all the clients. Moreover, the convergence of DQN-Fed is
075
      fast, as the rate of change in the local loss functions follow quasi-Newton methods.
076   In summary, the contributions of the paper are as follows:
077
      • We introduce distributed quasi-Newton federated learning (DQN-Fed), a method designed to assist
078
      the server in updating the global model to achieve both fairness and fast convergence in FL.
079
080   • We present a closed-form solution for calculating the global updating direction, distinguishing
081   our approach from many existing fair FL methods that depend on iterative or generic quadratic
082
      programming techniques.
083   • Leveraging common assumptions in FL literature, we establish the convergence proof for DQN-Fed
084   algorithm across various FL setups. In addition, we prove the convergence rate of the proposed
085   method, and show that DQN-Fed exhibits a linear-quadratic convergence rate. Specifically,
                                                                                                     the

      convergence is either quadratic, with Tϵ = O log log 1ϵ , or linear, with Tϵ = O log(1 λ ) log 1ϵ ,

089   • Through comprehensive experiments conducted on seven different datasets (six vision datasets and

      and converges faster compared to the state-of-the-art fair alternatives.

      3.2.2       N EWTON - TYPE METHODS
163
164   First-order FL methods face challenges with slow convergence, measured in terms of the number of
165   iterations or communication rounds. Additionally, their convergence speed is intricately linked to
166   the condition number, influenced by factors such as the model’s structure, choice of loss function,
167   and distribution of training data. In contrast, second-order methods exhibit significantly faster
168   performance due to their additional computational effort in estimating the local curvature of the loss
169
      landscape. This, in turn, yields faster and more adaptive update directions. Despite requiring more
      computations per communication round, second-order methods achieve fewer communication rounds.
170
      In the context of FL, where communication often poses a bottleneck rather than computation, the
171
      appeal of second-order methods has grown. Notably, the Newton’s direction is obtained as

180   4.1       M OTIVATION
181
      We begin with finding out how much the local loss function fk (·), k ∈ [K], changes when the server
182
      updates the global model as θ t+1 = θ t − η t dt at round t. In other words, we want to determine the
183
      rate of change ∆fk (θ t ) ≜ fk (θ t+1 ) − fk (θ t ) for the local loss functions. To do this, by writing the
184   first-order Taylor expansion for the local loss function fk (·), we obtain:
185
                                   fk (θ t+1 ) = fk (θ t − η t dt ) ≈ fk (θ t ) − η t gtk · dt                 (4)
186
187                                ⇔     ∆fk (θ t ) ≈ −η t gtk · dt .                                                  (5)
188   As per Equation (5), fk (·) changes by amount of −η t gtk · dt when the server updates the global
189   model. Hence, if gtk · d ≥ 0, the global updating direction is descent for client k, and ∆fk (θ t ) ≤ 0.
190
      Nevertheless, updating toward a descent direction does not guarantee any meaningful convergence.
191
      Indeed, what can guarantee the convergence of GD-like algorithms is the rate of change in the loss
192   function in each iteration3 . This is in fact what makes the second-order methods to converge faster as
193   the rate of change in the loss functions is automatically determined by the Hessian matrix.
194
195
      This motivates us to see how the server can update the global model such that the rate of change in
      the local loss functions is the same as that when local clients update their local loss function using
196
      second-order methods.
197
198   Specifically, let dtk denote the rate of change in local loss function fk when it updates its local model
199
      using Newton method; then, we have

200                                 dtk = gtk · dN = gtk · (Hkt )−1 gtk = (gtk )T (Hkt )−1 gtk .                       (6)
201
      Our goal is to assist the server in updating the global model such that, after the update, the rate of
202   change for client k becomes dtk . Achieving this is not a straightforward task. In the following section,
203   we derive a closed-form solution to meet this criterion.

521   5.5    A NALYSIS OF R ESULTS
522
      Based on the insights gleaned from Tables 1 to 4, several noteworthy observations emerge:
523
524   (i) Naive second-order FL methods, namely FedNL and FedNew, tend to train unfair models, despite
525   achieving high average accuracy across clients.
526   (ii) Compared to the benchmark models, DQN-Fed consistently trains models that demonstrate
527   significantly higher levels of fairness across clients.
528
529
      (iii) The average accuracy of the model learned by DQN-Fed is higher compared to both first-order
      and second-order FL methods.

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper introduced distributed quasi-Newton federated learning (DQN-Fed), a novel approach
535   designed to ensure fairness while harnessing the fast convergence properties of quasi-Newton methods
536   in FL setting. DQN-Fed aids the server in updating the global model by ensuring (i) all local loss
537   functions decrease, promoting fairness; and (ii) the rate of change in local loss functions matches
538   that of the quasi-Newton method. We prove the convergence of DQN-Fed and establish its linear-
539   quadratic convergence rate. Furthermore, we validate DQN-Fed’s effectiveness across various
      federated datasets, demonstrating its superiority over state-of-the-art fair FL methods.

       K.1    U NDERSTANDING L ABEL N OISE IN FL
1330   Label noise in FL refers to inaccuracies or errors present in the ground truth labels associated with
1331   the data used for training FL models. These inaccuracies manifest when the labels assigned to data
1332   points are incorrect or noisy due to various reasons. Label noise can originate at different stages
1333   of the FL process, including data collection, annotation, or transmission phases. Addressing label
1334   noise is crucial as it can substantially impact the performance and reliability of FL models, making it
1335   essential to develop robust strategies to mitigate its effects.
1336   Addressing label noise in FL presents unique challenges due to its reliance on decentralized data
1337   sources, where participants may have limited control over label quality in remote environments.
1338   Mitigating label noise in FL requires the development of robust models and FL algorithms capable of
1339   adapting to inaccuracies in the labels. This adaptation is essential for maintaining model performance
1340   and reliability in real-world FL scenarios where label noise is prevalent.
1341
1342   K.2    ROBUSTNESS OF FAIR FL A LGORITHMS TO L ABEL N OISE
1343
1344   The core objective of fair FL algorithms, such as DQN-Fed, is to uphold fairness among clients while
1345   preserving average accuracy across them. However, it’s important to note that these algorithms are
1346
       not inherently robust against label noise, which refers to instances where data points are mislabeled.
1347   However, by integrating DQN-Fed with label-noise resistant methods from existing literature, we
1348   can develop a FL approach that not only ensures fairness among clients but also exhibits robustness
1349   against label noise. Specifically, among the label-noise resistant FL algorithms available in the
       literature, we choose FedCorr (Xu et al., 2022) to be integrated with DQN-Fed. This integration


                                                           25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       offers a promising avenue for enhancing the performance and resilience of FL models in real-world
1351   scenarios affected by label noise.
1352
1353   FedCorr introduces a dimensionality-based filter to identify noisy clients, achieved through the
1354
       measurement of local intrinsic dimensionality (LID) within local model prediction subspaces. They
       illustrate the feasibility of distinguishing between clean and noisy datasets by monitoring the behavior
1355
       of LID scores throughout the training process. For further insights into FedCorr, we defer interested
1356
       readers to the original paper for a comprehensive discussion.
1357
1358   Following a methodology similar to FedCorr, we utilize a real-world noisy dataset known as Cloth-
1359   ing1M. This dataset comprises 1 million clothing images across 14 classes and is characterized
1360
       by noisy labels, as it is sourced from various online shopping websites, incorporating numerous
       mislabeled samples.
1361
1362   For our experiments with Clothing1M, we adopt the identical settings as utilized by FedCorr, which
1363   are available in their GitHub repository (https://github.com/Xu-Jingyi/FedCorr). Specifically, we
1364   employ local SGD with a momentum of 0.5, utilizing a batch size of 16 and conducting five local
1365   epochs. Additionally, we set the hyper-parameter T1 = 2 in accordance with their algorithm.

1369   fair client accuracy, aligning with expectations.
1370   Conversely, when DQN-Fed is combined with FedCorr, there is a noticeable enhancement in average
1371   accuracy while still preserving satisfactory fairness among clients. This integration showcases the
1372   potential of leveraging both methodologies to achieve improved performance and fairness in FL
1373   scenarios affected by label noise.

1386
1387   L.1   S OURCES OF UNFAIRNESS IN FEDERATED LEARNING
1388
1389   Unfairness in FL can arise from various sources and is a concern that needs to be addressed in FL
1390   systems. Here are some of the key reasons for unfairness in FL:
1391   1. Non-Representative Data Distribution: Unfairness can occur when the distribution of data across
1392   participating devices or clients is non-representative of the overall population. Some devices may
1393   have more or less relevant data, leading to biased model updates.
1394
       2. Data Bias: If the data collected or used by different clients is inherently biased due to the
1395
       data collection process, it can lead to unfairness. For example, if certain demographic groups are
1396
       underrepresented in the training data of some clients, the federated model may not perform well for
1397   those groups.
1398
1399   3. Heterogeneous Data Sources: Federated learning often involves data from a diverse set of sources,
1400
       including different device types, locations, or user demographics. Variability in data sources can
       introduce unfairness as the models may not generalize equally well across all sources.
1401
1402   4. Varying Data Quality: Data quality can vary among clients, leading to unfairness. Some clients
1403   may have noisy or less reliable data, while others may have high-quality data, affecting the model’s
       performance.


                                                            26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
       5. Data Sampling: The way data is sampled and used for local updates can introduce unfairness. If
1405   some clients have imbalanced or non-representative data sampling strategies, it can lead to biased
1406   model updates.
1407
1408
       6. Aggregation Bias: The learned model may exhibit a bias towards devices with larger amounts of
       data or, if devices are weighted equally, it may favor more commonly occurring devices.
1409
1410
1411
       L.2   FAIRNESS IN CONVENTIONAL ML V S . FL
1412
       The concept of fairness is often used to address social biases or performance disparities among
1413   different individuals or groups in the machine learning (ML) literature (Barocas et al., 2017). However,
1414   in the context of FL, the notion of fairness differs slightly from traditional ML. In FL, fairness
1415   primarily pertains to the consistency of performance across various clients. In fact, the difference in
1416   the notion of fairness between traditional ML and FL arises from the distinct contexts and challenges
1417   of these two settings:
1418
       1. Centralized vs. decentralized data distribution:
1419
1420
              • In traditional ML, data is typically centralized, and fairness is often defined in terms of
1421            mitigating biases or disparities within a single, homogeneous dataset. Fairness is evaluated
1422            based on how the model treats different individuals or groups within that dataset.
1423
1424          • In FL, data is distributed across multiple decentralized clients or devices. Each client may
1425            have its own unique data distribution, and fairness considerations extend to addressing
1426
                disparities across these clients, ensuring that the federated model provides uniform and
                equitable performance for all clients.
1427

1445
1446   A seminal method in this domain is Agnostic Federated Learning (FedLF) Mohri et al. (2019).
1447   FedLF optimizes the global model for the worst-case realization of the weighted combination of
1448   user distributions. Their approach involves solving a saddle-point optimization problem, and they
1449
       employ a fast stochastic optimization algorithm for this purpose. However, FedLF exhibits strong
       performance only for a limited number of clients. In addition, Du et al. (2021) adopted the framework
1450
       of FedLF and introduced the AgnosticFair algorithm. They linearly parameterized model weights
1451
       using kernel functions and demonstrated that FedLF can be considered as a specific instance of
1452   AgnosticFair. To address the challenges in FedLF, the q-fair Federated Learning (q-FFL) method was
1453   introduced by Li et al. (2019a). q-FFL aims to achieve a more uniform test accuracy across users,
1454   drawing inspiration from fair resource allocation methods employed in wireless communication
1455   networks Huaizhou et al. (2013). Following this, Li et al. (2020) introduced TERM, a tilted empirical
1456   risk minimization algorithm designed to address outliers and class imbalance in statistical estimation
1457   procedures. In numerous FL applications, TERM has shown superior performance compared to
       q-FFL. Adopting a similar concept, Huang et al. (2020b) introduced a method that adjusts device

1459   (2021) minimizes the loss of the worst-performing client, effectively presenting a variant of FedLF.
1460   Subsequently, Li et al. (2021) introduced Ditto, a multitask personalized FL algorithm. By optimizing
1461   a global objective function, Ditto enables local devices to perform additional steps of SGD, within
