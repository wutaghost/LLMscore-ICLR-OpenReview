009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks (GNNs) using local message passing were recently shown
012            to inherit the intrinsic limitations of local algorithms in solving combinatorial
013            graph optimization problems such as finding shortest distances (Loukas, 2020). To
014            address this issue, Awasthi et al. (2022) proposed architectures based on Bourgain‚Äôs
015            (1985) seminal work on Hilbert space embeddings. These architectures enhance
016            local message passing in GNNs with a single global computation, yielding a
017            local-global algorithm. This paper focuses on the average-case analysis of more
018            general local-global algorithms for finding shortest distances (of which GNN+
019
               is a particular case). Our primary contribution is a theoretical analysis of these
               algorithms on ErdoŐčs-R√©nyi (ER) random graphs. We prove that, on random graphs,
020
               these algorithms have lower distortion of shortest distances for most pairs of nodes
021
               w.h.p. while requiring a lower embedding dimension. Inspired by Awasthi et al.
022            (2022), and to automate local computations and improve computational efficiency
023            in practical scenarios, we further propose a modification to these algorithms that
024            incorporates GNNs in the local computation phase. Empirical results on ER
025            graphs and benchmark graph datasets demonstrate the enhanced performance of
026            the GNN-augmented algorithm over the traditional approach.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Finding shortest paths on networks is an important combinatorial optimization problem arising in
032   many practical applications, such as transportation networks Fu et al. (2006) and integrated circuit
033
      design Cong et al. (1998). Unlike other optimization problems on graphs, exact solutions for shortest
      paths can be found using classical algorithms such as Dijkstra‚Äôs algorithm in polynomial time.
034
      Moreover, advancements in indexing techniques have made exact shortest-path distance queries
035
      highly efficient, with solutions capable of handling large-scale graphs and providing microsecond-
036   level query times in certain settings (Akiba et al., 2013; Hayashi et al., 2016; Ouyang et al., 2018;
037   Farhan et al., 2018).
038
039
      However, not all scenarios allow for such efficient indexing. For example, dynamic networks
      with frequently updated edge weights or applications requiring real-time computation on resource-
040
      constrained devices may not benefit from precomputed indexes. In such cases, approximate methods
041
      are particularly valuable due to their adaptability and lower computational overhead. This has
042   motivated the exploration of machine learning approaches to shortest path finding, particularly those
043   employing graph neural networks (GNNs).
044
045
      Despite their promise in combinatorial optimization (Lemos et al., 2019; Cappart et al., 2023; Li et al.,
      2018; 2023; VelicŐĆkovicŐĀ et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023), GNN-based approaches face significant
046
      challenges in the shortest path problem. Local message-passing algorithms like GNNs are constrained
047
      by impossibility results (Loukas, 2020; Sarma et al., 2012), requiring prohibitively large embedding
048   dimensions or numbers of convolutions to achieve even a constant-factor approximation of distances
049   in the worst-case. A promising direction to address these limitations is the combination of local
050   message-passing and global methods, which can provide a better tradeoff between efficiency and
051   accuracy. For example, Awasthi et al. (2022) propose GNN+, a two-part architecture where GNNs
052   compute local path distances, and a global fully connected layer combines their outputs (Awasthi
053   et al., 2022). Fundamentally, such approaches are inspired by Bourgain‚Äôs seminal result on metric
      space embeddings into Hilbert spaces (Bourgain, 1985), which quantifies the error incurred when

055   theorem also prescribes minimum sketch sizes for the class of local-global algorithms that includes

      The use of GNNs in the local step is motivated by their demonstrated alignment with dynamic
      programming (DP). DP underlies many reasoning tasks, including shortest paths which can be solved
092
      using the Bellman-Ford algorithm. Recent works have shown that GNNs align well with DP, meaning
093
      their computation structures naturally reflect the algorithmic processes of tasks like shortest path
094   computation, which improves learning efficiency and generalization (Xu et al., 2019b, Theorem 3.6).
095   In (Dudzik and VelicŐĆkovicŐĀ, 2022), this alignment has been theoretically quantified, suggesting that
096   GNN architectures are particularly well-suited for reasoning tasks where DP plays a central role.
097

098

099

100   social networks. This underscores the importance of analyzing graph algorithms in the context of
101   random graphs to inform their practical applications.

113   2     S HORTEST PATH P ROBLEMS AND L OCAL -G LOBAL A LGORITHMS
114
115   Given graph G = (V, E) and a pair of nodes u, v ‚ąą V , the shortest path problem consists of finding
116   the path with the smallest number of edges between u and v, and the number of edges in this path, or
117
      the shortest path distance between u and v, denoted d(u, v). This is one of the most fundamental
      combinatorial optimization problems on graphs.
118
119   The classical algorithm for finding graph shortest paths is Dijkstra‚Äôs algorithm. Starting from a source
120   node u, Dijkstra‚Äôs algorithm returns the exact shortest paths between u and every other node v ‚ąą V
121   along with the corresponding distances d(u, v) via breadth-first search (BFS). It proceeds as follows:
122
            (0) Initialize d(u, v) = ‚ąě for all v ‚ąą V . Set s = u and ‚ąÜ = 1.
123
124
            (1) From s, visit s‚Äôs neighbors v ‚ąą N (s) and assign them distance d(u, v) = min(d(u, v), ‚ąÜ).
125         (2) Mark s as visited and update ‚ąÜ = ‚ąÜ + 1.
126         (3) Select the unvisited node with smallest distance to u, say t, and set s = t.
127         (4) Repeat (1)‚Äď(3) until convergence.
128
129   Using naive data structures to store nodes‚Äô visited statuses and current distances, the complexity of
130   Dijsktra‚Äôs algorithm is O(n2 ). This can be improved to O(m + n log n) by using more efficient data
131   structures like heaps Schrijver (2012), but is still prohibitive for large graphs.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      We introduce an average-case analysis of algorithms combining local and global computations for
489   solving shortest distance problems on ER graphs, complementing Bourgain‚Äôs worst-case result. In
490   particular, our theoretical analysis demonstrates that on ER graphs these algorithms can achieve
491   a (1 ‚ąí őĶ)-factor lower bound and a (1 + ŌĶ)-factor upper bound of shortest distances with high
492   probability. Additionally, we propose a modification to Bourgain‚Äôs algorithm, which incorporates
493   GNNs in the local computation phase to further enhance practical performance. Empirical results on
494   both ER graphs and benchmark datasets demonstrate the superior performance of the GNN-augmented
495   approach.
496   Limitations and future work. Our analysis focuses on ER random graphs, which provided a
497   simplified framework to develop theoretical tools and insights for local-global algorithms. These
498   methods are broadly applicable to graphs with local expansion properties, such as inhomogeneous
499   random graphs, and extending our analysis to such models is a key direction for future work. However,
500   for other important graph classes in shortest path problems, such as planar graphs, our techniques are
501   unlikely to apply. Addressing these cases will require the development of new methods, which is an
502   interesting direction for future work.

      F    E XPERIMENT D ETAILS
891
892   In our experiments, we train GNNs to learn to compute the shortest path distances from every seed to
893
      every node in sparse, undirected, and unweighted connected random graphs. Using the trained GNNs,
      we generate node embeddings as in local step of Algorithm 1. Finally, we evaluate the performance
894
      of the embeddings in shortest path approximations and test the model‚Äôs transferability.
895
896   To construct the GNNs, we consider four standard GNN architectures (GCN, GraphSage, GAT, and
897   GIN) with sum aggregation, dropout and ReLU between the convolutions, and ReLU activation
898   function. For each GNN architecture, we experiment with nine models that differ   ‚ąö in widths and
899
                      hidden layers. The first and the last GNN layers both consist of ‚Ćä n‚Ćč nodes, which
      depths of their ‚ąö
      correspond to ‚Ćä n‚Ćč seeds inputted into the GNNs. The widths and depths of the hidden layers are as
900
      follows:
901
902          ‚ÄĘ Depth-6 GNNs: 128-64-32-16, 64-32-16-8, 32-16-8-4
903
             ‚ÄĘ Depth-5 GNNs: 128-64-32, 64-32-16, 32-16-8
904
905          ‚ÄĘ Depth-4 GNNs: 128-64, 64-32, 32-16
906
907   We train our GNNs on ER graphs generated by ERn (őĽ/n). To ensure that the graph are sparse
908
      and each has a giant component with high probability, it is necessary to have 1 < őĽ ‚Č™ n. We thus
      evaluate őĽ ‚ąą {3, 4, 5, 6} with n ‚ąą {25, 50, 100, 200, 400, 800, 1600, 3200}. We treat each graph as
909
      a batch of nodes and have train-validation-test size of 200-50-50 batches. The training occurs in
910
      1000 epochs with early stopping patience of 100 epochs, mean squared error (MSE) loss, Adam
911   optimizer with a learning rate of 0.01 and weight decay of 0.0001, and a cyclic-cosine learning rate
912   scheduler with cyclical learning rate between 0.001 and 0.1 for 10 iterations in the increasing half in
913   combination with the default cosine annealing learning rate for a maximum of 20 iterations.
