009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-Armed Bandit (MAB) formulations are commonly used to model the prob-
012            lem of Optimal Dose-Finding. However, in many practical applications, it is nec-
013            essary to receive data about the patientвҖҷs current state and then administer a drug
014            dosage adapted to that state. To overcome this issue, we adopt a linear contex-
015            tual bandit formulation with stage-wise constraints. At each round, the learner
016            selects a dosage and receives both a reward signal and a cost signal. The learnerвҖҷs
017            goal is to maximize the drugвҖҷs efficacyвҖ”captured as the expected cumulative re-
018
               wardвҖ”while ensuring that the toxicity, reflected by the cost signal, remains below
               a known threshold. Satisfying the cost signal constraint only in expectation can
019
               be dangerous, as it may lead to over-dosage complications in certain cases. To
020
               address this issue, we introduce a novel model that controls the realization of
021            the cost signal with high probability, in contrast to previous works where control
022            was only applied to the expected cost signal. Our algorithm follows the UCB ap-
023            proach, for which we establish a regret bound over T rounds and run numerical
024            experiments. We further generalize our results to non-linear functions and pro-
025            vide a regret bound in terms of the eluder dimension, a measure of function class
026            complexity.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Ensuring patient safety while maximizing therapeutic efficacy is a critical challenge in medical
032   treatments, especially in optimal drug dosing where overdosing can have serious consequences. In
033
      these scenarios, the objective is to find an appropriate treatment regime, while ensuring patients
      are not exposed to dangerous drug dosages. Online learning methods, widely employed in various
034
      decision-making systems, offer promising solutions to this challenge by adapting decisions based
035
      on observed outcomes (Matsuura et al. (2022),Lee et al. (2020)).
036
037   This framework is extensively applied in Optimal Dose-Finding Huckvale et al. (2023); Shen et al.
038   (2020); Chen & Khademi (2024), where adaptive methods aim to determine the optimal drug dosage
039
      that maximizes therapeutic efficacy while safeguarding patients from overdosing. However, most
      existing approaches are setting a binary variable specifying the treatmentвҖҷs outcome , which fails
040
      to capture the nuances of these signals. Moreover, in order to guarantee drug dosages do not cause
041
      dangerous side effects, current studies have proposed algorithms that control the expected toxicity
042   per time step (Pacchiano et al. (2024),Amani et al. (2019),Amani et al. (2021)). Unfortunately,
043   satisfying this solution concept may not prevent the harmfulness of the drug dosages administered
044   to many individual patients. Consequently, there remains a risk of overdosing, posing serious health
045   risks to patients. Additionally, not all approaches incorporate contextual information, despite its
046   importance in medical treatments where dosage decisions are often based on patient-specific tests or
047   conditions.
048   In the bandit literature, constraints are typically based either on the history of previous rewards (e.g.,
049   knapsack bandits Badanidiyuru et al. (2018), fairness constraints Joseph et al. (2016)) or involve
050   simultaneous reward and cost signals, with constraints depending on the cost, as in Amani et al.
051   (2019); Pacchiano et al. (2021; 2024). The latter formulation is particularly useful for modeling
052   applications advertising, and drug dosing. Problems in this domain can be cast in the reward/cost
053   model of Pacchiano et al. (2021), the drug dosage administered generates a reward signal (efficacy)
      and a cost signal (toxicity). This is precisely the scenario encountered in Phase II clinical trials,

054
      where physicians aim to adaptive adjust dosages to maximize efficacy while ensuring toxicity re-
055   mains below a safe threshold П„ .
056
057   Previous works on contextual bandits with stage-wise constraints Pacchiano et al. (2024) propose
058
      contextual bandits algorithms that ensure the expected cost remains below a safe threshold П„ . How-
      ever, this does not prevent individual instances where the cost exceeds the threshold, which can be
059
      unacceptable in critical applications like medicine. Therefore, there is a pressing need for models
060
      that guarantee, with high probability, that the realized cost stays within safe limits in each instance.
061
062   In this paper, we address this need by developing a UCB-like algorithm, which we call High Prob-
063   ability Constrained UCB. Our algorithm is grounded in the Optimism in the Face of Uncertainty
064
      (OFU) principle Auer (2002) and does not require prior knowledge of any initial safe dosageвҖ”a sig-
      nificant advantage over existing methods. It operates effectively under both adversarial and stochas-
065
      tic contexts and relies only on the standard assumption of sub-Gaussian noise distributions. By
066
      leveraging this assumption, we formulate a constraint event that ensures, with high probability, that
067   the cost signal remains below the desired threshold in each round.
068
069   Furthermore, we note that knowing the exact noise distribution allows for even tighter constraints,
070
      ensuring the cost realization stays within safe limits with high probability. Our approach accounts
      for the randomness in constraint satisfaction due to noise and is validated with high probability
071
      based on past observations of contexts, rewards, and costs. Another approach to this problem is
072
      Distributional Reinforcement Learning, where noise distributions are characterized, and instance-
073   dependent bounds are derived Wang et al. (2023).
074
075   Through
          вҲҡ rigorous analysis, we prove that our algorithm achieves a T-round regret bound of order
076   OМғ(d T ). We also demonstrate the practical effectiveness of our approach through numerical ex-
077   periments. Additionally, we extend our results to settings with non-linear reward and cost functions
078
      by parameterizing our analysis in terms of the eluder dimension, a complexity measure for function
      classes.
079
080
      Contributions In this work, we introduce a high-probability model for dose finding in the contex-
081
      tual bandit framework, which, to the best of our knowledge, has not been explored in the literature.
082   Despite the crucial importance of controlling realized adverse effects during clinical interventions,
083   existing models and algorithms primarily focus on controlling expected effects. One of the main con-
084   tributions of this work is the introduction of a more realistic high-probability model that addresses
085   this gap. Additionally, we have developed algorithms with provable guarantees for this setting. Our
086   contributions are both conceptual and technical, offering a new perspective on dose-finding method-
087   ologies within contextual bandits.

      an initial safe dosage.

404   Ideally, we had hoped to test our algorithms on real data, such as patient data or cell cultures.

406   data. Before analyzing our procedure, we should note that in the linear model, the dot product can
407   take both positive and negative values. Therefore, we expect that the expected regret plot, in addition
408   to exhibiting sub-linear behavior, may also show decreasing behavior.
409   To generate ОёвҲ— and ВөвҲ— , we sampled them from a d-dimensional normal distribution and then normal-
410   ized them. The contexts were also generated from a multivariate normal distribution and normalized
411   accordingly. We ran our experiments with 5 and 10-dimensional vectors, using multiple values of
412   П„ and for 105 iterations. In practice, it is interesting to examine the relationship between П„ and
413   max вҲҘXвҲҘ, вҲҘОёвҲ— вҲҘ, and вҲҘВөвҲ— вҲҘ.
414
      We observed that for small values of П„ , such as 0.2, the set of feasible dosages has a very small
415   length, and the results are dominated by noise. However, for larger values of П„ , like 0.6 and 0.8,
416   the results showed that the sub-linear behavior was already visible after 104 iterations. Remarkably,
417   after 4 Г— 105 iterations, we noticed decreasing behavior, which is a sign that our estimators have
418   converged to the true ОёвҲ— and ВөвҲ— .
419
      We also plotted the number of times the constraint was violated when we satisfied the constraint
420
      in expectation. We plotted this over 104 rounds, and the behavior was linear, indicating that until

422   experiments 10 times and plotted the mean value and the standard deviation around the regret.
