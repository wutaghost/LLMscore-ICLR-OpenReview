011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Blind face restoration (BFR) is a fundamental and challenging problem in com-
014            puter vision. To faithfully restore high-quality (HQ) photos from poor-quality
015
               ones, recent research endeavors predominantly rely on facial image priors from
               the powerful pretrained text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models. However, such pri-
016
               ors often lead to the incorrect generation of non-facial features and insufficient
017
               facial details, thus rendering them less practical for real-world applications. In
018            this paper, we propose a novel framework, namely AuthFace that achieves highly
019            authentic face restoration results by exploring a face-oriented generative diffusion
020            prior. To learn such a prior, we first collect a dataset of 1.5K high-quality images,
021            with resolutions exceeding 8K, captured by professional photographers. Based
022            on the dataset, we then introduce a novel face-oriented restoration-tuning pipeline
023            that fine-tunes a pretrained T2I model. Identifying key criteria of quality-first and
024            photography-guided annotation, we involve the retouching and reviewing process
025            under the guidance of photographers for high-quality images that show rich facial
026
               features. The photography-guided annotation system fully explores the potential
               of these high-quality photographic images. In this way, the potent natural image
027
               priors from pretrained T2I diffusion models can be subtly harnessed, specifically
028
               enhancing their capability in facial detail restoration. Moreover, to minimize ar-
029            tifacts in critical facial areas, such as eyes and mouth, we propose a time-aware
030            latent facial feature loss to learn the authentic face restoration process. Extensive
031            experiments on the synthetic and real-world BFR datasets demonstrate the supe-
032            riority of our approach. Codes and datasets will be available upon acceptance.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Face images captured in natural settings often exhibit various forms of degradation, including com-
038   pression, blur, and noise (Wang et al., 2021; 2023c). Capturing high-quality (HQ) face images is
039
      crucial, as humans are highly sensitive to subtle facial details. Blind face restoration (BFR) aims
      to reconstruct HQ images from degraded inputs and has rapidly progressed in recent years due
040
      to significant research interest. However, BFR remains an ill-posed problem due to the unknown
041
      degradation and the loss of valuable information resulting from these complex conditions (Zhou
042   et al., 2022).
043
044   Sufficient prior information is critical for HQ reconstruction. Researchers have used geometric and
045
      reference priors from sources like (Bulat & Tzimiropoulos, 2018; Kim et al., 2019; Chen et al.,
      2021; Shen et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2018; Hu et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2022; Ren
046
      et al., 2019; Dogan et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020a;b; 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Ma et al., 2020) to guide
047
      face restoration. These priors, however, are limited by their sensitivity to degradation and inability
048   to capture fine facial details, and can even result in corrupted texture details due to incorrect prior
049   information (Lu et al., 2021). With advancements in generative models, such as StyleGAN (Karras
050   et al., 2020) and VQVAE (Razavi et al., 2019), recent works (Chen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021;
051   Chan et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022a;b; Zhou et al., 2022; Tsai et al., 2023) have
052   leveraged pretrained networks to derive facial priors, achieving superior results compared to ear-
053   lier methods. Nonetheless, these approaches still face significant performance declines in handling
      unseen cases. Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020) have shown

095

096   to-image tasks, SD models often fail to retain essential facial details, like skin texture (see Fig. 2
097   (b)). Therefore, these methods typically produce overly smooth images in the T2I task. Moreover,
098   their extensive image priors can lead to the incorrect generation of non-facial features, resulting in
099   artifacts, especially for images with ambiguous degradation. These specific limitations – incorrect
100   generation of non-facial features and missing facial details (red box at 3rd row of Fig. 1)– severely
101   limit the practical deployment of these models in real-world applications.
102   To tackle these problems, we propose Authface, a novel BFR method with face-oriented generative
103   diffusion prior, designed to restore highly authentic face images. The highlight of our Authface
104   is that it brings a paradigm shift for BFR – with a two-stage training pipeline: 1) Face-oriented
105   Fine-tuning on Pretrained T2I Model, and 2) Highly Authentic Face Restoration. The underlying
106   premise for Stage I is that pretrained T2I models e.g., SD models, can serve as effective generative
107   diffusion priors for restoration tasks (Sec. 3.1). They can be customized for face-centric applications
      via fine-tuning while retaining their generation capabilities.

127   thentic facial images, we identify two key criteria as our face-oriented generative diffusion prior:
128   1) Quality-first image collection. Contrary to training T2I base models with large datasets like
129   LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), the quality of the dataset, rather than its size, dictates the gen-
130   eration quality in the fine-tuning process. 2) Photography-guided image annotation. Fine-tuning
131   the pretrained T2I models for HQ facial tasks requires more than just basic annotations like human
132
      accessories, especially for HQ face images with a pronounced stylistic orientation (see Fig. 2 (a)).
      In line with our established criteria, we collect a curated dataset of 1.5K HQ face images – each
133
      enriched with detailed photographic annotations – to fine-tune the pretrained T2I models for the first
134
      stage. With the curated dataset, we are able to fine-tune the T2I models following their original op-
135   timization strategies, as illustrated in Fig. 3. With fine-tuning, the pretrained T2I model is required
136   with the detailed facial prior, which can be demonstrated with the T2I task as shown in Fig. 2 (b). To
137   achieve the goal of highly authentic face restoration in Stage II, we leverage the ControlNet (Zhang
138   et al., 2023) for training (Sec. 3.2). However, directly following the protocol of training ControlNet
139   with the MSE loss tends to contribute to the loss of key facial details, such as eyes and mouths.
140   To resolve this issue, we propose a time-aware latent facial feature loss to directly constrain the
141   regions where humans are sensitive in the latent space. Our extensive experiments demonstrate the
142   superior authentic detail generation performance on synthetic and real-world datasets.
143   In summary, our major contributions are three-fold: I) Novel Research Direction: Our work pio-
144   neers a new approach by enhancing the generative capabilities of pretrained T2I models for authentic
145   face restoration, moving beyond traditional model design. II) New Methodology: Our AuthFace,
146   a novel framework, enhances the detail handling of pretrained T2I models through a unique face-
147   oriented restoration tuning pipeline. Our method significantly sharpens fine facial details and in-
148   cludes a time-aware latent facial feature loss, which effectively reduces artifacts in critical areas
149
      like the eyes and mouth. III) New High-quality Dataset: We have compiled a dataset of 1.5K
      high-resolution images. We expect it can serve as a foundational and important resource to further
150
      advance the field of high-fidelity authentic face restoration.

      2       R ELATED W ORKS
154
      Prior-based Blind Face Restoration Blind face restoration (BFR) employs a variety of priors,
155
      classified into geometric, reference, and generative categories. Geometric priors, such as facial
156   landmarks (Bulat & Tzimiropoulos, 2018; Kim et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2018; Ma et al., 2020),
157   face parsing maps (Chen et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2018), facial
158   component heatmaps (Yu et al., 2018), and 3D face shapes (Hu et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2022; Ren
159   et al., 2019), provide crucial structural information for restoring degraded faces. Reference-based
160   methods use images to deliver identity information, enhancing the fidelity of the restored faces (Do-
161   gan et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020a;b; 2018). Moreover, some researchers have implemented generative
      facial priors, like StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2020), to refine facial details (Chen et al., 2021; Wang


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al., 2021; Chan et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022a). Another approach involves
163   using pretrained Vector-Quantize codebooks that contain detailed facial information (Wang et al.,
164   2022b; Zhou et al., 2022; Tsai et al., 2023). Given their remarkable performance in image gener-
165   ation, denoising diffusion probabilistic models (Ho et al., 2020) have become increasingly popular
166   in BFR. Notable examples, such as DifFace (Yue & Loy, 2022), DR2 (Wang et al., 2023c), and
167   PGDiff (Yang et al., 2024), utilize denoising U-Nets pretrained on HQ face datasets to achieve face
168   restoration at pixel level. Specifically, Zhao et al. (Zhao et al., 2023) attempts to improve the au-
169   thentic performance via feeding network with enhanced ground-truth images. Recently, large-scale
170   pretrained text-to-image models like StableDiffusion (SD)(Stability.ai, 2024) have been employed
171
      to address the BFR problem. DiffBIR (Lin et al., 2024) leverages SD priors for real-world image
      super-resolution and BFR by incorporating degraded input image information in the latent space.
172
      Specifically targeting BFR, BFRfusion (Chen et al., 2024) extracts multi-scale facial features in the
173
      latent space from low-quality face images. However, achieving authentic BFR with pretrained T2I
174   models in the latent space remains underexplored.
175
176   Fine-Tuning Fine-tuning is widely used to align pretrained large language models (LLMs) with
177
      human preferences, improving their effectiveness (Betker et al., 2023). This technique, successful
      in LLMs with small, HQ datasets (Touvron et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024), has been adapted to
178
      text-to-image models to enhance text-image alignment (Dai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a;b). For
179
      example, Emu (Dai et al., 2023) improves aesthetic alignment using fine-tuned HQ image-text pairs.
180   Playground v2.5 (Li et al., 2024a) enhances human features using a quality-controlled dataset, and
181   CosmicMan (Li et al., 2024b) generates superior human-centric content with large, refined datasets.
182   However, these methods often produce overly smooth images, which may not be ideal for BFR tasks
183   where authentic and realistic images are essential.
184
185   3     M ETHODOLOGY
186
187
      The goal of our work is to achieve authentic face restoration by minimizing unrealistic outcomes
      and enhancing the rendition of human-preferred features. It is structured into two distinct stages:
188
      1) Face-oriented Tuning on Pre-trained T2I Model. We integrate supervised fine-tuning (Ouyang
189
      et al., 2022) and quality-tuning (Dai et al., 2023) strategies to refine StableDiffusion-XL (SDXL),
190   enhancing it with detailed facial features as our face-oriented generative diffusion prior (Sec. 3.1);
191   2) Highly Authentic Face Restoration. Utilizing the face-oriented generative diffusion prior, we
192   implement ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) to direct the restoration process based on the quality
193   of input degradation (Sec.3.2). Moreover, we introduce a time-aware latent facial feature loss to
194   improve key facial features during restoration (Sec.3.2).
195
196   3.1   S TAGE I: FACE - ORIENTED F INE - TUNING ON P RE - TRAINED T2I M ODEL
197
      The face-oriented tuning procedure for a pre-trained T2I model consists of two main parts: 1) a
198   quality-first dataset preparation process to obtain and filter HQ face images, and 2) photography-
199   guided data annotation to move beyond basic labels that only convey semantic information.
200
201
      Quality-first Image Collection. Training T2I models typically requires large datasets. However,
      akin to the significant performance improvements seen in large language models fine-tuned with just
202
      1K HQ examples (Zhou et al., 2024), it has been demonstrated that enhancing the aesthetic quality
203
      of generated results can be achieved with only a few thousand extremely HQ images (Dai et al.,
204   2023). This highlights that dataset quality is more important than size in the fine-tuning process.
205   Inspired by this, we apply the quality-first principle to prepare our fine-tuning dataset.
206
207
      Collecting HQ real-world face images is challenging due to privacy and copyright concerns, and
      existing datasets like FFHQ (Karras et al., 2019) suffer from issues such as JPEG degradation, blur,
208
      and Gaussian noise (Zhao et al., 2023). To overcome these challenges, we source extremely HQ
209
      face images from the professional photography website Unsplash (uns, 2023), which offers a license
210   supporting both commercial and non-commercial use. Although the collected images are captured
211   and post-processed by professional photographers, not all images prominently feature faces. To
212   address this, we implement a set of data filtering strategies to create an HQ, face-centric subset.
213   These strategies include face detection to remove images without faces or with small faces, and
214   image quality assessment to filter out images with excessive artifacts such as pepper noise. Also, we
215   use face landmark detection to locate eyes and mouth, enabling us to follow the alignment process
      used in FFHQ (Karras et al., 2019; Kazemi & Sullivan, 2014), better suited for the BFR task.

249
250   Fig. 4 illustrates the structure of stage II. Given the fine-tuned SDXL model as our face-oriented
251   generative diffusion prior, we need an adaptor that can control the fine-tuned SDXL to generate
252   high-quality facial images based on its degraded input. With the successful application of Control-
253   Net (Zhang et al., 2023) in real-world image super-resolution (Wu et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2024), we
254
      apply it as the controller for the BFR task.
255   The training of stage II is as follows. The latent representation of an HQ facial image is obtained
256   by the encoder of a pretrained VAE, denoted as z0 . The diffusion process progressively introduces
257   noise to z0 , resulting in a noisy latent zt , where t represents the randomly sampled diffusion step.
258   The restoration is conditioned on the additional input c, which is the degraded face image, guiding
259   the generation process. For each diffusion step t, the noisy latent zt is processed together with
260
      the control condition c, and null prompts [””]. We train the ControlNet by minimizing the L2 loss
      between the predicted noise ϵθ and the added noise ϵ (ϵ ∼ N (0, I)). The optimization objective is:

348   Implementation Details: Our base model is initialized from StableDiffusion-XL (SDXL) (Podell

350   AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) optimizer with the learning rate of 5e − 7 during the fin-
351   tuing process, where the batch size and the training iteration are set to 96 and 50k, respectively.
352
      We apply the same optimizer with the learning rate of 2e − 5 with the batch size 48 for training
      ControlNet. All experiments are conducted on four NVIDIA L40s GPUs in the resolution of 1024
353
      × 1024 for fintuning model and 512 × 512 for training ControlNet.
354
355   Training and Test Dataset: The training dataset for the fine-tuning process of our face-oriented
356   model comprises 1.5K high-quality face images, each enriched with detailed photographic annota-
357   tions. For training our AuthFace network, we resize the FFHQ dataset (Karras et al., 2019) from a
358
      resolution of 1024×1024 to 512×512. To form training pairs, we follow the settings, including degra-
      dation types and degrees, as outlined in previous methods (Wang et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024).
359
      Following (Zhou et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024), we evaluate our method on a
360
      synthetic dataset, CelebA-Test (Liu et al., 2015), and three real-world datasets: LFW-Test (Wang
361   et al., 2021), WebPhoto-Test (Wang et al., 2021), and WIDER-Test (Zhou et al., 2022).
362
363   Metrics: To evaluate our method’s performance on the Celeb-A dataset with ground truth, we
364
      use PSNR (Hore & Ziou, 2010), SSIM (Wang et al., 2004), and LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018). Be-

      rics, MUSIQ (Ke et al., 2021), ManIQA (Yang et al., 2022) ,ClipIQA (Wang et al., 2023a), and

      5     C ONCLUSION
529
530   This paper presented a new approach for achieving authentic face restoration by avoiding incor-
531   rect generations and enhancing facial details. Specifically, we proposed a face-oriented restoration-
532   tuning paradigm to fine-tune the pretrained T2I model with high-quality face images, enabling the
533   pretrained T2I model, SDXL, to develop a prior for facial details. Utilizing this face-oriented gener-
534   ative diffusion prior, we introduced AuthFace for the blind face restoration task, achieving authentic
535   face restoration. Additionally, we introduced the time-aware latent facial feature loss to further
536
      improve the robustness of restoration in key facial features. Experimental results demonstrate the
      superiority and effectiveness of our method.
537
538   Limitation and Future Work: The process of collecting high-quality images requires significant
539   human resources to filter out low-quality images. We plan to develop an aesthetic-oriented image
      quality assessment network to reduce labor costs.
