011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The capability to learn latent representations plays a key role in the effectiveness
014            of recent machine learning methods. An active frontier in representation learn-
015            ing is understanding representations for combinatorial structures which may not
016
               admit well-behaved local neighborhoods or distance functions. For example, for
               polygons, slightly perturbing vertex locations might lead to significant changes in
017
               their combinatorial structure (expressed as their triangulation or visibility graph)
018
               and may even lead to invalid polygons. In this paper, we investigate representa-
019            tions to capture the underlying combinatorial structures of polygons. Specifically,
020            we study the open problem of Visibility Reconstruction: Given a visibility graph
021            G, construct a polygon P whose visibility graph is G. Visibility Reconstruction
022            belongs to the Existential Theory of Reals (∃R) complexity class (which lies be-
023            tween NP and P-SPACE). Currently, reconstruction algorithms are available only
024            for specific polygon classes. Establishing the hardness of the general problem is
025            open.
026            We introduce VisDiff, a novel diffusion-based approach to reconstruct a polygon
027            from its given visibility graph G. Our method first estimates the signed distance
028            function (SDF) of P from G. Afterwards, it extracts ordered vertex locations
029
               that have the pairwise visibility relationship given by the edges of G. Our main
               insight is that going through the SDF significantly improves learning for recon-
030
               struction. In order to train VisDiff, we make two main contributions: (1) We
031
               design novel loss components for computing the visibility in a differentiable man-
032            ner and (2) create a carefully curated dataset. We use this dataset to benchmark
033            our method and achieve 21% improvement in F1-Score over standard methods.
034            We also demonstrate effective generalization to out-of-distribution polygon types
035            and show that learning a generative model allows us to sample the set of poly-
036            gons with a given visibility graph. Finally, we extend our method to the related
037            combinatorial problem of reconstruction from a triangulation. We achieve 95%
038            classification accuracy of triangulation edges and a 4% improvement in Chamfer
039            distance compared to current architectures. Lastly, we provide preliminary results
040
               on the harder visibility graph recognition problem in which the input G is not
               guaranteed to be a visibility graph.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
044
045
      Many types of objects ranging from molecules to organs to maps can be represented geometrically.
046
      Polygons are one of the most commonly used geometric representations. They are planar objects
047
      specified as a cyclically ordered set of points. The line segments connecting these pairs of points in
048   the given order represent the boundary of an object such as the hand shown in Figure 1-left. As one
049   considers the hands of various people, they realize that shape parameters such as the relative length
050   and thickness of fingers or palm sizes vary across samples. At the same time, intuitively, most hands
051   seem to share a common structure. This intuition can be formalized by studying the underlying
052   combinatorial structures of the corresponding polygons representing the hands. For example, one
053   can triangulate each polygon and construct its dual. The dual, with the appropriate embedding,
      closely resembles a skeleton (Figure 1-middle). Graphical structures such as the triangulation dual

      or the visibility graphs of polygons provide insights about the underlying combinatorial structures of
055   shapes. In this paper, we study representations that link polygons to their combinatorial structures.
056   We study polygons which are simple (the boundary does not self intersect) and simply-connected
057   (no holes).

076   The main question we study is the following: Suppose we are given a graph G representing the com-
077   binatorial structure of a polygon. G could be the visibility graph or a triangulation of the polygon.
078   Note that G does not contain any coordinate information X. What can we say about the polygon, or
079   the set of polygons, that have this structure G? It might be tempting to use standard metric embed-
080   ding methods such as Isomap (Tenenbaum et al., 2000) to reconstruct X but since G does not admit
081   a natural distance metric such methods are doomed to fail as shown by the example in the right
082   figure. Formally, let X(P ) be the vertex locations of a polygon P and G(P ) be a graphical property
083
      of P . In this paper, we consider visibility graphs and triangulations. We consider the following
      problems in increasing difficulty:
084
085
      Problem 1 (Reconstruction) Given a valid G, generate a polygon P such that G(P ) = G.
086
087   Problem 2 (Characterization) Given a valid G, generate all polygons P such that G(P ) = G.
088
089   Note that in these two problems, the input G is assumed to be valid – i.e., there exists a polygon P
090   whose visibility graph or triangulation dual is G. While we primarily focus on reconstruction and
091   characterization problems in this paper, we also provide insights into the more general recognition
092   problem in which G is arbitrary:
093
      Problem 3 (Recognition) Given an arbitrary graph G, determine whether there exists a polygon
094
      P such that G(P ) = G.
095
096   The primary combinatorial structure we study in this paper is the visibility graph. The visibility
097   graph of P , denoted V is(P ) is a graph which has a vertex for each vertex of P . There is an edge
098   between two vertices u and v if and only if u and v are visible to each other in P . In other words,
099   the line segment connecting them is completely inside P . The visibility graph is an important
100   combinatorial structure because it is unique for a given polygon, and contains many other important
101   structures such as triangulations and shortest path trees (Guibas et al., 1986).
102   Our contributions: We present VisDiff: a generative model which takes a visibility graph G as
103   input and a seed for diffusion, and first generates a polygon P represented as a signed distance
104   function (SDF). Next, vertex locations on the zero level set are selected so that V is(P ) = G. Our
105   main insight is that going through the SDF as an intermediate representation yields superior results
106   over using established methods to predict the vertex locations directly. In order to train VisDiff we
107   design novel loss functions for evaluating the validity of the output polygon and comparing its visi-
      bility graph to the input in a differentiable manner. We also design a carefully curated dataset which

      captures a wide range of combinatorial properties of polygons. Current random polygon generation
109   methods struggle to faithfully represent the visibility graph space. They are biased towards high
110   concavity as the number of points increases. We address this problem by systematically rebalancing
111   the dataset by the link diameter – which quantifies concavity.
112
      We show that VisDiff can also be used for characterization since we can sample the set of polygons
113
      which have a given visibility graph. To show the generality of VisDiff, we apply it for the problem
114
      of reconstructing a polygon from its triangulation graph. Finally, we present preliminary results on
115   how VisDiff can be used for recognition by turning it into a classification problem based on the
116   difference between the input graph (which may not be a visibility graph) and the visibility graph of
117   the output polygon. This last result suggests that VisDiff is learning a meaningful representation over
118   the space of all polygons. Overall, our results provide evidence that recent architectures can learn
119   representations of non-trivial combinatorial structures such as polygons. We start with overview of
120   related work.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
123
124
      We summarize the related work in three main directions: Visibility graph reconstruction and recog-
125
      nition, representation learning for shapes and graph neural networks.
126
127   Visibility graph reconstruction and recognition: The problem of reconstructing and recognizing
128   visibility graphs is studied extensively in the computational geometry literature. Yet, it is still an
129   open problem (Ghosh & Goswami, 2013). In the current literature, there are reconstruction and
130
      recognition for polygons of certain categories: Ameer et al. (2022) solved the recognition and re-
      construction problems for pseudo polygons. Silva (2020) showed that visibility graphs of convex
131
      fans are equivalent to visibility graphs of terrain polygons with an addition of a universal vertex. Ev-
132
      erett & Corneil (1990) proposed an algorithm to solve the recognition problem in spiral polygons.
133   Boomari & Zarei (2016) proposed reconstruction and recognition algorithm for anchor polygons.
134   Colley et al. (1997) proposed a linear time algorithm to recognize visibility graphs for tower poly-
135   gon. Dehghani & Morady (2009) solved the reconstruction problem for embedded planar graphs.
136   On the hardness side, the complexity of the visibility graph recognition and reconstruction problem
137   is known to belong to PSPACE (Everett, 1990) specifically in the Existential Theory of the Reals
138   class (Boomari et al., 2018). The exact hardness of the problem is still open. In this work we ex-
139   plore it from the representation learning perspective to understand if generative models can learn the
140   underlying manifold of the space of polygons and their visibility graphs in a generalizable fashion.
141   Representation Learning: 3D shape completion (Chou et al., 2023) (Chen et al., 2024) (Cheng
142   et al., 2023) (Shim et al., 2023) is a closely related application. In 3D shape completion, the input
143   contains partial geometric information for example as a point cloud. In our case, the input is only
144   a combinatorial description such as the visibility graph. There might be many shapes consistent
145   with the input graph and extracting them without any geometric information as part of the input
146   is challenging. Another body of work related to our problem is mesh generation (Gupta et al.,
147
      2023) (Wang et al., 2020). Two recent results in this domain are MeshGPT (Siddiqui et al., 2024)
      and PolyDiff (Alliegro et al., 2023). Both of these approaches generate high-quality 3D triangular
148
      meshes by learning to output a set of triangles from a fixed set of triangles. PolyDiff discretizes the
149
      3D space into bins and MeshGPT works over a predefined set of triangles. In our work, we seek to
150   learn the space of all polygons and their visibility graphs.
151
152   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs): GNNs are one of the standard representations for graphs. The
153
      current literature on GNNs primarily focuses on graphs with features associated with a well-defined
      metric space. In the literature, the closest to our work is generating graph embeddings for a given dis-
154
      tance matrix. Li et al. (2024) showed that a GNN given all-pairwise Euclidean distance information
155
      which is known as Vanilla DisGNN, fails to differentiate between symmetric graph structures. To ad-
156   dress the limitation of Vanilla DisGNN, they propose k-DisGNN. k-DisGNN captures information
157   not just from immediate neighbors but from a k-hop neighborhood around each node. The ability
158   to utilize the k-hop neighbourhood results in building richer geometric representations for differen-
159   tiating between symmetric structures efficiently. Cui & Wei (2023) proposed MetricGNN, which is
160   capable of generating graph embedding from a given embedding distance matrix. Shi et al. (2021)
161   proposed ConfGF which uses GNN for determining molecular conformation given the inter-atomic
      distances and bond characteristics. Yu et al. (2024) proposed a GNN architecture, PolygonGNN,


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      which efficiently represented multipolygon data for graph classification tasks by leveraging visi-
163   bility relationships between polygons. Specifically, PolygonGNN showed that augmenting vertex
164   embeddings of individual polygons with the information of both spatial locations and visibility rela-
165   tionships to other polygon vertices is much more effective in capturing the geometric structure. All
166   the above work assumes the presence of an underlying metric space or spatial position information
167   which is absent in visibility graph reconstruction. We develop VisDiff to learn embeddings in this
168   challenging combinatorial domain.

195   Graph Encoding: The visibility graph is represented as a binary adjacency matrix. To condition
196   other components of VisDiff on this input, we train a U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) autoencoder
197   with Binary Cross Entropy (BCE) Loss to reduce the dimensionality of the 25×25 (polygon with 25
198   vertex locations) input matrix to 512. We pretrain the autoencoder separately and freeze the encoder
199   layer during encoding visibility graph G in other modules.
200   SDF Diffusion: Diffusion models have shown the ability to efficiently learn the space of all im-
201   ages (Ramesh et al., 2022). Motivated by this success, we represent polygons with their signed
202   distance functions which in turn can be represented as images (each pixel stores the distance to the
203   nearest point on the polygon boundary). We can now learn the space of polygons as a diffusion
204   process using a Denoising Diffusion Implicit Model (DDIM) (Song et al., 2020). DDIM primarily
205   involves two steps: forward diffusion and the reverse diffusion processes.
206   Forward Diffusion process involves adding noise to the SDF representation in a scheduled manner.
207   Let the SDF sample from the valid polygon distribution be denoted by x0 ∈ R40×40 . Given the
208   standard deviation of the noise level denoted by σt > 0 at timestep t of the diffusion step, the noise
209   addition process is defined by xt = x0 + σt ϵ where ϵ ∼ N (0, I) is a sample from the Gaussian
210   distribution. In this way, noise is continuously injected into the SDF, eventually transforming it into
211   a pure Gaussian sample at the end of the forward noising process. VisDiff uses a linear log sched-
212
      uler (Permenter & Yuan, 2023) to control the noise level throughout the forward noising process.
213   Reverse Diffusion involves recovering the original SDF from the final Gaussian sample generated
214   during the forward diffusion process. In this step, we start with Gaussian noise and predict the noise
215   added to the sample given the σt . The reverse diffusion is parameterized through a neural network
      that learns to predict the added noise given the input noise sample and σt .

      Specifically, we train a U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) encoder-decoder architecture to predict
217   the noise added to the original SDF sample. Additionally, we condition the U-Net CNN blocks
218   on encoded visibility using multiple Spatial Transformer Cross Attention (Ngo et al., 2023) blocks.
219   The cross-attention blocks directly incorporate visibility information into the U-Net spatial features
220   during the learning process. The key and value components of the cross-attention block are the
221   spatial CNN features, while the query is the encoded visibility embedding. Figure 2 shows the
222   architecture of the SDF Diffusion block. The model is trained using LM SE mean-squared error loss
223   (MSE) between the predicted noise and the actual noise added to the sample. Given the visibility
224   graph G, the trained model is then used to sample polygon SDF.
225   Sampling of the SDF is performed using a DDIM sampler. The sampling process draws a sample
226   from a Gaussian distribution N (0, I) denoted by xt along with a schedule of decreasing noise lev-
227   els proportional to the number of steps in the sampling process. Each diffusion step is given by
228   Equation 1.
229                                    xt−1 = xt + (σt−1 − σt )ϵθ (xt , σt , G)                            (1)
230   where ϵθ (xt , σt , G) represents the noise predicted by the U-Net encoder-decoder architecture given
231   the visibility graph G, the noise sample from the previous step xt and the standard deviation of the
232   noise level σt . This process reconstructs the SDF of the polygon, ensuring it adheres to the visibility
233   constraints defined by G.
234   Vertex Prediction: The generated SDF of the polygon is then used to determine the final vertex
235   locations whose visibility relationship corresponds to the visibility graph G. The process of picking
236   vertex locations over the zero level-set is challenging as the corners of the polygons are not well-
237   defined in the SDF image. Furthermore, as the number of vertex locations increases, a small change
238   in the placement of points on the SDF will significantly alter the visibility of the entire polygon.
239   We formulate the polygon vertex extraction as a separate estimation problem of determining ver-
240   tex locations given the SDF and the visibility graph G. Specifically, we train a CNN encoder to
241   encode the SDF into an embedding space. The embedding process is also conditioned on the visibil-
242   ity graph G encoding using Spatial Transformer Cross Attention (Ronneberger et al., 2015) layers,
243   which helps relate vertex generation to the visibility constraints. The keys and values for the spatial
244   transformer are the spatial CNN features similar to the diffusion block, while the query is the en-
245   coded visibility embedding. The generated SDF embedding is then passed through multiple MLP
246   layers to predict the ordered vertex locations of the polygon. See Figure 2.
247   We experimented with predicting vertex locations simultaneously with the SDF. Comparisons pre-
248   sented in Appendix (Section C, Table 11) show that training the vertex prediction model indepen-
249   dently from the SDF generation model is significantly more accurate than joint training and predic-
250   tion. Hence, we train the vertex prediction model separately with the ground truth SDF.

      5     DATASET G ENERATION
325
326
      The problems of Visibility Characterization and Visibility Reconstruction require the dataset distri-
327
      bution to have a key characteristic of multiple polygons P corresponding to the same visibility graph
328   G. Additionally, the dataset should also represent a high diversity of visibility graphs. We address
329   these characteristics by uniformly sampling polygons based on graph properties described below
330   and also generate multiple augmentations of the same polygon.
331
      The dataset generation process involves sampling 60,000 polygons with 25 vertex locations arranged
332
      in fixed anticlockwise ordering. The vertex locations are drawn from a uniform distribution within
333
      [−1, 1]2 . We use the 2-opt move (Auer & Held, 1996) algorithm to generate polygons from the
334   drawn locations.We observed that the dataset generated from the 2-opt move algorithm exhibited
335   non-uniformity with respect to the link diameter of the visibility graph. Link diameter quantifies the
336   maximum number of edges on the shortest path between any two graph nodes. A higher diameter
337   indicates greater concavity in the polygon. Hence, to have a balanced distribution, we resample the
338   large dataset based on the link diameter of the visibility graph. The resampling process results in a
339   subset of 18,500 polygons. In the appendix (Section D, Figure 8b) we present additional statistics
340   showing that that our dataset is uniformly distributed in terms of link diameter.
341   We further augment each polygon to generate 20 samples by applying shear transformation and ver-
342   tex perturbation while preserving the visibility graph G. The augmentations introduce the property
343   of multiple polygons with the same visibility graph G . The augmentation and resampling are criti-
344   cal for learning the representative space of Visibility Characterization and Visibility Reconstruction
345   problems. The final dataset consists of 370,000 polygons and their respective visibility graphs. The
346   total dataset size including all polygons consists of 400,000, which will be made publicly available.

      6.1     E VALUATION M ETRICS
379
380   To evaluate our algorithm, we compute the visibility graphs of the output polygons and formulate
381   the evaluation of the visibility graph as a classification problem. We report the accuracy, precision,
382   recall, and F1-Score between the generated and the ground-truth visibility graphs. Specifically, each
383   edge of the visibility graph is classified as either a visible or non-visible edge. Each visibility graph
384   is evaluated individually, and the average over the dataset is reported as a collective quantitative
385   metric. Since the ratio of visible and non-visible edges can be vastly different across polygons, we
386
      use the F-1 score to evaluate model performance.
387
388   6.2     Q UALITATIVE AND Q UANTITATIVE E VALUATION
389
      We compare VisDiff against baselines, which generate vertex representation of a polygon from
390
      the visibility graph. In particular, we compare against various state-of-the-art encoders such as
391
      Transformer-Decoder [Seq] (Vaswani, 2017), Graph Neural Network [Gnn] (Veličković et al., 2017),
392   DDIM [VD] (Song et al., 2020), Encoder - Decoder [E.D], VAE [VA] (Kingma & Welling, 2022)
393   and a direct optimization approach based on Nelder-Mead [NM] (Gao & Han, 2012) optimization.
394   Nelder-Mead optimizes the configuration of vertex locations by using the difference between the
395   predicted and actual visibility graph as a loss which is backpropagated to the vertex locations. The

      6.2.3   Visibility Recognition
487
488   We present preliminary results on the Visibility Recognition problem. We generate a set of 50 valid
489   and non-valid visibility graphs for the Visibility Recognition problem. We use polygons with holes as
490   samples of non-valid visibility graphs. A polygon with a hole is a polygon with an outer boundary,
491   but also has an inner boundary which makes it non simple. We determine the visibility graph in the
492   same way as that of simple polygon. An edge through the hole is a non visible edge since the hole
493
      is considered outside the polygon.
494   We utilize the model’s ability to sample multiple polygons and sample a set of polygons S for
495   each visibility graph. If any of the polygons from S are valid and has a F1-Score over a certain
496   threshold X, it is classified as a valid visibility graph. Figure 7e shows the performance of our
497   model on Visibility Recognition problem using different thresholds on F-1 Score. Figure 15a to 15d
498   shows qualitative results on the polygon generation for two non-valid visibility graphs. VisDiff is
499
      able to correctly classify 80% of the samples from the set of valid and non-valid visibility graphs
      when the F-1 threshold is selected to be close to mean performance on the Visibility Reconstruction
500
      problem. Classification performance of 80% shows that VisDiff is able to represent the underlying
501
      valid visibility graph space efficiently. Appendix E.4 shows more qualitative results on Visibility
502   Recognition.
503
504   6.2.4   T RIANGULATION
505
506   In this section, we change the input from the complete visibility graph to the triangulation to show
507   case the versatility of VisDiff. Note that a polygon may have many different triangulations. Each
508   triangulation contains n − 2 triangles where n is the number of vertices (De Berg, 2000). We use
509
      the Constrained Delauney Triangulation (Rognant et al., 1999) to triangulate the polygons in our
      dataset, ensuring a unique triangulation for a polygon (Dinas & Banon, 2014).
510
511   We evaluate the model on the classification metrics of the triangulation and the Chamfer dis-
512   tance (Borgefors, 1988). The classification metrics are calculated by comparing the existence of
513   triangulation edges in the visibility graph of the generated polygon. In the case the model predicts
514   a convex polygon given a triangulation of a non-convex polygon. It would have 100% triangulation
515
      accuracy which is misleading. Hence, the Chamfer distance between the points is also evaluated as
      the triangulation is unique to the spatial locations of the points. The Chamfer distance is calculated
516
      with polygons rotated to have the first edge aligned with the x-axis to account for rotation variations.
517
      Table 1 shows the quantitative results of VisDiff with baselines. VisDiff performs much better than
518   all the models in maintaining the triangulation while also has the minimum Chamfer distance. We
519   present additional qualitative results in Appendix (Section E.3, Figure 14)

522   7   C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we studied the problems of Visibility Reconstruction, Characterization and Recog-
525
      nition for simple polygons. We presented VisDiff a diffusion-based approach which first predicts
      the Signed Distance Function (SDF) associated with a polygonal boundary conditioned on the input
526
      visibility graph G. The SDF is then used to generate vertex locations of a polygon P whose visi-
527
      bility graph is G. Our method showed an improvement of 21% on F1-Score compared to baseline
528   approaches on the Visibility Reconstruction problem. We then showed the capability of VisDiff to
529   sample multiple polygons for a single visibility graph G as a realization of Visibility Characteriza-
530   tion problem. We also presented preliminary results of 80% accuracy on the Visibility Recognition
531   problem. VisDiff has been shown to generalize to accept triangulations as input where it maintains
532   95% triangulation edges and achieves 4% improvement on Chamfer distance compared to baselines.
533   We also proposed loss components for computing the visibility graph in a differentiable manner and
534   demonstrated its effectiveness compared to guidance solely on L2 loss between vertex locations.
535   At a high-level, our results show that modern neural representations are capable of encoding the
536   space of all polygons in such a way that the distances on the learned manifold are faithful to the
537   combinatorial properties of polygons. In terms of future work, the presented VisDiff architecture
538   represents the SDF as a grid, which creates a bottleneck in terms of computation time and space.
539   In our future work, we will investigate encoding the SDF using more efficient representations such
      as (Park et al., 2019; Mitchell et al., 2020).

711                                                         vi is specified by its coordinates (xi , yi ). Let
