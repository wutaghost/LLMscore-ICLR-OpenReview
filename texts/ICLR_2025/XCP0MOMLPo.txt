011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The design of optimal linear block codes capable of being efficiently decoded is of
014            major concern, especially for short block lengths. As near capacity-approaching
015            codes, Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes possess several advantages over
016
               other families of codes, the most notable being its efficient decoding via Belief
               Propagation. While many LDPC code design methods exist, the development
017
               of efficient sparse codes that meet the constraints of modern short code lengths
018
               and accommodate new channel models remains a challenge. In this work, we
019            propose for the first time a gradient-based data-driven approach for the design of
020            sparse codes. We develop locally optimal codes with respect to Belief Propagation
021            decoding via the learning of the Factor graph under channel noise simulations. This
022            is performed via a novel complete graph tensor representation of the Belief Propa-
023            gation algorithm, optimized over finite fields via backpropagation and coupled with
024            an efficient line-search method. The proposed approach is shown to outperform
025            the decoding performance of existing popular codes by orders of magnitude and
026            demonstrates the power of data-driven approaches for code design.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Reliable digital communication is of major importance in the modern information age and involves
032   the design of codes that can be robustly and efficiently decoded despite noisy transmission channels.
033
      Over the last half-century, significant research has been dedicated to the study of capacity-approaching
      Error Correcting Codes (ECC) (Shannon, 1948). Despite the initial focus on short and medium-length
034
      linear block codes (Berlekamp, 1974), the development of long channel codes (Forney, 1966; Costello
035
      & Forney, 2007) has emerged as a viable approach to approaching channel capacity (Berrou et al.,
036   1993; MacKay, 1999; Richardson et al., 2001; Richardson & Urbanke, 2001; Arikan, 2008; Luby
037   et al., 2001; Kudekar et al., 2011).
038
039
      While the NP-hard maximum likelihood rule defines the target decoding of a given code, developing
      more practical solutions generally relies on theories grounded upon asymptotic analysis over conven-
040
      tional communication channels. However, modern communication systems rely on the design of short
041
      and medium-block-length codes (Liva et al., 2016) and the latest communication settings provide
042   new types of channels. This is mainly due to emergent applications in the modern wireless realm
043   requiring the transmission of short data units, such as remote command links, Internet of Things, and
044   messaging services (De Cola et al., 2011; Boccardi et al., 2014; Paolini et al., 2015; Durisi et al.,
045   2016; ESTI, 2021). These challenges call for the formulation of data-driven solutions, capable of
046   adapting to various settings of interest and constraints, generally uncharted by existing theories.
047
      The vast majority of existing machine-learning solutions to the ECC problem concentrate on the
048   design of neural decoders. The first neural models focused on the implementation of parameterized
049   versions of the legacy Belief Propagation (BP) decoder (Nachmani et al., 2016; 2018; Lugosch &
050   Gross, 2017; Nachmani & Wolf, 2019; Buchberger et al., 2020). Recently, state-of-the-art learning-
051   based de novo decoders have been introduced, borrowing from well-proven architectures from
052   other domains. A Transformer-based decoder that incorporates the code into the architecture has
053   been recently proposed by (Choukroun & Wolf, 2022a), outperforming existing methods by sizable
      margins and at a fraction of their time complexity. This architecture has been subsequently integrated


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      into a denoising diffusion models paradigm, further improving results (Choukroun & Wolf, 2022b).
055   Subsequently, a universal neural decoder has been proposed in (Choukroun & Wolf, 2024c), capable
056   of unified decoding of codes from different families, lengths, and rates. Most recently and related
057   to our work, (Choukroun & Wolf, 2024a) developed an end-to-end learning framework capable of
058   co-learning binary linear block codes along with the neural decoder.
059
      However, neural decoding methods require increased computational and memory complexity com-
060
      pared to their well-established classical counterparts. Due to these challenges, and the non-trivial
061   acceleration and implementation required, neural decoders were never deployed in real-world systems,
062   as far as we know.
063
064
      In this work, given the ubiquity and advantages of the Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm (Pearl,
      1988; Richardson et al., 2001) for sparse codes, we consider the optimization of codes with respect
065
      to BP via the learning of the underlying factor/Tanner graph. From a graphical probabilistic model
066
      perspective (Koller & Friedman, 2009), BP being a marginalization algorithm, a gradient-based of
067   a score metric method is given for the structure learning of BP’s underlying Bayesian network in
068   an end-to-end fashion. As far as we can ascertain, this is the first time a gradient-based data-driven
069   solution is given for the design of the codes themselves based on a classical decoder. Such a solution
070   induces a very low overhead (if any) for integration into the existing decoding solutions.
071
      Beyond the conceptual novelty, we make three technical contributions: (i) we formulate the data-
072
      driven optimization objective adapted to the setting of interest (e.g., channel noise, code structure),
073   (ii) we reformulate BP in a tensor fashion to learn the connectivity of the factor graph through
074   backpropagation, and (iii) we propose a differentiable and fast optimization approach via a line-
075   search method adapted to the relaxed binary programming setting. Applied to a wide variety of
076   codes, our method produces codes that outperform existing codes on various channel noise settings,
077   demonstrating the power and flexibility of the method in adapting to realistic settings of interest.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
080
081
      Neural decoder or data-driven contributions generally focus on short and moderate-length codes for
082
      two main reasons. First, classical decoders reach the capacity of the channel for large codes, and
083
      second, the emergence of short data units applications driven by the Internet of Things (e.g., smart
084   metering networks, messaging services, etc.) requires effective decoders for short to moderate-length
085   codes. For example, 5G Polar codes have code lengths of 32 to 1024 (Liva et al., 2016; ESTI, 2021).
086
087
      Previous work on neural decoders is generally divided into two main classes: model-free and model-
      based. Model-free decoders employ general types of neural network architectures (Cammerer et al.,
088
      2017; Gruber et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2018b; Bennatan et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2019a; Choukroun &
089
      Wolf, 2022a;b; 2024c;b). Model-based decoders implement parameterized versions of classical Belief
090   Propagation (BP) decoders, where the Tanner graph is unfolded into an NN in which scalar weights
091   are assigned to each variable edge. This results in an improvement in comparison to the baseline
092   BP method for short codes (Nachmani et al., 2016; Nachmani & Wolf, 2019; Raviv et al., 2020;
093   2023; Kwak et al., 2023). While model-based decoders benefit from a strong theoretical background,
094   the architecture is overly restrictive, which generally enforces its coupling with high-complexity
095   NN (Nachmani & Wolf, 2021). Also, the improvement gain generally vanishes for more iterations
096   and longer codewords (Hoydis et al., 2022) and the integration cost remains very high due to both
097   computational and memory requirements.
098   While neural decoders show improved performance in various communication settings, there has been
099   very limited success in the design of novel neural coding methods, which remain impracticable for
100   deployment (O’Shea & Hoydis, 2017; Kim et al., 2018a; Jiang et al., 2019b). Recently, (Choukroun
101   & Wolf, 2024a) provided a new differentiable way of designing binary linear block codes (i.e.,
102   parity-check matrices) for a given neural decoder also showing improved performance with classical
103   decoders.
104   Belief-propagation decoding has multiple advantages for LDPC codes (Gallager, 1962; Richardson
105   & Urbanke, 2001; Richardson et al., 2001). A large number of LDPC code (parity check matrix)
106   design techniques exist in the literature, depending on the design criterion. Among them, Gallager
107   (Gallager, 1962) developed the first regular LDPC codes as the concatenations of permuted sub-
      matrices. MacKay (MacKay & Neal, 1995) demonstrated the ability of sparse codes to reach near-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      capacity limits via semi-randomly generated matrices. Irregular LDPC codes have been developed
109   by (Richardson et al., 2001; Luby et al., 2001; Chung et al., 2001) where the decoding threshold
110   can be optimized via density-evolution. Progressive Edge Growth (Hu et al., 2001; 2005) has been
111   proposed to design large girth codes. Certain classes of LDPC array codes have been presented
112   in (Eleftheriou & Olcer, 2002) and LDPC codes with combinatorial design constraints have been
113   developed in (Vasic & Milenkovic, 2004). Finite geometry codes have been developed in (Lucas
114   et al., 2000; Kou et al., 2001) and repeat-accumulate codes have been proposed by (Yang et al., 2004;
115   Jin et al., 2000; Narayanaswami, 2001). However, the classical methods are not data-driven and
116   are difficult to adapt to the design of codes under constrained settings of interest (e.g., short codes,
117
      modern channels, structure constraints, etc.). Most related to our work are methods for structure
      learning (Koller & Friedman, 2009) for Bayesian networks such as the Chow-Liu Algorithm (Chow &
118
      Liu, 1968) or search-based methods (Tian, 2013). Related to greedy search-based methods, Elkelesh
119
      et al. (2019) recently suggested the application of classical genetic algorithms for the discovery of
120   better IRA codes.

      3    BACKGROUND
124
      We assume a standard transmission protocol using a linear block code C. The code is defined by a
125
      generator matrix G ∈ {0, 1}n×k and the parity check matrix H ∈ {0, 1}(n−k)×n is defined such that
126
      HGT = 0 over the order 2 Galois field GF (2). The parity check matrix H entails what is known as
127
      a Tanner graph (Tanner, 1981), which consists of n variable nodes and (n − k) check nodes. The
128   edges of this bipartite graph correspond to the on-bits of the matrix H.
129
130   The input message m ∈ {0, 1}k (column vector) is encoded by G to a codeword c ∈ C ⊂ {0, 1}n
131
      satisfying Hc = H(Gm) = 0 and transmitted via a Binary-Input Symmetric-Output channel, e.g.,
      an AWGN channel. Let y denote the channel output represented as y = cs + ε, where cs denotes
132
      the transmission modulation of c (e.g., Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK)), and ε is random noise
133
      independent of the transmitted c. The main goal of the decoder fH : Rn → Rn conditioned on the
134   code (i.e., H) is to provide a soft approximation x̂ = fH (y) of the codeword.
135
136   The Belief Propagation algorithm allows the iterative transmission (propagation) of a current code-
137
      word estimate (belief) via a Trellis graph determined according to a factor graph defined by the code
      (i.e., the Tanner graph). The factor graph is unrolled into a Trellis graph, initiated with n variable
138
      nodes, and composed of two types of interleaved layers defined by the check/factor nodes and variable
139
      nodes. An illustration of the Tanner graph unrolled to the Trellis graph is given in Figure 1.
140
141   As a message-passing algorithm, Belief Propagation operates on the Trellis graph by propagating
142   the messages from variable nodes to check nodes and from check nodes to variable nodes, in an
143
      alternative and iterative fashion. The input layer generally corresponds to the vector of log-likelihood
      ratios (LLR) L ∈ Rn of the channel output y defined as

183   4    M ETHOD
184
185   The performance of BP is strongly tied to the underlying Tanner graph induced by the code. BP
186
      and its variants are generally implemented over a fixed sparse graph, such that the only degree of
      freedom resides in the number of decoding iterations. While several recent contributions (Nachmani
187
      et al., 2016; Nachmani & Wolf, 2019) aim to enhance the BP algorithm by augmenting the Trellis
188
      graph with neural networks, these approaches assume and maintain fixed codes. Here, we propose
189   optimizing the code for the BP algorithm on a decoding setting of interest. Given a trainable binary
190   parity check matrix H, we wish to obtain BP-optimized codes by solving the following parameterized
191   optimization problem
192
193
          H∗ =

194                 arg min       Em∼Bernk (1/2),ε∼Z,T ∈N+ D fH,T ϕ(G(H)m) + ε , m + R(H)                 (4)
195              H∈{0,1}(n−k)×n
196   Here, G(H) denotes a generator matrix defined by H (i.e., HGT = 0), ϕ denotes the modulation
197   function such that cs = ϕ(c), and Z is the channel noise distribution. fH,T denotes the BP decoder
198   built upon H with T discrete iterations (sampled uniformly from a given set), D denotes the distance
199   metric of interest, and R denotes the potential hard/soft regularization of interest, e.g., sparsity or
200   constraints on the code structure.
201
      Several challenges arise from this optimization problem: (i) the optimization is highly non-
202   differentiable and results in an NP-hard binary non-linear integer programming problem, (ii) the
203   computation of the codewords c = Gm is both highly non-differentiable (matrix-vector multiplication
204   over GF (2) in case symmetry is not maintained during the optimization(Richardson & Urbanke,
205   2001)) and computationally expensive (inverse via Gaussian elimination of H), (iii) the modulation
206   ϕ(·) can be non-differentiable, and last but most important, (iv) BP assumes a fixed code (i.e., the
207   factor graph edges) upon which the decoder is implemented.
208

246       Belief Propagation Codes Optimization The tensor reformulation solves the major challenge of
247       graph learning (challenge (iv)). Challenges (ii) and (iii) are also eliminated in our formulation. First,
248       since for any given H the conditional independence of error probability under symmetry (Richardson
249       & Urbanke, 2001) is satisfied for message passing algorithms, it is enough to optimize the zero
250       codeword only, i.e., c = Gm = 0, removing then any dependency on G in the objective (challenge
251
          (ii)). As a byproduct, we obtain that the optimization problem is invariant to the choice of modulation,
          whether differentiable or not (challenge (iii)).

294        Binary Line-Search Conventional first-order optimization methods with small learning rate
295        regimes have two major drawbacks with binarization (Rastegari et al., 2016; Courbariaux et al.,
296        2016). First, they are generally slow since only gradient steps modifying the sign of the binarized
297        tensor induce a modification of the loss. Second, they have difficulties in converging to local minima
298
           because of oscillating behavior around zero.
299        In general, efficient line search methods (Nocedal & Wright, 2006) assume local convexity or a smooth

302        setting. While classical grid search methods look for the optimal step size on handcrafted predefined
303        sample points, in our binary setting we can search only for the step sizes inducing a flip of the sign in
304
           Ω, provably limiting the maximum number of relevant grid samples to n(n−k). Thus, the line-search

515   7    C ONCLUSIONS
516
517   We present a novel gradient-based optimization method of binary linear block codes for the Belief
518   Propagation algorithm. The proposed framework enables the differentiable optimization of the factor
519   graph via weighted tensor representation. The optimization is efficiently carried out via a tailor-made
520   grid search procedure that is aware of the binary constraint of the optimization problem.
521
      A common criticism of ML-based ECC is that the neural decoder cannot be deployed directly without
522
      the application of massive deep-learning acceleration methods. Here, we show that the code can
523   be designed efficiently in a data-driven fashion on differentiable formulations of classical decoders.
524   The optimization of codes may open the door to the establishment of new industry standards and
525   the creation of new families of codes. Future work includes the development of more efficient
526   optimization methods, able to define better initializations and to escape bad local minima.
