011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            While most existing federated learning (FL) approaches assume a fixed set of
014            clients in the system, in practice, clients can dynamically leave or join the sys-
015            tem depending on their needs or interest in the specific task. This dynamic FL
016
               setting introduces several key challenges: (1) the objective function dynamically
               changes depending on the current set of clients, unlike traditional FL approaches
017
               that maintain a static optimization goal; (2) the current global model may not serve
018
               as the best initial point for the next FL rounds and could potentially lead to slow
019            adaptation, given the possibility of clients leaving or joining the system. In this
020            paper, we consider a dynamic optimization objective in FL that seeks the optimal
021            model tailored to the currently active set of clients. Building on our probabilis-
022            tic framework that provides direct insights into how the arrival and departure of
023            different types of clients influence the shifts in optimal points, we establish an
024            upper bound on the optimality gap, accounting for factors such as stochastic gra-
025            dient noise, local training iterations, non-IIDness of data distribution, and devia-
026            tions between optimal points caused by dynamic client pattern. We also propose
027
               an adaptive initial model construction strategy that employs weighted averaging
               guided by gradient similarity, prioritizing models trained on clients whose data
028
               characteristics align closely with the current one, thereby enhancing adaptability
029
               to the current clients. The proposed approach is validated on various datasets and
030            FL algorithms, demonstrating robust performance across diverse client arrival and
031            departure patterns, underscoring its effectiveness in dynamic FL environments.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Federated learning (FL) is a decentralized machine learning paradigm that facilitates collaborative
037   model training across multiple clients, such as smartphones and Internet of Things (IoT) clients,
038   without exchanging individual data. Instead of transmitting raw data to the central server, each
039   client performs local training using its proprietary data, sending only model updates to the server.
040   These updates are then aggregated to refine the global model. In conventional FL frameworks, the
041   cohort of clients engaged in training is typically static, implying the objective function is also fixed.
042   In practical FL systems, the dynamic nature of client arrival and departure presents significant chal-
043   lenges to maintaining a robust and accurate model. For instance, clients may lose interest when their
044   local data no longer aligns with the central task, such as when a user’s application shifts from text
045   predictions to image recognition. On the other hand, clients may join when their current objectives
046   align with those of other clients, such as when multiple users are working with similar data types
047   or models addressing the same problem, like medical institutions collaborating on disease detection
048   models. Additional factors, such as evolving privacy policies, or shifts in data-sharing preferences,
049   further exacerbate these arrival and departure fluctuations, complicating the overall learning process.
050   Challenges: This dynamic FL setting introduces new challenges that are not present in traditional
051   static FL scenarios with a fixed objective function. Specifically, the arrival and departure of clients
052   dynamically alter the FL system’s objective, as the model must adapt to the current set of clients and
053   their associated tasks. For instance, if a client contributing unique data classes withdraws, the model
      no longer needs to classify those classes, fundamentally altering the training objective. Conversely,

082   Illustrative Examples: Figure 1 illustrates our system model, highlighting its key differences from
083   traditional FL with partial client participation. In traditional FL with partial client participation,
084   although the set of active clients may change as some clients intermittently disengage, the optimiza-
085   tion goal remain constant throughout the training process. The objective is to converge towards the
086
      optimal model w∗ , which minimizes the aggregated loss functions across all clients, expressed as
      F1 (w) + F2 (w) + F3 (w). In this line of research, authors typically aim to design a client sampling
087
      strategy (e.g., (Chen et al., 2022)) or modify the aggregation weight (e.g., (Wang & Ji, 2024)) to
088
      minimize a static global loss function that remains constant over time. In contrast, our approach
089   addresses a more intricate scenario where both the set of clients and the optimization goals evolve
090   dynamically. In each round g, the set of clients changes, and the objective shifts to finding the round-
091   specific optimal model w(g)∗ , defined as the minimizer of the loss function Fk (w) corresponding
092   to the clients in that round. This dual dynamic creates substantial complexity, necessitating con-
093   tinuous adjustment of the optimization target to reflect the evolving composition of clients, thereby
094   introducing challenges far beyond those encountered in traditional FL settings.

      Contributions: Given the challenges introduced by the dynamic nature of client arrival and depar-
096   ture, this work makes the following key contributions to advance the field:
097
098      • We first propose a comprehensive probabilistic framework that models the formation of local
099        datasets and classifies clients into distinct types based on their underlying probability distribu-
100        tions. This framework sheds light on the dynamics of how the optimal point shifts across global
101        iterations, offering a detailed view of the impact of client variability on model performance. By
102        examining the probabilistic relationships among client types and their associated data distribu-
103        tions, our approach highlights how changes in local datasets influence global optimization.
104      • We provide a novel theoretical analysis where an upper bound on the optimality gap is derived,
105        quantifying the discrepancy between the global model and the theoretical optimal point. Our
106        analysis considers a dynamic optimization goal, where each round aims to find an optimized
107        model that minimizes the loss function of the currently active clients, in contrast to existing
           literature that focuses on a static goal of minimizing the loss across all clients that have ever

111         data distribution, and (4) the deviations between optimal points caused by the dynamic arrival

125   2    R ELATED W ORK
126
127   Federated learning (FL) has emerged as a prominent paradigm for distributed machine learning,
128   enabling the collaborative training of models across decentralized data sources while preserving
129   data privacy (McMahan et al., 2017; Kairouz et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020). One of the foundational
130
      works introduced the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm, which remains a cornerstone of
      many FL systems (McMahan et al., 2017). Subsequent studies have explored various aspects of
131
      federated learning, including communication efficiency (Yang et al., 2019), robustness to adversarial
132
      attacks (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020), and personalization strategies (Smith et al., 2017). However,
133   these approaches typically assume a fixed set of participating clients throughout the training process.
134   Reviews of FL techniques often consider static client participation, where all clients are expected
135   to remain available for the entire training duration (Kairouz et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020). This
136   assumption simplifies the modeling of convergence and performance but does not adequately capture
137   real-world scenarios characterized by dynamic client pattern. Addressing the dynamic nature of
138   client arrival and departure is a critical gap in the current literature, motivating the need for adaptive
139   methods that can effectively handle the entry and exit of clients during the training process.
140   To address the limitations of static client sets in federated learning, research on dynamic client se-
141   lection and flexible participation has gained momentum, particularly in response to the challenges
142   posed by varying client availability (Fu et al., 2023; Nishio & Yonetani, 2019; Yoshida et al., 2020;
143   AbdulRahman et al., 2020; Martini, 2024; Li et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2021; Chai et al., 2020; Gu et al.,
144   2021; Jhunjhunwala et al., 2022). These studies explore strategies such as optimizing client selec-
145   tion based on resource constraints, modeling participation patterns probabilistically, and employing
146   adaptive algorithms to address the effects of non-IID data. The goal is to enhance overall model
147
      performance by strategically managing client participation during training, balancing computational
      efficiency, communication costs, and data representativeness. However, these works often assume
148
      a fixed client set, neglecting the dynamic nature of client arrival and departure. While (Ruan et al.,
149
      2021) propose a flexible federated learning framework that allows for inactive clients, incomplete
150   updates, or dynamic client participation, their analysis is limited to scenarios where the optimiza-
151   tion goal changes only once, specifically when a single client joins during training. This restricts the
152   applicability of their approach to more complex and realistic patterns of client pattern.
153
      In contrast to previous works, our approach addresses the challenge of a dynamically changing
154
      optimization goal that evolves across global rounds, focusing on the more stringent issue of dy-
155
      namic client arrival and departure over time. We introduce a probabilistic framework to model the
156   formation of local datasets, incorporating the concept of client types. These client types are charac-
157   terized by the underlying probability distributions that dictate how local datasets are sampled from
158   the global dataset. To mitigate the performance degradation caused by dynamic client arrival and
159   departure, we propose an adaptive method for constructing the initial model. This adaptive strategy
160   ensures robust performance, even as clients dynamically join or leave the system, leading to varying
161   data distributions throughout training. By addressing these complexities, our framework provides a
      more flexible and resilient solution for federated learning in dynamic environments.

      3    DYNAMIC FL WITH CLIENT A RRIVAL AND D EPARTURE

      We consider a dynamic FL system where clients may join or leave the training process based on their
165   interest or task needs. Clients may join when the model aligns with their objectives, when they have
166   sufficient new data to contribute, or when the system offers financial or computational incentives.
167   Conversely, they may leave if the global model drifts from their needs, if they lack sufficient data, or
168   if the model’s performance is not beneficial. Privacy or security concerns, such as adversarial threats
169   or inadequate privacy guarantees, may also lead clients to leave. Additionally, clients may leave to
170   allocate computational resources to other tasks or due to poor local model validation, while others
171   may rejoin when they see improvements in these factors.
172   To mathematically characterize these scenarios, each round of FL training, denoted by g ∈ G =
173   {1, . . . , G}, involves a set of clients collected in K(g) = {1, . . . , K (g) }. All clients are connected
174   to a central server, with each client maintaining its own ML models tailored to specific tasks such
175   as pattern recognition and natural language processing. These models are trained locally on client-
176   specific data, allowing the system to leverage diverse data sources while preserving user privacy.
177

333
334   We present the key concepts of our proposed algorithm in this section, with the full pseudocode and
335   detailed explanations available in Appendix C.
336   Motivation: Although our analysis applies to any client pattern, machine learning performance
337   can be further improved by utilizing historical data distributions. Intuitively, if the data distribution
338   in round g closely resembles that of a previous round g ′ , where g ′ < g, initializing the global
339   model in round g with the model from round g ′ can lead to significant performance gains. This
340   approach leverages past knowledge to accelerate convergence and mitigates the adverse effects of
341   sporadic or unpredictable client patterns, which often cause fluctuations in model quality. By reusing
342   model states from rounds with similar data distributions, the learning process becomes more robust,
343   reducing the need for the model to relearn from scratch when encountering familiar data patterns.
344   Intuition: In complex scenarios, where data distributions are heterogeneous or where no data dis-
345   tribution resembles the current one, it is more advantageous to initialize the model using a weighted
346   sum of models from multiple prior rounds. The weight assigned to each model should ideally reflect
347   the degree of similarity between data distributions in a past round g ′ and the current round g. How-
348   ever, systematically calculating this similarity and determining the appropriate weights remains a
349   challenging task. To address this, we propose utilizing a “pilot model” to compute gradients, which
350
      are then employed to assess similarity and derive the weights for the weighted sum.
351   Initialization and Local Training: The algorithm starts with random initialization of the global
352   model w(0) . Each session is defined by a consistent data distribution and begins whenever data
353   distribution changes due to client arrivals or departures. A session comprises at least one global
354   round, during which the data distribution remains stable. Within each session, clients conduct local
355   training based on the current global model, which could be a newly constructed initial model or
356   the latest model at the server. Each client trains locally for several epochs. Upon completion,
357
      clients transmit their final local models to the server, where they are aggregated through a weighted
      summation. The global model from the last round of each session is preserved for future use, either
358
      for pilot model formation or constructing initial models in future sessions. The number of archived
359
      global models matches the number of completed sessions.
360
361   Pilot Model Formation and Gradient Computation: This step focuses on constructing the pilot
362   model and computing gradients that accurately capture the characteristics of the current data distri-
363
      bution. After completing the predefined P sessions in the pilot preparation stage, the pilot model wp
      is constructed by averaging only those global models with good accuracy, as models with poor accu-
364
      racy fail to adequately capture the underlying data distribution. Suppose there are J (≤ P ) models
365
      that perform well on the data distribution during that round, denoted by w(j) for j = 0, . . . , J − 1.
366                                                         PJ−1

398   FL Algorithm and Baseline: We consider FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017), FedProx (Li et al.,
399   2020), and SCAFFOLD (Karimireddy et al., 2020) as the federated learning algorithms in our ex-
400   periments to evaluate proposed algorithm. For all algorithms, the baseline continues model training
401   using the previous model from the last round, without constructing an appropriate initial model.
402   Task and Dataset: We conducted extensive experiments to evaluate our proposed algorithm (full
403   pseudocode provided in Algorithm 1 of Appendix C). The task of interest is image classification.
404   We used five image datasets, ranging from the simplest to the most challenging: MNIST (LeCun
405   et al., 1998), Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky
406   & Hinton, 2009), and CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009) The models for MNIST, Fashion-
407   MNIST, and SVHN are outlined in the Appendix D.2.
408   Label Distribution: In our experiments, we simulate a system of 10 clients. We consider four
409   methods for distributing the labels across these clients:
410
411       • Two-Shard(McMahan et al., 2017; Hsu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020; Fallah et al., 2020; Karim-
412         ireddy et al., 2020): For datasets with 10 labels, each label is divided into two equally-sized
413
            shards, and each client receives two different label shards. For datasets with 100 labels, the la-
            bels are divided into 10 non-overlapping batches, and each batch is split into two shards, which
414
            are then assigned to two randomly selected clients. Each client ends up with data from two labels
415
            for 10-label datasets, or 20 labels for 100-label datasets .
416
          • Half: Half the clients have one half of the labels, and the rest have the other half. Each client
417
            has all the labels from their assigned half (i.e., 5 labels for 10-label datasets or 50 labels for
418
            100-label datasets), and the data is evenly distributed, meaning that the amount of data for each
419         label is equally divided among the clients.
420
          • Partial-Overlap: Two sets of labels are selected, each containing 60% of the total labels, with
421
            a 20% overlap between them. Each client in the first set has 6 labels for 10-label datasets (or
422
            60 labels for 100-label datasets), and each client in the second set has a similar distribution.
423         The overlapping labels are split between the two halves of clients, with half of the data for
424         overlapping labels going to the first set of clients and the other half to the second set. The non-
425         overlapping labels are assigned to the clients within each set, and the data corresponding to these
426         labels is evenly distributed across the clients, meaning that each client receives an approximately
427         equal share of the data for their assigned labels.
428       • Distinct: Each client is assigned a unique set of labels. For datasets with 10 labels, each client
429         receives 1 unique label, while for datasets with 100 labels, each client receives 10 unique labels.

531
      In this paper, we addressed the challenges of dynamic federated learning by introducing an opti-
532
      mization framework that adapts to dynamic client arrival and departure. Our approach accounts for
533
      these fluctuations and provides insights into how they influence shifts in optimal points. By estab-
534   lishing an upper bound on the optimality gap and proposing an adaptive initial model construction
535   strategy guided by gradient similarity, we demonstrated enhanced adaptability to the current client
536   set. Empirical results validate the robustness of our method across various datasets and dynamic
537   client participation patterns. One promising direction for future work is to refine the initial model
538   construction process, such that the model is only updated when beneficial or necessary, potentially
539   reducing computational overhead while maintaining performance. This opens avenues for more
      efficient FL systems that can dynamically balance the trade-offs between adaptation and stability.

1163
1164   Algorithm Details: Algorithm 1 outlines the pseudocode for our proposed “dynamic initial model
1165   construction for fast adaptation” algorithm. In this context, a “session” differs from the “number
1166   of global rounds.” A new round is initiated whenever there is a change in data distribution, such
1167   as clients joining or leaving, while the set of clients remains constant within a round. Each round
1168   comprises at least one global iteration. The implementation of Algorithm 1 requires specifying the
1169   number of global iterations per round T , the number of rounds for pilot model preparation P , the
1170   number of rounds dedicated to model training S, and the number of global iterations used to compute
1171
       the gradient reflecting the characteristics of the current dataset V .
1172   Initially, in line 1, training begins with a randomly initialized global weight w(0) , and several lists
1173   are initialized: Q1 to store trained models, Q2 to store computed gradients, and Q3 to store the
1174   two-norm values of differences between gradients. From lines 23 to 29, each client in the current set
1175   performs local model training based on the current global model, which may be either a new initial
1176   model or the latest model at the server. Upon completion of local training, each client transmits its
1177   final local model to the server, where global aggregation is performed using a weighted sum. From
