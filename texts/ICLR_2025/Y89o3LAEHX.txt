011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurate time series forecasting, predicting future values based on past data, is
014            crucial for diverse industries. Many current time series methods decompose time
015            series into multiple sub-series, applying different model architectures and training
016            with an end-to-end overall loss for forecasting. However, this raises a question:
017            does this overall loss prioritize the importance of critical sub-series within the de-
018            composition for the better performance? To investigate this, we conduct a study
019            on the impact of overall loss on existing time series methods with sequence de-
020
               composition. Our findings reveal that overall loss may introduce bias in model
               learning, hindering the learning of the prioritization of more significant sub-series
021
               and limiting the forecasting performance. To address this, we propose a hybrid
022
               loss framework combining the global and component errors. This framework in-
023            troduces component losses for each sub-series alongside the original overall loss.
024            It employs a dual min-max algorithm to dynamically adjust weights between the
025            overall loss and component losses, and within component losses. This enables the
026            model to achieve better performance of current time series methods by focusing on
027            more critical sub-series while still maintaining a low overall loss. We integrate our
028            loss framework into several time series methods and evaluate the performance on
029            multiple datasets. Results show an average improvement of 0.5-2% over existing
030            methods without any modifications to the model architectures.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Time series analysis is a powerful tool for understanding and forecasting sequential data points typ-
037   ically measured over time. It finds applications across various domains such as climate science (Wu
038   et al., 2023), transportation (Yin et al., 2021), and energy (Qian et al., 2019b), where recognizing
039
      patterns and predicting future values are crucial.
040   Remarkably, deep learning methods have proven highly effective in time series forecasting by pro-
041   viding robust backbones/model architectures like Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs) (Zhang et al.,
042   2022b; Chen et al., 2023), Transformers (Vaswani, 2017), and even Large Language Models
043   (LLMs) (Jin et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023), which are adept at learning complex patterns from large
044   datasets (Godahewa et al., 2021). However, besides the improvement of the model architectures,
045
      most of these methods also rely on time series decomposition(Cleveland et al., 1990; Qian et al.,
      2019a) to better capture various features. Among these, sliding-window decomposition is the most
046
      common method, which forms the basis of all model architectures discussed previously (Wu et al.,
047
      2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Nie et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2023). It decomposes a raw time series
048   into seasonal and trend sub-series, representing high-frequency feature (detailed changes) and low-
049   frequency feature (overall trend changes), respectively (Faltermeier et al., 2010). However, although
050   many methods utilize these sub-series, they still employ an end-to-end overall loss function. This
051   loss function computes the difference between the final combined sub-series and the true series. This
052   raises the question: Does optimizing this overall loss guarantee that the features of each sub-series
053   are equally well-learned? Or, could an optimal overall loss fail to optimize the performance of the
      decomposition-based deep learning model?

      To further investigate this, we conduct additional statistical analysis and case studies. Our statistical
055   findings reveal that deep learning methods employing time series decomposition often exhibit sig-
056   nificant discrepancies in losses across different sub-series on various datasets. Specifically, the loss
057   on the seasonal sub-series is frequently one to two times smaller than the loss on the trend sub-series,
058   which represents the overall movement of the time series. This disparity in losses suggests that the
059   worse trend component may lead to substantial deviations in the overall trend of the forecast. We
060   further illustrate this issue with a detailed case study.
061   To address this challenge, we propose a hybrid loss framework combining the global (the overall
062   loss) and component error (the sub-series losses). Inspired by the principles of distributionally ro-
063   bust optimization (DRO) (Wiesemann et al., 2014; Namkoong & Duchi, 2016; Duchi & Namkoong,
064   2019), we formulate this loss framework as a dual min-max problem. First, we construct a global
065   min-max problem to balance the overall loss and the losses across all sub-series, ensuring that while
066   minimizing the overall loss, the model also dynamically attends to the overall sub-series loss. Fur-
067   thermore, recognizing that the overall sub-series loss is composed of individual sub-series losses, we
068   formulate a second min-max problem to encourage the model to dynamically focus on potentially
069
      higher-loss components during training, thus prioritizing the optimization of critical components
      like the trend sub-series. We evaluate our loss framework on multiple datasets using existing model
070
      architectures and demonstrate an average performance improvement of 0.5-2% without requiring
071
      any modifications to the underlying model structures.
072
073   In this paper, we make the following contributions:
074
             • Our investigation reveals that the end-to-end overall loss function commonly used in deep
075
               learning for time series forecasting may not lead to optimal model performance. Sub-series
076            critical to the overall forecasting might not be sufficiently optimized under the overall loss.
077
             • We propose a novel hybrid loss framework that balances global and component errors to
078
               improve time series forecasting by dual min-max.
079
080          • The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our loss across diverse time series
081
               datasets, varying in both length and size, as well as across different models.

      As revealed in the previous section, a overall loss indeed introduces bias when training deep learning
165   methods on decomposed sub-series, potentially leading to significant errors, particularly in the Trend
166   sub-series. To address this issue, we propose a hybrid loss framework in this section, which incor-
167   porates component-specific (sub-series) losses alongside the overall loss, and dynamically adjusts
168   their weights to improve the overall and sub-series forecasting.
169
      Specifically, we define the overall loss as LossG (compute the MSE on the final results), the com-
170
      ponent loss as LossC , which is the sum of LossS and LossT for Seasonal and Trend sub-series
171   (compute the MSE on the Seasonal sub-series and Trend sub-series results), respectively. We use a
172   dual min-max problem to first balance the losses of LossG and LossC , and then balance the losses
173   of LossS and LossT . This aims to maintain the overall forecasting performance while also focusing
174   on and dynamically balancing the forecasting of sub-series with larger errors.
175
176   3.1   O PTIMIZATION O BJECTIVE
177
178   Drawing inspiration from distributionally robust optimization (DRO), our previous goal is to achieve
179   optimal forecasting for the max loss part in our hybrid loss framework by adjusting the losses of
180
      LossG and LossC , and the losses of LossS and LossT through dual min-max weighting. We
      define the first (min-max) optimization objective as follows:
181

185
      overall loss LossG and the component loss LossC respectively, and the LossC is associated with
186
      the second (min-max) optimization objective:

422   5   R ELATED W ORKS
423
424   The deep learning backbones in time series forecasting. Deep learning dominates time series
425   forecasting in recent years. These methods leverage different powerful neural network architectures
426   as backbones, adapting them to capture the characteristics of time series and learn effective predic-
427
      tive patterns from large datasets. For example, Prior to the rise of transformers, CNNs (Hewage
      et al., 2020) and RNNs (Lai et al., 2018) demonstrated the potential of deep learning to surpass
428
      traditional forecasting methods. Subsequently, transformers became the prevalent backbone, with
429
      models like Informer (Zhou et al., 2021b), Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), Fedformer (Zhou et al.,
430
        8
431       We also conduct this ablation study on FEDformer using the ETTh1 and ETTh2 datasets, presented in
      Appendix C.


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      2022), iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023), and PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022) specifically designed to ex-
433   ploit the sequential nature of time series. However, recent work suggests that simpler architectures,
434   like MLP-based models such as DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023), TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024), and
435   TimesNet (Wu et al., 2022), can also achieve comparable or even superior performance. Further-
436   more, the impressive reasoning and generalization abilities of recent large language models (LLMs)
437   (Jin et al., 2023) have spurred exploration of their potential for zero-shot time series forecasting (Jin
438   et al., 2023; Das et al., 2023). While these backbone architectures constitute the majority of time se-
439   ries forecasting research, many of them still employ time series decomposition techniques to better
440   capture temporal dynamics by learning representations for individual sub-series. Furthermore, these
441
      models still rely on end-to-end overall loss functions(Jadon et al., 2024), leaving the relationship
      between the loss and the effectiveness of the learning of sub-series unexplored.
442
443   The times series decomposition in time series forecasting. Time series decomposition is a crucial
444   component in many contemporary time series forecasting models, employed across various back-
445   bone architectures (Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Nie et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2023). Its core
446   principle involves decomposing a raw time series into two or more sub-series, each representing spe-
447
      cific characteristics of the original series. For example, the widely used sliding window approach
      (Faltermeier et al., 2010) decomposes a time series into seasonal and trend components, capturing
448
      the local fluctuations and overall trajectory, respectively. Other models explore alternative decom-
449
      position methods based on mathematical principles. Fedformer (Zhou et al., 2022) builds upon the
450   sliding window approach by further decomposing sub-series using Fourier transforms, focusing on
451   dominant frequencies. TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024) utilizes a multi-scale decomposition to cap-
452   ture information at different granularities. In this work, we specifically investigate how to enhance
453   the learning of decomposed sub-series, particularly focusing on the commonly used sliding window
454   decomposition method.
455
456
      6   C ONCLUSION
457
458
      We explore the potential shortcomings of existing deep learning time series forecasting methods
459
      that incorporate time series decomposition. We find that the end-to-end overall loss employed by
460   these methods may hinder the effective learning of decomposed sub-series, ultimately impacting the
461   final performance. Therefore, we propose a novel hybrid loss framework designed to address this
462   balance between different sub-series and the overall series in time series forecasting. By employing
463   a dual min-max loss framework, our approach dynamically emphasizes both the overall series and
464   the sub-series that require enhanced learning. This avoids the bias that occurs when focusing solely
465   on overall loss, which may lead to suboptimal model performance. Our framework achieves state-
466   of-the-art performance across a wide range of datasets and experiments demonstrate that this loss
467   framework can yield an average improvement of 0.5-2% across existing time series models.
468
469   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
470
471   Despite the work presented in this study, from problem identification to solution development for
472   decomposition-based deep learning methods in time series forecasting, our work has some limita-
473   tions that we hope to address in future work.
474
      First, although the investigated time series forecasting methods represent the current state-of-the-art,
475   they all rely on sliding window decomposition. While alternative decomposition methods are less
476   common, their performance under our loss framework may also need further investigation.
477
478
      Second, due to computational constraints associated with averaging results across multiple predic-
      tion lengths for each datasets, the datasets used in our preliminary and ablation experiments could be
479
      expanded further to provide more comprehensive validation of our loss framework’s effectiveness.
