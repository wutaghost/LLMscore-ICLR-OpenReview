011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013
               In human-AI interaction, one of the cutting-edge research questions is how AI
014            agents can assist a human to attain their desirable outcomes. Most related work
015            investigated the paradigm where a human is required to physically interact with
016            AI agents, which we call direct human-AI interaction. However, this paradigm
017            would be inapplicable when the scenarios are hazardous to humans, such as mine
018            rescue and recovery. To alleviate this shortcoming, we consider indirect human-
019            AI interaction in this paper. More detailed, a human would rely on some AI
020            agents which we call AI proxies to interact with other AI agents, to attain the
021            human’s desirable outcomes. We model this interactive process as multi-agent
022
               influence diagrams (MAIDs), an augmentation of Bayesian networks to describe
               games, with Nash equilibrium (NE) as a solution. Nonetheless, in a MAID there
023
               may exist multiple NEs, and only one NE is associated with a human’s desirable
024
               outcomes. To reach this optimal NE, we propose pre-strategy intervention which is
025            an action to provide AI proxies with more information to make decision towards
026            a human’s desirable outcomes. Furthermore, we demonstrate that a team reward
027            Markov game can be rendered as a MAID. This connection not only interprets the
028            successes and failures of prevailing multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL)
029            paradigms, but also underpins the implementation of pre-strategy intervention in
030            MARL. In practice, we incorporate pre-strategy intervention into MARL for the
031            team reward Markov game to model the scenarios where all agents are required
032            to achieve a common goal, with partial agents working as AI proxies to attain a
033
               human’s desirable outcomes. During training, these AI proxies receive an additional
               reward encoding the human’s desirable outcomes, and its feasibility is justified
034
               in theory. We evaluate the resulting algorithm ProxyAgent in benchmark MARL
035
               environments for teamwork, with additional goals as a human’s desirable outcomes.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
040
041
042   In human-AI interaction, the research questions are focused on how AI agents can assist a human to
043   attain their desirable outcomes (Dash et al., 2023; Niszczota & Abbas, 2023; Wang et al., 2023), and
044   ultimately how AI agents can provide societal benefits in manufacturing, healthcare, and financial
045
      decision-making (Amershi et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2020). However, most of these
      works belong to direct human-AI interaction where a human is required to physically interact with
046
      AI agents, which may not be applicable to scenarios which may be hazardous to humans such as
047
      mine rescue and recovery (Murphy et al., 2009), and humans are not allowed to physically join such
048   as remote-controlled interventional surgical robots (Wang et al., 2010). In this paper, we consider
049   indirect human-AI interaction, where a human relies on some AI agents which we call AI proxies to
050   convey their intentions, and interact with other AI agents (see Definition 1.1).
051
052   Definition 1.1. Human-AI interaction can be categorized into two following types:
053
          (1) Direct interaction, where a human and AI agents physically interact in an environment;

054
          (2) Indirect interaction, where a human would rely on some AI agents which we call AI proxies,
055           to interact with other AI agents1 in an environment.
056
057   One promising approach to address the indirect human-AI interaction is modelling this process as a
058   game-theoretical model, and it would be particularly interpretable if a Nash Equilibrium (NE) of the
059   game can be aligned to a human’s desirable outcomes, referred to as the optimal NE. Specifying the
060   optimal NE is a challenging problem since there almost always exist multiple NEs in a game model.
061   Related work has been explored under the term Nash equilibrium selection problem (Harsanyi et al.,
062
      1988) and Pareto optimality (Pardalos et al., 2008), to decide on a specific NE. Nonetheless, these
      methods encountered significant shortcomings which prevent seeking the optimal NE aligned to a
063
      human’s desirable outcomes: (1) It is infeasible to get comprehensive information from a human to
064
      articulate their intentions; and (2) These AI agents are not specifically designed to assist a human to
065   attain their desirable outcomes. To address these issues, this paper aims to design an approach which
066   we call pre-policy intervention, which intervenes AI proxies’ decision making to facilitate seeking
067   the optimal NE and thus attaining a human’s desirable outcomes.
068
      More specifically, we model the indirect human-AI interaction as a multi-agent influence diagram
069
      (MAID) (Koller & Milch, 2003), an augmentation of Bayesian networks to describe multi-agent
070
      decision making to maximize the total utility. In MAIDs, the multi-agent decision process can be
071   described as a directed acyclic graph, with variables (nodes) to describe decisions, contexts and
072   utilities. The pre-strategy intervention is an action that assigns a probability measure called pre-
073   strategy determined by a pre-policy, to newly added variables as parents of AI proxies’ decision
074   variable, which provides more information to influence their decisions, referred to as strategies. In
075   this MAID, in addition to the utility variables indicating a common goal among agents, we introduce
076   additional utility variables indicating human’s desirable outcomes, which can only be influenced
077   by AI proxies’ strategies. Our goal is finding the optimal pre-policy, so as to reach the optimal NE
078   induced by the total utility variables.
079   Contribution Summary. The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows: (1) We consider
080   indirect human-AI interaction in this paper, where a human would rely on AI proxies to interact with
081   other AI agents, to attain their desirable outcomes. We model this interactive process between AI
082   proxies and other AI agents via MAIDs introduced above, where the goal is to reach the optimal
083   NE indicating a human’s desirable outcome. (2) To mitigate the issue of multiple Nash equilibria,
084   we propose a theoretically-guaranteed method called pre-strategy intervention. (3) We propose to
085
      leverage causal effects to measure the performance of pre-strategy intervention, which also serves as
      an objective function to optimize the pre-policy. (4) We show that team reward Markov games (which
086
      can simulate multi-agent teamwork) (Littman, 2001) can be rendered as MAIDs. Underpinned by
087
      this evidence, we implement pre-strategy intervention in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL)
088   (a promising solution to solve team reward Markov games), referred to as ProxyAgent, but with
089   an additional reward function encoding a human’s desirable outcomes. We rigorously prove that
090   the informed shaping reward can effectively facilitate learning the optimal pre-policy. (5) Based
091   on the theoretical results from the perspective of MAIDs, we discuss the successes and failures of
092   two MARL paradigms: independent learning and centralised training. (6) We evaluate ProxyAgent
093   in Multi-Agent Particle Environment (Lowe et al., 2017) and JAX-based StarCraft Multi-Agent
094   Challenge (Samvelyan et al., 2019; Rutherford et al., 2023), where only partial agents representing
095   AI proxies are under pre-strategy intervention. The results confirm the effectiveness of our method.

      3.1   R ELEVANCE G RAPH
190
191   A relevance graph as shown in Definition 3.4 defines a directed graph describing the binary relation
192   between two decision variables. If there exists an edge D′ → D, it implies that the decision variable
193   D is strategically relies on another decision variable D′ . In other words, the decision rules for D′
194   is required to evaluate the decision rules for D. If there exist both D′ → D and D → D′ , then the
195   relevance graph is cyclic. Furthermore, if D and D′ belong to two agents respectively, their payoffs
196   depend on the decisions at both D and D′ . In this situation, the optimality of one agent’s decision
197   rule is coupled with another agent’s decision rule, and the only way is to make these two agents’
198
      decision rules matched (Koller & Milch, 2003), such as choosing both agents’ decision rules together,
      analogous to centralised training in multi-agent reinforcement learning (Oliehoek et al., 2008).
199
200   Definition 3.4 (Koller & Milch (2003)). A node D′ is s-reachable from a node D in a MAID M if
201   there is some utility node U ∈ UD such that if a new parent D̂′ were added to D′ , there would be
202   an active path (Appendix 8.2) in M from D̂′ to U given P a(D) ∪ {D}, where a path is active in
203   a MAID if it is active in the same graph, viewed as a Bayesian network. The relevance graph for a
204   MAID M is a directed graph whose nodes are the decision nodes of M, and which contains an edge
205   D′ → D if and only if D′ is s-reachable from D.

214   other AI agents. Owing to the fact that an induced MAID Mσ is a causal Bayesian network, we
215   formally define the causal effect of pre-strategy intervention, and introduce a systematic method to
      identify the optimal pre-strategy.

356   4.3.1   K EY I NSIGHTS INTO MARL PARADIGMS
357
      Having rendered a team reward Markov game as a MAID, we now give some insights into the popular
358
      MARL paradigms such as independent learning (Claus & Boutilier, 1998) and centralised training
359
      (Oliehoek et al., 2008), through the lens of MAIDs.
360
361   Independent Learning. It is not difficult to observe that the team reward Markov game is a
362   simultaneous move game. If each agent learns independently, it would lead to an issue called non-
363
      stationarity dilemma (Hernandez-Leal et al., 2019). Literally, this is caused by the situation that each
      agent is not informed with others’ decisions and independently updates its policy, with regarding
364
      other agents as part of the environment. If we express the team reward Markov game as a s-relevance
365
      graph as shown in Figure 2(b), a cycle would appear between decision variables at each timestep.
366   As per the discussion in Section 3.1, it is not guaranteed to reach a Nash equilibrium by solely
367   determining each agent’s decision variables, with a generalized backward induction algorithm. This
368   is in principle aligned with the temporal-difference (TD) learning (Sutton, 2018)[Chap. 6] and the
369   actor-critic algorithms (Konda & Tsitsiklis, 1999), which underpin the modern on-policy and online
370   algorithms for single-agent reinforcement learning. In turn, this association can well explain the
371   failure of independent learning, as a single-agent reinforcement learning algorithm.
372   Centralised Training. Recall that the non-stationarity dilemma above can be well solved by
373   centralised training (Oliehoek et al., 2008), which treats a team of agents as a whole executing joint
374   actions. Thereby, Markov game is reduced to a Markov decision process as a single-agent case. This
375   paradigm can be interpreted from the perspective of MAID, as transforming a cyclic s-relevance
376   graph to a component graph with the maximal strongly connected components (SCCs) as nodes, as
377   shown in Figure 2(c). More specifically, a maximal SCC includes the decision variables forming a
      cyclic s-relevance graph at each timestep. Koller & Milch (2003) showed that solving the acyclic

      component graph 2 via the generalized backward induction algorithm can reach a Nash equilibrium.
379   This is associated with centralised training employed to reach the maximum cumulative team rewards,
380   as a Nash equilibrium in a team reward Markov game (Littman, 2001; Oliehoek et al., 2008).

519   6    C ONCLUSION , D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATION
520
521   In this paper, we contributed a novel method for indirect human-AI interaction, building on the
522   concept of pre-strategy intervention within multi-agent influence diagrams (MAIDs). Our method
523   allows AI proxies to represent a human to interact with other AI agents, to attain their desirable
524   outcomes but still attempt to complete the task as much as possible. The pre-strategy intervention
525
      aims to provide more information to attain the human’s desirable outcomes. Based on the theory we
      established, we can implement pre-policy intervention in multi-agent reinforcement learning with
526
      theoretical guarantees and interpretation. We evaluate our proposed method called ProxyAgent in
527
      two benchmarks: Multi-Agent Particle Environment (Lowe et al., 2017) and JAX-based StarCraft
528   Multi-Agent Challenge (Samvelyan et al., 2019; Rutherford et al., 2023), where only partial agents
529   representing AI proxies are under pre-strategy intervention. The experimental results verify the
530   effectiveness of our method and validity of our theory established on MAIDs.
531
      Discussion and Limitation. As seen from Figure 3(b) and 5(b), there exists some outcome specified
532
      by intrinsic rewards which cannot be attained under some task goal specified by team rewards. This
533
      is highly dependent on the consistency between the design of intrinsic rewards and the definition
534   of team rewards. In the future, it is a valuable research avenue to study the relation amongst the
535   function class of intrinsic rewards, team rewards and the existence of the optimal NE to complement
536   the framework of MAIDs. On the other hand, as Figure 3(a) shows, the effectiveness of encoding
537   observations into graphs with pre-process by GNNs is limited, though in theory it should be more
538   effective. The main reason could be that the graph structures we formed could deviate from the
539   optimal structure. To remedy this issue, some research about causal discovery (Glymour et al., 2019)
      could be incorporated in the future.

       12.1      A LGORITHM 1 E XPLANATION
987
       The ProxyAgent framework is designed to guide the multi-agent system towards desirable outcomes
988    by modifying the reward structure for AI proxies and iteratively training the agents. Agents are divided
989    into two groups: those following a pre-policy and those following a normal policy. The algorithm
990    alternates between training both groups in a standard environment and one with additional intrinsic
991    rewards for the AI proxies, encouraging specific behaviors toward desired outcomes. Through
992    iterative training, agents can adapt and respond to the pre-policy, fostering dynamic interactions
993    between the two groups. In implementation, an additional graph-based feature extraction approach
994    using GNNs models dependencies between observation semantics, enhancing the learning process
995    by incorporating prior knowledge about the complex interactions in the systems. Furthermore, we
996
       observed that updating both groups of policies simultaneously, rather than fixing one group per stage,
       leads to more effective training.
997

1011   depend on other elements within the game3 . Algorithm 2 provide an implementation how we can build
1012

       incorporate prior knowledge about the game to help guide agents’ policy-making in practical. The
1013
       feasibility of learning the causal graph during training has been demonstrated by Richens & Everitt
1014
       (2024), where agents can learn the causal model implicitly during interaction with the environment.

1022   the features of each node based on its neighbors Pearl (2014). The output node features are then
1023   aggregated using a mean-pooling operation to produce a graph embedding. This embedding is used
