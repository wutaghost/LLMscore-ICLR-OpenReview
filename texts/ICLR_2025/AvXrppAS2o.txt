009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In limited data settings as in the medical domain, causal structure learning can be a
012            powerful tool for understanding the relationships between variables and achieving
013            out of sample generalisation for the prediction of a specific target variable. Most
014            methods that learn causal structure from observational data rely on strong assump-
015            tions, such as the absence of unmeasured confounders, that are not valid in real
016            world scenarios. In addition, due to evolving conditions and treatment approaches,
017            causal relationships between the variables change over time. Moreover in a clini-
018
               cal setting, symptoms often need to be managed before finding the root cause of a
               problem, which puts the emphasis on accurate outcome prediction. Consequently,
019
               prediction of a specific target variable from retrospective observational data based
020
               on causal relationships alone will not be sufficient for generalisation to prospec-
021            tive data. To overcome these limitations, we opt for the best of both worlds in this
022            work by learning a shared representation between causal structure learning and
023            outcome prediction. We provide extensive empirical evidence to show that this
024            would not only facilitate out-of-sample generalisation in outcome prediction but
025            also enhance robust causal discovery for the outcome variable. We also highlight
026            the strengths of our model in terms of time efficiency and interpretability. Code is
027            available at:

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Personalised medicine is a branch of medicine, which aims at providing individualised therapy based
033
      on patient’s phenotype. This is a closed loop process involving analyses of treatment response or
      outcomes, and treatment adjustment. The treatment response and patient outcomes are influenced by
034
      various factors such as disease characteristics, patient traits and the environment (Ambrosone et al.,
035
      2006). Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) enable the prospective evaluation of treatment response
036   in randomised groups of patients under controlled conditions. Therefore, RCTs provide a reliable
037   means to assess the cause-effect relationships between treatments and outcomes by eliminating con-
038   founding bias (Hariton & Locascio, 2018; Bhide et al., 2018). However, it is not always feasible to
039   conduct RCTs as they can be time-consuming, expensive and suffer from post-randomisation biases
040   (Fernainy et al., 2024).
041
      In recent times, machine learning-based methods have been successfully used for the prediction of
042   patient outcomes based on observational data (Lee et al., 2021; 2024; Babaei Rikan et al., 2024; Alaa
043   et al., 2017). Most modern machine learning methods find linear or non-linear associations between
044   observational data and outcome. As the associations are learnt on a sample of the data, larger sample
045   sizes increase the generalisability of the associations to unseen samples of the data (Chekroud et al.,
046   2024). Ideally, these methods are evaluated using observed outcomes or expert annotations, which
047   are both time expensive. Consequently, data in the medical domain is limited, unstructured and
048   incomplete. This in turn makes generalisation to out-of-sample data more difficult for machine
049   learning based outcome prediction methods (Goetz et al., 2024). The lack of transparency in some
050
      of these machine learning methods makes them difficult to interpret, compounding the challenges.
051   Causal structure learning is concerned with learning causal relationships from observational data.
052   Popular techniques employ machine learning methods to model the causal relationships between
053   the variables of observational data by imposing certain topological constraints (Zheng et al., 2018;
      Yu et al., 2019; Ng et al., 2019). Causal structure learning methods have the potential to improve

054
      interpretability in the medical domain by finding causal relationships between observed variables
055   and the outcome for various downstream analyses (Feuerriegel et al., 2024; Piccininni et al., 2020).
056   Consequently, they can bridge the gap between observational studies and RCTs. However, most of
057   these methods make strong assumptions about the data, which might not be valid in a real world
058   setting (Montagna et al., 2024).
059
      One such assumption is the absence of unmeasured confounders (Kalisch & Bühlman, 2007;
060
      Shimizu et al., 2006). This is not realisable without domain knowledge or time-expensive expert
061   intervention (Bica et al., 2021). Moreover, patient outcomes are also influenced by evolving knowl-
062   edge, treatment approaches and the environment (Futoma et al., 2020; Petzschner, 2024). Conse-
063   quently, relying solely on causal relationships to predict outcomes presents a significant challenge
064   for generalizing methods to prospective data, as these associations are derived from retrospectively
065   observed data. This limitation underscores the difficulty of ensuring that findings translate effec-
066   tively to future scenarios.
067   We overcome these limitations in this work by opting for the best of both worlds — causal structure
068   learning and machine learning-based outcome prediction. Our contributions are as follows.
069
070
      • We designed our approach to learn outcome prediction and causal structure simultaneously. In
      our framework, causal structure learning functions as an auxiliary task to support outcome predic-
071
      tion, sharing representations of the input through the hidden layers of our network architecture and
072
      employing task-specific heads for refined predictions.
073
074   • We provide empirical evidence to show that this learning strategy enables (i) interpretability by
075   visualisation of the learnt causal graph (ii) out-of-sample generalisation for outcome prediction.
076
      The primary focus of our work is to improve generalisation for outcome prediction in the medical
      domain. Causal structure learning functions as an auxiliary task to support outcome prediction.
077
      Despite this, we also provide evidence demonstrating the benefits of our approach in robust causal
078
      discovery for the outcome variable.
079
080   • We provide a case study by applying the method to survival analysis. We show that the proposed
081   framework improves interpretability of the model and generalisability to unseen data in real world
082
      scenarios. We also comment on the clinical relevance of the results.

      2   R ELATED WORK
085
086
087
      Most causal structure learning methods have been developed to learn causal relationships from ob-
      servational data based on the foundations of causal graphical model (Pearl, 2009). These methods
088
      can be classified into three broad categories: (i) constraint based methods that use conditional inde-
089
      pendence tests to infer the direction of causal relationship between variables (Kalisch & Bühlman,
090   2007), (ii) methods that use functional causal models to identify the causal structure by making as-
091   sumptions about the data distribution (Shimizu et al., 2006), (iii) score-based methods which either
092   adopt greedy search algorithms to determine the causal structure (Chickering, 2002) or impose topo-
093   logical constraints to learn the causal structure (Zheng et al., 2018). Most of these methods make
094   strong assumptions about the data (Montagna et al., 2024) which might not be realisable in a real
095   world setting.
096   Recent works (Kyono et al., 2020; Ge et al., 2023) use causal structure learning to improve general-
097   isation in supervised learning. Ge et al. (2023) build upon (Zheng et al., 2018) to learn robust causal
098   structures that are invariant to the data environments by getting rid of spurious correlations arising
099   from the data environment. This is contradictory to our aim of banking on the rich information from
100   evolving conditions to predict the target. Kyono et al. (2020) introduce a causal structure learning
101   based regularizer, CASTLE, for improving generalisation in supervised learning. They add a super-
102   vised loss term to the non-linear framework from Zheng et al. (2018) to learn the target variable.
103   CASTLE (Kyono et al., 2020) is one of the revolutionary works which demonstrated the superior
104
      performance of causality based regularaisation over commonly used regularisation techniques for
      deep learning such as L1-norm, L2-norm, dropout and early stopping (Tibshirani, 1996; Hoerl &
105
      Kennard, 1970; Goodfellow et al., 2016).
106
107   However, we observe several unsolved challenges of this work: (i) the feed-forward architecture
      used by CASTLE does not scale with the feature variables, (ii) CASTLE treats the target variable


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      reconstructed as a part of causal structure learning as the final output which hinders not only causal
109   structure learning but also outcome prediction. We address these research gaps by (i): adopting a
110   graph autoencoder-based causal structure learning method (Ng et al., 2019), which builds a single
111   graph for all the variables and scales with the number of features, (ii) we introduce an additional task-
112   specific head for outcome prediction, which exploits the representation shared with causal structure
113   learning to reliably predict the outcome and generalise well to unseen data.

118   (X1 , X2 , ....Xd ), score based methods learn an optimal causal directed acyclic graph (DAG), G(W)

121   (SEM) defined by the weighted adjacency matrix W ∈ Rd×d as in (Hoyer et al., 2008),

208   We present empirical evidence of our model’s generalization performance through a series of exper-
209   iments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, as detailed below. Furthermore, we demonstrate
210   the model’s ability to learn robust representations while enhancing interpretability and scalability.
211   Through a case study, we also show the clinical relevance of our model.
212   Experimental setup. We perform the experiments by splitting the data into 90% training and 10%
213   test datasets. The training dataset is used in a 10-fold cross validation setting to train the models.
214   We choose CASTLE network (Kyono et al., 2020), which has outperformed various regularisation
215   methods like like dropout, data augmentation and batch normalisation, as our primary baseline.
      We also compare our method with Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) (Pedregosa et al., 2011) and its
