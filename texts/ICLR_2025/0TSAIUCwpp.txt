009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion-based extreme image compression methods have achieved impressive
012            performance at extremely low bitrates. However, constrained by the iterative de-
013            noising process that starts from pure noise, these methods are limited in both fi-
014            delity and efficiency. To address these two issues, we present Relay Residual
015            Diffusion Extreme Image Compression (RDEIC), which leverages compressed
016            feature initialization and residual diffusion. Specifically, we first use the com-
017            pressed latent features of the image with added noise, instead of pure noise, as the
018
               starting point to eliminate the unnecessary initial stages of the denoising process.
               Second, we design a novel relay residual diffusion that reconstructs the raw image
019
               by iteratively removing the added noise and the residual between the compressed
020
               and target latent features. Notably, our relay residual diffusion network seam-
021            lessly integrates pre-trained stable diffusion to leverage its robust generative capa-
022            bility for high-quality reconstruction. Third, we propose a fixed-step fine-tuning
023            strategy to eliminate the discrepancy between the training and inference phases,
024            further improving the reconstruction quality. Extensive experiments demonstrate
025            that the proposed RDEIC achieves state-of-the-art visual quality and outperforms
026            existing diffusion-based extreme image compression methods in both fidelity and
027            efficiency. The source code and pre-trained models will be released.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Extreme image compression is becoming increasingly important with the growing demand for ef-
033
      ficient storage and transmission of images where storage capacity or bandwidth is limited, such as
      in satellite communications and mobile devices. Conventional compression standards like JPEG
034
      (Wallace, 1991), BPG (Bellard, 2014) and VVC (Bross et al., 2021) rely on hand-crafted rules and
035
      block-based redundancy removal techniques, leading to severe blurring and blocking artifacts at low
036   bitrates. Hence, there is an urgent need to explore extreme image compression methods.
037
038   In recent years, learned image compression methods have attracted significant interest, outperform-
039
      ing conventional codecs. However, distortion-oriented learned compression methods (Xie et al.,
      2021; Zhu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a) optimize for the rate-distortion func-
040
      tion alone, resulting in unrealistic reconstructions at low bitrates, typically manifested as blurring or
041
      over-smoothing. Perceptual-oriented learned compression methods (Agustsson et al., 2019; Mentzer
042   et al., 2020; Muckley et al., 2023; Yang & Mandt, 2023) introduce generative models, such as gener-
043   ative adversarial networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020),
044   to enhance the perceptual quality of reconstructions. However, these methods are optimized for
045   medium to high bitrates instead of extremely low bitrates such as below 0.1 bpp. As a result, these
046   methods experience significant quality degradation when the compression ratio is increased.
047
      Recently, diffusion-based extreme image compression methods (Lei et al., 2023; Careil et al., 2024;
048   Li et al., 2024b) leverage the robust generative ability of pre-trained text-to-image (T2I) diffusion
049   models, achieving superior visual quality at extremely low bitrates. Nonetheless, these methods are
050   constrained by the inherent characteristics of diffusion models. Firstly, these methods rely on an iter-
051   ative denoising process to reconstruct raw images from pure noise, which is inefficient for inference
052   (Li et al., 2024b). Secondly, initiating the denoising process from pure noise introduces significant
053   randomness, compromising the fidelity of the reconstructions (Careil et al., 2024). Thirdly, there is
      a discrepancy between the training and inference phases. During training, each time-step is trained

080   where consistency between the reconstruction and the raw image is crucial.
081   In this work, we propose Relay Residual Diffusion Extreme Image Compression (RDEIC) to over-

083   a novel relay residual diffusion framework. Specifically, we construct the starting point using the
084   compressed latent features combined with slight noise, transitioning between the starting point and
085   target latent features by shifting the residual between them. This approach significantly reduces the
086   number of denoising steps required for reconstruction while ensures that the starting point retains
087   most of the information from the compressed features, providing a strong foundation for subse-
088
      quent detail generation. To leverage the robust generative capability of pre-trained stable diffusion
      for extreme image compression, we derive a novel residual diffusion equation directly from stable
089
      diffusion’s diffusion equation, rather than designing a diffusion equation from scratch as Yue et al.
090
      (2023). To address the third limitation, we introduce a fixed-step fine-tuning strategy to eliminate the
091   discrepancy between the training and inference phases. By fine-tuning RDEIC throughout the en-
092   tire reconstruction process, we further improve the reconstruction quality. Moreover, to meet users’
093   diverse requirements, we introduce a controllable detail generation method that achieves a trade-off
094   between smoothness and sharpness by adjusting the intensity of high-frequency components in the
095   reconstructions. As shown in Fig. 1, the proposed RDEIC achieves state-of-the-art perceptual per-
096   formance at extremely low bitrates, and significantly outperforms existing diffusion-based extreme
097   image compression methods with fewer inference steps.
098   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
099
100          • We propose RDEIC, a novel diffusion model for extreme image compression that outper-
101            forms existing diffusion-based extreme image compression methods in both reconstruction
102            quality and efficiency.
103          • We propose a relay residual diffusion process that seamlessly integrates pre-trained stable
104            diffusion. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to successfully integrate stable
105            diffusion into a residual diffusion framework.
106          • To eliminate the discrepancy between the training and inference phases, we design a fixed-
107            step fine-tuning strategy that refines the model through the entire reconstruction process,
               further improving reconstruction quality.

113
114
      Learned Image Compression. As a pioneer work, Ballé et al. (2017) proposed an end-to-end image
115
      compression framework to jointly optimize the rate-distortion performance. Ballé et al. (2018) later
116   introduced a hyperprior to reduce spatial dependencies in the latent representation, greatly enhanc-
117   ing performance. Subsequent works further improved compression models by developing various
118   nonlinear transforms (Xie et al., 2021; He et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a) and entropy
119   models (Minnen et al., 2018; Minnen & Singh, 2020; He et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2021). However,
120   optimization for rate-distortion alone often results in unrealistic reconstructions at low bitrates, typ-
121   ically manifested as blurring or over-smoothness (Blau & Michaeli, 2019). To improve perceptual
122   quality, generative models have been integrated into compression methods. Agustsson et al. (2019)
123   added an adversarial loss for lost details generation. Mentzer et al. (2020) explored the generator and
124
      discriminator architectures, as well as training strategies for perceptual image compression. Muck-
      ley et al. (2023) introduced a local adversarial discriminator to enhance statistical fidelity. With the
125
      advancement of diffusion models, some efforts have been made to apply diffusion models to image
126
      compression. For instance, Yang & Mandt (2023) innovatively introduced a conditional diffusion
127   model as decoder for image compression. Kuang et al. (2024) proposed a consistency guidance
128   architecture to guide the diffusion model in stably reconstructing high-quality images.
129
130
      Extreme Image Compression. In recent years, extreme image compression has garnered increasing
      attention, aiming to compress image to extremely low bitrates, often below 0.1 bpp, while maintain-
131
      ing visually acceptable image quality. Gao et al. (2023) leveraged the information-lossless property
132
      of invertible neural networks to mitigate the significant information loss in extreme image compres-
133   sion. Jiang et al. (2023) treated text descriptions as prior to ensure semantic consistency between
134   the reconstructions and the raw images. Wei et al. (2024) achieved extreme image compression by
135   rescaling images using extreme scaling factors. Lu et al. (2024) combined continuous and codebook-
136   based discrete features to reconstruct high-quality images at extremely low bitrates. Inspired by the
137   great success of T2I diffusion models in various image restoration tasks (Lin et al., 2023; Wang
138   et al., 2024), some methods have incorporated T2I diffusion models into extreme image compres-
139   sion frameworks. Lei et al. (2023) utilized a pre-trained ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) to recon-
140   struct images based on corresponding short text prompts and binary contour sketches. Careil et al.
141
      (2024) conditioned iterative diffusion models on vector-quantized latent image representations and
      textual image descriptions. Li et al. (2024b) combined compressive VAEs with pre-trained T2I dif-
142
      fusion models to achieve realistic reconstructions at extremely low bitrates. However, constrained
143
      by the inherent characteristics of diffusion models, these diffusion-based extreme image compres-
144   sion methods are limited in both fidelity and efficiency. In this paper, we propose a solution to these
145   limitations through a relay residual diffusion framework and a fixed-step fine-tuning strategy.
146
147
      Relay Diffusion. Conventional diffusion models, such as denoising diffusion probabilistic models
      (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) and its variants, have achieved remarkable results in low-resolution sce-
148
      narios but face substantial challenges in terms of computational efficiency and performance when
149
      applied to higher resolutions. To overcome this, cascaded diffusion methods (Ho et al., 2022; Sa-
150   haria et al., 2022) decompose the image generation into multiple stages, with each stage responsible
151   for super-resolution conditioning on the previous one. However, these methods still require com-
152   plete resampling at each stage, leading to inefficiencies and potential mismatches among different
153   resolutions.
154
      Relay diffusion, as proposed by Teng et al. (2024), extends the cascaded framework by continu-
155
      ing the diffusion process directly from the low-resolution output rather than restarting from pure
156   noise, which allows the higher-resolution stages to correct artifacts from earlier stages. This de-
157   sign is particularly well-suited for tasks such as image restoration and image compression, where
158   degraded images or features are available. PASD (Yang et al., 2023) and SeeSR (Wu et al., 2024)
159   directly embed the LR latent into the initial random noise during the inference process to alleviate
160   the inconsistency between training and inference. ResShift (Yue et al., 2023) further constructs a
161   Markov chain that transfers between degraded and target features by shifting the residual between
      them, substantially improving the transition efficiency. However, its redesigned diffusion equation

196   3.1   OVERALL F RAMEWORK
197
198
      Fig. 2 shows an overview of the proposed RDEIC network. We first use an encoder E and anal-
      ysis transform ga to convert the input image x to its latent representation y. Then we perform
199
      hyper transform coding on y with the categorical hyper model (Jia et al., 2024) and use the space-
200
      channel context model Cm to predict the entropy parameters (µ, σ) to estimate the distribution
201   of quantized latent representation ŷ (He et al., 2022). The side information lp is quantized through
202
      vector-quantization, i.e., l̂p is the mapping of lp to its closest codebook entry. Subsequently, the syn-
203
      thesis transform gs is used to obtain the image content dependent features zc . Random noise is then
204   added to zc , which is the starting point for reconstructing the noise-free latent features z0 through
205   an iterative denoising process. The denoising process is implemented by a frozen pre-trained noise
206   estimator ϵsd of stable diffusion with trainable control network for intermediate feature modulation.
207   Finally, the reconstructed image x̂ is decoded from z0 using the decoder D.

287   3.4      C ONTROLLABLE D ETAIL G ENERATION
288
      Although the fixed-step fine-tuning strategy significantly improves reconstruction quality, it requires
289
      a fixed number of denoising steps in the inference phase, making it impossible to achieve a trade-off
290   between smoothness and sharpness by adjusting the number of denoising steps (Li et al., 2024b).
291   To address this limitation, we introduce a controllable detail generation method that allows us to
292   dynamically balance smoothness and sharpness without being constrained by the fixed-step require-
293   ment, which enables more versatile and user-specific image reconstructions.
294
      Since the compressed latent feature already contains image information, directly using stable diffu-
295
      sion’s noise estimator ϵsd to predict noise ϵsd (zn , n) results in low-frequency reconstructed images,
296   as shown in the second column of Fig. 8 and Fig. 17. Inspired by classifier-free guidance (Ho &
297   Salimans, 2021), we decompose the predicted noise ϵθ (zn , c, n) into a low-frequency control com-
298   ponent ϵsd (zn , n) and a high-frequency control component ϵθ (zn , c, n)−ϵsd (zn , n), and control the
299   balance between smoothness and sharpness by adjusting the intensity of the high-frequency control
300   component:
301
302
                               ϵ̂n = ϵsd (zn , n) + λs (ϵθ (zn , c, n) − ϵsd (zn , n)),                  (11)
303   where λs is the guidance scale. By adjusting the value of λs , we can regulate the amount of high-
304   frequency details introduced into the reconstructed image. In the experiments, we set λs = 1 by
305   default unless otherwise specified.

      4.3        A BLATIONS
467
468   To provide a more comprehensive analysis of the proposed method, we conduct ablation studies,
469   with the results presented in Fig. 6 and Table 2. For the baseline, we employ the same diffusion
470   framework as DiffEIC (Li et al., 2024b), where the denoising process starts from pure noise. As
471   shown in Fig. 6, our baseline performs similarly to DiffEIC (Li et al., 2024b).
472
      Effectiveness of relay residual diffusion. We first investigate the effectiveness of our proposed
473
      relay residual diffusion framework. As shown in Fig. 6 and Table 2(left), by incorporating the pro-
474   posed relay residual diffusion framework, we achieve better distortion performance and comparable
475   perceptual performance with 2/5 denoising steps compared to the Baseline, which uses 50 denoising
476   steps. The reason behind this is that starting from the compressed latent feature, instead of pure
477   noise, avoids the error accumulation in the initial stage of the denoising process and provides a solid
478   foundation for subsequent detail generation. Since the time required for the denoising stage is di-
479   rectly proportional to the number of denoising steps, incorporating RRD reduces the denoising time
480   by a factor of 10× to 25× compared to the baseline, as shown in Table 2(right).
481   Analyze of denoising steps. Next, we analyze the impact of denoising steps on “Baseline+RRD” to
482   select an appropriate value of L for FSFT strategy. As shown in Fig. 7, for λr ∈ {2, 1}, the number
483   of denoising steps has minimal effect on compression performance, so that we set L to 2 in this case.
484   For λr ∈ {0.5, 0.25, 0.1}, increasing the denoising steps achieves better perceptual results (lower
485   LPIPS and DISTS values), but leads to degraded fidelity (lower PSNR and MS-SSIM values). To
      achieve a balance between fidelity and perceptual quality, we set L to 5 here.

532   5        C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we propose an innovative relay residual diffusion-based method (RDEIC) for ex-
535   treme image compression. Unlike most existing diffusion-based methods that start from pure noise,
536   RDEIC takes the compressed latent features of the input image with added noise as the starting point
537   and reconstructs the image by iteratively removing the noise and reducing the residual between the
538   compressed latent features and the target latent features. Extensive experiments have demonstrated
539   the superior performance of our RDEIC over existing state-of-the-art methods in terms of both re-
      construction quality and computational complexity.

826   Additional implementation details. We use Stable Diffusion 2.1-base as the specific implemen-
827   tation of stable diffusion. Throughout all our experiments, the weights of stable diffusion remain
828   frozen. Similar to DiffEIC (Li et al., 2024b), the control module in our RDEIC has the same encoder
829   and middle block architecture as stable diffusion and reduces the channel number to 20% of the orig-
830   inal. The variance sequence {βt }Tt=1 used for adding noise is identical to that in Stable Diffusion.
831   The number N of denoising steps is set to 300. For the update of codebook, we use the clustering
832
      strategy proposed in CVQ-VAE (Zheng & Vedaldi, 2023).
833   For training, we use the Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) optimizer with β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999
834   for a total of 300K iterations. To achieve different compression ratios, we train five models with
835   λr selected from {2, 1, 0.5, 0.25, 0.1}. The batch size is set to 4. As described in Section 3.3, the
836   training process is divided into two stages. 1) Independent training. During this stage, the initial
837   learning rate is set to 1×10−4 and images are randomly cropped to 512×512 patches. We first train
838
      the proposed RDEIC with λr = 2 for 100K iterations. The learning rate is then reduced to 2×10−5
      and the model is trained with target λr for another 100K iterations. 2) Fixed-step fine-tuning. In this
839
      stage, the learning rate is set to 2×10−5 and images are randomly cropped to 256×256 patches. We
840
      fine-tune the model through the entire reconstruction process for 100K iterations. When λr ∈ {2, 1},
841   the fixed number L is set to 2, otherwise, it is 5. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA
842   GeForce RTX 4090 GPU.

856   Role of the diffusion mechanism. To further investigate the role of the diffusion mechanism in
857   RDEIC, we design two variants for comparison: 1) W/o denoising process: In this variant, the
858   compression module is trained jointly with the noise estimator, but the denoising process is bypassed
859   during the inference phase. 2) W/o diffusion mechanism: In this variant, the compression module

      E     L IMITATIONS
963
      Using pre-trained stable diffusion may generate hallucinated lower-level details at extremely low
964
      bitrates. For instance, as shown in Fig. 13, the generated human faces appear realistic but are
965
      inaccurate, which may lead to a misrepresentation of the person’s identity. Furthermore, although
966   the proposed RDEIC has shown promising compression results, the potential of incorporating a text-
967   driven strategy has not yet been explored within our framework. We leave detailed study of this to
968   future work.
