                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Disentangled representation learning allows data to be mapped to a latent space
013            where factors of variation can be individually manipulated. These factors define
014
               a direct notion of similarity between observations that naturally groups them into
               clusters with shared factors of variation. While this has been empirically shown to
015
               be effective on simple datasets, it is unclear how or when complex real-world data
016
               can be disentangled into representations that allow the same degree of manipula-
017            tion and clustering. To advance the field of disentangled representation learning
018            and clustering, we provide a new theoretical perspective by equating disentangle-
019            ment with clustering by using factors of variation as a measure of element-wise
020            similarity. This leads to a simple yet important observation: Instead of explicitly
021            clustering the elements of a dataset, we can implicitly cluster them by learning to
022            represent and generate the elements of each cluster. Furthermore, this observation
023            reveals that implicit clusters have a lower bound because (I) explicit clusters are a
024            subset of implicit clusters, and (II) implicit clusters can generate novel elements
025
               not present in the finite dataset through combinatorial generalization. Building
               on these insights, we derive an implicit neural clustering approach based on iden-
026
               tifying factors of variation in the latent space. We validate our findings through
027
               experiments on synthetic image data and empirical evidence from related state-
028            of-the-art works. This demonstrates the practical relevance of our approach and
029            promising potential for synthesizing complete datasets from limited data, address-
030            ing data distribution gaps, improving interpretability in cluster analysis, enhancing
031            SSL and classification tasks, and reducing data storage space.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Understanding and controlling the underlying factors of variation in data is central to disentangled
038   representation learning (Wang et al., 2022). Disentangled latent spaces not only group similar el-
039   ements naturally into clusters (Ding et al., 2022) but also allow precise data manipulation when
040   combined with a generator (Higgins et al., 2017). Recent advances in deep generative clustering
041   have shown the potential both to learn disentangled representations and cluster data apoints simulta-
042   neously, enabling the generation of high-quality synthetic data (Chen et al., 2016; Mukherjee et al.,
043   2019; Lee et al., 2020; Yu & Welch, 2021; Ding et al., 2022). These approaches move away from
044   traditional clustering algorithms, which rely on explicit partitioning based on learned or handcrafted
045
      features, towards generative models that leverage disentangled representations. In this context, gen-
      erative models for controllable image synthesis, such as GANs (Karras et al., 2020; Brock et al.,
046
      2019) and Diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Croitoru et al., 2023), now produce synthetic
047
      images realistic enough to improve downstream tasks like classification (Azizi et al., 2023; Fan
048   et al., 2023) and can help self-supervised learning (SSL) methods learn better general purpose em-
049   beddings (Chai et al., 2021; Jahanian et al., 2021; Tian et al., 2024). While prior work on deep
050   generative clustering has focused primarily on improving clustering in the traditional sense, a more
051   implicit approach has the potential to synthesize full datasets and in turn fill gaps in data distri-
052   butions, improve cluster interpretability, reduce storage needs, or enhance SSL embeddings and
053   classification tasks. This leads to our main research question: What if instead of explicitly clustering
      the elements of a dataset, we could represent and generate these clusters implicitly?

      To answer this question, we put forward a simple sampling method from a disentangled latent
055   space, which we call Implicit Neural Clustering. Rather than explicitly assigning data points to
056   clusters, we implicitly represent and generate the clusters with controllable factors of variation.
057   With Implicit Neural Clustering, we can theoretically
058   equate clustering and generative models with disentan-
059   glement, which leads to an implicit neural perspective of
060   clustering. Previous work by Zhao et al. (2020); Ding
061   et al. (2022) has already pointed out that using factors of
062   variation as a measure of similarity will naturally group
063
      data into clusters. We move a step further and stress that
      clusters emerge naturally from controlling factors of vari-
064
      ation in the latent space, i.e., control over a disentangled Figure 1: Latent traversals of factors of
065
      semantic latent space inherently dictates cluster member- variation changes cluster memberships.
066   ship (see Figure 1). This shift in perspective provides two key theoretical insights: (I) explicit
067   clusters are a subset of implicit clusters, and (II) implicit clusters have a lower bound, which is
068   given by the ability of an encoder to disentangle factors of variation and by the realism as well as
069   combinatorial generalization of a controllable generator.
070
      Implicit Neural Clustering samples and modifies encoded disentangled representations of elements
071
      in a dataset with atomic group actions to implicitly generate clusters. On top of the group-based
072   definition of disentangled representations by Higgins et al. (2018), we define an atomic group ac-
073   tion as a partition of a latent traversal direction. After an atomic group action modifies a latent
074   representation, a generator produces a data point reflecting this change while keeping other factors
075   unchanged. Using disentangling variational autoencoder (VAEs), we show that atomic group ac-
076   tions exist and can be identified using Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) as a partitioning algorithm
077   on each dimension of the disentangled representations of dataset elements separately. Furthermore,
078   probing for atomic group actions leads us to an effective and simple qualitative measure for disen-
079   tanglement, which is more informative than the commonly-used disentanglement visualization with
080
      Hinton Matrices (Eastwood & Williams, 2018; Montero et al., 2022).
081   We conduct experiments with different unsupervised and semi-supervised VAE-based disentangled
082   representation learning methods in the well-known dSprites (Higgins et al., 2017), 3DShapes (Kim
083   & Mnih, 2018), and MPI3D real (Gondal et al., 2019) datasets. Our experiments show the validity
084   of our findings by showing that atomic group actions can be identified in disentangled models and
085   used to implicitly cluster datasets. This demonstrates that the potential of synthesizing a full dataset,
086
      even from a limited or incomplete dataset, seems promising, which also may help increase the
      interpretability of cluster analysis and reduce storage space.
087
088   The main contributions of this paper are: (i) We introduce Implicit Neural Clustering, a simple
089   sampling procedure that allows to implicitly cluster a dataset. (ii) Based on Implicit Neural Cluster-
090   ing, we provide a theoretical analysis of what happens when we equate clustering with disentangled
091   representation learning, which leads to the discovery of a lower bound to clustering. (iii) We provide
092
      a practical implementation of Implicit Neural Clustering for disentangling VAEs and validate our ap-
      proach through experiments on multiple datasets. (iv) We show an effective qualitative measure for
093
      disentanglement, which is more informative than the commonly-used disentanglement visualization
094
      with Hinton Matrices Eastwood & Williams (2018); Montero et al. (2022).

130   2.1      D EFINITION OF D ISENTANGLED R EPRESENTATIONS
131
132   Implicit Neural Clustering builds on top of the established symmetry group-based definition for
133   disentangled representations by Higgins et al. (2018). We briefly introduce essential parts needed
134   from this definition. Let G be a symmetry group acting on a set of world states (ground truth factors
135   of variation) W , and let O be a set of observations (e.g., pixel space) and Z the internal agent
136
      representation of W . A generative process b : W → O leads from world to observation states, and
      an inference process h : O → Z leads from observation to an agent’s internal representation of W .
137
      In this context, we have a dataset D = {o1 , ..., oN } of observations oi ∈ O. We now define the
138
      inference process h : O → Z, as a parameterized feature extractor 1 hφ : O → F with parameters
139   φ, which yields a disentangled representation F of any o ∈ D. Under the assumption that G can be
140   decomposed into a direct product G = G1 × ... × GM , the representation F is disentangled with
141   respect to G provided that the following conditions are satisfied. (i) There are group actions that act
142   on F, · : G × F → F, (ii) There is a mapping d : W → F, which is equivariant between the actions
143   of G on W and Z: g · d(w) = d(g · w), ∀g ∈ G, ∀w ∈ W , and (iii) F decomposes into its factors of
144   variation F = F1 × ... × FM so that any Fi is only affected by Gi and invariant to any Gj , ∀j ̸= i.
145   Finally, we assume to have access to a parameterized generator Gθ : F → O with parameters θ that
146   transforms samples from the disentangled representation space F to the observation space O.

150
      rived from a disentangled representation F of D. As an initial intuition, if we assume that any o ∈ D
151

152   modifying the desired parts of the factor in the representation. Figure 2 provides an overview of
153   Implicit Neural Clustering with its main differences to explicit clustering. Following the definition

455   4    D ISCUSSION
456
457
      Our experiments show that Implicit Neural Clustering has a lower bound and is particularly lim-
458   ited by the assumptions on hφ regarding disentanglement (A.H), and Gθ (·) regarding realism of
459   synthetic samples (A.G1), compositionality (A.G2), and combinatorial generalization (A.G3). Es-
460   pecially for disentanglement, we notice a huge gap between unsupervised and weakly-supervised
461   approaches. While combinatorial generalization is feasible in relaxed synthetic settings and to some
462   extent in real-world data (Wiedemer et al., 2024a; Montero et al., 2022; Okawa et al., 2024), effec-
463   tive methods for learning disentangled representations and achieving combinatorial generalization
464   from complex real-world data remain elusive. In real-world tasks, general-purpose embeddings
465   learned through SSL methods like SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) or DINOv2 (Caron et al., 2021;
466
      Oquab et al., 2023) can learn effective representations that disentangle real-world data to limited
      extent. Empirical evidence of disentanglement in SSL representations, as shown by Bordes et al.
467
      (2022), demonstrates that training a generator on SSL features allows for concept swapping in rep-
468
      resentations, producing samples that reflect these changes. While our approach naturally extends
469   to SSL representations and would make it applicable to datasets like CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al.,
470   2009) or ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), atomic group actions do not exist in the simple form as in
471   disentangling VAEs. New methods to identify potential subspaces in these representations may lead
472   to new insights. A different way to apply our method on real-world data would be through finding
473   a way that effectively partitions the interpretable directions learned by latent navigators from GANs
474   (e.g., (Voynov & Babenko, 2020; Georgopoulos et al., 2022)), or Diffusion Models (e.g., (Yang
475   et al., 2023)). Finally, it is easy to see that our sampling procedure can be easily applied to gen-
476   erative models trained on ground truth factors of variation to synthesize datasets. We point to an
477
      important closely related work by (Okawa et al., 2024), where a conditional diffusion model is
      trained on ground truth factors of variation and combinatorial generalization is achieved. In this
478
      context, our work provides valuable insights on a research question by Jahanian et al. (2021): “If we
479
      have good enough generative models, do we still need datasets?” With Implicit Neural Clustering,
480   we can potentially generate any realistic synthetic variations of a dataset with a corresponding class
481   label, fill gaps in its distribution, and it can be a basis to replace datasets in order to save valuable cost
482   for storage and acquisition of data. Applications of Implicit Neural Clustering are not only limited
483   to datasets of images but can also be applied to completely different kinds of data, such as natural
484   language, videos, or time series. Negative societal impact can occur when a model can achieve com-
485   binatorial generalization under “full” disentanglement for, e.g, DeepFakes in the imaging or video
      domain.

      5   R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      Existing related works on disentangled representation learning, deep generative clustering, and con-
489   trollable image generation have shown the following points. (i) Factors of variation are embedded in
490   single (e.g., (Locatello et al., 2019; 2020; Wang et al., 2022)) or multiple dimensions (e.g., (Bordes
491   et al., 2022; Falck et al., 2021)) of a disentangled latent space, which can be learned with disen-
492   tangled representation learning approaches that are unsupervised (e.g., VAE-based (Higgins et al.,
493   2017; Kim & Mnih, 2018; Locatello et al., 2019; Falck et al., 2021), from pre-trained generative
494   models (Ren et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023), deep-clustering (Mukherjee et al., 2019; Lee et al.,
495   2020; Yu & Welch, 2021; Ding et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2020)), or (weakly) supervised (e.g., (Hris-
496   tov et al., 2018; Locatello et al., 2020; Montero et al., 2020; 2022; Wang et al., 2022)), (ii) Deep
497
      generative clustering approaches (e.g., (Mukherjee et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020; Yu & Welch, 2021;
      Ding et al., 2022)) can simultaneously learn a disentangled latent space, cluster assignments, and
498
      allows controllable generation of elements for each cluster with disentanglement. (iii) Learned dis-
499
      entangled representations are often composable and can be used to control the factors of variation
500   in images using latent traversal or the recombination/swapping of different latent dimensions be-
501   tween images (e.g., (Bordes et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Falck et al., 2021; Montero et al., 2020;
502   2022)). (iv) Both generative and disentangled representation learning models can learn combinato-
503   rial generalization in rare synthetic and real-word settings (e.g., (Okawa et al., 2024; Montero et al.,
504   2020; 2022; Wiedemer et al., 2024b)), which allows to synthesize novel cross-combinations be-
505   tween factors of variation not observed in the data. (v) Depending on the degree of disentanglement,
506   meaningful directions to traverse a disentangled latent space to control images can be straightfor-
507   ward one-dimensional and linear (e.g., (Higgins et al., 2017; 2018)), or non-linear multi-dimensional
508
      traversals can be learned from disentangled latent spaces in VAEs (e.g., (Ren et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
      2023)), GANs (e.g., (Voynov & Babenko, 2020; Georgopoulos et al., 2022)), or Diffusion Models
509
      (e.g., (Yang et al., 2023)) in the form of a navigator.
510
511   Based on the points above, existing methods can already effectively control and sample data for
512   implicit cluster generation. However, they primarily focus on improving clustering performance,
513   disentanglement performance, controllable generation, realism, or combinatorial generalization in
514
      isolation. Instead of treating these problems individually, with Implicit Neural Clustering, our work
      takes a new unified perspective by equating disentanglement with clustering and generative models.
515
      This reveals that existing methods are inherently limited by a clustering lower bound, given by
516
      disentanglement capabilities, realism of generated samples, and combinatorial generalization.
517
518
519   6   C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this paper, we present Implicit Neural Clustering, a sampling method for generating clusters im-
522   plicitly through disentangled representations. Through theoretical analysis and empirical validation,
523
      we show that equating disentangled representation learning with clustering and generative mod-
      els reveals that this method has lower bound, governed by the degree of disentanglement, realism
524
      of generated samples, and combinatorial generalization in generative models. This lower bound
525
      of Implicit Neural Clustering highlights strong potential for relevant future applications, such as
526   implicitly generating clusters driven by factors of variation in real datasets, synthesizing complete
527   datasets from limited data, improving interpretability in cluster analysis, enhancing SSL and clas-
528   sification tasks, and reducing data storage needs. At last, in line with many prior works, we also
529   underscore the importance of focusing on combinatorial generalization in future research.

      C     A PPLICATION OF I MPLICIT N EURAL C LUSTERING TO OTHER D OMAINS
825
826   Multi-partition clusters exist in many different domains. Different from the hard challenge of learn-
827   ing the factors of variation with an encoder hφ , they are often provided in ground truth or can be
828   obtained analytically. In such contexts, where factors of variation might be already available, can
829   be obtained analytically, or with a zero-shot classifier like CLIP, Implicit Neural Clustering is also
830   applicable when we relax the assumption on having an encoder hφ . To show this, in the following
831   experiment, we relax the assumption that there exists an encoder hφ that extracts factors of variation
832   from a given dataset D to show that the compositionality and combinatorial generalization require-
833   ments for our approach can be fulfilled in different applications. More specifically, we assume that
834
      factors of variation have been obtained by, e.g., applying an “optimal” encoder h∗ (e.g., a human,
      an analytical relaxation, or a zero-shot classifier Radford et al. (2021)) on D. In such cases, Implicit
835
      Neural Clustering is applicable to generate implicit clusters resembling the conceptual overview of
836
      our approach in Figure 2. While this relaxation might seem trivial, we can effectively show a novel
837   application of disentangled representations, while also providing a new perspective on clustering.
838
839   Experimental Setup and Models. To show different applications of Implicit Neural Clustering
840   with analytical or provided ground truth factors of variation and different domains, we evaluate our
841   approach with the following datasets. (i) the Grand Central Station (GC) Zhou et al. (2012) dataset
842   that consists of time series that resemble trajectories of pedestrians traversing a public train station.
843   In GC, we can vary the start and goal position of the agent to traverse the underlying environment,
844   which passed to a generator as continuous inputs. In this dataset, we train a goal-conditioned policy
845   with behavior cloning as the generator Gθ Kreutz et al. (2024). (ii) the CLEVR Relations Johnson
846   et al. (2017) dataset, which allows us to vary three ground truth factors of variation, the number
847
      of objects, as well as their respective X and Y coordinates. We use a pre-trained compositional
      diffusion model Liu et al. (2022) provided by the authors6 that is conditioned on natural language
848
      prompts. In this way, we show an application to text-to-image generative models. (iii) a synthetic
849
      dataset of simple shapes provided by Okawa et al. (2024) (in the remainder referred to as Simple-
850   Shapes). SimpleShapes has shape, color, and size as factors of variation, which are used as continu-
851   ous input to the generator. We train a conditional diffusion model 7 based on Okawa et al. (2024) in
852   SimpleShapes on the provided ground truth factors of variation. All models are trained from scratch
853   with a single 46GB RTX A6000. We will provide additional training details on hyperparameters,
854   code, and further setups upon acceptance of this publication.
