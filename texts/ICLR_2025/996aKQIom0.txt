011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce a benchmark for evaluating the role-playing capabilities of language
014            models. Our approach leverages language models themselves to emulate users
015            in dynamic, multi-turn conversations and to assess the resulting dialogues. The
016            framework consists of three main components: a player model assuming a spe-
017            cific character role, an interrogator model simulating user behavior, and several
018            judge models evaluating conversation quality. We conducted experiments com-
019            paring automated evaluations with human annotations to validate our approach,
020
               demonstrating strong correlations across multiple criteria. This work provides a
               foundation for a robust and dynamic evaluation of model capabilities in interactive
021
               scenarios.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Language models, which predict plausible language, have dominated natural language processing
028   since BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), with models like ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) showcasing
029   advanced conversational capabilities.
030
031
      In this paper, we focus on role-playing language models for entertainment purposes. These models
      are assigned specific characters or personas and are tasked with maintaining these roles while en-
032
      gaging and entertaining users. While there are other important applications of role-playing language
033
      models, such as training mental health specialists (Louie et al., 2024) or simulating human opinion
034   dynamics (Chuang et al., 2024), they are beyond the scope of this paper.
035
036   We introduce a novel benchmark for evaluating role-playing language models. From our experience
037
      with language models, we believe direct interaction is the most effective way to assess a language
      model’s conversational abilities. However, humans often lack time to test new models manually, and
038
      many popular benchmarks are limited to single-turn interactions (Dubois et al., 2024a; Hendrycks
039
      et al., 2021). These benchmarks are also becoming less reliable due to test data contamination (Deng
040   et al., 2024). To address this, we propose using language models to emulate users in role-playing
041   conversations and automatically evaluate the resulting dialogues.
042
043
      Our methodology, illustrated in Figure 1, involves three key components: a player model assum-
      ing a character role, an interrogator model simulating user behavior, and a judge model evaluating
044
      conversation quality. Our work builds upon existing benchmarks, such as EQ-bench (Paech, 2023),
045
      introducing an approach to role-playing evaluation.
046
047   Our contributions:
048         • We propose a benchmark for assessing the role-playing abilities of language models. The
049           combination of the following traits makes it novel:
050             – Multi-turn: All conversations have multiple turns to be closer to the real usage of
051               role-play models.
052             – Dynamic: Interrogator questions are generated by language models with sampling
053               and are not pre-defined. Each evaluation run produces different questions, making it
                  harder for models to memorize responses to make test data contamination harder.

092
093   Role-play capabilities and evaluation. Various commercial services exploit role-play abilities of
094
      language models, including Character.ai3 and Chai (Irvine et al., 2023). There are academic and
      community attempts to create similar systems with open datasets, code, and models, such as PIPPA,
095
      ChatHaruhi, Character-LLM (Gosling et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023), MythoMax4 ,
096
      or Magnum5 . Several static benchmarks for role-playing exist, including ECHO, InCharacter, and
097   CharacterEval (Ng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Tu et al., 2024).
098

100
      based on the environment (“situation” in our terminology) and the currently selected persona. There
      is also a very similar dynamic benchmark, RPBench-Auto6 . It is based on the same assumptions and
101
      features and has a structure similar to one of the versions of our benchmark, which is surprising since

117   using this method include AlpacaEval, EQ-bench, Creative Writing, and BiGGen Bench (Dubois
118
      et al., 2024a; Paech, 2023; Kim et al., 2024). The validity of these benchmarks relies on their high
      correlation with human annotations, specifically with Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024).

120   However, all these benchmarks rely on a single model as a judge, which may introduce various
121   biases, including the self-evaluation bias (Panickssery et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). PoLL (Verga
122   et al., 2024) authors aggregate evaluations from different language models in a similar way we
123   do, with average pooling. They show that ensembling different models for evaluation increases
124
      correlation with human annotations. There is also another more agentic approach (Chan et al.,
      2023) with a referee team.
125
126   Multi-turn evaluation and data contamination. Most benchmarks are single-turn, which contrasts
127   with the real-world usage of language models. There are multi-turn benchmarks, such as MT-Bench-
128   101 (Bai et al., 2024) and MT-Eval (Kwan et al., 2024), though they focus on specific capabilities,
129   and their evaluation procedures still differ from how humans implicitly rate language models.
130   Another major problem for the static public benchmarks is data leakage into the pre-training datasets
131   of language models (Deng et al., 2024). It’s challenging to avoid contamination since such tests are
132   usually stored online and considered “code” during pre-training. This can occur even without ma-
133   licious intent from model creators. The most obvious solution is to close benchmarks completely,
134   which requires trusting benchmark organizers, which is difficult in a highly competitive environ-
135   ment. Alternative solutions include regularly updating benchmarks with new test data (White et al.,
136
      2024) or dynamically generating test data using existing language models.

143   3.1   ROLE DEFINITIONS
144
145
146   Our framework comprises three principal roles: player, interrogator, and judge, inspired by the
147   Turing test (Turing, 1950). However, our approach differs in the number of agents, the player’s
148
      objective, and the use of machine-based interrogators and judges.
149   Language models can take three possible roles.
150
             • Player assumes the role of a specific character based on a provided character card.
151
             • Interrogator engages with the player within a given situation or towards a specific goal,
152
               simulating user behavior.
153          • Judge evaluates the player’s responses against predetermined criteria.
154
155   Role assignments are implemented through a combination of system and user prompts. We use only
156   models that support chat templates. For models lacking dedicated system prompts, such as Gemma
157   2 (Gemma, 2024), all instructions are incorporated into the user prompt.
158   This setup is asymmetrical since the player only gets the character description while the interrogator
159   only gets the situation information. This is intentional, as typical use cases of role-playing models
160   are asymmetrical. However, it is possible to modify it to make it symmetrical by providing character
161   descriptions and situations both to the player and the interrogator. Symmetrical setups might be
      useful in other domains.

      3.2      J UDGE
163
164   The scoring is single-point, with no reference examples or pairs. The judge used three main evalua-
165   tion criteria:
166
                • Character consistency: The player’s answers align perfectly with the assigned character;
167
                  they correspond to the character’s description.
168
                • Entertainment value: The player’s responses are engaging and entertaining.
169             • Language fluency: The player’s language use is of the highest quality, without any mis-
170               takes or errors. The player is perfectly fluent.
171
172
      These criteria reflect the main things we expect from the model during role-playing. In addition to
      them, we ask whether the player refused to answer.
173
174   We prompt a model to explain itself before giving a score, using quotes from the conversation. It
175   must also return a set of scores for every turn of the conversation.
176
177   3.3      V ERSION 1: COMBINED INTERROGATOR AND JUDGE
178
179   In the initial version, the roles of interrogator and judge were merged. This combined entity receives
180   the player’s character card, a situational context, and a list of evaluation criteria. It evaluates the
181   player’s most recent response and generates the subsequent user utterance.
182   We selected Claude 3.5 Sonnet as the interrogator/judge model based on the Judgemark7 results,
183   hypothesizing a correlation between creative writing and role-play capabilities. The evaluation uses
184   a 10-point scale for every criterion.
185
      The key issues of this approach are:
186
187             • Unrealistic user emulation: In many real-world use cases, users lack complete informa-
188               tion about character profiles, and to correctly emulate it, we should not provide complete
189
                  character information to the interrogator.
                • High costs: The task of the interrogator is much easier than the task of the judge, so it
190
                  doesn’t make sense to use the same expensive model for both of them.
191
                • Non-optimal decoding strategies: Some decoding strategies are good for judgment but
192               not for interrogation. For instance, a higher temperature benefits the interrogator but not
193               the judge.
194
195
      3.4      V ERSION 2: SEPARATED ROLES AND MULTI - MODEL EVALUATION
196
197   Recognizing the limitations of the combined approach, we developed a second version with distinct
198   interrogator and judge roles. It allows flexible control of costs and information flow.
199
200
      Furthermore, we identified the inadequacy of single-model evaluation. To address this, we imple-
      ment a multi-model evaluation system. This approach involves averaging scores from different judge
201
      models. In this particular setup, we used Claude 3.5 Sonnet and GPT-4o, the top two models, by cor-
202
      relation with manual annotations. We tried several more sophisticated approaches, but the average
203   worked best.
204
205   As an interrogator, we take GPT-4o Mini. According to the version 1 leaderboard (still available
206
      online), it has the same generation quality as GPT-4o but is cheaper. This version uses a 5-point
      Likert scale to match human annotations instead of a 10-point scale.

      4.2     L EADERBOARDS
231
232
      We calculated automatic metrics across language model families, both proprietary and open-source.
233   For each model, we report the mean scores per metric, proportion of conversations with refusals,
234   overall metric average, and confidence intervals (via bootstrapping) for final metrics.
235
236
      We evaluate each model using 64 conversations across 8 characters and 8 situations, with varying
      conversation lengths. The evaluation process is computationally efficient, costing less than $3 per
237
      model. Since the judge gives annotations for every turn, the overall number of annotations is not 64
238
      but 288. We do not want to make this sample bigger since it will increase the runtime and costs, and
239   we have budget constraints.
240
241   We covered diverse sources in selecting characters and situations, including computer games, TV
242
      shows, movies, books, and anime. Situations fall into two categories: common user patterns and
      attempts to break model behavior. In Appendix C, we estimate the fraction of real user situations
243
      covered by our set of situations.

246   4.2.1    L ENGTH PENALTY
247
248   Both language models and humans exhibit verbosity bias (Dubois et al., 2024b). The longer the
249
      output, the higher the chance of being positively evaluated. We use a length penalty similar to the
      Creative Writing8 benchmark to account for this. We calculate length-normalized scores for all
250
      models, penalizing models with a median length of player messages higher than a global median
251
      length.
252
253
254   4.2.2    T ECHNICAL DETAILS
255
      We utilize OpenAI-like API for all models. Some models are used directly from their providers,
256
      some are taken from OpenRouter9 , and some are hosted in different modes with RunPod10 .
257
258   We use the same sampling parameters for most players: temperature=0.6, top p=0.9 (Holtzman
259   et al., 2020). Some models, such as Gemma 2, frequently repeated phrases. We addressed this by
260   increasing the temperature and applying an additional frequency penalty. For the interrogator, we
261
      use temperature=0.8 and top p=0.95; for the judge, we use temperature=0.1 and top p=0.95.
262   We try to cover different popular families of models, namely OpenAI GPT (OpenAI et al., 2024),
263   Anthropic Claude, Meta Llama (Dubey et al., 2024), Gemini (Gemini et al., 2024), Gemma (Gemma,
264   2024), and Qwen (Yang et al., 2024). We also evaluate popular role-play and creative writing models
265   featured in OpenRouter and in the Creative Writing benchmark. We do not use base models, only
266   their chat versions.

296   4.3       C OMPARING TO OTHER BENCHMARKS
297
298   We hypothesize a correlation between creative writing and role-play capabilities of language models
299   because both creative writing and role-playing require similar capabilities: maintaining consistent
300   character voices/personas, generating engaging and entertaining content, producing fluent and co-
301   herent language, and understanding and working within given constraints.
302   If our hypothesis is true, there should be a correlation between our benchmark and the Creative
303   Writing benchmark. Since we have scores from both benchmarks for each model, we can directly
304   calculate the Spearman correlation between the rankings.
305
      Another benchmark we compare with is RPBenchAuto11 . Its scene-based setting is the closest to
306   our work. The major difference is that it utilizes side-by-side comparisons with a baseline model
307   instead of single-point evaluations.

      6        C ONCLUSION
433
434
      We acknowledge the limitations of this work, particularly the relatively small sample size and sim-
435   plified evaluation criteria. Firstly, the sample size of 64 conversations per model, while computa-
436   tionally efficient, may limit the statistical robustness of our findings. Secondly, the simplicity of our
437   evaluation criteria may not fully capture the nuanced aspects of role-play abilities.
438
      We hope this work will serve as a foundation for a family of benchmarks evaluating various abilities
439
      of language models. We believe that the future of benchmarks lies in interactions with other models.
440
      Language models are already better than humans in many tasks (Wang et al., 2019), and improving
441   through using other models seems to be the way to push them further.

444   E THICS STATEMENT
445
446   We acknowledge several ethical considerations in developing this benchmark. Our primary focus
447   is advancing model capabilities in various entertainment contexts, including potential applications
448
      in mature or sensitive content areas, which we view as ethically neutral when used responsibly by
      consenting adults. However, all the characters and situations used in the benchmark are designed to
449
      be appropriate for general audiences to minimize rejections from judge models, which often have
450
      strict content filters. We’ve strived for diversity in our character and situation design to mitigate
451   bias, though we recognize the inherent limitations in achieving full representation. Using language
452   models to evaluate language models’ performance presents potential concerns regarding echo cham-
453   bers or bias amplification, which we’ve addressed through multi-model evaluation. Our benchmark
454   utilizes only artificially generated conversations, thus avoiding privacy concerns related to real user
455   data.
