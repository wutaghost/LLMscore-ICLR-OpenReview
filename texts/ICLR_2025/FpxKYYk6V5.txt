011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Leveraging the development of structural causal model (SCM), researchers can
014            establish graphical models for exploring the causal mechanisms behind machine
015            learning techniques. As the complexity of machine learning applications rises,
016            single-world interventionism causal analysis encounters theoretical adaptation
017            limitations. Accordingly, cross-world counterfactual approach extends our under-
018            standing of causality beyond observed data, enabling hypothetical reasoning about
019
               alternative scenarios. However, the joint involvement of cross-world variables,
               encompassing counterfactual variables and factual variables, challenges the con-
020
               struction of the graphical model. Existing approaches, e.g., Twin Network and
021
               Single World Intervention Graphs (SWIG), establish a symbiotic relationship to
022            bridge the gap between graphical modeling and the introduction of counterfactuals
023            albeit with room for improvement in generalization. In this regard, we demonstrate
024            the theoretical limitations of certain current methods in cross-world counterfactual
025            scenarios. To this end, we propose a novel teleporter theory to establish a general
026            and simple graphical representation of counterfactuals, which provides criteria
027            for determining teleporter variables to connect multiple worlds. In theoretical
028            application, we determine that introducing the proposed teleporter theory can di-
029            rectly obtain the conditional independence between counterfactual variables and
030
               factual variables from the cross-world SCM without requiring complex algebraic
               derivations. Accordingly, we can further identify counterfactual causal effects
031
               through cross-world symbolic derivation. We demonstrate the generality of the
032
               teleporter theory to the practical application. Adhering to the proposed theory, we
033            build a plug-and-play module, and the effectiveness of which are substantiated by
034            experiments on benchmarks.

038   1    I NTRODUCTION
039
040   Causal inference is a specialized field that presents promising potential with respect to improving
041   machine learning methods, conventionally encompassing four steps: 1) causal model construction for
042   modeling causality in machine learning applications in a qualitative analysis manner (Liu et al., 2021;
043   Chen et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b); 2) causal model validation, including independence and causality
044   testing, to demonstrate the correctness of the causal model construction (Daniusis et al., 2012;
045
      Zhang et al., 2012; Lu et al., 2021); 3) causal model-based deconfounding approach implementation,
      which prospers in various machine learning fields, e.g., eliminating spurious correlations (Mao et al.,
046
      2021; Liu et al., 2022a) and performing counterfactual reasoning (Chang et al., 2021) in computer
047
      vision, learning the intrinsic rationale of the graph (Ji et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024) in graph neural
048   networks, overcoming selection bias (Li et al., 2023a) and popularity bias (Zhao et al., 2022) in
049   the recommendation systems; 4) deconfounding approach estimation improvement, focusing on
050   enhancing the accuracy of causal model-based deconfounding (Frauen et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024).
051   Benefiting from the establishment of graphical models, the advances of the structural causal model
052   (SCM) concentrate greater potential onto exploring the causal mechanisms behind machine learning
053   techniques, e.g., the analysis of independent relationships among variables and the identification of
      causal effects for various machine learning applications.

054
      In practice, the involvement of derived discrete data with extra stringent structural constraints
055   increases the complexity of machine learning application scenarios, resulting in a lack of adaptability
056   of conventional interventionism causal analysis theories, i.e., single-world SCM-based theory (Xia
057   et al., 2021; Zečević et al., 2021; Pawlowski et al., 2020). cross-world counterfactuals (Correa et al.,
058   2021; Richens et al., 2022; Shah et al., 2022; Alomar et al., 2023) provide a framework to estimate
059   “what-if” scenarios that transcend the observed world, aiding in better-informed causal inference,
060   which is crucial for understanding causal relationships in a more comprehensive manner, as it enables
061   the exploration of causality under different hypothetical conditions (Shalit et al., 2017; Ibeling &
062   Icard, 2020; Khemakhem et al., 2021; Sanchez-Martin et al., 2021; D’Amour et al., 2022). A focal
063
      issue is the exclusivity of counterfactual variables and factual variables in an invariant graphical
      model, challenging the construction of cross-world counterfactual SCMs. In this regard, Twin
064
      networks (Balke & Pearl, 1994; Han et al., 2022; Vlontzos et al., 2023) demonstrate a symbiotic
065
      relationship of graphical modeling in counterfactual and real-world scenarios. SWIG (Richardson &
066   Robins, 2013; Hernán & Robins, 2020) presents a simple graphical theory unifying causal directed
067   acyclic graphs (DAGs) and potential (aka counterfactual) outcomes for identifying counterfactual
068   queries. Yet, in this paper, we provide multiple scenarios of cross-world counterfactual causal
069   analysis, where the applications of representative approaches are limited, detailed in Section 3.
070
      To this end, we propose the teleporter theory to establish a complete graphical representation of
071
      counterfactuals, providing a general and simple approach for modeling cross-world counterfactual
072   causality. Concretely, according to the framework of probabilistic causal models, each variable can
073   ultimately trace its changes back to the exogenous nodes that influence it by iteratively applying
074   the structural equations of its parent nodes over a finite number of steps. Variables that have
075   consistent structural equations in both the real world and the counterfactual world possess equivalence,
076   which is determined as a teleporter, and thus we can construct cross-world SCM by using the
077   teleporter variables. Accordingly, we provide sufficient causal analysis from the structural equation
078   perspective, substantiating the theoretical correctness of the proposed teleporter theory. In terms
079   of theoretical applications, we focus on two main aspects: 1) we apply d-separation to test the
080
      conditional independence between any two cross-world variables of a cross-world SCM constructed
      by introducing the teleporter theory, which can prove the correctness and generalization of our theory;
081
      2) we use the teleporter theory to build the cross-world SCM and further leverage the cross-world
082
      symbolic derivation to compute counterfactual probability, which can avoid the complex calculation
083   of the probability distribution of background variables, demonstrating the effectiveness and simplicity
084   of our theory. Adhering to the proposed theory, we build a practical plug-and-play module to address
085   the intrinsic issue in the field of Graph Out-Of-Distribution (GraphOOD) (Gui et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
086   2022; Jia et al., 2024). The consistency and effectiveness of the proposed module are substantiated
087   by experiments on benchmarks.
088
      Our contributions are as follows: (1) We provide multiple motivating examples to elucidate the
089   incompleteness of current approaches in certain cross-world counterfactual scenarios with sufficient
090   causal analysis. (2) We propose a general and simple approach for modeling cross-world counterfac-
091   tual causality, namely the teleporter theory, which is proved as a complete causal analysis method.
092   (3) We provide sufficient evidence to prove the theoretical correctness of the proposed teleporter
093   theory by introducing the structural equation analysis. (4) We conduct extensive validations on
094   commonly adopted benchmarks, demonstrating the generalized applicability of the teleporter theory
095   from theoretical and practical perspectives.

137   3       L IMITATIONS OF EXISTING CROSS - WORLD GRAPHICAL MODELS
138
139   Before formally introducing our proposed new graphical model, we reviewed existing classical
140   and widely influential cross-world graphical models for estimating counterfactual causality: Twin
141   Network (Balke & Pearl, 1994) and Single World Intervention Graphs (SWIG) (Richardson & Robins,
142   2013). In this section, we provide detailed case studies to analyze the limitations and constrained
143   scenarios of the existing cross-world graphical models.

145   3.1     T HEORETICAL I NAPPLICABILITY OF T WIN N ETWORK
146
147
      Twin network (Balke & Pearl, 1994) is formed to model cross-world counterfactual causality by
      connecting the real world and counterfactual world, sharing exogenous variables between them.
148
      The constructed sub-networks of real world and counterfactual world are structurally identical,
149
      except that the arrows pointing to the intervened variable are removed in the counterfactual sub-
150   network. The specific construction steps are as follows: 1) duplicating the endogenous variables
151   X = {X1 , X2 , ..., Xn } from the real world as endogenous variables X ∗ = {X1∗ , X2∗ , ..., Xn∗ } in the
152   counterfactual world; 2) selecting the intervened variable Xi and removing all arrows pointing to the
153   counterfactual variable Xi∗ ; 3) connecting X and X ∗ through existing exogenous variables U to form
154   the twin network. Fig. 1(b) illustrates an example of cross-world SCM constructed by using twin
155   network, where the intervened variable is A, the value of the counterfactual variable A∗ is a, and the
156   existing exogenous variables are only U and W . We also provide an analysis in the Appendix B.2
157   for the example that constructs twin network using all exogenous variables.
158   However, the benchmark twin network encounters theoretical inapplicability in certain scenarios
159   of modeling cross-world counterfactual causality. Concretely, we explore the firing squad example
160   in “Causality” (Pearl, 2009b) p. 213, Fig. 7.2, as depicted in Fig. 1(a) and (b). We aim to test
161   whether Da is independent of A given B or C, i.e., A á Da ∣ B or A á Da ∣ C. The corresponding
      twin network of this example causal graph is illustrated in Fig. 1(b). To assess the conditional

      3.2   D EFICIENCIES OF S INGLE W ORLD I NTERVENTION G RAPHS
194
195   Establishing conditional independencies of counterfactuals in graphical models is a significant area
196   of research. A prominent example is the Single World Intervention Graph (SWIG) (Richardson &
197   Robins, 2013), which possesses this property and is covered extensively in relevant textbooks (Hernán
198   & Robins, 2020, Ch. 6). However, we identified a subtle limitation of the SWIG model: when
199   conditioning on certain factual variables of interest, it does not intuitively reveal independence
200
      relations from the graphical model. For instance, in Fig. 9 of the Appendix C, while the SWIG model
      can derive X á Y (x)∣L1 and X á Y (x)∣L1 , L2 (x), it fails to capture that X ̸ Y (x)∣L1 , L2 .
201
202   Additional comparison is presented in the Appendix C with three examples in Fig. 8–10, demon-
203   strating that our theory which is detailed in Section 4 can indeed construct a complete cross-world
204   graphical model: 1) Consistency with SWIG: Both provide a new graphical view of the back-door
205   formula, yet the twin network has a counterexample; 2) Superiority over SWIG: SWIG cannot
206
      intuitively display all variables in a single graph, making it difficult to encompass the conditional
      independence relationships of all variables in both the real world and counterfactual scenarios.

      Lemma 1. (Categories of counterfactual variables) Suppose the counterfactual world with the
      intervention do(X = x), and by removing all arrows pointing to the intervened variable X, we obtain
253
      the counterfactual causal graph Gx . The counterfactual variables can be divided into two categories:
254
      1) In Gx , the set of descendants of the intervention variable X = x, denoted as D∗ ; 2) In Gx , the set
255   of variables d-separated from the intervention variable X = x, denoted as Z ∗ . The values of the
256   variables in set Z ∗ remain the same as in the real world, i.e., Z ∗ = Z. In contrast, the values of the
257   variables in set D∗ differ from those in the real world. Hence, we denote D∗ as Dx to indicate that
258   its values differ from those in the real world.

261   dogenous variables as uppercase letters1 X, Y, ..., Z. Accordingly, we provide the detailed theoretical

      description of our theory.

264   in both the real world and the counterfactual world: Z ← UZ → Z ∗ . We implement a merging
265
      operation on these pairs of variables (in the cross-world SCM graph, this is represented by merging
      three variables into one variable Z), thus Z is called the Teleporter.

284   Theorem 1. (Teleporter theory for modeling cross-world counterfactual causality) Suppose we
285   intervene on the endogenous variable X. Let Wr = ⟨M, u⟩ denote the real world before the
286   intervention, and Wc = ⟨Mx , u⟩ denote the counterfactual world, where u denotes the variable of the

288   connected to form a cross-world SCM Wm by adhering to the following rules:
289
              • Rule 1: In the counterfactual world Wc , the endogenous variables Z, that are d-separated
290             from X = x, can be determined as the teleporter. Wm is obtained by connecting Wr and
291             Wc through the teleporter Z.
292
293           • Rule 2: All descendants of X in Wr , e.g., D, have the potential value influenced by the
294             intervention do(X = x) in Wc , e.g., Dx .
295           • Rule 3: The exogenous variable UZ associated with the teleporter Z is removed, and the
296             teleporter Z is introduced via the Merging Operation to connect Wr and Wc . The exogenous
297             variable UD associated with Dx is retained to connect D ← UD → Dx .

300   an example. Fig. 2(a) investigates the causal relationship between X and Y , where Z acts as a
301
      confounder (Pearl, 2009b), and all exogenous variables are depicted in the graph, which is treated as
      the real-world SCM Wr . Fig. 2(b) represents the intervention do(X = x) on X, where all arrows
302
      pointing to X are removed, which is treated as the counterfactual world Wc . Next, we clarify the
303
      variables in the counterfactual SCM Wc . According to Rule 1 of Theorem 1, in Fig. 2(b), the only
304   endogenous variable that is d-separated from X = x is Z ∗ , with its structural equation denoted
305   as fZ ∗ (uZ ). X is not a parent node of these variables, and therefore they are not influenced by
306   the intervention do(X = x). The value of Z ∗ in the counterfactual world equals that of Z in the
307   real-world graph in Fig. 2(a), thus it is called the teleporter. Furthermore, we determine that the value
308   of structural equation for Y in Wc , denoted as fYx (X = x, Z, uY ), is clearly different from the value
309   of structural equation for Y in Wr , denoted as fY (X, Z, uY ). Therefore, the meaning and value of
310   Y are different in the two worlds. According to Rule 2 of Theorem 1, the descendants of x consist
311   only of Y in Wc , denoted as Yx . For further discussion on the meaning of factual and counterfactual
312
      variables, please refer to the additional analysis in Appendix D.1.
313   To derive the cross-world SCM, which connects the real world and counterfactual world, we introduce
314   the teleporter theory. According to Rule 3 of Theorem 1, the exogenous variable UZ associated with
315   the teleporter Z is removed, and the connecting path between Wr and Wc , X ← Z ← UZ → Z ∗ → Yx ,
316   is merged into X ← Z → Yx . In addition, the exogenous variable UY associated with Yx is retained,
317   connecting Y ← UY → Yx . By utilizing the common teleporter Z shared between Wr and Wc as a
318
      connecting node along with the remaining exogenous variables, we derive the cross-world SCM Wm
      depicted in Fig. 2(c).

338       single graph G due to involving cross-world considerations. Twin network (Balke & Pearl, 1994) is
339       the first attempt to address this issue, yet such a method fails in certain scenarios, shows its theoretical
340
          incompleteness. In the following two subsections, we demonstrate that the teleporter theory can
          provide a complete graphical representation of counterfactuals.
341
342
343       5.1    I NDEPENDENCE BETWEEN C ROSS -W ORLD VARIABLES
344
          The cross-world independence between counterfactual variables and factual variables is difficult
345
          to derive from the separated real-world and counterfactual SCMs or the corresponding structural
346       equations. The significant advantage of the teleporter theory lies in the graphical representation of
347       counterfactuals, enabling us to analyze the (conditional) independence between any pair of cross-
348       world variables. Concretely, considering the inapplicability of twin network in Section 3.1, we
349       propose to demonstrate the theoretical completeness and generalization of the proposed teleporter
350       theory as follows. In the cross-world SCM Wm of Fig. 1(c) obtained through the teleporter theory,
351       we conclude that both A á Da ∣ B and A á Da ∣ C hold. This is because the path from A to Da ,
352       i.e., A ← C → B → Da , is blocked by B or C. Similarly, upon adding D as a condition, new paths
353       between A and Da are opened through the collider node D. Therefore, conditional on {D, C}, A
354
          and Da are not d-separated, satisfying A ̸ Da ∣ {D, C}. However, conditional on {D, B}, A and
          Da are d-separated, satisfying A á Da ∣ {D, B}.
355
356       Thus, the proposed teleporter theory can widely empower the (conditional) independence testing
357       between cross-world variables. To better demonstrate the implementation of independence testing
358       in the cross-world SCM built by using the teleporter theory, we propose the following Theorem 2,
359       summarizing the d-separation theorem for counterfactuals. The proof can be found in the Appendix
360
          F.
361       Theorem 2. (d-separation for cross-world variables under the teleporter theory) In the cross-world
362       SCM Wm constructed by following the teleporter theory, the path p between the factual variable X
363       and the counterfactual variable Yx is d-separated by the node set Z if and only if:
364       1. p contains either a chain structure or a fork structure, with intermediate nodes in Z, or
365
          2. p contains a collider structure, with neither the intermediate node nor its descendants in Z.
366
367       The set Z d-separates X from Yx if and only if Z blocks all paths from X to Yx .

          5.2    C ROSS -W ORLD A DJUSTMENT
370
371       The joint distribution of counterfactual statements requires computation, storage, and utilization of
372       the marginal probability of values of the exogenous variables, i.e., P (u). For example, P (Yx =
373       y, X = x′ ) = ∑{u∣Yx (u)=y,X(u)=x′ } P (u). Classic works summarize three steps for estimating the
374       counterfactual P (Yx ∣ e) in (Pearl, 2009b), where e denote the observed variable values: 1) abduction:
375       updating P (u ∣ e) using the fact e; 2) action: updating the SCM M to Mx ; 3) computing P (Yx ∣ e)
376       in the counterfactual world Wc = ⟨Mx , P (u ∣ e)⟩. However, obtaining the distribution of exogenous
377       variables U is extremely difficult. The teleporter theory provides a simple method to compute
          P (Yx ∣ e), facilitating cross-world adjustment.

      6     P RACTICAL A PPLICATION OF T ELEPORTER T HEORY
445
446
      To demonstrate the practical applicability of teleporter theory, we utilize our theory to model the set
447   of variables in GraphOOD (Gui et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Jia et al., 2024), and propose a method
448   to perform the counterfactual conditional probability estimation between cross-world variables.
449
450   Preliminary of GraphOOD. The area of OOD learning deals with scenarios in which training and
451   test data follow different distributions. Furthermore, GraphOOD problems focus on not only general
452   feature distribution shifts but also structure distribution shifts (Gui et al., 2022). Accordingly, Graph
453   neural networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Xu et al., 2018) are designed based on node features
454   and adjacent matrix to pass messages, which perform well in solving GraphOOD problems. Previous
455   GNN-based methods (Yang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Zhuang et al., 2024) aim at disentangling
456   the invariant part and the environment part of the input graph to find rationales, thereby addressing
457
      the problem of domain shift. The common learning paradigm is shown in Fig. 6, where the input
      graph is processed and split into two latent variables, i.e., the invariant representation z Inv and the
458
      environment-dependent representation z Env , and only z Inv is used for label prediction, which is
459
      achieved by leveraging the empirical observation encompassing the available labeled samples.
460
461
      6.1    C ROSS -W ORLD C OUNTERFACTUAL C AUSALITY M ODELING VIA T ELEPORTER T HEORY
462
463   The obtained invariant representation unavoidably contains significant environment-dependent infor-
464   mation due to the inherent inductive bias arisen from the learning paradigm of benchmark methods.
465   On the contrary, the desired invariant representation is expected to solely contain pure environment-
466   agnostic predictive information. However, such a representation can barely be acquired in the real
467   world yet feasibly obtained in the counterfactual world.
468   To this end, we propose to explore the causal mechanism behind both factual and counterfactual
469   variables, which is accomplished by modeling the cross-world counterfactual causality. Concretely,
470   we establish an SCM at first, as depicted in Fig. 5(a), which elaborates on the causality among the
471   variables in GraphOOD in the real world. In Fig. 5(a), there exist four endogenous variables in the
472   real world: the input graph X, the learned representation R of X, the predicted label Y and the
473   environment-dependent information E. UX , UR , UE and UY are four exogenous variables corre-
474   sponding to the endogenous variables. According to Theorem 1, the variable E can be determined as
475
      the teleporter, so the cross-world SCM is demonstrated in Fig. 5(b), where x represents the intrinsic
      causal subgraph, Rx denotes the environment-agnostic invariant representation, and Yx denotes the
476
      predicted label corresponding to the graph x, which is also the true label, since ideally, x and Rx
477
      only include environment-agnostic task-dependent information in the counterfactual world.

      7     C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
531
532
      We strive to explore graphical representation of counterfactuals and propose the teleporter theory
533
      to address the challenge of simultaneously representing factual and counterfactual variables in a
534   single SCM. The cross-world SCM constructed by using the teleporter nodes can well avoid the
535   theoretical limitations of current approaches in various cross-world counterfactual scenarios, thereby
536   demonstrating the completeness and generalization of the teleporter theory. However, the rules
537   that teleporter variables are required to adhere are quite stringent, and introducing such constraints
538   increases the complexity of constructing cross-world SCMs. In future work, we will explore to
539   simplify the proposed rules for determining teleporter variables and attempt to apply our theory in
      the scenarios involving multiple counterfactual worlds.

758   We recap the necessary preliminaries of causal background knowledge relevant to our work. For a
759   more in-depth understanding, please refer to the literature (Pearl, 2009a;b; Pearl et al., 2016).
760
761   Structural Causal Models. A SCM (Pearl, 2009b; Peters et al., 2017) is a causal model in a triple
762   form, i.e., M = ⟨X, U, F ⟩, where U presents the exogenous variable set, determined by external
763   factors of the model. X = {X1 , X2 , ..., Xn } presents the endogenous variable set, determined by
764   the internal functions F = {f1 , f2 , ..., fn }. Each fi represents {fi ∶ Ui ∪ P Ai → Xi }, where Ui ⊆ U ,
765   P Ai ⊆ X/Xi , satisfying:
766                                      xi = fi (pai , ui ) , i = 1, 2, ..., n.                            (7)
767   P Ai denotes the parent nodes of Xi . Note that, in SCM, uppercase letters conventionally denote
768   variables, and lowercase letters conventionally denote values of the corresponding variables, e.g.,
769   xi is the value of Xi . For ease of discussion, we omit such clarification in the following sections.
770
      Each causal model M corresponds to a directed acyclic graph G, where each node corresponds to a
      variable in X ∪ U , and directed edges point from Ui ∪ P Ai to Xi . It is worth noting that exogenous
771
      variables U have no ancestor nodes, and each endogenous variable Xi is at least a descendant of one
772
      exogenous variable.
773
774   Once we define the probability distribution of exogenous variables U , we can obtain the probabilistic
775   causal model. A causal world is a tuple ⟨M, u⟩ where u is a realization of the exogenous variables
776
      U , and a probabilistic causal model ⟨M, P (u)⟩ is a distribution over causal worlds.
777
      Interventions and Do-operator. The causal model M describes intrinsic causal mechanisms,
778
      characterized by the observed distribution PM (X) = ∏ni=1 P (xi ∣ pai ). Intervention3 is defined
779
      as forcing a variable Xi to take on a fixed value x, modifying the model M = ⟨X, U, F ⟩ to Mx =
780   ⟨X, U, Fx ⟩, where Fx = {F /fi } ∪ {Xi = x}. This is equivalent to removing Xi from its original
781   functional mechanism xi = fi (pai , ui ) and modifying this function to a constant function Xi = x.
782   Formally, we denote the intervention as do(xi = x), called the do-operator. It explores how causal
783   mechanisms will change when external interventions, or experiments, are introduced. We denote the
784   distribution after the intervention as PMx (X) = P (x1 , ..., xn ∣ do(xi = x)), where
785

786                          P (x1 , ..., xn ∣ do(xi = x)) = { j≠i                           .                    (8)

788   Counterfactuals. If Mx defines the effect of the action do(X = x) on M , what is the potential
789   change of another endogenous variable Y due to the intervention effect Mx ? We denote Mx as
790   the SCM of the counterfactual world (Pearl, 2009b) derived by adopting the intervention X = x.
791   The potential value of Y influenced by the intervention do(X = x) is denoted as Yx (u), which is a
792   solution to the equation set Fx , i.e., Yx (u) = YMx (u). Concretely, Yx (u) presents the counterfactual
793   statement “Under condition u, if X were x, then Y would be Yx (u).”
794
795   Path and d-separation. We recap two classic definitions (Pearl et al., 2016) to help us determine
796   the independence between variables in the SCM graph.
797   Definition 2. (Path) In the SCM graph, the paths from variable X to Y include three types of
798   structures: 1) chain structure: A → B → C or A ← B ← C; 2) fork structure: A ← B → C; 3)
799   collider structure: A → B ← C.
800   Definition 3. (d-separation) A path p is blocked by a set of nodes Z if and only if:
801
              1. p contains a chain of nodes A → B → C or a fork A ← B → C such that the middle node
802
                 B is in Z, i.e., A and C are independent conditional on B, or
803
804           2. p contains a collider A → B ← C such that the collider node B is not in Z, and no
805              descendant of B is in Z, i.e., A and C are marginal independent.
806
      If Z blocks every path between two nodes X and Y , then X and Y are d-separated, conditional on
807
      Z, i.e., X and Y are independent conditional on Z, denoted as X á Y ∣ Z.

1062         VARIABLE Dx

1064
1065
       The factual variable D refers to the collected observed data, encompassing different groups with
       various values of X. The counterfactual variable Dx corresponds to the unique group after the
1066
       intervention X = x.
1067
1068   For instance, consider the numerical example corresponding to Fig. 8. E(YX=1 ∣Z = 1) repre-
1069   sents the expected salary for individuals with a skill level Z = 1 if they had received higher
1070   education. In this scenarios, these individuals with Z = 1, there exist both the ones who have
1071
       received higher education (X = 1) and the ones who have not (X = 0). In contrast, the expectation
       E(Y ∣do(X = 1), Z = 1) refers to the group of individuals in the post-intervention world, which only
1072
       includes the ones who have received higher education(X = 1) (i.e., after intervening on X = 1, we
1073
       then condition on Z = 1). Since E(Y ∣do(X = 1), Z = 1) only represents the post-intervention world.
1074   E(YX=1 ∣Z = 1) represents a cross-world scenario, but do-operator cannot capture counterfactual
1075   queries: E(Y∣do(X = 1), Z = 1) ≠ E(YX=1 ∣Z = 1). E(Y ∣do(X = 1), Z = 1) can be easily con-
1076   verted into the counterfactual notation E(YX=1 ∣ZX=1 = 1), where ZX=1 explicitly designates the
1077   event Z = 1 in the post-intervention world. This leads to E(YX=1 ∣ZX=1 = 1) ≠ E(YX=1 ∣Z = 1),
1078   which is why we believe it is necessary to distinguish between factual variable Z = 1 and counterfac-
1079   tual variable ZX=1 = 1. Therefore, after intervention, we transform Z into Zx , and in the cross-world
       SCM graph, Z and Zx are represented as two distinct nodes.

1084   exogeneity) to control for confounding bias, which is where the teleporter theory and SWIG are in
1085   agreement. Furthermore, the teleporter theory, by knowing the independence of all variables, helps
1086   avoid incorrect adjustments of factual variables.

1119   D.3    C OMPUTATION OF C OUNTERFACTUAL Q UERIES
1120
1121   Another significant potential of Teleporter theory is in computing complex counterfactual queries.
1122   The standard approach, which encompasses Abduction, Action, and Prediction, albeit correct, is
1123   computationally expensive.
1124   Typically, when we aim to compute P (Yx = y∣E = e), we need to obtain the distribution of exogenous
1125   variables, i.e., ⟨Mx , P (u∣e)⟩. However, teleporter theory allows us to construct a cross-world network,
1126   reducing the problem to computing a conditional probability P (y ∗ ∣e) in an augmented Bayesian
1127   network. This computation can be performed using standard evidence propagation techniques,
1128   leveraging conditional independence and adopting a local computation approach.
1129   We have identified a neural network architecture constrained by twin network: deep twin net-
1130   work (Vlontzos et al., 2023), which is a neural network implementation of the aforementioned
1131   Bayesian inference techniques. Teleporter theory removes most exogenous variables while preserving
1132   the topology of the cross-world network, which can significantly reduce the graph size required for
1133   inference in the twin network for counterfactual queries. This demonstrates that our teleporter theory
       also has the potential to be combined with neural networks for estimating counterfactuals.
