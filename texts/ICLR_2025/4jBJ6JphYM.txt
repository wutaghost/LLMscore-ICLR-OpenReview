034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Structural injustice refers to circumstances in which social practices, social structures, or the
037   environment reinforce and compound prior histories of injustice (Carmichael et al., 1967; Sowell,
038   1972; Tilly, 1998; Rothstein, 2017; Alexander, 2020). We use the term “social determinants of
039
      opportunity” to refer to the specific aspects of social practices, social structures, or the environment
      that have a profound impact on the opportunities of individuals. When members of specific
040
      demographic groups have been the subject of histories of unjust treatment, their demographic
041
      membership often correlates with circumstances in which they face significant social impediments to
042   opportunity (Gee & Ford, 2011; Yearby, 2018; Robinson et al., 2020; Yearby et al., 2022; Chetty et al.,
043   2024). Because the social determinants of opportunity are features of places, institutions, policies,
044   or practices, they persist even if attitudes that cause unjust treatment have been subject to significant
045   reform. Their effects may not be tied directly to demographic group membership but to broader traits
046   (such as income level or job type) or to geographic areas. As a result, individuals within the same
047   demographic group, depending on their unique circumstances, may experience different impediments
048   to opportunity due to intersecting social determinants, e.g., various environmental impacts on
049   health in different geographic locations (Comber et al., 2011; Yeum et al., 2016; Tan et al., 2020).
050
      Conversely, individuals from different demographic groups in the same geographic neighborhood
      may encounter similar impediments to opportunity, e.g., poverty and pollution in the neighborhood,
051
      lack of educational resource in the community (Connell, 1994; Tilak, 2002; Rose & Dyer, 2008).
052
053   Previous research on algorithmic fairness has focused on protected features, e.g., race, sex, gender,
      and age (Romei & Ruggieri, 2014; Loftus et al., 2018; Corbett-Davies & Goel, 2018; Mitchell et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2018; Narayanan, 2018; Verma & Rubin, 2018; Caton & Haas, 2020; Chouldechova & Roth, 2020;
055   Makhlouf et al., 2020; Mehrabi et al., 2021; Zhang & Liu, 2021; Pessach & Shmueli, 2022; Tang
056   et al., 2023b). Various fairness metrics that are directly defined upon protected features are proposed
057   to estimate or bound empirical violations of fairness, based on observational statistics (Calders et al.,
058   2009; Hardt et al., 2016; Zafar et al., 2017), causal properties and/or quantities (Kilbertus et al., 2017;
059   Kusner et al., 2017; Nabi & Shpitser, 2018; Chiappa, 2019; Coston et al., 2020), and dynamic mod-
060   elings (Liu et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2023a). In terms of auditing potential fairness
061   violations, the focus on protected features is natural since these are the features in virtue of which
062   individuals might be subject directly to unfair treatment or might experience disproportionate burdens.
063
      However, the goal of mitigation goes beyond auditing fairness violations by seeking to intervene
      in ways that will reduce burdens and promote fairer outcomes in the future. Both protected features
064
      and social determinants of opportunity play important roles in the underlying causal mechanism,
065
      and therefore, need to be explicitly addressed when designing and evaluting mitigation strategies.
066
067   We consider procedural fairness that pertains directly to the data generating process itself (Rawls,
068   1971; 2001; Sen, 2011), and propose a framework that incorporates social determinants of opportunity
069
      for understanding and achieving procedural fairness. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
070   • We advocate explicitly considering social determinants of opportunity because they capture key
071     aspects of contexts and environments that have direct causal influences on specific attributes of in-
072     dividuals, overlooking which may result in incomplete or inaccurate claims for procedural fairness.
073   • We propose a modeling framework that incorporates social determinants of opportunity, and
074     demonstrate how our approach facilitates nuanced analyses of benefits and burdens experienced
075     by individuals with different demographic backgrounds as well as contexts and environments.
076   • Our findings suggest the importance of recognizing various factors in the underlying data
077     generating process that have procedural fairness implications, some are individual-level protected
078     features, others correlate with protected features but do not pertain to any specific individual.

086   2.1   A B RIEF I NTRODUCTION TO C AUSAL M ODELING
087
088   We use uppercase letters to denote random variables, lowercase letters to denote values taken by
089   variables, and calligraphic letters to denote corresponding domains of values. For instance, for a
090   random variable Z, it can take a value z from its domain of values Z. For two random variables
091   W and V , we say that W is a direct cause of V if there is a change in distribution of V when we
092
      apply an intervention on W while holding all other variables fixed (Spirtes et al., 1993; Pearl, 2009).
      We can represent causal relations among variables via a directed acyclic graph (DAG), where nodes
093
      correspond to variables, and edges denote causal relations between variables and their direct causes.
094

096   2.2   OVERVIEW OF D IFFERENT A DMISSION P ROCEDURES
097
098   There are three mainstream categories of admission procedures that were implemented in the history
099
      or are still in use today: quota-based admissions, holistic review with plus factors, and top-percentage
      plans. Variant forms of these procedures have been evaluated in law cases related to affirmative
100
      actions. At the core of these cases is the “compelling interest,” a legally necessary and highly justified
101
      purpose that must be demonstrated to validate government measures that differentiate individuals
102   based on race (Supreme Court, 1978; 2003a;b; 2013a;b; 2023a;b). Beyond jurisprudence, it is also
103   established that the way and the extent to which race and ethnicity is utilized in the admission
104   procedure should be under strict scrutiny, for instance, the studies on impacts of affirmative actions in
105   education from economic literature (Sowell, 1972; 2004; Zimmerman, 2014; Bleemer, 2022) and
106   from algorithmic fairness literature (Kusner et al., 2017; Nabi & Shpitser, 2018; Kannan et al., 2019;
107   Chiappa, 2019). In this paper, we consider college admissions as a running example and present
      nuances in implications on procedural fairness, when employing different admission procedures.

      We discuss in Section 3.1 issues of previous causal modeling for procedural fairness. In Section 3.2,

129
130   Definition 3.1 (Social Determinants of Opportunity). A social determinant of opportunity is a
131   variable representing an aspect of the data generating process, in which one or more characteristics of
132   the environment where individuals live or operate, that are not an attribute of any specific individual,
133
      have direct causal influence on the attributes of an individual.
134   3.1      C AUSAL M ODELING IN P REVIOUS W ORKS
135
136   Previous works in causal fairness represent discriminations in terms of problematic edges or paths
137   in the causal graph. As shown in Figure 1(a), one can represent causal relations among relevant
138   variables with a DAG, and further indicate objectionable aspects of data generating process with
139   red edges or paths originating from protected features (Kilbertus et al., 2017; Kusner et al., 2017;
140
      Nabi & Shpitser, 2018; Chiappa, 2019; Wu et al., 2019; Nilforoshan et al., 2022; Nabi et al., 2022).
      Three potential issues may arise from this modeling choice.
141
142
      The Recapitulation of Inappropriate Stereotypes In Figure 1(a), there are causal edges or paths
143
      originating from protected features to certain other variables, for instance, from sex or gender
144
      to annual income, and from race to educational status. While it seems intuitive to use the edge
145   Race → Education Status to capture the potential racial discrimination in education (Kusner
146   et al., 2017; Nabi & Shpitser, 2018; Chiappa, 2019; Nabi et al., 2022), the interpretation of the edge
147   according to the definition of causal intervention reveals its potential controversies. Specifically,
148   by definition of causality, this edge asserts that there is a difference in the distribution of education
149   status, when we “intervene” on individual’s race while keeping all other things unchanged.2 Such
150   modeling choice, although seemingly neutral from a technical perspective, unintentionally aligns with
151   the controversial ideology of racial essentialism (racial groups possess underlying intrinsic essences,
152   e.g., intellectual and biological, that make them different), which has been widely criticized due to the
153
      lack of scientific evidence supporting its claims (Roberts, 2011; Smedley, 2018; Delgado & Stefancic,
      2023). If a certain edge or path in the causal model does not reflect an actual real-world causal process,
154
      subsequent causal fairness analyses based on causal effects may not provide informative conclusions.
155
156
      The Limited Scope of Only Modeling Individual-Level Variables Compared to fairness notions
157
      based on observational statistics, causal fairness notions incorporate causal relations among features
158
      of individuals, such that interventional and counterfactual analyses can be conducted to reason about

187   3.2   O UR M ODELING A PPROACH
188
      Presented in Figure 1(b), we unpack the semantics of edges or paths originating from protected
189
      features in Figure 1(a), and replace them with causal edges from potential social determinants of
190
      opportunity (Definition 3.1) in contextual environments to the individual. For instance, in our running
191   example of college admissions, instead of using Race → Education Status to model potential
192   racial discrimination in college admission, we consider the household and geographic location
193   related to the individual (through applicant’s address) and model causal influences from contextual
194   environments. Historical or current racial discrimination is not instantiated through inherent biological
195   or intellectual differences across demographic groups. Instead, it manifests through patterns where
196   individuals from certain racial backgrounds are more likely to reside in areas with weaker socio-
197   economic profile and fewer educational resources (Sowell, 2004; Rothstein, 2017; Alexander, 2020).
198   More generally, disadvantage can perpetuate through many means, which are not necessarily limited
199   to particular properties of any specific individual. Even if we completely eliminate discrimination
200   in terms of directly rejecting admission on the basis of group membership, disadvantage can get
201   perpetuated through social determinants of opportunity (Carmichael et al., 1967; Sowell, 1972; Tilly,
202   1998; Rothstein, 2017; Young, 2008; Powers & Faden, 2019; Alexander, 2020). As we will see in
203   Section 4, our approach captures nuances in data generating processes and enables more fine-grained
204   and to-the-point analyses for procedural fairness.

298   The quota-based admission is a type of affirmative-action admission strategy that sets specific limits
299   on the number of admissions for applicants from different demographic backgrounds. This admission
300   strategy was originally designed to rectify historical injustice by directly setting aside admission
301   quotas to increase the representation of URM students. However, due to the rigid nature of the quota-
302

303

304

305

335   4.3   H OLISTIC R EVIEW WITH P LUS FACTORS
336
337   Holistic review with plus factors is another type of affirmative-action admission strategy, involving
338
      consideration of multiple factors that together define each individual applicant. The key element of this
      process is the use of plus factors, where certain characteristics, for instance, race and ethnic group, are
339
      given additional weight to promote diversity in the student body and rectify historical disadvantages.
340

342   & UNC (2023) (Supreme Court, 2023a;b), effectively banning race-conscious admissions.
343
344

391   The top-percentage plans are college admission policies that guarantee admission to students who

393   generally not considered traditional affirmative-action admission strategies. Instead, these policies
394   are race-neutral alternatives aiming to promote diversity by drawing students from a wide range of
395   schools with different socioeconomic and geographic backgrounds, without explicitly considering
396
      race. A prominent example is the University of Texas’s Top 10% Rule, which guarantees admission to

397
      of University of California, which was introduced after the 1996 California Proposition 209 banned

421   4.5   R EMARK ON P ROCEDURAL FAIRNESS I MPLICATIONS OF D IFFERENT P ROCEDURES
422
423
      Although all three types of admission procedures share the goal of promoting fairness and diversity
      within the student body, the limited availability of selective admissions leads to varying redistributions
424
      of benefits and burdens among applicants. Quota-based admissions, while being rigid and mechanical,
425
      are more direct in reserving spots for URM applicants. However, as an unintended consequence, non-
426   URM applicants from less well-off areas can be further disadvantaged when quota-based admissions
427   are employed (Theorem 4.5). Holistic review with plus factors, in comparison, takes a more flexible
428   approach when granting preferences to URM applicants. However, the increase in selective admission
429   probability for URM applicants, which is reallocated from non-URM applicants, rewards the rich re-
430   gion more than the poor region (Theorem 4.6). Top-percentage plans, which provide race-neutral alter-
431   natives to the previous two affirmative-action strategies, transfer opportunities from rich region to poor
      region, operating in proportion to natural region-specific demographic compositions (Theorem 4.7).

            6                                     C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
523
524        In this paper, we advocate the explicit consideration of social determinants of opportunity in causal
525
           mechanisms for the purpose of understanding and achieving procedural fairness. We propose
           a modeling framework that encompasses variables characterizing influences from contexts and
526
           environments to individuals, namely, social determinants of opportunity. In the running example
527
           of college admissions, we demonstrate nuanced analyses that our framework facilitates, and explicate
528        procedural fairness implications when different decision-making procedures are employed.
529
530      Because social determinants correlate with protected features, explicitly considering social deter-
531
         minants through which structural injustice potentially perpetuates can help us better understand
         the underlying data generating process. This, in turn, facilitates more precise and comprehensive
532
         characterizations of procedural fairness implications, and makes it more transparent to see benefits
533
         and burdens experienced by individuals with different demographic backgrounds as well as contexts
534      and environments, when they are subject to different algorithmic decision-making procedures. Future
535      works naturally include designing and utilizing appropriate measurements of social determinants of
536      opportunity to develop fairness auditing and mitigation strategies, so that we can achieve procedural
537      fairness in an effective, principled, and transparent way.

      A.1     FAIRNESS N OTIONS BASED ON O BSERVATIONAL S TATISTICS AND C AUSAL A NALYSIS
954
955
956   Various notions have been proposed in the algorithmic fairness literature to characterize fairness with
957
      respect to the prediction or the prediction-based decision-making (Dwork et al., 2012; Hardt et al.,
      2016; Chouldechova, 2017; Zafar et al., 2017), and also notions that are based on causal modeling
958
      of the data generating process (Kusner et al., 2017; Kilbertus et al., 2017; Nabi & Shpitser, 2018;
959
      Chiappa, 2019; Wu et al., 2019; Coston et al., 2020). Recent survey papers have presented overviews
960   on fairness notions in static settings (Loftus et al., 2018; Makhlouf et al., 2020; Mehrabi et al., 2021),
961   dynamic settings (Zhang & Liu, 2021), and also the connection between algorithmic fairness and the
962   literature from moral and political philosophy (Tang et al., 2023b).
963
      The type of information utilized reflects different emphases of algorithmic fairness studies. Notions
964
      based on observational statistics analyze the fairness implications in terms of the outcome of predic-
965
      tions or decision-making (Dwork et al., 2012; Hardt et al., 2016; Chouldechova, 2017; Zafar et al.,
966   2017; Kearns et al., 2018; Foulds et al., 2020). Approaches that capture causal influences from the
967   protected feature to the target variable at the individual-level (Kusner et al., 2017; Kilbertus et al.,
968   2017; Nabi & Shpitser, 2018; Chiappa, 2019; Wu et al., 2019) and the (sub-)group-level (Coston
969   et al., 2020; Imai & Jiang, 2020; Mishler et al., 2021) put more emphases on the procedural aspect of
970   algorithmic fairness inquiries, focusing on the data generating process of interest. Recent work has
971   also proposed to address procedural fairness over all objectionable data generating components (Tang
      et al., 2024) according to John Rawls’s advocacy for pure procedural justice (Rawls, 1971; 2001).

982    Pearl, 2009; Peters et al., 2017), we use “counter-factual” (with a hyphen, as an opposite to “factual”)
983    to denote that something does not happen in the current reality. Previous causal fairness approaches
984    have utilized interventional (Kilbertus et al., 2017; Nabi & Shpitser, 2018; Nabi et al., 2019; 2022)

987    Question A.1 (Counter-Factual Analysis Starting from Protected Features). Under certain

991    Based on estimating or bounding certain causal effects among variables, including the protected
992
       feature, the (predicted) outcome, and certain variables that are closely related to but not the protected
       feature itself, e.g., proxy variables (Kilbertus et al., 2017), redlining attributes (Zhang et al., 2017),

       admissible variables (Salimi et al., 2019), and so on, the fairness violation is quantified in terms of
994
       causal effects between the protected feature and the (predicted) outcome. There is a reductive focus
995    solely upon the protected feature when modeling the discrimination. For instance, it is a common
996    practice for causal fairness notions to consider varying the value of protected feature (Kilbertus et al.,
997    2017; Kusner et al., 2017; Nabi & Shpitser, 2018; Nabi et al., 2019; 2022; Chiappa, 2019; Wu et al.,
998    2019) as the starting point. Recently, Tang et al. (2024) have also proposed to consider not only edges
999    or paths originating from the protected feature, but also all objectionable components in the data
1000   generating process, to address procedural fairness.
1001   However, the modeling choice of “summarizing” discrimination only through edges/paths originating
1002   from protected feature, or solely among individual-level variables, falls short of the need to capture
1003   procedural unfairness and structural injustice. The characteristics of the environment and the context
1004   that individuals operate in typically do not correspond to individual-level attributes, and are not
1005   considered in previous literature. Different from causal fairness approaches, our framework explicitly
1006   incorporates the influence of contextual environments, and aims to address the following question:
1007   Question A.2 (Factual Analysis Starting from Social Determinants of Opportunity). Under cer-
1008   tain conditions and assumptions, what are the aspects of the data generating process that characterize
1009   the influence from contextual environments to the individual?

1017   A.2.2    N O C ONFLICT IN P RINCIPLE WITH C AUSAL FAIRNESS
1018
1019   In principle, our framework is not in conflict with previous causal fairness approaches, and the two
1020   complement each other. Both our framework and previous causal fairness approaches aim to model
1021   the data generating process, and both emphasize the procedural fairness implications.
1022   However, our framework extends the scope of consideration beyond individual-level variables, and
1023   explicitly incorporates the influence of contextual environments. For instance, when operationalizing
1024   our framework, we do not drop relevant variables, e.g., the Address of an individual, which is often
1025   omitted in previous literature (Kilbertus et al., 2017; Kusner et al., 2017; Nabi & Shpitser, 2018;
       Chiappa, 2019; Wu et al., 2019; Mary et al., 2019; Ding et al., 2021). Furthermore, the findings of

1035   The term “structure” and “structural” are utilized in different ways by related disciplines. For the
1036   literature of causal learning and reasoning, the term “structure” and “structural” are often used to
1037
       describe how causal structures look like among variables of interest (Spirtes et al., 1993; Pearl, 2009;

1039
       “structural” is used to denote the systemic ways in which society is organized, e.g., through policies,
1040   laws, and social norms, that perpetuate discrimination and animus towards certain groups (Carmichael
1041   et al., 1967; Sowell, 1972; Tilly, 1998; Yearby, 2018; Robinson et al., 2020; Alexander, 2020; Yearby
1042   et al., 2022). There are interests in the social determinants of health literature to use DAGs as a tool
1043   for illustrative purposes, abstracting key concepts or areas that are interrelated at a high level, and
1044   modeling the mechanism through which structural forms of discriminations get realized (racism,
1045   sexism, etc.) (Robinson et al., 2020; Yearby et al., 2022).
