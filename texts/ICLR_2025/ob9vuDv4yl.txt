                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Image restoration aims to recover a high-quality clean image from its degraded
012
               version. Recent progress in image restoration has demonstrated the effectiveness
               of All-in-One image restoration models in addressing various unknown degrada-
013
               tions simultaneously. However, these existing methods typically utilize the same
014
               parameters to tackle images with different types of degradation, forcing the model
015            to balance the performance between different tasks and limiting its performance on
016            each task. To alleviate this issue, we propose HAIR, a Hypernetworks-based All-
017            in-One Image Restoration plug-and-play method that generates parameters based
018            on the input image and thus makes the model to adapt to specific degradation dy-
019            namically. Specifically, HAIR consists of two main components, i.e., Classifier and
020            Hyper Selecting Net (HSN). The Classifier is a simple image classification network
021            used to generate a Global Information Vector (GIV) that contains the degradation
022            information of the input image, and the HSN is a simple fully-connected neural
023
               network that receives the GIV and outputs parameters for the corresponding mod-
               ules. Extensive experiments demonstrate that HAIR can significantly improve the
024
               performance of existing image restoration models in a plug-and-play manner, both
025
               in single-task and All-in-One settings. Notably, our proposed model Res-HAIR,
026            which integrates HAIR into the well-known Restormer, can obtain superior or com-
027            parable performance compared with current state-of-the-art methods. Moreover,
028            we theoretically demonstrate that to achieve a given small enough error, our pro-
029            posed HAIR requires fewer parameters in contrast to mainstream embedding-based
030            All-in-One methods. Code is available in supplementary materials.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Image restoration is an important task in the field of computer vision, aiming to reconstructing
035   high-quality images from their degraded states. The presence of adverse conditions such as noise,
036   haze, or rain can severely diminish the effectiveness of images for a variety of applications, such as
037   autonomous navigation (Valanarasu et al., 2022; Chen Yu-Wei, 2023), augmented reality (Girbacia
038   et al., 2013; Saggio et al., 2011; Dang et al., 2020). Therefore, developing robust image restoration
039
      methods is of great importance. The use of deep learning in this domain has made remarkable
      progress, as evidenced by a suite of recent methodologies (Zhang et al., 2017b; Liang et al., 2021;
040
      Zamir et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2023; Dudhane et al., 2024). Nonetheless, the
041
      predominant approach in current research is to employ task-specific models, each tailored to address
042   a particular type of degradation (Zhang et al., 2018b; Liang et al., 2021; Zamir et al., 2022; Chen
043   et al., 2022b;a). This tailored approach, while precise, presents a constraint in terms of universality,
044   as it restricts the applicability of models to scenarios with varied or unknown degradations (Zamir
045   et al., 2020a;b; Purohit et al., 2021). To overcome this limitation, many researchers have focused
046   on developing All-in-One image restoration models recently. These models are designed to tackle
047   various degradations using one single model. Pioneering efforts in this area (Li et al., 2022; Zamir
048   et al., 2021; Valanarasu et al., 2022; Potlapalli et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Dudhane et al., 2024;
049   Conde et al., 2024) have utilized a variety of advanced techniques such as contrastive learning (Li
050
      et al., 2022), meta-learning (Zhang et al., 2023), visual prompting methods (Potlapalli et al., 2024;
      Wang et al., 2023; Dudhane et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Conde et al., 2024). These approaches have
051
      undoubtedly made substantial contributions to this field.
052
053   However, these All-in-One models share a common drawback, i.e. they rely on a single model
      with fixed parameters to address various degradations. This one-size-fits-all method can hinder

146   2.2   DATA - CONDITIONED H YPERNETWORKS
147
148   Hypernetworks (Ha et al., 2016) are a class of neural networks designed to generate weights (parame-
149   ters) for other networks. They can be classified into three types, i.e task-conditioned, data-conditioned,
150   and noise-conditioned hypernetworks (Chauhan et al., 2023). Among these methods, data-conditioned
151   hypernetworks are particularly noteworthy for their ability to generate weights contingent upon the
152   distinctive features of the input data. This capability allows the network to dynamically adapt its
153   behaviour to specific input patterns or characteristics, fostering flexibility and adaptability within
154
      the model. Consequently, this results in enhanced generalization and robustness. Data-conditioned
      hypernetworks have been applied in many computer vision tasks, e.g., semantic segmentation (Nirkin
155
      et al., 2021) and image editing (Alaluf et al., 2022). Despite previous attempts to integrate hyper-
156
      networks into image restoration (Aharon & Ben-Artzi, 2023; Fan et al., 2019), these have primarily
157   leveraged task-conditioned hypernetworks, which take a task embedding (like an embedding of
158   derain) as the input and output the weights. They have two main drawbacks: (1) They require prior
159   knowledge of the input image’s degradation type. (2) They cannot dynamically generate weights
160   based on image content. Although Klocek et al. (2019) tries to use Data-conditional Hypernetworks
161   in Super-Resolution, it direcly maps a coordinate to a pixel, which is unreliable. To the best of our
      knowledge, our work is the first to introduce data-conditioned hypernetworks into the domain of
      All-in-One image restoration.

      3.1     H YPERNETWORK - BASED A LL - IN -O NE I MAGE R ESTORATION M ODULE (HAIR)
192
193   In this subsection, we introduce HAIR, a hypernetworks-based All-in-One image restoration module
194   designed to dynamically generate parameters for an image-to-image network based on the input
195   image’s features in a plug-and-play manner. HAIR comprises two main components: the Classifier
196   and the Hyper Selecting Net (HSN). Next, we will introduce the two components in detail.
197

      3.2     P ROPOSED A LL - IN -O NE I MAGE R ESTORATION M ODEL (R ES -HAIR)
241
242   Integrating HAIR into an existing image-to-image network involves a simple two-step process: (1)
243   Insert the Classifier at the junction between the first and second halves of the network to produce a
244   Global Information Vector (GIV) from the features of the first half. (2) Incorporate HSNs into the
245   second half of the network, using the GIV to generate weights for all modules within this section
246   dynamically. In this work, we integrate our proposed HAIR module into the popular image restoration
247   model Restormer (Zamir et al., 2022), and propose our All-in-One model Res-HAIR. Although we
248   only use the example of integrating HAIR with Restormer to illustrate the process, this example is
249
      representative, and integration with other networks follows in a virtually identical way.
250   Overall Architecture. As shown in Fig. 3, our network architecture is consistent with Restormer
251   (Zamir et al., 2022). Given a certain degraded image input X ∈ RH×W ×3 , Res-HAIR utilizes a
252   3 × 3 convolution to transform X into feature embeddings F0 ∈ RH×W ×C , where C denotes the
253   number of channels. These feature embeddings are then proceeded through a 4-level hierarchical
254   encoder-decoder, resulting in deep features Fr . Each level of the encoder-decoder incorporates
255
      several Transformer blocks, with an increasing number from the top to the bottom level, ensuring
      computational efficiency. The left three blue "Transformer Blocks" function as an Encoder, designed
256                                                                                            W   H

257
      to extract features from F0 and ultimately produce a global feature map Fg ∈ R 8 × 8 ×8C with
      a large receptive field. Fg is then input into a Classifier to yield a Global Information Vector
258
      (GIV) Vg ∈ R2C . The right four orange "HyperTrans Blocks" operate as a Decoder, aiming to
259
      adaptively fuse features at each Decoder level based on degradation, culminating in a restored image
260
      X̂. Specifically, at each Decoder level, the HyperTrans Block receives two inputs, i.e. the input feature
261
      and the GIV Vg . Vg is fed into the Hyper Selecting Net to generate weights for the corresponding
262
      Transformer Blocks, which are then applied to the input feature to produce the output. It is important
263   to note that all HyperTrans Blocks use the same Vg derived from Fg . Since the weights in the
264   Decoder are generated based on Vg , which contains the degradation information of X, our method
265   can thus adapt to different degraded images.

      Assumption 1. The target function y i ∈ Wr,3HW , i = 1, 2, · · · 3HW . The Sobolev space Wr,3HW

353   The experimental settings follow previous research on general image restoration (Zhang et al., 2023;
354   Potlapalli et al., 2024) in two different configurations: (a) All-in-One and (b) Single task. In the All-in-

356   both three and five unique degradations. In contrast, the Single-task configuration involves training

360
361   Implementation Details. Our Res-HAIR method is designed to be end-to-end trainable, eliminating
362   the need for pre-training any of its components. The architecture comprises a 4-level encoder-decoder
363
      structure, with each level equipped with a varying number of Transformer blocks. Specifically,
      the number of blocks increases progressively from level-1 to level-4, following the sequence [4, 6,
364
      6, 8]. HyperTrans Blocks are employed throughout level-4 and the decoding stages of levels 1-3.
365
      Additionally, the Weight Box parameter N is set according to the sequence [5, 7, 7, 9] for each
366   respective level. In the All-in-One setting, the model is trained with a batch size of 32, while in
367   the single-task setting, a batch size of 8 is used. The network optimization is guided by an L1 loss
368   function, employing the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov et al., 2017) with parameters β1 = 0.9 and
369   β2 = 0.999. The learning rate is set to 2e − 4 for the initial 150 epochs and then changed to 2e − 5
370   for the final 10 epochs. To enhance the training data, input patches of size 128 × 128 are utilized,
371   with random horizontal and vertical flips applied to the images to augment the dataset.
372   Datasets. We follow previous approaches (Li et al., 2022; Potlapalli et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023)
373   for our All-in-One and single-task experiments, using these benchmark datasets. For single-task
374   image denoising, we use the BSD400 (Arbelaez et al., 2010) and WED (Ma et al., 2016) datasets,
375   adding Gaussian noise with levels σ ∈ {15, 25, 50} to generate training images. Testing is performed
376   on the BSD68 (Martin et al., 2001) and Urban100 (Huang et al., 2015) datasets. For deraining, we
377   employ the Rain100L dataset (Yang et al., 2020). Dehazing experiments utilize the SOTS dataset (Li
      et al., 2018). Deblurring and low-light enhancement tasks use the GoPro (Nah et al., 2017) and

527   5    C ONCLUSION & F UTURE P ROSPECT
528
529   This paper introduces HAIR, a novel plug-and-play Hypernetworks-based module capable of being
530   easily integrated and adaptively generating parameters for different networks based on the input image.
531
      Our method comprises two main components: the Classifier and the Hyper Selecting Net (HSN).
      Specifically, the Classifier is a simple image classification network with Global Average Pooling,
532
      designed to produce a Global Information Vector (GIV) that encapsulates the global information
533
      from the input image. The HSN functions as a fully-connected neural network, receiving the GIV
534   and outputting parameters for the corresponding modules. Extensive experiments indicate that HAIR
535   can significantly enhance the performance of various image restoration architectures at a low cost
536   without necessitating any changes to their logical structures. By incorporating HAIR into the widely
537   recognized Restormer architecture, we have achieved State-Of-The-Art performance on a range of
538   image restoration tasks. The potential for further exploiting data-conditioned Hypernetworks in tasks
539   such as image restoration, editing, and generation is substantial, given their robust adaptability to
      diverse inputs over the mainstream conditional embedding techniques.

876   A.3.1    D OES HAIR A LSO U TILIZE F IXED PARAMETERS AS P REVIOUS M ETHODS D O ?
877
878
      In the Introduction (Section 1), we highlighted that our proposed HAIR method differs from previous
      approaches, which typically employ a static set of parameters to handle various image degradations.
879
      In contrast, HAIR employs a data-conditioned Hypernetwork to dynamically generate parameters
880
      based on the input image’s content. However, a potential point of confusion arises from the fact
881   that the Hypernetworks in HAIR (i.e., the Hyper Selecting Nets) are indeed fixed during inference,
882   suggesting that HAIR also relies on a set of fixed parameters to address different degradations.
883
884
      To clarify this confusion, it is crucial to understand our motivation for using Hypernetworks. Our goal
      is to mitigate the performance loss caused by conflicting degradations. For example, by generating
885
      distinct parameters, we aim to enable the model to function either as a low-frequency or high-
886
      frequency filter, depending on the input image’s requirements. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the primary
887   focus should be on the main networks that directly process the image.
888
889   Therefore, when we refer to "fixed parameters" and "dynamic parameters," we are referring to the
890
      parameters of the main network that interacts with the image, not the Hypernetworks responsible
      for parameter generation, which do not directly engage with the input image. The point lies in the
891
      dynamic adaptation of the main network’s parameters to the specific characteristics of the input
892
      image, which is the innovative aspect of HAIR.

      A.4.1    T RANSFORMER B LOCK IN R ES -HAIR F RAMEWORK
919
920   (This section is directly borrowed from the paper of PromptIR. (Potlapalli et al., 2024)) In this section,
921   we present the block diagram5 of the transformer block and further, elaborate on the details of the
922   transformer block employed in the Res-HAIR framework. The transformer block follows the design
923   and hyper-parameters outlined in (Zamir et al., 2022)
924   To begin, the input features X ∈ RHl ×Wl ×Cl are passed through the MDTA module. In this module,
925   the features are initially normalized using Layer normalization. Subsequently, a combination of
926   1 × 1 convolutions followed by 3 × 3 depth-wise convolutions are applied to project the features into
927   Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V) tensors. An essential characteristic of the MDTA module is
928   its computation of attention across the channel dimensions, rather than the spatial dimensions. This
929   effectively reduces the computational overhead. To achieve this channel-wise attention calculation,
930
      the Q and K projections are reshaped from Hl × Wl × Cl to Hl Wl × Cl and Cl × Hl Wl respectively,
      before calculating dot-product, hence the resulting transposed attention map with the shape of Cl ×Cl .
931
      Bias-free convolutions are utilized within this submodule. Furthermore, attention is computed in a
932
      multi-head manner in parallel.
933
934   After MDTA Module the features are processed through the GDFN module. In the GDFN module,
935   the input features are expanded by a factor γ using 1 × 1 convolution and they are then passed through
936
      3 × 3 convolutions. These operations are performed through two parallel paths and the output of one
      of the paths is activated using GeLU non-linearity. This activated feature map is then combined with
937
      the output of the other path using element-wise product.

941   This section elaborates on specific details presented in Section 3.1.2. Within the Restormer framework
942   (Zamir et al., 2022), each Transformer consists of a total of 12 logical convolutional layers, as depicted
943   in Fig. 5. For code implementation, we utilize 6 convolutional layers to effectively emulate the
944   functionality of 12 convolutional layers within each Transformer Block. Consequently, the generated
945   parameters w ∈ RP described in Section 3.1.2 encompass all the parameters necessary for these
946
      6 convolutional layers of a single Transformer Block. The Weights Box W ∈ RN ×P , therefore,
      represents the aggregate parameters for N Transformer Blocks.
947
948   Given a fixed Global Information Vector (GIV) Vg , it is processed through the Fully-Connected
949   Neural Network (FCNN) followed by a Softmax operation to yield the Selecting Vector Vs ∈ RN .
950   This vector is subsequently employed to "select" the definitive parameters from the Weights Box.
951   Each Decoder level possesses a distinct Weights Box, which is shared among all the HyperTrans
952   Blocks at that level. It is crucial to highlight that all HyperTrans Blocks across all levels operate
953   using the same GIV derived from a single Classifier. Meanwhile, each single HyperTrans Block is
954
      equipped with its own Hyper Selecting Net, resulting in a unique Selecting Vector and, consequently,
      its own set of parameters for each.
