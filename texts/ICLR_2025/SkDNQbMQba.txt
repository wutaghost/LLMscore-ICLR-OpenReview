                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have transformed AI research thanks to their
012            powerful internal capabilities and knowledge. However, existing LLMs still fail
013            to effectively incorporate the massive external knowledge when interacting with
014            the world. Although retrieval-augmented LLMs are proposed to mitigate the
015            issue, they are still fundamentally constrained by the context length of LLMs,
016            as they can only retrieve top-K raw data chunks from the external knowledge
017            base which often consists of millions of data chunks. Here we propose Thought-
018            Retriever, a novel model-agnostic algorithm that helps LLMs generate output
019
               conditioned on arbitrarily long external data, without being constrained by the
               context length or number of retrieved data chunks. Our key insight is to let an
020
               LLM fully leverage its intermediate responses generated when solving past user
021
               queries (thoughts), filtering meaningless and redundant thoughts, organizing them
022            in thought memory, and retrieving the relevant thoughts when addressing new
023            queries. Besides algorithmic innovation, we further meticulously prepare a novel
024            benchmark, AcademicEval, which requires an LLM to faithfully leverage ultra-
025            long context to answer queries based on real-world academic papers. Extensive
026            experiments on AcademicEval and two other public datasets validate that Thought-
027            Retriever remarkably outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, achieving an average
028            increase of at least 7.6% in F1 score and 16% in win rate across various tasks.
029            More importantly, we further demonstrate two exciting findings: (1) Thought-
030
               Retriever can indeed help LLM self-evolve after solving more user queries; (2)
               Thought-Retriever learns to leverage deeper thoughts to answer more abstract user
031
               queries.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION AND R ELATED W ORK
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized AI research thanks to their powerful internal
037   capabilities (Zhao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) and knowledge (Peng et al., 2023b). When building
038   LLMs, researchers further expect LLMs to interact with the world by effectively incorporating the
039
      external knowledge as their long-term memories, e.g., collected from facts (Sun et al., 2023) or
      interactions with other AIs (Wu et al., 2023; Kannan et al., 2023). Importantly, the scale of the
040
      external knowledge for LLMs could be arbitrarily large; ultimately, all the digitized information
041
      within our universe could serve as the external knowledge for these LLMs. In practice, when
042   building personalized LLM applications (Bill and Eriksson, 2023) or LLM-powered domain experts
043   (Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023), e.g., AI doctor, the relevant external knowledge for
044   the LLMs could also easily get extremely large, e.g., billions of tokens. Therefore, our paper aims to
045   raise attention to the pressing research question: how to effectively and efficiently help LLMs utilize
046   (arbitrarily) rich external knowledge.
047
      To help LLMs better incorporate external knowledge, existing research mainly falls into two cate-
048   gories: long-context LLMs and retrieval-augmented LLMs (RALMs). (1) Long-context LLMs, such
049   as MPT (MosaicML, 2023) and LongChat (LM-SYS, 2023), aim to expand the LLM’s context
050   window, e.g., via novel training algorithms (Tay et al., 2022), inference algorithms (Xiao et al.,
051   2023), new architectures (Peng et al., 2023a; Gu and Dao, 2023), or system optimization (Xu et al.,
052   2023). Although these methods improve the working memory size of LLMs, they cannot funda-
053   mentally address the issue of interacting with ultra-rich external knowledge using LLMs, since the
      computational complexity is often quadratic to the context length. (2) RALMs retrieve pertinent

077
      Here, we propose Thought-Retriever, an LLM-agnostic self-evolving retrieval framework that lever-
078   ages historical LLM responses to answer new queries. Our key insight is that LLM responses can be
079   transformed into thoughts with little computational overhead and that the thoughts can be organized
080   as a thought memory for the LLM to facilitate future tasks. Psychological studies (Kurzweil, 2013;
081   Snell, 2012) support our insight, revealing that human memory is organized hierarchically, which
082   not only aids in retrieving relevant information for problem-solving but also gradually deepens our
083   understanding of the world through continuous processing and summarizing these interactions into
084   complex cognitive thoughts. Notably, through continuous interaction with diverse user queries,
085   Thought-Retriever progressively generates more novel and expansive thoughts. This is achieved by
086
      organizing new data chunks from external knowledge into thoughts after addressing each query, filter-
      ing out meaningless and redundant thoughts, and ultimately incorporating high-quality thoughts into
087
      the thought memory. Therefore, Thought-Retriever gives an LLM the potential to utilize arbitrarily
088
      rich external knowledge long-term memories and achieve self-evolution in capabilities.
089
090   In addition to algorithmic advancements, we also meticulously developed a novel benchmark, Aca-
091   demicEval, which challenges a Large Language Model (LLM) to accurately utilize extensive con-
092
      text to answer queries based on real-world academic papers. Our comprehensive experiments on
      AcademicEval and two additional datasets confirm that Thought-Retriever significantly surpasses
093
      state-of-the-art baselines, achieving an average increase of at least 7.6% in F1 score and 16% in
094
      win rate across various tasks. Furthermore, we present two intriguing discoveries: (1) Thought-
095   Retriever can indeed facilitate the self-evolution of an LLM after addressing more user queries; (2)
096   Thought-Retriever is capable of harnessing deeper insights to respond to more abstract user queries.
097
098
      In summary, our main contributions are as follows: (1) Thought-Retriever framework enables an
      LLM to efficiently and effectively utilize external knowledge, and further allowing it to self-evolve
099
      through continuous interactions. (2) AcademicEval, a real-world benchmark for testing LLM’s
100
      understanding of ultra-long context. (3) Thought-Retriever consistently outperforms all state-of-the-
101   art retrieval-augmented and long-context baselines and presents exciting new findings.

167   2.4 T HOUGHT-R ETRIEVER F RAMEWORK
168
      Method Overview. Figure 2 offers an overview of the proposed Thought-Retriever framework,
169   which consists of four major components: (1) Thought retrieval, where data chunks from external
170   knowledge and thought memory are retrieved; (2) Answer generation, where an LLM generates the
171   answer for the user query based on the retrieved data chunks; (3) Thought and confidence generation,
172   where an LLM further generates thought and its confidence in validation to avoid hallucination based
173   on the user query and the generated answer; (4) Thought merge, where similarity is calculated to
174   measure whether generated thought will cause redundancy in data chunks; (5) Thought memory
175   update, where meaningless and redundant thoughts are removed; the thought memory is updated
176   with the remaining novel thoughts, rather than adopting all the new thoughts. We summarize the
177
      pipeline of Thought-Retriever in Algorithm 1, whose details are shown as follows. Detailed prompts
      for this section can be found in Appendix A.2.
178

195   Thought Retrieval. After receiving a user query Qi , Thought-Retriever R retrieves relevant infor-
196   mation Ti from external knowledge K and previously generated thought memory T via embedding
197   similarity ranking. This process is formulated as Ti ← R(Qi , K ∪ T ).
198
199   Answer Generation. Based on the retrieved information Ti , we design a prompt to combine Ti
200
      and user query Qi and feed the prompt to an LLM L to get the answer Ai . It can be articulated as
      Ai ← L(Qi , Ti ).
201
202   Thought and Confidence Generation. We can generate thoughts via LLM L using the obtained
203   answer Ai and its query Qi (an example is shown in Table 2.4). However, meaningless thoughts
204   during the generation process may cause hallucinations for LLM and harm performance since
205   some queries may be irrelevant to the external knowledge and thought memory. To solve this
206   issue, we design a special prompt so that LLM L can generate thought Ti and thought quality
207   confidence ci based on the user’s query Qi and corresponding answer Ai . This can be described as
208   Ti , ci ← L(Qi , Ai ). Specifically, ci is a discrete binary value, where 1 indicates that the generated
209
      thought Ti is meaningful, and 0 indicates that it is meaningless or hallucinated. This confidence
      generation is also validated through our experiment in Sec 4.7.
210
211   Thought Merge. Redundant thoughts may cause LLM to retrieve duplicate information, which is
212   also harmful to the performance of LLM. Therefore, we calculate the similarity si between Ti and
213   data chunks (Tm , Kj ) to measure whether generated thought Ti will cause redundancy in data chunks.
214   It can be formulated as si ← 1{∃j,m ;sim(Ti ,Kj /Tm )≥ϵ)} , where sim denotes embedding similarity
215   based on Contriever (Izacard et al., 2022) (same as retriever used elsewhere in Thought-Retriever)
      and ϵ means the threshold of similarity.

           U NDERSTANDING
248
249   Current benchmarks for assessing LLM long-context memory utilization involve tasks such as
250   question-answering, long-context summarization, and classification. Despite being well-constructed,
251   they are limited in flexibility and real-world impact and are costly to acquire. To address these issues,
252   we introduce an innovative benchmark, AcademicEval, based on academic papers from arXiv updated
253   daily. AcademicEval comes with two datasets: AcademicEval-abstract and AcademicEval-related.
254
      AcademicEval-abstract. This dataset focuses on the summarization of single (Abstract-single in
255
      Table 2) or multiple (Abstract-multi in Table 2) academic papers. The LLM is presented with one or
256
      more papers with the abstract and conclusion sections removed and is tasked with writing an abstract.
257   For Abstract-single, the generated abstract is directly compared with the paper’s original abstract. For
258   Abstract-multi, the generated abstract is compared with a summary of abstracts from all the provided
259   papers, which is generated by an expert LLM as a label.
260
261   AcademicEval-related. This dataset (Related-multi in Table 2) introduces a challenging task for
262   assessing an LLM’s ability to understand the connections between heterogeneous segments of its
263
      long-context memory. The task is to write a related work section based on the title and abstract of a
      target paper. The LLM needs to use the title and abstract as the query to retrieve memory chunks
264
      to complete this task. To be specific, memory chunks depict the abstracts of several papers (each
265
      memory chunk corresponds to the abstract of a paper), where some papers are cited in the related
266   work section of the target paper, while others are randomly sampled from the same broader field. The
267   generated related work is then compared to the original related work of the target paper for evaluation.
268
269   Benefits and Contributions. AcademicEval offers several advantages over existing benchmark
      datasets. Firstly, we maintain an up-to-date dataset from arXiv that benefits from the continuous


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      publication of new papers. This dynamic nature eases overfitting and label leakage problems in static
271   benchmarks and enables the evaluation of LLM self-adaptability. Secondly, high-quality labels can
272   be generated with no extra cost as opposed to manually crafted datasets that require human effort.
273   Thirdly, our dataset is not only valuable for evaluating LLM but also serves as a practical academic
274   tool in the real world to assist researchers in better understanding their fields and boost productivity.
275   We developed a highly automated codebase for dataset construction that will be released soon. We
276   also launched a public platform that will enable users to easily create similar datasets or utilize LLMs
277   for academic tasks (see details in Appendix F). In addition, the detailed dataset introduction and

514   5     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
515
516   (1) Long-context LLMs. In response to the challenge of long-context processing in LLMs, the
517   most intuitive strategies involve expanding the LLM’s context window, enabling it to process longer
518
      inputs. These methods include training larger, more advanced models (MosaicML, 2023; LM-SYS,
      2023), fine-tuning existing language models to handle wider windows (Tay et al., 2022), and applying
519
      positional encoding to extend the context window size (Xiao et al., 2023). However, these methods
520
      have shortcomings in their high costs associated with model training and a lack of flexibility, as they
521   do not address the fundamental issue of long context. For instance, to process longer memories,
522   it becomes necessary to engage in additional parameters or model training, which is both rigid
523   and resource-intensive. (2) Retrieval-Augmented Language Models. RALM offers a flexible,
524   cost-effective alternative to long-context LLMs by retrieving relevant information from extensive
525   context chunks. Current methods use techniques like token embeddings (Izacard et al., 2022; Lin
526   et al., 2023), keyword searches (Robertson et al., 2009), and fine-tuned rerankers (Ram et al., 2023).
527   Despite promising results, these methods are still limited by LLMs’ context capacity, often falling
528   short with growing context lengths. Recently, context summarization methods, such as hierarchical
529
      tree structures (Chen et al., 2023), have been proposed to mitigate these limitations. However, these
      methods are rigid and costly. We address these challenges with a Thought-Retriever framework based
530
      on RALM, which summarizes retrieved LLM context segments into thoughts using the user’s query.
531
532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
      We propose Thought-Retriever to help LLMs utilize rich external knowledge efficiently, enhancing
534   LLMs by enabling dynamic access without context length limitations. This innovative strategy uses
535   ”intermediate thoughts” from past interactions, allowing continuous improvement and understand-
536   ing. Thought-Retriever demonstrates superior performance across various datasets, including the
537   AcademicEval benchmark, showing its potential to revolutionize AI systems with more adaptive, real-
538   time, context-aware responses. This advancement promises significant improvements in industries
539   like customer service, healthcare, and legal advisory, and lays the groundwork for future research
      towards achieving more general AI, pushing technology’s role in society.

1028          Original Abstract:
1029
1030          Benchmarks such as GLUE have helped drive advances in NLP by incentivizing the creation
1031          of more accurate models. While this leaderboard paradigm has been remarkably
              successful, a historical focus on performance-based evaluation has been at the expense of
1032
              other qualities that the NLP community values in models, such as compactness, fairness,
1033          and energy efficiency. In this opinion paper, we study the divergence between what is
1034          incentivized by leaderboards and what is useful in practice through the lens of
1035          microeconomic theory. We frame both the leaderboard and NLP practitioners as
1036          consumers and the benefit they get from a model as its utility to them. With this framing,
              we formalize how leaderboards – in their current form – can be poor proxies for the NLP
1037
              community at large. For example, a highly inefficient model would provide less utility to
1038          practitioners but not to a leaderboard, since it is a cost that only the former must bear. To
1039          allow practitioners to better estimate a model’s utility to them, we advocate for more
1040          transparency on leaderboards, such as the reporting of statistics that are of practical
1041          concern (e.g., model size, energy efficiency, and inference latency).
