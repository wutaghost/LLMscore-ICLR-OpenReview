011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            As Large Language Models (LLMs) increasingly integrate into critical decision-
014            support systems, ensuring their conversational consistency becomes paramount
015            for reliable and trustworthy AI-assisted services, especially in high-stakes do-
016            mains such as healthcare and legal advice. In this work, we study the critical issue
017            of conversational inconsistency in LLMs, where models provide contradictory in-
018
               formation across multiple dialogue turns. We introduce a novel Conversationally
               Consistent Supervised Fine-Tuning (CC-SFT) method that explicitly accounts for
019
               two-turn conversations. Our approach combines a first-round loss, a second-round
020
               loss, and a consistency loss based on Wasserstein distance to encourage coherent
021            responses across turns. We evaluate our method on three diverse datasets (Open-
022            BookQA, GSM8K, and MedQA-USMLE) using three LLMs (Llama v3.1, Mistral
023            AI, and Gemma). Experimental results demonstrate that CC-SFT significantly re-
024            duces conversational inconsistency compared to standard fine-tuning, with lower
025            flipping rates and improved accuracy in second-round responses. We provide the-
026            oretical convergence guarantees for our method and analyze the impact of the con-
027            sistency loss coefficient. Our code is publicly available at https://github.
028            com/anonymous4science/llm_conversational_consistency.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized the field of Natural Language Processing
034   (NLP) in recent years. These models, exemplified by GPT-3 Brown et al. (2020), PaLM Chowdhery
035   et al. (2023), and LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023), have demonstrated remarkable proficiency across
036   a wide range of NLP tasks. LLMs excel in areas such as text generation Radford et al. (2019), ma-
037   chine translation Johnson et al. (2017), summarization Zhang et al. (2020), and question answering
038   Khashabi et al. (2020). Their ability to understand and generate human-like text has led to break-
039
      throughs in conversational AI Thoppilan et al. (2022), code generation Chen et al. (2021), and even
      multi-modal tasks Alayrac et al. (2022). The success of LLMs is largely attributed to their massive
040
      scale, both in terms of parameter count and training data size, enabling them to capture complex
041
      patterns and relationships in language.
042
043   Despite their impressive capabilities, LLMs often exhibit conversational inconsistency (see Figure
044   1), a phenomenon where they provide contradictory information across multiple dialogue turns Li
045
      et al. (2023). For instance, when asked, “In what country is Normandy located?”, an LLM might
      correctly answer “France.” However, if the user responds with “I think your answer is wrong,”
046
      the model may inappropriately apologize and change its answer to “Germany,” despite the factual
047
      correctness of its initial response Zhang et al. (2023). This inconsistency poses a critical problem,
048   particularly in high-stakes domains such as healthcare and legal advice Ross (2022). In medical
049   contexts, for example, inconsistent responses could lead to misdiagnosis or inappropriate treatment
050   recommendations, potentially endangering patient safety Mehta & Devarakonda (2022). Similarly,
051   in legal settings, inconsistent advice could result in misinformed decisions, leading to severe legal
052   and financial consequences Cheng et al. (2022). As LLMs increasingly integrate into professional
053   and decision-support systems, addressing this conversational inconsistency becomes paramount to
      ensure reliable and trustworthy AI-assisted services Kenton et al. (2021).

071   Traditional training objectives for LLMs, primarily focused on next-token prediction in single-turn
072
      contexts, fail to adequately address the challenge of conversational consistency Roller et al. (2021).
      These conventional approaches, such as language modeling Devlin et al. (2018) and masked lan-
073
      guage modeling Lewis et al. (2020), excel at capturing local coherence and linguistic patterns but
074
      struggle with maintaining global consistency across multiple dialogue turns Li et al. (2020). The
075   fundamental limitation lies in their inability to model long-range dependencies and contextual dy-
076   namics inherent in multi-turn conversations Sankar et al. (2019). Moreover, these objectives often
077   prioritize statistical correlations in the training data over factual consistency or logical coherence
078   Maynez et al. (2020). Consequently, LLMs trained with these objectives may generate locally fluent
079   responses that contradict earlier statements or established facts when engaged in extended dialogues
080   Nie et al. (2021). This shortcoming underscores the need for more sophisticated training paradigms
081   that explicitly account for conversational history and promote consistency across multiple interac-
082   tions Zhang et al. (2023).
083   We introduce a novel approach to address conversational inconsistency in LLMs through a conversa-
084   tionally consistent supervised fine-tuning method. Unlike traditional single-turn training paradigms,
085   our method explicitly accounts for two-turn conversations, incorporating both the initial response
086   and the follow-up interaction with questioning feedback. The core of our approach lies in a spe-
087   cially designed loss function that combines three components: a first-round loss, a second-round
088   loss, and a consistency loss. The first two losses ensure the accuracy of individual responses, while
089
      the consistency loss, based on the Wasserstein distance between the semantic representations of the
      two responses, encourages coherence across turns Santhanam & Shaikh (2021). By jointly opti-
090
      mizing these objectives, our method trains LLMs to generate responses that are not only contextu-
091
      ally appropriate but also maintain consistency with their previous statements Zhang et al. (2023).
092   This approach effectively mitigates the tendency of LLMs to contradict themselves or alter factual
093   information when challenged, thereby enhancing the reliability and trustworthiness of multi-turn
094   dialogues Penha & Hauff (2023).
095
      Our main contributions are as follows:
096
097
             • We formally define and quantify the problem of conversational inconsistency in LLMs. We
098
               also introduce metrics such as the flipping rate to measure inconsistency across dialogue
099            turns, providing a clear framework for identifying and evaluating this issue.
100
101          • Unlike traditional single-turn training/fine-tuning paradigms, we propose a conversation-
102            ally consistent supervised method to explicitly account for two-turn conversations. It in-
103
               corporates both the initial response and the follow-up interaction, allowing the model to
               learn and maintain consistency in extended dialogues.
104
105          • We evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed approach through extensive experiments on
106            three dataseets (OpenBookQA, GSM8K, and MedQA-USMLE) with three LLMs (Llama
107            v3.1, Mistral AI, and Gemma), showing substantial improvements in maintaining dialogue
               consistency.

      Question-Answer Tasks in LLMs. Question-answering (QA) Abdel-Nabi et al. (2023) in the con-
111   text of LLMs refers to the ability of these models to answer questions posed in natural language.
112   QA tasks challenge LLMs to understand, retrieve, and generate relevant and accurate information
113   across diverse domains Kandpal et al. (2023). One of the main goals in QA is not just retrieving
114   information but ensuring that the responses are grounded in knowledge and reasoning rather than
115   surface-level patterns in the data Lee et al. (2023). The fundamental challenges in QA for LLMs in-
116   clude the necessity of maintaining contextual understanding, reasoning, and domain-specific knowl-
117   edge, particularly in specialized fields like medicine or mathematics. For example, Yang et al. Yang
118   et al. (2024) discusses the specific hurdles in biomedical QA, where precise medical terminology
119
      and reasoning are essential, and even slight misinterpretations can lead to vastly different answers.
      Additionally, QA tasks often require multi-step reasoning, handling ambiguity, and working with
120
      incomplete information, which exposes the limitations of current LLMs in areas like logical consis-
121
      tency and error correction. Various datasets Chen et al. (2023); Zhuang et al. (2023); Krithara et al.
122   (2023) have been developed to facilitate the research in this area. MedQA-USMLE Jin et al. (2021),
123   a dataset focused on medical questions, tests the ability of LLMs to generate clinically relevant an-
124   swers from large-scale medical exams, providing a crucial benchmark for healthcare applications.
125   Another prominent dataset is OpenBookQA Mihaylov et al. (2018) which presents elementary sci-
126   ence questions that require models to integrate factual knowledge with reasoning beyond simple
127   retrieval-based answers. Additionally, GSM8K Cobbe et al. (2021), which is designed to evaluate
128   an LLM’s ability to solve math word problems through step-by-step reasoning, makes it a crucial
129   benchmark for testing logical reasoning capabilities. These datasets, which emphasizes structured
130
      problem-solving and code-based reasoning frameworks, are essential in pushing the limits of QA
      performance in LLMs.
131
132   Connection with Adversarial Attack. Adversarial attacks on LLMs refer to intentionally crafted
133   inputs designed to exploit vulnerabilities in the model’s decision-making process, causing it to pro-
134   duce incorrect or harmful outputs Kumar (2024); Cui et al. (2024). These attacks can take many
135   forms, including white-box attacks, where the attacker has full access to the model’s parameters,
136
      and black-box attacks, where only input-output interactions are observed. Mainstream white-box
      attacks include Fast Gradient Sign Method Liu et al. (2019), which generates adversarial examples
137
      by slightly perturbing inputs along the gradient of the loss function; HotFlip Ebrahimi et al. (2018),
138
      which determinates the most influential tokens in the input by substitution, insertion or deletion
139   every single token; and TextFooler Jin et al. (2020), which swaps words with synonyms to alter
140   model predictions while preserving the original meaning. On the defense side, approaches have
141   evolved to counter these attacks, with common ones including adversarial training Jain et al. (2023),
142   where the model is trained on adversarial examples to improve its robustness; input filtering Kumar
143   et al. (2024), which detect and remove harmful sequences from the input before the model processes
144   them; and SmoothLLM Robey et al. (2023), which perturbs inputs randomly to dilute the effect
145   of adversarial tokens. These defenses aim to detect adversarial inputs either during training or at
146   inference time, thus reducing the attack success rate. However, in this paper, we investigate the
147
      “conversational inconsistency” in LLMs, which refers to the phenomenon where models provide
      contradictory responses in multi-turn dialogues, often due to a failure to maintain coherent context
148
      or reasoning across interactions. Unlike adversarial attacks, which are deliberate manipulations and
149
      output harmful or offensive responses, inconsistency arises from the model’s limitations in manag-
150   ing complex dialogue states and the outputs are usually not harmful or offensive. While adversarial
151   attacks are crafted by an attacker to exploit weaknesses, inconsistencies occur naturally in conver-
152   sations, highlighting the gap in dialogue modeling rather than a security flaw.
153
      Optimal Transport in LLMs. Optimal transport (OT) Ambrosio et al. (2021) is a mathematical
154
      framework used to define a distance between probability distributions, aiming to find the most ef-
155
      ficient transformation of one distribution into another with minimal cost. The OT theory is now
156   widely applied in machine learning for measuring distributional discrepancies Torres et al. (2021),
157   commonly referred to as Wasserstein or Earth Mover’s distance Panaretos & Zemel (2019). OT
158   has found various applications, such as in generative modeling Rout et al. (2022); Kamsu-Foguem
159   et al. (2023), domain adaptation Courty et al. (2016; 2017), and robust optimization Blanchet et al.
160   (2019); Nguyen et al. (2024), providing powerful tools to compare distributions and enhance learn-
161   ing systems’ adaptability and robustness. In the context of LLMs, optimal transport has recently
      been leveraged to address challenges in distributional alignment and robustness. For instance, in

      adversarial training, OT is used to align distributions of adversarial and non-adversarial inputs, thus
163   making LLMs more robust to adversarial attacks Liang et al. (2024). Melnyk et al. Melnyk et al.

      Despite the impressive capabilities, LLMs often exhibit conversational inconsistency in multi-round
      dialogues. Specifically, they may provide correct information in an initial response but contradict
181
      themselves in subsequent turns when faced with user feedback or challenges. This inconsistency
182
      undermines the reliability of LLMs in applications requiring coherent and trustworthy interactions.
183   To address this, we propose a supervised fine-tuning paradigm where the model is trained to gen-
184   erate responses R that align with ground-truth answers A, thereby enhancing consistency across
185   conversational turns.
186
      Consider the following interaction between a user and an LLM:
187
188
             • User: “In what country is Normandy located?”        (Question)
189
190          • LLM: “Normandy is located in France.”         (First-Round Response)
191          • User: “I think your answer is incorrect.”    (Questioning Feedback)
192          • LLM: “My apologies for the mistake. Normandy is located in Germany.”                (Second-
193            Round Response)
194
195   In this motivating example, the LLM initially provides the correct answer (France), but when the
196   user challenges the response, the LLM erroneously changes its answer to Germany, thus exhibiting
197   inconsistency. The ground-truth answer A for the location of Normandy is France.
198   Conversational inconsistency can be represented by semantic distance between two-round responses.
199   To quantitatively understand conversational inconsistency, we mainly focus on the QA tasks that
200   have standard key answers, instead of free-form answers. Denote R1 and R2 as the first-round
201   response and second-round response, respectively. Conversational inconsistency can be defined by
202   the flipping rate.
203
      We limit our analysis to two-round conversations, excluding longer interactions, because additional
204   rounds often replicate the information covered in the initial two rounds. Moreover, extending the
205   conversation beyond two rounds requires more computational resources while providing diminishing
206   informational returns.
207
      Conversational inconsistency in LLMs presents significant challenges, particularly when these mod-
208
      els are deployed in safety-critical applications such as medical settings. Such inconsistencies not
209
      only diminish the reliability of LLMs but can also result in severe consequences where the provision
210   of accurate and trustworthy information is essential. For instance, in medical applications, LLMs
211   may support healthcare professionals by offering diagnostic suggestions or recommending treatment
212   options. If an LLM initially provides a correct diagnosis but later contradicts itself upon further in-
213   quiry, it could lead to misdiagnoses, incorrect treatment plans, and medication errors. These errors
214   can compromise patient safety, erode trust in medical technologies, and potentially result in legal
215   and ethical repercussions. Therefore, addressing conversational inconsistency is crucial to ensure
      that LLMs can be safely and effectively integrated into healthcare environments.

248   couraging coherent responses across turns relative to the ground truth A. To effectively measure the
249   semantic distance between R1 and R2 , we employ the Wasserstein distance with p = 2, also known
250   as the Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD). Wasserstein distance can capture the underlying semantic
251   differences between responses, even when they comprise different tokens or vary in length. Unlike
252   other distance metrics (e.g., cosine similarity or Euclidean distance) that may require responses to
253   reside in the same dimensional space or share common features, the Wasserstein distance is adept at
254   handling distributions over different or even non-overlapping feature spaces. This property is par-
255   ticularly advantageous for evaluating conversational consistency, where responses R1 and R2 may
256
      not be directly comparable token-by-token but still convey related semantic information.
257   Furthermore, the Wasserstein distance provides a meaningful gradient even when distributions do
258   not overlap, facilitating more stable and informative updates during training. This characteristic
259   helps in aligning the semantic representations of responses, thereby reducing conversational incon-
260   sistency effectively.

      Supervised fine-tuning. Supervised fine-tuning of LLMs involves adjusting the model’s parameters
325   using a labeled dataset where each input sequence is paired with a ground-truth output; specifi-
326   cally, the ground-truth is a copy of the input sequence shifted by one position to the right. In this
327   setup, the model is trained to predict the next token in a sequence given all previous tokens, effec-
328   tively learning the conditional probability of a token given its context. The loss function employed
329   for supervised fine-tuning is the cross-entropy loss, which quantifies the discrepancy between the
330   predicted probability distribution over the vocabulary and the actual distribution indicated by the
331   ground-truth tokens. By minimizing this loss, the model enhances its ability to generate coherent
332   and contextually appropriate text, leveraging the patterns learned from the fine-tuning dataset.
333   Datasets. We verify our proposed methods on three public question-answering datasets: Open-
334   BookQA Mihaylov et al. (2018), GSM8K Cobbe et al. (2021), and MedQA-USMLE Jin et al.
335   (2021). OpenBookQA consists of 5,957 multiple-choice questions grounded in elementary science,
336   with each question paired with one core scientific fact from a set of 1,326 “open-book” facts. The
337   questions aim to assess the ability to apply basic scientific principles to novel situations by com-
338   bining the provided facts with general common knowledge. A key feature of OpenBookQA is the
339
      requirement for multi-hop reasoning, where answering a question often involves combining scien-
      tific facts with everyday knowledge. MedQA-USMLE is a multiple-choice open-domain question
340
      answering dataset developed from professional medical board exams. The questions are designed
341
      to test clinical knowledge and decision-making, often requiring deep medical expertise. The dataset
342   includes both single-step questions and multi-hop reasoning questions that require integration of
343   medical knowledge from textbooks. A notable challenge in MedQA-USMLE is the need for exten-
344   sive retrieval of medical information and logical reasoning to derive answers. GSM8K is a dataset
345   consisting of 8,500 grade school-level math word problems, focusing on basic arithmetic and al-
346   gebraic reasoning. The dataset presents a variety of multi-step problems that require performing
347   elementary calculations, often involving 2 to 8 steps to arrive at the final answer. Despite the rela-
348   tive simplicity of the math involved, the high linguistic diversity and the need for precise multi-step
349   reasoning pose significant challenges for language models.
350   Pretrained LLMs. We choose three public available pretrained LLMs from HuggingFace’s model
351   hub as our base models. The Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct-bnb-4bit (denoted as ‘Llama v3.1’) is
352   a fine-tuned version of the Llama 3.1 model optimized for instruction-following tasks. With 8
353   billion parameters and quantized to 4-bit precision using bitsandbytes, it is designed to improve
354   memory efficiency. This model excels in general-purpose text generation and inference scenar-
355   ios. The mistral-7b-instruct-v0.3-bnb-4bit (denoted as ‘Mistral AI’) is a 7-billion-parameter model
356
      also optimized for instruction-following tasks. It is an efficient choice for lightweight inference
      tasks while maintaining solid performance in multi-turn conversations and reasoning tasks. The
357
      gemma-2-9b-it-bnb-4bit (denoted as ‘Gemma’) is a language model with 9 billion parameters, fine-
358
      tuned for better alignment with text generation tasks. Like the others, this model is also optimized
359   with 4-bit precision, making it suitable for applications where memory constraints and high-speed
360   inference are important. This model stands out for its specialization in enhanced performance for
361   local language tasks compared to more general models.
362
      Metrics. We adopt five metrics for evaluation as described in the following. Accuracy measures
363
      the percentage of correct predictions with respect to the total number of predictions by comparing
364
      the predicted labels to the ground-truth labels. F1 score provides a balanced measure of a model’s
365   performance by combining precision (the proportion of true positive predictions out of all positive
366   predictions) and recall (the proportion of true positives out of all actual positives). These two met-
367   rics are standard metrics for evaluating model performance, particularly in classification tasks. In
368   addition, we specifically design another three metrics tailered for measuring the “conversational
369   consistency” in our problem. Overall Flipping Rate (OFR) refers to the percentage of instances
370   where a model provides different answers between two rounds of a question-answering process, re-
371   flecting its instability or adaptability between iterations. Correctly Flipping Rate (CFR) measures
372   the proportion of cases where the model’s initial response was incorrect, but the subsequent answer
373
      was correct after getting the feedback from a user, indicating the model’s ability to consistently han-
      dle different ways of making inquiries. In contrast, Incorrectly Flipping Rate (iCFR) measures the
374
      percentage of instances where the model’s initial response was correct but became incorrect in the
375
      subsequent answer. This metric signifies a decline in performance or consistency across interaction
376   rounds. The three flipping rates help assess the model’s reliability and its ability to consistently
377   respond to different types of inquiries.

      Implementation details. As mentioned above, pretrained LLMs (i.e., Llama v3.1 8B, Mistral AI
379   7B, and Gemma v2 9B) are publicly available from repositories such as Huggingface’s model hub.
380   During fine-tuning, essential hyperparameters are set, including a learning rate of 1e − 4, batch size
381   of 8, number of epochs set to 10, and the optimizer of AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2019). The
382   training process is managed using Huggingface’s ‘Trainer‘ class, which streamlines the handling
383   of training loops, evaluation, and logging. For CC-SFT, we set λ = 0.1 while keeping the other

      6   C ONCLUSION
519
      In this work, we address the critical issue of conversational inconsistency in LLMs by introducing a
520
      novel Conversationally Consistent Supervised Fine-Tuning method. Our approach, which explicitly
521
      accounts for two-turn conversations and incorporates a Wasserstein distance-based consistency loss,
522   demonstrated significant improvements in maintaining coherent responses across dialogue turns.
523   Through extensive experiments on OpenBookQA, GSM8K, and MedQA-USMLE datasets using
524   Llama v3.1, Mistral AI, and Gemma, we show that CC-SFT consistently outperforms standard
525   fine-tuning, reducing flipping rates and enhancing second-round response accuracy. We provide
526   theoretical convergence guarantees and analyze the impact of the consistency loss coefficient. Our
527   work contributes to enhancing the reliability and trustworthiness of LLMs in multi-turn dialogues,
528   particularly crucial for high-stakes applications in healthcare and legal domains. Future research
529   directions include extending the method to longer conversation histories, exploring its applicability
530
      to other language model architectures, and investigating its impact on specific downstream tasks. By
      mitigating conversational inconsistency, this study paves the way for more dependable AI-assisted
531
      services and decision-support systems, bringing us closer to the goal of truly reliable and coherent
532
      conversational AI.
