009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011             Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) is the bread and butter of Large Language Model
012             (LLM) finetuning. LoRA learns an additive low-rank perturbation of a pretrained
013             matrix parameter to align the model to a new task or dataset. We identify three
014             core limitations to LoRA for finetuning–with only a limited amount of training
015             steps. First, it employs Dropout as a means to prevent overfitting. We prove that
016             Dropout is only suitable for long training episodes but fails to reliably regularize
017             training for short training episodes, e.g., finetuning. Second, LoRA’s parameters
018
                initialization is at 0 makes the optimization landscape poorly conditioned dur-
                ing the first steps of training. That poor conditioning combined with the need to
019
                move away from 0 lead to slow training dynamics. Third, the scaling factor that
020
                multiply each LoRA additive perturbation create “short-sighted” interactions be-
021             tween the LoRA modules of different layers. Motivated by principled analysis of
022             those limitations, we find an elegant solution: a Dropout-free, scaling-free, LoRA
023             with Adaptive Learning rate–coined ALLoRA. By scaling the per sample and per
024             parameter gradients with a coefficient inversely proportional to parameters’ ℓ2
025             norm, ALLoRA alleviates those three limitations. As a by-product, ALLoRA re-
026             moves two hyper-parameters from LoRA: the scaling factor and the dropout rate.
027             Empirical results show that ALLoRA admits better accuracy than LoRA on vari-
028             ous settings, including against recent LoRA variants such as Weight-Decomposed
029
                Low-Rank Adaptation (DoRA). Ablation studies show our solution is the optimal
                in a family of weight-dependent / output-dependent approaches.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
034
      Large Language Models (LLMs) Hoffmann et al. (2022); Touvron et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023)
035
      are Deep Neural Networks (DNNs)–commonly built from Transformer with self-attention–built for
036
      sequence processing, e.g., Natural Language Processing (NLP). LLMs have radically changed the
037   way we approach NLP Chowdhary (2020) by removing the need for handcrafted feature engineering
038   such as bags of words Zhang et al. (2010). Instead, current solutions directly operate on the input
039   data–or a lossless compression known as tokens Shibata et al. (1999). Because we now have access
040   to humongous amount of text data, the standard training pipeline for LLMs take the following form.
041   First, the LLM is pretrained a large text corpus through next-token prediction. That autoregressive
042   pretext-task enables the LLM to learn the underlying dynamic of the language. Commonly, RLHF
043   is also employed after pretraining to make the model’s behavior shift from autoregressive to agentic.
044   Then, the LLM is fine-tuned on a more specific downstream task or dataset. That fine-tuning is
045
      user-specific and plays a fundamental role in making LLMs practically useful but relies on much
      more limited datasets, as we formalize below.
046
047       Premise: The training regime involved in pretraining and fine-tuning are drastically different.
048       The former takes places on large “industrial” computational clusters with limitless data and
049       training steps. The latter takes place on small user-owned computational resources with limited
050       data and training steps.
051
052   That premise is now widely accepted upon as the latest state-of-the-art LLM solutions stem from
053   the numerous open-source industry groups such as Meta’s Llama Dubey et al. (2024), Google’s
      Gemma Team et al. (2024), Apple’s OpenELM Mehta et al. (2024), or Cohere’s Aya. Hence, as

054
      LLM practitioners, most of the attention is now turning into deriving fine-tuning strategies that meet
055   the very particular needs of fine-tuning LLMs.
056
057   To tackle that paradigm shift introduced by the pretraining-finetuning strategy, specialized meth-
058
      ods have been developed, such as the eponymous Low-Rank Adaption (LoRA). LoRA has fueled
      countless deployment of LLMs–as it took a gigantic leap in accommodating for the fine-tuning
059
      regime. In short, LoRA proposes to fine-tune a LLM by learning an additive low-rank matrix per-
060
      turbation to some of the LLM’s internal parameter matrices. Core to its design, LoRA leverages
061   (i) Dropout Srivastava et al. (2014) as a mean to prevent overfitting to the fine-tuning task, and (ii)
062   zero-initialization to ensure that training starts from the LLM’s pretrained mapping, and (iii) a scal-
063   ing factor that rescales the LoRA’s matrix factorization. While the impact of LoRA is ubiquitous,
064   we nonetheless believe that LoRA could be further improved based on three observations.
065
066
          LoRA’s three fatal flaws for finetuning: First, Dropout–a stochastic regularizer–whose ben-
          efits quickly vanish when considering fine-tuning, and can in fact introduce detrimental ad-
067
          ditional variance to the training. Second, the zero-initialization which is difficult to escape
068
          from as the fine-tuning parameters start from a saddle point. Third, the scaling parameter that
069       introduces nonlinear interactions between LoRA modules of different layers.
070
071
      While each of those three design choices are well-motivated when considering long training, e.g.,
072   pretraining, it becomes harder to prove their benefits when considering fine-tuning that only employs
073   a minimal amount of training steps. That is why, after carefully bringing to light and studying the
074   above flaws of LoRA–in the context of fine-tuning–in section 3, we will propose a novel variation
075   of LoRA that we coin ALLoRA for Adaptive Learning rate LoRA (section 4). ALLoRA proposes
076   to remove the Dropout regularizer and the scaling factor while adding an adaptive learning rate for
077   the low-rank matrices entries. As depicted in listing 1, the implementation is straightforward with
078   theoretical and practical benefits. First, by removing the Dropout regularization and the scaling
079   factor, ALLoRA is simpler to employ as it no longer requires cross-validation of those parameters.
080
      Second, we demonstrate how ALLoRA improves performances over LoRA and alternatives such as
      DoRA. In short, our adaptive learning rate strategy is able to prevent over-fitting, learn competitive
081
      solutions, and converge more quickly than alternatives–all while employing less hyper-parameters.
082
083   We summarize our contributions below:
084
085
             1. We identify three inefficiencies (sections 3.1 to 3.3) in the current LoRA design that make
                it unfit for short training, i.e., finetuning.
086
087          2. We propose a novel adaptive learning rate variation of LoRA–coined ALLoRA–free of two
088             of the original LoRA’s complicated designs: the Dropout regularizer and the scaling factor.
089          3. We empirically validate the benefits of ALLoRA over numerous datasets and model ar-
090             chitectures including the latest Llama3 family. We obtain that despite ALLoRA employing
091             less hyperparameters than LoRA, it is able to outperform its counter part and recent variants
092             such as DoRA consistently.

097   2     R ELATED W ORKS
098
099   LoRA is a type of Parameter Efficient Fine Tuning (PEFT) method designed to reduce the cost of
100   finetuning LLMs. As LLMs typically have large number of parameters–in the scale of billions–one
101   can not afford to finetune all those parameters on a particular downstream task or dataset. Existing
102   PEFT can be divided into three categories, namely Adapter-based Methods, Prompt-based Methods,
103   and LoRA.
104   Adapter-based methods (Houlsby et al. (2019), He et al. (2022), Karimi Mahabadi et al. (2021b),
105   and Karimi Mahabadi et al. (2021a)) introduce additional trainable modules, a.k.a. the adapters,
106   into the original backbone whose weights are frozen during the finetuning. In Houlsby et al. (2019),
107   linear modules were added in sequence to the existing layer, while in He et al. (2022), they were
      added in parallel to the existing layer for the sake of better performance.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Prompt-based methods (Lester et al. (2021), Razdaibiedina et al. (2023), and Wang et al. (2023))
109   introduce soft tokens as trainable parameters and prepend them to the prompt. This category is the
110   least intrusive as the finetuning can be done by only prompting the LLMs. However, prompt-based
111   methods are in general sensitive to initialization and their overall effectiveness is affected.
112
      LoRA Hu et al. (2021) uses low-rank matrices to simulate weight changes of the pretrained weights.
113
      Since low-rank matrices can be merged back to original weights, LoRA does not incur any additional
114
      cost at inference, which is a significant advantage over the other two categories. Many variants
115   were proposed lately. For example, in Zhang et al. (2023), SVD decomposition was employed to
116   determine significance of singular values, and less important ones are pruned. Hyeon-Woo et al.
117   (2022) applies low-rank Hadamard product to federated learning. Qiu et al. (2023) and Liu et al.
118   (2024b) adopt orthogonal factorization and applied to diffusion models. Renduchintala et al. (2023)
119   introduces weight tying and realizes more savings on number of parameters. A unified LoRA family
120   was introduced for Stable diffusion in Yeh et al. (2024). Different combinations of LoRA are chosen
121   for different tasks in Ponti et al. (2022). A scaling vectors is learnt to adjust a pair of frozen random
122   matrices shared across layers in Kopiczko et al. (2023).
123   More recently, Liu et al. (2024a) proposes decomposing the weights into directional and magnitude
124   components to boost accuracy. Hayou et al. (2024a) studies the optimal initialization of the low-
125   rank matrices, and a follow-up work Hayou et al. (2024b) proposes to apply different learning rate
126   to different low-rank matrices. Superficially this is similar to one of our idea to adapt learning rate,
127   though our idea is inspired by a principled study of dropout (Srivastava et al. (2014)).
128   More broadly, Zhao et al. (2024) applies the low-rank concept to compute low-rank gradients di-
129   rectly. Jang et al. (2024) provides rigours theatrical study on the existence and convergence of
130   LoRA solutions. And Zhang & Pilanci (2024) is a study of the potential ill conditioned low-rank
131   matrices.

148   3.1   F IRST F LAW: A S TOCHASTIC R EGULARIZATION THAT W ILL NOT C ONVERGE
149
150   LoRA’s regularization hinges on using Dropout Srivastava et al. (2014), i.e., on applying a multi-
151   plicative binary mask to the latent space of the matrix factorization. In fact, it has been long known
152   that Dropout is a great solution to prevent overfitting. However, those benefits have either been
153   found in the context of (pre-)training, or through theoretical derivations assuming infinite training
154   time, i.e., on expectation. For example, Wager et al. (2013) shows how in the linear regime dropout
155
      acts as some variant of ℓ2 regularization on normalized design matrix (inputs), a result also found in
      the previous study of Wang & Manning (2013). However, we now argue that both previous empiri-
156
      cal and theoretical studies showing the benefits of dropout do not hold in a fine-tuning setting where
157
      the number of training steps is highly limited.

368   accuracy just as dropout does, and therefore ALLoRA-OD may not be as good as ALLoRA.

370   from adaptive learning rate to Adaptive Scaling Factor, or ASF-LoRA, defined by

516   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
517
518
      This paper identifies three major flaws of LoRA, namely dropout, poor optimization landsacpe, and
519   scaling factor. We conducted principled analysis and proved that dropout is not a must-have in the
520   finetuning regime. After uncovering the hidden connection between dropout, scaling factor, and
521   learning rate, we proposed a unified adaptive learning framework to address them all. Motivated by
522   which, we proposed a novel LoRA variant: ALLoRA. Empirical results show that ALLoRA admits
523   better accuracy than plain LoRA over multiple backbones, datasets, and learning rates; and better
524   accuracy than recent successful LoRA variants such as DoRA. Ablation study shows that ALLoRA
525   is the optimal in a family of adaptive methods.
526   Below we list a few interesting research directions and invite researchers to explore the frontier
527   opened-up by our research:
528
529         • The adaptive learning framework introduced by this paper is generic and may find broad ap-
530           plications beyond LoRA. Other use cases such as pretraining may not have the constraints
531           that the weight matrix must be initialized at 0. But they may have other types of constraints,
532           which may be solved by adaptive learning with a different adaptor function.
533         • Within the LoRA use case, we only examine one particular adaptor function, there could
534           be other adaptor functions that have superior performance.
535
            • We only provide empirical evidence that ALLoRA admits better accuracy. Theoretical
536
              guarantee is needed, especially for the convoluted case where the base model has multiple
537
              layers.
538
539         • Starting from 0 weights may avoid the lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin (2019)),
              for good or bad, where adaptive learning rate can be a handy tool.
