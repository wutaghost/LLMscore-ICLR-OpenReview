009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Multilingual capability is an essential aspect for large multimodal models, since
012            they are usually deployed across various countries and languages. However, most
013            existing benchmarks for multilingual multimodal reasoning struggle to differentiate
014            between models of varying performance; even language models without visual
015            capabilities can easily achieve high scores. This leaves a comprehensive evaluation
016
               of leading multilingual multimodal models largely unexplored. In this work, we
               introduce M4U, a novel and challenging benchmark for assessing the capability
017
               of multi-discipline multilingual multimodal understanding and reasoning. M4U
018
               contains 8,931 samples covering 64 disciplines across 16 subfields in Science,
019            Engineering, and Healthcare in Chinese, English, and German. Using M4U, we
020            conduct extensive evaluations of 21 leading Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) and
021            Large Language Models (LLMs) with external tools. The evaluation results show
022            that the state-of-the-art model, GPT-4o, achieves only 47.6% average accuracy on
023            M4U. Additionally, we observe that the leading LMMs exhibit significant language
024            preferences. Our in-depth analysis indicates that leading LMMs, including GPT-4o,
025            suffer performance degradation when prompted with cross-lingual multimodal
026            questions, such as images with key textual information in Chinese while the
027
               question is in German. We believe that M4U can serve as a crucial tool for
               systematically evaluating LMMs based on their multilingual multimodal reasoning
028
               capabilities and monitoring their development.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Multimodal reasoning is an essential aspect of human-level intelligence. AI systems with strong
034   multimodal reasoning capabilities have extensive applications, including automatic scientific dis-
035   covery, autonomous driving, and healthcare. The rapid advancements in Large Language Models
036   (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023) have led to the development of Large
037   Multimodal Models (LMMs) (Anil et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a; AI, 2024), which
038   demonstrate remarkable performance across a broad range of tasks, such as image captioning and
039
      visual question answering. Numerous benchmarks have been established to comprehensively evaluate
      these leading LMMs in real-world scenarios (Liu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a;d; Koh et al., 2024; Fu
040
      et al., 2023). Unlike perceptual tasks (Goyal et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2015), multimodal reasoning
041
      tasks, including mathematical reasoning (Lu et al., 2024b) and scientific question answering (Lu
042   et al., 2022; Kembhavi et al., 2016), present significant challenges for neural models. These tasks
043   necessitate an understanding of domain-specific knowledge and the ability to perform complex logical
044   reasoning alongside visual content. Additionally, multilingual capability is crucial for real-world
045   applications, as these models are typically deployed across various countries and languages.
046
      Many datasets are curated to evaluate the capability of multilingual multimodal reasoning. However,
047
      the multimodal component of existing benchmarks (Zhang et al., 2023b; Das et al., 2024; Wang
048   et al., 2023b) is limited in scale. We observe that the current data on multilingual multimodal
049   reasoning suffers from language disparities in task complexity. For instance, the multimodal part
050   of M3Exam (Zhang et al., 2023b) contains 61% high-difficulty questions in English, but only 23%
051   high-difficulty questions in Chinese. Furthermore, the existing benchmark struggles to differentiate
052   between models of varying multimodal capabilities. As shown in Table 2, without any visual
053   information, the multilingual LLM, Qwen-1.5-14B Chat, easily achieves high scores: 66.4% and
      56.0% accuracy on the Chinese and English sections of M3Exam, respectively. Consequently, a

078   To advance the development of multilingual LMMs, we introduce M4U, a novel and challenging
079   benchmark for evaluating foundational models on expert-level multilingual multimodal understanding
080
      and reasoning. Specifically, we assembled a team of over 10 college and graduate students to collect a
      high-quality dataset and assess its difficulty and correctness. As shown in Figure 1, M4U consists of
081
      8,931 multiple-choice questions covering 64 disciplines across 16 subfields in Science, Engineering,
082
      and Healthcare. To minimize the risk of data contamination, samples are collected from college
083   exams and quizzes from online video lectures. Additionally, a significant portion (35%) of the
084   questions in M4U are written by our team based on textbooks. Figure 2 illustrates an example from
085   the Chemistry-Inorganic part of M4U, demonstrating that our dataset requires expert-level multimodal
086   reasoning and multilingual capability.
087
      With M4U, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation, both quantitative and qualitative, on the zero-
088
      shot performance of 17 leading LMMs and 4 LLMs. Furthermore, we assess the performance
089   of the LMMs with chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023c) and the
090   LLMs with external tools, such as a powerful captioning model. As shown in Table 3 in §3.3,
091   the most advanced model, GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a), achieves only 47.6% average accuracy with
092   zero-shot prompting on the M4U dataset, demonstrating the significant challenge M4U poses for
093   existing models. Additionally, we observe significant language preferences among the leading LMMs:
094   InstructBLIP Vicuna-7B achieves 29.8% accuracy on the English section, but only 13.7% and 19.7%
095   accuracy on the Chinese and German sections, respectively. Further results (§3.4) indicate that leading
096   LMMs suffer performance degradation when prompted with cross-lingual multimodal questions,
097
      such as images with key textual information in Chinese while the question is in German. Our
      in-depth analysis (§4) reveals that the errors of GPT-4V(ision) are mainly due to limited perception
098
      ability, domain-specific knowledge, and reasoning. These findings demonstrate that LMMs still have
099
      significant room for improvement, particularly in multilingual multimodal reasoning.

      2.2   DATA C OLLECTION
187
      Data sources. Following MMMU (Yue et al., 2023), we go through the educational programs of
188
      top universities, then select 64 subjects of 16 subfields from Science, Engineering and Healthcare
189   whose applications highly rely on visual information. We recruit a team of over 10 college students
190   to collect multiple-choices questions from public available sources. To minimize the risk of data
191   contamination for foundation models, different from M3Exam (Zhang et al., 2023b), we do not
192   include the samples from the official exam papers, e.g., National Postgraduate Entrance Examination
193   and national professional exams. Although these resources usually have higher quality and are well
194   organized, they are also easy to be curated for the training of LLMs. Therefore, we carefully select
195   the data sources for M4U: most questions of our dataset are collected from the quizzes of online
196   video lectures and college exams in PDF documents. Further 35% of questions are written by our
197
      team according to the textbooks. As shown in Table 3, the state-of-the-art open-source multilingual
      LLM, Qwen-1.5-14B Chat (Bai et al., 2023a), only has 25.1% average accuracy on M4U dataset
198
      without any visual information. This proves that our data is less exposed in the training of language
199
      models.
200
201
      Data processing. The primary sources of M4U include college exams, the quizzes of online video
202
      lectures and the written questions. Most of college exams are uploaded by their students as images or
203
      scanned PDF documents, while the quizzes of online video lectures can be taken as the screenshot.
204   We first adopt the OCR tools to convert these images into plain texts, then manually correct the
205   potential errors of OCR results. Besides, we also write a large portion (35%) of questions according to
206   the textbooks. For the mathematical formulas and the chemical structures, we require the annotators
207   to convert them into LATEX format. Since the samples of M4U may include multiple images in the
208   questions or options, the annotators also annotate the location and type of each image (e.g. tables,
209   blueprints and medical images).
210   After collecting the data, we design a two-stage post-processing pipeline to further improve the
211   quality of M4U. We first design the guidelines to allow each annotator to score the collected samples
212   from three dimensions: image quality, question description quality and the difficulty of visual
213   understanding, and filter out the questions with average scores lower than 2.0. We present the
214   distribution of image resolution for M4U in Figure 7 of Appendix B. The detailed guidelines are
215   summarized in the Appendix A.2. Then we recruit a team of graduate students of related major to
      assess the difficulty and quality of the curated questions. We further filter out the questions with

      2.3   E VALUATION
221
222   We evaluate the zero-shot performance of 17 leading LMMs of different scales on M4U. The models
223   are required to follow the instruction to directly generate the predicted option for each question. To
224   minimize the format discrepancy between training and evaluation, we handle models that support
225   interleaved image-text documents by inserting the visual tokens of each image into the corresponding
226   position as in training. For models that only support image-text pairs as input, we place all visual
227   tokens at the beginning of the sentence and use annotated positions (e.g., <image_1>, <image_2>)
228
      to refer to each image. Furthermore, we also evaluate the performance of various LMMs with
      chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023c) and LLMs equipped with detailed
229
      visual captions.

453   4    Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS
454
455   We conduct qualitative analysis for GPT-4V with the chain-of-thought prompting. Specifically, we
456   randomly sample 75 questions (2.5%) from different disciplines of each language. In these instances,
457   GPT-4V has errors in responses and analysis in at least one language. We analyze the cause of these
458   wrong cases, and divided them into six categories: perceptual error, lack of knowledge, reasoning
459   error, textual understanding, annotation error and answer extraction error.
460   The distribution of selected samples across different categories are illustrated in Figure 4. Perceptual
461   error, lack of knowledge, and reasoning error account for the major causes of failed cases (96% in
462   Chinese, 95% in English, and 92% in German). Regarding perceptual error illustrated in Figure 14,
463   GPT-4V identified the position labeled 2 as the support above the compass, but it actually points to
464   the compass needle. For reasoning error, as shown in Figure 26, GPT-4V only considers the power
465   supply on the left and does not consider the power supply on the right. We observe that GPT-4V
466   tends to exhibit lack of knowledge on the Chinese part of M4U, while reasoning errors are more
467   likely to occur in German and English. More results can be found in the Appendix C.
468
469   Perceptual Error. Perceptual error is the most frequent error made by GPT-4V. It corresponds to
470   the illusion phenomenon that occurs when extracting visual information from images provided by the
471
      questions. These kinds of hallucination could be divided in two main categories: visual information
      deficiency and misinterpretation. As presented in Figure 22, visual information deficiency occurs
472
      when GPT-4V overlooked crucial conditions and information provided in the image associated with
473
      the question, such as dimensions and scales annotated in engineering blueprints and numerical values
474   provided in physics experiments thereby failing to complete the reasoning chain. Figure 16 shows a
475   typical case for the visual information misinterpretation: the extracted information is complete but
476   contains mistakes. A portion of these mistakes are common perceptual errors in OCR and visual
477   localization.
478
479   Lack of Knowledge. We define the lack of knowledge as the model has factual misunderstanding
480   about the key concepts in questions and provides erroneous premise to the reasoning process.
481   Figure 24 illustrates that GPT-4V ignores that the amplitude needs to be judged on an equivalent basis
482   to the same period, and as shown in Figure 25, the model equates the average kinetic energy of a
483   molecule to the kinetic energy of a single molecule, overlooking key preconditions of physical laws.
484
485   Reasoning Error. The reasoning error is categorized as the mathematical miscalculations and
      logical errors in the analysis procedure, which often occur in subjects need numerical computations

      5   R ELATED W ORK
496
497
498   Recent years have witnessed a trend towards large-scale multimodal pre-training, which aims to
499
      unify various vision-language tasks with a single model (Dai et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024a; Bai
      et al., 2023b; OpenAI, 2023; 2024a). With the rapid progress of LMMs, previous benchmarks (e.g.,
500
      VQA-v2 (Goyal et al., 2017), GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019)) are insufficient to comprehensively
501
      evaluate the general multimodal capability of these models. Therefore, many datasets are curated
502   for evaluate different aspects of multimodal capability, spanning from robustness and hallucination
503   (e.g., POPE (Li et al., 2023d)) to general perception capability (e.g., MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2024) and
504   MMBench (Liu et al., 2023)). As for multimodal reasoning tasks, MathVista (Lu et al., 2024b)
505   presented a collection of diverse challenging mathematical and visual tasks. After that, instead
506   focusing on the mathematical domain, MMMU (Yue et al., 2023) introduced a large-scale collection
507   of more difficult expert-level problems that cover 30 different subjects. However, these benchmarks
508   are primarily focused on English.
509   Multilingual capability is crucial for LLMs and LMMs. Many holistic evaluations have been con-
510   ducted for LLMs, such as PAWS-X (Yang et al., 2019), XCOPA (Ponti et al., 2020), XStoryCloze (Lin
511   et al., 2022), MGSM (Shi et al., 2022), MMMLU OpenAI (2024b). As for the evaluation of LMMs,
512   M3Exam (Zhang et al., 2023b) collects the official exam papers of 9 different languages. However,
513   they mainly focus on the evaluation of language capability. Despite M3Exam contains the samples
514   with image as the input, its multimodal part is limited in scale. Besides, M3Exam struggles to
515   differentiate between models of varying multimodal performance. As shown in Table 2, a language
516
      model without visual capabilities, Qwen-1.5-14B-Chat can easily achieve high scores. Different
      from previous works, M4U covers over 64 disciplines across Science, Engineering and Healthcare.
517
      We conduct the strict collection guidelines and quality control, which ensures that M4U requires
518
      significant visual efforts and domain-specific knowledge to perform multi-step reasoning.
519
520
521   6   C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this work, we introduce M4U, a novel and challenging benchmark for evaluating the capability
524
      of multilingual multimodal understanding and reasoning. M4U contains 8,931 multiple-choice
525
      questions, covering 64 disciplines across 16 subfields in Science, Engineering, and Healthcare in
526   Chinese, English, and German. Table 2 demonstrates that M4U requires significant visual efforts
527   compared with M3Exam. As shown in Table 3, the state-of-the-art model, GPT-4o, achieves only
528   47.6% average accuracy with zero-shot prompting, indicating that M4U is quite challenging for
529   existing models. Furthermore, we observe that the leading LMMs exhibit significant language
530   preferences. These results demonstrate that there is still significant room for improvement in LMMs,
531   especially in expert-level multilingual multimodal reasoning.
532
533
534   7   L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
535
536   Currently M4U focuses on the evaluation of science problems for multimodal reasoning. In the future,
537   we aim to extend M4U to support more languages and investigate the performance of multilingual
538   LMMs on questions associated with cultural backgrounds (e.g., history and politics). Additionally,
539   we plan to include multilingual rationales for M4U to construct a fine-grained metric that considers
      the correctness of both reasoning steps and final predictions.

706   M4U consists of 8,931 multiple-choices questions, covering 64 disciplines of 16 subfields from

710   the importance of strictly following copyright and licensing rules from the original data sources,
711   particularly avoiding materials from websites that prohibit copying and redistribution. If you come
712
      across any data samples that may violate copyright or licensing regulations, please inform us. Once
      verified, such samples will be promptly removed.

      A.2   F IRST- STAGE P ROCESSING G UIDELINES
716   We summarize the detailed first-stage processing guidelines for the annotators. For each dimension,
717   we require the annotator to score the sample following the below guidelines. The question with the
718   higher scores indicates higher quality. We filter out the questions with average scores lower than 2.0.
719   As for the image quality, the standard is:
720
721          • 0 score: The image is extremely blurry, difficult to recognize, or most of it is cropped,
722            resulting in severe information loss.
723          • 1 score: The image is relatively blurry, details are hard to discern, or parts of the image are
724            cropped, leading to some information loss.
725          • 2 score: The image is slightly blurry; most content is recognizable but details are unclear, or
726            the image is slightly cropped, but most information is complete.
727
             • 3 score: The image is mostly clear; all major content is recognizable, though some details
728            may not be clear.
729
             • 4 score: The image is clear; all content and details are easily recognizable with no apparent
730
               defects.
731
732          • 5 score: The image is very clear; details are excellently represented, complete without any
733
               cropping or obstructions, meeting or exceeding the expected quality standards.
734
      The guideline for measuring the question description quality is:
735
736          • 0 score: The question is vague and completely unintelligible, with no clear intent.
737
             • 1 score: The question statement is ambiguous, difficult to fully understand its intent, with
738            multiple possible interpretations.
739
             • 2 score: The question statement is basically clear, but there are some ambiguities or lack of
740
               rigor that need further clarification.
741
742          • 3 score: The question statement is clear, though there are some details that are not rigorous
743
               or there is slight ambiguity.
744          • 4 score: The question statement is both clear and rigorous, with details well handled, and
745            only very minor issues present.
746          • 5 score: The question statement is extremely clear and rigorous, logical, without any
747            ambiguity, fully meeting high standards.
748
749   The standard for measuring the difficulty of visual understanding is:
750
             • 0 score: The question almost does not rely on visual ability, can be fully understood without

               any visual information.

753
             • 1 score: The question does not completely rely on visual ability, both visual and non-visual
               information are balanced.

               visual information assists understanding.
