                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
               Symbolic regression seeks to uncover physical knowledge from experimental data.
012
               Recently a line of work on deep reinforcement learning (DRL) formulated the
013            search for optimal expressions as a sequential decision-making problem. How-
014            ever, training these models is challenging due to the inherent instability of the
015            policy gradient estimator. We observe that many numerically equivalent yet sym-
016            bolically distinct expressions exist, such as log(x21 x32 ) and 2 log(x1 ) + 3 log(x2 ).
017            Building on this, we propose Deep Symbolic Regression via Reasoning Equivalent
018            eXpressions (DSR-R EX). The high-level idea is to enhance policy gradient esti-
019            mation by leveraging both expressions sampled from the DRL and their numer-
020            ically identical counterparts generated via an expression reasoning module. Our
021
               DSR-R EX (1) embeds mathematical laws and equalities into the deep model, (2)
               reduces gradient estimator variance with theoretical justification and (3) encour-
022
               ages RL exploration of different symbolic forms in the search space of all expres-
023
               sions. In our experiments, DSR-R EX is evaluated on several challenging scien-
024            tific datasets, demonstrating superior performance in discovering equations with
025            lower Normalized MSE scores. Additionally, DSR-R EX computes gradients with
026            smaller empirical standard deviation, compared to the previous DSR method.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Mathematical modeling of observed phenomena is essential to many scientific and engineering dis-
032   ciplines. Symbolic regression has emerged as a promising approach to automatically discover new
033   physical laws from experimental data (Schmidt & Lipson, 2009; Wang et al., 2019; Udrescu &
034   Tegmark, 2020; Cory-Wright et al., 2024). Recent researchers proposed the use of deep reinforce-
035   ment learning (DRL) to guide the search for optimal expressions by framing the problem as a se-
036   quential decision-making process (Petersen et al., 2021; Landajuela et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024).
037   The main challenge of DRL is the unstable training, which arises primarily from the high variance
038   of the policy gradient estimator (Wu et al., 2018). In literature, the common solution to reduce the
039   variance is to subtract a baseline from the estimator (Weaver & Tao, 2001). Another approach is
040   reward-shaping (Ng et al., 1999), which smooths the reward function in RL by designing an extra
041   potential function. However, this potential is hard to design, since the reward function is sensitive to
042   small modifications in the expression. The rest of the works are discussed in the related work.
043   We observe that different symbolic formats can represent identical mathematical expressions. For
044   example, log(x21 x32 ), log(x21 ) + log(x32 ), and 2 log(x1 ) + 3 log(x2 ) are a group of numerically equiv-
045   alent but symbolically distinct expressions. Such a group can be obtained by a symbolic reasoning
046   engine that combinatorially applies mathematical equalities of addition, exp, log, etc. From the
047   DRL model perspective, this group is obtained by exploring the search space of all expressions
048   using different sequences of step-by-step prediction from the model.
049   Building on this observation, we introduce Deep Symbolic Regression via Reasoning Equivalent
050   eXpressions (DSR-R EX). DSR-R EX integrates an existing deep reinforcement learning model with
051   a proposed symbolic reasoning module to accelerate the discovery of governing expressions. By
052   comparing with the existing works, the major advantages of DSR-R EX are (1) embedding domain-
053   specific knowledge into the deep model by encoding known mathematical rules, laws, and equalities,
      (2) achieving variance reduction of the gradient estimator with a theoretical guarantee (in Theo-

084       Symbolic Expression. Let x ∈ Rn be a set of input variables and c ∈ Rm be a set of constants. The
085       expression ϕ connects a set of input variables x and a set of constant coefficients c by mathematical
086       operators. Variables are allowed to vary and coefficients remain the same. The mathematical opera-
087       tors can be addition, multiplication, etc. For example, ϕ = ec1 x1 cos(c2 x2 ) is a symbolic expression
088       with one variable x1 , two constant {c1 , c2 } and three operators {×, exp, cos}. To cope with the
089
          deep reinforcement learning, expression is represented as the traversal sequence of the expression
          tree (Petersen et al., 2021), the traversal sequence of expression graph (Kahlmeyer et al., 2024), or
090
          the sequence of grammar rules (Gec et al., 2022). We adopt the grammar definition in this work,
091
          because of it is clear definition and easy integration with the proposed reasoning module.
092

094       widely applied in diverse scientific domains (Ma et al., 2022; Brunton et al., 2016). Given a dataset

132   3    M ETHODOLOGY
133
134   Motivation. We take Figure 1 as an illustrative example to explain a new perspective on symbolic
135
      regression. One possible predicted expression is ϕ = ec1 x1 cos(c2 x1 ), describing the time-evolving
      behavior of the damped harmonic oscillator. This equation can be symbolically rewritten as ϕ′ =
136
      cos(c2 x1 )ec1 x1 by simply switching the operands of the multiplication operator. Additionally, it can
137
      be transformed into ϕ′′ = ec1 x1 sin(c2 x1 + π/2) using a trigonometric identity, that is cos(a) =
138   sin(a + π/2). Despite their different symbolic forms, these expressions produce the same numerical
139   output for the same input x, i.e., ϕ(x) = ϕ′ (x) = ϕ′′ (x). We refer to such sets of symbolically
140   distinct expressions that yield the same numerical output as numerically equivalent. They can be
141   generated by systematically applying mathematical identities or laws. Figure 1(b) shows part of the
142   applicable mathematical laws.
143
      From a reinforcement learning (RL) perspective, generating symbolically distinct expressions cor-
144   responds to exploring different subspaces of the expression space, by applying various sequences of
145   grammar rules during decision-making. Denote τ, τ ′ , τ ′′ as three sequences of predicted rules from
146   the RL that can be converted into expression ϕ, ϕ′ , ϕ′′ accordingly. We know τ ̸= τ ′ ̸= τ ′′ . Since the
147   reward function in DRL is based on the error between the output from the predicted expression and
148   the ground truth, all three expressions are assigned the same reward R(τ ) = R(τ ′ ) = R(τ ′′ ). Thus,
149   these expressions are equivalent under the RL reward function and are distinct under the prediction
150   order of the RL policy.
151   Our idea is to utilize the sequences sampled from the RL model and additional sequences gener-
152   ated by a symbolic expression reasoning module. These additional sequences capture mathematical
153   equality knowledge and also promote better exploration during RL policy learning. In the space
154   of all possible expressions, the RL model explores those sub-spaces directly sampled according to
155   the policy distribution and additional sub-spaces resulting from different predicted orders of the
156   grammar rules. By integrating this reasoning-driven exploration, our proposed method, DSR-R EX,
157   has the potential to discover higher-quality expressions with fewer iterations compared to the Deep
158
      Symbolic Regression (DSR) (Petersen et al., 2021).
159   Main Procedure. As depicted at the bottom of Figure 1, DSR-R EX consists of three key compo-
160   nents: (1) a sequential decoder that samples sequences of grammar rules following its probability
161   distribution step-by-step, (2) a symbolic reasoning module that extracts those equivalent expres-
      sions as well as the corresponding sequences of grammar rules, and (3) a parameter update module

215   The implementation of the symbolic reasoning module relies on the expression representation. We
      first brief the expression representation and present how we generate symbolic variants.

      Expression Representation. We use a context-free grammar defined by a tuple ⟨V, Σ, R, S⟩, where
217   V is a set of non-terminal symbols, Σ is a set of terminal symbols, R is a set of production rules and
218   S ∈ V is the start symbol (Todorovski & Dzeroski, 1997; Sun et al., 2023). We use (1) a set of non-
219   terminal symbols representing sub-expressions as V = {A}. (2) Set of input variables and constants
220   {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn , const} as Σ. (3) Set of rules representing possible mathematical operations such
221   as addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division, as R. For example, the addition operation is
222   represented as A → (A + A), where the rule replaces the left-hand symbol with the right-hand side.
223   (4) An start symbol A ∈ V . Given a sequence of rules that begin with the start symbol A, each
224   rule replaces the first non-terminal symbol A iteratively. The obtained output with only terminal
225
      symbols is a valid mathematical expression. Figure 1(a) presents a sequence of grammar rules that
      corresponds to equation ϕ = ec1 x1 cos(c2 x1 ).
226
227   To generate numerical-equivalent expressions, we use two strategies: (1) directly modifying the
228   sequence of grammar rules through pattern matching with mathematical laws, and (2) manipulating
229   the symbolic form using simplification and transformation rules from libraries like Sympy.
230   If we are given a sequence of grammar rules, the process begins by converting the sequence into
231   recursive arrays. If a rule contains two non-terminal symbols on the right-hand side, we group
232   the array into two sub-arrays, each representing a sub-expression. The next step involves pattern
233   matching with available mathematical laws, enabling element exchanges within these arrays. After
234   each modification, a copy of the entire array is saved. For example, commutative properties such
235   as a + b = b + a and a × b = b × a, as well as trigonometric, exponential, and logarithmic
236   identities like cos(x − y) = cos(x) cos(y) + sin(x) sin(y), can be applied. A selected list of these
237
      mathematical laws is provided in Figure 1(b), while more rules can be found in Appendix Table 2.
      Finally, the recursive array is flattened back into a sequence, and the sequential decoder is queried
238
      for its probability value. Summing these probabilities yields the grouped probability value qθ (ϕ), as
239
      defined in equation 4. Use the example in Figure 1(a), we have:

351
      Reinforcement Learning for Scientific Discovery. Recent advancements in artificial intelligence,
352
      particularly in deep reinforcement learning (RL), have demonstrated its potential for automating
353
      discoveries across various scientific fields (Kirkpatrick et al., 2021; Jumper et al., 2021; Wang
354   et al., 2023). Early work in this area focused on learning symbolic representations of scientific
355   concepts (Bradley et al., 2001; Bridewell et al., 2008). In domains such as materials discovery
356   and chemical engineering, RL agents have been applied to propose novel materials with desirable
357   properties (Beeler et al., 2024; Popova et al., 2018).
358
      Variance-Reduced Policy Gradient. Several techniques have been introduced to reduce the vari-
359
      ance of policy gradient estimates, a common challenge in reinforcement learning. One widely used
360   approach is the control variate method, where a baseline is subtracted from the reward to stabilize
361   the gradient (Weaver & Tao, 2001). Recent developments, such as Trust Region Policy Optimization
362   (TRPO) (Schulman et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2021) and Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schul-
363   man et al., 2017), leverage second-order information to enhance training stability. Other approaches,
364   such as reward reshaping (Zheng et al., 2018), modify rewards for specific state-action pairs. In-
365   spired by stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods (Johnson & Zhang, 2013; Deng et al., 2021),
366   Papini et al. (2018) proposed a variance-reduction technique tailored for policy gradients. Unlike
367   these methods, our proposed DSR-R EX is the first to reduce variance through symbolic reasoning
368
      over expressions, providing a novel contribution to this field.
369   Symbolic Regression with Domain Knowledge. Recent efforts have explored incorporating phys-
370   ical and domain-specific knowledge into the symbolic discovery process. AI-Feynman (Udrescu &
371   Tegmark, 2020; Udrescu et al., 2020; Keren et al., 2023; Cornelio et al., 2023) constrained the search
372   space to expressions that exhibit compositionality, additivity, and generalized symmetry. Similarly,
373   Tenachi et al. (2023) encoded physical unit constraints into equation sampling to eliminate physi-
374
      cally impossible solutions. Other works, such as (Bendinelli et al., 2023; Kamienny, 2023), further
      constrained the search space by integrating user-specified hypotheses and prior knowledge, offering
375
      a more guided approach to symbolic regression.
376
377   Thinking Fast and Slow. The interplay between fast and slow cognitive processes is a key feature
      of human intelligence (Kahneman, 2011; Anthony et al., 2017; Booch et al., 2021). We argue that

      rather than relying solely on the brute-force approach of learning from big data and extensive com-
379   putation (fast thinking), incorporating careful meta-reasoning to guide the discovery of ground-truth
380   equations (slow thinking) can lead to more efficient and effective outcomes.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488   In this paper, we presented Deep Symbolic Regression via Reasoning Equivalent eXpressions
489   (DSR-R EX), a novel approach that enhances deep reinforcement learning with symbolic reason-
490   ing. DSR-R EX effectively leverages mathematically equivalent expressions to stabilize the policy
491   gradient estimator, reducing its variance and encouraging exploration across the search space. Our
492   theoretical justification and empirical results demonstrate that DSR-R EX not only improves gradi-
493   ent estimation but also outperforms existing DRL-based methods in discovering governing equations
494   from real-world scientific data.
495   In terms of future work, we plan to include laws for vector-field operators, like div, curl, and
496   Laplacian operators. Another possible future direction is to give theoretical convergence analysis
497   for DSR-R EX.
