009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the rapid development of multimedia, the shift from unimodal textual senti-
012            ment analysis to multimodal image-text sentiment analysis has obtained academic
013            and industrial attention in recent years. However, multimodal sentiment analy-
014            sis is affected by unimodal data bias, e.g., text sentiment is misleading due to
015            explicit sentiment semantic, leading to low accuracy in the final sentiment classi-
016
               fication. In this paper, we propose a novel CounterFactual Multimodal Sentiment
               Analysis framework (CF-MSA) using causal counterfactual inference to construct
017
               multimodal sentiment causal inference. CF-MSA mitigates the direct effect from
018
               unimodal bias and ensures heterogeneity across modalities by differentiating the
019            treatment variables between modalities. In addition, considering the information
020            complementarity and bias differences between modalities, we propose a new op-
021            timisation objective to effectively integrate different modalities and reduce the
022            inherent bias from each modality. Experimental results on two public datasets,
023            MVSA-Single and MVSA-Multiple, demonstrate that the proposed CF-MSA has
024            superior debiasing capability and achieves new state-of-the-art performances. We
025            will release the code and datasets to facilitate future research.

      Sentiment analysis has been the fundamental research in the field of artificial intelligence. Early
030
      research on sentiment analysis (Wang et al., 2016; Bhange & Kasliwal, 2020) mainly focus on a
031   single text modality, making it difficult to deal with the complex multimodal scenarios and thus to
032   accurately capture the accurate users’ sentiments. Benefited from the development of information
033   technology and the widespread popularity of mobile device, a large amount of multimodal data such
034   as text and image can be uploaded and disseminated without delay. Multimoal data is also verified
035   to enlarge broader real-world application prospects, such as personalized recommendations (Gao
036   et al., 2016), sentiment cross-modal retrieval (Xu & Li, 2023) and user depression estimation (Sun
037   et al., 2022), etc. Such habits and prospects prompt the academic and industrial institutions to shift
038   from unimodal sentiment analysis to the multimodal one (Gandhi et al., 2023). attracts increasing
039
      research and industrial attention, as Therefore, it has become a research trend to shift from unimodal
      sentiment analysis to multimodal sentiment analysis.
040
041   In recent years, with the booming development of deep learning techniques, many works have ex-
042   tensively penetrated the field of multimodal sentiment analysis (Hu et al., 2022; Shi & Huang, 2023;
043   Jiang et al., 2023; Xu & Mao, 2017; Zhu et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024). The mainstream of them,
044   including multimodal large language model (MLLM) Achiam et al. (2023); Dubey et al. (2024)
045
      mainly relies on the joint learning of multimodal data, i.e., predicting a unified sentiment by fusing
      the multimodal features. Despite the promising performances, such mainstream mixes up the hid-
046
      den multimodal features without tracking the causal links to ensure the actual semantic elements to
047
      resolve the sentiment judgment. For example, Zhu et al. (2023a) proposes an image-text multimodal
048   interaction network for sentiment analysis, exploring the inter-modal alignment during modality
049   fusion. However, they still only focus on inter-modal interactions and ignore the effects of causal-
050   ity between multimodal features which leads to misleadingness of the sentiment expressions in the
051   wrong modality, i.e. modality bias. In fact, modality bias is a common phenomenon in multimodal
052   sentiment prediction models due to different semantic expressivity of different modalities, such as
053   explicit textual sentiment keywords and shallow visual semantic. For example, Figure1 shows that
      the red-font text causes the higher negative possibility while cat causes the higher positive possibil-

      ity. Therefore, how to perform unbiased inference during the training process with modality bias
055   remains a major challenge for multimodal sentiment prediction.
056
057   In real world, humans are able to rely on causal reasoning to effectively distinguish good bias from
058
      bad bias and thus make more unbiased predictions. Inspired by this, we introduce counterfactual
      causal reasoning to help the model eliminate individual modal biases, thereby identifying the main
059
      modality causal effects that truly determine sentiment classification. Specifically, counterfactual
060
      causal inference allows the model to think: “Would the same prediction have been made if the
061   particular modality information had not been seen?” With this inference pattern, the model can
062   simulate scenarios that weed out the effects of individual modalities, helping the model to identify
063   the main causal effects in multimodal scenarios that actually determine affective categorization, and
064   thus make more unbiased predictions. To do this, we formulated the modality bias in the multimodal
065   sentiment prediction as the direct causal effect of each modality on labeling, and mitigated the
066   modality bias by subtracting the direct modality effect from the total causal effect.

091   As shown in Figure1, the upper part demonstrates the traditional likelihood-based biased prediction
092   process, where these models rely on the multimodal joint prediction under biased training. It leads
093
      to over-reliance on a particular modality with high sentimental properties in prediction, such as the
      explicit sentiment words in text, and ignores the potential information of other modalities. This type
094
      of learning is prone to modality bias, especially when containing the explicit semantic information,
095
      and the model is more inclined to bias the prediction towards the dominant modality. In contrast, the
096   following counterfactual reasoning analyzes the prediction results in the absence of the influence of
097   a single modality by simulating the case where the modality is missing, and by comparing the differ-
098   ence between the two cases we can determine the influence of a single modality on the prediction,
099   thus eliminating the bias caused by the single modality. We use the generalized conventional model
100   and unimodal branching to train the ensemble model in the model training phase, and in the test-
101   ing phase, the reasoning is done through counterfactual thinking, aiming to make the model focus
102   more on the unbiased multimodal fusion information and thus make unbiased predictions. Experi-
103   mental results show that the CF-MSA method outperforms current state-of-the-art methods on the

      MVSA-Single and -Multiple datasets.

113   2     R ELATED W ORKS
114
115   In recent years, multimodal sentiment analysis has become an important research direction in the
116   field of sentiment computing (Gandhi et al., 2023). Studies (Zhu et al., 2023a) show that fusing fea-
117   tures from different modalities can significantly improve the performance of sentiment classification,
118   especially in the analysis of unstructured data such as social media. Therefore, As technology has
119   progressed, sentiment analysis has expanded from single-text analysis to multimodal sentiment anal-
120   ysis, which more comprehensively captures subtle changes in sentiment and provides more accurate
121   sentiment predictions. Early work in this area has focused on developing methods to better fuse data
122
      from different modalities. For example, Xu & Mao (2017) proposed MultiSentiNet , a deep semantic
      network for multimodal sentiment analysis in 2017. The model improves the accuracy of sentiment
123
      classification by extracting object and scene semantic features from images and combining them
124
      with sentiment information from text. De Toledo and Marcacini introduce a joint fine-tuning-based
125   transfer learning approach (de Toledo & Marcacini, 2022) for multimodal sentiment analysis. Their
126   approach combines pre-trained unimodal models (e.g., text and images) and achieves a multimodal
127   representation by jointly optimizing these models in a fine-tuning step, which requires significantly
128   less computational resources compared to complex models such as CLIP (Radford & et al., 2021).
129   Rajan et al. (2022) explore the relationship between Cross-Attention and Self-Attention in their
130   study. Meanwhile, Zhu et al. (2023b) introduce a multimodal approach for multi-level emotion clas-
131   sification. Their work further improves the accuracy and robustness of emotion classification by
132   combining multiple pre-trained unimodal models and effectively integrating them in the multimodal
133
      space.
134   However, these methods do not go further in the study of the causal relationships implied in the task.
135   In data-driven model learning, due to modality bias, the model cannot distinguish the impact of
136   unimodal information from that of multimodal fused information, which leads to the model learning
137   spurious correlations. Aware of this drawback in many existing models, counterfactual thinking and
138   causal inference have received attention in related fields in recent years, and have provided solutions
139
      to eliminate bias (Tang et al., 2020), enhancing model robustness (Louizos et al., 2017), and for
      supporting complex decision-making applications (Gencoglu & Gruber, 2020; Liu et al., 2023a;b)
140
      have provided solutions to these problems. In the multimodal context, researchers and scholars have
141
      applied causal inference to various domains, including video question answering (Zang et al., 2023),
142   image caption generation, visual tasks (Wang et al., 2020; 2021), and the design of various visual-
143   language reasoning tasks (Yang et al., 2021; Rao et al., 2021). Inspired by this idea, this paper
144   proposes a counterfactual multimodal sentiment analysis (CF-MSA) model that introduces causal
145   reasoning to mitigate the negative impact of modality bias on performance, thereby achieving more
146   accurate multimodal sentiment analysis.

      The study further proposes a counterfactual multimodal sentiment analysis (CF-MSA) framework
258   based on causal reasoning. As shown in Figure 3, sentiment analysis is accomplished by fusing
259   three independent model branches: pure text, pure image, and text-image integration, which process
260   different input modalities respectively, and combine them through a fusion function h to form the
261   final sentiment polarity prediction.
262
      Parameterization and model definition. The model used in the experiments is parameterized as
263
      follows, based on three branching models FT , FI , FIT and a fusion function h:
264
265                     Zt = FT (t) ,                 Zi = FI (i) ,            Zk = FIT (i, t) ,                 Zt,i,k = h (Zt , Zi , Zk )                               (11)
266
267   where FT is a pure text branch dealing with the mapping from text to emotion (i.e. T → Y ), FI is
268   a pure image branch dealing with the mapping from image to emotion (i.e. I → Y ), and FIT is a
269   synthesized branch of text-image synthesis (i.e. I, T → K → Y ). The output scores are fused by
      the function h to obtain the final score Zt,i,k .

      A    C ONCEPTS AND THEORIES RELATED TO CAUSAL REASONING
652
      In this section, we introduce the basic theory and related methods of causal reasoning, with special
653
      emphasis on the application of counterfactual ideas.
654
655   Causal graph is a directed acyclic graph (DAG) representing causal relationships between vari-
656   ables, notated as G={V, E}, where V denotes the set of variables and E denotes the set of causal
657   relationships. For example, if we are going to study the relationship between a student’s test score S
658
      and his level of effort in studying at school, E, and the amount of homework, H, we can construct a
      causal diagram as shown in the Figure 5, where the causal diagram contains three variables, E has a
659
      direct effect relationship on S, while H is an intermediate.
660
661   Counterfactual representation to facilitate the understanding of what may be a potential outcome
662   in reality in the counterfactual strategy, there are symbols set up here for counterfactual represen-
663   tation. Where in the exposition of this paper uppercase letters are used to symbolically represent
664
      the variables themselves, while the corresponding lowercase letters mark the specific values of these
      variables in the actual observations. For example, Se,h = S (E = e, H = h) denotes the value of S
665
      when E is set to e and H is set to h. In practice, we usually have a counterfactual representation of
666
      a variable as a function of its value. In practice, we usually have h = He = H (E = e), implying
667   that H is the value at which E is set to e. As shown in figure 5, there exist the following four
668   counterfactual case representations, and it is important to note here that only in the counterfactual
669   world can two cases of taking values occur.

708   In this section, we detail the implementation settings, including the model architecture, hyperparam-
709   eter configurations, and training procedures used in our experiments.
710
711   B.1   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
712
713   The model is a multi-modal framework that fuses information from both text and image inputs. It
714   consists of three main components:
715
             • Text Encoder: We utilize a pre-trained RoBERTa-base model for encoding the text
716
               input. The text encoder takes tokenized text sequences and their corresponding attention
717
               masks as input. The parameters of the RoBERTa model are partially fine-tuned with a
718            learning rate of 5 × 10−6 .
719
             • Image Encoder: For image encoding, we employ a ResNet-101 architecture pre-trained on
720
               ImageNet. The input images are resized to 224 × 224 and processed through the network,
721
               whose parameters are partially fine-tuned with a learning rate of 5 × 10−6 .
722
723
             • Fusion Module: After extracting the text and image features, they are fused using a multi-
               head attention mechanism. This fusion module consists of 8 attention heads with a dropout
724
               rate of 0.4. The fused features are then passed through fully connected layers to generate
725
               the final predictions.
726
727
      B.2   H YPERPARAMETERS
728
729   The hyperparameters used in the model are as follows:
730
731          • Text Encoder Learning Rate: 5 × 10−6
732          • Image Encoder Learning Rate: 5 × 10−6
733
             • Fusion Module Learning Rate: 3 × 10−3
734
735
             • Weight Decay: 0 for all components
736          • Batch Size: 16 for training and validation, 8 for testing
737          • Number of Epochs: 20
738
             • Dropout Rate: 0.2 for the text and image encoders, 0.4 for the fusion module
739
740
             • Number of Attention Heads: 8
741          • Loss Weights: The loss function is weighted with the class distribution [1.68, 9.3, 3.36] to
742            account for the imbalanced dataset.
743
744   B.3   T RAINING P ROCEDURE
745
      The model is trained for 20 epochs using the AdamW optimizer. We use two separate optimizers:
746
747          • AdamW for main parameters: This optimizer is used for fine-tuning the text encoder
748            (RoBERTa), image encoder (ResNet), and fusion module parameters. The learning rate is
749            set to 3 × 10−3 .
750
             • AdamW for specific parameters: A separate optimizer is used to update the KL
751            divergence-related parameters (c, c2, c3, c4) with a learning rate of 1 × 10−5 .
752
753   During each training iteration, the KL divergence loss is computed first and backpropagated using
754   the specific optimizer, followed by the main cross-entropy loss for classification tasks. Gradients are
755   computed and updated after each batch, and logging is performed to track the progress of the KL
      parameters over the training process.
