009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Optimization algorithms such as AdaGrad and Adam have significantly advanced
012             the training of deep models by dynamically adjusting the learning rate during the
013             optimization process. However, adhoc tuning of learning rates poses a challenge,
014             leading to inefficiencies in practice. To address this issue, recent research has
015             focused on developing “learning-rate-free” or “parameter-free” algorithms that
016
                operate effectively without the need for learning rate tuning. Despite these ef-
                forts, existing parameter-free variants of AdaGrad and Adam tend to be overly
017
                complex and/or lack formal convergence guarantees. In this paper, we present
018
                AdaGrad++ and Adam++, novel and simple parameter-free variants of AdaGrad
019             and Adam with convergence guarantees. We prove that AdaGrad++ achieves com-
020             parable convergence rates to AdaGrad in convex optimization without predefined
021             learning rate assumptions. Similarly, Adam++ matches the convergence rate of
022             Adam without relying on any conditions on the learning rates. Experimental re-
023             sults across various deep learning tasks validate the competitive performance of
024             AdaGrad++ and Adam++.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In recent years, optimization algorithms such as AdaGrad (Duchi et al., 2011) and Adam (Kingma,
029   2014) have emerged as powerful tools for enhancing the training of deep learning models by ef-
030   ficiently adapting the learning rate during the optimization process. While these algorithms have
031
      demonstrated remarkable performance gains in various applications, a notable drawback lies in the
      necessity of manual tuning for suitable learning rates. The process of learning rate tuning can be
032
      laborious and often requires extensive trial and error, hindering the efficiency and scalability of deep
033
      learning model development.
034
035   The intricate nature of learning rate tuning has motivated a large number of recent works to de-
036
      velop “learning-rate-free” or “parameter-free” algorithms that can work well under various differ-
      ent settings without learning rate tuning. Among the vast literature of parameter-free optimization
037
      methods, Ivgi et al. (2023) proposed a framework called distance over gradients (DoG), which
038
      gives a parameter-free version of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that shares certain features
039   as the AdaGrad-Norm algorithm (Streeter & McMahan, 2010; Ward et al., 2020). Motivated by
040   AdaGrad-Norm, another recent work (Defazio & Mishchenko, 2023) also gave a framework named
041   D-adaptation, and parameter-free variants of SGD and Adam were proposed under this framework.
042   More recently, Defazio et al. (2024) proposed a different approach for schedule-free online optimiza-
043   tion, based on which the authors developed new variants of schedule-free SGD and Adam/AdamW.
044   Despite the recent advances of parameter-free optimization algorithms, research on parameter-free
045   adaptive gradient methods1 remains relatively limited. Specifically, most of the existing parameter-
046   free algorithms are essentially variants of SGD, and entry-wisely adaptive learning rates in standard
047   AdaGrad and Adam algorithms are rarely considered in most of the existing parameter-free meth-
048   ods. Although Defazio & Mishchenko (2023); Mishchenko & Defazio (2023); Defazio et al. (2024)
049        1
             Adaptive gradient methods usually have multiple hyperparameters other than learning rates. For example,
050
      Adam implements exponential moving averages of first and second moments of gradients, which are controlled
051   by parameters β1 and β2 . Here we clarify that when discussing parameter-free adaptive gradient methods, we
052   still allow the algorithm to have such hyperparameters which do not require extensive tuning. This is consistent
053   with the convention in recent works on parameter-free optimization (Defazio & Mishchenko, 2023; Mishchenko
      & Defazio, 2023; Defazio et al., 2024).

      recently proposed variants of parameter-free AdaGrad, Adam and AdamW that implement entry-
055   wisely adaptive gradients, these algorithms all introduce rather significant modifications to the orig-
056   inal algorithms, and the parameter-free versions of Adam/AdamW are not backed up by theoretical
057   convergence guarantees.
058
      Motivated by the limitations of existing studies, in this work, we propose simple but efficient ver-
059
      sions of AdaGrad and Adam with provable convergence guarantees, which we name AdaGrad++
060   and Adam++ respectively. The main contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
061
062   1. We propose the AdaGrad++ algorithm, which is a parameter-free version of AdaGrad. We
063      demonstrate
               √        that without any assumptions on learning rates, AdaGrad++ can still achieve a
064      O(1/ T ) worst-case convergence rate in convex optimization, which is the same as AdaGrad.
065      This highlights the efficacy and versatility of AdaGrad++ as a more accessible and user-friendly
066      optimization method.
067   2. We also introduce the Adam++ algorithm as a parameter-free variant of Adam. By eliminating
068      the reliance on a well-tuned learning rate schedule, Adam++ offers more enhanced adaptabil-
069      ity and robustness compared to Adam. Our theoretical results demonstrates the capability of
070      Adam++ to match the convergence rate of Adam in convex optimization, even in the absence of
071
         any assumptions regarding learning rates.
072   3. We conduct experiments on image classification and large language model pretraining tasks to
073      evaluate the performance of the proposed algorithms. For CIFAR-10, with minimal parameter
074      tuning, Adam++ outperforms Adam by 0.27% using a constant learning rate schedule on ResNet-
075
         50, and by 1.35% using a cosine learning rate schedule on VGG16. For GPT-2 small and medium
         tasks, AdamW++ surpasses Adam by 0.02 in both training and test losses. Additionally, we
076
         perform an ablation study on the choice of initial and base learning rates, which confirms our
077
         theoretical findings.

      In this section, we give a more comprehensive review of the existing literature on parameter-free
      optimization and adaptive gradient methods.
089
090   Parameter-free optimization. Several recent works have explored parameter-free algorithms based
091   on modifications of the Polyak step size (Loizou et al., 2021; Gower et al., 2021; Orvieto et al.,
092   2022; Rolinek & Martius, 2018; Berrada et al., 2020). In addition, several studies have investigated
093
      step-size selection methods derived from Line-Search algorithms (Vaswani et al., 2019; Paquette
      & Scheinberg, 2018). Another line of works, including LARS (You et al., 2017a), LAMB (You
094
      et al., 2017b), Adafactor (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014), and Fromage (Bernstein et al., 2020),
095
      introduced learning rate adjustment schemes based on the norms of iterates. Moreover, Chandra
096   et al. (2022) proposed a scheme to adjust the learning rates based on certain automatically calculated
097   hypergradients. Several recent works (Orabona & Tommasi, 2017; Chen et al., 2022) have also
098   proposed parameter-free algorithms by reducing the optimization process to a game of betting on a
099   coin. Another recent work (Kleinsorge et al., 2023) proposed a novel rotation invariant parameter-
100   free algorithm based on exponential learning rate adaption. Finally, a line of recent works (Orabona,
101   2014; Kempka et al., 2019) have studied parameter-free algorithms in solving specific learning tasks
102   such as linear and kernel regression.
103   Adaptive gradient methods. There is a large body of literature on variants of AdaGrad and Adam.
104   Specifically, RMSProp (Kurbiel & Khaleghian, 2017) was the first work that proposed using an
105   exponential moving average instead of a cumulative sum to handle the second moment in Ada-
106   Grad. Reddi et al. (2019) pointed out an extreme case where Adam may face convergence issues,
107   and proposed AMSGrad accordingly with convergence guarantees. RMSProp, Adam and AMS-
      Grad have also inspired many variants, including SC-AdaGrad, SC-RMSprop (Mukkamala & Hein,

      6     E XPERIMENTS
343   In this section, we evaluate the performance of Adam++ across image classification and large lan-
344   guage model pretraining tasks to test its efficacy. For image classification problems, we train mod-
345   els on the CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2009). To demonstrate Adam++’s versatility and
346   stability across different network structures, we apply it to neural network architectures including
347   VGG16 (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014), ResNet-18, and ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016). We use Adam
348   as the baseline, and also compare Adam++ against two state-of-the-art parameter-free algorithms:
349
      D-Adaptation (Defazio & Mishchenko, 2023) and Prodigy (Mishchenko & Defazio, 2023). For
      large language model pretraining tasks, we use a reproduced GPT-2 model with 125M and 355M
350
      parameters respectively on the OpenWebText dataset (Gokaslan & Cohen, 2019). Our training set-
351
      tings are based on those from NanoGPT and Sophia (Liu et al., 2023). We omit the experiments
352   for AdaGrad++ as we found it consistently underperforms compared to Adam and Adam++, despite
353   being better than AdaGrad.

359   converge, so we adjusted to a smaller learning rate of 1e−4 for Adam. For all parameter-free algo-

      7           C ONCLUSIONS
529   In this paper, we propose two simple but effective algorithms, namely AdaGrad++ and Adam++,
530   that are parameter-free variants of AdaGrad and Adam respectively. We demonstrate that, despite
531   the simple intuition, AdaGrad++ and Adam++ are guaranteed to converge with a reasonable conver-
532   gence rate, and also perform surprisingly well in various experiments. These theroetical and empiri-
533   cal results highlight the potential of AdaGrad++ and Adam++ to be robust and practical choices for
534   a wide range of optimization tasks.
535   Several topics that are not covered in this paper are worth future studies. First of all, the current
536   convergence analyses of AdaGrad++ and Adam++ are limited to the convex setting. Establishing
537   convergence guarantees for AdaGrad++ and Adam++ under the setting of nonconvex optimiza-
538   tion is an important future research direction. Moreover, establishing convergence guarantees for
539   AdamW++ (which is used in our experiments without proof) is another promising area for future
      work.
