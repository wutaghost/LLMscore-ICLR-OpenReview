009                                                  Abstract
010
011              Reinforcement Learning (RL) enables agents to solve tasks by autonomously
012              acquiring policies by interacting with the environment receiving sparse or noisy
013              feedback in the form of a reward. However, achieving successful optimization in RL
014              requires efficient exploration, which remains a significant challenge, particularly
015              in continuous action spaces. Existing exploration techniques often exhibit limited
016
                 state-space reach and fail to overcome local optima, resulting in suboptimal policies.
                 Additionally, these techniques can cause erratic movements, posing risks when
017
                 applied to real-world robots. In this work, we introduce a novel exploration strategy
018
                 leveraging Perlin Noise, a gradient noise function that generates smooth, continuous
019              disturbances, thus enhancing the agent’s performance by promoting structured
020              exploration and fluid motions. We quantitatively demonstrate the benefits of our
021              approach compared to state-of-the-art methods, showing that it outperforms both
022              unstructured and structured techniques in thorough experimental evaluations.

025   1     Introduction
026
027   It is well known that exploration in Reinforcement Learning (RL) is essential to successfully train the
028   agent (Jiang et al., 2023). The policy is updated based on reward feedback to generate actions that
029
      control the agent to high-reward state regions. Visiting unseen and novel states in a broad range of the
      state space during this optimization is therefore essential to overcoming sub-optimal policies and
030
      converging to a high-performing policy. At the same time, the agent should explore the state space
031
      smoothly to prevent damage to itself (Raffin et al., 2022).
032
033   In the discrete action space, exploration has been addressed by various strategies such as epsilon
034   greedy exploration (Amini & Solemany, 2008), Boltzmann exploration (Derthick, 1984; Amini &
035
      Solemany, 2008), or upper confidence bounds (Mizukami et al., 2017). Similarly, there has been
      extensive research on exploration strategies for continuous action spaces in the RL community
036
      (Fortunato et al., 2018; Osband et al., 2016; Plappert et al., 2018). Commonly RL methods rely on
037
      simple exploration strategies such as factorized Gaussian noise where in each decision step the action
038   emerges by sampling from a Gaussian distribution (Schulman et al., 2017; 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2018).
039   While these simple exploration strategies ensure good local exploratory behavior (see Fig. 1(a)), they
040   might lack exploring relevant state-action regions that are far away from the initialization and might
041   result in poor performance (Schumacher et al., 2023; Raffin et al., 2022). Additionally, the resulting
042   motions are usually shaky and might damage agents such as robots (Raffin et al., 2022). Researchers
043   have therefore proposed different exploration strategies to mitigate the aforementioned issues.

      Intrinsic motivation-based methods (Pathak et al., 2017; Burda et al., 2019) augment the objective
055   function for the policy update with novelty functions that reward the agent if novel state spaces are
056   visited. Alternative approaches apply exploration on the trajectory level by applying Gaussian noise
057   in the parameter space that represents a trajectory rather than applying noise in each decision step
058   (Otto et al., 2022; 2023; Celik et al., 2021; 2024; Li et al., 2023; 2024). However, these methods
059   require additional treatment of the underlying optimization method by changing the objectives or
060   introducing higher-level abstractions of the actions. In contrast, methods proposed by (Raffin et al.,
061   2022; Eberhard et al., 2023; Hollenstein et al., 2024), propose simply exchanging the underlying
062   exploration mechanism while maintaining the RL method.
063   This work proposes Perlin noise for exploration in RL for continuous action spaces, inspired
064   by techniques from computer graphics (Perlin, 1985). Perlin noise generates smooth, temporally
065   correlated disturbances by assigning random gradient vectors to points on a grid and interpolating
066   between them, creating continuous transitions across space. The noise value at any given point is
067   calculated by taking the dot product between the surrounding grid gradients and the vectors to that
068   point, then blending these values using interpolation to ensure naturally flowing patterns.
069   We introduce a process to turn Perlin noise into a tractable distribution, allowing its usage as a drop-in
070   replacement for White noise in Gaussian policies commonly used in RL algorithms such as PPO
071   (Schulman et al., 2017), TRPO (Schulman et al., 2015), TRPL (Otto et al., 2021), and SAC (Haarnoja
072   et al., 2019). This approach preserves the structure of these algorithms, requiring no modifications to
073   their core objective functions or abstractions of actions.
074   In a qualitative analysis of Perlin noise compared to other exploration strategies, we show that it
075   produces smoother and more coherent trajectories, leading to higher state-space coverage and broader
076   exploration (see Fig. 1(c)).
077
      We conduct extensive quantitative experiments on various benchmark environments, comparing
078
      Perlin noise to state-of-the-art exploration strategies, such as generalized State-Dependent Exploration
079   (gSDE), White noise, and colored noise (Eberhard et al., 2023; Hollenstein et al., 2024), across
080   multiple environments from diverse benchmark suites (Tunyasuvunakool et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2019;
081   Kanagawa, 2023; Ellenberger, 2018; Towers et al., 2024). The results demonstrate that Perlin noise
082   is capable of solving hard exploration problems, outperforming or performing on par with these
083   baselines in both state-space coverage and task performance.

      2     Background and Related Works
087
      2.1   Exploration in Reinforcement Learning
088
089
      For RL algorithms, it is important to effectively balance exploitation and exploration, which is crucial
090   for discovering new behaviors in the environment to achieve optimal performance while avoiding
091   local optima.
092
093
      Action noise is the most common and simplest exploration method for continuous control, utilized
      by various RL algorithms (Schulman et al., 2015; 2017; Otto et al., 2022; Haarnoja et al., 2019;
094
      Abdolmaleki et al., 2018). Specifically, most agents leverage white noise, i.e., noise from an
095
      independent Gaussian distribution at each step, by sampling from a stochastic policy. While white
096   noise can help in exploring new actions, it often leads to unstructured and jerky movements, which
097   can be problematic in robotic applications (Peters et al., 2010; Otto et al., 2023). Maximum entropy
098   RL, which typically also leverages white noise, further encourages exploration by adding an entropy
099   term to the reward function, promoting policies that maximize both expected return and entropy.
100   This approach results in more stochastic policies, thus enhancing exploration (Ziebart et al., 2010;
101   Haarnoja et al., 2019).
102   Instead of white noise, Lillicrap et al. (2015) applies Ornstein-Uhlenbeck noise as actions noise. More
103   recently, colored noises (Eberhard et al., 2023; Hollenstein et al., 2024) have also been introduced
104   in deep RL, which incorporate temporally correlated disturbances instead of the traditionally used
105   uncorrelated white noise. This temporal correlation leads to more structured exploration patterns,
106   potentially enhancing exploration efficiency and performance in certain environments. With a similar
107   goal, Rückstieß et al. (2008); Raffin et al. (2022) propose random sampling of a function for each
      episode that deterministically modifies action selection. Additionally, Schumacher et al. (2023)

188   3     Perlin Noise for Exploration in Reinforcement Learning
189
190   Many RL methods, such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), TRPO (Schulman et al., 2015), TRPL
191   (Otto et al., 2021), require calculating the likelihood of the current policy. This calculation becomes
192   challenging when naively using Perlin noise, as the underlying probability density function generating
193   these samples is unclear. Others generate gradients via reparameterization, e.g. SAC (Haarnoja et al.,
194   2019). We can ensure correct operation and gradient generation for both these cases by ensuring the
195
      Perlin samples to follow the Gaussian policy distribution normally used in these methods. This also
      ensures Perlin-based exploration is usable as a drop-in replacement.
196
197
198
      3.1   Sampling Actions using Perlin
199
      Perlin noise on its own does not follow a Gaussian distribution and is not related to it. To use Perlin
200   noise for smooth exploration, we wish to generate noise that aligns with the policy, which we continue
201   to model as a Gaussian distribution. For a given state 𝑠𝑡 , sampling from a Gaussian policy with mean
202   𝜇 𝜋 and covariance Σ = 𝐿 𝑇Σ 𝐿 Σ is formalized as
203
                                              𝑎 𝑡 = 𝜇 𝜋 + 𝐿 Σ 𝜖,       𝜖 ∼ N (0, 1),
204
205   where 𝐿 Σ is the Cholesky factor of the covariance. Sampling 𝜖 ∼ N (0, 1) leads to generating samples
206   from the parameterized Gaussian policy. However, for sampling actions from a policy that applies
207   Perlin noise, we reformulate the sampling process to
208               𝑎 𝑡 = 𝜇 𝜋 + 𝐿 Σ 𝜖,   𝜖 ∼ P (𝑥, 𝑦),         where P𝑖 (𝑥, 𝑦) = Normalize(Perlin𝑖 (𝑥, 𝑦)).

210   We sample from Perlin noise with 𝑥 = 𝑡 · 𝑘 𝑠 𝑝𝑒𝑒𝑑 and 𝑦 = 𝑖 · 𝑘 𝑜 𝑓 𝑓 𝑠𝑒𝑡 , where 𝑡 is the timestep index and
211   𝑖 is the action dimension index. These define the sampling line, as depicted in Figure 4. Since Perlin
212

      3.2   The Normalization Function for Perlin Noise
240   In order to establish the existence of an appropriate normalization function, we consider that Perlin
241   noise inherently centers itself around zero, a characteristic stemming from its generation mechanism
242   (derivation in Appendix D.1). Further, the autocorrelation function 𝜌(𝑘), governing the relationship
243   between two samples 𝑥𝑖 and 𝑥 𝑗 , clearly exhibits a diminishing trend as the lag parameter 𝑘 = 𝑗 − 𝑖
244   approaches infinity. Consequently, the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) becomes applicable, asserting
245   that the expected empirical mean of our samples converges to 𝜇 = 0. Furthermore, due to Perlin’s
246   construction, it restricts its moments to finite orders beyond the second (derivation in Appendix D.2).

286   3.3   Comparison of Sampling Behavior to existing noises
287
288   To analyze the sampling behavior of various exploration noises, we first focus on their application
289   to a static Gaussian policy distribution N (0, 1), without considering an RL setup. The diagrams
290   in Figure 5 show the resulting samples. The top diagrams depict 100 steps of the trajectory over
291   time, while the bottom ones display histograms of 2000 sampled steps, with the solid black curves
292
      representing the true policy distribution. Additionally, the dotted black lines in the histograms match
      a bell curve to the actual samples, providing a visual comparison of how well the noise types conform
293
      to the expected distribution. We provide a Google Colab2 that allows testing various parameters and
294
      replicating these results.
295
296   White Noise (Figure 5(a)) is the default noise used in Random Exploration (REX). As expected, the
297   empirical mean and variance align closely with the parameters of the Gaussian policy. However,
298
      because the disturbances are sampled independently at each time step, the resulting motions are
      jerky and unstructured. In physical systems like robotics, these sudden, erratic movements can cause
299
      damage. Furthermore, White noise fails to achieve significant displacement in the state-space, limiting
300
      the range of exploration, as shown in Figure 1(a), where a particle driven by White noise spends most
301   of its time in a limited area.
302
303   Colored Noise (Figure 5(b)), such as Pink noise, introduces temporal correlations into the disturbances,
304
      allowing for more structured movements and greater displacement in the state-space. While colored
      noise theoretically converges to the true policy distribution as sample size approaches infinity, in
305
      practice, with finite rollouts, there can be a significant mismatch between the empirical and true policy
306
      parameters. This can lead to suboptimal exploration, as the agent may over-explore or get stuck, as
307   shown in Figure 1(b), where a particle driven by Pink noise spends much of its time against walls.
308
309   gSDE cannot be evaluated independently, as its noise generation depends on the latent activations
310
      of the policy network’s last hidden layer. Consequently, the behavior of gSDE varies between
      tasks and across different stages of training. While gSDE provides smooth, structured motions, its
311
      realized variance depends on the neural network’s architecture and weight initialization, making it
312
      less consistent. The periodic resampling mechanism introduces some discontinuity, but overall, gSDE
313   smooths the exploration trajectory compared to White and Colored noise.
314
315   Perlin Noise (Figure 5(c)) provides a smooth and temporally correlated alternative to both White and
316
      Colored noise without relying on periodic resampling. The smoothness of the trajectory is controlled
      by a speed parameter, making it easier to tune and adapt across environments. Unlike gSDE, Perlin
317
      noise is not dependent on the architecture of the neural network or the latent activations, leading to
318
      more predictable and consistent behavior. As shown in Figure 5(c), Perlin noise achieves structured
319   exploration with minimal drift from the true policy parameters, even in finite rollouts. This makes it
320   particularly suited for on-policy exploration. In the exploration box scenario (Figure 1(c)), Perlin
321   noise successfully escapes the box, similar to Pink noise, but without the drawbacks of getting stuck
322   against walls.

339   4                            Experiments
340
341
342   We evaluate the performance of Perlin noise-based exploration against existing methods, including
343   generalized State-Dependent Exploration (gSDE), White noise, Pink noise, and HalfBeta noise.
344   We use the term HalfBeta noise to refer to colored noise with 𝛽 = 0.5, which was found to be the
345   optimal coefficient for on-policy settings in Hollenstein et al. (2024). Benchmarking exploration
346   capabilities can be particularly challenging, as many established environments were designed with
347   current algorithms in mind. To address this, we introduce custom maze environments that present
348   difficult exploration tasks. Additionally, we assess the performance of Perlin noise across a wide
349   range of standard benchmark suites to ensure a comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art
350
      (SOTA) methods.
351   We use on-policy reinforcement learning, specifically Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), for
352   all experiments. The performance of the noise on each environment was evaluated using 20 runs
353   (only 10 for MetaWorld), each with a different random seed. To calculate stratified bootstrapped
354   confidence intervals, we use the methodology proposed by rliable (Agarwal et al., 2021). The resulting
355   interquartile mean (IQM) and confidence intervals (CI) for all tested environments are presented in
356
      the appendix (see Appendix A). Our summary bar chart shows the mean and standard error (SE)
      across all evaluated environments in the specific suite. Here, we use the regular mean instead of
357
      the IQM, as we do not treat exceptionally good or poor performance across entire environments as
358
      statistical outliers. This contrasts with handling over- or under-performance in a single run within an
359   environment.
360
361   In addition to reward performance, we measure the smoothness of the action trajectories, quantified
362
      by the mean squared jerk. Lower jerk values indicate smoother actions, which are desirable for many
      physical systems and tasks requiring stable, continuous actions. Similar to reward, smoothness is
363
      evaluated using IQM and stratified bootstrapped confidence intervals (CI). A formal description of
364
      Mean Squared Jerk can be found in Appendix B.1, the results in Appendix B.2.
365

391   4.1   MountainCar (Gymnasium)
392
393   The Mountain Car Continuous environment (Moore, 1990) from Gymnasium (Towers et al., 2024) is
394   a deterministic MDP where a car starts at the bottom of a sinusoidal valley. The goal is to accelerate
395   the car strategically to reach the top of the right hill, which requires continuous and consistent actions.
396   The challenge lies in overcoming the gravitational pull by building up momentum, making this task
397
      particularly difficult for exploration methods that rely on random, chaotic actions.
398   In our tests (see Figure 6(a)), White noise performed very poorly, as it failed to apply the consistent
399   accelerations needed to push the car uphill. While environment-specific hyperparameter tuning could
400   likely improve its performance, we conducted all tests without such adjustments to ensure consistency.
401   On the other hand, all other methods, including Perlin noise, performed similarly well, showing stable
402   results in this challenging task. Moreover, in Figure 6(b), we show the smoothness of generated action
403
      trajectories, measured by the mean squared jerk (lower values indicate smoother trajectories). Perlin
      noise outperforms all other methods, producing the smoothest trajectories overall. Notably, there
404
      were significant differences in performance between algorithms. For example, the HalfBeta method
405
      performed exceptionally poorly, producing extremely jerky actions.

412   left corner, with the goal in the top right for the static maze, or in any other corner for the random
413   maze. We found (see Figure 7 (c,d)) that the exploration challenge in this environment is sufficiently
414   hard, causing all baseline methods tested to fail in learning a reliable policy. In contrast, Perlin noise
415   enabled the agent to successfully solve these environments. This shows that there are exploration
416   challenges that require more advanced techniques than current SOTA methods, and Perlin noise can

      provide such a solution.

506   5   Discussion
507
508
509
      As demonstrated in Figure 9, Perlin noise consistently delivers strong and reliable results across
      all tested suites. While Perlin noise is not always the optimal choice, its consistent and generally
510
      favorable performance makes it a dependable exploration method for various applications without the
511
      need for extensive tuning or task-specific adjustments.
512
513   In subsection 3.3, we had showed that Perlin noise produces significantly smoother trajectories than
514   other methods, and this is now validated in our experiments. As shown in Appendix B, Perlin noise
515
      consistently shows lower jerk compared to other methods. While Pink noise also exhibits low jerk,
      its smoothness often comes at the cost of poor task performance, as its learned policies sometimes
516
      tend to remain close to a null policy. Perlin noise, on the other hand, achieves both smooth action
517
      generation and high task performance, making it a well-balanced choice for structured exploration.
518
519   Additionally, in the earlier analysis, we demonstrated that Perlin noise has superior state-space
520   coverage compared to White and colored noise. This is now reflected in its superior performance
521
      on the custom and suite-provided maze environments, where better state-space reach is crucial for
      effective exploration.

525
      6   Conclusion & Limitations
526
527
528   Exploration remains a critical component in the success of reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms,
529   as it drives agents to visit novel and high-reward states during training. In this work, we introduced a
530   novel exploration strategy that utilizes Perlin noise, a smooth, temporally correlated gradient noise
531   function. Perlin noise distinguishes itself by its ability to provide structured exploration. Unlike
532   conventional noise strategies like White noise, which can result in jerky, erratic movements, Perlin
533   noise promotes fluid motion, making it particularly suitable for tasks where smooth and stable actions
534   are required, such as in real-world robotic applications. As demonstrated in our experiments, Perlin
535   noise offers high state-space coverage, ensuring the agent explores effectively across a broad range of
536
      tasks. Our approach was validated across various benchmark suites, showing competitive performance
      when compared to state-of-the-art exploration strategies such as gSDE and colored noise.
537
538   Despite these advantages, Perlin noise has limitations. Its inherent smoothness, while beneficial
539   in many tasks, may hinder performance in environments where abrupt, high-frequency actions are
      necessary for success.

       B.1    Mean Squared Jerk
1407
1408   Jerk is defined as the rate of change of acceleration with respect to time. The approximation of jerk
1409   depends on whether the actions represent speeds or accelerations. In both cases, jerk is numerically
1410   derived by estimating the change in acceleration over time.
1411   When actions represent speeds, jerk is approximated by first calculating acceleration (the derivative
1412   of speed) and then computing the rate of change of acceleration. Given a discrete trajectory of speed
1413   actions {𝑣 1 , 𝑣 2 , . . . , 𝑣 𝑇 }, where 𝑣 𝑡 represents the velocity at time 𝑡, the acceleration at time 𝑡 is given
1414   by:
1415                                                               𝑣 𝑡+1 − 𝑣 𝑡
                                                            𝑎𝑡 =               .
1416                                                                   Δ𝑡
1417   The jerk is then calculated as the change in acceleration between consecutive time steps:
1418                                                              𝑎 𝑡+1 − 𝑎 𝑡
1419
                                                             𝑗𝑡 =              .
                                                                       Δ𝑡
1420
       Thus, when actions are speeds, we need to compute both acceleration and jerk, requiring two numerical
1421   steps.
1422
1423   When actions represent accelerations, jerk is directly computed as the change in acceleration between
1424
       consecutive time steps. For a discrete trajectory of acceleration actions {𝑎 1 , 𝑎 2 , . . . , 𝑎 𝑇 }, where 𝑎 𝑡 is
       the acceleration at time 𝑡, the jerk is given by:
1425
1426                                                       𝑎 𝑡 − 𝑎 𝑡 −1
                                                     𝑗𝑡 =               .
1427                                                           Δ𝑡
1428   In this case, jerk is calculated in a single step, as it directly measures the change in acceleration.
1429
       The Mean Squared Jerk (MSJ) is then calculated as the average of the squared jerk values across the
1430
       trajectory:
1431                                                       𝑇 −2
1432                                                  1 ∑︁ 2
                                            MSJ =               𝑗 ,
1433                                                𝑇 − 2 𝑡=1 𝑡
1434   where 𝑇 is the total number of steps in the trajectory.
1435
1436   This metric provides a quantitative measure of smoothness for action sequences. Lower MSJ values
1437
       indicate smoother trajectories, with less variation in the rate of acceleration. Whether the actions are
       speeds or accelerations, the MSJ helps assess the smoothness of exploration in reinforcement learning,
1438
       as both the exploration noise and the learned policy dynamics influence the resulting jerk values.

       D.1     Perlin Noise Has Zero Mean
2163
2164   We aim to show that Perlin noise has a zero mean. Let AntiPerlin(𝑥) denote the inverse of Perlin noise, defined
2165   as:
2166                                            AntiPerlin(𝑥) = −Perlin(𝑥).
2167
2168   D.1.1    Construction of AntiPerlin from Perlin
2169
2170
       The Perlin noise function is constructed by generating unit-length gradient vectors at lattice points and
       interpolating between them. To construct AntiPerlin(𝑥), we can simply flip the sign of all gradient vectors used
2171   in the construction of Perlin noise:
2172
                                                       gAnti
                                                         i   = −gi ,
2173
2174   where gi is a gradient vector at lattice point i.
2175   This transformation yields AntiPerlin(𝑥) = −Perlin(𝑥), since flipping the sign of all gradients results in the
2176   negation of the entire noise function.
2177
2178   D.1.2    Equivalence of Distributions
2179
       Perlin noise gradients are uniformly sampled from a distribution 𝑃(𝑔). Therefore, the probability of sampling a
2180
       gradient 𝑔 is equal to the probability of sampling −𝑔:
2181
2182                                                       𝑃(𝑔) = 𝑃(−𝑔).
2183
       As a result, the distribution of gradients used to generate Perlin(𝑥) is identical to that used for AntiPerlin(𝑥).
2184
       Thus, AntiPerlin(𝑥) follows the same distribution as Perlin(𝑥) under random seeds of the PRNG.
2185
2186
       D.1.3    Conclusion
2187
2188   Since AntiPerlin = −Perlin and both functions are equal in distribution, it must be that
2189
2190
                                                     E[Perlin] = E[−Perlin],
2191   which implies that Perlin noise is symmetric around zero and has a zero mean.

2239   D.3     Analyticity of the Normalization Function
2240
       We aim to show that the normalization function 𝑁 (𝑥), which transforms Perlin noise into a Gaussian-like
2241   distribution, is analytic. An analytic function is one that is infinitely differentiable and can be represented as a
2242   polynomial expansion around a point 𝑥0 .
2243
2244   D.3.1    Smoothness of Perlin Noise
2245
       Perlin noise is generated using smooth gradient functions. As these gradients are continuous and differentiable,
2246   the Perlin noise function 𝑃(𝑥, 𝑦) inherits these properties, making it smooth and infinitely differentiable.
2247

2249
2250   The normalization function 𝑁 (𝑥) maps Perlin noise values to a standard Gaussian-like distribution. If 𝐹 (𝑥) is
       the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of Perlin noise, we can express 𝑁 (𝑥) using the inverse CDF of the
2251
       standard normal distribution Φ −1 :
2252                                               𝑁 (𝑥) = Φ −1 (𝐹 (𝑥)).
2253
2254   D.3.3    Smoothness of the Transformation
2255
2256   Since the CDF 𝐹 (𝑥) of Perlin noise is smooth and the inverse Gaussian CDF Φ −1 (𝑥) is smooth, their composition
2257
       𝑁 (𝑥) = Φ −1 (𝐹 (𝑥)) is also smooth and infinitely differentiable.

2259
2260   Because 𝑁 (𝑥) is infinitely differentiable, it is analytic and can thus be expressed as a polynomial expansion.
