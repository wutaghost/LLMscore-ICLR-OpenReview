                                                 A BSTRACT
043
044
045
               Diffusion models have demonstrated strong performance in sampling and editing
               multi-modal data with high generation quality, yet they suffer from the iterative
046
               generation process which is computationally expensive and slow. In addition,
047
               most methods are constrained to generate data from Gaussian noise, which limits
048            their sampling and editing flexibility. To overcome both disadvantages, we present
049            Contrastive Optimal Transport Flow (COT Flow), a new method that achieves fast
050            and high-quality generation with improved zero-shot editing flexibility compared
051            to previous diffusion models. Benefiting from optimal transport (OT), our method
052            has no limitation on the prior distribution, enabling unpaired image-to-image (I2I)
053            translation and doubling the editable space (at both the start and end of the tra-
               jectory) compared to other zero-shot editing methods. In terms of quality, COT

               Flow can generate competitive results in merely one step compared to previous
055            state-of-the-art unpaired image-to-image (I2I) translation methods. To highlight
056            the advantages of COT Flow through the introduction of OT, we introduce the
057            COT Editor to perform user-guided editing with excellent flexibility and quality.

060   1   I NTRODUCTION
061
062   Diffusion models, with flexible training and sampling principles rooted in Statistical Physics, have
063   achieved unprecedented success in generating data from noise Ho et al. (2020); Song & Ermon
064
      (2019); Ramesh et al. (2022); Saharia et al. (2022); Nichol et al. (2021); Rombach et al. (2021);
      Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Ho & Salimans (2022); Kazerouni et al. (2023); Janner et al. (2022);
065
      Poole et al. (2022); Li et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024). However, the fundamental limitations of
066
      diffusion-based models, namely the sampling inefficiency and restrictive prior distribution, still
067   barricade them from wider applications, despite the recent series of improved methods Nichol &
068   Dhariwal (2021); Karras et al. (2022); Song et al. (2023). With a similar iterative sampling process,
069   flow-based methods Chen et al. (2018); Kidger et al. (2020) also suffer from the computational in-
070   efficiency problem. From a high-level perspective, the current deep generative models still cannot
071   simultaneously satisfy three performance indicators: (1) high-quality generation, (2) mode coverage
072   and diversity, and (3) fast sampling, which is identified as the generative learning trilemma Xiao
073   et al. (2021) shown in Fig.2a.
074   To tackle the generative learning trilemma and eliminate the constraints on prior distribution, we
075   present a novel flow-based model called Contrastive Optimal Transport Flow (COT Flow), which
076   fundamentally addresses the computational inefficiency problem through the optimal transport (OT)
077   formulation. We claim that OT enables the fastest sampling for diffusion/flow-based methods with
078   two key features to overcome sampling inefficiency: (1) straight lines from source to target and (2)
079   no crossing among the trajectories. Similar principles were approached implicitly in a few latest
080   work Liu et al. (2022); Lipman et al. (2022); Tong et al. (2023); Esser et al. (2024); Karras et al.
081
      (2022). Specifically, many recent breakthroughs Song et al. (2020a); Nichol & Dhariwal (2021);
      Karras et al. (2022) focused on the following strategies: optimizing the sampling trajectories towards
082
      straight lines, improving the time schedule of the diffusion process Song et al. (2020a); Karras et al.
083
      (2022), adjusting the noise schedule or forward diffusion process Song et al. (2020b); Nichol &
084   Dhariwal (2021); Lin et al. (2023); Bartosh et al. (2024), introducing fast samplers Nichol & Dhari-
085   wal (2021); Lu et al. (2022a;b); Karras et al. (2022), using distillation techniques Song et al. (2023);
086   Liu et al. (2022); Salimans & Ho (2022); Xu et al. (2023); Meng et al. (2022), and eliminating the
087   crossing among the trajectories to improve sample stability and efficiencyLiu et al. (2022); Lipman
088   et al. (2022); Tong et al. (2023); Esser et al. (2024). We note that these improved techniques, though
089   from different angles, approached the similar concept of OT between Gaussian and data distribution,
090   as shown in Fig.2b. Another prominent group of recent works (Korotin et al. (2022a;b); Fan et al.
091   (2021; 2022); Rout et al. (2021)) enforce direct OT by training two neural networks on saddle point
092
      problems Boyd & Vandenberghe (2004).
093   The proposed COT Flow satisfies the three performance requirements in the trilemma:
094
      Sample efficiency: The proposed COT Flow explicitly builds the bridge between diffusion/flow-
095
      based models and OT, and thus enforces straight trajectories and eliminates the crossing to improve
096   sample efficiency. With the benefit of both diffusion/flow-based models and the OT formulation,
097   COT Flow enables one-step or few-step sampling by design, while still producing high-quality and
098   high-diversity results from arbitrary prior distributions. Furthermore, COT Flow allows zero-shot
099   editing, and introduces diverse editing possibilities (Fig.1b).
100
      Sample quality: COT Flow leverages the intriguing similarities between consistency models Song
101
      et al. (2023); Song & Dhariwal (2023); Luo et al. (2023) and contrastive learning He et al. (2019);
102   Chen & He (2020); Chen et al. (2020); Grill et al. (2020) to produce high-quality generation using
103   indirect loss functions. In particular, the objective of consistency models consists of the similarity
104   between time-adjacent data pairs ⟨xt , xt+1 ⟩, which function exactly the same as the positive sample
105   pairs in contrastive learning (He et al. (2019) Eq.1). In addition, consistency models use a series
106   of similar techniques as those in contrastive learning, such as exponential moving average (EMA)
107   weights of the teacher model and ”stopgrad” operator Song et al. (2023); Song & Dhariwal (2023);
      Chen & He (2020); Grill et al. (2020), suggesting the hidden link between the two state-of-the-art

      three prominent methodologies in optimization and machine learning. For a quick understanding
      of the proposed COT Flow, we first briefly present the three core methodologies and discuss their
159
      interconnections in Section 3.1.

194
      With impressive results on multiple visual tasks, contrastive learning methods learn data representa-
195   tions by attracting the embeddings of positive sample pairs and (optionally) repulse the embeddings
196   of negative sample pairs in an unsupervised manner He et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2020). For the
197   methods that only consider the positive pairs Chen & He (2020); Grill et al. (2020), the core method-
198   ology can be described as minimizing the loss function:
199

200
201   where E is the target network, which we consider as an encoder. θ− denotes the exponential moving
202   average (EMA) of the past values of the network’s weights θ. d(·, ·) is the distance function between
203   the data embedding E(x) and its corresponding positive pairs E(x+ ), whose inputs x+ are aug-
204   mented from the same sample x. Combined with the EMA weights θ− and the ”stopgrad” operator,
205
      an additional prediction head qθ is introduced on top of the encoder Eθ to prevent model collapse

206
      we introduce the similarities between contrastive learning and consistency models.

      2.3   C ONSISTENCY M ODELS
209
210   Consistency models (CMs) are an emerging family of generative models whose key idea is maintain-
211   ing consistency along the ordinary differential equation (ODE) trajectory derived from the diffusion
212   models, which we briefly introduce in Appendix E. One drawback of diffusion models is their slow
213   sampling speed. CMs, on the other hand, learn the consistency along the trajectories {x̂t }t∈[0,T ]
214   of the probability flow ODE 28 and map all the points on these trajectories to their origin x̂0 . This
215   mapping can be described as the consistency function f ∗ : (xt , t) → x0 which satisfies the boundary
      condition f ∗ (x, 0) = x0 . We then approximate f ∗ (x, t) by training the consistency model fθ (xt , t).

252   3.1   S IMILARITIES BETWEEN C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING AND C ONSISTENCY M ODELS
253
      One may raise a question on the mechanism of CMs: Why do they work well by simply minimizing
254
      the difference between two points on the same trajectory, especially with no guidance of the trajec-
255
      tory’s origin x0 in the loss function? Here we put forward a hypothesis on why they learn to map to
256   the origin by exploring the systematic similarities between CMs and contrastive learning: The con-
257   sistency function fθ (x, t) is a trajectory’s origin encoder Eθ (x), which has the same functionality of
258   the encoder in contrastive learning.
259
      Firstly, we notice the similarity between the CM loss 5 and the contrastive loss 4, which are both
260
      summarized by a distance metric d(·, ·). Specifically, the CM loss indicates the distance between
261
      the two output points fθ (xti+1 , ti+1 ) and fθ− (xti , ti ) from the same trajectory, while the contrastive
262
      loss indicates the distance between the embeddings of the positive pairs Eθ (x) and Eθ− (x+ ) from the
263   same image. This suggests that CMs have the capability of learning representations from complex
264   distributions and are capable of mapping denoising trajectories {xt }t∈[ϵ,T ] to their origins x0 .
265
266
      Secondly, the strategies and training recipes of the two methods are similar, especially those for
      preventing mode collapsing. They both utilize weight-sharing Siamese networks θ, θ− to minimize
267
      the distance metric d(·, ·) of the entities, and they both use ”stopgrad” operations to distinguish the
268
      networks and prevent collapsing:
269
                                   θ− ← θ− − η∇θ d Eθ (·), stopgrad(Eθ− (·))

      5   C ONCLUSION
500
501
      We presented COT Flow, a new method that provides a tangible approach to tackle the generative
502
      learning trilemma, achieving fast and high-quality generation and flexible zero-shot image editing.
503
      Benefiting from OT reformulation, we achieved competitive sample quality on a great variety of
504   unpaired I2I translation tasks, representing flow between diverse distributions. With the proposed
505   COT Editor, We demonstrated flexible zero-shot editing capacities with three scenarios, namely,
506   COT composition, shape-texture coupling, and COT augmentation.
507
      Our method explicitly built the bridge between diffusion/flow-based models and OT by combining
508
      consistency models and contrastive learning, opening up new directions for future work. The pro-
509
      posed COT Editor expanded the possibility of zero-shot image editing by the dual-channel editing
510   spaces, enabling new directions for zero-shot editing applications.
