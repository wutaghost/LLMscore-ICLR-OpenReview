011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In medical image analysis, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Vision
014            Transformers (ViTs) have set significant benchmarks. However, CNNs exhibit
015
               limitations in long-range modeling capabilities, whereas Transformers are ham-
               pered by their quadratic computational complexity. Recently, State Space Models
016
               (SSMs) have gained prominence in vision tasks as they offer linear computational
017
               complexity. State Space Duality (SSD), an improved variant of SSMs, was in-
018            troduced in Mamba2 to enhance model performance and efficiency. Inspired by
019            this, we have tailored the Vision State Space Duality (VSSD) model for medi-
020            cal image segmentation tasks by integrating it within a UNet-like architecture,
021            which is renowned for its effectiveness in the field. Our modified model, named
022            VSSD-UNet, employs skip connections to preserve spatial information and uti-
023            lizes a series of VSSD blocks for feature extraction. In addition, VSSD-UNet
024            employs a hybrid structure of VSSD and self-attention in the decoder part, ensur-
025            ing that both local details and global contexts are captured. Finally, we conducted
026
               comparative and ablation experiments on two public lesion segmentation datasets:
               ISIC2017 and ISIC2018. The results show that VSSD-UNet outperforms several
027
               types of UNet in medical image segmentation under the same hyper-parameter
028
               setting. Our code will be released soon.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In the medical imaging domain, segmentation is vital for advancing clinical diagnostics, informing
034   treatment strategies, and enabling a deeper understanding of anatomical and pathological charac-
035   teristics. The ability to accurately segment images into distinct regions corresponding to different
036   tissues, organs, or abnormalities is crucial for a range of medical applications, from oncology to
037   neurology. The integration of deep learning techniques, particularly Convolutional Neural Net-
038   works (CNNs) LeCun & Bengio (1998), has marked a significant leap forward in the accuracy and
039
      efficiency of medical image segmentation. CNNs have demonstrated their prowess in capturing
      local features and spatial hierarchies, leading to significant improvements in segmentation tasks.
040
      Furthermore, the advent of Vision Transformers (ViTs) Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) has introduced a
041
      new paradigm, harnessing self-attention mechanisms to capture global dependencies and long-range
042   interactions within images, which is particularly beneficial for understanding the complex patterns
043   present in medical imaging data.
044
045
      Despite the remarkable achievements of CNNs and ViTs, there are inherent challenges that limit
      their effectiveness in medical image segmentation. CNNs, while excellent at capturing local fea-
046
      tures, often struggle to model long-range spatial dependencies that are essential for accurately seg-
047
      menting large or complex anatomical structures. This limitation can result in segmentation inaccu-
048   racies. On the other hand, ViTs, despite their ability to provide a more comprehensive view of the
049   image, are hindered by their quadratic computational complexity. This complexity becomes a sig-
050   nificant bottleneck when scaling to the high-resolution images that are common in medical imaging,
051   where detailed and precise segmentation is critical for clinical decision-making. The computational
052   demands of ViTs can be prohibitive, particularly in time-sensitive clinical settings where real-time
053   processing is desirable. Consequently, how to efficiently enhance the long-range dependency re-
      mains an open question.

      Recently, structured state-space models (SSMs) Gu (2023); Gu et al. (2021b) inspired by classical
055   state-space models have garnered significant interest for their computational efficiency and excel-
056   lent performance in modeling long-range dependencies. Notably, Mamba, a state-of-the-art selec-
057   tive structured state-space model, addresses the inherent limitations of previous SSMs. It success-
058   fully demonstrates efficiency and effectiveness in long sequence modeling and achieves cutting-
059   edge performance in continuous long sequence data analysis, such as in natural language process-
060   ing and genomic analysis. Internally, Mamba integrates time-varying parameters and employs a
061   novel hardware-aware algorithm for highly efficient training and inference, thereby avoiding the
062   high quadratic computational complexity caused by self-attention mechanisms. Recent studies have
063
      tentatively delved into the effectiveness of SSMs across a range of visual tasks, including ImageNet
      classification Zhu et al. (2024b); Liu et al. (2024b), classifying remote sensing images Chen et al.
064
      (2024a), image dehazing Zheng & Wu (2024), analyzing point clouds Liang et al. (2024), and seg-
065
      menting medical images Ruan & Xiang (2024b); Ma et al. (2024). This inspires us to explore the
066   potential of using Mamba blocks to enhance long-range dependency modeling in medical image
067   segmentation tasks.

094
      features within medical images effectively. It employs skip connections to preserve spatial hierar-
095
      chies and integrates VSSD blocks for feature extraction, ensuring that the model can extract fine
096   details while maintaining a broader contextual understanding. In addition, we employ a hybrid
097   structure of VSSD and self-attention in the decoder part. Building on these techniques, our model
098   provides superior segmentation performance while maintaining computational efficiency, address-
099   ing the limitations of existing models. The VSSD-UNet model represents a significant advancement
100   in the field of medical image segmentation, offering a potential solution to the challenges faced by
101   current deep learning models.
102   In summary, this paper presents several key contributions to the field of medical image segmen-
103   tation. Firstly, we introduce VSSD-UNet, a novel model tailored for medical image segmentation
104   that combines the strengths of VSSD and UNet architectures. Secondly, we provide a compre-
105   hensive evaluation of VSSD-UNet against existing segmentation models on standardized medical
106   imaging datasets, demonstrating its superior performance. Finally, we offer an in-depth analysis of
107   the model’s computational efficiency and accuracy, highlighting its potential for real-world clinical
      applications.

      2.1   M EDICAL IMAGE SEGMENTATION
112
113
114   Medical image segmentation is a critical task that entails the pixel-wise classification of various
115   anatomical structures, such as lesions, tumors, or organs, across diverse imaging modalities like
116
      endoscopy, MRI, or CT scans Chen et al. (2021). U-shaped networks Ronneberger et al. (2015);
      Oktay et al. (2018); Zhou et al. (2018); Huang et al. (2020); Lou et al. (2021); Ibtehaz & Kihara
117
      (2023); Chen et al. (2022); et al. (2021) have become particularly popular due to their straightfor-
118
      ward yet effective encoder-decoder architecture. The UNet Ronneberger et al. (2015), a seminal
119   work in this area, employs skip connections to effectively fuse features at different resolution lev-
120   els. This design has been further refined by UNet++ Zhou et al. (2018), which introduces nested
121   encoder-decoder pathways with dense skip connections, and UNet 3+ Huang et al. (2020), which
122   presents comprehensive skip pathways for full-scale feature integration. DC-UNet Lou et al. (2021)
123   pushes the envelope by integrating a multi-resolution convolution scheme and residual paths into its
124   skip connections. The DeepLab series, including DeepLabv3 Chen et al. (2017) and DeepLabv3+
125   Chen et al. (2018), leverages atrous convolutions and spatial pyramid pooling to effectively handle
126   multi-scale information. SegNet Badrinarayanan et al. (2017) utilizes pooling indices for feature
127
      map upsampling, ensuring boundary detail preservation. The nnU-Net et al. (2021) automatically
      tailors hyperparameters based on dataset-specific characteristics and employs standard 2D and 3D
128
      UNets. These U-shaped models have collectively set a high benchmark in the field of medical image
129
      segmentation.
130
131   In recent years, vision transformers have emerged as a powerful force in medical image segmenta-
132   tion, capable of capturing pixel relationships at a global scale Cao et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2021);
133
      Dong et al. (2021); Rahman & Marculescu (2023a;b); Wang et al. (2022a); Zhang et al. (2021); Xie
      et al. (2021). TransUNet Chen et al. (2021) represents a novel fusion of CNNs for local feature
134
      extraction and transformers for global context understanding, thereby enhancing the capture of both
135
      local and global features. Swin-Unet Cao et al. (2021) further extends this concept by integrating
136   Swin Transformer blocks Liu et al. (2021) into a U-shaped model for both encoding and decoding
137   processes. Drawing on these ideas, MERIT Rahman & Marculescu (2023b) introduces a multi-scale
138   hierarchical transformer that employs self-attention across various window sizes, thereby enhancing
139   the model’s ability to capture multi-scale features that are crucial for medical image segmentation.
140   These advances demonstrate the potential of transformers to significantly impact the field of medical
141   image analysis.

146
147   The emergence of Vision Transformers (ViTs) Dosovitskiy et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Wang
148   et al. (2021); Dong et al. (2022); Touvron et al. (2021) has reinvigorated the computer vision do-
149   main, a field that was once predominantly governed by Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
150   Krizhevsky et al. (2012); Simonyan & Zisserman (2014); He et al. (2016); Xie et al. (2017); Huang
151   et al. (2019); Howard et al. (2017); Tan & Le (2019); Liu et al. (2022b). However, the self-attention
152   mechanism in ViTs, which entails quadratic computational complexity, presents considerable dif-
153
      ficulties when dealing with high-resolution imagery, necessitating substantial computational re-
      sources. To surmount this challenge, various strategies have been introduced, such as hierarchical
154
      model structures Liu et al. (2021; 2022a); Dong et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2021; 2022c); Han et al.
155
      (2021), windowed attention techniques Liu et al. (2021); Hassani et al. (2023); Tu et al. (2022);
156   Zhu et al. (2023), and alternative forms of self-attention mechanisms Wang et al. (2022b); Xia
157   et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2022). Additionally, linear attention methods Katharopoulos et al. (2020);
158   Choromanski et al. (2020); Qin et al. (2022); Han et al. (2024) have managed to scale down the
159   computational complexity to a linear rate by reordering the self-attention’s query, key, and value
160   operations. Yet, despite these improvements, the efficacy of linear attention still lags behind that of
161   the quadratic self-attention Vaswani et al. (2017) and its derivatives Hassani et al. (2023); Fan et al.
      (2024); Zhu et al. (2023).

      2.3     S TATE S PACE M ODELS
191
192   State Space Models (SSMs) Gu et al. (2020; 2021c;a); Smith et al. (2022); Fu et al. (2022); Gu &
193   Dao (2023) have garnered significant research interest due to their expansive receptive fields and
194   linear computational complexity. A notable SSM, Mamba Gu & Dao (2023), has introduced the
195   S6 block, which has demonstrated comparable or superior performance to transformers in Natural
196   Language Processing (NLP) tasks. This has spurred further explorations Pei et al. (2024); Huang
197   et al. (2024); Du et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024a); Chen et al. (2024b); Li et al. (2024); Yang et al.
198
      (2024b); Ruan & Xiang (2024b) into adapting the S6 block for visual tasks, with studies showing it
      can compete with both CNNs and Vision Transformer (ViT) models. However, a key challenge in
199
      developing Mamba-based models for computer vision lies in aligning the model’s causal nature with
200
      the non-causal aspects of image data. A common strategy to overcome this is to flatten 2D feature
201   maps into 1D sequences using various scanning methods before processing them through the S6
202   block. These diverse scanning approaches have been proven effective across multiple studies Zhu
203   et al. (2024a); Liu et al. (2024a); Huang et al. (2024); Pei et al. (2024); Shi et al. (2024). Recently,
204   Mamba2 Dao & Gu (2024) has identified a close relationship between SSMs and structured masked
205   attention, establishing them as dual concepts and introducing State Space Duality (SSD). We extend
206   this work to show that SSD can be adapted into a non-causal model through a simple transformation,
207   eliminating the need for specific scanning routes.

      are processed by several VSSD blocks and patch merging layers to generate hierarchical features.

256   3.2.2    VSSD BLOCK
257
258   State Space Duality (SSD) is an enhancement over traditional State Space Models (SSMs), offer-
259
      ing improved performance and efficiency in processing sequence data. However, SSD inherently
      operates in a causal manner, which limits its applicability to non-causal vision tasks where informa-
260
      tion from future steps is just as relevant as past steps. To address this, we utilize Non-Causal SSD
261
      (NC-SSD), which modifies the role of the state transition matrix A to enable non-causal processing.
262
263   In the traditional SSD framework, the model updates the hidden state h(t) and computes the output
264   y(t) as follows:
265

305   3.3   E NCODER
306
      In the encoder of the VSSD-UNet, C-dimensional tokenized inputs pass through two sequential
307
      VSSD blocks to extract features without changing their size or dimension. The patch merging layer
308   is utilized for downsampling in the encoder of VSSD-UNet, reduces the token count by 21 while
309
      doubling feature dimensions by 2×, by segmenting inputs into quadrants by 14 , concatenating them,
310   and then normalizing dimensions through a layernorm each time.
311
312
      3.4   D ECODER
313
314   The decoder also uses two VSSD blocks in succession to reconstruction the features. Instead of
315   merging layers, it uses patch expansion layers to upscale deep features. This process effectively
316   halves feature dimensions by 12 while enhancing image resolution (2× upscaling). It works by an
317   initial layer that doubles feature dimensions before reorganizing and reducing them for resolution
318   enhancement.
319   Moreove, Mamba2 demonstrates that integrating SSD with standard Multi-head Self Attention
320   (MSA) yields additional improvements. In a similar way, our model incorporates self-attention.
321   However, unlike Mamba2, which uniformly intersperses self-attention throughout the network, we
322   strategically replace the VSSD block with self-attention module exclusively in the last stage. This
323   modification leverages the robust capabilities of self-attention in processing high-level features, as
      evidenced by prior works Lin et al. (2023); Ren et al. (2023); Fan et al. (2024) in vision tasks.

346
347   In the VSSD-UNet bottleneck, we use two VSSD blocks to process the features. At each stage of
348
      the encoder and decoder, skip connections are utilized to blend features from multiple scales with

      In this section, we conducted extensive experiments using two prominent lesion segmentation

367   This approach allowed us to train and assess the performance of our proposed model across a broad
368   spectrum of lesion segmentation tasks.

370   4.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
371
372   We implemented our VSSD-UNet using PyTorch 1.13 and trained it on an A100-PCIE-40G GPU
373   with 24 GB of memory for 300 epochs with a batch size of 32. The input images are uniformly
374   resized to 224 × 224. We employed data augmentation techniques such as random flipping and
