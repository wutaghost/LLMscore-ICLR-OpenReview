009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Modern deep learning heavily relies on adaptive optimization methods like Adam
012            and its variants, celebrated for their robustness against model scale and ease of
013            hyperparameter tuning. However, the gradient statistics employed by these methods
014            often do not leverage sufficient gradient covariance information, leading to subop-
015            timal updates in certain directions of the parameter space and potentially slower
016
               convergence. In this work, we keep track of such covariance statistics in the form
               of a structured preconditioner matrix. Unlike other works, our approach does not
017
               apply direct approximations to estimate this matrix. We instead implement an in-
018
               vertible transformation that maps the preconditioner matrix into a new space where
019            it becomes approximately diagonal. This enables a diagonal approximation of the
020            preconditioner matrix in the transformed space, offering several computational
021            advantages. Empirical results show that our approach can substantially enhance
022            the convergence speed of modern adaptive optimizers. Notably, for large language
023            models like LLaMA, we can achieve a 2x speedup in sample efficiency compared
024            to Adam. In addition, our method can also be integrated with memory-efficient
025            optimizers like Adafactor to manage computational overhead.

      1.    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      In the realm of deep learning optimization, finding efficient and reliable solutions to complex problems
030   has become a central challenge. As model scales and datasets continue to expand, such optimization
031   problems usually demand extensive training time and substantial computational resources to achieve
032   state-of-the-art performances.
033
      Standard first-order methods, such as stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and its variants, have
034
      emerged as canonical tools for training large-scale deep networks. These methods are straightforward
035
      to implement using modern automatic differentiation frameworks and are easily adaptable to non-
036   conventional training setups (Konečnỳ et al., 2016; Li et al., 2020; Finn et al., 2017). However,
037   despite their strong theoretical grounding (Şimşekli et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2020; Smith et al., 2021;
038   Tian et al., 2023), first-order methods typically require meticulous tuning of hyperparameters to
039   ensure the optimization process can converge to the desired local optima. In practice, these methods
040   often struggle when navigating highly non-convex loss surfaces, a common characteristic of deep
041   learning models. Pathological features like saddle points, flat regions, and sharp valleys in the loss
042   landscape can significantly hinder convergence, leading to inefficient training.
043   To tackle these challenges, optimization techniques have evolved to incorporate curvature geometry
044   or second-order information, providing more adaptive and efficient updates. A classic family of such
045   algorithms is preconditioned gradient methods, in which the gradient is premultiplied by a matrix
046   called a preconditioner before each optimization step. Classic algorithms in this family include
047   Newton methods (Bonnans et al., 2006) and Natural Gradient (Martens, 2020), which employ the
048   inverses of local Hessian and Fisher Information Matrix as preconditioners, respectively. Although
049   preconditioning methods typically exhibit much faster convergence than first-order approaches, their
050
      practical application is limited by the size of most real-world problems, as they demand quadratic
      storage and cubic computation time for each gradient update (Fletcher, 2000; Bonnans et al., 2006).
051
052   In addition to preconditioning, adaptive moment estimation is another line of work that has been
053   highly influential in deep learning optimization. These methods, including AdaGrad (Duchi et al.,
      2011), Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014), AMSGrad (Reddi et al., 2019), and Adafactor (Shazeer & Stern,


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2018) dynamically adapt the learning rate of each parameter throughout the optimization process by
055   leveraging cumulative second-order gradient statistics. While their theoretical foundations are not
056   yet fully explored, these algorithms have demonstrated more robust empirical results than SGD in
057   various domains and often exhibit better convergence behaviors in practice (Loshchilov & Hutter,
058   2017; Zhang et al., 2020).
059
      Our approach. In this work, we explore adaptive moment-based algorithms from the perspective
060
      of preconditioning methods. We are driven by the understanding that the second-moment estimates
061   in these algorithms can be derived from the diagonal approximation of a structured preconditioner.
062   We propose to improve this approximation by applying an invertible transformation that maps the
063   preconditioner into a new space where it becomes approximately diagonal. In this transformed space,
064   the diagonal elements of the preconditioner can be accumulated to estimate second-order statistics
065   better. Since the transformation is invertible, we can formulate the update on the original parameter
066   space by doing a simple projection back.
067   Our key contributions are outlined as follows:
068
069   1. Our approach is designed to be both straightforward and versatile, facilitating easy integration into
070
      existing adaptive moment-based optimizers such as Adam, AMSGrad, Adafactor, and variants.
071   2. We establish a convergence guarantee for the general framework without requiring typical strong
072   assumptions. This guarantee is significant because it is broadly applicable to a wide range of adaptive
073   optimizers, ensuring reliable performances in diverse scenarios.
074   3. Empirical results show that our proposed methods can substantially enhance the convergence
075   speed and efficiency of adaptive moment-based optimization baselines. Particularly in pretraining
076   large-scale models like LLaMA, our approach can achieve a speedup of 1.5x to 2x compared to the
077   Adam, but with manageable computational overhead.

100
      designs of C(t) have been developed using local numerical approximations. Classic algorithms in this
101
      category, including Newton methods and Natural Gradient, utilize the inverse of Hessian and Fisher
102   Information Matrix, respectively, as preconditioners. These methods offer a built-in mechanism for
103   curvature awareness, promoting larger updates in directions associated with small Hessian eigenvalues
104   to swiftly navigate flat regions while limiting movement in directions with large Hessian eigenvalues
105   to avoid sharp valleys. However, for large-scale models, further approximations to the preconditioners
106   are necessary to ensure their practicality. Various techniques have been proposed for this purpose,
107   such as Quasi-Newton methods (Fletcher, 2000), Gaussian-Newton estimators (Botev et al., 2017;
      Martens, 2020; Liu et al., 2023), K-FAC (Martens & Grosse, 2015; Grosse & Martens, 2016).

363   In connection with other existing algorithms, several prior works on optimization are relevant to our
364   framework. George et al. (2018) and Liu et al. (2018) proposed utilizing the eigenbasis of the Fisher
365   Information Matrix to construct diagonal preconditioning approximations within the natural gradient
366   or online Laplace approximation families. Similarly, Anil et al. (2020) extends this idea by leveraging
367   the eigendecomposition of Shampoo’s preconditioners as a basis for the diagonal transformations.
368   Our method, in contrast, focuses on diagonalizing the preconditioner matrix within the generalized
369   family of adaptive moment-based optimization algorithms, which includes Adam and Adafactor
370   optimizers as specific cases. While primarily inspired by the critical idea of gradient projection in
371   GaLore, we explore the full-rank projection case and thus move beyond GaLore’s main focus on
372   memory efficiency. We also acknowledge the concurrent work by SOAP (Vyas et al., 2024), which
373   obtains the projection matrices Pt and Qt by performing eigendecomposition on the accumulators
374   of Gt G⊤           ⊤
                t and Gt Gt (referred to as Shampoo’s preconditioners), respectively. Essentially, the
375   eigenvector matrix retrieved from the eigendecomposition of Gt G⊤                ⊤
                                                                             t (and Gt Gt ) corresponds to
376   the left (and right) singular matrix of Gt . Our proposal is therefore effectively equivalent to SOAP

382   Wt and optimization states St = {Mt , Vt }. It is important to note that the second-order momentum

384   to the standard gradient descent with momentum, eliminating any potential improvements. This
385   implies that the algorithms cannot be reduced to a simpler form for analyzing convergence guarantees.
386   Moreover, applying SVD periodically to produce projection matrices also causes intricate behaviors

806   Intuitively, Adafactor tracks the moving averages of the row and column sums of squared gradients
807   throughout iterations, yielding factored second-moment estimators rt and st . It then reconstructs
808   a low-rank parameterization of the second-order momentum using a normalized outer product
               ⊤
809   rt s⊤
          t /(1m rt ). This method is computationally efficient and scalable, as it directly offers analytical
      formulations without requiring further approximations.
