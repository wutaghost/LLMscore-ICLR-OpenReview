011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013                Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly adopted for a variety of tasks,
014                including multi-hop question answering, knowledge probing, and symbolic com-
015                monsense reasoning. While LLMs have advanced the state-of-the-art in these ar-
016
                   eas, their ability to explicitly solve temporal logic reasoning problems—a com-
                   plex cognitive process involving the understanding, representation, and manip-
017
                   ulation of temporal information such as events, their durations, and relation-
018
                   ships—remains largely unexplored. To enhance understanding of LLM perfor-
019                mance in this common task widely explored in the traditional symbolic AI field,
020                we have developed a new set of synthetic benchmark for rule-based temporal logic
021                reasoning named T B EN. T B EN is developed within the context of DatalogMTL,
022                a powerful knowledge representation language designed for reasoning about the
023                properties of systems that evolve over time. Notably, this benchmark defined six
024                levels of rule complexity and provides flexible configurations, allowing for the
025                customization of temporal rules and task complexity to suit various needs.
026                We evaluated the close-sourced GPT-4o and the open-sourced Llama-31 using
027                three common prompting settings—zero-shot, few-shot, and zero-shot-CoT—on
028                our synthetic benchmark. Our key findings are as follows: (i) Without generating
029                the reasoning process (chain-of-thought), even advanced LLMs like GPT-4o ex-
030
                   hibited nearly random performance on these rule-based temporal logic reasoning
                   tasks. However, with chain-of-thought prompting, LLMs demonstrated prelim-
031
                   inary temporal logical reasoning abilities; (ii) Both GPT-4o and Llama-3 were
032
                   unable to solve temporal logical reasoning problems involving recursion, indicat-
033                ing a lack of advanced complex reasoning capabilities in understanding symbolic
034                representations involving time; (iii) There is significant room for improvement
035                in leveraging large language models to address problems widely explored in the
036                traditional logic-based AI domain. Prompts and datasets are available in the ap-
037                pendix, and a datasheet for T B EN is also provided.

153   DatalogMTL Brandt et al. (2018); Wał˛ega et al. (2019) is a powerful knowledge representation lan-
154   guage, which extends Datalog Abiteboul et al. (1995), a widely used declarative logic programming
155   language, with operators from metric temporal logic (MTL) Koymans (1990). Different Datalog
156   designed to handle static facts and rules due to lack of built-in temporal constructs, DatalogMTL
157
      equipped with MTL operators is enabled to reasoning about properties of systems that evolve over
      time. These operators build upon the standard linear temporal logic (LTL) Huth & Ryan (2004) op-
      erators, such as x standing for “sometime in the past”, ⊟ for “always in the past”, and S for “since”,

164   expression x[1,2] LiveIn(x, y) is true at time t if entity x lived in location y sometime between times

172   length matches the arity of P . In this paper, we restrict ourselves to a fragment in which metric
173   atoms are generated by the following grammar, where P (s) is a relational atom and ϱ an interval

      2.1   M AJOR T EMPORAL R EASONING P ROBLEMS
197
198   According to Brandt et al. (2018); Wał˛ega et al. (2019), temporal logic reasoning involves two major
199   problems: consistency checking and fact entailment. Consistency checking is the task of determining
200   whether a given program and dataset admit a common model Emerson (1990); Schnoebelen (2002).
201   Fact entailment involves checking whether a program and dataset together entail a specific relational
202   fact. Brandt et al. (2018) note that in DatalogM T L, consistency checking and fact entailment are
203   complementary problems. Consequently, this paper focuses solely on the fact entailment problem to
204
      evaluate the temporal reasoning capabilities of large language models.

248   3.1     L EVELING DATALOG MTL RULES
249
250   To address the aforementioned challenge and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the tem-
251   poral reasoning abilities of large language models, we aim to create a new synthetic benchmark with
252   flexible configurations for customizing rule structures and task complexity. We classify DatalogMTL
253   rules into six classes (SingleAtom, . . . , Recursive) based on their structural representations, consid-
254   ering factors such as the number of body atoms, the number of temporal operators used, the number
255
      of rules involved, and whether the rules are recursive.
                                                          4     While we are unable to quantify the degree
      of complexity of each level, we assume that higher levels correspond to greater complexity. This as-
256
      sumption is based on the observation that more complex rule structures require additional temporal
257
      reasoning steps when using a symbolic reasoner like MeTeoR Wang et al. (2022).
258
259
      SingleAtom The most simplest form of a rule is A ← ⊘[ρ] B, where ⊘ could be one of the four
260
      metric temporal operators ( ⊟, ⊞, x and |). In particular, we ensure that A and B are two different
261   atoms, so only one calculation operation. A SingleAtom example is given in Figure 2, where we
262   can derive A@[5, 7] based on the given dataset and the rule, entailing that A@6 is true. In particular,
263   we consider DatalogMTL over the integer timeline, a fragment of DatalogMTL Wał˛ega et al. (2020)
264   and use one type of MTL operator.
265
266   MultiAtoms In the SingleAtom, the body contains only one atom, so a single rule application is
267   sufficient to complete the derivation. In MultiAtoms, we increase the number of atoms in the rule
268   body, requiring not only the validation of each atom but also an intersection operation to obtain
      the final valid interval. As the example shown in Figure 2, the rule contains two atoms. First, we
      calculate the valid intervals for each atom. Based on the provided temporal facts, ⊟[3] B holds only
269

270
      at the punctual time point [4, 4]], and ⊟[2,3] A holds at the interval [4,5]. The intersection of these
271
      intervals, [4, 4] and [4, 5], is [4, 4]. Thus, we derive that A is true at the time point 4, so A@4 is
272   entailed. As with SingleAtom, we consider DatalogMTL over the integer timeline Wał˛ega et al.
273   (2020) and use only one type of MTL operator.
274
275   Rational Both SingleAtom and MultiAtoms focus solely on the integer timeline, which repre-
276   sents a relatively limited time space and simplifies reasoning due to the integer semantics Wał˛ega
277   et al. (2020). In Rational, we build on top of MultiAtoms by expanding the timeline to include the
278   rational numbers, incorporating decimal time points. Intuitively, rational-based numerical operations
279   are more complex than their integer-based counterparts, and we aim to determine if large language
280   models exhibit similar behavior. We continue to use only one type of MTL operator at this level.e
281   type of MTL operator in the level.

283   MixedOperators Using only one operator limits the expressiveness of DatalogMTL, preventing
284   the definition of complex temporal conditions. Thus, a natural expansion is to allow the use of MTL
285   operators. The four types of MTL operators can be used to define temporal conditions associated
      with both the past and the future. A MixedOperators example is shown in Figure 2, which involves
      two MTL operators (x and ⊞). To complete the derivation, we first calculate the valid interval where
286

      x[1,2.4] B with the past operator (x) holds, which is [2, 3.4]. Then, we calculate ⊞[1,2]C , whose valid
287
288
      interval is [1, 2]. After performing the interval intersection, we obtain that A holds at the time interval
289
      [2, 2]. Thus, the temporal A@2.3 is not entailed.
290
291
      MultiRules In the previous four levels, fact entailment is associated with only one temporal rule.
292
      However, in more practical scenarios, multiple temporal rules may be required to express complex
293   temporal conditions. In this level, we consider a multi-rule temporal reasoning case, where fact
294   entailment involves multiple temporal rules and rule applications must be executed across these
295   rules to complete the derivation. As the example in Figure 2, to derive the target atom A, we need
296   to know both D and C. However,the dataset only provides the information about C. We can derive
297   the D holds at 3 according to the first temporal rule D ← ⊟[2] ∧ ⊞[1,2] C; then, we can derive that A
298   holds at the interval [4.5, 5] according to the second rule. Hence, A@[4.5, 5] is entailed.
299
300   Recursive The fact entailment problem at this level is considered the hardest because it involves
301   recursion. Unlike static knowledge representation languages (e.g., Datalog), where all facts can be
302   derived after a certain number of rule applications, some recursive rules in DatalogMTL may require
303   an infinite number of applications. Even for symbolic-based approaches, this presents a significant
304   challenge, and researchers have devoted considerable effort to addressing it Wał˛ega et al. (2021;
305
      2023a). According to Wał˛ega et al. (2023a), in the recursive scenarios, periodic structures will ul-
      timately occur repeatedly, but calculating these periodic structures is challenging. From a human
306
      perspective, however, identifying such periodic structures can be straightforward. For instance, con-
307
      sider a recursive rule ⊞1year Bday(x) ← Bday(x), which states that anyone having their birthday at
308   a time point t will also be having their birthday at the same time the following year. If we know that
309   Ben has his birthday on Jun 8, 1991, it is easy to know that he will have his birthday on Jun 8, 1992,
310   Jun 8, 1993 and so on. However, this is difficult for traditional symbolic-based approaches to handle.
311   Therefore, we design fact entailment problems associated with recursive rules to test whether large
312   language models can perform well in this setting.
313
      Specifically, we use facts from both propositional logic Klement (2004) and first-order logic Barwise
314   (1977). The former contains declarative statements that are either ‘true’ or ‘false’, while the latter
315   includes expressions with one or more variables. For example, we allow both forms of temporal
316   facts: Raining and Immune(x). The former states that an event (raining) is occurring, while the
317   latter denotes that a property (immune) is associated with an entity, where x acts as a placeholder

390   4         E XPERIMENTS AND R ESULTS
391
392   Based on our synthesized benchmarks, we aim to investigate whether large language models can
393   solve temporal reasoning problems from the traditional logic-based AI domain by evaluating two
394   large language models (GPT-4o4 and Llama-35 ) with three different kinds of prompting settings.
395
396   Baselines We adopt three basic prompting approaches as baselines. Specifically, zero-shot prompt-
397   ing (ZERO - SHOT), few-shot in-context learning (FEW- SHOT) (Brown et al., 2020), chain-of-thought
398   prompting (C OT) (Wei et al., 2022), and zero-shot chain-of-thought (0-C OT) (Kojima et al., 2022)
399   are leveraged to tackle various temporal reasoning problems in our constructed benchmark.
400
401   Benchmark statistics and experimental settings Unless otherwise specified, each benchmark
402   level contains 200 samples selected from the facts derived using the chosen data and rule(s). For
403   negative samples, a random interval is chosen, ensuring that these intervals do not overlap with those
404   of the derived facts. Specifically, for all baselines, the temperature value is set to 0. For few-shot
405   prompting techniques, the input prompt includes two manually constructed exemplars. In this paper,
406   we use the F1 score as the evaluation metric. Details about the benchmark statistics, instructions,
407
      exemplars, and other relevant experimental information can be found in the the Appendix part.
408
409   4.1             M AIN R ESULTS
410
411   We observe that for GPT-4o, the zero-shot and few-shot prompting settings achieve extremely low
412   accuracy across the six benchmarks, with some experiencing more than a 50% accuracy drop com-
413
      pared to zero-shot-CoT. This indicates the necessity of using the chain-of-thought strategy with
      LLMs to solve temporal reasoning problems effectively. Notably, on the three benchmarks Sin-
414
      gleAtom, MultiAtoms, and Rational, we observe similar accuracy with less than 2% variance
415
      between them. Interestingly, we found that using multiple operators yields the best performance.
416   This is surprising because we assumed that employing more operators would require the model to
417   understand more semantics of DataloMTL, thus increasing the problem’s complexity compared to
418   using only one type of MTL operator. However, considering the reasoning process using the sym-
419   bolic reasoner, such results are also reasonable because if two rules contain the same number of
420   body atoms, the number of calculating operators is the same. From this perspective, LLMs exhibit
421   similar behavior to symbolic reasoner-based approaches.
422   In the MultiRules and Recursive benchmarks, we notice a significant performance drop, with
423   Recursive achieving the lowest performance at 64.5%. These results suggest that recursive rules are
424   particularly challenging for models, as they require not only understanding the language semantics
425   and performing step-by-step reasoning but also possessing strong inductive ability. Unfortunately,
426   the results achieved by Llama-3 are almost random, indicating that the model lacks the capability
427   to solve symbolic form temporal reasoning problems. One possible reason is the model size being
428   too small, preventing it from generalizing to unseen tasks. Additionally, the lack of relevant training
429   datasets during pre-training or fine-tuning stages could also contribute to this issue.

      4.2               RULE BASED VS . NATURAL L ANGUAGE (NL) BASED T EMPORAL L OGIC R EASONING
433
434   In this paper, our primary focus is on benchmarking and testing the temporal logic reasoning ca-
435   pabilities of LLMs by evaluating them in a rule-based format. However, it is also interesting to
436   compare this with another setting: verbalizing the rules before presenting them to the LLMs, as ex-
437   plored in many previous similar works like CronQuestions Saxena et al. (2021); Ismayilzada et al.
438   (2023). Given that manually converting each rule into its corresponding natural language expression
439   is a labor-intensive process, we adopt a template-based approach to automate this verbalization. Al-
440   though this method may result in expressions that are not entirely natural6 , it provides a practical
441
      alternative to manual translation.
442   From Figure 4, we observe that both the rule-based and natural language-based settings achieve
443   similar results, with the rule-based approach performing slightly better. The comparison indicates
444   that LLMs are also capable of understanding the semantics of input expressed in rules, provided
445   that each notation is clearly explained in the instructions. Notably, both settings struggle with the
446   MultiRulesand Recursivecases. One possible reason for this is that, while LLMs can understand
447
      the semantics of temporal logic language, they still face significant challenges in executing multi-
      ple deductions, retaining intermediate results, and recognizing repeated patterns—tasks that require
448
      delicate algorithms to accomplish effectively in the field of temporal logic Wał˛ega et al. (2023a).

451   4.3               A BLATION STUDY
452
453   To explore which component of the rule structure most significantly impact the reasoning complex-
454   ity for LLMs, we designed four sets of ablation study experiments. These experiments explored the
455
      effects of the number of relevant rules , the number of operators considered, the percentage of irrele-
      vant data, and the percentage of irrelevant rules. From Figure 5 (a), we observe that as the lengths of
456
      dependent rules increase, the model’s performance noticeably degrades.One possible reason is that
457
      when multiple rules are mutually dependent, the model needs to store intermediate results during the
458   derivation process to complete subsequent steps that rely on previously derived outcomes. Unlike
459   symbolic reasoners, which can explicitly store intermediate results, it may be challenging for large
460   language models (LLMs) to retain such information in an auto-regressive manner. Additionally,
461   Figure 5 (b) demonstrates that using more types of operators does not affect reasoning complexity,
462   indicating that understanding the semantics of the temporal logic language is not a major issue for
463   the model. Lastly, the results in Figures 5 (c) and (d) show that the model’s performance is only
464   minimally affected by irrelevant information, whether in the form of temporal data or rules, demon-
465   strating its ability to correctly select relevant rules and remain resistant to distracting information.

      5   R ELATED W ORKS
487
488   Symbolic approaches for temporal reasoning Knowledge representation languages, such as Lin-
489   ear Temporal Logic (LTL) Huth & Ryan (2004) and DatalogMTL Brandt et al. (2018), have become
490   the de facto standard for specifying temporal properties in both formal verification and artificial in-
491   telligence. Many temporal reasoning problems have proven to be PSPACE-complete Wał˛ega et al.
492   (2019); Fionda & Greco (2018); Bauland et al. (2009); Wał˛ega et al. (2020). Satisfiability checking,
493   that is, the problem of deciding whether a given formula admits a satisfying model, is one of the most
494   important computational tasks associated with the logic, and one of the first that have been carefully
495   studied Sistla & Clarke (1985). Similarly, the main reasoning tasks considered in DatalogMTL are
496
      fact entailment and consistency checking. These problems polynomially reduce to the complements
      of each other Brandt et al. (2018). Despite this theoretically high computational complexity, numer-
497
      ous techniques and tools have been developed to solve different temporal reasoning problems, which
498
      range from tableau systems Goré & Widmann (2009); Bertello et al. (2016) to reductions to model
499   checking Cavada et al. (2014), to automata techniques Li et al. (2014); Wang et al. (2022).
500
501   Prompting LLMs for temporal reasoning Although the aforementioned temporal reasoning
502   problems have been widely explored in the traditional logic-based AI domain, they remain under-
503   explored in the regime of LLMs. There is a substantial body of research evaluating the reasoning
504   abilities of LLMs in an in-context learning setting, covering areas such as arithmetic reasoning, logi-
505   cal reasoning, and commonsense reasoning. Notably, simple math problem datasets like AQUA Ling
506   et al. (2017), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), and SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021) are frequently used to
507   assess arithmetic reasoning (Touvron et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023). Welleck et al. (2021) developed
508   NaturalProofs, a multi-domain dataset for studying mathematical reasoning in natural language,
509
      while Welleck et al. (2022) investigated LLMs’ abilities to generate the next step in mathemati-
      cal proofs and complete full proofs. Additionally, LLMs have been evaluated on logical reasoning
510
      tasks, including symbolic tasks like Coin Flip and Last Letter Concatenation (Wei et al., 2022), and
511
      Logic Grid Puzzles on the BIG-BENCH (Srivastava et al., 2023). Commonsense reasoning datasets
512   (Talmor et al., 2019) have also been proposed for evaluating LLMs. Most relevant to our work are
513   various approaches to evaluating and enhancing the algorithmic reasoning abilities of LLMs (Zhou
514   et al., 2022). In this work, we focus on evaluating LLMs on temporal logic reasoning tasks.
515
516
      6   C ONCLUSION
517
518
      We present T B EN, a new synthetic benchmark consisting of rule-based temporal logic reasoning
519
      problems, designed to test the temporal reasoning abilities of LLMs. T B EN contains diverse tem-
520   poral reasoning problems of varying complexities, providing flexible configurations for customising
521   generated benchmarks. Our experimental results reveal that models have difficulties in understand-
522   ing symbolic data and performing temporal reasoning. Even though powerful LLMs like GPT-4o
523   have been trained on extensive data from across the Internet, they struggle with some simple tem-
524   poral reasoning tasks. By proposing this benchmark, we aim to draw attention to the data-based
525   temporal reasoning abilities of LLMs. Our goal is to facilitate the development of specialized meth-
526   ods to enhance these abilities, such as training models in the agent reasoning style with data as input
527   and equipping models with more temporal reasoning and symbolic knowledge.
528
529   7   L IMITATION
530
531   Our experiments were constrained by the speed, computational resources, and financial costs asso-
532   ciated with utilizing the closed-source GPT-4o. For instance, although our generator allows for the
533   creation of benchmarks of temporal data and rules with arbitrary sizes, we obtained results across
534   multiple temporal reasoning datasets of varying complexities on a relatively small scale due to the
535   financial costs associated with GPT-4o API calls.
536   Another limitation of this preliminary exploration into testing the temporal reasoning abilities of
537   LLMs is that we only present experimental results from three prompting settings, despite the avail-
538   ability of many other advanced prompting strategies. Furthermore, while we demonstrate that a
539   chain-of-thought approach is necessary for solving temporal reasoning problems, we do not offer an
      effective method for enhancing the LLM’s ability to handle temporal logic reasoning problems.
