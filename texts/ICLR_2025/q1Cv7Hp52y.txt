011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has struggled with pixel-based control-
014            ling tasks that have numerous entities, long sequences, and logical dependencies.
015            Methods using structured representations have shown promise in generalizing to
016
               different object entities in manipulation tasks. However, they lack the ability to
               segment and reuse basic skills. Neuro-symbolic RL excels in handling long se-
017
               quential decomposable tasks yet heavily relies on expert-designed predicates. To
018
               address these challenges, we introduce a novel pixel-based framework that com-
019            bines entity-centric decision transformers with symbolic planning. Our approach
020            first automatically discovers and learns basic skills through experiences in simple
021            environments without human intervention. Then, we employ a genetic algorithm
022            to enhance these basic skills with symbolic interpretations. Therefore, we convert
023            the complex controlling problem into a planning problem. Taking advantage of
024            symbolic planning and entity-centric skills, our model is inherently interpretable
025            and provides compositional generalizability. The results of the experiments show
026            that our method demonstrates superior performance in long-horizon sequential
027
               tasks and real-world object manipulation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has been successfully applied to various fields, including
033
      video games (Mnih, 2013), autonomous driving (Sallab et al., 2017), and robotics (Kober et al.,
      2013). However, building flexible and adaptive robotic agents that can accomplish a diverse set of
034
      tasks in novel and complex environments remains a significant challenge in DRL. Such tasks typ-
035
      ically demand the agent to formulate long-term plans for logically dependent goals, requiring it to
036   combine diverse skills in complex scenarios involving multiple objects. A significant challenge of
037   these tasks is the need for compositional generalization. We can assess it in terms of two distinct
038   factors: (1) different attributes of objects than in training, and (2) different compositions of goals
039   and their corresponding skills, including variations in logical order (Lin et al., 2023).
040
      To address the above challenge, several methods (Zadaianchuk et al., 2020; 2022; Mambelli et al.,
041
      2022; Haramati et al., 2024) incorporate structured representations into the DRL algorithms of de-
042   cision transformers through object-centric representations (OCR). With a powerfully structured rep-
043   resentation, they show certain generalizability on the types and numbers of objects in object ma-
044   nipulation tasks. However, they cannot simultaneously learn diverse skills due to the catastrophic
045   forgetting problem (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989), where the new information can distort the previ-
046   ously learned knowledge. Besides, they cannot segment the learned integrated policy into diverse
047   fundamental units and reform them to achieve new objectives.
048   On the other hand, some researchers suggest neuro-symbolic approaches that combine planning and
049   DRL. These approaches aim to handle the combinatorial explosion of possible action sequences by
050   providing high-level abstraction and compositing learned skills. Many existing methods (Illanes
051   et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2020; Zhuo et al., 2021; Mao et al., 2023; Silver et al., 2023) employ a top-
052   down structure by specifying symbolic representation for high-level action models and using them
053   to guide the learning of low-level policies. However, these methods can only work with fully ob-
      servable environment states and carefully hand-engineered predicates. These predefined predicates

054
      hinder the agent’s flexibility, thereby restricting its applicability to real-world tasks such as object
055   manipulation.
056
057   In this paper, we propose a pixel-based bottom-up framework that combines the idea of entity-centric
058
      decision transformers with planning. Our framework is capable of forming a plan that is composed
      of skills for complex tasks. It learns fundamental skills from scratch by exploring simple environ-
059
      ments through DRL algorithms (Li, 2017) without relying much on expert knowledge. Furthermore,
060
      our approach uses genetic programming (Ahvanooey et al., 2019) to induce symbolic interpretation
061   for those learned skills, including their preconditions and effects. These interpretations provide the
062   agent with a series of fundamental understandings of its learned skills, which is critical for effective
063   planning. During the evaluation, given a novel and composite task, our agent decomposes the task
064   based on its understanding of the task and basic skills, formulating a sequential plan by search algo-
065   rithms (Abualigah et al., 2021). Finally, the agent executes this plan and uses its skills to generate
066   specific actions to achieve the final goal. We experimentally verify the efficiency and effectiveness of
067   our framework in two domains: Minecraft, a 2D grid-world environment (Andreas et al., 2017) that
068   focuses on long-horizon planning, and IssacGym (Makoviychuk et al., 2021), a simulated tabletop
069
      robotic environment that evaluates the agent’s capacity to manage complex 3D object manipulation.
      Experimental results show that our method can schedule the sequence of skills in an appropriate or-
070
      der with symbolic interpretation. Moreover, the flexible combination of skills allows our approach
071
      to handle environments with varying object attributes.
072
073   We summarize our key contributions below:
074
075          • End-to-End Pixel-Based Controlling. Compared to the previous work using processed
076            state input, our pixel-based controlling framework can directly leverage raw image data to
077
               perform tasks instead of utilizing the actual state provided by the environment.
078          • Automatic Skill Discovery. Compared to previous work, our approach can automatically
079            discover and learn skills from the environment without any guidance of designed high-level
080            symbolic representations in advance, reducing the dependency on expert knowledge.
081
082          • Symbolic Interpretation. Our approach is inherently interpretable by planning with a
083            sequential symbolic plan composed of learned skills. By constructing each skill’s pre-
084
               conditions and effects, our model can infer the specific task of each skill, thus having a
               comprehensive understanding of the planning and alleviating the curse of dimensionality.

      Object-Centric RL. Many recent works employed the structured representation in model-free RL
      (Colas et al., 2019; Zadaianchuk et al., 2022; Mambelli et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022; Zhou et al.,
091
      2022; Ferraro et al., 2023; Feng & Magliacane, 2024). Among them, methods such as SMORL
092
      (Zadaianchuk et al., 2020) and ECRL (Haramati et al., 2024) leverage object-centric representa-
093   tions (Jiang et al., 2019; Francesco et al., 2020; Daniel & Tamar, 2023) in combination with goal-
094   conditioned attention policies to discover and learn useful skills from raw image data. However,
095   they cannot segment learned skills into fundamental units and reform them to solve novel and com-
096   plex tasks. In this work, we integrate the idea of entity-centric decision transformers into planning,
097   thus giving our method the ability to learn fundamental skills and understand how to compose them
098   when facing long-horizon tasks.
099
100
      Neuro-Symbolic RL. Several works have explored utilizing symbolic methods in DRL to deal
101   with robotic tasks (Belta et al., 2007; Blaes et al., 2019; Illanes et al., 2020; Kokel et al., 2021;
102   Sehgal et al., 2023; Silver et al., 2023; Acharya et al., 2024), including planning domain definition
103   language (Mao et al., 2023), automata (Hasanbeig et al., 2021), Spectrl (Jothimurugan et al., 2021;
104   Žikelić et al., 2024). Despite the success of previous works, they often require either predefined
105   symbolic structures or predefined skills, limiting their compositional generalizability to complex
106   real-world object manipulation. Therefore, we develop a bottom-up framework to automatically
107   discover skills from simple environments and utilize symbolic interpretation to reform these skills
      for novel and complex tasks.

      3     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
109
110
      We focus on the problem of learning a robotic agent from an environment with only fundamental
111   elements that can eventually achieve a novel and complex goal with learned skills. To form the
112   pixel-based control problem as a planning problem and handle it with the combination of skills, we
113   first introduce the concept of Factored Goal-Augmented Markov Decision Process (FGAMDP).
114   Then, we propose a novel approach to bridge the gap between MDP and planning.
115
116   3.1   FACTORED G OAL -AUGMENTED MDP
117
118   We start from a single goal-augmented MDP ⟨S, G, A, P, R, γ⟩ (Liu et al., 2022), where S is the
119   set of state s, G is the set of goal specification g, A is a set of actions a that the agent executes
120
      to interact with the environment, P is the environmental transition model P : S × A → S, R is
      defined as the set of reward r(st , at ), and γ ∈ (0, 1] is the discount factor for future rewards. Since
121
      the task involves manipulating multiple objects, it is natural that we want to separate them apart and
122
      accomplish each goal of the corresponding object. Nevertheless, separating the state is not trivial
123   because the state depicted by the image presents a mixture of relevant and irrelevant information.
124   Therefore, we refine and get the structured information of the state, or entity, which is defined as:
125
      Definition 1 (Entity). We define the set of entities e as E. Given a state s ∈ S, we then define a
126
      mapping from real state to entity Te : S → E, s.t. ∀s ∈ S, ∃e, e = Te (s) as the entity extraction
127   function, where the entity e = [e1 , e2 , . . . , em ] ∈ Rk×m , where k is the dimension of the structured
128   representation, and m is the number of factorization.
129
130   Hence, we factorize the state set S into the individual state set of each object Si as Guestrin et al.
131   (2003): S = S1 × S2 × · · · × SN . Similarly, the set of goal G can also be factorized. Thus, we can
132   define FGAMDP:
133   Definition 2 (Factored Goal-Augmented MDP). The tuple ⟨S, G, A, Pm , R, γ⟩ is a Factored Goal-
134   Augmented MDP over a set of states S if S can be further factorized.
135
136
      Given the initial state s0 ∈ S and goal g ∈ G, the agent can sample action from a parameterized
      policy π(a|s, g), thus generating a sequence of actions a1 , a2 , . . . , an to achieve the final goal g.

      3.2   F ROM MDP TO P LANNING
140
      The complexity arising from numerous entities makes controlling on MDP challenging. Therefore,
141   we group entities to form meaningful features and use ground operators to convert MDP to planning.
142
      Definition 3 (Feature). We define F as the set of features f , aggregating the environmental entities.
143
      For every f = [f1 , f2 , . . . , fn ] ∈ Rn , we named it as a feature state. We define the aggregation
144
      function Tf : E → F, s.t. ∀e ∈ E, ∃f , f = Tf (e), which maps a certain entity representation to a
145   feature state. We further define Fg as the set of goal features fg .
146
      Definition 4 (Ground Operator (Kokel et al., 2021)). Let O denote the set of ground operators o
147
      for the planning problem. Each operator o is a tuple ⟨pre(o), eff (o), β⟩, where pre(o) ∈ F is the
148   set of preconditions that should be satisfied before the ground operator executes, eff (o) is the set
149   of effect indicating the feature state change after the ground operator executes, and β ∈ Fg is the
150   termination state of O.
151
152   With feature states and ground operators, we can define the planning task as tuple ⟨F, O, Pp , Fg ⟩,
153   where Pp : F × O → F is the set of feature state transitions. Given an initial feature state f0 ∈ F
154   and a final goal feature state fg ∈ Fg , a sequence of grounding operations Π = o1 → o2 → · · · →
155
      on , known as a sequential plan, for the task can be formulated when it is possible to sequentially
      apply the operation starting at f0 and eventually reach the goal state fg .

231   5.2   S KILL L EARNING
232
      We aim to learn the policy πl for a skill l (s) = ⟨s, o, πl , pre l (s, f ), eff l (s, f )⟩ from scratch, which
233

234

235   traces according to the feature change. Finally, we train the agent to learn the skill policy for each
236   collection of traces.
237

239   of struggling with the complex environments where our agent should work during evaluation, we

242   between a single object and the environment.

      k-means clustering algorithm (Ahmed et al., 2020), with objective function:

268   symbolic interpretation is the precondition and effect of the skills. The precondition and effect are
269   presented in the form of a mathematical formula, which uses arithmetic operation {+, −, ×, ÷, >}
      as the operation set; that is, we want to form all the preconditions and effects as a polynomial.

332
333   To evaluate the performance of our model, we select two different types of environments. One
334
      is the Minecraft environment, which verifies a series of long-horizon compositional tasks. The
      other is IsaacGym, a robotic arm simulation environment employed to assess the performance of
335
      compositional generalization tasks.
336
337
      Environments. Minecraft is an n × n grid world environment. It is inspired by the computer
338
      game Minecraft and is similar to the environment in previous works (Brooks et al., 2021; Hasan-
339   beig et al., 2021; Kokel et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024). An agent can move along four directions
340   {up, down, left, right} and interact with objects with learned skills. Different from the previous
341   environment, our inputs are image maps with different objects in the map. The tasks are as follows:
342   Make-Stick: A basic experiment that urges the agent to produce a stick.
343   Make-Mass-Sticks: Produce a huge number of sticks that require repeating the same skills
344   many times.
345   Pickup-Iron: Make several tools and leverage them to pick up iron.
346   Multiple-Goals: Collect four items in an inherent order.
347
      Make-Enhance-Table: The most difficult long-horizon task that requires the cooperation of
      many skills to accomplish.
348
349   IsaacGym (Makoviychuk et al., 2021) is a simulated tabletop robotic object manipulation environ-
350   ment. The environment includes a robotic arm set in front of a table with various cubes and buttons
351   in different colors. The agent observes the system’s state through visual input and performs actions
352   in the form of deltas in the end effector coordinates a = (∆x, ∆y, ∆z, ∆g), where ∆g indicates
353
      whether the gripper is open or close. At the beginning of each episode, both the current cube posi-
      tions and the goal positions are randomly initialized on the table. The tasks are as follows:
354
      Push: Push cubes with randomized numbers and color to the goal location.
355
      Push-Grab-Lift: Manipulate cubes of randomized numbers and color to their goal positions by
356   pushing and lifting operation.
357   Ordered-Press: Press different buttons in an inherent order.

521   7   C ONCLUSION
522
523   We present a model combining a planning framework with DRL to solve pixel-based control chal-
524   lenges. Our model can autonomously acquire basic skills through interaction with the environ-
525   ment, minimizing the need for expert knowledge. Moreover, by providing symbolic interpretations
526
      for skills, we can form a sequential plan for long-horizon tasks through search algorithms such as
      MCTS. Additionally, our model leverages composable skills and a transformer-based action policy,
527
      which provides compositional generalizability to tasks that share similar features. Our model has
528
      shown great performance on long-horizon, pixel-based control problems based on this superiority.
529
530
      Limitation. Our model requires a pre-trained image segmentation model. While basic image seg-
531   mentation models have achieved promising results in our experiments, more complex tasks may
532   require further advancements and refinements in image semantic segmentation techniques. Ad-
533   ditionally, the approach using discrete features as an interface may induce some inaccuracy and
534   inflexibility. Some states with slight differences may share the same feature representation.
535
536   Future Work. For future work, one interesting direction is to explore more advanced ways for
537   automatic skills generation. Currently, the skill generation relies on classifying the collected traces.
538   We can further improve it with reward-based or entropy-based methods in the future. Another
539   possible direction is to employ generative models, such as diffusion models, to replace the current
      image segmentation approach to generate sub-goal images.

         Object-Centric Representation. Our OCR algorithm is based on the DLP algorithm. DLP
         (Daniel & Tamar, 2023) is an unsupervised object-centric model for images based on variational
752
         autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013). It provides the latent representation for all the particles.
753
754      The foreground representation e = [ec , es , ed , et , ef ] ∈ R11 is a disentangled latent variable includ-
755      ing the following learned attributes: spatial coordinate ec ∈ R3 , scale es ∈ R2 , depth ed ∈ R,

756
      Aggregation Function. We design an aggregation transformer inspired by entity-centric architec-
757   ture (Haramati et al., 2024), which processes the OCR entities into the feature state of the envi-
758   ronment. An architecture outline is presented in Figure 7. The aggregation transformer comprises
759   self-attention (SA) and cross-attention (CA) as its core components. The self-attention tries to grab
760   the relation of entities in a single object. It transforms the input vector, grouping all the entities of a
761   single object. At the CA layer, the transformer network tries to figure out the relation between dif-
762   ferent objects. After passing the SA and CA network, we let the model pass another (SA) network
763   again. This network considers the result from the previous steps and forms these two steps together,
764   placing self-attention calculation on the overall computing result. Finally, we get the output result,
765
      which is a feature vector.

867
868   SMORL. We reimplemented SMORL (Zadaianchuk et al., 2020), substituting its original visual

873
874   GAIL. GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016) was reimplemented with several modifications. We integrated
875   DLP to process image input and replaced the actor with the same controller used in our frame-
876   work. Furthermore, the critic and discriminator networks within GAIL were updated to employ a
877   transformer architecture.
878
879   DeepSynth. We reimplement DeepSynth (Hasanbeig et al., 2021), with the original image seg-
880
      mentation algorithm replaced by DLP. We directly implement the automaton synthesis algorithm
      based on the DLP result. For the low-level controller in DeepSynth, we also use the same controller
881
      as our framework to substitute the controller in DeepSynth to ensure a fair comparison.
882
883
      DiRL. DiRL (Jothimurugan et al., 2021) was reimplemented as a baseline model, incorporating
884
      domain-specific knowledge. Rules such as ”pick after push” and ”pick up wood before going to
885   the craft table” were established to provide high-level guidance for the low-level policy. The policy
886   within DiRL was also replaced with the same controller used in our framework for a more credible
