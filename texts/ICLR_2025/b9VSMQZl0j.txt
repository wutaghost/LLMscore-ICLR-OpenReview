011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013               Person re-identification (re-ID) via skeleton data is an emerging topic with im-
014               mense potential for safety-critical applications. Existing methods usually utilize
015               spatial or temporal skeleton semantics learning (SSL) tasks to facilitate skeleton
016
                  representation learning, while most SSL tasks are model-dependent and lack the
                  ability to capture general fine-grained (e.g., joint-level) spatial-temporal skeleton
017
                  patterns under different model architectures. To delve into multi-faceted generality
018
                  of SSL tasks, we first propose an SSL generality assessment framework termed
019               SCUT that identifies four key SSL properties: Spatial-temporal effectiveness, Co-
020               training compatibility, Unsupervised trainability, and Task transformability. By
021               formulating systematic evaluation criteria for each property, SCUT enables both
022               qualitative and quantitative analysis of SSL generality under varying models and
023               scenarios. Motivated by SCUT to fully harness skeleton context for semantics learn-
024               ing, we further devise a generic Probabilistic Masked Spatial-Temporal context
025               Reconstruction (Prompter) task to enhance performance of skeleton-based per-
026               son re-ID models. Specifically, Prompter first probabilistically and independently
027
                  masks joints’ structural locations to generate spatial context, and then randomly
                  conceal their motion trajectories to form temporal context. Through combining
028
                  both spatial and temporal skeleton context representations to jointly reconstruct
029
                  and infer skeleton sequences, Prompter encourages the model to capture general
030               valuable spatial-temporal skeleton patterns for person re-ID. Empirical evaluations
031               on SCUT and five benchmark datasets demonstrate the superiority of Prompter
032               to most state-of-the-art SSL tasks. We further validate its general effectiveness in
033               different skeleton modeling, RGB-estimated or cross-domain scenarios1 .

036   1       I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Person re-identification (re-ID) is a vital pattern recognition task to match and retrieve a certain
039   pedestrian from different views or scenes, which has driven many safety-critical applications such
040   as security authentication, smart surveillance, and human tracking (Vezzani et al., 2013; Ye et al.,
041
      2021). With recently more accessible skeleton data from low-cost and contactless depth sensors like
      Kinect (Shotton et al., 2011), skeleton-based person re-ID is attracting increasing attention in both
042
      academia and industry (Liao et al., 2020; Rao et al., 2021b; Rao & Miao, 2023). Different from
043
      conventional person re-ID methods that require visual appearance or facial features (Wang et al.,
044   2016), skeleton-based methods typically model body structural features and unique motion semantics
045   (e.g., gait (Murray et al., 1964)) from positions of key body joints to identify different persons, which
046   enjoy numerous advantages such as smaller data input, better privacy protection (e.g., without using
047   appearances), and higher robustness to view and background variations (Han et al., 2017).
048
      Early skeleton-based methods (Andersson & Araujo, 2015) extract hand-crafted descriptors such as
049
      pairwise joint distances to depict anthropometric and gait attributes of body for person re-ID. However,
050   these methods heavily rely on prior domain knowledge such as kinematics (Yoo et al., 2002) to model
051   skeleton data, which typically lack the ability to fully exploit latent skeleton semantics or features
052   beyond human cognition. To tackle this problem, recent mainstream methods (Liao et al., 2020; Rao
053

      & Miao, 2023) resort to deep neural networks to perform skeleton representation learning. In these
055   methods, skeleton semantics learning (SSL) tasks such as skeleton reconstruction are widely adopted
056   as either a main objective (Rao et al., 2020; Rao et al., 2021b;a) or enhancement task (Rao et al.,
057   2021c; Rao & Miao, 2022; Rao & Miao, 2023) to help capture spatial-temporal skeleton patterns
058   and high-level semantics (e.g., skeleton pattern consistency) for person re-ID. Despite the success
059   of existing SSL tasks, most of them are designed based on certain model architectures or feature
060   representations (e.g., sequence representations), and often lack the ability to fully mine fine-grained
061   (e.g., joint-level) spatial and temporal semantics for person re-ID. These properties inherently limit
062   their effectiveness and adaptability to different models. On the other hand, current skeleton-based
063
      person re-ID endeavors only provide a single performance metric (i.e., accuracy) of SSL tasks under
      a particular model, but rarely explore and compare different properties associated with their generality
064
      under varying models or scenarios. For example, they usually lack a general framework to define and
065
      quantify key SSL attributes, such as compatibility and trainability (e.g., learnable under unsupervised
066   scenarios), which hinders a fair and multi-faceted evaluation of SSL generality in practice.
067
068   To address the above challenges, we first present a systematic generality assessment framework
069
      termed SCUT that identifies and quantifies key characteristics of SSL tasks in terms of Spatial-
      temporal effectiveness (STE), Co-training compatibility (CTC), Unsupervised trainability (UT), and
070
      Task transformability (TT). Then, based on the SCUT framework, we for the first time evaluate the
071
      co-training performance of existing state-of-the-art SSL tasks under different models and scenarios
072   (e.g., datasets), and quantitatively measure their general effectiveness in combining spatial and
073   temporal skeleton semantics learning for person re-ID. Our study empirically reveals that existing
074   SSL tasks often exhibit different compatibility (CTC) when applied to other models, and the SSL task
075   that explicitly incorporates spatial-temporal semantics learning (STE) and jointly optimizes multiple
076   sub-tasks (TT) achieves higher performance in most cases. Motivated by these key properties
077   to fully exploit valuable skeletal context information (e.g., structural context of body) for both
078   spatial and temporal pattern learning, we propose a generic Probabilistic Masked Spatial-Temporal
079   Conext Reconstruction (Prompter) task to enhance general skeleton semantics learning of different
080
      models for person re-ID. In particular, Prompter leverages probabilistic spatial context masking
      (PSCM) to probabilistically and independently mask skeletal structural locations (defined as “spatial
081
      context”), and combines probabilistic temporal context masking (PTCM) to generate random partial
082
      skeletal motion trajectories (defined as “temporal context”) to perform complete skeleton sequence
083   reconstruction. Based on the masked spatial and temporal skeleton context representations, Prompter
084   simultaneously reconstructs the unmasked positions and infers the masked parts of skeleton sequences,
085   so as to encourage the model to capture useful key spatial-temporal skeleton semantics (e.g., structural
086   joint relations) for person re-ID.
087
      The main contributions can be summarized as:
088
089          • We identify the key properties of general skeleton semantics learning (SSL) to formulate the
090            first SSL generality assessment framework SCUT, and conduct a multi-faceted performance
091            evaluation of of existing state-of-the-art SSL tasks under varying models and scenarios.
092
             • We propose average co-training performance gain and spatial-temporal performance gain to
093            quantitatively compare model compatibility (CTC) and spatial-temporal effectiveness (STE)
094            of SSL tasks. We empirically reveal the importance of transformability (TT) in SSL.
095
             • We present a generic SSL task Prompter with probabilistic spatial (PSCM) and temporal
096
               skeleton context masking (PTCM) for reconstruction and inference of skeleton sequences,
097
               which enhances the general spatial-temporal skeleton semantics learning for person re-ID.
098
099          • Empirical evaluations on SCUT and five public datasets demonstrate the generality and
100
               superiority of Prompter in improving various models, and it is scalable to be applied to
               RGB-estimated skeletons, cross-domain person re-ID, and different skeleton modeling.

105   Skeleton-Based Person Re-Identification. Early works extract hand-crafted skeleton descriptors
106   such as anthropometric and gait attributes from body joints for person re-ID: Seven Euclidean
107   distances between certain joint pairs are calculated as discriminative features (Barbosa et al., 2012),
      while Munaro et al. (2014a) and Pala et al. (2019) further extend it to into 13 (D13 ) and 16 skeleton

      descriptors (D16 ) respectively for person re-ID. Recent mainstream methods leverage deep neural
109   networks to learn representations from skeleton sequences or graphs: Liao et al. (2020) propose CNN-
110   based PoseGait to encode joint-based motion descriptors (denoted as DPG ) for human recognition.
111   Rao et al. (2020) devise an attention-based encoder-decoder model (AGE) to encode gait features
112   from 3D skeletons, while SGELA (Rao et al., 2021b) further combines sequence contrastive learning
113   to enhance motion semantics learning. A masked contrastive learning framework SimMC is proposed
114   by Rao & Miao (2022) to learn skeleton prototypes and intra-sequence relations for person re-ID. The
115   multi-scale skeleton graphs are explored in MG-SCR (Rao et al., 2021c) and SM-SGE (Rao et al.,
116   2021a) to learn unique body relations and patterns at various levels. TranSG fuses both skeleton-level
117
      and sequence-level graph representations for contrastive learning (Rao & Miao, 2023). Some recent
      person re-ID works also combine RGB images and skeleton data to learn auxiliary anthropometric
118
      attributes (Wang et al., 2020), body parts correlations (Lu et al., 2023), and clothing-invariant features
119
      (Nguyen et al., 2024) to enhance their performance.
120
121   Skeleton Semantics Learning (SSL). Learning general spatial-temporal skeleton semantics is pivotal
122   to skeleton-based person re-ID Rao & Miao (2024). The attention-based reconstruction (AR) (Rao
123
      et al., 2020) and attention-based contrastive learning (AC) (Rao et al., 2021b) are devised to learn
      semantics of motion continuity within skeletons. In (Rao et al., 2021c), multi-level skeleton sequence
124
      prediction (MSSP) task is proposed based on multi-level graphs, while Rao et al. (2021a) further
125
      devise multi-scale skeleton reconstruction (MSR) to capture skeleton dynamics and cross-scale
126   component correspondence. The masked intra-sequence contrastive learning (MIC) is devised in
127   (Rao & Miao, 2022) to learn pattern invariance between different skeleton subsequences. A structure-
128   trajectory prompted reconstruction (STPR) task is proposed in (Rao & Miao, 2023) to learn structural
129   relations and pattern continuity of joints. As far as we know, our work is the first exploration and
130   assessment of multi-faceted generality of existing SSL tasks under different scenarios. Different from
131   previous tasks that rely on certain architectures or sequence-level representations, our method can be
132   generally applied to different models for both spatial and temporal joint-level semantics learning.

235   Qualitative Attributes:
236
               • Unsupervised Trainability (UT). An SSL task without using class labels can be trained in
237              more general scenarios (e.g., unsupervised skeleton learning). The UT property guarantees
238              that the SSL task can be commonly applied to different datasets and unknown classes (i.e.,
239              class-agnostic). In practice, it encourages the model to learn class-agnostic general skeleton
240              semantics (e.g., universal motion patterns), which can be combined with class-specific
241              learning of the downstream task objective to enhance person re-ID performance.
242            • Task Transformability (TT). Transformability is a key attribute in general SSL tasks, as
243              it enables flexibly adapting the semantics learning objective to a certain architecture or
244              downstream task. If an SSL task explicitly contains other SSL tasks (defined as sub-tasks)
245              or can be potentially transformed to them under different probabilities, this SSL task is
246              considered to possess transformability (TT). For example, an SSL task that directly combines
247              reconstruction and prediction tasks possesses the TT property. Performing such task can be
248              viewed as to simultaneously optimize different SSL sub-tasks (see Sec. 4.3), therefore often
249
                 possessing higher generality and effectiveness than same-type SSL tasks without TT.

      3.3   P ROBABILISTIC M ASKED S PATIAL -T EMPORAL C ONTEXT R ECONSTRUCTION
294
295   To realize general and effective SSL, it is essential to align its objective to the key of skeleton-based
296   person re-ID, which aims to capture both spatial skeleton features (e.g., body structural features) and
297   temporal motion attributes (e.g., gait (Murray et al., 1964)). Such spatial and temporal patterns can
298   be respectively characterized by positions and relations of different body joints within each skeleton
299   and their corresponding trajectories. A straightforward method is to perform skeleton reconstruction
300
      without explicit temporal or spatial modeling (Rao et al., 2021b), while such task usually lacks the
      flexibility to fully exploit varying valuable context information (e.g., temporal context of trajectory)
301
      of fine-grained skeleton representations (e.g., body joints) to capture richer skeleton semantics.
302
      According to the crucial properties of SSL identified by SCUT (see Sec. 3.2), a more general solution
303   to these challenges is to explicitly model both spatial and temporal skeleton patterns (i.e., implement
304   STE) while combining multiple skeleton context based learning sub-objectives (i.e., establish TT) to
305   build a more effective SSL task. To this end, we propose Probabilistic Masked Spatial-Temporal
306   Context Reconstruction (Prompter) that randomly and independently masks structural locations of
307   joints (defined as “skeletal spatial context”) and motion trajectories of joints (defined as “skeletal
308   temporal context”) to jointly reconstruct and infer spatial-temporal context (e.g., masked positions)
309   of skeleton sequences, so as to learn general effective skeleton semantics for person re-ID.

364   utilize useful spatial (e.g., key structural relations of joints) and temporal skeleton semantics (e.g.,

      Generalization of Prompter. The Prompter task can be viewed as a general probabilistic form
368
      of existing reconstruction or masked reconstruction based SSL tasks (Rao et al., 2021a; Rao &
369   Miao, 2023). It owns the task transformability (TT): The direct spatial reconstruction with all body
370   joints unmasked is contained in Prompter with the probability of PS (J) = (1 − pS )J (see Appendix
371   II), while the masked spatial skeleton reconstruction with  nS unmasked joints is a special case of
372   Prompter with the occurrence probability of PS (nS ) = nJS (pS )J−nS (1 − pS )nS . This enables it to
373   jointly optimize different SSL sub-tasks and achieve better semantics learning performance (see
374   Sec. 4.2 and 4.3). Intuitively, Prompter introduces more possible spatial-temporal reconstruction
375   cases (i.e., under varying partial spatial and temporal contexts) than both direct reconstruction and
376   masked reconstruction (Rao & Miao, 2023) that employs a fixed number of masks, thereby potentially
377   improving the reconstruction diversity to reduce model over-fitting. We further reveal its relations to
      model regularization methods (e.g., Dropout (Baldi & Sadowski, 2014)) in the appendices.

513   Evaluation on Cross-Domain Person Re-ID. To validate the generality of skeleton semantics
514   learned from Prompter, we co-train the base model with Prompter on the source datasets and evaluate
515   its generalized performance on the target datasets without model fine-tuning. As shown in Table 6,
516   applying Prompter achieves higher performance than using other SSL tasks when generalizing the
517   learned model to other domains (i.e., datasets), which suggests that our method could capture more
518   general skeleton semantics (e.g., domain-shared discriminative features) for person re-ID.
519   Transfer to Different Skeleton Modeling. We evaluate the effectiveness of transferring Prompter to
520   varying levels of skeleton modeling (e.g., part-level or body-level skeleton graphs (Rao et al., 2021a)).
521   As shown in Fig. 2, our method outperforms different state-of-the-art SSL tasks on both original
522   and higher-level skeleton representations. This demonstrates its generality and stronger effectiveness
523   under different-level skeletal structures to facilitate the model to learn more discriminative features.
524   Loss Visualization. As shown in Fig. 3, solely applying Prompter simultaneously reduces DR and
525   STPR losses, which validates its TT property that enables the model to jointly learn with contained
526   sub-tasks. Consistent with the analysis in Sec. 3.3, Prompter introduces more diverse random cases
527   into training (which could increase the fluctuations in loss) and can potentially reduce over-fitting to
528   achieve a lower convergence value. More results and analyses are provided in the appendices.

531   5                                             C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose the SCUT framework that identifies four key properties (STE, CTC, UT,
534   TT) to assess the generality of skeleton semantics learning (SSL) tasks across different models and
535   scenarios. Based on SCUT, we further devise a generic SSL task termed Prompter to probabilistically
536   and independently mask spatial context of structural locations and temporal context of motion
537   trajectories, which are exploited to reconstruct and infer complete skeleton sequences to capture
538   general effective spatial-temporal skeleton semantics for person re-ID. Extensive evaluations on
539   SCUT and five public datasets demonstrate the higher effectiveness and generality of Prompter than
      other state-of-the-art SSL tasks, and it is highly scalable to be applied to various models and scenarios.
