010
011              Inspired by cognitive neuroscience, representation engineering (RepE) seeks to
012              connect the neural activities within large language models (LLMs) to their behav-
013              iors, providing a promising pathway towards transparent AI. Despite its successful
014              applications under many contexts, the connection established by RepE is not al-
015              ways reliable, as it implicitly assumes that LLMs will consistently follow the roles
016
                 assigned in the instructions during neural activities collection. When this assump-
                 tion is violated, observed correlations between the collected neural activities and
017
                 model behaviors may not be causal due to potential confounding biases, thereby
018
                 compromising the reliability of RepE. We identify this key limitation and pro-
019              pose CAusal Representation Engineering (CARE), a principled framework that
020              employs matched-pair trial design to control for confounders. By isolating the
021              impact of confounders on neural activities and model behaviors, CARE grounds
022              the connection in causality, allowing for more reliable interpretations and control
023              of LLMs. Extensive empirical evaluations across various aspects of safety demon-
024              strate the effectiveness of CARE compared to the original RepE implementation,

      In the original RepE implementation (Zou et al., 2023), the authors implicitly assume that LLMS
      will follow the roles assigned in the instructions during neural activities collection. However, this
077
      assumption may not always hold; LLMs can exhibit behaviors inconsistent with the assigned roles,
078
      as illustrated in Figure 1. Furthermore, we highlight the vulnerability of RepE in Figure 2. The left
079   panel shows that RepE achieves nearly 100% accuracy when the test set only contains consistent
080   data 2 , creating an illusion of perfectly explaining model behaviors via neural activities. However, as
081   the test set includes more data on inconsistent behaviors, the accuracy drops significantly. The right
082   panel shows successful and failed cases of controlling model behaviors, with the only difference
083   being the stimulus set used to collect neural activities. These results highlight the need for more
084   reliable and principled methods to establish the link between neural activities and model behaviors.
085   To address these limitations, we introduce CAusal Representation Engineering (CARE), a frame-
086   work designed to improve the reliability of RepE. Specifically, CARE employs a matched-pair trial
087   design, a method commonly used in clinical and economic studies (Bai, 2022; Bai et al., 2022), to
088   rigorously control for confounding factors. In this design, we create a stimuli set comprising paired
089   text stimuli that differ only in the roles assigned in the instructions, and ensure that each pair leads
090   to opposite behaviors, e.g., generating safe versus unsafe responses. We use content moderation
091   models, such as Llama Guard (Inan et al., 2023), to label the generated responses, which provides
092   a efficient and low-cost solution to verify whether model behavior matches the assigned role and
093
      filter out pairs with consistent behaviors. This approach helps to isolate the impact of instructions
      on model behaviors so that the neural activities are collected under controlled conditions, free from
094
      confounding biases. We also stress the importance of using causality metrics, namely manipulation
095
      and termination scores, to complement the traditional metrics like accuracy that are commonly used
096   to evaluate the faithfulness of explanations. These metrics provide additional insights into the effec-
097   tiveness of the explanations, ensuring they are grounded in causation, not just correlations, and also
098   allow us to evaluate the performance of controlling model behaviors via neural activities.

113   2     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
114
115   To ground our discussions in causality, we first introduce some key concepts from causal infer-
116   ence Pearl (2009). Formally, a causal relationship between two random variables, X and Y (where
117
      X causes Y ), holds if an intervention on X changes the distribution of Y , but not the reverse. An
      intervention implies actively setting the value of X to a target value x to observe its effect on Y ,
118
      compared to passively observing the value of X and Y . Moreover, we use the language of potential
119
      outcomes to formally discuss causal relationships. This framework posits that for each unit u under
120   study, there exist multiple potential outcomes Yx = yx (u) corresponding to each possible treatment
121   X = x. Following (Pearl, 2009, Section 3.2), we define causal effect as P (yx ), a function that maps
122   the treatment X to the space of probability distributions on the outcome Y . The key difference be-
123   tween the causal effect P (yx ) and the conditional probability P (y|x) is that the latter fails to capture
124   the concept of intervention, as it only reflects the observed correlation between X and Y , not the
125   causal relationship between them.
126   We now formalize the problem of interpreting LLM behaviors using neural activities. Given an
127   LLM, our goal is to identify the neural activities that causally influence the model behaviors so that
128   we can use these activities to explain and control these behaviors. Let X denote certain type of neural
129   activities within the LLM, and Y the model behaviors. To achieve the goal, we should collect data
130   from the interventional distributions P (Yx ) to study the causal effect of X on Y and avoid being
131   affected by confounding factors. Unfortunately, due to the high-dimensional and complex nature
132   of neural activities, direct intervening on them is generally infeasible. An alternative approach is
133   to carefully distinguish between neural activities that lead to the target behavior and those that do
134
      not, so that we can approximatively create the interventional distributions P (Yx ) and attribute the
      differences in behaviors to the identified neural activities. In the next section, we will show how this
135
      can be achieved using content moderation models and matched-pair trial design.

139
140   In this section, we elaborate on our approach, CAusal Representation Engineering (CARE), which
141   consists of four key steps. These steps are illustrated in Figure 3. In the following, we will discuss
142   each step in detail.

144   3.1   M ATCHED -PAIR T RIAL
145
146   Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) are considered the gold standard for establishing causal rela-
147   tionships in many disciplines. However, when sample sizes are limited, RCTs may not sufficiently
148
      ensure that the treatment group and the control group are homogeneous, making it difficult to at-
      tribute the observed difference in outcomes Y to the treatment variable X. This is particularly true
149
      in the context of RepE, where the stimulus set T used to collect neural activities is often small,
150
      typically consisting of 5-128 pairs of stimuli, as recommended in Zou et al. (2023). To address this
151   issue, we employ a matched-pair trial design, which pairs each unit u in the treatment group with a
152   similar unit u′ in the control group, ensuring the paired units are comparable in all aspects except for
153   the treatment X. By randomizing treatment assignment within each pair3 , this experimental design
154   removes the edge from confounding factors to the treatment variable in the causal graph, thereby
155   effectively controlling for potential biases.

214
215   In this step, we apply linear models to learn the mapping between neural activities A and the target
      behavior Y (e.g., Y = 1 for safe responses and Y = 0 for unsafe responses). This approach

      is grounded in the linear representation hypothesis (Park et al., 2023), which suggests that complex
217   information, such as high-level semantic features or cognitive functions, are linearly representable in
218   the model’s representation space. Therefore, the relationship between neural activities and behaviors
219   can be effectively captured through linear methods.
220
      For each layer l, we train a linear model Ml using the collected neural activities {ai,l }N i=1 and their
221
      corresponding labels {Yi }N i=1 given  by a content moderation   model.  Under   this setting, the model
222
      parameters θl in Ml form a vector that maps the neural activities at layer l to a numeric value that
223
      reflects the likelihood of performing the target behavior. Analogous to neuroimaging techniques,
224   which measure the intensity of brain activation for a specific cognitive function, the dot product
225   θl⊤ ai,l can be interpreted as the “intensity” of neural activity at layer l for the target behavior Y .
226   Thus, θl functions as a “safety template”. When neural activities match this template, the model
227   demonstrates a high intensity of safety-inducing activities, making it more likely to generate safe
228   responses. Conversely, a mismatch indicates that the model may generate unsafe responses. Except
229   for interpreting the internal mechanisms of LLMs, this template also unlocks the potential to control
230   model behaviors. Given the template θl , we now can intervene on the neural activity ai,l by linearly
231   combining it with θl to increase or decrease the intensity of certain type of neural activities, thereby
232
      influencing the model’s behaviors. In the next step, we will further discuss how such interventions
      can be used to evaluate the causal grounding of the obtained explanations.

235   3.4       E VALUATION
236
      Traditional evaluation focuses on faithfulness metrics, such as accuracy and precision, which mea-
237
      sure how well the obtained explanations reflect the model behaviors. In CARE, we consider two
238   additional evaluation strategies to explore the causality of the explanations, i.e., whether the identi-
239   fied neural activities causally influence model behaviors. These two evaluation strategies are inspired
240   by cognitive neuroscience: Manipulation (Hallett, 2007; Filmer et al., 2014), which stimulates spe-
241   cific brain regions to observe their effects on cognitive functions; and Termination (Vaidya et al.,
242   2019), which nullifies specific neural activities to determine their necessity for performing the target
243   behavior 4 . They are implemented as follows:

257   4       R ELATED W ORK
258
259   Interpretability. Traditional interpretability research usually employs methods like feature attri-
260   bution to measure the relevance of input features (e.g., tokens) to predictions. These methods are
261   generally designed to generate local explanations specific to individual samples and their predic-
262
      tions. For example, gradient-based attribution methods (Simonyan et al., 2014; Sundararajan et al.,
      2017) analyze the derivatives of outputs with respect to inputs to determine feature importance.
263
      Sikdar et al. (2021) and Enguehard (2023) devised methods to calculate token-level and word-level
264
      attribution scores. Another notable approach is SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), which quantifies the
265   contribution of each token to model predictions by computing Shapley values. However, as LLMs
266   grow in complexity, these traditional methods often struggle with scalability and computational ef-
267   ficiency (Chen et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). Furthermore, there is a growing interest in global
268   explanations that delve into the inner workings of LLMs. Mechanistic interpretability (Wang et al.,
269

      2022; Conmy et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2023), aiming to reverse-engineer LLMs’ internal mechanisms
271   and break down their behaviors into understandable components named circuits, has emerged as a
272   promising direction. Although these approaches are useful for explaining and debugging LLMs,
273   they often require substantial human effort and may not scale to explaining higher-level cognitive
274   functions and behaviors (Zhao et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Zimmermann et al., 2024). In contrast,
275   Representation Engineering (RepE) (Zou et al., 2023), places representations at the core of analy-
276   sis, showing promise in understanding high-level cognitive functions such as honesty, safety, and
277   power-seeking behaviors. Our work builds on RepE, focusing on interpreting LLM behaviors using
278   neural activities within the representation space.
279
280   Representation Engineering Since the introduction of RepE (Zou et al., 2023), the field has seen
281
      a surge of interest in studying LLMs through their neural activities. For example, Li et al. (2024)
      proposed a method named Jailbreaking LLMs through Repersentation Engineering, which aims to
282
      challenge the safety boundaries of LLMs by exploiting their “safety patterns.” These patterns are
283
      not only useful for improving the success rate of jailbreaking, but also help to defend against mali-
284   cious attacks. Ball et al. (2024) further investigates the mechanisms behind successful jailbreaking
285   by analyzing the neural activities elicited by jailbreak and non-jailbreak instructions. They find
286   that successful jailbreaking can substantially suppress the model’s perception of harmfulness. To
287   avoid generating harmful responses, an important research direction is alignment, which ensures
288   that LLMs’ behaviors are aligned with human values. Liu et al. (2024b) explores the application
289   of RepE in alignment. The authors propose a novel method called representation alignment from
290   human feedback, which identifies the neural activities that best reflect human preferences and uses
291   them to achieve precise control over model behaviors. Furthermore, Wang et al. (2024a) introduces
292
      InferAligner, a method to align LLMs with human values at inference time, without requiring addi-
      tional training. It leverages the identified safety steering vectors to modify the model’s activations,
293
      thereby ensuring that the generated responses are harmless. Some recent works strive to use RepE to
294
      better understand LLMs in various contexts, e.g., hallucination (CH-Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
295   2024; Wang et al., 2024b), in-context learning (Liu et al., 2024a), knowledge encoding (Ju et al.,
296   2024), and unlearning (Huu-Tien et al., 2024). Our work complements these studies by focusing on
297   the reliability of RepE, which is fundamental to its applications in various contexts.

349   and PAIR. The former uses pseudo-labels based on predefined roles in the instructions, without

      Linear Approaches. In the modeling step, we employ different linear approaches to identify the
355
      safety templates. Once these templates are obtained, we can predict model behaviors by exam-
356   ining the match between the neural activities and the templates. In this work, we consider the
357   following linear approaches: 1) Principal Component Analysis (PCA), which identifies the principal
358   components of the difference vectors of paired neural activities, with the first principal component
359   (capturing the most variance) used as the safety template. 2) DiffMean (DM), which calculates
360   the difference between the mean vectors of centralized safe and unsafe neural activities, a vector
361   corresponding to the direction from risk-inducing to safe-inducing activities, serving as the safety
362   template. 3) Logistic Regression (LR), which fits a logistic regression model to predict the target
363   behavior using neural activity-label pairs, with the model’s coefficients used as the safety template.
364
365   Evaluation Protocols. Two types of metrics are employed in evaluation, namely faithfulness and
366
      causality metrics. The former includes accuracy, precision, true positive rate (TPR), and true nega-
      tive rate (TNR). The latter includes manipulation and termination scores, which have been discussed
367
      in Section 3. Specifically, manipulation scores measure the success rate of flipping model behaviors
368
      by suppressing or enhancing the neural activities. The termination score measures the percentage of
369   samples whose safety scores move in the opposite direction after modifying the neural activities. To
370   gain insights into performance across different types of behaviors, we report the metrics for safe and
371   unsafe samples separately.

      5.3   H YPERPARAMETER A NALYZES
470
471   In this section, we investigate the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of CARE.
472   Due to space constraints, the results are presented in Appendix A.5.
473
474
      The size of the stimulus set. In Zou et al. (2023), the authors suggest that a stimulus set of 5 to
475   128 instructions is sufficient for RepE. Here we choose sizes from {8, 16, 32, 64, 128}. We find that
476   the performance is relatively stable for accuracy and manipulation score (unsafe) when the stimulus
477   set size is larger than 16. The manipulation score (safe) benefit from a larger stimulus set size, but
478   the improvement becomes smaller as the size increases.
479
480   The number of layers selected to predict and control model behaviors. In RepE, the layers with
481   the highest accuracy are selected to interpret model behaviors. Here we choose the top 5, 10, 15, 20,
482   and all 32 layers to evaluate the performance. We find that 5 is already enough to achieve a good
483   accuracy, and the performance drops slightly as the number of layers increases since bringing in
484   more layers may introduce noise. For manipulation scores, 15 and 20 achieve the best performance.
485   Using fewer layers may be insufficient to capture the complexity of model behaviors, while more
      layers may deteriorate the original capabilities of the model.

      6     C ONCLUSION
518
519
520   In this paper, we identify the key limitation of representation engineering (RepE), particularly its
521   reliance on the implicit assumption that model behaviors are consistent with the roles assigned in
522   the instructions. To address this issue, we propose a novel method called Causal Representation En-
523   gineering (CARE), which introduces a matched-pair trial design to control for potential confounders
524   with the help of content moderation models. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that
525
      CARE consistently outperforms the baselines in causality metrics, while maintaining strong per-
      formance in faithfulness metrics, even in OOD settings. Overall, our work provides a principled
526
      framework to improve the reliability of representation engineering, taking a meaningful step to-
527
      wards understanding and controlling large language models.
528
529   While CARE shows promising results, our findings also reveal ongoing challenges and opportuni-
530   ties for future research in interpreting model behaviors. First, as shown in our experiments, there
531
      is still room for improvement in the faithfulness of the obtained explanations, e.g., the accuracy of
      predicting model behaviors based on neural activities, and we should be cautious when applying rep-
532
      resentation engineering in downstream tasks and interpreting the results. Second, this work focuses
533
      on decoder-only LLMs, and the reliability of representation engineering on other model types, such
534   as multi-modal models, remains unexplored. Third, while we focus on safety in this work, repre-
535   sentation engineering can be applied to a wide range of cognitive functions and complex behaviors,
536   such as honesty, fairness, and power-seeking behaviors, which are also worth exploring. Forth, we
537   use the same content moderation model in filtering and evaluation to provide a consistent standard,
538   but this may unintentionally amplify some inherent biases of the moderation model. Addressing
539   these challenges will further strengthen the reliability of representation engineering, making it a
      more useful tool for AI transparency research and applications.
