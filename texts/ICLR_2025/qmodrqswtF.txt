                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
                 Evaluating off-policy decisions using batch data poses significant challenges due
010
                 to high variance and limited sample sizes, making reliable evaluation difficult. To
011
                 improve Off-Policy Evaluation (OPE) performance, we must identify and address
012              the sources of this variance. Recent research on Concept Bottleneck Models
013              (CBMs) shows that using human-explainable concepts can improve predictions
014              and provide better understanding. We propose incorporating concepts into OPE
015              to reduce variance through targeted interventions. Shared disease characteristics,
016              for example, could help identify better treatment options, despite variations in
017              patient vitals. Our work introduces a family of concept-based OPE estimators,
018              proving that they remain unbiased and reduce variance when concepts are known
019              and predefined. Since real-world applications often lack predefined concepts, we
020
                 further develop an end-to-end algorithm to learn interpretable, concise, and diverse
                 parameterized concepts optimized for variance reduction. Our experiments with
021
                 synthetic and real-world datasets show that both known and learned concept-based
022
                 estimators significantly improve OPE performance. Crucially, we show that, unlike
023              other OPE methods, concept-based estimators are easily interpretable and allow for
024              targeted interventions on specific concepts, further enhancing the quality of these
025              estimators.

030    . In domains like healthcare, education, and public policy, where interacting with the environment
031   can be risky, prohibitively expensive, or unethical (Sutton & Barto, 2018; Murphy et al., 2001;
032   Mandel et al., 2014), estimating policy value from batch data before deployment is essential for the
033   practical application of RL. OPE aims to estimate the effectiveness of a specific policy, known as the
034   evaluation or target policy, using data collected beforehand from a different policy, referred to as the
035
      behavior policy.
036   OPE has been widely studied to determine when an evaluation policy outperforms a behavior policy
037   (e.g., Komorowski et al. (2018a); Precup et al. (2000); Thomas & Brunskill (2016); Jiang & Li
038   (2016)). Importance sampling (IS) methods adjust for distributional mismatches between behavior
039   and target policies by reweighting historical data, yielding generally unbiased and consistent estimates
040   (Precup et al., 2000). Despite their desirable properties (Thomas & Brunskill, 2016; Jiang & Li, 2016;
041
      Farajtabar et al., 2018), IS methods often face high variance, especially with limited overlap between
      behavioral samples and evaluation targets or in data-scarce conditions. Evaluation policies may
042
      outperform behavior policies for specific individuals or subgroups (Keramati et al., 2021b), making it
043
      misleading to rely solely on aggregate policy value estimates. While causal inference approaches
044   (e.g., Athey et al. (2019); Nie & Wager (2021)) explore individual-level outcome differences under
045   evaluation versus behavior policies, they do not address sequential settings. Work such as Keramati
046   et al. (2021b) has shown that with predefined groups, certain OPE estimators can yield more accurate
047   evaluations. However, in practice, these groups are often unknown, prompting the need for methods
048   to learn interpretable characterizations of the circumstances where the evaluation policy benefits
049   certain individuals over others.
050   In this paper, we propose performing OPE using interpretable concepts (Koh et al., 2020; Madeira
051   et al., 2023) instead of relying solely on state and action information. We demonstrate that this

      2    R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Off-Policy Evaluation. There is a long history of methods for performing OPE, broadly categorized
090   into model-based or model-free (Sutton & Barto, 2018). Model-based methods, such as the Direct
091   Method (DM), learn a model of the environment to simulate trajectories and estimate the policy value
092
      (Paduraru, 2013; Chow et al., 2015; Hanna et al., 2017; Fonteneau et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2018b).
      These methods often rely on strong assumptions about the parametric model for statistical guarantees.
093
      Model-free methods, like IS, correct sampling bias in off-policy data through reweighting to obtain
094
      unbiased estimates (e.g., Precup et al. (2000); Horvitz & Thompson (1952); Thomas & Brunskill
095   (2016)). Doubly robust (DR) estimators (e.g., Jiang & Li (2016); Farajtabar et al. (2018)) combine
096   model-based DM and model-free IS for OPE but may fail to reduce variance when both DM and IS
097   have high variance. Various methods have been developed to refine estimation accuracy in IS, such
098   as truncating importance weights and estimating weights from steady-state visitation distributions
099   (Liu et al., 2018a; Xie et al., 2019; Doroudi et al., 2017; Bossens & Thomas, 2024).
100
101
      Off-Policy Evaluation based on Subgroups. Keramati et al. (2021b) extend OPE to estimate
102   treatment effects for subgroups and provide actionable insights on which subgroups may benefit
103   from specific treatments, assuming subgroups are known or identified using regression trees. Unlike
104   regression trees, which are limited in scalability, our approach employs CBMs to learn interpretable
105   concepts that directly characterize individuals, enabling a new family of IS estimators based on these
106   concepts. Similarly, Shen et al. (2021) propose reducing variance by omitting likelihood ratios for
107   certain states. Our work complements this by summarizing relevant trajectory information using
      concepts, rather than omitting states irrelevant to the return. The advantage of using concepts as


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      opposed to states is that we can easily interpret and intervene on these concepts unlike the state
109   information.
110
111   Marginalized Importance Sampling (MIS) estimators (Uehara et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2018a; Nachum
112
      et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020b;a) mitigate the high variance of traditional IS by reweighting data
      tuples using density ratios computed from state visitation at each time step. These estimators enhance
113
      robustness by focusing on states with high visitation density ratios, thereby marginalizing out less
114
      visited states. However, MIS has its challenges: computing density ratios can introduce high variance,
115   particularly in complex state spaces, and it obscures which aspects of the state space contribute
116   directly to variance. Some studies, such as Katdare et al. (2023) and Fujimoto et al. (2023), improve
117   MIS by decomposing density ratio estimation into components like large density ratio mismatch and
118   transition probability mismatch. Our work differs from MIS by categorizing states using interpretable
119   concepts rather than solely relying on density ratios. This approach enables targeted interventions
120   that enhance policy adjustments, leading to better returns and reduced variance in OPE. Unlike MIS,
121   our method provides interpretability, which becomes increasingly important as problem complexity
122   grows. Proposals for hybrid estimators, such as those in Pavse & Hanna (2022a), suggest using
123
      low-dimensional abstraction of state spaces with MIS to manage high-dimensional spaces more
      effectively. Our research provides a foundational framework for developing such hybrid estimators.
124
125
      Concept Bottleneck Models. Concept Bottleneck Models (Koh et al., 2020) are a class of prediction
126
      models that first predict a set of human interpretable concepts, and subsequently use these concepts
127   to predict a downstream label. Variations of these models include learning soft probabilistic concepts
128   (Mahinpei et al., 2021), learning hierarchical concepts (Panousis et al., 2023) and learning concepts in
129   a semi-supervised manner (Sawada & Nakamura, 2022). The key advantage of these models is they
130   allow us to explicitly intervene on concepts and interpret what might happen to a downstream label
131   if certain concepts were changed (Marcinkevičs et al., 2024). Unlike previous works, we leverage
132   this idea to introduce a new class of estimators for off-policy evaluation where we group trajectories
133   based on interpretable concepts which are relevant for the downstream evaluation task.

167   4.1   F ORMAL D EFINITION OF THE C ONCEPT
168
169   Given a dataset D = {τ (n) }N   n=1 of n T -step trajectories, let ϕ : S × A × R × S → C ∈ R

170   denote a function that maps trajectory histories ht to interpretable concepts in d-dimensional concept
171   space C. This mapping results in the concept vector ct = [c1t , c2t , ..., cdt ] at time t, defined as ϕ(ht ).
172

      short-term rewards, influential states, interdependencies in actions across timesteps, etc. Without
173
      loss of generality, in this work, we consider concepts ct to be just functions of current state st .
174

175   criticalness of the state. The concept function ϕ satisfy the following desiderata: explainability,
176   conciseness, better trajectory coverage and diversity. A detailed description of desiderata is provided

      5.3   R ESULTS AND D ISCUSSION
292   Known concept-based estimators demonstrate reduced variance, improved ESS, and lower
293   MSE compared to traditional estimators, although they come with slightly higher bias.
294
      Figure 2 compares known-concept and traditional OPE estimators. We observe a consistent reduction
295   in variance and an increase in ESS across all sample sizes for the concept-based estimators. Although
296   our theoretical analysis suggests that known-concept estimators are unbiased, practical results indicate
297   some bias. While unbiased estimates are generally preferred, they can lead to higher errors when
298   the behavior policy does not cover all states. This issue is especially pronounced in limited data
299   settings, which are common in medical applications. Despite this bias-variance trade-off, the MSE
300   for concept-based OPE estimators shows a 1-2 order of magnitude improvement over traditional
301   estimators due to significant variance reduction. In the real-world MIMIC example, concept-based
302   estimators exhibit a variance reduction of one order of magnitude compared to traditional OPE
303
      estimators. This demonstrates that categorizing diverse states—such as varying gridworld positions or
      patient vital signs—into shared concepts based on common attributes improves OPE characterization.

308   While domain knowledge and predefined concepts can enhance OPE, real-world complexities and
309   limited human expertise often make these concepts suboptimal, inaccurate, or unknown, with few
310   interpretable features available. Here, we address cases where concepts are unknown and must be
311   estimated. We use a parametric representation of concepts via CBMs, which initially may not meet
312
      the required desiderata. This section introduces a methodology to optimize parameterized concepts
      to meet these desiderata, alongside improving OPE metrics like variance.

351   more deviation in the concept policies π˜c and original policies π. In line 11, we evaluate the OPE
352   estimator’s returns based on the concepts at the current iteration with metrics like variance. The
353   aggregate loss, Loss(θ), guides gradient descent on CBM parameters θ. Finally, the OPE estimator is
354   applied to TOPE using learned concepts, yielding concept-based OPE returns. Integrating multiple
355
      competing loss components makes this problem complex, and, to our knowledge, this is the first
      approach that incorporates the OPE metric directly into the loss function.

      6.4   R ESULTS AND D ISCUSSION
410   Figure 3 captures the OPE results from our unknown concepts, which we discuss further.
411
      Optimized concepts using Algorithm 1 yield improvements across all metrics except bias
412
      compared to traditional OPE estimators. Significant improvements in variance, MSE, and ESS
413
      are observed for the WindyGridworld and MIMIC datasets, with gains of 1-2 and 2-3 orders of
414   magnitude, respectively. This improvement is due to our algorithm’s ability to identify concepts
415   that satisfy the desiderata, including achieving variance reduction as specified in line 12 of the
416   algorithm. However, like known concepts, optimized concepts show a higher bias than traditional
417   estimators. This is because, unlike variance, bias cannot be optimized in the loss function without the
418   true on-policy estimate, which is typically unavailable in real-world settings. As a result, external
419   information may be essential for further bias reduction.
420   Optimized concepts yield improvements across all metrics besides bias over
421   known concept estimators. Our methodology achieves 1-2 orders of magnitude improve-
422   ment in variance, MSE, and ESS compared to known concepts. This suggests that our algorithm
423   can learn concepts that surpass human-defined ones in improving OPE metrics. This is particularly
424   valuable in cases with imperfect experts or highly complex real-world scenarios where perfect
425   expertise is unfeasible. However, these optimized concepts introduce higher bias, primarily because
426   the training algorithm prioritized variance reduction over bias minimization. This bias could be
427   reduced by incorporating variance regularization into the training process.
428   Optimized concepts are interpretable, show conciseness and diversity. We list the optimized
429   concepts in Appendix I. These concepts exhibit sparse weights, enhancing their conciseness, with
430   significant variation in weights across different dimensions of the concepts, reflecting diversity.
431   This work focuses on linearly varying concepts, but more complex concepts, such as symbolic
      representations Majumdar et al. (2023), could better model intricate environments.

      7     I NTERVENTIONS ON C ONCEPTS FOR I NSIGHTS ON E VALUATION
433
434
      Concepts provide interpretations, allowing practitioners to identify sources of variance—an advantage
435
      over traditional state abstractions like Pavse & Hanna (2022a). Concepts also clarify reasons behind
436   OPE characteristics, such as high variance, enabling corrective interventions based on domain
437   knowledge or human evaluation. We outline the details of performing interventions next.
438
439
      7.1 M ETHODOLOGY
440   Given trajectory history ht and concept ct , we define cint
                                                                t   as the intervention (alternative) concept an
441   expert proposes at time t. We define human criteria hc : (ht , ct ) → {0, 1} as a function constructed
442
      from domain expertise that takes in (ht , ct ) as input and outputs a boolean value. This human criteria
      function determines whether an intervention needs to be conducted over the current concept ct . As
443
      an example, if a practitioner has access to true on-policy values, he/she can estimate which concepts
444
      suffer from bias. If a concept doesn’t suffer from bias, the human criteria hc (ht , ct ) = 1 is satisfied
445   and the concept is not intervened upon, else hc (ht , ct ) = 0 and the intervened concept cint         t is used
446   instead. The final concept c̃t is then defined as: c̃t = hc (ht , ct ) · ct + (1 − hc (ht , ct )) · cint
                                                                                                           t .
447
448
      The human criteria hc for our experiments is described as follows. In Windygridworld, we assume
      access to oracle concepts, listed in Appendix G. When the learned concept ct matches the true
449
      concept, hc (ht , ct ) = 1, otherwise 0. In MIMIC, interventions are based on a patient’s urine output
450
      at a specific timestep. We observe in the next subsection that patients with low urine output generally
451   have higher variance, making urine output the human criteria. Thus, hc (ht , ct ) = 1 when urine
452   output > 30 ml/hr, and 0 otherwise. In this work, we consider 3 possible intervention strategies, 2
453   being based on state representations and the last being qualitative concept interventions based on
454   domain knowledge.
455
      Intervening with the state representation and policies. We intervene on the concept with the state and

458   concept and the state representations.

527   8    C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529   We introduced a new family of concept-based OPE estimators, demonstrating that known-concept
530   estimators can outperform traditional ones with greater accuracy and theoretical guarantees. For
531
      unknown concepts, we proposed an algorithm to learn interpretable concepts that improve OPE
      evaluations by identifying performance issues and enabling targeted interventions to reduce variance.
532
      These advancements benefit safety-critical fields like healthcare, education, and public policy by
533
      supporting reliable, interpretable policy evaluations. By reducing variance and providing policy
534   insights, this approach enhances informed decision-making, facilitates personalized interventions,
535   and refines policies before deployment for greater real-world effectiveness. A limitation of our work
536   is trajectory distribution mismatch when learning unknown concepts, particularly in low-sample
537   settings, which can lead to high-variance OPE. Targeted interventions help mitigate this issue. We
538   also did not address hidden confounding variables or potential CBM concept leakage, focusing
539   instead on evaluation. Future work will address these challenges and extend our approach to more
      general, partially observable environments.

728   Explainability: Explainability ensures that the concept function ϕ is composed of human-
729   interpretable functions f1 , f2 , . . . , fn . Each interpretable function fi depends on the current state,
730
      past actions, rewards, and states, i.e., st , a0:t−1 , r0:t−1 , s0:t−1 . Mathematically:
731   ct = ϕ(st , a0:t−1 , r0:t−1 , s0:t−1 ) = ψ(f1 (st , a0:t−1 , r0:t−1 , s0:t−1 ), . . . , fn (st , a0:t−1 , r0:t−1 , s0:t−1 ))
732                                                                                                                         (1)
733   Here, ψ maps the human-interpretable functions fi to the concept ct , and both ϕ and ψ share the
734   same co-domain space C. In essence, ϕ can be defined using a single interpretable function or a
735   combination of multiple interpretable functions.
736
      As a running example in this paper (applicable across domains), the concept function ϕ(st ) for
737   diagnosing hypertension can be expressed using human-interpretable features:
738
739            ct = ϕ(st )
740               = ϕ(SBP, DBP, HR, Glucose levels, GCS, Age, Weight)
741               = ψ(f1 (SBP), f2 (DBP), f3 (HR), f4 (Glucose levels), f5 (GCS), f6 (Age, Weight))
742
743   Where:
744
745           • f1 (SBP) maps Systolic Blood Pressure to a category (e.g., Low, Normal, High).
746           • f2 (DBP) maps Diastolic Blood Pressure to a category (e.g., Low, Normal, High).
747
748           • f3 (HR) maps Heart Rate to a category (e.g., Low, Normal, High).
749           • f4 (Glucose levels) maps blood glucose levels to a category (e.g., Low, Normal, High).
750
751
              • f5 (GCS) maps GCS scores to a category.
752           • f6 (Age, Weight) maps age and weight to Body Mass Index (BMI).
753
754   This ensures that the concept ϕ(st ) for diagnosing hypertension is built from human-interpretable
755   features, making the diagnostic process explainable. Each function fi translates raw medical data
      into intuitive categories that are meaningful to medical practitioners.

      Conciseness: Conciseness ensures that the concept function ϕ represents the minimal mapping of
757   interpretable functions f1 , f2 , . . . , fn to the concept ct . If multiple mappings ψ1 , ψ2 , . . . , ψm satisfy
758   ϕ, we choose the mapping ψ that provides the simplest composition of fi to describe ct .
759
760
      E.g. Obesity can be represented by different combinations of human-interpretable functions. We
      select the least complex representation that remains interpretable. The two possible representations
761

763                                  ct = ψ1 (f1 (height), f2 (weight), f3 (SBP), f4 (DBP))
764                                  ct = ψ2 (f5 (BMI), f3 (SBP))

766   Since BMI encapsulates both height and weight, and either SBP or DBP accurately summarizes blood
767   pressure pertinent to Obesity, the concept ct = ψ2 (f5 (BMI), f3 (SBP)) is more concise.

794
795
      Concepts capturing subgroups with short-term benefits. If ϕ maps state st and action at to immediate
796
      reward rt , the resulting concepts can identify subgroups with similar short-term benefits, facilitating
797   more personalized OPE, as seen in Keramati et al. (2021a). Unlike Keramati et al. (2021b), we do
798   not limit ϕ to a regression tree.
799
800
      Concepts capturing high-variance transitions. If ϕ highlights changes in state st and action at that
      cause significant shifts in value estimates, it can capture influential transitions or dynamics from
801
      historical data, similar to Gottesman et al. (2020).
802
803   Concepts capturing least influential states. If ϕ identifies the least (or most) influential states st , it
804   can help focus more on critical states, reducing variance by only applying IS ratios to those states
805   Bossens & Thomas (2024).
806   Concepts capturing state-density information. If ϕ extracts information from histories to predict
807   state-action visitation counts, concept-based OPE with ϕ functions similarly to Marginalized OPE
808   estimators, like Xie et al. (2019), which reweight trajectories based on state-visitation distributions.
809   However, density-based concepts may be less interpretable and harder to intervene in the context of
      OPE.

1665   regions with varying wind directions and penalties. The agent’s goal is to navigate from a randomly

1673   indicates northward and westward winds with a strength of 2 units each). This setup encourages the

1713   MIMIC-III We use the publicly available MIMIC-III database (Johnson et al., 2016) from Phys-
1714   ioNet (Goldberger et al., 2000), which records the treatment and progression of ICU patients at the
1715   Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center in Boston, Massachusetts. We focus on the task of managing
1716   acutely hypotensive patients in the ICU. Our preprocessing follows the original MIMIC-III steps
1717   detailed in Komorowski et al. (2018c) and used in subsequent works (Keramati et al., 2021b; Matsson
1718
       & Johansson, 2021). After processing the data in Excel, we group patients by ’ICU-stayID’ to form
       distinct trajectories.
1719
1720   The state space includes 15 features: Creatinine, FiO2 , Lactate, Partial Pressure of Oxygen, Partial
1721   Pressure of CO2 , Urine Output, GCS score, and electrolytes such as Calcium, Chloride, Glucose,
1722   HCO3 , Magnesium, Potassium, Sodium, and SpO2 . Each feature is binned into 10 levels from 0
1723   (very low) to 9 (very high).
1724   Treatments for hypotension include IV fluid bolus administration and vasopressor initiation, with
1725   doses categorized into four levels: "none," "low," "medium," and "high," forming a total action space
1726   of 16 discrete actions. The reward function depends on the next mean arterial pressure (MAP) and
1727   ranges from -1 to 0, linearly distributed between 20 and 65. A MAP above 65 indicates that the
       patient is not experiencing hypotension.

1734   Training and Hyperparameter Details: We use 400 training, 50 validation, and 50 test trajectories
1735   sampled from the behavior policy to train the CBMs, which predict the next state transitions from the
1736   current state. The model architecture includes an input layer, a bottleneck, two 256-neuron layers,
1737   and an output layer, all with ReLU activations. Training is performed using the Adam Optimizer with
1738   a learning rate of 1e-3 on an Nvidia P100 GPU (16 GB) within the Pytorch framework.
1739   Training targets multiple loss components: the OPE metric, interpretability, diversity, and CBM
1740   output. The non-convex nature of the loss landscape can lead to issues such as non-convergence and
1741   NaN values. To address this, we employ a three-stage training strategy.
1742
       In the first stage, we optimize all losses except the OPE metric to stabilize the initial training process.
1743   In the second stage, the OPE metric is gradually incorporated into the optimization until convergence
1744   is achieved. Finally, in the third stage, we freeze the CBM weights to refine the remaining losses
1745   while controlling variations in the OPE metric.
1746
       This strategy balances the learning of critical on-policy features with maintaining relevant OPE
1747
       metrics, thereby enhancing concept learning and policy generalization. Despite these efforts, man-
1748
       aging the complexity of the loss landscape remains a significant challenge, particularly in dynamic
1749   environments, and represents an important direction for future research.

       G.3    A DDITIONAL DESCRIPTION ON POLICES FOR MIMIC-III
1783
1784   For the MIMIC-III dataset, it is common to generate behavior trajectories using K-nearest neighbors
1785   (KNN) as the true on-policy trajectories are unavailable. Examples of works that generate behavior
1786   trajectories or policies using KNNs include (Gottesman et al., 2020; Böck et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
1787   2022; Keramati et al., 2021b; Komorowski et al., 2018b; Peine et al., 2021). In this paper, we employ
1788   a popular variant of KNN, known as approximate nearest neighbors (ANN) search.
1789   The advantages of ANN over traditional KNN include scalability, reduced computational cost,
1790   efficient indexing, and support for dynamic data. These benefits allow us to generate behavior and
1791   evaluation policies with a larger number of neighbors (200 in this study, which is double that used in
1792   prior works employing KNN) while achieving faster inference times. Examples of papers that use
1793   approximate nearest neighbors in medical applications include (Anagnostou et al., 2020; Gupta et al.,
1794   2022). For readers interested in the foundational work outlining the benefits of ANN over KNN, we
1795   refer to the seminal paper (Indyk & Motwani, 1998).
