011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper introduces AC-PKAN, an advanced framework for Physics-Informed
014            Neural Networks (PINNs) that integrates Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs)
015            with Chebyshev Type-I polynomials and incorporates both internal and exter-
016
               nal attention mechanisms. Traditional PINNs based on Multilayer Perceptrons
               (MLPs) encounter challenges when handling complex partial differential equa-
017
               tions (PDEs) due to vanishing gradients, limited interpretability, and computa-
018
               tional inefficiency. To address these issues, we enhance the model from both ex-
019            ternal and internal perspectives. Externally, we propose a novel Residual Gradient
020            Attention (RGA) mechanism that dynamically adjusts loss term weights based on
021            gradient norms and residuals, thereby mitigating gradient stiffness and residual
022            imbalance. Internally, AC-PKAN employs point-wise Chebyshev polynomial-
023            based KANs, wavelet-activated MLPs with learnable parameters, and internal
024            attention mechanisms. These integrated components improve both training effi-
025            ciency and prediction accuracy. We provide mathematical proofs demonstrating
026            that AC-PKAN can theoretically solve any finite-order PDE. Experimental results
027
               from five benchmark tasks across three domains show that AC-PKAN consistently
               outperforms or matches state-of-the-art models such as PINNsFormer, establish-
028
               ing it as a highly effective tool for solving complex real-world engineering prob-
029
               lems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Numerical solutions of partial differential equations (PDEs) are essential in science and engineer-
035
      ing (Zienkiewicz & Taylor, 2005; Liu, 2009; Fornberg, 1998; Brebbia et al., 2012). Physics-
036   informed neural networks (PINNs) (Lagaris et al., 1998; Raissi et al., 2019) have emerged as a
037   promising approach in scientific machine learning. Traditional PINNs typically employ multilayer
038   perceptrons (MLPs) (Cybenko, 1989) due to their ability to approximate nonlinear functions (Hornik
039   et al., 1989) and their success in various PDE-solving applications (Yu et al., 2018; Han et al., 2018).
040
      However, PINNs encounter limitations, including difficulties with multi-scale phenom-
041
      ena (Kharazmi et al., 2021), the curse of dimensionality in high-dimensional spaces (Jagtap &
042   Karniadakis, 2020), and challenges with nonlinear PDEs (Yuan et al., 2022). These issues arise
043   from both the complexity of PDEs and limitations in PINN architectures and training methods.
044
045
      To address these challenges, existing methods focus on improving either the internal architecture of
      PINNs or their external learning strategies. Internal improvements include novel architectures like
046
      Quadratic Residual Networks (Qres) (Bu & Karpatne, 2021), First-Layer Sine (FLS) (Wong et al.,
047
      2022), and PINNsformer (Zhao et al., 2023). External strategies are discussed in detail in Section 2.
048   Nevertheless, traditional PINNs based on MLPs still suffer from issues like lack of interpretabil-
049   ity (Cranmer, 2023), overfitting, vanishing or exploding gradients, and scalability problems (Bach-
050   mann et al., 2024). As an alternative, Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs) (Liu et al., 2024b),
051   inspired by the Kolmogorov–Arnold representation theorem (Kolmogorov, 1961; Braun & Griebel,
052   2009), have been proposed to offer greater accuracy and interpretability. KANs can be viewed as a
053   combination of Kolmogorov networks and MLPs with learnable activation functions (Köppen, 2002;
      Sprecher & Draghici, 2002). Various KAN variants have emerged by replacing the B-spline func-

054
      tions (SS, 2024; Bozorgasl & Chen, 2024; Xu et al., 2024). Although they still face challenges (Yu
055   et al., 2024), KANs have shown promise in addressing issues like interpretability (Liu et al., 2024a)
056   and catastrophic forgetting (Vaca-Rubio et al., 2024) in learning tasks (Samadi et al., 2024). Recent
057   architectures like KINN (Wang et al., 2024b) and DeepOKAN (Abueidda et al., 2024) have applied
058   KANs to PDE solving with promising results.
059
      Despite the potential of KANs, the original KAN suffers from high memory consumption and long
060
      training times due to the use of B-spline functions (Shukla et al., 2024). To address these limi-
061   tations, we propose the Attention-Enhanced and Chebyshev Polynomial-Based Physics-Informed
062   Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (AC-PKAN). Our approach replaces B-spline functions with first-
063   kind Chebyshev polynomials, forming the Cheby1KAN layer (SynodicMonth, 2024), eliminating
064   the need for grid storage and updates. By integrating Cheby1KAN with linear layers and incorpo-
065   rating internal attention mechanisms based on input features, AC-PKAN efficiently models complex
066   nonlinear functions and focuses on different aspects of input features at each layer. Additionally, we
067   introduce an external attention mechanism that adjusts loss weights dynamically based on gradi-
068   ent information and point-wise residuals, alleviating issues such as residual imbalance and gradient
069
      flow stiffness, and enhancing training stability and efficiency. To our knowledge, AC-PKAN is
      the first PINN framework to integrate internal and external attention mechanisms into KAN lay-
070
      ers, effectively addressing many issues of original KANs and PINNs. Our key contributions can be
071
      summarized as follows:
072
073          • Novel Loss Weighting Mechanism: We propose the Residual Gradient Attention (RGA)
074            mechanism, which dynamically adjusts loss term weights based on gradient norms and
075            residual magnitudes, alleviating gradient stiffness and residual imbalance, thereby enhanc-
076            ing convergence and predictive performance.
077
             • Innovative Framework: We develop AC-PKAN by integrating Cheby1KAN layers with
078
               attention-enhanced MLPs and a new learnable activation function, Wavelet. We prove that
079            our framework can approximate and solve arbitrary finite-order PDEs, significantly im-
080            proving performance on complex PDE problems and offering flexible activation function
081            learning, improved parameter efficiency, and superior generalization compared to tradi-
082            tional PINNs.
083
             • Extensive Experiments: We evaluate AC-PKAN on three categories of five benchmark
084            tasks involving 12 models, demonstrating that it achieves best or near-best performance.
085            We also analyze loss landscapes and conduct ablation studies to show the impact of our
086            proposed modules, addressing a research gap in KAN research.

090
091   External Learning Strategies for PINNs. Various external strategies have been proposed to ad-
092   dress the limitations of PINNs. Loss weighting methods, such as PINN-LRA (Wang et al., 2021),
093
      PINN-NTK (Wang et al., 2022), and PINN-RBA (Anagnostopoulos et al., 2024), rebalance loss
      terms using gradient norms, neural tangent kernels, and residual information to enhance training ef-
094
      ficiency. Optimizer improvements like MultiAdam (Yao et al., 2023) aid convergence during multi-
095
      scale training. Advanced sampling strategies, including AAS (Tang et al., 2023), which combines
096   optimal transport theory with adversarial methods, RoPINN (Pan et al., 2024), which utilizes Monte
097   Carlo sampling for regional optimization, RAR (Wu et al., 2023), which applies residual-driven
098   resampling, and PINNACLE (Lau et al., 2024), which adaptively co-optimizes the selection of all
099   types of training points, have been developed to improve performance. Enhanced loss functions
100   like gPINN (Yu et al., 2022) and vPINN (Kharazmi et al., 2019) incorporate gradient enhancement
101   and variational forms, respectively. Adaptive activation functions in LAAF (Jagtap et al., 2020a)
102   and GAAF (Jagtap et al., 2020b) accelerate convergence and handle complex geometries. Domain
103   decomposition methods such as FBPINN (Moseley et al., 2023) and hp-VPINN (Kharazmi et al.,
104
      2021) train subnetworks on subdomains and use higher-order polynomial projections for refinement.
105   Variants of KAN. Since the introduction of KAN (Liu et al., 2024b), various variants have been
106   developed to enhance performance and computational efficiency by modifying the basis functions.
107   FastKAN (Li, 2024) replaces third-order B-spline bases with radial basis functions (RBFs) for accel-
      erated computation. Chebyshev1KAN (SynodicMonth, 2024) and Chebyshev2KAN (SS, 2024) use


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      first and second kinds of Chebyshev polynomials, leveraging their advantageous properties. Rational
109   KAN (rKAN) (Afzalaghaei, 2024b) and Fractional KAN (fKAN) (Afzalaghaei, 2024a) incorporate
110   trainable adaptive rational-orthogonal and fractional-orthogonal Jacobi functions, enhancing adapt-
111   ability and approximation capabilities. FourierKAN (GistNoesis, 2024) replaces spline coefficients
112   with one-dimensional Fourier coefficients, serving as a substitute for linear layers and non-linear
113   activation functions. Preliminary comparisons (Jerry-Master, 2024) indicate that Cheby1KAN cur-
114   rently offers the best efficiency and performance.

      Approximation Ability Our AC-PKAN’s inherent attention mechanism eliminates the need for
349
      an additional bias function b(x) required in previous KAN models to maintain non-zero higher-
350   order derivatives (Wang et al., 2024b). This reduces model complexity and parameter count while
351   preserving the ability to seamlessly approximate PDEs of arbitrary finite order. By ensuring non-
352   zero derivatives of any finite order and invoking the Kolmogorov–Arnold representation theorem,
353   our model can approximate such PDEs.
354
355   Proposition 1. Let N be an AC-PKAN model with L layers (L ≥ 2) and infinite width. Then, the
356   output y = N (x) has non-zero derivatives of any finite-order with respect to the input x.
357
358   Proof sketch: The attention mechanism and sinusoidal activations in AC-PKAN ensure that the
359   output function has non-zero derivatives of all orders, enabling the approximation of high-order
360   PDEs without additional bias functions; the full proof is provided in Appendix A.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
379
380
      Goal. Our empirical evaluations aim to demonstrate three key advantages of the AC-PKAN model:
381
      (1) its intrinsic structure exhibits powerful symbolic representation and function approximation ca-
382   pabilities even without the RGA loss weighting mechanism; (2) it significantly improves general-
383   ization abilities and mitigates failure modes compared to PINNs and other KAN variants; and (3) it
384   achieves superior performance in complex real-world engineering environments. To validate these
385   claims, we designed three categories of experimental tasks across five experiments, comparing 12
386   models including PINN, PINNsFormer, KAN, and fKAN baselines. The experimental setup was in-
387   spired by methodologies in (SynodicMonth, 2024; Hao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b; Zhao et al.,
388   2023; Wang et al., 2023). The code for our model and experiments will be made publicly available

      B    E XPLANATION FOR THE S UPERIORITY OF C HEBYSHEV T YPE I
850        P OLYNOMIALS OVER B-S PLINES
851
852   Chebyshev polynomials of the first kind, defined by Tn (x) = cos(n arccos(x)), concentrate their
853   spectrum on high frequencies, with the frequency increasing linearly with the polynomial order n.
854
      This property makes them particularly suitable for capturing high-frequency oscillations, as their
      high-frequency components decay slowly. The even distribution of extrema further aids in capturing
855
      rapid variations, which is beneficial for representing high-frequency features. In contrast, B-splines,
856
      being piecewise polynomials, exhibit a rapidly decaying spectrum, limiting their ability to capture
857   high-frequency features effectively.
858
859   Chebyshev polynomials possess both global support and global orthogonality over the interval
860
      [−1, 1]. The value of a Chebyshev polynomial at any point depends on all points within the in-
      terval, making them highly effective at capturing global features and high-frequency components.
861
      They satisfy the orthogonality relation:
862                                Z 1
863                                      Tm (x) Tn (x)
                                           √           dx = 0, for m ̸= n.
                                     −1      1 − x2

                                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      This orthogonality allows Chebyshev polynomials to achieve minimax approximation, minimizing
865   the maximum error over the interval. In contrast, B-splines have local support; each basis function
866   is nonzero only within a specific subinterval. This local nature limits their ability to capture global
867   high-frequency features. Additionally, B-splines lack global orthogonality, reducing their efficiency
868   in approximating functions.
869
      B-spline-based Kernel Adaptive Networks (KANs) require substantial memory due to the stor-
870
      age of grids and coefficient matrices that scale cubically with grid size and spline order. They
871   store grids of size (in features, grid size + 2 × spline order + 1) and coefficient matrices of size
872   (out features, in features, grid size + spline order). Since B-splines are piecewise polynomials,
873   each segment requires maintaining basis function values and performing high-order interpolation
874   within its support interval. This involves generating polynomial bases, solving linear systems (e.g.,
875   using torch.linalg.lstsq), and executing recursive updates, resulting in high computational
876   and storage demands.
877   In contrast, Chebyshev polynomials are globally defined and require only a coefficient matrix of size
878   (input dim, output dim, degree + 1), eliminating terms directly related to grid size and spline order.
879   The memory complexity grows linearly with the degree. Chebyshev polynomials eliminate the need
880   for grid storage and do not require solving linear systems, interpolation, or recursive updates of
881   piecewise basis functions, which significantly reduce computational and storage requirements.

1212   Data Generation: To train and evaluate the PINNs, we generate collocation points within the
1213   defined spatial domains and enforce boundary conditions appropriately.
1214
1215          • Grid Creation: For both PDE problems, a uniform grid is established using 100 equidistant
1216            points in each spatial dimension, resulting in 101 × 101 = 10, 201 internal collocation
1217            points for the heterogeneous Poisson problem and an analogous number for the complex
1218            geometric Poisson problem.
1219          • Boundary Sampling:
1220               – Heterogeneous Poisson Problem: Boundary points are extracted from the edges of
1221                 the square domain Ω = [−1, 1]2 to impose Dirichlet boundary conditions.
1222               – Complex Geometric Poisson Problem: Boundary points are sampled from both the
1223                 outer boundary ∂Ωrec and the boundaries of the excluded circular regions ∂Ri for
1224                 i = 1, 2, 3, 4.
1225          • Tensor Conversion: All collocation and boundary points are converted into PyTorch ten-
1226            sors with floating-point precision and are set to require gradients to facilitate automatic
1227            differentiation. The data resides on an NVIDIA A100 GPU with 40GB of memory to
1228            expedite computational processes.

       The test datasets for both PDE problems mirror the training datasets in terms of spatial discretization,
1230

       Training Regimen: Both PDE problems are trained for a total of 50,000 epochs to allow sufficient
1233
       learning iterations. And the RBA attention mechanism for AC-PKAN is configured with smoothing
1234   factors η = 0.001 and βw = 0.001.
