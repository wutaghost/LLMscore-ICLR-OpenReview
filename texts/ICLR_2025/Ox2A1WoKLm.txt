011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models have achieved remarkable success in generative tasks across
014            various domains. However, the increasing demand for content moderation and
015            the removal of specific concepts from these models has introduced the challenge
016
               of unlearning. In this work, we present a suite of robust methodologies that sig-
               nificantly enhance the unlearning process by employing advanced loss functions
017
               within knowledge distillation frameworks. Specifically, we utilize the Cramer-
018
               Wold distance and Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence to facilitate more efficient and
019            versatile concept removal. Although current non-learning techniques are effec-
020            tive in certain scenarios, they are typically limited to specific categories such
021            as identity, nudity, or artistic style. In contrast, our proposed methods demon-
022            strate robust versatility, seamlessly adapting to and performing effectively across
023            a wide range of concept erasure categories. Our approach outperforms existing
024            techniques, achieving consistent results across different unlearning categories and
025            showcasing its broad applicability. Through extensive experiments, we show that
026            our method not only surpasses previous benchmarks but also addresses key limi-
027
               tations of current unlearning techniques, paving the way for more responsible use
               of text-to-image diffusion models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Diffusion models Ho et al. (2020); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Kawar et al. (2022); Gu et al. (2023)
      have advanced text-to-image generation, enabling the creation of high-quality visuals from diverse
034
      prompts. However, the extensive datasets used to train these models, often sourced indiscriminately
035
      from the Internet, pose significant ethical and safety challenges. It also presents a serious risk of
036   misuse. Their ability to generate realistic images can be exploited to produce misleading or harmful
037   content, such as deepfakes, disinformation, or unauthorized reproductions of copyrighted material.
038   These concerns underscore the need for effective safeguards, including methods that can selectively
039   unlearn or restrict certain concepts to prevent the misuse of these models.
040
      Recently, there has been increasing interest in developing techniques to unlearn or erase specific
041
      concepts from diffusion models. In this direction, progress has been made in unlearning nudity,
042   artistic, insignia, copyrighted images, and identity styles Kumari et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024a);
043   Heng & Soh (2024); Gandikota et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2023); Golatkar et al. (2024); Lu et al.
044   (2024). Although concept erasure is the primary objective of these methods, achieving consistent
045   effectiveness in various unlearning scenarios remains a significant challenge. Identities of well-
046   known figures, such as politicians or celebrities, are intricately woven into the latent space of the
047   model, making them difficult to remove. Unlike simpler concepts such as nudity, which can often be
048   managed by filtering outputs, identities are deeply embedded in the model’s knowledge, requiring
049   more sophisticated techniques for effective erasure.
050   Motivation. A widely used unlearning approach in diffusion models involves fine-tuning the origi-
051   nal model by conditioning the noise estimate on the target concept to be removed, guiding it to align
052   with the unconditional estimate Kumari et al. (2023); Gandikota et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2023).
053   All the existing methods use the L2 loss function during fine-tuning. The choice of loss function
      is critical for guiding the model’s ability to selectively remove undesirable concepts, such as nudity

054
      or artistic styles, while preserving image quality in retain set data. We empirically observe that L2
055   loss is limited in handling complex, multi-modal distributions within the model’s latent space. This
056   motivated us to explore other loss functions such as Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence and Cramer-
057   Wold distance. Both provide a nuanced way to align the model’s output distribution with the target
058   distribution, allowing for more effective unlearning without catastrophic forgetting.
059
      Our Contributions. In this work, we empirically demonstrate that employing L2 loss as the default
060
      loss function in the unlearning (fine-tuning) setup results in suboptimal performance in current un-
061   learning methods. We study the impact of employing advanced loss functions such as JS divergence
062   and Cramer-Wold distance within a knowledge distillation framework. We hypothise that L2 loss
063   provides a simple pointwise correction, whereas, JS divergence and Cramer-Wold distance enable
064   a more robust alignment of distributions within the latent space of diffusion models. These loss
065   functions are better at handling complex, multi-modal latent spaces. This allows for the removal of
066   targeted concepts while retaining the model’s generative capabilities. By leveraging these loss func-
067   tions, we ensure that unlearning is adaptable across different scenarios and minimizes interference
068   with non-targeted concepts, resulting in a more versatile and reliable unlearning process.
069   We demonstrate how JS divergence and Cramer-Wold distance can be effectively integrated into
070   the unlearning process to guide the removal of targeted concepts while preserving image quality
071   in the remaining dataset. We provide comprehensive experiments showing that our approach not
072   only outperforms existing L2-based methods in concept removal but also mitigates the risk of catas-
073   trophic forgetting, thereby maintaining the model’s versatility across various unlearning scenarios.
074   By introducing these loss functions in the knowledge distillation framework and demonstrating their
075
      effectiveness to erase concepts, we offer a significant step forward in the development of safe and
      reliable unlearning techniques for diffusion models.

      We discuss some of the major challenges in ensuring effective moderation of content generated by
081   text-to-image diffusion models. These challenges include handling NSFW and restricted content,
082   addressing rights and usage concerns. We review how prior research has approached these issues.
083
084
      NSFW and Restricted Content. Diffusion models can be misused to generate inappropriate im-
      ages, including violent or explicit content. Some methods attempt to mitigate this by filtering train-
085
      ing data or employing post-processing safety checks during inference Gandhi et al. (2020); Nichol
086
      et al. (2022); Bedapudi; Rando et al.; Schramowski et al. (2023). While effective, these methods can
087   be bypassed when open-source code and model weights are publicly accessible Sharma et al. (2024).
088   A more difficult-to-circumvent approaches involve altering the model’s knowledge by modifying at-
089   tention weights Gandikota et al. (2023), through fine-tuning Zhang et al. (2024b), or with continual
090   learning Heng & Soh (2024). However, they have been shown to be prone to partial diffusion based
091   attacks in Sharma et al. (2024). Our method proposes a more effective solution to erase unwanted
092   concepts that also performs well in the recently introduced unlearning metrics Sharma et al. (2024).
093   Rights and Usage Concerns. Stable diffusion models trained on extensive datasets such as LAION-
094   5B Schuhmann et al. (2022) have been found to infringe on copyrights related to artistic styles,
095   largely because of their tendency to memorize copyrighted material in the training data Zhang et al.
096   (2024c). Under U.S. copyright law 1 , the concept of Fair Use allows others to use copyrighted ma-
097   terial only if it is transformed in a way that differs significantly from the original. Legal precedents
098   have shown that structural similarity to the original work can result in infringement claims.
099    Somepalli et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024) examined how diffusion models can infringe on copy-
100   rights through memorization, enabling the generation of existing posters, artworks, and other images
101   that may be protected by copyright. To address this, some studies have suggested adding minor per-
102   turbations, acting as watermarks, into images to prevent their memorization by diffusion models Cui
103   et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2023). However, these watermarks can be readily eliminated using tech-
104   niques like denoising or blurring. Another approach involves post-training model adjustments to
105   delete undesired concepts, such as modifying model weights to remove specific styles Gandikota
106   et al. (2023); Heng & Soh (2024); Kim et al. (2023); Kumari et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024b).

      Unlearning in Diffusion Models. Unlearning in diffusion models presents a challenge due to the in-
109   tricate and interconnected nature of their latent space representations. Concepts within these models
110   are not independent but are woven into a larger knowledge distribution. Consequently, the removal
111   of specific elements, like copyrighted logos or identifiable human faces, without compromising the
112   model’s ability to produce high-quality images is difficult. Current leading methods in the field in-
113   volve fine-tuning the diffusion model by adjusting attention heads or using distillation techniques
114   that condition the noise estimate on the concept to be removed Gandikota et al. (2023); Heng &
115   Soh (2024); Kim et al. (2023); Kumari et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024b). However, recent findings
116   by Sharma et al. (2024) indicate that many of these approaches Kumari et al. (2023); Gandikota et al.
117
      (2023) do not completely erase the concepts, as they can still be generated using partial diffusion
      probing.

168   4.1   L IMITATIONS OF L2 DISTANCE IN U NLEARNING
169
170   We identify two main limitations of using L2 distance in unlearning. First, we demonstrate that L2
171   distance is highly sensitive to irrelevant dimensions. To this end we formally show:
172   Lemma 1. (L2 Distance is Sensitive to Irrelevant Dimensions)
173   Let zT , zS ∈ Rn be two n-dimensional vectors representing the teacher and student embed-
174   dings respectively, and let the concept c to be unlearned be represented in a subset of dimensions
175   Dc ⊆ {1, 2, . . . , n}. The L2 distance between zT and zS is non-zero even when the concept is fully
176   unlearned, as long as zT and zS differ in irrelevant dimensions, Dnc = {1, 2, . . . , n} \ Dc .
177
178   The proof for Lemma 1 shows that the L2 distance between vectors mathbf zT and mathbf zS can
179   be decomposed into concept-related and non-concept-related parts. If the concept is fully unlearned,
180   the contribution from concept-related components becomes zero. However, if differences exist in
181
      non-concept-related components, the overall L2 distance remains positive, indicating that the L2
      distance is not zero even when the concept is unlearned. The detailed proof is added in the Appendix.
182
183   Next we show that L2 distance have difficulty capturing correlated conceptual changes, which is
184   crucial property for any unlearned system.
185
      Lemma 2. (L2 Distance Fails to Capture Correlated Conceptual Changes)
186   Let zT , zS ∈ Rn be two n-dimensional vectors. Suppose the concept c is represented as a lin-
187   ear combination of multiple correlated dimensions in zT . If the unlearning process changes these
188   dimensions uniformly in zS , then the L2 distance will overestimate or underestimate the actual con-
189   ceptual difference.
190
191   The Lemma 2 argues that if a conceptc is represented by multiple correlated dimensions in zT , and
192   these dimensions are uniformly altered during the unlearning process in zS , the L2 distance will
193   not accurately reflect the true conceptual change. This is because L2 distance treats each dimension
194   independently, failing to capture the correlation between dimensions. As a result, the L2 distance
195   may either overestimate or underestimate the difference depending on the nature and scale of the
196   uniform changes, the detailed proof can be found in appendix.
197
198
      4.2   Q UANTIFYING C ONCEPTUAL D IFFERENCES WITH JS DIVERGENCE
199
200   We show JS divergence is well equipped to quantify conceptual differences.
201
202   Lemma 3. (Jensen-Shannon Divergence Accurately Quantifies Conceptual Differences)
203   Let zT and zS be n-dimensional embeddings represented as probability distributions P and Q
204
      over the same n-dimensional space. Suppose the concept c is encoded in a subset of dimensions
      Dc ⊆ {1, 2, . . . , n}. If P and Q differ only in the concept dimensions Dc and are identical in the
205
      remaining dimensions Dnc = {1, 2, . . . , n}\Dc , the Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence will accurately
206
      capture the concept difference while remaining invariant to changes in Dnc .
207
208
      The proof for Lemma 3 demonstrates that the Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence is a better measure
209
      for concept unlearning compared to L2 distance. It starts by defining the JS divergence between two
210   probability distributions P and Q, and decomposes these distributions into concept-related and non-
211   concept-related components. By showing that if the non-concept-related parts of P and Q are equal,
212   the JS divergence becomes zero for these dimensions, it highlights that JS divergence is invariant
213   to changes in irrelevant dimensions. In contrast, L2 distance would still capture these differences,
214   making it sensitive to irrelevant variations. Therefore, JS divergence accurately captures conceptual
215   changes, while L2 distance may overestimate the differences. The detailed proof is provided in the
      Appendix.

      4.3     C APTURING H IGH -O RDER C ONCEPTUAL C ORRELATIONS WITH C RAM ÉR -W OLD
240           D ISTANCE
241
242   Cramér-Wold distance has ability to capture higher-order correlations and joint distributional
243   changes between dimensions, which can be formally represented as follows:
244
      Lemma 4. (Cramér-Wold Distance Captures High-Order Conceptual Correlations)
245
      Let zT , zS ∈ Rn be two vectors, and let P, Q be the corresponding distributions over these vectors.
246   If the concept c is represented by correlations between multiple dimensions, then the Cramér-Wold
247   distance between the distributions of zT and zS will be zero if and only if c has been fully unlearned,
248   even if the individual L2 distances in some dimensions are non-zero.
249
250   The proof for Lemma 4 establishes that Cramér-Wold (CW) distance is a more suitable measure for
251   detecting conceptual changes compared to L2 distance. It begins by defining the CW distance as the
252   supremum over 1-dimensional linear projections of the distributions P and Q. It then shows that
253   CW distance captures correlations by analyzing projections aligned with the concept subspace. If
254   a concept is represented by correlated dimensions, changes in these dimensions will be detected by
255
      CW distance but may be missed by L2 distance, which only considers dimension-wise differences.
      Finally, the proof highlights that CW distance is invariant to changes in irrelevant projections, mak-
256
      ing it robust in measuring concept changes across different subspaces. The detailed proof is provided
257
      in the Appendix.
