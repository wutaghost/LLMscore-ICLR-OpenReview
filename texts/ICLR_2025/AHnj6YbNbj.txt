009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Flow matching models have emerged as a powerful method for generative modeling
012            on domains like images or videos, and even on unstructured data like 3D point
013            clouds. These models are commonly trained in two stages: first, a data compressor
014            (i.e. a variational auto-encoder) is trained, and in a subsequent training stage a flow
015            matching generative model is trained in the low-dimensional latent space of the
016            data compressor. This two stage paradigm adds complexity to the overall training
017            recipe and sets obstacles for unifying models across data domains, as specific
018
               data compressors are used for different data modalities. To this end, we introduce
               Ambient Space Flow Transformers (ASFT), a domain-agnostic approach to
019
               learn flow matching transformers in ambient space, sidestepping the requirement
020
               of training compressors and simplifying the training process. We introduce a
021            conditionally independent point-wise training objective that enables ASFT to make
022            predictions continuously in coordinate space. Our empirical results demonstrate
023            that using general purpose transformer blocks, ASFT effectively handles different
024            data modalities such as images and 3D point clouds, achieving strong performance
025            in both domains and outperforming comparable approaches. ASFT is a promising
026            step towards domain-agnostic flow matching generative models that can be trivially
027            adopted in different data domains.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recent advances in generative modeling have enabled learning complex data distributions by combin-
033
      ing both powerful architectures and training objectives. In particular, state-of-the-art approaches for
      image (Esser et al., 2024), video (Dai et al., 2023) or 3D point cloud (Vahdat et al., 2022) generation
034
      are based on the concept of iteratively transforming data into Gaussian noise. Diffusion models
035
      were originally proposed following this idea and pushing the quality of generated samples in many
036   different domains, including images (Dai et al., 2023; Rombach et al., 2022), 3D point clouds (Luo
037   & Hu, 2021), graphs (Hoogeboom et al., 2022) and video (Ho et al., 2022a). More recently, flow
038   matching (Lipman et al., 2023) and stochastic interpolants (Ma et al., 2024) have been proposed as
039   generalized formulations of the noising process, moving from stochastic gaussian diffusion processes
040   to general paths connecting a base (e.g. Gaussian) and a target (e.g. data) distribution.
041
      In practice, these iterative refinement approaches are commonly applied in a low-dimensional latent
042   space obtained from a pre-trained compressor model. Therefore, the training process for these
043   approaches is composed of two independent training stages: in the first stage, a compressor (e.g.
044   VAE (Vahdat et al., 2022), VQVAE (Ramesh et al., 2022), VQGAN (Rombach et al., 2022)) model
045   is trained, using architectures that are specific to the data domain (i.e. ConvNets for image data
046   (Rombach et al., 2022), PointNet for point clouds (Vahdat et al., 2022), etc. ) enforcing a bottleneck
047   on the data dimensionality, with the goal of reducing compute cost of training the subsequent stage.
048   In the second stage, general purpose transformer architectures are used for the generative modeling
049   step (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2024), where the distribution of latents is
050
      learnt. This type of generative modeling in latent space has become popular in the community due to
      its computational efficiency benefits obtained from compressed data dimensionality.
051
052   However, latent space generative modeling is not without drawbacks. An obvious shortcoming is that
053   latent space generative models cannot benefit from end-to-end optimization, as data compressors and
      the downstream generative models are trained separately. In particular, two stage approaches are more


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      complex to implement than single stage models and involve tuning several hyper-parameters that can
055   have a big impact on final performance (Rombach et al., 2022): spatial reduction ratio, adversarial
056   loss weights or KL terms in VAEs (Rombach et al., 2022) . As an illustrative example, setting a
057   high KL weight makes the problem of learning a distribution of latents trivial, yet results in very
058   poor generation results. A very small KL weight on the other hand allows for great reconstruction
059   performance for the first stage but fails to induce a suitable latent space for generative modeling (e.g.
060   a dirac delta for each training sample in latent space). Our goal in this paper is to provide a single
061   training stage approach that is domain-agnostic and simple to implement in practice, thus dispensing
062   with the complexities of two stage training recipes and enabling modeling of different data modalities
063
      in ambient (i.e. data) space.
064   It is worth noting that training diffusion or flow matching models in ambient space is indeed
065   possible when using domain specific architecture designs and training recipes. In the image domain,
066   approaches have exploited its dense nature and applied cascaded U-Nets Ho et al. (2021; 2022b),
067   joint training of U-Nets at multiple resolutions Gu et al. (2023), multi-scale losses (Hoogeboom et al.,
068   2023) or U-Net transformer hybrids architectures (Crowson et al., 2024), obtaining strong results.
069
      However, developing strong domain-agnostic models, using general purposes architectures that can
      be applied across different data domains remains an important open problem.

125                domains like images while also enabling resolution changes at inference time.

129   2       R ELATED W ORK
130
131   Diffusion models have been the major catalyzer of progress in generative modeling, these approaches
132   learn to reverse a forward process that gradually adds Gaussian noise to corrupt data samples (Ho
133   et al., 2020). Diffusion models are notable for their simple and robust training objective. Extensive
134   research has explored various formulations of the forward and backward processes (Song et al., 2021a;
135   Rissanen et al., 2022; Bansal et al., 2022), particularly in the image domain. In addition, different
136   denoising networks have been proposed for different data domains like images (Nichol & Dhariwal,
137   2021), videos (Ho et al., 2022a), and geometric data (Luo & Hu, 2021). More recently, flow matching
138   (Liu et al., 2023; Lipman et al., 2023) and stochastic interpolants (Ma et al., 2024) have emerged as
139
      flexible formulations that generalized Gaussian diffusion paths, allowing to define different paths
      to connect a base and a target distribution. These types of models have shown incredible results in
140
      the image domain (Ma et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2024) when coupled with transformer architectures
141
      (Vaswani et al., 2017) to model distributions in latent space learnt by data compressors (Peebles &
142   Xie, 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Rombach et al., 2022; Vahdat et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023; Gao et al.,
143   2023). Note that these approaches train two separate stages/models: first training the data compressor
144   (e.g. VAE (Vahdat et al., 2022), VQVAE (Ramesh et al., 2022), VQGAN (Rombach et al., 2022)) and
145   then the generative model, requiring careful hyper-parameter tuning.
146
      In an attempt to unify generative modeling across various data domains, continuous data repre-
147
      sentations1 have shown potential in different approaches: From Data to Functa (Functa) (Dupont
148   et al., 2022a), Generative Manifold Learning (GEM) (Du et al., 2021a), and Generative Adversarial
149   Stochastic Process (GASP) (Dupont et al., 2022b) have studied the problem of generating continuous
150   representations of data. More recently Infinite Diffusion (Bond-Taylor & Willcocks, 2023) and
151   PolyINR (Singh et al., 2023) have shown great results in the image domain by modeling images as
152   continuous functions. However, both of these approaches make strong assumptions about image
153   data. In particular, (Bond-Taylor & Willcocks, 2023) interpolates sparse pixels to an euclidean grid
154   to then process it with a U-Net. On the other hand, (Singh et al., 2023) uses a patching and 2D
155   convolution in the discriminator. Our approach also relates to DPF Zhuang et al. (2023), a diffusion
156
      model that acts on function coordinates and can be applied in different data domains on a grid at low
      resolutions (i.e. 64×64). Our approach is able to deal with higher resolution functions (e.g. 256x256
157
      vs. 64x64 resolution images) on large scale datasets like ImageNet, while also tackling unstructured
158
      data domains that do not live on an Euclidean grid (e.g. like 3D point clouds).

      3.1   DATA AS C OORDINATE → VALUE M APS
165
166   We interpret our empirical data distribution q to be composed of maps f ∼ q(f ). These maps take
167   coordinates x as input to values y as output. For images, maps are defined from 2D pixel coordinates
168   x ∈ R2 to corresponding RGB values y ∈ R3 , thus f : R2 → R3 , where each image is a different
169   map. For 3D point clouds, f can be interpreted as a deformation that maps coordinates from a fixed
170   base configuration in 3D space to a deformation value also in 3D space, f : R3 → R3 , as in the
171   image case, each 3D point cloud corresponds to a different deformation map f . For ease of notation,
172
      we define coordinates x and values y of any given map f as xf and yf , respectively. Fig. 1(a) shows
      an example of such maps in the image domain.
173
174   In practice, analytical forms for these maps f are unknown. In addition, different from previous
175   approaches (Dupont et al., 2022a; Du et al., 2021a), we do not assume that parametric forms of these
176   maps can be obtained, since that would involve a separate training stage fitting an MLP to each map
177   (Dupont et al., 2022a; Bauer et al., 2023; Du et al., 2021a). As a result, we assume we are only given
178
      sets of corresponding coordinate and value pairs resulting from observing these maps at a particular
      sampling rate (e.g. at a particular resolution in the image case). Therefore, we need a to develop an
179
      end-to-end approach that can take these collections of coordinate-value sets as training data.
180

212   3.3   F LOW M ATCHING FOR C OORDINATE -VALUE S ETS
213
214   We now turn to the task of formulating a flow matching training objective for data distributions of
215   maps f . We recall that in practice we do not have access to an analytical or parametric form for these
      maps f , and we are only given sets of corresponding coordinate xf and value yf pairs resulting from


                                                           4

216
      observing the mapping at a particular rate. As a result, we need to formulate a training objective that
217   can take these sets of coordinate-value as training data.
218
219   In order to achieve this, we first observe that the target velocity field ut (ft )dt can be decomposed
220
      across both the domain and co-domain of ft , resulting in a point-wise velocity field ut (xft , yft )dt ,
      defined for corresponding coordinate and value pairs of ft . As an illustrative example in the image
221
      domain, this means that the target velocity field can be independently evaluated for any pixel
222
      coordinate xft with corresponding value yft , so that ut (xft , yft ) ∈ R3 . Note that one can always
223   decompose target velocity fields in this way since the time-dependent forward process in Eq. 1
224   aggregates data and noise independently (e.g. point-wise) across the domain of f . Again, using the
225   image domain as an example, the time-dependent forward process of a pixel at coordinate xf is not
226   dependent on other pixel positions or values.
227
      Our goal now is to formulate a training objective to match this point-wise independent velocity
228
      field. We want our neural network vθ parametrizing the velocity field to be able to independently
229   predict a velocity for any given coordinate and value pair xft and yft . However, this point-wise
230   independent prediction is futile without access to additional contextual conditioning information
231   about the underlying function ft at time t. This is because even if the forward process is point-wise
232   independent, real data exhibits strong dependencies across the domain f that need to be captured by
233   the model. For example, in the image domain, pixels are not independent from each other and natural
234   images show strong both short and long spatial dependencies across pixels. In order to solve this, we
235   introduce a latent variable zft that encodes contextual information from a set of given coordinate and
236   value pairs of ft . This contextual latent variable allows us to formulate the learnt velocity field to be
237
      conditionally independent for coordinate-value pairs given zft . The final point-wise conditionally
      independent CFM loss, which we denote as CICFM loss is defined as:

247   One of the core challenges of learning this type of generative models is obtaining a latent variable
248   zft that effectively captures intricate dependencies across the domain of the function, specially for
249   high resolution stimuli like images. In particular, the architectural design decisions are extremely
250   important to ensure that zft does not become a bottleneck during training. In the following we review
251
      our proposed architecture.

253   3.4   N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
254
      We base our model on the general PerceiverIO design (Jaegle et al., 2022) which provides a flexible
255
      architecture to handle coordinate-value sets of large cardinality (i.e. large number of pixels in an
256   image). Fig. 2 illustrates the architectural pipeline of ASFT. At a high level, our encoder network takes
257   a set of coordinate-value pairs and encodes them to learnable latents through cross-attention. These
258   latents are then updated through several self-attention blocks to provide the final latents zft ∈ RL×D
259   . To decode the velocity field for a given coordinate-value pair we perform cross attention to zft ,
260   generating the final point-wise prediction for the velocity field vθ (xft , yft , t|zft ).
261
      The encoder of a vanilla PerceiverIO relies solely on cross-attention to the latents zft ∈ RL×D to
262
      learn spatial connectivity patterns between input and output elements, which we found to introduce
263
      a strong bottleneck during training. To ameliorate this, we make two key modifications to boost
264   the performance. Firstly, our encoder utilizes spatial aware latents where each latent is assigned a
265   “pseudo” coordinate. Coordinate-value pairs are assigned to different latents based on their distances
266   on coordinate space. During encoding, coordinate-value pairs interact with their assigned latents
267   through cross-attention. In particular, the learnable latent zft cross-attends to input coordinate-value
268   pairs of noisy data at a given timestep t. Latent vectors are spatial-aware, this means that each of the
269   L latents only attends to a set of neighboring coordinate-value pairs. Latent vectors are then updated
      using several self-attention blocks. These changes in the encoder allow the model to effectively utilize

      mentioned otherwise. On image generation, we train models with small (S), base (B), large (L), and

      4.4   R ESOLUTION AGNOSTIC G ENERATION
479
      An interesting property of ASFT is that it decodes each coordinate-value pair independently, allowing
480   resolution to change during inference. At inference the user can define as many coordinate-value pairs
481   as desired where the initial value of each pair at t = 1 is drawn from a Gaussian distribution. We show
482   qualitative results of resolution agnostic generation for both images and point clouds. Fig. 4(a) show
483   images sampled at resolution 512 × 512 (together with their 256 resolution counterparts generated
484   from the same seed) from ASFT trained on ImageNet-256. Even though the model has not been
485   trained with any samples at 512 resolution, it can still generate realistic images with high-frequency
      details. Fig. 4(b) shows point cloud with 100K points from ASFT trained on ShapeNet with only


                                                                   9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2048 points points per sample (we visualize the generated 2048 point could generated from the same
487   seed). Similarly, ASFT generates dense and realistic point cloud in 3D without actually being trained
488   on such high density points. These results show that ASFT is not trivially overfitting to the training
489   set of points but rather learning a continuous density field in 3D space from which an infinite number
490   of points could be sampled. Generally speaking, this also provides the potential to efficiently train
491   flow matching generative models without the need to use large amounts of expensive high resolution
492   data, which can be hard to collect in data domains other than images.

526   5    C ONCLUSION
527
528   We introduced Ambient Space Flow Transformers (ASFT), a flow matching generative model
529   designed to operate directly in ambient space. Our approach dispenses with the practical complexities
530
      of training latent space generative models, such as the dependence on domain-specific compressors
      for different data domains or tuning of hyper-parameters of the data compressor (i.e. adversarial
531
      weight, KL term, etc.). We introduced a conditionally independent point-wise training objective
532
      that decomposes the target vector field and allows to continuously evaluate the generated samples,
533   enabling resolution changes at inference time. This training objective also improves training efficiency
534   since it allows us to sub-sample the target vector field during training. Our results on both image
535   and 3D point cloud benchmarks show the strong performance of ASFT as well as its trivial adaption
536   across modalities. In conclusion, ASFT represents a promising direction for flow matching generative
537   models, offering a powerful and domain-agnostic framework. Future work could explore further
538   improvements in training efficiency and investigate co-training of multiple data domains to enable
539   multi-modality generation in an end-to-end learning paradigm.

      We provide detailed model configurations and training settings of ASFT for image (Tab. 5) and point
      cloud (Tab. 6) generation. For image generation, we develop model sizes small (S), base (B), large (L),
760
      and extra large (XL) to approximately match the number of parameters in previous works (Peebles &
761
      Xie, 2023). Similarly, for point cloud generation, we train a base sized model roughly matching the
762   number of parameters in LION (Vahdat et al., 2022) (i.e. 110M parameters), and a ASFT-L which
763   contains about twice the number of parameters as ASFT-B. For image experiments we implement the
764   “psuedo” coordinate of latents as 2D grids and coordinate-value pairs are assigned to different latents
765   based on their distances to the latent coordinates. Whereas in point cloud generation, since calculating
766   the pair-wise distances in 3D space can be time consuming, we assign input elements to latents
767   through a hash code, so that neighboring input elements are likely (but not certainly) to be assigned
768   to the same latent token. We found that the improvements of spatial aware latents in 3D to not be as
769   substantial as in the 2D image setting, so we report results with a vanilla PerceiverIO architecture
770
      for simplicity. To embed coordinates, we apply standard Fourier positional embedding (Vaswani
      et al., 2017) for ambient space coordinate input in both encoder and decoder. The Fourier positional
771
      embedding is also applied to the “psuedo” coordinate of latents. On image generation, we found that
772
      applying rotary positional embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) slightly improves the performance
773   of ASFT. Therefore, RoPE is employed for largest ASFT-XL model. For all the models including
774   image and 3D point cloud experiments, we share the following training parameters except the
775   training_steps across different experiments. On image generation, all models are trained with
776   batch size 256, except for ASFT-XL reported in Tab. 2 and Tab. 3, which are trained for 1.7M steps
777   with batch size 512. On ShapeNet, ASFT models are trained for 800K iterations with a batch size of
778   16.
