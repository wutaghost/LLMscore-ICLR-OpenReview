009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The conditional generation of proteins with desired functions and/or properties
012            is a key goal for generative models. Existing methods based on prompting of
013            language models can generate proteins conditioned on a target functionality, such
014            as a desired enzyme family. However, these methods are limited to simple, tok-
015            enized conditioning and have not been shown to generalize to unseen functions.
016            In this study, we propose ProCALM (Protein Conditionally Adapted Language
017            Model), an approach for the conditional generation of proteins using adapters to
018
               protein language models (PLMs). While previous methods have used adapters for
               structure-conditioned generation from PLMs, our implementation of ProCALM
019
               involves finetuning ProGen2 to condition generation on protein function, and it
020
               is flexible to function representations of enzyme family, taxonomy, or natural
021            language descriptions. ProCALM matches or exceeds the performance of existing
022            methods at conditional sequence generation from target functions. Impressively, it
023            can also generalize to rare and unseen functions. Overall, ProCALM is a flexible
024            and computationally efficient approach, and we expect that it can be extended to a
025            wide range of generative language models.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Proteins, sequences of amino acids, are important molecules in all living organisms and have many
031   industrial applications. Protein sequences can be modified or designed to have desired function(s)
032   or optimized properties so that they are more useful for applications ranging from greener chemical
033
      synthesis to gene-editing for disease treatment (Buller et al., 2023). Enzymes are a particularly
      useful subclass of proteins: these are ubiquitous proteins that catalyze chemical reactions and are
034
      particularly difficult to engineer, as enzymatic activities are complex and often difficult to predict
035
      from sequence (Arnold, 2018).
036
037   While directed evolution and other methods have been used to engineer proteins (Wang et al., 2021),
038   in recent years, generative models based on machine learning (ML) have emerged to tackle protein
039
      design (Ruffolo & Madani, 2024; Wu et al., 2021; Xie & Warshel, 2023; Barghout et al., 2023; Ferruz
      et al., 2023). The use of generative models is motivated by learning the distribution of known proteins,
040
      thus allowing one to sample functional proteins during inference. Diffusion models can generate
041
      protein structures that have certain symmetries, binding properties, or active site conformations
042   (Watson et al., 2023; Ingraham et al., 2023; Huguet et al., 2024; Lauko et al., 2024). Discrete
043   diffusion (Alamdari et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a), Bayesian flow models (Atkinson et al., 2024),
044   and other approaches (Yang et al., 2024a) are effective generative models for sequences, but to date,
045   protein language models (PLMs) based on autoregressive transformers have demonstrated the most
046   success for generating functional enzymes (Madani et al., 2023; Nijkamp et al., 2023; Ferruz et al.,
047   2022; Zvyagin et al., 2022; Hesslow et al., 2022).
048   An important downstream application for generative models is the conditional generation of proteins,
049   where a “condition” is typically defined by desired function and/or properties (Yang et al., 2024a;
050   Ferruz & Höcker, 2022; Dai et al., 2024; Notin et al., 2024; Hayes et al., 2024). For example, it
051   is desirable to design Cas9 nucleases that express well and have high activity and specificity for
052   gene-editing (Chen & Liu, 2023). Alternatively, enzyme engineers might be interested in thermostable
053   enzymes that can catalyze unannotated, non-native reactions for chemical synthesis (Chen & Arnold,
      2020), among numerous other applications (Buller et al., 2023). Conditional generation with ML

054
      models to maximize a desired property such as stability has been explored using direct preference
055   optimization and guided diffusion (Widatalla et al., 2024; Nisonoff et al., 2024; Gruver et al., 2023;
056   Lisanza et al., 2024). Here, we focus on methods for generating sequences with a target function,
057   which is more qualitative rather than quantitative.
058
      A typical approach for generating sequences with a target function, such as specific enzymatic activity,
059
      involves finetuning a PLM on a specific class or family of enzymes and then generating novel enzymes
060
      with that function (Madani et al., 2023; Ruffolo et al., 2024b) – an approach we will refer to as
061   Target Finetuning. This approach has been successfully applied to ProGen and other similar models
062   with wet-lab validation (Nicolini et al., 2024; Zvyagin et al., 2022; Munsamy et al., 2024). Other
063   successful recent approaches for conditional protein sequence generation (such as ZymCTRL) are
064   related to Prompting (Munsamy et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2023; Nathansen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024).
065   Despite these advances, a major limitation of existing models is that they can only be finetuned for
066   known function. This requires extensive prior knowledge of a protein family with that function (Table
067   1), but protein engineers are often interested in new functions, such as enzymes with new-to-nature
068   activities (Arnold, 2018). Thus, current methods are not flexible to complex conditioning (such as
069
      jointly across enzymatic function and organism) and may not generalize well to functions that lie
      out-of-distribution (OOD).
070
071   To address this challenge, our key contribution in this study is introducing a broadly applicable
072   strategy (Conditional Adapters) for parameter efficient finetuning of language models. While previous
073   approaches using adapters have been largely limited to protein structure conditioning, we show that
074   Conditional Adapters are flexible and useful for conditioning generation from PLMs based on
075
      different types of desired functions. Specifically, we apply this approach to finetune ProGen2 to
      conditionally generate protein sequences based on enzyme family, taxonomy, and natural language
076
      descriptions – called ProCALM (Protein Conditionally Adapted Language Model). Our approach
077
      demonstrates several advantages compared to existing methods:
078
079         1. ProCALM is parameter efficient and computationally inexpensive to train.
080         2. ProCALM is a general approach that is flexible to various types of conditioning (beyond
081            just structure) – including enzyme class, taxonomy, and textual descriptions.
082         3. ProCALM matches or surpasses existing methods at generating protein sequences with
083            target functions.
084         4. ProCALM can generalize toward conditions that lie OOD, namely by generating sequences
085            for combinations of taxonomy and enzyme class that are not seen in the training set.

      Prompting and Related Approaches. While many approaches have been explored for steerable
090   generation from language models, the most common approaches involve using prompts, or tokens at
091   the beginning of a sequence, to guide the generation of the remaining sequence (Prompting). For
092   example, in the CTRL transformer architecture, control tags are used to guide natural language
093   generation towards specific styles – such as science vs politics (Keskar et al., 2019). Munsamy
094   et al. (2024) trained a PLM based on the CTRL transformer architecture, where the control tags
095   were enzyme commission (EC) numbers describing enzyme families (ZymCTRL). Many of the
096   protein sequences generated from ZymCTRL were successfully validated in the wet-lab as functional
097   enzymes.
098   While CTRL-type models are trained from scratch, prompt tuning and related parameter-efficient
099   approaches involve finetuning pretrained language models using prompts (Lester et al., 2021; Li &
100   Liang, 2021; Zeldes et al., 2020). The weights associated with the pretrained model are fixed, and only
101   the weights associated with the new tokens are trained. There are a few examples of prompt tuning
102   being applied to protein generation. In PrefixProt, ProtGPT2 was finetuned to generate antimicrobial
103   peptides enriched in alpha helices (Luo et al., 2023). In Finenzyme, ProGen was finetuned using
104
      prefix tuning on seven broad EC classes, and a few selected specific ECs (Nicolini et al., 2024).
      Nathansen et al. (2023) explored this strategy for finetuning RITA to generate a specific protein
105
      family.
106

      prompt tuning to finetune PLMs for conditional generation based on backbone, secondary structure,

144   Adapters in Language Models. Adapter-based tuning of language models has emerged as a
145   parameter-efficient strategy (Pfeiffer et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2021) to finetune language models for
146   specific tasks (Ribeiro et al., 2021; Houlsby et al., 2019), such as describing protein sequences with
147
      natural language (Carrami & Sharifzadeh, 2024; Huo et al., 2024). Recently, using Conditional
      Adapters has shown utility in PLMs for the conditional generation of proteins. Several studies have
148
      used adapters to condition generation from PLMs based on desired structures (Zheng et al., 2023;
149
      Lee & Kim, 2024; Qiu et al., 2024). In proseLM, generation was conditioned on protein structure
150   (Ruffolo et al., 2024a), and generated gene editing enzymes and antibodies that were successfully
151   validated in the wet lab. LM-Design is particularly interesting because the authors also evaluated
152   generalization toward unseen protein folds (Zheng et al., 2023). Conditioning on structure is useful,
153   as structure often determines function, but a goal of protein engineering is often to find proteins
154   with novel functions (such as for new-to-nature enzyme activity (Arnold, 2018)), without any known
155   structure or sequence performing this function. Thus, there is a need to explore models that can
156   condition directly on function and generalize to new functions.
157   Overall, using Conditional Adapters is promising and offers several advantages (Table 1, Fig. 1). Most

159   because conditioning is performed in continuous space, rather than through initial tokenization
160   like Prompting. Still, a few key questions remain, which we aim to explore in this study. (1) Are
161   Conditional Adapters amenable to different types of conditioning information for protein generation
      (i.e. beyond structure)? (2) How does the quality of generated sequences from such a model compare

211
212   ProCALM is trained via parameter-efficient finetuning of ProGen2 (Nijkamp et al., 2023) with
213   conditional adapters; the original ProGen2 weights are fixed while the conditional adapter layers and
214   conditioning encoder are trained (Fig. 1A). The conditioning encoder learns a latent representation of
215   conditioning information, which is concatenated with a low-rank projection of the language model
      hidden embedding inside each conditional adapter layer (corresponding to each transformer layer).

246   The training objective is autoregressive residue prediction, and model training is flexible to any type
247   of representation as conditioning. During inference, sequences are generated conditioned on a target
248   function, which is represented in conditioning space (Fig. 1B). In this study, we explore conditioning
249   using (1) enzyme function associated with the EC number, (2) taxonomy, and (3) textual descriptions
250   of protein function. Multiple conditions can be considered jointly (Fig. A.2). More details are
251   provided in Section A.2.
252   ProCALM aims to address the limitations of existing approaches for conditional sequence gener-
253   ation (Table 1). Unlike models trained from scratch with prompts, using Conditional Adapters is
254   advantageous as it transfers knowledge from the pretrained ProGen2 model. This approach may also
255   enable better OOD generalization compared to Prompting approaches, as the conditioning lies on a
256   continuous conditioning space (Fig. 1C). Overall, ProCALM is a promising and flexible approach
257   to unlock conditional generation of proteins with useful and unseen combinations of functions.
258   ProCALM will be made publicly available.

308   4.1   P RO CALM MATCHES THE PERFORMANCE OF EXISTING METHODS
309
      We first sought to understand if our approach could achieve similar performance to ZymCTRL,
310
      a state-of-the-art language model for generating enzyme sequences conditioned on EC number
311
      (Munsamy et al., 2024). We trained two different ProCALM models – one on 9 billion tokens of
312   enzyme sequences from Uniref, and one on 1.5 billion tokens of enzyme sequences from Swissprot
313   Train, with EC number represented as a one-hot (OH) encoding. While the model trained on Uniref
314   for longer was better able to match the performance of ZymCTRL, we found that shorter training on
315   Swissprot enabled sufficient generation quality (valid and correctly conditioned enzymes, Fig. 2A)
316   and sequence generation with greater diversity (Fig. 2B). In general, training for longer increased the
317   fraction of sequences with correct conditioning, but reduced sequence diversity (Fig. A.4). Because
318   Swissprot has the highest quality annotations, we decided to do remaining analysis with models
319   trained using the Swissprot Train dataset.
320   Parameter efficient finetuning is advantageous due to its low computational cost and greater robustness
321   to overfitting (Sledzieski et al., 2024), compared to full parameter training. Impressively, our Uniref-
322   9B and Swissprot-1.5B models only required 240 and 40 A100-hours to train, respectively, whereas
323   ZymCTRL required 15,000 A100-hours (Fig. 2D). By comparison, ZymCTRL trained all model
      parameters for approximately 50 billion tokens and required a higher memory footprint than our

      4.2   P RO CALM ACCOMMODATES MULTIPLE TYPES OF CONDITIONING
358
      ProCALM can use other types of information, such as taxonomy, to condition sequence generation.
359
      We trained two additional models, one conditioned on taxonomy, and one conditioned jointly on
360   taxonomy and EC. For the jointly conditioned model, we used a modified architecture, where parallel
361   adapters accept multiple sources of conditioning information (Fig. A.2). In an ablation study, we
362   found that parallel adapters work better than merging the joint conditioning in the conditioning
363   encoder and using a shared adapter (Fig. A.8). Overall, the taxonomy conditioned and jointly
364   conditioned models enriched generation for the desired condition(s) (Fig. 3), showing that ProCALM
365   is easily adaptable. Performance is highest for bacterial sequences, as they constitute the majority of
366   the training data (Fig. A.1). However, learning multiple (EC and taxonomy) conditions sacrifices
367   performance slightly compared to models trained to learn either EC or taxonomy conditioning alone.
368   To highlight the broad utility and inherent flexibility of ProCALM, we explored another task, con-
369   ditional generation based on natural language prompts. While there are several models that enable
370   text-guided protein sequence generation (Liu et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2024; Hayes et al., 2024; Praljak
371   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b), we focused on comparing to ProteinDT, which is publicly available
372   and focuses on using natural language as conditioning. In ProteinDT (Liu et al., 2024), a multimodal
373   representation is first learned between textual description and protein sequence using CLIP loss
374
      (ProteinCLAP representation), and this is decoded into a generated sequence using a PLM that has
      been subjected to indirect prompt tuning (finetuned to condition generation on different first token
375
      embeddings, i.e. conditions).

      4.3   P RO CALM GENERALIZES TO OUT- OF - DISTRIBUTION CONDITIONS
433
434   We further explored ProCALM’s ability to generalize, motivated by the fact that generative models
435   able to generate sequences for novel, unseen functions would be highly impactful. For example,
436   enzyme engineering often involves finding enzymes that can perform interesting non-native activities

479   5     D ISCUSSION
480
481   We have shown that conditional adapters are an efficient, flexible, and generalizable approach for
482   enabling language models to perform steerable sequence generation. Our specific implementation,
483   ProCALM, demonstrates strong performance for enzyme sequence generation conditioned on known
484   EC numbers, at a significantly lower computational cost than prior approaches. ProCALM easily
485   adapts to different types of conditioning (beyond protein structure), such as taxonomy and textual
      description of function, and to different representations for the same condition, such as OH, DRFP,


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      and CREEP representations of enzyme function. Our approach is also parameter efficient, resulting
487   in lower memory usage and potentially faster training.
488
489   Most usefully, ProCALM can perform previously unexplored generation tasks, such as generating
490
      from unseen EC classes. Still, performance is limited, and there is significant room for improvement
      in this area. For certain EC classes, ProCALM was able to generate bacterial sequences corresponding
491
      to the correct EC, despite not having bacterial sequences belonging to those EC classes in the train
492
      set. 7.1.1.2 and 7.1.1.9 are transmembrane enzymes part of large complexes, but it is not clear why
493   these particular functions were successfully generated. In the future, a more in depth examination of
494   success and failure modes for ProCALM will be interesting.
495
496
      For known functions, it is relatively straightforward to use function prediction tools such as BLAST
      to filter generated sequences to the valid ones with correctly conditioned function, though future work
497
      will benefit from better function prediction oracles for evaluation (Yu et al., 2023; Ayres et al., 2024;
498
      Huo et al., 2024). Future evaluation may also include more detailed analysis of generation quality
499   (Ye et al., 2024) using various tools, with wet-lab validation. In particular, evaluation would benefit
500   from a deeper consideration of how to measure similarity between functions such as EC classes or
501   textual descriptions.
502
      In general, we believe that the most interesting research direction for future improvement is generation
503
      of sequences with OOD functions and/or properties. In real-world application, evaluation is particu-
504
      larly intractable for these unannotated functions, so it will be even more critical to increase the hit
505   rate for enzyme discovery on those functions. Having a smooth and navigable latent space for OOD
506   functions will be especially important, along with training data encompassing more comprehensive
507   annotations of natural and engineered proteins. While we explored various representations here based
508   on fingerprints and contrastive learning, further exploration of multiple modalities such as structure,
509   sequence, function, and natural language with different representation learning techniques could lead
510   to breakthroughs (Yang et al., 2024b; Mikhael et al., 2024; Ramesh et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2024). For
511   convenience, we finetuned our model using annotated proteins in Swissprot, but greater sequence
512   diversity by incorporating sequences from unannotated and metagenomic datasets could be beneficial
513
      in the future.
514   Overall, we have demonstrated that using conditional adapters to finetune language models offers
515   several advantages, including computational efficiency, flexibility to many types of conditioning, and
516   generalization for OOD generation. This approach could easily be applied to other autoregressive
517   PLMs (Hesslow et al., 2022; Ferruz et al., 2022). ProCALM will be publicly available, and we
518   encourage researchers to apply our approach to other language models and further explore its potential
519
      for conditional protein generation.

890
891   A.1    DATASETS AND R EPRESENTATIONS
892
893
894   Uniref and Swissprot were downloaded from Uniprot (The UniProt Consortium et al., 2023) as all
895
      sequences with EC numbers on June 17, 2024. We left Uniref unprocessed for training. For Swissprot,
      we only considered the sequences corresponding to EC numbers with associated reactions from
896
      CARE (Yang et al., 2024b), and we first resampled it by weighting each sample with 1+ln1 (n) relative
897
      probability of being sampled, where n is the size of the cluster that a sequence belongs to when
898
      clustered at 50% identity using MMseqs2 (Steinegger & Söding, 2017). Effectively, this up-samples
899
      the sequences from smaller clusters. Afterward, we generated the held-out splits outlined in Table 2
900   and Fig. A.1. The remaining non held-out sequences in Swissprot Train were then used for training
901   most models. For Fig. 6, we updated Swissprot Train slightly by also holding out bacterial sequences
902   associated with the 9 EC classes explored.
903
      Different representations of EC numbers were obtained for the Swissprot dataset. For OH, each of
904
      the four levels of the EC number were OH-encoded and concatenated, resulting in a 630-dimensional
905
      vector. The EC number is a hierarchical scheme consisting of four levels where each subsequent level
906   describes enzyme function more specifically. For DRFP, DRFP representations (Probst et al., 2022)
907   with dimension 2048 were obtained for all reactions associated to each EC based on CARE (Yang
908   et al., 2024b), and the EC was encoded as the mean DRFP representation. DRFP is a fingerprint
909   associated with the set change from reactants to products and has shown state-of-the-art ability for
910   reaction classification. For CREEP, we used the model trained with default parameters on the medium
911   train-test split for Task 2, which was trained using contrastive learning to align representations of
912   protein sequences, reactions, and textual description (Yang et al., 2024b). We note that there is no data
913   leakage in the CREEP representation, as it was trained with the same held-out EC numbers. From
914
      this model, we extracted representations for the textual description associated to each EC number.
915   The OH representation of taxonomy was simply encoded as a four-dimensional vector to describe
916   the four possible kingdoms: bacteria, eukaryota, archaea, and viruses. While we also explored more
917   specific encodings of taxonomic hierarchy, generation quality was not as good, as this conditioning
      information was more difficult to learn.

938   A.2   A RCHITECTURE AND T RAINING D ETAILS
939
940   Unless otherwise noted, we finetuned ProGen2-Base, which is a decoder-only autoregressive language
941   model consisting of 27 layers, an embedding dimension of 1536, and 764 million parameters (Nijkamp
942   et al., 2023). By default, the conditioning encoder consisted of two layers with a dimension of 256
943   each. The output was then concatenated with a low-rank projection of the language hidden state
944
      (dimension 16). Each adapter MLP consisted of 3 layers with dimension 2*(16 + 256) and a final layer
      with dimension (256 + 16). For the default ProCALM model, the adapter layers and conditioning
945
      encoder together consisted of approximately 70 million additional parameters, depending on the
946
      condition dimension. The adapter parameters dominated compared to the conditioning encoder
947   weights. During parameter efficient training, the original ProGen2 weights were fixed, and the adapter
948   and conditioning encoder were trained (pseudocode provided by Ruffolo et al. (2024a)). During full
949   finetuning, all weights were trained. For the Small model, dimensions were reduced by half and
950   only two layers were used in the adapter MLP. For the parallel adapter architecture, conditioning
951   information was passed through separate adapters before being summed as the embedding update
952   (Fig. A.2), resulting in approximately twice the number of trainable parameters. We also briefly
953   explored the impact of scaling to larger ProGen2 models.
954   We used composer to perform training across 4 40GB A100s using distributed data parallel, and
955   batches were sampled to minimize the usage of padding tokens. Each batch consisted of 144k tokens.
956   Training took about 10 hours for every 1.5 billion tokens. One epoch for the processed Swissprot
957   Train dataset corresponds to about 6 × 107 tokens.
