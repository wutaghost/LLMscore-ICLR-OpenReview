011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Knowledge tracing (KT) is a popular approach for modeling students’ learning
014            progress over time, which can enable more personalized and adaptive learning.
015            However, existing KT approaches face two major limitations: (1) they rely heav-
016
               ily on expert-defined knowledge concepts (KCs) in questions, which is time-
               consuming and prone to errors; and (2) KT methods tend to overlook the semantics
017
               of both questions and the given KCs. In this work, we address these challenges and
018
               present KCQRL, a framework for automated knowledge concept annotation and
019            question representation learning that can improve the effectiveness of any existing
020            KT model. First, we propose an automated KC annotation process using large
021            language models (LLMs), which generates question solutions and then annotates
022            KCs in each solution step of the questions. Second, we introduce a contrastive
023            learning approach to generate semantically rich embeddings for questions and
024            solution steps, aligning them with their associated KCs via a tailored false negative
025            elimination approach. These embeddings can be readily integrated into existing
026            KT models, replacing their randomly initialized embeddings. We demonstrate
027
               the effectiveness of KCQRL across 15 KT models on two large real-world Math
               learning datasets, where we achieve consistent performance improvements.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The recent years have witnessed a surge in online learning platforms (Adedoyin & Soykan, 2023;
033
      Gros & García-Peñalvo, 2023), where students learn new knowledge concepts, which are then tested
      through exercises. Needless to say, personalization is crucial for effective learning: it allows that new
034
      knowledge concepts are carefully tailored to the current knowledge state of the student, which is
035
      more effective than one-size-fits-all approaches to learning (Cui & Sachan, 2023; Xu et al., 2024).
036   However, such personalization requires that the knowledge of students is continuously assessed,
037   which highlights the need for knowledge tracing (KT).
038
039
      In KT, one models the temporal dynamics of students’ learning processes (Corbett & Anderson,
      1994) in terms of a core set of skills, which are called knowledge concepts (KCs). KT models are
040
      typically time-series models that receive the past interactions of the learner as input (e.g., her previous
041
      exercises) in order to predict response of the learner to the next exercise. Early KT models were
042   primarily based on logistic or probabilistic models (Corbett & Anderson, 1994; Cen et al., 2006;
043   Pavlik et al., 2009; Käser et al., 2017; Vie & Kashima, 2019), while more recent KT models build
044   upon deep learning (Piech et al., 2015; Abdelrahman & Wang, 2019; Long et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
045   2023b; Huang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024).
046
      Yet, existing KT models have two main limitations that hinder their applicability in practice (see
047
      Fig. 1). 1 They require a comprehensive mapping between KCs and questions, which is typically
048   done through manual annotations by experts. However, such KC annotation is both time-intensive and
049   prone to errors (Clark, 2014; Bier et al., 2019). While recent work shows that LLM-generated KCs
050   may be more favorable for human subjects (Moore et al., 2024), its success has not yet translated into
051   improvements in KT. 2 KT models overlook the semantics of both questions and KCs. Instead, they
052   merely treat them as numerical identifiers, whose embeddings are randomly initialized and are learned
053   throughout training. Therefore, existing KT models are expected to “implicitly” learn the association
      between questions and KCs and their sequential modeling for student histories, simultaneously. In

078

081   curated from online math learning platforms. We compare 15 state-of-the-art KT models, which
082   we combine with our KCQRL framework. Here, we find consistent evidence that our framework
083   improves performance by a large margin.

      3         P ROPOSED KCQRL F RAMEWORK
148
149   Our KCQRL framework has three main modules (see Fig. 2): (1) The first module takes the question
150   content and annotates the question with a step-by-step solution and the corresponding knowledge
151   concepts (KCs) (Sec. 3.1). (2) The second module learns the representations of questions by
152   leveraging solution steps and KCs via a tailored contrastive learning objective (Sec. 3.2). (3) The
153
      third module integrates the learned question representations into existing KT models (Sec.3.3).

186   3.2 R EPRESENTATION L EARNING OF Q UESTIONS (M ODULE 2)
187
      Our KCQRL framework provides a tailored contrastive learning (CL) approach to generate embed-
188                                                                                                     3
      dings for questions and solution steps that are semantically aligned with their associated KCs. A
189
      naïve approach would be to use a pre-trained LM, which can then generate general-purpose em-
190   beddings. However, such general-purpose embeddings lack the domain-specific focus required in
191   education. (We provide evidence for this in our ablation studies in Sec. 5.2.) To address this, CL
192   allows us to explicitly teach the encoder to bring question and solution step embeddings closer to the
193   representations of their relevant KCs. After CL training, these ‘enriched’ embeddings are aggregated
194   and used as input for the downstream KT model to improve the modeling capabilities of the latter.
195
      We achieve the above objective via a carefully designed CL loss. CL has shown to be effective in
196   representation learning of textual contents in other domains, such as information retrieval (Karpukhin
197   et al., 2020; Khattab & Zaharia, 2020; Zhu et al., 2023), where CL was used to bring the relevant
198   query and document embeddings closer. However, this gives rise to an important difference: In
199   our framework, we leverage CL to bring question embeddings closer to their KC embeddings, and
200   solution step embeddings to their KC embeddings, respectively.
201
      To bring the embeddings of relevant texts closer, our CL loss should learn similar representations for
202   the positive pair (e. g., the question and one of its KCs) in contrast to many negative pairs (e. g., the
203   question and a KC from another question) from the same batch. In our setup, different questions in the
204   same batch can be annotated with semantically similar KCs (e. g., U NDERSTANDING OF A DDITION
205   vs. A BILITY TO P ERFORM A DDITION). Constructing the negative pairs from semantically similar
206   KCs adversely affects representation learning. This is an extensively studied problem in CL for other
207   domains, and it is called false negatives (Chen et al., 2022; Huynh et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022;
208   Sun et al., 2023b; Byun et al., 2024). Informed by the literature, we carefully design a mechanism
209   to pick negative pairs from the batch that avoids false negatives. For this, we consider the semantic
210
      information in annotated KCs, i. e., before the CL training, which we use to cluster the KCs that share
      similar semantics. Then, for a given KC in the positive pair, we discard other KCs in the same cluster
211
      when constructing the negative pairs.

503   Knowledge tracing (KT) aims to model the temporal dynamics of students’ interactions with learning
504   content to predict their knowledge over time (Corbett & Anderson, 1994). A large number of machine
505   learning models, including deep neural networks, have been proposed for this purpose (Piech et al.,
506   2015; Yeung & Yeung, 2018; Lee & Yeung, 2019; Sonkar et al., 2020; Long et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
507
      2023a; Chen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2017; Yeung, 2019; Guo et al., 2021; Pandey & Karypis, 2019;
      Choi et al., 2020a; Ghosh et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023).
508
509   We later use all of the above-mentioned KT models in our experiments, as our KCQRL framework
510   is designed to enhance the performance of any KT model. However, existing works (1) require a
511   comprehensive mapping between knowledge concepts and questions, and (2) overlook the semantics
512   of question content and KCs. Our KCQRL effectively addresses both limitations, which leads to
513
      performance improvements across all of the state-of-the-art KT models.
514   KC annotation aims to infer the KCs of a given exercise in an automated manner. (i) One research
515   line infers KCs from students’ learning histories as latent states (e.g., Barnes, 2005; Cen et al., 2006;
516   Liu et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2023). However, these latent states lack interpretability, limiting their
517   use in KT. (ii) Another approach reformulates KC annotation as a classification task (e.g., Patikorn
518   et al., 2019; Tian et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024a;b), but this requires large annotated datasets, making it
519
      often infeasible. (iii) Recent works have also explored KC annotation in free-text form (Moore et al.,
      2024; Didolkar et al., 2024), but their annotations are not informed by the question’s solution steps,
520
      which we show is suboptimal in our ablations. In our work, we leverage LLMs’ reasoning abilities to
521
      generate step-by-step solutions and produce grounded KCs for each question.
522
523    7                       D ISCUSSION
524
525
      We present a novel framework for improving the effectiveness of any existing KT model: automated
      knowledge concept annotation and question representation learning, which we call KCQRL. Our
526
      KCQRL framework is carefully designed to address two key limitations of existing KT models.
527
      Limitation 1: Existing KT models require a comprehensive mapping between knowledge concepts
528   (KCs) and questions, typically done manually by domain experts. Contribution 1: We circumvent
529   such a need by introducing a novel, automated KC annotation module grounded in solution steps.
530   Limitation 2: Existing KT models overlook the semantics of both questions and KCs, treating
531   them as identifiers whose embeddings are learned from scratch. Contribution 2: We propose a
532   novel contrastive learning paradigm to effectively learn representations of questions and KC, also
533   grounded in solution steps. We integrate the learned representations into KT models to improve their
534   performance. Conclusion: We demonstrate the effectiveness of our KCQRL across 15 KT models on
535   two large real-world Math learning datasets, where we achieve consistent performance improvements.
536   Implications: Our KCQRL allows even the simplest KT models to reach near state-of-the-art (SoTA)
537   performance, suggesting that much of the existing literature may have “overfit” to the paradigm of
538   modeling sequences based only on IDs. Future KT research can advance by focusing on sequential
539   models “designed to” inherently understand semantics. As a first step, we release an English version
      of the XES3G5M dataset with full annotations, including question content, solutions, and KCs.

      A    R ELATED W ORK (E XTENDED )
811
812
813   Knowledge tracing (KT) aims to model the temporal dynamics of students’ interactions with
814
      learning content to predict their knowledge over time (Corbett & Anderson, 1994). Early works in
      KT primarily leveraged logistic and probabilistic models (Corbett & Anderson, 1994; Cen et al.,
815
      2006; Pavlik et al., 2009; Käser et al., 2017; Vie & Kashima, 2019). Later, many KT works focused
816
      on deep learning-based approaches, which can loosely be grouped into different categories: 1) Deep
817   sequential models that leverage auto-regressive architectures such as RNN or LSTM (Piech et al.,
818   2015; Yeung & Yeung, 2018; Lee & Yeung, 2019; Liu et al., 2019; Nagatani et al., 2019; Sonkar
819   et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2021; Long et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
820   2023a), 2) Memory augmented models to externally capture the latent states of the students during
821   the learning process (Zhang et al., 2017; Abdelrahman & Wang, 2019; Yeung, 2019), 3) Graph-based
822   models that use graph neural networks to capture the interactions between knowledge concepts and
823   questions (Nakagawa et al., 2019; Song et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2024), 4) Attention-based models that
824   either employ a simple attention mechanism or transformer-based encoder-decoder architecture for
825
      the modeling of student interactions (Pandey & Karypis, 2019; Pandey & Srivastava, 2020; Choi
      et al., 2020a; Ghosh et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2023; Im et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Yin et al.,
826
      2023; Ke et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c). Outside these categories, Lee et al. (2024) fits entire history
827
      of students exercises into the context window of a language model to make the prediction, Guo et al.
828   (2021) designs an adversarial training to have robust representations of latent states, and Zhou et al.
829   (2024) develops a generative model to track the knowledge states of the students. However, the
830   existing works (1) require a comprehensive mapping between knowledge concepts and questions
831   and (2) overlook the semantics of questions’ content and KCs. Our KCQRL framework effectively
832   addresses both limitations.
833
      KC annotation aims at inferring the KCs of a given exercise in an automated manner. (i) One line of
834   research infers KCs of questions by learning patterns from students’ learning histories in the form of
835   latent states (e.g., Barnes, 2005; Cen et al., 2006; Liu et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2023). Yet, such latent
836   states lack interpretability, because of which their use in KT is typically prohibited. (ii) Another
837   line of research reformulates KCs annotation as a classification task, typically via model training
838   (Patikorn et al., 2019; Tian et al., 2022) or LLM prompting (Li et al., 2024a;b). Although these works
839   do not require human experts at inference, they still require them to curate a large annotated dataset
840   for models to learn the task. (iii) Recent works also explored the KC annotation in free-text form
841   (Moore et al., 2024; Didolkar et al., 2024). However, their KC annotations are not informed by the
842
      solution steps of the question. We show that this is suboptimal in our ablations.
843   In our work, we leverage the reasoning abilities of LLMs (Wei et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2023; Shridhar
844   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024) to generate the step-by-step solution steps of a given
845   question and generate the associated KCs in a grounded manner.
846   Representation learning for textual data has been extensively studied in the context of LLMs. While
847   pre-trained LLMs generate effective general-purpose semantic representations, these embeddings
848   often turn out to be suboptimal for domain-specific tasks (Karpukhin et al., 2020). Through CL
849   training, the model learns to bring the positive pairs of textual data closer together (but not the
850   negative pairs in the embeddings).
851
      Many works designed tailored CL losses (Oord et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020;
852   Ozyurt et al., 2023b) to improve the representations of textual data in various domains. Examples are
853   information retrieval (Karpukhin et al., 2020; Khattab & Zaharia, 2020; Lee et al., 2023; Sun et al.,
854   2023a; Zhu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024), textual entailment (Gao et al., 2021; Li & Li, 2024; Ou
855   & Xu, 2024), code representation learning (Zhang et al., 2024), and question answering (Yue et al.,
856   2021; 2022). To our knowledge, we are the first to leverage a CL loss for the representation learning
857   of questions specific to KT tasks.
858   The prevalence of false negative pairs can significantly degrade the quality of CL (Saunshi et al.,
859   2019), as it causes semantically similar samples to be incorrectly pushed apart. To address this,
860   several works in computer vision have proposed methods to detect and eliminate false negative pairs
861   by leveraging the similarity of sample embeddings during training (Chen et al., 2022; Huynh et al.,
862   2022; Yang et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023b; Byun et al., 2024). Different from these works, we develop
863   a custom CL approach that is carefully designed to our task. Therein, we detect similarities between
      samples (i. e., KCs) in advance (e. g., U NDERSTANDING OF A DDITION vs. A BILITY TO P ERFORM

      A DDITION), and eliminate false negative question-KC and solution step-KC pairs during our custom
865   CL training.

       In our KCQRL framework, we enhanced the performance of 15 KT algorithms on two real-world
1029   large online math learning datasets. The summary of these KT algorithms are given below:
1030
1031         • DKT (Piech et al., 2015): It is the first deep learning based KT algorithm. It uses the LSTM
1032           to model the temporality in students’ learning histories. The original DKT turns each KC
1033           into a one-hot or a random vector. The recent implementation from pykt (Liu et al., 2022b)
1034           learns the embeddings for each KC, which are then processed by an LSTM layer.
1035         • DKT+ (Yeung & Yeung, 2018): It adds regularization to the existing DKT model. Specif-
1036           ically, it adds a reconstruction loss to the last exercise’s prediction. Further, it adds a
1037           regularization term to make the predictions of the same KC consistent across the time
1038           dimension.
1039         • KQN (Lee & Yeung, 2019): It models the students’ learning histories via RNN. Further, it
1040           has an explicit neural network mechanism to capture the latent knowledge states.
1041         • qDKT (Sonkar et al., 2020):It is a variant of DKT that learns the temporal dynamics of
1042           students’ learning processes via the questions rather than KCs.
1043
             • IEKT (Long et al., 2021): It models the students’ learning histories via RNN. In addition,
1044
               it has two additional neural network modules, namely student cognition and knowledge
1045           acquisition estimation.
1046
             • AT-DKT (Liu et al., 2023a): It has the same model backbone as in DKT. On top of it,
1047
               AT-DKT has two auxiliary tasks, question tagging prediction and student’s individual prior
1048
               knowledge prediction.
1049
1050
             • QIKT (Chen et al., 2023): It combines three neural network modules to model the learning
               processes, question-centric knowledge acquisition module via LSTM, question-agnostic
1051
               knowledge state module via another LSTM and question centric problem solving module
1052
               via MLP.
1053
1054
             • DKVMN (Zhang et al., 2017): It employs key-value memory networks to model the
               relationships between the latent concepts and output the student’s knowledge mastery of
1055
               each concept.
1056
1057         • DeepIRT (Yeung, 2019): It incorporates the architecture of DKVMN and further leverages
1058
               the item-response theory (Rasch, 1993) to make interpretable predictions.
1059         • ATKT (Guo et al., 2021): It models the students learning histories via LSTM. Further,
1060           it employs an adversarial training mechanism to increase the generalization of model
1061           predictions.
1062         • SAKT (Pandey & Karypis, 2019): It develops a simple self-attention mechanism to model
1063           the learning histories and make the prediction.
1064         • SAINT (Choi et al., 2020a): It employs a Transformer-based model that is using mulitple
1065           layers of encoders and decoders to model the history of exercise information and responses.
1066         • AKT (Ghosh et al., 2020): This model employs a monotonic attention mechanism between
1067           the exercises via exponential decay over time. It also employs Rasch model (Rasch, 1993)
1068           to characterize the question’s difficulty and the learner’s ability.
1069
             • simpleKT (Liu et al., 2023b): As a simple but tought to beat baseline, this model employs
1070           and ordinary dot product as an attention mechanism to the embeddings of questions.
1071
             • sparseKT (Huang et al., 2023): On top of simpleKT, this model further introduces a sparse
1072
               attention mechanism such that for the next exercise’s prediction, the model attends to at
1073
               most K exercises in the past.

       Here we explain the implementation details of each part of our KCQRL framework.
1083
                                                                                                    6
1084   KC Annotation (Sec. 3.1): We leverage the reasoning abilities of OpenAI’s GPT-4o model as our
1085   LLM Pϕ (⋅) at each step. We set its temperature parameter to 0 to get the deterministic answers from
1086   the model. For each question in the dataset, GPT-4o model is prompted three times in total: one for
1087   solution steps generation, one for KC annotation and one for solution step-KC mapping. Overall, the
1088   cost is around 80 USD for XES3G5M dataset with 7,652 questions and 50 USD for Eedi dataset with
1089
       4,019 questions.
1090   Representation learning of questions Sec. 3.2: We train BERT (Devlin, 2019) as our LLM encoder
1091   Eψ (⋅). For the elimination of false negative pairs, we use HDBSCAN (Campello et al., 2013) as the
1092   clustering algorithm A(⋅). The clustering is done over the Sentence-BERT (Reimers & Gurevych,
1093   2019) embeddings of KCs, which is a common practice in identifying relevant textual documents
1094   (Liu et al., 2022a; Ozyurt et al., 2023a). For HDBSCAN clustering, we set minimum cluster size
1095
       to 2, minimum samples to 2, metric to cosine similarity between the embeddings. For contrastive
       learning training, we train BERT (Devlin, 2019) as our LLM encoder. To better distinguish three
1096
       types of inputs, i. e. question content, solution step, and KC, we introduce three new tokens [Q], [S],
1097
       [KC] to be learned during the training. These new tokens are added to the beginning of question
1098   content, solution step, and KC, respectively, for both training and inference. For all input types, we
1099   use [CLS] token’s embeddings as the embeddings of the entire input text. For the training objective,
1100   we set α = 1. Since both losses Lquestioni and Lstepi are already normalized by the number of KCs and
1101   steps, our initial exploration (α varying from 0.2 to 5.0) indicated that setting α = 1 yields the best
1102   performance. We train the encoder for 50 epochs with the following hyperparameters: batch size =
1103   32, learning rate = 5e-5, dropout = 0.1, and temperature (of similarity function) = 0.1. For training of
1104   Eψ (⋅), we use Nvidia Tesla A100 with 40GB GPU memory. The entire training is completed under
1105   4 hours.
1106   After the training, the question embeddings are acquired by running the inference on question text
1107   and solution step texts for each question in the dataset. Specifically, we take the embeddings of "[Q]
1108   <question content>" for the question content and "[S] <solution step>" for each solution step. Then,
1109   we aggregate these embeddings as explained in Sec. 3.3. As a result, the inference does not require
1110   the KC annotations. This has the following advantage: When a new question is added to the dataset,
1111   the earlier KC annotation module can be skipped completely and one can directly get the embeddings
1112
       in this module and start using them for the downstream KT model.
1113   Improving KT via learned question embeddings: We adopt the following strategy for the fair
1114   evaluation of KT algorithms and their improvements via our novel KCQRL framework. We first did
1115   a grid search over the hyperparameters of each KT model (see Table 4 for parameters) to find the
1116   best configuration for each model. Then, we replaced their embeddings with our learned question
1117   embeddings and trained/tested the KT models with the same configurations found earlier (i. e., no
1118
       grid search is applied). To ensure the fair evaluation between the KT algorithms, and between their
       default versions and improved versions (via our framework), we fixed their embedding dimensions
1119
       to 300. As BERT embeddings’ default dimensionality is larger (i. e., 768), we just added a linear
1120
       layer (no non-linear activation function is added) on top of the replaced embeddings to reduce its
1121   dimensionality to 300 to ensure that model capacities are subjected to a fair comparison. As KT
1122   models are much smaller than the LLM encoders, this time we used NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090
1123   with 24GB GPU memory for even larger batch sizes.
1124
       Evaluation of KC-centric KT models: Some KT models (such as DKT (Piech et al., 2015),
1125
       simpleKT (Liu et al., 2023b) etc.) expand the sequence of questions into the sequence of KCs for
1126
       both training and inference. For instance, if there are 3 questions with 2 KCs each, they transform the
1127   sequence {q1 , q2 , q3 } into {c11 , c12 , c21 , c22 , c31 , c32 } and assign the labels of original questions
1128   to each of their corresponding KCs for training. This paradigm causes information leakage in the
1129   evaluation as highlighted by Liu et al. (2022b). The reason is, during the prediction of c32 , the label of
1130   c31 is already given as the history, which is the same label (to be predicted) for c32 . To eliminate the
1131   information leakage, we followed the literature (Liu et al., 2022b; 2023c) and applied the following
1132   procedure. Again from the same example, to predict the label of q3 , 1) we provided the expanded KC
1133

               https://platform.openai.com/docs/models/gpt-4o

       sequence of earlier questions as before, i. e., {c11 , c12 , c31 , c32 }. 2) Then we appended c31 and c32
1135   separately to the given sequence and run the predictions independently. 3) Finally we aggregated
1136   these predictions by taking their mean, and used it as the model’s final prediction. Important note:
1137   With our KCQRL framework, these KT models do not suffer the information leakage anymore, as all

       In this section, we provide the details how we evaluate the quality of our KCQRL’s KC annotations
1353   as part of our ablation study in Sec. 5.2.
1354
1355   For this, we compared our annotations against two baselines: 1) KC annotations from the original
1356
       datasets and 2) KC annotations of our KCQRL without leveraging the solution steps.
1357   We picked 1,000 random questions from each dataset, XES3G5M and Eedi. We used Llama-3.1-405B
1358   (Dubey et al., 2024) for the evaluations of these questions. Here, we chose a Llama-based model
1359   instead of GPT-based models (e. g., GPT-4o) to prevent the potential bias of GPT models towards
1360   their own generations, as we already used GPT-4o for our KC annotations earlier.
1361   For a structured comparison, we defined 5 criteria. Correctness is to measure which annotation has
1362   correct KC(s), relative to the other annotations. Coverage is an evaluation criterion for questions
1363   with multiple KCs. It measures how well the KCs from an annotation cover the set of KCs that
1364   the question is associated with. Specificity is a measure to compare which annotation has a more
1365   modular set of KCs rather than long and complicated ones. Of note, modular and simpler KCs enable
1366   identifying the common skills required for different questions. We additionally defined ability of
1367   integration to evaluate how well the described KCs are widely applicable to other Math problems,
1368
       beyond the question being solved. Finally, overall is about choosing the best KC annotation by
       considering all criteria.
1369
1370   As explained in Sec. 5.2, we made a pair-wise comparison between KC annotations: (i) Original vs.
1371   KCQRL w/o sol. steps, (ii) Original vs. KCQRL, and (iii) KCQRL w/o sol. steps vs. KCQRL. To
1372   eliminate any potential bias from the order of KC annotations in the prompt, each time we randomly
1373   assigned KC annotations to groups A and B. Our prompt is given in Appendix G.2.
1374
1375   G.1   E XAMPLE KC A NNOTATIONS
1376
       Here, we follow our example question from Fig. 2 and provide the KC annotations of two baselines
1377
       and our framework.
1378
1379   Question: 65 ⋅ 34 + 65 ⋅ 45 + 79 ⋅ 35 =?
1380   Solution steps: Below is the extended version of the solution steps, provided by our framework:
1381
1382    • First, factor out the common factor from the first two terms, which is 65. So, the expression
1383      becomes 65 × (34 + 45) + 79 × 35.
1384    • Next, simplify the addition inside the parentheses: 34 + 45 = 79. So, the expression now is
1385      65 × 79 + 79 × 35.
1386
        • Notice that 79 is a common factor in both terms, so factor it out: 79 × (65 + 35).
1387
1388
        • Simplify the addition inside the parentheses: 65+35 = 100. So, the final result is 79×100 = 7900.
1389
       For the above Math question, the KC annotations are the following:
1390
1391   Original from the dataset: a) Extracting common factors of integer multiplication (ordinary type).
1392   KCQRL w/o solution steps: a) Understanding of addition. b) Ability to perform multiplication.
1393
1394
       Our complete KCQRL framework: a) Understanding of multiplication. b) Understanding of
       addition. c) Factoring out a common factor. d) Simplifications of expressions. e) Distributive
1395
       property.
1396
1397   Takeaway: The original KC annotation is just one phrase with complex combinations of multiple
1398   KCs. It is also missing out some KCs such as addition and simplifications of expressions. On the
1399   other hand, KC annotations of KCQRL w/o solution steps are missing the important techniques asked
1400
       by the problem, such as extracting the common factor. The reason is, this technique is hidden in
       the solution steps, which need to be provided for a better KC annotation. Overall, our complete
1401
       framework provides correct set of KCs with better coverage and in a modular way in comparison to
1402
       two baseline annotations.

       G.2   P ROMPT FOR Q UALITY C OMPARISON OF KC A NNOTATIONS
1405
1406   Below is the prompt for comparing the qualities of different KC annotations via LLMs.
1407
1408      Prompt
1409
          You are provided with a Math question. First, you are asked to solve the given question step
1410
          by step. Your task is to choose the best knowledge concept (KC) annotation (A or B) for each
1411
          of the following criteria.
1412
1413      - Correctness: You will choose the KC annotations in terms of the correctness, considering
1414       the question and your answer to that question.
1415      - Coverage: KC annotations may contain multiple KCs. If you think the question is originally
1416       linked to multiple KCs, choose the KC annotation that covers the most of them. If you think
1417       the question is linked to only one KC, choose the KC annotation that covers it. If both covers
1418       it, then choose the KC annotation with the last number of KCs.
1419      - Specificity: Choose the least specific KC annotation, ie, consisting of multiple simple
1420
           elements instead of consisting of a single complex element or a few highly detailed elements.
           In other words, consider the complexity and specificity of the individual sub-concepts, rather
1421
           than just the overall number of concepts. A knowledge concept with multiple simple sub-
1422
           concepts should be ranked as less specific than a knowledge concept with a single, highly
1423       detailed sub-concept.
1424      - Ability of integration: Choose the KC annotation that best represents a skill or concept that
1425       is widely applicable to other problems or contexts. In other words, select the KC that is more
1426       transferable and versatile across various types of math questions, beyond the specific question
1427       being solved.
1428      - Overall: Choose the best KC annotation, considering all the metrics above.
1429
1430      The presented KC annotations might have different formats (one with bullet points and the
1431
          other with new lines etc.). For your evaluation, do not pay attention to the formatting of them,
          and only focus on their textual content.
1432
1433
          These two knowledge concept annotations are given as Group A and B. You will output your

       M         D ISCUSSION ON KCQRL U SAGE IN R EAL -T IME
1783
1784
       In this section, we elaborate on how our complete KCQRL framework can be deployed and maintained
1785   in real-world scenarios. Below, we discuss various situations that may arise when KCQRL is used for
1786   knowledge tracing in online learning platforms.
1787
1788
       What happens when a new student joins the platform? This scenario has no impact on Module
       1 (KC annotation via LLMs in Sec. 3.1) and Module 2 (representation learning of questions in
1789
       Sec. 3.2) as the database of questions remains unchanged. KT models in our Module 3 (Sec. 3.3)
1790
       are time-series models that can adapt to new sequences during inference when a new student joins.
1791   However, KT models often suffer from cold start issues (Zhang et al., 2014; Fatemi et al., 2023)
1792   when students solve only a few exercises. To address this, one can determine the minimum number
1793   of exercises required to achieve satisfactory prediction performance, as described in our multi-step
1794   ahead prediction analysis in Sec. 5.1. Students can then be guided to follow a curriculum designed
1795   by education experts until they complete enough exercises for the KT models to deliver effective
1796   predictions.
1797   What happens when an instructor adds a new question to the platform? After training the
1798   representation learning module (Sec. 3.2), the encoder LM Eψ (⋅) learns to associate questions and
1799   their solution steps with relevant KCs based on annotations provided by a more capable LLM, such
1800             8
       as GPT-4 . Consequently, when instructors add a new question to the platform, they can input the
1801   question and its solution step into the representation learning module and run the encoder LM Eψ (⋅)
1802   to generate new embeddings. Details of the inference behavior of Eψ (⋅) are provided in Appendix D.
1803   To evaluate student knowledge on these new questions, the downstream KT models can then run
1804   inference using the newly generated embeddings.
1805   What happens if the LLM used for KC annotation becomes obsolete? As mentioned earlier, the
1806   LLM for KC annotation is required only during the training of the encoder LM in the representation
1807   learning module. Once the encoder LM is trained, our KCQRL framework no longer depends on
1808   the LLM from Module 1 during deployment. Therefore, even if the LLM used for KC annotation
1809   becomes obsolete, online learning platforms can continue admitting new students and allowing
1810   instructors to add new questions. These new questions can still be used to assess student knowledge
1811   effectively using our KCQRL framework.
1812   The only exception to this scenario occurs if the platform expands its knowledge tracing to a new
1813   subject (e. g., from Math to Chemistry or Physics). In this case, KC annotation for the new question
1814   corpus will be necessary to adapt the representation learning module to the new domain via training.
1815   If the new subject is unrelated to the existing one (i. e., knowledge in one subject does not inform
1816   another), we recommend training and deploying separate branches of the KCQRL pipeline to ensure
1817   better performance for the downstream KT models.
1818   How does KCQRL framework apply to subjects other than Math? Module 1 (KC annotation via
1819   LLMs in Sec. 3.1) in our framework can be extended to other subjects. For subjects where questions
1820   do not require multiple solution steps to arrive at the correct answer, the solution step generation part
1821   of Module 1 can be skipped, starting directly with the KC annotation process. As demonstrated in our
1822   ablation study (Sec. 5.2), our framework still outperforms the original KC annotations even without
1823   the inclusion of solution steps. Once KC annotations are obtained, the solution step–KC mapping
1824   part of Module 1 can also be omitted, as there are no solution steps to map.
1825   In this scenario, the representation learning module (Sec. 3.2) can be trained solely using Lquestioni
1826   (Eq.3). As shown in our ablation study (Sec. 5.2), this approach still improves the performance of
1827   downstream KT models. After generating the embeddings, the downstream KT model can then be
1828   trained as usual.
1829   Finally, we emphasize that, at the time of writing, KT datasets with available question content are
1830   extremely limited; we identified only two such datasets, both in Math. We hope our work highlights
1831   the need for KT datasets in other subjects with available question corpora and inspires future research
1832   to further integrate question semantics into downstream KT models.
