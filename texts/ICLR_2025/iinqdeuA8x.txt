009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Dynamic Graph Neural Networks (DyGNNs) have garnered increasing research
012            attention for learning representations on evolving graphs. Despite their effective-
013            ness, the limited expressive power of existing DyGNNs hinders them from captur-
014            ing important evolving patterns of dynamic graphs. Although some works attempt
015            to enhance expressive capability with heuristic features, there remains a lack of
016            DyGNN frameworks with provable and quantifiable high-order expressive power.
017            To address this research gap, we firstly propose the k-dimensional Dynamic WL
018            tests (k-DWL) as the referencing algorithms to quantify the expressive power of
019
               DyGNNs. We demonstrate that the expressive power of existing DyGNNs is up-
               per bounded by the 1-DWL test. To enhance the expressive power, we propose
020
               Dynamic Graph Neural Network with High-order expressive power (HopeDGN),
021
               which updates the representation of central node pair by aggregating the interac-
022            tion history with neighboring node pairs. Our theoretical results demonstrate that
023            HopeDGN can achieve expressive power equivalent to the 2-DWL test. We then
024            present a Transformer-based implementation for the local variant of HopeDGN.
025            Experimental results show that HopeDGN achieved performance improvements
026            of up to 3.12%, demonstrating the effectiveness of HopeDGN.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as dominant tools for learning low-dimensional rep-
032   resentations of graph-structured data (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Veličković et al., 2017; Hamilton et al.,
033   2017; Gasteiger et al., 2018). However, many real-world graphs exhibit dynamic properties with
034   continuously evolving topological structures. Due to this prevalence, an increasing number of re-
035   search has focused on learning effective representations of dynamic graphs using Dynamic Graph
036   Neural Networks (DyGNNs). Most DyGNNs employ a message-passing framework, where histor-
037   ically interacted nodes are aggregated using techniques such as sum-pooling (Wen & Fang, 2022),
038   local self-attention (Xu et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2021), and Transformers (Yu et al., 2023). DyGNNs
039
      have been successfully applied to various tasks such as financial fraud detection (Huang et al., 2022),
      traffic prediction (Han et al., 2021), and sequential recommendation (Kumar et al., 2019).
040
041   One crucial requirement for designing (dynamic) GNNs is sufficient expressive power; that is, the
042   (dynamic) GNNs should be capable of distinguishing non-isomorphic (dynamic) graphs. Xu et al.
043   (2019) and Morris et al. (2019) underscored that the expressive power of message-passing-based
044   GNNs is bounded by the 1-Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) test, which prompts extensive studies on GNNs
045
      with expressive power beyond 1-WL test (Maron et al., 2019a; Zhang et al., 2024). However, these
      investigations have predominantly focused on static graphs. As we discuss in Section 4.1, existing
046
      DyGNNs remain facing limitations in expressive power when applied to dynamic graphs. Conse-
047
      quently, existing DyGNNs fail to detect some evolving substructures such as triangle structures (an
048   illustrative example is provided in Figure 1), which are important for capturing the evolution patterns
049   of dynamic graphs (Paranjape et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2018; Zitnik et al., 2019). Few works have
050   targeted at designing DyGNNs with stronger expressive power. Souza et al. (2022) proposed rela-
051   tive positional features to enhance the expressive power of DyGNNs. However, from a theoretical
052   perspective, it remains unclear how the relative positional features quantitatively affect DyGNNs’
053   expressive power. To summarize, how to design DyGNNs with provably and quantitatively high-
      order expressive power remains unexplored.

079   To address this research gap, we begin by presenting a theoretical framework to quantify the expres-
080   sive power of existing DyGNNs. Specifically, we extend the Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) hierarchy
081
      tests and propose the k-dimensional Dynamic WL (DWL) tests (k ≥ 1) as the referencing algo-
      rithms to check the isomorphism on dynamic graphs. We demonstrate that the expressive power
082
      of existing DyGNNs is upper bounded by the proposed 1-DWL test. To enhance the expressive
083
      power of existing DyGNNs, we propose the Multi-Interacted Time Encoding (MITE), which en-
084   codes the bi-interaction history of target node pairs with other nodes, thereby capturing the indirect
085   dependencies between target node pairs. MITE is a plug-and-play module that can be seamlessly
086   integrated into a wide range of models. Equipped with MITE, we introduce the Dynamic Graph
087   Neural Network with High-order expressive power (HopeDGN), which updates the representations
088   of target node pairs by aggregating their neighboring node pairs as well as the multi-interaction
089   history. Our theoretical results demonstrate that HopeDGN can achieve expressive power equiv-
090   alent to the 2-DWL test with injective aggregation and updating functions. We further present a
091   Transformer-based implementation of the local version of the proposed HopeDGN. Experimental
092
      results demonstrate that the proposed HopeDGN achieves superior performance on seven datasets
      compared to other baselines, underscoring the effectiveness of the proposed HopeDGN. In summary,
093
      the main contributions of this work are three-fold:
094
095          • We establish a theoretical framework to quantify the expressive power of DyGNNs, and
096            prove that the expressive power of existing DyGNNs is upper bounded by the 1-DWL test.
097          • We propose HopeDGN which can achieve expressive power equivalent to the 2-DWL test,
098            thus being provably and quantitatively more expressive than existing DyGNNs.
099
             • Extensive experiments on both link prediction and node classification tasks demonstrate
100
               the superiority of the proposed HopeDGN over existing models.

105   Dynamic Graphs Neural Networks. A dynamic graph is a network whose topological structure
106   or node attributes evolve over time. Depending on whether the timestamps are discrete or con-
107   tinuous, dynamic graphs can be categorized into Discrete-Time Dynamic Graphs (DTDGs) and
      Continuous-Time Dynamic Graphs (CTDGs). Existing studies on DTDGs typically integrate Graph

      Neural Networks (GNNs) with sequential models to learn structural representations of graph snap-
109   shots and their evolution patterns (Yu et al., 2017; Sankar et al., 2020; You et al., 2022; Zhu et al.,
110   2023; Zhang et al., 2023b). Some approaches also utilize sequential models to update the weights
111   of GNNs (Pareja et al., 2020). Recently, CTDGs have emerged as a more general form of dynamic
112   graphs, garnering increasing attention from the research community. Most existing works on CT-
113   DGs adopt an aggregation-then-update framework (see Section 3) (Wen & Fang, 2022; Souza et al.,
114   2022). Various aggregation techniques have been proposed, including local self-attention (Xu et al.,
115   2020), Transformers (Yu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), and MLP-mixers (Cong et al., 2023).
116   Memory mechanisms have also been employed to retain long-term interaction information (Kumar
117
      et al., 2019; Trivedi et al., 2019; Rossi et al., 2020). Additionally, some studies leverage temporal
      random walks to learn representations (Wang et al., 2021b; Jin et al., 2022). Compared to existing
118
      works, the proposed HopeDGN learns representations of node pairs rather than individual nodes.
119
      More importantly, the proposed HopeDGN achieves the equivalent expressive power of the 2-DWL
120   test, which is significantly more powerful than existing DyGNNs.
121
122
123   Expressive power of GNNs. The expressive power of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) is mea-
124   sured by their ability to distinguish non-isomorphic graphs. Since the seminal works of Xu et al.
125
      (2019) and Morris et al. (2019) demonstrated that the expressive power of message-passing based
      GNNs is upper-bounded by the 1-WL test, extensive efforts have been made to enhance the ex-
126
      pressive power of GNNs. Some methods proposed high-order GNNs that mimic the procedure
127
      of higher-order WL tests (Maron et al., 2018; 2019b; Azizian & Lelarge, 2020; Geerts & Reut-
128   ter, 2022). Other methods aggregated the learned node representations on pre-generated subgraphs
129   (Cotta et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021; Bevilacqua et al., 2021). Furthermore, some works incorpo-
130   rated substructure information into the learning of node representations (Chen et al., 2020; Bouritsas
131   et al., 2022; Horn et al., 2021). Zhang et al. (2023a) also proposed evaluating the expressive power
132   of GNNs via graph biconnectivity. While the expressive power of static GNNs has been extensively
133   studied, few works have investigated the expressive power of Dynamic GNNs (DyGNNs). Souza
134   et al. (2022) proposed a DyGNN with an expressive power equivalent to the 1-Temporal WL test,
135   further enhanced by relative position features. Gao & Ribeiro (2022) studied the equivalent expres-
136
      sive power of two types of dynamic graphs, namely time-then-graph and time-and-graph. Despite
      these efforts, DyGNNs with quantifiable high-order expressiveness are still lacking.

306   respectively. Note that MITE degenerates to NCOE by setting f in Eq. (7) to output the number of
307   non-infinity elements. Compared to NCOE, MITE additionally captures the timestamps information
308   of bi-interaction, which contains richer semantic information.

356
      In this section, we present the implementation details of local HopeDGN. Considering that some
357   interactions may happen long time ago, we leverage Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) as the back-
358   bone due to its capability of modeling long-term dependency.
359
360   Neighborhood Encodings. Let DG = {V, E} be a dynamic graph, and its node feature and edge
361   feature are denoted as X ∈ R|V|×dN and E ∈ R|E|×dE , respectively. Based on Eq. (8), given
362   the target node pair s = (u, v) at time t, we need to aggregate the joint neighborhood of u and
363   v, denoted as N (u, t) and N (v, t), respectively. Here we only learn from the one-hop joint neigh-
364   borhood for efficiency of computation. For each (w, t′ ) ∈ N (u, t) ∪ N (v, t), the combined node
365   encodings of w is represented as XC,w = [Xw ||Xu ||Xv ] ∈ RdC . The edge encodings of (w, t′ )
366   are retrieved from E, denoted as XE,w ∈ RdE . Following Xu et al. (2020), the time encoding
367   of w is learned
                p      by applying random Fourier feature on time interval ∆t = t − t′ , computed as
368   XT,w = 2/dT [cos(w1 ∆t), sin(w1 ∆t), ..., cos(wdT /2 ∆t), sin(wdT /2 ∆t)] ∈ RdT . The MITE of
369   w with respect to (u, v) is denoted as XB,w ∈ RdB (Section 4.2). The corresponding encodings
370   of the complete joint neighborhood are denoted as XC ∈ RS×dC , XE ∈ RS×dE , XT ∈ RS×dT ,
371   XM ∈ RS×dM with S = |N (u, t) ∪ N (v, t)|.
372
373   Patching Technique. Since the length of joint neighborhood may be very large, inspired by Doso-

376   as an example. XC ∈ RS×dC will be reshaped into RNp ×(P ·dC ) with Np = ⌈S/P ⌉ (neighborhood
377   sequence is padded if S cannot be divided by P ). Similarly, XE , XT and XM will be reshaped

424

428   randomly select 10% of nodes from the test set as masking nodes. The interactions associated with

430   tions involving masking nodes only during the validation and testing phases. Average Precision (AP)

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
526
527
      In this work, we propose a novel DyGNN framework that can achieve provable and quantifiable
528   high-order expressive power. We propose the k-Dynamic WL (DWL) tests to quantify the expres-
529   sive power of DyGNNs. We underscore that the expressive power of existing DyGNNs is bounded
530   by the proposed 1-DWL test, which limits their capabilities to capture significant evolving patterns.
531   To address this limitation, we propose HopeDGN, which learns node-pair level representation by
532   aggregating interaction histories with neighboring node-pairs. We prove that HopeDGN can achieve
533   expressive power equivalent to the 2-DWL test. We present a Transformer-based implementation for
534   the local variant HopeDGN. Experiments on link prediction and node classification tasks demon-
535   strate the effectiveness of HopeDGN.
536   There are some promising future directions for this work. Firstly, the expressive power of the pro-
537   posed HopeDGN is bounded by 2-DWL test. It remains an open problem of designing DyGNNs
538   with higher order expressiveness than 2-DWL test. Secondly, some recent works study the expres-
539   siveness of GNNs via alternative metrics beyond WL test such as graph bi-connectivity (Zhang et al.,
      2023a). Designing DyGNNs based on other metrics beyond DWL test may bring novel insights.

      A     A N INTRODUCTION OF W EISFEILER -L EHMAN TEST
757
758
      In this section, we briefly introduce the Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) test. The WL tests for graph
759   isomorphism (Leman & Weisfeiler, 1968; Xu et al., 2019) are effective algorithms that have been
760   proven capable of discriminating a broad class of non-isomorphic graphs.
761
762   1-WL test. Given a G = {V, E} with a labeling function l, at iteration 0, the 1-WL test initializes
763   the color of each node c(0) = l. At iteration j > 0, the node color is refined as:
764
765                           c(j) (u) = HASH(c(j−1) (u), {{c(j−1) (v)|v ∈ N (u)}})                        (13)
766   where HASH is a hashing function and {{·}} denotes the multiset. To test whether two graphs G and
767   G ′ are isomorphic, we run 1-WL test on both graphs in parallel. If the multisets of node colors in
768   two graphs are not equal at any iteration, the 1-WL test concludes that G and G ′ are non-isomorphic.
769
      Due to the limited expressive power of 1-WL test, the k-dimensional Weisfeiler-Lehman tests (k ≥
770
      2) are proposed to serve as more powerful algorithms for checking graph isomorphism. In the
771   literature, there exists two variants of k-WL test, known as Folklore k-WL test (k-FWL) (Cai et al.,
772   1992) and Oblivious k-WL test (k-OWL) (Grohe, 2021). We will introduce the details of them.
773
774   k-FWL test. Given a graph G = {V, E}, and let s = (v1 , ..., vk ) ∈ [V]k be a k-node tuple. Let
775   c(j) : [V]k → N be a k-node tuple coloring function at iteration j. At iteration 0, two tuples s and s′
776   get the same color if there exists a isomorphism between s and s′ . Then at iteration j (j ≥ 1), the
777   color of s is updated as follows:
778
                                 c(j) (s) = HASH c(j−1) (s), {{ϕ(j−1) (s, w)|w ∈ V}}

779
                                                                                                         (14)
780                       ϕ(j−1) (s, w) = c(j−1) r1 (s, w) , ..., c(j−1) rk (s, w)

781
782   where ri (s, w) = (v1 , ..., vi−1 , w, vi+1 , ..., vk ). Here the neighboring node tuples of s is obtained
783   by replacing each element in s with other nodes. We run the algorithm on two graphs in parallel. If
784   two color multisets are not equal at any iteration, the k-FWL test will output that these two graphs
785   are non-isomorphic. The algorithm terminates if c(j) (s) = c(j) (s′ ) ⇐⇒ c(j+1) (s) = c(j+1) (s′ )
786   holds for all s, s′ ∈ [V]k .
787
788   k-OWL test. At iteration j (j ≥ 1), k-OWL test has a slightly different update rule for the colors
789   of s ∈ [V]k :
790
                     c(j) (s) = HASH c(j−1) (s), M (j−1) (s)

791                                                                                                 (15)
                 M (j−1) (s) = {{c(j−1) r1 (s, w) |w ∈ V}}, ..., {{c(j−1) rk (s, w) |w ∈ V}}

792
793
794   Note that 1-OWL test and 2-OWL test have the same expressive power, and (k + 1)-OWL test has
795
      the same expressive power as the k-FWL test for k ≥ 2 (Grohe, 2021). The reason why k-FWL test
      inherits more expressive power than k-OWL test is that k-FWL firstly groups the color of k-node
796
      tuple based on replacing nodes then makes aggregation, while k-OWL aggregates the colors for the
797
      single replacing node.

1062   students and course content units. Each link indicates a student’s access to a specific content unit
1063   and is characterized by a 4-dimensional feature.

1065
       LastFM. LastFM6 is a bipartite dataset that contains information about which songs were listened
1066   to by which users over the course of one month. In this dataset, users and songs are represented as

1069
       CanParl. Canparl7 is a dynamic political network documenting the interactions between Canadian
1070
       Members of Parliament (MPs) from 2006 to 2019. In this network, each node represents an MP from
