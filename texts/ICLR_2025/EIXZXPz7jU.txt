011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Singularities in the source functions of partial differential equations (PDEs) can
014            pose significant challenges for physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), of-
015            ten leading to numerical instability and necessitating a large number of sampling
016            points thereby increasing the computational time. In this paper, we introduce a
017            novel sampling point selection method to address these challenges. Our approach
018            is based on diffusion models capable of generative sampling from the distribution
019
               of PDE residuals. Specifically, we apply the optimal transport coupling flow-
               matching technique to generate more sampling points in regions where the PDE
020
               residuals are higher, enhancing the accuracy and efficiency of the solution. In
021
               contrast to existing approaches in the literature, our method avoids explicit mod-
022            eling of the probability density proportional to residuals, instead using the bene-
023            fits of flow matching to generate novel and probable samples from more complex
024            distributions, thereby enhancing PINN solutions for problems with singularities.
025            We demonstrate that this method, in certain scenarios, outperforms existing tech-
026            niques such as normalizing flow-based sampling PINN. Especially, our approach
027            demonstrates effectiveness in improving the solution quality for the linear elastic-
028            ity equation in the case of material with complex geometry of inclusion. A detailed
029            comparison of the flow matching sampling method with other approaches is also
030
               provided.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) are used to solve Partial Differential Equations (PDEs)
036
      using neural networks. With the rapid development of computing resources and machine learning
037   algorithms, PINNs have become popular for a wide range of realistic simulations Raissi et al. (2019)
038   . PINNs utilize automatic differentiation mechanisms to encode PDEs into loss functions, incorpo-
039   rating PDE residuals and boundary conditions. PINNs may be preferred over classical numerical
040   solvers due to their easy coding algorithms for both forward and inverse problems, and their ability
041   to handle high-dimensional problems. Despite the widespread success of PINNs in various PDE-
042   related problems they often struggle with complex PDEs, leading to ”failure modes” Wang et al.
043   (2022). Specifically, PINN loss function is very non-convex making it challenging to find a global
044   minimum using conventional optimization algorithms for neural network training, such as Adam.
045
      Moreover, according to the F-principle Xu (2020), in PINNs the low frequency features of the solu-
      tion are captured emerge first, while it will take several training epochs to reproduce high frequency
046
      features. In this regard, PINNs may be not efficient for solving PDEs with high-frequency solu-
047
      tions, as shown in Chuprov et al. (2023). For simple PDEs (single-scale, single-mode), conventional
048   PINNs can quickly achieve satisfactory solutions Buzaev et al. (2023). However, for more complex
049   PDEs, conventional PINNs often fall short as the low-frequency global solution deviates from the
050   exact solution. To address these issues, recent years have seen the emergence of efficient imple-
051   mentations of the PINNs method. For instance, loss re-weighting methods McClenny & Braga-Neto
052   (2023) and adaptive sampling strategies Gao et al. (2023) have been developed to find a balance be-
053   tween loss and probability distribution on weight or sampling, enhancing the performance of PINNs
      in complex scenarios.

      The goal of this paper is to design a novel method of point sampling to address these challenges.

057   more sampling points in regions where the PDE residuals have large values, enhancing the accuracy
058   and efficiency of the solution.

      3.1   A DAPTIVE SAMPLING AND ITS RELATION TO GENERATIVE LEARNING
089
090   The easiest method for selecting points is a mesh grid (regular grid), that is often used in finite
091   difference schemes. However, in Wu et al. (2023) it was shown that this approach can potentially
092   yield trivial solutions and that the PINN solution derived on a uniform grid is more accurate than
093   that obtained on a mesh grid. In addition, sampling based on pseudo-random series (e.g. Sobol
094   sequences, Latin hypercube etc) can be utilized.
095
      Adaptive sampling methods are based on the principle to select points based on their influence on
096   the loss function. One of the algorithms that pioneered this approach is the so called Residual Aided
097   Refinement (RAR) algorithm Lu et al. (2021). The RAR algorithm aims to improve the distribution
098   of residual points during training by introducing additional points in areas where the PDE loss values
099   are large after a certain number of iterations. An advanced version of RAR is called residual adaptive
100   distribution algorithm: RAD Wu et al. (2023). The PINN training begins with uniformly distributed
101   points. After a few iterations, residual values are evaluated, and new points are added in areas
102   with high residuals. The PINN model is retrained with the updated set of points, and this process
103   is repeated to improve accuracy. This algorithm is similar to the classical importance sampling
104
      method, an extension of Monte-Carlo methods.
105   The algorithm that apply importance sampling idea to estimation of loss and sampling points for
106   PINN is Nabian et al. (2021), where they propose to a special proposal distribution that is based
107   on calculation of residual error at nearest to a point specially selected seed points. For instance,
      the proposal is a distribution that is a PDE residual loss at nearest seed point divided by sum of all


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      residual values. It means that points with larger values of PDE residual are more likely to be added
109   to a batch. A similar methods are presented in Nabian et al. (2021).
110
111   One of the first methods introduced for sampling for variance reduction purposes was normalizing
112
      flows. In image generation such methods as GANs Goodfellow et al. (2020), VAEs and diffusion
      models can be used to digest data distribution and get a new sample from the population. In Bond-
113
      Taylor et al. (2021) it was shown that GANs generation can achieve higher quality for high resolution
114
      images than VAEs. However, GANs can be unstable due to mode covering problem while VAEs are
115   able to cover all modes of distribution.
116
117   Diffusion models Song et al. (2020) do not suffer from mode collapse and can beat GAN models
118
      in image quality. They are based on the idea of iterative refinement of an input noise signal until
      it converges to a specific data distribution, such as an image. Diffusion models are trained by the
119
      forward process of incremental noise injection into data image and sampling represents the inverse
120
      process of image generation from noise. However, they require a substantial time for training Xiao
121   et al. (2021) as compared to GANs and VAEs.
122
123   To improve the inference time of diffusion models with minimal depreciation in quality flow match-
124
      ing for image generation, a method Lipman et al. (2022) was introduced that is based on the refine-
      ment of loss function of the continuous normalizing flows Mathieu & Nickel (2020). This model
125
      do not implicitly approximate the probability distribution but can produce high quality samples at
126
      reasonable time. Flow matching is more stable during training because of its loss function, making
127   it a preferable choice for generation tasks compared to score-based diffusion methods, especially for
128   low-dimensional data such as points of collocation for training PINNs. This is the reason why this
129   model was used as a tool to add samples that move in the direction of large residual regions.
130

      3.2   A DAPTIVE SAMPLING STRATEGIES FOR PINN S
132
133   In Tang et al. (2023a), the so called DAS PINN was proposed, in which a normalizing flow was
134   applied for adaptive PDE residual sampling for solution of Poisson equation with singular source
135   peaks, while in Wang et al. (2024a) a similar approach was used for a cavity flow problem. In
136   Tang et al. (2023b) the Wassertein GAN-like model (WGAN) was proposed to solve the Poisson
137   equation with narrow peaks in the source function. It generalizes a sampling approach for any
138   normalized residual distribution pα but also uses the KR-net architecture same, as in Tang et al.
139   (2023a). The main difference of AAS-PINN Tang et al. (2023b) from DAS-PINN is that AAS PINN
140
      learns the distribution of residual with WGAN like a loss function that also applies regularization to
      the gradient of pα : ∇pα , while Tang et al. (2023a) uses KL divergence loss.
141
142   Due to the fact that KR net model for probability density function (PDF) approximation is invertible,
143   it implicitly models PDF. However let us note that it may face difficulties to approximate more
144   complex probability density functions of points with large residual. Moreover, as it is articulated in
145   Wang et al. (2024b) due to the fact that normalizing flows preserve the topology of the input space
146
      through continuous transformations, they face difficulties in representing certain simple classes of
      function Dupont et al. (2019).
147
148   This property leads to limited representation capabilities, high computational costs, and training
149   problems in practical implementations Ho et al. (2019). That is why in this paper, we decided to
150   use an architecture different from KR-net and other normalizing flow architectures that represent
151   invertible transformations for our proposed method.
152
153   3.3   N ORMALIZING F LOW PINN
154
155   In Tang et al. (2023a), a model was developed, which integrates the PINN training with sampling
156   from a normalizing flow. Subsequently, the flow model is refined through the minimization of
157   the cross entropy loss between the residual and the flow’s output logarithmic density. The flow
158
      model, implemented as KR-net, is constructed with affine coupling layers and the Knothe-Rosenblatt
      rearrangement. This architecture is uniquely designed to calculate both the forward and inverse
159
      probability density.
160
161   The optimization problem for the flow model is articulated as minimizing the Kullback-Leibler (KL)
      divergence between the residual function and the probability density generated by the flow. This

519   5    C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this paper a novel approach referred to as flow-matching sampling is proposed. It allows to select
522   points for PINNs training, at which the evaluation of the PDE residual is performed. The idea of the
523   method is based on the generative matching flows and adaptive sampling.
524   The numerical experiments show that our approach helps to solve singular problems and enhance
525   the solution. We have examined an efficiency of the proposed method for the Poisson equation and
526   linear elasticity equation system. It has been shown that the proposed method in several cases allow
527   to achieve more accurate solution than the normalization flow approach. The latter can be considered
528   as the closest competitor of the flow-matching method. It has been shown that the flow-matching
529   method is efficient in the case of singularities in the solution. In our future work we will examine
530
      this method on larger number of epochs.
