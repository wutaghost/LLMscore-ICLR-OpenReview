009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Retrieving a specific moment from an untrimmed video via a text description is a
012            central problem in vision-language learning. It is a challenging task due to the so-
013            phisticated temporal dependency among moments. Existing methods fail to deal
014            with this issue well since they establish temporal relations of moments in a way
015            that visual content and semantics are coupled. This paper studies temporal de-
016
               pendence schemes that decouple content and semantic information, establishing
               semantic-enhanced Dual Temporal Adjacent Maps for video moment retrieval,
017
               conferred as DTAM. Specifically, DTAM designs two branches to encode visual
018
               appearance and semantic knowledge from video clips respectively, where knowl-
019            edge from the appearance branch is distilled into the semantic branch to help
020            DTAM distinguish features with the same visual content but different semantics
021            with a well-designed semantic-aware contrastive loss. Besides, we also develop
022            a moment-aware mechanism to assist temporal adjacent maps’ learning for better
023            video grounding. Finally, extensive experimental results and analysis demonstrate
024            the superiority of the proposed DTAM over existing state-of-the-art approaches
025            on three challenging video moment retrieval benchmarks, i.e., TACoS, Charades-
026            STA, and ActivityNet Captions.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Retrieving moments in an untrimmed video based on text descriptions involves joint reasoning about
031   visual, linguistic and temporal dependency. It therefore remains a challenging problem in video
032   understanding. With the rapid development of vision-language learning, video moment retrieval
033   has achieved significant advances, including applications in video question answering (Wang et al.,
034   2024a; Xiao et al., 2024a), interactive video editing (Tu et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024), and intelligent
035   personal assistants (Nasri et al., 2023). Specifically, the video moment retrieval task aims to localize
036   segments based on a given text query. For example, given a description “A man pushes a child on
037   the swing set of a large park area” as shown in Fig. 1 (Query A) and a paired video, this task is
038   asked to retrieve the best matching moment from the video.
039   Prevailing solutions for video moment retrieval have achieved remarkable advances and mainly fall
040   into two groups, i.e., two-stage (Zhang et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2019; Chen &
041   Jiang, 2019; Zhang et al., 2021b) and one-stage (Mu et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2020; Lin et al.,
042   2023; Lei et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2019a). The former generates candidate moments by operating
043   well-designed sliding windows on raw videos. Then each candidate moment is combined with the
044   corresponding text description to determine whether they are matched. Apparently, such a pipeline is
045
      inspired by object detection techniques for images, but ignores the flexibility and complexity of mo-
      ment description, resulting in an inability to encode temporal dependencies between moments that
046
      are crucial for moment retrieval. For example, as displayed in Fig. 1 (Query C) “The man pushes
047
      the swing to get the child started again”, the sentence query requires algorithms to realize that the
048   action happened twice. As a result, two-stage models will generate false positives since each candi-
049   date moment is independent and the model only observes part of the video. To address this problem,
050   one-stage approaches attempt to localize moments directly. Especially, some efforts (Zhang et al.,
051   2020; Gao et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021a; Soldan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021) introduce tempo-
052   ral adjacent maps to represent candidate moments with their coordinates naturally. Such temporal
053   adjacent networks can cover diverse moments with various lengths while expressing their adjacent
      relations, which is suitable for capturing temporal dependencies among moments. In this fashion,

110   2.1   T EMPORAL ACTION L OCALIZATION
111
112   Temporal action localization aims to localize and identify action instances in untrimmed videos.
113   The representative paradigms include two-stage temporal detection pipelines (Lin et al., 2021; Shi
114   et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2020) and one-stage single-shot methods (Wang et al., 2023;
115   2024c; Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022c; Rizve et al., 2023). Specifically, the two-stage pipeline
116   first generates candidate action instances along the temporal dimension, and then the boundaries
117
      of these candidate regions are further refined via well-designed regressors. Instead, the one-stage
      methods directly predict action probability scores of each video clip. After that, some well-designed
118
      aggregation operations are utilized to generate instance-level results. However, the action categories
119
      of this task are limited to a predefined set and fail to deal with complex and open-set actions in real
120   scenarios. As a consequence, moment retrieval with natural language query (Anne Hendricks et al.,
121   2017; Gao et al., 2017) is proposed to alleviate this problem.
122
123   2.2   V IDEO AND L ANGUAGE C ROSS -M ODAL F USION
124
125   Video and language cross-modal fusion is a fundamental problem for multi-modal video understand-
126   ing, which plays an important role in many downstream tasks (Liu et al., 2022b; Chen et al., 2024;
127   Wang et al., 2024b; Wang & Chen, 2021). Different from image-language fusion, video-language
128   cross-modal fusion involves the complicated temporal context of video and semantic interactions
129
      between video representations and their corresponding referring expressions, which is a fairly in-
      tractable problem. Existing solutions mainly include sequential modeling (Chen et al., 2018; Zhang
130
      et al., 2019c) and attention-based strategy (Lei et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023; Barrios et al., 2023; Mu
131
      et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023; Soldan et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021a). For sequential modeling, the
132   core idea is to temporally align video and text descriptions using recurrent neural networks. Chen
133   et al. (Chen et al., 2018) propose this paradigm for the first time, and attempt to capture evolving
134   fine-grained frame-by-word interactions between video and text. Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2019c)
135   further investigate solutions based on a bidirectional GRU to align video-text features. For attention-
136   based strategy, algorithms employ the attention mechanism to adaptively fuse video features with
137   referring expressions. Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2019b) and Hendricks et al. (Hendricks et al.,
138   2018) adopt a hard attention strategy to refine visual representations based on text descriptions. In-
139   stead, some efforts investigate the soft attention paradigm (Xu et al., 2023; Barrios et al., 2023; Mu
140
      et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023; Soldan et al., 2021) for cross-modal representation fusion. For instance,
      MH-DETR (Xu et al., 2023) adopts a combination of self-attention and cross-attention to emphasize
141
      the aspects of the visual content most relevant to the textual semantics. Barrios et al. (Barrios et al.,
142
      2023) explore a multi-modal attention mechanism to guide feature fusion. In this paper, we utilize
143   a simple multiplication operation for efficient and effective multi-modal feature fusion, instead of
144   expensive attention paradigms or complicated temporal strategies.
145
146   2.3   V IDEO M OMENT R ETRIEVAL
147
148   Existing popular methods of video moment retrieval can be roughly divided into two categories, i.e.,
149   one-stage and two-stage. One-stage methods directly retrieve moments without proposal generation.
150   Some algorithms predict the onset and offset probabilities (Liu et al., 2021b; Nan et al., 2021; Zhao
151   et al., 2021; Zhang & Radke, 2022) or optimize anchors (Wang et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2022; Zhang
152   et al., 2019a) for each moment. Another line of work utilizes learnable semantic queries (Lin et al.,
153
      2023; Lei et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2024) and global representations (Li et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021;
      Li et al., 2021) to infer accurate moment boundaries. Inspired by point-based object detection (Tian
154
      et al., 2022), some works treat moment proposals as points (Mu et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2020; Fang
155
      et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2020). The moment boundaries are regressed relative to
156   these points. Although efficient, the retrieval performance of one-stage methods is unsatisfactory.
157   In contrast, two-stage approaches first generate candidate segment proposals, then assess their “ac-
158   tionness” and optionally refine their temporal boundary. These researches (Anne Hendricks et al.,
159   2017; Gao et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2024) sample candidate proposals using dense sliding windows
160   and evaluate them independently. To capture the dynamics of videos, temporal feature modeling is
161   studied, such as adopting RNN (Rodriguez et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2019) and graphs (Sun et al.,
      2023; Zheng et al., 2023) to explore the dynamic relations of videos. Recently, some efforts (Ma

301   3.4   L EARNING S EMANTIC T EMPORAL A DJACENT M APS

303   In this paper, we argue that the retrieved fragments corresponding to the same query should have
304   similar semantics. To achieve this goal, a semantic-aware contrastive loss is developed to encourage
305   normalized embeddings of clips corresponding to the same query to be pulled closer together, while
306   embeddings corresponding to different queries are repelled apart. Formally, the knowledge from
307
      the appearance branch is distilled to instruct the semantic branch’s learning. For a given video,
      valid moments with a score from the Pa higher than a pre-defined threshold σa are regarded as
308
      positive samples, while moments with a score lower than a threshold σb = 21 (1 − σa ) are regarded
309
      as negative samples. Besides, the center of these positive samples serves as an anchor. Then, we
310   apply the InfoNCE loss on the normalized embedding anchors and positives against the embedding
311   of negative samples:

343   3.5      M OMENT- AWARE M ECHANISM
344
345   To enhance the semantics of temporal adjacent maps, we propose a novel moment-aware mechanism
346   to emphasize the importance of each moment, whose visualization can be found in Appendix A.1.
347   Given a temporal adjacent map M ∈ RN ×N ×d , each element Mij indicates a candidate moment
348   starting from the clip vi and ending at vj . The core idea is to evaluate each clip’s importance as the
349   beginning and end of a moment. Specifically, we squeeze the values of M along the first dimension
350
      with an average operation and acquire Qs ∈ R1×N ×d , representing the likelihood that each clip
      is the start of a moment. Similarly, we squeeze the values of M along the second dimension with
351
      an average operation and acquire Qe ∈ RN ×1×d , representing the likelihood that each clip is the
352
      end of a moment. Then, Qs and Qe are fed into a squeeze module of “Conv-ReLU-Conv-Sigmoid”,
353   leading to the attention scores Q′s ∈ R1×N ×1 and Q′e ∈ RN ×1×1 . Afterwards, they are applied on
354   the original M to yield the refined temporal adjacent map as follows:
355
                                                             M′ = Q′s ⊗ M + Q′e ⊗ M,                                                                         (8)
356
357   where ⊗ indicates element-wise multiplication with a broadcast mechanism in Python. In this fash-
358   ion, we learn explicitly semantic-enhanced temporal adjacent maps by perceiving moment impor-
359   tance, and the effectiveness is also verified and analyzed in experiments.
360
361   3.6      OVERALL L OSS F UNCTION
362
      To optimize the proposed DTAM during training, the overall loss function is formulated as:
363
364                                                            Lall = Lscore + λLnce ,                                                                       (9)
365   where λ is a hyper-parameter that controls the importance of different terms.

      Each video has an average duration of two minutes and ∼3.6 text queries. We follow (Zhang et al.,

424   various temporal intersection-over-union thresholds θ (R@k, tIoU=θ) for all datasets. It measures
425   the percentage of text queries with at least one of the top-k moment predictions whose temporal
426   overlap with the ground-truth moment is larger than θ.

428   Zisserman, 2017) features as visual representations for Charades-STA, and C3D (Tran et al., 2015)
429   features as visual representations for ActivityNet Captions and TACoS. LSTM is applied to extract
430   language representations. The size of all hidden states is 512 in DTAM. Besides, the number of
431   sampled video clips is set to 16 for Charades-STA, 64 for ActivityNet Captions, and 128 for TACoS.
      For the ConvNet block, L = 8 and K = 3 are adopted for Charades-STA and TACoS, and L = 4 and

      4.2   C OMPARISON WITH S TATE - OF - THE -A RT M ETHODS
439
440   In this section, we conduct extensive experiments on three challenging video moment retrieval
441   benchmarks, i.e., Charades-STA, ActivityNet Captions, and TACoS, and report R@k with the tIoU
442   threshold θ. Specifically, k ∈ {1, 5} and θ ∈ {0.3, 0.5} are adopted for TACoS and k ∈ {1, 5} and
443   θ ∈ {0.5, 0.7} are adopted for Charades-STA and ActivityNet Captions. We compare the retrieval
444   performance of the proposed DTAM with state-of-the-art methods under the same settings.
445   Comparisons with state-of-the-art methods on the TACoS dataset are shown in Table 1. Benefiting
446   from the proposed dual temporal adjacent maps and semantic-enhanced mechanism, we observe
447   that DTAM achieves the best retrieval performance on all evaluation criteria. Compared to previous
448   best practices, DTAM achieves a 1.8% R@1 (against MATN (Zhang et al., 2021a)) and 2.1% R@5
449   (against VLG-Net (Soldan et al., 2021)) improvements at tIoU=0.3. It also acquires 5.7% R@1
450   (against MATN (Zhang et al., 2021a)) and 1.8% R@5 (against MATN (Zhang et al., 2021a)) im-
451   provements at tIoU=0.5. Besides, we also conduct experiments on a more challenging ActivityNet-
452
      Caption benchmark, and results are displayed in Table 2. Compared with state-of-the-art approaches,
      DTAM also achieves the best retrieval performance on all evaluation criteria. Compared to previ-
453
      ous best practices, DTAM achieves a 1.1% R@1 (against MGSL-Net (Liu et al., 2022a)) and 1.3%
454
      R@5 (against MGSL-Net (Liu et al., 2022a)) improvements at tIoU=0.5. It also acquires 1.9%
455   R@1 (against G2L (Li et al., 2023)) and 0.5% R@5 (against G2L (Li et al., 2023)) improvements at
456   tIoU=0.7. Furthermore, comparisons with state-of-the-art approaches on the Charades-STA dataset
457   are displayed in Table 3, and the proposed DTAM also achieves superior performance. Specifically,
458   DTAM acquires 0.34 (R@1 at tIoU=0.5), 0.49 (R@1 at tIoU=0.7), and 0.69 (R@5 at tIoU=0.7)
459   absolute percentage points higher than the latest SSRN (Zhu et al., 2023) respectively. Our DTAM
460   is slightly worse than SSRN against R@1 at tIoU=0.5. The reason is that SSRN further performs
461   temporal modeling on the original C3D features of videos. This is not the focus of this article and
462   our focus is on the multi-modal vision-language matching ability of the model.
463   We also investigate the influence of hyper-parameters involved in the loss function, i.e., λ, and
464   results for R@1 at tIoU=0.5 are reported in Fig. 3. We can observe that λ = 0.01, λ = 0.001,
465   and λ = 0.005 are the best choice for TACoS, Charades-STA, and ActivityNet Caption respectively,
466   which are employed as default values for all other experiments in this paper. To evaluate DTAM’s
467   computational efficiency, the training and inference speed on the ActivityNet Caption dataset is
468   tested on a Nvidia Geforce RTX4090 GPU, where the classic algorithms 2D-TAN (Zhang et al.,
469
      2020) and MS-2D-TAN (Zhang et al., 2021b) using 2D temporal adjacent maps are compared as
      baselines. From Table 4 (d), we observe that the complete DTAM outperforms the complex MS-2D-
470
      TAN in both speed and prediction accuracy. At the same time, it only takes almost negligible time
471
      more than 2D-TAN, but it achieves better retrieval accuracy by a large margin.
472
473   Since the core motivation of this paper is learning semantic knowledge, we explore four instruction
474   strategies from the appearance branch into a semantic branch, where the threshold σa is restricted
475
      to vary from 0.8 to 0.5, results are shown in Fig. 3 (d). We observe that a dynamic adjustment of
      σa is better than using a fixed value and also the higher threshold is superior to the lower one in
476
      the initial stage (version with a cosine decline is better than linear decline), which is in line with
477
      our understanding. In the early stages of training, the outputs of model are unreliable, so a higher
478   threshold is required. However, as the model’s capabilities improve, the threshold can be relaxed.
479   Another interesting phenomenon is that the dynamic σa with a cosine decline is slightly superior to
480   using a pre-trained appearance branch with a fixed σa , which reveals that dynamic joint learning of
481   two branches can complement each other.

534
535   In this paper, we proposed to learn semantic-enhanced dual temporal adjacent maps i.e., DTAM, for
536   efficient video moment retrieval. In this paradigm, we investigate temporal dependency schemes that
537   decouple the visual appearance and semantics of moments. Moreover, a novel moment-aware mech-
538   anism is developed to assist temporal adjacent maps’ learning, enhancing discriminability. Finally,
539   extensive experiments reveal the superiority of DTAM over existing methods on three challenging
      video moment retrieval benchmarks, while revealing the utilities of proposed components.
