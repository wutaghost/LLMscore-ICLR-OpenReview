009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In the training of large language models, parameter-efficient techniques such as
012            LoRA optimize memory usage and reduce communication overhead during the fine-
013            tuning phase. However, applying such techniques directly during the pre-training
014            phase results in poor performance, primarily because the premature implementation
015            of low-rank training significantly reduces model accuracy. Existing methods like
016            ReLoRA and GaLore have attempted to address this challenge by updating the
017            low-rank subspace. However, they still fall short of achieving the accuracy of full-
018
               rank training because they must limit the update frequency to maintain optimizer
               state consistency, hindering their ability to closely approximate full-rank training
019
               behavior. In this paper, we introduce SwitchLoRA, a parameter-efficient training
020
               technique that frequently and smoothly replaces the trainable parameters of LoRA
021            adapters with alternative parameters. SwitchLoRA updates the low-rank subspace
022            incrementally, targeting only a few dimensions at a time to minimize the impact
023            on optimizer states. This allows a higher update frequency, thereby enhancing
024            accuracy by enabling the updated parameters to more closely mimic full-rank
025            behavior during the pre-training phase. Our results demonstrate that SwitchLoRA
026            actually surpasses full-rank training, reducing perplexity from 15.23 to 15.01 on
027            the LLaMA 1.3B model while reducing communication overhead by 54% on the
028            LLaMA 1.3B model. Furthermore, after full fine-tuning the SwitchLoRA pre-
029
               trained model and the full-rank pre-trained model on the GLUE benchmark, the
               SwitchLoRA pre-trained model showed an average accuracy gain of about 1% over
030
               the full-rank pre-trained model. This demonstrates enhanced generalization and
031
               reasoning capabilities of SwitchLoRA.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The size of large language models (LLMs) has increased rapidly due to the advent of the transformer
037   architecture Vaswani et al. (2017). To support the training of large models, distributed training
038   techniques such as data parallelism Dean et al. (2012); Li et al. (2014), tensor parallelism Shoeybi et al.
039   (2019), pipeline parallelism Huang et al. (2019); Narayanan et al. (2021) and the Zero Redundancy
040   Optimizer Rajbhandari et al. (2020) have been employed. However, distributed training of trillion-
041   scale models incurs significant inter-node communication overhead from synchronizing extensive
042   parameter gradients across multiple nodes.
043   To address these challenges, various parameter-efficient strategies have been proposed. Techniques
044   such as model sparsification Alistarh et al. (2018); Stich et al. (2018) and progressive model pruning
045   during training Frankle & Carbin (2019) have shown promise. Additionally, methods leveraging
046   Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to approximate full-rank matrices in low-rank spaces have
047   been explored Sui et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2021); Zhao et al. (2023). Beyond the entire training
048   process, several techniques improve adaptability and efficiency during the fine-tuning phase. For
049   example, methods such as the Adapter Houlsby et al. (2019); He et al. (2022) and Prefix-tuning Li &
050
      Liang (2021) introduce additional trainable layers while freezing the remaining parameters.
051   Another noteworthy fine-tuning strategy is Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2022), which
052   introduces no computational overhead during inference while maintaining training accuracy. However,
053   previous studies Wang et al. (2021; 2023); Lialin et al. (2023) have observed that parameter-efficient
      methods such as LoRA perform less efficiently during the pre-training phase because the premature

      2     M ETHODOLOGY
115
116
      A substantial body of research, such as various pruning methods Han et al. (2015); Blalock et al.
117
      (2020), has demonstrated that neural networks tend to exhibit low-rank characteristics after certain
118   stages of training. Techniques for parameter-efficient fine-tuning, such as LoRA, capitalize on
119   this observation. Concurrently, studies like Li et al. (2020); Gunasekar et al. (2017) have revealed
120   that overparameterization in neural networks can lead to implicit regularization, thereby enhancing
121   generalization. These findings underscore the importance of training with full parameters during the
122   initial phase. Further empirical evidence supporting this phenomenon is provided in works like Wang
123   et al. (2021; 2023); Lialin et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2024b). Based on these insights, this section
124   proposes a method designed to train a substantial number of parameters while selectively updating
125   only a portion of the parameters at any one time to reduce communication overhead.
126
127   2.1   L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION (L O RA)
128
      Introduced in Hu et al. (2022), LoRA is designed specifically for the fine-tuning stage of model
129
      training.
130
131   Consider a pre-trained model with a weight matrix W ∈ Rm×n from a specific linear layer. LoRA
132   proposes an innovative modification: transforming W into W + αr BA. Here, B ∈ Rm×r and
133   A ∈ Rr×n are newly introduced matrices, where r is a positive integer significantly smaller than
134   both m and n. And α is a constant hyperparameter, set to r in the following description to clarify
135   the algorithm’s mechanics. Then during fine-tuning, W is kept frozen while matrices B and A are
136
      trained. At the inference stage, BA is added to W which preserves the model’s original structure.
      The matrix A is initialized using Kaiming initialization He et al. (2015), while B is initially set to a
137
      zero matrix to ensure consistency.

173   If we adopt the strategy in LoRA to add BA to W and only update B and A from the pre-training
174   stage, according to equation 2, the rank of updated parameters of the local linear system through
175

177

183

185   When selecting candidate vectors, we have the option to choose randomly from C(B) or C(AT ).
186   Alternatively, we can select candidate vectors sequentially from C(B) or C(AT ), restarting from the
187   beginning once the end of the set is reached. We find that varying the matching orders of vectors bk
188
      and ak yields only minor differences in outcomes. A theoretical explanation for this phenomenon is
      provided in Appendix A. Additionally, to conserve GPU memory, spare candidate vectors can be
189
      offloaded to the CPU.

      Switching frequency As mentioned in Frankle & Carbin (2019); Wang et al. (2021); Lialin et al.
193   (2023), the model initially exhibits full internal rank during pre-training, and the internal rank of
194   each layer decreases progressively over time. Consequently, we have adopted an exponential decay
195   function for the switching frequency, namely f requency = Ce−θstep , where the coefficients are
196   determined empirically. Besides, the selection of LoRA rank r for BA is influenced by the final
197   internal rank of the layers, which has been extensively explored in Hu et al. (2022); Valipour et al.
198   (2023); Zhang et al. (2023b).
199
200
201   Optimizer states resetting Currently Large Language Models (LLMs) predominantly utilize Adam
202   Kingma & Ba (2015) and AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2019) optimizers over SGD, which rely
203   on optimizer states. It is crucial to note that after switching LoRA vectors, the gradients associated
204
      with these parameters are also changed, which prevents the reuse of optimizer states. To address
      this issue, when ak is switched, we reset the optimizer states of bk . And conversely, when bk is
205
      switched, we reset optimizer states of ak . Note that we reset optimizer states of counterpart pair
206
      rather than optimizer states of the switched parameters itself. This approach will be further explained
207   in Appendix A. Additionally, when the optimizer states are reset to zero, we freeze corresponding
208   parameters for N steps to maintain the robustness of the training. In this study, N is set to 5.

      3    R ELATED WORK
248
249
      Direct low-rank factorization method Numerous studies Denton et al. (2014); Tai et al. (2016);
250
      Wen et al. (2017); Idelbayev & Carreira-Perpinán (2020) have demonstrated the effectiveness of using
251   low-rank factorization to approximate the weights of linear layers in deep neural networks. They
252   employ methods such as SVD to achieve a factorization UV that minimizes ∥W − UV∥. Later
253   on, Pufferfish Wang et al. (2021) and subsequent work in Cuttlefish Wang et al. (2023) employ full-
254   rank training prior to low-rank training to enhance efficiency. Additionally, they introduce adaptive
255   strategies to determine the necessary duration of full-rank training and to select the appropriate rank
256   for each linear layer for SVD. Further developments in this field include InRank Zhao et al. (2023),
257   which proposes a low-rank training approach based on greedy low-rank learning Li et al. (2021).
258   Additional research such as Sui et al. (2024) integrates orthogonality into the low-rank models to
259
      enhance training accuracy, while Horváth et al. (2024) introduces low-rank ordered decomposition, a
      generalization of SVD aimed at improving low-rank training efficiency.
260
      These innovations mainly focus on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and smaller-scale language
261
      models.
262
263
      LoRA variants After the introduction of LoRA in Hu et al. (2022), which facilitated fine-tuning
264   with very few trainable parameters, numerous works are proposed to improve the performance of
265   LoRA. Improvements include better initialization strategies for LoRA matrices as demonstrated in
266   Wang et al. (2024); Wang & Liang (2024); Meng et al. (2024). Additionally, Hayou et al. (2024);
267   Kalajdzievski (2023) have adjusted learning rates for B and A to optimize training outcomes. Other
268   research efforts, such as those in Kopiczko et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024), have modified the training
269   process of LoRA. Moreover, some studies, such as Han et al. (2024); Zhao et al. (2024a), focus on
      training models from scratch within a sparse model structure.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Similar to our approach, various LoRA variants employ strategies to increase the rank of updated
271   parameters by merging parameters of adapters into W. For instance, Chain of LoRA Xia et al. (2024)
272   and ReLoRA Lialin et al. (2023) merge BA into W and restart training at regular intervals. ReLoRA
273   enables low-rank training during the early phases, yet it still requires 33% of the steps to be full-rank
274   training. Delta-LoRA Zi et al. (2023), another variant, targets the fine-tuning phase by updating the
275   matrix W using the gradients from the LoRA matrices B and A as they are updated, enhancing
276   accuracy for fine-tuning.
277
278   Other compression methods In addition to previously discussed techniques, there are many
279   other methods to compress models during training. For instance, several studies have introduced
280   quantization to LoRA Dettmers et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023b); Jeon et al. (2024), effectively reducing
281
      memory overhead during fine-tuning. Other research employs iterative pruning and growth techniques
      during training Frankle & Carbin (2019); You et al. (2019); Lym et al. (2019); Evci et al. (2020).
282
      Additionally, some works focus on compressing gradients through quantization Dettmers et al. (2022);
283
      Li et al. (2023a) or gradient projection Zhao et al. (2024b). Notably, Zhao et al. (2024b) presents
284   a recent method for training from scratch that utilizes SVD to project gradients into a periodically
285   updated subspace. This approach also enables the addition of quantization, offering enhanced memory
286   efficiency compared to LoRA.

      5    L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
493
494
      While our results are promising, there are several areas for future exploration. In our experiments, we
495
      have demonstrated that selecting a larger LoRA rank is necessary to achieve accuracy comparable to
496   full-rank training. Additionally, finely tuning the switching frequency of the LoRA vectors presents
497   significant challenges. To address these limitations, we propose the following directions for future
498   work, as illustrated in Figure 5.
499
500          • In our experiments, we simply used exponentially decreasing switching frequencies, which
501            may not be the optimal approach. Guidelines should be developed to help set appropriate
502            switching frequencies throughout the training process.
503          • Going further, a more detailed idea is to examine each layer of the model to adjust the
504            switching frequencies. For instance, LoRA-drop Zhou et al. (2024) evaluates whether the
505            rank is sufficient using a norm of ∆Wx. This is rational because different types of layers,
506            such as the Q, K, V matrices in transformer layers, exhibit significantly varied behaviors.
507          • In our work, we simply chose candidate vectors at random or sequentially. However, during
508            training, all candidates are updated separately, leading to significant differences among them.
509            The selection of these candidates may improve the training outcomes.
510
511
512   6    C ONCLUSIONS
513
514
      In this work, we introduce SwitchLoRA, a novel training strategy designed for parameter-efficient
      pre-training. Our approach achieves comparable accuracy to full-rank training while reducing the
515
      trainable parameters to approximately 50% to 60% of those in traditional full-rank training. Moreover,
516
      the computational overhead and memory usage are nearly identical to those of LoRA when using
517   the same number of trainable parameters. We further validate the reasoning abilities of models
518   trained with SwitchLoRA using the GLUE benchmark. The results from the 1.3B model indicate
519   that SwitchLoRA not only matches but also slightly outperforms full-rank training by about 1% in
520   accuracy.

1100   D    I MPLEMENTATION OF L O RA VECTOR SWITCHING
1101
1102   We discuss the code implementation of SwitchLoRA, focusing on its efficiency and memory con-
1103   sumption.
1104
1105   Implementation Adjustments in Optimizer The primary distinction in the implementation of
1106   SwitchLoRA from conventional approaches lies in its handling of gradients and optimizer states at
1107   the granularity of row or column vectors within matrix parameters. Consider the scenario when using
1108   the AdamW optimizer: typically, each trainable parameter group in AdamW is associated with a
1109   “step” state which is implemented as a float scalar value in the code. To facilitate the resetting of
1110   specific rows or columns in matrices, we modify the type of ”step” in the optimizer to a 32-bit float
1111   matrix with the same shape as the corresponding parameters. In fact, this modification does incur
1112
       some extra memory overhead. An alternative approach would be to implement “step” as a row vector
       for A and a column vector for B. However, this would require more complex code management, and
1113
       thus, we have not adopted this strategy in our implementation. With the capability to manipulate
1114
       optimizer states and gradients at the level of rows and columns, we can now execute operations such
1115   as resetting optimizer states and freezing specific rows or columns of parameter matrices.
1116
1117
       Implementation of the Switching Process We can either randomly select or sequentially select
1118   candidate vectors. However, fragmented operations on a GPU can’t fully utilize its capabilities.
1119   Since several candidate vectors are switched at each step, this will impact training efficiency. As an
1120   example, during the initial phase of SwitchLoRA training for the 1.3B LLaMA model with a LoRA
1121   rank of 512, approximately 512
                                    40 ≈ 13 candidate vectors are switched for each LoRA matrix at every
1122   step.
1123
       By organizing a list of candidate vectors into a matrix and selecting vectors sequentially, we can
1124   perform operations on multiple vectors simultaneously. For example, consider a scenario where we
1125   need to set the values of candidate vectors C(B) at indices 4, 5, 6 to the values of B at indices 7, 8, 9,
1126   respectively. Let C B be a matrix defined as C B ∈ Rm×min(m,n) , where each column C B :, i = C(B)[i]
                                                                  B
1127   for i = 1, . . . , min(m, n). We can then directly assign C:,4:7 = B:,7:10 . This arrangement enables us
1128   to consolidate operations on multiple contiguous indices into a single operation, enhancing efficiency.
1129   Consequently, we employ a sequential selection approach and apply this technique. By implementing
1130   this approach, the switching process now occupies only about 1/40 of the training time during the
1131   initial training phase.
1132
1133   Memory offloading for candidate vectors The use of candidate vectors leads to additional GPU
       memory usage. This memory overhead can be reduced by offloading it to the CPU. The offloading

       process can be decoupled from other training processes. By utilizing non-blocking CPU offloading, we
1135   can handle both offloading and other training processes in parallel, which can be readily implemented
1136   using frameworks like PyTorch.

       The amount of parameters offloaded at each step is approximately switch f req ×
       lora rank/hidden dim × total param. For the 1.3B LLaMA model, using 16-bit precision for
1139
       model parameters, this translates to: 1/40 × 512/2048 × 1.3e9 × 2 bytes ≈ 16.25M B.
