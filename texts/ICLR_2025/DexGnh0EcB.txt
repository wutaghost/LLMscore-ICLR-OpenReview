011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            Mathematical reasoning is a fundamental aspect of intelligence, encompassing a spectrum
014            from basic arithmetic to intricate problem-solving. Recent investigations into the mathemat-
015            ical abilities of large language models (LLMs) have yielded inconsistent and incomplete
016            assessments. In response, we introduce MathEval. A comprehensive benchmark designed
017
               to methodically evaluate the mathematical problem-solving proficiency of LLMs across
               varied contexts, adaptation strategies, and evaluation metrics. MathEval consolidates 22
018
               distinct datasets, encompassing a broad spectrum of mathematical disciplines, languages
019
               (including English and Chinese), and problem categories (ranging from arithmetic and
020            competitive mathematics to higher mathematics), with varying degrees of difficulty from
021            elementary to advanced. In order to surmount the complexities associated with mathemati-
022            cal reasoning output, which lacks a unified pattern for discerning the true answers, and to
023            adapt to the outputs of various models and prompts, we propose the utilization of GPT-4
024            as an automated pipeline for answer extraction and comparison. To broaden the utility of
025            MathEval beyond the scope of GPT-4, we have harnessed the extensive results from GPT-4
026            to train a deepseek-7B-based answer comparison model, enabling precise answer validation
027            for those without access to GPT-4. This model will also be made publicly available. To
028
               mitigate potential test data contamination and truly gauge progress, MathEval incorporates
               an annually refreshed set of problems from the latest Chinese National College Entrance
029
               Examination (Gaokao 2023, Gaokao 2024), thereby benchmarking genuine advancements
030
               in mathematical problem solving skills.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Mathematics stands as a cornerstone to human intelligence (Ahn et al., 2024), encompassing a comprehensive
036   range of abilities from basic arithmetic to reasoning. Recently, there has been a rapid increase in research
037   related to large-scale language models (LLMs) in mathematics, which has swiftly propelled the enhancement
038   of LLMs’ mathematical reasoning abilities. However, the evaluation of these models remains challenging
039   due to three primary issues: “incomprehensiveness”, “inadequate adaptation” to varying model types and
040   datasets, and “inconsistency”.
041
      “Incomprehensiveness” indicates that evaluations often do not cover a wide array of datasets, neglecting
042   factors such as language diversity, problem types, and complexity levels. This limited scope can skew
043   perceptions of a model’s versatility and effectiveness. “Inadequate adaptation” highlights the shortcomings
044   in current evaluations to flexibly accommodate different types of models and datasets. For instance, chat
045   models, which have been fine-tuned during the alignment phase, are especially sensitive to the structure of
046   prompts. Similarly, evaluations should also adapt prompts to fit the specific characteristics of each dataset.

067
068   For example, multiple-choice problems may require prompts that include hints to guide the selection from
069   provided options, whereas math word problems might benefit from prompts that encourage Chain-of-Thought
070   (CoT) reasoning. “Inconsistency” arises when the same model yields different performances on identical
071   datasets, complicating the accurate estimation of its true capabilities. This issue primarily stems from the
072   difficulty in verifying answers to mathematical word problems, where outputs may include reasoning steps,
073   equations, and final answers in various formats (e.g., 1/2 and (\frac{1}{2})). Extending this to different
074
      models and various types of datasets further complicates the evaluation. Rule-based methods for extracting
      and comparing answers, commonly utilized in benchmarks such as OpenCompass (Contributors, 2023)
075
      and HELM (Liang et al., 2023), often lack robustness. Even minor modifications can significantly alter
076
      the evaluation outcomes, making it impractical to tailor these rules for each specific model and dataset.
077   Consequently, standardizing the process of extracting and comparing outputs continues to pose a significant
078   challenge in benchmark evaluations. More related works are discussed in Section 4.
079
080
      To address these challenges, we introduce MathEval, a comprehensive and unified benchmarking framework,
      as illustrated in Figure 1. MathEval incorporates 22 datasets in both Chinese and English, covering a wide
081
      range of mathematical problems from primary to high school levels, and includes a dynamically updated
082
      dataset to prevent test data contamination. Each dataset is meticulously categorized; for instance, the classic
083   GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) dataset represents the math scenario of English, math word problems, and
084   primary school tasks. To tackle the adaptation challenge, MathEval employs tailored prompts suitable for
085   various models and datasets, ranging from zero-shot to few-shot settings. This ensures a thorough assessment
086   of each model’s capabilities across diverse problem sets, promoting a fair comparison of mathematical
087   abilities across models. MathEval leverages GPT-4 for both answer extraction and comparison, thereby
088   avoiding the complexities of regular expression rules and setting a consistent evaluation standard. We have
089   validated GPT-4’s effectiveness by comparing its outputs against human-annotated result, with only minimal
090   discrepancies noted. To our knowledge, this is the first comprehensive benchmark specifically designed to
091
      evaluate the mathematical capabilities of LLMs holistically. We have evaluated 52 models across 22 datasets
      under varied adaptation conditions, making the results publicly accessible .

163   2.2   P ROMPTS A DAPTATION
164
165   As shown in Figure 3, the process involves four stages: model and datasets preparation, template encapsulation,
166   computing scheduling, and answer generation.
167   Model and Dataset Preparation: This phase encompasses the establishment of model and dataset configu-
168   rations for the ensuing stages. Users have the option to employ their own dataset and model configurations to
169   expand the current benchmark or to evaluate their own models using MathEval. For the model configuration,
170   the elements include: (1) Model name: The identifier for the model being used; (2) Prompt template: The
171   general structure of prompts used by the model; (3) System prompt (MSP): The official system prompt from
172   the model description or technical report; (4) User prompt (MUP): A token or phrase indicating the start or
173   end of a user message; (5) Bot prompt (MBP): Similar to the user prompt, it indicates the start or end of a bot
174
      response; For the dataset configuration, the components consist of: (1) Data metadata: Information used to
      populate different parts of the template; (2) Question prompt (DQP): Indicates where the question is located
175
      and specifies the different types of questions; (3) Answer prompt (DAP): Specifies the kind of answer that
176
      needs to be generated, option from A to D or a specific answer; (4) Options prompt (DOP): Indicates where
177   the options are located within the template; (5) Chain of Thought (CoT) prompt: Guides the model to output
178   different types of CoT reasoning for each dataset. For example, in multiple-choice questions, the CoT should
179   reason through each choice before providing the final answer; This detailed preparation ensures that both the
180   model and datasets are configured correctly to facilitate accurate and contextually appropriate responses in
181   the following stages. More details are discussed in Appendix H.1
182
      Template Encapsulation: We encapsulate our final input prompt based on both model and dataset configura-
183   tions. There are two scenarios: zero-shot prompt and few-shot prompt. Both settings use a combination of the
184   previously discussed configuration elements. We include these two scenarios because base LLM models are
185   generally not proficient in zero-shot scenarios, as they tend to continue generating content beyond the desired
186   response. Introducing few-shot examples allows for a fair comparison by providing context and examples,
187   thereby guiding the model to generate more accurate and contextually appropriate answers.

      2.3     E VALUATION M ETHODS
218
      The conventional metric for evaluation entails designing specific answer extraction rules tailored to the models
219
      and datasets, followed by a matching process. While this traditional approach can yield stable results, it
220   suffers from a lack of robustness. Minor variations in the output can lead to significantly different outcomes.
221   Moreover, crafting answer extraction rules for each model and dataset based on their output formats introduces
222   quadratic complexity, making rule-based evaluation criteria inefficient. Consequently, we adopt a general
223   evaluation method that can be easily and cost-effectively extended to new datasets and models, thereby
224   enhancing the fairness of the evaluation process through a unified standard.
225
      We initially employed a two-stage evaluation method, as depicted in Figure 4a and 4b. In the first stage,
226   answer extraction, the generated response is processed to isolate the specific answer. In the subsequent stage,
227   answer verification, the extracted answer is compared against the ground truth to produce a comparison
228   result. Given the robustness of methods based on LLMs, such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), these models exhibit
229   strong comprehension capabilities and can handle diverse output formats. In contrast, rule-based methods
230   offer greater stability in obtaining results. Consequently, in both evaluation pipelines, we primarily utilize
231   outputs from GPT-4, supplementing them with regex-based results when GPT-4 fails. Detailed instructions for
232   answer extraction and verification using GPT-4 are provided in Appendix F. Additionally, a comprehensive
233

      comparison between the regex-rule-based method and our GPT-4-as-judgement method is presented in

238   Subsequently, as illustrated in Figure 4c and 4d, we developed an answer comparison model that takes as
239   input the question, the model-generated answer, and the reference (golden) answer, and outputs a detailed,

242

244   (Shao et al., 2024) based answer comparison model. While both methodologies offer distinct advantages, we
245   predominantly employ the initial method—the two-stage process leveraging GPT-4—as our principal source
246   of results.

272   In this section, we explore the methodologies selected for the compare-answer task, a notably challenging
273   aspect of mathematical benchmark. This complexity is well-acknowledged within the academic community,
274   as highlighted in several case studies included in the Appendix G.2. Many benchmarks in this field also
275   encounter difficulties due to their reliance on rule-based extraction and matching approaches. These methods
276   typically struggle to accommodate the diverse output behaviors exhibited by different models. Furthermore,
277   there has been a lack of focused research on this domain, particularly in terms of a systematic analysis of the
278   accuracy of answer matching across various models. This section aims to address these gaps by detailing our
279
      approach and the rationale behind our methodological choices.
280   To authenticate the precision of our methodology, we have organized a large-scale human annotation process
281   of the model output results, which was carried out over the course of approximately one month. We have

                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


282
      selected GPT4, DeepSeek-Math-7B-Base, DeepSeek-Math-7B-Instruct, and DeepSeek-Math-7B-RL as the
283
      basis for validation. Five annotators were assigned to annotate each line of outputs, and the majority vote
284   result was considered the final decision on the correctness of the model output. The details of the data can
285   be found in Appendix G.3. The overall Fleiss’ Kappa (Fleiss, 1971) achieved a score of 0.8871, indicating
286   significant inter-annotator agreement. We believe the human annotation result is reliable and treat the overall
287   average accuracy as the golden standard.
288
      We evaluated two methods for answer comparison. The first is our two-stage GPT4-based judgment depicted
289
      in Section 2.3. The second is a fine-tuned DeepSeek-7B-Base model (Finetuned-DeepSeek) trained on our
290   private answer-comparison data and GPT4 output comparison data (partially verified by human annotators to
291   fix potential errors). We computed the overall average accuracy for each answer-comparison method using
292   19 selected datasets out of 22 from the four chosen models. The results are shown in Figure 5, where the
293   y-axis represents the absolute difference between the proposed answer-comparison method and the human
294   evaluation result, with larger values indicating worse performance. We present the zero-shot, few-shot,
295   and best-of-two previous settings in this figure, focusing primarily on the best-of-two setting. Initially, we
296   observed that GPT4 performed consistently well across all models, with an absolute difference ranging from
297   0 to 0.1. Both methods performed poorly on the output of DeepSeek-Math-7B-Base, likely due to the base
298
      model’s tendency to output useless tokens and inability to stop at the correct position, which poses challenges
      for the answer-comparison model. Notably, Finetuned-DeepSeek achieved the same performance as human
299
      annotators on the output of DeepSeek-Math-7B-RL, demonstrating the effectiveness of our method. Given
300
      GPT4’s consistent performance, it will be our primary model for further analysis. We have open-sourced our
301   custom Finetuned-DeepSeek model to provide a viable alternative for those without access to GPT-4.

440   4   R ELATED WORK
441
442   Benchmarks orient LLMs. General benchmarks provide a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs and are
443   widely used across various natural language understanding tasks to assess their performance. The MMLU
444   (Hendrycks et al., 2021a) benchmark is notable for its extensive collection of 57 tasks covering diverse
445   domains, offering comprehensive challenges across varying subjects and levels of complexity. AGIEval
446   (Zhong et al., 2023) is centered on standardized exams like the SAT, LSAT, and GRE, testing models’
447   reasoning, problem-solving, and language comprehension skills. The broader BIG-Bench (Srivastava et al.,
448
      2022) initiative includes a diverse set of tasks designed to probe models on novel and complex linguistic
      capabilities, challenging them to demonstrate their robustness and versatility in a wide array of cognitive
449
      tasks beyond traditional benchmarks.
450
451   Domain-specific benchmarks are crucial for evaluating how well LLMs handle specialized tasks requiring
452   deep field knowledge. HaluEval (Li et al., 2023) assesses hallucination detection in LLMs using annotated
453   samples, revealing that models frequently generate unverifiable information. LongBench (Bai et al., 2023b)
454
      tests long-context comprehension in English and Chinese across 21 datasets, showing that expanding context
      windows and enhancing memory mechanisms improve long-sequence understanding.
455
456   To the best of our knowledge, there is currently no comprehensive mathematical evaluation benchmark. A
457   similar mathematical benchmark, Lila (Mishra et al., 2023), focuses on extending datasets by collecting task
458   instructions and solutions as Python programs and then exploring some models’ out-of-domain capabilities.
459   A comprehensive benchmark for assessing the mathematical abilities of various models remains absent.
460
461
      5   C ONCLUSION
462
463
      In this paper, we proposed MathEval, the first comprehensive evaluation benchmark for the mathematical
464
      capabilities of large language models (LLMs). Our evaluation encompassed 52 models across 22 datasets,
465   organized into distinct scenarios along three dimensions. Our pipeline facilitates flexible adaptation to
466   various datasets and models. Moreover, we propose an LLM-based approach for the automatic extraction
467   and verification of mathematical answers, serving as a general and precise metric. We hope that MathEval
468   will help provide an impartial evaluation of the mathematical abilities of LLMs, advancing the continuous
469   improvement of LLM mathematical capabilities and expanding practical applications.

      B.1       U NIQUE DATASET D ESCRIPTION
664
      Arith3K is a high quality arithmetic evaluation dataset we constructed, consists of 3 main categories and
665   15 sub-categories, totaling 3,000 problems. It includes 12 types of mathematical operations, ranging from
666   simple arithmetic and logarithmic operations to factorials, trigonometric functions, and complex compound
667   operations. We systematically combined numbers and operators, and used Python code along with SymPy to
668   verify the correctness of each expression. This makes Arith3K the most challenging dataset among arithmetic
669   collections in our benchmark, designed to comprehensively assess the computational abilities of LLMs across
670   varied difficulty levels.
671   TAL-SCQ5K-EN and TAL-SCQ5K-CN are comprehensive mathematical competition datasets available in
672   English and Chinese, respectively. Each dataset comprises 5,000 multiple-choice questions, divided into
673   3,000 for training and 2,000 for testing, covering primary, junior high, and high school level mathematics.
674   These datasets are particularly valuable for Chain of Thought (CoT) training as they include detailed solution
675   steps. Furthermore, all mathematical expressions within the questions are formatted in standard text-mode
676   LaTeX, ensuring clarity and consistency in presentation. To maintain the high quality of the TAL-SCQ
677   datasets, each question undergoes a rigorous review process by two qualified teachers before being included.
678   Moreover, an independent quality validation was conducted on a randomly selected sample of 200 problems,
679
      all of which were approved by independent teachers, with no identified issues.
680   The GAOKAO-2023 and GAOKAO-2024 datasets are derived from the most recent Chinese National College
681   Entrance Examination and consist of both multiple-choice and mathematical word problems. These datasets,
682   which reflect actual exam content, will be updated on an annual basis with forthcoming versions such as
683   GAOKAO-20252 . These consistent updates are designed to help alleviate potential contamination of test data.
684   We focus on K-12 education levels due to their broad applicability and the availability of extensive datasets.
685   However, we recognize that incorporating higher-level mathematics—such as undergraduate topics and
686   competition math problems like PutnamBench—would provide deeper insights into the models’ capabilities
687   across varying difficulty levels. We are actively working to include these more challenging problems in future
688   iterations of MathEval.

      B.2   DATASET C ATEGORIZATION AND D ETAILED P ROBLEM A NALYSIS
706
707   In this section, we conduct a detailed analysis of the differences among the datasets and our categorization of
708   them to avoid the issue of measuring the same ability dimensions. First, in Table 2, we present each of our
709   datasets and their corresponding classifications, including language, problem type, and corresponding grade
710   level (which can partially reflect the difficulty level).
711   To further analyze the distinctions between the datasets, we examined the data distribution of problems in
712   the different datasets to ensure that they are dissimilar. For each dataset, we first randomly sampled 200
713   query problems and obtained their representations using the LLaMa-3-8B Dubey et al. (2024) model. We
714   performed t-SNE dimensionality reduction on these representations, with the visualization shown in Figure 9
715   and the cosine similarity situation in Figure 8.
716
      For the calculation of cosine similarity, we computed the cosine similarity between each query across every
717   pair of datasets and finally took the absolute value of the average as their correlation. We found that the
718   correlation scale between dataset queries ranges from 0 to 0.8, and according to statistics, 75.32% are less
719   than 0.6, and 60.17% are less than 0.5. This demonstrates the dissimilarity between dataset queries, reflecting
720   that to some extent they measure different abilities.
721
      Moreover, we made a surprising discovery in the t-SNE results: the t-SNE naturally formed three clusters. One
722
      cluster consists of English datasets (SVAMP, GSM8K, MathQA, MATH, Dolphin-1878, OlympiadBench-EN,
723   etc.), another cluster consists of Chinese datasets (CMMLU-Math, Math23K, Ape210K, TAL-SCQ5K-CN,
724   etc.), and a third cluster consists of arithmetic problems (Big-Bench-Hard-Math, Math401, Arith3K). From
725   component 1, we can observe that on the left are the English datasets, in the middle are the arithmetic
726   problems, and on the right are the Chinese datasets.
727
      Further observing the Chinese and English clusters, we find that as the t-SNE component 2 value decreases
728
      (from top to bottom in the figure), the problems become increasingly difficult, and the corresponding grade
729   levels also rise. This perfectly reflects that our difficulty levels are distributed across various grades, with
730   corresponding datasets at each level. This shows that our 22 datasets measure different aspects, whether in
731   terms of difficulty or language.

1011   in Chinese consistently trail those in English. To account for the potential impact of problem difficulty,

1013   subsets. As Figures 11b and 11c demonstrate, this trend persists in the primary school subset, while the
1014
       differences between Chinese and English capabilities are negligible in the high school subset. This could be

1024   mathematical domain for continued pre-training, supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning (RL).

1026   double the improvement, highlighting that post-training significantly boosts the model’s specialized abilities,
1027   extending beyond specific datasets.

1029   models tend to exhibit consistent performance within the same dimension, such as language, grade, or
1030   problem type. For example, a model that performs well on English problems is likely to perform similarly
1031   on Chinese problems. However, evaluating different types of mathematical abilities is crucial not only for
1032   completeness but also to identify relative differences in model capabilities. These differences, often resulting

1137   The initial challenge lies in answer extraction, a task that can be complex due to the variability of model
1138   outputs. As demonstrated in Figure 22, the use of regular expressions (regex) can often lead to errors due to
1139
       its inability to understand semantics. On the other hand, GPT-4, with its capability to comprehend semantics,
       can usually extract the correct answer. However, there are instances where GPT-4 may not return a result,
1140
       highlighting the potential for regex to serve as a complementary approach.

1142   Answer comparison presents another level of complexity, even when the extraction process is correctly
1143   executed. As illustrated in Figure 23, comparing answers can be challenging due to variations in the way
1144   answers are represented. For instance, GPT-4 can correctly compare cases like ”9” and ”nine cookies were
1145
       eaten” as shown in Figure 23b. It’s able to understand and display the compared answer, a task that regular
       expressions (regex) would fail to accomplish due to their inability to comprehend semantic equivalences.
