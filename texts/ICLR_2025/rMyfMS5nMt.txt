009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating graph-structured data is crucial in various domains but remains chal-
012            lenging due to the complex interdependencies between nodes and edges. While
013            diffusion models have demonstrated their superior generative capabilities, they
014            often suffer from unstable training and inefficient sampling. To enhance generation
015            performance and training stability, we propose GGFlow, a discrete flow matching
016
               generative model incorporating optimal transport for graph structures and it incor-
               porates an edge-augmented graph transformer to enable the direct communications
017
               among edges. Additionally, GGFlow introduces a novel goal-guided generation
018
               framework to control the generative trajectory of our model towards desired prop-
019            erties. GGFlow demonstrates superior performance on both unconditional and
020            conditional generation tasks, outperforming existing baselines and underscoring its
021            effectiveness and potential for wider application.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Graph structural data generation has become critically important across various domains, including
027
      social networks (Grover et al., 2019), drug design (Bilodeau et al., 2022), and neural architecture
      search (NAS) (Lee et al., 2020). Effective modeling of the intrinsic joint distribution and accurate
028
      description of topological structures of graphs are essential for these applications. Deep generative
029
      models have increasingly demonstrated success in graph generation by effectively modeling the
030   complex structural properties of graphs. These models are typically categorized into autoregressive
031   and one-shot types. Autoregressive models, such as GraphRNN (You et al., 2018), generate graphs
032   sequentially, often overlooking the interdependencies among all graph components. In contrast, one-
033   shot methods generate entire graphs in a single step, more effectively capturing the joint distribution
034   (Ma et al., 2018; Luo et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2020).
035
      Diffusion models have shown great promise and achieved significant performance in various domains
036   (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2022). In the context of graph generation, diffusion
037   models have been adopted to enhance generative capacity. EDP-GNN and GDSS are among the
038   first to utilize diffusion models for graph generation, adding continuous Gaussian noise to adjacency
039   matrices and node types, which may lead to invalid graph structures (Niu et al., 2020; Jo et al., 2022b).
040   Due to the inherent sparsity and discreteness of graph structures, GSDM enhances model fidelity by
041   introducing Gaussian noise within a continuous spectrum space of the graph, and DiGress and PPGN
042   apply discrete diffusion models for graphs (Luo et al., 2023; Vignac et al., 2022; Austin et al., 2021;
043   Haefeli et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023).
044   Despite their potential, diffusion models often face challenges with unstable training and inefficient
045   sampling. Flow matching generative models offer a more stable and efficient alternative by trans-
046   forming the generative process from stochastic differential equations (SDEs) to ordinary differential
047   equations (ODEs), enhancing generative efficiency (Lipman et al., 2022; Song et al., 2024; Yim
048   et al., 2023). Additionally, the use of optimal transport (OT) straightens the marginal probability
049   path, reducing training variance and speeding up sampling (Bose et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2023; Klein
050
      et al., 2024; Pooladian et al., 2023). While the application of OT in graph-based systems is often
      hampered by significant computational demands, primarily due to the complexity of the OT metric
051
      (Chen et al., 2020b; Petric Maretic et al., 2019).
052
053   In this paper, we introduce GGFlow, a novel generative model that leverages discrete flow matching
      techniques with optimal transport to improve sampling efficiency and training stability in graph

      generation. The model preserves graph sparsity and permutation invariance, which is essential
055   for realistic graph generation. Additionally, GGFlow employs a goal-guided framework using

061
062          • GGFlow introduces the first discrete flow matching generative model with optimal transport

075   2.1   F LOW M ATCHING AND D IFFUSION M ODELS
076
077   Diffusion models have gained widespread popularity in various fields, including computer vision,
078   natural language processing, and biological sciences, demonstrating notable success in generative
079   tasks (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Watson et al., 2023; Ingraham et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a;
080   Ren et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024). However, these models often suffer from inefficiencies in sampling
081
      due to the complexity of their underlying diffusion processes and the convergence properties of the
      generative process.
082
083   Flow matching generative models have emerged as a more efficient and stable alternative (details in
084   Appendix A.1), improving sampling by straightening the generative probability path (Lipman et al.,
085   2022; Song et al., 2024; Campbell et al., 2024). Some approaches further enhance performance by
086   incorporating optimal transport. The generative processes of these models are summarized in Figure
087
      1.
088   Previous works (Campbell et al., 2024; Gat et al., 2024) extended flow matching to discrete spaces,
089   while Eijkelboom et al. (2024) applied variational flow matching to graphs, but without adequately
090   addressing key graph-specific properties such as adjacency matrix sparsity. GGFlow tackles these
091   challenges by introducing a discrete flow matching model with optimal transport tailored for graph
092   data. Furthermore, we propose a novel framework for guiding the generative process, enhancing its
093
      practical applicability.

      2.2     G RAPH G ENERATIVE M ODELS
109
110   Graph generative models are typically categorized into two main types: autoregressive and one-shot
111   models. Autoregressive models, such as generative adversarial networks (Wang et al., 2018), recurrent
112   neural networks (You et al., 2018), variational autoencoders (Jin et al., 2018), normalizing flows (Shi
113   et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2021) and diffusion model (Kong et al., 2023), generate graphs sequentially.
114   While effective, these models are often computationally expensive and fail to account for permutation
115   invariance, a crucial property for graph data, resulting in potential inefficiencies. In contrast, one-shot
116
      models aim to capture the distribution of all graph components simultaneously (De Cao and Kipf,
      2018; Ma et al., 2018; Zang and Wang, 2020), better reflecting the inherent interactions within graphs.
117
      Despite the advantages, diffusion-based one-shot models (Niu et al., 2020; Jo et al., 2022b; Vignac
118
      et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Bergmeister et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Haefeli et al., 2022; Yan
119   et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2023; Madeira et al., 2024; Bergmeister et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023;
120   Minello et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024) show promising results in downstream tasks
121   but remain limited by sampling efficiency. GGFlow addresses these limitations by employing a
122   discrete flow-matching generative model, achieving superior generative performance with fewer
123   sampling steps. More comparisons with recent works are presented in Appendix B.

      3.4   P ERMUTATION P ROPERTY A NALYSIS
271
272   Graphs are invariant to random node permutations, and GGFlow preserves this property. To ensure
273   permutation invariance, we analyze the permutation properties of our neural network, training
274   objectives, and conditional probabilities path. First, we analyze the permutation invariance of the
275   training objectives (Vignac et al., 2022). Since the source and target distributions are permutation
276   invariant, the independent coupling also exhibits this invariance. Our optimal transport map, derived
277   from Equation 7, similarly demonstrates invariance to identical permutations. Further clarifications
278   regarding optimal transport can be found in Appendix C.4.
279   Theorem 1. If the distributions p(G0 ) and p(G1 ) are permutation invariant, and the cost function
280   maintains invariance under identical permutations, i.e., H(G0 , G1 ) = H(πG0 , πG1 ) for any per-
281   mutation π, then the optimal transport map ϕ also exhibits invariance under identical permutations,
282   such that ϕ(G0 , G1 ) = ϕ(πG0 , πG1 ).
283
284   Proof of this theorem can be found in Appendix C.4. To ensure that the generated graph retains
285
      its identity under random permutations, the generated distribution must remain exchangeable, and
      GraphEvo must be permutation equivariant.
286
287   Proposition 1. The distribution generated by the conditional flow is exchangeable with respect to
288   nodes and graphs, i.e. p(V, E) = p(π ∗ V, π ∗ Eπ), where π is a permutation operator.
289   Proposition 2. GraphEvo is permutation equivariant.
290
291   The proofs of Proposition 1 and 2 are provided in Appendix C.3 and Appendix D.1, respectively.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we introduced GGFlow, a discrete flow matching generative model for graphs that
489   incorporates optimal transport and an innovative graph transformer network. GGFlow achieves state-
490   of-the-art performance in unconditional graph generation tasks. Additionally, we presented a novel
491   guidance method using reinforcement learning to control the generative trajectory toward a preferred
492   distribution. Furthermore, our model demonstrates the ability to achieve the best performance across
493   various tasks with fewer inference steps compared to other baselines which highlights the practical
494   impact of our guidance method. A primary limitation is scalability to larger graphs (|V| > 500),
495   attributable to the increased time complexity from triangle attention updates and spectral feature
496   computations. Generation times for different graph scales are provided in Appendix J.3. Future work
497
      will focus on enhancing our model’s scalability in larger graphs.

      A.1    C ONTINUOUS F LOW M ATCHING G ENERATIVE M ODEL
816   The generative model aims to establish a mapping f : Rd → Rd that transforms a noise distribution
817   q0 into a target data distribution q1 . This transformation is dependent on a density function p0
818   over Rd , and an integration map ψt , which induces a pushforward transformation pt = [ψt ]# (p0 ).
819   This denotes the density of points x ∼ p0 transported from time 0 to time t along a vector field
820   u : [0, 1] × Rd → Rd .
821
      The vector field u is formulated as:
822
                                                    dx = ut (x)dt.
823
824
      The solution ψt (x) to this ODE, with the initial condition ψ0 (x) = x, represents the trajectory of the
      point x governed by u from time 0 to time t.
825
826   The evolution of the density pt , viewed as a function p : [0, 1] × Rd → R, is encapsulated by the
827   continuity equation:
828                                             ∂p
                                                    = −∇ · (pt ut ),
829                                             ∂t
830   with the initial condition given by p0 . Here, u is the probability flow ODE for the path of marginal
831   probabilities p, generated over time.
832   In practical applications, if the probability path pt (x) and the generating vector field ut (x) are known
833   and pt (x) is tractably sampled, we leverage a time-dependent neural network vθ (·, ·) : [0, 1] × Rd →
834   Rd to approximate u. The neural network is trained using the flow matching objective:
835
                                LFM (θ) = Et∼U (0,1),x∼pt (x) ∥vθ (t, x) − ut (x)∥2 ,                      (11)
836
837   which enhances the model’s capability to simulate the target dynamics accurately. Avoiding the
838   explicit construction of the intractable vector field, recent works
                                                                    R     express the probability path as a
839   marginal over a joint involving a latent variable z: p(xt ) = p(z)pt|z (xt |z). (Lipman et al., 2022;
840   Tong et al., 2023) and the pt|z (xt |z) is a conditional probability path, satisfying some boundary
841   conditions at t = 0 and t = 1.
842   The conditional probability path also satisfies the transport equation with the conditional vector field
843   ut (x|x1 ):
844                                 ∂pt (x|xt )
845
                                                = −∇ · (ut (x|x1 )pt (xt |x1 )).                          (12)
                                        ∂t
846   We can construct the marginal vector field ut (x) via the conditional probability path pt|1 (xt |x1 ) as:
847
848                                          ut (x) = Ex1 ∼p1|t [ut (x|x1 )].                              (13)
849
      We can replace the flow matching loss LFM with an equivalent loss regressing the conditional vector
850   field ut (x|x1 ) and marginalizing x1 instead:
851

985    C.4   P ROOF OF T HEOREM 1
986
987    First, we want to clarify the rationale and foundation of our theorem. The goal of optimal transport is
988
       to pair source and target data points with minimal cost during training, which is beneficial for our
       interpolation (Bose et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024). Thus, we design our optimal transport approach
989
       from the perspective of interpolation.
990
991    We define the node order of the graph G as the order of the nodes and edges in matrix representation.
992    For example, if the node set of G is {A, B, C}, the possible node orders include (A, B, C), (B, A, C)
993    or (C, B, A).
994    In the interpolation process, we transform the graph representation to a matrix representation before
995    performing interpolation. For example, for source data G0 = (V 0 , E 0 ), V 0 ∈ Ra×n , E 0 ∈ Ra×a×m
996    and target data G1 = (V 1 , E 1 ), V 1 ∈ Ra×n , E 1 ∈ Ra×a×m , where a is the number of nodes, n is
997    the class number of nodes, and m is the class number of edges, the node orders of G0 and G1 have
998    been fixed. Therefore, interpolation is performed directly on these fixed node orders.
999    The optimal transport aims to find pairs with the minimum cost for interpolation, and the interpolation
1000   is conducted on a fixed node order. Additionally, during optimal transport calculations, we also
1001   utilize the matrix representation of these graphs and our prior distribution is permutation invariant.
1002   Therefore, we aim to match source data with the target data G1 whose node order is fixed, to achieve
1003   minimal transport cost. Furthermore, we assume that all pairs of source and target data share the same
1004   node order during optimal transport, which also facilitates the identification of pairs with minimal
1005   cost.
1006   Regarding the permutation of the intermediate graph Gt , we have πGt = tπG0 + (1 − t)πG1 ,

1369   I.4.2    M ETRICS
1370
1371   For generic graph datasets, we employ Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to assess the distri-
1372   butions of graph statistics, specifically degree distribution, clustering coefficient, the number of
1373   occurrences of 4-node orbits, and eigenvalues of the normalized graph Laplacian. In alignment
1374
       with prior research (Jo et al., 2022b), we utilize specialized kernels for MMD calculations: the
       Gaussian Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) kernel for degree distribution and clustering coefficient,
1375
       the Gaussian Total Variation (TV) kernel for eigenvalues of the normalized graph Laplacian, and a
1376
       standard Gaussian kernel for the 4-node orbits. To ensure a fair comparison, the size of the prediction
1377   set matches that of the test set.
1378
1379   Validity We permit atoms to exhibit formal charges during valency checks because of the presence
1380
       of formal charges in the training molecules. It is the fraction of valid molecules after valency
       correction or edge resampling.
1381
1382   Validity w/o correction This metric explicitly evaluates the quality of molecule generation before
1383   any correction phase, providing a baseline for raw generation performance.
1384   FCD FCD quantifies the functional connectivity density within a molecule by computing distances
1385   and connectivity between atoms, based on both structural and chemical features. It describes the
1386   three-dimensional structure, topological features, and chemical properties of molecules, making it
1387   valuable in fields such as drug design, compound screening, and molecular simulations.
1388
       NSPDK NSPDK assesses molecular similarity by comparing shortest paths within their graphical
1389
       structures. It captures connectivity patterns and chemical environments, effectively describing
1390   relationships and similarities between molecules. For two distributions p and q, the MMD using
1391   NSPDK is calculated as:
