                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               The problem of question ambiguity, while highlighted as an open issue, is of-
012
               ten overlooked in the literature on Embodied Question Answering (EQA) and
013            Episodic Memory Question Answering (EM-EQA). This paper proposes a struc-
014            tured approach to handle ambiguity in the egocentric data. Our benchmark, called
015            TAG-EQA, utilizes spatial and temporal grounding to distinguish between objects,
016            positions, and events and ensures that obtained structured answers preserve infor-
017            mation fully while effectively resolving ambiguity. We introduce a new dataset,
018            specifically designed for ambiguous grounded Episodic Memory QA. The dataset
019            incorporates situated spatial reasoning, temporal conditions, and diverse visual
020            features. Our new evaluation procedure tackles grounded natural language an-
021
               swers. It reveals that some of the most modern approaches still struggle with
               efficient information extraction and processing in ambiguous scenarios. We hope
022
               that TAG-EQA will serve as both a valuable tool for generating complex EM-EQA
023
               data and that the proposed evaluation benchmark will propel progress in agentic
024            AI and embodied reasoning.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      A deep understanding of the environment is a prerequisite for successful planning and task execution
030   for agents and robots. One way to measure the ability to reason about surroundings is through the
031   task of Embodied Question Answering (EQA). In that setting, the agent is either provided with an
032   observation sequence (passive EQA, or Episodic-Memory EQA (Datta et al., 2022)) or placed in a
033   3D environment (active EQA (Das et al., 2018)) with the ability to navigate it, and asked a question
034   about the environment. (EM-) EQA is inherently multi-modal and often requires comprehension of
035   spatial, visual and temporal components.
036   A realistic aspect of EM-EQA, which is often sidelined in the literature, is the potential ambiguity of
037   the questions. For example, when asking “How many spoons are there on the countertop” we may
038   have different answers when there exist multiple countertops present in the kitchen scene (see Fig. 1
039   for an example). Such questions are usually avoided during dataset generation in favor of obtaining
040   an easily verifiable and definite answer, as it keeps the evaluation simpler (Das et al., 2018; Yu et al.,
041   2019; Cangea et al., 2019; Ma et al.; Zhao et al., 2022).
042   A related issue is conditioning with 3D grounding in the dataset. The exact 3D coordinates of the
043   mentioned object allow for questions and task disambiguation, and are useful for further navigation
044   and manipulation (Singh et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2024). Additionally, providing the coordinates proves
045   that the agent truly understands the environment and lowers the probability of learning spurious
046   correlations. This feature is usually present in the datasets devoted to 3D Visual Question Answering
047   (VQA), but is quite frequently absent from EQA.
048   A further realistic assumption is that the agent actually changes the scene in the provided experience.
049   This factor provides an opportunity to test the understanding of the temporal component of the
050   agent’s experience by asking questions like “Where was the green cup before you entered the living
051   room?”. While agentic actions can be present in the related task of VideoQA, the questions there
052   usually target action recognition or very simple temporal aspects of the video (e.g. predicates like
053   “where you seen it last time”), and do not take into account the potential scene changes, induced by
      the agent.

093   2         R ELATED WORK
094
095   Embodied Question Answering (EQA) was first introduced by Das et al. (2018) as an “active setting”
096   alternative to the passive VideoQA. An agent was placed in a simulated 3D household environment,
097   AI2-THOR (Kolve et al., 2017), and was asked a question that was generated from nine possible
098   predefined templates. The setting was active in that the agent had to navigate the environment and
099
      gather information to be able to answer correctly. Yu et al. (2019) increased the complexity of the
      original dataset by the possibility of multiple target objects in the questions. Ever since, many ex-
100
      tensions have been proposed. Tan et al. (2023) introduced object functions like “used to cut food” as
101
      conditions in the questions. Islam et al. (2023) added multi-modality in terms of verbal and nonver-
102   bal gestures. Dorbala et al. (2024) proposed situational queries that require reasoning about multiple
103   objects, and Gordon et al. (2018) introduced a requirement that agents should not only navigate
104   but also interact with objects to answer questions. Despite the ever-increasing sophistication in the
105   types of questions, the active nature of Embodied Question Answering relying on predefined tem-
106   plates makes it difficult to ask questions with a temporal footprint, such as “where was the green
107   mug before you picked it up and placed it on the coffee table?”, as no preceding history is usually
      provided to the agent.

120   While the active setting of EQA benchmarks is valuable and interesting, in practice, the agent per-
121
      forming tasks in the environment must first determine that it lacks the necessary information to an-
      swer the question. This is why the task of Episodic Memory EQA (EM-EQA) precedes Active EQA.
122
      A dataset exemplifying this is VideoNavQA (Cangea et al., 2019), where the input to the model
123
      consists of an agent’s trajectory and a question from one of eight predefined categories. Datta et al.
124   (2022) introduced a spatio-temporal localization task, requiring the agent to locate an object based
125   on temporal conditions, such as “seen for the first time” or “seen last time.” Similarly, EgoVQ3D
126   (Grauman et al., 2022) tasks the agent with finding the last visible position of an object. QaEgo4D
127   (Bärmann & Waibel, 2022) does not require precise localization and accepts natural language an-
128   swers, as does the EM-EQA subset of OpenEQA (Majumdar et al., 2024). However, these works
129   generally involve relatively simple temporal conditions (e.g. first or last time), and natural language
130   variations do not provide 3D coordinates as grounding in their QA pairs, making them vulnerable to
131   guessing.
132   For understanding nuanced spatial relationships, 3D VQA datasets like ScanQA (Azuma et al.,
133   2022) or FE-3DGQA (Zhao et al., 2022) are more appropriate. These datasets typically rely on
134   realistic 3D reconstructions of environments and use these data as input (e.g., ScanNet, Matterport
135   3D (Dai et al., 2017; Chang et al., 2017)). SQA3D (Ma et al.) introduces situated embodied rea-
136   soning, where the agent’s position is described textually (e.g., “sitting on the edge of the bed and
137   facing the table”) and is used as a condition in the question (e.g., “Which direction should I go to
138
      heat my lunch?”). Although 3D VQA datasets can incorporate not just top-down views or complete
      reconstructions but also provide as input sequences of observations, grounding, and narrow down
139
      the sim-to-real visual gap, they still suffer from the same limitations as other realistic datasets, such
140
      as OpenEQA (Majumdar et al., 2024) and Wijmans et al. (2019). Specifically, they lack agent-
141   environment interactions, which limits the diversity of temporal conditions that can be explored.
142
143   Although Embodied Question Answering (EQA) has clearly been studied extensively in the liter-
144
      ature, it still suffers from key limitations in the complexity of questions it admits. An obvious
      limitation is the explicit avoidance of ambiguity in questions (Das et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2019;
145
      Cangea et al., 2019; Ma et al.; Zhao et al., 2022). For instance, a question like “Where did you leave
146
      the book after you went to the living room?” might have multiple possible answers if the agent was
147   carrying multiple books. This limitation may be a forced measure, but nevertheless an unrealistic
148   assumption. The grounding in the form of 3D coordinates of objects, which would resolve spatial
149   ambiguity and provide useful information for further planning, is also not provided in the EM-EQA
150   setting. Another limitation, which was mentioned earlier, is the lack of temporal conditioning in the
151   questions due to the small diversity of events (i.e. EM-EQA), or inherent inability for incorporating
152   such conditions (i.e. Active EQA, 3D VQA).
153   Our benchmark that adds support to Embodied Question Answering for all the aforementioned types
154   of conditions (i.e. spatial, temporal, visual, and functional) and combinations thereof, as we also
155   summarize in Table 1.

158   3   T EMPORAL , A MBIGIOUS , AND G ROUNDED E MBODIED Q&A
159
160   Here, we present Temporal, Ambigious, and Grounded Embodied Q&A (TAG-EQA), an EM-EQA
161   benchmark designed to evaluate question answering in natural language across diverse spatial, tem-
      poral, and visual dimensions, with a focus on grounding and ambiguity resolution. To generate the

      questions, we use ground truth data from the environment, including the positions and states of both
163   objects and the agent. The agent’s trajectory – comprising a sequence of actions, observations, and
164   position recordings at each timestep – serves as the primary input. Each question is constructed in a
165   structured, functional manner, following the original EQA approach (Das et al., 2018):
166
167

169
      We use a set of filtering functions, each representing a specific predicate for the objects (e.g., prop-
170
      erties like “color = green”, or spatial relations like “location: on the object = countertop”), spatial
171   situated predicates (e.g., “where = a chair is in front of a dining table”, “where = two green cups are
172   in front of you”), and temporal context (e.g., “when = before you picked up a cup”). Each predicate
173   consists of a “key” and a “value.” These predicates are combined using an AND operator to form the
174   conditions of the question. The data is then filtered through the corresponding filtering functions,
175   and a random query function (e.g., “How many . . . ”, “Where . . . ”) is applied to generate the ques-
176   tion. Unlike most (EM-)EQA datasets, we do not apply the “unique()” operator at the end, meaning
177   the answer to the question may remain ambiguous. For a complete list of predicates and queries,
178   refer to Table 3 and Table 4 respectively.
179
180   Ambiguity. In our benchmark, ambiguity arises when two distinct objects, agent positions, or
181   moments in time satisfy the same predicate key but have different values. For example, two spoons
182
      on countertop #1 and countertop #2 both satisfy the predicate “location: on the object = countertop”,
      but each has a different value for the predicate. To handle such questions and provide an answer, we
183
      employ a structured approach, grouping answers by the specific predicate values they satisfy. This
184
      ensures that the ambiguity in the question is resolved and the answer is complete, and no information
185   has been lost. For an example, refer to Fig. 2.
186
187   Grounding. To obtain such a structure, where the predicate values are distinct (e.g. “on the coun-
188   tertop #1” and “on the countertop #2”), it is necessary to differentiate between objects of the same
189   type by assigning tags. Since we have access to the ground truth state of the environment, we use the
190   3D coordinates of objects, captured at the correct moment, as their tags. The 3D coordinate system
191   is chosen such that the agent is placed at the origin at the time of the first observation. For each am-
192   biguous question, we provide a grounded counterpart by referencing the coordinates of each object

217   is similar to pointing at the object, effectively resolving any potential spatial ambiguity.
218
219   Multiple factors. Our framework includes a rich set of predicates, such as diverse visual and
220   functional properties, temporal conditions involving various events, and both situated and relative
221   spatial reasoning. To combine these predicates and generate meaningful questions, we employ sev-
222   eral strategies. First, we assess whether each predicate condition in the question is effective, meaning
223   that adding the condition alters the final answer. This allows us to avoid a common sense conditions
224   like “cups that are used for drinking”. Additionally, as the final answer changes with every new
225   condition, the effectiveness property allows to detect if the model is ignoring parts of the question.
226   The same principle is applied to grounded questions; for example, if there are only green objects
227
      on the dining table, the question “What green objects are located on the dining table (x=1.1, y=0.8,
      z=-2.3)?” is not considered effective. Second, we measure the diversity of predicates to prevent
228
      questions in which the majority of conditions are of the same type. This ensures the model works
229
      with a mix of spatial, temporal, visual, and other data, increasing the complexity of the question.
230   Lastly, to avoid giving unintended hints to the model, we restrict some predicates from appearing
231   alongside specific query functions, such as “Where are the spoons located on the countertop?”, since
232   “countertop” would also be part of the answer.
233
234   Natural Language Question-Answer Generation. After generating question-answer pairs in
235   structured form, we convert them into natural language by prompting a Large Language Model
236   (LLM). To achieve this, we provide the LLM with several examples from the training portion of the
237   dataset. The goal is to produce natural-sounding and diverse question formulations while preserving
238   all essential information.
239
240   Fine-grained evaluation. Since our generation process is controllable, allowing us to track the
241
      predicates used, we can control the stratification in the train-test split. Moreover, with fine-grained
      evaluation, we can now identify specific conditions or combinations of conditions where the model
242
      struggles. The synthetic nature of the dataset provides ground truth object semantic segmentation,
243
      object matching, and access to precise visual properties. This allows for the isolated testing of
244   specific components of a composite model, such as evaluating the model’s retrieval abilities without
245   introducing errors from object recognition.
246
247   Dataset statistics We use FILM (Min et al., 2021) as the agent model, and the test subset ALFRED
248   dataset (Shridhar et al., 2020) of everyday household tasks in AI2-THOR (Kolve et al., 2017) virtual
249   environments. The choice of the test part of ALFRED is determined by the desire to obtain realistic
250   trajectories, in which the agent can be looping in some place, and perform unsuccessful actions.
251   The utility properties of each object were extracted using prompted GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023).
252   In total, we generated around 1.5k trajectories, with a maximum length of 250 time steps. Using
253
      these trajectories as data, we produced a total of 14k QA pairs, 70% of which we reserved for train
      part, and the rest was split into validation and test. The stratification strategy was chosen such that
254
      half of the validation and test sets have questions from environments that are not present in the train
255
      part. To avoid skewing into one question type, we perform further stratification by the generation
256   parameters, e.g. predicate and query types, and presence of grounding. We used the Gemma-2
257   27B tuned instruction (Team et al., 2024) as our LLM for the generation and evaluation of natural
258   language QA.

260   3.1   C ODE -J UDGE LLM E VALUATION
261
262   To evaluate the answers in natural language, we employ LLM-as-a-judge (Zheng et al., 2023), sim-
263   ilar to the OpenEQA. However, the OpenEQA method provides no reasoning behind the score, it
264   does not take into account factors like the proximity of the identified objects to their true positions,
265   and it relies only on the agreement between the LLM score and the human judgement, which is not
266
      suitable for our grounded and structured question-answer generation. We, instead, prompt the LLM
      to provide the scoring function for the given sample, which shall take into account the 3D positions
267
      of the objects, their relative locations, their type, visual features, etc.
268

322
323   We run a number of baselines that are capable of generating a natural language output. We adapt
      them to handle temporal component (e.g. manipulative actions) and grounding in our dataset.


                                                                 6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Socratic LLM + Image captions. Following Majumdar et al. (2024), we run Socratic LLM (Zeng
325   et al., 2022) with image captions (Socratic LLM+IC). We prompt the model with the history of ac-
326   tions and observations, where each action consists of a natural language description and an indicator
327   of success, and each observation consists of a caption and an agents position. To reduce the redun-
328   dancy of the input information and to satisfy the LLMs context length limit, we subsample up to
329   K=50 observations. We make sure that the question is answerable from the provided information,
330   by keeping the subset of important observations such that every manipulation action like ”opened
331   the fridge” or ”picked up apple” is picked and each object is visible at least once. For this, we treat
332   each manipulation action event and each object as a node in a graph, and each observation as an
333
      edge. The nodes have an edge between them if they are captured in the same observation. We ex-
      tract the minimum spanning tree of this structure to obtain the final list of essential observations. We
334
      use instruction-tuned Gemma 2 with 9B parameters (Team et al., 2024) as a base LLM, and employ
335
      CogVLM 2 (Hong et al., 2024) for image captioning as our Vision-Language Model (VLM) here
336   and in further baselines.
337
338   Socratic LLM + Image captions + Object recognition. As the obtained image captions do not
339   include the 3D positions of objects, outputting grounded answers by the Socratic LLM + Image
340   captions baseline is almost impossible. Besides, the produced image captions may not include the
341   information on the valuable objects in the image and their relationships. To tackle this limitation,
342   we enhance the previous baseline with object recognition information. We add the visible objects
343   tags to the VLM prompt to obtain object-focused image captions, and add a list of objects with their
344   estimated 3D positions to the observation description. We use ground truth objects masks, object
345   matching, and depth estimation, obtained from the simulator.
346
347   LLM + ConceptGraphs. ConceptGraphs (Gu et al., 2024) is a structured method for representing
348
      scenes, capturing objects’ 3D positions, their relationships, and descriptive captions. We adapt this
      method for our EM-EQA setting. We generate captions for objects with our VLM and then summa-
349
      rize them with the LLM. We assume there is only one acting agent in the scene and first prompt the
350
      Large Language Model (LLM) with the history of actions and observations. Each observation here
351   consists of a list of observed object IDs obtained from the ConceptGraph. For manipulation actions,
352   we also include a short description of the target object, such as its last recorded 3D bounding box
353   and center, and ID. Finally, we provide a complete list of objects from the ConceptGraph, along
354   with their relationships. This approach enables us to capture both the dynamics of the scene and the
355   accumulated knowledge of object positions.
356
357   LLM + API. Even structured approaches like LLM + ConceptGraph can suffer from information
358   redundancy and may exceed the context length limit of the LLM. To address this, we propose a
359   new baseline based on Retrieval-Augmented Generation (Lewis et al., 2020), built on top of the
360   ConceptGraph approach. This baseline splits the generation process into two steps.
361   First, we provide the LLM with a retrieval API consisting of three functions:
362
363          • filter_by_semantic_similarity(observations, query, ...), which filters
364            observations based on the similarity between the provided natural language query and ei-
365            ther the observation caption, object caption, or action description.
366          • filter_by_position(observations, position, ...), which retrieves positions
367            within a specified threshold. In our case, we set the threshold to one step length, i.e.,
368            0.25m.
369          • are_semantically_similar(text1, text2), which computes the semantic similar-
370            ity between two text entries, used to check object properties such as utility based on the
371            object tag.
372
373   For semantic similarity estimation, we use the Sentence-T5 large model (Ni et al., 2022) and com-
374
      pute cosine similarity between the text embeddings. Additionally, we include the question in the
      retrieval prompt to ensure query-specific responses. In the second step, the retrieved relevant ob-
375
      servations, along with the query, are provided to the LLM, which is prompted to generate the final
376
      answer.
377
      The complete API description is provided in Fig. 8.

      Structure helps providing valid answers. We dive deeper into the performance of the baselines,
456
      first analyzing the distribution of validity in the answers in Fig. 4. Valid answers are answers that
457   are not a priori incorrect, e.g., because they are empty. As the overall percentage of invalid answers
458   drops with both (a) adding more object-centric information (i.e. Socratic LLM + IC + OR) and (b)
459   adding structure and retrieval (i.e. LLM+API), we conclude that the they are both valuable, with
460   the following analogy: the first one for the “recall” part, i.e. making sure that the valuable for the
461   answer information is in the input, and the second for the “precision”, i.e. retrieval of this relevant
462   information and its efficient processing.
463
464   Strong baselines struggle with even simple predicates. In Fig. 6 we analyze in further depth the
465   accuracies of the different baselines per category of question. While there are some fluctuations, it
466   seems all baselines struggle equally as much in all categories of predicates: situational and relative
467   spatial (e.g., “where = the couch is in behind the cabinet”, “location = in the fridge”), visual and
468   functional (e.g., “color = green”, “purpose = used for cutting food”), temporal (e.g. “when = right
469   before you lit the candle”) and so on. We notice that the object-targeted captions in Socratic LLM
470   + IC + OR help with relative spatial relations, probably by capturing these relationships with the
471
      VLM, but still struggle with situational spatial questions, that require advanced understanding of the
      geometry of the provided experience (e.g. “I turned right, now the previous observation is on my
472
      left”.). The accurate retrieval helps the most with both kinds of spatial question categories. Quite
473
      naturally, adding more specific visual information processing in the Socratic LLM + IC + OR is
474   important for successful visual and functional question answering. As all models essentially struggle
475   even with simple one-condition questions to the point close to failure, adding multiple conditions
476   does not have much of an effect. Thus, we conclude that the LLM-based baselines do not have the
477   required tools or capabilities to reason about various factors of 3D environment.
478
479
      Distribution of types of errors. An interesting pattern can be noticed in the Fig. 5. As we add
480   more structure to the input, although the model tends to include more irrelevant objects in the answer
481   (i.e. increase in “Objects False Positive”), it also more frequently retrieves the relevant objects (i.e.
482   reduced “Objects False Negatives”). Thus the structure and retrieval increases the coverage in the
483   objects, but reduces the precision. For the predicates, the picture is unexpectedly inverted: more
484   structure means less hallucinations in the predicates (fewer false positives), but the correct predicates
485   may be missing from the answer, which means that the model is struggling to resolve the ambiguity
      using multiple predicates.

506
      Limitations. Due to the increased complexity of processing and filtration, we avoided the potential
507
      temporal ambiguity in our dataset by adding the counter to each event (e.g. “open the fridge for the
508
      second time”), thus making it unique. Though the temporal predicates are still diverse, we think that
509   handling this ambiguity could be a straightforward extension of our dataset which doesn’t require
510   any modifications to the data generation procedure.
511
512
      While incorporation of temporal component into the questions of Active EQA is not obvious, we can
      propose a new Mixed EQA, a harder task, where the agent is provided with both preceding trajectory
513
      and is placed in the environment. This task is the most challenging and realistic, and would allow
514
      us to use temporal predicates to ask questions like “What objects were to the right of you when you
515   placed the cup on the table?”.
516
517   Reproducibility Statement. To ensure the reproducibility of our results, we aim to make the
518   TAG-EQA dataset and evaluation procedures publicly available, along with detailed documentation
519   on data preprocessing, generation, LLM prompts and code for baselines. All model architectures
520   and parameters used in our experiments are clearly specified in the main text. We encourage the
521   community to build upon our work by evaluating their models and generating more sophisticated
522   versions of the benchmark.
523   In this paper, we presented TAG-EQA, a benchmark designed to address the issue of complex,
524   ambiguous, and multi-factor questions in Episodic Memory Question Answering (EM-EQA). By
525   leveraging structure and spatial grounding, our approach effectively provides answers that resolve
526   ambiguities related to objects, positions, and events, ensuring the preservation of essential informa-
527   tion. Through a novel dataset and evaluation procedure, we demonstrated that while structured input
528   data and retrieval techniques improve performance in spatial reasoning tasks, challenges remain with
529   even simple one-condition queries. Our results show that current strong methods continue to struggle
530   in ambiguous and grounded scenarios, underscoring the need for further advancements in embodied
531
      reasoning. We hope that TAG-EQA will foster new research directions in the development of more
      robust systems for handling complex, multi-factor, ambiguous queries in EQA environments.
