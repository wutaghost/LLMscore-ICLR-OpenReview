009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in generative AI have enabled the creation of highly realistic
012            synthetic audio, posing significant challenges in voice authentication, media veri-
013            fication, and fraud detection. While deep learning models are frequently used for
014            fake audio detection, they often struggle to generalize to unseen and complex ma-
015            nipulations, particularly partial fake audio, where real and synthetic segments are
016
               seamlessly combined. This paper explores the use of Spiking Neural Networks
               (SNNs) for fake and partial fake audio detection, an area that has not yet been
017
               investigated. SNNs, known for their energy-efficient computation and ability to
018
               process temporal data, offer a promising alternative to traditional Artificial Neural
019            Networks (ANNs). We propose an SNN-based approach for fake audio detection
020            and comprehensively evaluate its performance through a series of experiments, in-
021            cluding hyperparameter tuning, cross-dataset generalization and partial fake audio
022            detection. Our results show that SNNs achieve accuracy comparable to state-of-
023            the-art ANN models with fewer number of parameters. Although, SNNs did not
024            offer significant improvements in generalization capabilities, they provided ad-
025            vantages such as reduced model sizes and computational efficiency, making them
026            more suitable for resource-constrained and real-time voice authentication appli-
027
               cations. This study lays the groundwork for further exploration of SNNs in au-
               dio spoofing countermeasures, providing a foundation for future advancements in
028
               security-critical voice applications.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Rapid advancements in generative AI have led to the creation of highly realistic synthetic media, in-
034   cluding images, video, and audio. These technologies are proving to be highly useful across various
035   domains such as entertainment, customer service, education, and healthcare (Ramdurai & Adhithya,
036   2023). However, the ease of generating convincing artificial content also introduces significant eth-
037   ical, security, and societal challenges. While much attention has been focused on synthetic images
038   and deepfake videos (Rana et al., 2022) synthetic speech and audio technologies are emerging as
039
      equally critical, particularly in areas where voice authenticity is essential, such as voice authentica-
      tion systems, customer service, and media. Specifically, modern Text-to-Speech (TTS) (Shen et al.,
040
      2018; Dieleman et al., 2016; Ren et al., 2020) and Voice Conversion (VC) technologies (Kameoka
041
      et al., 2018; Qian et al., 2019; Hsu et al., 2016), which are driven by generative AI models, leverage
042   advanced Deep Learning (DL) architectures to generate speech that is nearly indistinguishable from
043   natural human voices (Mai et al., 2023; Prudký et al., 2023). The ability to produce such realistic
044   synthetic speech has enabled malicious activities such as impersonation, fraud, and disinformation
045   (Bleisch, 2024; Hickey, 2023). Moreover, one particularly concerning development is the seamless
046   merging of synthetic and genuine audio segments, resulting in partial fake audio (Zhang et al., 2021)
047   which further complicates the distinction between authentic and altered content.
048   The current approaches for detecting fake audio, or synthetic speech, initially relied on Machine
049   Learning (ML) algorithms and have since evolved to incorporate advanced Deep Leaning (DL) mod-
050   els (Dixit et al., 2023). The performance of these models also relies heavily on the quality of the
051   training datasets. Among various options, the ASVSpoof-2019 (Wang et al., 2020b) and Fake
052   or Real (Reimao & Tzerpos, 2019) datasets are widely used benchmarks for fake audio detection.
053   Certain models proposed in the literature such as RawNet2 (Jung et al., 2020) and DeepSonar (Wang
      et al., 2020a) have shown considerable performance on these datasets because of their sophisti-


                                                         1
054
      cated deep neural network (DNN) architectures over earlier traditional machine learning approaches
055   (Singh & Singh, 2021; Rodríguez-Ortega et al., 2020). However, despite these advances, recent
056   studies reveal that existing models continue to struggle with generalization to newer/previously un-
057   seen audio content generated by TTS and VC techniques (Chen et al., 2020). Although deep models
058   are effective at learning complex patterns, they may overfit to specific datasets and fail to generalize
059   well to unseen or evolving attack methods. In addition, the detection of partial fake audio—where
060   real and synthetic audio segments are seamlessly merged—remains an underexplored area. This
061   poses an additional challenge, as existing models typically assume fully fake or real audio, leaving
062   them ill-equipped to handle such complex cases.
063   In light of these challenges, this paper explores the use of Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) as a novel
064   approach for detecting both fully fake and partial fake audio. While traditional Artificial Neural Net-
065   works (ANNs) have been extensively applied to this domain, SNNs have not yet been explored for
066   fake audio detection. SNNs are inherently designed to process temporal data due to their ability to
067   capture the timing and sequence of events, which makes them particularly suitable for tasks involv-
068   ing audio, which has rich temporal dynamics (Baek & Lee, 2024). By leveraging these capabilities,
069
      our work explores the feasibility of using SNNs for fake audio detection and comprehensively eval-
      uate their performance across various tasks, including cross-dataset generalizability evaluation and
070
      the detection of partial fake audio. Our study aims to provide insights into how well SNNs may
071
      be suited for fake audio detection, offering lessons on their strengths and limitations in handling
072   complex, evolving audio manipulation techniques. These insights can help guide future work on
073   enhancing voice authentication, media verification, and fraud detection systems, where reliable and
074   efficient detection of audio manipulations is increasingly important.
075
      In this work, we propose two specific SNN models based on their suitability for handling temporal
076
      patterns in audio: a four-layer feed-forward SNN and a hybrid convolutional SNN. The feed-forward
077
      model is selected for its simplicity and efficiency in processing sequential data, making it an appro-
078   priate baseline for evaluating the core capabilities of SNNs in this context. The hybrid convolutional
079   SNN, on the other hand, incorporates convolutional layers to capture more complex spatial-temporal
080   features in the audio signal, making it more-suited for detecting intricate manipulations like partial
081   fakes. Our result show that the proposed SNN and CSNN models performed comparably to ANN
082   models when trained and tested on the same dataset, but struggled with cross-dataset generaliza-
083   tion, similar to other baselines and prior works. Nevertheless, our CSNN model achieved 16.55%
084   improvement over the state-of-the-art (SOTA) model (Firc et al., 2024) when trained on Fake or
085   Real dataset and tested on ASVspoof-2019 dataset. For the more challenging partial fake au-
086
      dio detection task, we achieve an accuracy of 85.59% on partial fake audio dataset created using
      Fake or Real dataset. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to apply SNNs to
087
      the task of fake audio detection, highlighting their potential in this rapidly evolving field. Our main
088
      contributions are as follows.
089
090   • Propose two novel SNN models for detecting both fake and partial fake audio, including a feed-
091     forward SNN and a hybrid convolutional SNN model.
092   • Conduct comprehensive hyperparameter tuning experiments for SNN models, optimizing sur-
093     rogate gradients (Fast Sigmoid, Arctangent), and loss functions (CE-count, CE-rate) to maximize
094     performance in fake audio detection.
095   • Perform a cross-dataset generalization study using the Fake or Real and
096     ASVspoof-2019 datasets to assess robustness of SNNs in detecting fake audio across
097     diverse and unseen data.
098   • Develop and evaluate on a new partial fake audio dataset, combining real and synthetic sam-
099     ples from the Fake or Real dataset, demonstrating SNNs’ effectiveness in detecting partial
100     fake audio at the frame level.

      Partial Fake Audio Detection Partial fake audio consists of a mixture of fake and real utterances,
139   making it particularly difficult for deep learning models to detect. The existing models in literature,
140   typically trained on datasets containing entirely fake or entirely real samples, struggle to identify the
141   manipulated portions when genuine audio is present (Rahman et al., 2022). This limitation arises
142   because most current models are designed for binary classification and they lack the granularity to
143   detect individual fake/real segments within a single audio file. Time-variant models, such as those
144   based on DNNs with variable input and output lengths, have been proposed to address this challenge.
145   For instance, Zhang & Sim (2022) implements a three-stage approach to localize partial fake seg-
146   ments within an audio sample. While this approach shows promise, its multi-stage nature introduces
147
      latency, making it less suitable for real-time applications where fast processing is rather important.
      Furthermore, there is a significant lack of open-source datasets containing diverse range of attacks
148
      designed by utilizing partial fake audio, which limits the development and evaluation of more ad-
149
      vanced models capable of detecting specific manipulated segments. Currently, the PartialSpoof
150   dataset (Zhang et al., 2021), which was created using the ASVspoof-2019 dataset, is the only
151   publicly available dataset containing partially fake audio. However, PartialSpoof dataset also
152   labels partially fake audio as fully fake audio instead of labeling individual segment of audio into
153   fake or real.

      Spiking Neural Networks Recently, SNNs have gained attention as a biologically inspired alter-
155
      native to traditional ANNs due to their temporal dynamics and energy efficiency (Yamazaki et al.,
156   2022). Due to recurrent nature of spiking neurons, SNNs are well-suited for handling temporal data,
157   and have been successfully applied to tasks such as sound localization and classification (Baek &
158   Lee, 2024). Convolution-based SNNs, in particular, have demonstrated strong performance in im-
159   age processing by combining the strengths of both ANNs and SNNs (Mozafari et al., 2019; Zhou
160   et al., 2020; Kirkland et al., 2020). While SNNs have proven effective in sound-related tasks, their
161   application to fake or partially fake audio detection remains largely unexplored. This work aims to
      bridge that gap by investigating their potential in this area.

173   3     P RELIMINARIES
174
175   SNNs can be represented using various models, such as the Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) model,
176   Hodgkin-Huxley model, and Spike Response Model, each capturing distinct aspects of neuronal
177   dynamics and behavior. Given its proven effectiveness for power-efficient deep learning (Rozenberg
178
      et al., 2019), our work employs the LIF model for implementing SNNs.
179   Leaky Integrate and Fire Neuron The LIF neuron is a simplified model of a biological neuron,
180   widely used in computational neuroscience to simulate the electrical activity of neurons in a network
181   (Dayan & Abbott, 2001). The LIF neuron has a membrane potential U(t) which increases with input
182   I(t) (synaptic current or stimulus) and decay with membrane potential decay rate β. The neuron
183   “fires” or generates a spike when the membrane potential reaches a certain threshold and resets its
184
      membrane potential according to reset mechanism. Popular reset mechanisms include subtracting
      with threshold potential and setting membrane potential to zero. The membrane potential of a neuron
185
      can be described by the following equation:
186
187                        U (t + 1) = β × U (t) + I(t + 1) − R(β × U (t) + I(t + 1))                     (1)
188   where R is the reset mechanism for reset to zero. R is set to 1 when the neuron fires, and 0 otherwise.
189
190   Surrogate Gradient Descent Training SNNs through supervised learning is challenging due to the
191
      discrete nature of spikes. During the forward pass, spikes are represented using a shifted Heaviside
      step function. To calculate gradients (partial derivative of the loss with respect to parameters) during
192
      the backward pass, spikes are approximated using a smooth surrogate functions such as Fast Sigmoid
193
      (FS) (Zenke & Ganguli, 2018) and Arctangent (Fang et al., 2021).
194
195   Loss Functions To train SNN models for classification task, two commonly used loss functions
196   for backpropagation are Cross Entropy Spike Count (CE-count) and Cross Entropy Rate (CE-rate).
197
      The CE-count loss function calculates the total spike count over time for the output neurons of
      each class. The predicted spike counts are compared to the target spike counts, which are derived
198
      by multiplying the ground truth labels by the number of time steps. These values are then passed
199
      through the CE function to compute the loss. This approach encourages consistent spiking of the
200   correct class throughout the time steps while minimizing spikes from incorrect classes. On the other
201   hand, the CE-rate loss function processes spike outputs sequentially at each time step. At each
202   time step, the spike outputs and the corresponding ground truth values are passed through the CE
203   function, with the resulting losses accumulated over time. Similar to CE-count, CE-rate promotes
204   consistent spiking of the correct class and suppresses incorrect spikes, but it does so by considering
205   spike activity at each individual time step rather than across the entire time sequence.

      4.2   A RTIFICIAL N EURAL N ETWORK M ODELS
244
      Due to the lack of cross-dataset evaluation studies on assessing the generalization ability of ANNs
245
      for fake audio detection problem, we implemented three representative ANN models, MLP, CNN,
246   and TE. This aims to establish baseline performance by ANN models that utilize similar resources,
247   including datasets, as those employed by SNN models. Model details are as follows.
248
249
      Multi Layer Perceptron We implement a 5-layer Fully-Connected Feed-Forward Neural Network
      (FC-FFNN). The layers contain 2520 (input), 256, 128, 10, and 2 (output) neurons, respectively.
250
      A Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation function is used at each hidden layer to introduce non-
251
      linearity allowing the model to learn complex patterns. The input to the network consists of flattened
252   MFCCs of size 40 × 65 = 2520. The output of the final layer is used for binary classification (real
253   or fake audio).
254
255
      Convolutional Neural Network We then implement a CNN model as demonstrated in fig. 2. The
      convolution layer in the model uses 1D convolutional and 1D max-pooling, applied over the time
256
      domain, to extract deep features from the MFCCs of input audio. 40 MFCCs are treated as 40 input
257
      channels. The subsequent convolutional layers have 20, 20, and 10 channels, respectively. In both
258   the convolutional and max-pooling layers, a stride of one and padding (zero-padding) of one is used
259   to preserve the temporal dimensions of the data. The output from the final max-pool layer is flattened
260   into a 630-dimensional vector and fed into a FC-FFNN for classification. The FC-FFNN consists of
261   three layers with 630, 10, and 2 neurons, respectively, where the ReLU activation function is applied
262   to the intermediate layers. The output of the final layer is used for binary classification (real or fake
263   audio).
264   Transformer Encoder We also implement a TE model which uses the self attention mechanism
265   proposed by Vaswani (2017) to encode input MFCCs. Encoding layer consist of 6 encoding blocks,
266   each with 8 attention heads and a feed-forward network with dimension 1024. The encoded input
267   maintains the same shape as the input MFCCs (40×63). Flattened encoded input is than passed
268   through FC-FFNN for classification. FC-FFNN consist of 4 layers with 2520 (flattened), 1024, 10
269   and 2 (output) neurons with ReLU activation function in between the hidden layers. Similar to
      previous ANN models, the final output layer is used for binary classification.

      4.3   S PIKING N EURAL N ETWORK M ODELS
271
272   SNN Compared to traditional ANNs, SNNs differ in the way they process and transmit information.
273   Instead of using continuous values to represent activation, SNNs communicate via discrete spikes,
274   where each spike represents a binary value of 1 (spike) or 0 (no spike). In this work, for the purpose
275   of detecting fake and partial fake audio, we propose a SNN model consisting of an input layer
276   with 40 neurons, followed by four spiking layers containing 256, 126, 10, and 2 (output) neurons,
277   respectively. Each spiking layer comprises a Fully Connected (FC) layer from an ANN model and
278
      a corresponding Leaky layer. The leaky layer consists of LIF neurons, which are connected one-
      to-one with the neurons in the preceding FC layer, similar to the ReLU activation function in ANN
279
      models. The leaky layer serves as an activation mechanism, outputting either a spike (1) or no spike
280
      (0). We set the decay parameter (β) for the LIF neurons to 0.9, and the spike threshold is learned
281   during training. The input is processed sequentially, with the 40 MFCCs from one time frame
282   passed into the network at a time. This sequential input allows the SNN model to capture temporal
283   dependencies, making it independent of input length. Additionally, the membrane potential of the
284   LIF neurons is preserved across time frames, introducing a recurrent component that enables the
285   model to retain information over multiple time steps. These temporal and recurrent properties allow
286   the SNN to effectively model the dynamic nature of audio signals. In SNN, input audio is classified
287   based on spike count of two output neurons for binary classification. For the partial fake audio
288   detection problem, each time frame is classified based on output of two output neurons at each time
289
      step.
290
      Convolutional SNN While models such as the TE are computationally and power-intensive, simpler
291   models such as MLP may lack the complexity needed to effectively capture subtle patterns in large
292   audio datasets (Müller et al., 2022). CNNs strike a balance by efficiently extracting complex features
293   through convolutional layers while utilizing smaller fully connected layers for classification. On
294   the other hand, while SNNs are generally energy efficient, they may also lack complexity to fit
295   diverse datasets. To this end, we propose a novel Convolutional Spiking Neural Network (CSNN)
296   based approach that combines the feature extraction power of CNNs with the temporal processing
297   capabilities of SNNs for the task of fake and partial fake audio detection. As shown in fig. 2, CSNN
298   retains the CNN architecture up to the final maxpool layer, where deep features are extracted from
299
      the MFCCs. These deep features are then passed through three spiking layers containing 128, 10,
      and 2 neurons, respectively. Similar to the earlier SNN model, we set the decay parameter (β) to
300
      0.9, and the spike threshold is learned during training. Fake and partial fake audios are classified
301
      using mechanism similar to the SNN model.

      4.4   DATASETS
304
305
      To evaluate the proposed fake audio detection models, we use two publicly available datasets,
306   ASVspoof-2019 (Wang et al., 2020b) and Fake or Real (Reimao & Tzerpos, 2019). The
307   primary motivation for selecting multiple large-scale datasets is to evaluate and compare the gener-
308   alization capabilities of the ANNs and the proposed SNN models.
309
      ASVspoof-2019 The first ASVspoof dataset was released as a part of Automatic Speaker Verifi-
310
      cation (ASV) challenge in 2015 (Wu et al., 2014). Updated versions of the dataset are released
311
      every two years, with the latest being ASVspoof-2021 (Yamagishi et al., 2021). In this study, we
312   utilize the ASVspoof-2019 dataset, as it specifically focuses on spoofing countermeasures. The
313   ASVspoof-2019 dataset is divided into two subsets: Logical Access (LA) and Physical Access
314   (PA). The LA subset addresses spoofing attacks where the attacker can access the target device re-
315   motely, while the PA subset focuses on attacks where the attacker has physical access to the device.
316   For this study, we focus exclusively on the LA subset (referred to as ASVspoof for the remainder
317   of the paper), where spoofed samples are generated using 4 TTS and 2 VC models for the training
318   and validation sets followed by 7 TTS and 6 VC models for testing set (Wang et al., 2020b). The
319   audio samples in this dataset are sampled at 16 kHz.
320   Figure 6 (in appendix A) shows the distribution of sample lengths for both fake and real audio
321   samples in the dataset, indicating that the average length of both fake and real samples are around
322   3 seconds. To ensure consistency across both the datasets used in this study, all audio samples in the
323   dataset are standardized to a length of 2 seconds. Samples longer than 2 seconds are trimmed, while
      shorter samples are padded with zeros (silence) to match the required length. As it can be seen in


                                                        6
324
      table 3 (in appendix A) and fig. 6, there is a severe class imbalance between real and fake samples
325   in the ASVspoof dataset. To mitigate this imbalance, we reduce the fake samples in the training
326   set to 2,580 (by randomly selecting 430 samples from each of the 4 TTS and 2 VC spoofing models
327   used in the training set).
328
      Fake or Real This dataset (detailed in appendix A.1) was designed for the evaluation of spoofing
329
      countermeasures in ASV systems (Reimao & Tzerpos, 2019). For our experiments, we utilize the
330
      FoR-2seconds (referred to as FoR for the remainder of the paper) variation of the dataset as
331   it provides samples of uniform length of 2 seconds, ensuring consistency. As it can be seen in
332   table 3 (in appendix A) the distribution of samples in the training, validation, and testing sets of
333   the FoR dataset are perfectly balanced. The testing set contains previously unseen fake samples
334   (generated using Google Cloud TTS with Wavenet), along with previously unseen real samples. To
335   further assess whether the proposed models can adapt TTS audio generated using new algorithms,
336   inspired by Reimao & Tzerpos (2021) we created the FoR-mix dataset. The FoR-mix dataset is
337   constructed by removing 200 randomly selected samples from the test set and adding them into the
338   training set of the FoR dataset.
339   Partial Fake Audio Dataset We then create a partial fake-audio dataset (PFA dataset) to assess the
340   performance of the baseline ANN models and our proposed SNN models in the presence of partial
341   fake audio. Additionally, we utilize this newly created dataset to train SNN models to classify audio
342   inputs by segmenting them into shorter temporal frames, rather than classifying the entire audio
343   sample as a whole. The ground truth of each audio sample is created by aggregating the ground
344   truth of each time frame. This approach also eliminates the constraint of classifying an entire audio
345
      sample based on a fixed initial length. For example, an adversary could attempt to bypass spoofing
      detection by appending fake audio after an initial segment of real audio. By segmenting the audio
346
      into shorter temporal frames and classifying them all, a model can analyze the entire audio sample,
347
      improving its ability to detect such spoofing attempts and making it more robust against partial fake
348   audio based evasion strategies. The dataset consists of four types of audio samples: (1) two-second
349   fake, (2) two-second real, (3) one second fake followed by one second real, and (4) one second real
350   followed by one second fake. Samples are generated using fake and real samples from the FoR
351   dataset.
352
      The training, validation, and test sets are constructed using samples explicitly from the training,
353
      validation, and test sets of the FoR dataset. Table 4 (in appendix A.3) shows the balanced class dis-
354   tribution in the newly constructed PFA dataset. To achieve the objective of improving generalization
355   to TTS audio generated by new algorithms, we applied a similar strategy (to FoR-mix dataset cre-
356   ation) by removing 800 randomly selected samples from the test set and adding them to the training
357   set.

373   fake audio detection. The hyperparameters tested included two loss functions—CE-rate and CE-
374   count—and two surrogate gradients—Fast Sigmoid (FS) and Arctangent. Both SNN and CSNN
375   models were trained with all four combinations of these loss functions and surrogate gradients, us-
376   ing the ASVspoof and FoR datasets independently. For each dataset, the optimal hyperparameters
377   were selected based on model performance on the validation set. These selected models were then
      utilized in subsequent experiments.

      5.2   E XPERIMENT 2: FAKE AUDIO D ETECTION
379
380   Spoofed or fake data generated by newer and more advanced TTS or VC models can easily evade
381   detection in older spoofing detection systems (Müller et al., 2022). Consequently, it is essential to
382   assess spoofing detection model’s ability to identify and adapt to spoofed audio generated by spoof-
383   ing algorithms that were not encountered during training. To address this challenge, this experiment
384   assesses the generalization capability of SNN and CSNN models for fake audio detection by testing
385   their performance on datasets different from those used during training. To this end, we train and
386
      test the baseline ANN models and the proposed SNN models on the ASVspoof and FoR datasets
      in a cross-dataset setting. Spoofed samples in the ASVspoof test set are created using 11 unknown
387
      spoofing models (TTS and VC) and 2 known spoofing models (TTS and VC). Whereas, in the FoR
388
      test set, fake samples are created using previously unseen more commercialized sourced services
389   such as Google Cloud TTS with WaveNet (Reimao & Tzerpos, 2019). Furthermore, the real utter-
390   ances in both test sets are distinct from the real utterances in both train sets. This type of diversity
391   in test sets further challenges proposed models’ generalization ability. Additionally, to evaluate
392   proposed models’ ability to learn spoofed samples generated using previously unseen spoofing al-
393   gorithms, proposed models are trained and evaluated using FoR-mix dataset (see section 4.4).
394
      To evaluate SNN model performance on the FoR dataset, we use the implemented ANN models and
395   the Stacked Temporal Convolution Network (Stacked TCN) model proposed by Firc et al. (2024)
396   (which provides a similar cross-dataset evaluation). Additionally, we compare against three models
397   presented by Reimao & Tzerpos (2021), which uses the FoR dataset. These models include Ran-
398   dom Forest (RF) using MFCC features, RF using CQT features, and VGG-19 using STFT features.
399   The first two models represent the SOTA, non-deep-learning approaches, while the VGG-19 model
400   provides a benchmark for deep learning. For the ASVspoof dataset, we compare our results with
401   the performance of Baseline 1 and Baseline 2 models by Wang et al. (2020b), as well as the Stacked
402   TCN model by Firc et al. (2024).
403
404   5.3   E XPERIMENT 3: PARTIAL FAKE AUDIO D ETECTION
405
      This experiment proposes a novel approach that utilizes temporal nature of SNN models to detect
406
      partial fake audio by classifying audio at the individual time frame level. We use our newly created
407   PFA dataset (see section 4.4) to train the proposed SNN and CSNN models. We use the CE-rate
408   as the loss function due its ability to compare the predicted output of individual time frames with
409   their respective ground truths. Based on the hyperparameter tuning (see section 6.1), we choose
410   Arctangent as the surrogate gradient. The accuracy is then determined by the the number of correctly
411   predicted time frames over the total number of time frames. To assess how training on the PFA
412   dataset could enhance the models’ ability to detect partial fake audio, we compare the performance
413   of these models against SNN and CSNN models with similarly configured hyperparameters trained
414   on the FoR-mix dataset. To further demonstrate the vulnerability of ANN and SNN models to
415
      partial fake audio, we evaluate the baseline ANN models and SNN models, trained on the FoR-mix
      dataset for fake audio detection, by testing them on PFA dataset. In this experiment, only fully real
416
      audio samples are categorized as real, while both fully fake and partially fake samples are classified
417
      as fake.

      7     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we explored the use of SNNs for the detection of fully and partially synthesized
533
      fake audio, addressing a gap in existing research. Our experiments demonstrated that SNN mod-
534   els, particularly the CSNN, achieved performance comparable to ANNs and other SOTA models
535   with considerably lower number of parameters. Despite this efficiency, the SNN models did not
536   demonstrate substantial improvements in generalization in the presence of fake audio generated by
537   previously unseen algorithms. However, the temporal dynamics inherent to SNNs enabled a novel
538   approach for detecting partial fake audio at the frame level, offering a promising direction for future
539   advancements. This work lays the foundation for future research into enhancing the robustness and
      generalization of SNN models, especially in security-critical audio manipulation detection.

      B     I MPLEMENTATION
757
758
759   All our experiments were implemented in Python 3.11 on a server running Ubuntu 22.04 with
760
      350 GB RAM and a Nvidia L40S (48 GB VRAM), 48 CPU cores, and 400 GB swap memory)
      GPU. ANN models were built using the PyTorch library (Paszke et al., 2019), while SNN models
761
      were developed using PyTorch alongside snnTorch (Eshraghian et al., 2023). All our proposed mod-
762
      els and baseline models were trained for 200 epochs, and the epoch with the minimum validation
763   loss was selected for evaluation. The ANN models were trained with CE loss function, the Adam
764   optimizer with learning rate of 0.0001 and an L2-penalty of 0.000005 to reduce over-fitting. The
765   SNN models were trained with Adam optimizer and learning rate of 0.0005.

768   C     M ETRICS
769
770
      We use the following metrics to evaluate the performance of our proposed models.
771
772
773   1. Accuracy: The model’s accuracy is measured as the proportion of correctly classified instances
774
         out of the total instances provided to the model.
775   2. Equal Error Rate (EER): The point where the False Positive Rate (FPR) (proportion of real
776      audio incorrectly classified as fake) equals the False Negative Rate (FNR) (proportion of fake
777      audio incorrectly classified as real). A lower EER is an indicator of a more accurate and balanced
778      model.
779
780   In the context of fake audio detection, accuracy can be misleading when dealing with imbalanced
781   datasets. Therefore, we use EER as a more reliable metric, as it balances the trade-off between FPR
782   and FNR. EER is particularly useful in situations where both types of errors (failing to detect fake
783   or real audio) are critical.

828   E    F URTHER D ISCUSSION
829
830   Our study highlights key areas for improvement in the development of fake audio detection models,
831   particularly in terms of generalizability. One major challenge revealed in our results is that the
832   models, including both SNNs and ANNs, often fail to generalize well when tested on data from
833   unseen voice synthesizing algorithms. This suggests that the models are overfitting to the specific
834
      patterns present in the training data and struggle to adapt to novel manipulations introduced by new
      algorithms. However, when the training set includes samples from these unseen algorithms, model
835
      performance improves considerably. This indicates that the limited generalization is not necessarily
836
      a failure of the model architecture itself, but rather a limitation of the diversity of the training data.
837
838   In adversarial settings, one of the key challenges is the unpredictability of the specific algorithm
839   used to generate synthetic audio, making it difficult for detection models to generalize effectively.
840
      Attackers may use novel or customized voice synthesizing techniques that the model has never
      encountered, resulting in significant detection blind spots. This is particularly problematic because
841
      the rapid pace of advancements in TTS and VC technologies means new, highly realistic algorithms
842
      are constantly emerging, further complicating the task for existing models. To address this challenge,
843   it is essential to maintain a continuously evolving, comprehensive dataset that captures a wide array
844   of known voice synthesizing algorithms. Another key area for further development is the creation
845   of more sophisticated partial fake audio datasets. There is a need for a more advanced dataset
846   that captures a wider range of manipulations, including more complex synthetic audio generation
847   techniques. Moreover, making such a dataset publicly available, with frame-level annotations for
848   real and fake segments, would allow other researchers to benchmark their models and drive progress
849   in this domain.
850   A potential direction for future research is to compare the power efficiency of SNNs and traditional
851   ANNs by implementing SNNs on neuromorphic hardware platforms, such as Intel Loihi (Davies
852   et al., 2018). Such a comparison could provide valuable insights into the practical advantages of
853   SNNs over conventional ANNs in fake audito detection, especially in large-scale deployment sce-
854   narios.
