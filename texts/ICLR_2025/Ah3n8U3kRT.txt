011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we consider non-smooth convex optimization with a zeroth-order
014            oracle corrupted by symmetric stochastic noise. Unlike the existing high-probability
015
               results requiring the noise to have bounded κ-th moment with κ ∈ (1, 2], our
               results allow even heavier noise with any κ > 0, e.g., the noise distribution can
016
               have unbounded expectation. Our convergence rates match the best-known ones
017
               for the case of the bounded variance, namely, to achieve function accuracy ε our
018
               methods with Lipschitz oracle require Õ(d2 ε−2 ) iterations for any κ > 0. We
019            build the median gradient estimate with bounded second moment as the mini-
020            batched median of the sampled gradient differences. We apply this technique to the
021            stochastic multi-armed  bandit problem with heavy-tailed distribution of rewards
                               √
022            and achieve Õ( dT ) regret. We demonstrate the performance of our zeroth-order
023            and MAB algorithms for different κ on synthetic and real-world data. Our methods
024            do not lose to SOTA approaches and dramatically outperform them for κ ≤ 1.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
028   During the recent few years, stochastic optimization problems with heavy-tailed noise received a lot
029   of attention from many researchers. In particular, heavy-tailed noise is observed in various problems,
030   such as the training of large language models [3; 44], generative adversarial networks [13; 14], finance
031   [35], and blockchain [43]. One of the most popular techniques for handling heavy-tailed noise in
032   theory and practice is the gradient clipping [15; 6; 31; 34] which allows deriving high-probability
033   bounds and considerably improves convergence even in case of light tails [37].
034   However, most of the mentioned works focus on the gradient-based (first-order) methods. For some
035   problems, e.g., the multi-armed bandit [10; 1; 23; 4], only losses or function values are available, and
036   thus, zeroth-order algorithms are required. Stochastic zeroth-order optimization is being actively
037   studied. For a detailed overview, see the recent survey [11] and the references therein. The only
038   existing works that handle heavy-tailed noise in convex zeroth-order optimization are [19; 20] which
039   combine clipping and gradient smoothing [12] techniques. Under noise with bounded κ-th moment
040   for κ ∈ (1, 2], the authors obtain optimal high-probability convergence
                                                                       √       for d-dimensional non-smooth
                                                                                  κ
041   convex problems, i.e., function accuracy ε is achieved in Õ( dε−1 ) κ−1 oracle calls. These rates
042   match the optimal rates for first-order optimization [15], however, they degenerate as κ → 1, and the
043
      convergence is not guaranteed for κ = 1.
044   For symmetric (and close to symmetric) heavy-tailed noise distributions, the degeneration issue can
045   be handled via median estimators [46; 34], which are frequently used in robust mean estimation
046   and robust machine learning [27]. In the case of first-order methods, the authors of [34] achieve
047   better complexity guarantees and show that the narrowing of the distributions’ class is essential for
048   it. However, the possibility of application of the median estimators to the case of the zeroth-order
049
      optimization and multi-armed bandit remains open. In this paper, we address this question.

131   3.1     T HEORY
132
133   We consider a non-smooth convex optimization problem on a convex set Q ⊆ Rd :
134                                            min f (x),                                               (3)
135                                                x∈Q

136   where f : Rd → R is d-dimensional, µ-strongly convex (As 1) and M2 -Lipschitz (As 2) function. A
137   point x∗ denotes one of the problem’s solutions. In zeroth-order setup, the optimization is performed
138   only by accessing the pairs of function evaluations rather than sub-gradients.
139   Two-point oracle. For any two points x, y ∈ Rd , an oracle returns the pair of the scalar values
140   f (x, ξ) and f (y, ξ), which are noised evaluation of real values f (x) and f (y). Moreover, noised
141   values have the same realization of the stochastic variable ξ and can be written as
142                               f (x, ξ) − f (y, ξ) = f (x) − f (y) + ϕ(ξ|x, y),
143
      where ϕ(ξ|x, y) is the stochastic noise, whose distribution depends on points x, y.

      3.1.1    N OISE DISTRIBUTION .
146
147   We propose our novel assumption on distribution of ϕ(ξ|x, y), induced by a random variable ξ. It
148   allows us to introduce symmetry and heavy-tailed noise with bounded up to κ-th moments, κ > 0.
149   Assumption 3 (Symmetric noise distribution). Symmetry. For any two points x, y ∈ Rd , noise
150   ϕ(ξ|x, y) has symmetric probability density p(u|x, y), i.e. p(u|x, y) = p(−u|x, y), ∀u ∈ R.
151   Heavy tails. We assume that there exist κ > 0, γ > 0 and scale function B(x, y) : Rd × Rd → R,
152   such that ∀u ∈ R holds
153                                                  γ κ · |B(x, y)|κ
                                    p(u|x, y) ≤                        .                         (4)
154                                              |B(x, y)|1+κ + |u|1+κ

164
165           • Independent oracle: f (x, ξ) = f (x) + ξx , f (y, ξ) = f (y) + ξy with independent ξx , ξy .
166           • Lipschitz oracle: f (x, ξ) = f (x)+⟨ξ, x⟩, f (y, ξ) = f (y)+⟨ξ, y⟩, where ξ is d-dimensional

169   Comparison with previous oracles. Our Assumption 3 is quite different from the standard assump-
170   tions from [8; 20]. We make our assumption on variable ϕ(ξ|x, y) with fixed x, y. It allows us to set
171   and use fine-grained properties of the noise distribution, e.g., symmetry or heavy tails of particular
172
      type (4). In [20], the authors fix ξ and make assumption on x, y. Hence, they can not access the
      distribution of the noise and use only the fact of having bounded κ-th moment. Nevertheless, when
173
      κ ∈ (1, 2], our Assumption 3 can be reduced to the standard one with the same constant, Remark 3.
174
175   We would like to highlight the fact that the common proof techniques from previous works can not
176   be trivially generalized to apply symmetry without our novel assumption. For example, the proof
177   of median estimator’s properties Lemma 1 is based on completely different approach. We refer to
178
      Appendix A for more details and intuition behind Assumption 3.
