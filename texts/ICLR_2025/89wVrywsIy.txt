009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a novel approach to Transformer circuit analysis using Sparse Autoen-
012            coders (SAEs) and Transcoders. SAEs allow fine-grained feature extraction from
013            model activations, while Transcoders handle non-linear MLP outputs for determin-
014            istic circuit tracing. Our Hierarchical Tracing method isolates interpretable circuits
015            at both local and global levels, enabling deeper insights into tasks like subject-verb
016
               agreement and indirect object identification. Additionally, we introduce an auto-
               mated workflow leveraging GPT-4o for scalable circuit analysis. This framework
017
               provides a clearer understanding of Transformer model behavior and its underlying
018
               mechanisms.
019
020
021   1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Recent years have seen the rapid progress of mechanistically reverse engineering Transformer lan-
024   guage models (Vaswani et al., 2017). Conventionally, researchers seek to find out how neural networks
025   organize information in its hidden activation space (Olah et al., 2020a; Gurnee et al., 2023; Zou et al.,
026   2023) (i.e. features) and how learnable weight matrices connect and (de)activate them (Olsson et al.,
027
      2022; Wang et al., 2023; Conmy et al., 2023) (i.e. circuits). One fundamental problem of studying
      attention heads and MLP neurons as interpretability primitives is their polysemanticity, which under
028
      the assumption of linear representation hypothesis is mostly due to superposition (Elhage et al.,
029
      2022; Larson, 2023; Greenspan & Wynroe, 2023). Thus, there is no guarantee of explaining how
030   these components impact model behavior out of the interested distribution. Additionally, circuit
031   analysis based on attention heads is coarse-grained because it lacks effective methods to explain the
032   intermediate activations.
033
      Probing (Alain & Bengio, 2017) in the activation for a more fine-grained and monosemantic unit has
034
      succeeded in discovering directions indicating a wide range of abstract concepts like truthfulness (Li
035
      et al., 2023) and refusal of AI assistants (Zou et al., 2023; Arditi et al., 2024). However, this supervised
036   setting may not capture features we did not expect to present.
037
038   Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) (Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023) provide a promising
039
      alternative for unsupervised feature extraction from superposition. They offer a new perspective on
      understanding model internals by interpreting the activation of SAE-derived features. This raises an
040
      important question: How can we effectively leverage SAEs for circuit analysis in Transformer
041
      models? To address this, we introduce several innovations in this area. Compared to previous
042   work (Cunningham et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Marks et al., 2024), our main contributions are as
043   follows:
044
045          • We propose a novel framework that utilizes Transcoders, generalized forms of SAEs, to
046            overcome the non-linearity of MLPs in Transformer models. Transcoders allow for sparse
047            decomposition of MLP outputs, enabling fine-grained circuit analysis while maintaining
048            deterministic connections between upstream and downstream features.
049          • We introduce a fully automated Hierarchical Tracing methodology to streamline the
050            discovery and interpretation of circuits at both local and global levels, by tracing the flow of
051            information based on sparse features extracted by SAEs and Transcoders.
052          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach by applying it to tasks including subject-
053            verb agreement and indirect object identification, offering more detailed insight into how
               each single SAE feature contributes to a desired behavior.

189   For an SAE S, with encoding function gE (x) = ReLU(WE x + bE ) and decoding function gD (x) =
190   WD x, we mount the SAE at a specific node v (Figure 1(a)), corresponding to where the SAE was

192
193         1. Attaching a feature node f to v via an edge v → f with the functional dependence gE .

199   In practice, to account for the imperfect reconstruction ability of SAEs, we create an SAE error
200   node as a leaf node (Marks et al., 2024), capturing the difference between av and gD (gE (av )). This
201
      error term ensures that the forward pass remains consistent with the original computation, while the
      gradient computation now incorporates the effect of the SAE.
202
203   For Transcoders, the process is similar. The Transcoder is mounted at the pre-MLP activation node
204   v, and the reconstructed node v̂ is connected to the successors of the MLP output node, effectively
205   replacing the original MLP computation with the Transcoder’s functionality.
206   To separate the contributions of different features, we can split the feature node f into multiple nodes,
207   each corresponding to an individual feature extracted by the SAE or Transcoder, allowing for more
208   fine-grained control and interpretation.
209
210   Attributing Nodes to Upstream Candidates. Once SAEs and Transcoders are integrated into the
211   computational graph, it becomes possible to identify the key upstream nodes that contribute directly
212   to the target output. Previous approaches, such as circuit analysis using activation patching (Wang
213   et al., 2023; Conmy et al., 2023) and attribution patching (Kramár et al., 2024; Marks et al., 2024),
214   have primarily focused on understanding the indirect effect—which captures the aggregate influence
215   of intermediate nodes across all possible paths. While these methods are effective at discovering
      important nodes, they do not guarantee the formation of a coherent and connected subgraph, nor do

      they offer a self-contained, interpretable circuit. Additionally, these indirect effects can vary across
217   different tasks due to the complexity and nonlinearity of the underlying neural network functions.
218
219   To mitigate such issue, our method centers on computing the direct effect of individual nodes by
220
      analyzing the path-based gradients (as defined in Equation 4) (Figure 1(b)). This method provides
      a more precise and interpretable view by isolating the direct contributions of upstream nodes. We
221
      define a set of intermediate nodes V I as gradient barriers, which block path-based gradients from
222
      propagating through these nodes, except for those originating directly from them. The direct effect of
223   a node v on an output node u, considering the intermediate nodes V I , is represented by an attribution
224   score:

230   Nodes with high direct attribution scores can be identified as critical upstream candidates, providing
231   a more interpretable and connected subgraph for further analysis. In practice, we treat the outputs of
232
      the attention heads from SAEs and the features generated by Transcoders as the set of intermediate
      nodes V I . Given that the direct effect computation in this setting is relatively straightforward (linear
233
      for Transcoders and bilinear-softmax-linear for attention mechanisms), we expect these inter-layer
234

235   results.
236
237   Selecting Critical Candidates for Further Tracing. Once key upstream candidates are identified,
238   the next step is to prioritize the most critical nodes for detailed tracing. This selection is based on
239   their direct attribution scores and their contextual importance within the network. To determine which
240   nodes warrant further analysis, we can employ either of these two strategies:
241
242          • Apply thresholds on the attribution scores or use sparsity-promoting techniques to limit the
243            focus to a small subset of paths and nodes (Section 3.3).
244          • Conduct a more in-depth inspection of the candidates by utilizing top activations of features
245            and direct logit attributions (DLAs), selecting those with the strongest contextual relation-
246            ships. This selection can be performed either automatically using large language models
247            (LLMs) (Section 4) or manually by human experts (Section 5).
248
      By focusing on the most critical nodes, we reduce complexity while simultaneously enhancing the
249
      interpretability of the resulting model, yielding clearer insights into how key features influence the
250   final predictions.

      3.3   E VALUATING THE G LOBAL N ECESSITY OF T RACED R ESULTS
253
254   After tracing key nodes and subgraphs using Hierarchical Tracing, it is important to evaluate the
255   significance of the traced results from a broader perspective. Specifically, we assess the necessity of
256   the traced results by ablation testing. We hypothesize that the removal of key nodes from the traced
257   subgraph should result in a significant drop in model performance if the traced nodes are truly critical
258   to the final output.
259   For instance, in a text input scenario, we first run Hierarchical Tracing with a sparsity-promoting
260   selector that identifies the top 10 features by its direct effect attribution score from each layer. Next,
261   for a range of values 1 ≤ k ≤ 40, we mean-ablate the top-k nodes and measure the probability
262   decrease from the original output. The mean ablation is done by replacing the current activation with
263   the average value at current node across the task. This experiment is compared against a neuronal
264   approach (where intermediate nodes are defined as all model activations that write to the residual
265   stream) and a baseline approach involving the random ablation of activated features. We further
266
      mean-ablate a single feature/neuron that is ordered exactly the k-th to examine if there are deeper
      correlations between the effect measured by Hierarchical Tracing and the overall significance.
267
268   We evaluate through 500 prompts from the subject-verb agreement task. The results (Figure 2(a))
269   suggest that ablating the top 20 critical features from the traced subgraph is enough to cause a sub-

      4           F ULLY AUTOMATED W ORKFLOW FOR I NTERPRETABLE C IRCUIT T RACING
294
295
296
      To streamline the tracing of interpretable circuits in any model forward pass, we propose a fully
      automated workflow that combines the hierarchical tracing methodology with GPT-4o, leveraging
297
      LLMs to automate the analysis of intermediate activations, select critical nodes, and generate
298
      comprehensive explanations of the model’s internal mechanisms.
299
300   The workflow consists of three main steps:
301
302                                  1. Feature Interpretation: We utilize GPT-4o to interpret individual features extracted by
303                                     SAEs and Transcoders (Bills et al., 2023a; Bricken et al., 2023). By providing GPT-4o with
304
                                        activation contexts, direct logit attributions (nostalgebraist, 2020), and task descriptions, it
                                        generates concise explanations of the conditions under which each feature activates, aiding
305
                                        in understanding the semantic or syntactic roles of features.
306
307                                  2. Candidate Selection: GPT-4o selects important intermediate nodes that significantly con-
308                                     tribute to the model’s inference. It considers the current node to trace, candidate upstream
309                                     nodes with brief explanations, and relevant task information. Following structured inter-
310                                     action guidelines, GPT-4o iteratively selects nodes to trace further, building a coherent
311
                                        and connected subgraph of critical nodes. In practice, to prevent an overload of distractor
                                        candidates, we provide GPT-4o with the top 10 features exhibiting the highest attribution
312
                                        scores, as outlined in Section 3.3, and request further selection.
313
314                                  3. Circuit Interpretation: Finally, GPT-4o synthesizes the traced information to generate a
315                                     comprehensive explanation of the model’s internal information flow, detailing the progres-
316                                     sion from low-level token patterns to high-level semantic understanding. This provides a
317
                                        transparent view of the model’s decision-making process.
318
319   By integrating LLM-based interpretation at each stage, our automated workflow not only identifies
320
      critical components within the model but also generates human-readable explanations of how these
      components contribute to the model’s behavior. This approach significantly reduces the need for
321
      manual analysis, enabling scalable and efficient interpretability for complex neural networks. Our
322
      detailed prompts used for GPT-4o interactions are listed in Appendix D.

364   Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) serve as powerful unsupervised feature extractors in the expansive

367   rather than solely on end-to-end circuit behavior.

      6    R ELATED W ORK
498
499
      Mechanistic and Representational Interpretability. Mechanistic Interpretability (Olah et al.,
500   2020b;a) deems model components, e.g., attention heads and MLP neurons, as primitives and explains
501   how they interact with model input and output. This line of research has succeeded in identifying
502   attention-based circuits implementing various NLP tasks (Olsson et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023;
503   Stefan Heimersheim, 2023). Efforts are also made to interpret polysemantic MLP neurons (Gurnee
504   et al., 2023) and editing information stored in MLP parameters (Meng et al., 2022; Sharma et al.,
505   2024).
506   By placing intermediate activations at the center of analysis, Representational Interpretability ap-
507   proaches mostly use linear probes to isolate a targeted behavior in a supervised manner (Kim et al.,
508   2018; Geiger et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023). However, such methods may fail to capture unanticipated
509   behaviors.
510
511   Sparse Autoencoders stand in between these two approaches. SAEs disentangle features in
512   the model’s hidden activation (Chen et al., 2017; Subramanian et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019;
513   Panigrahi et al., 2019; Yun et al., 2021; Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023) into more
514   interpretable primitives than MLP neurons, in an unsupervised manner. Albeit reconstruction errors,
515   Rajamanoharan et al. (2024); Wright & Sharkey (2024) have proposed to improve SAE training with
516
      lower loss and more sparsity.
517
      Circuit Discovery with SAE Features. Previous work mechanistically interprets circuits connect-
518
      ing attention heads and MLP neurons (Olsson et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Conmy et al., 2023).
519
      As for SAE circuits, He et al. (2024) makes a linear approximation of MLP layers by fixing the gate
520   mask of the non-linear activation function; Marks et al. (2024) estimates the indirect effect of each
521   SAE feature with attribution patching (Kramár et al., 2024), which also makes linear assumption of
522   non-linear functions. In contrast, we refactor our computation graph to be completely linear w.r.t. OV
523   and MLP circuits without approximation.
524
525
      7    C ONCLUSION
526
527
      Our framework employs Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) to extract fine-grained features from model
528
      activations, providing a clearer understanding of how Transformer layers and neurons process
529   information. To address the challenges posed by non-linear MLP structures, we introduce Transcoders,
530   enabling the deterministic tracing of MLP outputs. We further present Hierarchical Tracing, a
531   methodology that allows for both local and global analysis of circuits, facilitating the discovery of
532   how different parts of a Transformer contribute to model behavior.
533
      Through various automatic and manual experiments on tasks like subject-verb agreement and IOI, we
534
      have demonstrated the robustness of our approach in isolating critical circuits. The analysis of in-
535   bracket features and indirect object identification circuits showcases the depth of interpretability made
536   possible by using SAEs. Additionally, our automated workflow integrated with GPT-4o streamlines
537   the tracing process, offering scalable and interpretable results.
