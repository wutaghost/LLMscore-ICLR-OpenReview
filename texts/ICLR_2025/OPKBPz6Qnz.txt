009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper presents a spectral framework for quantifying the differentiation be-
012            tween graph data samples by introducing a novel metric named Graph Geodesic
013            Distance (GGD). For two different graphs with the same number of nodes, our
014            framework leverages a spectral graph matching procedure to find node correspon-
015            dence so that the geodesic distance between them can be subsequently computed by
016            solving a generalized eigenvalue problem associated with their Laplacian matrices.
017            For graphs of different sizes, a resistance-based spectral graph coarsening scheme
018            is introduced to reduce the size of the larger graph while preserving the original
019
               spectral properties. We show that the proposed GGD metric can effectively quan-
               tify dissimilarities between two graphs by encapsulating their differences in key
020
               structural (spectral) properties, such as effective resistances between nodes, cuts,
021
               the mixing time of random walks, etc. Through extensive experiments comparing
022            with the state-of-the-art metrics, such as the latest Tree-Mover’s Distance (TMD)
023            metric, the proposed GGD metric shows significantly improved performance for
024            graph classification and stability evaluation of GNNs, especially when only partial
025            node features are available.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      In the era of big data, comparison and distinction between data points are important tasks. A graph
031   is a specific type of data structure that represents the connections between a group of nodes or
032   agents. Comparing two graphs often involves using a pairwise distance measure, where a small
033   distance indicates a high structural similarity and vice versa. To understand the generalization
034   between distribution shifts, it is important to use an appropriate measure of divergence between
035   data distributions, both theoretically and experimentally (Chuang et al., 2020). Determining suitable
036   distance metrics for non-Euclidean data entities, like graphs with or without node attributes, which
037   are fundamental to many graph learning methods such as graph neural networks (GNNs), remains
038   a significant challenge, even though distance metrics for data points in Euclidean space are readily
039
      available. The need to develop new analytical techniques that allow the visualization, comparison,
      and understanding of different graphs has led to a rich field of research study (Haslbeck & Waldorp,
040
      2018). This study dives into the exploration of a novel framework for computing geometric distances
041
      between graphs, which can be immediately leveraged for many graph-based machine learning (ML)
042   tasks, such as graph classification or the stability evaluation of GNNs.
043
044   Many distance metrics for comparing graphs have previously been proposed (Borgwardt et al., 2020).
045
      Some of them are merely based on graph local structures (Tam & Dunson, 2022; Haussler et al., 1999;
      Xu et al., 2013; Zhu et al., 2020; Fernández & Valiente, 2001; Bunke & Shearer, 1998), whereas
046
      others exploit both graph structural properties and node attributes (Shervashidze et al., 2011; Morris
047
      et al., 2019). For example, the Graph Edit Distance (GED) has been proposed to measure the distance
048   between graphs considering the number of changes needed to match one graph to another (Sanfeliu
049   & Fu, 1983; Gao et al., 2010; Li et al., 2017); Distance metrics based on the graph kernel have also
050   been investigated (Shervashidze et al., 2011; Vishwanathan et al., 2010), such as the Wasserstein
051   Weisfeiler-Leman metric (WWL) (Morris et al., 2019) and the Gromov–Wasserstein metric (Mémoli,
052   2011), which allow computing graph distances based on low-dimensional graph representations or
053   optimal transport (OT) (Titouan et al., 2019; Chapel et al., 2020), leading to the development of the
      state-of-the-art graph distance metric called TMD (Chuang & Jegelka, 2022).

054
      However, the existing graph distance metrics have distinct limitations. For example, the GED metric
055   can capture local node or edge changes but struggles with global perturbations (Sanfeliu & Fu, 1983;
056   Gao et al., 2010; Li et al., 2017); the WWL and TMD metrics heavily rely on node features (attributes)
057   for calculating the distance between graphs, leading to degraded performance when only partial node
058   features are available (Rossi et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022).
059
      To address these limitations of prior methods, we propose the Graph Geodesic Distance (GGD) metric,
060
      a novel approach that leverages spectral graph theory and Riemannian geometry to effectively quatify
061   topological distance between graphs. This framework handles graphs of the same size by using
062   spectral graph matching to determine node correspondence and computes distances on a Riemannian
063   manifold of modified Laplacian matrices. We show that the proposed GGD metric can theoretically
064   capture key structural (spectral) dissimilarities between two graphs, such as mismatches in Laplacian
065   eigenvalues/eigenvectors, cuts, effective-resistance distances, etc.
066
      One distinct advantage of the proposed GGD metric is its capability to compute distances between
067   graphs based on their spectral (structural) properties, while including node feature information into
068   our framework can further improve the accuracy. Therefore, GGD is suitable for analyzing many
069   real-world graphs that may only have partial or even no node features. Moreover, the proposed
070   framework for computing GGDs is more computationally efficient than existing OT-based metrics,
071   such as the TMD metric.
072
      Our empirical results show that GGD can effectively measure the dissimilarities between graphs:
073   (1) support vector classifiers (SVC) using GGDs perform competitively with state-of-the-art GNN
074   models and graph kernels on graph classification benchmarks; (2) we demonstrate that the GGD
075   metric allows us to quantify the stability of GNN models for graph classification tasks by checking
076   whether two graphs with a small GGD will lead to a significant dissimilarity in the GNN output
077   embeddings. We also show that the GGD metric has a better correlation with established GNN
078   outputs compared to the state-of-the-art TMD metric (Chuang & Jegelka, 2022) when only partial
079   node features are available: up to a 10% accuracy gain and a 9× runtime speedup have been achieved
080   in various graph classification tasks.

126   3   GGD: A G EODESIC D ISTANCE M ETRIC FOR G RAPHS
127
128   Modified Laplacian matrices on the Riemannian manifold One way to represent a simple
129   connected graph is through its Laplacian matrix, which is a Symmetric Positive Semidefinite (SPSD)
130   matrix. Graph representation using adjacency and Laplacian matrices is briefly discussed in Appendix
131   A.2. Adding a small positive value to each diagonal element allows us to transform the original
132   Laplacian matrix into a Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD) matrix, which is referred to as the
133
      Modified Laplacian Matrix in this work. In Appendix A.8, we describe the effect of this small value
      on the GGD calculation. We can then consider the cone of such modified Laplacian matrices as a
134
      natural Riemannian manifold (Lim et al., 2019), where each modified Laplacian, having the same
135
      dimensions (same number of rows/columns), can be regarded as a data point on this Riemannian
136   manifold (Vemulapalli & Jacobs, 2015; Pennec et al., 2006). Details about the Riemannian manifold
137   are provided in Appendix A.3. Finally, the geodesic distance between two graphs can be defined as
138   the shortest path distance on the Riemannian manifold, assuming their node correspondence is known
139   in advance. This approach is more appropriate than directly comparing the graphs in Euclidean space
140   (Lim et al., 2019; Crane et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2015). We will later demonstrate (Section 4.3)
141   that such a geodesic distance metric can effectively capture structural (spectral) mismatches between
142   graphs.
143
144   A two-phase spectral framework for computing GGDs Before computing GGDs, it is necessary
145
      to establish the node-to-node correspondence between two graphs. This can be achieved by leveraging
      existing graph-matching techniques (Livi & Rizzi, 2013; Emmert-Streib et al., 2016; Caetano et al.,
146
      2009). The proposed GGD metric can be computed in the following two phases. Phase 1 consists of
147
      a spectral graph matching step, using combinatorial optimization with the eigenvalues/eigenvectors
148   of the graph adjacency matrices to identify the approximate node-to-node correspondence. Phase 2
149   computes the GGD between the modified Laplacian matrices of the matched graphs by exploiting
150   generalized eigenvalues. An algorithmic flow is provided in Appendix A.1 to ensure a clear under-
151   standing of the process. The proposed GGD metric differs from previous OT-based graph distance
152   metrics in its ability to accurately represent structural discrepancies between graphs, enabling us to
153   uncover the topological variations between them more effectively. Since only the graph Laplacian
154   (adjacency) matrix is required to calculate the GGD, our metric can even work effectively for graphs
155   without node feature information.

184   Computing the GGD metric between two input graphs requires solving a graph-matching problem in
185   advance. Without knowing the node-to-node correspondence that can be achieved through a graph-
186   matching step, the distance between modified Laplacian matrices may be significantly higher than
187   the minimum possible distance. In this work, we aim to find the infimum between two SPD matrices
188   on the Riemannian manifold formed by modified Laplacian matrices, which can be accomplished
189   through a graph-matching phase. Graph matching techniques can be used to establish node-to-node

306
      Submatrix selection methods To calculate geodesic distances between SPD matrices of different
307
      sizes, prior studies have proposed a submatrix adaptation method (Lim et al., 2019). In this approach,
308   a principle submatrix with the same size as the smaller matrix is obtained from the larger matrix
309   (Ye & Lim, 2016), and subsequently used to calculate the GGD. Furthermore, this method can be
310   extended to project the smaller matrix into a larger one with the same size as the larger matrix (Lim
311   et al., 2019). While these methods are efficient for handling SPD matrices, for our application taking
312   the submatrix of the modified Laplacian can lose important nodes/edges, compromising critical graph
313   structural properties.
314
315   Graph coarsening methods In this work, we will leverage spectral graph coarsening to address
316   the issue. Spectral graph coarsening is a widely adopted process (Loukas, 2019; Aghdaei & Feng,
317
      2022) for reducing graph sizes while preserving key spectral (structural) properties, such as the
      Laplacian eigenvalues/eigenvectors. Recent spectral graph coarsening methods aim to decompose an
318
      input graph into many distinct node clusters, so that a reduced graph can be formed by treating each
319
      node cluster as a new node, with a goal of assuring that the reduced graph will approximately retain
320   the original graph’s structure (Loukas, 2019; Han et al., 2024; Aghdaei & Feng, 2022). Therefore,
321   when computing GGDs for graphs of different sizes, we can first adopt spectral graph coarsening to
322   transform the bigger graph into a smaller one, so that our framework in Section 4 can be subsequently
323   utilized. However, existing state-of-the-art graph coarsening methods do not allow us to precisely
      control the size of the reduced graphs.

326   In this work, we introduce a spectral graph coarsening method using effective-resistance clustering
327   (Aghdaei & Feng, 2022). Our approach starts with estimating the effective resistances of all edges in
328   the original graph. We can also incorporate the difference between node features (if available) as an
329   additional parameter. In the graph coarsening phase, our method will rank edges according to their
330   resistance distances and only the top few edges with the smallest resistances will be coarsened into
331   new nodes. This approach enables precise control over the size of the reduced graphs while preserving
332
      crucial structural properties, such as the eigenvalues/eigenvectors of the adjacency matrices, which
      are essential for the subsequent spectral graph matching step (Phase 1 in Section 4.1).

366
367
      Assuming the graph matching problem can always find the exact correspondence between nodes,
368   we prove that the GGD metric (based on AIRM) between any two nonempty graphs is a metric that
369   satisfies the following conditions:
370
371          • The distance between a graph and itself or between two isomorphic graphs is zero:
372            GGD(G, G) = 0.
373          • (Positivity) The distance between two distinct graphs is positive: GGD(G1 , G2 ) ≥ 0.
374          • (Symmetry) The distance between G1 and G2 is the same of the one between G2 and G1 :
375            GGD(G1 , G2 ) = GGD(G2 , G1 ).
376          • The triangle inequality: GGD(G1 , G3 ) ≤ GGD(G1 , G2 ) + GGD(G2 , G3 ).
377
      Detailed proofs of the above four properties are provided in Appendix A.5.

394   7.1   A PPLICATION OF GGD S IN GNN S TABILITY A NALYSIS
395
396
      To analyze the stability of GNN models (Bronstein et al., 2017; Garg et al., 2020; Duvenaud et al.,
397   2015), we conducted multiple experiments with the GGD and TMD metrics. GNNs typically operate
398   by a message-passing mechanism (Gilmer et al., 2017), where at each layer, nodes send their feature
399   representations to their neighbors. The feature representation of each node is initialized to its
400   original features and is updated by repeatedly aggregating incoming messages from neighbors. In
401   our experiment, we relate GGD to the Graph Isomorphism Networks (GIN) (Xu et al., 2019), one of
402   the most widely applied and powerful GNNs, utilizing the MUTAG dataset (Morris et al., 2020) as
403   our reference graph dataset. The objective is to analyze the relationship between the input distance
404   GGD(G1 , G2 ) and the distance between the output GIN vectors, ∥h(G1 ) − h(G2 )∥ for randomly
405
      selected pairs of graphs.
406   We employed a three-layer GIN network as described in (Xu et al., 2019). This network uses GIN
407   convolutional layers to update tensors of nodes based on their neighboring nodes and then aggregates
408   those outputs in a vector representation, followed by linear layers for classification tasks. Thus it
409   outputs a single vector h(G) for the entire graph G. The result is illustrated in Figure 3.
410   We observe a strong correlation between GGD and the output distance, as indicated by a high
411   Pearson correlation coefficient. This finding implies the effectiveness of the proposed GGD metric for
412   analyzing the stability of GNN models (Chuang & Jegelka, 2022). To compare GGD with existing
413   metrics, we repeat this experiment using TMD without considering node attributes (features). As
414   shown in Figure 3, GGD demonstrates a better correlation with GIN outputs than the TMD metric
415   across different levels. These findings indicate that when dealing with graphs without node features,
416   GGD should be adopted for the stability analysis of graph learning models.

      7.3   RUNTIME C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS AND C OMPARISON
460
461   When comparing various graph distance metrics, a primary consideration is their computational
462   complexity. Conventional approaches usually require intricate computations that frequently have
463   cubic time or higher complexities. For our problem, the spectral graph matching step requires the
464   eigenvalue decomposition of adjacency matrices and solving the linear assignment problem (LAP).
465   Eigenvalue decomposition of an n × n matrix has a complexity of O(n3 ) (Borodin & Munro, 1975;
466   Flamary et al., 2021), while solving the LAP using the Hungarian algorithm also has a runtime
467
      complexity of O(n3 ). Similarly, calculating the generalized eigenvalue of two SPD matrices entails a
      cubic complexity. Consequently, the overall complexity of GGD calculation is O(n3 ). On the other
468
      hand, TMD is an OT-based distance metric with a complexity of O(n3 log(n)) (Chuang & Jegelka,
469
      2022; Flamary et al., 2021). Therefore, GGD exhibits slightly better (lower) runtime complexity than
470   the TMD metric.
471
472   To evaluate runtime performance, we conduct extensive experiments to compare the runtime of
473
      computing TMD at various levels with GGD on both small graphs (MUTAG, BZR) and large graphs
      (PC-3H, SW-620H) collected from the TUDataset (Morris et al., 2020). Table 3 presents the average
474
      runtime (in seconds) for computing 100 distances between different graphs obtained by repeating the
475
      experiment five times. The results demonstrate that GGD consistently outperforms TMD in terms of
476   runtime across all datasets and scenarios, particularly when dealing with larger graphs that contain
477   more nodes. The TMD metric computation usually requires more levels to effectively capture the
478   entire graph structure. In such cases, GGD exhibits runtime performance approximately 6 − 9 times
479   faster than of TMD. Hence, we conclude that GGD is significantly more computationally efficient
480   than TMD, especially when working with large graphs. More details about our experimental setup
481   can be found in Appendix A.12.

      A.3    R IEMANNIAN M ANIFOLD
796
797
      A manifold is a type of topological space that resembles Euclidean space in small, local regions
798   around each point. In other words, for every point on a manifold, there is a neighborhood that is
799   similar to a flat multidimensional space. A Riemannian manifold is a type of manifold equipped
800   with a smoothly varying inner product on the tangent spaces at each point. This means that for every
801   point on the manifold, the tangent space has a way of measuring distances and angles, and these
802   measurements change smoothly from point to point (Lee, 2018). In simpler terms, a Riemannian
803   manifold is a smooth, curved space that locally behaves like Euclidean space but has its own geometric
804   properties, such as how distances, angles, and volumes are defined. These properties are determined
805   by a Riemannian metric, which generalizes the concept of measuring lengths and angles in flat space
806
      to curved spaces (Lee, 2018).
807   A Riemannian manifold can have curvature, unlike a flat space. This curvature allows the study of
808   geometric shapes ranging from spheres and cylinders to more abstract surfaces. The Riemannian
809   structure enables us to compute geodesics, volumes, and various types of curvature. This makes
      Riemannian manifolds fundamental in fields like differential geometry and physics, and increasingly


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      important in data science, where curved spaces are used to model complex datasets (You & Park,
811   2021).

      A.4     E FFECTIVE R ESISTANCE IN G RAPH T HEORY
814
815   Effective resistance, also known as resistance distance, is a concept in graph theory that measures the
816   electrical resistance between two nodes in a weighted or unweighted graph represented as a network
817   of resistors (Ghosh et al., 2008). It draws an analogy between electrical networks and graphs, helping
818   to quantify how easily current can flow between two nodes, where the edges are treated as resistors.
819   The effective resistance between nodes provides insight into the connectivity between the network.
820   This means two nodes with lower effective resistance values have higher connectivity (Ellens et al.,
821   2011).
