009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Improving the efficiency and performance of implicit neural representations in 3D,
012            particularly Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) and Signed Distance Fields (SDF) is
013            crucial for enabling their use in real-time applications. These models, while ca-
014            pable of generating photo-realistic novel views and detailed 3D reconstructions,
015            often suffer from high computational costs and slow inference times. To address
016            this, we introduce a novel neural network layer called the “magnituder”, designed
017            to reduce the number of training parameters in these models without sacrificing
018            their expressive power. By integrating magnituders into standard feed-forward
019            layer stacks, we achieve improved inference speed and adaptability. Furthermore,
020
               our approach enables a zero-shot performance boost in trained implicit neural rep-
               resentation models through layer-wise knowledge transfer without backpropaga-
021
               tion, leading to more efficient scene reconstruction in dynamic environments.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027    In recent years, the integration of advanced machine learning techniques into robotics has trans-
028   formed the field of robotic manipulation and trajectory planning. As robots increasingly operate
029   in unstructured environments, the ability to perceive and interact with complex scenes becomes es-
030   sential. Traditional robotic perception methods often rely on explicit geometric models, which can
031   limit flexibility and adaptability. In contrast, implicit neural representations such as Neural Radiance
032
      Fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2020) and incremental Signed Distance Fields (iSDF) (Ortiz et al.,
      2022) have emerged as powerful tools that offer robust ways to model 3D scenes with high fidelity.
033
034   NeRF, initially developed for novel view synthesis, uses multiple feed-forward layers (FFLs) to en-
035   code 3D scenes into a continuous volumetric representation, enabling photo-realistic rendering from
036   arbitrary viewpoints. Its ability to learn intricate scene details from sparse data makes NeRF partic-
037   ularly appealing for robotics applications, especially in dynamic or poorly structured environments.
038
      On the other hand, iSDF provides a versatile framework for capturing the geometric properties of
      objects, facilitating real-time collision detection and path planning. By representing surfaces as
039
      implicit functions, iSDF seamlessly integrates shape information into robotic control systems.
040
041   Despite the exciting opportunities that NeRF and iSDF offer for enhancing robotic manipulation and
042   trajectory planning, their practical deployment in real-world scenarios still requires improvements
043   in training and inference speed. While iSDF provides real-time mapping, it relies on known camera
044
      poses, which adds computational overhead in real-world applications. Accelerating both training
      and inference can make the entire system faster, expanding the potential for real-time applications.
045
046   In real-world applications, robots often encounter novel objects and unpredictable conditions. Rapid
047   adaptation to new scenarios is crucial for maintaining operational efficiency and safety. Quickly re-
048   training models or adapting them on-the-fly can significantly improve a robot’s performance in tasks
049   such as grasping or navigating cluttered spaces. Slow training cycles may result in outdated models
050
      that fail to accurately represent the current environment, reducing reliability and effectiveness.
051   Moreover, inference speed is vital for real-time decision-making in robotic systems. Delays in
052   processing visual and spatial data can negatively impact a robot’s ability to perform timely actions,
053   which is particularly important in fast-paced environments. For instance, in collaborative settings
      where robots work alongside humans, latency can create safety risks and hinder task efficiency.

054
      Thus, optimizing inference times while maintaining high accuracy is essential for deploying these
055   advanced representations in practical applications.
056
057   The main computational block for NeRF and iSDF are FFLs. To reduce the computational com-
058
      plexity, various methods have been proposed that reduce the burden of FFLs by exploiting explicit
      representations (Chen et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2022; Müller et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021; Fridovich-
059
      Keil et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020; Barron et al., 2023), reducing the number of network inferences
060
      through sample pruning with additional occupancy flags (Sun et al., 2022; Müller et al., 2022; Li
061   et al., 2023a), or introducing a novel volume rendering procedure (Han et al., 2024).
062
063   In this work, we address this challenge by introducing a novel neural network layer, called magni-
064
      tuder that can be combined with standard FFLs in an implicit neural representation. The magnitud-
      ers disentangle the processing of weights and inputs to the layer, only connecting them at the end
065
      through simple linear operations. The magnituders use structured transformations of the input to
066
      the layer that depend solely on the magnitude of the input (hence their name). This design allows
067   for a reduction in the training parameters of the FFL blocks while maintaining the same level of
068   expressivity as a standard FFL.
069
070
      To summarize, our contributions in this work are the following :
071          • We introduce a novel random feature mechanism called magnituders that optimizes the
072            computation of FFLs in implicit neural representations (Section 3).
073
             • We demonstrate how this mechanism can approximate FFL with commonly used activation
074            functions like ReLU and Softplus (Section 4.1).
075
             • We showcase the versatility of our method across a wide range of scenes and a variety of
076
               implicit neural representations (Section 4.2).
077
             • We distill existing MLP layers in an implicit neural representation with our magnituders
078
               which are trained analytically without backpropagation. Our method allows for zero-shot
079
               integration of our novel layer with pretrained models, improving inference speed without
080            costly retraining. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first successful application of
081            knowledge distillation without backpropagation for implicit neural representations, demon-
082            strating its effectiveness in downstream tasks (Section 4.3).
083
084   Note that our method is orthogonal to the sparse sampling and the partitioning techniques and can
085
      be combined in such to further reduce the computational footprint.

      A common challenge in training neural networks is that their space and time complexity scale
      quadratically with the size of their hidden layers. This challenge is also inherited by implicit neural
124
      representations. One of the ways to tackle this issue is via dimensionality reduction à la Johnson-
125
      Lindenstrauss Transform (or JLT) (Dasgupta & Gupta, 2003; Dasgupta et al., 2010; Ailon & Liberty,
126   2013) or kernel methods (Rahimi & Recht, 2007). Kernel methods have a rich history, spanning
127   the linearization of 2-layer neural networks (Cho & Saul, 2009; 2011), Neural Tangent Kernels
128   (NTK)(Jacot et al., 2018), and the linearization of attention mechanisms in Transformers (Choro-
129   manski et al., 2020). Kernel methods using random feature mechanisms have shown promise in
130   reducing the computational complexity of neural networks (Choromanski et al., 2020; Rahimi &
131   Recht, 2007). However, these approaches are not directly applicable to the MLP layers that are
132   the backbone of NeRF and iSDF systems. To address the computational complexity of FFLs, Se-
133   hanobish et al. (2024) introduced the Universal Random Feature (URF), which disentangles weights
134
      and inputs in specific MLP layers. Their method relies on Fourier transforms of activation functions,
      limiting its applicability to commonly used activation functions in machine learning.

139   In this section we define our magnituder-layers (MAG-layers in short). Consider a layer M : Rd →
140   Rl taking as input a d-dimensional vector, outputting an l-dimensional vector and given as follows:
141                                      MW,θ (x) = E [Wvθ (∥x∥2 )] ,                                     (1)
142
143   where W ∈ Rl×d is a learnable matrix and vθ : R → Rd is a map operating on the magnitude
144   (length) of the input x. This transformation is potentially learnable (with a set of learnable param-
145   eters θ) or probabilistic (in the latter setting, θ encode the parameters of the distribution Dθ used to
146
      sample the values of vθ (x)). The expectation is with respect to Dθ . In practice, in the probabilistic
      setting, MW,θ (x) is not computed exactly, but unbiasedly approximated by sampling.
147
148   Magnituders disentangle the processing of the input x and weights W, only to connect them via
149   simple linear matrix-vector multiplication at the end. In magnituders, randomness is introduced
150   via multiplications of x with a Gaussian matrix G ∈ Rm×d where the entries are drawn iid from
151   N (0, 1), for some m > 0 which are subsequently followed by purely deterministic transformations.
152
      The parameter m is called the number of random features in a MAG-layer. Under this randomiza-
      tion strategy, taking a kernel perspective, magnituders can be interpreted as outputting a vector of
153

407
408    In our final application, we explore iSDF (Ortiz et al., 2022), a real-time module designed to recon-

410   casing its effectiveness in real-time scenarios. The network architecture consists of a 7-layer MLP
411   (See Fig. 14). We replace Softplus with ReLU for initial speedup and then evaluate whether further
412   acceleration can be achieved with MAG layers (See Appendix D and Fig. 16 for justification).
413
      We modify the original iSDF network using a similar strategy as in our NeRF experiments, resulting
414   in our new variant, MAG-iSDF (details are provided in Appendix C.6). We evaluate the accuracy
415   of our MAG-iSDF in ReplicaCAD (Szot et al., 2021) sequences using three metrics: average SDF
416   error (L1avg ) over the entire scene, surface SDF error (L1surf ) on GT mesh surface, and the one-way
417   Chamfer Distance (CD) from the reconstructed to the GT mesh. We also report the training time per
418   step (ttrain ) including sample generation and forward-backward passes, and inference time (tinfer ) by
419   measuring 1000 random rays’ forward pass.
420   In Table 5, we compare the original iSDF with MAG-iSDF. Our method is only 2% behind the
421   baseline while being 23% faster. We show detailed ablation results with different number of random
422   features in Table 12. The results reveal a trade-off between computational speed and reconstruction
423   quality (Fig. 7 (Right)). Moreover, when we visualize slices of the reconstructed SDFs on the xy-
424   plane at z = 70 cm, the reconstruction quality remains quite similar (Fig. 6).
425
      Reducing hidden dimensions is a straightforward method to accelerate training. For fair compar-
426   ison, we reduce the number of hidden channels in the last layer to 32 and replace the last hidden
427   layer with a MAG layer containing 32 random features. We evaluate this on the longest sequence,
428   apt 2 nav, from the Replica dataset in iSDF. In Figure 7, we plot the inference time and Chamfer
429   Distance. While hidden dimension reduction (DR) reduces inference time at the expense of quality,
430   our MAG layer achieves a similar speed-up while maintaining reconstruction quality. The bundling
431   process combines the last two layers into a single operation, leading to additional speed-up without
      compromising quality (Figure 7).

475   4.3   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION WITH MAGNITUDER
476
477    In previous sections, we demonstrate that our MAG layer can significantly accelerate the inference
478   time of implicit neural representations. However, this acceleration requires retraining the model
479   from scratch, a process that is both computationally intensive and time-consuming, especially for
480   complex models like NeRF and Zip-NeRF.
481   To address this limitation, we introduce a layer distillation mechanism that eliminates the need for
482   retraining on a per-scene basis. This method allows us to accelerate existing, pretrained models
483   without having to rebuild them from the ground up. By storing the inputs and outputs of the target
484   hidden layer in trained implicit neural representations, and then optimizing the MAG layer using
485   a mean squared error objective, we can efficiently replicate the behavior of the original model.
      This optimization can be performed without the need for backpropagation due to the existence of a


                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      closed-form solution (Additional details in Section B). We present additional details on distillation
487   experiments with trainable G in Appendix D.5).
488
489   First, we distill a pretrained Zip-NeRF model with a MAG layer and test the distillation in bicycle
490
      dataset (additional details in Appendix C.3). We set the number of random features to 64, with abla-
      tions provided in Appendix D.3 (Figure 18 and Table 15). The qualitative results of the distillation
491
      are shown in Figure 9. Notably, this distillation process is lightning fast, taking only 0.12 seconds
492
      on a single RTX 4090, thanks to the closed-form solution for the input-output pairs. Since we use
493   the same number of random features as in Section 4.2.2, we achieve the same 6% rendering acceler-
494   ation without needing to train from scratch. We show that we can recover the quality of the original
495   NERF model while reducing the inference speeds by up to 42% (Table 13 and Fig 8 (right)).

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534    We introduce a novel type of neural network layer, termed ‘magnituders’, which can efficiently
535   approximate the computations of ReLU and Softplus linear layers. By integrating magnituders into
536   Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) and Signed Distance Fields (SDF) models, we achieve a reduction
537   in training parameters while preserving expressivity. Our approach enhances inference speed and
538   adaptability for real-time robotic applications. Additionally, the straightforward design of magnitud-
539   ers facilitates layer-wise knowledge distillation without requiring backpropagation. We demonstrate
      that this distillation process leads to efficient inference.

        For training NERF, we use a RF layer followed by a linear layer. In this section, we explain how
975    this choice of architecture allows for more efficient inference. In this section, we use the PyTorch

       Recall the RF layer be given by the following equation :
979                                              Y1 = ϕ2 (X1 )W1 ⊤                                           (8)

982
983

985                                           = ϕ2 (X1 )W1 ⊤ W2 ⊤ + b2
986
                                              = ϕ2 (X1 )Ŵ2⊤ + b2
987
988    where Ŵ2 := W2 W1 .

       Note that the above compression works when a non-linearity is applied on the weights (as in the
990    case of (Sehanobish et al., 2024)) or an optional bias in Equation 8.

992    This simple compression technique allows us to collapse the final 2 layers in our iSDF experiments

995    B     K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION M ADE E ASY
996
997    The simple form of the RF layer makes knowledge distillation particularly simple.
998
       To be precise, we want to train a MAG layer M to match the outputs of a trained FFL using MSE
999    as the metric. If x (resp. y) is the input (resp. output of a trained FFL, we want to find the weights
1000
       Ŵ, which minimizes the following error :
1001
1002                                              ||MW (x) − y||2
1003
1004   The optimal Ŵ has a closed form :
                                                          ⊤
1005                                            Ŵ = (x′ x′ )−1 x′⊤ y                                        (9)
1006           ′
       where x := ReLU(Gx), G is an (orthogonal) random matrix.
1007
1008   The above solution is clearly unique iff x′⊤ x′ is invertible. In the case where x′⊤ x′ is not invertible,
1009   one can replace the inverse by the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse.
1010   Thus the problem of distilling specific input-output pairs in the MAG layer can be solved without
1011   backpropagation.

1220   D-NeRF (Pumarola et al., 2021) extends NeRF to represent dynamic scenes using a deformation
1221   network. When rendering an image, the deformation network takes the position and time as inputs,
1222   producing a 3-dimensional offset that captures the current positional shift. This offset allows the ray
1223   to bend, simulating how the scene moves over time.
1224   We use Pytorch implementation (Tang, 2022) for D-NeRF, which integrates the hash-grid (Müller
1225   et al., 2022) for further acceleration. Since it exploits the explicit grid structure, the network is
1226   shallower than the original D-NeRF. To accelerate this model, we target the deformation network,
1227   by replacing two of its layers with our MAG layers (See Figure 12 for more details).
1228   For a fair apple-to-apple comparison between the baseline and our MAG variants, we minimize
1229   randomness by disabling certain acceleration methods in the implementation. Specifically, we use

1605   class MAG(nn.Module):
1606      def init (self, num rf, in dim, out dim):
1607          self.projection matrix = nn.Parameter(
1608                torch.randn(self.in dim, self.num rf),
1609                requires grad=False)
1610
              self.weight = nn.Parameter(torch.randn(out dim, num rf))

             outputs = inputs @ self.weight.t() # shape = [*, out dim]
             return outputs
