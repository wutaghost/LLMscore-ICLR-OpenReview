009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Discrete representations play a crucial role in many deep learning architectures,
012            yet their non-differentiable nature poses significant challenges for gradient-based
013            optimization. To address this issue, various gradient estimators have been devel-
014            oped, including the Straight-Through Gumbel-Softmax (ST-GS) estimator, which
015            combines the Straight-Through Estimator (STE) and the Gumbel-based reparam-
016
               eterization trick. However, the performance of ST-GS is highly sensitive to tem-
               perature, with its selection often compromising gradient fidelity. In this work, we
017
               propose a simple yet effective extension to ST-GS by employing decoupled tem-
018
               peratures for forward and backward passes, which we refer to as Decoupled ST-
019            GS. We show that our approach significantly enhances the original ST-GS through
020            extensive experiments across multiple tasks and datasets. We further investigate
021            the impact of our method on gradient fidelity from multiple perspectives, includ-
022            ing the gradient gap and the bias-variance trade-off of estimated gradients. Our
023            findings contribute to the ongoing effort to improve discrete optimization in deep
024            learning, offering a practical solution that balances simplicity and effectiveness.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Discrete representations have been widely used as a powerful tool in deep learning, offering several
029
      advantages compared to their continuous counterparts. These representations can lead to more effi-
030   cient data compression (Ballé et al., 2017; Toderici et al., 2017) as well as improved interpretability
031   in comparison to their continuous counterparts (Vahdat & Kautz, 2020). Their advantage is that the
032   structure of discrete variables often aligns with categorical semantics, making them easier to inter-
033   pret in high-level tasks like image synthesis or reinforcement learning (Van Den Oord et al., 2017).
034   Furthermore, discrete representations can serve as useful inductive bias, enhancing systematic gen-
035   eralisation across various architectural paradigms (Liu et al., 2021).
036   The use of discrete latents is prominent across a variety of applications. Notable examples include
037   discrete latent variable models such as Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoders (VQ-VAEs) (Van
038   Den Oord et al., 2017), categorical and Bernoulli VAEs (Jang et al., 2016), and hard attention mech-
039   anisms (Xu, 2015). In reinforcement learning, discrete actions are naturally used for action selection
040   policies (Mnih et al., 2015). Other critical applications include neural architecture search (Liu et al.,
041   2018), and model quantization for efficiency (Han et al., 2015).
042   Despite their advantages, training models with discrete latent variables poses a significant chal-
043   lenge: non-differentiability. In deep learning, optimization is typically performed via gradient-based
044   methods, which require differentiable operations to propagate error signals during backpropagation
045   (Rumelhart et al., 1986). The discrete nature of latent variables, however, breaks this smoothness,
046   making traditional gradient descent inapplicable. This challenge has spurred considerable research
047   into techniques for bypassing the non-differentiability of discrete variables during optimization.
048   Three primary strategies have emerged for dealing with this challenge: policy-based REINFORCE-
049   style estimators, which uses Monte Carlo-based method that estimates gradients for discrete choices
050   (Williams, 2004), relaxation-based approaches such as the Gumbel-Softmax trick, which uses con-
051   tinuous approximations of discrete distributions (Jang et al., 2016), and the Straight-Through Es-
052   timator (STE) that “short-circuits” the non-differentiability by treating non-differentiable functions
053   as differentiable during the backward pass (Bengio et al., 2013). Each of these methods aims to
      provide gradient estimates for non-differentiable functions in discrete models.

      One popular approach that combines elements from the latter two paradigms is the Straight-Through
055   Gumbel-Softmax (ST-GS) estimator. This method allows backpropagation through discrete vari-
056   ables by employing the Gumbel-Max trick for the forward pass and using the STE to approximate
057   the gradient in the backward pass. While ST-GS has shown promise due to its simplicity and prac-
058   tical utility, its performance is highly sensitive to the temperature parameter. A lower temperature
059   sharpens the probability distributions in the forward direction, making the model more determin-
060   istic, while higher temperatures introduce more stochasticity. However, if the relaxation in the
061   backward direction is insufficient, gradients fail to propagate effectively through the discretization
062   step, impairing optimization. This overload poses a trade-off between gradient fidelity and model
063
      performance, with temperature selection often becoming a bottleneck for optimization (Jang et al.,
      2016; Maddison et al., 2016).
064
065   In this work, we propose a simple yet highly effective improvement to the Gumbel-ST estimator:
066   decoupling the temperature parameters used for the forward and backward passes. By introducing
067   distinct temperatures for these passes, we mitigate the issues associated with using a single tem-
068   perature, which can lead to sub-optimal performance despite extensive hyperparameter tuning. Our
069
      approach, which we refer to as the Decoupled ST-GS, enables more flexible control over the trade-off
      between relaxation smoothness during inference and gradient fidelity during training.
070
071   Through extensive experiments on both reconstruction and generative modelling tasks, we demon-
072   strate that the Decoupled ST-GS approach consistently outperforms the vanilla ST-GS. We show
073   that the use of a single temperature compromises both performance and gradient fidelity, while our
074   method leads to substantial performance gains by removing the constraint of shared temperatures.
075
      Furthermore, we thoroughly investigate the effect of our method on gradient fidelity by analyzing
      the gradient gap and the bias-variance trade-off of the estimated gradients.
076
077   Our contributions in this work are threefold:
078
079          • We propose Decoupled ST-GS, a novel extension to the Straight-Through Gumbel-Softmax
080            (ST-GS) estimator, which decouples the temperature parameters for the forward and back-
081            ward passes, allowing for control of relaxation smoothness during inference independently
082
               of gradient fidelity during training.
083          • We demonstrate that Decoupled ST-GS achieves significant performance improvements
084            over the vanilla (state of the art) ST-GS estimator through extensive experiments on diverse
085            tasks and datasets.
086
087
             • We conduct a comprehensive analysis of the impact of Decoupled ST-GS on gradient fi-
               delity from different perspectives, such as the gradient gap and the bias-variance trade-off
088
               of estimated gradients, providing deeper insights into how our approach improves gradient-
089
               based optimization in discrete latent models.

092   2   R ELATED W ORKS
093
094
      Discrete optimization in deep learning has garnered significant attention due to its applicability in
095
      various tasks such as data compression, generative modelling, and reinforcement learning (RL). A
096   common challenge in training discrete models is the non-differentiable nature of categorical vari-
097   ables, which obstructs the use of standard gradient-based optimization methods. To address this,
098   three major approaches have been proposed: policy-based estimators, relaxation-based methods,
099   and the Straight-Through Estimator (STE).
100
      Policy-based estimators, such as the REINFORCE algorithm (Williams, 2004), provide a Monte
101
      Carlo-based gradient estimation for discrete variables. However, these methods often suffer from
102   high variance, making them challenging to apply effectively in large-scale models. Previous works
103   have sought to reduce this variance by combining control variates with continuous relaxations, yield-
104   ing low-variance, unbiased gradient estimates (Tucker et al., 2017). Relaxation-based approaches,
105   such as the Gumbel-Softmax trick (Jang et al., 2016), provide a continuous approximation to cate-
106   gorical distributions, which allows for smooth gradient flow but comes at the cost of approximating
107   discrete variables. The temperature parameter in Gumbel-Softmax controls the trade-off between
      smoothness and sharpness in the approximation, making it a crucial factor in performance tuning.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      The STE (Bengio et al., 2013) offers an alternative by approximating gradients through non-
109   differentiable functions. It effectively treats discrete operations as differentiable during backprop-
110   agation, enabling the use of categorical variables in neural networks. Extensions like ReinMax
111   (Liu et al., 2024) have been proposed to enhance the accuracy of gradient approximation, using
112   second-order methods to achieve better performance without introducing significant computational
113   overhead. The Straight-Through Gumbel-Softmax (ST-GS) estimator (Jang et al., 2016; Maddison
114   et al., 2016) combines the advantages of both Gumbel-based relaxation and the STE, allowing for
115   continuous relaxation during training while retaining discrete sampling in the forward pass.
116   Despite the advantages of ST-GS, its performance is highly sensitive to the temperature parameter,
117   leading to trade-offs between model performance and gradient fidelity. Previous research has em-
118   phasized the importance of careful temperature tuning (Jang et al., 2016), yet this often becomes
119   a bottleneck for optimization, particularly when shared temperatures are used for both the forward
120   and backward passes. This limitation motivated our work to explore decoupling the temperature for
121   these two passes, providing greater flexibility in balancing the smoothness of relaxation and gradient
122   fidelity.

239   4     M ETHODOLOGY
240
241   In this section, we discuss the limitation of using a single temperature in ST-GS and describe our
242   proposed method, Decoupled ST-GS, which improves the original ST-GS by introducing separate
243   independent temperature parameters for the forward and backward passes. Our approach is designed
244   to increase gradient fidelity and performance by addressing the trade-offs inherent in the selection
245   of a single temperature in the standard ST-GS estimator.
246
247   4.1   R ETHINKING A S INGLE T EMPERATURE
248
      The traditional ST-GS estimator relies on a single temperature parameter to control the smoothness
249
      of the relaxation in both the forward and backward passes. However, we argue that using a single
250   temperature throughout the training process, even after performing a comprehensive hyperparame-
251   ter search, cannot adequately capture the asymmetry between the forward and backward operations
252   of the model. This limitation stems from the distinct roles played by the encoder and decoder: the
253   encoder maps continuous inputs into discrete latent variables, while the decoder reconstructs contin-
254   uous outputs from these discrete representations. Given this fundamental asymmetry, the gradients
255   flowing through the encoder and decoder should be handled differently to optimize performance.
256   Secondly, in the standard ST-GS estimator, a single temperature τ controls the sharpness of the
257   categorical distribution in both the forward and backward passes. This shared temperature often
258   presents a trade-off between sharpness and gradient fidelity. A low temperature τ sharpens the
259   categorical distribution, leading to a discrete-like sampling process, but can introduce high variance
260   in the gradients. Conversely, a higher τ smooths the distribution and reduces gradient variance, but
261   compromises the sharpness of the forward pass, which is critical for performance in tasks requiring
262   discrete representations.
263   These observations motivate our proposal to decouple the temperature values for the forward and
264   backward passes, enabling finer control over both the relaxation smoothness in the forward pass and
265   the gradient fidelity in the backward pass.
266

355   5.1.1    R ECONSTRUCTION TASK
356
357   We first evaluate our method on a standard reconstruction task using binary autoencoders across
358   three datasets: MNIST (Deng, 2012), CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky, 2009), and SVHN (Netzer et al.,
359   2011). The architecture used for this experiment consists of a convolutional encoder and decoder
360   with residual connections (He et al., 2016) and a latent space dimension of 8 × 8 × 32. Each model
361   was trained for 100 epochs with a batch size of 64.
362   The first set of results in Fig. 2 show the heatmaps of the validation loss for different datasets across
363   various forward (τ f ) and backward (τ b ) temperature settings, averaged over 5 seeds. The plots
364   highlight that configurations with higher backward temperatures and lower forward temperatures
365   yielded the best reconstruction performance. Specifically, performance plateaued when τ f was set
366   to 0.3 and τ b reached 3. Beyond these values, further improvements in reconstruction loss were
367   minimal, suggesting the optimal temperature range is constrained within these bounds.
368   While the heatmap provides visual insight into the effective temperature ranges, we further visualise
369   these results using line plots, shown in the second row of Fig. 2. Notably, when only one temperature
370   was tuned, performance improvements were relatively minor, as seen in the red markers on the line
371   plot (which correspond to the heatmap’s diagonal elements). This supports our hypothesis that a
372   single-temperature approach is insufficient to capture the inherent asymmetry between the encoder
373   and decoder in autoencoders. In contrast, the use of decoupled temperatures in our method led to
374
      significant performance gains, as shown in the line plot. Additionally, the small error bars indicate
      low variance across experiments, confirming the robustness of these results.
375
376   We also experimented with temperature scheduling, where forward and backward temperatures were
377   dynamically adjusted throughout training. However, as detailed in B, this approach did not yield any
      substantial improvements over fixed temperature settings.

       5.2.1                                       G RADIENT G AP
453   In the binary autoencoder experiments, due to the large latent space, computing exact gradients was
454   computationally prohibitive (see appendix C for details about exact gradient calculation). Therefore,
455   we used a proxy metric called the gradient gap (Huh et al., 2023) G to assess the fidelity of the
456   gradients produced by the Gumbel-Softmax relaxation. The gradient gap measures the squared
457   L2 norm of the difference between the gradients computed from forward passes using continuous
458   relaxations and discrete samples (see appendix D for more details).
459   Figure 5 presents the gradient gap for different temperature configurations. We observed a significant
460   decrease in the gradient gap with increasing backward temperature. This suggests that the backward
461   temperature plays a key role in enhancing gradient fidelity by aligning the discrete gradients more
462   closely with the relaxed gradients. These results underscore the importance of carefully tuning τ b to
463   minimize the gradient gap and improve the quality of the gradient estimates.

       5.2.2                                       B IAS -VARIANCE A NALYSIS
480   For the 8 × 4 categorical VAE, we conducted an exact gradient analysis, allowing for a detailed bias
481   and variance study. Specifically, we calculated the bias of the estimated gradients by comparing them
482   to the exact gradients obtained during the categorical VAE training process. These exact gradients
483   were computed by considering all possible hidden states, calculating the error for each, and then
484   computing the expected loss weighted by the probability of each configuration being selected (see
485   appendix C for more details). This analysis provided insights into the accuracy of the gradient
      estimates generated by the Gumbel-Softmax relaxation.

486
      In this experiment, we focused on the optimal configuration derived from the performance results:
487   a forward temperature of τ f = 1.6 and a backward temperature of τ b = 1.3. We varied one
488   temperature while keeping the other fixed and plotted the resulting bias and variance of the gradients.
489   Figure 6 shows that increasing the backward temperature reduced both the bias and variance of the
490   gradient estimates, indicating that the backward temperature has a stabilizing effect on gradient
491   estimation, reducing noise and improving gradient reliability.
492
      Conversely, increasing the forward temperature while holding the backward temperature constant
493   led to an increase in both bias and variance, suggesting that higher forward temperatures introduce
494   more variability into the gradient estimates. These findings highlight that forward and backward
495   temperatures have distinct, complementary effects on gradient fidelity, reinforcing the need to tune
496   each temperature separately.

528   6    C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we introduced the Decoupled ST-GS estimator, a simple but effective extension of the
531   ST-GS method that allows for distinct temperature values in the forward and backward passes. Our
532   approach addresses the limitation of using a single temperature, which often compromises perfor-
533   mance and gradient accuracy in ST-GS. Through extensive experiments across various tasks and
534   datasets, we demonstrated that our method consistently outperforms the vanilla ST-GS estimator,
535   offering substantial improvements in both model performance and gradient fidelity.
536   The flexibility provided by decoupling the temperature allows for finer control over the trade-off
537   between relaxation smoothness during inference and gradient fidelity during training. We also pro-
538   vided an in-depth analysis of the gradient gap and the bias-variance trade-off in gradient estimation,
539   showing that our approach mitigates these issues by allowing independent tuning of temperatures
      for the forward and backward passes.

650
      A.1   B INARY AUTOENCODER A RCHITECTURE
651
652   For the binary autoencoder experiments, we used a convolutional encoder and decoder with residual
653   connections. The encoder consists of several convolutional layers which project the data into a latent
654   space of dimension 8 × 8 × 32. The decoder mirrors this architecture, upsampling the latent space
655   back to the input dimensionality.
656
      We used the MNIST, CIFAR10, and SVHN datasets, without any form of input normalisation. All
657
      models were trained for 100 epochs with a batch size of 64, and we applied the Adam optimizer
658   with a learning rate of 3 × 10−4 . Each experiment was carried out with 5 different seeds.
659
660
661
      A.2   C ATEGORICAL VAE A RCHITECTURE
662
      For the categorical VAE experiments, we employed a 3-layer MLP encoder and decoder. The en-
663
      coder architecture consists of layers projecting the input from 784 dimensions (flattened images)
664   down to 512, then to 256, and finally to the categorical latent space with dimensions 8 × 4 or
665   16 × 12. The decoder mirrors this structure, projecting from the latent space back to the original
666   dimensionality.
667
      We trained the models for 160 epochs using the RAdam optimizer without any input normalisation
668
      on MNIST. Two experimental setups were tested: 1) 8 categorical dimensions × 4 latent dimensions
669
      with a learning rate of 5×10−4 . 2) 16 categorical dimensions × 12 latent dimensions with a learning
670   rate of 7 × 10−4 . Each experiment was carried out with 10 different seeds.
671
672
673
      A.3   T EMPERATURE T UNING P ROCESS
674
      The forward (τ f ) and backward (τ b ) temperatures were tuned by conducting grid searches over a set
675   of predefined values. For the binary autoencoder experiments, τ f was varied between 0.3 and 3.0,
676   while τ b was varied between 0.3 and 6.0. For the categorical VAE, we experimented with values of
677   τ f and τ b ranging from 0.3 to 3.0.
678
      Through the heatmap visualizations (see Figures 2, 3 and 4 in the main text), it became clear that
679
      distinct temperature configurations were crucial for performance optimization and that symmetric
680
      temperature settings (i.e., τ f = τ b ) led to suboptimal results in both reconstruction and generative
681   tasks.
682
683
684   B     T EMPERATURE S CHEDULING E XPERIMENTS
685
686   In an effort to explore whether temperature scheduling could further improve performance, we con-
687   ducted a series of experiments where forward (τ f ) and backward (τ b ) temperatures were dynami-
688   cally adjusted during training. The motivation for this approach stemmed from the observation that
689   a fixed temperature combination of τ f = 0.3 and τ b = 3 yielded the best performance in the CI-
690   FAR10 reconstruction task. We hypothesized that starting or ending with this combination, while
691   varying the temperatures over time, might lead to additional improvements in reconstruction quality.
692   Table B.1 summarizes the results of these scheduling experiments. Several scheduling strategies
693   were tested, but the results indicate that scheduling yielded no substantial improvement over the
694   fixed temperature settings. For instance, while schedules such as τ f : 1 → 0.3, τ b : 1 → 2
695   and τ f : 0.3 → 0.03, τ b : 1 → 3 showed a marginally better validation loss of 2.642 × 10−3 ,
696   this improvement was not significant when compared to the fixed combination of τ f = 0.3 and
697   τ b = 3. Other schedules generally performed worse, with some configurations like τ f : 1 → 2 and
698
      τ b : 1 → 0.3 showing considerably higher validation losses.
699   In conclusion, while temperature scheduling offered a flexible way to explore various configurations,
700   it did not provide a meaningful improvement over the fixed best-performing setting. Therefore, we
701   recommend using fixed temperatures for this particular task, as they offer simpler implementation
      without sacrificing performance.
