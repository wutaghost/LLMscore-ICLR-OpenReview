009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs), particularly in language rea-
012            soning and tool-use capabilities have sparked the rapid development of Language
013            Agents to assist humans across various real-world applications. Among these,
014            travel planning stands out as a significant domain, presenting both academic chal-
015            lenges and practical value due to its inherent complexity and real-world relevance.
016
               However, existing travel plan benchmarks do not test language agents with human
               users or their ability to follow customized requirements, both of which are vital
017
               for deploying them in real-world applications. In this paper, we propose China-
018
               Travel, a new benchmark tailored to authentic Chinese travel requirements, aiming
019            to provide a more realistic evaluation framework for future language agents. We
020            collect the travel requirements through questionnaires and employ an efficient and
021            faithful evaluation process with 46 metrics covering feasibility, constraint satis-
022            faction, and preference comparison. Moreover, we identify three challenges in the
023            real-world deployments of travel planning, including constraint recognition, con-
024            cept openness, and customized preference. The empirical studies show that even
025            state-of-the-art neural-symbolic agents succeed in 51.3% constraint validation of
026            human queries. Our findings point to the need for methods that can improve the
027
               ability of agents to understand diverse intentions or keep track of constraints with
               emerging concepts from human requirements.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   A long-standing goal in AI is to build planning agents that are reliable and general, able to as-
033   sist humans in real-world environments. Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al.,
034   2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Achiam et al., 2023) have demonstrated remarkable potential in achiev-
035   ing human-level understanding and planning capabilities. This has sparked the rapid development
036   of a field called Language Agents, employing LLMs to perceive the surroundings, reason the solu-
037   tions, and take appropriate actions, ultimately building an autonomous planning agent (Shinn et al.,
038   2024; Yao et al., 2023; Xi et al., 2023). Equipping LLMs born from web-scale corpora, language
039
      agents demonstrate a proficient ability to understand general natural language instructions and col-
      lect domain-specific information via tools (Yao et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023; Jimenez et al., 2024).
040
      It alleviates the need for intensive domain-specific goal definition and model deployment with tra-
041
      ditional rule-based or reinforcement-learning-based agents, showing few-shot generalization across
042   various domains. This presents a solid step toward the goal of building general artificial intelligence.
043
044   Travel planning stands out as a significant domain, presenting both academic challenges and prac-
045
      tical value due to its inherent complexity and real-world relevance. However, LLMs are still not
      able to accurately solve complex combinatorial optimization problems and tend to provide infeasi-
046
      ble plans in travel planning. In a recently proposed U.S. domestic benchmark TravelPlanner (Xie
047
      et al., 2024) with intercity itinerary planning, the advanced LLM, GPT-4, only achieves a success
048   rate of 0.6%. This result is disappointing and might make one pessimistic about the capabilities
049   of Language Agents in travel planning. However, a few months later, Hao et al. (2024) introduced
050   a neural-symbolic solution, which incorporates formal verification tools into language agents and
051   achieved a 97% success rate on the LLM-synthesized from TravelPlanner benchmark. This progress
052   has dual implications. On one hand, it leads to optimism regarding the potential of Neuro-symbolic
053   Language Agents. On the other hand, it prompts further inquiry into the practical applicability of
      these solutions in addressing real-world travel requirements.

077   In this work, we introduce ChinaTravel, tailored to authentic Chinese travel requirements, providing
078   a more practical evaluation framework within diverse travel requirements. ChinaTravel concentrates
079   on multi-point-of-interest (multi-POI) itineraries within specified cities (as illustrated in Figure 1),
080   which are in higher demand compared to the intercity itineraries provided by TravelPlanner. China-
081   Travel is built in a modular framework with (1) a rich sandbox environment with Chinese travel in-
082   formation, (2) diverse evaluation metrics covering feasibility, constraint satisfaction, and preference
083   comparison, and (3) realistic travel requirements contain both LLM-synthetic and human question-
084
      naire queries. We constructed ChinaTravel in five stages, including manual schema and API design,
      LLM-assisted generation of data entries, manual quality control, data collection from human users
085
      with open requirements, and preference data construction. Our evaluation pipeline automatically
086
      verifies the provided plans with the requirements annotations. An additional subset with rich travel
087   preferences is constructed to provide an evaluation for future language agents. Moreover, we identify
088   three challenges in the real-world deployments of travel planning, including constraint recognition,
089   concept openness, and customized preference. The empirical studies show that even state-of-the-art
090   neural-symbolic agents succeed in 51.3% constraint validation of the human queries. Our findings
091   point to the need for methods that can improve the ability of agents to understand diverse intentions
092   or keep track of constraints with emerging requirements from humans.

149
150   Motivated by the significant travel demand in China, this benchmark offers a sandbox environment
151   for generating multi-day, multi-POI itineraries for specified cities. It includes arrangements for
152
      attractions, restaurants, accommodations, and transportation between events, aiming to advance the
      practice of language agents solutions for real-world travel planning.
153
154   ChinaTravel comprises 46 diverse evaluation metrics, including 23 environment constraints, 10 hard
155   logical constraints, and 13 preference requirements, which are summarized in the Table 1. Through
156   manual annotation and formalized code construction, we have built an automated evaluation pipeline
157   for these requirements of given natural language queries, enabling developers to effectively evaluate
158
      the capabilities of language agents in addressing real-world challenges.
159   To evaluate capabilities in real applications, ChinaTravel provides both LLM-synthesized and hu-
160   man queries. We develop pure-LLM-based and neuro-symbolic language agents using the LLM-
161   synthesized queries as a validation set. We then test these agents on human queries, creating an
      open test environment with real-world dilemmas. The details are provided in the subsection 3.5.

201   3.1   E NVIRONMENT I NORMATION
202
203   ChinaTravel provides a sandbox with real-world travel information. We collect information from 10
204   of the most popular cities in China, including Beijing, Chengdu, Chongqing, Guangzhou, Hangzhou,
205   Nanjing, Shanghai, Shenzhen, Suzhou, and Wuhan. There are 720 airplanes and 5770 trains across
206   these cities. Each record contains departure and arrival times from origin to destination, as well as
207
      the corresponding ticket prices. We also collect information on 3413 attractions, 4655 restaurants,
      and 4124 hotels. Each record contains the name, location, opening hours, and the corresponding
208
      price per person. Moreover, there are type annotations for these POIs as information to meet user
209
      needs. Figure 2 has demonstrated the travel information from Beijing and Nanjing, two of the
210   most popular cities in China. For a more realistic interaction, we simulate the API interface of
211   real market applications to query real-time information. The detailed designs of the sandbox are
212   available in Appendix A. The environmental constraints are designed to ensure the reliability of the
213   results. That is, the POIs visited in the plan must exist in the corresponding city, the transportation
214   methods provided in the plan must be feasible, and the corresponding time information should also
215   be reliable. For example, there should indeed be a subway line that can depart from Beijing Capital
      International Airport and arrive at the Palace Museum in 80 minutes.

      3.2   L OGICAL C ONSTRAINT
217
218   A crucial ability for agents is to effectively satisfy personalized user needs. We extend the log-
219   ical constraints from TravelPlanner (Xie et al., 2024) to adapt to the multi-POI itinerary plan-
220   ning problem. These user needs are termed logical constraints, which could be defined through
221   logical expressions based on human-defined symbolic concepts. Taking the query in Figure 1
222   as an example, the user has mentioned “visit the museum”, “taste Beijing cuisine”, and “budget
223   is 5000 yuan”, the provided plan should satisfy the following logical expressions: museum ∈
224
      attractions type visited (plan), Beijing cuisine ∈ restaurants type visited (plan), and cost(plan) ≤
      5000, where these symoblic concpets, attractions type visited, restaurants type visited and cost
225
      could be extracted from the formulated plans (as illustrated in Figure 1). ChinaTravel invloves
226
      16 travel-related symoblic concepts to meet the various user needs. We provide a summary and the
227   detailed descriptions of these concepts in Table 1.

      3.3   P REFERENCE R EQUIREMENT
230
231   Travel requirements not only include hard logical constraints but also soft preferences. The “soft”
232   means these requirements cannot be defined as constraint validation on discrete symbolic concepts,
233   but rather as quantitative comparisons with the related continuous concepts. This makes the eval-
234   uation of preference requirements different from logical constraints. In ChinaTravel, we define 20
235   concepts for the 13 preferences to provide a ranking-based evaluation. Specifically, we extract rel-
236   evant concepts from plans generated by different agents, such as the number of attractions visited,
237   walking distance, total cost, etc. We then use these statistics to rank the agents, ultimately providing
238
      an automated evaluation mechanism. The detailed concept descriptions are provided in Table 1.

240   3.4   B ENCHMARK C ONSTRUCTION
241
      ChinaTravel establishes a travel environment in terms of a rich database, API code, and the users’
242
      queries with personal requirements. The overall benchmark is created in a five-stage approach with
243
      a mix of LLM generation and human survey.
244
245
      Stage I: Manual design of database schema and APIs. We started collecting travel information
246
      with the motivation of multi-day multi-POI itinerary planning in four aspects: attractions, accom-
247   modation, activities, and transportation. Developers first determine the POI description information
248   that needs to be obtained from the user’s perspective, such as cuisine and hotel features. Based on
249   this feature set, we collect public information to construct the database. For the design of APIs,
250   we directly support queries based on the regular expressions from agents, which we hope will pro-
251   mote the use of advanced tools during planning. At the same time, we expect the design of APIs
252   to have similar features and characteristics to existing commercial APIs, enabling our dataset to be
253   applicable to more realistic scenarios.
254
255   Stage II: Automatic data generation with LLMs. We designed common travel information (ori-
256   gin, destination, days, number of people) and logical constraints based on the nature of travel tasks.
257   To facilitate scalable queries for ChinaTravel, we randomly constructed query skeletons from the
258
      aforementioned information and used advanced large language models (e.g. GPT4o) to generate
      natural language queries from these skeletons. The automatically generated data is categorized into
259
      two difficulty levels: Easy and Medium. In Easy level, the logical constraints are straightforward,
260
      and the descriptions for the defined concepts in natural language queries align perfectly with these
261   constraints. At the Medium level, the natural language expressions of logical constraints are more
262   varied and human-like. For example, the logic ‘Beijing cuisine ∈ restaurants type visited(plan)’
263   might correspond to the natural language query: ‘I want to try local food in Beijing’. We employ
264   prompt engineering to guide LLMs in modifying the natural language expressions to achieve auto-
265   mated generation.
266
267   Stage III: Manual quality control and automaticed validation. To ensure data quality, we man-
268   ually check whether the generated queries conform to symbolic skeletons, and re-calibrate natural
269   language descriptions that contain ambiguities. Additionally, we calibrate the natural language con-
      cept descriptions in Medium to closely align with human questioning habits. Based on the symbolic

      skeletons, we could verify whether the plan can pass the required logical constraints by executing
271   the corresponding Python code. Building on this, we ensure that each query has at least one solution

274
      Stage IV: Open requirements from humans. After the first round of closed-loop development
275   based on LLM-generated queries, including data generation and annotation, baseline development,
276   and evaluation, we further collected travel requirements from more than 250 humans through ques-
277   tionnaires. Based on a new round of manual quality control on these open requirements, a more
278   challenging set with 154 queries is constructed. These queries even include logical constraints on
279   undefined concepts in the deployment process, such as ‘departure time’ and ‘hot spots’, reflecting
280   the real challenges of neural-symbolic systems in travel planning. We carefully annotate the re-
281   quired logical constraints for each query, enabling the automated evaluation of these challenging
282   samples and forming the Human level dataset. While we have supported the automated testing of
283
      logic constraints with undefined concepts, we hope future researchers avoid making these concepts
      transparent when utilizing the Human set, in order to maintain their openness.
284
285
286
      Stage V: Preference data construction. Through our investigation of human-annotated queries,
      we identified that certain human requirements could not be expressed as hard logic constraints,
287
      such as “minimize cost” and “maximize convenience in transportation.” We classified these as soft
288
      preferences of human needs. To better evaluate the performance of these preferences, we distilled
289   and summarized preferences found in Human and automatically constructed Preference set of
290   146 samples using the method in stage II. We provided annotations of these preferences for each
291   sample and manually cleaned the data to facilitate further research.
292

293
      the ChinaTravel Benchmark. Despite this, we recommend that researchers mainly use the Chinese
294
      version, which can reflect the needs of native speakers more accurately. As discussed above, this

      3.5   K EY C HARACTERISTICS
298
299   Arbitrary description for the defined concepts. The success of neural-symbolic solutions relies
300   on accurate translation from natural languages to human-defined concepts. We find that even for
301   advanced LLMs, it is still challenging to understand the diverse descriptions of human queries.
302   The variability in human language, including ambiguous phrasing, context-dependent meanings,
303   and open-ended expressions, makes it difficult for LLMs to map these descriptions to predefined
304   concepts. This gap often results in failures when the models attempt to reconcile flexible human
305   input with pre-defined symbolic structures, hindering their performance in tasks requiring precise
306   constraint recognition and adherence to user preferences.
307
308   Emergence of the undefined concepts. In real-world applications, language agents will encounter
309   symbolic concepts that were not predefined during development, making it challenging to satisfy the
310   related constraints. Real-world concepts are dynamic and consistently evolving, making it unrealis-
311
      tic to rely on a closed concept library to handle open-world demands. Therefore, neural-symbolic
      language agents must learn to recognize and adapt to new concepts as they emerge in an open-world
312
      environment, expanding their symbolic knowledge base to ensure scalability and robustness.
313
314
315
      Diverse preference requirements. Real requirements also involve customized preferences which
      are challenging for language agents. On the one hand, due to the diversity of human expressions,
316
      LLMs often struggle to accurately interpret these preferences. For instance, a query like ‘prefer
317
      not to be under the sun’ implies a preference for ‘more indoor attractions’ necessitating robust in-
318   tent analysis and a deep understanding of user behavior patterns. Currently, most methods rely
319   on general-purpose models, such as GPT-4, which may lack the specialized capabilities required
320   for this task. On the other hand, even if the LLM can accurately identify human preferences, the
321   symbolic search component lacks effective techniques for efficient searching. This is because inte-
322   grating preferences with logical constraints transforms the problem into a complex multi-objective
323   mixed discrete constraint optimization problem. Current SMT-based methods and heuristic search
      techniques often fail to find satisfactory solutions within a limited time frame.

338   4.1   N EURAL -S YMBOLIC S OLUTIONS
339
340
      Based on the success of the neural-symbolic solution in the TravelPlan benchmark, we adapt the two-
341   stage SMT-based solution to our benchmark, which we call NeSy Planning. Following the (Hao
342   et al., 2024), we first extract the logical constraints from the natural language. Based on the ex-
343   tracted constraints, we present a step-by-step plan generation process with depth-first search, that
344   is, mimicking human travel planning by arranging the next activity one by one. Specifically, we
345   first generate the next activity type based on the current plan, and then recursively generate the next
346   activity until the goal is reached. The generated plan is then used to solve the problem.
347
348
349   Algorithm 1 Depth-First Greedy Search
350   Require: Constraints C, current plan p,
351     if the least activity is an intercity-transport from destination to origin then
352         return ConstraintValidation(p, C), p          ▷ The plan p is finished, return the validation result.
353
        end if
        type = GetNextActivityType(p)               ▷ Select the next type of activities, e.g. lunch, attraction.
354
        candidates = ToolUse(type)               ▷ Collect the corresponding information for the activity type
355
        scores = RuleScore(candidates, p, C)                         ▷ Score candidates through constraints C.
356     for activity in candidates do
357         p.push(activity)                                  ▷ Perform a greedy search with priority ranking.
358         flag, p = Depth-FirstGreedySearch(C, p)
359         if flag then
360             return True, p                               ▷ Return the solution p if the validation is passed.
361         end if
362         p.pop(activity)
363     end for
364
        return False, p                                      ▷ Fail to find a solution with the given conditions.

366
367   For the first step, we follow the (Hao et al., 2024) to implement the translation from natural languages
368   to logical constraints through prompting. The detailed prompts are provided in the Appendix B. In
369
      the second step, we define the rule-based activity selection and score function. For example, if the
      current time is in the [10:30, 12:30] and there is no scheduled lunch in the current plan, then the
370
      agent should find a restaurant to have lunch at this time. If the current time is after 22:00 and there
371
      are no open-time attractions nearby, the agent should choose to return to the hotel. For the score
372   function, we select the restaurants that satisfy the required cuisine and sort the candidates by the
373   price if there a budget constraints in the constraints C. These ranking functions will help us to find
374   a feasible solution as soon as possible. In ChinaTravel, the duration arrangement of activities is
375   continuous and difficult to enumerate and search. We pre-define a meal or a visit to an attraction as
376   90 minutes, and when there are less than 90 minutes until closing time, the event continues until the
377   closing time. Given these designs, we adapt the neural-symbolic solution into a multi-POI planning
      problem and evaluate it in the ChinaTravel benchmark.

      4.2    M AIN R ESULTS
425
426   We provide the main results in Table 2. For Easy set, we observe that while most models exhibit
427   a high delivery rate using Act and React (Yao et al., 2023) methods, they perform poorly in con-
428   straint satisfaction. Given that the logical constraints in this set are relatively simple (e.g., mostly
429   only involving the number of people and travel days), these methods achieve a favorable logical
430   pass rate. Unlike TravelPlanner (Xie et al., 2024), our task involves multi-day multi-POI scenar-
431   ios, where satisfying environmental constraints becomes more challenging as the number of POIs
      increases. Consequently, purely LLM-based methods tend to fail in the environmental pass rate met-


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      ric, thus resulting in a low final pass rate, with many models failing entirely. We find that the need
433   to document transportation details between large number of POIs often lead to a high frequency
434   of hallucinations in LLMs. Specifically, these models frequently invent transportation information
435   rather than providing the requested result from APIs in the final plan. Our attempts to address this
436   issue through prompt engineering alone have proven insufficient. Notably, Deepseek-V2.5 (Liu
437   et al., 2024a) achieves a 7.26% pass rate in ReAct due to its strong capability in following Chi-
438   nese instructions. In this set, NeSy unsurprisingly achieved the best results, with the final pass rate
439   approaching 100%. This aligns with the observations in the SMT-based method (Hao et al., 2024),
440   which demonstrates that when LLMs successfully translate natural language into logical constraints,
441
      symbolic search can resolve many issues related to constraint satisfaction.
442   For Medium set, we observed that the performance of Act and React shows little difference com-
443   pared to the Easy set. However, the NeSy planning method has a significant performance decline.
444   This is attributed to arbitrary descriptions of defined concepts in the set, which hinder the LLM’s
445   ability to accurately translate natural language into logic constraints. This performance decrease
446   aligns with our expectations, indicating that the NeSy planning approach remains insufficient for

      5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we introduced ChinaTravel, a benchmark specifically designed to evaluate language
534   agents in the domain of travel planning, with a focus on authentic Chinese travel requirements. We
535   addressed the limitations of existing benchmarks by incorporating human users and their customized
536   requirements, which are essential for real-world applications. ChinaTravel provides a realistic eval-
537   uation framework with diverse metrics covering feasibility, constraint satisfaction, and preference
538   comparison. By addressing the challenges identified in the benchmark, we can pave the way for the
539   deployment of language agents that better meet the customized requirements of users and provide
      reliable and satisfactory travel planning experiences.
