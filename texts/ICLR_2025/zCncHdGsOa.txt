011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Optimization with orthogonality constraints frequently arise in various fields such
014            as machine learning, signal processing and computer vision. Riemannian opti-
015
               mization offers a powerful framework for solving these problems by equipping
               the constraint set with a Riemannian manifold structure and performing optimiza-
016
               tion intrinsically on the manifold. This approach typically involves computing
017
               a search direction in the tangent space and updating variables via a retraction
018            operation. However, as the size of the variables increases, the computational
019            cost of the retraction can become prohibitively high, limiting the applicability of
020            Riemannian optimization to large-scale problems. To address this challenge and
021            enhance scalability, we propose a novel approach that restricts each update on
022            a random submanifold, thereby significantly reducing the per-iteration complex-
023            ity. We introduce two sampling strategies for selecting the random submanifolds
024            and theoretically analyze the convergence of the proposed methods. We provide
025            convergence results for general nonconvex functions and functions that satisfy
026
               Riemannian Polyak–Łojasiewicz condition as well as for stochastic optimization
               settings. Additionally, we demonstrate how our approach can be generalized to
027
               quotient manifolds derived from the orthogonal manifold. Extensive experiments
028
               verify the benefits of the proposed method, showcasing its effectiveness across a
029            wide variety of problem instances.

      constraint forms a smooth manifold known as the Stiefel manifold, denoted by St(n, p) := {X ∈
055   Rn×p : X ⊤ X = Ip }. By equipping the manifold with a suitable Riemannian metric, optimization
056   can be performed intrinsically on the manifold. A crucial step in this process is the retraction
057   operation, which ensures that iterates remain on the manifold after each update. Various retractions
058   have been proposed for the Stiefel manifold, such as those based on QR factorization (Absil et al.,
059   2008), polar decomposition (Absil & Malick, 2012), the Cayley transform (Wen & Yin, 2013), and the
060   matrix exponential (Edelman et al., 1998). All these retractions require non-standard linear algebra
061   operations with a complexity of at least O(np2 ) (see Section 2 for details). As a result, the retraction
062   step becomes the primary bottleneck for Riemannian optimization solvers as n and p increase.
063   In this work, we propose a novel approach that updates the variable on a random submanifold. In
064   particular, our contributions are summarized as follows.
065
      • We propose to parameterize the update via the action of orthogonal group on the current iterate.
066
        Based on the parameterization, we update the current iterate in a random submanifold of orthogonal
067     group via Riemannian gradient descent. This reduces the complexity of non-standard linear algebra
068     operations from O(np2 ) to O(r3 ), where r is the dimension of the submanifold selected.
069
      • We introduce two strategies for the parameterization, through permutation and orthogonal trans-
070
        formation. We derive the convergence results both in expectation and in high probability. We
071
        show the trade-off between the two in terms of efficiency and convergence guarantees. We show
072     the other computations are reduced from O(np2 ) to O(nr2 ) or O(npr) under permutation and
073     orthogonal sampling respectively. Nevertheless, the orthogonal sampling incurs an extra cost of
074     O(nr2 ) for QR decomposition.
075
      • We establish convergence guarantees for a range of settings, including general nonconvex opti-
076     mization problems, nonconvex functions that satisfy the Riemannian Polyak-Łojasiewicz (PL)
077     condition, and stochastic settings under both general nonconvex and PL conditions. We show how
078     our developments can be extended to quotient manifolds derived from the orthogonal manifold,
079     including Grassmann and flag manifolds.
080
      • We validate the effectiveness of the proposed method through extensive experiments, showcasing
081     its fast convergence across a variety of problems.

      1.1   R ELATED WORKS
084
085   Recently, Shalit & Chechik (2014); Gutman & Ho-Nguyen (2023); Yuan (2023); Han et al. (2024);
086   Cheung et al. (2024) extend the idea of coordinate descent to Stiefel manifold by only updating a
087   few rows/columns while adhering to the orthogonality constraint. Despite the promise in cheap per-
088   iteration update, they either suffer from poor runtime on modern hardware, such as GPUs by requiring
089   a significant number of iterations to converge (Shalit & Chechik, 2014; Gutman & Ho-Nguyen, 2023;
090   Han et al., 2024) or involve a subproblem that may become difficult to solve in general (Yuan, 2023).
091   It is worth highlighting that Cheung et al. (2024) lift the coordinate updates to the ambient space
092   and then project back to the manifold. Their algorithms still require non-standard linear algebra
093
      operations that cost O(nr2 ), where our method scales with O(r3 ). As we elucidate the differences
      to these works in Section 4, our proposed submanifold update includes the coordinate descent as a
094
      special case, yet being more efficient in runtime empirically. Another line of research, including
095
      (Gao et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2022; Ablin & Peyré, 2022; Ablin et al., 2023) develop infeasible
096   methods for solving (1), where the updates do not necessarily satisfy the orthogonality constraint. A
097   recent work (Shustin & Avron, 2024) proposes a randomized sketching method on the generalized
098   Stiefel manifold with constraint X ⊤ BX = Ip . However, they assume B = Z ⊤ Z, with Z ∈ Rd×n
099   with d ≫ n. The aim is to reduce complexity in constructing B and improve the conditioning of
100   optimization, which is different to our setting where B = In and the aim is to reduce the complexity
101   related to retraction.
102   Apart from orthogonality constraints, Han et al. (2024) derive efficient coordinate updates for other
103   matrix manifolds, such as Grassmann and positive definite manifolds. Vary et al. (2024) extend the
104   idea of infeasible update for generalized Stiefel manifold and Darmwal & Rajawat (2023) propose
105   efficient subspace descent algorithms for positive definite manifold with affine-invariance metric.
106   Several other studies (Huang et al., 2021; Peng & Vidal, 2023) investigate (block) coordinate descent
107   on a product of manifolds, where each update targets an individual component manifold. This
      however is less relevant to our setting, where we exploit submanifolds on a single manifold.

      2    P RELIMINARIES
109

      We start by introducing basics of Riemannian optimization and geometry of Stiefel manifold. We
111   refer to (Edelman et al., 1998; Absil et al., 2008; Boumal, 2023) for more detailed exposition. Stiefel
112   manifold St(n, p) = {X ∈ Rn×p : X ⊤ X = Ip } is the set of column orthonormal matrices. When
113   n = p, St(n, p) ≡ O(n), which is called orthogonal manifold, also forming a group. The tangent
114   space of Stiefel manifold is TX St(n, p) = {U ∈ Rn×p : X ⊤ U + U ⊤ X = 0}. One can choose the
115   Euclidean metric (restricted to the tangent space) as a Riemannian metric for St(n, p), i.e., for any
116   X ∈ St(n, p), and U, V ∈ TX St(n, p), Riemannian metric ⟨U, V ⟩X = ⟨U, V ⟩ where we use ⟨·, ·⟩
117   to represent the Euclidean inner product. Other metrics such as canonical metric (Edelman et al.,
118   1998) can also be considered. Orthogonal projection of any W ∈ Rn×p to TX St(n, p) with respect
119
      to the Euclidean metric is derived as PX (W ) = W − X{X ⊤ W }S , where we denote {A}S :=
      (A + A⊤ )/2. For a smooth function F : St(n, p) → R, Riemannian gradient of F at X ∈ St(n, p),
120
      denoted as gradF (X) is a tangent vector that satisfies for any U ∈ TX St(n, p), ⟨gradF (X), U ⟩X =
121
      ⟨∇F (X), U ⟩, where ∇F (X) denotes the classic Euclidean gradient. The Riemannian gradient on
122   Stiefel manifold can be computed as gradF (X) = PX (∇F (X)) = ∇F (X) − X{X ⊤ ∇F (X)}S .
123
124   Riemannian optimization works by iteratively updating the variable on the manifold following some
125
      descent direction. Throughout the process, a retraction is required to ensure that the iterates stay on the
      manifold. Specifically, a retraction, denoted as RetrX : TX St(n, p) → St(n, p) is a map from tangent
126
      space to the manifold that satisfies RetrX (0) = X and DRetrX (0)[V ] = V for any V ∈ TX St(n, p),
127
      where D is the differential operator. There exist various retractions on Stiefel manifold, including
128   (1) QR-based retraction: RetrX (U ) = qf(X + U ), where qf extracts the Q-factor from the QR
129
      decomposition; (2) Polar retraction: RetrX (U ) = (X + U )(Ip + U ⊤ U )−1/2 ; (3) Cayley retraction:
130
      RetrX (U ) = (In − W )−1 (In + W )X where U = W X for some skew-symmetric                 W ∈ R n×n ; (4)

136   One classic Riemannian solver is the Riemannian gradient descent (Udriste, 2013) that updates      the

      In this section, we introduce two sampling strategies and respectively analyze the resulting per-
      iteration complexity of Algorithm 1. We propose to sample Pk from two distributions, (1) a uniform
197
      distribution over the set of orthogonal matrices and (2) a uniform distribution over the set of
198
      permutation matrices. The second strategy of sampling from a permutation matrix is considered due
199   to its sampling and computational efficiency compared to the orthogonal sampling.
200
201   The per-iteration complexity of Algorithm 1 is attributed to four parts, i.e., sampling, gradient
202
      computation, gradient descent update and iterate update. For both sampling strategies, the gradient
      descent update (Step 5) shares the same complexity. In particular, the gradient update involves the
203
      retraction on O(r), which is on the order of O(r3 ). Next we respectively discuss the sampling and
204
      computational cost of each sampling strategy. As we show later, the per-iteration cost of orthogonal
205   sampling strategy is O(npr) while the per-iteration cost of permutation sampling strategy is O(nr2 ).
206
207   Uniform orthogonal. We first analyze the case where Pk is uniformly sampled from O(n). We
208   consider sampling Pk from the unique translation invariant probability measure (i.e. the Haar
209   measure) on O(n). Because only r rows of Pk is required, the sampling can be performed using QR
210   decomposition (with Gram-Schmidt method) on a randomly sampled Gaussian matrix P ∈ Rr×n
211   (Meckes, 2019). Hence, the cost of sampling is O(nr2 ). Furthermore, from (4), the computation

      7.1   P ROCRUSTES PROBLEM
433
434   We first consider solving the Procrustes problem as to find an orthogonal matrix that aligns two
435   matrices, i.e., minX∈St(n,p) f (X) = ∥XA − B∥2 for some matrix A ∈ Rp×p , B ∈ Rn×p . The
436   optimal solution of this problem is X ∗ = U V ⊤ where U ΣV ⊤ = BA⊤ is the thin-SVD of the matrix
437   BA⊤ . Hence the optimal solution is computed as f (X ∗ ) = ∥A∥2F + ∥B∥2F − 2tr(Σ).
438
      Setting and results. We explore small-size as well as large-size problem by considering (1) n =
439
      p = 200 and (2) n = p = 2000. For the two settings, we set r = 150 and r = 900 for RSDM
440
      respectively. We generate A, B where each entries follows a random Gaussian distribution. For
441   this problem, we compute the closed-form solution X ∗ by taking SVD of BA⊤ and measure the
442   optimality gap in terms of optgap(X) = |f (X) − f (X ∗ )|/|f (X ∗ )|. We notice that for feasible
443   methods, like RGD, RCD and proposed RSDM, the problem can be reduced to a linear function as
444   maxX∈St(n,p) ⟨X, BA⊤ ⟩ while for infeasible methods, the problem remains quadratic. We highlight
445   that because n = p, RCD and TSD are equivalent.
446
      In Figure 2(a) under the setting n = p = 200, we see RCD performs notably worse compared to
447   other benchmarks in runtime. This is because, although requiring fewer floating point operations (as
448   shown in Han et al. (2024)), RCD requires more iterations, which is not GPU-friendly. On the other
449   hand, we see the proposed RSDM performs competitively compared to RGD. When increasing the
450   dimensionality to n = p = 2000, we notice RCD requires overly long runtime to progress and thus
451   we remove from the plots. From Figure 2(b), we verify the superiority of RSDM over RGD.

      7.2   PCA PROBLEM
454
455   Next, we consider a quadratic problem, originating from principal component analysis (PCA),
456   as to find the largest eigen-directions of a covariance matrix. This can be formulated as
457   minX∈St(n,p) F (X) = −tr(X ⊤ AX), where A ∈ Rn×n . This problem also has analytic solu-
458   tion given by the top-p eigenvectors of A.
459
      Setting and results. We create A to be a positive definite matrix with a condition number of 1000
460   and exponentially decaying eigenvalues. Due to the existence of analytic solution, we measure
461   the optimality gap the same as in Section 7.1. In Figure 2(c) and (d), we consider the setting of
462   n = 2000, p = 1500 and n = 3000, p = 2500, which represent large-scale scenarios. For the two
463   settings, we set r = 700, 1000 respectively. We see RSDM achieves the fastest convergence among all
464   the baselines. Especially around optimality, we see RSDM switches from the sublinear convergence
465   to linear in contrast to other baselines that maintains the sublinear convergence throughout. This
466   behavior may be attributed to the random projection, which potentially provides a more favorable
467   optimization landscape close to optimum (Fuji et al., 2022). In addition, this can also be justified by
468
      the fact that random submanifold descent enables a rapid search for regions where the Riemannian
      PL condition or local error bound condition (Liu et al., 2019) holds. A formal theoretical verification
469
      of this claim is left for future work.
470
471   To further validate the robustness of RSDM, we conduct additional experiments by varying the low
472   dimension r, altering the random seed and utilizing different retractions. The results, presented in
473   Figure 3, demonstrate that the performance of RSDM is largely insensitive to the choice of r (within a
474
      reasonable range) and the randomness throughout the iterations. RSDM also consistently outperforms
      RGD across all available retractions.

525      8                    C ONCLUSIONS
526
527
        In this paper, we have introduced a novel randomized submanifold approach for optimization problems
528     with orthogonality constraints in order to reduce the high complexity associated with the retraction.
529     We have derived convergence guarantees of the proposed method on a variety of function classes
530     and empirically demonstrated its superiority in a number of problem instances. We also discuss two
531     sampling strategies based on orthogonal and permutation matrices, and discuss the trade-off in terms
532     of computational efficiency versus convergence guarantees.
533
        We believe our developments represent a significant advancement in scalable Riemannian optimization
534     by offering a simple, yet effective solution for large-scale problems with orthogonality constraints.
535     In the paper, we only discuss the application of randomized submanifold strategy to Riemannian
536     gradient descent. Nonetheless, we believe such a strategy can be combined with more advanced
537     optimization techniques, such as line-search (Boumal & Cartis, 2019), momentum (Li et al., 2020;
538     Kong et al., 2023), preconditioning (Kasai et al., 2019) and higher-order methods (Huang et al.,
539     2015; Absil et al., 2007), to further enhance convergence efficiency and robustness with orthogonality
        constraints and beyond.
