009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we focus on the out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization of self-
012            supervised learning (SSL). By analyzing the mini-batch construction during the
013            SSL training phase, we first give one plausible explanation for SSL having OOD
014            generalization. Then, from the perspective of data generation and causal infer-
015            ence, we analyze and conclude that SSL learns spurious correlations during the
016            training process, which leads to a reduction in OOD generalization. To address
017            this issue, we propose a post-intervention distribution (PID) grounded in the Struc-
018            tural Causal Model. PID offers a scenario where the spurious variable and label
019
               variable is mutually independent. Besides, we demonstrate that if each mini-batch
               during SSL training satisfies PID, the resulting SSL model can achieve optimal
020
               worst-case OOD performance. This motivates us to develop a batch sampling
021
               strategy that enforces PID constraints through the learning of a latent variable
022            model. Through theoretical analysis, we demonstrate the identifiability of the la-
023            tent variable model and validate the effectiveness of the proposed sampling strat-
024            egy. Experiments conducted on various downstream OOD tasks demonstrate the
025            effectiveness of the proposed sampling strategy.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Self-supervised learning (SSL) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for training machine learning
031   models without relying on labeled data. SSL models aim to generate general-purpose representa-
032   tions and are typically used as pre-trained weights to effectively initialize downstream tasks. They
033   have demonstrated significant progress in computer vision, achieving competitive or superior perfor-
034   mance on various downstream tasks compared to supervised learning approaches (Chen et al., 2020;
035   Grill et al., 2020a; Zbontar et al., 2021; He et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2022). However, despite their
036   superior performance, SSL models face significant challenges in generalizing to out-of-distribution
037   (OOD) data. Understanding and improving the OOD generalization capabilities of SSL is crucial
038   for deploying these models in real-world scenarios where the data distribution can shift over time.
039   To investigate the OOD generalization properties of SSL, we propose examining the batch con-
040   struction process during training. SSL methods are generally categorized into two main types:
041   discrimination-based SSL (D-SSL) (Chen et al., 2020; Grill et al., 2020a) and generation-based SSL
042   (G-SSL) (He et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2022). The core principle of D-SSL is augmentation invari-
043   ance, ensuring that the feature representations of two different augmentations of the same sample
044   are similar. In contrast, G-SSL focuses on the mask and reconstruction principle, where a portion
045
      of a sample is masked and then reconstructed using an encoder-decoder structure. Leveraging these
      principles, augmented samples derived from the same original sample, as well as samples before and
046
      after masking, can be considered anchor-related pairs. During SSL training, each pair is treated as a
047
      distinct class, effectively framing each mini-batch as a multi-class learning task. Consequently, the
048   SSL training process can be perceived as learning a distribution over tasks based on discrete training
049   tasks, enabling the trained SSL model to generalize to new, unseen tasks, thus demonstrating its
050   OOD generalization capability. However, machine learning is prone to learning spurious correla-
051   tions that vary between classes and environments (Wang et al., 2023a; 2022). Therefore, although
052   SSL is highly effective in OOD generalization, from a multi-task perspective, different mini-batches
053   in the SSL training process can be considered as different tasks or environments. Consequently, it
      may still face the challenge of mitigating spurious correlations.”

054
      Building upon the analysis presented in Section 3, we examine the aforementioned challenge from
055   the perspectives of data generation and causal inference. First, we conclude that the similarity or
056   reconstruction between samples within a pair is affected by several unobservable factors, such as
057   background or texture information independent of the foreground. We also find that the spurious
058   correlation between the anchor and the unobservable variable can vary with the tasks, making it
059   difficult to eliminate it using the unified causal criterion proposed by (Pearl et al.; Pearl, 2009).
060   Furthermore, we demonstrate that, under these circumstances, the SSL model learns to measure
061   similarity or reconstruct using spurious causal factors. This reliance leads to a lack of discriminabil-
062   ity within each mini-batch task, preventing the SSL model from effectively learning the true task
063
      distribution and consequently resulting in diminished OOD generalization. To address this issue,
      we define a new distribution called the post-intervention distribution (PID), characterized by mutual
064
      independence between the unobservable variable and the anchor. We demonstrate that when the
065
      task distribution adheres to PID, the SSL model trained under this condition achieves the lowest
066   worst-case risk, thereby attaining optimal worst-case OOD performance. This insight motivates us
067   to design a new mini-batch sampling strategy that ensures the resulting mini-batches satisfy PID
068   constraints, thereby enhancing the OOD generalization capability of SSL.
069
      Based on the above analysis and discussion, we propose a novel mini-batch sampling strategy con-
070
      sisting of two stages. In the first stage, we aim to learn a latent variable model to capture the
071
      correlations between different variables, i.e., conditional distributions. We prove the identifiability
072   and uniqueness of the resulting latent variable model under a given equivalence relation. In the sec-
073   ond stage, we propose a sufficient condition to obtain the balancing score. Using this, we obtain the
074   mini-batch samples through balancing score matching. We also provide a theoretical guarantee that
075   the mini-batches obtained by the proposed sampling strategy approximately satisfy the PID.
076
      In summary, we make the following contributions: 1) Analysis of SSL Batch Construction: We pro-
077
      vide a detailed analysis of how mini-batch construction in SSL influences OOD generalization; 2)
078   Causal Framework for SSL: We introduce a causal framework to understand and mitigate the impact
079   of spurious correlations on SSL models; 3) PID-Based Sampling Strategy: We propose a theoret-
080   ically grounded mini-batch sampling strategy that ensures the generated batches conform to PID,
081   improving OOD performance; 4) Empirical Validation: We validate our approach through extensive
082   experiments, demonstrating significant improvements in OOD generalization across multiple tasks.

098   The objective of D-SSL methods typically consists of two components: alignment and regularization

106   SSL methods can be seen as gradually pulling the other sample in this pair towards the anchor.
107   The concept of anchor is also applicable to G-SSL, where x2i is viewed as the anchor, and thus the

      3     M OTIVATION AND C AUSAL A NALYSIS

117
      In this section, we first offer a plausible explanation for the OOD generalization capability of SSL
      models from a task distribution perspective. Next, based on data generation principle and causal in-
118
      ference, we demonstrate that SSL methods may measure similarity or reconstruction using spurious
119
      correlations between pairs, which reduces their OOD generalization performance. Finally, through
120   theoretical analysis, we present that even in the case of spurious associations, we can further improve
121   the OOD generalization of SSL by constraining the data distribution.

      3.1   F ORMATION OF THE P ROBLEM : C AUSAL P ERSPECTIVE
124
125   According to Section 2, different mini-batches correspond to distinct classification tasks. Therefore,
126   the training process of SSL can be described as follows: given a distribution over tasks and a data
127   distribution for each task (refer to Appendix E for more details), the SSL model is learned based
128   on various training tasks and their corresponding data. The performance of the SSL model is then
129   evaluated on test tasks that are disjoint from the training tasks. This learning paradigm involves
130
      estimating the true task distribution from discrete training tasks (refer to Appendix E for more
      details), enabling the SSL model to generalize to new, unseen tasks (i.e., test tasks). This also
131
      explains well why the SSL model exhibits good performance in transfer tasks (Chen et al., 2020;
132
      Grill et al., 2020a; Zbontar et al., 2021), i.e., it has good OOD generalization. However, machine
133   learning models are prone to learning spurious correlations during the training phase (Wang et al.,
134   2023a; 2022). For example, compared to the foreground features of input data, researchers have
135   found that machine learning models tend to rely on the superficial texture information or background
136   information of the data for decision-making (Geirhos et al., 2018; Qiang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2020).
137   Therefore, although the SSL model has been effective in OOD generalization, we find that it still
138   faces the challenge of spurious correlations.

517                Causality Analysis in SSL plays a crucial role by helping to identify and understand the underlying
518                relationships between variables. Recent works Sontakke et al. (2021); Zuo et al. (2021); Qiang et al.

529
530                In this paper, we focus on the OOD generalization of SSL models. First, we establish the connec-
531
                   tion between mini-batches formed during the SSL training phase and multi-class tasks. Next, we
                   explain the rationale for OOD generalization of SSL from a multi-task learning perspective. We
532
                   then analyze how existing SSL models, when learning mini-batch tasks, rely on spurious correla-
533
                   tions to measure sample similarity, leading to suboptimal performance. This reliance affects the SSL
534                model’s approximation of the task distribution, resulting in reduced OOD generalization. We pro-
535                vide a causal analysis of this issue and theoretically examine the intrinsic reasons for incorporating
536                spurious correlations during the learning process. Based on our causal analysis, we demonstrate that
537                when mini-batches satisfy a specific distribution, e.g., PID, SSL models achieve optimal worst-case
538                OOD performance. This insight guides us to propose a new mini-batch sampling strategy that en-
539                sures the resulting mini-batches satisfy the PID constraints. We provide a theoretical analysis of the
                   effectiveness of this method and validate its efficacy through various downstream tasks.

1106   In this section, we provide the details of the settings and datasets for each experiment.
1107   Unsupervised Learning Following the widely adopted protocol Chen et al. (2020); Wang et al.
1108   (2024b), we freeze the feature extractor and train a supervised linear classifier on top of it. The
1109   Adam optimizer is used, with Momentum set to 0.8 and weight decay set to 10−4 . The linear
1110   classifier is trained for 500 epochs, with a batch size of 128. The learning rate starts at 5 × 10−2
1111   and decays to 5 × 10−6 . For this experiment, we utilize several benchmark datasets to evaluate
1112   the model’s performance. CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 are small-scale image classification datasets
1113
       consisting of 60,000 32×32 color images in 10 and 100 classes, respectively. STL-10 is another
       small-scale dataset that contains 100,000 unlabeled images and 5,000 labeled examples from 10
1114
       classes, with a higher image resolution (96×96). Tiny ImageNet contains 100,000 64×64 images
1115
       across 200 classes and serves as a more challenging small-scale benchmark. For these datasets, we
1116   use ResNet-18 as the feature extractor. For larger datasets, we employ ImageNet-100 (a subset of
1117   ImageNet with 100 classes) and the full ImageNet dataset, which consists of over 1.2 million images
1118   in 1,000 classes, using ResNet-50 as the feature extractor.
1119
       Semi-Supervised Learning In accordance with the standard protocol Zbontar et al. (2021), we
1120
       create two balanced subsets by sampling 1% and 10% of the training dataset. Specifically, we use
1121
       the ImageNet dataset, a large-scale benchmark for visual recognition tasks, comprising 1.2 million
1122   images in 1,000 categories. The subsets contain 1% and 10% of the labeled training data, which are
1123   used for fine-tuning the model. The models are fine-tuned for 50 epochs, with learning rates set to
1124   0.05 and 1.0 for the classifier and 0.0001 and 0.01 for the backbone on the 1% and 10% subsets,
1125   respectively.
1126
       Transfer Learning We conduct three transfer learning experiments, including object detection and
1127
       instance segmentation, transfer to other domains, and video-based tasks. For object detection, we
1128   evaluate the model on two benchmark datasets: Pascal VOC and COCO. Pascal VOC is widely
1129   used for object detection tasks, containing around 20,000 images across 20 categories. We train a
1130   Faster R-CNN Ren et al. (2015) model on the combined VOC 2007 and 2012 datasets (VOC 07+12),
1131   which contains around 16,000 images, and adjust the learning rate at 18K and 22K iterations. We
1132   also conduct experiments on a smaller version of Pascal VOC, the VOC 07 set (5K images), with
1133   a reduced number of iterations. For instance segmentation, we use the COCO 2017 dataset, which
       contains over 118,000 images and covers 80 object categories. We train a Mask R-CNN He et al.


                                                             21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       (2017) with the standard 1× schedule and C4-backbone Wu et al. (2019), reporting results on the
1135   validation split.
1136
1137   Few-shot Learning The protocol outlined in Wang et al. (2024b; 2023b) is followed for few-shot
1138
       learning, where we evaluate the proposed method on three standard few-shot learning benchmarks:
       miniImageNet, Omniglot, and CIFAR-FS. miniImageNet is a widely used few-shot learning bench-
1139
       mark derived from the ImageNet dataset, consisting of 60,000 84×84 images across 100 classes.
1140
       Omniglot is a dataset designed for character recognition, containing 1,623 different characters from
1141   50 different alphabets, making it suitable for testing few-shot learning algorithms. CIFAR-FS is a
1142   few-shot version of the CIFAR-100 dataset, specifically adapted for few-shot learning tasks, con-
1143   taining 100 classes with 600 images per class. For each task, N samples without class-level overlap
1144   are randomly selected, and K-times data augmentation is applied to create an N -way K-shot task.

1256   D    R ELATED W ORKS FOR S PURIOUS C ORRELATION
1257
1258   In the recent work on SSL, there has been growing interest in understanding its vulnerability to
1259   spurious correlations Hamidieh et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2022; 2023a). These correlations arise
1260   when models learn associations from data that do not truly reflect the underlying causal structure, but
1261
       instead are coincidental or context-specific patterns Pearl (2009). This susceptibility can undermine
       the effectiveness of SSL, particularly when dealing with diverse data environments.
1262
1263   Some works have been proposed to alleviate the effects of spurious correlations in SSL. Hamidieh
1264   et al. Hamidieh et al. (2024) introduced a method that counteracts these correlations by expanding
1265   the feature space, thereby providing more diverse training views to mitigate misleading associations.
1266   Park et al. Park et al. (2024) proposed that spuriously correlated attributes make neural networks in-
1267
       ductively biased towards encoding lower effective rank representations and used rank regularization
       to eliminate biased samples. Another notable contribution comes from Chen et al.Zhu et al. (2023),
1268
       who explored the use of a data reweighting strategy to reduce the importance of data samples that
1269
       may contain spurious correlations. These methods attempt to eliminate spurious correlations by
1270   filtering or enhancing SSL samples at the sample level. Although this approach has proven effec-
1271   tive—by excluding samples that may contain spurious correlations—it is difficult to ensure that the
1272   learned features are still reliable due to the partial unobservability of spurious correlations and vari-
1273   able coupling. In contrast, our work directly addresses the impact that spurious correlations might
1274   cause, utilizing the independence between unobserved variables and anchors under post-intervention
1275   distributions to ensure the reliability of the learned representations.

1279
       Task Distribution: Task distribution refers to a set of tasks and their underlying distribution, where
1280
       each task has its own specific objectives and associated data distribution. It is often used in meta-
1281
       learning or multi-task learning scenarios to describe the diversity and variation across tasks.
1282
1283   For example, in a meta-learning scenario, the task distribution could include:
1284
1285
              • A ”cat vs dog” classification task (Task 1).
1286          • A ”car vs airplane” classification task (Task 2).
1287
              • A ”bird vs fish” classification task (Task 3).
1288
1289   These tasks form the task distribution, and the meta-learning model is trained across this task space.
1290
1291   Data Distribution: Data distribution refers to the statistical distribution of data samples within a
1292
       single task, typically described as the joint distribution P (X, Y ) of input X and labels Y .
1293   Task distribution describes the variability between tasks in a learning system, focusing on gener-
1294   alization across tasks. Data distribution focuses on the variability within a single task, addressing
1295   adaptation to specific data characteristics. The two concepts are hierarchical: task distribution gov-
       erns the diversity of tasks, while each task has its own distinct data distribution.

1385   To better address the identifiability of spurious variables in the context of SSL, we organize the
1386   response into the following steps:

1388
       Step 1: First, we need to clarify that in Section 2 and Section 3.1, we propose a new perspective for
       understanding SSL. Taking classification as an example, under this new perspective, each mini-batch
1389
       during the training phase of SSL can be treated as an independent multi-classification task. Different
1390
       mini-batches correspond to different classification tasks. In contrast, the traditional perspective of
1391   SSL considers the entire dataset as a single task for unsupervised learning.
1392
1393   Therefore, under the new perspective, the training samples in each mini-batch can be considered
1394
       labeled. Whether these labels are accurate is not our concern for now, as this falls under the domain
       of Bayesian error. Consequently, in this sense, spurious variables can be identifiable.
1395
1396   Step 2: We first explain what we mean by the distribution of tasks, using classification as an ex-
1397   ample. A learning task can be narrowly defined as assigning a label to each sample in a dataset,
1398   where the label types are finite. This dataset can represent the task, and the entirety of the dataset
1399   can be regarded as the data distribution of that task. Thus, different tasks correspond to different
1400
       datasets, with distinct label types (tasks with the same label types are considered the same). In this
       way, a task distribution is essentially the distribution over these datasets, with each element of the
1401
       task distribution corresponding to a specific dataset.
1402

       space since it is represented by a high-dimensional vector. The values of this vector can be arbitrary.
