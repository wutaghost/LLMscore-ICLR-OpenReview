009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) and Code-LLMs (CLLMs) have significantly
012            improved code generation, but, they frequently face difficulties when dealing with
013            challenging and complex problems. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) ad-
014            dresses this issue by retrieving and integrating external knowledge at the inference
015            time. However, retrieval models often fail to find most relevant context, and gener-
016            ation models, with limited context capacity, can hallucinate when given irrelevant
017            data. We present a novel framework that leverages a Programming Knowledge
018
               Graph (PKG) to semantically represent and retrieve code. This approach enables
               fine-grained code retrieval by focusing on the most relevant segments while re-
019
               ducing irrelevant context through a tree-pruning technique. PKG is coupled with
020
               a re-ranking mechanism to reduce even more hallucinations by selectively inte-
021            grating non-RAG solutions. We propose two retrieval approaches—block-wise
022            and function- wise—based on the PKG, optimizing context granularity. Eval-
023            uations on the HumanEval and MBPP benchmarks show our method improves
024            pass@1 accuracy by up to 20%, and outperforms state-of-the-art models by up to
025            34% on MBPP. Our contributions include PKG-based retrieval, tree pruning to en-
026            hance retrieval precision, a re-ranking method for robust solution selection and a
027            Fill-in-the- Middle (FIM) enhancer module for automatic code augmentation with
028            relevant comments and docstrings.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have significantly improved the performance of tasks related to
      code, such as code generation (Huang et al., 2023; Roziere et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
034
      2023). As code-related models continue to emerge rapidly (Chen et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; 2022;
035
      Roziere et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2024), most of these models rely on a natural language-to-code
036   (NL-to-Code) paradigm, which often lacks the ability to leverage existing contextual information
037   (Wang et al., 2024). Generating a solution from scratch, without access to supplementary context,
038   poses significant challenges (Wang et al., 2024), even for humans (Zhong et al., 2024). Retrieval-
039   Augmented Generation (RAG) enables retrieving and integrating relevant context from external
040   knowledge sources during the inference time (Guu et al., 2020; Lewis et al., 2020), minimizing
041   the necessity of embedding all knowledge within the model’s parameters (Asai et al., 2024).
042   RAG-based approaches can enhance accuracy across different scenarios (Izacard et al., 2022), with-
043   out the need for further training of the model (Mallen et al., 2022; Ram et al., 2023). RAG-methods
044   for code generation were previously proposed for retrieving information from library documenta-
045   tion (Zhou et al., 2022) and file repositories (Zhang et al., 2023). Wang et al. (2024) explored the
046   impact of different retrieved chunk sizes or including the entire data cells during the retrieval for
047   code generation; showing that both factors have a negative effect on the performance of code gener-
048   ation tasks by introducing irrelevant data. They identified two main challenges in retrieval for code
049   generation. First, accurately identifying and retrieving helpful documents, and second, the limited
050
      context capacity of models that can lead to hallucinations when given irrelevant data. Our work aims
      to alleviate these challenges through two main contributions.
051
052   To retrieve accurate data, we propose Programming Knowledge Graph (PKG) to represent source
053   code. Each node in PKG represents an enhanced version of a code block extracted from a function’s
      context-flow graph and refined with semantic details using a FunctionEnhancer. PKG supports en-

055   pruning to remove irrelevant branches, ensuring that only the most useful information is passed to
056   the generative model through two code retrieval approaches: block-wise considering path similarity

100   search results by focusing on meaningful and contextually relevant code blocks; and 4 Enhancer
101   Module using Fill-in-the-Middle (FIM) Objective that enhances functions by automatically inserting
102   relevant docstrings and comments at appropriate locations within the code.
103   Our findings demonstrate that the proposed PKG approach along with re-ranker effectively address
104   complex problems while maintaining minimal negative impact on solutions that are already correct
105   without RAG.

255   2.3   S OLUTION R E - RANKING
256
      In our results, we demonstrate that even with access to a powerful PKG or other retrieval sources,
257
      the model can still hallucinate when provided with additional information in certain scenarios. This
258   highlights the necessity of incorporating a re-ranking mechanism to effectively select the best solu-
259   tion from multiple approaches. A visual representation of this motivation is provided in Figure 1,
260   which compares the performance of different approaches on the HumanEval benchmark for both
261   CodeLlama-7B and DeepSeek-Coder-7B models. The figure shows that when both BM25 and PKG
262   are applied, 10 problems are solved incorrectly, whereas these same problems are solved correctly
263   without the additional context.
264   To address this issue, we implemented a simple yet effective re-ranking approach consisting of three
265   key steps. First, the solution candidates are passed through AST analysis to filter out those with syn-
266   tactical errors. In the second step, we execute the remaining candidates to eliminate any solutions
267   containing runtime issues, such as undefined variables. Finally, we perform a semantic similarity
268   check by comparing the embeddings of the remaining candidates with the query embedding, return-
269   ing the solution with the highest similarity score. This multi-step process ensures the selection of a
      robust and valid solution, significantly improving the reliability of the model’s output.

      3.1       P ROGRAM G ENERATION U SING LLM S
273
274   The generation of code using LLMs and CLLMs has been widely studied, as highlighted in recent
275   works (Dubey et al., 2024; Lozhkov et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Roziere et al., 2023a). These
276   studies primarily assess performance using the pass@k metric (Chen et al., 2021), which measures
277   the success rate of generating correct code within a set number of attempts. Models are trained
278   with various objective functions, including code infilling (Roziere et al., 2023a), handling long input
279   contexts (Roziere et al., 2023a), fill-in-the-middle techniques (Li et al., 2023), and instruction fine-
280
      tuning (Li et al., 2023; Roziere et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2024). While knowledge is embedded
      within the model’s parameters during training, our approach stores code-specific domain knowledge
281
      separately in a graph structure and retrieves it during code generation when relevant prompts are
282
      encountered.
283
284
      3.2       R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTED G ENERATION
285
286   RAG approaches have been extensively explored in the domain of general text generation (Guu et al.,
287   2020; Lewis et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023). These approaches can be categorized
288   into three types (Gao et al., 2023): (1) Naive RAG, which uses a simple dataset and retriever to fetch
289   content similar to the input prompt; (2) Advanced RAG, which incorporates additional steps such as
290   query rewriting before retrieval and solution re-ranking after retrieval to refine the results; and (3)
291   Modular RAG, which combines multiple RAG strategies and selects the most relevant documents
292   from different retrieval methods. Our framework fits into the Modular RAG category, as it utilizes
293
      multiple retrieval cores composed of both naive and advanced RAG components.
294
295   3.3       RAG FOR C ODE G ENERATION
296
      The use of RAG in code-related tasks remains underexplored (Wang et al., 2024). Previous studies,
297
      such as Parvez et al. (2021), have experimented with smaller code language models like Code-
298
      BERT (Feng et al., 2020) and GraphCodeBERT (Guo et al., 2020), focusing on tasks like code
299   summarization and generation. Unlike their work, which involved fine-tuning the retriever mod-
300   ule to extract relevant data, our approach applies RAG during inference time without requiring any
301   model fine-tuning. While (Wang et al., 2024) presents a more similar approach to ours by compar-
302   ing the performance of LLMs and CLLMs across various data sources and retrieval methods, they
303   highlight challenges with retrievers extracting similar content and models’ limited capacity for ad-
304   ditional context. Our work differs by representing knowledge in a granular way, allowing retrievers
305   to more accurately extract relevant information and prompting models with only useful content to
306   reduce hallucinations.

      5   R ESULTS
339
340
341   In this section we carry out experiments to answer the following research questions. The questions
342   and their results are explained in the following.
343   RQ1: Does PKG improve code generation?
344
345
      In this research question, we aim to explore the potential of leveraging graph-based retrieval-
      augmented methods to improve code generation task. Specifically, we will investigate how the
346
      relevant context retrieved from PKG can enhance the performance of LLMs and CLLMs in gener-
347
      ating accurate code.
348
349   The proposed approach retrieves relevant information related to the programming problems from
350   the PKG and integrates it into the code generation process. We evaluated our method against sev-
351
      eral baselines, which are detailed in Table 1 and Table 2 for HumanEval and MBPP benchmarks,
      respectively. The tables outline different retrieval and augmentation settings: 1) None: No retrieval-
352
      augmented generation is applied. 2) BM25: This baseline applies the BM25 algorithm to the entire
353
      dataset without any pre-processing. 3) VoyageEmb: In this setting, embeddings for each question-
354   answer pair in the dataset are extracted and used for retrieval. 4) Func-BM25: This involves ap-
355   plying BM25 on functions extracted by the FunctionAnalyzer module we developed, ignoring all
356   parts of data except python functions. 5) Func-PKG: Semantic search is performed over function-
357   related nodes in PKG. These nodes are enhanced by the FunctionEnhancer module, which enriches
358   their contextual information. 6) Block-PKG: A more granular retrieval is conducted by performing
359   semantic search over specific code blocks in PKG, providing a deeper context for code genera-
360   tion. 7) Reranked: A re-ranking method selects the best candidate output from the retrieval settings
361   (None, Func-BM25, Func-PKG, Block-PKG). 8) Ideal Re-ranker: This setting demonstrates an up-
362
      per bound for the re-ranker model, simulating ideal conditions. It assumes a perfect re-ranker that
      always selects the correct candidate, showing the maximum possible accuracy.
363
364   As demonstrated in Table 1 and 2, our approach outperforms NoRAG and other RAG approaches
365   across most CLLMs, under identical environmental conditions. This ensures that all methods have
366   equal access to the same data source, providing a fair comparison. However, Deepseek-Coder ben-
367   efits less from others in HumanEval. This aligns with observations from a related study by (Wang
368
      et al., 2024), where it exhibited similar behavior. Based on these findings, we hypothesize that
      DeepSeek-Coder may not be effectively utilizing additional contextual information during training.
369
370   Figure 1 illustrates the motivation for the necessity of a re-ranking algorithm. While applying RAG
371   can lead to solving additional problems, it also introduces a downside: providing external context
372   can degrade some of the previously correct solutions.
373   Our re-ranking algorithm addresses this issue by selecting the best candidate solution from the differ-
374   ent approaches, thereby optimizing the overall performance. The impact of this re-ranking process is
375   reflected in the “Reranked“ column in Tables 1 and 2, which shows that when PKG coupled with our
376   re-ranker, consistently outperforms both benchmarks across all baseline CLLMs and LLM models.
377   In conclusion, our approach significantly improves the Pass@1 accuracy for both HumanEval and
      MBPP benchmarks.

529
530   We introduced PKG for code generation task and evaluated our approach using standard Python
531
      benchmarks, HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021) and MBPP (Austin et al., 2021). PKG enables us
      to retrieve code at a fine-grained level, focusing on highly relevant segments. Meanwhile, our re-
532
      ranker is designed to ignore suboptimal solutions, ensuring that only high-quality code is selected.
533
      The key findings from our experiments are: 1) PKG-based approaches significantly outperform other
534   RAG and non-RAG approaches for code generation tasks. 2) Both LLMs and CLLMs are highly
535   vulnerable to irrelevant data, which can negatively affect performance. 3) The inclusion of a code re-
536   ranker is essential for optimizing RAG-based approaches for code generation. 4) Different types of
537   problems benefit differently from RAG-based approaches, indicating that problem-topic specificity
538   is an important factor. As future work, more advanced techniques are needed during instruction-
539   tuning to enable models to learn more effectively from additional context. Additionally, the lack of
      code re-ranker models remains a notable gap in the current literature.

      7.1   C HALLENGES IN R ETRIEVING I NFORMATION FROM PKG
657
      This section discusses scenarios where the PKG fails to retrieve accurate or relevant information.
658   One notable challenge arises when the task requires domain-specific expertise. For example, if
659   the task involves a specialized framework or project-specific code, the PKG must be populated
660   with relevant data from the corresponding domain or project. Failures occur when queries target a
661   graph that lacks such domain knowledge. Addressing this issue necessitates updating the graph with
662   appropriate domain-specific information.
663
      Through topic analysis, we identified that the PKG often struggles with certain problem categories,
664   such as string manipulation. Experimental observations indicate that this challenge stems from the
665   limitations of both the embedder model and the baseline model, which tend to prioritize semantic
666   meaning over structural characteristics of strings.
667
      Example Problem: Write a Python function to convert lowercase characters to uppercase and vice
668
      versa, transforming inputs such as ”Hello” into ”hELLO” and ”pYthon” into ”PyTHON”.
669
670   Challenges:
671
672         • Embedding Model’s Semantic Bias:
673           In RAG, the embedder retrieves content primarily based on semantic meaning rather than
674           formatting or structural patterns. For example, it might interpret ”Hello” as a greeting,
675
              ignoring the case transformation requirement.
676         • LLM’s Tokenization and Semantic Prioritization:
677           LLMs tokenize text based on meaning rather than formatting. Consequently, tokens like
678           ”Hello” and ”hello” are often treated identically, making tasks involving case transforma-
679           tions particularly challenging.
680
681   In summary, both RAG retrieval and LLM tokenization emphasize semantic understanding over
682
      structural or formatting details, complicating the handling of tasks like string manipulation. This
      limitation reduces the effectiveness of PKG-based approaches for such problem categories.

690

698
      object. This process resulted in a set of nodes and relations that formed the basis of our PKG, then
      we could continue with our proposed method to extract the embeddings and retrieve information
699
      from the PKG.
