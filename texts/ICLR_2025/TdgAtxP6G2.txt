009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive in-context learning
012            (ICL) capability. However, it is still unclear how the underlying transformers
013            accomplish it, especially in more complex scenarios. Toward this goal, several
014            recent works studied how transformers learn fixed-order Markov chains (FOMC)
015            in context, yet natural languages are more suitably modeled by variable-order
016
               Markov chains (VOMC), i.e., context trees (CTs). In this work, we study the
               ICL of VOMC by viewing language modeling as a form of data compression and
017
               focusing on small alphabets and low-order VOMCs. This perspective allows us to
018
               leverage mature compression algorithms, such as context-tree weighting (CTW)
019            and prediction by partial matching (PPM) algorithms as baselines, the former of
020            which is Bayesian optimal for a class of priors that we refer to as the CTW priors.
021            We empirically observe a few phenomena: 1) Transformers can indeed learn to
022            compress VOMC in-context, while PPM suffers significantly; 2) The performance
023            of transformers is not very sensitive to the number of layers, and even a two-
024            layer transformer can learn in-context quite well; and 3) Transformers trained and
025            tested on non-CTW priors can significantly outperform the CTW algorithm. To
026            explain these phenomena, we analyze the attention map of the transformers and
027
               extract two mechanisms, on which we provide two transformer constructions: 1) A
               construction with D + 2 layers that can mimic the CTW algorithm accurately for
028
               CTs of maximum order D, 2) A 2-layer transformer that utilizes the feed-forward
029
               network for probability blending. These constructions can explain most of the
030            phenomena mentioned above. One distinction from the FOMC setting is that
031            a counting mechanism appears to play an important role. We implement these
032            synthetic transformer layers and show that such hybrid transformers can match
033            the ICL performance of transformers, and more interestingly, some of them can
034            perform even better despite the much-reduced parameter sets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Large language models (LLMs) are capable of completing various tasks (Kasneci et al., 2023; Wu
      et al., 2023; Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022). The transformer model (Vaswani et al.,
040
      2017), the key behind current prevailing LLMs, is known to have strong in-context learning (ICL)
041
      capabilities, and concrete ICL results for transformers have been established for some simple tasks
042   (Garg et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024; Ahn et al., 2024). Despite these results,
043   the mechanism for transformers to learn in context is still not fully understood, especially when the
044   scenario is complex or the sequences have memories. Toward this goal, several recent works studied
045   how transformers can learn fixed-order Markov chains (FOMCs) either in training or in-context
046   (Makkuva et al., 2024; Edelman et al., 2024), where insightful observations and theoretical results
047   were obtained. The FOMC is however a poor match for natural languages, for which variable-order
048   Markov chains (VOMCs), also known as context tree (CT) models (Rissanen, 1983; Willems et al.,
049   1995), are often viewed as a more suitable model (Begleiter et al., 2004).
050   In another related line of research on LLMs, several recent works made explicit the close connection
051   between language models and data compression (Valmeekam et al., 2023; Delétang et al., 2023),
052   the latter of which was aptly titled “Language Modeling Is Compression”. Indeed, despite their
053   difference in the eventual applications, the key driver behind both is an accurate (auto-regressive)
      probability distribution estimator. We adopt this perspective and use the compression rates in a fixed

054
      context window as our main evaluation metric. This allows us to use several well-known compression
055   algorithms, such as prediction by partial matching (PPM) (Cleary & Witten, 1984) and context
056   weighting algorithms (CTW) (Willems et al., 1995), as baselines. In particular, the CTW algorithm is
057   Bayesian optimal under certain priors, which gives us a fundamental lower bound in such settings.
058
      In this work, we consider the ICL of VOMCs from the data compression perspective and refer to this
059
      task as ICL-VOMC. The VOMC sources have finite memory, however, each new symbol may depend
060
      on different numbers of previous symbols, i.e., the length of the memory may vary. We emphasize
061   that ICL-VOMC is considerably more complex than ICL of FOMCs. A naive strategy would be to
062   view a VOMC as an FOMC of the largest possible order, which however leads to highly inefficient
063   ICL, since the in-context samples are not utilized well. The PPM algorithms can be viewed as an
064   approximate surrogate with this naive approach in the small alphabet setting we consider.
065
      We first train a set of shallow transformers of various numbers of layers for VOMCs of various
066
      maximum orders, and empirically observe a few phenomena: 1) Transformers can indeed learn to
067   compress VOMC in context, while PPM suffers significantly; 2) The performance of transformers is
068   not very sensitive to the number of layers, and even a two-layer transformer can learn in-context quite
069   well, tracking the CTW performance closely; and 3) Transformers trained and tested on non-CTW-
070   priors can significantly outperform the CTW algorithm. To explain these phenomena, we carefully
071   analyze the attention maps of the transformers, and discover two attention mechanisms, which form
072   suffixes and perform suffix matching, respectively. Equipped with these mechanisms, we answer the
073   question of whether transformers have the modeling capability to mimic the CTW algorithm. One
074   difficulty is that the CTW algorithm has a recursive structure, which is not directly compatible with
075
      the transformer architecture. We first propose an alternative algorithm representation, based on which
      a transformer construction with D + 2 layers is proposed, that can mimic CTW accurately for CTs of
076
      maximum order D. This establishes a fundamental capability of transformers for ICL-VOMC. The
077
      alternative representation enjoys an intuitive interpretation as blending probability estimates along a
078   path on the context tree.
079
080   The CTW algorithm relies heavily on counting the occurrences of suffixes of varying lengths to
081
      determine the blending coefficients, and a significant component of our transformer construction is
      for such counting in the second layer. Given its importance, we postulate that 2-layer transformers
082
      perform well because such information already allows close-to-optimal blending. We propose such a
083
      simple 2-layer transformer, by providing one feed-forward (FF) layer with the probability estimates
084   and the corresponding counts directly. The FF layer’s role then largely reduces to approximating
085   the proper blending coefficients. We implement several synthetic transformer layers and show that
086   the hybrid transformers can mostly match the ICL performance of transformers. This construction
087   provides an explanation for 2-layer transformers to perform well in ICL-VOMC, and also for the
088   superior performance on non-CTW-priors. More interestingly, some of these synthetic transformers
089   can perform even better despite the much-reduced parameter sets.
090   Among existing works, the mostly closely related is Edelman et al. (2024), which studied two-layer
091   transformers and investigated the training behavior from which the transformer obtains its ICL
092   capability of FOMC, leading to conclusive results on a simple binary Markov model. As mentioned
093   before, we focus on variable-order Markov chains (VOMC) and on the ICL behavior in the context
094   window, since we believe it is not only important to be able to learn in context eventually, it is equally
095   important to learn in context quickly. Appendix A gives a more detailed discussion of related works.
096   Main Contributions: We believe that ours is the first study of ICL for VOMC, and the contributions
097   are summarized as follows. (i) We demonstrate that transformers can indeed (numerically) learn to
098   compress VOMC in-context, close to optimal CTW algorithm for appropriate CTW-prior. (ii) We
099   show that transformers can outperform CTW when trained and tested on non-CTW-priors. (iii) We
100   give an explicit D + 2-layer transformer construction to imitate CTW establishing its capabilities,
101   based on a novel Bayesian optimal next token prediction representation, which can be of independent
102   interest. (iv) Our construction allows us to investigate the relative insensitivity to the number of
103   layers, of the transformer performance, i.e., even 2-layer transformers perform well. It also gives
104
      partial explanations of the ICL capabilities of transformers on VOMC.

346   To understand why and how the trained transformers perform comparable to CTW, we first analyze
347   their attention maps, which reveal interesting patterns and behaviors. We then propose a transformer-
348   friendly representation of the Bayesian optimal next token prediction by CTW, and motivated by the
349   attention map observations, we provide transformer constructions to interpret its ICL capabilities
350   and capacities. We next implement these synthetic transformer layers, and show that the hybrid
351   transformers can match the ICL performance of the original version of transformers, which serves as

      evidence for the proposed constructions.

354   4.1      A NALYSIS OF ATTENTION M APS
355
      We analyzed the attention maps of the trained transformers where a few distinguished patterns emerge.
356
      One pattern is solely relative-position dependent. In the left two panels of Fig. 7, we observe
357   off-diagonal stripes for these two attention heads, which are a few positions below the main diagonal.
358   They can be a single off-diagonal or a collection of several off-diagonals. This indicates that the
359   query position is attending positions at a few fixed but close distances ahead of itself. This pattern
360   usually appears in the first or second layers of the transformers. Combining with the suffix structure
361   in compression algorithms such as CTW, such an attention pattern suggests the suffix is being copied
362   into the current query position for subsequent processing. The off-diagonal stripes may have a width
363   greater than 1, as shown in the second panel, which can be viewed as copying a mixture of the tokens
364   in the suffix, suggesting the flexibility of transformers in forming certain “soft" suffixes.
365   Another pattern, shown in the third panel has more sophisticated spotty patterns, and the attention
366   appears to depend more explicitly on the current token features instead of the position alone, and
367   they usually appear in the second layer or above in the transformers. Taking query positions 350 and
368   362 for the attention head shown in the third panel of Fig. 6, we plot in Fig. 7 the positions in the
369   data sequence that match their suffixes of length-3 using the stem plots with a black circle on top,
370   and the attention values as the red stems with the diamonds on top. It can be seen that the positions
371
      match perfectly, though the attention weights have some variations among them. The left panel has
      more matching locations, due to the inherent Markov chain structure. This attention pattern suggests
372
      that it is collecting information for those positions with the matched suffix of a fixed length. Several
373
      attention heads present similar patterns but with different suffix lengths.

524    5         C ONCLUSION
525
       We considered the in-context learning of transformers for VOMC sources. By drawing a close analogy
526
       of ICL and Bayesian universal compression, we leverage the CTW and PPM as baselines. Experimen-
527    tally, we observe the performances of the trained transformers greatly surpass the performance PPM
528    and are close to that of CTW even with just two layers under CTW priors; moreover, transformers
529    are superior to CTW under non-CTW prior. To understand the mechanism of transformers’ ICL
530    ability, we analyzed the attention maps and extracted two likely mechanisms. We then constructed
531    the finite-memory context extension layer, and the statistics collection layer, corresponding to these
532    two mechanisms, respectively. The latter collects both the forward and backward statistics, which
533    are vital as theoretically demonstrated by a novel representation of the CTW optimal next-token
534    prediction. We also provide empirical evidence that the statistics collected by the constructed second
535
       layer, in particular the counting statistics, are indeed necessary.
536    Although we empirically showed transformers can perform ICL-VOMC tasks, and constructed an
537    idealized transformer to mimic the CTW algorithm, it is not clear whether a trained transformer will
538    indeed utilize the upper layer mechanisms. Extending the existing approach (Edelman et al., 2024) to
539    answer this question appears quite difficult, given the complexity of the constructed transformer and
       the underlying VOMCs; this is part of ongoing investigations.

      A     R ELATED W ORK

705
706   There have been many efforts in studying the ICL capabilities of transformers. A significant recent
707
      development is the elucidation of the connection to gradient descent, particularly for linear regression
      tasks (Von Oswald et al., 2023; Akyürek et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2024). Li et al.
708
      (2023) formulated the ICL problem as a multi-task learning problem and considered ICL for several
709
      simple problem settings for which the authors provide risk bounds for ICL of supervised learning
710   algorithms in these problem settings. Kirsch et al. (2022) viewed the ICL problem as a meta-learner
711   and studied the relation between tasks and model sizes. These line of approaches focused on the ICL
712   of supervised learning tasks, such as classification and regression, while this work belongs to another
713   directions of studying ICL for the next token prediction of some unknown underlying dynamics.
714
      Olsson et al. (2022) studied the induction head, i.e., the forming of small k-gram attention in LLMs.
715
      Reddy (2023) studied the balance between ICL and in-weights learning, and observed the abrupt
716   emergence of the induction head corresponds to the emergence of ICL. The induction head was
717   generalized to the statistical induction head in (Edelman et al., 2024) mainly to study bigrams. We
718   adopted it but further allowed more statistical induction heads for more suffixes to be included
719   together, in the first two layers of the idealized transformer.
720
      There have also been efforts to study transformers and learning of Markov chains. Xie et al. (2021)
721
      viewed ICL as a Bayesian inference problem, where a latent concept determines an HHM, and the
722   observations from the HHM can lead to the identification of the hidden concept. They studied the
723   eventual ICL capability, i.e., when the number of in-context examples goes to infinity. Hu et al.
724   (2024) studied the limitations of transformer on learning to perform belief inference for HMMs
725   sources compared to recurrent neural networks. The work in (Bietti et al., 2024) allowed a fixed-order
726   Markov chain to switch to a new deterministic mode, and the authors study the training behavior of
727   the corresponding ICL task with this mode transition. Akyürek et al. (2024) made a comprehensive
728   empirical comparison of various language models on random finite automata, and showed that the
729   transformer performs the best among these models. Makkuva et al. (2024) studied the loss landscape
730
      during transformer training on sequences generated from a single fixed-order Markov chain, using a
      single-layer transformer. Their study does not consider ICL. More recently Rajaraman et al. (2024)
731
      considered ICL of FOMCs with single-head transformers, and provided a construction to show that it
732
      is possible to use a single attention head to capture longer memory in the sequence. The work most
733   relevant to us is (Edelman et al., 2024), where ICL of a fixed-order Markov chain was considered,
734   and the training behavior was studied both empirically and theoretically, and the forming of induction
735   heads in a two-layer network was demonstrated. All these existing work assumed fixed-order Markov
736   models or fixed-order HHMs, usually with orders kept at 1 or 2; moreover, they almost all focus on
737   the emergence of the induction heads during training or the training landscape. Our study is different
738   firstly in the variable-order nature of the Markov models, and secondly the focus on the on-time ICL
739   performance instead of the training landscape and behavior.
740   Lossless data compression has a long history, with many different algorithms being developed over
741   the years. The most popular general-purpose compression algorithms are perhaps the Lempel-Ziv
742   compression algorithms (Ziv & Lempel, 1977; 1978) and their variants, which belong to dictionary-
743   based compression algorithms. These algorithms do not explicitly maintain any probabilistic models,
744   and their efficiency comes from maintaining an efficiency dictionary of sequences that have been seen
745   before, and to be matched with future sequences. More powerful compression algorithms usually
746   maintain probability models explicitly, which are then plugged into an AC module (Rissannen, 1976;
747
      Pasco, 1976; Rissanen & Langdon, 1979) for efficient compression. The most well-known classes of
      algorithms in this category is the context-tree weighting algorithm (Willems et al., 1995; Begleiter
748
      et al., 2004; Kontoyiannis, 2023) and prediction by partial matching (Cleary & Witten, 1984). The
749
      former enjoys a strong theoretical guarantee, particularly on binary sources (Willems et al., 1995), but
750   has some difficulty in its practical implementation (Willems, 1998; Willems et al., 1996; Sadakane
751   et al., 2000; Begleiter et al., 2004), particularly for large alphabet sizes and sequential data. The latter
752   is based more on heuristics, and has been improved and extended in various ways (Cleary & Teahan,
753   1997; Moffat, 1990; Shkarin, 2002). Methods based on probabilistic modeling are usually more
754   resource-extensive, though they have gained more popularity recently due to the increased availability
755   of computing resources. The evaluation given in (Begleiter et al., 2004) suggests that CTW and
      PPM are the two most powerful compression algorithms in practice. There are other compression

      algorithms such as those based on the Burrows-Wheeler transformation (Burrows, 1994) which does
757   not explicitly maintain a probabilistic model, but are also not dictionary-based.
