011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Optimal stopping of stochastic processes is a fundamental problem in optimization
014            that has found applications in risk management, finance, economics, and recently
015            in the fields of computer science. We extend the standard framework to a multi-
016
               agent setting, named multi-agent optimal stopping (MAOS), where a group of
               agents cooperatively solves finite-space, discrete-time optimal stopping problems.
017
               Solving the finite-agent case is computationally prohibitive when the number of
018
               agents is very large, so this work studies the mean field optimal stopping (MFOS)
019            problem, obtained as the number of agents approaches infinity. We prove that
020            MFOS provides a good approximate solution to MAOS. We also prove a dynamic
021            programming principle (DPP), based on the theory of mean field control. We
022            then propose two deep learning methods: one simulates full trajectories to learn
023            optimal decisions, whereas the other leverages DPP to compute the value function
024            and to learn the optimal stopping rule with backward induction; both methods
025            train neural networks for the optimal stopping decisions. We demonstrate the
026            effectiveness of these approaches through numerical experiments on 6 different
027
               problems in spatial dimension up to 300. To the best of our knowledge, this is the
               first work to study MFOS in finite space and discrete time, and to propose efficient
028
               and scalable computational methods for this type of problems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Optimal stopping (OS) has emerged as a powerful approach to tackle real-world problems character-
034
      ized by uncertainty and sequential decision-making, in which the goal is to find the best time to stop
035
      a stochastic process (see Shiryaev (2007), Ekren et al. (2014)). Famous real-world OS problems are
036   the job search (also called house selling or secretary problem) (Lippman and McCall, 1976) and in
037   machine learning, the question of when to stop training a neural network can also be viewed as an
038   instance of OS problems (Wang et al., 1993; Dai et al., 2019).
039
      The OS framework has been extended to cover multi-agent scenarios, in which the aim is to stop
040
      several (possibly interacting) dynamical systems at different times in order to minimize a common
041
      cost function. We will refer to this setting at multi-agent optimal stopping (MAOS). MAOS has
042   gained significant importance in a variety of fields. In robotics, for example, this theory has found
043   applications in mission monitoring task, where multiple robots must observe the progress of other
044   robots performing a specific task (Best et al., 2018). In finance the problem of pricing options with
045   multiple stopping times (see Kobylanski et al. (2011)) can be viewed as an MAOS problem and was
046   a motivation for Talbi et al. (2023; 2024).
047
      However, the problem’s complexity increases drastically with the number of agents. To tackle this
048   issue, mean-field approximations can be used. In the case of multi-agent control, this leads to the
049   theory of Mean Field Control (MFC), which aims to approximate very large systems of interacting
050   agents who are cooperatively minimizing a social cost by choosing optimal controls; see (Bensous-
051   san et al., 2013; Carmona and Delarue, 2018). Applications include crowd motion (Achdou and
052   Lasry, 2019), flocking (Fornasier and Solombrino, 2014), finance (Carmona and Laurière, 2023),
053   opinion dynamics (Liang and Wang, 2019), and artificial collective behavior (Gu et al., 2021; Cui
      et al., 2024), among others. In contrast with optimal control, mean field approximations have not

054
      been used for OS problems, except for (Talbi et al., 2024; 2023) in the continuous time and contin-
055   uous space setting, and computational methods have not yet been developed.
056
057   Our work makes a first step in this direction: focusing on discrete time, finite space MFOS models,
058
      we provide a theoretical foundation and then introduce two deep learning methods, which can solve
      MFOS with many states by learning optimal stopping decisions that are functions of the whole
059
      population distribution. We call these methods direct approach (DA) and dynamic programming
060
      approach (DP), and test them on several environments.

062       Main Contributions       Our main contributions are twofold:
063
          Theoretically: (1) we prove that MFOS in discrete space and time√yields an approximate
064
          optimal stopping decision for N -agent MAOS with a rate of O(1/ N ) (Thm 3.2); (2) we
065
          prove a DPP for MFOS by interpreting the model as a special kind of MFC problem (Thm 4.1).
066
067       Computationally: (1) we propose two deep learning methods to solve MFOS problems, by
068       learning the optimal stopping decision as a function of the whole population distribution (Alg. 1
069       and 2); (2) we illustrate the performance of both algorithms on six environments of increasing
070
          complexity, with distributions’ dimension and time horizon up to 300 and 50 respectively.
071
072   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to study discrete-time, finite-space MFOS prob-
073   lems. Our theoretical results relay on the interpretation of MFOS problems as MFC problems, which
074   provides a new perspective and opens up new direction to study MFOS problems. Additionally, it is
075   the first time that computational methods are proposed to solve MFOS. This is a first step towards
076
      solving complex multi-agent optimal stopping problems with very large number of agents.
077
078
      Related works MFOS has been recently studied in continuous time and space from a purely the-
      oretical view by Talbi et al. (2023; 2024) who studied the connection with finite-agent MAOS prob-
079
      lems and characterized the solution of (continuous) MFOS using a PDE on the infinite-dimensional
080
      space of probability measures, which is intractable. Instead, we focus on discrete time scenarios
081   with finite state space (i.e., an individual agent’s state can take only finitely many different values),
082   and hence the distribution is finite dimensional. This setting can be viewed as an approximation of
083   the continuous setting. Deep learning methods have been proposed for discrete time single-agent
084   OS problems. Becker et al. (2019) proposed to learn the stopping decision at each time using a deep
085   neural network. Herrera et al. (2023) extended the approach using randomized neural networks.
086   Damera Venkata and Bhattacharyya (2024) proposed to use recurrent neural networks to solve non-
087   Markovian OS problems. Other approaches have been proposed, particularly for continuous time
088   OS problems, such as learning the stopping boundary (Reppen et al., 2022). These single-agent
089
      OS approaches cannot be easily adapted to solve MAOS problems: The solution which consists
      in treating the whole system as one agent would lead to stopping all the agents at the same time,
090
      and single-agent methods do not capture the interdependence between agents. Furthermore, these
091
      approaches are not suitable to tackle continuous space MFOS problems as introduced by Talbi et al.
092   (2023) because the value function must be a function of the population distribution, which leads to
093   an infinite dimensional problem. For this reason, there are no existing computational methods for
094   MFOS. In this work, we focus on a finite space setting and propose the first computational methods
095   for MFOS problems by leveraging the aforementioned deep learning literature to tackle the (finite
096   but) high dimensionality of the population distribution. Another difference with (Talbi et al., 2023;
097   2024) is that these work purely rely on an OS viewpoint, while we unveil a connection with MFC
098   problems. This is a conceptual contribution of our work. Recently, MFC problems in discrete time
099   and finite space have been studied using reinforcement learning methods (Gu et al., 2023; Motte and
100
      Pham, 2022; Carmona et al., 2023). However, here we focus on situations in which the model is
      known and we develop deep learning algorithms. This allows us to solve problems in much higher
101
      dimension (up to 300 for the neural network’s input) than these works.

104   2     M ODEL
105
106   When the number of agents tends to infinity an aggregation effect takes place, enabling us to rep-
107   resent the influence of the community using an “average” term, commonly referred to as the mean
      field term. As the number of agents approaches infinity, they become independent and identically

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      distributed (i.i.d.), and the behavior of each individual agent is determined by a stochastic differen-
109   tial equation (SDE) of McKean-Vlasov type. This phenomenon is often known as the “propagation
110   of chaos” (Sznitman, 1991). The objective is to discern the properties of the solutions to the limiting
111   problem. By integrating these properties into the formulation of the N -agent control framework, we
112   can derive approximate solutions to the latter problem (for more theoretical background on MFC,
113   see (Bensoussan et al., 2013; Carmona et al., 2013; Carmona and Delarue, 2018)).

      Problem Dimensions: For the problem dimension, we count it as the sum of the dimension of the
384
      information input to the neural network. Since the state is in X , which is finite, we encode it as a one-
385
      hot vector in R|X | before passing it to the neural network to ensure differentiability. For the mean-
386   field distribution with stopped and non-stopped parts, it is an element of the (2|X | − 1)-simplex, and
387
      is represented as a non-negative vector in R2|X | . Therefore, MFOS tasks are intrinsically of spatial
388   dimension |X | + 2|X | = 3|X |, where |X | is the dimension of an individual agent’s state space.
389
390   Comparison of the Two Proposed Algorithms: While in theory both algorithms are equally capa-
391
      ble of tackling MFOS problems, in practice these algorithms have advantages in different settings.
      Empirically, we found that the optimal stopping decision is learned faster by DA than by DP, when
392
      compute power is not a restriction. However, DA requires differentiating through the whole trajec-
393
      tory at each gradient step, which requires a large amount of memory when the dimension is high.
394   The minimum required memory for training with DA increases with T , whereas DP requires only
395   constant order memory that is independent of the time horizon, since it trains time step per time
396   step. Therefore, when targeting an MFOS problem with a long time horizon T , DPP becomes more
397   efficient, at least memory-wise. Similar observations were made in the context of continuous time
398   optimal control by Germain et al. (2022).

526         7                                        C ONCLUSION
527
528        We proposed a discrete-time, finite state MAOS problem with randomized stopping times and its
529        mean field version. We proved that the latter is a good approximation of the former, and we estab-
530        lished a DPP for MFOS. These new problems cannot be tackled using traditional PDE approaches
531        or adapting previous methods for single-agent OS problems. To overcome these challenges, we
532        proposed two deep learning methods and evaluated their performance over six different scenarios.
533        When an analytical solution is available, we demonstrated that our methods recover this solution
534        in only a few iterations. In more complex environments, our approach is able to effectively solve
535        the task with high performance. The approach presented in this work can be effectively extended to
536
           other contexts and applications, especially given the growing importance of MAOS problems.
537        Limitations and Future Works: First, we did not prove convergence of the algorithms due to
538        the difficulty of analyzing deep networks. We also left for future work a detailed analysis of the
539        comparison between synchronous and asynchronous stopping. Last, we would like to continue the
           numerical experimentation on more complex, real-world examples.

       Neural Network Architectures: We have 4 variants of neural networks.
973
974    For the direct approach, the neural network takes an input time t, while for the DPP approach, the
975    neural network does not need time input.
976    For the asynchronous stopping problem, besides time, the neural network has two spatial inputs 1)
977    the state x, represented as an integer, goes through an embedding layer with learnable parameters
978    and the results are fed to other operations. 2) the distribution ν, represented as a vector, is inputted
979    to the neural net directly. For the synchronous stopping problem, the neural network only has one
980    spatial input, which is the distribution ν, and is treated as the same way as discussed before.
981    In general, our neural network has the following structure. Our neural network takes an input pair
982    (x, t), where x is the spatial input, t is the time. If t is a needed input, then it is passed through
983    a module to generate a standard sinusoidal embedding and then fed to 2 fully connected layers
984    with Sigmoid Linear Unit (SiLU) and generate an output tout . Spatial input x is passed through an
985    MLP with k residual blocks, each containing 4 linear layers with hidden dimension D and SiLU
986    activation. This generates an output yout . Our final output out is computed through,
987
                                     out = Outmod(GroupNorm(yout + tout ))
988
989    where Outmod is an out module that consists of 3 fully connected layers with hidden dimension D
990    and SiLU activation, GroupNorm stands for group normalization. If t is not a needed input, then
991    set tout = 0.
992    For all the test cases we have experimented with, we use k = 3, D = 128 for all the 1D experiments
993    and k = 5, D = 256 for the 2D experiments.
