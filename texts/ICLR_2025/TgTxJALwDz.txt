                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
               Quantum communication has the potential to revolutionise information process-
012
               ing, providing unparalleled security and increased capacity compared to its classi-
013            cal counterpart by using the principles of quantum mechanics. However, the pres-
014            ence of errors poses a significant challenge to realising these advantages. While
015            strategies like quantum error correction and quantum error mitigation have been
016            developed to address these errors, they often come with substantial overhead, hin-
017            dering the practical transmission of large texts. Here, we introduce an application
018            of machine learning frameworks for natural language processing to enhence the
019            performance of noisy quantum communications, particularly superdense coding.
020            Using bidirectional encoder representations from transformers (BERT), a model
021
               known for its capabilities in natural language processing, we demonstrate that
               language-model-assisted quantum communication protocols can substantially im-
022
               prove the efficiency of large-scale information transfer. This brings us closer to
023
               the practical realisation of a quantum internet.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      In our increasingly interconnected world, effective communication is essential for facilitating the
029
      exchange of information, fostering collaboration, and driving problem-solving across various do-
030
      mains. It serves as the foundation of a modern society, enabling the development of technological
031   landscapes. Entering the spotlight, quantum communication Bouwmeester et al. (1997); Gisin &
032   Thew (2007); Cozzolino et al. (2019); Xing et al. (2024) is emerging as a revolutionary field that
033   uses the principles of quantum mechanics to achieve unprecedented levels of security and efficiency,
034   signaling a new era in data transmission. Today, quantum information can already be transmitted
035   from satellites to the ground Ren et al. (2017); Liao et al. (2017); Lu et al. (2022); Li et al. (2024a),
036   and quantum networks are being established within cities (Yin et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2024; Knaut
037   et al., 2024). One notable example of the advantages of quantum communication is superdense cod-
038   ing Bennett et al. (1999); Harrow et al. (2004); Barreiro et al. (2008); Hu & et al. (2018); Li et al.
039
      (2024b), which enables a single qubit channel to transmit the equivalent of two classical bits. This
      is made possible through shared entanglement between the sender and receiver, effectively doubling
040
      the amount of information transmitted.
041
042   The next step in advancing quantum communication involves utilizing the superdense coding pro-
043   tocol as a subroutine for transmitting text. However, two practical issues need to be addressed. The
044   first, which is relatively straightforward, is transforming the text into a bit string that can be used for
045
      superdense coding. This can be easily achieved using standard ASCII code. The second, more com-
      plex challenge arises from the influence of noise on quantum communications, which can degrade
046
      performance. Current strategies, including quantum error correction and quantum error mitigation,
047
      have their pros and cons. For instance, quantum error correction Knill & Laflamme (1997); Aoki
048   et al. (2009); Terhal (2015); Krinner et al. (2022); Sivak et al. (2023) can efficiently handle errors
049   but often requires substantial additional resources that may not be available in practice. On the other
050   hand, quantum error mitigation Endo et al. (2018); Cai et al. (2023) necessitates multiple experi-
051   mental repetitions to minimize the impact of errors, potentially undermining the advantages offered
052   by superdense coding. Given these challenges, is there an alternative protocol that can effectively
053   mitigate noise in quantum communication without requiring extra quantum resources or multiple
      communication repetitions?

      Here, we address this question by introducing a language-model-assisted quantum protocol which
055   benefits from both superdense coding and natural language processing (NLP). In particular, we in-
056   corporate BERT, a pre-trained language model recognized for its ability to understand the context
057   and predict corrections in tasks such as sentiment analysis Hoang et al. (2019); Xu et al. (2019);
058   Sousa et al. (2019); Cao et al. (2021); Prottasha et al. (2022) and spell error correction Zhang et al.
059   (2020); Tan et al. (2020); Nguyen et al. (2020), into the superdense coding framework, resulting in
060   what we term post quantum communicaton BERT (PQC-BERT). PQC-BERT incorporates both a
061   word-level repairing module (WLRM) and a sentence-level repairing module (SLRM), significantly
062   enhancing the fidelity of text transmission in quantum communications. We assess the model’s per-
063
      formance by comparing the bit error rate, word error rate, and sentence error rate before and after its
      implementation through numerical experiments, validating its effectiveness in ensuring reliable text
064
      communication. This language-model-assisted quantum communication protocol presents a new
065
      method for mitigating noise in quantum communication, potentially accelerating the advancement
066   of hybrid classical-quantum networks.

183
184   In this section, we integrate superdense coding with machine-learning-based NLP to explore the
185   language-model-assisted quantum communications. This modifies the traditional process of trans-
186   mitting information – encoding → noise → decoding – into a more robust pipeline: pre-encoding →
187   encoding → noise → decoding → post-decoding, as illustrated in Figure. 1(a). Specifically, super-
188
      dense coding offers an efficient way for transmitting classical information using quantum commu-
      nication, with the potential to double the transmission capacity compared to classical approaches.
189
      However, superdense coding is typically limited to bit string inputs. To enable large-scale text trans-
190
      mission, the text is first converted into 8-bit ASCII code in the pre-encoding process, making it
191   compatible with superdense coding. The encoded bit strings are then transmitted, though noise dur-
192   ing the process can degrade performance. After decoding, machine learning-based NLP techniques
193   are employed in a post-processing phase, helping to recover and refine the transmitted information.
194   This approach mitigates the impact of noise and enhances the overall reliability and efficiency of
195   quantum communications.

197   3.1   Q UANTUM SUPERDENSE CODING
198
199   Quantum superdense coding is a protocol that enables the transmission of classical bits using fewer
200   qubits, provided that the sender and receiver share a pre-established entangled resource. In the
201   qubit case, if the parties pre-share a maximally entangled state [qq], and the sender has access to a
202   noiseless qubit channel [q → q] to transmit a qubit, it is possible to communicate 2 classical bits of
203   information. In other words, this simulates the process of 2[c → c].
204                                         [qq] + [q → q] ⩾ 2[c → c].                                   (6)
205
206   The formal procedure is outlined as follows:
207
208         1. Entanglement Distribution. A third party prepared the maximally entangled state |Φ00 ⟩
209            and distributed it to the sender, Alice, and the receiver, Bob.
210         2. Encoding. Alice encodes two bits of classical information zx ∈ {0, 1}2 into one of the
211            Bell states |Φzx ⟩ by applying Pauli operations Z(z)X(x) to her qubit.
212
            3. Noisy Communication. Alice then sends her qubit to Bob through the noisy quantum
213
               channel E.
214
215         4. Decoding. Upon receiving the qubit from Alice, Bob performs a Bell measurement. Based
               on the measurement outcome, Bob decodes the classical information zx that Alice encoded.

      4     N UMERICAL EXPERIMENTS
379
380
      In this section, we present numerical experiments to demonstrate the advantages of our language-
381   model-assisted communication protocols, emphasizing the role of machine learning, particularly
382   NLP, in enhancing communication. We compare the performance of bit and qubit communication in
383   the presence of bit-flip errors, finding that quantum communication consistently surpasses classical
384   methods in terms of sentence accuracy, recall, precision, and F1 score. Furthermore, our evalua-
385   tion of the language-model-assisted communication protocols shows that the PQC-BERT module
386   effectively mitigates noise, significantly reducing the bit error rate, word error rate, and sentence
387   error rate. These results highlight the superior performance of language-model-assisted quantum
388   communication.

      (CoLA) (Warstadt et al., 2019). The Flickr-8k dataset includes 8, 000 images, each paired with 5
394   distinct captions that effectively describe the key entities and events within the images. In contrast,
395   CoLA serves as a benchmark for single-sentence classification, containing sentences sourced from
396   23 linguistic publications, expertly annotated for acceptability by their original authors. To assess
397   the performance of our models on text transmission tasks, we manually extracted 12, 459 corrected
398   English sentences from Flickr-8k and 9, 078 from CoLA, resulting in two new datasets: Mini-Flickr
399   and Mini-CoLA.
400
401   4.2   S ETTING
402
403   Given a noisy channel E and ideal text T . we first simulate the effects of E and then apply WLRM
404
      to generate the modified text T w through word-level correction. During training, we use T w as the
      input and T as the output to train our neural network. For testing, we evaluate the similarity between
405
      the original ideal text T (before it passes through the noisy channel E) and the text produced after
406
      the post-decoding process. This evaluation encompasses the entire language-model-assisted com-
407   munication protocol. To validate our approach, we conduct independent and replicated experiments
408   to demonstrate the average performance of our method.

504   5   C ONCLUSION
505
506
      Quantum communications offer significant advantages over classical protocols, particularly in ca-
507
      pacity and privacy. However, these benefits are often undermined by noise, which is nearly unavoid-
508   able in practical applications. In this work, we leverage the power of language models in machine
509   learning to bolster the performance of a key quantum communication protocol – quantum super-
510   dense coding – in the presence of noise. We present PQC-BERT, a model that extends conventional
511   superdense coding to enable the transmission of text while effectively mitigating errors that arise
512   during quantum communication. Distinct from conventional quantum error correction techniques,
513   our method protects information without the need for additional systems. Furthermore, unlike typ-
514   ical quantum error mitigation strategies, PQC-BERT operates without requiring extra samples to
515   address noise effects. This makes our approach both resource-efficient and easy to implement, high-
516
      lighting the remarkable potential of classical machine learning techniques in advancing quantum
      communications.
517
518   The fusion of classical machine learning with quantum superdense coding also opens up new av-
519   enues for exploration and raises intriguing questions for future research: (1) Superdense coding is
520   typically used to transmit classical bit strings through quantum communication, demonstrating its
521   advantage in conveying more information per channel use. A key challenge now lies in extending the
522
      protocol to support more complex data types, such as images and audio. Given the distinct structure
      and features of such data, more advanced pre-encoding schemes – beyond the “ASCII encoding”
523
      – may be needed to fully optimize the transmission process. Developing these schemes could un-
524
      lock even broader applications of quantum communication protocols in the future. (2) While our
525   focus has been on language models and superdense coding, quantum communication encompasses
526   a wider array of tasks. As we move towards building a quantum internet, there is exciting poten-
527   tial to integrate artificial intelligence with other key protocols, such as quantum teleportation and
528   quantum repeaters, to further push the boundaries of this field. (3) In NLP domain, various models
529   have emerged following the BERT architecture. Will these subsequent models outperform BERT
530   in the post-decoding phase of superdense coding? Could different language models reveal distinct
531   advantages for different noise models? Our numerical experiments are based on processed datasets,
532   Mini-Flickr and Mini-CoLA, masked with simulated quantum noise. It will be beneficial to further
533
      evaluate the performance of language-model-assisted quantum communications in real physical sys-
      tems, such as photonics. However, exploring this aspect falls beyond the scope of the current work
534
      and will be addressed in future research.
