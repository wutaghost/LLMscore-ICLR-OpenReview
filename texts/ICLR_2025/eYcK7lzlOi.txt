009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Transformers (GTs) are rapidly emerging as superior models, surpassing
012            traditional message-passing neural networks in graph-level tasks. For optimal
013            performance, it is essential to design GT architectures that embed graph induc-
014            tive biases and utilize global attention mechanisms through effective structural
015            encodings (SEs). In this work, we introduce novel SEs derived from a rigorous
016            theoretical analysis of random walks (RWs), specifically leveraging the Green
017            and Martin kernels. The Green and Martin kernels are mathematical tools used to
018            observe the long-term behavior of RWs on graphs. By integrating these kernels into
019
               the encoding process, we enhance their capability to accurately represent complex
               graph structures. Our empirical evaluations demonstrate that these approaches en-
020
               able GTs to achieve state-of-the-art performance on 7 out of 8 benchmark datasets.
021
               These include molecular datasets characterized by intricate, non-aperiodic sub-
022            structures such as benzene rings, and directed acyclic graphs common in the circuit
023            domain. We attribute these performance improvement to the effective capture of the
024            characteristics of non-aperiodic substructures and directed acyclic graphs by our
025            extending encodings. The results not only validate the effectiveness of integrating
026            the Green and Martin kernels into RW-based encodings but also underscore their
027            potential to substantially enhance the learning capabilities of GTs across diverse
028            applications.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Graph Transformers (GTs) (1; 2; 3; 4; 5; 6) have been proposed as a superior alternative to conven-
034   tional Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) (7), mitigating MPNNs’ well-known issues such
035   as over-smoothing (8; 9), over-squashing (10; 11), under-reaching (12; 13), and limited expressive
036   power (14; 15). For GTs to perform effectively, it is essential to incorporate inductive biases (16)
037   specific to graph data. Additionally, global attention of GTs requires structural encodings (SEs) that
038   enable precise differentiation of nodes within the graph and its substructures (17). Recent model,
039
      GRIT (6), has shown state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance for various benchmarks by implicitly
      incorporating the graph inductive bias from message-passing and the global attention advantages of
040
      Transformer (18) by using Relative Random Walk Probabilities (RRWP) as the SE.
041
042   Random walks (RWs) have been extensively studied as a means of exploring the structure of graphs.
043   Several results (19; 20; 21) have demonstrated that the long-term behavior of a RW encapsulates
044   the topological information of the graph. There are two mathematical quantities that capture this
045
      long-term behavior, providing valuable insights into the graph’s characteristics. The Green kernel
      is classically defined as a “pseudo-inverse operator” used to find solutions to equations (22). Anal-
046
      ogously, the Green kernel on a graph can be defined by the Moore-Penrose Inverse of the graph
047
      Laplacian matrix in (23; 24), in which the Green kernel is utilized for calculating various probabilistic
048   quantities. In the context of RWs on graphs, it represents the expected number of visits to one node
049   from another (25). The Martin kernel, on the other hand, is an important function in probability
050   theory and potential theory, particularly within the framework of Martin boundary theory (26). By
051   utilizing Martin kernel, one can construct the harmonic potential function, which is essential tool for
052   analyzing behavior of the Brownian motion (27). The Martin kernel is defined on graphs analogously
053   and used for constructing the harmonic potential function (20). Moreover, it is equal to the probability
      that a RW starting from one node will reach another node within a finite time (21).

      In this study, we introduce two extended concepts from the theory of RWs—the Green and Martin
055   kernels (21; 25; 28)—as new SEs. To the best of our knowledge, our paper is the first to introduce
056   these concepts to design SEs. We apply these new SEs to the recently developed GRIT model (6)
057   and demonstrate that they outperform existing methods on various benchmark datasets, including the
058   molecular and circuit domains. Through this targeted innovation, we present new SEs that are not
059   only specialized for non-aperiodic substructures and DAGs, but also enhanced performance across
060   diverse benchmarks.
061   In summary, our contributions are as follows. (i) We propose new SEs, Green Kernel Structural
062   Encoding (GKSE) and Martin Kernel Structural Encoding (MKSE), based on the Green and Martin
063   kernels, which extend the RWs. (ii) We integrate GKSE and MKSE into the GRIT, demonstrating that
064   GKSE and MKSE surpass SOTA performance across various graph benchmarks, including those with
065   small, medium, large, and long-range interactions. (iii) In molecular graphs containing numerous
066   non-aperiodic substructures (e.g., benzene rings), our methods demonstrate efficient learning and
067   strong performance on ZINC (29) and PCQM4Mv2 (30). (iv) We demonstrate that GKSE and MKSE
068   efficiently learn circuit domain data, which is represented as a DAG, and provide several baseline
069
      benchmark results on Open Circuit Benchmark (OCB), the first graph benchmark dataset in the
      circuit domain.

074   2   R ELATED W ORK
075
076
077   Graph Transformers. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have advanced considerably, evolving
078   from MPNNs (7) to sophisticated GTs. GTs capitalize on Transformers’ flexibility and scalability,
079   incorporating SEs to enhance learning from graph data. Notably, NAGphormer (31), AGT (32) and
080   TokenGT (33) leverage Laplacian eigenvectors as SEs, demonstrating strong performance in node
081
      classification tasks by effectively capturing the global structure of graphs. In addition, the magnetic
      Laplacian have been explored for SEs, emphasizing the importance of directed graphs (34).
082
083   GT models have also evolved to incorporate edge attributes, improving their abbility to capture the
084   structural information present in graphs. The Graphormer (1) and EGT (2) enhance the self-attention
085   mechanism by integrating edge attributes, which improve the interaction between node attributes
086   during the learning process. Moreover, several models have introduced relative SEs to handle edge
087
      attributes more effectively. GraphGPS (5) applies relative SEs to facilitate interaction between
      local message-passing and global self-attention mechanism. In particular, the GRIT (6) employs a
088
      multimodal approach to incorporating both node and edge attributes into the self-attention mechanism.
089
      It achieves high performance by utilizing RRWP, an SE based on RWs, effectively capturing the
090   structural properties of graphs. For comprehensive insights into GTs, readers can refer to detailed
091   surveys that cover recent methodologies, challenges, and future research opportunities (35; 36).
092
093
      Structural Encodings for Graphs. GTs encounter notable challenges in encoding structural informa-
      tion, which are crucial for distinguishing non-isomorphic structures and utilizing graph symmetries.
094
      In this paper, we consider and utilize SE as node and edge representations that are invariant to graph
095
      isomorphisms, in order to better capture the structural information of the graph (37).
096
097   For SEs, the use of graph Laplacians in graph analysis has been widely explored. One study intro-
098   duced globally consistent anisotropic kernels using Laplacian eigenvectors to incorporate directional
099
      information in GNNs (38). Additionally, other research has generalized graph Laplacians, demon-
      strating their effectiveness in capturing the geometric structure of graphs (17; 39). Researchers have
100
      tackled the constraints of spectral methods through the development of SignNet and BasisNet, which
101
      maintain invariance to sign flips and the basis symmetries of eigenvectors (40). Additionally, another
102   study leveraged the eigenvectors of the Magnetic Laplacian to integrate directional information
103   into SEs (34; 41). Furthermore, an alternative approach utilized the Hodge 1-Laplacian spectrum
104   for creating edge-level SEs (42). On the other hand, the Random Walk-based Structural Encoding
105   (RWSE) has been proposed (43), while direction- and structure-aware SEs for directed graphs based
106   on directional RWs have been developed (34). In addition, the RRWPs were proposed using RW
107   probabilities and learned relative SEs (6). This was extended by applying edge-level RWs on a
      simplicial complex for edge SEs in graphs (42).

      3     M ATHEMATICAL BACKGROUND
109
110
      The Green and Martin kernels are mathematical tools that capture the long-term behavior of RWs on
111   graphs. Specifically, both kernels are functions of node pairs, and from this perspective, we utilize
112   them as absolute or relative SEs for GNNs or GTs. When used as SEs, these kernels leverage the
113   RW information that inherently reflects the topological properties of the graph, enabling the model
114   to better capture structural patterns. We begin by describing RWs on grpahs as a stochastic process,
115   focusing on the Green and Martin kernels.

176   3.3   A DAPTING G REEN AND M ARTIN K ERNELS FOR R ECURRENT G RAPHS
177
178   Most graph data in practical applications tends to be recurrent rather than transient, which makes the
179   computation of the traditional Green and Martin kernels less meaningful. In finite graphs, RWs are
180   recurrent unless there is a sink region that terminates the walk (i.e., a killed process). This issue arises
181   because the traditional Green and Martin kernels capture the long-term behavior of RWs over infinite
182
      time, where RWs repeatedly revisit nodes. To address this limitation, we developed new versions
      of the Green and Martin kernels by restricting the RW to a finite number of steps. This approach
183
      allows us to create new kernels for our proposed SEs, which reflect meaningful RW properties even
184
      in recurrent graphs by capturing the behavior over a finite horizon.
185
186   One further issue is that, for all x ∈ V, the value of the Martin kernel at (x, x) is always 1 because the
187   RW immediately revisits itself at step 0. As a result, even when using the finite-step Martin kernel,
188
      the absolute SE consists of constant 1s. To resolve this, we replace the first hitting time map τx with
      the first return time map τx+ : Ω → N ∪ {0}, which is defined as
189
190                              τx+ (ω) := min{i ∈ N | Xi (ω) = x},         ∀ω ∈ Ω.                           (8)
191
192   By modifying the definition of the Martin kernel to use the first return time map, the absolute
193   SE reflects the topology of the graph. More specifically, it becomes the probability that a RW
194   starting from a node returns to itself. This adjustment ensures that the SE reflects more meaningful
195
      information about the graph structure. Importantly, the relative SE remains unaffected by this change.

      5.3    A NALYSIS OF E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
472
473
      Our proposed SEs exhibited SOTA on 7 out of 8 benchmarks, with superior performance on regression
474   tasks compared to classification tasks. In particular, our proposed SEs outperformed on PCQM4Mv2
475   and Ckt-Bench101, which we attribute to the advantage of our proposed approaches in encapsulating
476   graph structural information more effectively than existing SEs in certain graphs. We have explored
477   which properties of our proposed SEs contirubte to the improvement in performance compared with
478   another RW-based SE, specifically RRWP. We investigated the unique characteristics of molecule
479   and circuit graphs and observed that GKSE and MKSE, compared to RRWP, represents these features
480   in fundamentally different ways.
481   First, the molecular graph dataset is characterized by a large number of substructures, such as
482   hexagonal benzene rings. In Figure 1, we visualize three SE values, RRWP, GKSE, and MKSE, on
483   the fluorescein molecule graph. For RRWP, the edges with higher RRWP values form hexagons when
484   k is odd, on the other side they form a star shape when k is even. This phenomenon arises because
485   the hexagonal subgraph is non-aperiodic. In fact, transition probabilities on non-aperiodic graphs
      oscillate indefinitely. Mathematical details supporting this stability are provided in Appendix D.1. In

503   Based on our experimental results, we hypothesize that the stability of our proposed SEs in handling

508   Second, our proposed SEs also demonstrate strong performance on datasets with DAG structures,
509   such as OCB, which is common in the circuit domain. In DAGs, RWs terminate after a finite number

511   when using RRWP–especially for values of K that are larger than diameter of the graph. This
512   sparsity can weaken the representational power of the graph structure. Indeed, in datasets where
513
      graph samples have varying diameters, a fixed hyperparamter K may fit well for some samples but
      result in overly sparse SEs for others with low diameters. Such imbalance can negatively affect the
514
      overall learning performance. However, our proposed SEs maintain consistent representations even
515
      for large K, making it suitable for capturing the structural information of DAGs, as shown in Figure 2.
516   This enhanced efficacy on directed graphs can be attributed to the intrinsic properties of the SEs.
517
518   Overall, we infer that our proposed SEs are particularly beneficial for regression tasks involving
519
      non-aperiodic substructures or DAGs. The performance advantage of GKSE and MKSE can be
      attributed to their ability to effectively capture intricate structural details in such graphs, thereby
520
      enhancing the learning capabilities of GTs across diverse applications.

523   6   C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this work, we introduced novel SEs, GKSE and MKSE, to expedite GTs by leveraging theoretical
526   insights into the Green and Martin kernels within graph data. These encodings provide a foundational
527   approach to extending RW-based methods, enhancing the expressiveness and efficiency of GTs. Our
528
      proposed SEs demonstrated significant improvements across multiple benchmarks, outperforming
      SOTA methods in 7 out of 8 tasks. These results confirm that our methods not only achieve
529
      superior performance but also effectively represent both molecular and circuit data, aligning with
530
      our theoretical analyses. The ability of GKSE and MKSE to capture unique structural features
531   across diverse graph domains suggests promising directions for future research. We plan to further
532   explore these capabilities to develop more expressive SEs with theoretically provable properties
533   and to design model architectures that fully leverage this enhanced expressiveness. By providing a
534   deeper understanding of the underlying kernels and a practical approach to improve GTs, this study
535   contributes to the advancement of graph representation learning. It paves the way for developing
536   more sophisticated and capable GT models in future research.

      ZINC (29) comprises 12,000 molecular graphs sampled from the ZINC database (48) of commercially
779
      available chemical compounds. These molecular graphs contain between 9 and 37 nodes, where each
780   node corresponds to a heavy atom from one of 28 possible atom types, and each edge represents one
781   of three possible bond types. The task associated with this dataset is to predict a molecular property
782   known as constrained solubility (logP). The dataset is provided with a predefined split of 10,000
783   training, 1,000 validation, and 1,000 test samples.
784
      MNIST and CIFAR10 (29) are derived from their corresponding classical image classification
785
      datasets by constructing 8 nearest-neighbor graphs of SLIC superpixels for each image. The resulting
786   graphs contain 40-75 nodes for MNIST and 85-150 nodes for CIFAR10. The 10-class classifica-
787   tion tasks and standard dataset splits mirror the original image classification datasets, specifically
788   55K/5K/10K for MNIST and 45K/5K/10K for CIFAR10 in terms of train/validation/test graphs.
789   These datasets serve as sanity checks, with most GNNs expected to achieve near 100% accuracy for
790   MNIST and perform sufficiently well for CIFAR10.
791
      PATTERN and CLUSTER (29) are synthetic datasets derived from a probabilistic block model,
792   specifically designed for inductive node-level classification tasks. In the PATTERN dataset, the
793   objective is to identify nodes that belong to one of 100 possible subgraph patterns. These patterns
794   are randomly generated using different Stochastic Block Model (SBM) parameters from the rest of
795   the graph. In the CLUSTER dataset, each graph is divided into six clusters, all generated using the
796   same SBM distribution. Within each cluster, only one node has a unique cluster ID. The task is to
797   determine the cluster ID for each node based on the structure of the graph.
798   Peptides-func and Peptides-struct (46) datasets consist of atomic graphs of peptides. Derived from
799   a collection of 15,535 peptides encompassing a total of 2.3 million nodes from SATPdb (49), these
800   two datasets share the same set of graphs but differ in their prediction tasks. In the Peptides-func
801   dataset, the task is to classify each graph into one or more of 10 non-exclusive peptide functional
802   classes. In the Peptides-struct dataset, the goal is to regress 11 distinct 3D structural properties of
803   the peptides. These graphs are designed to require inference of long-range interactions (LRI) for
804   robust performance. With an average of 150.9 nodes per graph and a mean graph diameter of 57, they
805   provide a challenging benchmark for GTs and other GNNs aimed at capturing LRIs.
806   PCQM4Mv2 (30) dataset is an extensive graph regression benchmark comprising almost 3.7 million
807   molecular graphs. The objective is to predict the HOMO-LUMO gap, a quantum mechanical property
808   computed using Density Functional Theory. The true labels for the original "test-dev" and "test-
809   challenge" dataset splits are withheld by the OGB-LSC challenge organizers to ensure the integrity
      of the competition. Thus, we utilized the original validation set as our test set, excluding 150,000

       D.1   A PERIODICITY OF G RAPHS
1258
1259   Let G = (V, E) be a graph. The period pG of G is defined by the greatest common divisor of the
1260   lengths of its cycles:
1261                             pG := gcd{n : X0 (ω) = Xn (ω), ω ∈ Ω}.                          (58)
1262
       We call a graph G is aperiodic if pG = 1 and non-aperiodic if pG > 1.
1263
1264   One important remark about the period of a graph is it affects the spectrum of the transition probability
1265   matrix P of the graph. By the Perron-Frobenius theorem for irreducible non-negative matrix (66),
1266
       there exists exact pG eigenvalues attaining the maximal absolute value.
1267   For example, the hexagon graph C6 , which is the cycle graph on 6 nodes as illustrated in Fig-
1268   ure 3, has pC6 = 2 and its transition probability matrix has eigenvalues 1 and −1. In this
1269   case, the eigenvectors associated with the eigenvalues 1 and −1 is ϕ1 = (1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1)T and
1270   ϕ−1 = (1, −1, 1, −1, 1, −1)T , respectively. It can be observed by spectral analysis that for any
1271   vector v ∈ R6 that is not spanned by ϕ1 or ϕ−1 , Pk v oscillates between ϕ1 + ϕ−1 and ϕ1 − ϕ−1 as
1272
       k → +∞. This phenomenon may leads to the unstability of RRWP.
1273   Formally, let 1x be the one-hot vector supported at a node x ∈ V. Then we have
1274
1275
                                         P(x, y) = 1Tx P 1y ,    ∀x, y ∈ V.                                (59)
1276   Since 1y is not spanned by ϕ1 or ϕ−1 , P1y oscillates as k → +∞ and hence P(x, y) also oscillates
1277   as k → +∞.
1278
       However, G(k) (x, y) diverges to +∞ and M(k) (x, y) converges to 1 as k → +∞ for recurrent
1279
       graphs. Thus, G(k) and M(k) do not oscillate indefinitely as illustrated in Figure 3, indicating that
1280
       they are more stable under the choice of K ∈ N ∪ {0} and capture the structural property of a graph
1281
       well.
1282
1283   Despite the above observations, it remains unclear whether the absence of oscillation in GKSE and
1284   MKSE actually enables better detection of non-aperiodic substructures. As noted in (67), detecting
1285
       specific substructures is an extremely challenging task and is proven to be infeasible with many
       existing GNN models. Nevertheless, since GKSE and MKSE exhibit distinct patterns compared to
1286
       traditional methods, we are optimistic that they could provide some advantage. Further research is
1287
       necessary to confirm this hypothesis.
