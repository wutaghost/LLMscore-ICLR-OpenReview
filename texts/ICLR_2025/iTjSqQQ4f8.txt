                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Despite widespread adoption of machine learning throughout industry, many firms
010
               face a common challenge: relevant datasets are typically distributed amongst mar-
011            ket competitors that are reluctant to share information. Recent works propose data
012            markets to provide monetary incentives for collaborative machine learning, where
013            agents share features with each other and are rewarded based on their contribution
014            to improving the predictions others. These contributions are determined by their
015            relative Shapley value, which is computed by treating features as players and their
016            interactions as a characteristic function game. However, in its standard form, this
017            setup further provides an incentive for agents to replicate their data and act under
018            multiple false identities in order to increase their own revenue and diminish that
019
               of others, restricting their use in practice. In this work, we develop a replication-
               robust data market for supervised learning problems. We adopt PearlРђЎs do-calculus
020
               from causal reasoning to refine the characteristic function game by differentiating
021
               between observational and interventional conditional probabilities. By doing this,
022            we derive Shapley value-based rewards that are robust to this malicious replication
023            by design, whilst preserving desirable market properties.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   When faced with machine learning task, it can often be the case that a firm would benefit from using
029   the data of others. For example, rival distributors of similar goods may improve supply forecasts by
030   sharing sales data, hoteliers could find value in data from airline companies for anticipating demand,
031
      hospitals could reduce social biases from diagnostic support systems by sharing patient details, and
      so forth. In this work, we consider the example of renewable energy producers exposed to uncertain
032
      levels of production and therefore require reliable forecasts to competitively participate in electricity
033
      markets, with their revenue a function of predictive performance. It is well-studied that, with access
034   to distributed data, in both a geographic and ownership sense, these agents could exploit spatial and
035   temporal correlations between sites to improve their forecasts (Tastu et al., 2013).
036
037
      In practice, firms may be reluctant to share information due to privacy concerns or perceived conflicts
      of interest. Whilst methods from the field of federated learning (Lalitha et al., 2018) could indeed be
038
      used to train models on local servers without the need to centralize any data, this relies on altruistic
039
      sharing of information amongst market competitors. An alternative approach is to provide incentives
040   for data sharingРђћrecent works propose data markets (Bergemann & Bonatti, 2019), where agents
041   can collaborate by sharing features with each other to improve the predictions of others, without
042   transferring any raw data between them (Pinson et al., 2022). With foundations in informational ef-
043   ficiency of financial markets (Hayek, 1986), data markets have similar economic roots as prediction
044   markets (Waggoner et al., 2015), mechanisms designed to consolidate information with the goal of
045   forecasting outcomes of future events (Frongillo & Waggoner, 2018).
046   Whilst prediction markets can also be used to crowdsource data for machine learning (Abernethy &
047   Frongillo, 2011), data owners themselves need to decide which tasks to contribute to a priori, when
048   the relevance of their dataset is unknown. In contrast, data markets serve as real-time mechanism that
049   match features to machine learning tasks based their capacity to improve predictive performance.
050   Market revenue is a function of the value this brings to the task owner, and each feature owned by a
051   distributed agent is rewarded based on its marginal contribution to the improvement.
052
053   Challenges Marginal contributions are hard to quantify when features are correlated. For example,
      if features are valued sequentially, it has been shown that agents would eventually sell their data for


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      less than their own costs as the information becomes redundant (Acemoglu et al., 2022). Whilst
055   this is not the case if valuation occurs in parallel, the value of overlapping information is inherently
056   combinatorial. To address this issue, recent works propose to adopt concepts from cooperative game
057   theory, treating features as players and their interactions as a characteristic function game (Ghorbani
058   & Zou, 2019). For many practitioners, the Shapley value (Shapley, 1997) is the solution concept of
059   choice for such a game, which allocates each player its expected marginal contribution towards a set
060   (or coalition) of other players, satisfying a collection of axioms that yield several desirable market
061   properties by design (Agarwal et al., 2019). However, when computing this expectation, these works
062   make implicit assumptions about the distribution of in- and out-of-coalition feautres, which creates
063
      incentives that result in grossly undesirable outcomes.
064   Specifically, these works model observational conditional probabilities for out-of-coalition features,
065   which we show allows agents to replicate their data and act under multiple false identities to increase
066   their revenue. For instance, if an agentРђЎs feature is highly correlated with that of another agent, they
067   can simply submit many replicates of their feature under different identities, increasing their overall
068   revenue and driving that of the other agents to zero. This stems from the fact that data, unlike mate-
069
      rial commodities, can be replicated at no additional cost. Whilst several attempts have been made to
      remedy this problem, doing so typically requires a trade-off. For instance, Ohrimenko et al. (2019)
070
      propose a more elaborate mechanism design, necessitating that each seller also owns a machine
071
      learning task, which has practical limitations. Agarwal et al. (2019) propose a modification to the
072   Shapley value which penalizes similar features, thereby preventing replication, yet foregoes budget
073   balance and remains vulnerable to spiteful agentsРђћthose who seek to minimize the revenue of other
074   agents whilst maximizing their own profits. A similar shortcoming is observed in the proposal of
075   Han et al. (2023), as both natural correlations and deliberate replications are penalized.
076
077   Contributions The main contributions of our paper are as follows: (i) we propose a general data
078   market design for supervised learning problems that subsumes many existing proposals in literature;
079   (ii) we show that there are many ways in which Shapley values can be used to derive rewards and
080   that the differences between them can be explained from a caused perspective; (iii) we leverage
081   PearlРђЎs seminal work on causality (Pearl, 2012) to show that by replacing the conventional approach
082
      of conditioning by observation with conditioning by intervention, we can design a data market in a
      way that is replication-robust whilst also accounting for spiteful agents, thereby taking a step toward
083
      practical application of these markets; finally (iv) we demonstrate our findings using a real-world
084
      case studyРђћout of many potential applications, we choose to study wind power forecasting due to
085   data availability, the known value of sharing distributed data, and the fact it is a sandbox that can be
086   easily shared and used by others.
087
088
      The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: Section 2 presents our general market design
      framework. In Section 3 we derive variants of the characteristic function and analyse each from a
089
      causal perspective. In Section 4 we discuss the implications of these to replication-robustness of the
090
      market. Section 5 then illustrates our findings on a real-world case study. Finally, Section 6 gathers
091   a set of conclusions and perspectives for future work.

      Interpreting Rewards We can explore this last conjecture by considering how the rewards of the
255
      support agents may differ depending on the choice of lift. We know that the predictive performance
256   of the regression model out-of-sample is contingent upon the availability of features that were used
257   during training, which, in practice, requires data of the support agents to be streamed continuously in
258   a timely fashion, particularly for an online setup. If a feature was missing, the efficacy of the forecast
259   may drop, the extent to which would relate not to any root causes or indirect effects regarding the
260   data generating process, but rather the magnitude of direct effects.
261
      Specifically, larger rewards would be made to support agents with features to which the predictive
262   performance of the model is most sensitive, providing incentives to reduce data being unavailability,
263   resembling reserve payments in electricity markets, where assets are remunerated for being available
264   in times of need. For the observational lift, it would instead be unclear as to whether comparatively
265   larger rewards in the regression market are consequential of features having a sizeable impact on pre-
266   dictive performance, or merely a result of indirect effects through those that do. The interventional
267   lift therefore better aligns with desirable intentions of the market.

458   6   C ONCLUSIONS
459
460   Many machine learning tasks could benefit from using the data of others, however convincing firms
461   to share information, even if privacy is assured, poses a considerable challenge. Rather than relying
462   on data altruism, data markets are recognized as a promising way of providing incentives for data
463   sharing, many of which use Shapley values to allocate rewards. Nevertheless, there are a number of
464   open issues that remain before such mechanisms can be used in practice, one of which is vulnerabil-
465
      ity to replication incentives, which we showed leads to undesirable reward allocation and restricts
      the practical viability of these markets.
466
467   We introduced a general framework for data markets for supervised learning problems that sub-
468   sumes many of these existing proposals. We demonstrated that there are several different ways to
469   formulate a machine learning task as cooperative game and analysed their differences from a causal
470   perspectives. We showed that use of the observational lift to value a coalition is the source of these
471
      replication incentives, which many works have tried to remedy through penalization methods, which
      facilitate only weak robustness. Our main contribution is an alternative algorithm for allocating re-
472
      wards that instead uses interventional conditional probabilities. Our proposal is robust to replication
473
      without comprising market properties such as budget balance. This is a step towards making Shapley
474   value-based data markets feasible in practice.
475
476   From a causal perspective, the interventional lift has additional potential benefits, including reward
477
      allocations that better represent the reliance of the model on each feature, providing an incentive for
      timely and reliable data streams for useful features, that is, those with greater influence on predictive
478
      performance. It is also favourable with respect to computational expenditure. There is of course, no
479
      free lunch, as using the interventional conditional expectation can yield undesirable rewards when
480   feautres are highly correlated and the number of observations is low. Nevertheless, future work could
481   examine the extent to which the mentioned remedies mitigate this issue, as well as their impact on
482   the market outcomes. Ultimately, when it comes to data valuation, the Shapley value is not without
483   its limitationsРђћit is not generally well-defined in a machine learning context and requires strict
484   assumptions, not to mention its computational complexity. This should also incite future work into
485   alternative mechanism design frameworks, for example those based on non-cooperative game theory
      instead.
