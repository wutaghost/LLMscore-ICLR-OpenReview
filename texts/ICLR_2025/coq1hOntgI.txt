009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Safe learning is central to AI-enabled robots where a single failure may lead to
012            catastrophic results. Barrier-based method is one of the dominant approaches for
013            safe robot learning. However, this method is not scalable, hard to train, and tends
014            to generate unstable signals under noisy inputs that are challenging to be deployed
015            for robots. To address these challenges, we propose a novel Attention BarrierNet
016
               (ABNet) that is scalable to build larger foundational safe models in an incremental
               manner. Each head of BarrierNet in the ABNet could learn safe robot control
017
               policies from different features and focus on specific part of the observation. In
018
               this way, we do not need to one-shotly construct a large model for complex tasks,
019            which significantly facilitates the training of the model while ensuring its stable
020            output. Most importantly, we can still formally prove the safety guarantees of the
021            ABNet. We demonstrate the strength of ABNet in 2D robot obstacle avoidance,
022            safe robot manipulation, and vision-based end-to-end autonomous driving, with
023            results showing much better robustness and guarantees over existing models.

042   Robot learning usually requires to leverage scalable training and vast amount of data. There are
043   many large models Li et al. (2022) for complex robotic tasks including manipulation, locomotion,
044   autonomous driving Bommasani et al. (2021) Singh et al. (2023) Wang et al. (2023a). However,
045   these models are not trustworthy and have no safety guarantees. Existing methods that incorporate
046   guarantees or certificates into neural networks are not scalable and hard to train Pereira et al. (2020)
047   Xiao et al. (2023a) Wang et al. (2023b). It is desirable to merge these models as we can get better
048   performance controllers in general Beygelzimer et al. (2015) Agarwal et al. (2020). Traditional
049   mixture of expert methods Shazeer et al. (2017) Riquelme et al. (2021) Zhou et al. (2022) or other
050
      merging approaches Huang et al. (2023) Ramé et al. (2023) Wang et al. (2024) are hard to retain the
      safety of the models. In this work, we explore to leverage the collective power of many safety-critical
051
      models to handle complex tasks while preserving the safety of the models.
052
053   There are various definitions of safety for robotics and autonomy, and safety can be basically
      defined as something bad never happens. Mathematically, safety can be defined as a continuously

054
      differentiable constraint with respect to the system state and it can be further captured by the forward
055   invariance of the safe set over such a constraint Ames et al. (2017) Xiao & Belta (2022) Glotfelter
056   et al. (2017). In other words, we can use different constraints and approaches to enforce safety. The
057   way we learn such safety enforcement methods may depend on the focused observation feature,
058   which corresponds to the attention mechanism. For instance, some human drivers may focus on the
059   left lane boundary in driving in order to achieve safe lane keeping, while others may focus on the
060   right lane boundary, as shown in Fig. 1. Both attention mechanisms can achieve similar purpose.
061   Merging these models or attention mechanisms enables us to build robust and powerful learning
062   models. However, retaining safety is non-trivial.
063   In the literature, barrier-based learning methods Robey et al. (2020) Pereira et al. (2020) Srinivasan
064   et al. (2020) Xiao et al. (2023b), such as the BarrierNet Xiao et al. (2023a) Wang et al. (2023b)
065   Liu et al. (2023), are widely used to equip deep learning systems with safety guarantees. We may
066   incorporate control-theoretic based optimizations into learning systems in the form of differentiable
067   quadratic programs (dQPs) Amos & Kolter (2017). There are several limitations of these barrier-based
068   learning methods: (i) it can only implement a single safety enforcement method as the last layer of
069
      the neural network, which is not scalable to larger safe learning models; (ii) the model is not robust
      such that it is hard to be trained to work for complicated robotic applications; (iii) these methods tend
070
      to generate unstable output under noisy observation, which is intractable to be deployed for robots.
071
072   In this paper, we propose a novel Attention BarrierNet (ABNet) to merge many safety-critical models
073   while preserving the safety guarantees.The ABNet is scalable, robust to noise, and easy to be trained
074   in an incremental manner. As shown in Fig. 1, we may build multi-head BarrierNets within the
075
      ABNet. Each head of the BarrierNet may pay attention to different observation features to generate a
      safe control policy. We linearly combine the outputs of all the BarrierNets in a way that is provably
076
      safe. The weights of this combination quantify the importance of each head of BarrierNet, and they
077
      are trainable. The structure of the ABNet allows us to build larger foundational safe models for
078   various and complicated robotic applications as we can incrementally train safe models corresponding
079   to different robot skills and this will simply increase the head h of BarrierNets.
080
081
      In summary, we make the following new contributions:
082   • We propose a novel ABNet that merges many safety-critical learning models, and this new model is
083     scalable, robust, and easy to be trained.
084   • We formally prove the safety guarantees of the proposed ABNet.
085   • We demonstrate the strength and effectiveness of our model on a variety of robot control tasks,
086     including 2D robot obstacle avoidance, safe robot manipulation, and vision-based end-to-end
087     autonomous driving in an open dataset. We also show that existing models/policies merging could
088     make safety worse in complicated tasks (such as in vision-based driving).

      BarrierNet. The BarrierNet Xiao et al. (2023a) is a neural network layer that incorporates
126
      CBF/HOCBF-based QPs as differentiable QPs (dQPs) Amos & Kolter (2017), in which all the
127
      CBFs/HOCBFs are differentiable in terms of their parameters (such as those in class K functions).
128   Those parameters are crucial to the system conservativeness or performance in guaranteeing safety.
129   In summary, the BarrierNet frees us from handing-tuning all the parameters in safety-critical controls,
130   and simply uses data to optimize them. It can be trained using either imitation learning Xiao et al.
131   (2023a) or reinforcement learning Liu et al. (2023). Referring to Fig. 1, a BarrierNet only has a single
132   head in the model (i.e., h = 1) and it is placed as the last layer of the model when used in conjunction
133   with other neural networks (such as CNN and LSTM).

239   3.2   M ODEL T RAINING
240
241   The ABNet can be trained incrementally or in one-shot. This is due to the fact that each head of
242
      BarrierNet can generate a control policy that is applicable to system (1). The linear combination
      weights wk , k ∈ {1, . . . , h} in the ABNet denote the importance of the corresponding control policies.
243
244   Scalable training. In ABNet, we may train each head k, k ∈ {1, . . . , h} of the BarrierNet in a scalable
245   way as we wish to minimize the loss between their output uk and the label u∗ as well. The training can
246   be done using the batch QP training method proposed in Amos & Kolter (2017). There are some cross
247   connections via pi (z|θ p ) between BarrierNets in the ABNet that may prevent the implementation
248
      of the training. We may address this by training a pi (z|θ p ) for each head of the BarrierNet. After
      we train all heads of the BarrierNet, we may fix the parameters of those models, choose a pi (z|θ p )
249
      from one of the BarrierNets (or take an average of all pi (z|θ p ) among the BarrierNets) to build the
250
      cross connection, and train the weights wi for some more iterations. Another way is to fuse these
251   BarrierNets by their testing loss. In other words, the weight wk , k ∈ {1, . . . , h} can be determined by:

440   5    R ELATED W ORKS
441
442   Scalability, merging and uncertainty in safe robot learning. Machine learning has been widely
443   used in robot control Bommasani et al. (2021) Singh et al. (2023) Wang et al. (2023a). Mixture of
444
      expert methods Shazeer et al. (2017) Riquelme et al. (2021) Zhou et al. (2022) are scalable but hard to
      retain the property (such as safety) of the models. The uncertainty resulting from noisy model input
445
      or dataset is preventing the deployment to real robots Loquercio et al. (2020) Kahn et al. (2017). To
446
      address this, predictive uncertainty quantification Gal & Ghahramani (2016) Lakshminarayanan et al.
447   (2017), also a model merging approach, has been widely adopted. It has been shown to work well in
448   vision-based autonomous driving under noisy input Wang et al. (2023b) using the Gaussian kernel
449   with Scott’s rule Scott (2015) to select bandwidth. The main challenge of this technique is that it
450   may make the system lose performance guarantees, such as safety. Other model merging approaches
451   Huang et al. (2023) Ramé et al. (2023) Wang et al. (2024) do not preserve safety either. We address
452   the uncertainty and scalablibity problem using the proposed ABNets with provable safety.
453   CBFs and set invariance. In control theory, the set invariance has been widely adopted to prove and
454   enforce the safety of dynamical systems (Blanchini, 1999) (Rakovic et al., 2005) (Ames et al., 2017)
455   Xiao & Belta (2022) Xiao et al. (2023a). The Control Barrier Function (CBF) (Ames et al., 2017)
456   Xiao & Belta (2022) is such a state of the art technique that can enforce set invariance (Aubin, 2009),
457   (Prajna et al., 2007), (Wisniewski & Sloth, 2013), and transforms a nonlinear optimization problem
458   to a quadratic problem that is very efficient to solve. CBFs originates from barrier functions that are
459   originally used in optimization problems (Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2004). However, the CBF tends to
460   make the system conservative (i.e., at the cost of performance) in order to enforce safety, and it is not
461
      scalable to build large models. Our proposed ABNet can address all these limitations.
462   Safety in neural networks. Safety is usually enforced using optimizations. Recently, differentiable
463   optimizations show great potential for learning-based control with safety guarantees (Pereira et al.,
464   2020; Amos et al., 2018; Xiao et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023). The quadratic program (QP) can be
465   employed as a layer in the neural network, i.e., the OptNet (Amos & Kolter, 2017). The OptNet
466   has been used with CBFs in neural networks as a safe filter controls (Pereira et al., 2020), in which
467
      CBFs themselves are not trainable, which can significantly limiting the learning capability. Neural
      network controllers with safety certificate have been learned through verification-in-the-loop training
468
      (Deshmukh et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2021; Ferlez et al., 2020). However, this verification method
469
      cannot ensure to cover the whole state space. CBFs are also used in neural ODEs to equip them with
470   specification guarantees Xiao et al. (2023b). None of these methods are scalable to larger models,
471   and are subject to uncertainty, which the proposed ABNet can address.

473
474   6    C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
475
476   We propose a novel Attention BarrierNet (ABNet) that merge many safety-critical learning models
477   while preserving the safety in this paper. The proposed ABNet is scalable to larger safe learning
478   models, can achieve better performance, and is robust to input noise. We have demonstrated the
479   effectiveness of the model on a series of robot control tasks. Nonetheless, our model still have a few
480   limitations motivating for further research.
481   Limitations. First, all the ABNets have the same safety constraints. We will explore how to
482   merge ABNets with different safety constraints in the future. Second, the ABNet also requires
483   safety specifications that may be unknown in some robot control tasks, we may learn the safety
484   specifications from data Robey et al. (2020), Srinivasan et al. (2020), and this can also be done in
485   conjunction with ABNet. Finally, the model merging is done in the output space, future work will
      further focus on model merging with safety guarantees in the parameter space.
