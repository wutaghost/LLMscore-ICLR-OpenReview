009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Black-box tuning has attracted recent attention due to that the structure or inner
012            parameters of advanced proprietary models are not accessible. Proxy-tuning (Liu
013            et al., 2024) provides a test-time output adjustment for tuning black-box language
014            models. It applies the difference of the output logits before and after tuning a
015            smaller white-box ”proxy” model to improve the black-box model. However, this
016            technique serves only as a decoding-time algorithm, leading to an inconsistency
017            between training and testing which potentially limits overall performance. To ad-
018            dress this problem, we introduce Consistent Proxy Tuning (CPT), a simple yet
019            effective black-box tuning method. Different from Proxy-tuning, CPT addition-
020
               ally exploits the frozen large black-box model and another frozen small white-box
               model, ensuring consistency between training-stage optimization objective and
021
               test-time proxies. This consistency benefits Proxy-tuning and enhances model
022
               performance. Note that our method focuses solely on logit-level computation,
023            which makes it model-agnostic and applicable to any task involving logit classifi-
024            cation. Extensive experimental results demonstrate the superiority of our CPT in
025            both black-box tuning of Large-Language Models (LLMs) and Vision-Language
026            Models (VLMs) across various datasets.

      1 I NTRODUCTION
029
      Although large-scale pretrained models have demonstrated strong generalization capabilities, they
030
      can perform better on specific downstream tasks by fine-tuning. Several parameter-efficient fine-
031   tuning techniques have been developed to fine-tune Large Language Models (LLMs), such as
032   soft prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021), adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019), Low-Rank Adaption
033   (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) and sparse tuning (Zaken et al., 2021). Similar approaches are also ap-
034   plied to fine-tune the pretrained Vision-Language Models (VLMs), including text/visual prompt
035   tuning (Zhou et al., 2022b;a; Bahng et al., 2022), adapter-based tuning (Zhang et al., 2022; Gao
036   et al., 2024), etc. Notice that these fine-tuning methods are usually under the strong assumption
037   that the model architectures are known and model parameters are accessible. However, for privacy
038   or commercial reasons, some advanced proprietary models are closed-source (i.e., black-box mod-
039
      els). For instance, users of GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) can only interact with the model through a
      controlled interface and cannot access the model’s parameters or intermediate embeddings. There-
040
      fore, these white-box optimization methods are infeasible for tuning the black-box models. Some
041
      methods do focus on tuning the black-box models. For example, BBT (Sun et al., 2022b) and BBT-
042   v2 (Sun et al., 2022a) employs gradient-free strategies for fine-tuning of black-box LLMs, while
043   CBBT (Guo et al., 2023) and LFA (Ouali et al., 2023) fine-tune VLMs by adaptive or aligned
044   features. Nevertheless, all these methods require access to features within the model, which is not
045   applicable for more strict black-box scenarios.
046
      Recently, Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024) improves the large black-box model with proxy strategy,
047
      i.e., improves the large black-box model by tuned/untuned smaller white-box models. Specifically,
048   during inference, the difference in logits between a tuned small model and an untuned small model
049   is used as an offset and added to the output logits of the large black-box model to generate the fi-
050   nal prediction. Proxy-tuning is more widely applicable and privacy-preserving compared to other
051   existing methods in that it requires minimum access to black-box models—basic access to output
052   logits will suffice. However, we note that there is an inconsistency between the optimization objec-
053   tive of Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024) and the form of output ensemble during inference, i.e., only
      a small white-box model alone is used for tuning while the ensemble of three models are used for

054
      predictions. Such inconsistency may lead to sub-optimal solutions for the proxy tuning optimization
055   objective, causing bottlenecks in model performance.
056
057   To reconcile the inconsistency in Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024), in this paper we propose Consistent
058
      Proxy Tuning (CPT), a simple yet effective proxy-tuning method. Specifically, during training stage
      of the tunable small white-box model, CPT additionally incorporates the frozen large black-box
059
      model and another frozen small white-box model. Given a training sample, these three models first
060
      compute the logit scores for the input sample respectively. Then, the three sets of logits are ensem-
061   bled by using the same logits calculation formula of a test-time proxy in Proxy-tuning (Liu et al.,
062   2024). Finally, the tunable white-box model is optimized with the loss function computed with the
063   ensemble logits and the ground truth. During inference stage, we follow Liu et al. (2024) to employ
064   the proxy tuning for large black-box model. The whole pipeline of CPT is illustrated in Fig. 1.
065   Compared to vanilla Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024), our CPT ensures consistency between the opti-
066   mization objective during the training of the small white-box model and the test-time inference with
067   proxy. This benefits the Proxy-tuning process and enhances the performance of the model.
068   Note that our method focuses solely on logit-level computation, thus holding the potential of being
069   a plug-and-play improvement for any black-box model fine-tuning tasks which involve logit-level
070   classification. In this paper, we show the effectiveness of CPT by applying it to two representa-
071   tive black-box tuning tasks respectively: black-box tuning of Large-Language Models (LLMs) and
072   black-box tuning of Vision-Language Models (VLMs). For black-box tuning of LLMs, we use a
073   LLAMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023) model with a lightweight architecture (e.g., LLAMA2-7B) to con-
074   sistently proxy-tune the LLAMA2 model with heavier architecture (e.g., LLAMA2-13B) on various
075
      downstream natural language processing tasks. Our CPT outperforms Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024)
      by 2.20% in terms of mean accuracy across seven natural language processing datasets. For black-
076
      box tuning of VLMs, we use a CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) with a lightweight image encoder (e.g.,
077
      ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016)) to consistently proxy-tune the CLIP model with a heavier image en-
078   coder (e.g., ViT-B/16 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020)) on image classification task. Our CPT outperforms
079   Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024) by 1.24% in terms of mean accuracy across eight image classification
080   datasets.
081
      In a nutshell, the main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
082
083        1) We propose Consistent Proxy Tuning (CPT), a simple yet effective proxy-tuning method
084           for black-box model optimization.
085
           2) CPT introduces a frozen black-box large model and a frozen white-box small model into
086
              the training of another tunable white-box small model, which ensures that the optimization
087           objectives during white-box training are consistent with the form of proxy-tuning during
088           inference.
089
           3) CPT can be widely applied to a variety of black-box model fine-tuning tasks. Extensive ex-
090
              periment results of the black-box tuning for VLMs and LLMs on various datasets demon-
091           strate the effectiveness of our CPT.

093   2   R ELATED W ORK
094
      Efficient Fine-tuning. Large pretrained models, which are extensively trained on vast datasets,
095
      demonstrate broad generalization capabilities across various tasks. To further improve the perfor-
096   mance of these models on specific downstream tasks, efficiently fine-tuning methods have been
097   proposed for large pretrain models. In the field of natural language processing, some approaches fo-
098   cus on designing lightweight components to fine-tune pretrained Large Language Models (LLMs).
099   For example, soft prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021) introduces continuous learnable prompts other
100   than hard prompts. Adapter-based method (Houlsby et al., 2019) inserts learnable adapters into
101   Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), thus transferring to downstream tasks while preserving pre-
102   trained knowledge. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) freezes the pretrained model
103   weights and injects trainable rank decomposition matrices into each layer of the Transformer. BitFit
104
      (Zaken et al., 2021) only tunes the bias terms of the model.
105   Many other works also explore how to efficiently fine-tune pretrained VLMs (e.g.CLIP (Radford
106   et al., 2021)). CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022b) designed learnable text prompts to better understand
107   natural language context. Then CoCoOp (Zhou et al., 2022a) further uses images as conditions
      to constrain the optimization of text prompts. Visual prompting (Bahng et al., 2022) also shows


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      that visual prompting is particularly effective for CLIP. Some works (Zhang et al., 2022; Gao et al.,
109   2024) adopts add adapters to the encoders of CLIP, thus to fit different tasks while preserving pretrain
110   knowledge.
111
112
      However, these above strategies require access to the model’s internal parameters (white-box ac-
      cess), which is not feasible for many of today’s sophisticated models. Such infeasibility calls for
113
      new paradigms in fine-tuning black-box pretrained models.
114
115
      Black-Box Tuning. Black-box large pretrained models require a special set of fine-tuning meth-
116
      ods. For large language models, BBT (Sun et al., 2022b) achieves gradient-free optimization by
117
      using covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) (Hansen et al., 2003).However,
118   it requires permission from black-box model to use customized prompt embedding, which is not
119   feasible with some popular language models e.g., GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023). BBT-v2 (Sun et al.,
120   2022a) injects learnable prompt into layers of the LLM, which is also not applicable for language
121   model APIs. BDPL (Diao et al., 2022) investigates the possibilities of using discrete prompt to
122   help LLMs understand the task better. Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024) considers training smaller
123   white-box models as proxy instead, and use the fine-tuned white-box experts to enhance black-box
124   LLMs. This approach has shown both effectiveness and promise, but it overlooks the inconsistency
125   between the training objective of small model and the joint test-time ensemble of large black-box
126
      model and smaller proxy models. Zhang et al. (2020) also uses small models to indirectly fine-tune
      large models, but they need access to the parameters of intermediate layers, which is not suitable for
127
      scenarios where the parameters of the model cannot be accessed.
128
129   For vision-language models, BlackVIP (Oh et al., 2023) optimizes the coordinator which generates
130   visual prompts by zeroth-order optimization. However, the improvement in performance is limited.
131   Linear Feature Alignment (LFA) (Ouali et al., 2023) optimizes a projection layer to enhance the
132
      alignment between pre-computed image features and class prototypes. CBBT (Guo et al., 2023)
      optimizes textual prompt and feature output adaptation collaboratively. These two methods interact
133
      with the black-box models at a feature level and requires access to output features, which leaves a
134
      potential risk of being vulnerable to attacks e.g.membership inference attacks (MIA) (Carlini et al.,
135   2022). We focus on a more restrict black-model setting where only output logits other than output
136   features of a model is accessible.
137
138   Logits Arithmetic. Recently, some methods (Dou et al., 2019) have demonstrated the capability
139   of logits ensembling from multiple models in enhancing model performance. For example, Dou
140   et al. (2019) assembles multiple logits from models pretrained on different domains to achieve do-
141   main adaptation. DExperts (Liu et al., 2021) uses the difference in logits output between a toxic
142   model and a non-toxic model to assist language models in language detoxification. This paper also
143   explores the use of proxy (Liu et al., 2024) to “fine-tune” Large Language Models (LLMs) during
144   the inference stage. Contrastive Decoding (CD) (Li et al., 2022) leverages the differences in log-
145   likelihood between expert and amateur language models (LMs) of varying sizes by selecting tokens
146
      that maximize this discrepancy. Some subsequent studies have also explored the effects of ensem-
      bling output logits from different layers of models (Gera et al., 2023; Chuang et al., 2023) or the
147
      effects of output logits from varying inputs (Pei et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023). This paper proposes a
148
      method that enhances a large black-box model using the output from a small white-box model and
149   an untuned one. Unlike Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024), which overlooks the consistency between
150   proxy-independent training and proxy-dependent testing and results in suboptimal outcomes, our
151   method employs ensembled output logits from both black-box and white-box models as optimiza-
152   tion objectives. This approach ensures consistency in proxy techniques, thereby enhancing model
153   performance.

      3.2 C ONSISTENT P ROXY T UNING (CPT)
199
      In this paper, we aim to bridge the inconsistency between the use of test-time proxies and the separate
200   training process small white-box model. To this end, we propose Consistent Proxy Tuning (CPT)
201   method, which is illustrated in Fig. 1 (c). In contrast to the vanilla Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024),
202   CPT additionally incorporates frozen Ms (·; θsp ) and Ml (·; θlp ) into the fine-tuning process of the
203   small white-box model, and the optimization objective is improved to compute the loss function
204   based on the ensemble of the outputs from the three models and the ground truth. Formally, the
205   optimization objective is modified as follows:
206               θst = arg min E(x,y)∼D [L(Ms (x; θs ) + αtrain (Ml (x; θlp ) − Ms (x; θsp )), y)],                                                                       (3)
207                           θs
208   where αtrain is the coefficient that controls the impact of the offset obtained from Ml (x; θlp ) −
209   Ms (x; θsp ) on the training of Ms (; θs ). Correspondingly, we introduce another coefficient αtest to
210   Eqn. 2 during inference stage:
211

214   tent form of ensembling output logits from three models during both training and inference stages.
215   While when αtrain = 0 in Eqn. 3 and αtest = 1 in Eqn. 4, our CPT will degenerate into the “in-
      consistent” vanilla Proxy-tuning, i.e., Eqn. 1 and Eqn. 2. In fact, Proxy-tuning can be considered

      as a special case of our CPT. In Sec. 4.3, we will explore how the combinations of different αtrain
217   and αtest impact model performance. Note that our method focuses solely on the computation be-
218   tween the output logits of the model. Therefore, CPT can be widely applicable to various black-box
219   model fine-tuning tasks which involve logit-level classification, such as image classification, image
220   segmentation (pixel-level classification), text generation (in-vocabulary classification), etc. Further-
221   more, the large black-box model Ml and the smaller model Ms are not required to be from the
222   same model family. They only require to share the same output space, e.g., the same classification

226   3.3 E XTENDING CPT TO V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODEL
227   To demonstrate that our method can be applied to other black-box model fine-tuning tasks involv-
228   ing logit-level classification, in this section we extend CPT to black-box Vision-Language Model
229   (VLM) fine-tuning. VLMs have shown impressive capabilities across a diverse array of applica-
230   tions. Here, we mainly focus on applying CPT to black-box tuning for CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)
231   on downstream image classification tasks. CLIP achieves image classification by calculating the
232
      similarity between image embeddings and different text embeddings. Formally, CLIP employs an
      image encoder Eimg (·|θimg ) and a text encoder Etxt (·|θtxt ), which are jointly pretrained with a
233
      vast number of image-text pairs using a contrastive learning approach. Given an input image x and
234
      multiple tokenized descriptive texts T = {t1 , t2 , . . . , tC } corresponding to C classes, the image
235   encoder and text encoder extract image embedding f , and text embeddings {gc }C        c=1 respectively,
236   where f = Eimg (x|θimg ) and gc = Etxt (tc |θtxt ). Then, the predicted logit score of class c is
237   computed by ⟨∥f ∥2 , ∥gc ∥2 ⟩, where ∥ · ∥2 denotes the L2 -normalization, and ⟨·, ·⟩ denotes the cosine
238   similarity of two embeddings. In the context of our CPT, we use a single symbol M∗ (·|θ∗ ) (∗ can be
239   s or l) to briefly represent both Eimg (·|θimg ) and Etxt (·|θtxt ) of CLIP, where θ∗ represents the pa-
240   rameters of both θimg and θtxt . We use M∗ (x, T|θ∗ ) to represent the classification logits predicted
241   by the CLIP model for given x and T. Therefore, the optimization objective of CPT for fine-tuning
242   the black-box VLM can be expressed as:
243

247   In practice, we use the CLIP with a heavy image encoder (e.g., ViT-B/16 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020))
248   as the large black box model, and an image encoder with a lighter image encoder (e.g., ResNet-
249   50 (He et al., 2016)) as the small white box model. During the training stage, we use templates like
250   “a photo of a [CLS]”, where [CLS] represents a certain class name, as inputs for the text encoder.
251   Regarding the fine-tuning strategy of the small white-box model, we fine-tune all parameters of its
252   image encoder and text encoder. In contrast to existing black-box fine-tuning methods for VLMs,
253   which require access to image and text embeddings (Ouali et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023), our method
254   only requires access to cosine similarities (i.e., output logits). This illustrates that our method can
255
      be applied to stricter black box model fine-tuning scenarios, where only logits are accessible.

      Effect of varied αtrain and αtest . αtrain in Eqn. 3 and αtest in Eqn. 4 are hyper-parameters of
      our CPT. They determines the extent to which the offset calculated by Ml (x; θlp ) − Ms (x; θsp )
448
      affects the optimization objective and the proxy process. Intuitively, choosing a large αtrain will
449
      amplify the impact of the difference between the outputs of the frozen large model and the small
450   model on the optimization objective, while choosing a smaller one reduces this impact. Similarly,
451   choosing different values of αtest will affect the final performance of the proxied model. Moreover,
452   the relative values of αtrain and αtest will affect the consistency between the training objective and
453   the proxy process during testing. Specifically, the closer these two coefficients are, the stronger
454   the consistency between the training objective and the proxy process during testing; conversely, the
455   further apart they are, the weaker the consistency.
456   In all main experiments, both of these two coefficients are set to 1 to keep a strict consistency. Here,
457   we further explore the impact of varied αtrain and αtest on performance of CPT. Fig. 2 shows the
458   performance of CPT under varied αtrain and αtest on ARC-challenge, Stanford Cars (c), Oxford-
459   IIIT Pets (d) and the results of their average (a). Each cell in this matrix represents the accuracy
460   under a specific set of αtrain and αtest . From Fig. 2, we can obtain the following observations: 1)
461   The areas where the model performs well are concentrated near the main diagonal of the matrix,
462   e.g., marked with a yellow elliptical curve in Fig. 2 (a). When αtrain and αtest are relatively close,
463   the model tends to perform better; conversely, when they are further apart, the model’s performance
464
      tends to decline. This result indicates that ensuring the consistency between αtrain and αtest will
      be beneficial to the model’s performance. This also indirectly supports our proposal in this paper
465
      for ”ensuring the consistency between the optimization objectives and the proxy during testing can
466
      benefit for fine-tuning.” In specific datasets, such as ARC-challenge and Stanford Cars, we can
467   also observe similar conclusions. Due to the limitations of our hyperparameter selection range, this
468   phenomenon is not observed in Fig. 2 (d). In Fig. 3 of supplemental material, we show the results
469   under a wider range of hyperparameters, where the phenomenon is consistent with the other two
470   datasets. 2) From the results of each dataset, it can be seen that when αtrain and αtest are close
471   but both are relatively small, the performance of CPT is poor. This phenomenon may suggest that
472   we should choose a relatively larger alpha when performing CPT. From the average results (2 (a)),
473   when αtrain and αtest are close and their values are between 1.2 and 2.0, the model performs well.
474   We suggest choosing these two hyperparameters from the above range to perform CPT, for example,
475
      selecting αtrain = αtest = 1.2.

516
      Increase in computational resources. While CPT does not necessarily increase computational
517   expenses compared to directly tuning the black-box model during training, it surely increases com-
518   putational expenses at inference stage. Facing the same problem with Proxy-tuning (Liu et al.,
519   2024), i.e., the time of inference increases because multiple models compute output logits jointly.
520   Also, compared to the normal inference of a single black-box model, Proxy-tuning and CPT require
521   more GPU memory to deploy the proxy models. Although we can implement CPT effectively by
522   computing classification logits of pretrained models on train/test dataset and storing them for further
523   use, the increase in computational expenses in inevitable during inference on new data.

530   7   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper we proposed a simple yet effective black-box model tuning method named Consistent
533
      Proxy Tuning (CPT). We notice that vanilla Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024) trains the white-box
534   small model independently but uses an ensemble of the white-box and black-box models for in-
535   ference. This inconsistency between the training objective and inference can lead to the proxy
536   process being sub-optimal. In contrast, our CPT introduces the frozen black/white models into the
537   fine-tuning process of the small model, thereby ensuring consistency between training-stage opti-
538   mization objective and test-time proxy process. Our CPT can be plug-and-played for any black-box
539   model fine-tuning tasks which involve logit-level classification. Extensive experiment results of the
      black-box tuning for VLMs and LLMs on many datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of our CPT.

786   large black-box model θlp and another small white-box θsp are only responsible for providing output

793   and model is trained for 300 epochs. For all the experiments, we set αtrain = αtest = 1 by default.

      D    B ROADER I MPACTS
797
798
      Positive Impact: Fairness in AI. CPT aims to tune a black-box model with smaller ”proxies” con-
      sistently, but it can also serve as a fine-tuning method for smaller models guided by large pretrained
799
      models. Large-scale pretraining is more often on general knowledge than domain-specific knowl-
800
      edge, so downstream tasks barely benefit from large-scale pretrained models without computation-
801   ally expensive fine-tuning. It is even less possible when large pretrained model is only accessible as
802   black-boxes. CPT fills the gap by joining the strength of large-scale general knowledge pretraining
803   and small-scale task-specific fine-tuning. From this perspective, CPT brings positive social impact
804   in that it finds a way of using general pretrain model to elevate task-specific fine-tuning of smaller
805   models. It is of great significance for individuals, organizations and regions without resources to
806   fine-tune large pretrain models for their own well-being, thus improving fairness of AI.
807
808   Negative Impact: Potential Misuse. Black-box language models, compared with white-box ones,
809   are more difficult to tune for specific tasks. While this limits the application of black-box language
      models, it also prevents them from being misused to generate malignant content. However, the way
