009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011                Low-quality or scarce data has posed significant challenges for training deep neural
012                networks in practice. While classical data augmentation cannot contribute very
013                different new data, diffusion models opens up a new door to build self-evolving AI
014                by generating high-quality and diverse synthetic data through text-guided prompts.
015
                   However, text-only guidance cannot control synthetic images’ proximity to the
                   original images, resulting in out-of-distribution data detrimental to the model
016
                   performance. To overcome the limitation, we study image guidance to achieve
017
                   a spectrum of interpolations between synthetic and real images. With stronger
018                image guidance, the generated images are similar to the training data but hard
019                to learn. While with weaker image guidance, the synthetic images will be easier
020                for model but contribute to a larger distribution gap with the original data. The
021                generated full spectrum of data enables us to build a novel “Diffusion CurricuLum
022                (DisCL)”. DisCL adjusts the image guidance level of image synthesis for each
023                training stage: It identifies and focuses on hard samples for the model and assesses
024                the most effective guidance level of synthetic images to improve hard data learning.
025                We apply DisCL to two challenging tasks: long-tail (LT) classification and learning
026
                   from low-quality data. It focuses on lower-guidance images of high-quality to learn
                   prototypical features as a warm-up of learning higher-guidance images that might
027
                   be weak on diversity or quality. Extensive experiments showcase a gain of 2.7%
028
                   and 2.1% in OOD and ID macro-accuracy when applying DisCL to iWildCam
029                dataset. On ImageNet-LT, DisCL improves the base model’s tail-class accuracy
030                from 4.4% to 23.64% and leads to a 4.02% improvement in all-class accuracy. 1 .

033   1       I NTRODUCTION
034
      While existing machine learning approaches can train representation or discriminative models
035   with promising generalization performance, their success highly relies on the quality and quantity
036   of the training data. However, in enormous practical scenarios, the data are collected from real
037   environments so neither the quality nor the quantity can always be guaranteed. For example, it is
038   difficult to control the light conditions, weather, motion blur, or the position of objects in the scenes
039   captured by trail/animal cameras, traffic cameras, motion cameras, or robot cameras. Likewise, it
040   is also difficult to keep different classes in the collected data balanced so the model may perform
041   much poorer on tail classes with scarce data. On the other hand, the low-quality/quantity of data also
042   makes the model more prone to the gap between the test and training distributions, thereby posing
043
      an out-of-distribution challenge. In many cases, such “hard” training data hinders effective learning,
      introduces biases or outliers, and may even impact the learning of other data.
044
045   Data augmentation and synthesis have been studied to address the challenges of hard real data. By
046   applying pre-defined transformations (Ahn et al., 2023) to data in scarce classes or modifying their
047   backgrounds (Beery et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2022), data augmentation helps learn representations
048   robust to these task-irrelevant variations. While the augmented data may lack sufficient diversity or
049
      non-trivial difference to the original data, the recent text-to-image generative models such as GAN
      or Stable Diffusion enable more sophisticated data synthesis (Dunlap et al., 2024) of diverse higher-
050
      quality samples, while the text prompts retain the task-related features. Despite these advancements,
051
      existing methods (Han et al., 2024) still struggle to train robust and reliable models or representa-
052   tions for hard classes. Although text-to-image synthesis improves the data quality and quantity, the

      synthetic data are solely controlled by text prompts but lack sufficient visual similarity to the original
055   image, which leads to a distribution gap to the original data and hurts the generalization performance.
056
057   To maximize the merits of synthetic data for learning hard data in real applications and address the
058
      syn-to-real gap, we harness the image guidance in diffusion models to generate a full spectrum of
      interpolations between synthetic data (i.e., generated only from text prompts) and real data (i.e.,
059
      original images that may suffer from low-quality and low-quantity). The synthetic data at each level of
060
      interpolation are generated under the weighted guidance of both the text prompt (e.g., the class name)
061   and the real images. While stronger image guidance preserves visual similarities to the original image,
062   for low-quality or low-quantity data, weaker image guidance could lead to high-quality, diverse, and
063   potentially easier (e.g., with prototypical features) data. Hence, the syn-to-real interpolations create a
064   novel space of synthetic data to design a generative curriculum that can adjust the quality, diversity,
065   and/or difficulty of data for different training stages, by selecting the guidance level according to a
066   pre-defined schedule or training dynamics.
067   In this paper, we develop novel generative curriculum learning approaches for two types of challenging
068   applications with hard real images: long-tail classification, and learning from low-quality images. In
069   long-tail classification, learning the tail classes’ features is challenging due to their data deficiency
070   and the lack of diversity compared to “head classes”. To address this challenge, we propose a
071   curriculum that first learns synthetic images with lower image guidance for tail classes since they
072   enhance the diversity and quantity of the original data. The curriculum then gradually increases
073   the guidance level and learns synthetic images closer to the original images, thereby progressively
074   bridging the syn-to-real gap. In learning from low-quality data, the primary challenge is to capture the
075
      critical features of the target classes, which is hard due to intricate background, occlusion, or motion
      blur in the original images. In contrast, images generated with lower image guidance usually contain
076
      prototypical features easier to learn. That being said, an overly high or low guidance level may
077
      enlarge the domain gap between the training data and the target (in-distribution or out-of-distribution)
078   data. To avoid negative transfer caused by the domain gap and to maximize the merits of synthetic
079   data, we develop an adaptive curriculum that selects the guidance level of synthetic data leading to
080   the greatest progress of each training stage.
081
      We examine two DisCL curricula on benchmark datasets, ImageNet-LT (Liu et al., 2019) and WILD-
082
      iWildCam (Beery et al., 2021), for long-tail classification and learning from low-quality images,
083
      respectively. Our DisCL curricula improve OOD and ID accuracy by 2.7% and 2.1% respectively on
084   iWildCam. On ImageNet-LT, DisCL improves the minority classes’ accuracy by 19.24% and leads to
085   a 4.02% improvement in the overall accuracy. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
086
087   • We harness image guidance in diffusion models to create a spectrum of synthetic-to-real data for
088     each sample that can be used to design effective training curricula addressing hard data learning.
089   • We propose the “Diffusion CurricuLum (DisCL)” paradigm that selects synthetic data of different
090     guidance levels for the needs of each training stage. We propose two novel DisCL curricula to
091     address two important applications, long-tail classification and learning from low-quality data.
092   • We examine the two DisCL curricula on challenging datasets and demonstrate that DisCL signifi-
093     cantly boosts the performance of existing image classifiers especially on the hard data.

100

101

107   Azizi et al. (2023) have finetuned the Imagen model using ImageNet data to enhance the alignment
      of synthetic data with their classes, while improving the sample diversity. In this work, we utilize

145   by diffusion models, with broader applications in learning from long-tail or low-quality data.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
148
149   We propose diffusion curriculum (DisCL) to “close the distribution gap between original data and the
150   target data distribution”. DisCL comprises two phases: (Phase 1) Synthetic-to-Real Data Generation
151   that generates a syn-to-real spectrum of interpolated data for hard samples, and (Phase 2) Generative
152   Curriculum learning based on the synthetic data from Phase 1. The two phases are illustrated in Fig. 1.
153

155   Hard Sample Identification We first identify the difficult samples where the model struggles to
156   extract helpful features for target classification. The difficulty estimation can be task-specific. For
157   instance, in long-tail classification with scarce data, the difficulty of each sample depends on whether
158
      it belongs to tail classes. For tasks with low-quality data, we can utilize the loss or confidence on the
      ground-truth class to measure the difficulty. These samples are marked as “hard samples” within the
159
      training set (see Fig. 1), to highlight their role in the model’s learning process.

      3.2       G ENERATIVE C URRICULUM L EARNING WITH S YNTHETIC DATA
235
236   With the full spectrum of syn-to-real generated data, we achieve a smooth transition from images
237   of prototypical features and high diversity to task-specific features with high resemblance to real
238   images. This enables us to design a curriculum selecting data with according to their diversity and
239
      feature types for different training stages. With a curriculum of rich synthetic data, we can enhance
      the model’s performance in challenging and diverse cases which are otherwise difficult to using only
240
      the real data. On the other hand, it also allow us to control the distribution gap to the original data.
241
242   We apply our method to two challenging applications in the following sections: long-tail classification
243   and learning from low-quality data. In long-tail classification, the scarcity of data in minority/tail
244   classes makes it difficult for models to extract useful features for these classes, leading to poor
245
      generalization on balanced test set. To address this, we develop a curriculum strategy that initially
      exposes the model to diverse synthetic samples of tail classes, and then progressively focuses on
246
      task-specific features. This helps mitigate distribution differences between synthetic and real data.
247
      In learning from low-quality data, the poor quality of data limits the model’s ability to detect and
248   extract critical visual features. By employing an adaptive curriculum of synthetic data, we can warm
249   up the model training by learning from varying levels of prototypical features, gradually aiding the
250   model in extracting features useful for out-of-domain generalization.

255   4.1       L ONG -TAIL (LT) C LASSIFICATION
256
      Synthetic Data Generation For synthetic data generation, we follow a standard split of tail classes
257   in the studied dataset. Given the real tail-class samples and the associated text prompts, we generate
258   a full spectrum of synthetic data by techniques in §3.1. To mitigate the imbalance among classes,
259   the key is to increase data diversity and quantity for tail classes. We employ a diverse set of textual
260   prompts to achieve the goal3 .
261
262   Generative Curriculum The generated spectrum of synthetic data provides varying degrees of
263   data diversity: the images generated with text-only guidance display the highest diversity but may
264   suffer from visual discrepancies to the original images, resulting in a distribution gap that may
265
      undermine model performance. To bridge the gap, we progressively shift the synthetic data to a task-
      specific distribution closer to the original images. This yields a non-adaptive “Diverse-to-Specific”
266
      curriculum that starts with synthetic data with a lower guidance scale (λ → 0) and gradually moves
267
      toward data of a higher guidance scale (λ → 1).

      4.2       L EARNING FROM L OW- QUALITY DATA
271
272
      The data collected in real-world scenarios may suffer from low qualities, such as obscurity in images
      from traffic, motion, or wildlife observation cameras. We investigate wildlife observation as an
273
      example application of DisCL to enable effective learning under such challenging scenarios.
274
275   Synthetic Data Generation For low-quality images from camera traps, we aim to generate simpler
276   images containing more prototypical features of the animals that can warm up the training and
277
      generalize to more challenging cases. We first identify hard samples based on the ground-truth class
      probability by a pretrained classifier: a lower probability indicates more difficulty. We vary the image
278
      guidance scale to generate a full spectrum of synthetic data for these hard samples, ranging from
279
      prototypical to in-the-wild images. The class information is used in the text prompts4 to steer the
280   diffusion model to generate images relevant to the animals and their wild environment.
281
      Generative Curriculum Training on text-only synthetic data hinders performance due to their
282
      distribution gap to the real data and their differences in hardness. A flexible curriculum strategy
283
      that integrates both text-only synthetic and real images during training can mitigate this gap. Unlike
284   long-tail classification, various features in the hard samples of low-quality data are not prototypical
285   or generalizable. Synthetic data with higher image guidance can mitigate the issue to some extent but
286   may remain difficult for models to learn from. In contrast, synthetic data with lower image guidance
287   are more prototypical and easier but they are out-of-distribution (OOD) of the real images.
288
      A predefined curriculum of image guidance may introduce OOD features at the early stage, causing
289
      a distribution shift, or overemphasizing hard and outlier features, downplaying the prototypical
290   patterns. DoCL (Zhou et al., 2021a) proposed an adaptive curriculum that selects real data for each
291   training stage that can achieve the greatest progress on the original distribution. The curriculum
292   aims to optimize the training dynamics. Inspired by DoCL, we propose an adaptive curriculum
293   to dynamically select the guidance level that helps the model achieve the best improvement on the
294   real data distribution. This approach effectively advances the model from learning simple features to
295   mastering more complex and difficult scenarios.

301   experiments with ImageNet-LT (IN-LT) dataset (Liu et al., 2019). This dataset includes 1000
302   classes, with class cardinality ranging from 5 to 1,280. To assess the robustness of DisCL more
303   comprehensively, we conduct experiments on two additional datasets: a synthetically imbalanced
304   dataset, CIFAR100-LT (Cao et al., 2019), and a real-world benchmark, iNaturalist2018 (Van Horn
305   et al., 2018). CIFAR100-LT is provided with imbalanced classes by synthetically sampling the
306
      training data with multiple imbalance ratios {100, 50}. iNaturalist2018 dataset represents a naturally

307
      accuracy and the accuracy across three categories of classes: many (most frequent), medium, and few
308
      (least frequent, tail) classes on the standard balanced test sets of three datasets. For synthetic data
309   generation, we use DDIM (Song et al., 2020) as our noise scheduler. For training, following Ahn

      6.2   E FFECT OF C URRICULUM L EARNING S TRATEGY
458
459   Long Tail Classification We compare the impact of our Diverse to Specific curriculum strategy
460
      tailored for IN-LT task against other strategies, notably All-Level Guidance which employ no curricu-
      lum and uses all synthetic data. The Diverse to Specific demonstrate a higher few-class accuracy with
461
      a margin of 4.47%, see Fig. 3b. We then compare it with a reverse strategy Specific-to-Diverse, and
462
      found the latter one to be worse. The reverse strategy can overfit model to real distribution early on,
463   increasing the gap between real and synthetic data; hence, later-stage training on the data with larger
464   distribution gap can decrease models’ few-class accuracy. For IN-LT, we also try Adaptive strategy
465   (mainly developed for learning from low-quality data), in which strategy’s progression is based on a
466   validation set, comprising few tail images sampled from each guidance scale and few original images.
467   But, validation set is scarce interms of tail samples, which renders it ineffective for identification of
468   truly useful guidance. Hence, this strategy ranks as the least effective for LT task.
469   Learning from Low Quality Data For iWildCam task, we study the effect of our designed Adaptive
470   strategy, catering to the challenge of learning from low quality data. As shown in Fig. 3d, for this task,
471   Adaptive surpasses the All-Level Guidance with a clear margin, underscoring the benefit of using
472   progressive curriculum over using all synthetic data. Further comparisons with the Non-Adaptive
473   curricula including Easy-to-Hard and Random, show an impactful increase in OOD F1, while using
474   our Adaptive.
475   These findings highlight how the structured data selection used in Diverse-to-Specific, is more
476   effective in directing model’s focus on scarce data (classes), however, when dealing with real-world
477   low-quality data, an Adaptive strategy is more successful in adjusting to models’ needs by adaptively
478   selecting the suited data.

513   7    C ONCLUSION
514
515   In this paper, we introduce DisCL, a novel paradigm designed to enhance model performance when
516   dealing with low-quality or scarce data. DisCL effectively bridges the distribution gap between
517   original and target data using a spectrum of synthetic data, particularly for challenging samples. Our
518
      method utilizes image guidance in diffusion models to generate a comprehensive range of interpolated
      data from synthetic to real. Additionally, we design specific curricula to maximize the benefits of
519
      synthetic data for learning hard samples and closing the gap between synthetic and real data. The
520
      efficacy of DisCL is demonstrated through its significant and robust performance improvements in
521   long-tail classification and learning from low-quality data, across various base model settings. Our
522   analyses reveal that the interpolation of synthetic-to-real data, the selection of guidance intervals, and
523   the proposed curriculum strategy are all essential components contributing to these gains.
524
      Despite the promising results, the performance of DisCL is influenced by certain limitations. The
525
      quality of the generated data spectrum is dependent on the capabilities of the diffusion model
526
      and the visual-text alignment ability of filtering models. These dependencies constrain the overall
527   performance of DisCL. Additionally, the current approach to generate text prompts for long-tail
528   classification relies solely on category names derived from large language models (LLMs). To better
529   align with the real data distribution and to reduce the gap between synthetic and real data, future
530   works could focus on generating text prompts from image captions. Lastly, discrepancies in the
531   position and size of class objects between real and synthetic images can widen the distribution gap.
532   Addressing this issue may involve detecting objects and performing crop operations on real images or
533   using detailed prompts to control these properties in synthetic data. These areas present opportunities
534   for further research and improvement.

796
      Selection of Text prompts Following previous work (Clark & Jaini, 2024; Trabucco et al., 2023),
797
      we first define prompts for each class using the template "a photo of <class>". However, the
798
      classnames in iWildCam comprises of scientific names, which are usually unseen/unknown concepts
799   to the diffusion text encoder. For example, "canis lupus" is the class name for "wolf" animal. To
800   address this, we replace the scientific names with their common names and add a postfix "in the wild"
801   in the prompt to drive the generation of wild images. The final text prompt we use is "a photo of
802   <common name of class> in the wild".
803
      Selection of Images Guidance Levels Based on the generated data with multiple image guidance
804   scales, we search for effective image guidance scales for this task using CLIP cosine similarity
805   scores between synthetic image embeddings and real image embeddings. As shown in Fig. 5a, as
806   the difference between real images and synthetic images increases, the cosine similarity between
807   image embeddings decreases from λ = 1 to λ = 0.3. However, when the image guidance continues
808   to decrease to λ = 0, the cosine similarity score increases slightly. With low image guidance scales,
809   the diffusion model tends to generate images that heavily rely on text information, maintaining only
      global information (such as the color of the image background) in the synthetic data for some images.

950   A.3.1 L ONG -TAIL C LASSIFICATION WITH N ON -A DAPTIVE S TRATEGY
951   For long-tail classification, we propose a non-adaptive curriculum learning strategy that starts with
952   the lowest guidance and progressively increases to the highest guidance within the defined interval
953   Λ. We employ a linear scheduler to adjust the guidance levels during training, allowing the model
954   to train with data from various guidance levels for equal durations. Furthermore, the test set of
955   ImageNet-LT is in-distribution to its training data; unlike the training data, it is a class-balanced set.
956   To mitigate the potential negative effects of the distribution gap between synthetic and real data, all
957
      the hard tail samples from original data are involved into training at all times. Furthermore, with
      DisCL, number of samples for tail classes increases along with the introduction of synthetic data at
958
      each stage, however the ratio of tail-to-nontail samples is still very skewed. To preserve a constant
959
      imbalance-ratio throughout all training stages and experiments, we undersample the non-tail samples
960   at "each stage" so that ratio of tail-samples to non-tail samples matches the proportion of tail classes
961   to non-tail classes present in the original data (13.6%).
962
963
      All experiments are conducted based on this proportion setting. Complete strategy details are covered
      in Algorithm 1.

1179      A.7 S OCIETAL I MPACT
1180      Our proposed method is beneficial for diverse fields, where inadequate quantity and low quality of
1181      data is common, e.g. medical domain. The synthetic data generation, as followed by DisCL approach
1182      can reduce the need for extensive data collection, therefore mitigating the ethical concerns related
1183      to data-privacy. Overall, our method DisCL can democratize the access of effectively training ML
1184      models in the low-resource environments. However, by leveraging the pretrained generative models,
1185      the potential biases of models can perpetuate into the synthetic data and eventually affect the sensitive
1186      real-world applications consuming this data, such as medical diagnosis, law enforcement etc.
