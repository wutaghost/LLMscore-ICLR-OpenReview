009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph generation is a fundamental task that has been extensively studied in social,
012            technological, and scientific analysis. For modeling the dynamic graph evolu-
013            tion process, traditional rule-based methods struggle to capture community struc-
014            tures within graphs, while deep learning methods only focus on fitting training
015            graphs. This limits existing graph generators to producing graphs that adhere
016
               to predefined rules or closely resemble training datasets, achieving poor perfor-
               mance in dynamic graph generation. Given that graphs are abstract representations
017
               arising from pairwise interactions in human activities, a realistic simulation of
018
               human-wise interaction could provide deeper insights into the graph evolution
019            mechanism. With the increasing recognition of large language models (LLMs)
020            in simulating human behavior, we introduce GraphAgent-Generator (GAG), a
021            novel simulation-based framework for dynamic text-attributed graph generation.
022            Without training or fine-tuning process of LLM, our framework effectively repli-
023            cates seven macro-level structural characteristics in established network science
024            theories while surpassing existing baselines in graph expansion tasks by 11% on
025            specific evaluation metrics. Through node classification task, we validate GAG
026            effectively captures the intricate text-structure correlations in graph generation.
027
               Furthermore, GAG supports generating graphs with up to nearly 100,000 nodes or
               10 million edges through large-scale LLM-based agent simulation with parallel ac-
028
               celeration, achieving a minimum speed-up of 90.4%. The source code is available
029
               at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/GraphAgent-2206.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Graphs are mathematical structures representing pairwise interactions between entities through
035   nodes and edges, serving as a fundamental concept in network science. They are widely used to
036   model interaction behaviors across various domains, including social analysis (Fan et al., 2019),
037   parallel computing (Hendrickson & Kolda, 2000), and program synthesis (Nguyen et al., 2012).
038   A longstanding task in network science is graph generation. Given an observed graph dataset,
039
      researchers extract the underlying generative mechanisms to create models that can scale these
      observations into larger graphs. Network science theories constitute macro properties such as power-
040
      law degree distribution (Clauset et al., 2009). In contrast, micro properties include graph structure
041
      metrics like degree distribution and clustering coefficient (Martinkus et al., 2022). By comparing the
042   macro and micro properties of the generated and real-world graphs, researchers gain deeper insights
043   into graph evolution mechanisms.
044
045
      Existing graph generation methods can be categorized into two types: (1) Rule-based methods, which
      rely on preset rules to generate graphs (Erdos et al., 1960; Barabási & Albert, 1999). These methods
046
      are designed to capture specific macro properties observed in real-world networks. However, the need
047
      for tailored models to capture each property complicates the integration of these methods into a unified
048   framework. Additionally, they fall short in capturing micro-level properties in dynamic graph genera-
049   tion (Bergmeister et al., 2024). (2) Deep learning-based methods, which leverage self-supervised
050   learning to capture graph structures. These methods mainly include auto-regressive methods (You
051   et al., 2018) and one-shot methods (Vignac et al., 2023; Bergmeister et al., 2024; Simonovsky &
052   Komodakis, 2018). While these techniques excel in fitting micro properties of observed graphs, they
053   face challenges when generating larger graphs beyond the size of the observed dataset (Bergmeister
      et al., 2024) and struggle to capture macro properties in dynamic graph generation.

054
      The limitations of previous methods stem from their attempts to use a single model to represent
055   all forms of entity-wise interaction process. Instead of merely adhering to preset rules or fitting
056   training data, a desirable graph generator understands how graphs are formed to generate structures
057   that align with the underlying physical interaction process. For instance, the dynamics of human
058   social interactions significantly influence the social network evolution Fowler & Christakis (2010).
059   Fortunately, the emergence of LLMs like LLaMA (AI@Meta, 2024) and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) has
060   opened new avenues for graph generation. With their ability for human-like reasoning, LLM-based
061   agents can effectively simulate complex interaction processes in human activities (Park et al., 2023).
062   In this work, we introduce GraphAgent-Generator (GAG), a novel human behavior simulation-
063   based framework via LLM-based agents for graph generation. We propose the S-RAG algorithm to
064   model the human interaction process with carefully designed LLM-based agents for human behavior
065   simulation. Through continuous simulations, we can extract diverse graphs as abstract representations
066   of collected interaction data. Furthermore, we propose N-ACTOR to accelerate the simulation process
067   by parallel processing. Through experiments conducted in GAG, our main contributions are:
068   (1) Graphs of Real-World Network Structures: The generated graphs exhibit seven essential
069   structural characteristics observed in real-world networks, including power-law degree distribution,
070   small-world, shrinking diameter and etc. Specifically, GAG surpasses the best-performing baseline
071   by 11% on specific evaluation metrics for graph expansion tasks.
072
      (2) Interpretability in Graph Generation: To our knowledge, GAG is the first framework to
073   generate text-rich data for modeling graph evolution, offering clear process interpretability. In the
074   context of node classification using Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), the graph generated by GAG
075   retains accuracy comparable to that of the real-world graph. This demonstrates the capability of GAG
076   to produce effective text features for graphs.
077
      (3) Graph Generation via Large-Scale LLM-based Agent Simulation: The framework supports
078
      the generation of graphs across ten distinct types, accommodating up to 10 million edges or nearly
079   100,000 nodes through simulations with up to nearly 100,000 LLM-based agents. Additionally,
080   the N-ACTOR component accelerates the simulation process through parallel computing with a
081   minimum speed-up of 90.4%.

086   Graph Generation As an extensively explored foundational task, existing graph generation methods
087   mainly fall into two categories: (1) Rule-based methods, which gradually add nodes based on
088   random (Erdos et al., 1960) or preferential attachment (Barabási & Albert, 1999) rules; Though
089   (Barabási & Albert, 1999) successfully models power-law degree distribution, they struggle to capture
090   the community structures prevalent in real-world networks (You et al., 2018). (2) Deep Learning
091   based methods: In recent years, deep learning offer a new approach, aiming to capture the complex
092   and diverse structures of real-world networks, which mainly fall into two categories: Autoregressive
093
      methods (You et al., 2018; Dai et al., 2020; Bergmeister et al., 2024) predict edges incrementally for
      each new node, while one-shot methods (Simonovsky & Komodakis, 2018; De Cao & Kipf, 2018;
094
      Liu et al., 2019; Vignac et al., 2023) generate entire graphs in a single step. However, these methods
095
      require large-scale training data and struggle to generate graphs outside the training distribution.
096   Although some progress is made with extrapolating to out-of-distribution graphs (Bergmeister et al.,
097   2024; Limnios et al., 2023), the maximum size of graph is limited to thousands of nodes.
098
099
      LLM-based Human Behavior Simulation With LLMs demonstrating advanced capabilities in
      human-like responses and autonomous planning (Gao et al., 2023), they are increasingly recognized
100
      as a new paradigm for simulations across fields such as education (Chen et al., 2024), social dynam-
101
      ics (Park et al., 2023), and economics (Li et al., 2024b). In graph generation, De Marzo et al. (2023)
102   first explores the scale-free property of power-law distributions in LLM-based agent interactions.
103   Subsequently, Papachristou & Yuan (2024); Chang et al. (2024) examines additional social network
104   properties. However, these simulations often lack realism due to simplified modeling of human
105   behavior, such as name selection, and are typically constrained to fewer than 100 agents. Recently,
106   Pan et al. (2024) introduced AgentScope, a framework enabling large-scale multi-agent simulations
107   for simplified human behavior, demonstrated through number-guessing games. Building on this, we
      have enhanced AgentScope to simulate more complex human behaviors at a large scale.

      3.1   P ROBLEM S ETUP
138
139   Graphs serve as mathematical structures that represent relationships and interactions among en-
140   tities (Bergmeister et al., 2024-05). In this paper, we consider dynamic graph generation for
141   text-attributed graphs. In the graph generation task, we consider a seed graph G(V, E, X ), where
142   V = {vi }ni=1 represents a set of nodes, E = {eij } denotes the set of edges connecting these nodes,
143   and X = {x1 , . . . , xn } comprises the text attribute values associated with each vertex. Here, xi
144   indicates the text attribute linked to node vi . The graph generation model seeks to learn how G
145   evolves into a significantly larger graph G′ (V ′ , E ′ , X ′ ), where n′ ≫ n and m′ ≫ m, over time.
146   The objective is for the generated graph G′ to accurately mirror the macro patterns of the original
147
      graph during evolution while maintaining its essential structural properties. In citation networks, for
      example, graph evolution closely reflects the temporal interactions involved in paper-citing behavior
148
      among authors. By simulating these interactions to capture the dynamics of graph evolution, we gain
149
      a deeper understanding of how human behavior patterns drive changes in graph structure.
150
151   To design an effective dynamic graph generator, we develop the GAG framework to simulate pairwise
152   interactions in human activities, modeling graph evolution as a process of collecting interaction
153
      data repeatedly. Leveraging the ability of LLMs to mimic human-like behaviors (Li et al., 2023;
      Park et al., 2022; 2023), we construct LLM-based agents for human behavior simulation, denoted
154
      as A = {a1 , a2 , . . . , aN }, where N is the number of agents. Each interaction process between A
155
      corresponds to an update of the G. In the k-th simulation round, agent al ∈ A observes the data from
156   G and generate a subgraph Glk . Then, the subgraph produced by all agents is Gk = G1k ∪G2k ∪. . .∪GNk .
157   Therefore, after k rounds of simulation, G gradually evolves into G′ : G′ = G0 ∪ G1 ∪ G2 ∪ · · · ∪ Gk .
158   Specifically, the GAG framework employs a three-step simulation workflow, as illustrated in Figure
159   1: (1) Agent Formulation: Diverse agents with unique profiles and memory capacities are initialized.
160   (2) Pairwise Interaction: Agents engage in pairwise interactions through a virtual environment. (3)
161   Graph Extraction: G is extracted as abstract representations of interaction data. Additionally, we
      implement a Parallel Acceleration framework to enhance the efficiency of the simulation process.

      3.2   AGENT F ORMULATION
163
164   To achieve a credible simulation of human behaviors, we adhere to the general paradigm for con-
165   structing LLM-based Agents (Park et al., 2023), each agent al is equipped with three key components:
166   Agent Profile, denoted as pl ; Memory Stream, denoted as ml ; and Action State, denoted as sl .
167
      Agent Profile Previous research (Chan et al., 2024) has shown that persona-enhanced prompting
168   effectively guides LLMs in generating distinctive role-play synthetic data. To develop agents with
169   distinct roles, we create agent profiles pl , which store various aspects of human personal information
170   in textual format. Each agent al is initialized using its corresponding profile. These profiles are
171   derived from both real and LLM-generated datasets, with the detailed information provided in
172   Appendix A.1. We then construct heterogeneous agents with diverse profiles, enhancing the realism
173   and scope of our simulations.
174   Memory Stream Memory stream ml collects activity history of al in the simulation environment.
175   The memory component acts as a vital reference, enabling the agent to make informed decisions
176   based on past actions and interactions within the simulation environment. We organize the memory
177   stream using reflection (Shinn et al., 2023) and summarization techniques. This design allows the
178   agent to adaptively learn from past interactions.
179
      Action State al is equipped with action state sl its interaction with the environment, which consists
180   of two states: when sl = sact , al engages in interaction; when sl = sinc , al doesn’t.

187   graph G into its textual form, denoted as C. To achieve this, we utilize an environment template T
188   to convert the graph G(V, E, X ) into its textual format. We map each vertex vi to a corresponding
189
      real-world entity, creating a set of textual descriptions for each entity Feng et al. (2024), details
      in Appendix A.2. For vi , we consider its neighboring edges Ei = {(vi , vj ) | vi , vj ∈ V}, which
190
      compasses all edges connecting vi to its neighbors; and the vertex’s text attribute xi . Consequently,

282   3.5   PARALLEL ACCELERATION
283
284   The S-RAG allows us to significantly reduce the interaction rounds between agents. However,
285
      there remains technical barriers in supporting agent interaction simulation at the scale of n = 1e5 .
      Additionally, we note that the inference time of LLMs is substantial, leading to prolonged IO wait
286
      times for idle LLM-based agents. Various solutions have been proposed to address this issue, such
287
      as async mechanisms in Langchain (Kansal, 2024) and ACTOR architectures in Agentscope (Gao
288   et al., 2024). For further acceleration, we propose a Nested ACTOR Parallel Processing Schema (N-
289   ACTOR). As highlighted by (Clauset et al., 2004), network structures often display tightly connected
290   communities with loosely connected inter-community links. In the context of GAG, we categorize
291   agents into distinct groups marked by strong internal interactions and weaker intergroup interactions.
292   Agent groups can run in parallel on different CPU cores of a computation machine with P ports. The
293   specific algorithm of N-ACTOR is detailed in the Appendix A.4.

      In network science, there has long been an interest in graph structures that emerge within scientific,
298   technological, and sociological contexts (Leskovec et al., 2007). To assess our framework, we
299   conduct scenario modeling of these representative domains for graph generation, each illustrating
300   different types of human activity: (1) Creative Activity (CA): This includes the creation of items
301   like academic papers. The agent acts as an author to interact with an academic paper database,
302   producing networks such as Citation, Bibliographic Coupling, Co-Citation, Author Citation, and
303   Co-Authorship networks (Garfield, 2000). Simulation stops at the citation network reaching 1e4
304   nodes. (2) Item Interaction Activity (IA): This includes user-item interactions, including rating
305   movies, purchasing products and etc. The agent acts as a user interacting with an item database,
306
      producing networks such as the Movie Rating and User Projection networks (Zhou et al., 2007).
      Simulation stops at the movie rating network reaching 1e5 edges. (3) Social Activity (SA): This
307
      includes interaction and communication behavior. The agent acts as a user interacting with an online
308
      social media platform database (e.g., Twitter), producing networks such as Follow, Friend, and Action
309   networks (De Domenico et al., 2013). The simulation stops after 5 simulation rounds. For each
310   simulation scenario, we extract corresponding networks from each simulation scenario.

529
530   In this study, we present GAG, a novel and general framework designed for generating large-scale
531
      graphs with human behavior simulation. Our comprehensive experiments show that GAG can
      generate large-scale graphs with high quality and efficiency, with specified focus on graphs arise from
532
      scientific, technological and sociological context. The generated graphs exhibit seven macro-level
533
      characteristics of real-world networks, including power law, small world and shrinking diameter. In
534   the citation network expansion task, GAG demonstrates superior performance compared to existing
535   deep-learning-based graph generation methods in accurately capturing the power-law distribution
536   property. Furthermore, we present the S-RAG algorithm for simulating diverse human interaction
537   processes at scale, complemented by the N-ACTOR for parallel acceleration, achieving a speed-up of
538   at least 90.4%. Our framework successfully produces high-quality graphs with up to nearly 100,000
539   nodes or 10 million edges. Overall, GAG serves as a promising step towards efficient large-scale
      human interaction simulation and graph generation.

1110

1113   CPU cores of the computational machine. Let P denote the number of ports. Each supervisor actor

1115   message queue for agents’ execution of message processing, state updating, and message sending

1117   communities with loose inter-community connections, N-ACTOR introduces two levels of parallel

1284   Evaluation Metrics In accordance with established evaluation metrics for graph generation
1285   Bergmeister et al. (2024), we report the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) between the generated
1286   graphs and the test graphs, specifically focusing on degree distribution and clustering coefficient.
1287   Furthermore, we place particular emphasis on evaluating whether the degree distribution of the
1288   generated graphs conforms to a power law after expanding the graph to out-of-distribution sizes
1289   Clauset et al. (2009). To this end, we employ six key metrics in all:
1290   (1) MMD.D: maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) of degree distribution between the generated
1291   graphs and the test graphs.
1292
       (2) MMD.C: maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) of clustering coefficient between the generated
1293
       graphs and the test graphs.
1294
1295   (3) MMD.S: maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) of spectrum between the generated graphs and the
       test graphs Liao et al. (2019).


                                                                                                                                                     24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       (4) MMD.O: maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) of node orbit counts between the generated graphs
1297   and the test graphs You et al. (2018).
1298
1299   (5) α: The power-law exponent of the graph degree distribution.
1300   (6) Dk : The Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance between the degree distributions of the generated and
1301   test graphs.
1302
       (7) Valid: Research demonstrates that degree distributions in complex networks are typically charac-
1303
       terized by a power-law exponent α ∈ [2, 3] (Clauset et al., 2009). Accordingly, we define the validity
1304   measure for a graph as the proportion of graphs meeting the criteria Dk < 0.1 and α ∈ [2, 3]. Set
1305   kmin = 2 for the uniform calculation of the power-law fitness of both undirected and directed graphs.
1306
1307
       (8) GEM: To quantify the level of structural alignment for the expanded graph, we establish the
       Graph Expansion Metric (GEM). Firstly, for the negative indicator MMD metrics, we utilize the
1308                         1
       transformation 1 − 1+emetric , which maps the metrics to a range between 0 and 1. We then calculate
1309
       the average of MMD and Valid metrics as GEM.

1658   Previous work on employing LLMs for graph generation typically relied on predefined network
1659   structure features or a set of example networks Yao et al. (2024). Similarly, after understanding the
1660   reasons behind different structural characteristics of networks within GAG, we aim to enable users to
1661   control the entire simulation process by inputting prompts. This will guide and influence the various
1662
       structural features of the final network.
1663   To achieve this, we establish a control agent that accepts instruction frm users. Control agent transfers
1664   the instruction to a control profile for managing the simulation process of GAG.

       As shown in Fig. 12,specifically, the hub rate controls the proportion of recommended core users,
1666   subsequently affecting the ratio of hub nodes in the network. The parameter Nr determines the
1667   number of items recommended by the system, influencing the overall degree distribution. Additionally,
1668   parameter Nf dictate the number of filter items in the ReRanking stage, impacting the network’s
1669   clustering coefficient. Furthermore, the overall simulation time is adjusted by the number of agents
