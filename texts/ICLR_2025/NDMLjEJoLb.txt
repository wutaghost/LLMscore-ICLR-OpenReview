009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Many image generation tasks, such as content creation, editing, personalization,
012            and zero-shot generation, require generating unseen concepts without retraining
013            the model or collecting additional data. These tasks often involve blending ex-
014            isting concepts by conditioning the diffusion model with text prompts at each de-
015            noising step, a process known as “prompt mixing”. We introduce a novel approach
016
               for prompt mixing that forecasts predictions regarding the generated image and
               makes informed text conditioning decisions at each diffusion step. By leveraging
017
               the connection between diffusion models, rooted in non-equilibrium thermody-
018
               namics, and the Black-Scholes model for pricing options in finance, we derive an
019            appropriate algorithm for prompt mixing. Specifically, the parallels between diffu-
020            sion models and the Black-Scholes model enable us to leverage properties related
021            to the dynamics of the Markovian model derived in the Black-Scholes algorithm.
022            Our prompt-mixing algorithm is data-efficient, requiring no additional training,
023            and operates without human intervention or hyperparameter tuning. We highlight
024            the benefits of our approach by comparing it, both qualitatively and quantitatively
025            using CLIP scores, to other prompt mixing techniques. These include linear in-
026            terpolation, alternating prompts, step-wise prompt switching, and CLIP-guided
027
               prompt selection across various scenarios such as single object per text prompt,
               multiple objects per text prompt, and objects against backgrounds. Code will be
028
               made publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Text-to-image diffusion models Ho et al. (2020; 2022) often need to generate new and unseen con-
034
      cepts for tasks such as content creation, editing, personalization, and zero-shot generation without
035
      retraining the model or collecting additional task-specific data. While advanced versions of these
036   models attribute their improved capabilities to factors like increased training data, enhanced text un-
037   derstanding networks, and improved image generation architectures, data-efficient solutions Wang
038   et al. (2024) leveraging pre-trained diffusion models can enhance results without requiring additional
039   data collection or increasing model complexity.
040
      Often, the desired results can be formulated at the intersection of various individual manifolds fa-
041
      miliar to the model. This can be seen as blending existing concepts by conditioning the diffusion
042   model using text prompts at each denoising step, a process known as “prompt mixing” Patashnik
043   et al. (2023); Balaji et al. (2022); Repo (2023); Pinkney (2024). For instance, generating an image
044   resembling a hybrid of a pink cat and a dog. While current diffusion models Adobe (2024); Saharia
045   et al. (2022); Betker et al. (2023); Esser et al. (2024) excel at combining individual text prompts to
046   create visually appealing combinations in simple scenarios, these capabilities are often restricted to
047   advanced model versions and rely on large-data techniques.
048   Data-efficient prompt mixing techniques can enhance image generation without additional data.
049   While linear interpolation Kawar et al. (2023) of text embeddings from individual prompts is a sim-
050   ple approach, it may not be optimal due to the highly non-linear nature of the text-image manifold
051   and potential bias issues. Alternatives include switching between prompts during diffusion denois-
052   ing, either by alternating Kothandaraman et al. (2023a) or using a step-wise technique Patashnik
053   et al. (2023). However, these methods often require human involvement and careful prompt engi-
      neering for optimal results.

094   The question arises: what’s the best way to switch between text prompts for prompt-mixing? An
095   automated approach considers the model’s varying capabilities with different prompts. While atten-
096   tion maps Hong et al. (2023); Chefer et al. (2023) and layout guidanceZheng et al. (2023); Cheng
097   et al. (2023) methods excel at guiding the model toward distinct scene entities, they may not be as
098   effective for blending concepts within the same entity. To address this, during each step of diffu-
099   sion denoising, the network should automatically focus on the text prompt that corresponds to the
100   image’s deficiencies.
101
102   Main contributions. In this paper, we introduce a novel approach to prompt mixing by integrating
103   concepts from economics and finance. Our objective is to leverage pretrained diffusion models for
104   prompt-mixing to generate images at the intersection of various text-image manifolds. During each
105   step of the diffusion denoising process, our method dynamically conditions on the text prompt that
106   requires the highest level of attention. To achieve this, our algorithm assesses the ‘cost’ associated
107   with each relevant text prompt and selects the optimal conditions for the subsequent step of image
      generation based on the ‘cost’ that requires maximum optimization.

      Key Insight: The inspiration for our approach lies in non-equilibrium thermodynamics Sohl-
109   Dickstein et al. (2015), which is at the core of the development of diffusion models. These models
110   share a conceptual foundation with the Black Scholes modelMerton (1976), a Nobel Prize-winning
111   mathematical framework extensively employed in financial markets for pricing European call op-
112   tionsBlack & Scholes (2019). The denoising steps within a diffusion model, aimed at image gen-
113   eration, constitute a Markovian time-series. In our analogy, the image itself represents a valuable
114   ‘stock’ or ‘asset’ that we aim to ‘purchase’ (or generate) at the most favorable ‘cost’ (or alignment
115   with the text prompts). During each step of the diffusion denoising process, we extrapolate the
116   generate latents to compute the ‘stock’ prices at the corresponding time-step, and use the proper-
117
      ties of diffusion models to compute the various variables involved in the Black Scholes algorithm.
      Consequently, we leverage the Black Scholes model to predict a score for each text prompt. This
118
      score serves as an indicator of how the image should be conditioned in the subsequent timestep. By
119
      adopting this approach, our model dynamically focuses on aspects that require attention, ultimately
120   generating an image that optimally satisfies all relevant text prompts.
121
122   We perform experiments on prompts with varying complexities to assess our method’s ability to
123
      seamlessly blend different objects and backgrounds across scenarios. Through CLIP-based quanti-
      tative and qualitative comparisons against several baselines such as vanilla stable diffusion, linear
124
      interpolation, alternating sampling, prompt switching, and CLIP guided prompt switching, we high-
125
      light the superiority of our approach.

131   Prompt mixing. Prompt engineering Witteveen & Andrews (2022) is an effective approach that
132   includes techniques like rephrasing prompts to enhance model generalization. Another method in-

134   prompts and identify useful priors for image generation. Additionally, attention maps Chefer et al.
135   (2023) and prompt mixing techniques contribute to achieving better results. Prompt mixing is a
136   technique where different text prompts are used at various steps during the diffusion denoising
137   process. Patashnik et al. Patashnik et al. (2023) follow a step-wise approach, while Aerial Dif-
138
      fusion Kothandaraman et al. (2023a) alternates between prompts to create semantically consistent
      aerial-view images. Tools such as Image Mixer Diffusion Pinkney (2024) and CLIP Guided Image
139
      Mixing Repo (2023) follow similar techniques of prompt switching to blend multiple text prompts.
140
      However, these methods often require complex hyperparameter tuning.
141
142
143   Mixing step. The mixing time or mixing step Levin & Peres (2017) of a Markov chain refers to
144   the duration it takes for the chain to reach its steady-state distribution. Mixing time has found appli-
145   cations in image editing Zhu et al. (2024) and synthesizing out-of-distribution (OOD) images Zhu
146   et al. (2023). When switching between prompts using mixing time, a step-wise approach is fol-
147   lowed, akin to the work by Patashnik et al. Patashnik et al. (2023). The advantage of mixing-time
148   approaches over the method proposed by Patashnik et al. Patashnik et al. (2023) is that it mathe-
149   matically determines the optimal switching time without requiring complex hyperparameter tuning.
150   To approximate the mixing time, it estimates the radius of the latent space. However, there are a
151
      few limitations to these methods. Firstly, most of these approaches are applied to diffusion mod-
      els trained on relatively small, problem-specific datasets. Secondly, identifying clear boundaries on
152
      large-scale foundation models remains an unsolved challenge. Additionally, these methods lack the
153
      flexibility to choose the most optimal prompt at each timestep.
154
155
156   Understanding the latent space. Tangential to our work, there has been substantial work Karras
157   et al. (2017); Abdal et al. (2019); Gal et al. (2022) on exploring the latent space. These investigations
158   have yielded valuable insights and practical solutions for downstream tasks such as image editing
159   and manipulation applications Zhu et al. (2016); Shen et al. (2020); Kwon et al. (2022); Zhu et al.
160   (2020); Preechakul et al. (2022). Furthermore, a deeper understanding of the latent space within
161   diffusion models has led to advancements in various methods Rombach et al. (2022); Kwon et al.
      (2022); Yang et al. (2023b).

166   In this section, we provide a brief overview of the Black Scholes pricing model Merton (1976) used
167   to determine the price of European call options of assets. In simple terms, a European call option
168   allows an investor to lock in the price of an asset at any time but permits stock purchase (if desired)
169   only upon expiration. Regardless of whether the stock price moves favorably or unfavorably over
170   time, this option structure remains consistent. Investors rely on the Black-Scholes model to predict
171   stock prices over time and make informed decisions about the optimal timing for stock purchases.
172
      The Black Scholes formula involves 5 key variables:
173
              1. Underlying stock price or spot price S: This represents the current price of the asset.
174
175

176           3. Time to expiration t: It measures the time difference between the current moment and the
177              expiry time.
178           4. Volatility σ: Volatility reflects the variation in prices of the asset.
179
              5. Risk free rate r: The risk-free rate is the minimum return on an investment when the in-
180              vestor faces zero risks.

209   In finance, volatility refers to the standard deviation in the way stock prices change over time.
210   The mathematical description of diffusion models, derived from non-equilibrium thermodynam-
211   ics, shares similarities with the derivation of the Black-Scholes model used in pricing European call
212
      options within financial markets. Both models emerge from similar assumptions and conditions,
      underpinned by a shared mathematical structure.
213
214   The process of diffusion can be understood using tools from statistical mechanics, where the gen-
215   erative dynamics undergo phase transitions and symmetry breaking. The dynamic equation of the
      generative process can be interpreted as a stochastic adiabatic transformation that minimizes free

      4.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
327
328   Consider a set of N text prompts denoted as P1 , ...PN and T diffusion denoising steps. Note that
329   text-to-image diffusion architectures often include a text encoder that maps the text prompt to a joint
330   text-image space. Starting from random noise and conditioned on the embedding in the text-image
331   space, the neural network generates the final image.
332   Our objective is to generate an image that aligns with multiple text prompts simultaneously i.e. at
333   the intersection of the various text-image manifolds of the diffusion models. We pose this task as a
334   prompt mixing Patashnik et al. (2023) problem. During each step of diffusion denoising, our aim is
335   to select the most relevant text prompt (or the text prompt with respect to which the image requires
336   further refining) for conditioning the model, ensuring the generation of an optimal image satisfying
337   all individual text prompts.
338
339   4.2   M ETHOD
340
341   Selecting the most effective text prompt to condition a text-to-image diffusion model can be ap-
342   proached in various ways. Naively switching Kothandaraman et al. (2023a); Patashnik et al. (2023)
343   between prompts is sub-optimal due to the significant human effort involved. Moreover, it fails to
344
      address the critical issue of prioritizing the most relevant prompt in an automatic manner. Alterna-
      tively, one can compute the CLIP score at each step and move toward the text prompt with the lowest
345
      CLIP score. While this approach is reasonable, it overlooks essential factors related to the dynam-
346
      ics Ho et al. (2020) of the diffusion denoising process, which significantly impact the final image
347   generation. Leveraging the Black Scholes model allows us to model these dynamics effectively.
348
349   We utilize the CLIP score to quantify the “price” of the generated image, treating it as analogous
350
      to a stock. The CLIP score has been widely used to measure text-image alignment, making it a
      suitable metric for evaluating how well our generated image aligns with each text prompt. Consider
351
      diffusion denoising at step i out of total steps T . We derive the relevant variables for Black Scholes
352
      formula as follows:
353
354         1. Underlying stock price S: The underlying stock price corresponds to the current value
355            of the asset. Analogously, we define S as the alignment of the generated image with the
356            input text, at the current stage of the diffusion-based generation process. Let zt represent
357            the predicted latents at timestep t. We denote z0,t as the latents of the final predicted
358            image extrapolated from zt . In other words, if the denoising process were to proceed in
359            a straightforward manner following the same direction as computed for zt , the latents of
360
               the final predicted image would be z0,t . The underlying stock price, denoted as S, is
               determined by the CLIP score with respect to text prompt Pp . This CLIP score can be
361
               computed using the image decoded from z0,t . To maintain values within the range of 0 to
362
               100, we multiply the CLIP score by 100.
363
364         2. Strike price K: This represents the estimated asset price at expiry. Analogously, we define
365
               K as the alignment of the generated image with the input text, at the last step of diffusion
               denoising, which predicts the final image. We set the strike price as the average CLIP
366
               score that the underlying diffusion model achieves when generating an image based on a
367
               combination of prompts, using a straightforward approach. This choice is driven by the
368            maximum potential that the diffusion model can realize for that specific set of prompts.
369
370
            3. Time to expiration t: This is the time left before the asset expires. In the case of diffusion
               model, we define t as the number of steps remaining for the diffusion denoising process to
371
               complete, i.e. t = T − i.
372
373         4. Volatility σ: This variable refers to the variability in the price of the asset. Analogously,
374            we compute the volatility as the square root of the variance used by the diffusion denoising
375
               scheduler at timestep i, which is reflective of the volatility in the diffusion process.
376         5. Risk-free rate r: In finance, the risk-free rate is computed as the inflation rate subtracted
377            from the nominal rate. In our formulation w.r.t. diffusion models, the nominal rate can be
               ignored. The nominal rate in pricing markets i usually set by financial institution based

408   5   E XPERIMENTS AND R ESULTS
409
410   Metrics. We evaluate performance using the following metrics:
411
412        1. CLIP Score: We utilize two variants:
413              • CLIP-combined: This variant assesses the overall alignment with text by comparing
414                 the generated image against a combination of individual text prompts.
415              • CLIP-add: This averages the CLIP scores for the generated image across each indi-
416                 vidual text prompt, reflecting alignment with each specific concept.
417        2. BLIP Score ⊙ DINO:
418
                 • The BLIP score is calculated by comparing the generated image to a combination of
419
                    individual text prompts, measuring overall text-level alignment.
420
                 • The DINO score evaluates the generated image against each individual text prompt,
421
                    assessing how well the image preserves the characteristics of each concept. This in-
422
                    dicates the fidelity of the attributes in the generated image relative to the individual
423                 concepts. Thus, while BLIP focuses on overall concept blending, DINO provides in-
424                 sights into the preservation of characteristics for each concept. We aim for high values
425                 in both scores, calculating a net score by multiplying the DINO and BLIP scores for
426                 each prompt and averaging across all prompts.
427        3. KID: This metric assesses the realism of the generated samples, serving as an indicator of
428           their overall quality.
429
430   This structured approach allows us to comprehensively evaluate the performance of generated im-
431   ages in relation to the provided text prompts. To calculate our metrics, we generate five images for
      each text prompt and incorporate all of them into the metric computations.

435   achieved through linear interpolation between text embeddings Kawar et al. (2023). This method
436   equally weights the text embeddings associated with each text prompt. (iii) Switching between text
437   prompts. We consider two variations here: (iii-a) Step: Following Patashnik et. al. Patashnik et al.
438   (2023), we use one text prompt for the initial 7th to 17th denoising steps and then switch to the other
439   text prompt for the remaining steps, (iii-b) Alt.: Following Kothandaraman et. al. Kothandaraman
440   et al. (2023a), we alternate between the two text prompts. (iv) CLIP Minimum: Score-based com-
441
      bination, denoted as CLIP-min, where we select the text prompt corresponding to the lowest CLIP
      score from the previous denoising iteration.
442
443
444
      Hyperparameters. Based on our experiments for the vanilla combination usig Stable Diffusion
      2.1 for the dataset under consideration, where we found that a CLIP score of approximately 0.25
445
      indicates reasonable text-image alignment, we opted for a constant value of 0.25 for the strike price.
446
      The ordering of prompts does not matter. This is because, at every step of diffusion denoising, the
447   algorithm chooses the prompt that should be selected by computing the Black Scholes score wrt each
448   prompt, and this process is agnostic to the ordering of the prompts. More details on hyperparameters
449   and the backbone architecture can be found in the appendix.

452
453   We construct a dataset with 4 types of scenarios to analyze varying complexities, spanning simple
454   text prompts with single object, multiple objects per text prompt, object actions against backgrounds
455   (prompt mixing w.r.t. object) and object performing action against a background/ style (concept
456   blending w.r.t. background and style). More information can be found in the appendix. We show
457
      qualitative results in Figure 1 and Figure 2. More qualitative results can be found in the appendix.
      The quantitative comparisons are in Figure 1. A detailed analysis of our method and benefits over
458
      prior art is as follows:
459
460
             • Vanilla stable diffusion (SD): Vanilla stable diffusion v2.1 can create plausible combina-
461            tions of the provided text prompts. However, due to hallucination issues, it is constrained
462            by the distributions it learned during training. As a result, it struggles to capture clear
463            characteristics of individual text prompts. For example, in Figure 1, the distinct features
464            of the parrot, cat, dog/penguin, and sunset/penguin are missing. Similarly, in Figure 2, the
465            avocado/raccoon, muffin, parrot, and oil painting style lack distinct characteristics.
466          • Linear interpolation Kawar et al. (2023): Linear interpolation is ill-suited for generating an
467            image that combines two text prompts. This limitation arises from the non-linear nature of
468            the text-image space. While linear interpolation assumes a simple linear relationship, the
469            actual mapping between textual descriptions and image features is intricate. Furthermore,
470            linear interpolation can only traverse a straight path between two points in the latent space,
471            failing to capture nuanced variations or produce novel features beyond the endpoints.
472          • Alternating Sampling Kothandaraman et al. (2023a),: Alternating sampling generates fea-
473            tures related to both text prompts in the final output image. However, the objects produced
474            suffer from poor definition, unrealistic appearance, and low quality in many instances. Ar-
475            tifacts are prevalent, and several generated images appear implausible, resulting in a high
476            KID score. The underlying issue lies in the algorithm’s routine alternation between the two
477            text prompts during diffusion denoising, without adequately emphasizing the most relevant
478
               prompt at each step. Despite these limitations, the model achieves a reasonable CLIP Score
               by broadly aligning with the overall image description.
479
480          • Step-wise switching Kothandaraman et al. (2023a): Step-wise switching is similarly inef-
481            fective for the same reasons as Alternating sampling.
482          • CLIP-min: The CLIP-min results exhibit bias toward one of the text prompts in most cases,
483            preventing the model from generating an image that aligns with the intersection of both
484            prompts. This bias arises because the model overlooks the dynamics of diffusion denoising
485            and instead selects the text prompt with the lowest CLIP score at each step, resulting in
               bias-related issues.

791           not relevant in the context of diffusion models. Instead, we can think of the computational

793

794         • Constant Volatility and Risk-Free Rate (Assumption iv): In diffusion models, while volatil-

796           process can be likened to having a consistent framework for how noise is added and refined.
797
              The “risk-free rate” does not have a direct counterpart, but the idea of a stable environment
              for generating images can be considered.
798
799         • Normally Distributed Returns (Assumption v): This assumption can be related to the dis-
800           tribution of pixel values or features in the generated images. In many diffusion models, the
801
              outputs are conditioned on a distribution that can be approximated as normal, especially
              when considering the latent space representations.
