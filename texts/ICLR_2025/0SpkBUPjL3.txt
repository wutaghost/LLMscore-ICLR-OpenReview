009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The recent explosion of high-quality language models has necessitated new meth-
012            ods for identifying AI-generated text. Watermarking is a leading solution and
013            could prove to be an essential tool in the age of generative AI. Existing approaches
014            embed watermarks at inference and crucially rely on the large language model
015            (LLM) specification and parameters being secret, which makes them inapplica-
016
               ble to the open-source setting. In this work, we introduce the first watermarking
               scheme for open-source LLMs. Our scheme works by modifying the parame-
017
               ters of the model, but the watermark can be detected from just the outputs of
018
               the model. Perhaps surprisingly, we prove that our watermarks are unremovable
019            under certain assumptions about the adversary’s knowledge. To demonstrate the
020            behavior of our construction under concrete parameter instantiations, we present
021            experimental results with OPT-6.7B and OPT-1.3B. We demonstrate robustness to
022            both token substitution and perturbation of the model parameters. We find that the
023            stronger of these attacks, the model-perturbation attack, requires deteriorating the
024            quality score to 0 out of 100 in order to bring the detection rate down to 50%.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      As generative AI becomes increasingly capable and available, reliable solutions for identifying AI-
      generated text grow more and more imperative. Without strong identification methods, we face risks
029
      such as model collapse (Shumailov et al., 2024), mass disinformation campaigns (Ryan-Mosley,
030
      2023), and detection false positives leading to false plagiarism accusations (Ghaffary, 2023). Wa-
031   termarking is a prominent and promising approach to detection. Recent works (Kirchenbauer et al.
032   (2023); Aaronson (2022); Zhao et al. (2024); Christ et al. (2024); Kuditipudi et al. (2024); Fairoze
033   et al. (2023); Christ & Gunn (2024)) construct “sampler-based watermarks,” for the setting where
034   the watermark is embedded at generation time and users have only query access to the model. Such
035   watermarks modify the algorithm used by the LLM to sample from the probability distribution over
036   the next token, leaving the underlying neural network untouched. For example, Zhao et al. (2024)
037   shifts this distribution to prefer sampling tokens on a fixed “green list.” These existing approaches
038   are ill-suited for scenarios where an attacker has access to the code for the watermarked model and
039
      can simply rewrite the sampling algorithm to sample from the unmodified probability distribution
      — yielding the original, unwatermarked model, with no loss in quality.
040
041   Such scenarios are gaining importance as open-source models become more widely available and
042   higher quality (e.g., LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023), Mistral Jiang et al. (2023), OPT Zhang et al.
043   (2022)). However, watermarks for open-source models have been severely understudied. In this
044   work we initiate a formal study of such watermarks, in the setting where the model parameters and
045
      associated code are publicly available, and the watermark is embedded by altering the weights of the
      neural network. We consider the standard notion of autoregressive language models used in existing
046
      watermarking works (see Appendix A.2 for a precise definition). Constructing watermarks that are
047
      unremovable by an attacker with such comprehensive model access is a significant challenge.
048
049   Our contributions. We put forth a formal definition of an unremovable watermark for neural net-
050   works, then construct such a watermarking scheme. Unlike sampler-based watermarks, ours mod-
051   ifies the weights of the model. Our watermark is unremovable in the sense that it can be detected
052   even after an adversary modifies the weights of the model. In fact, it offers unremovability not only
053   with access to the weights, but even from a modest amount of text (∼300 tokens) produced by the
      model.

      Of course, one must place a restriction on the allowable ways in which the adversary may modify the
055   weights, otherwise it can ignore the watermarked model and generate unwatermarked text of its own.
056   We do so by imposing a quality condition: We prove that if an adversary removes our watermark, the
057   resulting model is low-quality, assuming that the attacker has some uncertainty about the distribution
058   of high-quality text. This assumption is necessary because otherwise, the adversary could simply
059   ignore the watermarked model and train a new model itself. We precisely formalize this assumption
060   and the tradeoff between quality degradation and watermark strength.
061   In addition to proving unremovability, we provide a suite of experiments using OPT-6.7B and OPT-
062   1.3B, showing that our theoretical guarantees translate to practice. We find that the strongest attack
063   we consider requires deteriorating text quality to zero out of 100 in order to bring the detection rate
064   to 50%. Our approach is quite general, and our techniques may be of independent interest due to
065   their applicability in other settings where one wishes to watermark high-dimensional data.

067   2    T ECHNICAL OVERVIEW
068
069   Formalizing unremovability (Section 4.1). The first challenge in constructing an unremovable
070
      watermark is defining unremovability. Recall that the goal of the adversary is to take a watermarked
      model and produce a high-quality unwatermarked model. One cannot hope for a watermark to
071
      be unremovable by an adversary with sufficient knowledge to train its own unwatermarked model.
072
      Therefore, we must consider only adversaries with limited knowledge about the distribution of high-
073   quality text. We formalize this notion as follows: we model ideal-quality language as being described
074   by an original neural network M ∗ . That is, the ideal-quality language is the distribution of text that
075   would be produced by an LLM using M ∗ to compute the probability distribution over each token.
076   This neural network M ∗ is then watermarked, and the resulting M ′ is given to the adversary. The
077   adversary’s uncertainty is captured by its posterior distribution over M ∗ , given M ′ .
078
      We assume that the adversary’s posterior distribution over M ∗ is normally distributed in a particular
079   representation space of models, and centered at M ′ . Under this assumption, we prove that there is a
080   steep trade-off between the magnitude of changes the adversary must make to remove the watermark,
081   and the magnitude of changes required to add the watermark. That is, text produced by the adversary
082   with access to M ′ is either watermarked or low-quality.
083
084   Our scheme (Section 5). Our scheme is quite natural: We modify the bias of each neuron in the
085   last layer of the model by adding a perturbation from N (0, ε2 ), as described in Algorithms 1 and 2.
086   The watermarking detection key is the vector of these perturbations. Given the weights of a model,
087
      we can detect the watermark by checking the correlation between the biases of its last layer and
      the watermark perturbations. More precisely, we compute the inner product between the watermark
088
      perturbations, and the difference between the biases of the given model and the original model
089
      (Algorithm 3).
090
091   Furthermore, we observe that it is possible to approximate this inner product given only text pro-
092   duced by the model. This text detector (Algorithm 4) computes a score that is the sum of the
093
      watermark perturbations corresponding to the distinct tokens observed in the text. We show that
      in expectation, this score is approximately the inner product from the detector from weights (Algo-
094
      rithm 3), where the approximation error is lower for higher-entropy text. We prove that in sufficiently
095
      high-entropy text, our text detector succeeds with overwhelming probability.
096
097   Proving unremovability (Theorem 3). We show unremovability in two steps: (1) Any high-quality
098   model produced by altering the watermarked model must have a high inner product score (Theo-
099   rem 1), and (2) If a language model has a high inner product score, its responses are watermarked
100   (Theorem 3).

140   3    R ELATED WORK
141
      We describe the most relevant related works here, but refer readers to Tang et al. (2023); Piet et al.
142
      (2023) for more comprehensive surveys.
143
144   Existing watermarks change the sampling function of the LLM, and are therefore easily removable
145   given code for this sampling function. Aaronson (2022); Kirchenbauer et al. (2023); Zhao et al.
146   (2024); Christ et al. (2024); Fairoze et al. (2023) sample each token by partitioning the token set into
147
      red and green lists (that depends on the previously-output tokens), then increasing the probability of
      the tokens in the green list. To detect the watermark, one computes the fraction of green tokens in
148
      the given text and compares it to a threshold. Kuditipudi et al. (2024); Christ & Gunn (2024) operate
149
      slightly differently—for each response, they choose a random seed. In Kuditipudi et al. (2024) there
150   are polynomially many possible seeds for a given watermarking key, and in Christ & Gunn (2024)
151   there are exponentially many. They then choose the ith token of the response to be correlated with
152   the ith value of the random seed. To detect the watermark, one essentially computes the correlation
153   between the response and the seed. While not unremovable, some of these watermarks satisfy a
154   weaker robustness property: Given a watermarked response, it is difficult to make a bounded number
155   of edits to yield an unwatermarked text.
156   Our scheme is most similar in spirit to that of Zhao et al. (2024), though theirs is a sampler-based
157   watermark. They choose a fixed red/green partition that is used to change the sampling function for
158   all responses. Similarly, we fix the partition of tokens whose biases we increase and decrease. The
159   two major differences are that (1) we make this change by altering the weights of the model, and (2)
160   while Zhao et al. (2024) increases the logits of all green tokens by the same amount, our increases
161   are normally distributed. This normal distribution is crucial for unremovability, and it results in a
      different detector being most effective for our scheme. The detector of Zhao et al. (2024) simply


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025
162
      computes the fraction of green tokens, while our inner product detector computes a score that is
163   essentially a weighted count of the number of green tokens. These weights correspond to the size of
164   the perturbations added by our watermark.
165
166
      The works closest to our setting are Gu et al. (2024); Sander et al. (2024), which show empirically
      that certain sampler based watermarks can be learned. That is, a model trained on watermarked
167
      texts may learn to generate watermarked responses itself. The watermark therefore is embedded in
168
      the weights of the model; however, there is no unremovability guarantee, and the detectability of
169   these responses is much lower than their sampler-based counterparts. In fact, Gu et al. (2024) finds
170   empirically that the watermark is destroyed after the model is fine-tuned and leaves the question of
171   unremovable open-source watermarks for future work.
172
      We also note that prior work Zhang et al. (2024) shows that any LLM watermark is removable by
173
      a sufficiently strong adversary. This underscores our need to consider an adversary with limited
174
      knowledge about high-quality language when defining and showing unremovability.

299     5.2   D ETECTING THE WATERMARK FROM TEXT
300     In this section, we will show that the inner product from WeightDetect can be approximated given
301     text. Our detector from text, TextDetect (Algorithm 4), simply computes the sum of the pertur-
302     bations of the biases of tokens in the given text. We observe that a positive perturbation increases
303     the probability of outputting the given token, and the magnitude of the perturbation corresponds
304     to the magnitude of the increase. Similarly, a negative perturbation decreases a token’s likelihood.
305
        Therefore, we expect the frequency of a token in a watermarked response to have an observable
        correlation with the sign and magnitude of the perturbation added to its bias.
306
307     We prove in Theorem 3 that this intuition is indeed correct. In particular, this sum of perturbations
308     of observed tokens approximates a value that is 0 in expectation for natural text, and grows linearly
309     with the text length for watermarked text. This is true even when the watermarked model is modified
310     by an adversary attempting to remove the watermark. The accuracy of the detector’s approximation
311
        depends on the entropy of the text, and the quality of the model produced by the adversary. That is,
        low-entropy responses and low-quality adversarial models will have lower watermark detectability.

512   the strong asymptotic tradeoff in Section 5 is somewhat dampened by the concrete parameters we
513   consider. For example, the false negative rate is exponential in −ε4 T , where T is the number of
