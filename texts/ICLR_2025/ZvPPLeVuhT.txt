009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Visual Foundation Models (VFMs) such as the Segment Anything Model (SAM)
012
               have significantly advanced segmentation tasks. However, SAM and its variants
013
               necessitate substantial manual effort for prompt generation and additional training
014            for specific applications. Recent approaches have addressed these limitations by
015            integrating SAM into one-shot and few-shot segmentation, enabling auto-prompting
016            through semantic alignment between query and support images. Despite these
017            advancements, they still generate inadequate prompts that degrade segmentation
018            quality due to visual inconsistencies between support and query images. To tackle
019            this limitation, we introduce ReGRAF (Refinement via Gradient Flow), a training-
020            free method that refines prompts through gradient flow derived from SAM’s mask
021            decoder. ReGRAF easily integrates into auto-prompting segmentation frameworks
022
               and is theoretically proven to refine segmentation masks with high efficiency and
               precision. Extensive evaluations demonstrate that ReGRAF consistently improves
023
               segmentation quality across various benchmarks, effectively mitigating inadequate
024
               prompts without requiring additional training or architectural modifications.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large models have paved a new era of foundation models, e.g., Large Language Models (LLMs)
031   (Zeng et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020), demonstrating exceptional versatility
032   and generality across various tasks. In computer vision, Visual Foundation Models (VFMs) have
033
      emerged to revolutionize vision tasks such as image classification (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Touvron
      et al., 2021), object detection (Carion et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021) and segmentation (Cheng et al.,
034
      2021; Strudel et al., 2021) by leveraging advanced architectures and enormous data. Specifically in
035
      segmentation, Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023) arises as a universal VFM
036   which addresses challenges of segmenting objects with diverse appearance by taking prompts such as
037   points, bounding boxes, and coarse masks.
038
039
      While SAM is versatile, it often struggles with coarse mask boundaries and small holes scattered
      throughout the segmentation mask in complex situations. (e.g. segmenting multiple objects or small
040
      parts of an object.) This limitation has led to utilizing fine-tuning methods aimed at improving its
041
      precision (Chen et al., 2023; Ke et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023). These approaches introduce additional
042   optimization for SAM with different segmentation scenarios to handle diverse object appearances and
043   complexities. However, notice that SAM and its fine-tuned variants are not automated; they require
044   label-intensive efforts to identify “proper prompts” for segmenting objects of interest. Additionally,
045   these fine-tuned variants necessitate extra learnable parameters and training on additional datasets.
046
      Recent works address the issues above by integrating SAM and its variants into one-shot and few-shot
047
      segmentation scheme, enabling auto-prompting by leveraging the semantic alignment between a query
048   and support images. For instance, PerSAM (Zhang et al., 2024) relies on one-shot data, comprising a
049   support image and a rough mask of the target object. It utilizes a similarity map between a query and
050   target object embeddings to locate the target object precisely to facilitate prompt generation. Further
051   more, PerSAM-F, a fine-tuning variant of PerSAM, introduces learnable parameters to fine-tune
052   SAM, producing a linear combination of potential segmentation masks of different hierarchical levels
053   to resolve ambiguity (Zhang et al., 2024). Similarly, Matcher effectively combines pre-existing VFMs
      to tackle one/few-shot segmentation tasks without additional training (Liu et al., 2024).

066   While the aformentioned methods have been successful, variations in appearance (e.g., color, view-
067   point, and shape) between query and support images often lead to misleading similarity maps. This
068   causes auto-prompting segmentation frameworks (e.g. PerSAM-F and Matcher) to generate inade-
069   quate prompts (false-positive, semantically ambiguous, or semantically insufficient prompts) that
070   confuse SAM’s decoder in capturing the target object(s), thereby compromising the quality of the
071   segmentation mask. For example, Fig. 1b and Fig. 1c illustrate instances where PerSAM-F and
072
      Matcher generate semantically insufficient prompts, failing to fully capture the entire target object
      (e.g., the elephant) within the scene. In such a scenario, we hypothesized that the gradient flow
073
      from the mask decoder encapsulates comprehensive contextual information learned during SAM’s
074
      pretraining. By iteratively refining the initial prompts based on this information, we expect obtaining
075   higher-quality segmentation masks, as illustrated in Fig. 1d.
076
077   To this end, we propose a framework Refinement via GRAdient Flow (ReGRAF) of mask decoder
078
      which minimizes the entropy-regularized Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the query and
      support embeddings through the gradient flow derived from the mask decoder. This flow aligns the
079
      query embedding with the semantics of the target object, improving the similarity map and eventually
080
      enhancing the quality of segmentation masks. Additionally, we provide a rigorous theoretical
081   analysis that guarantees the convergence of this process. Specifically, we prove that the gradient flow
082   converges exponentially to the optimal probability density function (pdf) of the VFM’s embedding
083   space, ensuring robust refinement with a limited number of iterations. As the existing methods
084   significantly rely on similarity, our method improves upon existing methods that are grounded on
085   auto-prompting segmentation frameworks. In summary, our key contributions are as follows:
086
087           • Simple and Effective Training-Free Refinement Method: We introduce a novel, training-
088             free refinement method that improves the segmentation mask quality of promptable segmen-
089             tation models, without additional learnable parameters or datasets.
090           • Broad Applicability to Auto-Prompting Segmentation Frameworks: Our method offers
091             wide adaptability and can be easily integrated with any existing segmentation framework
092             that utilizes auto-prompting techniques.
093           • Theoretical Guarantee of Convergence: We provide a convergence analysis demonstrating
094             that ReGRAF drives query embeddings to converge exponentially to the optimal embedding
095             distribution for segmentation masks.
096
097   We empirically show the effect of ReGRAF through qualitative and quantitative experimental results.
098   ReGRAF is validated on five independent datasets with two different tasks (i.e., semantic and part
099   segmentations), which demonstrate improvements as we initially hypothesized.

101   2     R ELATED WORKS
102
103   2.1    O NE / FEW- SHOT SEGMENTATION
104   Few-shot segmentation aims to develop models capable of segmenting new classes using only a few
105   annotated samples (e.g., one to five) (Shaban et al., 2017), making it particularly useful in scenarios
106   where acquiring large amounts of annotated data is difficult. Typically, these models use a pre-trained
107   feature extractor to process both support and query images, combining information from support
      images to perform segmentation (Zhang et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2022).


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Recently, several approaches leveraging SAM as a pre-trained model have been proposed. PerSAM
109   relies solely on one-shot data, comprising a support image and a rough mask of the target object. It
110   utilizes a similarity map between a query and target object embeddings to locate the target object
111   precisely, facilitating prompt generation. However, PerSAM faces challenges in cases of visual
112   ambiguity, such as objects with visually distinct subparts or hierarchical structures (Zhang et al.,
113   2024).
114
      PerSAM-F, fine-tuning variant of PerSAM, addresses this problem by introducing two learnable
115   parameters to fine-tune SAM, producing a linear combination of potential segmentation masks of
116   different hierarchical levels to resolve ambiguity (Zhang et al., 2024). This approach improves seg-
117   mentation accuracy and prevents overfitting on one-shot data, demonstrating enhanced performance
118   in challenging scenarios.
119
      Similarly, Matcher (Liu et al., 2024), effectively combines pre-existing VFMs to tackle one/few-shot
120
      segmentation tasks without additional training. Matcher utilizes DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024) as an
121   encoder to compute a similarity map with semantic understanding to accurately identify the location
122   of a target object in the query image. It then employs SAM as a segmenter to obtain segmentation
123   masks, benefiting from the zero-shot segmentation performance of the SAM.
124
125   2.2   F INE TUNING VARIANTS OF SAM
126   For efficient adaptation of SAM to various downstream tasks, a range of fine-tuning variants has
127   emerged. HQ-SAM, for instance, adds a high-quality output token and trains on the HQSeg-44K
128   dataset to improve mask precision (Ke et al., 2024). VRP-SAM (Sun et al., 2024) implements a
129   visual reference prompt encoder, requiring specific reference images and detailed training. SAM-
130   Adapter (Chen et al., 2023) modifies the architecture by adding lightweight adapter layers while
131   keeping most of SAM’s original parameters frozen, reducing training effort. MobileSAM (Zhang
132   et al., 2023) replaces the heavy ViT-H encoder with Tiny-ViT, reducing the model size and complexity
133   while still requiring adaptation.
134   All of these methods, however, necessitate additional training data and structural changes to the
135   model architecture. These fine-tuning strategies still heavily depend on manual prompts, with training
136   datasets requiring carefully crafted prompt annotations alongside segmentation data. This reliance on
137   curated prompts limits their applicability in real-world settings, where obtaining accurate prompts
138   and consistent user interaction may be challenging.
139
140
      2.3   P ROMPT TUNING
141   Prompt tuning has emerged as a lightweight and efficient fine-tuning strategy, enabling models to
142   leverage their extensive pre-trained knowledge without the need to significantly modify their internal
143   parameters (Lester et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023).
144   In computer vision, especially with vision foundation models like SAM, prompt tuning can be thought
145   of as modifying the visual clues that are provided to guide the model in segmentation (e.g. points,
146   bounding boxes, or masks). Instead of retraining SAM for new segmentation tasks, prompt tuning
147   focuses on refining these input prompts to better capture object boundaries and features.
148
      Recent works like PerSAM-F and Matcher are specialized forms of prompt tuning, refining the
149   input prompts or embeddings for segmentation tasks. However, these methods primarily rely on
150   similarity between query and support images, which can lead to inadequate prompts and sub-optimal
151   segmentation performance when the images have appearance variations.

305   3.3   T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
306
      This section discusses the convergence of ReGRAF. We pose the following key questions:
307
308         How does the gradient flow approach the optimal probability density function (pdf) µ∗ ,
309                        and what is the rate at which this convergence occurs?
310
311   This question is crucial for two reasons: First, ReGRAF should be theoretically designed to be
312   a convergent algorithm, which underscores its foundational robustness. Second, considering the
313
      approximation of the density ratio used in ReGRAF, even a limited number of iterations should lead
      to improvements in the quality of segmentation masks. In the following theorem, we demonstrate
314
      that ReGRAF addresses the questions above.
315
316
      Theorem 1. Let ρt be a candidate pdf in 2-Wasserstein space W2 evolving according to the gradient
      flow of the entropy-regularized KL divergence Fµ (ρ) with local minimum µ∗ . If the initial pdf ρ0 lies
317
      in a neighborhood of µ∗ , then ρt converges to µ∗ exponentially as t → ∞.
318
319   The proof of Theorem 1 is given in Appendix A, where we outline each step to establish the
320   convergence. We assume that the initial probability density function (pdf) ρ0 is close to the optimal
321   pdf µ∗ . By applying a second-order Taylor expansion around µ∗ , we approximate the entropy-
322   regularized KL divergence Fµ (ρ) which confirms that it reaches its minimum at µ∗ . This results in a
323   differential equation demonstrating that the squared distance between ρt and µ∗ decreases over time,
      leading to exponential convergence toward µ∗ as t approaches infinity.


                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Given that the VFM has been trained on a diverse and extensive dataset, enabling it to generalize
325   across various tasks, it is reasonable to assume that the distribution of the VFM’s image embedding
326   ρ0 lies in the neighborhood of the optimal embedding distribution µ∗ for the VFM’s mask decoder.
327   Consequently, Theorem 1 guarantees that ρ0 converges to µ∗ at an exponential rate.
328
      In summary, Theorem 1 yields two important implications: 1) it validates the density ratio approxi-
329
      mation of ReGRAF, thereby reinforcing the model’s theoretical foundation, and 2) it underscores
330
      ReGRAF’s robustness in refining segmentation masks as a convergent algorithm.

334   4.1   E XPERIMENT SETTING
335
      Baseline method and backbone models. We utilize PerSAM-F and Matcher as our baseline methods
336
      and demonstrate that ReGRAF enhances the segmentation mask for both methods. PerSAM-F uses
337   SAM as its backbone model, fully leveraging it to capture visual clues of target objects in the support
338   images. In contrast, Matcher employs DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) with the ViT-L/14 architecture
339   (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) as an image encoder to extract embeddings from both query and support
340   images, and uses SAM’s decoder as the segmenter. To illustrate that ReGRAF can be seamlessly
341   integrated with various SAM variants, we incorporate HQ-SAM (Ke et al., 2024) into both baselines,
342   resulting in HQ-PerSAM-F and HQ-Matcher. HQ-PerSAM-F substitutes HQ-SAM for SAM, while
343   HQ-Matcher replaces the SAM’s mask decoder and prompt encoder with those from HQ-SAM,
344   leaving the other modules unchanged.
345   Hyperparameter setting of PerSAM-F/HQ-PerSAM-F. The hyperparameters for fine-tuning
346   PerSAM-F and HQ-PerSAM-F adhere to the experimental settings outlined in Zhang et al. (2024).
347   We used the SAM with ViT-H as the segmenter for both PerSAM-F and HQ-PerSAM-F, utilizing the
348   AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov, 2017) with a weight decay of 0.01, betas of (0.9, 0.999), and epsilon
349   set to 1e−8 . The learning rate was fixed at 1e−3 , and the model was fine-tuned for 1000 epochs on
350   each query image.
351   Hyperparameter setting of Matcher/HQ-Matcher. We slightly modified the prompt filtering
352   options for Matcher and HQ-Matcher while keeping the other hyperparameters unchanged. This
353   adjustment was made to enhance the robustness of the selection process for accepted masks among
354   the proposal masks generated by Matcher, ultimately reducing the number of accepted masks. The
355   reduction aims to decrease the GPU resources required for computing gradient flow and to minimize
356   optimization errors caused by false positive candidate masks.
357   Hyperparameter setting of ReGRAF. We describe the hyperparameter settings used for ReGRAF.
358   Across all the datasets, we set the number of iterations T = 5, as equation (6) implies that the gradient
359   estimation in equation (12) becomes inaccurate with a large number of iterations. The coefficient
360   of the entropy regularization γ was set as 0.1 without any tuning process, while the step size η
361   was determined from 10 randomly sampled images from left-aside validation sets of COCO-20i
362   and PACO-part. Specifically, η of semantic segmentation was 0.001, and that of part segmentation
363
      was 0.0001. Since the tuning for semantic/part segmentation has already been completed during
      validation, these values were directly applied to other datasets or methods. Additionally, the number
364
      of points selected based on similarity (in the case of Matcher, the final number of clustering centers)
365
      was set as follows: for semantic segmentation, Matcher used 8 points, HQ-Matcher used 7 points,
366   and PerSAM-F and HQ-PerSAM-F extracted 5 points as prompts to perform the task. For part
367   segmentation, the number of points was reduced to 5 for the Matcher/HQ-Matcher and to 3 for the
368   PerSAM-F/HQ-PerSAM-F, to improve the localization of smaller objects. We used sim as proposed by
369   Matcher for Matcher, and as proposed by PerSAM-F for PerSAM-F. Finally, the gradients computed
370   during ReGRAF were clipped to ensure stability throughout the overall process.
371

508   alignment results from ReGRAF’s capability to refine the query image embeddings in relation to a

510   4.2.3   D ISCUSSION ON COMPUTATION OF R E GRAF.
511
512   ReGRAF requires at least 20GB and 8GB GPU memory when used with Matcher and PerSAM-
513   F respectively. We argue that the advantages of our approach outweigh the associated memory
514   consumption because ReGRAF enhances the quality of segmentation masks without requiring
515   additional learnable parameters and new training datasets for fine-tuning or modifications to the
516
      model architecture as in previous approaches. This efficiency not only reduces the complexity of the
      implementation but also facilitates broader applicability across various segmentation frameworks.
517
518   Moreover, the additional time required for gradient flow computation per image is minimal compared
519   to the total iteration time. For instance, the gradient update takes only 0.02 seconds per iteration, while
520   the running time for PerSAM-F with ReGRAF is 8.02 seconds (Tab. 3). Thus, the computational
521   overhead introduced by our method is negligible, further reinforcing its practicality and effectiveness
522
      in enhancing segmentation mask quality.

528   5    C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we introduce a novel training-free refinement method that enhances the quality of
531
      segmentation masks using a promptable segmentation model. Our method is widely applicable to
      auto-prompting frameworks, and we comprehensively evaluated the effectiveness of our approach
532
      through extensive quantitative and qualitative assessments, demonstrating its superiority. ReGRAF
533
      effectively refines visual clues for SAM’s mask decoder, allowing it to understand the semantics of
534   target objects within a scene. Furthermore, theoretical analysis on the convergence of ReGRAF and
535   the experiment results emphasize the robustness and adaptability of our method across both semantic
536   and part segmentation tasks. Our methodology stands out from existing approaches by eliminating
537   the need for learnable parameters, modifications to model architecture, and reliance on additional
538   training datasets. This is highly efficient, as it requires significantly less time for refinement. These
539   advantages highlight the practicality and effectiveness of our method in enhancing segmentation
      mask quality without the complexities.
