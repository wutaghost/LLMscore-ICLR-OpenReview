                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               We propose to use automatically generated instruction-following data to improve
               the zero-shot capabilities of a large multimodal model with additional support
011
               for generative and image editing tasks. We achieve this by curating a new mul-
012
               timodal instruction-following set using GPT-4V and existing datasets for image
013            generation and editing. Using this instruction set and the existing LLaVA-Finetune
014            instruction set for visual understanding tasks, we produce GenLLaVA, a Generative
015            Large Language and Visual Assistant. GenLLaVA is built through a strategy that
016            combines three types of large pretrained models through instruction finetuning:
017            Mistral for language modeling, SigLIP for image-text matching, and StableDiffu-
018            sion for text-to-image generation. Our model demonstrates visual understanding
019            capabilities superior to LLaVA and additionally demonstrates competitive results
020            with native multimodal models such as Unified-IO 2, paving the way for build-
021
               ing advanced general-purpose visual assistants by effectively re-using existing
               multimodal models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      The field of multimodal models has become increasingly popular in the research community as they
      are one of the key building blocks for general-purpose assistants (Achiam et al., 2023; Gemini Team
028
      et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023). One of the main directions researchers have pursued is to combine
029
      Large Language Models (LLMs) with Vision Models for multimodal tasks i.e. creating LVLMs. The
030   recently proposed LLaVA model (Liu et al., 2023b) is among the latest wave of works that have
031   demonstrated the effectiveness of instruction tuning for multimodal models (Liu et al., 2024a; Zhao
032   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Karamcheti et al., 2024). In these works, a two-stage pipeline is
033   followed: (1) multimodal pre-training where the unimodal models are combined and trained on a
034   large corpus of captioning data Schuhmann et al. (2022); Ordonez et al. (2011); and (2) a supervised
035   fine-tuning (SFT) Liu et al. (2024a;b) stage where the model is trained on domain-specific data and
036   enables it to better perform various downstream tasks of interest.
037   Visual generation is another research direction that combines visual and language modalities. There
038   are two common approaches for text-to-image generation. One approach employs diffusion mod-
039   els (Rombach et al., 2022), which have shown unprecedented performance in image synthesis,
040   becoming the de facto method for visual generation. The other line of work converts visual content
041   into discrete tokens using vector quantization (VQ) and then leverages an autoregressive transformer
042   for high-quality image synthesis (Chang et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022).
043   As visual understanding and generation capabilities advance rapidly and independently, a growing
044   trend is to combine these into a unified Large Multimodal Model (LMM). There are two main
045   approaches to achieving such unification. Many LMMs (Koh et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024b; Fu
046   et al., 2024) produce conditional embeddings to be used by a pretrained diffusion model for image
047   generation. On the other hand, there are LMMs (Lu et al., 2024a; Chameleon Team, 2024; Yu et al.,
048   2023) that adopt VQ encoders to project visual inputs into discrete tokens and use the same next-token
049   prediction paradigm as Language Models.
050   There has been a considerable amount of work building on top of the LLaVA model ranging from
051   image generation (Koh et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024b;a), grounding (You et al., 2024), image
052   editing (Fu et al., 2024) to video understanding (Lin et al., 2024). These works share the same
053   principles; they extend the ideas of visual instruction tuning to one or more capabilities. However,
      after adding a new capability (i.e., image generation), the resulting models often lose some, if not

154   existing pretrained models within a decoder-only architecture. This approach not only reduces
155   computational costs but also maintains competitive performance across benchmarks.

181   We append N visual tokens [IMG] after the instruction E, with their word embeddings being
182   trainable. The LMM learns to generate these tokens through its language modeling (LM) head.

184   between the language and vision modalities. We follow the same visual generation framework of
185   GILL (Koh et al., 2024) and MGIE (Fu et al., 2024) in extracting visual features, which we summarize
186   here for succinctness.
187   We employ a generation head T to convert [IMG] into concrete visual guidance. The model T is
188   a sequence-to-sequence model that translates the sequential visual tokens from the LMM into the
189   semantically meaningful latent set C = {c1 , c2 , . . . , cL } for visual guidance:

241   4.1   G ENERATIVE V ISUAL I NSTRUCTION DATA
242
243   Multimodal instruction tuning is a crucial process that equips the model with a wide range of skills and
244   capabilities across different modalities while also enabling it to adapt to novel and unique instructions.
245   We build the multimodal instruction tuning dataset by aggregating a diverse set of supervised datasets
246   and tasks. Each task is provided with a clear prompt, either by using existing prompts or crafting new
247   ones using GPT4-V.
248   Natural Language. [1.93%] We use the publicly available ShareGPT (ShareGPT, 2023) dataset,
249   which was used to train the Vicuna LLM (Chiang et al., 2023). This dataset contains mostly English
250   natural language conversations but also contains code and markdown. We filter inappropriate or
251   low-quality entries following the same methodology of Chiang et al. (2023). As a final preprocessing
252   step, entries that surpass 2048 tokens are truncated rather than split into multiple conversations. This
253   results in ∼40K conversations.
254   Image Editing. [9.63%] We create a subset of the Instruction Prompt-to-Prompt dataset
255   (IPr2Pr) (Brooks et al., 2023) for editing our editing data. We use ∼200K from the CLIP-filtered data
256   version of IPr2Pr, where editing instructions are generated by GPT-3, and images are synthesized by
257   the Prompt-to-Prompt model (Hertz et al., 2023).
258   Image Generation. [26.88%] For text-to-image generation, we use the same image & text pairs
259   that were used to pre-train the LLaVA model. This dataset, named LLaVA-Pretrain, is inverted
260   and presented to our model in the format (caption, image) with a dynamically pre-generated e.g.
261   “Please generate an image of caption”. These prefixes are created using the GPT4 language model.
262   This dataset contains ∼558K data points originally sourced from the LAION (Schuhmann et al.,
263   2022), SBU (Ordonez et al., 2011), and CC3M (Changpinyo et al., 2021) datasets and captioned by
264   the BLIP-2 model (Li et al., 2023).
265   Image Understanding.[61.56%] For image understanding, we combine the dataset used to fine-tune
266   the LLaVA model. This dataset, named LLaVA-Finetune, contains ∼665K samples. We also
267   add the LVIS-INSTRUCT4V (Wang et al., 2023a) dataset—a new visual instruction tuning dataset
268   constructed in the same way as the original LlaVA dataset but using GPT4-V (Achiam et al., 2023) as
269   the captioner instead of BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023). We remove duplicates from the resulting dataset;
      this results in ∼880K samples. We additionally add the following instruction datasets:

282
      In this section, we evaluate our model on a broad range of tasks that require visual understanding and
283
      generation. We do not perform task-specific finetuning in any experiments. The Supplementary
284
      section details additional results on GenLLaVA ’s instruction capabilities.
285
286   We adopt LLaVA-v1.5-7B (Liu et al., 2024a) architecture, then tune it on the constructed GVIT-mix-
287   2076K. We named this model GenLLaVA and it is made of the following components:
288
             • Image Processing & Visual Representations. We implement all image processing logic us-
289
               ing the default image transforms provided by torchvision and the TIMM library (Wight-
290            man, 2019). We normalize pixel values using the default ImageNet values. The default
291            backbone employed by all visual representations Encvis that we evaluate in this work is a
292            Vision Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021); we extract patch features from the penultimate
293            layer, following LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b).
294          • Vision-Language Projector. We use a simple 2-layer GELU MLP as the projector W,
295            which projects each patch independently into the embedding space of the language model.
296
             • Language Model. We choose the Mistral-7B LLM (Jiang et al., 2023). In order to combine
297
               the projected visual patch embeddings, we perform simple sequence-wise concatenation,
298            placing the patch embeddings before the text embeddings.
299
             • Visual Generation Head. The generation head T is a lightweight 4-layer encoder-decoder
300
               Transformer, which takes word embeddings e and hidden states h from the [IMG] tokens,
301
               as well as L learnable query tokens as the input and generates the visual latent U, we use
302            the L = 77, and the dimension of each ut ∈ U is 768.
303
304
             • Diffusion Image Decoder. We adopt Stable Diffusion v1.4 (SDv1.4) (Rombach et al., 2022)
               trained on 512×512 resolution. Similar to the visual encoder, the SD model is frozen without
305
               any modifications or training throughout the whole process.
306
307   We implement our training codebase in PyTorch. We train all models in BF16 mixed precision. For a
308   fair comparison, the rest of the model training protocol is kept unchanged from the original LLaVA.
309   Generative Visual Instruction tuning takes about 48 hours for both full-parameter tuning and LoRA
310   tuning on 8 NVIDIA Tesla A100 GPUs, each with 48GB memory, with DeepSpeed ZeRO Stage
311   3 (Rajbhandari et al., 2020) to distribute training across GPUs.
312   Single-stage training. Unlike its predecessor LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b), our model does not use a
313   two-stage training pipeline and instead directly finetunes the Vision-Language projector, the Language
314   model, and the Visual Generation Head. We found that the logical extension of the original pipeline—
315   a three-stage training pipeline—consisting of (1) multimodal alignment, (2) instruction tuning, and
316   (3) image generation tuning to teach the models progressively simply does not work as shown in
317   Table 2d where we tried two different variations of the original pipeline. The model performance on
318   visual understanding tasks decreases significantly. We instead choose a single-stage pipeline as it
319   has been shown to work previously in the work of Karamcheti et al. (2024). This comes with other
320
      unintentional advantages: training cost is reduced by 20%, and we can use the LLaVA-Pretrain
      data as image-to-text data instead of having to collect more.
321
322   Task Tokens. To perform unified learning on multimodal understanding and generation, we introduce
323   special tokens, which we name task tokens, to format the data. Specifically, we create the tokens
      [T2I] and [I2T] to indicate the learning (generation or understanding) task for the input sequence.

410   4.4   M AIN R ESULT
411
412   We evaluate GenLLaVA against a broad range of models across tasks involving advanced knowledge,
413   general understanding, editing, and generative capabilities, as summarized in Table 1. The results
414   demonstrate GenLLaVA’s robust performance despite its compact size of 7B parameters, often out-
415
      performing larger models. In advanced knowledge tasks such as MathVista and MMMU, GenLLaVA
      achieves the highest scores, matching the performance of significantly larger models like Emu2-34B
416
      and demonstrating its superior mathematical reasoning capabilities. For general understanding,
417
      GenLLaVA excels in datasets like SEED-B and MMB, showcasing its ability to generalize effectively
418   across diverse scenarios. It consistently surpasses competitors such as Unified-IO 2 and Emu-13B on
419   these benchmarks.
420
421
      Although specialized editing models like MGIE have a marginal advantage in image editing (EVR),
      GenLLaVA maintains competitive performance, demonstrating its adaptability as a generalist model.
422
      In visual generation, GenLLaVA achieves high alignment between generated images and prompts,
423
      with results closely matching Unified-IO 2 on CC3M and COCO benchmarks. Overall, GenLLaVA
424   balances multimodal capabilities without compromising performance in individual domains. Its con-
425   sistent strength across advanced reasoning, general understanding, and generative tasks emphasizes
426   its versatility and potential as a robust vision-language model.

440   4.6      A BLATIONS
441
442   We investigate the effect of scaling the data used to train GenLlaVA, the effect of using different
443
      image backbones, and the number of visual tokens used for image generation.
444   Instruction data. We start with the original instruction tuning dataset from LlaVA-1.5, basically
445   reproducing the original results using a one-stage training recipe. We add the LLaVA-Pretrain
446   and generation head to our model, and we notice that adding generation capabilities significantly
447   affects the visual understanding capabilities, with all metrics degrading between 2.3% (MM-Vet)
448   and 7.9% (MathVista). To compensate for this loss in performance, we modify the ratio of image
449
      generation to image understanding data in our dataset from ∼50%-50% to ∼70%-30%, by adding
      more image instruction data from LVIS-INSTRUCT4V, LRV-Instruction and other chart
450
      understanding datasets. This results in a model with significant generation capabilities that maintain
451
      its image-understanding capabilities. We finally add image-generation capabilities using our selected
452   subset of the IPr2Pr dataset. This reduces the image understanding capabilities, but we consider this
453   a small enough change that balances the three tasks while maintaining commendable performance.
454   Finally, task tokens are added to the resulting instruction set to condition the model on the desired
455   task. The results can be seen in Table 2a.
456
      Choice of Visual encoder. The quality of the vision encoder can also have big effects on the
457
      final LMM performance. We start by using a CLIP/B, which has the lowest performance, then we
458   compare against a stronger visual encoder and create versions of GenLLaVA, which is trained with
459   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and SigLIP (Zhai et al., 2023), respectively. We can see in Table 2b that
460   the SigLIP encoder generally achieves better performance than the CLIP encoder. This shows that
461   SigLIP is a better vision encoder for LMM development.
462
      Number of visual generation tokens. We experiment with varying the number of visual generation
463
      tokens, N , to determine the optimal number required for balancing image generation and understand-
464   ing tasks. We noticed that we need more visual generation tokens than GILL (N = 4) and MGIE
465   (N = 8) to achieve the best performance. We find that N = 16 is the best choice for our model. We
466   hypothesize that this is because our model has to balance image generation and editing in the same
467   head and thus needs more visual tokens to capture the complexity of the tasks. The results can be
468   seen in Table 2c.

470   4.7      C OMPARISON WITH THE STATE OF THE ART.
471
472   When compared with state-of-the-art models, GenLLaVA maintains similar performance to models
473   of the LLaVA family when evaluated using the average of the scores on the MathVista, MMMU,
474
      MMVet, SEED-B, and MMB datasets. However, it lags behind larger and more specialized models.
      However, some of these models lack the generative capabilities present in GenLLaVA.
475
476   When compared with models of similar size and setup (∼ 7b parameters), our model surpasses the
477   original LlaVAv1(Liu et al., 2023b) model by 9% points (37.5% vs. 46.9%), and LlaVA-1.5 (Liu
478   et al., 2024a) by ∼1% points (45.3% vs. 46.9%). It is surpassed by the LlaVA-Next (Liu et al., 2024b)
479   family of models by ∼4% points; by Idefics2 (Laurençon et al., 2024) by ∼8% points (55.7% vs.
480
      46.9%). Compared with the absolute state-of-the-art open-source models, GenLLaVA lags behind
      Yi-VL (Young et al., 2024) by ∼2% points; and surpasses Emu2 (Sun et al., 2024a) by ∼1% points.
481
      It lags behind LlaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024b) (34b) by ∼12% points, and InternVL 1.5 (Chen et al.,
482
      2024b) by ∼15% points. Compared with the absolute state-of-the-art closed models, GenLLaVA lags
483   behind GPT-4o by ∼26% points, GPT-4V (Achiam et al., 2023) by ∼20% points; and the Gemini
484   family (Gemini Team et al., 2023) of models by 13% points.

527   5   C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In this paper, we have introduced the Generative Large Language and Visual Assistant (GenLLaVA),
531
      a comprehensive framework for enabling Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) to excel simultaneously
      in image understanding, generation, and editing, while maintaining competitive performance. By
532
      balancing multimodal capabilities within a single model through the curation of a diverse multimodal
533
      instruction dataset and the development of an innovative single-phase training methodology, Gen-
534   LLaVA sets a new benchmark in the development of multimodal systems. Our results show that
535   unifying generation and understanding under a single framework is possible without compromising
536   their strengths. Our work sets a new standard for building visual assistants with extra capabilities,
537   and we hope our open-source contributions, including datasets, codebase, and model checkpoints,
538   will serve as valuable resources for the research community, driving further advancements in the field
539   of multimodal AI. GenLLaVA’s capabilities can be extended to video understanding, audio-visual
      tasks, and more advanced real-time multimodal interactions.

757
       A chat between a curious user and an artificial intelligence assistant. The assistant gives helpful, detailed, and politely answers to the user's questions.
758
       (a) VQA (Short)
759
       USER: Based on the image, please answer the question. <IMAGE> <QUESTION> Please provide an accurate answer within one word.
760    ASSISTANT: The answer is: <ANSWER>
761    (b) VQA (Long)
762    USER: This is an exam, please answer according to the image and question. <IMAGE> <QUESTION>
763    ASSISTANT: The answer is: <ANSWER>

764    (c) ADVANCED KNOWLEDGE

765    USER: This is a hard exam, please answer according to the image and the question. <IMAGE> <QUESTION> Please think step by step.
       ASSISTANT: The answer is: <ANSWER>
766
       (d) GENERATION
767
       USER: Generate an image with the following description. <DESCRIPTION>
768    ASSISTANT: <GENERATED IMAGE>
769    (e) EDITING
770    USER: Based on the image, please follow the instruction. <IMAGE> Edit the image according to the description. <DESCRIPTION>
771    ASSISTANT: <GENERATED IMAGE>
