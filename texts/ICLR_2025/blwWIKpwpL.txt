009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reward engineering is one of the key challenges in Reinforcement Learning (RL).
012            Preference-based RL effectively addresses this issue by learning from human
013            feedback. However, it is both time-consuming and expensive to collect human
014            preference labels. In this paper, we propose a novel Vision-Language Preference
015            learning framework, named VLP, which learns a vision-language preference model
016
               to provide preference feedback for embodied manipulation tasks. To achieve
               this, we define three types of language-conditioned preferences and construct
017
               a vision-language preference dataset, which contains versatile implicit prefer-
018
               ence orders without human annotations. The preference model learns to extract
019            language-related features, and then serves as a preference annotator in various
020            downstream tasks. The policy can be learned according to the annotated pref-
021            erences via reward learning or direct policy optimization. Extensive empirical
022            results on simulated embodied manipulation tasks demonstrate that our method
023            provides accurate preferences and generalizes to unseen tasks and unseen language
024            instructions, outperforming the baselines by a large margin. The code and videos
025            of our method are available on the website: https://VLPref.github.io.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Reinforcement Learning (RL) has made great achievements recent years, including board games (Sil-
030   ver et al., 2017; 2018), autonomous driving (Kiran et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2021), and robotic
031   manipulation (Kober et al., 2013; Andrychowicz et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022). However, one of the
032   key challenges to apply RL algorithms is reward engineering. First, designing an accurate reward
033   function requires large amount of expert knowledge. Second, the agent might hack the designed
034   reward function (Hadfield-Menell et al., 2017), obtaining high returns without completing the task.
035   Also, it is difficult to obtain reward functions for subjective human objectives.
036   To address the above issues, a variety of works leverage expert demonstrations for imitation learning
037   (IL) (Ho & Ermon, 2016; Torabi et al., 2018). Nevertheless, expert demonstrations are often expensive
038   and the performance of IL is limited by the quality of the demonstrations. Another line of work
039   leverages Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to provide multi-modal rewards for downstream policy
040   learning (Nair et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023a; Rocamonde et al., 2024). However, the reward labels
041   produced in these works are often of high variance and noisy (Ma et al., 2023a). Preference-based
042   RL is more promising way that learns from human preferences over trajectory pairs (Christiano et al.,
043   2017; Lee et al., 2021). On the one hand, we can learn a reward model from preferences and then
044
      optimize the policy according to the reward model (Christiano et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2023). On the
      other hand, the policy can be directly optimized according to the preferences (Hejna & Sadigh, 2023;
045
      Hejna et al., 2024).
046
047   However, preference-based RL requires either querying a large number of expert preference labels
048   online (Lee et al., 2021; Park et al., 2022) or a labeled offline preference dataset (Kim et al., 2023;
049   Hejna et al., 2024), which is quite time-consuming and expensive. Previous methods propose to use
050
      the reasoning abilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) to provide preference labels (Wang et al.,
      2024), but the generated labels are not guaranteed to be accurate and it is assumed to have access to
051
      the environment information.
052
053   In this paper, we propose a novel Vision-Language Preference alignment framework, named VLP, to
      provide preference feedback for video pairs given language instructions. Specifically, we collect a

089   Problem Setting. We formulate the RL problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) (Sutton
090   & Barto, 2018) represented as a tuple M = (S, A, P, R, γ, p0 ), where S is the state space, A
091   is the action space, P : S × A → S is the transition function, R : S × A → R is the reward
092   function, γ ∈ [0, 1) is the discount factor, and p0 : S → [0, 1] is the initial state distribution. At
093   timestep t, the agent observes a state st and selects an action at based on a policy π(at |st ). Then,
094   the agent receives a reward rt from the environment, and the agent transits to st+1 according to
095   the transition
                 P∞function. The agent’s goal is to find a policy that maximizes the expected cumulative
096   reward E [ t=0 γ t rt ]. In multi-task setting, for a task T ∼ p(T ), a task-specific MDP is represented
097   as MT = (S T , A, P T , RT , γ, pT0 ).
098
099   Preference-based RL. Preference-based RL differs from RL in that it is assumed to have no access
100

      teachers provide preference labels over trajectory pairs, and a reward model is learned from these
101

102

103   where 0 indicates σ 1 is preferred (i.e., σ 1 ≻ σ 2 ), 1 indicates σ 2 is preferred (i.e., σ 2 ≻ σ 1 ), and 0.5
104   represents two segments are equally preferred. Previous preference-based RL approaches construct a
105   preference predictor with the reward model rbψ via Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry, 1952):

133
      vision-language preference dataset. Then, we introduce language-conditioned preferences and the

138   3.1   F RAMEWORK
139
140   The goal of VLP is to learn a generalized preference model capable of providing preferences for novel
141   embodied tasks. To achieve this, the preference model receives videos and language as inputs, where
142   videos serve as universal representations of agent trajectories and language act as universal and flexible
143   instructions. To obtain high-quality representations of these two modalities, we utilize CLIP (Radford
144
      et al., 2021), which is pre-trained on extensive image-text data, as our video and language encoders.
      The extracted video and language features are fed into to a cross-modal transformer for cross-modal
145
      attention interaction to capture video features associated with the language and language features
146
      related to the video. These features are subsequently utilized for predicting preference scores in
147   vision-language preference learning. The overall framework is illustrated in Figure 2.
148
149   Model Architecture. A video v is represented as a sequence of video frames, i.e., v =
150   {v1 , v2 , . . . , v|v| }, where vi ∈ RH×W ×3 , H and W are the height and width of each video frame,
151   and |v| denotes the number of video frames. The video encoder is employed to obtain the video
152   tokens z = {z1 , z2 , . . . , z|v| }, where zi ∈ RM ×Dv , M = H/p × W/p is the number of visual tokens,
153   p is the patch size of CLIP ViT, and Dv is the dimension of the visual tokens. Given language input l,
154   the language tokens u ∈ RN ×Dl are obtained via the language encoder, where N is the number of
155   language tokens, and Dl is the dimension of the language tokens.
156   With video tokens z and language tokens u, a cross-modal encoder is employed to facilitate multi-
157   modal feature learning, making tokens of different modalities fully fuse with each other. Video
158   tokens and language tokens are separately inputted into the self-attention layers. Then, utilizing the
159   output video tokens as queries and the output language tokens as keys and values, the cross-attention
160   layer, as shown in Figure 2(b), generates language features that are closely related to the input video.
161   Similarly, the cross-attention layer produces language-related video features. The multi-modal tokens
      are averaged along the first dimension and then concatenated as w ∈ RDw , where Dw = Dv + Dl .

      These new tokens are fed into the final Multi-layer Perceptron (MLP) for vision-language preference
163   prediction, outputting a trajectory-level preference score.

      Vision-Language Preference Dataset. While there are open-sourced embodied datasets with
166   language instructions (Mu et al., 2023), there lacks a multi-modal preference dataset for generalized
167   preference learning. To this end, we construct MTVLP, a multi-task vision-language preference
168   dataset built upon Meta-World (Yu et al., 2020). To that end, we consider the following aspects:
169   (i) trajectories of various optimality levels should be collected to define clear preference relations
170   within each task; (ii) each trajectory pair should be accompanied with a corresponding language
171   instruction for learning language-conditioned preferences.
172   It is easy to describe the optimality of expert trajectories and random trajectories because it is easy to
173   understand the agent’s behavior in these trajectories. However, it is challenging to define a medium-
174   level policy without explicit rewards. Fortunately, we find most robot tasks can be divided into
175   multiple stages, where each stage completes a part of the overall task. Thus, we define a medium-
176   level policy as successfully completing half of the stages of the task. For example, we divided the
177   task of opening the drawer into two subtasks: (i) moving and grasping the drawer handle and (ii)
178   pulling the drawer handle. A medium-level policy only completes the first subtask.
179   We leverage a scripted policy for each task to roll out trajectories of three optimality levels: expert,
180   medium, and random. For expert-level trajectories, we employ the scripted policy with Gaussian
181   noise to interact. The medium-level trajectories are also collected with the scripted policy but are
182   terminated when the half of subtasks are completed. As for random-level trajectories, actions are
183   randomly sampled from a uniform distribution during rollout. For the corresponding language, we
184   obtain diverse language instructions to improve the generalization abilities of our model by aligning
185   one video with multiple similar language instructions. Following (Adeniji et al., 2023), we query
186
      GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023) to generate language instructions with various verb structure examples and
      synonym nouns of each task. Details of collecting trajectories and language instructions for each task
187
      are shown in Appendix B.

      3.2   V ISION -L ANGUAGE P REFERENCE A LIGNMENT
191
      Language-conditioned Preferences. Previous RLHF methods define trajectory preferences ac-
192   cording to a single task goal. However, this uni-modal approach struggles to generalize to new tasks
193   due to its rigid preference definition. In contrast, by integrating language as a condition, we can
194   establish more flexible preference definitions. Consider two videos, v11 and v21 , along with a language
195   instruction l1 from task T 1 , and another video v 2 paired with a language instruction l2 from task
196   T 2 . We categorize three forms of language-conditioned preferences: Intra-Task Preference (ITP),
197   Inter-Language Preference (ILP), and Inter-Video Preference (IVP), as shown in Table 1.
198
199                        Table 1: Three types of language-conditioned preferences.
200
201               Preference Type              Videos                  Language   Criterion
202               Intra-Task Preference        v11 , v21 ∼ T 1         1
                                                                       l ∼T   1
                                                                                  optimality
203               Inter-Language Preference    v11 , v21 ∼ T 1         l2 ∼ T 2   equally preferred
204               Inter-Video Preference       v11 ∼ T 1 , v12 ∼ T 2   l1 ∼ T 1   v11 ≻ v21 |l1
205
206   ITP corresponds to the conventional case of preference relation within the same task (Christiano et al.,
207   2017), where the videos and language instructions are from the same task, and the preference relies
208   on the optimality of videos w.r.t. the task objective. ILP considers a scenario where the language
209   instruction differs from the task of the videos. Thus, both videos are equally preferred under this
210   language condition. IVP deals with preferences of two videos from different tasks, with the language
211   instruction from either task. It is straightforward to define the preference that the vision-language
212
      come from the same task is preferred to the other pair.
213   This framework allows for the establishment of universal and adaptable preference relations, wherein
214   videos from the same task can yield varying preference labels depending on the language condition.
215   Notably, even random trajectories paired with language instructions from a specific task is preferred
      to expert trajectories from other tasks.

      Vision-Language Preference Learning. With language-conditioned preferences defined above,
217   we further introduce our vision-language preference learning algorithm. We aim to develop a vision-
218   language preference model that predicts the preferred video under specific language conditions.
219   However, directly inputting two videos and a language instruction into the model would affect compu-
220   tational efficiency. So, we consider the conventional way to learn from preference labels (Christiano
221   et al., 2017), i.e., first constructing preference predictors via Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry,
222   1952). Previous work has revealed the advantages of learning a preference model over a reward
223   model (Zhang et al., 2024). Based on these insights, our proposed preference model fψ (v|l) takes a
224   video and a language instruction as inputs and outputs a scalar preference score. Then the preference
225
      label can be obtained by comparing preference scores of two videos with a given language instruction,
      i.e., v1 ≻ v2 |l if fψ (v1 |l) > fψ (v2 |l).
226
227   Given videos v1 representing σ1 and v2 representing σ2 , the language-conditioned preference distri-
228   bution Pψ [v1 ≻ v2 |l] is the probability that σ1 is preferred over σ2 under the condition l:

      Vision-Language Models for Reinforcement Learning. Our work is related to the literature on
281   VLM rewards and preferences for embodied manipulation tasks (Radford et al., 2021; Nair et al.,
282   2023; Ma et al., 2023a; Rocamonde et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a). These methods
283   can be divided into three categories: (i) representation-based pre-training, (ii) zero-shot inference,
284   and (iii) downstream fine-tuning. For representation-based approaches, R3M (Nair et al., 2023) is pre-
285   trained on the Ego4D dataset (Grauman et al., 2022) to learn useful representations for downstream
286   tasks. LIV (Ma et al., 2023b), which extends VIP (Ma et al., 2023b) to multi-modal representations, is
287   pre-trained on EpicKitchen dataset (Damen et al., 2018), and can also be fine-tuned on target domain.
288   For zero-shot inference methods, VLM-RM (Rocamonde et al., 2024) utilizes CLIP (Radford et al.,
289
      2021) as zero-shot vision-language rewards. RoboCLIP (Sontakke et al., 2023) uses S3D (Xie et al.,
      2018), which is pre-trained on HowTo100M dataset (Miech et al., 2019), as video-language model to
290
      compute vision-language reward with a single demonstration (a video or a text). RL-VLM-F (Wang
291
      et al., 2024) leverages Gemini-Pro (Team et al., 2023) and GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023) for zero-shot
292   preference feedback. CriticGPT (Liu et al., 2024a) is the representative method of (iii), which
293   fine-tunes multimodal LLMs on a instruction-following dataset, and utilizes the tuned model to
294   provide preference feedback for downstream policy learning. VLP differs from these approaches that
295   we do not suffer from burdensome training of (i) and (iii), showing great computing efficiency. And
296   VLP learns more embodied manipulation knowledge compared with VLMs pre-trained on natural
297   image-text data.
298
299   Preference-based Reinforcement Learning. Preference-based RL is a promising framework
300   for aligning the agent with human values. However, feedback efficiency is a crucial challenge in
301   preference-based RL, with multiple recent studies striving to tackle. PEBBLE (Lee et al., 2021)
302
      improves the efficiency by unsupervised pre-training. SURF (Park et al., 2022) proposes to obtain
      pseudo labels using reward confidence. RUNE (Liang et al., 2022) employs reward uncertainty to
303
      guide exploration. Meta-Reward-Net (Liu et al., 2022) takes advantage of the performance of the Q-
304
      function as an additional signal to refine the accuracy of the reward model. Hejna III & Sadigh (2023)
305   leverages meta-learning to pre-train the reward model, enabling fast adaptation to new tasks with few
306   preference labels. Recently, a growing number of studies focus on offline preference-based RL with
307   the population of offline RL (Levine et al., 2020; Kostrikov et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2024). PT (Kim
308   et al., 2023) introduces a Transformer-based architecture for reward modeling. OPPO (Kang et al.,
309   2023) proposes to learn policies without a reward function. IPL (Hejna & Sadigh, 2023) learns the
310   Q-function from preferences, also eliminating the need of reward learning. CPL (Hejna et al., 2024)
311   further views preference-based RL as a supervised learning problem, directly learning policies from
312   preferences. FTB (Zhang et al., 2024) introduces a diffusion model for better trajectory generation.
313
      PEARL (Liu et al., 2024b) proposes cross-task preference alignment to transfer preference labels
      between tasks and learn reward models robustly via reward distributional modeling. VLP addresses
314
      the labeling cost by learning a vision-language preference model via vision-language alignment,
315
      thereby providing generalized preferences to novel tasks.

      Baselines. We evaluate VLP by combining it with recent offline RLHF algorithms: (i) P-IQL
352
      (Preference IQL), which first learns a reward model from preferences and then learns a policy via
353   IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2022); (ii) IPL (Hejna & Sadigh, 2023), which learns a policy without reward
354   learning by aligning the Q-function with preferences; (iii) CPL (Hejna et al., 2024), which directly
355   learns a policy using a contrastive objective with maximum entropy principle, eliminating the need
356   for reward learning and RL.
357
358   Evaluation. For each evaluation task, we train each RLHF method with scripted labels (Christiano
359   et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021) and VLP labels (denoted as +VLP), respectively. Scripted preference
360   labels mean the preference labels computed based on the ground-truth rewards (Christiano et al.,
361   2017; Lee et al., 2021). The number of preference labels is set to 100 for all tasks. The evaluation is
362   conducted over 25 episodes every 5000 steps. Following (Hejna et al., 2024), we average the results
363
      of 8 neighboring evaluations and take the maximum value among all averaged values as the result.
      Detailed hyperparameters of RLHF algorithms can be found in Appendix C.

368
      Window Open may not accurately represent the task goal (Ma et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024). However,

516   6    C ONCLUSION
517
518   In this paper, we propose VLP, a novel vision-language preference learning framework providing
519   generalized preference feedback for embodied manipulation tasks. In our framework, we learn a
520
      vision-language preference model via proposed language-conditioned preference relations from the
      collected vision-language preference dataset. Experimental results on multiple simulated robotic
521
      manipulation tasks demonstrate that our method exceeds previous VLM rewards approaches and
522
      predicts accurate preferences compared with scripted labels. The results also show our method
523   generalizes well to unseen tasks and unseen language instructions.
524
525   Limitations In this paper, we focus on providing preferences for robotic manipulation tasks. First,
526   VLP is limited to the tasks that can be specified via videos and language instructions. While this
527   covers a wide range of robotic tasks, certain tasks cannot be fully expressed via videos and language,
528   such as complex assembly tasks requiring intricate spatial reasoning. Consequently, the risk of
529   predicting incorrect preferences grows for complex tasks that are difficult to express. Second, if the
530   language instruction lacks sufficient information of the task goal, the risk of giving incorrect labels
531   still grows, as shown in Table 6. Last, the theoretical analysis assumes access to all possible segments,
532
      which introduces a gap between the theoretical guarantees and empirical applications.

801   (Lambert et al., 2024) is more difficult. To this end, we propose a novel architecture that adopts a
802   video encoder to obtain the video tokens and mean pooling to obtain a video representation. Then, a
803   cross-modal encoder is employed to facilitate vision-language feature learning. In the architecture
804   design, a particular detail is we don’t use actions in the segment for regret learning. The reason is, as
805   shown in previous works (Du et al., 2023; Ajay et al., 2023), the actions can be easily reconstructed
806   by training an inverse-dynamic model from state sequence. As a result, the representation of videos
807   implicitly contains the information of actions.

1066   How do ILP and IVP benefit VLP? The inclusion of ILP and IVP in our training data serves
1067   critical roles in enhancing the generalization and robustness of our model. ILP allows our model to
1068
       learn to disregard language variations when they do not impact the preference outcomes, thus training
       the model to focus on task-relevant features rather than linguistic discrepancies. On the other hand,
1069
       IVP facilitates the model’s ability to generalize across different tasks by learning to associate videos
1070
       with their corresponding task-specific language instructions effectively. This capability is crucial
1071   when the model encounters new tasks or language contexts, as it must discern relevant from irrelevant
1072   information to make accurate preference predictions. By training with both ILP and IVP, our model
1073   learns a more holistic understanding of the task space, which not only improves its performance on
1074   seen tasks but also enhances its adaptability to new, unseen tasks or variations in task descriptions, as
1075   evidenced by our experimental results where the model demonstrated generalization capabilities.
