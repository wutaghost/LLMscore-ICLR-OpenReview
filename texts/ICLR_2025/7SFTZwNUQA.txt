011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models have achieved excellent success in solving inverse problems due
014            to their ability to learn strong image priors, but existing approaches require a large
015
               training dataset of images that should come from the same distribution as the test
               dataset. When the training and test distributions are mismatched, artifacts and hal-
016
               lucinations can occur in reconstructed images due to the incorrect priors. In this
017
               work, we systematically study out of distribution (OOD) problems where a known
018            training distribution is first provided. We first study the setting where only a single
019            measurement obtained from the unknown test distribution is available. Next we
020            study the setting where a very small sample of data belonging to the test distribu-
021            tion is available, and our goal is still to reconstruct an image from a measurement
022            that came from the test distribution. In both settings, we use a patch-based dif-
023            fusion prior that learns the image distribution solely from patches. Furthermore,
024            in the first setting, we include a self-supervised loss that helps the network output
025            maintain consistency with the measurement. Extensive experiments show that in
026
               both settings, the patch-based method can obtain high quality image reconstruc-
               tions that can outperform whole-image models and can compete with methods that
027
               have access to large in-distribution training datasets. Furthermore, we show how
028
               whole-image models are prone to memorization and overfitting, leading to arti-
029            facts in the reconstructions, while a patch-based model can resolve these issues.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In image processing, inverse problems are of paramount importance and consist of reconstructing
035   a latent image x from a measurement y = A(x) + ε. Here, A represents a forward operator
036   and ε represents random unknown noise. By Bayes’ rule, log p(x|y) is proportional to log p(x) +
037   log p(y|x), so obtaining a good prior p(x) is crucial for recovering x when y contains far less
038   information than x. Diffusion models obtain state-of-the-art results for learning a strong prior and
039
      sampling from it, so similarly competitive results can be obtained when using them to solve inverse
      problems (Chung et al., 2022a; 2023a; Song et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2022; Kawar et al., 2021; Li
040
      et al., 2023a).
041
042   However, training diffusion models well requires vast amounts of clean training data (Song et al.,
043   2021; Ho et al., 2020), which is infeasible to collect in many applications such as medical imaging
044   (Chung et al., 2022b; Song et al., 2022; Jalal et al., 2021), black hole imaging (Feng et al., 2023;
045
      2024), and phase retrieval (Li et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2019). In particular, no ground truth images
      are known for very challenging inverse problems such as black hole imaging (Feng et al., 2023)
046
      and Fresnel phase retrieval (Gureyev et al., 2004), so one must obtain a reconstruction from only
047
      a single measurement y. In other applications such as dynamic CT reconstruction (Reed et al.,
048   2021) and single photo emission CT (Li et al., 2023b), obtaining a high quality measurement that
049   can lead to a reconstruction that closely approximates the ground truth can be slow or potentially
050   harmful to the patient, so only a very small dataset of clean images are available. Thus, in this paper
051   we consider two settings: the single measurement setting in which we are given one measurement
052   y whose corresponding x belongs to a different distribution from the training dataset, and the small
053   dataset setting in which we are only given a small number of samples x that belong to the same
      distribution as the test dataset.

054
      In recent years, some works have aimed to address these problems by demonstrating that diffusion
055   models have a stronger generalization ability than other deep learning methods (Jalal et al., 2021), so
056   slight distribution mismatches between the training data and test data may not significantly degrade
057   the reconstructed image quality. However, in cases of particularly compressed or noisy measure-
058   ments, as well as when the test data is severely out of distribution (OOD) with a significant domain
059   shift, an improper choice of training data leads to an incorrect prior that causes substantial image
060   degradation and hallucinations (Feng et al., 2023; Barbano et al., 2023). To address these challenges
061   in the single measurement case, recent works use each measurement y to adjust the weights of a
062   diffusion network at reconstruction time Barbano et al. (2023); Chung & Ye (2024), aiming to shift
063
      the underlying prior learned by the network toward the appropriate prior for the latent image in the
      test case. However, as the networks have huge numbers of weights, an intricate and parameter-
064
      sensitive refining process of the network is required during reconstruction to avoid overfitting to the
065
      measurement. Furthermore, there is still a substantial gap in performance between methods using an
066   OOD prior and methods using an in-distribution prior. Finally, these methods have only been tested
067   in medical imaging applications (Barbano et al., 2023; Chung & Ye, 2024). On the other hand, in
068   the small dataset case, various methods (Moon et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024) have been devised
069   to fine-tune a diffusion model on an OOD dataset, but these methods still require several hundred
070   images and have not used the fine-tuned network to solve inverse problems.
071
      Patch-based diffusion models have shown success both for image generation (Wang et al., 2023;
072   Ding et al., 2023) and for inverse problem solving (Hu et al., 2024). In particular, the method
073   of Hu et al. (2024) involves training networks that take in only patches of images at training and
074   reconstruction time, learning priors of the entire images from only priors of patches. In cases of
075   limited data, Hu et al. (2024) shows that patch-based diffusion models outperform whole image
076   models for solving certain inverse problems. These works motivate our key insight that patch-
077   based diffusion priors potentially obtain stronger generalizability than whole-image diffusion priors
078   for both the single measurement setting and the small dataset setting due to a severe lack of data.
079   Inspired by this, we propose to utilize patch-based diffusion models to tackle the challenges arising
080
      from mismatched distributions and lack of data in a unified way. We first develop a method to
      take a network trained on patches of a mismatched distribution and adjust it on the fly in the single
081
      measurement setting. We also show how in the small dataset setting, fine-tuning a patch-based
082
      network results in a much better prior than fine-tuning a whole-image network, leading to higher
083   quality reconstructed images.
084
085   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
086
             • We integrate the patch-based diffusion model framework with the deep image prior (DIP)
087
               framework to correct for mismatched distributions in the single measurement setting. Ex-
088            perimentally, we find this approach beats using whole-image models in terms of quantita-
089            tive metrics and visual image quality in image reconstruction tasks, as well as achieving
090            competitive results with methods using in-distribution diffusion models.
091
             • We show that in the small dataset setting, fine-tuning patch-based diffusion models is much
092
               more robust than whole-image models and very little data is required to obtain a reasonable
093            prior for solving inverse problems.
094
095
             • We demonstrate experimentally that when fine-tuning on very small datasets, whole im-
               age diffusion models are prone to overfitting and memorization, which severely degrades
096
               reconstructed images, while patch-based models are much less sensitive to this problem.

138   Methods without clean training data. When no in-distribution data is available, one approach is to
139   use traditional methods that do not require any training data, such as total variation (TV) (Li et al.,
140
      2019) or wavelet transform (Daubechies, 1992) regularizers that encourage image sparsity. More
      recently, plug and play (PnP) methods have risen in popularity (Sun et al., 2021; Sreehari et al.,
141
      2016; Hong et al., 2020; 2024b); these methods use a denoiser to solve general inverse problems.
142
      Although these methods often use a trained denoiser, Ryu et al. (2019) found that using an off-
143   the-shelf denoiser such as block matching 3D (Dabov et al., 2006) can yield competitive results.
144   Nevertheless, with the rise of deep learning in image processing applications, methods that harness
145   the power of these tools may be desirable.
146
      The deep image prior (DIP) is an extensively studied self-supervised method that is popular when no
147
      training data is available and reconstruction from a single meausurement y is desired. The method
148
      consists of training a network fθ using the loss function
149
150
                                   L(θ) = ∥y − A(fθ (z))∥22 , z ∼ N (0, I),                                (5)
151   so that fθ (z) produces the reconstruction. Although the neural network acts as an implicit regular-
152   izer whose output tends to lie in the manifold of clean images, DIP is prone to overfitting (Ulyanov
153   et al., 2020). Various methods have been proposed involving early stopping, regularization, and net-
154
      work initialization (Liu et al., 2018; Jo et al., 2021; Barbano et al., 2022). Nevertheless, the method
      is very sensitive to parameter selection and implementation and can take a long time to train (Jo
155
      et al., 2021).
156
157   Most DIS methods learn a prior from a large collection of clean in-distribution training images,
158   but recently Barbano et al. (2023) and Chung & Ye (2024) proposed self-supervised diffusion model
159   methods that are based off the DIP framework. These methods involve alternating between the usual
160
      reverse diffusion update step to gradually denoise the image and a network refining step in which
      the score network parameters are updated via the loss function

173   Diffusion model fine-tuning. In the small dataset setting, various fine-tuning methods exist to shift
174   the underlying prior learned by a score network away from a mismatched distribution and toward a
175   target distribution. Given a pretrained diffusion network on a mismatched distribution, Moon et al.
176   (2022), Zhang et al. (2024), and Zhu et al. (2024) among others have studied ways to fine-tune
177   the network to the desired dataset. These methods generally involve freezing certain layers of the
178
      original network, appending extra modules that contain relatively few weights, or modifying the loss
      function to capture details that differ greatly between distributions. However, these methods usually
179
      still require thousands of images from the desired distribution and focus on image generation. When
180
      solving inverse problems, the reconstructed image should be consistent with the measurement y,
181   reducing the number of degrees of the freedom for the image compared to generation, so with proper
182   fine-tuning the data requirement should be lower.

299   4     E XPERIMENTS
300
301   Experimental setup. For the CT experiments, we used the AAPM 2016 CT challenge data from
302   McCollough et al. (2017). We applied the same data processing methods as in Hu et al. (2024) with
303
      the exception that we used all the XY slices from the 9 training volumes to train the in distribution
      networks, yielding a total of approximately 5000 slices. For the deblurring and superresolution
304
      experiments, we used the CelebA-HQ dataset (Liu et al., 2015) with each image having size 256 ×
305
      256. The test data was a randomly selected subset of 10 of the images not used for training. In all
306   cases, we report the average metrics across the test images: peak SNR (PSNR) in dB, and structural
307   similarity metric (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004). For the training data, we trained networks on generated
308   phantom images consisting of randomly placed ellipses of different shapes and sizes. See Fig. 20
309   for examples. These phantoms can be generated on the fly in large quantities. We used networks
310   trained on grayscale phantoms for the CT experiments and networks trained on RGB phantoms for
311   the deblurring and superresolution experiments. Appendix A.4 contains precise specifications of the
312   phantoms.
313   We trained the patch-based networks with 64 × 64 patches and used a zero padding value of 64,
314   so that 5 patches in both directions were used to cover the target image. We used the network
315   architecture in Karras et al. (2022) for both the patch-based networks and whole-image networks.
316   All networks were trained on PyTorch using the Adam optimizer with 2 A40 GPUs.
317
      Single measurement setting. In cases where no training data is available and we only have the
318   measurement y, we applied Algorithm 1 to solve a variety of inverse problems: CT reconstruction,
319   deblurring, and superresolution. For the forward and backward projectors in CT reconstruction, we
320   used the implementation provided by the ODL Team (2022). We performed two sparse-view CT
321   (SVCT) experiments: one using 20 projection views, and one using 60 projection views. Both of
322   these were done using a parallel beam forward projector where the detector size was 512 pixels. For
323   the deblurring experiments, we used a uniform blur kernel of size 9 × 9 and added white Gaussian
      noise with σ = 0.01 where the clean image was scaled between 0 and 1. For the superresolution ex-

      5     C ONCLUSION
478
479
      This paper presented a method of using patch-based diffusion models to solve inverse problems
480   when the data distribution is mismatched from the trained network. In particular, we conducted
481   experiments in the setting when only a single measurement is available as well as the setting when
482   a very small subset of in-distribution data is available. In both settings, the proposed patch-based
483   method outperformed whole-image methods in a variety of inverse problems. In the future, more
484   work could be done on using acceleration methods for faster reconstruction, exploring other less
485   computationally expensive methods of fine-tuning the network geared toward inverse problem solv-
      ing, and methods of refining the prior when a set of measurements are available (Yaman et al., 2020).

      A.3   A BLATION STUDIES
929
930   We performed four ablation studies to evaluate the impact of various parameters on the proposed
931   methods. Similar to the main text, all quantitative results are averaged across the test dataset.
932
      Low rank adaptation. To avoid overfitting to the measurement in self-supervised settings, Barbano
933
      et al. (2023) proposed using a low rank adaptation to the weights of the neural network, reducing
934
      the number of weights that are adjusted during reconstruction by a factor of around 100. Here we
935   investigate the effect of using different ranks of adaptations on two inverse problems: 60 view CT
936   reconstruction and deblurring. Consistent with Barbano et al. (2023) and Chung & Ye (2024), we
937   only used the LoRA module for attention and convolution layers. We also allowed the biases of the
938   network to be changed.
939
      Tables 6 and 7 show the quantitative results of these experiments, where a rank of “full” represents
940
      fine-tuning all the weights of the network. In all cases, using LoRA for this fine-tuning process
941   results in worse reconstructions than simply fine-tuning the entire network. The visual results are
942   especially apparent in Figure 15: the reconstructed image becomes oversmoothed when using LoRA
943   and artifacts become present when using the whole image model. This is likely due to the large
944   distribution shift between the initial distribution of images and target distribution of faces: the low
945   rank adaptation to the mismatched network is not sufficient to represent the new distribution and
946   thus the self-supervised loss function results in smoothed images.
