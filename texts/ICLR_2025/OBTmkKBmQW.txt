034                                               A BSTRACT
035
036            Generating videos guided by camera trajectories poses significant challenges in
037            achieving consistency and generalizability, particularly when both camera and
038            object motions are present. Existing approaches often attempt to learn these
039            motions separately, which may lead to confusion regarding the relative motion
040            between the camera and the objects. To address this challenge, we propose a
041            novel approach that integrates both camera and object motions by converting them
042
               into the motion of corresponding pixels. Utilizing a stable diffusion network, we
               effectively learn reference motion maps in relation to the specified camera trajectory.
043
               These maps, along with an extracted semantic object prior, are then fed into an
044
               image-to-video network to generate the desired video that can accurately follow
045            the designated camera trajectory while maintaining consistent object motions.
046            Extensive experiments verify that our model outperforms SOTA methods by a large
047            margin. Please visit our anonymous project page to watch the generated videos.

050   1    I NTRODUCTION
051
052   Through extensive and in-depth research on diffusion models(Song et al., 2020; Saharia et al., 2022;
053   Yu et al., 2023), researchers have significantly improved the quality and diversity of video generation.
      Recent models, such as DynamicCrafter (Xing et al., 2023), exhibit the ability to generate long,

054
      high-quality videos with complex dynamics. However, these methods focus on prompts like texts,
055   images, depth maps, contour maps, human pose or projection maps(Guo et al., 2023a; Hu et al., 2023;
056   Guo et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023; Mou et al., 2023; Peruzzo et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023; Zhang &
057   Agrawala, 2023; Hu et al., 2023), often neglecting the precise control over camera motions, which is
058   essential in applications such as film production, virtual reality (VR), and augmented reality (AR).
059   These fields require not only high-quality video contents but also precise adherence to specified
060   camera movements to achieve the desired visual effects.
061   To enable precise camera control in video generation, several strategies have been developed to
062   integrate trajectory information into generative networks. Although they have made certain progress
063   in improving the flexibility in control and video quality, the generalizability and consistency still need
064   improvement. AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023b) aggregate camera information through LoRA (J. et al.,
065   2022) to conditionally guide video generation along specific directions. However, its flexibility is
066   limited when handling user-customized camera trajectories. MotionCtrl (Wang et al., 2024c) utilizes
067   the camera transformation matrix as trajectory information and injects it into the generative model.It
068   employs separate modules to learn camera and object motions, allowing independent motion control
069
      in small-scale scenes such as indoor scenes. Nevertheless, Training the two types of motion separately
      while ignoring their relationship may lead to confusion regarding the relative positions of objects and
070
      the scene. Additionally, its relatively simple encoding of camera information restricts its applicability
071
      to precise camera control for large-scale outdoor scenes. CameraCtrl (He et al., 2024) adopts plücking
072   embeddings (Sitzmann et al., 2021) as the primary camera trajectory representation, which enhances
073   the implicit mapping between camera motion and pixels. However, the generalizability is still limited,
074   as it struggles to generate videos that differ substantially from the training data. Additionally, these
075   methods primarily support text prompt inputs, which often fail to accurately convey the visual details
076   of the desired video.
077
      To address the above issues, we propose MotionFlow, a novel video generation network guided by
078   camera trajectories, which is capable of producing consistent and coherent 3D videos. Considering the
079   limited visual information provided by text guidance, we instead opt for image guidance to accurately
080   capture the desired video details. Unlike previous methods that modeled camera trajectories and
081   object motions separately to guide the video generation process, we adopt a pre-trained image stable
082   diffusion model that progressively and synchronously interacts with both camera motions and image
083   semantic features. This approach enables us to jointly encode semantic information as object-aware
084   motion priors during video generation. By mapping camera and object motions onto pixel trajectories
085   and training a network to learn these pixel movements, we effectively avoid the confusion associated
086
      with separate learning methods.
087   Our network is built on AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023b), a pre-trained image-to-video generation
088   framework, to leverage its high-quality video generation capabilities. To enable precise camera
089   trajectory-guided control and ensure the generalizability of the method across various scenarios, we
090   employ an image stable diffusion network to progressively fuse the features of the reference image
091   with the camera motion trajectory. This information is iteratively injected into the video generation
092
      network using cross attention mechanism as a pixel motion prior. Moreover, to directly enhance the
      geometric consistency of the synthesized videos, we implicitly learn the semantic object features from
093
      the reference image and incorporate them into the video generation network through cross-attention
094
      mechanism using these features. This approach improves the quality of the synthesized video frames
095   that contain the semantic objects during the iterative denoising steps of the diffusion model. Extensive
096   experimental results demonstrate that our method demonstrates superior 3D consistency, visual
097   quality, and camera trajectory controllability compared to previous approaches.
098
      We summarize our main contributions as follows:
099
100
             • We introduce a framework that iteratively uses an image diffusion network to learn implicit
101            pixel-level motion flows from camera trajectories and image guidance jointly, achieving
102            high-quality video generation results that adhere to the input camera trajectories.
103
104
             • We propose to extract semantic object information from the image guidance to enable the
               video generation network to be aware of these objects through object attention, thereby
105
               improving the quality of these objects’ pictures in the generated videos.
106
107          • We conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate the superiority of our MotionFlow network
               over SOTA methods both qualitatively and quantitatively.

111   2.1   C ONDITIONAL IMAGE TO VIDEO GENERATION
112
113   Conditional image to video (I2V) generation aims to synthesize videos guided by user-provided
114
      cues. Recent methods often leverage diffusion models for their stability in training and flexibility
      in manipulation. Ni et al. (2023) proposes to generate latent flow sequences using a non-pretrained
115
      diffusion model to animate images. Their work effectively synthesize motions like facial expression,
116
      human actions, and gestures against an almost static background. Shi et al. (2024) introduce a
117   two-stage training approach, each utilizing a diffusion model to generate explicit motion maps and
118   corresponding video. Although these methods could produce vivid motions in open-domain scenarios,
119   they can not incorporate explicit and precise camera control for the video generation task.
120
121
122   2.2   V IDEO GENERATION WITH CAMERA CONTROL
123
124   To facilitate camera control, most methods typically use text prompts and specific camera movement
125
      information to guide the generation of corresponding videos through cross-modal large models.
126   To effectively control camera motion, AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023b) employs the motion LoRA
127   modules to enable specific camera movements, though quantitative control can be challenging.
128   Focusing on the explicit control of both camera and object motion, MotionCtrl (Wang et al., 2024c)
129   achieves flexible motion control. However, its camera motion control module relies on 12 pose
130   matrix parameters as input, which may not sufficiently capture the geometric cues needed for precise
131
      camera control. Direct-a-video (Yang et al., 2024) proposes a camera embedder to manipulate camera
      poses, but it only conditions on some basic camera parameters(such as pan or zoom), limiting its
132
      control capabilities. In contrast, CameraCtrl (He et al., 2024) adopt plücker embeddings as the
133
      camera trajectory parameters, effectively injecting the camera information. Based on this embedding,
134   VD3D (Bahmani et al., 2024) and CamCo Xu et al. (2024) respectively introduce a ControlNet-like
135   conditioning mechanism and an epipolar attention module to better incorporates camera embeddings.
136   Our method further employs a twin diffusion model to encode these plücker embeddings, allowing for
137   the adaptive and progressive introduction of camera pose information during the iterative denoising
138   process of the diffusion model. Additionally, we enhance plücker embeddings into progressive
139   implicit embeddings, further improving global consistency and stability in the generated videos.
140   By adopting image prompts, our approach provides a more direct and accurate condition for genera-
141   tion. Moreover, it can be combined with cross-modal large models to facilitate multi-stage, text-driven
142   video generation.

147
      The core idea of our method involves the progressive injection of semantic and pixel-level motion
148
      information into a diffusion-based video generation network. As illustrated in Figure 2, we employ a
149
      reference motion network to extract pixel-level motion with semantic information from a reference
150   image and a camera trajectory, which serves as a motion flow to guide video generation through
151   reference attention. To further identify potential moving foreground objects and stationary back-
152   grounds within the scene, we introduce a semantic extractor to capture semantic information. This
153   information is then injected into the video generation network through both pixel-wise addition and
154   object attention. For the video generation network, we utilize the pre-trained model of AnimateDiff
155   (Guo et al., 2023b), enhancing it by incorporating reference attention and object attention in each
156   block of the UNet architecture.
157   This section is structured as follows: Section 3.1 provides a brief overview of the base video
158   generation network; Section 3.2 delves into the camera encoding information; Section 3.3 discusses
159   the integration of semantic information into the video generation backbone network; Section 3.4
160   explores the incorporation of pixel motion, generated by the reference motion network, into the video
161   generation framework to progressively guide the process; Finally, Section 3.5 presents a detailed
      analysis of pixel-level motion with semantic and camera motion information.

      3.2   T RAJECTORY E NCODER
209
      The trajectory of camera motion can be represented through the transformation of the camera between
210   various frames within a video sequence. This transformation can be succinctly described using the
211   rotation matrix R ∈ SO(3), and the translation matrix t ∈ R3 . MotionCtrl (Wang et al., 2024c)
212   represents camera motion by flattening camera parameters into 12D vectors and concatenating them
213   with time embeddings from the base video generation network. A lightweight fully connected network
214   then ensures dimensional consistency between the new combined embeddings and the original time
215   embeddings. However, this representation has limitations in accurately describing camera motion for
      two primary reasons: 1. Camera parameters inherently reflect characteristics of the camera space,


                                                                      4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      where rotation matrices and translation vectors carry distinct semantic meanings and should be treated
217   separately rather than flattened into a single vector. 2. Modeling the correlation between raw camera
218   parameters and pixel space is challenging, potentially limiting the model’s generalization capacity,
219   particularly for large-scale complex outdoor scenes. In order to better describe the camera pose, we
220   use plücker embeddings (Sitzmann et al., 2021) as the representation of camera trajectory. Given the
221   extrinsic and intrinsic camera parameters R, t, Kf for the f -th frame, we derive a plücker embedding
222   p̈f,h,w v ∈ R6 for each pixel located at (h, w). This embedding represents the vector from the camera
223   center to the pixel’s position as:

244   3.4      R EFERENCE M OTION N ETWORK
245
      Recent works mainly rely on textual prompts combined with camera features for the camera trajectory
246
      control in video generation (He et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c). However, due to the loss of
247
      scene details in the prompts, it is difficult for them to accurately learn the features to represent
248   the motion of foreground objects and background scenes, potentially leading to low-quality results.
249   Therefore, images are often preferred in I2V tasks since they can convey more fine-grained scene
250   details compared to textual prompts. Some methods (Chen et al., 2023; Xing et al., 2023) adopt
251   CLIP image features in place of CLIP text features and feed them into the diffusion network through
252   cross-attention to achieve image guidance. However, the CLIP features emphasize the text-image
253   alignment and are learned with low-resolution(224×224) image input, which still prioritize high-level
254   abstraction of images and ignore image details.
255   Another approach is to leverage the ControlNet architecture (Zhang et al., 2023) to achieve camera
256   trajectory control. While this approach can utilize different conditional information to guide video
257   generation, its key limitation is that the supplementary conditional information must be well-aligned
258   with the underlying image features to enable effective interaction. Yet, the trajectory information
259   in camera space and the features in image space exist in different domains, making it challenging
260   for ControlNet to directly convert the trajectory information into motion guidance that the video
261   generation network can understand. Furthermore, the lack of high-quality video data that contains
262
      precise camera poses also hinders the generalization capability of such approaches, as discussed in
      Section 4.2,.
263
264   Our idea is to introduce a separate reference motion network to integrate the camera motion trajectory
265   and the semantic information of the reference image, directly producing reference motion maps that
266   can be used in the image space. Specially, as shown in Figure 2, this network adopts a pretrained
267   image stable diffusion(SD1.5 Unet 1 ) architecture (Voleti et al., 2024) that incorporates both self and
268
      cross-attention layers within the spacial transformer. We inject the multi-scale camera features into

      3.5   V IDEO GENERATION N ETWORK
287
288   We adopt AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023b) as the foundation of our video generation network.
289   We utilize the camera-related frame-by-frame reference motion maps generated by the reference
290   motion network to guide pixel-level motion in the video generation process. Specifically, for each
291   block of the video generation network, given the feature map mi ∈ Rf ×c×h×w from the i-block
292   of the base network and the pixel motion pi ∈ Rf ×c×h×w from reference motion network, we
293   concatenate pi with mi along the w dimension to obtain m′i ∈ Rf ×c×h×2w . Then we perform
294
      cross-attention and extract the first half along the w dimension of the m′i as the input of the objection
      attention, referred to as reference attention. As described in Section 3.3, we subsequently employ
295
      the semantic encoder to capture semantic information, addressing potential moving foreground
296
      objects and stationary backgrounds. This information is injected into the video generation network
297   through addition and object attention operations, as illustrated in Figure 2. We not only integrate
298   semantic information during the noise input process but also compute an attention map as a semantic
299   mask between the semantic feature map and the output of reference attention, referred to as object
300   attention. Simultaneously, inspired by the architectural concepts of AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023b),
301   we apply temporal attention to the output of reference attention to consolidate consistency between
302   consecutive frames. Specifically, we reshape the input feature map mi ∈ Rc×f ×h×w to the shape
303   (h × w) × f × c. Then we calculate the self-attention across the temporal dimension f . The final
304   result is then pointwise multiplied with the semantic mask to produce the output of this block.

306   3.6   T RAINING S TRATEGY
307
      The video generation network is initialized using the pre-trained weights from AnimateDiff (Guo
308
      et al., 2023b), while the reference motion network is initialized with the pre-trained Stable Diffusion
309   V1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022) model. The semantic encoder is initialized from ViT-S based DINO
310   (Caron et al., 2021). All other modules are initialized to zero. The MotionFlow is trained using
311   the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015), 8× GPUs, and batch size of 1 per GPU. In the first
312   stage, we train the Trajectory Encoder and motion extractor with a learning rate of 1e−4 for one day,
313   keeping the reference motion network and video generation network fixed. Subsequently, we train
314   all parts for three days with a reduced learning rate of 1e−5 . We use RealEstate10K dataset (Zhou
315   et al., 2018) for training, which consists of 62 992 video clips for training and 7 391 video clips
316   for testing accompanied by diverse camera trajectories. To further evaluate the generalizability
317
      of our model, we test it on the large-scale outdoor dataset DL3DV-10k (Ling et al., 2024), which
      contains approximately 10 000 videos with known camera poses as well as the generation of videos
318
      for animals.

355   4.1   M ETRICS
356
357   We evaluate our method from two aspects: the alignment of camera trajectories and the visual quality
358   of the generated videos. The distance between predicted and ground-truth camera trajectories is used
359   to measure the alignment. Specifically, we predicted the camera trajectories for both the generated
360   and real videos using the same methods to eliminate the potential scale differences caused by different
361   Structure-from-Motion techniques. Considering the scale and differences between the rotation and
362
      translation parameters in the camera matrix, we use rotation error and translation error to assess
      them individually, following the approach of Wang et al. (2024c); He et al. (2024).
363
364   Four classical image-level quality metrics, including Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al.,
365   2017), SSIM (Wang et al., 2004), PSNR (Hore & Ziou, 2010) and LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018), are
366   used to evaluate the quality of the generated video frames, and video-level metric Fréchet Video
367   Distance (FVD) (Unterthiner et al., 2018) is further applied to assess video-level quality, similar
368
      to previous video generation methods(Wu et al., 2023; Khachatryan et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2023b;
      Ceylan et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2023a).

507   Analysis of Reference Motion Maps. After translating reference motion maps (RMMs) into images
508   through the decoder of the pre-trained VAE, we observe that these images seem to record the salient
509   object movements in generated videos, as shown in the Figure 6 (a). We thus hypothesize that RMMs
510   contain optical flow (OF) information, and conduct a toy experiment to verify it. First, we estimated
511   OFs from a generated video (de Armas, 2019), and then treated them and the corresponding RMMs as
512   data pairs. Here we choose the video shown in the second example of Figure 5 as an example, where
513   the first 12 pairs of RMMs and OFs are taken as the training set and the 2 pairs at the last two frames
514   as the test set. Second, a shallow network is adopted to translate the RMMs to OFs. The network
515
      consists of 3 deconvolution (Noh et al., 2015) layers for decoding features to image resolution and 2
      convolution layers for feature translation, as shown in Figure 6 (b). We trained the network for 1k
516
      epochs with a batch size of 12, L1 loss, and the Adam optimizer, taking approximately 40 seconds.
517
      As the results illustrated in Figure 6 (c), the OF information can be directly extracted from RMMs of
518   test frames. While this experiment verifies the correlation between RMMs and OFs, we argue that the
519   learned RMMs have more abundant information than OFs to facilitate the camera trajectory control.

      5      C ONCLUSION , L IMITATION , AND F UTURE W ORK
531
532   Through a comparative study with existing camera-guided video generation frameworks, such as
533   CameraCtrl and MotionCtrl, our method demonstrates superior performance in controlling camera
534   movements across different scales. Furthermore, our model also demonstrates a significant advantage
535   in image and video quality compared to other video generation approaches.
536   Although our method can generate high-quality dynamic scenes while considering potential object
537   motion during camera movement, it lacks explicit guidance for object motion control. In the future,
538   we plan to employ image or point-based motion guidance to precisely control the spatial object
539   movement and the changes over time, while maintaining the generation quality and camera trajectory
      alignment.
