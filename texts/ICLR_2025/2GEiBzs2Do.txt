                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Traditional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) tend to use 3 × 3 small ker-
010
               nels, but can only capture limited neighboring spatial information. Inspired by the
011            success of Vision Transformers (ViTs) in capturing long-range visual dependen-
012            cies, recent CNNs have reached a consensus on utilizing large kernel convolutions
013            (e.g., astonishingly, 111 kernel). Nevertheless, these approaches are unfriendly to
014            hardware, imposing a serious computation burden on training or inference. This
015            paper introduces a Simple and Fast Convolutional Neural Network (SFCNN) that
016            employs a sequence of stacked 3 × 3 convolutions but surpasses state-of-the-art
017            CNNs with larger kernels. In particular, we build a thin and deep model, which
018            encourages more 3 × 3 convolutions to capture more spatial information under
019
               the limited computing complexity rather than opting for a heavier and shallower
               architecture. To further enlarge the receptive field, we redesign the traditional in-
020
               verted residual bottleneck with two 3 × 3 depthwise convolutions. In addition,
021
               we propose a novel Global Sigmoid Linear Unit (GSiLU) activation function to
022            capture global coarse-grained spatial information. Our SFCNN performs better
023            than state-of-the-art CNNs and ViTs on various tasks, including ImageNet-1K
024            image classification, COCO instance segmentation, and ADE20K semantic seg-
025            mentation. It also has good scalability and outperforms existing state-of-the-art
026            lightweight models. All materials containing codes and logs have been included
027            in the supplementary materials.

038   viewed as a variant of the large kernel. Thus, some novel CNNs use large convolutional kernels to

      compete with ViTs. Both DWNet (Han et al., 2022) and ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022) obtain better

      2   R ELATED W ORK
151
152   Convolutional Neural Network Architectures. The introduction of AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al.,
153
      2012) marked a significant milestone in the rapid development of Convolutional Neural Networks
      (CNNs), with subsequent architectures (Szegedy et al., 2015; He et al., 2016; Szegedy et al., 2017)
154
      continually pushing the boundaries of performance. One recent trend in CNNs is the utilization of
155
      large convolutional kernels to achieve larger receptive fields and capture more long-range informa-
156   tion. ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022) has made a noteworthy discovery, revealing that scaling the kernel
157   size from 3×3 to 7×7 significantly contributes to performance. Similarly, DWNet (Han et al., 2022)
158   has reached a similar conclusion by replacing the local attention layer in Swin (Liu et al., 2021) with
159   a 7×7 DWConv. Following this large kernel design, some novel methods, such as VAN (Guo et al.,
160   2023), RepLKNet (Ding et al., 2022), ConvNeXt-1d++ Kirchmeyer & Deng (2023), SLaK (Liu
161   et al., 2023), PeLK (Chen et al., 2024), and ParCNetV2 (Xu et al., 2023), have also demonstrated
      impressive outcomes in many vision tasks, employing even larger kernel sizes from 21 to even 111.

162
      Other architectures, like InceptionNeXt (Yu et al., 2024), FocalNet (Yang et al., 2022), and UniRe-
163   pLKNet (Ding et al., 2024), and MogaNet (Li et al., 2024) combine large kernel and small kernel
164   is one block to introduce multi-scale information, However, these methods introduce complicated
165   architecture to employ large kernels. In addition, using large kernels or multi-branch structures will
166   increase training difficulty and is unfriendly to hardware, resulting in longer training and inference
167   times. Our SFCNN is a simple and fast architecture with pure 3 × 3 DWConv, thus obtaining an
168   ideal speed and accuracy tradeoff.
169   Transformer-based Architectures. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have made significant
170   breakthroughs in computer vision tasks. ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) first introduces a pure Trans-
171   former architecture for visual representations. However, directly applying self-attention to vision
172   tasks leads to large computational costs, which is unacceptable for dense prediction tasks. Swin (Liu
173   et al., 2021) solves this problem by utilizing window-based multi-head self-attention (MHSA) for
174   effective feature extraction. PVT (Wang et al., 2021) proposes the pyramid hierarchical structure to
175   extract spatial features at lower resolution. SMT (Lin et al., 2023) introduces multi-scale DWConv in
176   one block, to avoid detail missing and retain more spatial information by information fusion across
177
      different heads in MHSA. BiFormer (Zhu et al., 2023) uses dynamic sparse attention via bi-level
      routing to allocate computations more flexibly. However, compared to CNNs, ViTs face hardware
178
      compatibility limitations that restrict their wider application (Zhang et al., 2023). Our SFCNN has
179
      a large receptive field with only small kernel convolutions, thus obtaining better accuracy, fewer
180   computations, and faster speed.

197   3.2   C OMPUTING THE R ECEPTIVE F IELD
198
199   The ultimate objective of introducing large kernel convolution is to increase the receptive field. For
200   a convolution with L layers, feature map fl ∈ Rcl ×hl ×wl , l = 1, 2, ..., L denotes the output of the
201
      l-th layer, with channel cl , height hl , and width wl . We denote the input image by f0 , and the final
      output feature map corresponds to fL . Each layer l’s spatial configuration is parameterized by kernel
202
      size kl and stride sl . Define rl as the receptive field size of l-th layer, we give a simplified equation
203
      from Araujo et al. (2019) to compute the receptive field:
204

207                                                                 i=1

208   According to this equation, increasing the kernel size and stride is feasible to enlarge the receptive
209   field. However, we have also noticed it is a recurrence equation, increasing the number of recursion
210   iterations could also increase the receptive field, which means adding more DWConvs.

      3.5   G LOBAL S IGMOID L INEAR U NIT
290
291   Sigmoid Linear Unit (SiLU) is a widely used activation function, which was originally coined in
292   GELU (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016), and later works (Ramachandran et al., 2018; Elfwing et al.,
293   2018) demonstrate its effectiveness. After GPT using GELU, many subsequent models follow it
294   by default, including recent ViTs (Liu et al., 2021) and MLPs (Lai et al., 2023). GELU can be
295   approximated as
296                                                           p
297          GELU (x) = x × Φ(x) ≈ 0.5 × x × (1 + tanh( 2/π) × (x + 0.044715 × x3 )),             (2)
298
      where Φ means the cumulative distribution function for the Gaussian distribution. Another approx-
299   imate formula for GELU is:
300
                                    GELU (x) ≈ x × σ(1.702 × x),                                    (3)
301
302   where σ is a sigmoid function. Similarly, Swish (Ramachandran et al., 2018) proposes to take
303   advantage of automatic search techniques to discover a new activation function named Swish, which
304   can be formulated as
305                                      Swish(x) = x × σ(β × x).                                   (4)
306   It is easy to see that Swish has a similar formulation of GELU. The difference is that the learnable
307   parameter in Swish is set to a fixed value of 1.702. Meanwhile, in reinforcement learning, to achieve
308   the same goal of output from one hidden unit in the expected energy restricted Boltzmann machine
309   (EE-RBM), SiLU (Elfwing et al., 2018) proposes an activation function for the approximation of
310   neural network functions:
311                                           SiLU (x) = x × σ(x).                                      (5)
312
313
      SiLU is a simplified version of Swish and GELU, and it does not require a learnable parameter
      or a fixed value inside the sigmoid function. However, SiLU computes the results in all positions
314
      individually. It is unable to capture spatial information. We hope it achieves a global receptive field
315
      to let our SFCNN closer to those large-kernel CNNs. Thus, we propose a Global Sigmoid Linear
316   Unit (GSiLU) activation function to capture global spatial visual cues. The formula is as follows:
317
318                                     GSiLU (x) = x × σ(GAP (x)),                                      (6)
319
320
      where GAP is a global average pooling operation. It embeds global information from every channel
      into a single value to produce the importance of these channels.
321
322   However, GSiLU is very similar to the famous Squeeze-and-Excitation (Hu et al., 2018) module,
323   but considering the huge extra parameter as shown in Table 8, we use GSiLU because it is a non-
      parametric module.

507   4.4   A BLATION S TUDY
508
      The Impact of DWConv. As shown in Table 7 lines one to three, the result is markedly declined
509
      when we remove one DWConv in the SFCNN block. The receptive field will become almost halved
510   by using only one DWConv in a block.
511
512   The Impact of GSiLU. As shown in Table 7 lines four to six, adding GSiLU could bring at least
513
      +0.2% top-1 accuracy, but adding two GSiLU obtains the same performance as the one. One GSiLU
      could capture enough global spatial information for a single block.
514
515   The Impact of Activation. In table 8, we replace the GSiLU with other widely used activation
516   and the SE module. GSiLU could obtain better results with the same FLOPs and parameters. This
517   proves the importance of capturing long-range visual cues because SiLU only uses original feature
518   maps as input, while GSiLU could capture global spatial information. SE is better than GSiLU, but
519
      it introduces extra huge parameters, thus we choose GSiLU because it is a non-parametric module.
520   The Impact of Model Depth. Table 9 shows five models with different depths. A thinner and
521   deeper architecture could obtain better results than heavier and shallower models. The main reasons
522   may be a larger receptive field and better non-linear fitting capability. However, the deepest model
523   has a much thinner channel width, which will lose information and even get a -0.2% performance.
524   Visualization of the Receptive Field. Figure 3 visualizes the receptive field of many CNNs. Our
525   SFCNN could capture long-range dependency and the local context features simultaneously, while
526   other CNNs only capture local information or introduce global noises. ParCNetV2 only provides
527   the code but does not provide the pre-train weight, thus we cannot visualize it.

      5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We propose the Simple and Fast Convolutional Neural Network (SFCNN) that mainly employs a
532
      sequence of stacked 3 × 3 convolutions to capture visual cues of various sizes. Though the archi-
533
      tecture is simple, SFCNN surpasses the state-of-the-art CNNs with larger kernels. SFCNN is a thin
534   and deep model, encouraging more layers of DWConv to capture more spatial information under the
535   same computing complexity. Furthermore, we redesign the traditional inverted residual bottleneck
536   with two DWConv to enlarge the receptive field. We propose a novel Global Sigmoid Linear Unit
537   (GSiLU) activation function to capture global coarse-grained spatial information. SFCNN achieves
538   the best accuracy in ImageNet-1K image classification based on four evaluations of computational
539   complexity. Besides, experimental results on lightweight image classification, instance segmenta-
      tion, and semantic segmentation further verify the superiority of SFCNN.
