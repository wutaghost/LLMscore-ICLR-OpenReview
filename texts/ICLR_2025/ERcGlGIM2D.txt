009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In recent years, Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have shown remarkable perfor-
012            mance improvements in vision-language tasks. However, their large size poses
013            challenges for real-world applications where inference latency is a concern. To
014            tackle this issue, we propose employing Early Exit (EE) strategies in VLM. How-
015            ever, training exit classifiers in VLMs is challenging, particularly with limited
016            labeled training data. To address this, we introduce BLIPEE, an adversarial
017            training approach within a GAN-based framework. Here, each exit consists of
018            a transformer layer and a classifier, and the transformer layer is adversarially
019            trained to produce feature representations similar to the final layer, while a fea-
020
               ture classifier serves as the discriminator. Our method focuses on performing
               input-adaptive inference that mitigates the overthinking issue and increases infer-
021
               ence speed. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach in
022
               enhancing accuracy and model robustness by mitigating overthinking and the phe-
023            nomenon of mid-crisis that we highlight. The anonymized source code is available
024            at https://anonymous.4open.science/status/BLIPEE-3ED3.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Vision-Language Pre-training (VLP) has evolved significantly with the emergence of sophisticated
031   pre-trained Vision Language Models (VLMs). These models have consistently pushed the perfor-
032   mance boundaries across various vision-language tasks. However, their demanding computational
033
      requirements and inference latency pose challenges for real-time applications. Several models, such
      as BLIP-2 Li et al. (2023), leverage off-the-shelf large-scale pre-trained models as building compo-
034
      nents with their parameters frozen. This reduces VLMs training parameters but makes them suscep-
035
      tible to issues related to overthinking during inference, as highlighted in previous studies Kaya et al.
036   (2019); Zhou et al. (2020), leading to performance degradation and wasteful computation.
037
038   The use of the Language Model (LM) component with frozen parameters not only makes VLM sus-
039
      ceptible to overthinking but also to another phenomenon that we term mid-crisis. This phenomenon
      occurs when intermediate layers suffer performance drops due to the search for irrelevant features.
040
      While initial layers capture shallow representations and syntactic information, and deep layers learn
041
      semantic-fusion relations Fei et al. (2022), intermediary layers tend to capture dataset patterns that
042   degrade their performance, even losing the information learned by initial layers, and the model has
043   to regain the lost information again in deeper layers. We illustrate this phenomenon in Figure 2
044   and Section 3.1, showcasing BLEU-4 Papineni et al. (2002) scores of exits in the COCO Lin et al.
045   (2014) dataset used for image captioning. This raises the question: how can we mitigate mid-crisis
046   and overthinking to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of VLMs?
047
      We address this issue using ‘Early Exit’ (EE) techniques Xin et al. (2020); Zhou et al. (2020); Zhu
048   (2021). It is a widely adopted input-adaptive technique that seeks to alleviate computational costs
049   by bypassing certain layers while retaining the broad knowledge encoded in large-scale models. It is
050   based on the fact that real-world datasets consist of ‘easy’ and ‘hard’ samples; hence, each does not
051   require the same amount of computation. Implementing EE in VLMs introduces additional parame-
052   ters requiring training, necessitating substantial amounts of labeled data, which can be prohibitively
053   expensive. This limitation hampers the widespread adoption of EE methods in VLMs with good
      zero-shot capabilities.

054
      We introduce a novel EE training technique named BLIPEE: Faster BLIP with Adversarially Trained
055   EE designed to enhance the efficiency of VLMs while maintaining performance and speed with
056   minimal training requirements. Our method leverages the capabilities of the VLMs to generate high-
057   quality outputs alongside a feature classifier within a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)-based
058   framework Creswell et al. (2018) to minimize the discrepancy between feature representations at exit
059   points and the final layer in an adversarial setup. This approach significantly reduces unnecessary
060   computation at intermediate layers and elevates accuracy by learning feature representations directly
061   from the final layer.
062   Our method is tailored to VLMs with exits and Feature Classifiers (FCs) attached to intermediary
063   layers. Each exit consists of a single exit transformer (ET) and an exit classifier (EC). The exit
064   transformer is a replica of the layers in the LM of the BLIP-2 model. They are used as generators
065   and feature classifiers as discriminators in a GAN-based setup, as shown in Figure 1. The task of the
066   feature classifier is to correctly classify if the input is from the exit or final layer, and the task of the
067   exit transformer is to generate representations similar to the final layer to fool the feature classifier
068   of that exit.
069   The exit classifiers at each of the exits are the same as that of the final layer classifier with frozen
070   parameters and are used to map the outputs of exits to class probabilities. As the size of exit layer
071   parameters is significantly smaller than that of exit classifiers, it substantially reduces the number of
072   training parameters. In this way, a single LM layer attached to the exits helps produce similar feature
073   representations and reduces the training parameters by utilizing the final layer classifier without
074   training it at the exits. By attaching EEs, our method reduces the chances of overthinking and
075
      makes the inference process faster. Figure 3 shows how our method can speed up inference while
      maintaining comparable performance. The figure also provides a complete intuition of how our
076
      method can improve performance.
077
078   Adversarial training methods are susceptible to issues such as catastrophic forgetting Kirkpatrick
079   et al. (2017); Ryu et al. (2022) and mode collapse. To circumvent these issues, we propose both
080   supervised and unsupervised methods. In the supervised case, we use the hard labels from a small
081
      labeled dataset to remove the chances of catastrophic forgetting or exit training being stuck in a
      local optimum. In the unsupervised case, we replace the hard labels with the soft labels available as
082
      the output of vanilla BLIP-2, or we use the CapFilt Li et al. (2022b) used by the BLIP and BLIP-2
083
      model to create high-quality synthetic labels.
084
085          • We introduce an EE strategy BLIPEE for VLMs to effectively mitigate inference latency
086            by reducing unnecessary computations inherent in their large-scale architecture.
087          • To improve performance at EE classifiers, BLIPEE emulate the behaviour of the final layer
088            at the exits through adversarial learning. To handle mode collapse and catastrophic forget-
089            ting, we propose supervised and unsupervised methods.
090
             • Our model reduces the number of trainable parameters of the exits up to 37% by fixing the
091            weights of the classifier attached to the exits, reducing the size of training dataset require-
092            ments.
093
             • We experiment both qualitatively (see figure 3) and quantitatively on various tasks such
094            as image captioning using COCO Lin et al. (2014) and NoCaps Agrawal et al. (2019)
095            datasets and visual question-answering using VQAv2 Goyal et al. (2017), OK-VQA Marino
096            et al. (2019), VizWiz Gurari et al. (2018) and GQA Hudson & Manning (2019) datasets.
097            For visual dialogue, we use the VisDial Das et al. (2017) dataset. Our method provides
098            inference speed > 1.45× with comparable and sometimes even better accuracy than vanilla
099            BLIP-2 inference.

178   3     M ETHODOLOGY
179
180   We start with a pre-trained BLIP-2 model that consists of three components: Image encoder, Q-
181   former, and Language Model. We assume that LM consists of N layers, and we attach exits to the
182   K chosen layers. Each exit consists of one Exit Transformer (ET) layer with the same configuration
183   as one LM layer and an Exit Classifier (EC). The exit layer is such the parameters of the ET are
184   trainable, and those of EC are frozen. We motivate our method by investigating the phenomenon of
185
      mid-crisis in BLIP-2.
186
187   3.1   M OTIVATION
188
      In Fig. 2, we illustrate BLEU-1 score and VQA accuracy for various layers. As seen, performance
189   dips at the middle layers after the initial improvement. This is due to the LM component, which
190   is of large size and kept frozen during training. During pre-training, BLIP-2 aligns the features of
191   text and images using the Q-former, the querying transformer. However, the Q-former provides the
192   image-grounded text embeddings to the LM component in such a way that it produces high-quality
193   predictions only at the final layer and not the intermediary layer. We use this observation to add a
194   transformer layer at each exit instead of just adding a classifier at the exits. The transformer layer
195   is used to mimic the behaviour of the final layer. In this way, we have low-level features already
196   available at the initial layers and provide access to the high-level features at the exits by adversarially
197
      training them to produce representations similar to those of the final layer. This makes the exits more
      effective both in terms of accuracy and speeding up the inference.
198
199   We note that attaching a trainable classifier, as done in the previous works, can significantly increase
200   the number of parameters as the size of the dictionary is large. For instance, a classifier attached at
201   a single exit to BLIP-2 with OPT2.7B as a decoder has around 130M trainable parameters, and if we
202   attach only 7 exits, this scales up to 900M parameters! In our methods, only the LM layer in each
203
      exit has trainable parameters. This adds 84M trainable parameters, scaling to 588M parameters for
      7 exits. Hence, we reduce the training parameters by freezing the classifier weights and only training
204
      LM layer weights, thus reducing the trainable exit parameters by around 37%.
205
206   We next discuss our method which consists of two parts: 1) backbone finetuning and 2) exit training.
207

      3.5   I NFERENCE
297
298   We perform the caption in an autoregressive manner. This entails making a token-by-token pre-
299   diction for a given image, where the layer at which the token is predicted is determined by the
300   confidence score Si = maxv∈V CN (Ei (hit |y1:t−1 , I))(v) where V is the vocabulary. The input to
301   the decoder is processed sequentially through the decoder layers until the confidence score Si is
302   greater than a predefined threshold value α. This threshold is set based on the accuracy-efficiency
303   trade-off. The inference starts with the begin of the sentence token and the next token is predicted
304   either at the exits or at the final layer. Then the predicted token is appended to the generated caption
305   and passed as an input to the decoder for the next token prediction. The inference process stops
306
      when the end of the sentence token is predicted either at intermediary layers with a confidence score
      of more than α or at the final layer. Note that if the prediction confidence is below α for all the exits,
307
      then the sample is predicted at the final layer, irrespective of the confidence in the prediction.

350   FlanT5-XL Chung et al. (2024). We use the LAVIS Li et al. (2022a) library for implementation,
351
      training and evaluation. For training, we use the validation split of the datasets. We use 80% of
      validation split for training and the remaining 20% is reserved for development. We use labels of
352
      the validation dataset when the task is semi-supervised, else we just use the samples without labels.
353
      First, the backbone is fine-tuned for 10 epochs with a starting learning rate of 1e-5, which decays
354   by 0.5 every 3 epochs. The backbone weights are then frozen post-fine-tuning and exits are attached
355   to the backbone. We train exit weights for a further 3 epochs. We employ the Adam Kingma &
356   Ba (2014) optimizer and a batch size of 16. For the feature classifier, we have used MLP with one
357   hidden layer with a hidden state of size 3072 and a LeakyReLU Xu et al. (2015) activation function.
358
      For the unsupervised tasks, we train the model for 3 epochs on the validation dataset (without labels)
359
      with knowledge distillation from the final layer. Optimizers and learning rates are kept the same as
360   given above. Note that in CapFilt we apply the CapFilt method on the validation dataset (without
361   labels) and generate synthetic labels. After this, we perform a similar procedure by treating the
362   synthetic labels as the true labels as done for the semi-supervised tasks. Fine-tuning of the backbone
363   is not required in an unsupervised setup.
364
      Inference: Inference is conducted with a batch size of 1. We provide the results on the test dataset.
365
      For image captioning, we use a prompt as ‘a photo of’ as an initial input to the LM component.
366   The threshold is chosen as the best-performing threshold from the set {0.5, 0.6, 0.7, 0.8, 0.9, 1.0}
367   on the held-out split of the validation dataset. More details on the hyperparameter setting can be
368   found in Appendix 6 with the values of hyperparameters. All the experiments were performed with
369   a combination of 6 GPU setups consisting of two NVIDIA RTX A6000 and four NVIDIA GeForce

416   Visual Question Answering: We provide results on unsupervised (see table 3) as well as semi-
417   supervised setups (see table 2). We observe that our method outperforms all early exit methods in
418   terms of accuracy and speedup even with less number of trainable parameters. We even outperform
419   the vanilla BLIP-2 inference due to overthinking in the BLIP-2 backbone which is mitigated by our
420   input-adaptive inference. We also provide results on an unsupervised visual dialogue dataset where
421   the task is similar to VQA but there is an additional context before the question i.e. a dialogue
422
      history between the user and the model.
423   Image Captioning: We provide results of semi-supervised and unsupervised setup in table 1 and
424   4 respectively. We clearly outperform the existing models in terms of both accuracy as well as
425   speedup. For the NoCaps dataset, the model is fine-tuned on the COCO dataset. The speedup for
426   NoCaps dataset is lower as there is a domain change from the training which lowers the confidence
427   in prediction taking more samples to deeper exits for inference.
428   We observe performance improvement over previous baselines as we attached exits to the BLIP-
429   2 model and by performing input-adaptive inference, we perform better than the BLIP-2 model,
430   and as BLIP-2 outperforms other models, BLIPEE also outperforms others. For the early exiting
431   baselines on BLIP-2, we outperform them as we have an additional component in the exits rather
      than just a linear classifier which helps in better performance of exits in terms of both performance

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this study, we introduced a novel inference technique BLIPEE, which leverages adversarial train-
489   ing of exits alongside the zero-shot capabilities of the BLIP-2 model. By employing BLIPEE, we
490   effectively reduce the dependency on a vast amount of labeled training data typically required for
491   exit training. Our approach involves adversarially training exits to generate representations similar
492   to those of the final layer, thereby minimizing the need for extensive labeled data. Moreover, our exit
493   design reduces the number of trainable parameters, resulting in lower computational costs. Exper-
494   imental results demonstrate that our method significantly enhances inference speed while yielding
495   high-quality outputs, occasionally even surpassing those produced by the final layer.
496
497   7   L IMITATIONS
498
499   For attaching exits to a large model such as BLIP-2, the crucial part is to decide where to attach
500   exits within a given budget, i.e., what could be the best places for an exit in the LM component
501   of the backbone without exceeding a certain amount of parameters. We answered that question by
502   explaining the mid-crisis. However, the placements of exits with given budget criteria still remain
503   unexplored which can make these models even faster within computational boundaries.

779   A.2      ACCURACY VS SPEEDUP
780
      In figure 4b, we show the accuracy vs speedup curve which could be obtained by changing the
781   threshold parameter α. As we decrease the threshold parameter, samples exit from the initial layers
782   even with less confidence, in this way all the samples are more prone to be incorrect decreasing the
783   accuracy but as the threshold is decreased more samples exit from the initial layers and increase the
784   speedup. One key observation is as we start decreasing the threshold, we observe that sometimes
785   the performance even increases, this is the effect of overthinking, where some samples are correctly
786   predicted at initial layers and might become wrong as they reach the final layer. We have also plotted
787   the curves for other exiting methods and observed that our method has better stability as compared
788   to other early exiting methods.
789
790   A.3      I MPORTANCE OF DIFFERENT COMPONENTS
791
      In figure 4a, we show the importance of different components of our method. We observe that there
792
      is a huge performance drop if we remove the knowledge distillation or cross-entropy loss from the
793
      overall loss function. This occurs due to catastrophic forgetting or mode collapse where the model
794   gets stuck into local minima. On the other hand, if we remove the adversarial training part, there
795   is again a performance drop, as we only train the classifier but we are not mapping the feature
796   representations of the final layer and the exits hence exits only have low-level features which are
797   insufficient to make correct predictions, hence resulting in a performance drop.
