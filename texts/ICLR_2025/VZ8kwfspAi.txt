009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               We introduce an E(n)-equivariant Transformer architecture for spatio-temporal
012            graph data. By imposing rotation, translation, and permutation equivariance
013            inductive biases in both space and time, we show that the Spacetime E(n)-
014            Transformer (SET) outperforms purely spatial and temporal models without
015            symmetry-preserving properties. We benchmark SET against said models on
016            the N -body problem, a simple physical system with complex dynamics. While
017            existing spatio-temporal graph neural networks focus on sequential modeling, we
018            empirically demonstrate that leveraging underlying domain symmetries yields
019            considerable improvements for modeling dynamical systems on graphs.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Many problems that we wish to model with neural networks possess underlying geometric structure
025   with symmetries. Geometric Deep Learning, a term coined in the seminal work of Bronstein
026   et al. (2021), is an Erlangen program for deep learning that systematizes inductive biases as group
027
      symmetries G, arising through notions of invariance and equivariance.
028   Recent work, like SE(3)-Transformers Fuchs et al. (2020) and E(n)-Graph Neural Networks Satorras
029   et al. (2022), impose different notions of group equivariance on neural networks to inform architecture
030   choice. While these neural network architectures encode spatial inductive biases, they notably lack
031   a time component. Temporal Graph Networks Rossi et al. (2020) proposed an efficient framework
032   that learns from dynamic graphs. However, this architecture assumes the topology of graphs changes
033
      over time. In this paper, we discuss spatio-temporal graphs, which have a fixed topology with
      changing features over discrete time steps. Recent works Jin et al. (2023), Marisca et al. (2022), Cini
034
      et al. (2023) have treated node features as time series and edges as the relationships between these
035
      series. Such spatio-temporal graph neural networks (STGNNs) have a plethora of applications, from
036   simulating biomolecular interactions to modeling financial time series.
037
038   Similarly, while STGNNs improve representation learning of sequential graph data, minimal research
039
      has been done on preserving group symmetries in a spatio-temporal fashion. In particular, sequential
      models ought to preserve spatial group symmetries at each time step. Famously, Noether’s first
040
      theorem formalizes the notion of infinitesimal symmetries of the so-called Lagrangian of a physical
041
      system, in terms of perturbations with respect to both space and time, by determining conserved
042   quantities. Inspired by this intuition of temporal and spatial symmetry, we seek to derive a neural
043   network architecture that is equivariant in both temporal and spatial components.
044
045
      Classical neural network architectures like RNNs are discrete approximations to continuous time-
      domain signals, obeying a differential equation with respect to time. If an RNN is invariant to
046
      time-warping, a monotonically increasing and differentiable function of time, it takes the form of
047
      an LSTM Bronstein et al. (2021), which unlike a vanilla RNN, captures long-term dependencies.
048   Similarly, the dynamics of classical physical systems satisfy the Euler-Lagrange equations, i.e. the
049   equations of motion. Hence, we use the N -body problem, as described in Trenti & Hut (2008)
050   and alluded to in Satorras et al. (2022), as an ideal candidate to test our hypothesis that preserving
051   G-equivariance ameloriates long-term spatio-temporal graph modeling. We will use a Transformer
052   for the temporal component of the architecture, preserving long-term dependencies and, hence,
053   invariance to time-warping. Each node of the graph will have features, coordinates, and velocities.
      As such, the neural network should be equivariant under rotational and translational symmetries E(n)

      acting on coordinates. It should also be equivariant with respect to rotational symmetries SO(n)
055   acting on velocities. Lastly, the nodes should be permutation equivariant.

110   In this paper, we are interested in physical systems that can be modelled as a sequence of graphs
111   Gt = (Vt , Et ) for t = 1, . . . , L with nodes vi (t) ∈ Vt and edges eij (t) ∈ Et . In particular, we seek
112   to model the dynamics of the N -body problem Trenti & Hut (2008). For this task, we assume a priori
113   that the graph is complete since a charged particle will interact with every other particle in a Van der
114   Waals potential under Coulomb’s law. Similarly, a mass will interact with every other charged particle
115   in a gravitational potential under Newton’s law of universal gravitation. In addition, we assume that
116   particles are neither created nor destroyed as the system evolves in time, so the nodes Vt in the graph
117   remain the same. Let G := (Gt )1≤t≤L be a sequence of topologically-identical graphs with changing
118
      features, known as a spatio-temporal graph. The task under consideration is learning a function that
      predicts the associated features of graph. In particular, given G, we are interested in predicting the
119
      positions and velocities of all masses in the system after H additional time steps where H >> L.

123   neighborhoods of that node, we need to (1) aggregate nodes spatially to obtain spatially-contextual
124
      embeddings and (2) obtain temporally-contextual embeddings via temporal aggregation.

192   3.2   T EMPORAL ATTENTION FOR G RAPHS
193
194   The objective of this section is to obtain strong temporally-contextual representations of the spatial
195   graph embeddings. In the N -body problem, we are essentially solving the forward-time Euler-
196   Lagrange equations, a second-order partial differential equation. However, for a fixed node on the
197   spatio-temporal graph, the feature, position, and velocity form a time-series, for which RNN’s capture
198
      short-term dependencies. It was shown in Tallec & Ollivier (2018) that while vanilla RNNs are not
      time-warping invariant, LSTMs are a class of such time-warping invariant functions modeling a
199
      continuous time-domain signal. Employing this philosophy, the use of an attention-based Transformer
200
      architecture to model spatio-temporal graph data merits investigation.
201

      4   R ELATED W ORK
393
394
395   Temporal graph learning has a plethora of real-world applications, like COVID-19 contact tracing
396   Chang et al. (2021) Holme (2016) Ding et al. (2021) and misinformation detection Choi et al. (2021)
397   Song et al. (2021) Zhang et al. (2021).
398   Learning on continuous-time dynamic graphs was introduced by Rossi et al. (2020), which proposed
399   Temporal Graph Networks (TGNs) with a memory module, acting as a summary of what the model
400   has seen so far for each node. Causal Anonymous Walks Wang et al. (2022) is another branch of
401   temporal graph learning, which extracts random walks between edges; however, this is not our focus.
402   Other work like Jin et al. (2023) and Cini et al. (2023) treat node features as time series and edges
403   as correlations between the series. Under this framework, message passing must be able to handle
404   sequences of data from the neighborhood of each node, with RNNs Seo et al. (2016), attention
405
      mechanisms Marisca et al. (2022), and convolutions Wu et al. (2019).
406   The Dynamic Graph Convolutional Network (DynGCN) Choi et al. (2021) and DyGFormer Yu et al.
407   (2023) are similar to our method. DynGCN processes each of the graph snapshots with a graph
408   convolutional network to obtain structural information and then applies an attention mechanism to
409   capture temporal information. Similarly, DyGFormer Yu et al. (2023) learns from historical first-hop
410   neighborhood interactions and applies a Transformer architecture to historical correlations between
411
      nodes. However, unlike our paper, DynGCN and DyGFormer do not take into account the inductive
      biases of the underlying modeling task. Recent work of Wu et al. (2024) presents one of the first
412
      spatio-temporally equivariant architectures for graphs, arguing that such an inductive bias allows for
413
      the capturing of non-Markovian dynamics of physical systems. Unlike our method, they do not use a
414   continuous convolution for feature extraction. Moreover, they do not consider velocity and assume a
415   static adjacency matrix on which they extract information in the frequency domain by performing a
416   discrete Fourier Transform.
417
      E(n)-Equivariant Graph Neural Networks (EGNN) Satorras et al. (2022) defines a model equivariant
418
      to the Euclidean group E(n) and, unlike previous methods, does not rely on spherical harmonics
419
      such as the SE(3)-Transformer Fuchs et al. (2020) and Tensor Field Networks Thomas et al. (2018).
420   The SE(3)-Transformer paper briefly alludes to incorporating equivariant attention with an LSTM
421   for temporal causality; however, this is not the primary focus of their work. LieConv Finzi et al.
422   (2020) proposes a framework that allows one to construct a convolutional layer that is equivariant
423   with respect to transformations of a Lie group, equipped with an exponential map. However, the
424   EGNN is simpler and more applicable to problems with point clouds like the N -body problem Trenti
425   & Hut (2008) we consider.
426   As we concern ourselves with modeling a dynamical system, the works of Lagrangian Neural
427   Networks (LNNs) Cranmer et al. (2020) and Hamiltonian Neural Networks (HNNs) Greydanus
428   et al. (2019) are pertinent. HNNs parameterize the Hamiltonian of a system, but require canonical
429   coordinates, which makes it inapplicable to systems where such coordinates cannot be deduced.
430   LLNs parameterize arbitrary Lagrangians of dynamical systems with neural networks, from which it
431   is possible to solve the forward dynamics of the system; however, this requires an additional step of
      integration, which is cumbersome.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
510
511
512
      The imposition of group symmetries on graph neural networks is a promising area of research,
      demonstrating remarkable real-world results like AlphaFold2 Jumper et al. (2021). However, most
513
      research has been centered on spatial equivariance for representational learning on static graphs.
514
      For dynamical graph systems, little research has centered on preserving group symmetries across
515   time. We close this gap with the Spacetime E(n)-Transformer and show promising results for the
516   N -body problem. It will be interesting to see our method applied to harder tasks, such as sequential
517   bio-molecular generation.
518
      Although we chose a graph as the domain of interest, it is plausible to extend notions of spatio-
519
      temporal G-equivariance to other domains like grids and manifolds. Furthermore, while we leveraged
520
      the symmetries of the problem a priori, it may not always be possible to find a simple group for a
521   general problem. Hence, in future work, it would be interesting to learn a group symmetry from
522   underlying data and impose equivariance using methods like LieConv Finzi et al. (2020), which is
523   equivariant to the actions of Lie groups, i.e. the continuous group representation of infinitesimal
524   transformations. Noether’s first theorem implies a possible connection to conserved quantities, which
525   was discussed in Alet et al. (2021).
526
527   6.1    E THICS
528
529   While we only employed simulated datasets, graph neural networks have historically been used in
530   medical and biological applications. In such settings, careful consideration of ethical and responsible
531   data collection is of utmost importance. For instance, in the setting of drug discovery, the way in
532   which we administer synthetically-created drugs on humans and other species must be carefully
533   approached.
