011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Understanding the various properties of glycans with machine learning has shown
014            some preliminary promise. However, previous methods mainly focused on mod-
015
               eling the backbone structure of glycans as graphs of monosaccharides (i.e., sugar
               units), while they neglected the atomic structures underlying each monosaccha-
016
               ride, which are actually important indicators of glycan properties. In this work,
017
               we fill this blank by introducing the GlycanAA model for All-Atom-wise Glycan
018            modeling. GlycanAA models a glycan as a heterogeneous graph with monosac-
019            charide nodes representing its global backbone structure and atom nodes rep-
020            resenting its local atomic-level structures. Based on such a graph, GlycanAA
021            performs hierarchical message passing to capture from local atomic-level in-
022            teractions to global monosaccharide-level interactions hierarchically. To further
023            enhance the model capability, we pre-train GlycanAA on a high-quality unla-
024            beled glycan dataset in a self-supervised way, deriving the PreGlycanAA model.
025            Specifically, we design a multi-scale mask prediction algorithm to endow the
026
               model with knowledge about different levels of dependencies in a glycan. Ex-
               tensive benchmark results show the superiority of GlycanAA over existing glycan
027
               encoders and verify the further improvements achieved by PreGlycanAA.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Glycans, complex macromolecules composed of sugar molecules, play pivotal roles in life sci-
033   ence. They serve as essential structural components in cells, forming the backbone of extracel-
034   lular matrices and cell membranes (Yanagishita, 1993). Based on such structures, they modulate
035   intercellular communication (Liu & Wang, 2023) and impact biological processes such as immune
036   response (Zhang, 2006) and cell differentiation (Lau et al., 2007). With the accumulation of glycan
037   data in public repositories (Tiemeyer et al., 2017; Yamada et al., 2020), it is a promising way to
038
      understand various glycan properties and functions with data-driven methods like machine learning.
039   In this research direction, most existing works (Burkholz et al., 2021; Lundstrøm et al., 2022; Car-
040   penter et al., 2022; Alkuhlani et al., 2023) model a glycan as a graph with monosaccharides (i.e.,
041   sugar units) as its nodes, and use graph neural networks (GNNs) to predict various glycan properties,
042   e.g., glycosylation, immunogenicity, binding affinity with a protein, etc. Though performing well
043   on some tasks, these methods fail to capture the atomic-level structures underlying each monosac-
044
      charide, which are actually important determinants of many glycan properties and functions. For
      example, atomic-level interactions between a glycan and a protein determine their binding affinity.
045
046   There have been some preliminary attempts at modeling all-atom-wise glycan structures with state-
047   of-the-art small molecule encoders Xu et al. (2024). However, because of the gap between a small
048   molecule with tens of atoms and a glycan with hundreds of atoms (i.e., essentially a macromolecule),
049   these small molecule encoders are shown to be ineffective, which perform even worse than the
050
      models utilizing only monosaccharide-level information. Therefore, it is still to be answered how to
      realize the potential of all-atom glycan modeling on boosting glycan understanding.
051
052   To answer this question, in this work, we propose the GlycanAA model for All-Atom-wise Glycan
053   modeling. Note that, a glycan naturally possesses a hierarchical structure with (1) atoms making up
      the local structure of each monosaccharide and (2) different monosaccharides making up the global

054
      backbone structure of the glycan. Inspired by this fact, we design GlycanAA based on a hierarchical
055   modeling approach. Specifically, GlycanAA first represents a glycan as a heterogeneous graph
056   consisting of (1) a set of atom nodes for its local structures and (2) a set of monosaccharide nodes
057   for its global structure. Based on such a graph, GlycanAA then performs hierarchical message
058   passing to model from local atomic-level interactions to global monosaccharide-level interactions.
059   In this way, GlycanAA can completely capture the covalent bonds forming each monosaccharide
060   and the glycosidic bonds forming the whole glycan.
061   To further enhance the representation power of GlycanAA, we seek to endow it with the knowledge
062   stored in abundant unlabeled glycan data. We resort to self-supervised pre-training to achieve this
063   goal, where the PreGlycanAA model is developed as a pre-trained version of GlycanAA. Specifi-
064   cally, we first curate an unlabeled glycan dataset by selecting 40,781 high-quality glycan data from
065   the GlyTouCan database (Tiemeyer et al., 2017). GlycanAA is then pre-trained on this dataset with
066   a multi-scale mask prediction algorithm. In this algorithm, partial atom and monosaccharide nodes
067   are masked at the input, and the model is asked to recover these masked nodes. Through this ap-
068   proach, the derived PreGlycanAA model acquires the dependencies between different atoms and
069
      monosaccharides in a glycan, leading to informative glycan representations.
070   We evaluate the proposed models on the GlycanML benchmark (Xu et al., 2024). Experimental re-
071   sults show that PreGlycanAA and GlycanAA respectively rank first and second on the benchmark,
072   and they substantially outperform SOTA atomic-level small molecule encoders and glycan-specific
073   monosaccharide-level encoders. We further demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed hierarchi-
074   cal message passing and multi-scale mask prediction methods through extensive ablation studies.

079   Glycan modeling with machine learning. With the growing size of experimental glycomics
080   datasets, machine learning techniques are becoming increasingly important in glycoinformatics (Bo-
081
      jar & Lisacek, 2022; Li et al., 2022). Traditional machine learning approaches, such as support
      vector machines (SVMs), have been employed to learn patterns from mass spectrometry data (Ku-
082
      mozaki et al., 2015; Liang et al., 2014), predict glycosylation sites (Caragea et al., 2007; Li et al.,
083
      2015; Taherzadeh et al., 2019; Pitti et al., 2019), and classify glycans (Yamanishi et al., 2007).
084   Alongside the advancements in deep learning, recent models have showcased the potential of deep
085   learning in addressing glycomics challenges. Some approaches utilize sequence-based models, such
086   as DeepNGlyPred (Pakhrin et al., 2021) that employs the N-GlyDE dataset (Pitti et al., 2019) to iden-
087   tify N-glycosylated sequons. Other sequence-based models like SweetOrigins (Bojar et al., 2020b),
088   SweetTalk (Bojar et al., 2020a), and glyBERT (Dai et al., 2021) have utilized databases such as
089   SugarBase (Bojar et al., 2020b) to predict various glycan properties. On another line of research,
090   SweetNet (Burkholz et al., 2021), LectinOracle (Lundstrøm et al., 2022), GlyNet (Carpenter et al.,
091   2022) and GNNGLY (Alkuhlani et al., 2023) represent glycans as graphs with monosaccharides as
092
      their nodes and use graph neural networks (GNNs) for glycan property prediction. Among all, Gly-
      canML (Xu et al., 2024) established a comprehensive benchmark evaluating sequence-based models
093
      and GNNs on a diverse set of 11 tasks.
094
095   While GNNs have demonstrated their strong performance on specific tasks (Xu et al., 2024),
096   their potential remains constrained by the underutilization of atomic-level information. Moreover,
097   atomic-level encoders originally designed for small molecules have been shown to be ineffective
098
      in glycan modeling (Xu et al., 2024). In this study, we tackle these limitations by proposing the
      GlycanAA model, a hierarchical encoder for heterogeneous all-atom glycan graphs.
099

143   3     G LYCANAA: A LL -ATOM G LYCAN M ODELING WITH H IERARCHICAL
144         M ESSAGE PASSING
145
146   We propose the GlycanAA model for all-atom-wise glycan modeling. In the following parts, we
147   introduce its data representation method in Section 3.1 and its encoding approach in Section 3.2.
148
149   3.1    H ETEROGENEOUS G RAPH R EPRESENTATION OF A LL -ATOM G LYCAN S TRUCTURE
150
151
      For a glycan g, we represent its atomic-level structure as a heterogeneous graph g = (Va , Vm , E)
      composed of an atom node set Va , a monosaccharide node set Vm and an edge set E, as graphically
152
      illustrated in Figure 1(a). We state the details of each graph component as below:
153
154       • Atom node set Va : This node set contains all heavy atoms (i.e., non-hydrogen atoms) in a
155         glycan, i.e., Va = {ai }N
                                    i=1 (ai stands for an atom; N denotes the number of atoms in glycan g).
156       • Monosaccharide node set Vm : To clearly represent the backbone structure of a glycan, we
157         further introduce a set of nodes representing all monosaccharides that make up the glycan, i.e.,
158         Vm = {mj }M   j=1 (mj stands for a monosaccharide; M denotes the number of monosaccharides
159         in glycan g).
160       • Edge set E: We consider three kinds of edges to comprehensively represent atom-atom, atom-
161         monosaccharide and monosaccharide-monosaccharide interactions, i.e., E = Eaa ∪ Eam ∪ Emm ,
            as detailed below:


                                                                    3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
              – Atom-atom edge set Eaa : This set of edges represent the atomic-level structure of each
163             monosaccharide. Specifically, the covalent bonds in each monosaccharide are collected,
164             and each bond along with its bond type (single, double, triple or aromatic bond) makes up
165             an edge, i.e., Eaa = {(a, a′ , r)|r ∈ {single, double, triple, aromatic}}, where (a, a′ , r)
166             denotes an edge connecting atom a to atom a′ with bond type r. We include both directions
167             of a bond in this edge set.
168           – Atom-monosaccharide edge set Eam : We connect each atom with its corresponding
169             monosaccharide, such that a monosaccharide is aware of its atomic-level information,
170             and each atom recognizes the glycan backbone structure. This edge set is represented
171             as Eam = {(a, m, ram )} ∪ {(m, a, ram )}, where each corresponding pair of atom a and
172             monosaccharide m are connected by a bidirectional edge with the edge type ram indicating
173             atom-monosaccharide interaction.
174           – Monosaccharide-monosaccharide edge set Emm : We collect all glycosidic bonds in a gly-
175             can to represent its backbone structure. In specific, this edge set can be represented as
176
                Emm = {(m, m′ , r)|r ∈ Rg }, where (m, m′ , r) denotes an edge connecting monosac-
                charide m to monosaccharide m′ with bond type r, and Rg denotes all possible types of
177
                glycosidic bonds, e.g., alpha-1,6-glycosidic bond, beta-1,4-glycosidic bond, etc. We follow
178
                Thomès et al. (2021) to construct Rg and include both directions of a bond in this edge set.

      3.2     H IERARCHICAL M ESSAGE PASSING ON A LL -ATOM G LYCAN G RAPH
181
182
      Based on the all-atom glycan graph introduced above, GlycanAA extracts glycan representations
183   using the carefully-designed modules below. A graphical illustration is shown in Figure 1(b).
184
185   Node embedding: We employ two codebooks to store the embeddings of all possible types of atoms
186
      and monosaccharides, respectively. For each node, we look up the corresponding codebook to assign
      it an initial feature embedding.
187
188   Hierarchical message passing: A glycan possesses a hierarchical structure, where its local structure
189   in each monosaccharide is formed by atoms and covalent bonds in between, and different monosac-
190   charides are further connected by glycosidic bonds, deriving its global backbone structure. We
191   propose to encode such a structure from local to global hierarchically, which is proven to be effec-
192
      tive in modeling other biomolecules like small molecules (Yu & Gao, 2022; Han et al., 2023) and
      proteins (Hermosilla et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022). Specifically, in each message passing block,
193
      we sequentially perform atom-atom, atom-monosaccharide and monosaccharide-monosaccharide
194
      message passing to capture from local interactions to global interactions.
195
196   Note that, these interactions are essentially multi-relational, where atoms and monosaccharides in-
197   teract with different types of covalent and glycosidic bonds. To fully model such interactions, we
198
      adopt relational graph convolution (RGConv) (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018) as the basic message pass-
      ing module. Given a graph g0 = (V0 , E0 , R0 ) with node set V0 , edge set E0 and relation (i.e., edge

      To further improve the representation power of GlycanAA, we endow it with the knowledge stored
236
      in abundant unlabeled glycan data through self-supervised pre-training, deriving the PreGlycanAA
237
      model. In the following parts, we introduce the setup of the pre-training dataset in Section 4.1 and
238   the multi-scale pre-training algorithm in Section 4.2.
239
240
      4.1    C URATION OF H IGH - QUALITY U NLABELED G LYCAN DATASET
241
242   To ensure the quality of pre-trained model, we aim to collect as much informative and clean glycan
243   data as possible. We choose the GlyTouCan database (Tiemeyer et al., 2017) as the data source for
244   its high recognition in the glycoscience domain and instant update of the latest glycan structures. We
245   first collect all the glycans deposited in GlyTouCan, summing up to 219,857 glycans. Data cleaning
246   is then performed based on the following criteria:
247
248       • Data quality: We discard all the glycans whose structures are not fully solved. In specific,
249         if there is any monosaccharide or glycosidic bond with an undetermined type in a glycan, we
250
            regard it as a low-quality sample and remove it from pre-training.
251       • Data integrity: We preserve the glycan structures with a single connected component. Those
252         samples with multiple components are discarded, so as to focus on learning the interactions
253         within a single glycan structure.
254       • Without data leakage: We remove the glycans that occur in the dataset of any downstream task
255         used in our experiments, so as to prevent data leakage during pre-training.
256
257   After such a filtering process, we preserve a set of 40,781 high-quality, integral and data-leakage-
258   proof glycan samples for pre-training.
259
260   4.2    S ELF -S UPERVISED P RE - TRAINING VIA M ULTI -S CALE M ASK P REDICTION
261
262   To acquire the rich information underlying the curated unlabeled glycan dataset, we propose the
263   PreGlycanAA model that pre-trains GlycanAA with a multi-scale mask prediction task, as illustrated
264   in Figure 2. This algorithm endows the model with knowledge about the dependencies between
265   different atoms and monosaccharides in a glycan, realized by the following schemes.
266   Multi-scale masking: During pre-training, it is desired to simultaneously acquire atom-atom, atom-
267   monosaccharide and monosaccharide-monosaccharide dependencies. To achieve this goal, in an all-
268   atom glycan graph (Section 3.1), we mask partial atom nodes and partial monosaccharide nodes,
269   and the model is asked to recover these masked nodes by leveraging their neighboring atoms and
      monosaccharides. The two-scale masking is performed as below:

      Benchmark tasks: We evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed models on the GlycanML bench-
312   mark (Xu et al., 2024). This benchmark contains a comprehensive set of 11 glycan property and
313   function prediction tasks, including glycan taxonomy prediction, glycan immunogenicity predic-
314   tion, glycosylation type prediction and protein-glycan interaction prediction. Readers are referred
315   to the original paper for detailed task descriptions and dataset statistics.
316

317
      mark, both GlycanAA and PreGlycanAA are equipped with 3 hierarchical message passing blocks.

319   monosaccharide predictor are implemented as an MLP with 2 linear layers and a ReLU nonlinearity
320   in between. For each benchmark task, we follow Xu et al. (2024) to perform task prediction with a 2-
321   layer MLP with ReLU activation. In protein-glycan interaction prediction, the ESM-1b pre-trained

368   Evaluation metrics: As in the original benchmark, we use Macro-F1 score as the metric for tax-
369   onomy and glycosylation type prediction, AUPRC as the metric for immunogenicity prediction,
370   Spearman’s ρ as the metric for interaction prediction, and weighted mean rank as the metric for
371   a model’s comprehensive performance. Weighted mean rank computes the weighted average of a
372   model’s ranks over all tasks, where each taxonomy prediction task weighs 1/8 and each of the other
373   three tasks weighs 1, such that the task number imbalance between different task types is eliminated.
374   Baselines: We compare our models with the baselines studied in the GlycanML benchmark (Xu
375   et al., 2024), including four monosaccharide-level glycan sequence encoders (i.e., LSTM (Hochre-
376   iter & Schmidhuber, 1997), ResNet (He et al., 2016), Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) and
377   Shallow CNN (Shanehsazzadeh et al., 2020)), eight monosaccharide-level glycan graph encoders
      (GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017), GAT (Veličković et al., 2017), MPNN (Gilmer et al., 2017),

379   Net (Zhang et al., 2023b) and GearNet-Edge (Zhang et al., 2023b)), four state-of-the-art all-atom
380   molecular encoders (i.e., Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021), GraphGPS (Rampášek et al., 2022), Uni-
381   Mol+ (Lu et al., 2024) and VabsNet (Zhuang et al., 2024)). Given the strong performance of RGCN
382   on modeling monosaccharide-level glycan graphs as shown in Xu et al. (2024), we additionally eval-
383   uate it on modeling the all-atom molecular graphs of glycans, namely All-Atom RGCN, and also
384   pre-train it with a similar mask prediction algorithm as PreGlycanAA, namely PreRGCN. To study
385   pre-training more in depth, we employ the pre-training methods, attribute masking and context pre-
386   diction, proposed in Hu et al. (2019) to pre-train GlycanAA, deriving the GlycanAA-Attribute and
387
      GlycanAA-Context models to compare with PreGlycanAA.
388   Results: In Table 1, we report the performance of the proposed models and various baselines. Based
389   on these results, we highlight the findings below:
390
391     • The superiority of GlycanAA over existing glycan encoders illustrates the benefits of all-
392       atom glycan modeling. It is observed that GlycanAA outperforms the best baseline result on 7
393
          out of 11 tasks and also surpasses all baselines in terms of weighted mean rank. On 4 out of 11
          tasks, i.e., phylum prediction, family prediction, immunogenicity prediction and glycosylation
394
          type prediction, the performance of GlycanAA is not superior, where the performance difference
395
          is not significant based on the one tailed t-test (α = 0.025) on the first three of them, except for
396       glycosylation type prediction. The dataset of glycosylation type prediction is relatively small
397       (with 1,356 training, 163 validation and 164 test samples), which makes GlycanAA overfit the
398       training set, leading to inferior test performance.
399       It is worth noticing that, in terms of weighted mean rank, GlycanAA also outperforms the Pre-
400       RGCN model pre-trained with a similar approach as PreGlycanAA. This result verifies the
401       value of modeling glycans on the all-atom level and also illustrates the importance of hierarchi-
402       cal structures to our pre-training method.
403     • The performance gains of PreGlycanAA over GlycanAA demonstrate the effectiveness of
404       the proposed pre-training method. PreGlycanAA outperforms GlycanAA on 8 out of 11
405       tasks and ranks first among all models in terms of weighted mean rank. Given the same model
406       architecture between PreGlycanAA and GlycanAA, we confirm that the proposed multi-scale
407       pre-training method can enhance the model capability.
408       By comparison, both GlycanAA-Attribute and GlycanAA-Context models show performance
409       decay compared to the GlycanAA model without pre-training. We suggest that these two pre-
410       training methods actually lead to trivial tasks during pre-training, which mainly causes the neg-
411       ative results. Specifically, the attribute masking method does not consider the correlation be-
412       tween atom and monosaccharide nodes during masking, and thus leads to the trivial prediction
413       of a masked monosaccharide based on some of its characteristic atoms; similarly, the context
414
          prediction method could select highly correlated center and anchor nodes in an all-atom glycan
          graph, leading to a trivial prediction task. By comparison, the proposed PreGlycanAA model
415
          performs multi-scale masking carefully to ensure as little correlation left in the unmasked nodes
416
          as possible, leading to clearly better performance than the GlycanAA without pre-training.
417
418     • Directly applying performant small molecule encoders or monosaccharide-level glycan en-
419
          coders to all-atom glycan modeling is unpromising. Graphormer, GraphGPS and Uni-Mol+
          have been shown to be effective in modeling small molecules with tens of atoms (Shi et al.,
420
          2022). However, benchmark results show that they do not perform well when modeling all-
421
          atom molecular graphs of glycans with hundreds of atoms. Similarly, compared to the well-
422       performing monosaccharide-level RGCN, the performance of All-Atom RGCN is unsatisfac-
423       tory. These results illustrate the necessity of dedicated design for all-atom glycan modeling.

519   6     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
520
521   In this work, we aim to model all-atom-wise glycan structures. We first propose the GlycanAA
522   model to encode heterogeneous all-atom glycan graphs. GlycanAA captures from local atomic-level
523
      interactions to global monosaccharide-level interactions with a carefully-designed hierarchical mes-
      sage passing scheme. To further enhance the representation power of GlycanAA, we pre-train it on
524
      a set of high-quality unlabeled glycans, deriving the PreGlycanAA model. During pre-training, the
525
      model learns to solve a multi-scale mask prediction task, which endows the model with knowledge
526   about different levels of dependencies in a glycan. Through extensively evaluating the proposed
527   models on the GlycanML benchmark, we illustrate the superior performance of GlycanAA over
528   existing glycan encoders and verify the further improvements achieved by PreGlycanAA.
529
      In the future, we will focus on boosting real-world glycan-related applications with the proposed
530
      models and their variants. For example, we will study how vaccine design and cancer research can
531
      be promoted by all-atom glycan machine learning models.

778   Accuracy curve: The accuracy curves in Figure 5(a) illustrate the model’s ability to recover masked
779   atoms and monosaccharides correctly along the pre-training process. The initial steep incline sug-
780   gests rapid learning in the early stage, followed by a gradual approach towards an asymptote, sig-
781
      nifying the model’s convergence. We can observe the slower convergence of the monosaccharide
      recovery accuracy compared to the atom recovery accuracy, indicating that the masked monosac-
782
      charide prediction task is harder to learn.
783
784   Perplexity curve: Perplexity is a measurement of how well a probability distribution predicts a
785   sample, often used in the context of language modeling Devlin (2018). A lower perplexity indicates
786   that the model is more confident at recovering masked elements to their true values. The perplexity
787
      curves in Figure 5(b) reflect the reduction of model’s uncertainty as pre-training proceeds. Similar
      to accuracy curves, the convergence of the monosaccharide recovery perplexity is slower than that
788
      of the atom recovery perplexity, again indicating the higher difficulty of the masked monosaccharide
789
      prediction task.
