009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Autoregressive models have demonstrated remarkable success in natural language
012             processing. In this work, we design a simple yet effective autoregressive architec-
013             ture for robotic manipulation tasks. We propose the Chunking Causal Transformer
014             (CCT), which extends the next-single-token prediction of causal transformers to
015             support multi-token prediction in a single pass. Further, we design a novel atten-
016             tion interleaving strategy that allows CCT to be trained efficiently with teacher-
017             forcing. Based on CCT, we propose the Autoregressive Policy (ARP) model,
018
                which learns to generate action sequences autoregressively. We find that action
                sequence learning enables better leverage of the underlying causal relationships
019
                in robotic tasks. We evaluate ARP across diverse robotic manipulation environ-
020
                ments, including Push-T, ALOHA, and RLBench, and show that it outperforms
021             the state-of-the-art methods in all tested environments, while being more efficient
022             in computation and parameter sizes. Video demonstrations, our source code and
023             the models of ARP are all included in the supplementary material.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Autoregressive models are the basis of recent breakthroughs in natural language processing (Min
029   et al., 2023). These models predict the next token in a given sequence based on the previous tokens.
030   Autoregressive models are implemented with causal transformers, where each token attends only
031   to preceding ones, and they are trained with the single objective of maximizing the conditional
032   likelihood of each token. Despite their simplicity, autoregressive models such as GPTs (Mann et al.,
033
      2020) are shown to demonstrate a reasoning ability that can capture causal dependencies (Prystawski
      et al., 2024). In this work, we explore the design of a simple autoregressive architecture that can be
034
      used for various robot manipulation tasks in diverse environments.
035
036   Decision Transformer (DT) and Trajectory Transformer (TT) are the pioneering approaches that use
037   autoregressive models to solve control tasks (Chen et al., 2021; Janner et al., 2021). These methods
038   learn to generate trajectories as (R1 , s1 , a1 , . . . , RT , sT , aT ), where Rt , st , at respectively denote
039
      the reward-to-go (Tamar et al., 2016), the state, and the action at time-step t. In contrast, we propose
      to predict only the future action sequence, and condition the prediction on the current state (or
040
      observation). Action sequence learning is more attainable as an objective in robotics applications,
041
      where the underlying reward functions are unknown or ill-defined, and observations, given as images
042   or point clouds, are high-dimensional and cannot be predicted accurately (Kroemer et al., 2021).
043   DT and TT are mainly used in tasks where low-dimensional state variables are fully observed. To
044   deal with uncertainty, our model generates a new action sequence after every k time-steps, using
045   an updated new observation and following the Model Predictive Control (MPC) approach. Action
046   sequence modeling also enables a better leverage of the underlying causal dependencies in robotic
047   tasks. Examples of such causal dependencies include: logical dependencies, where low-level actions
048   depend on high-level plans, spatial dependencies, where orientation depends on the end-effector’s
049   position, and temporal dependencies, where latter actions depend on earlier ones. We showcase the
050
      action sequence designs of our real robot experiment, Push-T, ALOHA, and RLBench in Figure 3.
051   We propose the Chunking Causal Transformer (CCT), an auto-regressive model that is tailored for
052   robotic tasks. CCT extends the next-token predictions of causal transformer to support multi-token
053   predictions in a single pass. CCT predicts the future tokens, or a chunk of actions, from empty
      tokens rather than from the original sequence, as illustrated in Figure A1. In doing so, CCT is more

      In this section, we present the Auto-regressive Policy (ARP), which predicts actions using the
      Chunking Causal Transformer (CCT). The architecture is summarized in Figure 1.

154   Action sequence design. Unlike natural language, robot actions lack a universal vocabulary. As
155   shown in Figure 2, different robot tasks may require drastically different types of actions. We there-
156   fore propose to represent actions as structured sequences that follow a format that is pre-defined for
157   each family of tasks. Figure 3 showcases the formats of the action sequences generated in our real
158
      robot experiment, Push-T, ALOHA, and RLBench tasks.
159   Action embedding and decoding. Language models map each word to a continuous vector called
160   word embedding. The word embeddings of the input sentences are fed into a causal transformer.
161   The distribution of the next word is decoded from the output embedding of the last word with a
      linear layer. Figure A2 and A3 illustrate our embedding and decoding methods for robot actions.

184   Discrete actions are embedded by a table lookup on a weight matrix and decoded into a categorical
185   distribution with a linear layer, similar to words in language modeling. Continuous actions are

191   Chunking causal transformer. Figure A1 illustrates the essential difference between a causal
192   transformer and our CCT. A causal transformer modifies the token embedding with causal attention
193   so that the last token becomes the next token. Our CCT modifies the token embedding with causal
194   attention for the known tokens ai (past actions) and bidirectional attention for the empty tokens ei
195
      (future actions). The empty tokens become the next tokens. This allows the prediction of multiple
      next tokens at once in a single forward pass by adding empty tokens. The advantages are two-fold:
196
      (1) A better accuracy is achieved because error accumulation is reduced when actions are grouped
197
      in chunks and executed as one unit (Zhao et al., 2023). (2) A better efficiency is achieved with fewer
198   forward passes because each forward pass predicts multiple tokens at once. We study the impacts
199   of action chunking in detail in Section 4. In ARP, CCT alternates between self-attention within the
200   input embeddings and cross-attention with vision features, as in Figure 1. We extract vision features
201   from a standard backbone identical to the ones used in SoTA methods, as detailed in section 4.
202
      Train-time attention interleaving. During training, a causal transformer is taught to predict each
203
      token in a given sequence by consuming all previous ground-truth tokens as input. This training
204   strategy is named teacher-forcing (Williams & Zipser, 1989). As shown in Figure 4, only a single
205   forward pass is required for training samples such as a1 , a2 , a3 → a4 (predict a4 from a1 , a2 , a3 ),
206   a1 , a2 → a3 , and a1 → a2 . Causal transformers are therefore efficiently trained with teacher-
207   forcing. We follow this teacher-forcing strategy. However, training CCT yields separate forward
208   passes per chunk. For example, the prediction of a4 depends on a2 , a3 , as in a1 , a2 , a3 , e4 → a4 ,
209   but a2 , a3 need to be replaced with e2 , e3 to predict them from a1 , as in a1 , e2 , e3 → a2 , a3 . This
210   prohibits the use of a single forward pass for both a1 , a2 , a3 , e4 → a4 and a1 , e2 , e3 → a2 , a3 . Note
211   ai denotes the i-th action and ei denotes the empty token for i-th action. This issue increases the
212
      training cost and drastically complicates the training procedure.
213   To resolve this, we first divide the attention into three categories: (1) causal attention within the
214   known tokens, (2) bidirectional attention within the empty tokens, and (3) causal attention between
215   the empty and the known tokens, as marked in Figure 4. Figure A4 shows that the causal attention
      within known tokens is computed repeatedly. We avoid this redundancy by precomputing the causal

      Does action chunking improve performance? Instead of predicting only the next token, our
      chunking causal transformer (CCT) is able to predict multiple next tokens, that is, a chunk of ac-
406
      tions. Figure 7 illustrates the relationship between chunk size and success rate. The first plot shows
407
      that larger chunks significantly improve policy performance, a trend also observed by ACT (Zhao
408   et al., 2023). This advantage of chunking actions seems generalizable to high-frequency control
409   in short-horizon tasks. Interestingly, while larger chunk sizes for joint positions improve perfor-
410   mance, one-shot predictions, where both end-effector waypoints and joint positions are predicted
411   simultaneously, yield inferior results, as in Table 1.
412
      The second plot indicates that for Push-T, policy performance is largely insensitive to the chunk size
413
      of low-level trajectories because the standard deviation of the success rate ranges between 1 and 2.
414   In this case, a moderate chunk size can be a better choice, given the common practice of executing
415   only the first few predicted actions and then rerunning the policy, a test-time technique reduces error
416   accumulation. This technique benefits from a moderate chunk size through early termination of
417   autoregressive generation without sacrificing performance or computational efficiency.
418
      In the third plot, we explore a different action sequence format for Push-T, where we remove high-
419
      level waypoints and flatten the trajectories into a vector, as detailed in Figure A6. This design
420   yields a completely different trend, with the policy performing well only when the chunk size is
421   1. The fourth plot shows that increasing the chunk size for high-level waypoints improves policy
422   performance. These findings demonstrate that the optimal chunk size depends on both the task and
423   the action sequence format. As a result, CCT’s ability to flexibly adjust chunk sizes is essential for
424   maximizing performance. Currently, chunk sizes are manually specified for each task, but a more
425   principled approach to adaptively adjusting chunk size would be ideal, as discussed in section 5.

514   5   D ISCUSSION
515
516   We have shown that ARP is a strong and universal architecture that can be trained to perform diverse
517   manipulation tasks. In the following, we discuss its limitations and potential future directions.
518
519
      Learning to plan. Planning is a key ability of intelligent agents. It requires the agent to reason
      not only about its actions but also their impacts on its environment (Garrett et al., 2021). Motivated
520
      by the reasoning capacity of auto-regressive models in NLP, a promising direction is to incorporate
521
      planning into ARP. One possible solution is to predict sequences of both states and actions. States
522   in robotics are typically high-dimensional, such as images or point clouds. Therefore, it would be
523   desirable to predict only key states instead of generating every frame in the future. To solve this
524   problem, ARP can be extended to generate future states by using recent hybrid architectures of
525   auto-regression and diffusion, such as Diffusion Forcing (Chen et al., 2024a),
526
      Interactive robot learning. Human-Robot collaboration improves efficiency by allowing the robot
527
      to recover from its errors (Mukherjee et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). One possible future direction is
528   to integrate active learning techniques into ARP to learn from immediate human feedback. The auto-
529   regressive mechanism naturally supports conditioning action prediction on human input. Moreover,
530   ARP can estimate the likelihood of action sequences. Likelihood is a common measure for iden-
531   tifying the most informative samples in active learning (Taylor et al., 2021). This can be used, for
532   example, to prioritize demonstrations of tasks where the robot encounters more difficulties.
533
      Adaptive action sequence learning. Despite ARP’s impressive performance, it still requires a man-
534   ual design of action sequence formats and chunk sizes for each environment. Developing a general
535   approach to automatically determine the optimal chunk size would not only improve ARP’s per-
536   formance, but also deepen our understanding of the action chunking techniques for robot imitation
537   learning in general. We discuss when and why action chunking matters in Appendix A.3. Addi-
538   tionally, unlike natural language, robot actions lack a universal vocabulary. A promising direction is
539   to design a universal robot action language that is applicable across multiple environments, which
      would reduce the cost of defining new actions, unify training datasets, and improve generalization.

689   A.3   D ISCUSSION ON ACTION C HUNKING
690
691   Action chunking has a clear downside – when predicting multiple actions at a time, the agent doesn’t
692   receive information about what state was observed after the first action. This means that the agent
693   is operating with less information than if a single-step prediction was used. At the same time, in a
694   MDP the state is guaranteed to be a sufficient statistic for the optimal policy. Given this information,
695   why should action chucking be useful?
696   We propose two main reasons. First, as has been explored in other imitation learning works, using
697   expert data means that the dataset often lacks information on how to recover from errors, which
698   means that predictions grow worse over time. Using longer action chunks effectively shortens the
699   time horizon. However, we find that action chunking still has noticeable benefits even when the state
700   is well-covered, such as in the Push-T environment. Additionally, this problem becomes less severe
701   as the dataset grows – when the prediction error goes to zero, so does the effect of error recovery
      Ross et al. (2011).
