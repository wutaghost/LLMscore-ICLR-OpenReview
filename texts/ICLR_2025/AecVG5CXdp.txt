009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The management of elevator traffic in large buildings is crucial for ensuring low
012            passenger travel times and energy consumption. We optimize the Elevator Group
013            Control System (EGCS) using a novel Reinforcement Learning (RL) approach.
014            Existing methods, including heuristic-based and pattern detection algorithms, of-
015            ten fall short in handling the complex and stochastic nature of elevator systems.
016
               This research proposes an end-to-end RL-based approach. A custom elevator sim-
               ulation environment representing the 6-elevator, 15-floor system at Vrije Univer-
017
               siteit Amsterdam (VU) is developed as a Markov Decision Process (MDP). Key
018
               innovations include a novel action space encoding to handle the combinatorial
019            complexity of elevator dispatching, the introduction of infra-steps to model con-
020            tinuous passenger arrivals, and a tailored reward signal to improve learning effi-
021            ciency. Additionally, we explore various ways of adapting the discounting factor
022            to the infra-step formulation. We investigate RL architectures based on Duel-
023            ing Double Deep Q-learning, showing that the proposed RL-based EGCS adapts
024            to fluctuating traffic patterns, learns from a highly stochastic environment, and
025            thereby outperforms a traditional rule-based algorithm.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
029
      1.1   P ROBLEM DESCRIPTION
030
031   In large structures, complex multi-lift systems cater to the high transportation demands. The system
032   responsible for determining which elevators to dispatch in response to passenger requests is known
033   as the Elevator Group Control System (EGCS). The EGCS plays a vital role in ensuring low wait
034   times and energy efficiency (Fernandez & Cortes, 2015). Traditional rule-based control methods
035   can be efficient in simple environments but fail to adapt to the complexity of modern buildings with
036   varying traffic patterns. Reinforcement Learning (RL) offers a promising alternative for optimizing
037
      EGCSs by allowing systems to autonomously learn from experience and adapt to complex environ-
      ments.
038
039   Designing an EGCS to efficiently match passengers with available lifts presents several challenges.
040   First, upon registering a floor button press (hall call), the EGCS must choose from multiple carts with
041   varying speeds, positions, destinations, and passenger loads. It may even dispatch more than one
042   cart to serve a new passenger. Second, the system does not know the new passenger’s destination
043
      until they enter the elevator and register a car button press inside it (car call). Similarly, it only
      knows the number of passengers associated with a hall call once they board the elevator and register
044
      their destinations. The agent must therefore deal with a high level of stochasticity in environment
045
      transitions. Third, traffic patterns fluctuate throughout the day, with morning up-peaks and afternoon
046   down-peaks placing the highest demands on elevator capacity. The system can also undergo long-
047   term changes, such as if a building becomes busier over the years. The agent needs to be robust
048   to handle the varying traffic patterns or long term changes. Lastly, solutions must be computed in
049   real-time.
050
051   1.2   RULE - BASED APPROACHES
052
053   Traditional elevator control systems are often based on rule-based algorithms (Fernandez & Cortes,
      2015). An example is the ETD algorithm (Smith, 2002), which minimizes the total trip time for


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      every new passenger while considering the slowdown incurred to existing passengers. Another
055   popular rule-based approach is sectorization, where the individual elevators are assigned a specific
056   range of floors and respond to hall calls only within these floors (Kameli & Collins, 1996). Idling
057   floors are selected dynamically, for example based on Particle Swarm Optimization (Li et al., 2007)
058   or Dynamic Programming (DP) in combination with traffic pattern detection (Chan & So, 1995).
059   Despite their utility, these algorithms are often too inflexible to deal with varying usage patterns
060   throughout the time of the day, day of the weeks and other key parameters, limiting their optimization
061   potential (Cortés et al., 2012).
062
063   1.3     RL IN EGCS
064
065   RL is highly effective for control-related tasks, enabling an agent to autonomously learn a robust
066   policy through interaction with an environment. When integrated with Deep Learning, RL allows
067
      the agent to recognize patterns in the environment data and make fine-grained decisions. This self-
      learning ability makes RL adaptable to highly specific scenarios, enhancing its suitability for real-
068
      world applications. RL is a well-established paradigm, widely applied in domains such as reservoir
069
      operation (Castelletti et al., 2010) and complex game-playing (Vinyals et al., 2019).
070
071   RL has been applied to EGCSs in limited capacities. Early works such as Crites & Barto (1998)
072   demonstrated the feasibility of RL in optimizing elevator routing. Interestingly, they framed the
073
      problem as a Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) problem by having one separate RL
      controller per elevator. Given the use of a Q-table, the approach faces issues of scalability.
074
075   Recent works have integrated deep learning with RL, such as Wei et al. (2020). The authors for-
076   malize their solution as a Single-Agent Reinforcement Learning (SARL) problem and implement
077   an Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C) model. They give the model full access to the group sizes and
078   destination floors of future passengers, which are normally unknown to the system. This creates a
079
      performance advantage usually unachievable in the real-world.
080   Another approach developed recently by Wan et al. (2024) improves upon previous limitations by
081   using a Dueling Double Deep Q-learning agent trained in a discrete-event simulation. The agent
082   is only prompted for an action whenever a passenger arrives in the system or an elevator reaches a
083   new floor. They adapt the discounting factor formulation to be able to deal with varying time in-
084   between steps. The authors developed an end-to-end RL controller, capable of fully functioning as
085
      an EGCS in a 20-floor, 4-cart environment. One drawback of their implementation is the design of
      the action space. They require a decision from the RL controller every time any elevator reaches a
086
      new floor while moving. The controller should then choose whether to stop at the floor or continue
087
      past it. When stopped, the controller should decide at every new event whether to stay idle, go up
088   or go down. This induces a large and complex action space and a large action density in a learning
089   episode, making the problem difficult.
090

094   both methodological and practical contributions.

182   Wei et al. (2020) and Wan et al. (2024) define the action space for every elevator to either continue
183   or stop at the next floor, leading to an action space of 2n, where n is the amount of elevators. This
184   reduces the action space, but the action density is high as the system is prompted for an action every
185
      time any elevator reaches a new floor.
186   Alternatively, the EGCS can act on a higher level by matching each active hall call to an elevator,
187   and the lower-level movement decisions are then delegated to a module that makes the carts stop at
188   every floor for which the cart has a hall call assigned. However, in a multi-elevator system, each
189   elevator can respond to each hall call, leading to significant combinatorial complexity in the number
190   of possible actions. In a system with n elevators and m active hall calls, there are nm possible
191
      assignments (Hamdi & Mulvaney, 2007).
192   In order to avoid both problems, we formalize the action space differently. We only prompt the
193   EGCS for an action when a new passenger or passenger group enters the system and registers a
194   hall call. The EGCS then decides which elevator(s) to send to the new hall call. The hall call floor
195   is then added to the selected elevator(s)’s destination queue, and each elevator then independently
196   works on emptying their destination queue automatically, without requiring further input from the
197
      RL agent. Passenger expectations dictate that the elevator should always stop at a floor for which
      a car call is registered and it should empty its destination queue in the direction of travel before
198
      reversing. These constraints imply that there is only one course of action possible, and there is no
199
      need to make further action decisions.

      2.3.2                I NFRA - STEPS
202
203   Traditional RL frameworks typically operate in time-discrete MDPs. Because of the asynchronous
204   nature of elevator arrivals in our environment, there is a variable time in-between decision moments
205   where the agent is prompted for an action. We implement a discrete-event environment and introduce
206   the concept of infra-steps, defined as intervals of 0.1 seconds during which the system refreshes
207
      elevator positions and processes events. When an event appears, the environment fetches the present
      state and prompts the agent for an action. There can therefore be a variable number of infra-steps
208
      between steps. However, the agent still only interacts with the environment at every step, as in the
209
      basic formulation of discrete-time RL. This mechanism is illustrated in Figure 3.
210

305
306   To assess the performance of our RL algorithm, we implemented the rule-based ETD algorithm
307   developed by Smith (2002). The ETD algorithm aims to minimize the total time impact incurred by
308   serving a new passenger in the system. It minimizes the time to serve the passenger itself while also
309   considering the impact on other passengers’ journey time if a certain elevator were to pick up the
310   passenger. This is formalized with the following equation:
311

      where ET De is the Estimated Time to Destination in seconds for elevator e and Delaye,p is the
316
      delay in seconds incurred to passenger p currently being served by elevator e. The cost for elevator e
317   is the sum of the time it will take to serve the passenger plus the delay incurred to all other passengers
318   p currently served by elevator e. The elevator e with the lowest total cost is chosen.
319

      2.4.2     RL AGENT
325
326   At every step where a hall call is registered, the environment provides the current state and the agent
327   is then responsible for deciding which elevator(s) it will send to the hall call.
328   We created several variations of an RL agent based on a Dueling Double Deep Q-learning architec-
329   ture.
330
331           1. Combinatorial Action Space: The RL agent computes the Q-values for all combinations
332              of elevators responding to the new hall call, from one to three elevators. This results in an
333              action space of C(6, 3) + C(6, 2) + C(6, 1) = 41 outputs. The chosen action is the argmax
334              over all outputs.
335           2. Action Branching: Inspired by the work of Tavakoli et al. (2018), we employ an action
336              branching strategy. The state is processed commonly by a shared NN, and branches off to
337              six individual NN’s, where each elevator makes an independent binary decision (respond
338              or not to a call). This decomposes the high-dimensional action space into multiple lower-
339
                 dimensional sub-spaces, and results in an action space of 6 ∗ 2 = 12 outputs. The chosen
                 action is the aggregate of the argmax of every branch.
340
341
      Both architectures are shown in Figure 4.

      3.1     AGENT DESIGN AND COMPARISON TO BASELINE
351   We tested the difference in the NN architectures employed. We compared the Combinatorial ar-
352   chitecture, where every action combination is a single output, to the Branching architecture, where
353   separate outputs control one elevator each. We also compared both algorithms to the baseline ETD
354   algorithm. Figure 5 shows experimental results.
355
      Both versions of the RL agent beat the baseline by about 2 seconds per passenger, or approximately
356
      10%.
357
358   The Combinatorial agent is largely superior to the Branching agent. Interestingly, the average wait
359   time per passenger is similar, although the Branching agent spends more energy. Looking at the
360
      number of elevators sent per call, we see that the Branching agent often sends fewer than one elevator
      to a call on average, meaning it sometimes sends zero elevators to a hall call. It thereby incurs a
361
      large negative reward every time. It is unable to coordinate its branches well enough to avoid this
362
      situation. As coordination between multiple sub-actions is crucial in this problem, the agent benefits
363   from having one central decision-making module, outweighing the disadvantage of more complex
364   input and output spaces.

      Further experiments on the agent action space design were conducted, as well as additional tests
      to assess the robustness and adaptability of the agent by running it on a busier environment than it
367
      was initially trained on. The results of these experiments can be seen in Appendix A.4 and A.5,

      respectively.

477   5   C ONCLUSION
478
479   In this paper, we addressed the problem of optimizing complex elevator dispatching using a novel
480   RL approach. By formulating the problem as an MDP, we captured the inherent uncertainties and
481   complex nature of elevator systems, particularly with the introduction of infra-steps to simulate
482
      continuous passenger arrivals.
483   Methodologically, we gained valuable insights by comparing two discounting schemes: fixed and
484   variable. The fixed discounting strategy proved more stable and effective in managing varying time
485   intervals between actions. Additionally, we compared two RL agent variants: the branching and
      combinatorial agents. Our results show that the combinatorial architecture outperformed the branch-


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      ing strategy, leading to more efficient decision-making. The innovative encoding of the action space
487   allowed us to overcome the typical combinatorial complexity in this domain.
488
489   On the practical side, using a custom-built simulation environment of a 6-elevator, 15-floor system
490
      at VU Amsterdam, the proposed RL-based solution demonstrated superiority over a modern rule-
      based system. The RL agent, utilizing the Dueling Double Deep Q-Learning algorithm, was able to
491
      significantly reduce passenger travel times by efficiently adapting to complex traffic patterns. These
492
      promising results underline the potential for practical implementation of RL-based control for real-
493   world elevator systems, particularly in environments with complex, stochastic traffic patterns that
494   can be optimized from data.

623   A.2   A DDITIONAL BASELINES
624
      To verify the performance of our Baseline agent, we compared it to additional, simpler baselines.
625
626   Hereunder are the simple baselines used:
627
628         1. Random Agent This agent assigns a random elevator to every hall call
629         2. First Agent This agent assigns elevator 1 (out of 6) to every hall call.
630
            3. Closest Agent This agent assigns the elevator that is physically closest to the hall call
631            location, regardless of whether it is moving or how many passengers it is currently serving.
632
633
            4. Sector Agent This agent splits the building into six equal zones, and every elevator re-
               sponds to hall calls within its zone. This idea is still used in many elevator systems, al-
634
               though in a more sophisticated configuration (Crites & Barto, 1998).
635
636         5. Least Busy Agent This agent assigns the hall call to the agent with the smallest number of
637            destinations in its current queue (hall calls + car calls).
638
639
      We ran all baseline algorithms for 20 iterations on the test environment to get a reliable estimate of
      average performance. Figure 8 shows that most agents perform better than the absolute baseline of
640
      sending a random elevator to the hall call. The ETD baseline performs best overall. The LeastBusy
641
      agent performs comparably to the ETD agents, although it is slightly less efficient. This is likely
642   because the length of the destination queue of an elevator is strongly related to its ETD score.
643   Surprisingly, the Sector agent performed worse than the Random agent. This is likely because
644   some floors see more traffic than others, and assigning only one elevator to those busy floors would
645   be comparable to always choosing the busiest elevator to respond to those calls. Modern Sector
646   algorithms adjust the zones dynamically based on observed traffic patterns and can have multiple
647   elevators per zone (Al-Sharif et al., 2019), avoiding this problem. Our implementation of the agent
      is therefore too simplistic to reflect the performance of a modern Sector agent.

671   A.3   D ETAILS ON AGENT TRAINING
672
673   The Combinatorial agent has three hidden fully-connected layers of size 128, 512, and 256 neurons,
674   respectively, with ReLU non-linearities. The total input size is 28, while the maximum output size
675   is 41 in the case of up to three elevators being allowed to respond to a hall call. The Branching agent
676   has the same main body, but the last layer before the output splits into six distinct branches of 128
677
      neurons each before proceeding to the output layer. This NN has 28 input neurons and 12 output
      neurons.
678
679   The optimizer used for these NNs is AdamW, with a learning rate of 5e-4. We also apply a Cosine
680   Annealing scheduler to the learning rate, which reduces the learning rate throughout training from
681   5e-4 to 0. This is done to stimulate exploration at first and exploitation later in the training. We use
682   the Huber loss function.
683   All further run parameters are set as described in Table 2.
684
      For experiments, RL agents were trained on 10M steps of the train environment. The environment
685
      was composed of 27684 steps, so the agent saw every transition ≈ 361 times. The mean and standard
686
      deviation of state features of the training environment were used to normalize the state features of
687   the training, validation and test environment. The agent was evaluated 30 times at regular intervals
688   on the validation environment. The best agent on validation was retained as final agent. The selected
689   agents were then ran 20 times on a full run of the test environment to obtain an unbiased estimate of
690   the actual performance.

      A.4   ACTION SPACE SIZE
695
      We compared versions of the agent that could send up to one, two, or three elevators to a hall call.
696   The advantage of being able to send less elevators to a hall call is a reduced action space, at the cost
697   of a less expressive action space. The action space in case of being able to send one elevator max
698   is C(6, 1) = 6. In case of two elevators max, the action space is C(6, 2) + C(6, 1) = 21. In case
699   of three elevators max, the action space is C(6, 3) + C(6, 2) + C(6, 1) = 41 outputs. The agent is
700   never allowed to send zero elevators so that action was not included. We did not allow more than
701   three elevators to respond to a hall call as this would make the output size too large and would also
      not be realistic in practice.
