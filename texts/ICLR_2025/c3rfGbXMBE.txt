009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Topic modeling is a fundamental task in natural language processing, allowing
012
               the discovery of latent thematic structures in text corpora. While Large Language
013
               Models (LLMs) have demonstrated promising capabilities in topic discovery, their
014            direct application to topic modeling suffers from issues such as incomplete topic
015            coverage, misalignment of topics, and inefficiency. To address these limitations,
016            we propose LLM-ITL, a novel LLM-in-the-loop framework that integrates LLMs
017            with many existing Neural Topic Models (NTMs). In LLM-ITL, global topics
018            and document representations are learned through the NTM, while an LLM re-
019            fines the topics via a confidence-weighted Optimal Transport (OT)-based align-
020            ment objective. This process enhances the interpretability and coherence of the
021            learned topics, while maintaining the efficiency of NTMs. Extensive experiments
022
               demonstrate that LLM-ITL can help NTMs significantly improve their topic inter-
               pretability while maintaining the quality of document representation.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Topic modeling is an essential task in natural language processing that uncovers hidden thematic
029
      structures within large text collections in an unsupervised way. The ability to automatically ex-
030
      tract topics has proven to be invaluable across a range of disciplines, such as bioinformatics (Liu
031   et al., 2016), marketing research (Reisenbichler & Reutterer, 2019), and information retrieval (Yi
032   & Allan, 2009). Topic models are usually based on probabilistic frameworks that generate a set of
033   interpretable global topics, each represented as a distribution over vocabulary terms. These topics
034   are then used to represent individual documents as mixtures of topics, providing a structured and
035   interpretable view of the corpus. Recently, research on topic modeling has shifted from classical
036   Bayesian methods such as Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) (Blei et al., 2003) to Neural Topic
037   Models (NTMs) (Zhao et al., 2021; Churchill & Singh, 2022; Wu et al., 2024) that use deep neural
038   networks to model document-topic distributions, enabling more expressive and flexible representa-
039
      tions compared to their probabilistic counterparts.
040   While Large Language Models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a;b) have redefined
041   the landscape of natural language processing, topic models continue to hold their place as valuable
042   tools for text analysis. Specifically, LLMs can provide a fine-grained understanding of a document;
043   however, given a large collection of domain-specific documents, topic models are more suitable to
044   obtain a clear global view of the topics in a more interpretable way with much less computational
045
      cost. Unsurprisingly, it has been a trending research direction to use LLMs to improve topic mod-
      eling (Rijcken et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Pham et al., 2023; Mu et al., 2024; Doi et al., 2024;
046
      Chang et al., 2024). Despite the promising performance of these initial studies, most existing meth-
047
      ods involve prompting LLMs to generate topics for each document in the corpus, which may lead to
048   several limitations. As LLMs are asked to focus on a document individually, they may be unable to
049   cover all the topics across all the documents in the corpus (Doi et al., 2024), which is critical in topic
050   modeling. Moreover, although LLMs excel at capturing local context, they usually struggle with
051   long documents with multiple interrelated topics, which may evolve or shift throughout the text.
052   With their limited window of focus, LLMs may miss key topics of a document that are necessary to
053   fully understand its content. Finally, it is computationally expensive as LLMs have to do inference
      for documents in the corpus; thus, existing methods usually scale poorly with large datasets.

054
      To overcome the aforementioned limitations, we propose LLM-ITL, a framework that integrates
055   LLMs into NTMs and enhances the overall quality and interpretability of the learned topics, while
056   maintaining computational efficiency. Specifically, to enhance the interpretability of the topics
057   learned by an NTM, we introduce an LLM-based refinement step. The representative words for
058   each topic, as generated by the NTM, are provided to the LLM, which suggests improved words that
059   better capture the semantic meaning of the topic. The refinement process is guided by a novel plug-
060   in objective based on Optimal Transport (OT), which ensures that the topics learned by the NTM
061   align closely with the LLM’s refinements. Additionally, to mitigate potential hallucinations from
062   the LLM (i.e., the generation of inaccurate or irrelevant suggestions), we introduce a confidence-
063
      weighted mechanism that adjusts the influence of the LLM’s suggestions based on their confidence
      scores. Our proposed LLM-ITL framework offers the following key contributions:
064
065
             • Improved balance between topic coherence and document representation quality:
066            With the LLM’s refined topics and OT-based alignment, the topics generated are more
067            interpretable and semantically coherent. At the same time, LLM-ITL ensures that the
068            document-topic distributions, as learned by the NTM, remain high-quality and reflective
069            of the document’s content.
070          • Efficiency and scalability: Unlike most existing LLM-based approaches that rely on
071            document-level LLM analysis, LLM-ITL uses LLMs at the word level, significantly re-
072            ducing computational overhead for large datasets.
073
             • Flexibility: LLM-ITL is a modular framework that can integrate with a variety of NTMs
074
               and LLMs, offering flexibility in model selection depending on the application and compu-
075            tational constraints.
076
077
             • State-of-the-art performance: Extensive experimental results on multiple benchmark
               datasets show that LLM-ITL achieves state-of-the-art performance in both topic coherence
078
               and document representation quality.

083   2.1   P ROBLEM S ETUP FOR T OPIC M ODELING
084
085   Topic models have been popular across various fields for their ability to interpret text corpora in
086
      an unsupervised manner. Given a document collection D := {d1 , . . . , dN }, a topic model learns
      to discover a set of global topics T := {t1 , . . . , tK }, each of which is a distribution over the V
087
      vocabulary words t ∈ ∆V (∆ denotes the probability simplex). Ideally, each topic represents a
088
      semantic concept that can be interpreted with its top-weighted words. At the document level, the
089   topic model represents each document as a distribution over the K topics, i.e., z ∈ ∆K , which
090   indicates the topic proportion of each topic within the document. The interpretability of topic models
091   derives from both the corpus-level topics T , and the document-level topical representation z for each
092   document.
093
094   2.2   N EURAL T OPIC M ODELS
095
096   A Neural Topic Model (NTM) (Miao et al., 2017; Srivastava & Sutton, 2017; Card et al., 2017;
097   Dieng et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2023a;b) is typically trained by modeling p(z|x) and
098   p(x|z), where x ∈ NV represents the Bag-of-Words (BOWs) of a document. NTMs, which employ
099
      deep neural networks for topic modeling, are commonly based on Variational Auto-Encoders (VAEs)
      (Kingma & Welling, 2013) and Amortized Variational Inference (AVI) (Rezende et al., 2014). For
100

101

102   objective of VAE-NTMs is to maximize the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO):

      3.1   LLM- BASED T OPIC S UGGESTION
163
164   During the training of an NTM, it typically generates a set of topics, where each topic is represented
165   by a distribution over words, with the highest-probability words forming the core “meaning” of
166   the topic. While these words offer a rough semantic grouping, they often lack clarity or precision,
167   leading to difficulties in interpretation. For instance, topics may contain words that are too general,
168   too specific, or semantically ambiguous, making it hard for users to derive clear labels or understand
169   the thematic focus of the topic.
170   To address this, LLM-ITL proposes to use LLMs to suggest better words or labels that more clearly
171   express the same underlying concept. The LLM is prompted with the top words from each topic,
172   and it generates two outputs: a topic label, which is a concise and interpretable summary of the
173   topic; and a set of refined topic words, which better represent the underlying semantic concept of
174   the topic. This process capitalizes on the LLM’s ability to grasp language nuances and provide
175   more semantically rich suggestions for the topic. The LLM’s extensive pre-training on diverse and
176
      large datasets allows it to capture subtle relationships between words that may not be apparent in the
      purely statistical or neural-based methods employed by NTMs.
177
178   To obtain the topic label and refined words in a structured manner, chain-of-thought (CoT) prompt-
179   ing (Wei et al., 2022) is employed. CoT prompting encourages the LLM to reason step-by-step
180   through the task, ensuring that it carefully considers the topic words before generating a label and
181   refinement. The LLM’s output sequence s includes both the topic label and refined words, extracted
182
      as follows for each set of topic words:

      3.3   C ONFIDENCE -W EIGHTED T OPIC R EFINEMENT
224
      LLMs, despite their powerful language capabilities, can sometimes produce hallucinated out-
225   puts—irrelevant or incorrect suggestions that do not align with the input data (Ji et al., 2023). To
226   mitigate the impact of such hallucinations, LLM-ITL introduces a confidence-weighted refinement
227   mechanism. The confidence mechanism assesses the reliability of the LLM’s refinements and adjusts
228   their influence on the NTM’s training accordingly. This ensures that high-confidence refinements
229   have a greater impact on the final topic representation, and vice versa.
230
      We propose two methods for calculating topic labeling confidence, considering whether the LLM is
231   open-source or not: (1) Label token probability, applicable for open-source LLMs where the token
232   probability of their generation is accessible; (2) Word intrusion confidence, available for both open
233   and closed-source LLMs.
234

236   topic label generated by the LLM. It reflects the LLM’s certainty in generating the specific topic

      3.4   I NTEGRATION WITH NTM S
260
261   One of the core strengths of LLM-ITL lies in its flexibility to integrate with various NTMs while
262   leveraging the semantic capabilities of LLMs for topic refinement. The framework is designed to
263   complement and enhance NTMs, providing an efficient and interpretable topic modeling pipeline.
264   The LLM-ITL framework is highly modular and can be seamlessly integrated with a wide range
265   of NTMs. Here, we focus on the VAE-NTM framework that accommodate many NTMs (Miao
266   et al., 2017; Srivastava & Sutton, 2017; Card et al., 2017; Dieng et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2020;
267   Nguyen & Luu, 2021; Xu et al., 2023a;b), while our framework is not limited to VAE-NTMs only.

279   4     R ELATED W ORK
280
281   Topic Models Classical topic models, such as Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) (Blei et al., 2003)
282   and its variants (Blei & Lafferty, 2006; Rosen-Zvi et al., 2012; Yan et al., 2013), are Bayesian prob-
283   abilistic models with various generative assumptions about the documents. Neural Topic Models
284   (NTMs) (Miao et al., 2017; Srivastava & Sutton, 2017; Card et al., 2017; Dieng et al., 2020; Zhao
285
      et al., 2020; Nguyen & Luu, 2021; Xu et al., 2023a;b) use deep neural networks to learn topics and
      document representations, and are commonly based on Variational Autoencoders (VAE) (Kingma &
286
      Welling, 2013) and Amortized Variational Inference (AVI) (Rezende et al., 2014). Clustering-based
287
      topic models (Sia et al., 2020; Grootendorst, 2022) discover topics using clustering algorithms based
288   on embeddings from pre-trained language models. Ultimately, the capability of these models to in-
289   terpret a corpus is limited by the top words representation of each topic.
290
291
      LLMs in Topic Modeling LLMs have been involved in topic modeling in various ways. Rijcken
292
      et al. (2023) investigate the use of ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2022) to generate descriptions for topic words
293
      and found the effectiveness of these topic descriptions. Recent works leverage LLMs for topic model
294   evaluation in different ways, such as applying LLMs for word intrusion or topic rating for topics
295   (Rahimi et al., 2023; Stammbach et al., 2023), or keyword generation for documents (Yang et al.,
296   2024). LLM-based topic models have emerged (Wang et al., 2023; Pham et al., 2023; Mu et al.,
297   2024; Doi et al., 2024), which prompt LLMs to generate topics and assign topics to documents.
298   Different from these methods that focus on the document-level, ours prompts LLMs to suggest better
299   topic words which are used to refine the training of NTMs. More recently, Chang et al. (2024) show
300   that LLMs are effective at refining topic words, leading to improved topic coherence. However,
301   their method refines the topic words of trained topic models in a post-hoc manner, while ours is a
302
      regularization term for training NTMs. Our method is also loosely related to uncertainty estimation
      of LLMs and we omit the discussion on this in Appendix C.

      Settings of LLM-ITL LLM-ITL is a framework compatible with most NTMs and LLMs. We use
341
      ETM, SCHOLAR, and CLNTM as the base models for our experiments. We use LLAMA3-8B-
342
      Instruct2 in LLM-ITL for main experiments. For OT computation, we use GloVe (Pennington et al.,
343   2014) word embeddings pre-trained on Wikipedia to construct the OT cost matrix, and compute the
344   OT distance using the POT3 package. For the topic labeling confidence, we use label token proba-
345   bility in Eq. 8 for our main experiments. As for the hyper-parameters of LLM-ITL, we set the topic
346   refinement strength γ to 200; and the refinement step T refine to 150 for ETM and 450 for SCHOLAR
347   and CLNTM. We set the number of words for the topic label to 2 when prompting the LLM. All
348   hyper-parameters of LLM-ITL are studied in the following sections. As for the LLM generation, we
349   use greedy decoding to enable deterministic output and set the maximum new generation tokens to
350   300. Each trial4 of LLM-ITL in our experiment takes a few hours on a single 80GB A100 GPU.
351
352   Evaluation Metrics We evaluate both the topic quality and the document representation quality
353   for topic models. For topic quality, we apply the widely used topic coherence metric, Normalized
354   Pointwise Mutual Information (NPMI) (Lau et al., 2014). We report the average NPMI values (in
355
      percentage) of all topics. Moreover, topic diversity (Dieng et al., 2020) is also evaluated, which is
      reported in Appendix E.3. For document representation quality, we evaluate the alignment between
356
      a document’s true label and the top-weighted topic of its topical representation using external clus-
357
      tering metrics, known as topic alignment (Chuang et al., 2013; Pham et al., 2023). We compute
358   the commonly used Purity and Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) to evaluate clustering perfor-
359   mance. Since Purity and NMI are considered equally important, fall within the same range (from 0
360   to 1), and are often reported together, we report their average as PN, serving as an overall indica-
361   tor of topic alignment performance. Detailed results for Purity and NMI are provided in Appendix
362   E.4. Intuitively, topic coherence (e.g., NPMI) reflects how coherent the learned topic words are,
363   while topic alignment (e.g., PN) indicates how well the model represents the documents through the
364   learned topics. Further details on the calculation of these metrics are provided in Appendix D.3.

527   6   C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we introduced LLM-ITL, a novel framework that integrates Large Language Models
530   (LLMs) with Neural Topic Models (NTMs) to address the limitations of both traditional topic mod-
531
      els and the direct use of LLMs for topic discovery. By incorporating a confidence-weighted Optimal
      Transport (OT)-based topic alignment, LLM-ITL improves the interpretability and coherence of top-
532
      ics while maintaining the quality of document representations. Our framework effectively leverages
533
      the strengths of both LLMs and NTMs, offering a flexible, scalable, and efficient solution for topic
534   modeling. Extensive experiments on benchmark datasets demonstrate that the LLM-ITL variants of
535   NTMs achieve state-of-the-art performance in terms of topic coherence and document representa-
536   tion. In terms of limitations, the framework’s reliance on LLM-generated refinements introduces a
537   dependency on the quality of the LLM’s outputs, which may vary based on the model used. More-
538   over, the method has been primarily evaluated on benchmark datasets, and its performance in more
539   domain-specific corpora may require further investigation.

      C        R ELATED WORK : LLM U NCERTAINTY E STIMATION
919
920
      Uncertainty estimation for LLMs (Geng et al., 2024) is emerging with the rapid usage of LLMs and
921   their risk of hallucination (Ji et al., 2023). Sequence probability (Ren et al., 2022) leverages token
922   probabilities to measure answer confidence. Verbalized confidence (Tian et al., 2023; Xiong et al.,
923   2023) utilizes the LLM’s own capability to evaluate its answer uncertainty. Consistency-based (Lin
924   et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023) approaches sample multiple outputs from the LLM and measure
925   answer consistency as uncertainty. Entropy-based (Kuhn et al., 2023; Hou et al., 2023) approaches
926   use multiple LLM outputs to estimate the output space and compute entropy as uncertainty for the
927   answer. Hybrid frameworks (Chen & Mueller, 2023; Gao et al., 2024) combine different approaches
928   for a comprehensive estimation. Internal states (Chen et al., 2024) are another useful source for LLM
929
      uncertainty quantification. Unlike those works that estimate uncertainty for LLMs in general natural
      language generation tasks, ours focuses on task-specific uncertainty of the LLM in suggesting topic
930
      words.

984    D.3    D ETAILS OF E VALUATION M ETRICS
985
986    For topic evaluation, we apply the commonly-used topic coherence metric, Normalized Pointwise
987    Mutual Information (NPMI) (Lau et al., 2014), which evaluates topic coherence based on the co-
988    occurrence of the topic’s top words in a reference corpus. We use Wikipedia as the reference corpus
989
       for NPMI and consider the top 10 words of each topic, with implementation done using the Palmetto
       package18 (Röder et al., 2015). We report the average NPMI score (in percentage) of all learned top-
990
       ics. For documents’ topical representation (i.e., topic proportion) evaluation, a common practice is
991
       to compare the document clusters formed by topic proportions with those formed by the documents’
992    true labels, known as topic alignment. Following previous works (Chuang et al., 2013; Pham et al.,
993    2023), we assign each test document to a cluster based on the top-weighted topic of its topical repre-
994    sentation, and compute Purity and Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) based on the documents’
995    cluster assignments and their true labels. As Purity and NMI are often reported together and within
996    the same range, we report the average score of both metrics as PN. For all evaluations, we use the
997    model state at the end of the training iteration to compute all evaluation metrics.

1204   Considering the efficiency of including confidence scores during the training of LLM-ITL, we focus
1205   solely on single-sample approaches for LLM uncertainty estimation in our study, where we run a
1206   single round of LLM inference for a given topic. We consider the following confidence alternatives
1207   to our topic labeling confidence for our study: (1) No Conf: No LLM confidence estimation is in-
1208   cluded, and Conf(wkl ) = 1 in Eq. 10 during the training. (2) Verbalized confidence (Xiong et al.,
1209   2023), which directly asks the LLM for its confidence in solving a problem. The prompt we used
1210   for eliciting verbalized confidence is shown in Figure A2. (3) Self-Reflective confidence (Chen &
1211   Mueller, 2023), which prompts the LLM to evaluate its own answer in a two-stage manner. The
1212
       topic label is obtained in the first stage, and the LLM evaluates this answer in a follow-up ques-
       tion (Figure A3). (4) p(True) (Kadavath et al., 2022), which is similar to self-reflective confidence,
1213
       but asks a true/false question instead. It takes the token probability of the response as the confi-
1214
       dence measure (Figure A3). (5) SeqLike (Ren et al., 2022), which computes the length-normalized
1215   sequence likelihood of the output from the LLM.

       coherence better, it biases the topics towards the LLM’s knowledge rather than the topics within the

1219
       intrusion confidence consistently yield the best topic alignment performance, suggesting greater
1220
       relevance of the topics to the corpus.
