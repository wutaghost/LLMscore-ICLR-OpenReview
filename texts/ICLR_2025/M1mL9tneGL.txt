030                                                                A BSTRACT
031
032               Restoring authentic facial features from low-quality images presents an extremely
033               challenging task, due to the intricate real-world degradations and the inherently ill-
034               posed nature of the problem. Existing methods, which utilize a codebook prior, help
035               alleviate the complexity of the restoration process and produce visually plausible
036               outcomes. However, these methods struggle to accurately capture the mapping
037               between low-quality (LQ) and high-quality (HQ) images in the discrete latent
038               space, leading to suboptimal results. Inspired by the success of auto-regressive
039               generation paradigm in discrete modeling problems (e.g., large language models),
040
                  we propose an Auto-Regressive Transformer based Face Restoration (ART-FR)
                  method to mitigate this mapping challenge. Specifically, with the aid of a visual
041
                  tokenizer, we reformulate the face restoration task as a conditional generation
042
                  problem within the discrete latent space. Furthermore, a masked generative image
043               transformer is employed to model the distribution of this latent space, conditioned
044               on LQ features. Face restoration is subsequently performed in the latent space
045               through iterative sampling, with the HQ image reconstructed using a pretrained
046               decoder. Extensive experimental validation demonstrates ART-FR exhibits superior
047               performance across various benchmark datasets.

071   2023), end-to-end frameworks relying on minimizing fidelity measures (e.g., pixel-wise losses) tend

078   based methods, while promising, introduce unwanted randomness as a consequence of incorporating
079   random Gaussian noise (Wu et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023). Furthermore, the computational expense
080   is significant due to the necessity of executing numerous diffusion steps for the inference process.
081   With the aid of vector-quantized (VQ) autoencoder (Van Den Oord et al., 2017), state-of-the-art
082   works have simplified the face restoration problem from a pixel-level task to code prediction task in
083   discrete latent space, thereby reducing the complexity of the LQ-HQ mapping and demonstrating
084   significant robustness against severe degradation. Nevertheless, existing methods still struggle to
085   accurately model the mapping between LQ images and their corresponding discrete HQ latent
086   representations. VQFR (Gu et al., 2022) employs a simple nearest-neighbor code matching technique,
087
      while Codeformer (Zhou et al., 2022) and DAEFR (Tsai et al., 2023) utilize an additional prediction
      network for the task. None of these methods accurately capture the discrete HQ latent representations
088
      due to the significant domain gap between the feature maps of LQ and HQ images.
089
090   Auto-regressive generation paradigm have recently spurred remarkable advancements in the modeling
091   of discrete distributions. Notably, the GPT series (Radford et al., 2019; Brown, 2020) and other large
092   language models (LLMs) have showcased exceptional comprehension and reasoning capabilities
093
      across diverse scenarios. Likewise, auto-regressive image synthesis methods have delivered high-
      quality image generation (Tian et al., 2024; Chang et al., 2022). Recognizing that the code-prediction
094
      problem of VQ-based face restoration aligns seamlessly with the Auto-Regressive Transformers’
095
      capability to model the distribution of discrete tokens, we reconceptualize face restoration as a
096   generative task on discrete latent space, conditioned on LQ features. Under this framework, we
097   propose our masked Auto-Regressive Transformer for Face Restoration, referred to as ART-FR.
098
099
      The core concept of ART-FR is to establish a conditional auto-regressive modeling framework for
      restoration tasks, as depicted in Fig. 2. We adopt a two-stage learning strategy. First, following
100
      previous VQ-based methods, we train a visual tokenizer to discretize continuous image data into a
101
      grid of 2D tokens, which are subsequently flattened into a 1D sequence for Auto-Regressive (AR)
102   learning. In the second stage, we integrate a bidirectional transformer for Masked Auto-regressive
103   Modeling (Chang et al., 2022). Additionally, we treat LQ features (conditional information) as
104   supplementary tokens to guide the auto-regressive generation process, mapping the masked tokens to
105   HQ tokens. During inference, ART-FR generates predicted HQ tokens by iteratively sampling from
106   the learned latent distribution, which are then decoded back into HQ images using the visual token
107   decoder. Leveraging the generative capabilities of auto-regressive transformers, we can effectively
      mitigate the mapping challenge from LQ images to discrete HQ representations in latent space, also

      2.1   B LIND FACE R ESTORATION .
124
125
      Blind face restoration, a specialized subfield of image restoration, has garnered significant attention
126
      in recent years. Given the inherently ill-posed nature, the restoration process typically necessitates
127   auxiliary priors to regularize the solution space.
128
129   Previous works. Traditional approaches primarily relied on two types of face-specific priors:
130
      geometric priors and reference priors. Methods based on geometric priors, including facial landmarks
      (Chen et al., 2018), face parsing maps (Chen et al., 2021), and 3D shapes (Hu et al., 2020), struggle to
131
      accurately estimate geometric information from degraded LQ images. Reference-based approaches
132
      (Dogan et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020) use images of the same identity, which could be difficult to
133   obtain in practice. Leveraging pre-trained generative models, such as StyleGAN (Karras et al.,
134   2019) and DDPM (Ho et al., 2020), the generative priors can restore rich facial details and deliver
135   plausible performance. GAN inversion (Gu et al., 2020; Menon et al., 2020) aim to identify the most
136   compatible latent code in GAN’s latent space based on the LQ input. GPEN (Yang et al., 2021) and
137   GFPGAN (Wang et al., 2021) incorporate additional structural information from LQ images. Wang
138   et al. (2023b) and Lin et al. (2023) employ diffusion models as enhancement modules to restore
139   high-quality facial details from coarse restoration outputs.
140   Codebook priors. Codebook-based methods utilize a VQGAN to learn a codebook that captures rich
141   texture information for face restoration. VQFR (Gu et al., 2022) uses a parallel decoder to directly
142   integrate LQ information into the decoding process, while Codeformer (Zhou et al., 2022) introduces
143   an additional prediction network to match LQ images with correspinding codes from the codebook.
144   However, these methods still have limitations as they overlook the accurate modeling of the mapping
145   from LQ features to the discrete representations of the HQ images, leading to unrealistic facial details.
146   In contrast, our approach focuses on mitigating this particular challenge.

      2.2   AUTO -R EGRESSIVE T RANSFORMER
149
150   Image Tokenizer and Auto-Regression. Image tokenizer functions analogous to WordPiece algo-
151   rithms in language models, effectively encoding 2D images into 1D token sequences. VQVAE (Van
152   Den Oord et al., 2017) employs an encoder-decoder framework that quantizes patch-level features to
153   the nearest entry in a learned codebook through a self-reconstruction technique. VQGAN (Esser et al.,
154   2021) advances VQVAE by integrating adversarial and perceptual losses, resulting in more realistic
155   image reconstructions. ViT-VQGAN (Yu et al., 2021) further improves upon this by replacing the
156   CNN-based encoder-decoder with a Vision Transformer, achieving superior reconstruction quality.
157   As a downstream task in image quantization, auto-regressive models are tasked with modeling
158   distribution of the discrete token sequences. VQVAE employs PixelCNN (Van Den Oord et al.,
159   2016) to autoregressively capture the dependencies between image tokens. iGPT (Chen et al., 2020)
160   pioneers the application of the powerful Transformer architecture for image generation. More recently,
161   RQVAE (Lee et al., 2022) and VAR (Tian et al., 2024) introduce novel autoregressive frameworks
      that diverge from the standard raster-scan approach to a coarse-to-fine generation strategy.

162
      Masked Auto-Regressive models. Masked Auto-Regressive models originate from BERT (Devlin,
163   2018) in Masked language modeling task. MaskGIT (Chang et al., 2022) introduces the Masked
164   prediction framework to image generation for the first time. MagViT (Yu et al., 2023a) and its
165   successor, MagViT-2 (Yu et al., 2023b), extend this approach to video generation. MUSE (Chang
166   et al., 2023) scales up MaskGIT’s architecture to 3 billion parameters, achieving remarkable text-to-
167   image synthesis.

      The core idea of this work is to reformulate the image restoration problem as a conditional generation
172   problem in discrete latent space with lower complexity. The inherent characteristics of image
173   discretization allow us to leverage the powerful auto-regressive generative model to bridge the gap
174   between LQ images and HQ latent representations. An overview of ART-FR is illustrated in Fig. 2.
175
      The training of ART-FR can be divided into two distinct stages. In stage I, we pre-train a VQGAN
176
      to obtain discrete representations of the training data, as well as a high-quality facial texture bank
177
      (Sec. 3.1). For the stage II, we develop a Masked Visual Token Transformer that conditioned on LQ
178   features to model the distribution of the discrete token sequences corresponding to the training data
179   (Sec. 3.2). The details of the sampling procedure during inference is illustrated in Sec. 3.3.

292
      position is initialized as [MASK], denoted as q (0) . The masked tokens are progressively refined
293

294   be generated in a single inference step, thereby reducing the number of generation steps. In our
295   implementation, the decoding algorithm constructs an image over T = 8 steps.
296
297

      the same embedding space, and then fed into the Masked Auto-Regressive model. The model first
298

299

300

302   predictions, where N is the total sequence length and T is the number of iterations. Finally, we
303   update the mask M (t+1) from q (t+1) for subsequent iterations.

      Implementation. During the VQGAN training phase, a downsampling rate of 32 was applied,
318   meaning that an image of size 512×512×3 is converted into a 16×16 (256) token sequence. The
319   size of the codebook was set to 1024. In the auto-regressive modeling phase, the total number of
320   sampling steps was set to 8. The Adam (Kingma, 2014) optimizer was used throughout the training
321   process with a batch size of 24. The learning rate was set to 8 × 10−5 for Stage I and was decayed
322   from 2 × 10−4 to 2 × 10−5 using a cosine annealing schedule for Stage II. The two stages were
323   trained with 500K and 150K iterations, respectively. Our method was performed on the PyTorch with
      4 NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPUs.

364   landmark distance (LMD) are used to assess the retention of original facial features (Deng et al.,
365   2019).
366   Extensional applications. In this work, we further extended our model from the basic blind image
367   restoration task to image colorization, inpainting, old photo restoration, and super-resolution with
368   high downsampling rates, as illustrated in Figure 1. For each task, we fine-tuned or retrained our
369   model. The results demonstrate that ART-FR is applicable to a wide range of tasks and exhibits
370   strong robustness under severe degradations, such as large-area occlusions and high downsampling
371   rates. More results of the extensions are presented in the Appendix C.1.

479
480   In this paper, we propose ART-FR to address the problem of face restoration by reformulating it as a
481   conditional generative task within a discrete latent space. Drawing inspiration from language models
482   and auto-regressive image generation, we employed the discrete distribution modeling capabilities
483   of the Auto-regressive Transformer to map LQ images to HQ tokens, which are then translated
484   back into the image domain. Our method, encompassing Masked Visual Token Modeling, a tailored
485   auto-regressive architecture, and the strategic incorporation of LQ conditions, has synergistically
      enhanced the efficiency and robustness of face restoration.

      A.1    N ETWORK S TRUCTURE
659
660   Visual Tokenizer Structures. We adopt the same CNN basic structure as VQGAN (Esser et al.,
661   2021). The encoder of ART-FR utilizes six residual blocks with channel dimensions of [64, 128, 128,
662   256, 256, 512]. The first five residual blocks are followed by a downsample module to reduce the
663   image size from 512 × 512 to 16 × 16. When the image is downsampled to a resolution of 16, an
664   attention module is introduced to fully integrate global features. Similarly, the decoder of ART-FR
665   employs six residual blocks with channel dimensions of [512, 256, 256, 128, 128, 64] to upsample
666   the image back to its original resolution. The quantizer trains a codebook Z ∈ R1024×256 to restore
667
      facial texture information. A linear projector is added between the quantizer and the encoder (or
      decoder) to align the dimensions.
668
669   Masked Auto-regression Structures. Our masked auto-regressive model (training stage I) utilizes a
670   BERT-like architecture, which employs multiple layers of bidirectional Transformers to encode the
671   relationships between tokens in both directions. In our setting, 512 tokens are fed into the bidirectional
672   Transformers. The first 256 tokens, generated by the LQ encoder, serve as the LQ condition to guide
673
      the generation process, while the remaining 256 tokens are masked HQ tokens, with the masked parts
      replaced by a special token [MASK]. The model learns both a set of word embeddings and position
674
      embeddings with dimension 768 to encode the token indices and their positions, enabling the model
675
      to capture the distribution of the token sequence. The input to the Transformers can be represented by
676
677                   Input = Embedwords (token ids) + Embedposition (position ids).                        (9)
678
      Between the Transformer layers, shallow features hs and deep features hm are fused by
679   Linear(Concat(hm , hs )). At the model’s output, a prediction head is applied to estimate the
680   distribution of the positions where [MASK] located.
681
682   A.2    E XTENTION E XPERIMENTS SETTING
683
684   To achieve face inpainting and colorization, we fine-tune our pre-trained model for 20,000 iterations
685   using the Adam optimizer with a batch size of 24. In the construction of the inpainting dataset, we
686   generate LQ data by drawing random irregular polyline masks, following the methodology of GPEN
687   (Yang et al., 2021). For the colorization dataset, we employ random color jittering and grayscale
688   conversion as GFPGAN(Wang et al., 2021). We developed a super-resolution model with 200K
689
      iterations from scratch. As for old photo restoration, we utilized a pre-trained version of ART-FR
      without the need for additional training.
690
691
692   B     ROBUSTNESS OF ART-FR TO D EGRADATIONS
693
694   In this section, we present an analysis demonstrating the robustness of the ART-FR model across a
695   wide spectrum of image degradations. In this study, we validate the performance of our model on
696   the task of denoising. For the generation of LQ images, during each iteration, we randomly select
697   a noise level from the set 25, 50, 75, 100 to introduce into the HQ images. To test the limits of the
698
      robustness of the ART-FR model, we further escalate the noise levels to 125, 150, 175, 200. We

       D.4    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
973
974    While ART-FR achieved promising results in face restoration, there are two scenarios where it may
975    produce suboptimal outcomes. The first occurs when the degraded image includes hand features,
976    which we attribute to the limited reconstruction capabilities of VQGAN. As illustrated in Figure 15
977    (a), directly reconstructing HQ images with VQGAN still leads to unsatisfactory results. Since our
978    model relies on direct supervision from these reconstructed HQ images, enhancing the expressiveness
979    of VQGAN represents a potential avenue for improving restoration performance.
980    The second scenario emerges when the degraded image includes details unrelated to facial features,
981    as demonstrated in Figure 15 (b). ART-FR struggles to restore the logo on the hat, whereas DiffBIR,
982    despite producing less accurate facial details, reconstructs the hat more effectively. This difference
983    can be attributed to DiffBIR’s reliance on a pre-trained Stable Diffusion model, which has been
984    exposed to a vast array of images during training. As a result, refining the training datasets specifically
985    for face restoration emerges as another potential direction for improving performance.
