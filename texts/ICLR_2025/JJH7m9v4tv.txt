011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            The proposed method, post-hoc discriminator guidance (PDG) aims to take an
014            alternate route for Nash non-equilibrium issue in GANs’ training. This method
015            introduces an additional discriminator that gives explicit supervision with regard
016            to gradient of density ratio ∇x log ppfr (x)
                                                        (x) between real and fake probability density
017            function, steering the sample path towards more realistic regions in a post-hoc
018            way. We train the discriminator after adversarial optimization, making post-hoc
019            discriminator training stable and fast to converge. In generation process, anneal-
020            ing Langevin dynamics sampling with density ratio score reduces the Kullback-
021            Leibler divergence between the true and generated samples. Given an optimal dis-
022            criminator, the method can improve the sampling quality of various off-the-shelf
023
               models on the web without retraining required. Extensive experiments validate the
               advancements and effectiveness of PDG on content-varying data-limited datasets.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The building of GANs (Goodfellow et al. (2014); Karras et al. (2020b)) is mostly in place, but there
030   are still two dark clouds, one is Nash non-equilibrium, another is discrete data generation. Diffi-
031   culty in reaching Nash equilibrium during training is an inherent property of GANs for minimax
032   optimization, like the spin of a particle. Besides, the presence of zero real part and eigenvalues
033   with a large imageinary part in associated gradient vector field prevents GANs from convergence
034
      (Mescheder et al. (2017)). Furthermore, the disadvantage of adversarial optimization is more obvi-
      ous in the case of limited data (Karras et al. (2020a)), even causing mode collapse. Hence, many
035
      works are proposed recently for this problem by adapting the network architecture (Radford (2015);
036
      Liu et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2022)), redesigning objective function and regularization (Tseng et al.
037   (2021); Arjovsky et al. (2017); Gulrajani et al. (2017); Salimans et al. (2016)), and refining training
038   strategies (Jiang et al. (2021)).
039
040
      In this paper, inspired by classifier guidance in diffusion models (Dhariwal & Nichol (2021)), we fo-
      cus on a non-game sampling method specially designed for GANs, namely providing the correction
041
042   gradient information with regard to Cθ (x) = ∇x log ppfr (x) (x) during generator sampling by a post-hoc
043   well-trained discriminator, while annealing Langevin dynamics ensures that the direction of travel
044   is in the direction of ppfr (x)
                                  (x) with high probability. This unilateral optimization is more stable than ad-
045   versarial optimization, our method just keeps the pre-trained model fixed and steers generation path
046   towards more realistic regions with post-hoc discriminator estimating whether the sample trajectory
047   is reliatic or not.
048   At the same time, our proposed method is convenient and simple and can be applied to various
049   off-the-shelf models publicly available on the web, after all, just training an additional discriminator
050   always costs less computational resources and costs than re-training the entire GAN network. In
051   this way, the proposed method assists practitioners and researchers who only have limited training
052   samples or computational resources, to access more high-fidelity samples only with a pre-trained
053   generator and a optimally-trained post-hoc discriminator. In experiments, the training of post-hoc
      discriminator is a stable minimization problem and fast to converge, ablation experiments on various

055   summarized as follows:
056
057         • A GAN-specialized post-hoc discriminator guidance method is proposed to add a correc-

164
      Except Nash non-equilibrium, GANs are also confronted by mode collapse and discriminator over-
165   fitting. In short, it is difficult for GANs to reach the Nash equilibrium point under the constraint of
166   adversarial loss. This raises a question: how can we close the Kullback-Leibler distance between the
167   true and generated distribution without adversarial loss? The solution is simply a post-hoc discrim-
168   inator guided approach, namely, a discriminator is trained with generated images and real images to
169   guide the gradient of logarithm of density ratio ppfr (x)

      4.1     M AIN ABLATION EXPERIMENTS
357
358   According to statistic data from Table 1, 2 and 3 carried on fifteen datasets, it is concluded that
359   PDG is indeed able to close the KL distance between the true and generated distributions, simply
360   by training an additional discriminator post-hoc, sampling via annealing Langevin dynamics. In
361   fact, a post-hoc discriminator converges in less than 20 minutes on a Tesla V00 GPU, which is also
362   due to the fact that the training of the discriminator is a classification task, rather than a minimax
363   optimization. These findings highlight the potential of post-hoc discriminator as a supplementary
364   method to cope with poor optimization at large time in adversarial training.

462
463   6.1     C ONCLUSIONS
464
465   We tear up the rulebook and study a post-hoc discriminator guidance for mitigating the negative
466   effects induced by Nash non-equilibrium in GANs. We demonstrate that stochastic gradient descent
467
      (SGD) does not find the Nash equilibrium point in GANs’ training because the Jacobian matrix of its
      associated gradient vector field has eigenvalues with zero real part and eigenvalues with large imag-
468
      inary part. This method is more appealing than adversarial optimization as it is min-min problem
469
      instead of max-min GAN training. The optimal post-hoc discriminator predicts the score (density
470   ratio gradient information) between the generated and true probability density function, to generate
471   new samples that are more similar to the observed data. Our model is able to adapt to various off-the-
472   shelf models, and extensive ablation experiments also verify that the Kullback-Leibler divergence
473   between the true and generated samples can be reduced given an optimal discriminator.

      6.2     L IMITATIONS AND IMPROVEMENTS
477
      There are some training issues with regard to post-hoc discriminator, although our method is able
478
      to generalize to most datasets. Firstly, how well the post-hoc discriminator is trained determines the
479
      subsequent sampling performance, especially under limited-data condition, few data and long tail
480   effects hinder the presence of optimal discriminator. The second issue concerns the generalization
481   of post-hoc discriminator, which is influenced by many factors, including how well it fits the source
482   data, how long it takes to train, how many samples it takes, and so on, for we are training the gener-
483   ated data with real images for classification, and then sampling with the generator. This requires the
484   ability of the model to generalize. In the future, we will introduce classification networks for few-
485   shot and long-tail effects, as well as self-supervised learning into post-hoc discriminator training for
      better performance.

968   A.6.1     I MPLEMENT DETAILS OF E VALUATION METRICS
969
970   Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) is a metric used to evaluate the discrepancy between the generative
971   model and the real data distribution. It was proposed by Heusel et al. (2017) and is one of the widely
      used evaluation metrics nowadays.


                                                                18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       The FID measures the difference between the generative model and the real data distribution by
973    computing the Frechet distance between the two distributions. Frechet distance is a method to
974    measure the distance between two distributions, which takes into account the mean and covariance
975    matrix of the two distributions and can better describe the difference between the two distributions.
976
       To compute the FID, a set of samples are first drawn from the real data distribution and the generative
977
       model, respectively, and then a pre-trained inception network is used to extract feature vectors from
978
       these samples. Next, the mean and covariance matrices of the two distributions are calculated and
979    the Frechet distance between them is calculated to obtain the FID value. A smaller FID value means
980    that the images generated by the generative model are closer to the true data distribution.
981
982
       As an evaluation metric, FID is widely used in the training and evaluation of generative models. It
       can help us evaluate the quality of the generative model more accurately and select a better generative
983
       model. At the same time, FID is also an objective evaluation index, which can avoid the influence
984
       of human subjective factors on the evaluation results. The computation can be formulated as
985
986
                                                 2
                                                                   p
987                             F ID = ∥µ1 − µ2 ∥ + T r(Σ1 + Σ2 − 2 Σ1 Σ2 )                              (39)
988
989    where µ1 and µ2 represents the mean vector of real data distribution and generated data distribution
990    respectively, Σ1 and Σ2 denote the covariance matrix of the real and generated data, respectively.
991    T r(·) is trace of a matrix.
992
       The FID value can be obtained by computing the mean covariance matrix of the two distributions and
993
       computing the Frechet distance between them. The smaller the FID value, the closer the generated
994    image is to the real data distribution.
