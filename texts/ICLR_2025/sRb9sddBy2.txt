                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            As training datasets grow larger, we aspire to develop models that generalize well
012            to any diverse test distribution, even if the latter deviates significantly from the
013            training data. Various approaches like domain adaptation, domain generalization,
014            and robust optimization attempt to address the out-of-distribution challenge by
015            posing assumptions about the relation between training and test distribution. Dif-
016            ferently, we adopt a more conservative perspective by accounting for the worst-
017            case error across all sufficiently diverse test distributions within a known domain.
018            Our first finding is that training on a uniform distribution over this domain is opti-
019
               mal. We also interrogate practical remedies when uniform samples are unavailable
               by considering methods for mitigating non-uniformity through finetuning and re-
020
               balancing. Our theory aligns with previous observations on the role of entropy and
021
               rebalancing for o.o.d. generalization and foundation model training. We also pro-
022            vide new empirical evidence across tasks involving o.o.d. shifts which illustrate
023            the broad applicability of our perspective.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Machine learning usually starts with the assumption that the test data will be independent and iden-
029   tically distributed (i.i.d.) with the training set. In practice, distributional shifts are the norm rather
030   than the exception, leading to models that perform well in training but may stumble when faced with
031   the diversity the real world has to offer.
032   The challenge of out-of-distribution (o.o.d.) generalization has inspired a variety of approaches
033   aimed at bridging the training and inference gap. For example, approaches like domain adaptation
034   and generalization address o.o.d. challenges by assuming knowledge of the unlabeled test distri-
035   bution or by learning invariant features (Bengio et al., 2013; Peters et al., 2016; Arjovsky et al.,
036   2019; Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Koyama & Yamaguchi, 2020), whereas robust optimization (Ben-Tal
037   et al., 2009; Rahimian & Mehrotra, 2019) methods can be used to defend against data uncertainty
038
      by modifying and regularizing training.
039   We take a different perspective and seek models that perform well under any diverse test distribution
040   within a known domain. This is formalized through the concept of distributionally diverse (DD)
041   risk, which quantifies the worst-case error across all distributions with sufficiently high entropy.
042   Our postulate that test entropy is large reflects our intention to characterize a model’s performance
043   on a sufficiently diverse set of natural inputs rather on adversarial examples. Our focus on entropy
044
      is also motivated by the previous empirical finding that higher entropy in training and test data is a
      strong predictor of o.o.d. generalization (Vedantam et al., 2021).
045
046   The introduced framework provides a new angle to study o.o.d. generalization. Differently from
047   domain generalization, we do not assume that the training data are composed of multiple domains.
048   Further, unlike domain adaptation and robust optimization, we do not assume to know the unlabeled
049   test distribution nor that the latter lies close to the training distribution. A more comprehensive
050
      discussion of how our ideas relate to previous work can be found in Appendix A.
051   Our analysis starts in Section 3 by showing that DD risk minimization has a remarkably simple
052   solution when we have control over how training data are sampled. Specifically, we prove that
053   training on the uniform distribution over the domain of interest is optimal in the worst-case scenario
      and derive a matching bound on the corresponding DD risk.

054
      Section 4 then explores what happens when the training data is non-uniformly distributed. We ana-
055   lyze two approaches. First, we show that gentle finetuning of a pretrained model (as opposed to con-
056   siderably deviating from the pre-training initialization) can suffice to overcome the non-uniformity
057   issue. Second, we draw inspiration from test-time adaptation and formally consider the re-weighting
058   of training examples to correct distributional imbalances. Therein, we provide an end-to-end gen-
059   eralization bound that jointly captures the trade-off that training set rebalancing introduces between
060   in- and out-of-distribution error.
061   The above results agree with past observations in the training of large models. Our analysis of
062   finetuning provides an explanation for the observation of Chen et al. (2024) (specifically Figure 4)
063   that when finetuning large language models to respect human preferences, i.i.d. and o.o.d. met-
064   rics correlate only close to finetuning initialization. Further, our findings on the role of uniformity
065   and on benefit of rebalancing align with emerging empirical observation within foundation model
066   training, such as large language models (Gao et al., 2020; Furuta et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024)
067   and AlphaFold (Jumper et al., 2021; Abramson et al., 2024), where heuristic ways to do training
068   set rebalancing, such as clustering or controlling the data source mixture, are adopted to improve
069
      generalization and to remove bias.
070   Our insights are further evaluated in syntetic and real world tasks featuring distribution shifts. After
071   first validating the theory in a controlled and tractable setup involving a mixture of Gaussian dis-
072   tributions, we turn our attention to complex tasks involving covariate shift. Therein, we find that
073   rebalancing can enhance empirical risk minimization when the density can be reasonably estimated.
074   These results exemplify the practical benefits and pitfalls of the considered approaches.

093   unpredictable, otherwise. The focus of this work is to propose an alternative requirement that bridges
094   the gap between the worst- and average-case perspectives.
095

096

097

098   test distribution q. In the small and medium data regimes, the domain X should be defined by prior

120   3     U NIFORM IS OPTIMAL AND IMPLIED GUARANTEES
121
122   This section argues that –all other things being equal– it is preferable in terms of distributionally
123   diverse risk to train your classifier on the uniform distribution. We then characterize the DD risk of
124   a classifier that achieves a certain expected risk on the uniform distribution.
125
126   3.1   L EARNING FROM A UNIFORM DISTRIBUTION IS DD RISK OPTIMAL
127
128   Suppose that there exists some unknown function f ∗ : X → Y and that the learning algorithm

137   expected risk ε < 1/2 under any distribution. A classifier optimized for the uniform distribution will

142   The proof can be found in Appendix E. Intuitively, a uniform distribution is optimal because it
143   balances the model’s performance across the entire input space, preventing overemphasis of specific
144   areas. The reader might suspect that this result is a consequence of the maximum entropy principle,
145   stating that within a bounded domain the uniform distribution has the maximum entropy. This
146   is indeed accurate, although the derivation is not a straightforward application of this result: the
147   maximum entropy principle constrains the choice of the worst-case distribution q ∗ within Qγ .
148   A particularly appealing consequence of the theorem is that the exact entropy gap γ is not necessary
149   to determine the optimal training strategy. As we shall see later, γ does affect the DD risk that we
150   can expect. However, from a practical perspective it is preferable to have a training strategy that is
151   independent of γ, as it may not be straightforward to define it.
152
      It is also important to discuss when a uniform distribution is not the optimal choice for training.
153
      Our first disclaimer is that the theorem does not account for any inductive bias in learning, e.g.,
154   as afforded by the choice of data representation, model type, and optimization. The theorem also
155   does not consider the pervasive issue of i.i.d. generalization, meaning how close the empirical risk
156   approximates the expected one, which is analysed in Section 4.3. Finally, the theorem is less relevant
157   when there is additional information about the test distribution, such as unlabeled test samples.

508   5.3   P ITFALLS
509
      A key prerequisite for rebalancing to work is that we can successfully fit a density over the training
510
      set to derive importance weights. Our theory suggests that rendering the training set more uniform
511   can lead to models that are more robust to high entropy test distributions.
512
513   We already saw in the experiments above that introducing carefully tuned dimensionality reduction
514
      or label conditioning when fitting the density could yield significant benefits. Unfortunately, we also
      encountered datasets for which the density fit was so poor that the aforementioned modifications did
515
      not suffice to improve performance. In Appendix C.5 we take a deeper dive into these failures,
516
      showing how the failure of the density estimator impacts test performance.
517
518   Finally, we re-iterate that this work focuses on test distributions supported in the same domain as
519   the training distribution. Many tasks in the popular DomainBed and WILDS benchmarks contain
520
      significant domain and label shifts which require a different approach.
521
522   6     C ONCLUSION
523
524   As machine learning progresses toward larger-scale datasets and the development of foundation
525   models, it becomes increasingly important to move beyond traditional i.i.d. guarantees and to con-
526   sider worst-case scenarios in o.o.d. generalization. In this spirit, our work introduces a novel per-
527   spective that prioritizes minimizing worst-case error across diverse distributions. We have shown
528   that training on uniformly distributed data offers robust guarantees, making it a powerful strategy
529
      as models scale in complexity and scope. Even when uniformity cannot be achieved, we find that
      rebalancing strategies can provide practical avenues to enhance model resilience.
530
531   Further empirical work focusing on obtaining more robust density estimates as well as investigation
532   of gentle finetuning in the context of ensembles and foundation model training could bring additional
533   benefits. Other potential avenues to mitigate training data non-uniformity could include adding noise
534   to the input data (Bishop, 1995) and mixup (Zhang, 2017). We also note that the observations we
535
      have made about how our theory aligns with previous evidence in the literature on the training of
      foundation models do not establish a causal relation between our theory and reality. Further work
536
      will be needed to rigorously establish these links.
537
538   Overall, the shift in focus from average-case to worst-case generalization presents a compelling
539   framework for the next generation of machine learning models, particularly as they are trained on
      increasing larger datasets and deployed in increasingly unpredictable environments.

782   A    R ELATED WORK
783
784   Out-of-distribution generalization is a central challenge in machine learning, where models trained
785   on a specific data distribution are required to perform well on unseen distributions that may differ
786   significantly. For an in-depth survey of the flurry of work on o.o.d. generalization, we refer to
787   the following surveys (Liu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022). In the following,
788   we discuss the various approaches that have been developed to this challenge, emphasizing their
789
      similarities and differences with this work.
790   Domain generalization. Domain generalization and invariant risk minimization aim to enhance a
791   model’s ability to generalize across different environments or domains. These methods define a set
792   of environments from which data is gathered and seek to ensure that the model’s outputs are invari-
793   ant (i.e., indistinguishable) with respect to the environment from which the data originated (Bengio
794   et al., 2013; Peters et al., 2016; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Koyama & Yamaguchi,
795
      2020). The ultimate goal is to capture features that are discriminative across domains while ignoring
      spurious correlations. Similarly to these methods, our work acknowledges potential shifts between
796
      training and test distributions and aims to promote robust generalization. Unlike domain general-
797
      ization approaches, we do not assume that the data comes from multiple pre-defined environments.
798   Recent advances, particularly those that combine finetuning of pretrained models with ensembling
799   strategies, are relevant to our approach as they have shown promise in enhancing generalization
800   across varied domains (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021). Ensemble-based methods such as model
801   soups (Wortsman et al., 2022), DiWA (Rame et al., 2022), agree-to-disagree (Pagliardini et al.), and
802   model Ratatouille (Rame et al., 2023) have further extended these concepts, aligning closely with
803   our objectives.
804   Robustness to uncertainty and perturbation. Robust optimization (RO) is a well-established field
805   in optimization that deals with uncertainty in model parameters or data (Ben-Tal et al., 2009). In the
806   context of machine learning, RO has been applied to scenarios where the test distribution is assumed
807   to be within a set of known plausible distributions, with the goal of minimizing the worst-case loss
808   over this set (Caramanis et al., 2011; Singla et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). Distributionally robust
809   optimization (DRO) (Rahimian & Mehrotra, 2019; Duchi & Namkoong, 2019; 2021) extends this
      concept by considering a set of distributions over the unknown data, usually inferred through a prior


                                                                           15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      on the data, such as distributions that are close to the training distribution by some distance measure
811   such as the Wasserstein distance (Kuhn et al., 2019). Our approach shares similarities with DRO in
812   that the DD risk in that we both consider the worst case behavior over a set of distributions and that,
813   at the limit, both DRO and DD converge to the worst case risk over the domain. However, our work
814   diverges from traditional RO and DRO frameworks in significant ways. RO typically focuses on the
815   optimization aspect, often assuming a convex cost function and providing theoretical justification
816   for various regularizers such as Tikhonov and Lasso (Caramanis et al., 2011). These approaches
817   are particularly concerned with uncertainty within a bounded region near the training data, such as
818   noisy or partial inputs and labels (Singla et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). In contrast, our focus
819
      is on scenarios where the training data is neither noisy nor partial but where the test distribution
      can change almost arbitrarily within the support of the training distribution, provided that its en-
820
      tropy is not too small. Critically, as illustrated in Figure 5, the assumptions posed by DRO and our
821
      framework about the relation of the train and test distribution are different: we do not consider the
822   worst-case bounded distribution shift (as is done in DRO) but the worst case risk under any distribu-
823   tion of sufficient entropy. As such, the training distribution holds no special role in our framework,
824   whereas in practice DRO defines the set of potential test distributions as those some distance away
825   from it. This is a consequential conceptual and practical difference: Conceptually, whereas in DRO
826   one needs to think about how similar is the test distribution to the training distribution, we here
827   consider arbitrary distributions on the test domain and pose a constraint on entropy. Practically, the
828   two approaches lead to very different solutions to the problem of distribution shift. Within the deep
829   learning community, significant efforts have also been made to train models that are robust to small
830
      input perturbations, referred to as adversarial examples (Goodfellow et al., 2015; Madry et al., 2018;
      Alayrac et al., 2019; Bai et al., 2021). While these methods have influenced our understanding of
831
      robustness, our work is distinct in that we address broader shifts in the test distribution rather than
832
      specific adversarial perturbations.
833
834   Domain adaptation and covariate shift. Domain adaptation is a sub-area of transfer learning (Pan
835   & Yang, 2009) focused on transferring knowledge from a source domain to a target domain where
836
      the data distribution differs (Farahani et al., 2021). This is crucial when a model trained on one
      domain is expected to perform well on a new domain. Domain adaptation falls into three types of
837
      domain shifts: covariate, concept, and label shift. Our work is most closely related to the covari-
838
      ate shift scenario, in which the distribution of input features changes between the training and test
839   phases. Similarly to some domain adaptation methods that address covariate shift, we also con-
840   sider re-weighting strategies to account for differences between training and test distributions (Shi-
841   modaira, 2000; Huang et al., 2006) (other approaches consider ensemble disagreement (Jiang et al.;
842   Kirsch & Gal), model confidence (Garg et al., 2022), and neighborhood invariance (Ng et al.) on
843   the unlabeled data). A key difference is that we do not assume that we have access to unlabeled
844   target domain data but instead consider re-weighing to uniformly rebalance the training set. This
845   choice motivated the proven optimality of the uniform distribution when considering the worst-case
846   scenario across all possible distributions that are constrained by a given entropy threshold. This
847
      broader DD framework enables robust generalization across diverse scenarios, without relying on
      prior knowledge of specific target distributions or adaptations tailored to known shifts.
848
849   Active learning. Active Learning (AL) aims to efficiently train models by selecting the most in-
850   formative data points for labeling, thereby reducing the amount of labeled data needed to achieve
851   high performance (Cohn et al., 1996; Settles, 2009). Within AL, entropy maximization is a com-
852   mon practice, where the focus is on maximizing the entropy of the predictive distribution p(y|x)
853
      to identify the most uncertain and thus informative data points. Other acquisition functions in-
      clude Variation Ratios (which select data points based on the disagreement among multiple model
854
      predictions), mean standard deviation (Kendall et al., 2017) provide alternative ways to measure un-
855
      certainty. Additionally, methods like mutual information between predictions and model posterior
856   have been used to target data points that most influence the model’s posterior distribution (Houlsby
857   et al., 2011). The use of density estimation to bias the sampling towards low-density areas was also
858   considered in (Zhu et al., 2008). Therein, they define the density×entropy measure which combines
859   H(y|x) and p(x). For a recent overview of these sampling strategies in computer vision, refer to Gal
860   et al. (Gal et al., 2017). Despite the overlap in techniques, our work focuses on o.o.d. generalization
861   rather than iterative sample acquisition: while AL aims to improve model performance by selecting
862   specific data points during training, our study seeks to optimize generalization across all possible
863   distributions within a domain, offering a different perspective on managing uncertainty.

      B     J USTIFICATION OF THE DD RISK FORMULATION
865
866   The DD risk, that we defined as follows:
867                                                       h                 i
868                                 rdd (f ; γ) = max Ex∼q l f (x), f ∗ (x) ,
                                                 q∈Qγ
869
870   measures the behavior of a classifier across any test distribution of sufficiently high-entropy within
871
      a domain. In the following, we justify this definition from a mathematical, intuitive, and empirical
      perspective.
872
873   Mathematical justification. Our definition of DD risk follows from two desiderata about how to
874   model the generalization of a predictive model under covariate shift:
875         1. We know very little about the relation between the training and test distributions.
876         2. We wish to avoid judging the behavior of a classifier based on pathological examples, thus
877            the test distribution should not assign high likelihood to any small set of examples.
878
      Desideratum 1 is posed because the distance between training and test distribution (also referred
879
      to as train-test discrepancy) is often large in practice. This is supported by the literature where,
880
      train-test discrepancy has been empirically found to correlate poorly with generalization (Vedantam
881   et al., 2021) and concurrent theoretical work also advocates against it (Bhattacharjee et al., 2024).
882   On the other hand, desideratum 2 addresses the reason why worst-case analysis is non-meaningful
883   in learning: if no such constraint is placed, an adversary may always construct a pathological test
884   distribution that concentrates all its mass on a small set of inputs where the predictive model is
885   wrong. We directly avoid this situation by asserting that no such small high likelihood set can exist.
886
      Asserting that the test distribution entropy is high satisfies both these desiderata without imposing
887   any further assumptions. Lower bounding the test entropy ensures that the spread of a distribution is
888   large and does not constrain the shape of the test distribution based on that of the training one given
889   a fixed domain.
890
      Intuitive explanation. We argue that, a generally performant model is one that generally performs
891
      well on many test instances that the world throws at it—even if it might fail in specific instances.
892
      In other words, though we cannot hope that our model will generalize to any arbitrary sharp test
893   distribution, we can reasonably expect that it will performs well on test distributions that are more
894   spread out and thus more typical of the data domain. Our assumption of high test entropy exactly
895   corresponds to the minimal and intuitive assumption that the classifier will be evaluated on a diverse
896   set of possible inputs, rather than on few pathological examples.
897
      Empirical justification. Finally, to emphasize the practical applicability of our assumption in real-
898
      world applications, we remark that, as mentioned in the introduction, test entropy has already been
899   identified in the literature (Vedantam et al., 2021) as an intuitive and empirically predictive measure
900   for o.o.d. generalization.

      C.1   R EBALANCING IN PRACTICE
905
906   The aim of rebalancing is to fit a density function to the training examples p(x) such that, after
907   rebalancing, the training examples with weights w(x) ∝ 1/p(x)τ resemble a uniform distribution.
908   While temperature τ can be tuned as a hyperparameter, we kept τ = 1.0 for simplicity. As per
909   Theorem 4.2, we cap the weights w(x) ∝ min(1/p̂(xi )τ , β). In practice this is used as a way to
910   regularize the distribution and avoid oversampling outliers. From synthetic experiments we found
911   β = 0.99 quantile to be a robust choice and used it in all further real-world experiments.
912   We use a masked autoregresive flow (MAF) (Papamakarios et al., 2017b) to fit a density to the
913   training set embeddings as we found it performed better than alternatives in preliminary experiments.
914   In all setups we use a standard Gaussian as a base density. For real-world experiments we used MAF
915   with 10 autoregressive layers and a 2 layer MLP with a hidden dimension of 256 for each of them.
916   For synthetic experiments we used MAF with 5 autoregresive layers and a 2 layer MLP with a hidden
917   dimension of 64 for each of them. In all cases, when training MAF we hold out 10% random subset
      of the training data for early stopping, with a patience of 10 epochs. The checkpoint with the best


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      held-out set likelihood is used. MAF is trained with learning rate of 3e − 4 and the Adam (Kingma,
919   2014) optimizer.
920
921   When using label conditioning, we use the flow to estimate conditional density p(x) =
922
      pMAF (x|y)p(y), where p(y) is computed from label frequency in the training set. Further, in this
      case we ensure that final weights upsample minority label samples by scaling weights to inverse
923
      label frequency w′ = w/p(y).
924
925   To achieve good quality rebalancing we need the sample weights to capture the data landscape as-
926   pects relevant to the given problem. As we focus on generalization tasks where it is standard to use
927   pre-trained models (Koh et al., 2021) as a starting point, we aim to fit a density on the embeddings
928
      produced by the pre-trained backbone model. For real world experiments we use the hyperparam-
      eters and experimental setup proposed for ERM in the respective original papers (Koh et al., 2021;
929
      Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021).
930
931   All WILDS (Koh et al., 2021) datasets considered, except PovertyMap, use a pre-trained model that
932   is finetuned with a new prediction head. We use that pre-trained model as a featurizer to produce the
933   training set embeddings. For PovertyMap, the randomly initialized featurizer is used, to keep in line
934
      with the original experimental setup, even though using a model pre-trained on ImageNet marginally
      improves the results. In all cases the featurizer models used already produce one embedding vector
935
      per training sample, except CodeGPT (Lu et al., 2021) used for Py150, for which we apply mean
936
      pooling over the sequence to produce a single embedding vector to use for density estimation.
937
938   For ColorMNIST traditionally no pre-trained backbone is used (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021).
939   Thus, we use a ResNet-50 model pre-trained on ImageNet to build the embeddings for density
940
      estimation, but otherwise train the standard model architecture, with exactly the same experimental
      setup as proposed by Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz (2021).
941
942   To combat the curse of dimentionality and to potentially fit smoother densities we also explore di-
943   mensionality reduction. In all real-world datasets we perform a small grid search over transforming
944   embeddings using UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) to 8 or 64 dimensions or using PCA to 256 dimen-
945   sions before fitting the flow model. We use the validation loss to select the dimensionality reduction
946
      technique. We selected on these grid-search hyperparameters from preliminary experiments where
      we found that at lower target dimensions UMAP performs much better than PCA in our setup, while
947
      for larger target dimensions PCA is at least as good. A more exhaustive hyperparameter search was
948
      avoided to save computational resources. To facilitate the flow training, we normalize the embed-
949   dings. We grid search over two options: normalizing each embedding dimension to unit variance
950   and zero mean or normalizing all embeddings by the maximum vector length. To get a better be-
951   haved probability distribution, we scale log-likelihoods produced by MAF by the dimension size, to
952   get likelihoods in bits per dimension, before applying a softmax over the whole training set, to get
953   proper sample weights.
954
      In the synthetic experiments, we directly fit the flow to the data without employing any dimension-
955   ality reduction.

1003    tion, with a training set size n ranging from 100 to 10,000 examples. To obtain confidence intervals,
1004    in the following we sample 35 tasks and repeat the analysis for each one. Illustrative examples of
1005
        the task are provided in Figure 6.
1006    A key challenge we encountered was the reliable estimation of entropy, as the entropy estimators we
1007    tried were biased, a common issue in entropy estimation. To address this, we partition the space into
1008    100×100 bins B, sample a held-out
                                  P        set of m = 10, 000 examples, and P estimate the discrete entropy
1009    using the formula H(p) ≈ b∈B p̂b (x) log(1/p̂b (x)), where p̂b (x) = x 1{x ∈ b}/m.
1010    We select the test distribution using a greedy adversarial construction: first, we sample 10 000 points
1011    uniformly, then starting with all test points that the model mislabels, an adversarial set is iteratively
1012    expanded by adding the point that maximizes entropy at each step. Whereas set selection for entropy
1013    maximization is NP-hard, this procedure provides a 1 − 1/e approximation of the true optimum (Ko
1014    et al., 1995; Krause & Guestrin, 2012; Sharma et al., 2015).

1053     C.4                 C OMBINING REBALANCING WITH OTHER DATA SMOOTHING APPROACHES
1054
1055
         The main goal of rebalancing the training data is to more uniformly cover the data manifold, when
         training the model. Other approaches, such as mixup (Zhang, 2017) that interpolates data points in
1056
         the training set, can also have a similar smoothing effect. As C-Mixup (Yao et al., 2022) achieves
1057
         state of the art performance on PovertyMap dataset, it is natural to ask if these smoothing approaches
1058     can be combined. While C-Mixup was originally proposed for regression tasks and mixes datapoints
1059     based on their label similarity, we also adopted it to the categorical classification setup by mixing
1060     only points within the same class. We tested how combining C-Mixup with rebalancing would affect
1061     results on PovertyMap and iWildCam datasets. In all cases C-Mixup was used with CutMix (Yun
1062     et al., 2019) as proposed in the original paper for the PovertyMap task (Yao et al., 2022).
1063
         From Tables 4 and 5 we can see that combining C-Mixup and rebalancing tends to produce middle
1064     of the road results. In PovertyMap (Table 5) where C-Mixup is state of the art, the combined meth-
1065     ods achieve similar results (up to experimental variance0. While for iWildCam (Table 4), where
1066     rebalancing is superior, the combined methods again perform worse than rebalancing alone but bet-
1067     ter than C-Mixup. This shows that the approaches can be used together but their benefits do not
1068     necessarily stack up.
