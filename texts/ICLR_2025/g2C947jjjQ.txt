009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Given two knowledge sources, one containing unstructured documents and the
012            other comprising structured graph knowledge bases, how can we effectively
013            retrieve the relevant information to answer user questions? While Retrieval-
014            Augmented Generation (RAG) retrieves documents to assist the large language
015            model (LLM) in question answering, Graph RAG (GRAG) uses graph knowledge
016            bases as an additional knowledge source. However, there are many questions
017            that require information from both sources, which complicates the scenario and
018
               makes hybrid retrieval essential. The goal is to effectively leverage both sources
               to provide better answers to the questions. Therefore, we propose AGENT-G, a
019
               unified framework for GRAG, composed of an agent, a retriever bank, and a critic
020
               module. AGENT-G has the following advantages: 1) Agentic, it automatically
021            improves the agent’s action with self-reflection, 2) Adaptive, it solves questions
022            that require hybrid knowledge source with a single unified framework, 3) Inter-
023            pretable, it justifies decision making and reduces hallucinations, and 4) Effective,
024            it adapts to different GRAG settings and outperforms all baselines. The experi-
025            ments are conducted on two real-world GRAG benchmarks, namely STA RK and
026            CRAG. In STA RK, AGENT-G shows relative improvements in Hit@1 of 47%
027            in STA RK-MAG and 55% in STA RK-P RIME. In CRAG, AGENT-G increases
028            accuracy by 35% while reducing hallucination by 11%, both relatively.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020) enables large lan-
      guage models (LLMs) to have access to the unstructured document database in order to handle
034
      unknown facts and reduce hallucinations (Ram et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Béchard & Ayala,
035
      2024). However, RAG often overlooks relationships within the data and lacks a global context.
036   Graph RAG (GRAG) retrieves information from pre-constructed structured graph knowledge bases,
037   providing varying levels of granularity and relationships among text-attributed entities (Edge et al.,
038   2024), which allows the LLMs to better understand their relationships. Although utilizing graph
039   knowledge bases as an additional knowledge source facilitates GRAG in tackling a wider variety of
040   questions, the diversity of data modalities raises two crucial challenges.
041
      First, “hybrid” questions that require information from multiple knowledge sources need a tailored
042   solution. While most RAG approaches focus on textual questions (Fig. 1 left), which are presum-
043   ably answerable from text documents, current GRAG approaches (Sun et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024;
044   Mavromatis & Karypis, 2024) focus on relational questions (Fig. 1 middle), assuming that the an-
045   swers exist in the graphs. As a result, hybrid questions, that require both knowledge sources to be
046   answered correctly (Fig. 1 right), are often neglected. Developing tools to retrieve the necessary
047   information from multiple sources to solve all these questions remains a challenge.
048   Second, even when the appropriate tools are provided, tool-use methods may find it challenging to
049   operate them correctly on the first attempt. They may (i) perform a retrieval action on an incorrect
050   source (Fig. 1 left), or (ii) retrieve the information with incorrect (action) input (Fig. 1 right). It is
051   therefore essential to provide feedback for them to improve their actions. Despite existing works
052   showing that feedback improves LLM output, their feedback either lacks clear guidance (Yao et al.,
053   2023; Shinn et al., 2023), or requires a fine-tuned model (Paul et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024). Inferior
      feedback, especially without careful design, can even mislead the methods when operating the tools.

      To address these challenges, we propose AGENT-G, an agentic and unified framework for GRAG.
093
      The agent in AGENT-G solves questions that require hybrid knowledge sources with our designed
094
      retriever bank, including text and graph retrieval modules. Furthermore, it automatically improves its

097   without enough or proper context. We summarize the contributions of AGENT-G as follows:
098

099

100
             3. Interpretable: it justifies the decision making and reduces hallucinations; and
101          4. Effective: it adapts to different GRAG settings and outperforms all the baselines.

      2     AGENT-G: A N AGENTIC F RAMEWORK FOR GRAG
130
131
      Given a question, how can we answer it with the help of information from the most appropriate
132   source? In addition to RAG with access to unstructured documents, GRAG has access to structured
133   graph knowledge bases to assist in question answering. However, how can we leverage information
134   from both sources to answer the question? We first revisit two major challenges for GRAG.
135
      Challenge 1 (Multi-Sourcing Question). In GRAG, there are questions that require information
136   from different or multiple sources to be answered.
137
138   As shown in Fig. 1 left, while textual questions require information from documents, relational
139   questions require the information derived from the graph knowledge bases. Some questions even
140   require information from both sources to be answered, which we call “hybrid” questions in Fig. 1
141   right. Designing a single framework to answer all questions is challenging.
142   Challenge 2 (Refinement-Required Question). Due to the complicated scenario in GRAG, LLMs
143   struggle to answer questions correctly on the first attempt, necessitating refinement of their action.
144
145
      The questions in the complicated GRAG scenario involve uncertainty about the relevant knowledge
      sources and confusing cues for operating the retriever. These cause two common types of retrieval
146
      errors that can be made. The first type of error arises when selecting an incorrect retriever. In
147
      Fig. 1 middle, retrieving information from documents does not help answer a relational question.
148   The second type of error occurs when the retriever is operated incorrectly. In hybrid questions
149   that contain both relational and textual aspects, LLM can falsely identify the textual aspect as the
150   relational one. In Fig. 1 right, there is an error in retrieving the correct reference by LLM as it
151   confuses the textual aspect as an entity with type “field of study”.
152
      Therefore, we propose AGENT-G, an agentic framework for GRAG that consists of a retriever bank
153
      and a critic module, to address Challenge 1 and 2, respectively.
154

           2.2      R ETRIEVER BANK
180
181        To solve Challenge 1, we propose a retriever bank (in Fig. 3 green), which is composed of multiple
182        retrieval modules. Each retrieval module includes a retriever and a ranker, offering the flexibility
183        to cover a wide range of questions. The retriever first retrieves the most relevant references, and
184        then the ranker gives the ranking of them. At each iteration t, top-K references Xt will be retrieved
185        for answer generation. More specifically, we design two retrieval modules, namely text and graph
186        retrieval modules, to retrieve information from documents and graph knowledge bases, respectively.
187        The action at of an agent includes the selection and input of the retrieval module. The agent first
188        decides the action input by identifying the information of the question q, such as the domain dt 1 ,
189                                             (d )                        (d )
           and extracting the topic entities Et t and useful relations Rt t in the question q. The agent then
190        makes the selection st , deciding whether to use a text or a graph retrieval module. Deciding on the
191        input before selection can help optimize the choice of the retrieval module. For example, if there
192        is no entity extracted in this iteration, a text retrieval module can potentially be a better choice. In
193                                                             (d )   (d )
           summary, the action at is a set containing {dt , Et t , Rt t , st }.

           retrieval, which aims to solve textual questions. The graph retrieval module extracts the ego-graph

203        2.3      C RITIC M ODULE
204
205        In Challenge 2, when faced with a question in the more complicated GRAG scenario, not only the
206        selection of the retrieval module st can be incorrect in the first iteration, but also the action input,
                                                     (d )                        (d )
207        including the domain dt , topic entities Et t , and useful relations Rt t . To solve this, we propose the
208        critic module (in Fig. 3 purple), which has two parts, a validator LLM Cval to validate the correctness
209        of the output ŷt , and a commentor LLM Ccom to give good feedback ft . Unlike a traditional critic,
210        our essential divide-and-conquer step in the critic module offers two key advantages. First, by
211
           breaking a difficult task into two easier ones, we can now leverage pre-train LLMs to solve them
           while maintaining good performance, instead of fine-tuning a critic LLM with significant resources.
212
           Second, this step allows the validator and commentor LLMs to have their own exclusive contexts,
213
           preventing including information that is irrelevant to their tasks, which can seriously distract them.

242   The validator LLM Cval aims to identify if the given output ŷt is correct for the question q, which
243   is a binary classification task. To improve accuracy, we provide an additional validation context
244   for the validator LLM. The validation context can be used to verify if the output satisfies certain
245   requirements in the question. For example, if a hybrid question is asking for a document satisfying a
246   relational requirement (e.g., a paper from a specific author), then the context including the reasoning
247   paths is essential for verification (e.g. “{author} →{writes} →{paper}”). In addition, the validation
248
      context can be used to determine whether the output is generated based on a reliable reference. In
      this case, using the retrieved reference Xt as the context improves the accuracy of the validator.
249
250   The commentor LLM Ccom aims to provide feedback for the agent to improve its action. To effec-
251   tively guide the agent, we construct corrective feedback that the agent can easily understand. In more
252   detail, it points out the error(s) in each action, such as incorrect selection or input of the retrieval
253   module. Unlike natural language feedback, which may cause uncertainty or inconsistency depending
254
      on the LLM used, our corrective feedback provides the agent with clear guidance on how to improve
      its actions. Furthermore, to avoid fine-tuning, it leverages in-context learning (ICL) to provide so-
255
      phisticated feedback. We collect successful experiences as examples, with each experience {at , ft }
256
      comprising a pair of action and feedback, which is verified by the ground truth. During inference,
257   the commentor LLM gives high-quality feedback based on multiple pre-collected examples.

      3.1       STA RK
271
272   Description We use two datasets from the STA RK benchmark4 (Wu et al., 2024b), STA RK-MAG
273   and STA RK-P RIME. Each dataset contains a knowledge graph (KG) and unstructured documents
274   associated with some types of entities. The task is to retrieve a set of documents from the database
275   that satisfy the requirements specified in the question. Noting that the majority of questions are
276   hybrid questions, and there are very few textual questions.
277
278   AGENT-G Retriever Bank In the text retrieval module, we use the vector similarity search (VSS)
279   (Karpukhin et al., 2020) between question and documents in the embedding space as both the re-
280
      triever and the ranker. In the graph retrieval module, the retriever extracts the ego-graph connected
      by the useful relations for each topic entity. These documents associated with entities in the ego-
281
      graph or the intersection of ego-graphs are then ranked by a VSS ranker.
282
283
      AGENT-G Critic Module Since the task in STA RK focuses on the retrieval of hybrid questions,
284
      we use the reasoning paths between the topic entities and entities in the extracted ego-graph as
285
      the validation context. This helps the validator verify whether the retrieved documents meet the
286   relational requirement in the question. We construct the corrective feedback that points out errors in
287   the action, in order to refine the extracted entity and relation. The successful experiences are verified
288   by the ground truth entities and used for the commentor to perform ICL.
289
290   3.2       CRAG
291
292   Description In the CRAG benchmark (Yang et al., 2024), there are KGs from 5 different domains
293   that can be utilized to retrieve useful reference. For each question, a database that includes 50
294   retrieved web pages and all 5 KGs is given, but the answer is not guaranteed to be on the web pages,
295   KGs, or both. The task is to generate the answer to the question, with or without the help from the
296
      retrieved reference. There are textual and relational questions, covering various question types, such
      as simple, simple with condition, comparison, and multi-hop.
297
298
      AGENT-G Retriever Bank In the text retrieval module, the web search based on the question
299
      is used as the retriever, which is done by CRAG ahead of time. The VSS ranker ranks the web
300
      pages based on their similarity to the question in the embedding space. In this module, we provide
301   an additional choice for the agent. If the output generated based on the current batch of retrieved
302   web pages is rejected by the validator, the agent can choose to move on to the next batch in the
303   ranking list. In the graph retrieval module, the retriever extracts the ego-graph connected by the
304   useful relations for each topic entity. As there is no document associated with entity, the retriever
305   retrieves the reasoning paths from topic entities to entities in the extracted ego-graphs. The reasoning
306   paths are verbalized as “{topic entity} →{useful relation} →... →{useful relation} →{neighboring
307   entity}”, and ranked by VSS.
308
309   AGENT-G Critic Module The retrieved reference is used as the validation context to check if it
310   is reliable to answer the question. While the ground truth of the retrieval is not available in CRAG,
311   we construct the corrective feedback based on the agent’s action and the evaluation. If the graph
312
      retrieval module is used and the evaluation is incorrect, then either the retrieval input (extracted
      entity and relation or the domain) is incorrect, or selecting graph retrieval module is incorrect; if the
313
      text retrieval module is used and the evaluation is incorrect, then the information in the current batch
314
      of documents is considered as not useful to answer the question.

358   STA RK In Table 4, AGENT-G outperforms all baselines significantly in both datasets in STA RK,
359   including GRAG and self-reflective baselines. Most baselines are designed to handle either textual
360   or relational questions, and the results have shown that they are not able to handle hybrid questions
361   (Challenge 1). While GRAG approaches perform poorly, our graph retrieval module is the second-
362   best performing method, highlighting the importance of designing an effective graph retrieval mod-
363   ule. As there are much fewer textual questions than hybrid questions, the graph retrieval module
364   outperforms the text retrieval module. We also observe that AGENT-G performs much better than
365   the graph retrieval module, indicating that the extracted entity and relation are frequently incorrect
366
      in the first iteration (Challenge 2). By tackling Challenge 1 and 2 with a novel design of retriever
      bank and critic module, AGENT-G has a significant improvement in performance.

      CRAG In Table 5, AGENT-G outperforms all baselines in CRAG. It is shown that RAGs with a
      single retrieval module cannot handle both relational and textual questions (Challenge 1). RAG with
370
      a concatenated reference also performs poorly due to the lost-in-the-middle phenomenon caused by
371
      irrelevant and distracting retrieval results (Shi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). Although our effective
372   retriever bank is provided, self-reflective baselines still find it difficult to answer the question cor-
373   rectly (Challenge 2). Since ReAct heavily relies on the LLM’s capability to think and understand
374   natural language feedback, it often fails to improve its action. Without a fine-tuned retrieval evalua-
375   tor, Corrective RAG cannot identify the usefulness of a reference and thus makes incorrect decisions.
376   This demonstrates the advantages of our critic module with corrective feedback. Furthermore, we
377   show that AGENT-G is robust to the choice of LLM base models. Since it requires no fine-tuning, the
      base LLM can easily be replaced with a state-of-the-art model to seamlessly improve performance.

      5      R ELATED W ORKS
453
454
      We first introduce the related works of GRAG, including those that have different settings from
455   the one we study, and then the ones that address similar challenges: LLMs with tool-use and self-
456   reflective capabilities. In summary, AGENT-G is the only one that satisfies all properties in Table 1.
457
458   Graph RAG (GRAG) Different settings have been explored for GRAG (He et al., 2024; Edge
459   et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024). While most works are tailored to textual graphs (Wu
460   et al., 2024b) or KGs (Yasunaga et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024; Mavromatis & Karypis,
461   2024), other works (Li et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024) construct KGs with the given text database.
462
463   LLMs with Tool-Use Tool-use methods (Trivedi et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Patil et al., 2023;
464   Schick et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024) seek the tool that generates the best answer. The variability
465   in domains and question types within the graph further complicates the challenge of tool-use. In
466
      structured graph data, AVATA R (Wu et al., 2024a) is the most recent work on optimizing the tool-
      use prompt via contrastive reasoning between positive and negative examples from the training set.
467
468
      Self-Reflective LLMs For complex tasks, LLMs are unlikely to generate the correct output on
469
      their first attempt. Self-reflection (Yao et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023; Paul
470   et al., 2024; Gou et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024) solves this problem by optimizing the output through
471   an iterative reflection process. A critic is commonly used to give feedback. Previous works use
472   different approaches as critics: pre-trained LLMs (Shinn et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023), external
473   tools (Gou et al., 2024), or fine-tuned LLMs (Paul et al., 2024; Asai et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024).
474
475
      6      C ONCLUSIONS
476
477
      We propose AGENT-G, an agentic framework for Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation (GRAG).
478
      In summary, AGENT-G has following advantages:
479
480            1. Agentic: it automatically improves the tool-use action with self-reflection;
481            2. Adaptive: it solves textual, relational and hybrid questions with a unified framework;
482
               3. Interpretable: it justifies the decision making and reduces hallucinations; and
               4. Effective: it adapts to different GRAG settings and outperforms all the baselines.
483
484   Applied on two GRAG benchmarks, STA RK and CRAG, AGENT-G outperforms all baselines. In
485   STA RK, AGENT-G achieves an averaged relative improvement 48% in Hit@1; in CRAG, AGENT-
      G increases the accuracy by 35% while reducing hallucination by 11%, both relatively.

836   A.2.2          AGENT-G I MPLEMENTATION
837   STA RK The examples in the prompts are collected from the training set provided by STA RK. We
838   use the default entity and relation types provided by STA RK. The radius of the extracted ego-graph
839   is no more than two. Four self-reflection iterations have been done. When extracting the entity name
840   from the question, multiple entities in the knowledge base may have exactly the same name. In these
841   cases, we select the entity that has the answer in its one-hop neighborhood for disambiguation, since
842   it is not the focus of our paper. Moreover, these cases rarely happen, where only 3.83% and 0.07%
843   of questions have this issue in STA RK-MAG and STA RK-P RIME, respectively.

852   STA RK We implement Think-on-Graph with their provided code5 . As running the full experi-

856   CRAG ReAct and Corrective RAG share the same backbone with AGENT-G, while having differ-
857   ent critics. ReAct has three actions, namely “search web”, “search KG”, and “extract entity relation
858   domain”, and is given a few examples. The process iterates among action, observation, and thought
859
      for four iterations as AGENT-G. While Corrective RAG requires a fine-tuned retrieval evaluator, we
      implement a version with only a pre-trained LLM. It starts with the text retrieval module and vali-
