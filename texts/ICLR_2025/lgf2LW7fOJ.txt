025                                                             A BSTRACT
026
027                         Diffusion-based video generators are now a reality. Being trained on a large corpus
028
                            of real videos, such models can generate diverse yet realistic videos (Brooks et al.,
                            2024; Zheng et al., 2024). Given that the videos appear visually coherent across
029
                            camera changes, we ask, do the underlying generators implicitly learn camera
030
                            registrations? Hence, we propose a novel adaptation to repurpose the intermediate
031                         features of the generator for camera pose estimation by linking them to the SoTA
032                         camera calibration decoder of DUSt3R (Wang et al., 2024a). This effectively
033                         unifies the video generation and camera estimation into a single framework. On
034                         top of unifying two different networks into one, our architecture can directly be
035                         trained on real video and simultaneously produces correspondence, with respect
036                         to the first frame, for all the video frames. Our final model, named JOG3R can
037                         be used in text-to-video mode, and additionally it produces camera pose estimates
038                         at a quality on par with the SoTA model DUSt3R, which was trained exclusively
039
                            for camera pose estimation. We report that the synergy between video generation
                            and 3D camera reconstruction tasks leads to around 25% better FVD scores with
040
                            JOG3R against pretrained OpenSora.

043   1                   I NTRODUCTION
044
045   Video diffusion models have rapidly improved over the last two years, leading to the emergence
046   of many commercial and open-sourced models (Guo et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Brooks et al.,
047   2024; Menapace et al., 2024a; Blattmann et al., 2023a). They are trained on very large-scale datasets,
048   e.g., WebVid10M (Bain et al., 2021) or Panda-70M (Chen et al., 2024), and produce realistic, di-
049   verse, and temporally smooth videos, simply based on text or image prompts.
050   In another recent breakthrough, DUSt3R (Wang et al., 2024a) demonstrated that the long-standing
051   optimization-based structure-from-motion framework for camera estimation can be directly replaced
052   by the forward pass of a dedicated network that has been trained to establish correspondence be-
053   tween any given pair of video frames. This is in contrast to the current SoTA in optimization-based
      approach for structure-from-motion GLOMAP (Pan et al., 2024a).

070   Inspired by the emergent behavior of intermediate features of large-scale image generators towards
071   other tasks (e.g., correspondence, semantic segmentation, etc. Tang et al. (2023); Dutt et al. (2024)),
072   we ask if the pretrained video generator features have similar emergent behavior. In particular, we
073   investigate whether the pretrained features can be repurposed towards DUSt3R-like camera pose
074   estimation. Surprisingly, we find that the video generator features, OpenSora in our setting, do not
075
      natively have emergency behavior and cannot be used directly for camera tracking.
076   Instead, we investigate whether the video generator features can be adapted towards camera pose
077   estimation. In particular, we test if with a limited amount of fine-tuning, one can produce video
078   generator features that also can be reused for camera tracking, without sacrificing video generation
079   quality (see Figure 1). We present a JOint Generation and 3d camera Reconstruction network, in
080   short JOG3R, that combines video generation with camera pose estimation into a single network,
081
      and can be supervised with generation and 3D reconstruction losses. We demonstrate that this does
      not lead to a loss in video quality while setting a new SoTA with respect to camera tracking on
082
      real video using a feedforward network (see Figure 2). In fact, we find that training with camera
083
      reconstruction leads to improved video generation, leading to a notable improved FVD score on the
084   RealEstate10K-test.
085
086   In summary, the paper makes the following contributions:
087
               • The first model that can both generate videos and estimate 3D cameras;

090              estimation and ablating the various design choices; and
091            • Reporting SoTA video-based camera tracking results on both RealEstate10k-test and

097   2.1     D IFFUSION -BASED V IDEO G ENERATION
098
099
      Building on the success of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) in image synthesis
      (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Rombach et al., 2021), the research community has extended diffusion-
100
      based methods to video generation. Early works (Ho et al., 2022a;b) adapted image diffusion archi-
101
      tectures by incorporating a temporal dimension, enabling the model to be trained on both image and
102   video data. Typically, U-Net-based architectures incorporate temporal attention blocks after spatial
103   attention blocks and 2D convolution layers are expanded to 3D convolution layers by altering ker-
104   nels (Ho et al., 2022b; Wu et al., 2023). Latent video diffusion models (Blattmann et al., 2023b;
105   He et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b; Blattmann et al., 2023a) have been introduced to avoid exces-
106   sive computing demands, implementing the diffusion process in a lower-dimensional latent space.
107   Seeking to generate spatially and temporally high-resolution videos, another line of research adopts
      cascaded pipelines (Ho et al., 2022a; Singer et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023c;


                                                               2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Bar-Tal et al., 2024), incorporating low-resolution keyframe generation, frame interpolation, and
109   super-resolution modules. To maximize computational scalability, recent waves in video generation
110   (Chen et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Menapace et al., 2024b; Brooks et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024)
111   diverge from U-Net-based architecture and employ Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles & Xie,
112   2023) backbone that processes space-time patches of video and image latent codes. Following this
113   direction, we build our method on OpenSora (Zheng et al., 2024), a publicly available DiT-based
114   latent video diffusion model.

116   2.2   3D R ECONSTRUCTION
117
118   The fundamental principles of multiview geometry Wrobel (2001) including feature extraction Lowe
119   (2004); Brown et al. (2011), matching Agarwal et al. (2009); Lou et al. (2012); Wu (2013a);
120   Havlena & Schindler (2014), and triangulation with epipolar constraints are well known to pro-
121
      duce highly accurate (yet spare) 3D point clouds with precise camera pose estimation from mul-
      tiview images Schonberger & Frahm (2016). The efficiency of 3D reconstruction has been im-
122
      proved with linear-time incremental structure-from-motion Wu (2013b) and coarse-to-fine hybrid
123
      approaches Crandall et al. (2012); Cui et al. (2017). To improve robustness to outliers, researchers
124   proposed global camera rotation averaging Cui et al. (2017), camera optimization techniques based
125   on features of points vanishing with oriented planes Holynski et al. (2020) or from a learned neural
126   network Lindenberger et al. (2021) to prevent rotation and scale drift issues in the process of the
127   structure-from-motion. Global camera pose registration and approximation with geometric linear-
128   ity Jiang et al. (2013); Cai et al. (2021) or joint 3D point position estimation Pan et al. (2024a)
129   are designed to further push the scalability and efficiency of the 3D reconstruction as well as the
130   robustness particularly to the image sequence with small baselines.
131   Given estimated camera poses and sparse 3D point clouds, multiview stereo can then produce a dense
132   3D surface using hand-created visual features Schönberger et al. (2016) or neural features with a cost
133   volume Ma et al. (2022); Ummenhofer & Koltun (2021); Ma et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023c); Ye
134   et al. (2023) to predict globally coherent depth estimates. Existing neural rendering methods re-
135   construct such a dense surface by modeling the implicit or explicit cost volume and differentiable
136   rendering of the scene for photometric supervision from multiview images Li et al. (2023b); Sun
137   et al. (2022); Peng et al. (2023); Guo et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022); Oechsle
138
      et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021); Murez et al. (2020) or monocular depth estimation Sayed et al.
      (2022). Some pose-free methods further erase the requirement of camera calibration: test time opti-
139
      mization produces globally consistent depth map under unknown scale and poses using frozen depth
140
      prediction model Xu et al. (2023); the unsupervised signals from dense correspondences such as op-
141   tical flow is integrated to learn from unlabeled data Yin & Shi (2018); Teed & Deng (2018); Zhou
142   et al. (2019). Recent works proposed a direct regression framework for dense surface reconstruction
143   from pairwise images by learning to predict globally coherent depths and camera parameters Um-
144   menhofer et al. (2016) or to directly predict per-pixel 3D point clouds from two views Wang et al.
145   (2024b); Leroy et al. (2024) using a vision transformer with dense tokenization techniques Ranftl
146   et al. (2021).
147
148   2.3   D IFFUSION M ODEL AS F EATURES FOR 3D R ECONSTRUCTION
149
150   A generative diffusion model is often trained on millions of paired image and text prompts and in
151   the process develops a semantically meaningful visual prior. Naturally, researchers are interested
152   in using this strong prior for many downstream 3D vision tasks. Injecting 3D awareness into the
153
      diffusion prior greatly improves the accuracy and generalizability of the monocular depth estima-
      tion and correspondence search tasks El Banani et al. (2024); Yue et al. (2024). The latent features
154
      from the frozen pretrained diffusion model are often used as a backbone, and a task-specific decoder
155
      with cross attention is newly trained for semantic correspondences Tang et al. (2023); Zhang et al.
156   (2023b); Hedlin et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024); Hedlin et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2024), 3D cor-
157   respondences Dutt et al. (2024), semantic segmentation and monocular depth estimation Zhao et al.
158   (2023), material and shadow prediction Zhan et al. (2023), general object 3D pose estimation Örnek
159   et al. (2023); Cai et al. (2024). However, such image diffusion features do not inherently consider
160   the temporal relation between the frames, leading to temporally unstable 3D prediction results from
161   videos. In contrast, we propose to utilize the video diffusion features as a backbone for the multi-
      tasking prediction of video generation and 3D camera poses estimation.

165
166
      Video diffusion model. We consider OpenSora (Zheng et al., 2024) as our base video generation
167   model, which is a DiT-based video generator inspired by the impressive success of Sora (Brooks
168   et al., 2024). OpenSora performs the diffusion process in a lower-dimensional latent space defined
169   by a pre-trained VAE encoder E. Each frame x of the input video is first projected into this latent
170   space, z0 = E(x). Given a diffusion time step t, the forward process incrementally adds Gaussian
171   noise to the latent code z0 via a Markov chain and obtains noisy latent zt . The denoising model ϵθ
172   takes the noisy latents of all frames, the time step t, and the text prompt y as input to predict the
173   added noise: ϵθ ({ztf }F
                             f =1 , t, y), where F is the total number of frames and θ denotes the parameter
174   of the DiT network (Peebles & Xie, 2023). The network consists of m+1 spatial-temporal diffusion
175   transformer (STDiT) blocks {b0 , . . . , bm }, similar to Ma et al. (2024). The iterative process of noise
176   prediction and noise removal is referred to as the backward process.
177
      Camera pose estimation module. We employ the state-of-the-art multi-view stereo reconstruction
178
      (MVS) framework DUSt3R (Wang et al., 2024a) as our downstream camera tracking branch. Given
179   a pair of images, DUSt3R first encodes each one individually with a ViT encoder (Dosovitskiy
180   et al., 2021; Weinzaepfel et al., 2022). A pair of decoders take both features as input for cross-
181   view information sharing, followed by two separate heads estimating point maps X ∈ RH×W ×3
182   represented in the coordinate of the first view, denoted as X 1,1 and X 2,1 , respectively. The relative
183   camera pose is then estimated by aligning X 1,1 and X 1,2 (or, equivalently X 2,1 and X 2,2 ) using
184   Procrustes alignment (Luo & Hancock, 1999) with PnP-RANSAC (Lepetit et al., 2009; Fischler &
185   Bolles, 1981).

      We propose a unified network that is able to do both video denoising and camera tracking. We ob-
205
      serve that ViT and DiT actually share many architectural designs in common since they both belong
206   to the broad transformer family. Hence, our key insight is to replace the image-based ViT encoder
207   in DUSt3R with the video DiT backbone in OpenSora. In other words, we provide the features of
208   the denoising DiT network ϵθ to DUSt3R decoders and heads, see Figure 3 for illustration.

210

212   correspondence across frames.

254   Inference. Once trained, JOG3R naturally supports three ways of inference (see Figure 2 and sup-
255   plemental video): (i) Text-to-video (T2V): the input is sampled Gaussian noise and we iteratively
256   denoise it with the text guidance to generate a video. (ii) Video-to-camera (V2C): we add noise to
257
      the input video based on a sampled time step t, denoise it for one time step, route the feature maps
      to DUSt3R decoders and heads, followed by registration of point maps X to obtain camera poses.
258
259   Given the two inference modes above, a straightforward combination is using the generated video
260   of T2V as the input of V2C, which we denote as T2V→V2C, essentially chaining the two networks.
261   However, thanks to our novel network design, we can provide the feature map directly to the re-
262   construction module at the desired time step, without the overhead of adding noise and passing it
263
      through the network again. As a result, cameras are generated alongside the video in one go. We
      term such a tightly coupled joint inference mode as (iii) Text-to-Video+Camera (T2V+C). Fig. 4
264
      illustrates the pipeline of these three inference modes.
265
266   Implementation Details. We adopt OpenSora 1.0 as our video generator, which uses 2D VAE (from
267   Stability-AI) Rombach et al. (2022), T5 text encoder (Raffel et al., 2020), and an STDiT (ST stands
268   for spatial-temporal) architecture similar to variant 3 in Ma et al. (2024) as the denoising network.
269   Among the 28 STDiT blocks, we empirically set the first 4 frozen and update only the weights of
      the temporal attention layers for the remaining 24 blocks. We extract the output of the 26th block b25

      as feature maps for DUSt3R decoders. The final two blocks behave as a “generation” branch whose
271   weights are only updated by the gradient of generation loss Lgen .
272
273   We adopt the linear prediction head of DUSt3R for final pointmap estimation. DUSt3R originally
274
      uses a decoder with 12 transformer blocks that is duplicated for each of the pair of frames. How-
      ever, information sharing is enabled between the two decoders. In our experiments, we find that a
275
      decoder structure with six transformer blocks provides similar performance and report our results
276

277   video sequence, we also experiment with replacing the duplicate decoder architecture with a single

      During training, we sample the time step t ∈ [0, 10] (corresponding to 10% of noise level) and
282
      consider the empty prompt for computing the reconstruction loss Lrec , while for the generation loss

284   sample t ∈ [0, 5] to add noise to the input video for camera estimation (V2C). To perform joint

286   and when the time step hits the sampled t ∈ [0, 5], we provide the output of block b25 to DUSt3R

290
291   In this section, we evaluate the proposed method in three aspects. We follow standard approaches to
292   assess the generated video quality (T2V). Since there is no ground truth camera trajectories for the
293   videos generated from T2V+C, we focus on validating the accuracy of camera pose estimation on
294   real videos (V2C) and report self-consistency for T2V+C.
295
296   4.1   S ETUP
297
298   Data. We choose RealEstate10K Zhou et al. (2018) as the dataset, which has around 65K video clips
299   paired with camera parameter annotations. We use the captions of RealEstate10K provided in He
300   et al. (2024) and also follow their train/test split. As pre-processing, we pre-compute the VAE latents
301   of the video frames and the T5 text embeddings of the captions. To obtain point map annotation X̄,
302
      we first estimate metric depth with ZoeDepth (Bhat et al., 2023), un-project it to 3D and transform
      to the coordinate of the first frame using the camera parameters provided in RealEstate10K. All
303
      camera extrinsic parameters are expressed with respect to the first frame.
304
305   In addition, we consider DL3DV10K (Ling et al., 2024), which also provides camera annotations,
306   as a failed test set. We choose a random set of 70 videos for testing and caption the first frame of
307   each video using Li et al. (2023a). We prepare point map annotations using ZoeDepth (Bhat et al.,
308
      2023), similar to the RealEstate10K dataset.
309   Baselines. We compare with a pair-wise method DUSt3R Wang et al., 2024a with linear head and
310   a video-based SfM method GLOMAP (Pan et al., 2024b). For DUSt3R we consider two variants:
311   (i) off-the-shelf pretrained weights (DUSt3R† ) and (ii) trained from scratch with the same data as
312   ours (DUSt3R*). For GLOMAP we report the results before the global bundle adjustment part.
313
314   Metric. We validate the quality of camera tracking on real videos (V2C) by comparing the estimated
315
      camera poses (R, t) with the ground truth poses (R̄, t̄). For rotation, we compute the relative error
      angle between two rotation matrices. Since the estimated and ground truth translation can differ in
316
      scale, we follow Wang et al. (2023a) to compute the angle between the two normalized translation
317
      vectors, i.e., arccos(t⊤ t̄/(∥t∥∥t̄∥)). Besides reporting the average of the two errors, we also follow
318   Wang et al. (2024a) to report Relative Rotation Accuracy (RRA) and Relative Translation Accuracy
319   (RTA), i.e., the percentage of camera pairs with rotation/translation error below a threshold. Due
320   to limit of the number of frames, hence small rotation variations, we select a threshold 5◦ to report
321   RTA@5 and RRA@5. Additionally, we calculate the mean Average Accuracy (mAA@30), defined
322   as the area under the curve accuracy of the angular differences at min(RRA@30, RTA@30). We
323   also use FID (Heusel et al., 2017) and FVD (Unterthiner et al., 2019) to measure image and video
      quality respectively, ensuring that our method maintains high generation quality.

      can see that our method produces good camera trajectories similar to DUSt3R, which is a method
      tailored for reconstruction only, but no generation. In the last row we show a failure case where both

      our method and DUSt3R fail to estimate reasonable camera poses. We hypothesize this is due to

      the infinite depth in the sky region which could cause inconsistent scale normalization across each

      4.4   D ISCUSSION
397
398
      Synergy of two tasks. Our full model JOG3R is trained with two losses, generation loss Lgen and
399   reconstruction loss Lrec . In both Table 1 and 2, (1a) and (1b), we show that keeping the generation
400   loss Lgen helps the reconstruction branch attain better camera poses estimation. On the other hand,
401   Table 3 (1b) and (1c) also suggest that introducing the reconstruction task results in better video
402   generation quality. Empirically, we demonstrate a synergy between two tasks – learning to generate
403   helps reconstruction; learning to reconstruct also helps generation. It shares the same spirit with the
404   known “analysis and synthesis” analogy, but our architectural design tightly couples them in one
405   network and allow end-to-end training.
406
      Self consistency of T2V→V2C and T2V+C. Since one can use JOG3R to generate camera tra-
407
      jectories in two ways: cascading T2V and V2C or the tightly coupled T2V+C pipeline, it is worth
408   comparing how much the two results differ. We run the two pipelines with 100 prompts and report
409   0.45◦ average difference in rotation and 19.20◦ in translation, both of which are low errors com-
410   pared with the corresponding numbers in Table 1 and 2, indicating that the camera poses from joint
411   T2V+C pipeline is consistent with T2V→V2C. The qualitative results in Figure 7 also confirm this
412   conclusion.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
415
416
      We have presented the first framework to enable joint video generation and 3D camera reconstruc-
417   tion. Our method utilizes intermediate features of a pre-trained video generation model for predict-
418   ing relative 3D point maps and hence enabling camera registration. Specifically, by providing the
419   intermediate generation features to task specific decoders and prediction heads, we present a unified
420   framework for text-to-video generation (T2V), joint generation and camera estimation (T2V+C),
421   and camera estimation for real videos (V2C).
422
      While being first of its kind, our method is not without limitations. First of all, since it is not
423   trivial to obtain accurate camera annotations for dynamic scenes, our method is currently trained
424   and applicable for videos of static scenes only. The length of the video sequences our method
425   can handle is currently limited by the number of frames the generator can synthesize. Handling
426   longer sequences may require extending our method to operate in a sliding window manner. As the
427   video generators continue to improve to enable generation of longer sequences, our method will also
428   naturally extend to handling longer videos with larger baseline.
