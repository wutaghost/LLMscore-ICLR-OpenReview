009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The recent advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) has ushered sophisticated
012            reasoning capabilities into the realm of video through Video Large Language Mod-
013            els (VideoLLMs). However, VideoLLMs currently rely on a single vision encoder
014            for all of their visual processing, which limits the amount and type of visual in-
015            formation that can be conveyed to the LLM. Our method, MERV, Multi-Encoder
016            Representation of Videos, instead leverages multiple frozen visual encoders to
017            create a unified representation of a video, providing the VideoLLM with a compre-
018
               hensive set of specialized visual knowledge. Spatio-temporally aligning the features
               from each encoder allows us to tackle a wider range of open-ended and multiple-
019
               choice video understanding questions and outperform prior state-of-the-art works.
020
               MERV is up to 3.7% better in accuracy than Video-LLaVA across the standard suite
021            video understanding benchmarks, while also having a better Video-ChatGPT score.
022            We also improve upon SeViLA, the previous best on zero-shot Perception Test
023            accuracy, by 2.2%. MERV introduces minimal extra parameters and trains faster
024            than equivalent single-encoder approaches by parallelizing the visual processing.
025            Finally, we provide qualitative evidence that MERV successfully captures domain
026            knowledge from each of its encoders. Our results offer promising directions in
027            utilizing multiple vision encoders for comprehensive video understanding.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Inspired by the sophisticated reasoning abilities of recent Large Language Models (LLMs) (8; 9;
032   41), researchers have focused on using them in many other domains to great success. The video
033
      counterparts, known as Video Large Language Models (VideoLLMs) (4; 28; 31; 37; 39; 62), connect
      pretrained vision encoders to LLMs by training a modality bridge from the vision space to the
034
      language space, allowing for reasoning to happen in the highly expressive language domain.
035
036   Most multimodal LLMs, such as LLaVA (34) for images and Video-LLaVA (31) for videos, opt
037   for contrastively pretrained encoders like CLIP (45) and LanguageBind (70). Their vision-language
038   pretraining naturally lends itself as a bridge between the vision input and the LLM, circumventing
039
      the need to train heavy vision-language alignment modules like a QFormer (27). These encoders are
      almost always pretrained separately and vary in architecture, training data, and optimization strategy.
040
      Consequently, the features extracted by these encoders exhibit unique characteristics, each with
041
      inherent strengths and limitations. Contrastive encoders like CLIP (45) may be better suited with their
042   multimodal semantic alignment, but are inferior to models such as DINOv2 (42) at fine-grained object
043   level understanding. They also fail to take advantage of models trained specifically on videos, such
044   as ViViT (2). Despite this clear tension between vision backbones, previous research in VideoLLMs
045   has relied on only one vision encoder for visual processing as one was thought to be sufficient for
046   visual understanding, and already difficult enough to achieve vision-language alignment with. Any
047   more encoders was unnecessary and not an effective tradeoff of runtime for compute.
048   In this paper, we argue that this choice to not use multiple encoders in existing VideoLLMs unneces-
049   sarily restricts their capabilities. For example, in Figure 1 we can see cases where only one of four
050   different single-encoder models answers a given question correctly. While simple scene descriptions
051   can be answered by image-level models, other questions require temporal and action-level comprehen-
052   sion, benefiting from features encoded with video models like ViViT (2). Consequently, the reasoning
053   capabilities of these VideoLLMs are directly limited by the inherent weaknesses of their respective
      pretrained encoders. Therefore, employing multiple encoders could allow us to complement one

147   3     MERV: M ULTI -E NCODER R EPRESENTATION OF V IDEOS
148
149   Our goal for MERV is to systematically build a video model that leverages multiple encoders with an
150   LLM to process a video following the LLaVA/PrefixLM (34; 35) paradigm (see Figure 2). Unlike
151
      previous works, our focus is not on combining multiple modalities (3; 70), but instead on combining
      multiple image and video encoders trained on different datasets and objectives. We extensively ablate
152
      three key aspects to make this possible: our selection of multiple encoders, i.e., which visual encoders
153
      and how many to use (Sec 3.1); how we align the spatio-temporal representations of each encoder to
154   mix the information together, especially in an efficient manner (Sec 3.2); and our implementation
155   efficiencies, from the parallel visual processing to the training recipes (Sec 3.3).
156
157   3.1   M ULTI -E NCODER F EATURE E XTRACTION
158
159   Our final architecture uses four distinct types of models: spatial experts, fine-grained temporal experts,
160   image-language experts, and video-language experts. We found experimentally that our choice of
161   four performed the best across all types of questions, and ablate our choices in Section 4.2. More
      details about these four encoders and other encoders we considered are in Appendix Table 4.


                                                                        3

162
      Spatial expert: DINOv2 (42) is trained using unsupervised learning on local-to-global correspon-
163   dences in image data. The resulting features have a robust understanding of object parts, as well as
164   semantic and low-level image understanding, but can suffer from poor language grounding.
165
166
      Temporal expert: ViViT (2) is trained using supervised learning on short videos. The architecture is
      designed for modeling the interactions between frames using spatial and temporal attention, which
167
      lets it capture longer temporal dependencies than pure image models can.
168
169   Image-Language contrastive expert: SigLIP (64) is trained using sigmoid contrastive learning on
170   image-text pairs. The model is designed to learn a joint embedding space for images and text, which
171   makes it good at understanding vision-language associations. However, it can overlook the finer
172
      details of an image which are not well described by text in its training data.
173   Video-Language contrastive expert: Finally, our video-language expert is LanguageBind (70). Used
174   by Video-LLaVA (31), LanguageBind is trained through joint multimodal learning between text and
175   multiple modalities, including videos, infrared, and audio, and understands the relationship between
176   video and text and their high-level semantics. We only use the video encoder of LanguageBind.

      3.2   S PATIO -T EMPORALLY A LIGNED R EPRESENTATIONS FOR F EATURE F USION
179
180   Our input is a batch of text, image-text, or video-text queries. The visual part of the input, either
181   images I or videos V, is passed through each of the visual encoders to extract the respective features.
182   Here we describe the detailed care we took in pre-processing to prepare the features for alignment.
183
      First, images are treated as videos with repeated frames, so assume all inputs are videos from here on
184
      out. A video is of shape T × H × W , where T is the number of frames and H, W are the height and
185
      width of the frames, and produce an output of shape te × he × we for an encoder e. One obstacle
186   with using different visual encoders is that each model outputs features with a different structure.
187   For example, given an input of shape 16 × 224 × 224, ViViT outputs a feature of shape 8 × 14 × 14
188   whereas LanguageBind’s features are of shape 16 × 16 × 16. Image-based encoders will not change
189   the temporal dimension, whereas ViViT downsamples the frames by a factor of 2.
190
      For temporal alignment, as each encoder is flexible enough to handle varying input frames, we simply
191
      choose our input T for each encoder so that each output te is the same across all encoders, i.e. t.
192
193   Pre-fusion projection. Now we need to achieve spatial alignment among the features. Naïvely
194   combining them is not possible as they all have different spatial shapes, and would also be prohibitively
195
      expensive at full resolution. We design a pre-fusion projector to both align and compress them.
196   Suppose our feature from encoder e is ve ∈ Rt×he ×we ×de , where de is the dimension of encoder
197   e, and assume the output spatial representations are square (i.e. he = we , but we keep notation for
198   clarity). Our pre-fusion projector uses an adaptive 2D average pool P for each encoder to resize the
199   spatial dimensions to the same h × w for all encoders, where h < he and w < we . As t is the same
200   across each ve , this spatio-temporally aligns the representations.
201   Finally, we need to connect the varying embedding dimensions de to a same dimensional space. We
202   add a linear layer to project the features from dimension de to d, the LLM’s dimension. In total, our
203   pre-fusion projection is

      3.3   I MPLEMENTATION E FFICIENCIES
225
226   Parallelized visual encoding. At a first glance, using multiple encoders seems to be a large cost
227   to pay when comparing the raw FLOPs and parameters. However, a key benefit of the LLaVA
228   style architecture is that the entire feature extraction and projection pipeline can happen in parallel.
229   To make this possible, we build on top of the recent powerful advances in parallel processing for
230   LLMs and use PyTorch’s Fully Sharded Data Parallel (69). As the video encoders themselves are
231   much smaller than the LLM blocks and complete in around the same time, most of the overhead in
232   running four encoders is already covered by having one encoder. We provide some timing numbers
233
      in Section 4.2.3 and find that our step time is similar to that of the single-encoder methods.
234   Our code is built on top of the Prismatic VLM codebase (22), which efficiently implements vision-
235   language model (VLM) training. We add the ability to handle videos and an arbitrary number of
236   visual encoders, along with many useful features for training. Our training is efficient for using
237   multiple visual models, completing in under 24 hours using 8 L40-48GB GPUs, and down to 8 hours
238   using 8 H100s in limited access testing. The Video-LLaVA codebase runs Stage 2 in around 38 hours
239
      on the same L40 setup and could not easily support multiple encoders in our initial attempts.
240   MERV frozen and full. Many different recommendations for training LLaVA style models have been
241   made since its inception. This is only made more complicated by the introduction of new datasets
242   with every new VideoLLM architecture, making it difficult to properly determine the best recipe for
243   one’s own setup. We intentionally fix our dataset to be the same as Video-LLaVA’s so we can isolate
244   the impacts of the training setup, from which we find two viable settings. MERV (frozen), which
245
      performs only Stage 2 instruction tuning and achieves similar results to the original Video-LLaVA
      recipe in only 43% of the time, and MERV (full), which unfreezes the LLM during Stage 1 as well
246
      for a slight improvement on a few benchmarks. As MERV (frozen) is faster to train with similar
247
      performance, we adopt that recipe by default for analysis, and interchangeably use MERV to refer to
248   it for simplicity from here on out. Detailed analysis is provided in Section 4.2.3.

358
359   Next, we test different strategies for fusing the information from all of the features, with detailed

364   similar performance of 56.6% and a lightweight cost. However, our cross-attention shows slightly

368   learning the additive weights directly as a learnable scalar or by fixing the weights to be 0.25 for each

377   reasoning data for both projectors and the LLM. Many recent works have attempted some combination
      of other strategies, such as unfreezing the vision encoders (12) or skipping the Stage 1 (22).

      The original motivation of our work was to choose encoders with complementary visual knowledge

417
      by using more than one encoder, and 2) do we need all four encoders, i.e. does each one meaningfully
418
      contribute to the final performance?

428
      encoder removed, meaning that MERV is using their knowledge (Fig. 4a). The minor drop in FLOPs

524       6                                             C ONCLUSION
525
526   Previous VideoLLMs have been limited to relying on a single visual model for feature extraction,
527   which leads to limited understanding capabilities of vastly different video tasks. In our work, we break
528   this paradigm and explore various fusion strategies for combining information from multiple visual
529   experts to generate a representation that can leverage the capabilities of different video encoders.
530   We find that our multi-encoder feature fusion is able to outperform comparable methods by up to
531   3.79% on video reasoning benchmarks. We show that the method can obtain better performance
532
      than the best-performing single-encoder model with minimal computational overhead. Finally, we
      quantitatively and qualitatively observe the skill specializations our model learns on an MCQ format
533
      of Something-Something v2, which confirms both that encoders can be specialized and that our
534
      model captures both axes of knowledge. Our paper proposes some initial steps in rethinking how we
535   approach the use of multiple encoders. We are especially excited about this trend as it could allow
536   our model to scale visual processing with the number of GPUs with better sharding strategies. We
537   can place one expert on each device and obtain visual features in parallel while still retaining similar
538   runtimes to having just one expert. We hope that this inspires others to also consider this problem as
539   potentially another direction for scaling and improving their VideoLLMs.

      A.1         L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER I MPACT.
813
814   Our works are based on LLaMA-2 7B model (53), and as with many other VideoLLM models,
815   the performance of our method is hugely dependent on the capabilities of the LLM model, and
816   better-performing models often demand significantly more computation. MERV requires running
817   multiple encoders, which can be computationally intensive and can lead to out-of-memory errors in
818   limited resource settings. While FSDP (69) allows us to easily and effectively train larger models
819   across multiple GPUs than would otherwise be possible, its generality also makes it difficult for us to
820   design tailored sharding strategies that would maximize the performance of our model. However,
821   with future improvements to data parallelism, our model can still benefit greatly and run even more
822
      efficiently. Also, despite the improved speeds, there is still an upper bound for what constitutes a
      reasonable training time that still allows us to test many of our design assumptions, which limits the
823
      scale and number of experiments we can run.
824
825   While we show that our method can successfully leverage information from different visual encoders,
826   nevertheless if the encoders themselves are limited in video understanding capability, MERV cannot
827   fully compensate for that. Our work aims to facilitate video understanding, which can lead to
828
      positive social impacts such as a video captioning model for low-vision users, automatic detection of
      medical emergencies, or better self-driving cars. It can also lead to negative social impacts like easy
829
      surveillance by the authorities, and human-like internet bots being used for scamming purposes. We
830
      follow the same safeguards implemented by the original authors of the datasets, the visual models,
831   and the LLM models. We have not put any additional safeguards ourselves.
