011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We propose a novel approach to image classification inspired by complex nonlin-
014            ear biological visual processing, whereby classical convolutional neural networks
015            (CNNs) are equipped with learnable higher-order convolutions. Our model in-
016            corporates a Volterra-like expansion of the convolution operator, capturing mul-
017            tiplicative interactions akin to those observed in early and advanced stages of
018            biological visual processing. We evaluated this approach on synthetic datasets
019
               by measuring sensitivity to testing higher-order correlations and performance in
               standard benchmarks (MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR10, CIFAR100 and Ima-
020
               genette). Our architecture outperforms traditional CNN baselines, and achieves
021
               optimal performance with expansions up to 3rd /4th order, aligning remarkably well
022            with the distribution of pixel intensities in natural images. Through systematic
023            perturbation analysis, we validate this alignment by isolating the contributions of
024            specific image statistics to model performance, demonstrating how different or-
025            ders of convolution process distinct aspects of visual information. Furthermore,
026            Representational Similarity Analysis reveals distinct geometries across network
027            layers, indicating qualitatively different modes of visual information processing.
028            Our work bridges neuroscience and deep learning, offering a path towards more
029            effective, biologically inspired computer vision models. It provides insights into
030
               visual information processing and lays the groundwork for neural networks that
               better capture complex visual patterns, particularly in resource-constrained sce-
031
               narios.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   ”The correlation over space translates into a correlation over order.” (Koenderink & van Doorn,
038   2018)
039
      Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have become the cornerstone of modern computer vision
040
      tasks, demonstrating remarkable performance across a wide range of applications (Krizhevsky et al.,
041
      2012), (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014), (He et al., 2016). The success of CNNs is largely attributed
042   to their ability to capture local patterns and hierarchical features in images through their layered
043   structure and weight sharing mechanism (LeCun et al., 2015), (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014).
044
045
      However, natural images contain complex correlations and higher-order statistics that extend be-
      yond simple linear relationships (Field, 1993), (Olshausen & Field, 1996), (Schwartz & Simoncelli,
046
      2001). These include texture patterns, edge interactions, and intricate geometric structures that are
047
      prevalent in real-world visual scenes. Importantly, these correlations exhibit a hierarchical struc-
048   ture, with higher-order correlations becoming increasingly sparse (Koenderink & van Doorn, 2018).
049   Traditional CNNs, particularly those with limited depth, often struggle to effectively exploit these
050   higher-order correlations (Gatys et al., 2016), (Ustyuzhaninov et al., 2022), (Ding et al., 2020).
051   The limitation of standard CNNs in capturing higher-order correlations stems from their reliance on
052   linear convolutions followed by pointwise nonlinearities (Yu & Salzmann, 2017). Although deeper
053   networks can approximate complex functions through the composition of many layers, this approach
      may be computationally expensive and require large amounts of training data.


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      To address this limitation, we propose a learnable extension of the classical linear convolution op-
055   erator to incorporate higher-order terms, akin to a Volterra expansion (Volterra, 1887), (Volterra,
056   1930). This approach allows for the direct modeling of multiplicative interactions between neigh-
057   boring pixels, enabling the network to capture complex local structures more effectively, even in
058   shallow architectures.
059
      We design models by incorporating higher-order convolutions, and compare their performance
060
      against standard CNN baselines. We evaluated our model on a range of datasets, beginning with
061   synthetic images typically used to assess sensitivity to higher-order correlations. Subsequently, we
062   extended our experiments to compare our model against CNN baselines on widely-used datasets
063   including MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR10, CIFAR100 and Imagenette. Our findings reveal that
064   these higher-order convolutions improve image classification performance, offering a promising di-
065   rection for advancing deep learning models in computer vision tasks.
066
      Interestingly, our results show optimal performance at the 3rd or 4th order, aligning remarkably
067   with the distribution of pixel intensities in natural images as analyzed by Koenderink & van Doorn
068   (2018). This alignment suggests that our approach effectively captures the fundamental structure
069   of visual information in the natural world. Through systematic perturbation analysis, we isolate the
070   contributions of specific image statistics to model performance, providing insight into how differ-
071   ent orders of convolution process distinct aspects of visual information. Furthermore, we employ
072   Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA) (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008) to investigate the internal
073   representations learned by our model compared with standard CNNs, revealing distinct geometries
074   that indicate different strategies for encoding and processing visual information.
075   Our approach addresses the limitations of simple pointwise nonlinearities in capturing local image
076   structure, especially in networks of limited depth or width. In particular, we show that incorporating
077   such computations at the earliest stages of image processing, even within the first layer of a neural
078   network, yields significant benefits. This finding suggests that models benefit from embedding these
079   computations via direct engagement with the superficial structure of images (Koenderink, 1984).

      1.1   B IOLOGICAL I NSPIRATION AND R ELATED W ORK
082
083
      Our work draws inspiration from the sophisticated mechanisms of biological visual systems, where
084   visual information processing extends beyond simple pointwise nonlinearities and first-order convo-
085   lutions (Fitzgerald & Clark, 2015; Clark et al., 2014; Lettvin et al., 1959; Peterhans & Von der Heydt,
086   1993; Levitt et al., 1994). These non-pointwise nonlinearities, which consider spatial relationships
087   between multiple input points, enable more effective extraction and integration of spatial information
088   (Krieger et al., 1997; Zetzsche & Barth, 1990; Barth et al., 1993; Koenderink & Richards, 1988).
089
      This complex processing begins in the retina, where certain cells exhibit responses to visual stimuli
090   that cannot be explained by simple pointwise nonlinearities (Lettvin et al., 1959). In the visual cor-
091   tex, specialized neurons respond to complex visual features like corners or line endings (Hubel &
092   Wiesel, 1965), performing advanced computations that integrate information across their receptive
093   fields. Such multidimensional, non-pointwise operators are fundamental to visual processing, as em-
094   phasized by Zetzsche et al. (1993) and Koenderink & Richards (1988). Similar mechanisms appear
095   across species - fruit flies employ non-pointwise computations for motion detection as early as the
096   second dendritic layer (Fitzgerald & Clark, 2015), suggesting these computations are fundamental
097   for efficient visual processing (Gilbert, 2007). Recent research has further demonstrated that single
098
      dendrites can solve complex problems like XOR (Xu et al., 2012; Ran et al., 2020), a capability that
      pointwise nonlinearities struggle to model (Minsky & Papert, 1969).
099
100   In computational research, the concept of nonlinear receptive fields has been explored through var-
101   ious approaches. Berkes & Wiskott (2006) analyzed inhomogeneous quadratic forms as receptive
102   fields, while Zoumpourlis et al. (2017) extended this to CNN-based learning. For video classifica-
103   tion, Roheda et al. (2024) introduced Volterra Neural Networks using a low-rank approach. Related
104
      developments in fine-grained image classification include bilinear CNN models (Lin et al., 2015)
      and compact bilinear pooling (Gao et al., 2016), with applications extending to computational neu-
105
      roscience (Ahrens et al., 2008).
106
107   Our approach differs fundamentally from previous methods by embedding higher-order operations
      within the convolutional operator itself, rather than applying them after feature extraction. This de-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      sign choice enables the network to learn and apply complex nonlinear transformations throughout its
109   entire depth, creating a more flexible architecture that better constitutes an algorithmic implementa-
110   tion of biological visual processing. By integrating these operations directly into the network’s core
111   building blocks, we enable more efficient feature learning while maintaining architectural simplicity.

      2.1   A Q UANTITATIVE P ERSPECTIVE ON THE T IED -W EIGHT I SSUE
195
      To demonstrate how a Higher-order CNN (HoCNN) addresses the tied-weight issue introduced in
196
      Section 1.2, we conducted a systematic analysis of weight independence in the network’s represen-
197
      tations. We generated a fixed input image (32 × 32 pixels) containing binary textures exhibiting all
198   possible 1-, 2-, 3-, and 4-point correlations for 2×2 patches, corresponding to fixing the xi terms in
199   Equation 1. We then randomly initialized the same model architecture 10,000 times and analyzed
200   the activations after the convolutional block (Conv/HoConv + BatchNorm + ReLU + Max Pooling)
201   for two architectures: a CNN with 10 kernels of size 2×2 and a HoCNN with 2 kernels of size 2×2
202   with higher-order expansions.
203
      To quantify the degree of weight independence, we employed Principal Component Analysis of
204   these activations, measuring the number of principal components (PCs) needed to explain 95% of the
205   variance. Our hypothesis was that tied weights would reduce the number of principal components,
206   while greater weight independence would require more components to capture the same variance.
207   The results confirmed our expectations (see Figure 1 B): the CNN required only 0.9% of PCs (87
208   out of 9610) to explain 95% of the variance, while the HoCNN required 5.3% of PCs (102 out of
209   1922) with second-order expansion and 8.3% (159 out of 1922) with third-order expansion. While
210   the CNN has more total components due to its higher channel count (5×), additional analyses with
211   matched channel counts yielded similar results.
212   These findings quantitatively demonstrate that higher-order convolutions introduce more indepen-
213   dent weights, leading to a richer representation space with reduced weight coupling. The increasing
214   number of components needed for variance explanation directly shows how our approach mitigates
215   the tied-weight issue inherent in standard CNNs. Additional analyses with various nonlinearities
      showed consistent results, further supporting these conclusions (see Appendix A.5).

257   3.1   S YNTHETIC DATASET: S TRUCTURED V ISUAL T EXTURES
258
259   To rigorously test the sensitivity of our model to higher-order correlations, we generated a synthetic
260   dataset of structured visual textures based on the work of Victor & Conte (2012) (see Figure 2).
261   These textures allow precise control over the type and intensity of contained correlations.
262
      To generate the textures, we utilized a custom software library (Piasini et al., 2021) implementing
263
      the method developed by Victor & Conte (2012). This process involves sampling from a distribution
264   of binary textures with specific multipoint correlation probabilities while maximizing entropy. The
265   correlation intensity is parameterized by parity counts of white or black pixels within 1-, 2-, 3-,
266   or 4-pixel tiles (termed gliders, Figure 2)). Notably, two-point and three-point gliders can produce
267   multiple distinct multipoint correlations based on their spatial configurations. Two-point correlations
268   can arise from horizontal, vertical, or oblique gliders, while three-point correlations can give rise to
269   L patterns with various orientations. Our synthetic dataset comprises 2000 training images, 1000
      validation images, and 2000 testing images. All reported results are based on the testing set.

449
450
451   Our study introduces a novel approach to image classification by extending convolutional neural
452   networks with higher-order learnable convolutions, inspired by complex nonlinear biological vi-
453
      sual processing. Our findings indicate that expansion beyond the 4th order is unnecessary, a con-
      clusion supported by both theoretical insights and empirical evidence. Koenderink & van Doorn
454
      (2018) analysis of natural image statistics reveals that the quadric, cubic and quartic power domi-
455
      nates approximately 63%, 35%, and 2% of the pixels, respectively. Our experimental results align
456   with this distribution, showing no significant performance gains beyond the 3rd order in benchmark
457   tasks and the 4th in structured visual textures. Through systematic perturbation analysis, we vali-
458   date this alignment by isolating the contributions of specific image statistics to model performance.
459   While adversarial perturbations are typically employed to assess and improve network robustness
460   (Szegedy, 2013; Goodfellow et al., 2014) or analyze network behavior (Carter et al., 2019; Santurkar
461   et al., 2019), we leverage them to probe our model’s sensitivity to distinct orders of image statistics,
462   demonstrating how different orders of convolution process specific aspects of the input.
463   Our approach to structured visual textures relates our findings to current mechanistic models in
464   computational neuroscience, particularly those focusing on the Drosophila visual system, which is
465   known to be sensitive and selective to different 3-point correlations (Fitzgerald & Clark, 2015),
466   (Clark et al., 2014). This connection strengthens the relevance of our model and highlights its
467   potential to provide insight into visual processing in both biological and artificial systems.
468   The consistent outperformance of our Higher-order CNN (HoCNN) across various datasets demon-
469   strates the effectiveness of incorporating these biologically inspired computations. Representational
470   Similarity Analysis (RSA) reveals distinct geometries, providing evidence that our model processes
471   visual information differently from standard CNNs, suggesting different strategies for encoding and
472   processing visual information.
473
      Future research directions include exploring deeper architectures with multiple higher-order convo-
474   lutional layers, which presents challenges in balancing increasing nonlinearity across layers. To a
475   certain extent, the scaling technique we employed (see Section 2) revealed better results compared
476   to the case when it was not used. While computational complexity represents a current limitation,
477   we envision future improvements through low-rank tensor decomposition methods. These tech-
478   niques, which have proven successful in transformer architectures (Hu et al., 2021), could maintain
479   the benefits of higher-order interactions while significantly reducing computational overhead.
480   In conclusion, our higher-order CNN represents a promising direction for more effective and bio-
481   logically inspired computer vision models. By exploiting visual patterns and relationships more ef-
482   ficiently, even in shallow architectures, our approach opens new possibilities for advancing the field.
483   Future research should focus on understanding the interplay between different orders of nonlinear-
484   ity, especially for spatiotemporal inputs (i.e. videos), on exploring more complex architectures, and
485   investigating potential applications beyond image classification, such as object detection or semantic
      segmentation.

      A.2     P LACEMENT OF H IGHER -O RDER L AYER & E FFECTS OF L ARGER K ERNELS IN CNN
703
704   We conducted extensive experiments to investigate both the optimal placement of higher-order op-
705   erations within the network hierarchy and the effects of increased kernel sizes in standard CNNs.
706   These experiments provide important insights into the architectural choices made in our main study.
707
708   A.2.1    E FFECT OF H IGHER -O RDER L AYER P LACEMENT
709
      We tested the introduction of the higher-order block at different depths in the network, specifically
710
      at the second and third convolutional layers. Our results reveal a consistent pattern across datasets:
711   performance gradually degrades as the higher-order block is placed deeper in the architecture. For
712   CIFAR-10, accuracy decreases from 71.17 ± 0.51% when the higher-order block is at the second
713   convolutional layer to 70.29 ± 0.49% at the third layer. Similarly, for CIFAR-100, accuracy drops
714   from 37.27 ± 0.80% to 36.09 ± 0.81%.
715
      This performance degradation can be attributed to earlier layers potentially capturing and overfit-
716
      ting to spurious correlations. While standard convolutions theoretically preserve higher-order cor-
717   relations, the cascade of nonlinearities and pooling operations can introduce and amplify spurious
718   correlations, potentially compromising the network’s ability to learn meaningful representations at
719   deeper layers.
720
721   A.2.2    I MPACT OF K ERNEL S IZE
722
723   We also investigated whether simply increasing kernel sizes in standard CNNs could achieve benefits
724
      similar to our higher-order operations. Despite larger kernels theoretically being capable of captur-
      ing higher-order correlations (as demonstrated by improved performance on our synthetic texture
725
      dataset), experiments with 5×5 and 7×7 kernels showed decreased test accuracy compared to our
726
      baseline CNN. For CIFAR-10, accuracy decreased to 68.72 ± 0.63% with 5×5 kernels and 67.51 ±
727   0.61% with 7×7 kernels. The pattern was similar for CIFAR-100, where accuracy dropped to 36.04
728   ± 0.76% with 5×5 kernels and 34.01 ± 0.72% with 7×7 kernels.
729
730
      Our choice of 2×2 kernels for the main experiments was motivated by the generative process of our
      synthetic textures, which are based on gliders up to 4 pixels arranged in 2×2 patches. The results
731
      suggest that while larger kernels might have the capacity to capture higher-order interactions, the
732
      linear nature of standard convolutions makes it challenging to effectively learn these patterns during
733   optimization.
734
735   A.2.3    C OMPUTATIONAL C ONSIDERATIONS
736
737   The placement of higher-order operations in early layers offers computational advantages. In typi-
738   cal convolutional architectures, the number of channels increases with network depth. Introducing
739
      higher-order kernels in later layers, where channel count is larger, would substantially increase both
      parameter count and computational complexity. Our architectural choice of early placement thus
740
      provides an effective balance between computational efficiency and performance.
741
742   These findings support our design decision to introduce higher-order operations early in the network,
743   where they can directly process raw image statistics before potential distortion by successive pro-
744   cessing layers. The results demonstrate that explicitly modeling higher-order interactions through
745
      dedicated operations provides a more effective approach for capturing and leveraging complex sta-
      tistical features compared to simply increasing kernel sizes in standard CNNs.

857   tions, can outperform biologically-inspired models like VOneNet on standard image classification
858   tasks while maintaining a more compact parameter footprint.
