009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The utilization of open-source pre-trained models has become a prevalent practice,
012            but unauthorized reuse of pre-trained models may pose a threat to the intellectual
013            property rights (IPR) of the model developers. Model fingerprinting, which does
014
               not necessitate modifying the model to verify whether a suspicious model is reused
               from the source model, stands as a promising approach to safeguarding the IPR.
015
               In this paper, we revisit existing model fingerprinting methods and demonstrate
016
               that they are vulnerable to false claim attacks where adversaries falsely assert
017            ownership of any third-party model. We reveal that this vulnerability mostly
018            stems from their untargeted nature, where they generally compare the outputs of
019            given samples on different models instead of the similarities to specific references.
020            Motivated by these findings, we propose a targeted fingerprinting paradigm (i.e.,
021            FIT-Print) to counteract false claim attacks. Specifically, FIT-Print transforms the
022            fingerprint into a targeted signature via optimization. Building on the principles
023            of FIT-Print, we develop bit-wise and list-wise black-box model fingerprinting
024            methods, i.e., FIT-ModelDiff and FIT-LIME, which exploit the distance between
025
               model outputs and the feature attribution of specific samples as the fingerprint,
               respectively. Extensive experiments on benchmark models and datasets verify the
026
               effectiveness, conferrability, and resistance to false claim attacks of our FIT-Print.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Deep learning models, especially deep neural networks (DNNs), have been widely and successfully
032   deployed in widespread applications (Wang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022a; He et al., 2023). In general,
033
      obtaining a well-performed model requires considerable computational resources and human expertise
      and is, therefore, highly expensive. In particular, some models are released to the open-source
034
      community (e.g., Hugging Face) for academic or educational purposes. However, the development
035
      of model reuse techniques, such as fine tuning (Liu et al., 2018) and transfer learning (Zhuang et al.,
036   2020), poses a potential threat to the intellectual property rights (IPR) of these models. With these
037   methods, malicious developers can easily reuse open-source models for commercial purposes without
038   authorization. How to protect their IPR becomes a vital problem.
039
      Currently, ownership verification stands as a widely adopted post-hoc approach for safeguarding
040
      the IPR of model developers. This method intends to justify whether a suspicious third-party model
041
      has been reused from the protected model (Zhang et al., 2020b; Li et al., 2022d; Sun et al., 2023).
042   Existing techniques to implement ownership verification can be broadly categorized into two main
043   types: model watermarking and model fingerprinting. Model watermarking (Adi et al., 2018; Li et al.,
044   2022d; Yang et al., 2023) involves embedding an owner-specific signature (i.e., watermark) into the
045   model. The model developer can extract the watermark inside the model to verify its ownership.
046   On the contrary, model fingerprinting (Cao et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2022) aims to
047   identify the intrinsic feature (i.e., fingerprint) of the model instead of modifying the protected model.
048   The fingerprint can be represented as the outputs of some testing samples at a particular mapping
049   function. Comparing model fingerprints enables comparing the source and suspicious models to
050
      examine whether the latter is a reused version of the former. Arguably, model fingerprinting is more
      convenient and feasible than model watermarking since the former does not necessitate any alteration
051
      to the parameters, structure, and training procedure and thus has no negative impact on the model.
052
053   In this paper, we reveal that existing model fingerprinting methods, no matter whether they are
      bit-wise (Li et al., 2021) or list-wise (Jia et al., 2022) (i.e., extract the fingerprint bit by bit or as a

080
081   Motivated by the aforementioned understandings, we introduce a new fingerprinting paradigm, dubbed
082
      False-claIm-resistant Targeted model fingerPrinting (FIT-Print), where the fingerprint comparison is
      targeted instead of untargeted. Specifically, we optimize the perturbations on the testing samples to
083
      make the output of the fingerprinting mapping function close to a specific signature (i.e., the target
084
      fingerprint). It restricts the (potential) fingerprint space and significantly reduces the probability of a
085   successful false claim attack. Based on our FIT-Print, we design two targeted model fingerprinting
086   methods, including FIT-ModelDiff and FIT-LIME, as the representatives of bit-wise and list-wise
087   methods, respectively. FIT-ModelDiff exploits the distances between outputs, while FIT-LIME
088   leverages the feature attribution of testing samples as the fingerprint.
089
      Our main contributions are four-fold: (1) We revisit existing model fingerprinting methods and reveal
090
      that existing methods are vulnerable to false claim attacks. (2) We introduce a new fingerprinting
091   paradigm (i.e., FIT-Print), where we conduct verification in a targeted manner with a given refer-
092   ence. (3) Based on our FIT-Print, we design two black-box targeted model fingerprinting methods:
093   FIT-ModelDiff and FIT-LIME. (4) We conduct experiments on benchmark datasets to verify the
094   effectiveness and conferrability of FIT-Print, and its resistance to false claims and adaptive attacks.

      2.2   T HE F ORMULATION OF E XISTING F INGERPRINTING M ETHODS
116
117   In this section, we primarily focus on black-box instead of white-box model fingerprinting methods
118   since they are more practical in real-world applications. We outline the formulations of these methods
119   to aid in the analysis and design process in the subsequent sections of this paper and follow-up
120   research. In general, existing black-box model fingerprinting methods can be categorized into two
121   types: adversarial example-based (AE-based) fingerprinting methods and testing-based fingerprinting
122   methods. We also include a broader discussion about other model fingerprinting in Appendix L.
123
      AE-based Fingerprinting Methods. AE-based fingerprinting methods (Cao et al., 2021; Lukas et al.,
124
      2021; Pautov et al., 2024) assume that the independent model has a unique decision boundary. Based
125
      on this assumption, they exploit adversarial examples (AE) (Ren et al., 2020; Wan et al., 2024) to
126   characterize the properties of the decision boundary of a model. AE-based fingerprinting methods
127   validate whether the AEs are misclassified by the source model and the suspicious model. If so, the
128   suspicious model can be treated as a reused version of the source model. The proposition tested in
129   AE-based methods can be formulated as follows.
130   Proposition 1 (Ownership Verification of AE-based Methods). Let Mo be the source model and Ms
131   be the suspicious model, and g(x) is the function that always outputs the ground-truth label of any
132   input data x. If for any testing sample x ∈ XT (XT denotes the set of the testing samples) we have
133

135   the suspicious model Ms can be asserted as a reused version of the source model Mo .

195   3.1   D ESIGN O BJECTIVES
196
197   Following prior works (Lukas et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024), the objectives of model fingerprinting
198   methods can be summarized as effectiveness, conferrability, and resistance to false claim attacks.
199
      • Effectiveness: Effectiveness means that the model developer can successfully verify the ownership
200     of the source model through the model fingerprinting method.
201
      • Conferrability: Conferrability is defined to ensure that the model fingerprint needs to be conferable
202
        to the models that are reused from the source model. In other words, the fingerprints of the reused
203
        models and the source model need to be similar.
204
205
      • Resistance to False Claim Attacks: It requires that the fingerprints of independently trained
        models need to be different. Also, a malicious model developer cannot construct a transferable
206
        fingerprint that can be extracted from independently trained models.
207
208
      3.2   T HE I NSIGHT OF OUR FIT-P RINT
209
210   As discussed in Section 2.3, existing model fingerprinting methods are vulnerable to false claim
211   attacks. We argue that the vulnerability stems primarily from the ‘untargeted’ characteristic of the
212   fingerprinting methods. The untargeted characteristic leads to a large fingerprint space that can
213   accommodate transferable adversarial fingerprints. In this paper, we propose FIT-Print, a targeted
214   model fingerprinting framework to mitigate false claim attacks. Our main insight is that although it is
215   tough to find the space that can only transfer among reused models, we can turn the fingerprint into a
      target one to restrict the fingerprinting space and reduce the adversarial transferability of fingerprints.

      3.5.2    FIT-LIME
325
326   FIT-LIME is a list-wise method that extracts the fingerprint as a whole list. FIT-LIME implements the
327   mapping function f (·) via a popular feature attribution algorithm, local interpretable model-agnostic
328   explanation (LIME) (Ribeiro et al., 2016). LIME outputs a real-value importance score for each
329   feature in the input sample x. We enhance the LIME algorithm and develop FIT-LIME to better cater
330
      to the needs of ownership verification. The details of FIT-LIME are elaborated as follows.
331   The first step of FIT-LIME is to generate c samples that are neighboring to the input image x. We also
332   gather the adjacent pixels in the image into a superpixel. Instead of using a clustering algorithm, we
333   uniformly segment the input space into k superpixels, where k is the length of the targeted fingerprint.
334   Assuming that k = µ × ν, the image can be divided into µ rows and ν columns. Then, we randomly
335   generate c masks where each mask is a k-dimension binary vector, constituting A ∈ {0, 1}c×k . Each
336
      element in each row of the matrix A corresponds to a superpixel in the image x. After that, we exploit
      the binary matrix to mask the image x and generate the masked examples X m . If the element in the
337
      i-th row of the mask A is 1, the corresponding superpixel preserves its original value. Otherwise, the
338
      superpixel is aligned with 0. Each row of the mask can generate a masked sample and the c masked
339   samples constitute the masked sample set X m .
340
341   The second step is to evaluate the output of the masked samples X m on the suspicious model Ms .
342

      the label of x. The intuition is that if the important features are masked, the prediction entropy will
343

      4.5   T HE R ESISTANCE TO A DAPTIVE F INGERPRINT R EMOVAL ATTACKS
495
496   In real-world scenarios, the model reuser usually knows which model fingerprinting method is
497   leveraged by the model developer, and can accordingly design an adaptive attack against the utilized
498   model fingerprinting method. Generally speaking, there are two different ways to attack a model
499   fingerprinting method (Yao et al., 2023): (1) fine-tuning the model (i.e., model-based attacks) or (2)
500   perturbing or preprocessing the input data (i.e., input-based attacks) to obfuscate the fingerprint of
501
      the model. In this section, we primarily focus on the former attack. The details and discussions of the
      latter attack can be found in Appendix D.
502
503   In model-based adaptive attacks, the model reuser can fine-tune the model attempting to remove the
504   original fingerprint inside it. Based on the knowledge of the model reuser with the fingerprint of the
505   model developer, we consider two different model-based adaptive attacks.

529   5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we revisited existing model fingerprinting methods. We designed a false claim attack
      by crafting some transferably easy samples and revealed that existing model fingerprinting methods
532
      were vulnerable to the false claim attack. We found that the vulnerability can be attributed to the
533
      untargeted nature that existing methods compare the outputs of any given samples on different models
534   rather than the similarities to specific signatures. To tackle the above issue, we proposed FIT-Print,
535   a false-claim-resistant model fingerprinting paradigm. FIT-Print transformed the fingerprint of the
536   model into a targeted signature by optimizing the testing samples. We correspondingly designed
537   two fingerprinting methods based on FIT-Print, namely the bit-wise FIT-ModelDiff and the list-wise
538   FIT-LIME. Our empirical experiments demonstrated the effectiveness, conferrability, and resistance
539   to false claim attacks of our FIT-Print. We hope our FIT-Print can provide a new angle on model
      fingerprinting methods to facilitate more secure and trustworthy model sharing and trading.

      Unauthorized model reuse has posed a serious threat to the intellectual property rights (IPRs) of

770   A.1    D ETAILED T HREAT M ODEL OF M ODEL F INGERPRINTING
771
772   There are three parties involved in the threat model of model fingerprinting, including the model
773   developer, the model reuser, and the verifier. The model developer trains a model and the model
774
      reuser attempts to steal and reuse this model. The verifier is responsible for fingerprint registration
      and ownership verification. The assumptions of these three parties can be found in Section 2.1.
775
776   Process of Model Fingerprinting. Model fingerprinting can be divided into three steps, including
777   fingerprint generation, fingerprint registration, and ownership verification.
778
      1. Fingerprint Generation: In this step, the model developer trains its source model Mo and
779      generates the fingerprint of Mo .
780
      2. Fingerprint Registration: After generating the fingerprint, the model developer registers the
781
         fingerprint and the model with a timestamp to a trustworthy third-party verifier.
782
783   3. Ownership Verification: For a suspicious model Ms that could be a reused version of Mo , the
784
         verifier will first check the timestamps of these two models. If the registration timestamp of Ms
         is later than Mo , the verifier will further check whether the fingerprint of Mo is similar to the
785
         fingerprint Ms . If so, the suspicious model can be regarded as a reused version of Mo .
786
787
      A.2    D ETAILED T HREAT M ODEL OF FALSE C LAIM ATTACKS
788
789   There are three parties involved in the threat model of false claim attacks, including the malicious
790   developer, the verifier, and an independent developer. The formal definition of false claim attacks can
791   be found in Section 2.3.
792
      Assumption of the Malicious Developer. In false claim attacks, the malicious developer is the
793
      adversary who aims to craft and register a transferable fingerprint to falsely claim the ownership
794   of the independent developer’s model MI . The malicious developer is assumed to have adequate
795   computational resources and datasets to train a high-performance model and carefully craft transfer-
796   able model fingerprints. The primary goal of the malicious developer is that the registered model
797   fingerprints can be verified in as many other models as possible. By generating the transferable
798   fingerprint, the malicious developer can (falsely) claim the ownership of any third-party models (that
799   are registered later than that of the malicious developer).
800   Process of False Claim Attacks. The process of false claim attacks can also be divided into three
801   steps, including fingerprint generation, fingerprint registration, and false ownership verification.
802
803   1. Fingerprint Generation: In this step, the model developer trains its source model Mo and
804
         attempts to generate a transferable fingerprint of Mo .
805   2. Fingerprint Registration: After generating the fingerprint, the model developer registers the
806      transferable fingerprint and the model with a timestamp to a trustworthy third-party verifier.
807   3. Falsely Ownership Verification: The adversary tries to use the transferable fingerprint to falsely
808      claim the ownership of another independently trained model MI . Since the fingerprint is registered
809      beforehand, the ownership verification won’t be rejected due to the timestamp. Subsequently, the
         benign developer may be accused of infringement.

      the following five categories of model reuse techniques, including copying, fine-tuning, pruning,
844
      model extraction, and transfer learning.
845
846   • Copying: Copying refers to the adversary somehow gaining white-box access to the parameters
847     and architecture of the victim model. Subsequently, the adversary steals the model by directly
848     copying it. It may occur when the model is open-source and publicly available.
849   • Fine-tuning: Fine-tuning means the adversary re-trains the victim model with its own dataset which
850     is related to the primitive task of the victim model. We consider three types of fine-tuning denoted
851     as Fine-tuning(10%), Fine-tuning(50%), and Fine-tuning(100%), which means
852     we fine-tune the last 10%, 50%, and 100% layers of the victim models.
853   • Pruning: Pruning intends to compress the model by removing redundant parameters. We leverage
854     weight pruning (Han et al., 2015) as our pruning method, which prunes the neurons according
855     to their activations. We prune 10%, 30%, and 50% parameters of the victim model, denoted as
856     Pruning(10%), Pruning(30%), and Pruning(50%) respectively.
857   • Model Extraction: Model extraction attempts to steal the knowledge of the victim model via
858     only black-box access. The adversary can obtain the output of the victim model to train the
859     extracted model. In our experiments, we implement the model extraction in two different scenarios.
860     Extract(same) and Extract(different) refer to utilizing the same or different model
861     architectures to extract the source model, respectively.
862   • Transfer Learning: Transfer learning is an ML technique where the victim model trained on one
863     task is adapted as the starting point for a model on the second related task. In our experiments, we
        replace the last layer of the model to fit the second task and fine-tune the model for 200 epochs.

1076   H    T HE OVERHEAD OF FIT-M ODEL D IFF AND FIT-LIME
1077
1078   Compared with existing model fingerprinting methods, FIT-Print needs to optimize the testing
1079   samples and thus has an extra overhead. We hereby present a detailed analysis of the time and space
       complexity of the two fingerprinting methods, FIT-ModelDiff and FIT-LIME. FIT-ModelDiff and


                                                           20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       FIT-LIME are the representatives of bit-wise and list-wise methods, respectively. As such, they have
1081   different trade-offs in time and space overhead.
1082
1083   During the fingerprint verification stage:
1084   • For FIT-ModelDiff, the space complexity is O(1) and the time complexity is O(k) where k is the
1085     length of the targeted fingerprint. FIT-ModelDiff is a bit-wise fingerprinting method that extracts
1086     the fingerprint bit by bit. It only reads two samples to calculate one bit in the fingerprint in each
1087     step. The fingerprint can be extracted in k steps. Therefore, the space complexity is O(1), and the
1088     time complexity is O(k).
1089   • For FIT-LIME, the space complexity is O(k) and the time complexity is O(k/β) where β is the
1090     batch size. FIT-LIME is a list-wise method that extracts the fingerprint as a whole list. FIT-LIME
1091     needs to read all the samples at the same time to extract the fingerprint. On the other hand, FIT-
1092     LIME can calculate the outputs of an entire batch at the same time. As such, the space complexity
1093     is O(k), and the time complexity is O(k/β).
1094   During the testing samples extraction stage:
1095
       In each iteration of optimizing the testing samples, we need to perform one forward propagation
1096
       and one backward propagation of the fingerprint verification method. Assuming that we utilize ξ
1097
       augmented models during optimization, the time complexities of each iteration of testing sample
1098   extraction in FIT-ModelDiff and FIT-LIME are O(ξ · k) and O(ξ · k/β)**. k is the length of the
1099   targeted fingerprint and β is the batch size. For instance, in our main experiments, we utilize 10
1100   augmented models and a 256-bit targeted fingerprint. It takes nearly 3 seconds for one optimization
1101   iteration in FIT-ModelDiff and 1 second for that in FIT-LIME. As such, our FIT-ModelDiff and
1102   FIT-LIME are efficient in optimization and the overhead is acceptable.

1203
1204   In this section, we discuss the generalization of our proposed FIT-Print.
1205
       How to Transform Existing Fingerprinting methods into the FIT-Print Paradigm. In Section 3.5,
1206   we propose two targeted model fingerprinting methods as the representatives of bit-wise fingerprinting
1207   and list-wise fingerprinting. Based on the insight of these two methods, any existing testing-based
1208   model fingerprinting methods can be transformed into the FIT-Print paradigm within two steps.
1209
       • First, for the bit-wise fingerprinting methods that extract the fingerprint bit by bit, we need to
1210
         formulate a sort or location mapping rule to confirm the position of each bit in the fingerprint F .
1211
         The rule can ensure the fingerprint of the model is uniquely determined. The list-wise fingerprinting
1212     methods are not necessitated to do so since the fingerprint is extracted as a whole, and the position
1213     of the bits in the fingerprint is already determined.
1214
1215
       • Second, we need to transform the value range of each element in the fingerprint vector v into an
         interval containing both positive and negative values by a linear transformation.
1216
1217   After the above two steps, we can utilize the transformed mapping function to develop a new FIT-Print
1218   model fingerprinting method and leverage the procedures introduced in Section 3.3 and Section 3.4
1219   to extract the testing samples and ownership verification.
1220   The Design Criteria for a Mapping Function. The design criteria for a good mapping function are
1221   from the following four main aspects.
1222
1223   • Distinguishable: Different models need to exhibit different outputs in the output space of the
1224     mapping function. This can guarantee that applying the mapping function can distinguish different
1225
         independent models.
1226   • Task-agnostic: The mapping function needs to be able to process the outputs of models with
1227     different tasks (e.g., with different number of classes).
1228
       • Robust: The outputs of the mapping function on a model need to be robust against various model
1229     reusing techniques, i.e., the outputs do not change significantly after model reusing.
1230
1231   • Efficient: The calculation of the mapping function needs to be efficient and take a small overhead.

       L.1   M ODEL WATERMARKING
1236
1237   Model watermarking methods aim to embed an owner-specific signature (i.e., watermark) into the
1238   models. In case the watermarked model is reused or stolen by the adversary, the model developer
1239   can extract the watermark inside the adversary’s suspicious model. If the extracted watermark is
1240   similar to the watermark of the model developer, the model developer can accuse the adversary of
1241   infringement. Broadly, model watermarking methods can be divided into two categories, white-box
       model watermarking and black-box model watermarking (Sun et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024b).


                                                        23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       White-box Model Watermarking Methods: White-box model watermarking methods assume that
1243   the model developer can get full access to the suspicious model during ownership verification. It
1244   usually occurs when the adversary publishes the model as an open-source model. White-box model
1245   watermarking methods directly embed the watermark into the parameters of the models. For instance,
1246   Uchida et.al (Uchida et al., 2017) proposed to add a watermark regularization term into the loss
1247   function during fine-tuning to embed the watermark. Darvish et.al (Darvish Rouhani et al., 2019)
1248   chose to embed the watermark into the intermediate outputs of the protected models. The watermark
1249   can also be embedded into the model by adjusting the architecture of the models (Fan et al., 2019;
1250   Lv et al., 2023) or embedding external features (Li et al., 2022c). White-box model watermarking
1251
       methods need to know the parameters of the suspicious models during ownership verification. This
       assumption is difficult to realize in practical scenarios because the model is usually deployed in the
1252
       cloud and can only be accessed via API. Such a limitation restricts the application of the white-box
1253
       model watermarking methods in the real world.
1254
1255   Black-box model watermarking methods: Black-box model watermarking methods assume that
1256   the model developer can only observe the outputs from the suspicious models (Adi et al., 2018; Wei
1257
       et al., 2024). Due to such a constraint, black-box methods are primarily based on backdoor attacks (Li
       et al., 2022b; Xiang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2020). Backdoor-based model watermarking methods
1258
       leverage backdoor attacks to force the model to remember specific patterns and their corresponding
1259
       target labels (Ma et al., 2023). For ownership verification, the model developer can embed a specific
1260   dataset in which each data has a wrong label as watermarks into the model. The trigger set is
1261   unique to the watermarked model. The model developer can trigger the misclassification to verify its
1262   ownership. Backdoor-based methods can apply to various tasks, such as image classification (Adi
1263   et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019), image processing (Zhang et al., 2020a; 2021), federated learning (Liu
1264   et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2023), and prompt (Yao et al., 2024). For non-backdoor black-box methods,
1265   Miani et al. (Maini et al., 2020) proposed Dataset Inference to implement ownership verification.
1266   Recently, Shao et al. (Shao et al., 2024a) proposed embedding a multi-bit watermark into the feature
1267   attribution explanations of some specific samples, which can tackle the harmfulness and ambiguity of
1268
       the backdoor-based model watermarking methods.
1269   However, since model watermarking methods need to embed the watermark into the model through
1270   fine-tuning, they inevitably have a negative impact on the functionality of the protected models.
1271   Model watermarking methods might reduce the practical value of the models. In addition, as the
1272   parameter scale of the model gets larger (e.g., large foundation models (Chang et al., 2024)), it is
1273   more costly for the model developer to fine-tune the models, limiting the practical application of
1274
       model watermarking methods in real world.

1276   L.2   M ODEL F INGERPRINTING OR M ODEL F UNCTIONAL D ISTANCE
1277
1278   In this section, we provide a comprehensive discussion on model fingerprinting. Some existing
1279   studies have been developed to compute the functional distance between different models. Although
1280   these works serve a different purpose from model fingerprinting for ownership verification, they
1281   share technical similarities. Therefore, we collectively refer to these works as model fingerprinting.
1282   Similar to model watermarking methods, model fingerprinting methods can also be categorized into
1283   white-box and black-box (Sun et al., 2023).
1284   White-box Model Fingerprinting Methods: in the white-box scenario, since the model developer
1285   can get access to the parameters of the suspicious model, a direct way to compare the models
1286   is to compare the weights (or their hash values) of the models. Some existing white-box model
1287   fingerprinting methods leveraged the path of model training (Jia et al., 2021), the random projection
1288   of model weights (Zheng et al., 2022), or the learnable hash of the model weights (Xiong et al., 2022).
1289   Some recent works also explored utilizing the deep representations (e.g., gradients (Song et al., 2023))
1290   or the intermediate results (Chen et al., 2022a) of the testing samples as the fingerprint. However,
1291   similar to white-box model watermarking methods, the application of white-box fingerprinting
1292
       methods in real-world scenarios is also limited.
1293   Black-box Model Fingerprinting Methods: in the black-box scenario, the model developer is
1294   assumed to have only API access to the suspicious model. Existing black-box model fingerprinting
1295   methods can be classified into adversarial-example-based (AE-based) methods (Cao et al., 2021;
       Wang et al., 2021; Lukas et al., 2021) and testing-based methods (Li et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022b;a)


                                                         24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       and we have presented their formulations in Section 2.2. AE-based methods craft adversarial examples
1297   to identify the decision boundary of different models (Cao et al., 2021). Lukas et.al (Lukas et al.,
1298   2021) proposed to craft some reused models as augmented models to improve the conferrability of
1299   the fingerprint. On the contrary, testing-based methods compare the model behavior on the testing
1300   samples at the specific mapping function. ModelDiff (Li et al., 2021) and SAC (Guan et al., 2022)
1301   utilized the distances between the output logits of different input samples, while Zest (Jia et al., 2022)
1302   took the feature attribution map output by LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) as the mapping function. In
1303   addition, Chen et.al (Chen et al., 2022a) proposed a series of mapping functions to calculate model
1304   similarity. Compared to AE-based methods, testing-based methods have the capability to compare
1305
       models across different tasks and output formats, thereby attracting greater attention.
1306   There are also some existing works exploring other applications of model fingerprinting. For instance,
1307   He et al. (2019) attempts to verify whether the model parameters are altered by the adversary.
1308   Specifically, He et al. (2019) aims to generate a fragile fingerprint that can be destroyed when the
1309   model is modified by others.
1310   Unlike model watermarking methods, model fingerprinting does not need to alter the parameters,
1311   architectures, and training processes of the models. The efficiency of the extraction and verification
1312   of model fingerprinting methods is also much higher than model watermarking methods. As such,
1313   currently model fingerprinting may be a promising way to protect the IPR of the valuable models.

1317   M.1    T HE C OMPARISON OF FALSE C LAIM ATTACK TO OTHER W ORKS
1318
1319   Differences between False Claim Attack and Ambiguity Attack. Similar to the false claim
1320   attack (Liu et al., 2024), ambiguity attack (Fan et al., 2019) is another attack attempting to forge
1321   the ownership certificate and falsely claim to have ownership of another party’s model. The major
1322   difference between these two attacks is that the ambiguity attack is conducted on a given trained
1323   model while the false claim attack aims to create a transferable certificate to claim the ownership of
1324
       the third-party models trained afterward. Existing literature Liu et al. (2024); Waheed et al. (2024)
       demonstrates that the registration of ownership certificates (e.g., watermarks or fingerprints) can
1325
       effectively mitigate ambiguity attacks but is not effective in defending against the false claim attack.
1326
       As such, the false claim attack can be considered as an improved version of the ambiguity attack and
1327   we mainly focus on the false claim attack in this paper.
1328
1329   Differences between False Claim Attack and False Positive Rate. Some existing works (Li et al.,
1330   2021; Chen et al., 2022a) may involve the false positive rate, which evaluates whether a fingerprint
1331   can be extracted or verified on an independent model (instead of the reused model). The major
1332   difference is that the false claim attack is designed to maliciously generate a transferable fingerprint.
1333
       Contrarily, when calculating the false positive rate, the fingerprint is extracted innocently. Arguably,
       achieving resistance to the false claim attack is more difficult than a low false positive rate. We also
1334
       present the false positive rates of FIT-Print in Table 2.

       M.2    T HE C OMPARISON OF FIT-P RINT TO E XISTING F INGERPRINTING M ETHODS
1337
1338   Connections and Differences with ModelDiff: The insight of ModelDiff and FIT-ModelDiff
1339   is to compare the output differences between perturbed samples and original samples. However,
1340   primitive ModelDiff calculated the cosine similarity of these outputs as the similarity score. Since
1341   the value range of cosine similarity with positive vectors is always larger than 0, ModelDiff cannot
1342   be directly applied to FIT-Print. Also, ModelDiff cannot recognize the models extracted from the
1343   source model. FIT-ModelDiff tackled these issues by designing a value range transformation and
1344   leveraging augmented models to improve conferrability. The details can be found in Section 3.5.1.
1345   Connections and Differences with Zest: In our FIT-LIME, we exploit the feature attribution output
1346   by LIME as the fingerprint. An existing fingerprinting method, Zest (Jia et al., 2022), utilizes
1347   primitive LIME to compare different models. However, primitive LIME has two drawbacks in
1348   ownership verification: (1) LIME first clusters the pixels into several groups called superpixels
1349   via Quickshift (Vedaldi & Soatto, 2008). The clustering algorithm is time-consuming and these
       superpixels are irregular and unordered, making it hard to transform them into a bit string that


                                                          25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       represents the fingerprint. (2) Primitive LIME depends on the label of the input to calculate the
1351   importance scores, which is not applicable when the suspicious model has different predicted classes
1352   from the source model. Our proposed FIT-LIME has tackled the above issues, and the technical
1353   details can be found in Section 3.5.2.
1354
       Connections and Differences with MetaV: MetaV(Pan et al., 2022) introduces two critical compo-
1355
       nents, the adaptive fingerprint and the meta-verifier. The adaptive fingerprint is a set of adversarial
1356
       examples. The meta-verifier takes the suspicious model’s output of the adaptive fingerprint and
1357   outputs whether the suspicious model is reused from the original model. MetaV accomplishes such
1358   an objective by simultaneously optimizing the adaptive fingerprint (i.e., adversarial perturbations)
1359   and the meta-verifier (i.e., a fully-connected neural network). In conclusion, MetaV provided a
1360   task-agnostic fingerprinting framework. However, MetaV is vulnerable to false claim attacks. MetaV
1361   is an AE-based fingerprinting method and the adversary can craft transferable adversarial examples to
1362   achieve false claim attacks. This proposition is also presented in Liu et al. (2024). Moreover, MetaV
1363   cannot detect transfer learning models, which is one of the realistic stealing settings. MetaV depends
1364   on a pre-trained meta-verifier. Transfer learning models may have different output formats, e.g., the
1365
       number of classes. Therefore, the meta-verifier which has a fixed input format is not able to process
       the changed outputs of the suspicious model and detect whether it is reused from the original model.

1370
1371   In terms of positive societal impact, this paper aims to address the challenges associated with false
1372   claim attacks in ownership verification through the utilization of targeted model fingerprinting meth-
1373   ods. Our FIT-Print, as a method for protecting intellectual property rights (IPR) related to models,
1374   will assist both academia and industry in safeguarding the costly models’ IPRs and preventing unau-
1375   thorized model reuse and theft. Furthermore, FIT-Print has the potential to facilitate the emergence of
1376
       new business models such as model trading.
1377   On the other hand, one potential negative societal impact is that the insight of FIT-Print is to some
1378   extent similar to those of targeted adversarial attacks. Therefore, the insight of FIT-Print might also
1379   apply to adversarial attacks. However, FIT-ModelDiff changes the difference between the outputs
1380   and FIT-LIME changes the explanation. Neither of them directly turns the prediction classes into a
1381   target class. As such, although the insight might be transferred to adversarial attacks, the negative
1382
       impact of this attack is negligible to most of the AI applications.

1385   O    P OTENTIAL L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
1386
1387   One potential limitation of our FIT-Print is that FIT-Print depends on a trustworthy third-party
1388   institution to register the fingerprint with a timestamp. However, we argue that the existing intellectual
1389   property office (IPO) or artificial intelligence regulator (AIR) can be responsible for this duty. First,
1390   it is common for developers to register their intellectual property, including valuable models, with the
1391   IPO for copyright protection. Second, many countries and regions are in the process of establishing or
1392   have established the AIR (e.g., as exemplified in the EU Artificial Intelligence Act) to ensure security
1393   and transparency before deploying the AI models. As such, it is also feasible for the AIR to manage
1394
       model registrations and ensure that the new models do not infringe on other’s legal copyrights.
1395   Another potential limitation is that FIT-Print does not provide formal proof of the resistance to false
1396   claim attacks. As such, a more powerful adversary may still be able to conduct a successful false
1397   claim attack. We will investigate how to achieve a certified robust model fingerprinting method
1398   against false claim attacks in our future work.
1399   Furthermore, as discussed in Appendix J, for deterministic models that do not involve randomness
1400   (e.g., CNN and LLM), our FIT-Print can generalize to different types of models and datasets. However,
1401   for non-deterministic models that involve randomness (e.g., diffusion models), we have to admit that
1402   we do not know whether our methods and existing fingerprinting methods can be adapted to them
1403   since they have a completely different inference paradigm. We will conduct a comprehensive study
       in our future work.
