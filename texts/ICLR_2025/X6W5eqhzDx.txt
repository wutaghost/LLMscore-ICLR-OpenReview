009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             In multi-task reinforcement learning, agents train on a fixed set of tasks and have
012             to generalise to new ones. Recent work has shown that increased exploration im-
013             proves this generalisation, but it remains unclear why exactly that is. In this paper,
014             we introduce the concept of reachability in multi-task reinforcement learning and
015             show that an initial exploration phase increases the number of reachable tasks the
016             agent is trained on. This, and not the increased exploration, is responsible for the
017
                improved generalisation, even to unreachable tasks. Inspired by this, we propose a
                novel method Explore-Go that implements such an exploration phase at the begin-
018
                ning of each episode. Explore-Go only modifies the way experience is collected
019
                and can be used with most existing on-policy or off-policy reinforcement learn-
020             ing algorithms. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method when combined
021             with some popular algorithms and show an increase in generalisation performance
022             across several environments.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Despite major advances in reinforcement learning (RL), it is fairly rare to encounter RL outside
028   of the academic setting. One of the remaining challenges of adopting it in the real world is the
029   ability of an agent to generalise to novel scenarios, that is, those not encountered during training.
030   For example, we do not want a house-cleaning robot to stop working when the owner moves their
031
      couch. This is the main research question investigated in the zero-shot policy transfer setting (ZSPT,
      Kirk et al., 2023). Here the agent trains on several variations of an environment, known as tasks,
032
      and must generalise to new ones. This differs from the commonly studied single-task RL setting, in
033
      which the agent trains and tests on the same environment instance.
034
035   There exists a surprising interaction between ZSPT generalisation and exploration of the training
036   environments. A single-task RL agent must trade off between exploring for better futures and ex-
037
      ploiting what it already knows. Once a good enough policy is found, a single-task agent ceases
      exploration to focus on collecting rewards. In multi-task RL, however, Jiang et al. (2023) have re-
038
      cently demonstrated that more effective exploration, that never stops throughout the entire training
039
      process, improves generalisation to unseen tasks.
040
041   However, it is not yet entirely clear in which tasks we can expect exploration to improve generalisa-
042   tion, nor is it clear when to use it to benefit generalisation the most.1 For example, exploration might
043
      help a cleaning robot to know what to do when it is activated at an unusual location in the house.
      Having seen more of the environment means the robot will be familiar with that area. However, if
044
      the owner rearranges some furniture, the previous path to move might be blocked, and it is unclear
045
      if and how more exploration would help in this situation.
046
047   In this paper, we address these questions by introducing the concept of reachability to multi-task
048   RL. We define a task to be reachable if it contains states and rewards that also appear in at least one
049
      of the training tasks. Conversely, an unreachable task shares no states and/or rewards with any of the
      training tasks. In the example above, activating the robot in an unusual location is a reachable task,
050
      whereas moving the furniture creates an unreachable one. The key difference between the two is that
051
      reachable tasks have states that can be explicitly encountered and optimised during training, whereas
052

075   2       BACKGROUND
076
077   A Markov decision process (MDP) M is defined by a 6-tuple M = {S, A, R, T, p0 , γ}. In this
078   definition, S denotes a set of states called the state space, A a set of actions called the action space,
079   R : S × A → R the reward function, T : S × A → P(S) the transition function where P(S)
080   denotes the set of probability distributions over states S, p0 : P(S) the starting state distribution
081   and γ ∈ [0, 1) a discount factor. The goal is to find a policy π : S → P(A) that maps states to
082
      probability distributions  P∞ over actions in such a way that maximises the expected cumulative dis-
      counted reward Eπ [ t=0 γ t rt ], also called the return. The expectation Eπ is over the Markov chain
083
      {s0 , a0 , r0 , s1 , a1 , r1 ...} induced by policy π when acting in MDP M (Akshay et al., 2013). An
084
      optimal policy π ∗ achieves the highest possible return. The on-policy distribution ρπ : P(S) of the
085   Markov chain induced by policy π in MDP M defines the proportion of time spent in each state as
086   the number of episodes in M goes to infinity (Sutton & Barto, 2018).
087
088   2.1      C ONTEXTUAL M ARKOV DECISION PROCESS
089
090   A contextual MDP (CMDP, Hallak et al., 2015) is a specific type of MDP where the state space
091   S = S ′ × C can in principle be factored into an underlying state space S ′ and a context space C,
092   which affects rewards and transitions of the MDP. For a state s = (s′ , c) ∈ S, the context c behaves
093   differently than the underlying state s′ in that it is sampled at the start of an episode (as part of the
094   distribution p0 ) and remains fixed until the episode ends. The context c can be thought of as the task
095
      an agent has to solve and from here on out we will refer to the context as the task.
096   The zero-shot policy transfer (ZSPT, Kirk et al., 2023) setting for CMDPs M|C is defined by a
097   distribution over task space P(C) and a set of tasks C train and C test sampled from the same dis-
098   tribution P(C). The goal of the agent is to maximise performance in the testing CMDP M|C test ,
099   defined by the CMDP induced by the testing tasks C test , but the agent is only allowed to train in the
100   training CMDP M|C train . The learned policy is expected to perform zero-shot generalisation for
101
      the testing tasks, without any fine-tuning or adaptation period.
102

106   or dynamics of the environment. As a result, several existing fields of study like multi-goal RL (task

124   3.1    R EACHABILITY IN MULTI - TASK RL
125
126   To argue how exploration can benefit generalisation we introduce the reachability of tasks. To do
127
      so, we first define the reachability of states in a CMDP M|S0train . The set of reachable states
128   Sr (M|S0train ) (abbreviated with Sr from now on) consists of all states sr for which there exists a
129   sequence of actions that give a non-zero probability of ending up in sr when performed in M|S0train .
130   Put differently, a state sr is reachable if there exists a policy whose probability of encountering that
131   state during training is non-zero. In complement to reachable states, we define unreachable states
132   su as states that are not reachable.
133   Using these definitions, we define (un)reachable tasks as tasks that start in a(n) (un)reachable state.
134   We define two instances of the ZSPT problem as follows:
135   Definition 1 (Reachable/Unreachable generalisation). Reachable/Unreachable generalisation
136   refers to an instance of the ZSPT problem where the start states of the testing environments S0test
137   are/are-not part of the set of reachable states during training, i.e. S0test ⊆ Sr or S0test ∩ Sr = ∅.
138
139   This definition has some interesting implications: due to how reachability is defined, in the reach-
140   able generalisation setting all states encountered in the testing CMDP M|S0test are also reachable.
141   Note that the reverse does not have to be true: not all reachable states can necessarily be encoun-
142   tered in M|S0test . Furthermore, we assume in the unreachable generalisation setting that all states
143   encountered in M|S0test are also unreachable.4 Note that this is still considered in-distribution gener-
144   alisation since the starting states for both train and test tasks are sampled from the same distribution.
145
146   3.2    G ENERALISATION TO REACHABLE TASKS
147
148   In the single-task setting, the goal is to maximise performance in the MDP M in which the agent
149   trains. There, it is sufficient to learn an optimal policy in all the states s ∈ S encountered by
150   this policy in M. This is because acting optimally in all the states encountered by the optimal
151   policy in M guarantees maximal return in M. Exploration thus only has to facilitate learning the
                                                       ∗
152   optimal policy on the on-policy distribution ρπ of M. In fact, once the optimal policy has been
153
      found, learning to be optimal anywhere else in M would be a wasted effort that potentially allocates
      approximation power to unimportant areas of the state space.
154
155   Recent work has shown that this logic does not transfer to the ZSPT problem setting (Jiang et al.,
156   2023). In this setting, the goal is not to maximise performance in the training CMDP M|S0train , but
157   rather to maximise performance in the testing CMDP M|S0test . Ideally, the learned policy will be
                                                  ∗
158   optimal over the on-policy distribution ρπ in this testing CMDP.

      3.3   G ENERALISATION TO UNREACHABLE TASKS
208
209                                                                         ∗
      For unreachable generalisation, the states encountered in ρπ of M|S0test are not part of the reachable
210
      space Sr of M|S0train , so it is not obvious on which parts of Sr our agent should train.
211
212   To investigate this, we define an example CMDP in Figure 1a. This CMDP consists of a cross-shaped
213   grid world with additional transitions that directly move the agent between adjacent end-points of
214   the cross (e.g., moving right at the end-point of the northern arm of the cross will move you to the
215   eastern arm). The goal for the agent (circle) is to move to the centre of the cross (the green square).
      There are four training tasks which differ in the starting location of the agent and the colour of the


                                                               4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      background. In Figure 1c the states from the optimal trajectories are placed in the table according to
217   what task they are from (row) and what action is optimal (column).
218
219   To succeed in the single-task setting (consider just one of our four tasks), an agent only needs to
220
      learn to act in the states along the optimal trajectory. Along the optimal trajectories, the colour of
      the background is perfectly correlated with the optimal action, so a policy trained with a standard
221
      RL algorithm will likely overfit to this correlation. As a result, this policy is unlikely to generalise
222
      to new reachable states (empty cells from the same row/task in Figure 1c), and even less likely to
223   new unreachable states with an unseen background colour (a completely new row). We show this
224   empirically in Figure 1b where an agent trained with proximal policy optimisation (PPO, Schulman
225   et al., 2017, red) does not generalise to tasks with a new background colour (see Appendix C.1 for
226   more on this experiment).
227
      Suppose now, we have a policy that has learned over the entire reachable state space (see Figure 1d).
228
      This agent is more likely to learn to ignore the background colour, as it no longer correlates with the
229   optimal action. We see this ability to uncover the true relationships and generalise to new colours
230   when using our novel method PPO+Explore-Go (blue in Figure 1b), which effectively trains on all
231   reachable tasks (Explore-Go is further introduced in Section 4).
232
      More generally, we can view the inclusion of additional reachable states (those in Figure 1d which
233
      are not in Figure 1c) as a form of data augmentation. For example, the additional states from tasks
234
      2, 3 and 4 in the first column in Figure 1d, can be viewed as simple visual transformations of the
235   state from Task 1 that do not affect the underlying meaning. Data augmentation is commonly used
236   to improve generalisation performance in a wide variety of settings and applications (Shorten &
237   Khoshgoftaar, 2019; Feng et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021a; Miao et al., 2023) and is thought to
238   work by reducing overfitting to spurious correlations (Shen et al., 2022), inducing model invariance
239   (Lyle et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020) and/or regularising training (Bishop, 1995; Lin et al., 2022).
240   Considering the strong evidence of data augmentation’s effect on generalisation, we postulate that
241   generalisation to unreachable tasks can be improved by performing data augmentation in the form
242   of training on more reachable tasks.
243   Note that this data augmentation only works if we know the correct targets for the extra samples
244   (columns in Figure 1d). These targets can be optimal actions for policies, or expected returns for
245   (Q-)value functions. If the targets are not correct, the agent might still overfit to a spurious corre-
246   lation, or worse, learn the wrong function. From the model invariance perspective, not only does
247   training with the incorrect targets not learn the desired invariance, but it explicitly trains to not be
248   invariant. This will likely not improve generalisation and could instead drastically deteriorate it.
249   Extended exploration (as in Jiang et al., 2023) chooses trajectories that visit more states, but those
250   can sometimes provide poor target estimates. However, as we argue above, training on even a
251   small number of samples with incorrect targets can be harmful. Instead, the expected return is best
252   estimated using rollouts of the current policy. By treating the additional sample as the starting state
253   of a reachable task, we can rely on the RL algorithm to converge to an optimal policy from this
254   state, resulting in accurate targets. Most algorithms, both on- and even off-policy, collect mainly
255
      on-policy data towards the end of training. This reduces training on exploratory data with incorrect
      targets. The next section introduces our novel method Explore-Go, which achieves significantly
256
      better generalisation with this approach.

259   4    E XPLORE -G O : TRAINING ON MORE REACHABLE TASKS
260
261   As argued in the previous section, training on more reachable tasks is more desirable for generali-
262   sation than extended exploration. We propose a novel method Explore-Go5 which effectively trains
263   from more reachable tasks by artificially increasing the diversity of the starting state distribution. It
264   achieves this by introducing an exploration period at the start of each training episode.
265   Our method is implemented by modifying a fundamental part of most RL algorithms: the collection
266   of rollouts. At the start of every episode, before the agent collects its experiences, Explore-Go
267
268       5
            The name Explore-Go is a variation of the popular exploration approach Go-Explore (Ecoffet et al., 2021).
269   In Go-Explore the agent teleports at the start of each episode to a novel state and then continuous exploration.
      In our approach, the agent first explores until it finds a novel state and then goes and solves the original task.


                                                              5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      first enters a phase in which it explores the environment by following a pure exploration policy.
271   Pure exploration refers to an objective that ignores the rewards rt the agent encounters and instead
272   focuses purely on exploring new parts of the state space. This pure exploration phase will proceed
273   for k steps. Wherever the pure exploration phase ends will be treated by the agent as the starting
274   state of that episode. This means the rest of the episode continues as it would usually, including
275   any exploration that the agent might normally perform. To add some additional stochasticity to the
276   induced starting state distribution, the length of the pure exploration phase is uniformly sampled
277   between 0 and some fixed value K at the start of every episode. See Algorithm 1 in the appendix
278   for an example of a generic rollout collection protocol modified with Explore-Go.
279   The basic version of Explore-Go used in this paper does not use the experience collected during the
280   pure exploration phase in any way. In theory, this experience can be used by off-policy methods.
281   However, in Appendix D.1 we show that adding this experience to the replay buffer in deep Q-
282   learning (DQN, Mnih et al., 2015) does not improve performance. However, this experience can
283   be used to train a separate pure exploration agent in parallel to the main agent. In Appendix E we
284   provide the pseudo-code of this version of Explore-Go when combined with PPO.
285   Note that even though Explore-Go changes the distribution of the training data, it can be com-
286   bined with both off-policy and on-policy reinforcement learning methods. On-policy approaches
287   typically require (primarily) on-policy data for training, distributed along the on-policy state distri-
288   bution ρπθ (M|S0train ) of the current policy πθ . This means they won’t work with arbitrary changes
289   to the distribution of training data. However, Explore-Go only changes the distribution of the start-
290   ing states S0train . So, we can think of Explore-Go as generating on-policy data for a modified MDP
291   that differs only in its starting state distribution. As such, it can be combined with most on-policy
292
      approaches.

      5.2                              R EACHABLE S TATES VS R EACHABLE TASKS
345
346
347   Our method Explore-Go aims to create additional reachable tasks on which the agent trains. We
348
      argue that this, and not simply more continued exploration, will improve generalisation. To inves-
      tigate this, we compare Explore-Go with an exploration approach that is similar to what is used in
349
      Jiang et al. (2023). One of their core algorithmic components is the temporally equalised explo-
350
      ration (TEE) which assigns different fixed exploration coefficients to the parallel workers collecting
351   rollouts.6 This is necessary because, due to function approximation, the model may lose knowledge
352   acquired through exploration if it does not keep exploring throughout training.
353
354
      In the following experiment, we analyse the DQN agent from the previous section, which collects
      rollouts with 10 parallel workers. For the TEE agent, we assign each of the workers a different,
355                                                                                                   i
      fixed value of ϵ (used in ϵ-greedy exploration). We assign ϵ according to the relation ϵi = ( N −1 )α ,
356
      where ϵi is the exploration coefficient for worker i, N is the total number of workers (N = 10 in our
357
      case) and α is a coefficient determining a bias towards more exploration (α < 1) or less exploration
358   (α > 1).
359
360   We compare Explore-Go with a baseline DQN agent using TEE with coefficient α = 0.1. This was
361
      decided by evaluating multiple coefficients α and finding that DQN-TEE with coefficient α = 0.1
      does the most exploration, and thus acts as an upper bound on the performance achievable with
362
      this approach. (see Appendix D.2 for more results with different values of α). Figure 3 shows that
363
      Explore-Go achieves significantly higher testing performance for both the reachable and unreachable

      Next, we discuss related work on exploration in CMDPs. Zisselman et al. (2023) leverage explo-

491
      increasing test time performance. Our work differs in that we leverage exploration during training

      in order to increase the number of states from which the agent can confidently solve the test tasks.
493   More closely related is work by Jiang et al. (2023), Zhu et al. (2020) and Suau et al. (2024). Jiang
494   et al. (2023) do not make a distinction between reachable and unreachable generalisation and provide
495   intuition which we argue mainly applies to reachable generalisation (see Appendix A.2). Moreover,
496   their novel approach only works for off-policy algorithms, whereas ours can be applied to both off-
497   policy and on-policy methods. Zhu et al. (2020) learn a reset controller that increases the diversity of
498   the agent’s start states. However, they only argue (and empirically show) that this benefits reachable
499   generalisation. Suau et al. (2024) introduce the notion of policy confounding in out-of-trajectory
500   generalisation. The issue of policy confounding is complementary to our intuition for unreachable
501
      generalisation. However, it is unclear how out-of-trajectory generalisation equates to reachable or
      unreachable generalisation. Moreover, they do not propose a novel, scalable approach to solve the
502
      issue.

505   7   C ONCLUSION
506
507   Recent work shows that more thorough and prolonged exploration can improve generalisation to
508   unseen tasks in multi-task RL. This effect was explained as a result of encountering the same states
509   in testing as were seen during the additional exploration in training. To understand this phenomenon
510   better, we define the notion of reachability of states and tasks. This novel perspective makes it
511   clear the above explanation only applies to reachable tasks, whereas unreachable tasks only benefit
512   indirectly from the data augmentation that comes with training on more reachable tasks. It also
513
      implies that continuous exploration (as in TEE) is not optimal for multi-task generalisation, as the
      exploratory episodes find more reachable states, but do not learn the task starting from there.
514
515   Instead, we define the novel method Explore-Go, which begins each episode with a pure exploration
516   phase, before standard learning is resumed. This results in training on more reachable tasks, and thus
517   improves generalisation even to unreachable tasks by data augmentation. We show this empirically
518   in the Four Rooms environment: here TEE explores more states, keeps a more diverse replay buffer,
519
      and learns a policy that is optimal in more reachable states than Explore-Go. However, Explore-Go
      generalises better to both reachable and unreachable test tasks. This suggests that generalisation is
520
      not about how much you explore or how many of the reachable states you are optimal in, but rather
521
      when you explore and how many reachable tasks you can solve optimally.
522
523   As an added benefit, Explore-Go only requires a simple modification to the sampling procedure,
524   which can be applied easily to most RL algorithms, both on-policy and off-policy. We demonstrate
525
      that the method increases multi-task generalisation in the Four Rooms environment with SAC, DQN
      and PPO. We also show that Explore-Go scales up to more complex tasks from the DeepMind Con-
526
      trol Suite, both on the underlying state and on images of the task. We hope to provide practitioners
527
      with a simple modification that can improve the generalisation of their agents significantly.

      A     R ELATED WORK
865
866   A.1     E XTENDED RELATED WORK
867
868   A.1.1    G ENERALISATION IN CMDP S
869
870
      The contextual MDP framework is a very general framework that encompasses many fields in
      RL that study zero-shot generalisation. For example, the sim-to-real setting often encountered in
871
      robotics is a special case of the ZSPT setting for CMDPs (Kirk et al., 2023). An approach used
872
      to improve generalisation in the sim-to-real setting is domain randomisation (Tobin et al., 2017;
873   Sadeghi & Levine, 2017; Peng et al., 2018), where the task distribution during training is explic-
874   itly increased in order to increase the probability of encompassing the testing tasks in the training
875   distribution. This differs from our work in that we don’t explicitly generate more (unreachable)
876   tasks. However, our work could be viewed as implicitly generating more reachable tasks through
877   increased exploration. Another approach that increases the task distribution is data augmentation
878   (Raileanu et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021). These approaches work by applying a
879   set of given transformations to the states with the prior knowledge that these transformations leave
880   the output (policy or value function) invariant. In this paper, we argue that our approach implicitly
881
      induces a form of invariant data augmentation on the states. However, this differs from the other
      work cited here in that we don’t explicitly apply transformations to our states, nor do we require
882
      prior knowledge on which transformations leave the policy invariant.
883
884   So far we have mentioned some approaches that increase the number and variability of the training
885   tasks. Other approaches instead try to explicitly bridge the gap between the training and testing tasks.
886   For example, some use inductive biases to encourage learning generalisable functions (Zambaldi
887
      et al., 2018; 2019; Kansky et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2020; Tang & Ha, 2021).
      Others use regularisation techniques from supervised learning to boost generalisation performance
888
      (Cobbe et al., 2019; Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015; Igl et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2020; Eysenbach et al.,
889
      2021). We mention only a selection of approaches here, for a more comprehensive overview we
890   refer to the survey by Kirk et al. (2023).
891
892   All the approaches above use techniques that are not necessarily specific to RL (representation learn-
893
      ing, regularisation, etc.). In this work, we instead explore how exploration in RL can be used to
      improve generalisation.
894
895
      A.1.2    E XPLORATION IN CMDP S
896
897   There have been numerous methods of exploration designed specifically for or that have shown
898   promising performance on CMDPs. Some approaches train additional adversarial agents to help
899   with exploration (Flet-Berliac et al., 2021; Campero et al., 2021; Fickinger et al., 2021). Others try
900   to exploit actions that significantly impact the environment (Seurin et al., 2021; Parisi et al., 2021)
901   or that cause a significant change in some metric (Raileanu & Rocktäschel, 2020; Zhang et al.,
902   2021c;b; Ramesh et al., 2022). More recently, some approaches have been developed that try to
903
      generalise episodic state visitation counts to continuous spaces (Jo et al., 2022; Henaff et al., 2022)
      and several studies have shown the importance of this for exploration in CMDPs (Wang et al., 2023;
904
      Henaff et al., 2023). All these methods focus on trading off exploration and exploitation to achieve
905
      maximal performance in the training tasks as fast and efficiently as possible. However, in this paper,
906   we examine the exploration-exploitation trade-off to maximise generalisation performance in testing
907   tasks.
908
909
      In Zisselman et al. (2023), the authors leverage exploration at test time to move the agent towards
      states where it can confidently solve the task, thereby increasing test time performance. Our work
910
      differs in that we leverage exploration during training time to increase the number of states from
911
      which the agent can confidently solve the test tasks. Closest to our work is Jiang et al. (2023),
912   Zhu et al. (2020) and Suau et al. (2024). Jiang et al. (2023) don’t make a distinction between
913   reachable and unreachable generalisation and provide intuition which we argue mainly applies to
914   reachable generalisation (see Appendix A.2). Moreover, their novel approach only works for off-
915   policy algorithms, whereas ours could be applied to both off-policy and on-policy methods. In
916   Zhu et al. (2020), the authors learn a reset controller that increases the diversity of the agent’s start
917   states. However, they only argue (and empirically show) that this benefits reachable generalisation.
      The concurrent work in Suau et al. (2024) introduces the notion of policy confounding in out-of-


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      trajectory generalisation. The issue of policy confounding is complementary to our intuition for
919   unreachable generalisation. However, it is unclear how out-of-trajectory generalisation equates to
920   reachable or unreachable generalisation. Moreover, they do not propose a novel, scalable approach
921   to solve the issue.
922
923
924   A.2    D ISCUSSION ON RELATED WORK
925
926   Jiang et al. (2023) argue that generalisation in RL extends beyond representation learning. They do
927   so with an example in a tabular grid-world environment. In the environment they describe the agent
928   during training always starts in the top left corner of the grid, and the goal is always in the top right
929
      corner. During testing the agent starts in a different position in the grid-world (in their example, the
      lower left corner). This is according to our definition an example of a reachable task. They then
930
      argue (in the way we described in Section 3.2) that more exploration can improve generalisation to
931
      these tasks.
932
933   They extend their intuition to non-tabular CMDPs by arguing that in certain cases two states that
934   are unreachable from each other, can nonetheless inside a neural network map to similar represen-
935
      tations. As a result, even though a state in the input space is unreachable, it can be mapped to
      something reachable in the latent representational space and therefore the reachable generalisation
936
      arguments apply again. For this reason, the generalisation benefits from more exploration can go
937
      beyond representation learning.
938
939   Relating it to the illustrative example we provide in Figure 1, we argue this intuition considers the
940   generalisation benefits one might obtain from learning to act optimally in more abstracted states.
941
      For example, in Jiang et al. (2023)’s grid-world the lower states would have normally unseen values,
      which is represented by increasing the number of columns on which we train in Figure 1c and 1d.
942
      However, in Section 3.2 we argue that specifically unreachable generalisation can benefit as well
943
      from training on more states belonging to the same abstracted states (represented by increasing the
944   number of rows on which we train in Figure 1c and 1d). Training on more of these states could
945   encourage the agent to learn representations that map different unreachable states to the same latent
946   representation (or equivalently, abstracted states). As such, we argue the generalisation benefits from
947   more exploration can in part be attributed to an implicit form of representation learning.

950   B     G ENERALISATION TO REACHABLE TASKS
951
952
      In this section, we elaborate on why a policy that is optimal in all reachable states, is guaranteed to
953
      perform well when testing on reachable tasks. As a first step, we point out a corollary of definition
954
      1 about reachable states:
955
956   Corollary 0.1. Any state s′ that is reachable from a state s ∈ Sr (M|S0train ) in the reachable set,
957   has to be itself in the reachable set: s′ ∈ Sr (M|S0train ).
958
959   Why this is the case is clear to see with the definition of reachability in terms of sequences of actions:
960   concatenate the sequence of actions with a non-zero probability of ending up in s with the sequence
961   of actions with a non-zero probability of ending up in s′ when starting from s. This will result in a
962   sequence of actions with a non-zero probability of ending up in s′ . In short, this corollary states that
963   you cannot leave the reachable set Sr (M|S0train ) through interaction with the environment.
964   From this logically follows the following corollary:
965
966   Corollary 0.2. An optimal policy π that achieves maximal return from any state in the reachable
967   state space Sr (M|S0train ), will have optimal performance in the reachable generalisation setting.
968
969   Recall that performance in a ZSPT problem is defined as the performance in the testing MDP
970   M|S0test , which in the case of reachable generalisation, has a state space that consists only of reach-
971   able states (due to Corollary 0.1). It follows naturally that a policy that is optimal on the entire
      reachable state space Sr (M|S0train ) also has to be optimal in M|S0test .

1048   C.2   F OUR ROOMS
1049
1050   In all of our Four Rooms experiments, we will train on 40 different training tasks and test on either
1051   a reachable or unreachable task set of size 120. The 40 training tasks differ in the agent location,
1052   agent direction, goal location and the location of the doorways (see Figure 7 for some example tasks
1053   in Four Rooms).
1054   In this environment, reachability is regulated through variations in the goal location and location of
1055   the doorways. If two states share their doorways and goal location, then they are both reachable
1056   from one another. Conversely, if two states differ in either the doorways or goal location, they are
1057   unreachable. The reachable task set is constructed by taking every training task and changing only
1058   the agent location and agent direction (keeping the location of the doorways and goal location the
1059   same). This is repeated four times to generate a total number of reachable tasks of 4×40 = 120. For
1060   the unreachable task set, we take 40 different configurations of the doorways that all differ from the
1061
       ones in the training task. For each of those 40 different doorway configurations, we generate four
       new goal locations, agent locations and agent directions. This also generates a total of 4 × 40 = 120
1062
       unreachable tasks.

1066   The Four Rooms grid world used in our experiments is adapted from the Minigrid benchmark
1067   (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023) and differs in certain ways from the default Minigrid configura-
1068   tion. For one, the action space is reduced from the default seven actions (turn left, turn right, move

       When comparing Explore-Go, DQN and TEE we introduce four new metrics. The first measures
1135   the fraction of state-action space that is explored (Figure 4a). This is calculated by enumerating all
1136   possible state-actions in the reachable state space and keeping track of which ones are encountered
1137   at some point during training. This measures how effective the exploration approach is (a higher
1138   fraction means the agent explored more states). The second and third metrics measure the diversity
1139   present in the replay buffer throughout training (Figures 4b and 4c). They do so, again, by enumer-
1140   ating all possible state-actions (Figure 4b) or states (Figure 4c) in the reachable space and checking
1141   which ones are present in the buffer at that time. The last metric measures how optimal the agent
1142   is over the entire reachable space (Figure 4d). It measures this by enumerating all possible states in
1143
       the reachable space and checking for which ones the agent chooses an action that is optimal (there
       can be multiple).
