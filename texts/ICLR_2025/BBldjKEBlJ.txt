009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding complex animal behaviors hinges on deciphering the neural activity
012            patterns within brain circuits, making the ability to forecast neural activity cru-
013            cial for developing predictive models of brain dynamics. This capability holds
014            immense value for neuroscience, particularly in applications such as real-time
015            optogenetic interventions. While traditional encoding and decoding methods have
016
               been used to map external variables to neural activity and vice versa, they focus
               on interpreting past data. In contrast, neural forecasting aims to predict future
017
               neural activity, presenting a unique and challenging task due to the spatiotemporal
018
               sparsity and complex dependencies of neural signals. Existing transformer-based
019            forecasting methods, while effective in many domains, struggle to capture the
020            distinctiveness of neural signals characterized by spatiotemporal sparsity and in-
021            tricate dependencies. To address this challenge, we here introduce QuantFormer,
022            a transformer-based model specifically designed for forecasting neural activity
023            from two-photon calcium imaging data. Unlike conventional regression-based
024            approaches, QuantFormer reframes the forecasting task as a classification problem
025            via dynamic signal quantization, enabling more effective learning of sparse neural
026            activation patterns. Additionally, QuantFormer tackles the challenge of analyzing
027
               multivariate signals from an arbitrary number of neurons by incorporating neuron-
               specific tokens, allowing scalability across diverse neuronal populations.
028
               Trained with unsupervised quantization on the Allen dataset, QuantFormer sets a
029
               new benchmark in forecasting mouse visual cortex activity. It demonstrates robust
030            performance and generalization across various stimuli and individuals, paving the
031            way for a foundational model in neural signal prediction.
032            Source code available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/iclr2025_quantformer-

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Complex animal behavior is believed to stem from the electrical activity of coordinated ensembles
039
      of neurons within specific brain circuits (Yuste, 2015; Yuste et al., 2024). For example, during
      sensory perception (Ohki et al., 2005; 2006) and motor coordination (Harpaz et al., 2014; Omlor
040
      et al., 2019; Santos et al., 2015), correlated patterns of electrical activity in groups of neurons are
041
      observed in the primary sensory and motor cortices (Chen et al., 2024; Inagaki et al., 2022; Panzeri
042   et al., 2022). These activity patterns are structured both spatially and temporally, meaning different
043   subsets of neurons are activated at distinct times. The patterns are further distinguished based on the
044   sensory stimuli or motor outputs they represent (Kondapavulur et al., 2022; Miller et al., 2014; Rule
045   & O’Leary, 2022). Importantly, the activity at any given moment is influenced by the recent history
046   of the neuronal circuit (Boly et al., 2007; Leinweber et al., 2017; Luczak et al., 2022).
047
      Neuronal activity patterns can be recorded in the intact brain using various methods, including
048   electrophysiological recordings (Jun et al., 2017; Steinmetz et al., 2019) and optical techniques such
049   as two-photon microscopy (Denk et al., 1990; Helmchen & Denk, 2005) combined with fluorescent
050   activity reporters (Chen et al., 2013; Dana et al., 2019). These methods enable high-resolution, in
051   vivo imaging of brain cell activity, allowing researchers to observe coordinated neuronal responses
052   during sensory stimulation and motor execution. For instance, studies have shown specific neuronal
053   ensembles encoding stimulus features and behavior in the sensory cortex (Buetfering et al., 2022;
      Carrillo-Reid et al., 2019), and in the motor cortex during motor programs (Serradj et al., 2023).

054
      A key challenge in neuroscience is developing predictive models that can forecast neuronal activity
055   in a given brain network based on past observations. This task holds significant scientific value,
056   particularly for online closed-loop experiments, such as optogenetics, where real-time adjustments to
057   experimental conditions can enhance intervention effectiveness. Our approach to forecasting neural
058   activity differs fundamentally from traditional encoding and decoding methods. Decoding methods,
059   such as those detailed in Azabou et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2023); Antoniades et al. (2023), focus
060   on mapping internal neural variables (e.g., neural activity) to external variables, such as behavior
061   occurring simultaneously with the neural response or the stimulus that elicited it. On the other hand,
062   encoding methods Turishcheva et al. (2024a;b); Li et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023a); Sinz et al., aim
063
      to map external variables to internal neural activity. In contrast, our goal is to model future neural
      activity without relying on synchronous data, emphasizing the unique challenge of forecasting rather
064
      than decoding past stimuli/behaviors or encoding past activity.
065
066   The motivation for predicting neuronal activity stems from its demonstrated effectiveness in investi-
067   gating the sensorimotor cortex of humans and nonhuman primates (Truccolo et al., 2010). However,
068   the application of neural activity forecasting to guide online optogenetic manipulation represents
069
      a novel and original advancement in this field. A key element in achieving this goal is leveraging
      data that is accessible in real-time scenarios. Traditional methods often employ spiking activity
070
      data (Schrimpf et al., 2018; Pei et al., 2021; Turishcheva et al., 2024a), which presents challenges
071
      due to limited accuracy of real-time spike inference. We thus shift the focus on raw fluorescence
072   traces that provide a direct measure of neuronal activity, improving the precision of predictions and
073   enabling effective manipulation in real-time experimental settings.
074
075
      In this paper, we propose QuantFormer, a transformer-based model for two-photon calcium imaging
      forecasting using latent space vector quantization. Our approach reframes the forecasting problem as
076
      a classification task through vector quantization, enabling the learning of sparse activation spikes.
077
      Posing a regression problem as a classification task, even when the data is implicitly continuous,
078   facilitates sparse coding (as already demonstrated in pixel (Van Den Oord et al., 2016b) and audio
079   (Van Den Oord et al., 2016a) spaces), which is crucial given the relative rarity of neuronal activations.
080
081
      QuantFormer first learns, in a masked auto-encoding fashion (Devlin et al., 2018; He et al., 2022),
      to compress input neural signals into a sequence of quantized codes, thus allowing self-supervised
082
      training by predicting masked items in the sequence. This strategy facilitates the pre-training of the
083
      model for downstream tasks by quantization learning while providing a natural way to approach
084   forecasting as the prediction of masked future codes.
085
086   Scalability to an arbitrary number of neurons is achieved by learning and prepending a set of neuron-
087
      specific tokens to the input. These tokens empower the model to process data from all available
      neurons without the need to create one model for each neuron or to increase its complexity through
088
      multivariate analysis, thus facilitating the effective learning of neural dynamics.
089
090   QuantFormer was extensively evaluated on the publicly available Allen dataset (de Vries et al., 2020),
091   the only existing benchmark - to our knowledge - providing raw fluorescence traces, and significantly
092   surpassed other state-of-the-art forecasting methods in predicting both short- and long-term neural
093
      activity. Ablation studies also confirm the design choices underlying QuantFormer.
094   In summary, the key contributions of this work include:
095
096   • Forecasting neural responses for optogenetic manipulation: We propose a novel approach
097     that leverages neural activity predictions to guide optogenetic interventions, a completely original
098     concept in the literature.
099   • Reframing forecasting as a classification problem: By employing vector quantization, Quant-
100     Former learns a discrete representation of neural signals, enabling the use of classification tech-
101     niques to predict sparse neuronal activations effectively.
102
103
      • Handling arbitrary neural populations: Our model uses neuron-specific tokens to facilitate the
        analysis of multivariate signals from any number of neurons, ensuring scalability and generalization
104
        across individuals and sessions.
105
106   • Establishing a foundation model for mouse visual cortex: Leveraging unsupervised learning on
107     the Allen dataset, QuantFormer demonstrates robust forecasting across different stimuli, individuals,
        and experimental conditions, laying the groundwork for future research in neural signal prediction.

130   2    R ELATED WORK
131
132
      This paper introduces QuantFormer, a transformer-based method trained using self-supervision for
133   neural forecasting on two-photon calcium imaging data.
134
135
136
      In deep learning for two-photon calcium imaging, existing methods have predominantly focused
      on neuron segmentation (Soltanian-Zadeh et al., 2019; Sità et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2021; Xu et al.,
137
      2023b), as well as encoding and decoding tasks.
138
      In particular, decoding methods map neural activity (internal variables) to external outcomes like
139   behavior Azabou et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2023); Antoniades et al. (2023). These methods, which
140   use neural activity as input, focus on decoding synchronous patterns, such as behaviors occurring
141   alongside neural responses. However, their goal is not to predict future neural dynamics but to link
142   current neural signals to external events.
143   Encoding methods do the opposite, mapping external variables (e.g., stimuli) to neural activity.
144   Approaches such as Turishcheva et al. (2024a;b); Li et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023a); Sinz et al.
145   predict neural responses based on stimuli, but often rely on trial-averaged data and are not designed
146   to forecast future neural activity on a single-trial basis without the use of synchronous behaviour
147
      variables, which are not accessible in online settings.
      In contrast, the task we present in this work, neural forecasting, aims to predict future neural activity
148
      triggered by external inputs (e.g., visual stimuli) based on the neuron states, i.e., on its past neural data.
149
      This is essential for online, closed-loop experiments where forecasting future activity is required to
150   manipulate neurons in real time. The difference between encoding, decoding and neural forecasting
151   tasks is clarified in Fig. 1.
152
153
      In terms of model architecture, QuantFormer is aligned with the recent trend in modeling univariate
      and multivariate single-dimensional time-series signals through transformers. LogTrans (Li et al.,
154
      2019) pioneered the use of transformers in univariate forecasting, utilizing causal convolutions
155
      to enhance attention locality. Informer (Zhou et al., 2021) improves efficiency in long-sequence
156   forecasting with sparse attention. PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) handles multichannel data by processing
157   univariate signals in patches, limiting its ability to capture inter-variable correlations. Crossformer
158   (Zhang & Yan, 2022) applies attention across both time and variable dimensions to exploit multivariate
159   dependencies, with cross-window self-attention capturing long-range relationships. Pyraformer (Liu
160   et al., 2021a) introduces pyramidal attention to represent multiresolution features. FEDformer (Zhou
161   et al., 2022) replaces self-attention with Fourier decomposition and wavelet transforms for handling
      seasonal data patterns.

162
      QuantFormer employs transformers where multivariate signals are handled through prepending
163   tokens that encode specific neurons as well as tokens for stimulus encoding. It employs a pre-training
164   procedure based on reconstructing masked input, in an autoencoder configuration, thus leveraging
165   the extensive volumes of unlabeled neural signals from two-photon calcium imaging data in a
166   self-supervised learning setting. This strategy has already demonstrated remarkable results in various
167   research areas, including language modeling (Devlin et al., 2018), audio (Huang et al., 2022), and
168   vision (He et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2022). Additionally, during pre-training, we also learn to quantize
169   in a manner similar to image generation (Esser et al., 2021). However, this quantization approach has
170   not been applied to pose forecasting as a code classification task in the neural signal data domain.
171
      Though not directly applied to two-photon calcium imaging, methodologies like BrainLM
      (Ortega Caro et al., 2023), based on the vanilla transformer, and SwiFT (Kim et al., 2023), which
172
      leverages Swin transformers (Liu et al., 2021b) trained on fMRI data, are more closely aligned with
173
      our approach, as they perform pre-training through self-learning. Both models are then tuned on
174   downstream tasks, though only BrainLM includes brain state forecasting.
175
176
177
      Finally, regarding the data, all the encoding and decoding methods discussed above rely on spiking
      data (as illustrated in Fig. 1). While spiking data reflects a more processed stage of neural signals, its
178
      practical application in online settings is limited due to the challenges of real-time spike inference,
179
      which often misses a significant portion of spikes (Huang et al., 2021). Given these limitations, we
180   opted for raw fluorescence traces, which can be captured in real-time and circumvent the pitfalls
181   of spike inference, making them more suitable for online neural forecasting. This decision inher-
182   ently guided us towards the Allen Visual Coding dataset (de Vries et al., 2020), which provides a
183   comprehensive large-scale benchmark for the mouse visual cortex, encompassing both raw fluores-
184   cence traces (unlike other existing benchmarks such as BrainScore (Schrimpf et al., 2018), Neural
185   Latents’21 (Pei et al., 2021), and SENSORIUM (Wang et al., 2023)) and spiking activity.

190   3.1   OVERVIEW
191
192   Our approach consists of two distinct training phases: pre-training through masked auto-encoding
193   and downstream training addressing neural activity classification and forecasting. In the pre-training
194   phase, we train a vector-quantized auto-encoder to reconstruct a sequence of non-overlapping neuronal
195   signal patches - following similar procedures from computer vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; He
196   et al., 2022) - a fraction of which is replaced with a [MASK] token. The objective of this task is
197   twofold: first, it encourages the model to learn an expressive and reusable feature representation of
198   neuronal signal for downstream tasks; second, it lays the foundation for its use as a forecasting tool,
199
      by using [MASK] tokens as placeholders for future signal.
200   In the downstream phase, we employ the pre-trained encoder to predict neuron activations in response
201   to visual stimuli. As mentioned above, this prediction task can be framed as a time series forecasting
202   task, with the objective of predicting the temporal development of a neuronal response. Alternatively,
203   it can be seen as a classification problem, where an active (i.e., neuron activation) or inactive (i.e.,
204   normal neuron activity) label is associated to the neural signal recorded after stimulus visualization.
205
206
      3.2   P ROBLEM FORMULATION
207
208   Let S = {s1 , . . . , sS } be the set of stimuli to which subjects can be exposed, and let N =
209   {n1 , . . . , nN } be the set of neurons under analysis. We define an observation o = (xb , xf , n, s, a) to
210   be the set of signals associated to neuron n ∈ N when presenting stimulus s ∈ S: xb ∈ RLb is the
211   baseline activity, i.e., the neuronal activity before the stimulus onset, while xf ∈ RLf is the response
212   activity, i.e., the neuronal activity after the stimulus onset; a ∈ {0, 1} denotes whether neuron n is
213   active after the presentation of stimulus s, and Lb and Lf denote the temporal length of the different
214   portions in the recorded signals, for a given sample rate r. According to Chen et al. (2013), a neuron
215   is marked as active (a = 1) if the response window has an average gain of 10% over the average
      baseline luminescence.

244   3.3   P RE - TRAINING STAGE
245
246   We pose our self-supervised pre-training as a masked auto-encoding task, with the objective of
247   learning a general representation that models neuronal activity patterns with a view towards response
248   forecasting. In order to make the representation as general as possible (as downstream training
249
      will be responsible for specialization), in this stage we aim to reconstruct the entire signal for
      an observation o, i.e., the concatenation of xb and xf , while ignoring both the neuron identity
250
      and the presented stimulus. Formally, let p(x) be the distribution of concatenated baseline and
251
      response neuronal signals, with x ∈ RLb +Lf , and let p(m|x) be a masking function that removes
252   a random portion from x. We want to learn a latent representation z, from which an estimate of
253   the unmasked signal x can be reconstructed as p(x|z). Following common practices, we model
254   p(z|m) and p(x|z) as an encoder-decoder network sharing the latent representation. Additionally,
255   we introduce a quantization layer (Huh et al., 2023) on the latent representation, in order to enforce
256   that the latent representations are mapped to a predefined set of embeddings. While not strictly
257   necessary for pre-training, quantization yields a twofold usefulness for our purposes. First, it enables
258   a categorical representation of neuronal signal components, allowing downstream tasks to pose
259   forecasting as a classification problem rather than a regression one, which has been shown to be easier
260
      to optimize (Van Den Oord et al., 2016a). Second, quantization addresses the sparsity of neuronal
      activations as it forces the model to focus on a limited number of prototypes, encouraging reuse of
261
      codes corresponding to common patterns and potentially reducing the impact of over-represented
262
      components. We thus define a set of embeddings E = {e1 , . . . , eK }, with ei ∈ Rd and K being the
263   codebook dimension. In this setting, we distinguish between the continuous distribution p(ze |m)
264   produced by the encoder network and the categorical distribution p(zq |m) obtained after quantization,
265   defined as:

      3.4     D OWNSTREAM TASKS
298
299   After pre-training the encoder-decoder network, we employ it for adaptation to specific downstream
300   tasks, namely, neuron activation prediction and response forecasting.
301
302   3.4.1    N EURONAL ACTIVATION PREDICTION
303
      Given an observation o = (xb , xs , n, s, a), our goal is to predict whether the target neuron responds
304
      to the stimulus or not, by modeling p(a|xb , n, s). We adapt the pre-trained encoder to this task, with
305
      some modifications designed to inject neuron-specific and stimulus-specific knowledge, which was
306   ignored during the self-supervised training. We first introduce a learnable [CLS] token (Devlin
307   et al., 2018; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), whose representation at the output of the encoder is fed
308   to a linear binary classifier, marking the neuron as active or inactive. We then define a set of
309   stimulus-specific learnable tokens {[STIM]1 , . . . , [STIM]S }, one for each possible stimulus, and
310   a set of neuron-specific learnable tokens {[NEURON]1 , . . . , [NEURON]N }, one for each neuron
311   under analysis; all [STIM]i and [NEURON]j tokens are d-dimensional vectors, i.e., with the same
312   dimension as the encoder input tokens. Given the observation o = (xb , xs , n, s, a), we segment
313   and project the baseline signal xb into tokens {t1 , . . . , tP }, and then feed the encoder network with
314
      {[CLS], [NEURON]n , [STIM]s , t1 , . . . , tP }.
315   Feeding neuron and stimulus identifiers to the encoder is a key aspect of the approach: since our
316   backbone does not inherently handle multivariate data, we compensate for this lack by providing
317   learnable neuron identifiers, making the model able to learn distinct activation patterns for each
318   neuron; similarly, stimuli identifiers provide a means for the model to discover the specific stimuli a
319   neuron responds to. Also, we only feed the baseline signal xb to the encoder, since the response xf
320
      likely contains neuron activation information, which would defeat the purpose of the classifier.
321   The transformer-based architecture also allows us to tackle this task in two different training settings:
322   prompt-tuning and fine-tuning. With prompt-tuning, only soft prompts (i.e., [CLS], all [STIM]i
323   and all [NEURON]j ) can be optimized, while the encoder remains frozen. With fine-tuning, all
      encoder parameters can be updates. In this task, neither the quantizer nor the decoder are used.

      3.4.2    N EURONAL RESPONSE FORECASTING
325
326   The objective of this task is to model p(xf |xb , n, s), in order to predict the response time series
327   xf from the baseline signal xb , preceding the stimulus onset. A possible approach to this problem
328   consists in using the pre-trained encoder-decoder network, by masking all input tokens related to
329   the portion of signals to be predicted, and read the forecast signal as the encoder output. However,
330
      as mentioned above, the pre-trained model lacks neuron/stimulus specialization, which is needed
      to handle different neuronal activity dynamics. Moreover, while the pre-trained encoder is trained
331
      to capture the underlying patterns of the input data for filling in missing information, this does not
332
      necessarily imply the capability to directly predict future values of a time series. To address these
333   issues, we act in two ways: similarly to the previous task, [STIM]i and [NEURON]j tokens are
334   added to the beginning of the sequence, to provide the model with specific information; second,
335   rather than fine-tuning the entire model, we append a classification network after the encoder for
336   predicting the codebook indices corresponding to masked tokens only. This approach, besides
337   simplifying the architecture, can be specifically tailored to understand the dynamics of neuronal
338   activity post-stimulus.

      4.1     DATASET
362
363   The Allen Brain Observatory Dataset comprises over 1,300 two-photon calcium imaging experiments,
364   organized into more than 400 containers. Each container, representing all the experimental data from
365   a single mouse, consists of three 90-minute sessions foreseeing the administration of multiple stimuli.
366   We selected 11 containers (i.e., mice) previously used in Sità et al. (2022). Each container has at least
367   three complete sessions available. The original dataset includes various types of stimuli: drifting
368   gratings, static gratings, natural scenes, natural movies, locally sparse noise and spontaneous activity
369
      (Observatory, 2017). However, we excluded natural movies, as isolating individual neuron responses
      is challenging, and spontaneous activity, as it is not stimulus-related. Within each session, every
370
      stimulus type is presented across three distinct sub-sessions. Each stimulus may be shown once or
371
      multiple times. The presentation of a single stimulus, along with its corresponding neural response,
372   is referred to as a trial. In total, we used 236,808 multivariate signals representing neuron responses
373   from the selected mice (additional details in Table A-1).
374   Moreover, in our classification downstream task, we examine whether there is a response to a given
375   stimulus within a defined response window. Across all mice and their neurons, we identify a total
376   of 2,287,735 positive (active) responses, while normal activity (non-responsive, inactive) samples
377   amount to approximately 40 million.
      To mitigate temporal correlations and prevent overlap between training and test sets, we partition

      the data on a per-subsession basis. Specifically, we allocate two subsessions for training and one for
379   testing, with each subsession separated by 10-15 minutes. Furthermore, we ensure that training and
380   testing data are distinctly separated by exposing the mouse to other stimuli during the interim period,
381   thus eliminating potential temporal correlations between signals.
382
383   4.2   T RAINING PROCEDURE AND METRICS
384
385   QuantFormer is pre-trained in self-supervision as a masked auto-encoding task through quantization,
386   using data from all subjects. The full model consists of 6 layers and 8 attention heads for both the
387   encoder and the decoder, with a hidden size d of 128 and a mask ratio Pm of 0.2. We train with
388
      Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) for 50 epochs, a learning rate of 10−4 and a batch size of 32. In both
      downstream tasks, we fine-tune the encoder for 100 epochs with a learning rate of 10−3 . The length
389
      of baseline and response signals in each observation is respectively 3 seconds and 2 seconds at sample
390
      rate r = 30, resulting in padded sequence lengths Lb = 96 and Lf = 64. The number of quantized
391   codes K is set to 32. As we diverge from these values we note that performance decreases in both
392   tasks (see Tables A-2 and A-3 in the Appendix). This confirms the sparsity of crucial information in
393   brain signals, which can be encoded with as few as 32 indices (the performance decrease was less
394   sensitive to the dimensionality d).
395   Downstream tasks were conducted separately for each subject and stimulus category, with results
396   reported as mean and standard deviation across all runs. We also evaluate generalization using the
397   leave-one-category-out strategy, excluding specific stimulus categories or mice from pre-training and
398   using the excluded data for downstream training. As metrics, we use balanced accuracy, precision,
399
      recall, and F1 for classification, and MSE, SMAPE, Pearson correlation and structural similarity
      index (SSIM) for forecasting.
400
      The selected competitors for our approach, based on code availability and adaptability to the tasks,
401
      are Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), Cross-Former (Zhang & Yan, 2022),
402   and BrainLM (Ortega Caro et al., 2023). We use BrainLM pre-trained on large fMRI data (due
403   to observed similarities between mice and humans (Eppig et al., 2015)), fine-tuned on our data
404   (BrainLMf t ), and trained from scratch. Additionally, we include a simple LSTM-based baseline,
405   that we empirically found to mostly predict the signal’s mean. All experiments are conducted on a
406   workstation with an 8-core CPU, 64GB RAM, and an NVIDIA A6000 GPU (48GB VRAM).

524   5    C ONCLUSION
525
526   We presented QuantFormer, a transformer-based model using latent space vector quantization to
527
      capture sparse neural activity patterns in two-photon calcium imaging. By framing the regression
      problem as classification and leveraging unsupervised vector quantization, QuantFormer outperforms
528
      state-of-the-art methods in response classification and forecasting. Trained and tested on a subset of
529
      the Allen dataset, it excels in learning sparse activation spikes and capturing long-term dependencies,
530   making it a versatile and robust tool for understanding neural dynamics.
531   A possible limitation of QuantFormer includes the lack of an inhibition mechanism may lead to
532   sequences of high activation responses, contrary to the typical single activation observed in biological
533   neurons. As future work, QuantFormer will be trained on the entire Allen dataset, as well as adapted
534   to spiking neural data (in order to use other existing benchmarks), to enhance generalization capability
535   for creating a foundation model for the mouse visual cortex.

      The Allen Brain Data Observatory is a resource from the Allen Institute for Brain Science that
815   provides a comprehensive collection of data on the mouse visual cortex. This resource is designed to
816   facilitate research and understanding of brain function, particularly in the context of how sensory
817   information is processed. It contains various types of data regarding the mouse visual cortex ranging
818   from cell connectivity to spontaneous neuronal activity and to stimulus-response data.
819
      For the experiments conducted in this work, as explained in the dataset description subsection, we
820
      used the responses to the following four types of stimuli:
821
822
             • Drifting gratings: A full field drifting sinusoidal grating at a spatial frequency of 0.04
823            cycles/degree was presented at 8 different directions (from 0° to 315°, separated by 45°), at
824            5 temporal frequencies (1, 2, 4, 8, 15 Hz). Each pattern was shown for 2 seconds, followed
825            by 1 second of a uniform gray background before the next pattern appeared. Also blank
826            sweeps (shown every 20 gratings) are included in this type of stimulus. Each condition
827            (combination of temporal frequency and direction) was presented 15 times across session A.
828            The response time was evaluated on a window of 2 seconds after the stimulus onset.
829
             • Static gratings: A full field static sinusoidal grating was presented at 6 different orientations
830            (separated by 30°), 5 spatial frequencies (0.02, 0.04, 0.08, 0.16, 0.32 cycles/degree), and 4
831            phases (0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75). Each stimulus was presented for 0.25 seconds, without intergray
832            period. Also, blank sweeps were shown every 25 gratings are included in this type of
833            stimulus. Each condition (combination of spatial frequency, orientation and phase) was
834            presented 50 times across session B. The response time was evaluated on a window of 0.5
835            seconds after the stimulus onset.
836
             • Locally Sparse Noise: This type of stimulus consisted of a 16 x 28 array of pixels, each
837
               4.65 degrees on a side. In each medium gray frame of the stimulus (presented for 0.25
838            seconds) a small number (11) of pixels were randomly changed to be white or black. 9000
839            different frames was presented once across session C. The response time was evaluated on a
840            window of 0.5 seconds after the stimulus onset.
841
842
             • Natural Scenes: 118 natural images selected from Berkeley Segmentation Dataset (Martin
               et al., 2001), van Hateren Natural Image Dataset (van Hateren and van der Schaaf, 1998),
843
               McGill Calibrated Colour Image Database (Olmos and Kingdom, 2004) were presented in
844
               grayscale for 0.25 seconds each, with no inter-image gray period. Each image was presented
845            50 times, in random order, and the response period was evaluated in 0.5 seconds after the

1068   V   A PPLICATION OF SELF - SUPERVISED QUANTIZATION ON COMPETITORS
1069
1070
       One might question why our pre-training and quantization strategy was not applied to other methods,
       especially those based on transformer architectures. The primary reason lies in the substantial
1071
       modifications required to integrate auto-encoding pre-training and quantization into these approaches.
1072
1073   Firstly, quantization is infeasible for Informer (Zhou et al., 2021) and Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021),
1074   due to their reliance on embedding layers along the channel dimension, whereas our method embeds
1075   temporally patched data. The goal of quantization is to derive robust temporal representations and
1076
       patterns. Encoding channel combinations with single codes would create an information bottleneck,
       emphasizing channel patterns over temporal ones.
1077
1078   Secondly, quantization cannot be directly applied to Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2022). Although
1079   Crossformer performs patching and embedding both channel-wise and temporally, it introduces
       a two-stage attention mechanism across time and channels. Theoretically, quantization could be
