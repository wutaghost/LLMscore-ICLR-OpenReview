011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Videos contain a wealth of information, and generating detailed and accurate de-
014            scriptions in natural language is a key aspect of video understanding. In this paper,
015
               we present video-SALMONN 2, an advanced audio-visual large language model
               (LLM) with low-rank adaptation (LoRA) designed for enhanced video (with
016
               paired audio) captioning through directed preference optimization (DPO). We pro-
017
               pose new metrics to evaluate the completeness and accuracy of video descriptions,
018            which are optimized using DPO. To further improve training, we introduce a novel
019            multi-round DPO (mrDPO) approach, which involves periodically updating the
020            DPO reference model, merging and re-initializing the LoRA module as a proxy
021            for parameter updates after each training round (1,000 steps), and incorporating
022            guidance from ground-truth video captions to stabilize the process. To address
023            potential catastrophic forgetting of non-captioning abilities due to mrDPO, we
024            propose rebirth tuning, which finetunes the pre-DPO LLM by using the captions
025            generated by the mrDPO-trained model as supervised labels. Experiments show
026
               that mrDPO significantly enhances video-SALMONN 2’s captioning accuracy, re-
               ducing global and local error rates by 40% and 20%, respectively, while decreas-
027
               ing the repetition rate by 35%. The final video-SALMONN 2 model, with just 7
028
               billion parameters, surpasses leading models such as GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5-Pro
029            in video captioning tasks, while maintaining competitive performance to the state-
030            of-the-art on widely used video question-answering benchmark among models of
031            similar size. Upon acceptance, we will release the code, model checkpoints, and
032            training and test data. Demos are available at https://video-salmonn-2.github.io.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large language models (LLMs) have exhibited outstanding capabilities in a wide range of natu-
038   ral language processing (NLP) tasks, and in some instances, have even approached human-level
039
      performance (OpenAI et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023; Du et al., 2022; Bai
      et al., 2023a). LLMs’ remarkable ability to understand, generate, and reason with text has sparked
040
      widespread interest among researchers, attracting both academia and industry to extend them to mul-
041
      timodal understanding and generation. To endow LLMs with multimodal understanding capability,
042   recent studies adopted a paradigm of training modality adapters and aligners between multi-modal
043   encoders and LLMs. This approach leverages world knowledge in the textual LLM to interpret
044   diverse types of data perceived by multimodal encoders, enabling the generation of meaningful in-
045   sights. Over the past two years, many multimodal LLMs have emerged following this paradigm
046   across different modalities. These include models for image and silent video understanding (Liu
047   et al., 2024b;a; Li et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024;
048   Chen et al., 2024), audio understanding (Wu et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024b; Chu et al., 2023; 2024;
049   Gong et al., 2024; 2023; Tang et al., 2024c; Zheng et al., 2024), and audio-visual understanding
050
      (Team et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024b; Tang et al., 2024d).
051   Text descriptions of multimodal data are critical for building multimodal LLMs. This is because
052   most contemporary multimodal LLMs treat multimodal captions as a cornerstone task during pre-
053   training or supervised fine-tuning (SFT), to align the representation spaces of multimodal encoders
      with that of textual large language models, helping LLMs recognise and understand events in mul-


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      timodal data. Thus, collecting high-quality text descriptions paired with multimodal data is crucial
055   for constructing high-performance multimodal LLMs, which implies training the model with more
056   detailed and less hallucinated labels aligned with the multimodal data that could enhance the LLM’s
057   ability to perform multimodal understanding and reasoning. In video understanding, generating
058   detailed and accurate captions is crucial but challenging, as videos contain rich content that encom-
059   passes not only spatial features within individual visual frames but also audio-visual events that
060   unfold across multiple frames over time. However, very few multimodal LLM-related works focus
061   on improving the quality of video captions, due to the lack of quantitative metrics for evaluating
062   video captions and the absence of training methods to enhance the completeness of these descrip-
063
      tions while reducing the risks of hallucination. Additionally, while audio is typically paired with
      video and provides crucial, complementary information to the visual content, most current visual
064
      LLMs lack audio-understanding abilities, leading to the omission of audio information in the gener-
065
      ated captions.
066
067   In this paper, we introduce video-SALMONN 2, a multimodal LLM that supports both audio and vi-
068   sual inputs and primarily focuses on detailed and holistic audio-visual captioning. Building upon an
069
      already well-trained visual LLM, video-SALMONN 2 is further enhanced with auditory capabilities
      by training on audio-only data as well as videos with synchronized audio tracks. This enables the
070
      model to simultaneously “see” and “hear” the video, emulating the way humans perceive and inter-
071
      pret multimedia content. To accurately assess the performance of the model, new metrics to evaluate
072   captioning quality are proposed, which then serve as the objective to optimize during reinforcement
073   learning (RL) based on direct preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024). A novel multi-
074   round DPO (mrDPO) is proposed and performed based on the preferences guided by the metrics,
075   followed by a novel rebirth tuning stage to avoid the degradation of the non-captioning abilities
076   caused by the mrDPO. The rebirth tuning leverages the post-mrDPO model to revise the captions of
077   the videos in the training set, and trains the model after audio modality alignment using supervised
078   fine-tuning (SFT) with the revised training data. Experiments demonstrate that video-SALMONN
079   2 with 7 billion (B) parameters can generate complete and accurate video descriptions and even
080
      outperforms much larger commercial multimodal LLMs such as GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5-Pro, and it
      also maintains competitive performance to the state-of-the-art (SOTA) multimodal LLM of similar
081
      model size on commonly used video question-answering (QA) benchmarks including Video-MME
082
      (Fu et al., 2024a), NeXT-QA (Xiao et al., 2021), MVBench (Li et al., 2024a) and VideoVista (Li
083   et al., 2024b).
084
085   The main contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
086
      • We develop video-SALMONN 2, a powerful audio-visual LLM that generates high-quality video
087
        captions, outperforming larger commercial models such as GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5 in terms of
088     completeness and accuracy.
089
      • We introduce an evaluation pipeline that computes the missing and hallucination rates of audio-
090     visual events in video captions using text-based LLMs, breaking down the process into sub-tasks
091     suited for current LLMs. Additionally, we provide a new benchmark for video captioning with a
092     human-annotated test set.
093   • We propose the mrDPO approach to optimize multimodal LLMs for video captioning, incor-
094     porating periodic updates to the DPO reference model, merging and reinitializing the low-rank
095     adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) module, and smoothing the training loss using SFT based on
096     ground-truth captions. To our knowledge, this is the first work applying RL to audio-visual LLMs.
097   • We introduce rebirth tuning to ensure the resulting model maintains high performance in both
098     captioning and non-captioning tasks. The mrDPO process, followed by rebirth tuning, can be
099     iteratively applied to further enhance performance.

105   Following the paradigm of connecting multimodal encoders to LLMs using modality adapters, var-
106   ious models have been developed. For image-based LLMs, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b;a) applies
107   instruction tuning (Wei et al., 2022) to enhance performance on zero-shot tasks. BLIP-2 (Li et al.,
      2023) uses Q-Former to link a frozen encoder with an LLM, while VILA (Lin et al., 2023) explores


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      pre-training strategies, achieving impressive results in video QA. InternVL (Chen et al., 2023) scales
109   up the size of visual encoders for improved image representation. For silent video understanding,
110   Video-LLaVA (Lin et al., 2024) aligns both image and video adapters to learn unified representa-
111   tions. ShareGPT4Video (Chen et al., 2024) uses GPT-4 to generate dense video captions, improving
112   data quality, and LLaVA-Hound (Zhang et al., 2024) introduces DPO to enhance video LLMs’ un-
113   derstanding capabilities.
114
      In the realm of audio perception, SALMONN (Tang et al., 2024b) uses a dual-encoder structure
115   and can perform zero-shot audio reasoning tasks. LTU (Gong et al., 2024) and LTU-AS (Gong
116   et al., 2023) trained on a large audio question-answering dataset can answer open-ended questions
117   about audio. Qwen-Audio (Chu et al., 2023) and Qwen2-Audio (Chu et al., 2024) are built on large
118   amounts of audio data to achieve high performance on a wide range of carefully selected audio tasks.
119   Zheng et al. (2024) and Tang et al. (2024c) extend the LLM to perceive spatial audio information
120   obtained from microphone array recordings.
121   As the visual frame sequence is often paired with audio in real-world video recordings, some stud-
122   ies investigate understanding non-silent video. Vid2Seq (Yang et al., 2023) utilizes the transcription
123   of human speech to enhance video captioning. video-SALMONN (Sun et al., 2024) uses a multi-
124   resolution causal Q-Former to understand audio and video simultaneously. The Google Gemini
125   model achieves video understanding as a native multimodal LLM built upon text, audio, and visual
126   tokens (Team et al., 2024). AVicuna (Tang et al., 2024d) achieves audio-visual temporal under-
127   standing by introducing pseudo-untrimmed video data. Video-LLaMA (Zhang et al., 2023) and
128   Video-LLaMA 2 (Cheng et al., 2024) directly concatenating audio and visual tokens for joint audio
129
      and video understanding. InternVideo2 (Wang et al., 2024b) aligns video to audio events, speech
      and text through cross-modal contrastive learning for joint audio-video understanding.

132   2.2   RL FOR LLM S
133
134   RL with human feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) is commonly used to enhance text-based
135
      LLMs, with early efforts applying PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) alongside a reward model trained
      on human preference data. Building on this, DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) proposes that the LLM
136
      itself can serve as a reward model, using paired preference data to optimize the model without the
137
      need for an external reward model. KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) further eliminates the need for
138   paired preference data. Expanding on this, RLAIF (Lee et al., 2023) takes a cost-efficient approach
139   by utilizing feedback generated automatically by models, reducing reliance on human involvement.

      3.2   T RAINING S TRATEGIES
217
218   To introduce audio perceptual capabilities to the visual LLM, we employ a multi-stage training
219   approach that enables the model to fully utilize audio information for video understanding while
220   maintaining its performance in processing visual data. Building on a well-trained visual LLM, the
221   training proceeds through several stages: audio modality alignment, audio-visual SFT, RL based
222   on the proposed mrDPO, and the newly introduced rebirth tuning. The pre-trained visual LLM is
223   designed to understand video frames by integrating pre-trained components such as the LLM, visual
224   aligner, and visual encoder, and both the LLM and the video branch frozen during training to avoid
225
      catastrophic forgetting. Similarly, the parameters of the audio encoder remain fixed, as it has been
      pre-trained on a large-scale audio dataset and possesses strong audio perception capabilities.
226
227   Audio modality alignment extends the visual LLM by adding a parallel audio branch, enabling
228   auditory perception capabilities. During this stage, only the audio aligner is trained on a large audio
229   dataset, while the rest of the model remains frozen to preserve its original visual understanding
230   performance. Focusing on learning the audio branch, only audio data is used in this stage. Speech
231
      recognition and audio captioning are trained in this stage. The cross-entropy loss is used as the loss
      function and the reference speech transcriptions and audio captions are used as ground-truth labels.
232
233   After audio modality alignment, the backbone LLM can recognize both visual and audio tokens.
234   However, due to the lack of training with paired audio and visual token sequences, the model is
235   not yet capable of synchronizing and integrating audio-visual information for comprehensive video
236   understanding. To address this, we conduct audio-visual SFT using annotated video data. In the SFT
237
      stage, the model is trained using cross-entropy loss, with video descriptions serving as ground-truth
      labels that incorporate both audio and visual information. To enhance the backbone LLM’s ability
238
      to process audio-visual token sequences, the LLM is jointly trained with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022)
239
      during this stage. The audio aligner is trained to map the output of the audio encoder to the input
240   representation space of the LLM, facilitating the backbone LLM’s interpretation of audio tokens.
241
242   Although the model demonstrates the ability to describe synchronized audio-visual information in
243
      video after SFT, several issues persist, including missing information, hallucinations, and repetitive
      decoding. To address these shortcomings, we apply RL based on mrDPO to improve the model’s
244
      performance. Additionally, we introduce rebirth tuning after RL to further enhance the model’s
245
      performance in non-captioning tasks. Fig. 2 provides an overview of the entire training process
246   involving mrDPO and rebirth tuning, with further details explained in Section 3.3 and 3.4.

267   3.3   M ULTI - ROUND DPO
268
269   We aim to leverage DPO to improve the quality of video captions generated by the model. To
      establish an effective method for evaluating the completeness of video captions, we propose using


                                                                     5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      atomic events as a bridge to automatically assess the preference of caption samples through artificial
271   intelligence (AI) feedback, guiding the model to produce more accurate and detailed video captions.
272
273   Before DPO training, ground-truth video captions are first decomposed into atomic events using
274
      a powerful text LLM, such as GPT-4, to provide references for evaluating the captions generated
      by the model. Next, we apply the nucleus sampling method (Holtzman et al., 2020) to generate
275
      captions from the model’s output distribution for the input video. Depending on the input prompt,
276
      the captions can be either global, summarizing the entire video, or local, focusing on specific time
277   intervals within the video. For each video, two global captions and two local captions are sampled.
278
279   For both global and local caption pairs, the atomic events from the ground-truth video captions are
280
      used to identify the preferred sample within each pair. Specifically, we input a video caption and the
      corresponding list of ground-truth atomic events into a powerful text LLM, such as GPT-4. The LLM
281
      is prompted to identify which atomic events from the ground-truth list are missing in the caption and
282
      which events described in the caption are absent from the ground-truth list. These are referred to
283   as “missing events” and “hallucination events”, respectively. The missing and hallucination rates
284   for each caption are calculated by dividing the number of each type of event by the total number
285   of atomic events in the ground-truth caption. The total error rate of a caption is then obtained by
286   summing its missing and hallucination rates. Additionally, we penalize redundancy by measuring a
287   repetition rate for each generated caption, as detailed in Appendix B. The caption with a relatively
288   lower total error rate and repetition rate within a pair is selected as the preferred sample for DPO.
289   To improve efficiency and mitigate the impact of LLM evaluation noise, caption pairs with small
290   differences in these metrics are excluded from the DPO training set.

328   3.4   R EBIRTH T UNING
329
330   After mrDPO training, the model exhibits significant improvements in captioning abilities, high-
331   lighting the effectiveness of mrDPO in enhancing audio-visual understanding. While achieving
332   strong performance as measured by low event missing and hallucination rates, the model begins to
333
      produce unnatural or unexpected text patterns after multiple DPO rounds. These include phrases
      like “[VideoDescription]: ”, “###Audio Description”, and sequences of uninterpretable symbols.
334
      This behaviour may stem from discrepancies between LLM-generated feedback and actual human
335
      feedback for such unnatural text, leading the model to inadvertently adopt these patterns as training
336   progresses.
337
338   This paper proposes Rebirth Tuning, a method designed to address the degradation of language gen-
339
      eration abilities in mrDPO while preserving its benefits in ensuring information completeness and
      correctness. Rebirth tuning employs teacher-forcing training on self-generated data, promoting a
340
      more stable learning process. Teacher-forcing, which guides the model to predict the next token,
341
      helps prevent it from converging on limited and repetitive patterns. More specifically, before apply-
342   ing rebirth tuning, mrDPO is stopped once we observe a significant decline in the model’s language
343   capabilities. The final iteration of the model, which excels at generating complete and accurate video
344   descriptions, is then used to label a large dataset of videos. Since the model’s language abilities re-
345   main relatively intact, natural and fluent descriptions can be easily filtered by detecting problematic
346   patterns, with the remaining high-quality descriptions used as training data for rebirth tuning.
347
      Rebirth tuning is applied to the model after audio modality alignment, allowing it to be ”reborn”
348   from self-generated high-quality data to enhance video understanding. Following rebirth tuning,
349   the model not only avoids catastrophic forgetting of non-captioning abilities but also supports the
350   development of the next generation of models by applying mrDPO, followed by the subsequent stage
351   of rebirth tuning.

      video-SALMONN 2 is built on an internally trained high-performance visual LLM. It uses SigLIP
357
      (Zhai et al., 2023) as the visual encoder, Qwen 1.5 with 7B parameters as the backbone LLM, and
358
      two linear layers with GELU activation function (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016) as the visual aligner.
359   The model processes video frames at a per-second frame rate of 1 (i.e., ϕ = 1), and can handle up
360   to 30 frames. For videos longer than 30 seconds, 30 frames are uniformly sampled from the video.
361
362
      For the audio branch, we use the Whisper-Large-v3 encoder (Radford et al., 2023) as the audio
      encoder, and a window-level Q-Former (Tang et al., 2024a) with a window length of 0.2 seconds
363
      as the audio aligner, producing a total of 150 audio tokens for a 30-second input. The Whisper
364
      encoder has a maximum processing duration of tmax = 30 seconds. The rank r and scaling factor
365   α of LoRA are set to 256 and 2.0, respectively. During training, the visual encoder, visual aligner,
366   audio encoder, and LLM remain frozen.
367
368   4.2   DATA AND T RAINING S PECIFICATIONS
369
370   During the audio modality alignment stage, LibriSpeech-960h (Panayotov et al., 2015) and Audio-
371   Caps (Kim et al., 2019) are used to train the audio aligner. LibriSpeech-960h is utilized for speech
372   recognition training, while AudioCaps is employed for audio captioning training.
373   In the audio-visual SFT stage, experiments are conducted using an internal video dataset that will
374   be released upon acceptance. A total of 13k videos with rich audio information are automatically
375   selected, and high-quality audio-visual captions are regenerated with the assistance of GPT-4o (Ope-
376   nAI et al., 2024), Whisper-Large-v3 (Radford et al., 2023), and SALMONN (Tang et al., 2024b).
377   The detailed pipeline is described in Appendix C. Additionally, to further enhance the quality of the
      SFT data, around 1.5k video captions were manually refined.


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      In the mrDPO stage, two kinds of tasks are studied: global captioning for the whole video and local
379   captioning for a given time interval. Before each round, a pair of captions for both global and local
380   captioning are sampled from the model for each video in SFT data, respectively. We consider the
381   information missing rate, hallucination rate, and repetition rate to determine whether a sample pair
382   is suitable for DPO and determine the chosen and rejected samples if yes. The selecting methods for
383   each round are listed in Appendix F. We maintain a learning rate of 5 × 10−6 and set the weight λ
384   in Eqn.(11) to 0.1 throughout the entire mrDPO stage. Additionally, we provide the training curves
385   in Appendix G to support reproducibility.
386   After mrDPO, the language abilities of the model are reduced. We stop DPO training once signifi-
387   cant degradation in language abilities is detected. The checkpoint of the last DPO round is used to
388   label captions of a large number of videos. Unnatural captions are eliminated, and the remaining
389   high-quality captions form the training data for the rebirth tuning stage.
390
      For the test dataset, we curated a video captioning benchmark to evaluate the event missing rate
391   (Miss), hallucination rate (Hall), and text repetition rate (Rep). Details of the test data and evaluation
392   process can be found in Appendix D and Appendix E, respectively. The benchmark consists of 483
393   carefully selected videos, each labelled with complete audio-visual captions by human annotators.
394   Atomic events for the test dataset were initially obtained using GPT-4o and then manually refined.
395   For local captioning, we used Gemini-1.5-Pro (Team et al., 2024) to tag the start and end times of
396   each event within specific time intervals. Since Gemini could not process some videos, only 457
397   videos were used for the local captioning evaluation.
398   Regarding training settings, we conducted audio modality alignment using 8×A100 GPUs for 35k
399   steps and audio-visual SFT using 16×A100 GPUs for 4 epochs. Each mrDPO round was trained
400   with 64×A100 GPUs for 1k steps. After six rounds of mrDPO training, rebirth tuning was per-
401   formed. During the rebirth tuning stage, we used 64×A100 GPUs and trained for 4 epochs. The
402   batch size per device was set to 1, making the total batch size equal to the number of devices. The
403   final video-SALMONN 2 model was derived by applying a single round of gDPO training to the
404   model obtained after rebirth tuning.

407   5.1   OVERALL R ESULTS
408
409   The results of our video captioning benchmarks are presented in Table 1. video-SALMONN 2
410   outperforms other models in both information missing and hallucination rates for global and local
411   captioning. Among existing open-source multimodal LLMs, few can provide detailed and accurate
412
      video descriptions, whether purely visual models like Video-LLaVA (Lin et al., 2024) and VILA
      (Lin et al., 2023), or audio-visual models like Video-LLaMA 2 (Cheng et al., 2024) and video-
413
      SALMONN (Sun et al., 2024). Notably, many open-source models, such as Video-LLaVA and
414
      Video-LLaMA 2, tend to generate shorter captions, leading to high information missing rates but
415   low hallucination rates. GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5-Pro can generate more detailed captions and are of
416   higher quality than current open-source models. However, the purely visual version of GPT-4o lacks
417   audio comprehension, and Gemini’s understanding of visual content is somewhat limited, resulting
418   in both models exhibiting some degree of information missing and hallucination.
419
      Our visual base model, trained on a large dataset of images and silent videos, is capable of generat-
420
      ing detailed text descriptions based solely on visual information, with a relatively low information
421   missing rate. However, generating longer texts leads to a higher hallucination rate. After audio
422   modality alignment and audio-visual SFT, the model can leverage audio content to reduce both in-
423   formation loss and hallucinations in its descriptions. However, the inclusion of audio tokens may
424   confuse the visual LLM, resulting in frequent repetition during decoding. Building on the SFT
425   model, we applied mrDPT and rebirth tuning, achieving approximately a 35% absolute reduction
426   in the repetition rate and absolute reductions of around 40% and 20% in the total error rate for
427   global and local captioning, respectively. The final video-SALMONN 2 model outperforms some
428   commercial models like GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5-Pro in video captioning. Inspired by Chai et al.
429
      (2024), we also perform Elo ranking with human evaluators, which validates the alignment of our
      proposed metrics with human perception and highlights the strong video captioning capabilities of
430
      video-SALMONN 2. Further details are provided in Appendix H. In addition, as an audio-visual
431
      LLM, video-SALMONN 2 retains strong visual understanding capabilities and performs well on
      various video benchmarks, such as Video-MME (Fu et al., 2024a). Appendix I shows more details.

        5.2                    A NALYSIS OF MR DPO
472
473    This section investigates various training strategies in mrDPO. For loss functions, we compared the
474    standard DPO loss with our proposed gDPO loss, which incorporates an additional regularization
475    term based on the ground-truth captions. Moreover, we evaluated the LoRA proxy against direct
476
       tuning of the model’s original LoRA, referred to as “Proxy” and “Direct,” respectively. Figure 3a
       illustrates the total error rates for global video captioning across different methods over multiple
477
       DPO rounds. The training was halted when unnatural captions began to appear frequently. Examples
478
       of these unnatural cases are provided in Appendix L.
479
480    First, mrDPO training consistently outperformed single-round DPO training across various settings.
481    While single-round DPO effectively reduces the total error rate, the increasing divergence between
482
       the most recent policy model and the reference model indicates that the assumption of their approx-
       imate similarity no longer holds. As a result, model performance cannot be continuously improved
483
       through a single round of DPO training due to this growing deviation.
484
485    Besides, in terms of the loss functions, the classical DPO loss shows the fastest improvement in cap-
       tioning metrics, but unfortunately, it also quickly leads to outputs with frequent unnatural patterns.


                                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      This is likely because the model only sees self-generated labels rather than ground-truth labels. By
487   incorporating loss on ground-truth labels, gDPO makes the training process more stable, allowing
488   the model to generate text responses without unnatural patterns for a longer period of training across
489   multiple DPO rounds. This stability also preserves the model’s potential for further improvement,
490   with a significant drop in the error rate observed after three rounds of mrDPO.
491
      Using LoRA proxies that randomly initialize a new LoRA in each DPO round, is found to be more
492
      beneficial for mrDPO performance compared to directly training the same LoRA, especially af-
493   ter over 4 gDPO rounds. This demonstrates that the regularization effect introduced by the LoRA
494   proxies helps prevent the model from converging to a local optimum, thereby mitigating overfitting.
495   Since gDPO with LoRA proxy performs the best for mrDPO, we use the model after six gDPO
496   rounds using LoRA proxy to generate captions for a large number of videos. After excluding unnat-
497   ural patterns, a total of 180k video captions remain for rebirth tuning.
498
      We also provide some video captioning examples of models at different training stages to intuitively
499   and qualitatively see the improvement brought by mrDPO. Refer to Appendix K.

531   6     C ONCLUSIONS
532
      This work introduces video-SALMONN 2, a powerful audio-visual LLM designed for detailed video
533
      captioning, and proposes the mrDPO method. To our knowledge, this is the first study of applying
534   RL to audio-visual LLMs in literature. New metrics are designed to evaluate the information miss-
535   ing and hallucination rates in video captions, which are used to guide sample selection for DPO. To
536   further stabilize training, the setting with novel gDPO and LoRA proxy is introduced. After mrDPO,
537   we propose a novel rebirth tuning method to restore LLM’s performance on non-captioning tasks.
538   As a result, video-SALMONN 2 demonstrates significant improvements in video captioning, out-
539   performing notable models such as GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5-Pro, and setting a promising direction
      for achieving detailed and accurate video captioning for video understanding.

765   B    C ALCULATION P ROCEDURE OF T HE T EXT R EPETITION R ATE
766
767   The procedure to calculate the repetition rate of a long and detailed text is shown as follows:
768
769         1. Split the text into short phrases by punctuation;
770         2. Counting the number of occurrences of each phrase;
771
772
            3. The number of recurring phrase words divided by the total number of words in the text is
               the repetition rate.

775   C    P IPELINE FOR L ABELLING H IGH - QUALITY AUDIO - VISUAL C APTIONS
776
777   To curate training data for supervised fine-tuning, we employ GPT-4o to label the visual content in
778   each frame, while SALMONN-13B and Whisper-Large-v3 are used to annotate the speech content
779   and audio events in the audio track. This process is illustrated in Figure 4. Our initial aim is to
780   automatically filter out videos that contain limited speech. We begin by slicing each video into 10-
781   second segments, with the audio from each segment analyzed by SALMONN to generate automatic
782   audio captions (AAC). These captions help us filter out videos that lack descriptive speech such as
783   ”A man is speaking” or ”A woman says...”. This initial filtering step is somewhat coarse.
784   Next, the audio from each segment is processed by Whisper to produce automatic speech recognition
785   (ASR) results. If the transcribed text is too brief or nonsensical, the corresponding video is deemed
786   to lack rich audio content and is excluded from further consideration. For a video to be labelled, all
787   of its segments should pass this exclusion criterion.
788   The segments from the remaining videos are then sampled at a rate of 1 fps and fed into GPT-4o to
789   extract segment-level visual captions. Ultimately, the segment-level visual captions, AAC results,
790   and ASR results form the input to GPT-4o concurrently to generate a detailed global audio-visual
791   caption for each video.

844   E       P ROCESS OF E VALUATING D ETAILED C APTIONS
845
846   To evaluate the specific video caption generated by our model, we first use GPT-3.5 or GPT-4o to
847   split the labelled caption of this video into several atomic events, where we use GPT-4o for the
848   test set and GPT-3.5 for the RL training set. Then, the list of atomic events and the caption to be
849   evaluated are simultaneously fed into GPT-3.5 to determine what events in the atomic event list
850   are missed and what events in the caption are hallucinated. Specifically, we ask GPT-3.5 to list all
851   the missing events and hallucination events for better evaluation precision. Note that events that
852   are described incorrectly are also regarded as hallucinations. The quotient between the number of
853
      missing or hallucination events and the number of all events in the video is the final missing or
      hallucination rate. For more robust testing, GPT-3.5 is used to evaluate 7 times for each caption and
854
      the medium number of the metric is reported. We have manually confirmed that the score GPT-3.5
855
      gives is roughly plausible.
