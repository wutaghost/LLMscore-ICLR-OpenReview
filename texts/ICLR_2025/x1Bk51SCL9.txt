011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Faces and humans are crucial elements in social interaction and are widely in-
014            cluded in everyday photos and videos. Therefore, a deep understanding of faces
015
               and humans will enable multi-modal assistants to achieve improved response qual-
               ity and broadened application scope. Currently, the multi-modal assistant com-
016
               munity lacks a comprehensive and scientific evaluation of face and human under-
017
               standing abilities. In this paper, we first propose a hierarchical ability taxonomy
018            that includes three levels of abilities. Then, based on this taxonomy, we collect im-
019            ages and annotations from publicly available datasets in the face and human com-
020            munity and build a semi-automatic data pipeline to produce problems for the new
021            benchmark. Finally, the obtained Face-Human-Bench comprises a development
022            set with 900 problems and a test set with 1800 problems, supporting both English
023            and Chinese. We conduct evaluations over 25 mainstream multi-modal large lan-
024            guage models (MLLMs) with our Face-Human-Bench, focusing on the correlation
025            between abilities, the impact of the relative position of targets on performance, and
026
               the impact of Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting on performance. Moreover, in-
               spired by multi-modal agents, we also explore which abilities of MLLMs need
027
               to be supplemented by specialist models. The data and evaluation code of the
028
               Face-Human-Bench will be made publicly available.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Faces and humans are always the most crucial elements of photos and videos in our everyday
034   lives. Consequently, they are also critical focuses in multi-modal AI applications. In the past two
035   years, ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023a) and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023b) have achieved great success with
036   impressive instruction-following and multi-modal understanding capabilities respectively. Numer-
037   ous excellent works (Liu et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023) from
038   the open-source community have followed, collectively presenting the immense potential of multi-
039
      modal assistants. Since faces and humans are central to social interaction, a deep understanding of
      this information can make multi-modal assistants achieve improved response quality and broadened
040
      application scope. For instance, in movie understanding (Yue et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023; Wang
041
      et al., 2024), identifying characters is a prerequisite for multi-modal assistants to describe the plot
042   accurately. In multi-modal human-computer interaction (Fu et al., 2024), perceiving expressions and
043   body language can help multi-modal assistants accurately understand the context, generating more
044   personalized and humanized responses. In media forensics (Liu et al., 2024b;c; Jia et al., 2024),
045   determining whether deepfake artifacts exist on a face is crucial for multi-modal assistants to detect
046   misinformation.
047
      Comprehensive and scientific evaluation is the foundation for researching applications of multi-
048   modal assistants related to “faces and humans.” Existing benchmarks Fu et al. (2023); Li et al.
049   (2023a); Liu et al. (2023c) for large multi-modal models typically involve limited abilities of face
050   and human understanding, such as celebrity recognition, action recognition, identity reasoning, and
051   social relation, leaving many important abilities unexplored. On the other hand, since face and hu-
052   man understanding is one of the earliest research topics in artificial intelligence, there are numerous
053   datasets available for evaluating the performance of specialist models. The images and annotations
      from these datasets can serve as original material to evaluate multi-modal assistants.

081   As the starting point of our evaluation, we propose a hierarchical ability taxonomy, as shown in
082   Figure 1. This taxonomy consists of three levels. Level-1 (L1) has two perspectives to study:
083   from the target perspective, L1 includes face understanding and human understanding; from the
084   cognitive process perspective, L1 includes perception and reasoning. Subsequently, we incorporate
085   finer-grained abilities into the taxonomy and categorize them into 10 Level-2 (L2) and 18 Level-
086   3 (L3) ability dimensions. Then, based on this taxonomy, we collect datasets from the face and
087
      human community and use a semi-automatic data pipeline to transform original images and anno-
      tations into multi-modal QAs. The final obtained benchmark called Face-Human-Bench, including
088
      a development set with 900 problems and a test set with 1800 problems, supporting evaluations in
089
      both English and Chinese. For ease of evaluation, we adopt multiple-choice as the problem format
090   following MMBench (Liu et al., 2023c) and SEED-Bench (Li et al., 2023a).
091
092   In the literature, multi-modal assistants can be broadly categorized into two types: (1) Multi-modal
093
      large language models (MLLMs), which achieve end-to-end output by aligning visual information
      to the language domain with visual instruction-tuning (Liu et al., 2023b). (2) Multi-modal agents
094
      (Wu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023), where LLMs decide when to call specialist models to solve
095
      particular problems and then integrate the outputs of these specialist models. Compared to multi-
096   modal agents, MLLMs generally have better multi-modal perception and reasoning abilities with
097   more effective relationship modeling across modalities. In this study, the first research question
098   (RQ1) is: “How do existing MLLMs perform in face and human understanding?” In this question,
099   we focus on (a) the performance of 25 mainstream MLLMs, (b) the correlation between abilities at
100   different levels, (c) the impact of the relative position of targets on performance, and (d) the impact of
101   Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting on performance. Meanwhile, for face and human understanding
102   tasks in which specialist models significantly outperform MLLMs, we can draw inspiration from
103   multi-modal agents by utilizing the output of these specialist models to enhance the responses of
104
      multi-modal assistants. Thus, the second research question emerges (RQ2): In the field of face and
      human understanding, which tasks’ specialist models can achieve significantly better performance
105
      than current MLLMs?
106
107   In response to RQ1, our main findings are as follows: (a) The Face-Human-Bench effectively dis-
      tinguishes the abilities of MLLMs in faces and human understanding. Under the zero-shot setting,

      the best-performing closed-source model, GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), does not perform as well as the
109   best open-source model, InternVL-Chat-v1.2-Plus (Chen et al., 2023). (b) The correlation coeffi-
110   cients can reveal correlations between abilities at different levels. At L2 and L3, there are some
111   ability groups in which the ability dimensions exhibit significant positive correlations between each
112   pair. (c) Many models show substantial performance differences on the same task with different
113   relative positions of targets. We design a new metric called the relative position sensitivity score
114   (RPSS) to measure this phenomenon. On this metric, InternLM-XComposer2-VL-7B (Dong et al.,
115   2024) performs the best, indicating that its performance is almost unaffected by the relative posi-
116   tion of targets. (d) Introducing hints and CoT instructions into the prompts significantly improves
117
      the performance of the closed-source model GPT-4o, but has no effect on the open-source model,
      InternVL-Chat-v1.2-Plus. In response to RQ2, we find that in tasks of deepfake detection, crowd
118
      counting, and face recognition (under challenging scenarios), the performance of MLLMs is signif-
119
      icantly inferior to that of corresponding specialist models. Therefore, we recommend incorporating
120   specialist models in applications requiring these abilities to help improve the response quality of
121   multi-modal assistants.

      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
124   • We propose the Face-Human-Bench, the first benchmark dedicated to evaluating multimodal as-
125     sistants’ face and human understanding abilities. The Face-Human-Bench is based on a three-
126     level ability taxonomy and supports both English and Chinese.
127
      • Utilizing the Face-Human-Bench, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation of mainstream
128     MLLMs, revealing the correlation between abilities, and exploring the impact of the relative po-
129     sition of targets and CoT prompting on the performance of MLLMs.
130
      • We explore which specialist models significantly outperform MLLMs in certain face and human
131     understanding tasks. Based on this, we provide suggestions for enhancing the response quality of
132     multi-modal assistants.

      2.1   H IERARCHICAL A BILITY TAXONOMY
137
138   As shown in Figure 1, the proposed ability taxonomy includes three levels. Level-1 (L1) has two
139   research perspectives. From the target perspective, L1 includes face understanding and human un-
140   derstanding. From the cognitive process perspective, L1 includes perception and reasoning. In our
141   evaluation, perception involves direct comprehension of only one target, while reasoning requires
142   synthesizing information from multiple targets and environments to conclude. There are ten abilities
143   in total at Level-2 (L2). Five are focused on faces: facial attribute recognition, age estimation, facial
144   expression recognition, face attack detection, and face recognition, and five are focused on humans:
145
      human attribute recognition, action recognition, spatial relation understanding, social relation un-
      derstanding, and person re-identification. It should be noted that at L2, there are 6 abilities under
146
      perception and 4 abilities under reasoning. Level-3 (L3) further refines the ability dimensions at L2.
147
      Facial expression recognition can be categorized into basic and compound types. Face attack detec-
148   tion includes deepfake detection and face anti-spoofing. Face recognition involves five scenarios:
149   basic, cross-pose, cross-age, similar-looking, and occluded. Spatial relation understanding concerns
150   relative position and count. Social relation understanding includes social relationship recognition
151   and identity reasoning. Please refer to Appendix A.1 for detailed definitions and examples of these
152   abilities.
153
154   2.2   S EMI -AUTOMATIC DATA P IPELINE
155
156   Based on the hierarchical ability taxonomy defined in Section 2.1, we collect 16 public datasets from
157   the face and human community, covering each L3 ability. Then, we employ a semi-automatic data
158
      pipeline to produce problems for the Face-Human-Bench.
159   An original sample Si from public datasets can be represented as a binary tuple (Ii , Li ), where Ii
160   denotes an original image set and Li denotes an original label set. Note that we use “image set” and
161   “label set” to describe the composition of one sample because, in some datasets, a single sample may
      consist of multiple images or labels. For instance, in face recognition, a sample includes a pair of


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      face images to verify identity, and in facial attribute recognition, a sample may involve 40 attribute
163   labels.
164
165   For ease of evaluation, we adopt multiple-choice as the problem format in our Face-Human-Bench.
166
      Each problem Pi corresponds to a quadruple (Vi , Qi , Oi , Ai ). Here, Vi refers to the images obtained
      via the image processing pipeline pimage : I → V. pimage performs an operation such as cropping,
167
      concatenating, adding boxes, or leaving the original images unchanged, depending on the ability to
168
      test. Qi denotes the question. Each L3 ability includes a set of pre-written questions that share the
169   same semantics but exhibit diversity. When producing samples, a question Qi is randomly selected
170   from this question set. Oi is the set of n options (o1 , o2 , ..., on ), where 2 ≤ n ≤ 4. These options are
171   obtained through the text processing pipeline ptext : L → O. ptext converts the original labels into
172   one correct option and n − 1 incorrect options. For some tasks, ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023a) is used
173   within ptext to assist in generating incorrect options or adjusting options at the sentence level (fixing
174   grammar or re-wording sentences for fluency. Ai is the correct answer to the problem. The produced
175   Pi will be checked by data reviewers to ensure that the options are unambiguous and there is one
176   and only one correct answer. The problems that do not meet the requirements will be removed.
177   In summary, our semi-automatic data pipeline leverages image and text processing pipelines, pimage
178   and ptext , to transform original samples into multiple-choice format problems. These problems are
179   then manually checked to ensure quality. We obtain a benchmark with a development set of 900
180   problems for the MLLM community to evaluate during training iterations and a test set of 1800
181   problems for the formal evaluation in our paper. Additionally, the English problems are translated
182   into Chinese to create a Chinese version of the benchmark. For more details on data sources, statis-
183
      tics, and the semi-automatic data pipeline, please refer to Appendices A.2 and A.3.

      In this section, we explore whether specialist models corresponding to 13 L3 abilities can be used
466
      to enhance MLLMs. 8 We directly test the performance of MLLMs using original datasets from
467   the face and human community to facilitate comparison with specialist models. We design a set
468   of prompt templates to transform the classification problems into multiple-choice problems and the
469   regression problems (age estimation and crowd counting) into fill-in-the-blank problems. 9 Special-
470   ist models are generally trained and tested on data from the same distribution. They can achieve
471   high performance even if the test labels contain noise. However, the visual information learned by
472   MLLMs and the original datasets used for testing may exhibit data distribution bias. To enable an ef-
473   fective comparison, we utilize early specialist models (which emerged after the widespread adoption
474   of deep learning) as a reference to judge the performance of MLLMs on these tasks.10
475   We further define the relative performance score S to normalize performances across different tasks:

478   (the top three models in the main experiment). Pr is the performance of random responses, and Ps
479   is the performance of the early specialist model. This metric typically ranges from 0 to 1, where a
480   higher relative score indicates stronger abilities of MLLMs on the corresponding task. A relative

499   4   R ELATED W ORK
500
501   Evaluation of MLLMs about Face and Human Understanding. Currently, there is no dedicated
502   benchmark evaluating the face and human understanding abilities of MLLMs. Some efforts aim at
503   comprehensively benchmarking MLLMs, containing some ability dimensions about face and human
504
      understanding. LAMM (Yin et al., 2023) evaluates 9 different 2D vision tasks using 11 existing
      public datasets. Among these, the facial classification task utilizes the CelebA (Liu et al., 2015)
505
      dataset to evaluate the accuracy of smile detection and hair attribute classification. MME (Fu et al.,
506
      2023) includes the celebrity recognition ability, requiring MLLMs to respond with Yes/No answers.
507   SEED-Bench (Li et al., 2023a) includes the action recognition ability, where the inputs consist of
508   multiple frames taken from a video, and MLLMs are required to choose the correct answer from
509   four descriptions. MMBench (Liu et al., 2023c) includes the most extensive set of abilities related to
510   faces and humans: celebrity recognition, action recognition, identity reasoning, and social relation,
511   all of which are tested using multiple-choice problems. Considering the importance of faces and
512   humans in multimedia, these evaluations are insufficient.
513   Face and Human Understanding. Face and human understanding is among the earliest research
514   topics in artificial intelligence with successful applications. During the 2010s, the introduction of
515   deep learning, particularly convolutional neural networks, significantly advanced face and human
516   perception. In that era, numerous high-quality datasets were proposed for training and evaluat-
517   ing tasks of face attribute recognition (Liu et al., 2015), age estimation (Rothe et al., 2015; Escalera
518   et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2017), facial expression recognition (Barsoum et al., 2016; Li et al., 2017b;
519   Mollahosseini et al., 2019), deepfake detection (Rössler et al., 2019; Dolhansky et al., 2019), face
520   anti-spoofing (Liu et al., 2018; 2019), face recognition (Yi et al., 2014; Guo et al., 2016; Zheng
521
      et al., 2017; Deng et al., 2017; Zheng & Deng, 2018), human attribute recognition (Li et al., 2016;
      Liu et al., 2017), human-object interaction detection (Gupta & Malik, 2015; Xu et al., 2019), crowd
522
      counting (Zhang et al., 2016), social relationship recognition Sun et al. (2017); Li et al. (2017a) and
523
      person re-ideitification Li et al. (2014); Zheng et al. (2015). Entering the 2020s, a new paradigm
524   emerged, which initially pre-trains a task-agnostic backbone and then based on this, trains a uni-
525   fied face or human model (Ci et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Qin et al., 2024) to simultaneously
526   handle multiple face and human understanding tasks within a unified structure. In our evaluation,
527   we observe that in certain tasks, MLLMs do not perform as well as specialist models. Utilizing
528   these unified face or human models as the specialist models to help MLLMs can greatly facilitate
529   deployment.
530
531   5   C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this work, we propose the hierarchical Face-Human-Bench, the first benchmark specifically de-
534   signed to evaluate MLLMs’ face and human understanding abilities. We comprehensively and sci-
535   entifically assess the performance of 25 mainstream MLLMs with our benchmark. We reveal the
536   correlations between abilities and explore the impact of the relative position of targets and CoT
537   prompting on the performance of MLLMs. Inspired by multimodal agents, we investigate which
538   abilities of MLLMs need to be supplemented by specialist models. Our work will provide the face
539   and human community valuable insights on how to more effectively leverage multi-modal assistants
      in applications related to “faces and humans.”

      A.1    D EFINITION ABOUT E ACH L EAF A BILITY
921
922   We will sequentially describe the definitions of L2 abilities and the L3 abilities they encompass. We
923   provide examples of problems in Face-Human-Bench in Tables 4 to 11.
924
      Facial Attribute Recognition: Recognize various characteristics and traits from facial images.
925
926   Age Estimation: Estimate the age of the person in the image based on facial information.
927   Facial Expression Recognition: Recognize the emotions of the person in the image, categorized
928   into basic and compound types. Basic expressions include surprised, fearful, disgusted, happy, sad,
929   angry, and neutral. Compound expressions provide more nuanced emotional descriptions, including:
930   happily surprised, happily disgusted, sadly fearful, sadly angry, sadly surprised, sadly disgusted,
931   fearfully angry, fearfully surprised, angrily surprised, angrily disgusted, and disgustedly surprised.
932   Face Attack Detection: Determine whether the face in the image involves digital manipulation or
933   physical spoofing. The corresponding sub-abilities are referred to as Deepfake Detection and Face
934   Anti-Spoofing, respectively.
935
      Face Recognition Identify and verify individuals’ identities in images according to facial infor-
936
      mation. In our tests, this ability is mainly to determine whether two photos showcase the same
937
      individual. Five scenarios are involved: basic, cross-pose, cross-age, similar-looking, and occluded.
938
939   Human Attribute Recognition Recognize various characteristics and traits from human images.
940   Action Recognition Recognize human actions, including interactions with objects.
941
942
      Spatial Relation Understanding Understand the spatial positions of people in the image, including
      relative position understanding (comprehending the relative positions of one person to others and
943
      objects) and crowd counting (counting the number of people in the image).
944
945   Social Relation Understanding Including social relationship recognition (inferring social relation-
946   ships between people through their interactions) and identity reasoning (deducing social identity
947   based on a person’s attributes, actions, interactions with others, and environmental information).
948   Person Re-Identification Identify and verify individuals’ identities in images based on full-body
949   attributes (usually excluding the face, as facial features are often blurry).

1321   A.2   DATA S OURCES AND S TATISTICS
1322
1323   Table 12 provides information on the data sources for Face-Human-Bench, as well as the image
1324   processing pipeline, the number of problems in the development and test sets, and the weights, for
1325   each subset.
1326   We set the weights of all 10 L2 abilities to be equal. For L2 abilities that encompass multiple L3
1327   abilities, each L3 ability equally shares the weight of the corresponding L2 ability. For L3 abilities
1328   that encompass multiple image versions, each image version subset equally shares the weight of
1329   the corresponding L3 ability. Finally, we obtain the detailed weights of each subset, as shown in
1330   Table 12.
1331   We sequentially provide overviews of the public datasets we used for original samples.
1332
1333   CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) is a large-scale facial attributes dataset released by the Multimedia Labora-
1334
       tory of Chinese University of Hong Kong. It contains over 200,000 celebrity images, each annotated
       with 40 attributes. The dataset includes a wide range of body pose variations and complex, di-
1335
       verse background information. It comprises 10,177 identities, 202,599 face images, and 5 landmark
1336
       positions, with 40 binary attribute annotations for each image.
1337
1338   UTKFace (Zhang et al., 2017) dataset is a large-scale facial dataset with a wide age range, spanning
1339   from 0 to 116 years. It contains over 20,000 face images, annotated with age, gender, and ethnicity
1340
       labels.
1341   RAF-DB (Li et al., 2017b) is a large-scale facial expression database consisting of 29,672 real-
1342   world images, each accompanied by a 7-dimensional expression distribution vector. It includes two
1343   different subsets: a single-label subset with 7 basic expressions (RAF-DB Basic) and a two-tab
1344   subset with 12 compound expressions (RAF-DB Compound). Additionally, the dataset provides 5
1345   precise landmark locations, 37 automatic landmark positions, bounding boxes, and annotations for
1346
       ethnicity, age range, and gender attributes for each image.
1347   FF++ (Rössler et al., 2019) consists of 1,000 original video sequences processed using four different
1348   automated facial manipulation methods: Deepfakes, Face2Face, FaceSwap, and NeuralTextures.
1349   The data in FaceForensics++ comes from 977 YouTube videos, all featuring trackable frontal faces
       without occlusions, allowing the automated manipulation methods to generate realistic forgeries.

       SiW-Mv2 (Guo et al., 2022) collects 785 videos from 493 subjects, and 915 spoof videos from 600
1385   subjects. The dataset includes 14 types of spoofing, ranging from typical print and replay attack, to
1386   various masks, impersonation makeup and physical material coverings. SiW-Mv2 exhibits a good
1387   variance in spoofing modes, with each mode specified and validated by the IARPA project.
1388
       LFW (Huang et al., 2008) is a commonly used test set for face recognition, comprising 13,233
1389
       face images sourced from natural scenes in everyday life. Each image is associated with a name,
1390
       representing 5,749 individuals, with most people having only one image. The database randomly
1391   selected 6,000 pairs of faces to create face recognition image pairs to test the accuracy of face
1392   recognition systems, with 3,000 pairs containing two images of the same person and 3,000 pairs
1393   featuring one image of different individuals.
1394
       CPLFW (Zheng & Deng, 2018) builds upon LFW by considering the impact of pose variations. It
1395
       specifically searches for and selects 3,000 pairs of positive faces with differing poses, adding pose
1396
       variation to the intra-class variance. Additionally, it includes negative pairs with the same gender
1397   and ethnicity to minimize the influence of attribute differences between positive and negative pairs.
1398
1399   CALFW (Zheng et al., 2017) builds upon LFW by considering the impact of age variations. It
1400
       specifically searches for and selects 3,000 pairs of positive faces with age differences to increase the
       intra-class variance associated with the aging process. Negative pairs are chosen to have the same
1401
       gender and ethnicity to reduce the influence of attribute differences.
1402
1403   SLLFW (Deng et al., 2017) intentionally selects 3,000 pairs of visually similar faces through human
       crowdsourcing from the original image folder, replacing the random negative pairs in LFW.

       MLFW (Wang et al., 2022) dataset is created based on CALFW and focuses on masked faces. The
1405   masks generated for the faces in the dataset maintain good visual consistency with the original faces.
1406   It includes a variety of mask templates that cover most common styles encountered in everyday life,
1407   achieving diversity of the samples.
1408
       WIDER Attribute (Li et al., 2016) is a large-scale human attributes dataset containing 13,789
1409
       images across 30 scene categories, with 57,524 human bounding boxes. Each bounding box is an-
1410
       notated with 14 binary attributes, including male, long hair, sunglasses, hat, long shirt, long sleeves,
1411   formal, shorts, jeans, long pants, skirt, mask, logo, and checkered or striped patterns.
1412
1413   HICO-DET Xu et al. (2019) is a commonly used dataset in the Human Object Interaction (HOI)
1414
       domain, consisting of 47,776 images, with 38,118 in the training set and 9,658 in the testing set.
       The dataset includes 117 action (verb) categories, 80 object categories, and 600 verb-object combi-
1415
       nations.
1416
1417   SpatialSense Yang et al. (2019) is a dataset for spatial relation recognition, where the task is to
1418   determine whether a specific spatial relation holds between two given objects. The dataset contains
1419   17,498 relations on 11,569 images, involving 3,679 unique object classes, with 2,139 of these classes
1420
       appearing only once, presenting a challenging long-tail distribution.
1421   PISC Li et al. (2017a) is focused on the task of social relationship recognition in still images. It is
1422   used to benchmark models that analyze the relationships between people based on contextual and
1423   individual features. It contains 22,670 images with 76,568 annotated samples representing 9 types
1424   of social relationships.
1425   ShTech Zhang et al. (2016) is focused on the task of crowd counting, where the goal is to accurately
1426   estimate the number of people in an image with varying crowd density and perspective. It contains
1427   1,198 images with approximately 330,000 annotated heads. The dataset aims to address challenges
1428   in crowd counting that were not covered by previous datasets.
1429
       Market-1501 Zheng et al. (2015) is designed for the task of person re-identification. This dataset
1430   addresses the limitations of scale and realistic conditions found in previous datasets. The large-scale
1431   data supports training and testing models effectively for person re-identification. It includes over
1432   32,000 annotated bounding boxes and a distractor set of more than 500,000 images.

1524   text, inference, and code, while also demonstrating strong capabilities in multilingual, audio, and
1525   visual tasks. The API we applied in our experiments is “gpt-4o-2024-05-13”.

       Gemini (Team et al., 2023): Gemini is a multimodal large model developed by Google, available

       itly specified output formats, and the addition of task-specific datasets for training. Following that,

1814   B.3.1     U NEXPLORED L3 A BILITIES
1815
       We explain the reasons for not conducting experiments on the remaining 5 L3 abilities as follows.
1816
1817   Face/Human Attribute Recognition These two tasks include a large number of binary classifica-
1818   tion labels (40 labels in CelebA for face and 14 labels in WIDER Attribute for human). Using eval-
1819   uation protocols designed for specialist models to fully assess the performance of MLLMs would
1820
       result in huge computational costs. Additionally, many attribute labels have ambiguous semantics
       that are difficult to define accurately, such as ”attractive,” ”big lips,” and ”big nose.”
1821
1822   Relative Position Understanding In the face and human understanding community, there are no
1823   specialist models specifically constructed to perceive the spatial relationships between one person to
1824   others and objects.
1825   Identity Reasoning There is a lack of publicly available specialist models that perform occupation
1826   classification.
1827
       Social Relationship Recognition Existing evaluation datasets for specialist models, such as PIPA
1828
       and PISC, are not suitable for directly evaluating the social relationship recognition abilities of
1829
       MLLMs. Because many annotations in these datasets are ambiguous (it is often impossible to assert
1830   that people in an image belong to one relationship category rather than another) and semantically
1831   overlapping (for example, “couple” is a separate category, but a married couple is semantically also
1832   part of “family”). Fine-tuned specialist models can still learn to classify under unclear standards,
1833   but for zero-shot learning MLLMs, the lack of clear definitions greatly reduces performance. It is
1834   worth noting that in Face-Human-Bench, when we use PISC to construct problems for evaluating
1835   the social relationship recognition ability of MLLMs, we manually review and remove problems
       with ambiguous options or potentially non-unique answers, allowing us to assess accurately.
