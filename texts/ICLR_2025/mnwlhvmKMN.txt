                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               In this paper, we present a 4D embodied world model, which takes in an image
010
               observation and language instruction as input and predicts a 4D dynamic mesh
011            predicting how the scene will change as the embodied agent performs actions based
012            on the given instructions. In contrast to previously learned world models which
013            typically generate 2D videos, our 4D model provides detailed 3D information on
014            precise configurations and shape of objects in a scene over time. This allows us
015            to effectively learn accurate inverse dynamic models for an embodied agent to
016            execute a policy for interacting with the environment. To construct a dataset to train
017            such 4D world models, we first annotate large-scale existing video robotics dataset
018            using pretrained depth and normal prediction models to construct 3D consistent
019
               4D models of each video. To efficiently learn generative models on this 4D data,
               we propose to train a video generative model on this annotated dataset, which
020
               jointly predicts RGB-DN (RGB, Depth, and Normal) for each video. We then
021
               present an algorithm to directly convert generated RGB, Depth and Normal images
022            into high-quality dynamic 4D mesh models of the world. We illustrate how this
023            enables us to predict high-quality meshes consistent across both time and space
024            from embodied scenarios, render novel views for embodied scenes, as well as
025            construct policies that substantially outperform those from prior 2D and 3D models
026            of the world. Our code, model, and dataset will be made publicly available. Video
027            results can be found at our website: https://4d-worldmodel.github.io/.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The ability to simulate and construct learned models of the world (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Yang
033
      et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2024; Xiang et al., 2024) opens a rich set of opportunities for constructing
      intelligent embodied agents. Such models enable flexible policy synthesis (Du et al., 2024; Liang
034
      et al., 2024), data simulation and generation (Yang et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2024), and flexible
035
      long-horizon planning (Janner et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). However, while
036   the physical world is three-dimensional in nature, existing world models operate in the space of 2D
037   pixels. As a result, existing models fail to provide information about the precise state of world, such
038   as how far away a manipulated object is or what 6-DoF pose is needed to accurately manipulate an
039   object, which is often important to extract precise controls to execute a robot. In addition, simulated
040   dynamics will often not be consistent in the 3D world, such as having objects change dramatically in
041   size and shape over time, making it difficult to use such models for effective data driven simulation
042   and generation.
043   In this paper, we explore how we can instead learn a 4D embodied world model, which directly
044   simulates the dynamics of 3D world. By directly modeling the dynamics of the world in 3D, we
045   can more accurately simulate physical interactions, such as the grasping of an object or the opening
046   of drawer. Simultaneously, the detailed output space of 3D dynamics allows us to precisely extract
047   detailed 6-DoF actions of a robot directly from the 3D shape of the manipulator, allowing our world
048   model to effectively serve as a zero-shot policy.
049   However, the task of learning a 4D embodied world model is very challenging as the dynamics of
050   the world is extremely computationally expensive to train and learn, requiring models to generate
051   outputs in three-dimensional space and time. To efficiently represent and predict the dynamics of
052   the world, we propose a substantially more lightweight representation of the 4D world, consisting of
053   predicting a sequence of RGB, depth and normal maps of the scene. This combined representation
      accurately captures the appearance, geometry, and surface of a scene while being substantially lower

      A key challenge for training a 4D world model is a lack of access to existing large-scale datasets with
082   existing 4D annotations, or the high-quality image, depth and normal annotations needed to train our
083   approach. To construct such data, in principle, we can use pretrained depth and normal estimators (Ke
084   et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024) to obtain such estimates from video data, but these estimators are
085   typically limited to predicting relative value maps from individual frames. As a result, as a scene
086   changes, these estimators will produce depth and normal maps with significant inconsistencies across
087   time. To tackle this challenge, we develop a data collection pipeline that leverages optical flow
088   between frames in a video to enforce consistency between generated depth and normal maps across
089   timesteps. In particular, we use optical flow to guide a depth and normal diffusion model across
090
      frames in a video, which we find is sufficient to ensure consistent depth and normal predictions across
      all timesteps without the need for expensive ground-truth annotations, facilitating the training of our
091
      world model on a large scale.
092
093   Overall, our paper has the following contributions: (1) We introduce a 4D embodied world model,
094   and present an efficient representation of this model, in the form of RGB, depth and normal maps, and
095   illustrate how this representation can be used to construct a full 4D mesh of the scene. (2) We present
096
      a pipeline to automatically extract 4D world model data from existing video datasets, leveraging an
      optical flow guided depth and normal diffusion model. (3) We illustrate the efficacy of the approach
097
      in generating consistent 4D meshes across different environments, substantially outperforming other
098
      baselines, as well as illustrating its downstream use as an effective policy.

142
143   Learning 4D embodied world models requires large-scale 4D datasets, which are expensive to collect
144   in the real world. In this section, we present a data annotation pipeline that enables us to automatically
145
      construct 4D datasets from existing video datasets. For an illustration of this process, see Figure 2.
      We collected 87,212 episodes of (V, T ) directly from the RT-1 dataset (Brohan et al., 2022), which is
146
      a large dataset of real-world tabletop robotic manipulation tasks involving 17 objects.
147
148   Given the original input video, V and a robot text action description T , our data collection protocol
149   aims to automatically obtain 4D annotations of (F, D, N ) quadruplet structure, where F, D and N
150   denotes the optical flow, depth and normal information corresponding to V.
151   To generate the optical flow F, we employ RAFT (Teed & Deng, 2020), an efficient and high-
152   quality optical flow estimation method suitable for various video datasets. The optical flow between
153   consecutive frames is computed as F = RAFT(V). The next step involves generating the 3D
154   annotations: depth D and surface normal N . These 3D annotations provide richer visual context and
155   enhance the understanding of the 4D environment represented by V. Recent advances in monocular
156   depth and normal estimators, typically based on models like Vision Transformers (ViT) or Diffusion
157
      Models, have shown strong generalization capabilities across diverse datasets.
158   In our approach, we leverage the diffusion model Marigold (Ke et al., 2024) to estimate Di and N i
159   from each video frame V i . The frame is first encoded into a latent representation vi using a variational
160   autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013a; Rezende et al., 2014): vi = Encoder(V i ). At the same time, an
161   initial depth or normal latent map ziT is sampled from a Gaussian distribution: ziT ∼ N (0, 1). Then
      we iteratively apply the learned denoiser U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) ϵ to reconstruct the zi0 and

183   4      L EARNING 4D E MBODIED W ORLD M ODELS
184
185   We introduce the 4D Embodied World Model, which predicts future RGB, depth, and normal maps
186   based on a given input image and text, generating dynamic meshes from these outputs. We leverage a
187   pretrained video diffusion model as the backbone to predict this rich set of 2D geometric information.
188   Then, we propose an efficient method to convert the RGB-DN video into 4D meshes.

192   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) are capable of learning the data distribution
193   p(x) by progressively adding noise to the data until it resembles a Gaussian distribution through a
194
      forward process. During inference, a denoiser ϵ is trained to recover the data from this noisy state.
      Latent video diffusion models (Zheng et al., 2024) utilize a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) Kingma
195
      (2013a); Van Den Oord et al. (2017), in the latent space of the data, maintaining high-quality outputs
196

222   4.2   V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODELS FOR J OINT RGB, D EPTH AND N ORMAL P REDICTIONS
223
224   Training a diffusion model to model temporal RGB-DN data is a challenging task. To effectively train
225   RGB video models, large-scale video datasets with billions of high-quality samples are used (Zheng
226   et al., 2024). In contrast, even through automatic annotation, our dataset of RGB-DN data contains
227   only around one million data points, which is insufficient to train a world model from scratch. To
228
      address this, we finetune the Open-Sora (Zheng et al., 2024) as our RGB-DN prediction model and
      directly leverage the pretrained knowledge inside the model to effectively bootstrap our 4D model.
229
230   To implement this, we use the temporal VAE Kingma (2013a); Van Den Oord et al. (2017) in Open-
231   Sora (Zheng et al., 2024) to separately encode RGB, depth and normal images for each frame of
232   a video. We then train our video diffusion model to denoise and generate the concatenated RGB,
233   depth and normal images. We directly then expand Open-Sora’s input and output channels threefold
234
      and finetune the remaining parameters of the model to denoise RGB-DN images. The resultant is
      then able to directly leverage previous knowledge trained from video data, and use it to predict RGB,
235
      depth and normal frames representing the 4D dynamics of a scene.

      4.3   4D M ESH R ECONSTRUCTION FROM RGB-DN V IDEO
238
239   After obtaining the RGB-DN video, we further optimize the depth and reconstruct the surface to
240   generate a full final dynamic mesh of the scene. Similar to prior works (Ye et al., 2024; Ke et al.,
241   2024), our depth representation for each image is given by a relative map in the range [0, 1], and thus
242   cannot directly reconstruct the entire scene. While past work has sidestepped this by assuming either
243   a default scale for depth or by directly predicting metric depth, such reconstructions from depth are
244   often coarse and often cause reconstructed planes or walls to be tilted.
245   We instead leverage the normal maps N i and optical flow F i between frames in a video to obtain
246   precise depth estimates per pixel from relative depth maps Di . In particular, we use normal maps
247   to enforce constraints on the surface scene. We then use optical flow between frames to enforce 3D
248   consistency in the scene over time. Both maps in combination with the coarse depth Di allow to
249   optimize a refined depth map D̂ that corresponds to a consistent 4D scene.
250

307   4.4   I NVERSE DYNAMICS M ODELS FROM 4D M ESHES
308
309   After generating 4D meshes, which encapsulate both spatial and temporal information, we extract
310   geometric details that can significantly enhance downstream tasks in robotics. The detailed geometry
311
      captured by these 4D meshes plays a crucial role in robotic grasping tasks.
312   To achieve this, we employ an inverse dynamics model built on the 4D meshes, predicting the
313   appropriate robot action ai based on the current state si and the predicted future state si+1 .
314   Mathematically, this relationship is expressed as ai = ID(si , si+1 ). In our scenario, si represents the
315   scene at time step i. Specifically, we sample the meshes to obtain point clouds, which are encoded
316   by a PointNet (Qi et al., 2017) architecture within the inverse dynamics model to extract features.
317
      These features, combined with the instruction text embeddings, are further processed by an MLP
      to generate the final action.

366   compare our method to a 4D point cloud diffusion model. Our baselines include two main approaches:
367   the first is a 16-frame RGB diffusion model, where we obtain depth from the pretrained depth estimator
368   Marigold (Ke et al., 2024), and lift it to 3D via camera intrinsic and extrinsic parameters. We also

523   6      C ONCLUSION
524
525   Our current approach has several limitations. First, while our RGB-DN representation of a 4D world
526   model is cheap and easy to predict, it only captures a single surface of the world. To construct a more
527   complete 4D world model, it may be interesting in the future to have a generative model that generates
528   multiple RGB-DN views of the world, which can then be integrated to form a more complete 4D
529
      world model. In addition, we observe that generated RGB-DN maps from our 4D world model may
      not be fully consistent with each. Adding additional structure in the architecture or loss function
530
      constraints at training time to help enforce consistency is a rich direction of future work.
531
532   Overall, our work provides some first steps towards the goal of constructing 4D generative model
533   of the world. We believe that such world models will be increasingly powerful and useful in the
534   future, serving as a way to simulate the physical world and is an important step towards constructing
535
      intelligent embodied agents. Such models would then enable us to train policies in the real world in a
      fully offline manner, as well as roll out and imagine future plans in the world.

      A.2    V IDEO D IFFUSION I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS

789
      We trained our video diffusion model using the STDiT3-XL/2 architecture, fine-tuned on OpenSora-
790
      v1.2 (Zheng et al., 2024), employing 6 × 8 V100 GPUs. The model processes videos with 16
791   frames, using gradient checkpointing to optimize memory usage. For acceleration, we set a batch
792   size of 2, used bf16 precision, and applied the ZeRO2 (Rajbhandari et al., 2020) optimization plugin.
793   Additionally, we leveraged a T5 text encoder (Raffel et al., 2020) for conditioning. For sampling, we
794   use the rflow scheduler with logit-normal sampling across 30 steps and set a classifier-free guidance
795   scale of 7.0.
796
      Training spanned 40,000 iterations with an initial learning rate of 1e-4, 1.0 gradient clipping, and a
797   1,000-step warmup. The optimizer incorporated Adam with epsilon set to 1e-15, and an exponential
798   moving average (EMA) decay of 0.99 was used to stabilize training.

801   A.3    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS FOR ROBOTICS P LANING TASKS
802
803   For the RLBench training, we adopted the same architecture and methods as our video diffusion
804   model, with the primary difference being that we used 13 frames and fine-tuned the model on the
805   RT-1 dataset.
806   For the action prediction stage, we first filter out the background and floor from the data, focusing
807   only on the points of the table and the objects manipulated by the robotic arm, and then sample 1024
808   points from filtered the point cloud. In our inverse dynamic model, the PointNet extracts features
809   from this point cloud, which are then concatenated with the instruction’s language embedding and
      passed into a 4-layer MLP, finally outputting the 7DoF actions.
