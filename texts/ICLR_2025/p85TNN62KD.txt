009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Influence function, a technique rooted in robust statistics, has been adapted in
012            modern machine learning for a novel application: data attribution—quantifying
013            how individual training data points affect a model’s predictions. However, the
014            common derivation of influence functions in the data attribution literature is lim-
015            ited to loss functions that can be decomposed into a sum of individual data point
016            losses, with the most prominent examples known as M-estimators. This restricts
017            the application of influence functions to more complex learning objectives, which
018
               we refer to as non-decomposable losses, such as contrastive or ranking losses,
               where a unit loss term depends on multiple data points and cannot be decomposed
019
               further. In this work, we bridge this gap by revisiting the general formulation
020
               of influence function from robust statistics, which extends beyond M-estimators.
021            Based on this formulation, we propose a novel method, the Versatile Influence
022            Function (VIF), that can be straightforwardly applied to machine learning models
023            trained with any non-decomposable loss. In comparison to the classical approach
024            in statistics, the proposed VIF is designed to fully leverage the power of auto-
025            differentiation, hereby eliminating the need for case-specific derivations of each
026            loss function. We demonstrate the effectiveness of VIF across three examples:
027            Cox regression for survival analysis, node embedding for network analysis, and
028            listwise learning-to-rank for information retrieval. In all cases, the influence esti-
029
               mated by VIF closely resembles the results obtained by brute-force leave-one-out
               retraining, while being up to 103 times faster to compute. We believe VIF rep-
030
               resents a significant advancement in data attribution, enabling efficient influence-
031
               function-based attribution across a wide range of machine learning paradigms,
032            with broad potential for practical use cases.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Influence function (IF) is a well-established technique originating from robust statistics and has
038   been adapted to the novel application of data attribution in modern machine learning (Koh & Liang,
039   2017). Data attribution aims to quantify the impact of individual training data points on model out-
040   puts, which enables a wide range of data-centric applications such as mislabeled data detection (Koh
041   & Liang, 2017), data selection (Xia et al., 2008), and copyright compensation (Deng & Ma, 2023).
042   Despite its broad potential, the application of IFs for data attribution has been largely limited to
043   loss functions that can be decomposed into a sum of individual data point losses—such as those
044   commonly used in supervised learning or maximum likelihood estimation, which are also known
045   as M-estimators. This limitation arises from the specific way that IFs are typically derived in the
046   data attribution literature (Koh & Liang, 2017; Grosse et al., 2023), where the derivation involves
047   perturbing the weights of individual data point losses. As a result, this restricts the application of
048   IF-based data attribution methods to more complex machine learning objectives, such as contrastive
049   or ranking losses, where a unit loss term depends on multiple data points and cannot be further
050
      decomposed into individual data point losses. We refer to such loss functions as non-decomposable
      losses.
051
052   To address this limitation, we revisit the general formulation of IF in statistics literature (Huber &
053   Ronchetti, 2009), which can extend beyond M-estimators. Specifically, statistical estimators are
      viewed as functionals of probability measures, and the IF is derived as a functional derivative in a


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      specific perturbation direction. In principle, this formulation applies to any estimator defined as the
055   minimizer of a loss function that depends on an (empirical) probability measure, which corresponds
056   to the learned parameters in the context of machine learning. However, directly applying this general
057   formulation to modern machine learning models poses significant challenges. Firstly, deriving the
058   precise IF for a particular loss function often requires complex, case-by-case mathetical derivations,
059   which can be challenging for intricate loss functions and models. Secondly, for non-convex models,
060   the (local) minimizer of the loss function is not unique; as a result, the mapping from the probability
061   measure to the learned model parameters is not well-defined, making it unclear how the IF should
062   be derived.
063   To overcome these challenges, we propose the Versatile Influence Function (VIF), a novel method
064   that extends IF-based data attribution to models trained with non-decomposable losses. The pro-
065   posed VIF serves as an approximation of the general formulation of IF but can be efficiently com-
066   puted using auto-differentiation tools available in modern machine learning libraries. This approach
067   eliminates the need for case-specific derivations of each loss function. Furthermore, like existing
068   IF-based data attribution methods, VIF does not require model retraining and can be generalized to
069
      non-convex models using similar heuristic tricks (Koh & Liang, 2017; Grosse et al., 2023).
070   We validate the effectiveness of VIF through both theoretical analysis and empirical experiments.
071   In special cases like M-estimation, VIF recovers the classical IF exactly. For Cox regression, we
072   show that VIF closely approximates the classical IF. Empirically, we demonstrate the practicality of
073   VIF across several tasks involving non-decomposable losses: Cox regression for survival analysis,
074   node embedding for network analysis, and listwise learning-to-rank for information retrieval. In all
075
      cases, VIF closely approximates the influence obtained from the brute-force leave-one-out retraining
      while significantly reducing computational time—achieving speed-ups of up to 103 times. We also
076
      provide case studies demonstrating VIF can help interpret the behavior of the models. By extending
077
      IF to non-decomposable losses, VIF opens new opportunities for data attribution in modern machine
078   learning models, enabling data-centric applications across a wider range of domains.

081   2    R ELATED W ORK
082
083   Data Attribution. Data attribution methods can be roughly categorized into two groups: retraining-
084   based and gradient-based methods (Hammoudeh & Lowd, 2024). Retraining-based methods (Ghor-
085   bani & Zou, 2019; Jia et al., 2019; Kwon & Zou, 2021; Wang & Jia, 2023; Ilyas et al., 2022) typically
086   estimate the influence of individual training data points by repeatedly retraining models on subsets
087   of the training dataset. While these methods have been shown effective, they are not scalable for
088   large-scale models and applications. In contrast, gradient-based methods (Koh & Liang, 2017; Guo
089   et al., 2020; Barshan et al., 2020; Schioppa et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2023; Yeh et al., 2018; Pruthi
090
      et al., 2020; Park et al., 2023) estimate the training data influence based on the gradient and higher-
      order gradient information of the original model, avoiding expensive model retraining. In particular,
091
      many gradient-based methods (Koh & Liang, 2017; Guo et al., 2020; Barshan et al., 2020; Schioppa
092
      et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2023; Pruthi et al., 2020; Park et al., 2023) can be viewed as variants of IF-
093   based data attribution methods. Therefore, extending IF-based data attribution methods to a wider
094   domains could lead to a significant impact on data attribution.
095
096   Influence Function in Statistics. The IF is a well-established concept in statistics dating back at
097   least to Hampel (1974), though it is typically applied for purposes other than data attribution. Orig-
098   inally introduced in the context of robust statistics, it was used to assess the robustness of statistical
099
      estimators (Huber & Ronchetti, 2009) and later adapted as a tool for developing asymptotic theo-
      ries (van der Vaart, 2012). Notably, IFs have been derived for a wide range of estimators beyond
100
      M-estimators, including L-estimators, R-estimators, and others (Huber & Ronchetti, 2009; van der
101
      Vaart, 2012). Closely related to an example of this study, Reid & Crepeau (1985) developed the IF
102   for the Cox regression model. However, the literature in statistics often approaches the derivation of
103   IFs through precise definitions specific to particular estimators, requiring case-specific derivations.
104   In contrast, this work proposes an approximation for the general IF formulation in statistics, which
105   can be straightforwardly applied to a broad family of modern machine learning loss functions for the
106   purpose of data attribution. While this approach involves some degree of approximation, it benefits
107   from being more versatile and computationally efficient, leveraging auto-differentiation capabilities
      provided by modern machine learning libraries.

      5    C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this work, we introduced the Versatile Influence Function (VIF), a novel method that extends
523
      IF-based data attribution to models trained with non-decomposable losses. The key idea behind
      VIF is a finite difference approximation of the general IF formulation in the statistics literature,
524
      which eliminates the need for case-specific derivations and can be efficiently computed with the
525
      auto-differentiation tools provided in modern machine learning libraries. Our theoretical analysis
526   demonstrates that VIF accurately recovers classical influence functions in the case of M-estimators
527   and provides strong approximations for more complex settings such as Cox regression. Empirical
528   evaluations across various tasks show that VIF closely approximates the influence obtained by brute-
529   force leave-one-out retraining while being orders-of-magnitude faster. By broadening the scope of
530   IF-based data attribution to non-decomposable losses, VIF opens new avenues for data-centric appli-
531   cations in machine learning, empowering practitioners to explore data attribution in more complex
532   and diverse domains.
533
      Limitation and Future Work. Similar to early IF-based methods for decomposable loss (Koh &
534   Liang, 2017), the formal derivation of VIF assumes convexity of the loss function, which requires
535   practical tricks to adapt the proposed method to large-scale neural network models. While we have
536   explored the application of Conjugate Gradient and LiSSA (Agarwal et al., 2017) for efficient in-
537   verse Hessian approximation (see Appendix C), more advanced techniques to stabilize and accel-
538   erate IF-based methods developed for decomposable losses, such as EK-FAC (Grosse et al., 2023),
539   ensemble (Park et al., 2023), or gradient projection (Choe et al., 2024), may be adapted to further
      enhance the practical applicability of VIF on large-scale models.

       C     E FFICIENT I NVERSE H ESSIAN A PPROXIMATION
1081
1082   Existing methods for efficient inverse Hessian approximation used by the conventional IF for de-
1083   composable losses can be adapted to accelerate VIF. Specifically, we consider two methods used
1084   by Koh & Liang (2017), Conjugate Gradient (CG) and LiSSA (Agarwal et al., 2017). The appli-
1085   cation of CG to VIF is straightforward, as it can be directly applied toPthe  original Hessian matrix.
                                                                                  n
1086   LiSSA is originally designed for decomposable losses in the form i=1 ℓi (θ) and it accelerates
1087   the inverse Hessian vector product calculation by sampling the Hessians of individual loss terms,
1088   ∇2θ ℓi (θ). The adaptation of LiSSA to VIF depends on the specific form of the loss function. In
1089   many non-decomposable losses (e.g., the all three examples in this paper), the total loss can still be
1090
       written as the summation of simpler loss terms, even though they are not decomposable to individ-
       ual data points. In such cases, LiSSA can still be applied to efficiently estimate the inverse Hessian
1091
       vector product through sampling the simpler loss terms.
