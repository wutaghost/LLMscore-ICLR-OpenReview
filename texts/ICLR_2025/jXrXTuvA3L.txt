011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Neural network-based methods for solving Mean-Field Games (MFGs) equilib-
014            ria have garnered significant attention for their effectiveness in high-dimensional
015            problems. However, many algorithms struggle with ensuring that the evolution
016            of the density distribution adheres to the required mathematical constraints. This
017            paper investigates a neural network approach to solving MFGs equilibria through
018
               a stochastic process perspective. It integrates process-regularized Normalizing
               Flow (NF) frameworks with state-policy-connected time-series neural networks
019
               to address McKean-Vlasov-type Forward-Backward Stochastic Differential Equa-
020
               tion (MKV FBSDE) fixed-point problems, equivalent to MFGs equilibria. First,
021            we reformulate MFGs equilibria as MKV FBSDEs, embedding the density dis-
022            tribution into the equation coefficients within a probabilistic framework. Neural
023            networks are then designed to approximate value functions and gradients derived
024            from these equations. Second, we employ NF architectures, a class of generative
025            neural network models, and impose loss constraints on each density transfer func-
026            tion to ensure volumetric invariance and time continuity. Additionally, this paper
027            presents theoretical proofs of the algorithm’s validity and demonstrates its appli-
028            cability across diverse scenarios, highlighting its superior effectiveness compared
029
               to existing methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Mean-Field Games (MFGs), introduced independently by Lasry & Lions (2007) and Huang et al.
034
      (2006), provide a robust framework for addressing large-scale multi-agent problems. MFGs are
035
      widely applied in domains such as autonomous driving, social networks, crowd management, and
036   power systems.
037
038   Neural network-based algorithms have recently been employed to solve MFGs equations due to their
039
      ability to handle high-dimensional problems effectively. For example, Lin et al. (2021) reformulated
      MFGs as a generative adversarial network (GAN) training problem, and Ruthotto et al. (2020) in-
040
      troduced a Lagrangian-based approach to approximate agent states through sampling. Additionally,
041
      Chen et al. (2023) applied reinforcement learning and neural networks to model distributions and
042   address value functions. Most approaches focus on optimizing the loss term in MFGs coupled
043   equations using sampled agents, but they often neglect density dynamics, leading to challenges in
044   representing continuous state density distributions.
045
      Carmona et al. (2018) introduced a stochastic process perspective on MFGs, leveraging McKean-
046
      Vlasov Forward-Backward Stochastic Differential Equations (MKV FBSDEs) to address MFGs
047
      equilibria, and explored numerical methods for solving them. Achdou & Lauriere (2015) proposed
048   simplified MFGs models for pedestrian dynamics and demonstrated them with numerical simula-
049   tions. Ren et al. (2024) studied multi-group MFGs by solving asymmetric Riccati differential equa-
050   tions and established sufficient conditions for the existence and uniqueness of optimal solutions.
051   However, existing MKV FBSDE methods are often limited to linear-quadratic MFGs, where dis-
052   tributions are simplified to the expectation of agents’ states, rather than full distribution functions,
053   in nonlinear settings. Recently, Huang et al. (2023) proposed a data-driven Normalizing Flow (NF)
      approach to solve distribution-involved optimal transport stochastic problems. Nevertheless, con-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      straints from MFGs process dynamics, terminal loss, and equation coupling limit the availability of
055   NF frameworks for high-dimensional MKV FBSDEs.
056
057   In summary, solving the MFGs equilibrium reduces to addressing equivalent stochastic fixed-point
058
      problems that incorporate distribution flows. NF-MKV Net is proposed to solve the MKV FBSDEs
      problem by coupling the process-regularized NF and state-policy-connected time series neural net-
059
      works. The enhanced NF framework models flows of probability measures, constructing a density
060
      distribution flow that satisfies volumetric invariance at each time step. State-policy-connected time
061   series neural networks, grounded in MKV FBSDEs, model relationships between time-step value
062   functions and approximate their gradients, enabling solutions in a time-consistent manner. Using
063   the coupled frameworks, the fixed-point distribution flow equivalent to the MFGs equilibrium can
064   be determined while ensuring mathematical constraints are satisfied.
065
      Contributions: The main contributions and results are summarized as follows:
066
067          • NF-MKV Net is proposed to solve MKV FBSDEs, which are equivalent to MFGs equilib-
068            rium, from a stochastic process perspective. By integrating process-regularized NFs and
069            state-policy-connected time series neural networks into a coupled framework with alternat-
070            ing training, the method adheres to volumetric invariance and time-continuity constraints.
071
             • Process-regularized NF frameworks are designed to model probability measure flows by
072
               enforcing loss constraints on each density transfer function, ensuring volumetric invariance
073            at each time step.
074
075          • State-policy-connected time series neural networks, built upon MKV FBSDEs, capture the
076
               relationships between time-step value functions and approximate their gradients, enabling
               time-consistent solutions.
077
078          • The method demonstrates applicability in traffic flow, low- and high-dimensional crowd
079            motion, and obstacle avoidance problems. Additionally, it satisfies mathematical con-
080            straints better than existing neural network-based approaches.

138   2.2   MFG S ↔ NF
139
140   NNormalizing Flows (NF), introduced by Tabak & Vanden-Eijnden (2010), enable exact computa-
141   tion of data likelihood through a sequence of invertible mappings. A key feature of NF is its use of
142   arbitrary bijective functions, achieved through stacked reversible transformations. The flow model
143
      R consists of a sequence of reversible flows, expressed as R(x) = r1 ◦ r2 ◦ · · · ◦ rL (x), where each
      ri has a tractable inverse and Jacobian determinant.
144
145   Our algorithm leverages the volume-preserving property of NF, aligning with the consistency of
146   density flow in MFGs during evolution. This principle is essential for constructing the density flow
147   in the MFGs model.
148   The connection between MFGs and NF provides inherent advantages. For example, in MFGs, the
149   initial distribution is often represented in a simple analytical form. This parallels NF’s approach,
150   where a simple initial distribution transforms into a more complex one for density estimation. Ad-
151   ditionally, one advantage of NF over other generative modelsRis its preservation of total density
152   during transformation, consistent with the MFGs requirement µdx = 1. A challenge, however,
153   is that MFGs, unlike Optimal Transport (OT), lacks both initial and terminal density distributions.
154   In MFGs, only an initial distribution exists, and the terminal condition is governed by the terminal
155
      value function g(x, µ(·, T )). This complicates framing the problem as a complete density evolution
      problem.
156
157   To address this, we idealize the MFGs model with the assumption that the terminal value function
158   corresponds to an explicitly solvable optimal terminal density. For example, in trajectory plan-
159   ning problems, the terminal value function is often related to the destination, such as g(x, µ) =
                       2
          −e−||x−xT || dµ(x). In such cases, we assume the optimal terminal distribution is µ(xT ) = 1,
      R

161   enabling the MFGs problem to be framed with initial and terminal densities. We will show that this
      assumption is reasonable.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY: NF-MKV N ET
163
164
      We propose NF-MKV Net, an alternately trained model combining NF and McKean-Vlasov
165
      Forward-Backward Stochastic Differential Equations (MKV FBSDEs), to address MFGs equilib-
166   rium problems. The main advantage of MKV FBSDEs is their ability to capture both optimization
167   and interaction components in a single coupled FBSDE, eliminating the need for separate references
168   to Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) and Fokker-Planck-Kolmogorov (FPK) equations. NF generates
169   flows represented by neural networks, constraining each density transfer function to define density
170   distributions at specific times. The density flow from NF couples with the value function of the
171   MFG. The value function constrains the neural network generating the flow via the HJB equation,
172   while its gradient update depends on the current marginal distribution flow.
173   First, we reformulate the stochastic equations of MFGs using MKV FBSDEs and approximate value
174   function gradients with neural networks, effectively addressing the curse of dimensionality in tra-
175   ditional numerical methods. Second, to address the distribution-coupled challenge, we use NF ar-
176   chitectures to model agents’ state density distributions, alternately training the unknown transition
177   processes with value functions. Figure (1) illustrates the framework of NF-MKV Net.

      C    T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
703
704
      The theoretical work of our designed algorithm are primarily reflected in using the Representation
705
      Theorem for Strong Formulation to guarantee that the neural network solution corresponds to the
706   equilibrium of Mean-Field Games (MFGs).
707
708   In fact, MFGs do not always have an equilibrium; its existence depends on the form of the value
709   function in the equations. According to the conditions for the existence of equilibrium in MFGs[1]
710
      and the Representation Theorem for Strong Formulation, the objective function must satisfy the
      Lipschitz continuous, continuously differentiable and convexity condition (see Appendix A). If an
711
      objective function that fails to meet these conditions is directly used as the network’s loss function,
712
      the resulting solution cannot be guaranteed to correspond to the equilibrium of the MFGs, even if
713   the network provides a solution. At the same time, the solution of the two equations also requires
714   iterative solving, as the MFGs achieves a fixed point through their iteration.
715
716
      We employ two networks to represent the forward and backward equations, respectively. The value
      function of each network is strongly tied to the objective function of the equation, following an
717
      approach similar to that of Han et al.[2] , which effectively represents the equations. And by designing
718
      the iterative training structure of the two networks, we characterize the iterative solution process of
719
      the two equations in the MKV FBSDE solution process.
720
721   These measures theoretically guarantee the existence and uniqueness of the equilibrium in the MFGs
722   system, ensuring that the solution produced by our algorithm is indeed the equilibrium.
723   In order to show the existence of solutions to the MKV FBSDEs and the equivalence with the MFGs
724   under the given Solvability HJB conditions for the MKV FBSDEs used, we give theoretical proofs
725   which can illustrate the validity of our proposed transformational approach to the equilibrium of the
726   MFGs.
727   The objective is to prove that, for a given initial condition, the FBSDE has a solution with a bounded
728   martingale integrand, and that this solution is unique within the class of solutions with bounded
729   martingale integrands. Meanwhile, we must also construct a decoupling field. Below shows the
730   theoretical analysis.
