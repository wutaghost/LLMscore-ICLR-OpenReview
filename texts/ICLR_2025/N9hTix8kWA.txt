011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The image of fast-moving objects (FMOs) usually contains a blur stripe indicating
014            the blurred object that is mixed with the background. To deblur the stripe and
015            separate the object from the background in the single image, in this work we
016
               propose a novel Latent Decomposition-Interpolation Network (LDINet) to generate
               the appearances and shapes of the objects. In particular, under the assumption that
017
               motion blur is an accumulation of the appearance of the object over exposure time
018
               and the long blur can be decomposed into several shorter blur parts, the blurry
019            input is first encoded into latent feature maps and then an efficient Decomposition-
020            Interpolation Module (DIM) is introduced to break down the feature maps into
021            discrete time indexed parts corresponding to different small blurs. And the target
022            latent frames are further interpolated according to the provided time indexes with
023            affine transformations, where the feature maps are categorized into the scalar-like
024            and gradient-like parts to effectively capture the intrinsic properties of features
025            warping in the interpolation. Finally, the sharp and clear images are rendered with
026            a decoder. In addition, based on the generated images by LDINet, a Refining
027
               Conditional Deblatting (RCD) approach is presented to use post-image-to-image
               techniques to further enhance the fidelity of the textures and the accuracy of the
028
               masks. Extensive experiments are conducted and have shown that the proposed
029
               methods achieves superior performances compared to the existing competing
030            methods.
031
032
      Motion deblurring is a special case of the deblurring task that aims to perform high-quality image
033
      restoration from a blurred one caused by the possible moving of the object or the camera. Conventional
034   methods Kupyn et al. (2018; 2019); Wieschollek et al. (2017); Sim & Kim (2019) for motion
035   deblurring mostly adopt a setting that recovers a single clear and sharp image in motion. Recently,
036   some works Jin et al. (2018); Purohit et al. (2019); Xu et al. (2021); Zhong et al. (2022); Rozumnyi
037   et al. (2021a); Zhong et al. (2023) further focus on the finer structures of the blur and learn to generate
038   a sequence of clear images of the object in a chronological order, which is known as sequence from
039   blur or single image temporal super-resolution task.
040   In this work, we focus on a special case of the sequence from blur task, i.e., deblatting (deblurring
041   and matting) of fast-moving objects (FMOs) Kotera et al. (2019). FMOs, first defined by Rozumnyi
042   et al. (2017), are moving objects that move over a distance greater than their size within the exposure
043   time of the camera in the scene. As a result, the blurry portion of a FMO becomes a stripe due to the
044   long-distance moving, which makes it hard to distinguish the object’s appearance. Kotera et al. (2019)
045   have further formulated this deblatting problem to accomplish two goals, i.e., image deblurring which
046   generates a sequence of clear and sharp images from the blurry input, and image matting which
047   separates the object in the scene from the background.
048   Formally, given a pair of pictures as input, including one background picture and one picture with
049   the blurred fast-moving object, our goal is to generate a sequence of sharp appearances and masks
050   for deblurring and matting according to the given time indexes. From the physical perspective,
051   the formation process of the motion blurred input can be formulated as a temporal integral of the
052   underlying consecutive sharp sub-images over a short exposure time. Since solving the integral is
053   difficult, several approximations have been proposed to simplify the formation model. Kotera &
      Šroubek (2018) approximate the blurring and matting formation model by convolving a fixed moving

069   blur kernel with the appearance and shape of the object. However, a simple convolution kernel cannot
070   capture the delicate variance of a moving object. To this end, TbD-3D Rozumnyi et al. (2020) has
071   approximated the integral with a piecewise linear model and leveraged energy minimization to solve
072   the deblatting task. Specifically, the blurring stripe is considered as the sum of several small blurs
073   according to a partition of the exposure time and each small blur is approximated by a linear motion
074   blur. Since the linear approximation is performed in the image space directly, it cannot well capture
075
      motions with rotation when the shapes of the FMOs are complex. Furthermore, the inference time
      consumption caused by the energy minimization method is usually prohibitively expensive.
076
077   On the other hand, DeFMO Rozumnyi et al. (2021a) has firstly proposed to solve the deblatting task
078   with a deep generative structure, which embeds the blurred input into a latent space representation. In
079   particular, it adopts shared latent embedding for different time index and directly concatenates the
080   specific time index with the embedding as intermediate features for decoding, as shown in Figure
081
      1 (a). With a large-scale training dataset, DeFMO has achieved better performance than previous
      methods. However, since large relative motions inevitably exist between images from different time
082
      indexes, such shared embedding setup might not fully capture the time-varying property of a moving
083
      object. This would limit the flexibility of generating high-quality sequences with motion trajectory
084   consistency.
085
086   To address the limitations mentioned above, we propose a novel Latent Decomposition-Interpolation
087
      Network (LDINet) to elaborately construct varying latent representations for different time indexes,
      which encourages to capture the inherent property of a moving object and helps generate the satisfying
088
      image sequence. In particular, a simple yet effective Decomposition-Interpolation Module (DIM) is
089
      introduced in the neural network model for FMOs deblatting, where the feature maps in the latent
090   space output by the encoder are decomposed into several latent parts according to a fine partition of
091   the exposure time, as shown in Figure 1 (b). For each time index in the exposure time interval, an
092   interpolation method with affine transformations is proposed to aggregate the adjacent parts into a
093   latent frame, which is decoded with a bi-branched decoder to predict the corresponding appearance
094   and mask on the time index. Here, the affine transformations are well-desgined to enforce motion
095   continuity and consistency for the generated sequence. Specifically, considering that the convolutional
096   encoder introduces different coordinate-dependent components in the feature maps and the processing
097   ways of these components are different under affine transformation, we propose to split the latent
098
      feature map into a scalar part and a gradient part for the decomposition-interpolation process, which
      helps release the full potential of affine transformation.
099
100   In addition, a Refining Conditional Deblatting (RCD) approach is presented by using post hoc
101   image-to-image method to further enhance the fidelity of the textures and the accuracy of the masks.
102   Specifically, this model uses the output preliminarily generated by LDINet as a condition to refine
103   the quality of the synthesized sequence. In particular, multi-scale feature maps extracted from the
104
      preliminary results are fused with those of the original background and the blurred fast-moving object
      pictures to guide the model in mask repair and consistency enhancement.
105
106   Extensive comparison experiments and ablation studies are conducted to show the effectiveness of
107   our designs for FMOs deblatting and our LDINet and RCD have achieved competitive performance
      compared to the existing competing methods.

140   The overview of LDINet. As shown in Figure 2, the proposed LDINet is composed of an encoder,

236   1.2   T HE STRUCTURES OF THE LDIN ET
237
238   The structure of the Encoder. The structure of the encoder is based on the ResNet-50 He et al.
239   (2016) with the nuance that we only take the first three downsampling blocks and extend the last
240
      block with five ResNet bottlenecks. The channel number of the feature map generated by the encoder
      is 1024.
241
242   The structure of the Decoder. As shown in Figure 2, the decoder is composed of several shared
243   layers and two convolutional branches. To be specific, the shared layers are two residual blocks. Each
244   residual block is followed by a pixel shuffle layer Shi et al. (2016) which up-scales the spatial size of
245   the latent frame by a factor of two. The output channel numbers of the residual blocks are 256 and
246
      64, respectively. Given the up-scaled latent frame, we use two convolutional branches to estimate the
      RGB channels for appearance and the alpha channel for mask respectively. These two branches are
247
      similar in structure. In each branch, we first use a 3 × 3-convolution layer with 64 output channels.
248
      Then a pixel shuffle layer is applied to up-scale the size of the feature maps by a factor of two. Finally,
249   the feature maps go through two convolutional layers with the numbers of output channels being 16
250   and 4 respectively and are transformed into outputs with the last layer.
251
252
      The structure of DIM. Here we introduce the network structure in DIM, including the projector and
      the AffNet. The projector is a ResNet bottleneck block and its number of output channels is 512m
253
      where m is the number of the output parts. The AffNet accepts an input with 1024 channels which is
254
      concatenated by two latent parts. The structure of AffNet is shown in Figure 3 (a). The first ResNet
255   bottleneck block reduces the channel number to 64. And the rest three ResNet bottleneck blocks keep
256   the channel number unchanged. Finally, the predictor first reduces the channel number from 64 to 16
257   with the first 3 × 3-convolution layer. After a ReLU activation layer, the second 3 × 3-convolution
258   layer predicts 6 parameters for each affine transformation.

260   1.3   T HE TRAINING LOSS
261
262   In this section, we introduce the training objectives of our LDINet, which can be divided into two
263   categories according to the space where the constraints are performed, i.e., the image and the latent
264
      space. In particular, in the image space, a reconstruction loss LR is introduced to reconstruct the
      masks, the appearances, and the blurry input. Besides, a sharpness loss LS is employed to sharpen
265
      the masks. As for the latent space, three objectives LL , Lid , and LC are introduced to encourage
266
      the feature invariance to different backgrounds, stabilize the training of the AffNet, and improve the
267   feature consistency between adjacent latent parts, respectively.
268

369
      The synthesized dataset for training is based on the training dataset of DeFMO Rozumnyi et al.

      by a 3D object moving through a 6D linear trajectory over two background sequences and consists

      of two backgrounds for background reduction, one FMOs blur stripe for the construction of blurry

374   backgrounds are employed here. The 3D objects are sampled from ShapeNet Chang et al. (2015)
375   dataset applied with DTD Cimpoi et al. (2014) textures. The backgrounds for training are sampled

      validation.

      2.2   E VALUATION DATASET
379
380   The evaluation datasets are three real-world datasets from the FMOs deblatting benchmark Rozumnyi
381   et al. (2021a):
382
      TbD Kotera et al. (2019) is composed of 12 sports sequences with uniformly colored and mostly
383
      spherical objects. Each sequence contains 16 ~ 60 frames.
384
385   TbD-3D Rozumnyi et al. (2020) is composed of 10 sequences and contains objects with complex
386   textures, which makes it more difficult. Each sequence contains 37 ~ 81 frames. The rotations of the
387
      objects result in significant differences in their appearances. One limitation is that the objects are
      mostly spherical, so their shapes remain constant when rotated.
388
389   Falling Objects Kotera et al. (2020) is composed of 6 sequences and is the most challenging
390   benchmark with objects of complex textures and 3D shapes. Each sequence contains 11 ~ 22 frames.
391   For each dataset, the low-speed sequences are created by averaging over the full exposure high-speed
392   ground truths. The ground truths have a frame rate that is 8 times higher than that of the low-speed
393   sequences.
394
395   2.3   T RAINING SETTINGS
396
397   The training of LDINet contains two stages, a warm-up stage and a finetuning stage. In the warm-up
398   stage, we aim to train the AffNet and provide a guidance to disentangle the original latent part into
399   the scalar part and the gradient part. In particular, since DIM is not well trained at beginning, we use
400   the interpolation method with weighted summation over the latent parts during the warm-up. To train
401
      the AffNet, we first introduce a pseudo input by applying a small random affine transformation to the
      FMOs blur stripe in the image space. Then the latent parts of the original input and the pseudo input
402
      are fed into the AffNet to estimate this random affine transformation. Furthermore, a consistency
403
      is introduced to constraint between the transformation in the image space and the transformation in
404   the latent space. Please refer to Appendix B for more details. In the finetuning stage, we train the
405   model for 20 epochs in total. For the first 10 epochs, we use the learning rate lr = 1e − 4 and set
406   αC = 0.01. The learning rate is reduced to 1e − 5 and αC = 0 is set for the rest 10 epochs. As for
407   the refining version RCD method, we use the parameters of the LDINet to initialize the conditional
408   model and train the model for 25 epochs. The learning rate is reduced from 1e − 4 to 1e − 6 with
409   cosine annealing Loshchilov & Hutter (2016). During the training processes, the part number of
410   DIM is m = 16, and the kernel size of the average kernel Kavg is 11 × 11. We use Adam optimizer
411   Kingma & Ba (2015) with batch size 24. The model is trained on 8 Nvidia A5000 GPUs and the total
412
      training time is about 1.5 days for LDINet. The average results of three runs are reported.

531   3    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose a new LDINet for single image FMOs deblatting. In particular, we introduce
534   a decomposition-interpolation module in the latent space which first decomposes the feature maps
535   into several latent parts to incorporate the prior of the temporal sequential structure into the deblatting
536   process and then aggregates the adjacent parts with affine transformations to properly interpolate
537   the target latent frames for each time index. Further, we present a refining conditional deblatting
538   method based on the results of LDINet to further enhance the output quality. Extensive experiments
539   are conducted and the evaluation results show that our LDINet and RCD have achieved superior
      performances in most cases when compared with the existing methods.

      C    Q UALITATIVE COMPARISONS
717
718   In Figure 6, we show the qualitative results of different methods for the object cell in Falling Objects
719
      dataset, pen in Falling Objects dataset, and volleyball in TbD-3D dataset. From the figure, we
      first observe that comparing to DeFMO, the proposed LDINet and RCD provide better appearance
720
      reconstruction results for the FMOs. Specifically, in the results of DeFMO, there exists certain
721
      artifacts around the object, which might come from the inaccurate estimation of the masks. Moreover,
722   the appearance of the object across different frames are quite similar in DeFMO, which indicates that
723   DeFMO may be incapable of well modeling the appearance change of the object during the motion.
724   In contrast, with a well-designed decomposition-interpolation scheme, the proposed methods would
725   better capture the motion of the object and also produce a more accurate estimation for the mask. On
726   the other hand, the comparison results between two interpolation methods (i.e., affine transformation
727   v.s. linear interpolation) show the effectiveness of affine transformation in aggregation for latent
728   frames. In particular, the artifacts in the results of linear interpolation based method become heavier
729   near the two ends of the exposure time period, such as the frame 0 and 7, because of the lack of
730
      information. However, the affine transformation based method would always provide high-quality
      results, due to a more appropriate way for modeling the latent parts and aggregating the information
731
      from adjacent latent parts to generate the target latent frames.
