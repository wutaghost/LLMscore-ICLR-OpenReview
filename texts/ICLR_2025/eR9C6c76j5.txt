009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks excel in mapping genomic DNA sequences to associated
012            readouts (e.g., protein–DNA binding). Beyond prediction, the goal of these net-
013            works is to reveal to scientists the underlying motifs (and their syntax) which drive
014            genome regulation. Traditional methods that extract motifs from convolutional
015            filters suffer from the uninterpretable dispersion of information across filters and
016            layers. Other methods which rely on importance scores can be unstable and un-
017            reliable. Instead, we designed a novel mechanistically interpretable architecture
018
               for regulatory genomics, where motifs and their syntax are directly encoded and
               readable from the learned weights and activations. We provide theoretical and
019
               empirical evidence of our architecture’s full expressivity, while still being highly
020
               interpretable. Through several experiments, we show that our architecture excels in
021            de novo motif discovery and motif instance calling, is robust to variable sequence
022            contexts, and enables fully interpretable generation of novel functional sequences.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Transcription factors (TFs) are proteins that regulate genes by recognizing and binding to specific
028   short DNA sequence patterns—or “motifs”—in the genome (Lambert et al., 2018; Siggers & Gordân,
029   2014). High-throughput experiments measure regulatory activity—such as protein–DNA binding or
030   associated readouts—across the genome (Consortium, 2012). In general, the regulatory function of a
031
      DNA sequence is defined by the combination of motifs in that sequence, and these combinations have
      a soft syntax (i.e., density, spacing, orientation, and co-binders). These syntactical rules also depend
032
      on surrounding context and cell type. Although challenging, understanding the motifs (and their
033
      syntax) which regulate the genome is crucial for many scientific and medical tasks, such as disease
034   diagnosis and design of novel therapies (e.g., CRISPR). There is thus a great need for techniques
035   which perform de novo motif discovery (i.e., extracting motifs and syntax from these experiments).
036
037
      Recently, de novo motif discovery has been chiefly performed by sequence-to-function deep neural
      networks (DNNs). These DNNs take a DNA sequence as an input, and predict a functional label
038
      measured by a biological experiment (e.g., a binary label denoting if a TF bound to that sequence).
039
      These expressive models have achieved state-of-the-art performance in mapping DNA sequences to
040   regulatory labels, thus proving that they accurately learn underlying motifs and their syntax (Kelley
041
      et al., 2016; Žiga Avsec et al., 2021b; Linder et al., 2023). The common goal of these DNNs is to
042   ultimately reveal these learned motifs and syntax that underpin genome regulation (Novakovsky et al.,
043   2022a). Notably, recent work has shown that motif discovery from these sequence-to-function DNNs
044   has far surpassed the ability of traditional statistical methods (Tseng, 2022).
045
      There are two main classes of methods that extract motifs from a trained DNN. As these DNNs are
046
      almost universally convolution-based in early layers (Novakovsky et al., 2022a), a very common
047
      approach is to extract a motif from each first-layer filter (Kelley et al., 2016). This method, however,
048   suffers from the critical limitation that information (including motifs) tends to be distributed or
049   dispersed across filters and layers, thus there is no guarantee that any single filter will learn a
050   biologically meaningful motif (Figure 2a). The second class of methods relies on importance scores,
051   which attempt to measure the contribution of individual DNA bases to the output prediction, with
052   the assumption that bases in motifs have elevated importance. By integrating throughout the whole
053   DNN, importance scores bypass the problem of distributed information. Unfortunately, importance
      scores are known to be highly unstable and unreliable approximations of a model’s decision making

054
      (Ghorbani et al., 2017; Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018), and in genomic DNNs, importance scores
055   typically only show noisy and fragile motif instances (Supplementary Figure S1) (Tseng et al., 2020).
056   In practice, to improve robustness, motif discovery via importance scores requires complex pipelines
057   composed of many computationally expensive steps, which tend to be delicate and require constant
058   human intervention (Novakovsky et al., 2022a; Shrikumar et al., 2018).
059
      In this work, we propose a new method of recovering motifs from a sequence-to-function DNN,
060
      based on mechanistic interpretability. Mechanistic interpretability (MI) has recently emerged as a
061   key research direction to explain complex models (Bereska & Gavves, 2024). Our method, Analysis
062   of Regulatory Genomics via Mechanistically Interpretable Neural Networks (ARGMINN),
063   enables motifs and their syntax to be directly readable from the network architecture, without
064   compromising expressivity or relying on complex post hoc pipelines. In Section 3, we formally
065   describe the ARGMINN architecture, including several novel architectural contributions including:
066   1) a regularizer designed to ensure that the first layer’s convolutional-filter weights directly encode a
067   non-redundant set of relevant motifs; and 2) a modified attention mechanism which reveals motif
068   instances and their syntax in any query sequence with a single forward pass.
069   In Section 4, we show experimental results which demonstrate ARGMINN’s interpretability and
070   its main contributions to regulatory genomics, including: 1) superior motif discovery and motif-
071   instance/syntax analysis compared to existing approaches for de novo motif discovery; 2) robustness
072   against natural or adversarial sequence modifications; and 3) the novel ability for fully interpretable
073   sequence generation. In Section 5, we provide theoretical results on ARGMINN’s expressivity,
074   showing that it can learn any motifs and syntax, whereas previous MI architectures cannot.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
077
078
079
      Practically all prevalent genomic DNN architectures have convolutional filters as the first layer (Kelley
080   et al., 2016; Žiga Avsec et al., 2021b; Linder et al., 2023). To recover motifs, most works directly
081
      visualize the filter weights or average subsequences which highly activate each filter (Alipanahi et al.,
      2015; Kelley et al., 2016). Although this has shown some limited success, these methods assume
082
      that each filter learns one motif, and each motif is learned by one filter. This is generally not true
083
      because—without special constraints—motifs are typically learned in a distributed fashion, where
084   each motif is learned across many filters and layers (Figure 2a).
085
086   As a result, more sophisticated post hoc pipelines were developed to extract motifs from trained
087
      DNNs. These pipelines integrate over the entire DNN to compute importance scores across the dataset
      (e.g., via integrated gradients or DeepLIFTShap (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Shrikumar et al., 2017)),
088
      resulting in an importance score at each position for each sequence. These scores are then segmented
089
      into high-importance regions as putative motif instances. Due to the noisiness of importance scores,
090   however, these instances must be clustered into clean motifs by tools like MoDISco (Shrikumar
091   et al., 2018). Each step of this pipeline is computationally expensive, and for most datasets, the time
092   required to recover motifs is typically over an order of magnitude longer than the time needed to
093   train the model. Furthermore, these pipelines heavily rely on importance scores from a black-box
094   model, which can be extremely fragile, as importance scores frequently fail to reveal a model’s true
095   decision-making process (Ghorbani et al., 2017; Kindermans et al., 2017; Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola,
096   2018; Tseng et al., 2020).
097   Within the field of explainable AI, there has been some burgeoning work exploring MI, where the
098   patterns and rules learned by a DNN are reflected in its physical computation (Bereska & Gavves,
099   2024). In particular, our work is a type of intrinsic MI, where the DNN’s architecture (e.g., weights
100   and activations) directly encode learning (Liu et al., 2023; Barbiero et al., 2023; Kasioumis et al.,
101   2021). This is typically done by increasing sparsity, modularity, and the proportion of monosemantic
102   neurons (i.e., neurons which learn a single concept). DNNs may also improve their intrinsic MI by
103   learning logical rules in a more structured way (Riegel et al., 2020; Friedman et al., 2023).
104   Despite these promising works, constructing intrinsically MI architectures for very general problems
105   remains difficult. However, focusing on more constrained predictive tasks (e.g., motif discovery)
106   makes MI more feasible, allowing us to restrict computation and the solution space. ARGMINN uses
107   similar principles as other intrinsic MI works (e.g., sparsity, modularity, monosemanticity, and explicit
      logic), but in a biologically grounded manner, making it both highly interpretable and expressive.

129
130   Recently, the ExplaiNN architecture also aimed to interpretably learn motifs from DNA sequences
131   (Novakovsky et al., 2022b). ExplaiNN consists of a set of single-filter convolutional networks. Each
132   network ideally learns a distinct motif, and outputs a scalar summarizing binding strength over the
133   input. The final output is a learned linear combination of these scalars. In later sections, we will show
134   that ARGMINN surpasses ExplaiNN in both interpretability and expressivity.
135   Our work also relates to concept bottleneck models (CBMs) (Koh et al., 2020; Ghorbani et al., 2019;
136   Kim et al., 2017), which force decisions to be based on interpretable concepts from an intermediate
137   layer. While CBMs can provide meaningful explanations, they require pre-defined concepts and
138   concept-labeled inputs, which are labor-intensive to obtain. In our work, ARGMINN can be viewed as
139   a type of CBM, but the concepts are motifs, and the concepts are learned entirely from the data, thus
140   overcoming the typical limitations of CBMs. Furthermore, ARGMINN elucidates syntax between
141
      motifs—rules relating to positioning between concepts—in addition to the motifs themselves.

143   3     ARGMINN A RCHITECTURE
144
145   We propose a mechanistically interpretable DNN architecture designed to 1) accurately predict
146   regulatory function (e.g., protein binding) from DNA sequence; 2) reveal crucial motifs responsible
147   for function across the dataset; and 3) reveal motif instances and their syntax in any given query
148   sequence. Importantly, 2) and 3) are directly encoded in the model’s weights and activations.
149
      Our architecture consists of two modules trained end to end (Figure 1a). The motif-scanner module
150
      is a single convolutional layer which identifies motifs from the input sequence. We developed a novel
151   regularizer for the filters so that the filter weights directly encode non-redundant motifs, thereby
152   accomplishing goal 2) above. The convolutional activations are passed to the second module—the
153   syntax builder—consisting of several layers of modified attention that assemble the syntax and logic
154   between motifs to produce a final prediction. The syntax builder is designed such that the activations
155   and attention scores immediately reveal motif instances and syntax, thus achieving goal 3).
156

223   4     E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
224
225   In this section, we show experimental results demonstrating ARGMINN’s advantages in motif
226   discovery, motif instance calling, robustness, and interpretable generation.
227
228   4.1   I MPROVED MOTIF DISCOVERY
229
230
      To extract motifs from ARGMINN, we applied the procedure from Kelley et al. (2016) to the first-
      layer convolutional filters. Specifically, we obtained a motif from each filter by averaging test-set
231
      subsequences which highly activate the filter (filters which were never activated in the test set were
232
      dropped) (Supplementary Methods C.4).
233
234   Over several simulated and real-world experimental datasets (Supplementary Methods C.1), we
235   compared the motifs discovered by ARGMINN to those identified by several other methods: inter-
236
      preting the filters of a standard CNN, ExplaiNN (Novakovsky et al., 2022b), and importance-score
      clustering via DeepLIFTShap and MoDISco (Shrikumar et al., 2018). We systematically matched
237
      each discovered motif to the closest known relevant motif. For simulated datasets, we matched
238
      to ground-truth motifs; for experimental datasets, we matched to the closest relevant human mo-
239   tif. ARGMINN identified known, biologically relevant motifs, and compared to other methods, it
240   generally missed the fewest relevant motifs and discovered the fewest redundant motifs (Figure
241   2a–b, Supplementary Figures S2–S5, Supplementary Table S1). For example, ARGMINN trained
242   on FOXA2 in HepG2 (a pioneer factor) revealed factors in the FOX, HNF4, and CEBP families, all
243   known to co-localize or co-bind with FOXA (Seachrist et al., 2021; Geusz et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
244   2020). Notably, in the experimental datasets, the singular most similar motif (an extremely strict
245   requirement) to an ARGMINN filter is typically a known relevant motif (e.g., on this FOXA dataset,
246   ARGMINN identified 4 motifs whose top match was a relevant motif, whereas the traditional CNN
247
      only identified 2, with much weaker similarities). Additionally, ARGMINN’s motifs were generally
      most similar to the ground truth (Figure 2c, Supplementary Table S2), thus highlighting their quality.
248
      ARGMINN was also the method which consistently identified the fewest extraneous motifs—patterns
249
      which do not match any biologically relevant motif (Figure 2d, Supplementary Table S6).
250
251   In contrast, other methods significantly underperformed compared to ARGMINN. Even when the
252   standard CNN or ExplaiNN encoded motifs in their filters, ARGMINN’s filters were far more similar
253
      to the true motifs. ARGMINN also outperformed MoDISco (Shrikumar et al., 2018), which—despite
      recovering many relevant motifs—consistently identified less-accurate motifs than ARGMINN
254
      (Figure 2c). Due to the unreliability and noisiness of the importance scores themselves, as well as the
255
      frailty of clustering, MoDISco also found many redundancies and extraneous motifs (Figure 2b, 2d).

      4.2   I MPROVED MOTIF INSTANCE CALLING AND SYNTAX DISCOVERY
258
259   With previous DNN-based methods, identifying motif syntax required first learning motifs (e.g., via
260   MoDISco) and then scanning sequences to “call” motif instances. Not only is this computationally
261   expensive, but instance calling by sequence scanning tends to be highly inaccurate (e.g., due to partial
262   hits or missing context which the DNN would have considered).
263

264   on any sequence (Figure 3a). Since every attention layer derives keys/values directly from the

266   network deemed important for prediction. Specifically, for any input query sequence, we examine the
267   attention scores across all layers/heads on the forward pass. For each high score (e.g., > 0.9), we
268   identify the corresponding sequence position. We then check the motif activations at that position,
269   and call a motif instance if a motif/filter has high activation (Supplementary Methods C.4). For
      example, trained on an experimental dataset of REST binding, ARGMINN directly recovered the

407   4.4   ROBUSTNESS OF ARGMINN
408
409   Because ARGMINN makes decisions based on biologically meaningful motifs, it is more robust to
410   background variations. On several simulated datasets, we trained ARGMINN (along with a standard
411
      CNN and ExplaiNN) with a 50% GC background. We then computed predictive performance on test
      sequences with GC content ranging from 5% to 95%, thus simulating natural variation in background
412
      composition. ARGMINN’s performance suffered the least, with a higher and tighter distribution of
413
      performance in general (Supplementary Figure S8a).
414
415   Furthermore, because traditional CNNs learn filters which do not accurately represent motifs, they
416   are prone to adversarial attacks (Supplementary Methods C.4). On our SPI1 dataset, we trained
417
      a CNN to achieve 88% accuracy. We then easily constructed many sequences containing short
      substrings which highly activate its filters, but without any instances of the SPI1 motif. On this set
418
      of sequences, the standard CNN’s accuracy dropped to 55%. Reflexively, we also easily designed
419
      sequences containing the SPI1 motif, but we inserted substrings into the background which strongly
420   deactivate the filters, leading the CNN’s accuracy to drop to 48%. In contrast, because ARGMINN
421   encodes meaningful biological motifs in each filter, it remains robust against such an attack (i.e., one
422   cannot easily identify highly-activating or anti-activating sequences which trick the model into giving
423   the wrong prediction).
424
      Finally, we used Ledidi (Schreiber et al., 2020) to generate sequences which would bind to SPI1.
425
      Ledidi is a gradient-based sequence-design method, which optimizes input sequences to maximize
426   the probability of a positive prediction from a given model. Applying Ledidi on the ARGMINN
427   architecture generated sequences with the strongest instances of the true SPI1 motif (Supplementary
428   Figure S8b). The true binding strength of ARGMINN’s Ledidi-generated instances was signif-
429   icantly higher than those generated from the standard CNN (p = 8.95 × 10−18 ) and ExplaiNN
430   (p = 5.53 × 10−146 ). This is likely due to the fact that ARGMINN is explicitly trained to focus on
431   the relevant motifs, whereas the other models often focus on spurious, non-motif sequence patterns,
      resulting in ARGMINN’s being a more robust oracle for optimization.

480   5     T HEORETICAL RESULTS
481
482   For a MI architecture, ARGMINN is highly interpretable, yet uniquely retains high expressivity. Here,
483
      we show that ARGMINN is capable of learning any possible configuration of motifs—including
      positional and syntactic constraints—as long as the constraints are definable in first-order logic.
484
485   Theorem 1. Every configuration of motifs/subsequences which is definable by a sentence in first-order
      logic (with positional variables) is recognizable by an ARGMINN classifier.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Furthermore, ARGMINN is more expressive than previous interpretable architectures for regulatory
487   genomics, such as ExplaiNN (Novakovsky et al., 2022b), which learns an output label from a linear
488   combination of motif strengths:
489
      Corollary 1.1. There exists a configuration of first-order-logic-expressable motifs/subsequences
490
      which is not recognizable by any ExplaiNN classifier.
491
492   We formally prove Theorem 1 and Corollary 1.1 in Appendix A. Here, we briefly sketch the proof
493   of Theorem 1. Intuitively, ARGMINN’s motif-scanner module outputs motif binding strengths by
494   encoding each motif’s position-weight matrix (Benos et al., 2002) in a different filter. With expressive
495   positional encodings, the syntax-builder module learns syntax/interactions between motif instances.
496   Each attention head learns one syntactical “rule” (i.e., possible motif combinations), which is built up
497   in complexity over layers (i.e., combinations of k motifs are learned by the kth layer).
498
499   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL FOLLOW- UP TO THEORETICAL RESULTS
500
      To empirically reinforce our theoretical results, we consider the REST binding motif, which consists
501
      of two halves which must bind together (with variable spacings) (Figure 3b). Notably, both halves
502
      need to bind in the same orientation and order (half sites cannot be mixed/matched). On a simulated
503   REST dataset which explicitly tests these complex requirements, ARGMINN achieved 90.9% test-set
504   accuracy, whereas ExplaiNN only achieved 73.4%. This demonstrates that ARGMINN is sufficiently
505   expressive to capture complex grammars and syntax between motifs, which previous architectures
506   could not.
507
508
      6     D ISCUSSION
509
510
      We illustrated ARGMINN’s unique ability to reveal genome-regulatory motifs and their syntax
511
      directly from its weights and activations—an advantage which is entirely absent from traditional
512   (non-MI) DNNs. We then compared the predictive performance of ARGMINN to standard CNNs
513   and ExplaiNN (Supplementary Table S5). It is generally well known that more interpretable models
514   may suffer slightly in predictive performance (Dosilovic et al., 2018; Arrieta et al., 2020). This
515   is expected, as these models tend to base decisions on human-interpretable concepts (e.g., crucial
516   motifs), instead of spurious signals which can be informative, but are not useful for understanding
517   regulatory genomics (e.g., GC content, exceptionally rare motifs, etc.). Our empirical results fall in
518   line with these expectations. On simulated datasets where we ensured that the only informative signals
519   are motifs, ARGMINN achieved superior performance compared to the baselines. On experimental
520
      datasets with many more spurious signals, ARGMINN achieved competitive performance, but did
      not outperform its non-MI counterparts.
521
522   Additionally, to further show the benefit of our filter regularizer, we applied our regularization to
523   a standard CNN. As a result, the CNN’s filters also encoded relevant and non-redundant motifs
524   (Supplementary Figure S10). This shows that our filter-overlap regularizer can be readily applied
525   to standard genomic DNNs to achieve more interpretable first-layer filters in general. Importantly,
526
      however, without the other architectural novelties of ARGMINN (Equation 3), such a DNN would
      still not reap benefits such as motif-instance and syntax discovery.
527
528   Finally, we explored the robustness of ARGMINN to the loss weights (which are hyperparameters) for
529   our regularizers. We found that over many orders of magnitude, ARGMINN’s predictive performance
530   and interpretability both remained robust to the loss weights (Supplementary Figure S11). This
531   is partially due to the design of these regularizers, which are aimed to synergize with predictive
532
      performance and the learning of biological motifs, rather than compete with other losses.
533   Mechanistic interpretability is still a nascent field, and our research pioneers an architecture that
534   enables direct interpretation with minimal post hoc analysis. To our knowledge, ARGMINN is the
535   most MI (yet still fully expressive) architecture for genome regulation—a field where understanding a
536   model’s learned decision rules is equally as critical as its accuracy (Eraslan et al., 2019; Rudin, 2019)—
537   and is one of the few intrinsically MI architectures of its complexity in general. We showed that
538
      ARGMINN is expressive enough to accurately predict genome regulation, yet is uniquely constrained
      so the weights and activations directly encode the decision process in a human-interpretable way.
539
      Further work in this area can yield major benefits for scientific AI and explainable AI.

      A     S UPPLEMENTARY P ROOFS

      A.1   P ROOF OF T HEOREM 1
759
760   In this section, we prove that every configuration of motifs (which is definable by a first-order-logical
761   sentence with position-indexed variables) is recognizable by an ARGMINN classifier. We will first
762   show that ARGMINN’s motif-scanner module is sufficiently expressive to characterize motif-based
763   protein binding, and then subsequently that the syntax-builder module can recognize arbitrary logical
764   syntax between these binding sites.
765   Biophysical assumptions
766
767
      We begin with our biophysical assumptions which justify our modeling of motif biology by first-order
      logic (with position-indexed variables).
768
769
            1. For binary biological readouts of interest (e.g., protein-binding measured by ChIP-seq, DNA
770            accessibility measured by DNase-seq, etc.), the readout is fully characterized by the binding
771            of proteins, which recognize motifs in the sequence (Stormo & Fields, 1998; Lemon &
772            Tjian, 2000).
773
            2. The free concentration of any particular protein is constant across training and testing
774
               conditions.
775
776
            3. For a given potential binding site (e.g., a motif instance in a DNA sequence), the strength
               and likelihood of binding of a specific protein (these quantities are related through statistical
777
               mechanics) can be sufficiently summarized by a single scalar value (related to the Kd , or
778
               dissociation constant). Through statistical mechanics, the fraction/probability of binding is

803   Motif-scanner module
804
805   Given the above biophysical assumptions, the motif-scanner module’s convolutional filters are
806
      sufficiently expressive to capture the binding strengths/likelihoods of each position for each potential
      binding motif.
807
808   We simply design this module to have nf filters equal to the number of unique motifs m in σ, with
809   width w equal to the maximum width of any motif. We set the multiplicative weights of each filter
      with the PWM of each motif, so that this module precisely implements the PWM-scanning procedure

811   bias to be 0. Note that if the convolutional filters W are all PWMs, then the multiplicative weights
812   are all non-negative, and so the pre-ReLU activation will also be non-negative.
813

      for each possible motif. In other words, this module outputs bm,p for each relevant motif m, at each

817   Syntax-builder module

819   particular, our model will produce a pre-sigmoid output which is positive if and only if σ is true.

927   A.2    P ROOF OF C OROLLARY 1.1
928
929
      Here, we prove that there exists a configuration of motifs specified by first-order logic, which is not
      recognizable by ExplaiNN.
930
931   Consider a dataset of sequences defined by the presence of distinct motifs A, B, C, D. Every sequence
932   has exactly two instances of such motifs. A positive sequence is defined by having both A and B,
933   or both C and D. A negative sequence is defined by any other combination: AC, BD, AD, or BC.
934   This constructed example is a realistic one, as it is the binding rule seen in transcription factors with
935
      two half sites (e.g., REST (Tang et al., 2021)), or co-factor binding where the motifs and transcription
      factors are unidirectional (e.g., JUND and TEAD).
936
937   Within ExplaiNN, each CNN “unit” learns the presence of one such motif, and returns a scalar score.
938   Let there be four CNN units, one for each motif. For each input sequence, we obtain four such scores:
939   sA , sB , sC , sD .
940   Each CNN unit consists of a single convolutional filter whose activation is maximized by the motif
941   it learns. For simplicity, we assume that given a convolutional filter that learns substring X, the
942   distribution of activations of that filter on background sequences is identical to the distribution of
943   activations on non-X motifs. This could be realized, for example, by motifs whose composition is
944   base/letter-wise distributed identically to the background (e.g., uniform). Thus, for a convolutional
945   filter which recognizes X, the distribution of its activations is identical across all non-X substrings.
946   Given sufficiently long sequences, the CNN unit for some motif X will output a scalar score as follows:
947
      if X is in the sequence, sX = pX , a score for “positives”; if X is not present, sX = nX ̸= pX , a
      score for “negatives”.
948
949   Given these CNN units, suppose it is possible to distinguish positive and negative sequences with a
950   linear combination, as in ExplaiNN. The output of the model is wA sA + wB sB + wC sC + wD sD + β
951   for scalar weights wA , . . . , wD and bias β.
952   For the classifier to be sufficiently expressive, we require that positive examples have a final output
953   that is at least some threshold τ , and negative examples to have an output that is strictly less than τ .
954
      Thus, we have the following inequalities (one for each possible pair of motifs):
955
956
957                                 wA pA + wB pB + wC nC + wD nD + β ≥ τ
958                                 wA nA + wB nB + wC pC + wD pD + β ≥ τ
959                                 wA pA + wB nB + wC pC + wD nD + β < τ
960                                 wA pA + wB nB + wC nC + wD pD + β < τ
961                                 wA nA + wB pB + wC pC + wD nD + β < τ
962                                 wA nA + wB pB + wC nC + wD pD + β < τ
963
964
      Adding inequalities of the same type:
965
966                             wA (pA + nA ) + · · · + wD (pD + nD ) ≥ 2(τ − β)
967                            2wA (pA + nA ) + · · · + 2wD (pD + nD ) < 4(τ − β)
968
      This is a contradiction, as this requires wA (pA + nA ) + · · · + wD (pD + nD ) to be both at least
969
      2(τ − β) and strictly less than 2(τ − β).
970
971   Thus, ExplaiNN is not sufficiently expressive to capture every configuration of motifs expressable in
      first-order logic (e.g., exclusive disjunctions).

       C.2    M ODEL ARCHITECTURES
1732
1733   Our ARGMINN architecture consists of two modules: motif scanners and a syntax builder. The motif
1734   scanners consist of a single convolutional layer of nf filters, each of w bp in width (typical values are
1735   nf = 8, w = 10), which scan across a one-hot-encoded DNA sequence. The result is passed to a
1736   ReLU, and the output constitutes the “motif activations”.
1737   The motif activations are concatenated with a positional encoding of dimension d = 16. Our
1738   positional encoding is defined as follows:
1739
1740                                               i                                i
1741                             Pi,2j = sin(           2j   ), Pi,2j+1 = cos(          2j   )
                                                10000   d                        10000 d
1742
1743   where i is the position along the sequence, and j ∈ {0, . . . , d2 − 1}.
1744
1745
       Let A∥P be the motif activations and positional encodings concatenated together. This is passed to
       two consecutive memory-stream-based attention layers. The lth attention layer starts with a memory
1746
       stream ml−1 of dimension 128 (m0 is a vector of all 1s), and A∥P . In each layer, ml−1 is passed
1747
       through a linear layer to obtain a single query ql of dimension equal to the dimension of each input
1748   token in A∥P . Two separate linear layers also convert each token in A∥P into a set of key vectors and
1749   value vectors (of the same dimension as the input token). The query, keys, and values are reshaped
1750   to obtain 4 attention heads. We
                                     p then compute the attention scores by multiplying the query against
1751   all keys, and normalizing by dql , where dql is the dimension of the query/key/value vectors. We
1752   then perform dropout on the attention scores with dropout rate 0.1, and softmax the scores for each
1753   attention head. These attention scores are used to weight the value vectors in a weighted sum, which
1754   is then reshaped to reincorporate the heads. This is passed through another linear layer which retains
1755   the same dimension, followed by dropout and layer normalization. This is then fed to a 2-layer MLP
1756   with ReLU and dropout in between the two linear layers, mapping the result to the same dimension
1757
       as ml−1 . After a final dropout and layer norm, this is added to ml−1 to obtain ml .
1758   After all attention layers, the final ml is passed to a single linear layer which maps it to a scalar
1759   prediction which is passed to a sigmoid activation function (for binary prediction).
1760
       Our standard CNN follows an architecture which is common in the literature for single-task predic-
1761   tions. We apply 3 successive convolutional layers to the input sequence, each with nf filters. The
1762   filters of the first layer have width w. The next two convolutional layers have filters of width 5. After
1763   each layer we apply ReLU and batch normalization. We then perform max pooling with a filter size
1764   of 40 and a stride of 40. This is passed to two linear layers of 10 and 5 hidden dimensions each. After
1765   each linear layer, we apply ReLU and batch normalization. Finally, a final linear layer maps the result
1766   to a sigmoid-activated output.
1767   Our ExplaiNN implementation follows the description in Novakovsky et al. (2022b). As with the
1768   other architectures, we use the same number and width of first-layer convolutional filters.
1769
1770
       Depending on the complexity of the dataset’s syntax, we selected the ARGMINN architectural
       hyperparameters accordingly. Note that these values were not tuned at all, and were chosen at the
1771
       outset based on domain knowledge and never modified. We also ensured that for each dataset, we
1772
       always used the same number and length of first-layer convolutional filters (nf and w, respectively)
1773   in other architectures for a fair comparison.

1819   C.4    A NALYSES
1820
       Extracting motifs from convolutional filters
1821
1822   To extract motif PFMs from convolutional filters, we adopted a procedure based on that described in
1823   Kelley et al. (2016). Specifically, we passed the test set through the model and computed the average
1824   of all sub-sequences (pooling together all possible sub-sequences in the test set) which activated that
1825
       filter to at least 50% of the maximum activation achieved over all such windows.
1826   Computing importance scores
1827
       We computed importance scores using DeepLIFTShap, integrated gradients, or in silico mutagenesis
1828
       (Shrikumar et al., 2017; Sundararajan et al., 2017).
1829
1830   For DeepLIFTShap and integrated gradients, we used PyTorch Captum. For DeepLIFTShap, we
1831   used a reference of 10 baselines consisting of dinucleotide-shuffled sequences, as recommended in
1832
       Shrikumar et al. (2017). We also recovered the hypothetical importance scores at each position, as
       recommended for MoDISco (Shrikumar et al., 2018). For integrated gradients, we used a baseline of
1833
       all 0s.
1834
1835   To obtain in silico mutagenesis scores, we computed the importance of a base by first computing the
       difference between the output prediction of the original sequence versus every possible mutation that


                                                         34
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1836
       made at that position. We mean normalized over the base dimension to obtain a set of hypothetical
1837   importance scores. The actual importance score for a position was simply the hypothetical importance
1838   score (after mean normalization) for the base actually present in the sequence at the position.
1839
1840
       Discovering motifs with MoDISco
1841   In general, we discovered motifs using MoDISco from the standard CNN.
1842
       We computed importance scores over the entire positive-labeled dataset using the DeepLIFTShap
1843
       algorithm as described above. We then ran MoDISco-lite v2.2.0 (Shrikumar et al., 2018) using a
1844   maximum of 10000 seqlets and default parameters, as recommended by the authors.
1845
1846   Evaluating discovered motifs
1847   To compute matches to known motifs, we used TOMTOM (Bailey et al., 2015) to compute the
1848   q-value similarity between a PFM to known motifs (across all possible alignments). We reported the
1849   − log10 (q) value as similarity. For simulated datasets, we reported the closest match (i.e., highest
1850   similarity) to any motif in the dataset. For experimental datasets, we reported the closest match to
1851   any relevant motif in the JASPAR human-motif database (Fornes et al., 2020).
1852   Note that to ensure a fair comparison, the lengths of the motifs were kept the same in each dataset.
1853   For ARGMINN, the standard CNN, and ExplaiNN, all models are trained with the same first-layer
1854   filter sizes. MoDISco by default outputs longer patterns, so we trimmed MoDISco-discovered motifs
1855   to the same size as the filters used by other methods (maximizing the total information content in the
1856   post-trimmed window; we used a uniform background for computing information content).
1857   For our analyses on motif accuracy/similarity, redundancy, and number of extraneous motifs, we
1858   needed to match each motif discovered by each method to the closest relevant motif (or none at all).
1859   To do this, we first needed to identify the set of possible relevant motifs for each dataset (i.e., the set
1860   of motifs which would be considered biologically “accurate” for the task). For simulated datasets,
1861   the set of relevant motifs was simply the PFMs used to create the simulation. For experimental
1862   datasets, the set of relevant motifs was defined by first running TOMTOM against all known human
1863   motifs and pooling together the top matches (by motif family) over all methods and architectures
1864   (e.g., ARGMINN, MoDISco, etc.). We used a q-value cut off of 0.5. After pooling together all the
1865
       top TOMTOM matches over all methods, we extracted a set of relevant motifs or motif families by
       checking for supporting literature. Any motif/family with supporting literature was kept as a relevant
1866
       motif.
1867
1868   Finally, once the set of relevant motifs for each dataset was determined, we matched each motif
1869   (discovered by each method) to the closest relevant motif using TOMTOM. Discovered motifs which
1870   did not match any relevant motif (using the default TOMTOM threshold) were considered extraneous.
1871   To compute redundancy, we counted the number of times each relevant motif was matched to by
1872   a method’s discovered motifs. We kept track of forward and reverse-complement orientations. As
1873   long as one orientation was discovered, we considered that motif to had been found; we computed
1874   redundancy as the maximum number of times a relevant motif was matched to (maximum over
1875   orientations). For reverse-complement symmetric motifs/families, we did not consider orientations
1876   separately, and computed redundancy accordingly.
1877   To compute motif accuracy, we computed the similarity (measured by the TOMTOM q-value) of the
1878   closest motif discovered by each method to each relevant motif.
1879
       Tracing back motif instances and syntax
1880
1881   To trace back motif instances for a particular input sequence in ARGMINN, we performed a forward
1882   pass and retained the motif activations and attention scores. Over all layers and all attention heads,
1883   we examined the positions in the sequence which had an attention score of at least 0.9. We then
1884

1902

1904   Evaluating motif instances
1905   To evaluate our motif instances, we compared called motif instances to ground-truth instances. We
1906   computed the precision as the fraction of called instances which overlap ground-truth instances, and
1907   the recall as the fraction of ground-truth instances which overlap called instances. We also computed
1908   recall curves in a top-k fashion, where we ranked the called instances (described above) and for each
1909   top k called instances, we computed the recall relative to ground-truth instances.

       For simulated datasets, the ground-truth motif instances are completely known, as they are defined at
1911   the time of sequence generation.

1914
       independently collected set of DNA-binding footprints in various cell types. For each cell type of
       interest (e.g., HepG2), we simply pooled together the footprints of all experiments from that cell type
1915

1957   Interpretable sequence design
1958   To design novel sequences intrepretably using ARGMINN, we started with our ARGMINN model
1959   trained on HepG2 DNase accessibility. We extracted motifs from the filters as described above. Prior
1960   to designing, we trimmed and filtered the motifs. Specifically, we first cut off flanks with information
1961   content lower than 0.2. We then required that after trimming, the motif was at least 5 bp and had
1962   an average information content of 0.8. We used a uniform background for computing information
1963   content.
1964   We then extracted the motif syntax as described above, again only using the test set. This procedure
1965   takes each input sequence and assigns it a motif pattern (e.g., “CTCF - TEAD” or “FOXA” or
1966   “CTCF - FOXA - FOXA”). The motif pattern denotes which motifs were identified by ARGMINN in
1967   that sequence (in order) (e.g., CTCF followed by TEAD). Over the entire test set, we labeled each
1968   sequence with its motif pattern. For each pattern which has more than one motif, ARGMINN then
1969   gives a set of spacings that were identified over all sequences with that pattern.
1970   We took the top 20 patterns (by number of sequences which have that pattern, requiring at least 5
1971   sequences with that pattern). For each of the top 20 patterns, we generated 100 novel sequences
1972   with that pattern. To construct a novel sequence for a pattern, we started with an original 500 bp
1973   test sequence which falls under that pattern (sampled randomly). We then dinucleotide-shuffled the
1974   central 300 bp to destroy any functional motifs. We then inserted the motifs of that pattern (sampling
1975   from the ARGMINN-learned PFM(s)), at spacings sampled from the empirical distribution for that
1976   pattern (for single-motif patterns, the motif was simply placed in the center).
