                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               Virtual Network Embedding (VNE) is a challenging combinatorial optimization
010
               problem that refers to resource allocation associated with hard and multifaceted
011            constraints in network function virtualization (NFV). Existing works for VNE
012            struggle to handle such complex constraints, leading to compromised system per-
013            formance and stability. In this paper, we propose a CONstraint-Aware Learning
014            framework for VNE, named CONAL, to achieve efficient constraint management.
015            Concretely, we formulate the VNE problem as a constrained Markov decision pro-
016            cess with violation tolerance. This modeling approach aims to improve both re-
017            source utilization and solution feasibility by precisely evaluating solution quality
018            and the degree of constraint violation. We also propose a reachability-guided op-
019
               timization with an adaptive reachability budget method that dynamically assigns
               budget values. This method achieves persistent zero violation to guarantee the
020
               feasibility of VNE solutions and more stable policy optimization by handling in-
021
               stances without any feasible solution. Furthermore, we propose a constraint-aware
022            graph representation method to efficiently learn cross-graph relations and con-
023            strained path connectivity in VNE. Finally, extensive experimental results demon-
024            strate the superiority of our proposed method over state-of-the-art baselines. Our
025            code is available at https://github.com/GeminiLight/conal-vne.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Network Function Virtualization (NFV) is a promising technique that facilitates the deployment
030   of multiple Virtual Networks (VNs) tailored to user network demands within a shared Physical
031   Network (PN) infrastructure (Yi et al., 2018). It is vital for domains such as cloud computing and
032   edge computing, where dynamic and efficient resource management is essential (Zhuang et al.,
033
      2020). Virtual Network Embedding (VNE), a fundamental resource allocation problem in NFV, is
      critical for maintaining high Quality of Service (QoS). This process, involving the mapping of VNs
034
      to PNs, represents a significant challenge. It is an NP-hard Combinatorial Optimization Problem
035
      (COP) characterized by intricate and hard constraints (Rost & Schmid, 2020).
036
037   Traditional solutions to VNE range from exact to heuristic methods often struggle with either exces-
038   sive computation times or limited performance in complex network scenarios (Zhang et al., 2018;
039
      Dehury & Sahoo, 2019; Fan et al., 2023). Recently, Reinforcement Learning (RL) has been a poten-
      tial direction for VNE, which learns effective solving policies without the need of labeled datasets.         UPDATE
040
      Typically, existing RL-based methods solve the VNE problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP)
041
      (Haeri & Trajković, 2017; Yan et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023b). They build feature extractors with
042   various neural networks, and then learn policies that iteratively select a physical node to place each
043   virtual node until the solution is completed or constraints are violated. However, in the RL frame-
044   work, due to the hard constraints of VNE, a persistent zero-violation is required at each decision
045   timestep. This strict adherence to constraints often results in numerous failure samples where con-
046   straints are violated. For failure samples, existing studies (e.g., Yao et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2022))
047   consider them as noisy and only train with data that violate no constraint; or others, like Yan et al.
048   (2020) and He et al. (2023a), consider fixed penalties to them and discourage violations.
049   Although these RL-based algorithms (Zhang et al., 2022; He et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023b) have
050   shown efficacy, they still suffer from several significant problems in handling complex constraints of
051   VNE. Firstly, ignoring failure samples makes policies prone to violating critical constraints, while
052   employing fixed penalties in reward signals does not accurately reflect the severity of constraint vi-
053   olations. Thus, these methods underestimate valuable sample information and hamper the learning
      of constraint-aware policies, which results in low feasibility guarantees. Secondly, more seriously,

      it is hard to avoid to encounter unsolvable instances whose feasible sets are empty in practical sce-
055   narios, due to insufficient physical resource availability or excessive virtual resource requests. It is
056   impractical to distinguish solvable and unsolvable instances, since checking the instance solvability
057   of an NP-hard problem is time-consuming. Failure samples caused by unsolvable instances fur-
058   ther complicate the constraint learning process, and negatively impact the stability of training and
059   policy performance. We have conducted a preliminary study to highlight the negative impact of
060   unsolvable instances on training, please see Appendix C. Thirdly, VNE constraints are complex and
061   multifaceted, involving cross-graph status interactions and bandwidth-constrained path connectivity
062   assessments, which are not adequately captured by the feature extractors used in current studies. For
063
      a more comprehensive discussion on related work, please refer to Appendix A.
064   To address these challenges, we propose a CONstraint-Aware Learning framework for VNE, named
065   CONAL, achieving high solution feasibility guarantee and training stability. Concretely, to optimize
066   performance while enhancing constraint satisfaction, we formulate the VNE problem as Constrained
067   MDP (CMDP) (Altman, 2021). However, in the process of solution construction, if any constraints
068   are violated, the process will be early terminated and lead to incomplete solutions. This challenges
069
      precise measurement of both the quality of solutions and the degree of constraint violations. Thus,
      we develop a violation-tolerant mapping method to ensure complete solution construction and a
070
      measurement function to precisely evaluate constraint violations. Additionally, to achieve persistent
071
      zero constraint violation required by VNE, we introduce a reachability analysis into the optimization
072   objective. During training, due to the existence of unsolvable instances whose constraints are impos-
073   sibly satisfied, we propose an adaptive reachability budget method to make the policy optimization
074   more stable. It dynamically decides the violations caused by a surrogate policy as budgets based
075   on the instance’s solvability, rather than always setting budgets to zero. Furthermore, to finely per-
076   ceive the complex constraints of VNE, we propose a constraint-aware graph representation method
077   tailored for VNE. Specifically, we design a heterogeneous modeling module for cross-graph status
078   interactions. We also devise several feasibility-consistency augmentations and utilize contrastive
079   learning to bring representations under different views close, which enhances the sensitivity of pol-
080
      icy towards path-bandwidth constraints. Our contributions are summarized as follows.

      System Modeling. In real-world network systems, as illustrated in Figure 1, user network services
095
      are virtualized as VN requests that continuously seek resources from the PN. Each arrived VN
096   request, along with the current situation of the PN, constitutes an instance I, and we collect all such
097   instances with a set I. For each instance, I = (Gv , Gp ) ∈ I, where the PN Gp and VN Gv are
098   modeled as undirected graphs, Gp = (Np , Lp ) and Gv = (Nv , Lv , ω, ϖ), respectively. Here, Np
099   and Lp denote the sets of physical nodes and links, indicating servers and their interconnections; Nv
100   and Lv denote the sets of virtual nodes and links, representing services and their relationships; ω and
101   ϖ denote the arrival time and lifetime of VN request. We denote C(np ) as the computing resource
102   availability for the physical node np ∈ Np , and B(lp ) as the bandwidth resource availability of the
103   physical link lp ∈ Lp . Besides, C(nv ) and B(lv ) denote the demands for computing resource by a
104
      virtual node nv ∈ Nv and bandwidth resource by a virtual link lv ∈ Lv . Like most VNE studies (Wu
      et al., 2024), we focus mainly on bandwidth attributes in links, the generalizable critical bottleneck
105
      in various network scenarios, ensuring that the proposed method is universal and extensible.
106

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
139
140
      In this section, we propose the CONstraint-Aware Learning framework to handle complex con-
141
      straints of VNE, named CONAL, illustrated in Figure 2. Initially, we formulate the VNE problem
142   as a violation-tolerant CMDP, which ensures the acquisition of complete solutions and precise eval-
143   uation of solution quality and constraint violation (See the green area in Figure 2). Additionally,
144   we present a reachability-guided optimization objective to ensure persistent constraint satisfaction
145   while avoiding the over-conservatism of policy, which enhances both the quality and feasibility of
146   VNE solutions. Further to address the instability of policy optimization caused by instances with
147   no feasible solution, we propose an adaptive reachability budget method to improve the robustness
148   of training. This method dynamically decides the value of budgets rather than a fixed zero (See the
149   pink area in Figure 2). Furthermore, regarding the feature extractor, we propose a constraint-aware
150
      graph representation method to finely perceive the complex constraints of VNE. Concretely, we con-
      struct a heterogeneous graph to model the cross-graph status between VN and PN. We also devise
151
      several augmentations that preserve solution feasibility while enhancing the model’s sensitivity of
152
      bandwidth-constrained path connectivity through contrastive learning (See the orange area in Fig-
153   ure 2). Overall, we build the policy with the constraint-aware graph representation method and train
154   it with an actor-critic-based RL algorithm to achieve the reachability-guided optimization objective,
155   where the adaptive reachability budget improves the training stability. We provide the description of
156   both CONAL’s training and inference process in Algorithm 1 and 2, placed in Appendix E.

195   function. For an action at = ntp to host the current be-placed virtual node ntv , the environment will

201
      function that measures violations of constraints. We separately consider computing and bandwidth

202
      define H(s) = max (HN (s) , HL (s)) and allow it negative, which indicates keeping distance from

      Standard CMDPs focus on optimizing discounted cumulative costs to meet long-term safety, which
231   fails to meet the consistent satisfaction requirements of VNE in all states (Liu et al., 2021). To
232   guarantee the solution feasibility of VNE, we consider reachability analysis into CMDP to achieve
233   state-wise zero-violation optimization (Yu et al., 2022). This objective significantly expands the
234   feasible set of policies and mitigates the conservativeness of the policy. Additionally, to enhance
235   the stability of policy optimization, we propose a novel adaptive reachability budget method that
236   dynamically decides budgets, rather than always zero budget for unsolvable instances. For policy
237   training, we leverage the Lagrange version of the PPO algorithm (Ray et al., 2019).

      network λ = Λ(s) to dynamically adjust Lagrangian multipliers during training, similar to Ma
310   et al. (2021). To optimize the policy, we leverage the actor-critic framework with Proximal Policy
311   Optimization (PPO) as the training algorithm (Ray et al., 2019), similar to works (Yu et al., 2022;
312   Ma et al., 2021). See Appendix D.5 for the details of this training method. Next, we will introduce
313   our proposed constraint-aware graph networks used as the feature encoder of policy.

315   3.3   C ONSTRAINT- AWARE G RAPH R EPRESENTATION
316
317   The VNE processing is governed by complex and multifaceted constraints, presenting challenges in
318   representation learning. These include the interaction of cross-graph status and the assessment of
319   bandwidth-constrained path connectivity. To address this issue, we propose a constraint-aware graph
320
      representation method with a heterogeneous modeling module for cross-graph status fusion and a
      contrastive learning-based module to enhance path connectivity awareness. This method efficiently
321
      perceives the complex constraints of VNE, providing a higher feasibility guarantee of solutions.
322
323   Heterogeneous Modeling (HM). Instead of separate feature extraction of VN Gv and PN Gp , we
      integrate them into a heterogeneous graph GI by introducing several hypothetical cross-graph links.


                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      GI comprises two distinct node types: virtual and physical nodes, and we denote their attributes as
325   Xvn and Xpn . These node attributes include computing resource demands or availability, link counts,
326   and the aggregated bandwidth characteristics (maximum, minimum, and average) of adjacent links.
327   Similarly, there are two link types: virtual and physical links, whose attributes are link bandwidth de-
328   mands or availability. We denote these link attributes as Xvl and Xpl , respectively. Additionally, we
329   introduce specialized heterogeneous links to capture the current embedding state: already-mapped
330   links, which connect virtual nodes to their hosting physical nodes, and imaginary decision links,
331   which connect the current yet-to-be-decided virtual node with all potential physical nodes. Here,
332   potential physical node refers to one that hosts no virtual nodes and has enough computing resources
333
      available. We group these heterogeneous links into sets Lv,p,m for already-mapped links and Lv,p,d
      for decision-candidate links. We uniformly set these two types of links’ attributes to 1, denoted as
334      l            l
      Xv,p,m   and Xv,p,d  , respectively. To encode this graph’s topological and attribute information, we
335
      enhanced widely-used graph attention networks (GAT) (Veličković et al., 2018) by integrating het-
336
      erogeneous link fusion and link attribute encoding in the propagation process. See Appendix D.3 for
337   its details. Inputting the heterogeneous graph’s features into this network, we obtain the final node
338   representations Z = {Zv , Zp }, where Zv and Zp denote physical and virtual node representations.
339
340
      Path-bandwidth Contrast (PC). Bandwidth constraints of VNE significantly impact solution fea-
      sibility, particularly in the context of path routing complexity. At each decision timestep, we need
341
      to carefully select a physical node ntp for placing the current virtual node ntv . This selection is dom-
342
      inated by ensuring that feasible connective paths exist to all other physical nodes hosting the virtual
343   node’s neighbors. Here, the feasibility of the path is dominated by the bandwidth availability of
344   physical links to support the bandwidth requirements of all prepared incident links δ ′ (ntv ). GNNs
345   build up on the propagation mechanism along links to increase awareness of the topology informa-
346   tion. However, not all physical links contribute positively to this awareness; some with insufficient
347   bandwidths may even introduce noise into node representations. This emphasizes the necessity to
348   integrate bandwidth constraint awareness within GNNs to perceive the path feasibility.
349   To address this challenge, we propose a novel path-bandwidth contrast method to enhance bandwidth
350   constraint awareness through contrastive learning, whose core idea is creating augmented views with
351   feasibility-consistency augmentations and making node representations in these views close.
352
353   Definition 4 (Feasibility-consistency Augmentations). Let Φ denote a set of augmentation methods
354   and F denote the function indicating the feasibility of solutions. Given any VNE instance I and a
355   solution E = fG (I), we have F(E) = F(ϕ(E)), ∀I ∈ I, ϕ ∈ Φ.
356
357   These augmentations generate multiple views of the original heterogeneous graph, which main-
358   tain the same feasibility semantics before and after their application. Following this principle, we
359   develop several augmentation methods by modifying the topology of either VN or PN without im-
360   pacting solution feasibility, which are described as follows. (a) Physical Link Addition ϕA . We add
361   a specific number ϵ · |N p | of physical links in PN, whose bandwidth resources are equal to the differ-
362   ence between the smallest requirements among all virtual links and 1, i.e., minlv ∈Lv B(lv ) − 1. (b)
363
      Virtual Link Addition ϕB . We add a specific number ϵ · |N v | of virtual links that require a zero band-
      width resource to enhance the complexity and connectivity of the VN. Here, ϵ is an augment ratio
364
      that determines the proportion of links to be added based on the number of nodes in the network.
365
366   After applying these augmentations, we create two new views GA                          B      B
                                                                        I = ϕA (GI ) and GI = ϕ (GI ),
367   which have same feasibility semantics of VNE instance I. Using our heterogeneous graph network,
368   we extract node representations under views GA             B
                                                        I and GI , denoted as Z
                                                                                  A
                                                                                     and Z B , respectively.
369
      Subsequently, we utilize contrastive learning to enhance the proximity of node representations under
      the augmented views GA              B
                                I and GI . This necessitates that the model precisely discerns the noisy
370
      implications of those links with less bandwidth that play no impact on solution feasibility. Through
371
      this method, we aim to enhance the model’s sensitivity towards link bandwidth, effectively mitigate
372   the influence of irrelevant links in the GNN propagation process, and bolster its overall awareness of
373   bandwidth constraints. In this work, we adopt the Barlow Twins method (Zbontar et al., 2021) for its
374   simplicity and effectiveness, which circumvents negative sample selection and maintains the original
375   network architecture. Given the embeddings under two augmented views, H a and H b , we use this
376   contrastive loss LCL to reduce redundancy between embedding components by aligning their cross-
377   correlation matrix with the identity matrix. This unsupervised loss can be seamlessly integrated into
      the training process of RL. See Appendix D.4 for the details of Barlow Twins method.

526   5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we proposed the CONAL for VNE to enhance constraint management and training
529   robustness, which is critical to the performance and reliability of network systems. Specifically, we
530   formulated the VNE problem as a violation-tolerant CMDP to optimize both the quality and feasibil-
531
      ity of solutions. This method allows us to always obtain complete solutions to precisely evaluate the
      quality of the solution. Additionally, we presented a reachability-guided optimization objective with
532
      an adaptive feasibility budget method to ensure persistent constraint satisfaction while alleviating the
533
      conservativeness of policy. This approach also address the instability of policy optimization caused
534   by unsolvable instances. Furthermore, to finely perceive the complex constraints of VNE, we pro-
535   posed a constraint-aware graph representation method, which consists of a heterogeneous modeling
536   module for indicating cross-graph relations and a path-bandwidth contrast module that enhances the
537   sensitivity to bandwidth constraints. Finally, we conducted extensive experiments to verify the effec-
538   tiveness of our proposed methods. In the future, we plan to tackle additional constraints in specific
539   networking scenarios, e.g., latency-aware edge computing and energy-efficient green computing.
      This will require specialized design efforts to address scenario-specific challenges effectively.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
811
812
813   In this section, we discuss related work on VNE algorithms, RL for COPs, and safe RL methods.
814   VNE Algorithms. Resource management is a critical research direction in NFV, including tasks as
815   Scaling (Fei et al., 2018; 2020) and scheduling (Zhang et al., 2017). Among these, VNE plays a
816   key role in resource allocation. To solve this challenging and significant problem, many approaches      NEW
817   have been designed for VNE, which can be classified as exact, heuristics, and learning-based meth-
818   ods. Initially, exact algorithms formulate the VNE problem as integer linear programming (Shahriar
819   et al., 2018) or mixed integer linear programming (Chowdhury et al., 2009), and then solve them
820   with exact solvers. However, this is impractical due to extensive computations and time consump-
821
      tion. Thus, numerous heuristic algorithms have been proposed to offer solutions within an acceptable
      time, such as node ranking strategies (Zhang et al., 2018; Gong et al., 2014; Fan et al., 2023) (Jin
822
      et al., 2020), meta-heuristics (Dehury & Sahoo, 2019; Zhang et al., 2019; Jiang & Zhang, 2021), etc.     NEW
823
      However, they heavily rely on manual heuristic design and merely for specific scenarios. Recently,
824   reinforcement learning has emerged as a promising solution for VNE and many RL-based VNE al-
825   gorithms have been proposed (Haeri & Trajković, 2017; Wang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; He
826   et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023b; Geng et al., 2023) (Xiao et al., 2019). In general, they model the   NEW
827   solution construction process of each VNE instance as an MDP, but do not consider the fine-grained
828   constraint violations. Then, they leverage existing network networks (e.g., convolutional neural
829   network, GNN, etc.) to extract features from PN and VN, separately. Finally, they optimize the
830   model with different RL methods (e.g., asynchronous advantage actor-critic, PPO, etc.). In particu-
831   lar, Gu et al. (2020) proposed a model-assisted DRL framework that leverages heuristic solutions to
832
      guide the training process, reducing reliance on the agent’s blind exploration of actions. However,
      they struggle to handle such complex constraints of VNE thereby compromising performance. Zeng
833
      et al. (2024) introduced the SafeDRL algorithm that corrects constraint violations using high-quality
834
      feasible solutions through expert intervention. But this reliance on external corrections ignore the
835   aspects of policy-level constraints awareness, limiting its adaptability and performance. To address
836   these challenges, we explore to learn a constraint-aware VNE policy by innovating existing MDP
837   modeling, representation learning, and policy optimization                                               NEW
838
      RL for COPs. The application of RL to solve COPs has emerged as a hot topic in decision-making,
839
      which aims to learn efficient solving strategies from data (Bengio et al., 2021). Many efforts have
840
      been directed toward classic COPs such as routing (Zhou et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023), schedul-
841   ing (Zhang et al., 2023a; 2020), bin packing (Pan et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2021), etc. These ap-
842   proaches can be broadly categorized into two types based on their solving processes: construction
843   and improvement. While improvement methods start with an initial solution and use an RL policy
844   to iteratively refine it, construction methods build a solution incrementally from scratch (Mazyavk-
845   ina et al., 2021). Construction methods typically employ RL to guide the sequential selection to
846   form a complete solution. Given the real-time requirements of practical network systems, most ex-
847   isting RL-based VNE algorithms are designed as construction methods to provide solutions within
848   an acceptable time (Zhang et al., 2022; He et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023b). In contrast to these
849
      classic COPs, VNE presents unique complexities in representation learning due to its multifaceted
      and hard constraints, such as the interaction of cross-graph status and the assessment of bandwidth-
850
      constrained path connectivity. Furthermore, the existence of unsolvable instances in the training
851
      process can compromise robustness, potentially causing ineffective policies.
852
853   Safe RL Methods. Safe RL aims to maximize the expected rewards while ensuring safety con-
854   straints are not violated (Gu et al., 2022). Early efforts in safe RL focus on keeping cumulative
855
      constraint violations below a fixed cost budget (Achiam et al., 2017; Ray et al., 2019; Yang et al.,
      2022). To address the stricter constraint requirements of practical applications, recent works have
856
      proposed achieving state-wise safety, which ensures the satisfaction of instantaneous constraints at
857
      each decision timestep (Zhao et al., 2023; 2022; He et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2022). One promising
858   approach involves incorporating reachability analysis into the CMDP framework (Yu et al., 2022).
859   This method employs a reachability function to assess the state feasibility, which significantly ex-
860   pands the feasible set of policy, and mitigates the conservativeness of the policy. However, modeling
861   VNE as a reachability-guided CMDP presents challenges. Concretely, In the process of solution
862   construction, if any constraints are violated, the process will be early terminated and lead to incom-
863   plete solutions. This hinders precise measurement of both the quality of solutions and the degree of
      constraint violations. Additionally, these methods typically assume a non-empty feasible set. But


                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      for VNE, where it is hard to avoid facing unsolvable instances without any feasible solution, these
865   approaches often confront the challenges of unstable policy optimization, since the constraints of
866   these instances are impossibly satisfied.

       D.2   P ROOF OF L AGRANGE M ULTIPLIER C ONVERGENCE
973
974    Proposition 1. During online training, if there exists an instance without any feasible solution (i.e.
975    H(s) > 0, ∀s ∈ S), then the Lagrange multiplier can become infinite.
976
977
       Proof. According to the optimality condition of Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT), any optimal solution
978
       to the constrained optimization problem (4) must satisfy three conditions: the feasibility condition,
979    the non-negativity condition, and the complementary slack condition, expressed as follows:
980
981                                                    H(s) ≤ 0,
982                                                       λ ≥ 0,                                          (16)
983                                                λ · H(s) = 0.
984
985
       Given that H(s) > 0, ∀s ∈ S, the primal feasibility condition is violated; there are no feasible solu-
986    tions that satisfy H(s) ≤ 0. The complementary slackness condition requires that for each s ∈ S,
987    either λ = 0 or H(s) = 0. Since H(s) > 0, we must have λ = 0 to satisfy this condition. How-
988    ever, setting λ = 0 does not penalize the constraint violation, and the primal feasibility condition
989    remains unsatisfied. This leads to a contradiction: there is no finite λ ≥ 0 that satisfies all the KKT
990    conditions when H(s) > 0, ∀s ∈ S. The optimization problem is infeasible because the constraints
991    cannot be met. In practical online training, the Lagrange multiplier λ is adjusted iteratively to en-
992    force the constraints by increasing λ whenever the constraints are violated. Since H(s) > 0 always
993    holds, the Lagrange multiplier λ will continually increase in an attempt to penalize the constraint
994
       violations. As a result, λ can become arbitrarily large, theoretically approaching infinity.

1085   of physical nodes and links, K denotes the number of GNN layers, and d denotes the embed-

1089   the different neural network structures used, such as GAT   and GCN. One GAT and one GCN

       G.4   H YPERPARAMETER I MPACT S TUDY
1534
1535   In this section, we explore the impact of two proposed key hyperparameters on the performance of
1536   CONAL, including the update interval µ of surrogate policy proposed in the ARB method and the
1537   augment ratio (ϵ) used in the path-bandwidth contrast module.
1538
       The impact of update interval of surrogate policy. This parameter µ controls the update interval of
1539
       surrogate policy π ′ during training. We vary µ within the range [1, 5, 10, 20, 30] to explore its impact
1540   on key performance metrics, as shown in Figure 9. We observe that increasing µ initially leads
1541   to an improvement in all metrics. This suggests that extremely frequent updates of the surrogate
1542   policy make the budget values change rapidly, potentially leading to instability and divergence in
1543   the learning process. As µ increases beyond nearly 10, the improvements across these metrics
1544   tend to be stable and show minimal variation. This implies that while a moderate update interval
1545   enhances the model’s performance, too-slow updates do not offer a significant further improvement
1546   on performance metrics and may even increase computational overhead for training.
1547   The impact of the augment ratio. This parameter determines the proportion of links to be added
1548   based on the number of nodes in the network. We change the augment ratio ϵ within the range
1549   [0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0] and the results are illustrated in Figure 10. As the augment ratio initially
1550   increases from 0 to 1.0, we observe improvements across performance metrics. However, when
1551   the augment ratio is increased beyond 1.0, these improvements become marginal or even negative.
1552   This indicates that excessive enhancement of the graph structure can increase learning difficulty.
1553   The increasing disparity between the enhanced and original graph topologies may also negatively
1554
       impact performance. This study reveals that a reasonable augment ratio ϵ benefits the model by
       improving its sensitivity to bandwidth constraints. However, excessively high ϵ values provide only
1555
       slight improvements or can even degrade performance. Generally, setting ϵ = 1.0 or a value close to
1556
       it provides a balanced trade-off between performance enhancement and model robustness.                                                                                            NEW
