009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) have shown great potential
012            in generating high-fidelity, diverse, natural dances consistent with given music.
013            However, due to the scarcity of skinned human motion data and the complexity of
014            mesh data, existing methods mainly focus on generating dance moves in the form
015            of skeletons, overlooking the domain gap between the skeletal structure and the
016            human body geometry. When skeletal motions are visualized with human body
017            mesh, anomalies such as torso interpenetration and imbalanced movements be-
018
               come highly noticeable. This physical implausibility significantly diminishes the
               aesthetic appeal of the generated dances and hinders their practicality in real-world
019
               applications. To address this issue, we propose a physical reward to fine-tune the
020
               diffusion model. Specifically, We first train a motion imitation policy in a physi-
021            cal simulator and use it to evaluate the physical plausibility (e.g., penetration, foot
022            sliding) of generated motions. Ideally, generated motions that are more physically
023            plausible will be easier to imitate, which means higher rewards. So we fine-tune
024            the diffusion model to generate more physically plausible motions through Rein-
025            forcement Learning Fine-Tuning (RLFT). Furthermore, we find that the physical
026            reward tends to push the model to generate freezing motions for less torso in-
027            tersections. To mitigate it, we proposed an anti-freezing reward to balance the
028            preference for freezing motions. Experiments on the human dance dataset show
029
               that our method can significantly improve the physical plausibility of generated
               motions, thereby generating dances that are aesthetically pleasing and realistic.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Dance is a universal art form for humans to convey emotions, spread messages, and express their
035
      thoughts (LaMothe, 2019; Zambrano, 2023). Therefore, many applications such as film production,
036   game development, and virtual reality experiences have the need for dance generation or dance
037   animation. However, creating new choreography from scratch or capturing human dance movements
038   through motion capture is not only costly but also time-consuming. Recently, deep-learning-based
039   generative models have shown great potential in the art generation area, shading light into the music-
040   conditioned dance generation. Despite the complexity of skeletal movements in human dances and
041   their intricate relationship with the music condition, recent methods (Li et al., 2021; Siyao et al.,
042   2022; Tseng et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2022) have made significant progress in generating high-quality,
043   natural, and diverse dances that align well with the given music.
044   However, due to the complexity of representing and training with body meshes, most existing meth-
045   ods overlook the skinned mesh when generating dance data. Instead, they typically represent actions
046   through bone rotations in skeleton form. However, the skeletal structure is only the intermediate re-
047   sult. When skeletal actions are visualized as body meshes, many physically implausible phenomena
048   might occur, such as body interpenetration and imbalanced movement. These issues significantly
049   degrade the aesthetic appeal and realism of the final visual results (Hoyet et al., 2012).
050   We argue that the problem lies in the domain gap between the learned skeletal structure and the
051   human body geometry. To tackle this problem, we aim to incorporate physical constraints imposed
052   by body meshes into the learning process of the generative model. Specifically, we first train an
053   imitation policy using imitation learning on expert datasets AMASS (Mahmood et al., 2019) and
      AIST++ (Li et al., 2021), following prior works (Yuan et al., 2023; Yuan & Kitani, 2020), in a phys-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ical simulator (IssacGYM (Makoviychuk et al., 2021)). The imitation policy learns to mimic an
055   input action in the physical simulator, forcing the output action to comply with the physical laws.
056   Then, we construct a reward, called imitation reward, as a physical-aware signal for fine-tuning the
057   diffusion model through reinforcement learning (RL) as proposed in Black et al. (2024). The imita-
058   tion reward is designed to evaluate the physical plausibility of the generated motion. Since actions
059   with physical implausibility (e.g. with less penetration) are impossible for the imitation policy to im-
060   itate because of the constraints imposed by the physical simulator. Furthermore, such implausibility
061   can hinder subsequent imitations or even result in the failure of the entire process. Consequently,
062   these issues lead to the imitation policy receiving lower rewards. In this sense, the imitation reward
063
      can implicitly impose the skin-based physical constraints into the denoising diffusion process.
064   There is another way (Yuan et al., 2023) to inject physical constraint into the diffusion process, which
065   is using the imitation policy as a physical-guided motion projection module during the inference
066   process. Compared to this straightforward combination of the diffusion model and imitation policy,
067   our method further fine-tunes the diffusion model. The benefits are two-fold: i) Directly adopting
068   the imitated motion as the result may encounter issues such as jittering or even imitation failure.
069
      However, the results generated by the diffusion model can ensure the naturalness of the movements.
      ii) it is time-consuming to process the motion projection with the physical simulator, while our
070
      method can save the projection time.
071
072   During the training of the reinforcement learning, we find that the imitation reward tends to favor
073   movements with small magnitude, which may encourage the diffusion model to generate freezing
074   motions. Therefore, we propose an anti-freezing reward for the diffusion model to balance this
075
      preference for freezing motions. Specifically, we evaluate the magnitude of the generated motions
      by computing the velocity and acceleration of the pose and translation parameters. By combining
076
      the anti-freezing reward with the imitation reward, our method can mitigate the bias towards freezing
077
      motions and encourage large movements.
078
079   To validate our method, we leverage the state-of-the-art (SOTA) dance diffusion model
080   EDGE (Tseng et al., 2023) for finetuning and evaluate the results on the AIST++ dataset (Li et al.,
081
      2021). Experiments show that the occurrence of physical implausibility has significantly decreased,
      such as body interpenetration and abnormal foot-ground contact. Several metrics are designed to
082
      measure these physical improvements quantitatively.
083
084          • We propose an RL training methodology for fine-tuning the diffusion model, encouraging
085            the diffusion process to generate physically plausible dance motions. Through the careful
086            design of imitation reward and anti-freezing reward, our method can correct physically
087            implausible movements generated by a well-trained model, while preserving the original
088            semantics of the dance movements to the greatest extent.
089          • We train an imitation policy through a physical simulator on expert datasets. After train-
090            ing, the imitation policy can serve as a physical-aware reward that can impose physical
091            constraints, especially the one brought by skinned mesh, into the RL training.
092
             • Our experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement in the physical plausibility
093            of the generated dances, including penetration and foot-ground contact. The visual quality
094            of the generated results has also been greatly enhanced in terms of realism and aesthetics.

099   2.1   H UMAN M OTION G ENERATION AND M USIC - TO -DANCE G ENERATION
100
      Generating realistic human motion has been extensively studied. Previous approaches (Lee et al.,
101
      2002; Kovar et al., 2002; Arikan & Forsyth, 2002) primarily rely on graph-based methods. They de-
102   compose motions into clips and then recombine them according to predefined principles. However,
103   these methods struggle to generate diverse human motions, especially the dance that exhibits varia-
104   tions in speed, length, and tempos. This limitation arises from the reliance on fixed motion units and
105   rigid composition rules. In recent years, with the emergence of deep learning and large-scale human
106   motion datasets (Mahmood et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021), numerous works have explored the use of
107   various neural networks to generate diverse human motion (Tevet et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023;
      Liang et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2024). For instance, in the domain of music-to-dance generation, re-

      cent methods utilize various network structures, including CNNs (Holden et al., 2016), RNNs (Tang
109   et al., 2018; Yalta et al., 2019; Alemi et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2023), GCNs (Yan et al., 2019; Ren
110   et al., 2020; Ferreira et al., 2021), GANs (Lee et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2020), Transformers (Li et al.,
111   2023; 2020; 2021; Siyao et al., 2022) and Diffusion Models (Tseng et al., 2023; Alexanderson et al.,
112   2023; Li et al., 2024), to better capture the intricate relationship between dance joint movements and
113   accompanying music. However, most existing methods focus on improving the synchronization be-
114   tween music and human joint movements without considering constraints from the laws of physics
115   (e.g. skin collision and gravity). Therefore, these methods tend to generate physically implausible
116   motions. On the contrary, our method employs a physical simulator to model the laws of physics
117
      and instills physical knowledge into the diffusion model.

      2.2   P HYSICS -BASED H UMAN MOTION MODELING
120
121
      Physics-based human motion imitation is first utilized to generate realistic and controllable loco-
122
      motion for characters in the physical simulator (Liu & Hodgins, 2017; Yuan & Kitani, 2020; Liu
123   & Hodgins, 2018; Peng et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2017; Merel et al., 2017; Won et al., 2020; Park
124   et al., 2019; Bergamin et al., 2019). Recent advancements have also adopted physics-based human
125   motion imitation for more downstream tasks such as 3D human pose estimation (Zell et al., 2017;
126   Rempe et al., 2020; Shimada et al., 2020; 2021; Yuan & Kitani, 2018; 2019; Isogawa et al., 2020;
127   Yi et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2022b;a) and 3D human motion generation (Yuan et al.,
128   2023; Yao et al., 2023; Gillman et al., 2024). These studies leverage physics-based human motion
129   imitation during inference to convert the generated motion to the physically plausible motion. How-
130   ever, many of them heavily depend on the ability of the imitation policy. There are chances that
131
      the imitation policy may fail to accurately imitate certain motions. Moreover, the imitation process
      in the post-processing will be time-consuming. Unlike previous work, we propose to incorporate
132
      physical laws into the motion generation model through RL fine-tuning. As a result, the generative
133
      network can be trained to directly synthesize the physics-aware motion without post-processing,
134   thereby bypassing the inherent defects associated with the imitation policy.
135
136
137   2.3   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING F INE -T UNING OF D IFFUSION M ODELS
138
139   With the success of fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) with reinforcement learning (Bai
140   et al., 2022b;a; Lee et al., 2023a; Ouyang et al., 2022), recent research has proposed RLFT algo-
141   rithms for text-to-image diffusion models. RWR (Lee et al., 2023b) firstly introduces the human
142   feedback reward to fine-tune diffusion models. However, RWR ignores the sequential nature of the
143   denoising process. Therefore, DDPO (Black et al., 2024) and DPOK (Fan et al., 2023) treat the
144   denoising process of the diffusion model as a multi-step Markov Decision Process (MDP). Con-
145   currently, Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al., 2023) modifies the Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
146
      algorithm (Rafailov et al., 2023) to directly optimize diffusion models based on preference data.
147   In contrast to previous methods that primarily relied on data-driven learning or heuristic constraints,
148   our approach uniquely incorporates physics-based human motion imitation. By leveraging RLFT,
149   we ensure that the physical limitations of human motion are instilled into the diffusion models,
150   offering a more robust integration of physical constraints than previous approaches.

      3.1    I MITATION POLICY FOR I MITATION R EWARD
183
184   To enable the diffusion model to learn the physical constraints of the real world, we need a metric to
185   evaluate whether the generated motion obeys the physical laws. Inspired by Yuan et al. (2023), we
186   found that an imitation policy can effectively serve this purpose. Human motion imitation policy is
187   commonly used in robotics to control agents in the physical simulator to replicate complex move-
188   ments. Since the motions replicated in the simulator inherently satisfy the various physical laws
189   we set for the simulator (such as gravity, object collision, friction, etc.), we argue that the more the
190   original motion conforms to physical laws, the more accurately it should be replicated in the physics
191
      simulator. Therefore, to utilize this characteristic during the training of the human motion diffusion
      model, we train an imitation policy to control the character with SMPL skinned mesh (Loper et al.,
192
      2023), which inherently obeys physical constraints such as gravity and human-body collisions.
193

      Imitation Reward As aforementioned, we leverage the learned imitation policy as an evaluator
271   to determine whether the generated motion is physically plausible. Specifically, the imitation policy
272   manages to mimic the generated motion in a physical simulator, yielding an imitated motion per
273   generated motion. Ideally, if the generated motion obeys the real-world physical constraints, the
274   imitated motion should be the exact same sequence as the generated motion. However, if the gener-
275   ated motion somehow violates the physical laws, such as an arm penetrating through the body, the
276   imitation policy will attempt to produce a result that closely approximates the input motion while
277   eliminating the interpenetration. In this case, there will be a difference between the generated mo-
278   tion and imitated motion. Therefore, the greater the difference between the two motions, the more
279
      physically implausible the generated motion is, as it becomes harder to imitate in the physics simu-
      lator. Consequently, we measure this difference and assign a larger reward to motions with smaller
280
      differences. The reward can then be formulated as follows:

301
302   Dataset We train the imitation policy and dance diffusion model with the AIST++ (Li et al., 2021)
303   dataset, which is the most commonly used dataset in dance motion generation. It consists of 1,408
304   high-quality dance motions paired with music from a diverse set of genres. We follow the setup used
305
      in EDGE Tseng et al. (2023), the train/test splits are kept the same as the original dataset, and all the
      training examples are cut to 5 seconds, 30FPS.
306
307
      Implement Details For the imitation policy, we adopt Isaac Gym (Makoviychuk et al., 2021) as
308
      our physical simulator, in which we can detect the collision between the character’s torsos. The
309
      weighting factors for calculating the reward (ws and αs ) are set to (0.6, 0.1, 0.2, 0.1) and (60, 0.2,
310   100, 40) respectively. And wr and αr are 0.1 and 30. For the diffusion model, we test our RLFT
311   method on EDGE denoiser, and we employ denoising diffusion implicit models (DDIM) as proposed
312   in Song et al. (2020) with 50 diffusion steps and classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022). In
313   each fine-tuning iteration, we sample 2,048 motions from the training dataset of AIST++ (Li et al.,
314   2021). We accumulate gradients across 50 denoising steps of all samples and perform one gradient
315   update. Our optimizer adopts Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2017) optimizer, with learning rate set to 1e-6.

450   Ablation Study on RL Fine-tuning As another approach to leverage imitation policy, we can
451   also apply the imitation policy as a post-processing step to the generated motions, similar to Yuan
452   et al. (2023). Specifically, the imitation policy is used to mimic the motions generated by EDGE and
453   output refined motions within the physical simulator. We refer to this method as “EDGE w/ projec-
454   tion”. However, we identify some significant limitations with this method. First, the diffusion model
455   may generate some motions that are physically impossible for the imitation policy to mimic directly,
456   e.g. one leg passes through the support leg, which can lead to chaotic outcomes such as slipping

      or falling (As shown in Fig. 3(a)). The quantitative results in Tab. 2 also validate that although the

      This may be due to abnormal foot motion, where the feet lose contact with the ground. In contrast,

      our method still works by assigning a very low reward for such “failling” scenarios. Moreover, be-
460   sides a direct imitation that generates similar motion patterns, our method even gradually learns to
461   avoid such hard cases by replacing them with an easier and physically plausible motion, which is
462   also an advantage brought by RLFT. More video examples are provided in supplementary materials.

473   5   C ONCLUSION
474
475
476   In this paper, we propose a method that fixes the physical implausibility issue of the current dancing
477
      generation works through Reinforcement Learning Fine-Tuning (RLFT). Specifically, we utilize the
      physics-based human motion imitation policy as a reward to evaluate the physical plausibility of
478
      the generated motions. Through the physics-based reward, we can instill the physical law (e.g.
479
      body collision, gravity, and foot-ground contact), which was modeled by the imitation policy in the
480   physical simulator, into the diffusion model through RLFT. Furthermore, to prevent the generation
481   of freezing motions, we propose an anti-freezing reward to enhance the dynamic of the motions.
482   We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate our method across multiple metrics, showing that it
483   significantly improves physical plausibility, especially in terms of body penetration and foot-ground
484   contact. We believe our approach has the potential to advance the real-world applications of motion
485   generation. In the future, we aim to further refine both the physical plausibility and smoothness of
      the generated motions for direct use in downstream applications.
