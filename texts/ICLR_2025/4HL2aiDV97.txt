009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision-language pre-trained models (VLPs) have been deployed in numerous real-
012            world applications; however, these models are vulnerable to adversarial attacks.
013            Existing adversarial detection methods have shown their efficacy in single-modality
014            settings (either vision or language), while their performance on VLPs, as mul-
015            timodal models, remains uncertain. In this work, we propose a novel aspect of
016            adversarial detection called GAD-VLP, which detects adversarial examples by
017            exploiting vision and joint vision-language embeddings within VLP architectures.
018
               We leverage the geometry of the embedding space and demonstrate the unique
               characteristics of adversarial regions within these models. We explore the embed-
019
               ding space of the vision modality or the combined vision-language modalities,
020
               depending on the type of VLP, to identify adversarial examples. Some of the
021            geometric methods do not require explicit knowledge of the adversary’s targets in
022            downstream tasks (e.g., zero-shot classification or image-text retrieval), offering
023            a model-agnostic detection framework applicable across VLPs. Despite its sim-
024            plicity, we demonstrate that these methods deliver a nearly perfect detection rate
025            on state-of-the-art adversarial attacks against VLPs, including both separate and
026            combined attacks on the vision and joint modalities.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Vision-language pre-trained models (VLPs) enable the interpretation of both visual and textual
032   data by learning joint representations of multimodal inputs. This makes them highly effective for
033
      tasks requiring a deep understanding of both images and text. VLPs have achieved state-of-the-art
      results in many multimodal tasks (Yin et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2023; Gandhi et al., 2023), including
034
      image-text retrieval (Chen et al., 2020a), visual question answering (Lu et al., 2019), and zero-shot
035
      classification (Radford et al., 2021). Despite their success, VLPs remain vulnerable to adversarial
036   examples (Zhang et al., 2022a; Schlarmann & Hein, 2023), posing a challenge to their robustness in
037   real-world safety-critical applications. The safety of vision-language models is vital in critical tasks
038   like report generation and visual question answering, especially in healthcare, where errors can lead
039   to misdiagnosis or inappropriate treatment. Inaccuracies in image-text retrieval may also have serious
040   consequences in high-stakes domains, making the robustness of these models essential.
041
      Recent works have explored adversarial training as a means to improve the zero-shot robustness of
042   VLPs (Mao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024; Schlarmann et al., 2024). However, adversarial training is
043   known to be time-consuming (Madry et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2020) and often requires a trade-off
044   between performance and robustness (Zhang et al., 2019; Tsipras et al., 2019). Detecting adversarial
045   examples offers a more flexible alternative since it allows the model to reject queries by refusing to
046   provide output when they are identified as adversarial.
047
      Many methods have previously been proposed for detecting adversarial examples in unimodal models
048   (Feinman et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2018; Ma et al., 2018; Sotgiu et al., 2020; Kherchouche et al., 2020;
049   Aldahdooh et al., 2023). However, it remains unclear whether these methods generalize to VLPs that
050   involve two interacting modalities. Previous work primarily focused on detecting adversarial images
051   in classification models, which are trained using cross-entropy loss to minimize the discrepancy
052   between predictions and labels. However, in VLPs, the training process centers around minimizing
053   the distance between text and image embeddings. There is a lack of comprehensive investigation into
      detecting adversarial examples in multimodal pre-trained models, such as VLPs.

      In this paper, we propose an adversarial example detection method, GAD-VLP, which leverages
065
      geometric approaches, including local intrinsic dimensionality (LID), k-nearest neighbors distance
066   (k-NN), Mahalanobis distance, and kernel density estimation (KDE), to identify adversarial images
067   in VLPs. These metrics allow us to characterize the properties of adversarial subspaces, which is
068   crucial for detecting adversarial examples. By evaluating the geometric properties around points, we
069   can reveal the structural differences between adversarial and clean data regions in image classification
070   models (Ma et al., 2018). All approaches involve extracting distances and metrics from the unimodal
071   or multimodal encoders within VLPs, which are then used to detect adversarial examples. The
072   overview of GAD-VLP is illustrated in Figure 1. Figure 1a presents the diagram for adversarial
073   image detection using LID, while Figure 1b illustrates adversarial detection using the other three
074
      studied methods, k-NN, Mahalanobis distance, and KDE. The key difference is that LID operates in
      a layer-wise manner, evaluating the outputs of both multimodal layers and other intermediate layers,
075
      while the other three methods operate on the output of the image encoder.
076
077   The novelty of our research lies in exploring geometric approaches for adversarial detection in VLPs,
078   advancing the field by providing a robust method for different types of VLPs—aligned and fused.
079   Aligned VLPs (Zhang et al., 2022a) maintain separate embeddings for image and text modalities but
080
      ensure that they are aligned for effective interaction. In contrast, fused VLPs (Zhang et al., 2022a)
      integrate both image and text modalities into a single multimodal embedding. Our work reveals the
081
      unique characteristics of adversarial regions in VLPs, making them more distinguishable compared to
082
      traditional image classification models. Notably, GAD-VLP operates within the representation space,
083   independent of specific applications, and remains effective across diverse adversarial objectives in
084   downstream tasks, such as zero-shot classification and image-text retrieval. The main contributions
085   of this paper can be summarized as follows:
086
087             • Adversarial Detection in VLPs: By leveraging the concept of both supervised and unsu-
088               pervised geometric approaches, GAD-VLP effectively distinguishes between clean and
089               adversarial images.
090             • Architecture-Independent Applicability: GAD-VLP is universally applicable across various
091               types of VLPs. Through extensive experiments on benchmark datasets, we evaluate the
092               methods’ performance across different VLP designs, including both aligned and fused
093               architectures. Our results demonstrate that the proposed approach is effective for adversarial
094               detection in a wide range of VLPs, showcasing its versatility and robustness.
095             • Robust Detection Across Diverse Attacks: We evaluate GAD-VLP across a variety of attacks,
096               demonstrating its effectiveness with competitive AUC scores. Our results indicate that the
097               proposed approach maintains robust performance across different attack types, highlighting
098               its versatility and reliability in adversarial detection.

100   2     R ELATED W ORKS
101
102   Vision-Language Pre-Trained Models. Vision-language representation learning displays superior
103   performance across a wide range of tasks compared to visual representation learning models. For
104   instance, CLIP uses a contrastive objective (i.e., InfoNCE loss (Oord et al., 2018)) that aims to
105   align an image with its corresponding textual description in the feature space. VLPs aim to improve
106   multimodal task performance by pretraining extensive image-to-text pairs (Li et al., 2022). Several
107   recent methods utilize pre-trained object detectors with region features as a foundation for obtaining
      vision-language representations (Chen et al., 2020b).

108
      There are two primary types of VLPs depending on their architectures: fused VLPs and aligned VLPs
109   (Zhang et al., 2022a). Fused VLPs, such as ALBEF and TCL (Yang et al., 2022), utilize distinct uni-
110   modal encoders to handle token embeddings and visual characteristics separately. They subsequently
111   employ a multimodal encoder to produce integrated multimodal embeddings by combining image
112   and text embeddings. Conversely, aligned VLPs such as CLIP are composed solely of unimodal
113   encoders that have separate embeddings for image and text modalities. This research specifically
114   examines widely used architectures including both fused and aligned VLPs.
115   Adversarial Robustness. Adversarial attacks aim to deceive deep learning models into misclassifying
116   an input (Szegedy et al., 2013). Previous works are centered around image classification. Recent
117   studies show that vision-language models are also vulnerable to adversarial attacks. For example, Xu
118   et al. (2018) investigated attacks on visual question-answering models by altering the image modality.
119   In contrast, Agrawal et al. (2018); Shah et al. (2019) focused on disrupting vision-language models
120   through text modality perturbations. Zhang et al. (2022a) explored adversarial attacks on VLPs,
121   offering key insights into the development of multimodal attacks and improving model robustness.
122   Building on this, Lu et al. (2023); He et al. (2023); Han et al. (2023) worked on enhancing the
123
      transferability of multimodal adversarial examples by leveraging cross-modal interactions, data
      augmentation, and optimal transport theory. Additionally, Yin et al. (2023b); Zhou et al. (2023) aimed
124
      to improve upon the techniques introduced by Zhang et al. (2022a). However, many attack methods
125
      are not well-suited for transformer-based VLPs and primarily target vision-language classification
126   tasks, limiting their generalization to non-classification tasks. Therefore, we adopted the adversarial
127   attacks presented in Zhang et al. (2022a) as our baseline.
128
129
      With the rise of large-scale VLPs, their robustness towards adversarial attacks has become a major
      concern. For instance, Yang et al. (2021) explored the robustness of various multimodal models and
130
      proposed a defense strategy primarily designed to defend against attacks on a single modality, with
131
      unclear performance against multimodal attacks. It relies on redundancy between modalities, making
132   it less effective when modalities provide complementary information. Fine-tuning is another popular
133   approach to adapting pre-trained models for specific downstream tasks (Devlin, 2018). However,
134   fine-tuning vision-language models often results in overfitting. Mao et al. (2022) addressed this issue
135   by investigating adversarial example generation and proposing an adversarial fine-tuning algorithm
136   guided by textual supervision. Additionally, Li et al. (2024) enhanced model robustness by utilizing a
137   text encoder to generate fixed anchors (normalized feature embeddings) for each category and then
138   using these anchors for adversarial training. While fine-tuning shows promising results, it suffers
139   from issues such as inefficiency and overfitting. In light of these limitations, detecting adversarial
140
      examples presents an efficient alternative approach to defending VLPs against adversarial attacks.
141   Geometric Adversarial Detection in Unimodal Models. Several studies have employed geometric
142   approaches to detect adversarial examples in unimodal classification models. Grosse et al. (2017)
143   introduced the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a kernel-based two-sample statistical test that
144   distinguishes adversarial examples from a model’s training data. This model-agnostic approach serves
145   as a robust technique for detecting adversarial inputs. As an alternative to KDE, Ma et al. (2018)
146
      employed LID to evaluate the distance distribution of an input relative to its neighbors, capturing the
      local complexity of the sample’s surrounding space. Additionally, Lee et al. (2018) proposed using
147
      Mahalanobis distance, leveraging Gaussian Discriminant Analysis (GDA) to detect out-of-distribution
148
      and adversarial samples through a generative classifier, offering a more refined confidence score than
149   the traditional softmax classifier. Cohen et al. (2020) further explored k-NN for adversarial detection.
150   While these methods have shown promise in unimodal settings, their efficiency in VLPs remains
151   largely unexplored.

      Local Intrinsic Dimension (LID) LID is a concept of dimensionality modeling (Karger & Ruhl,
183   2002; Houle et al., 2012) that quantifies the intrinsic dimensionality in the proximity of a specific
184   point of interest in the dataset. LID evaluates the rate at which the number of encountered data objects
185   grows as the distance from the point increases (Houle, 2013). It is a statistical model that expands
186   upon the generalized expansion dimension model and presupposes the presence of an unknown
187   smooth distribution of distances from a reference point (Houle, 2017). LID focuses on estimating the
188   intrinsic dimensionality within a localized region surrounding a data point.
189   In practice, this quantity needs to be estimated with the query point x and a set of reference points
190   that can be used to select its nearest neighbors (Levina & Bickel, 2004; Amsaleg et al., 2015). For a
191   given reference sample x drawn from the data distribution P , the maximum likelihood estimator of
192   LID is defined as follows:

453   5.2     R ESULTS
454
455
      Performance of GAD-VLP in Zero-Shot Classification. As can be seen in Table 1, geometric
      approaches consistently outperform the MCM method across all datasets in CLIPCNN , achieving
456
      lower FPR and higher AUC. This highlights the effectiveness of GAD-VLP in detecting adversarial
457
      samples. Notably, k-NN surpasses other metrics (particularly Mahalanobis and KDE) in CLIPCNN ,
458   with LID showing comparable performance to k-NN in this context.
459
460   While recent advancements in VLPs have largely centered on CLIP, we extended our evaluation to
461
      include the ALBEF model. ALBEF, which features a fused multimodal encoder alongside separate
      encoders for image and text, presents a different dynamic: Mahalanobis distance outperforms other
462
      metrics (especially KDE), demonstrating its strength in detecting adversarial examples in the ALBEF
463
      model. Additionally, in the context of multimodal attacks (SepMulti ), LID shows similar performance,
464   underscoring the importance of multimodal embeddings in detecting such attacks.
465
466   For CLIPCNN , k-NN proves particularly effective due to the detailed representation of visual inputs
467
      provided by the image embeddings from the encoder, allowing k-NN to effectively capture local
      differences between clean and adversarial samples. Since adversarial perturbations in CLIP typically
468
      result in subtle shifts within the embedding space, k-NN’s neighbor-based distance calculations are
469
      well-suited to identifying outliers. Similarly, LID, by capturing the local dimensionality of the space,
470   further emphasizes its strength in detecting adversarial samples in CLIPCNN .
471
472   In ALBEF, the Mahalanobis distance, applied to image embeddings, excels in identifying deviations
473
      from the expected distribution. Although ALBEF integrates multimodal information, adversar-
      ial perturbations within the image modality still produce measurable changes in the embedding
474
      space. Mahalanobis, with its capacity to model the covariance structure of clean image embeddings,
475
      effectively identifies these deviations without relying heavily on multimodal interactions. Further-
476   more, LID, when incorporating multimodal embedding outputs as a feature in the detection process
477   (SepMulti ), demonstrates performance comparable to Mahalanobis distance. Additional results for
478   CLIPViT and TCL, provided in Appendix A.3, show consistent patterns with those in this subsection.

505   5.3    A BLATION S TUDY
506
507   The Effect of Multimodal Embeddings on Adversarial Detection. We evaluate the impact of
508   including a multimodal layer on LID value computation. As shown in Figure 4, the exclusion of this
509
      feature results in a reduction in AUC scores in most datasets. This indicates that for attacks based
      on the multimodal encoder, the inclusion of this layer is crucial. The LID difference in this layer is
510
      significantly more noticeable compared to other layers.
511
512
513   Generalization to Different Gradient-based Attacks. It is important to investigate whether the
514   detector can effectively detect samples from different attack strategies. To address this, we conduct
515   an evaluation to assess its ability to generalize to new attack baselines beyond the PGD-based attacks.
516   Specifically, for LID method, we train the detector using PGD-based attacks and then evaluate its
517   performance on samples generated from other attack strategies, including FGSM (Goodfellow et al.,
518   2014), R-FGSM (Tramèr et al., 2018), I-FGSM (Kurakin et al., 2018), and MI-FGSM (Dong et al.,
519
      2018). Both the training and test datasets follow the same preparation method as in our previous
      experiments, with PGD-based attacks applied to the training set, while evaluated attacks are used for
520
      the test set. For the other methods (k-NN, Mahalanobis, and KDE), since they rely on thresholds,
521
      we simply assess their performance against the new attack types. The results, presented in Table
522   3, demonstrate that the geometric-based method shows significant generalizability across various
523   gradient-based attack strategies.

      6     C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this paper, we address, for the first time, the problem of detecting adversarial attacks against
530   VLPs, which are increasingly applied across diverse domains. We demonstrate that our framework,
531
      GAD-VLP, which utilizes simple geometric metrics applied to image or joint representations, can
      effectively detect adversarial examples. Our detection approach generalizes across various tasks
532
      and state-of-the-art VLPs—CLIPCNN , CLIPViT , ALBEF, and TCL. A key insight from our study
533
      is the increased separation between clean and adversarial geometric scores in the latent space of
534   the CLIP model, in contrast to traditional classifiers. This distinction enhances the effectiveness of
535   geometric scores for adversarial detection. Our results demonstrate that the proposed framework
536   performs robustly across different VLP architectures, whether aligned or fused, and is effective
537   against state-of-the-art adversarial attacks. Moreover, the detection process is independent of the
538   downstream tasks. An open issue for future research is the examination of text-exclusive attacks, a
539   prominent concern within VLPs. Further exploration is necessary to identify robust methodologies
      for leveraging the embeddings in the detection of adversarial attacks in the text domain.
