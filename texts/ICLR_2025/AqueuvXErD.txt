                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               This paper looks into the critical area of deep learning robustness and challenges
015
               the common belief that classification robustness and explanation robustness in im-
016            age classification systems are inherently correlated. Through a novel evaluation
017            approach leveraging clustering for efficient assessment of explanation robustness,
018            we demonstrate that enhancing explanation robustness does not necessarily flat-
019            ten the input loss landscape with respect to explanation loss - contrary to flattened
020            loss landscapes indicating better classification robustness. To further investigate
021            this contradiction, a training method designed to adjust the loss landscape with
022            respect to explanation loss is proposed. Through the new training method, we un-
023            cover that although such adjustments can impact the robustness of explanations,
024
               they do not have an influence on the robustness of classification. These findings
               not only challenge the previous assumption of a strong correlation between the
025
               two forms of robustness but also pave new pathways for understanding the rela-
026
               tionship between loss landscape and explanation loss. Codes are provided in the
027            supplement.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In deep learning, the robustness of image classification systems against adversarial instances has
033   emerged as an important area of research. These systems, integral to modern artificial intelligence,
034   frequently encounter scenarios where adversarial instances—subtly altered images designed to de-
035
      ceive algorithms—pose significant challenges. At the heart of this challenge lie two critical con-
      cepts: classification robustness and explanation robustness. Classification robustness refers to a
036
      model’s ability to maintain accuracy under adversarial attacks (Szegedy et al., 2013; Madry et al.,
037
      2017), while explanation robustness pertains to the consistency of the model’s interpretative outputs
038   in such adversarial scenarios (Dombrowski et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2023). Traditionally, there’s
039   been a prevailing conclusion within the research community (Boopathy et al., 2020; Huang et al.,
040   2023):
041
042       Conclusion: Classification robustness and explanation robustness are strongly correlated:
043
          Increasing classification robustness can increase explanation robustness and vice versa.
044   This paper, however, unveils a finding that disrupts this conventional belief: a contradiction in the
045   assumed correlation between classification robustness and explanation robustness. This revelation
046   not only challenges established assumptions but also opens new avenues for understanding and
047   improving the resilience of deep learning models.
048   Adversarial attacks on classification aim at deceiving image classification models by introducing
049   perturbations to benign images (Szegedy et al., 2013). To defend against adversarial examples,
050   adversarial training (AT) (Madry et al., 2017; Goodfellow et al., 2014) is one of the most effective
051   approaches which explicitly augments the training process to enhance a model’s inherent robustness
052   against adversarial samples for classification. Classification robustness typically is referred as the
053   classification accuracy under adversarial attacks, and AT methods are effective in improving the
      classification robustness of a deep learning model.

      Explanation maps (Baehrens et al., 2010), also known as saliency maps, are proposed to explain deep
      learning methods by feature importance. However, explanation maps are themselves also vulnerable
077
      to adversarial attacks (Dombrowski et al., 2019; Ghorbani et al., 2019). For example, in Fig. 1a, by
078
      making small visual changes to the input sample which hardly influences the network’s output, the
079   explanations can be arbitrarily manipulated (Dombrowski et al., 2019). Explanation robustness is
080   referred as the error between victim explanation under adversarial attacks on the input and targeted
081   explanation.
082
      To better understand robustness, one important way is to explore the input loss landscape (Li &
083
      Spratling, 2023). Existing work has found out that a flat input loss landscape w.r.t classification
084
      loss indicates better classification robustness (Xie et al., 2020; Li & Spratling, 2023), as shown
085   in Fig. 1b. To visualize the input loss landscape, we add the random perturbation to the inputs with
086   magnitude α (detailed method in Section 4). The results in Fig. 1b show that models with higher
087   classification robustness have a flatter input loss landscape w.r.t classification loss.
088
      Then a natural question comes up for explanation robustness:
089
090       Q: Does increasing explanation robustness of a model also flatten input loss landscape w.r.t
091                                          explanation loss?
092
093   We visualize the input loss landscape w.r.t explanation loss in Fig. 4 using models with different
094   levels of explanation robustness and find that, surprisingly, increasing the explanation robustness
095   does not flatten the input loss landscape w.r.t explanation loss. Specifically, to obtain models with
096
      different levels of explanation robustness, we consider utilizing adversarial training methods that
      allow us to control the emphasis on classification robustness (Zhang et al., 2019) since previous
097
      works have proven that increasing classification robustness can also increase explanation robustness.
098
099   The previous observation that increasing the explanation robustness does not flatten the input loss
100   landscape w.r.t explanation loss is strange compared with increasing classification robustness could
101   flatten the input loss landscape w.r.t classification loss. To further explore this observation, we ask
102
      the previous question in a reverse way:
103     Q: Does flattening the input loss landscape w.r.t explanation loss not increase the robustness of
104                                          explanations as well?
105
106   The answer to this question is, flattening the input loss landscape w.r.t explanation loss will decrease
107   the explanation robustness. Specifically, we propose a new loss function to flatten the loss landscape
      w.r.t explanation loss. The results show that adding the loss will decrease the explanation robustness

108
      but not change the classification robustness measured by adversarial accuracy. This observation,
109   indicating that influencing explanation robustness does not impact classification robustness, chal-
110   lenges the previous conclusion: the correlation between explanation robustness and classification
111   robustness may not hold.
112
      Overall, we summarize our contributions as follows:
113
114         • We propose a sampling method based on cluster methods that can choose representative
115            pairs to evaluate explanation robustness more efficiently.
116         • We use TRADES (Zhang et al., 2019) to control the classification robustness and expla-
117            nation robustness and visualize the input loss landscape w.r.t explanation loss to find that
118            increasing the explanation robustness by increasing the classification robustness does not
119            flatten the input loss landscape.
120         • We propose a new training method that flattens the input loss landscape w.r.t explanation
121            loss. The training results show that explanation robustness may not be strongly correlated
122            to classification robustness.

126   Adversarial Attack and Adversarial Training (AT) It has been proven that convolutional neural
127   networks (CNNs) are vulnerable to the adversarial examples (Szegedy et al., 2013; Goodfellow et al.,
128   2014; Carlini & Wagner, 2017). Noise that is imperceptible to humans, when added to the original
129   inputs, can lead to the misclassification of models. Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) (Madry et al.,
130   2017) is one of the most popular methods that generate such a noise or evaluate models’ classifi-
131   cation robustness by calculating accuracy under its attack. Many methods have been introduced to
132
      defend against adversarial attacks including knowledge distillation (Papernot et al., 2016), quantiza-
      tion (Xu et al., 2017; Lin et al., 2019) and noise purification (Song et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022).
133
      However, these preprocessing methods do not involve a training process and may be vulnerable to
134
      adaptive attack (Athalye et al., 2018). Goodfellow et al. (Goodfellow et al., 2014) first introduced
135   adversarial training (AT), which trains a model from scratch with adversarial samples. Adversar-
136   ial Training (AT) proved its performance including adversarial competitions (Madry et al., 2017;
137   Brendel et al., 2020). In our paper, we also focus on classification robustness increased by AT.
138
      Many works tend to increase the performance of AT through external datasets (Hendrycks et al.,
139
      2019; Carmon et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023), metric learning (Pang et al., 2019), self-supervised
140
      learning (Chen et al., 2020a), ensemble learning (Tramèr et al., 2017), label smoothing (Chen et al.,
141   2020b) and Taylor Expansion (Jin et al., 2023). Wu et al. (Wu et al., 2020) found that obtaining a
142   flat loss landscape can help increase classification robustness, which inspired the ideas in this paper.
143   There is also a line of work that attempts to accelerate AT. For example, Shafahi et al. (Shafahi
144   et al., 2019) reused calculated adversarial noises, Liu et al. (Liu et al., 2021) introduced single-step
145   training. In this paper, we mainly consider the Madry adversarial training (Madry et al., 2017) and
146   TRADES (Zhang et al., 2019).
147
      Explanation Robustness Saliency maps (Simonyan et al., 2013; Shrikumar et al., 2017; Bach et al.,
148
      2015; Selvaraju et al., 2016) are widely used to explain image-related tasks in deep learning, and
149
      our focus is on the robustness of these explanations. However, similar to an adversarial attack,
150   it is possible to find an adversarial noise on original images so that it can easily manipulate the
151   saliency maps without changing classification results in both white-box (Dombrowski et al., 2019;
152   Ghorbani et al., 2019; Heo et al., 2019; Slack et al., 2020) and black-box settings (Tamam et al.,
153   2022). Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2020) further introduced a new method that can attack both
154   saliency maps and classification results. In order to evaluate the explanation robustness, Wicker et
155   al. (Wicker et al., 2022) introduced the max-sensitivity and average-sensitivity of saliency maps.
156   Alvarez et al. (Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018) estimated explanation robustness by the Local
157   Lipschitz of interpretation while Tamam et al. (Tamam et al., 2022) directly used attack loss to
158
      evaluate explanation robustness. In this paper, we use attack loss based on the proposed cluster
      method to evaluate explanation robustness.
159
160   Several works have also aimed to improve explanation robustness. Chen et al. (Chen et al., 2019)
161   introduced a regularization term during training to make the explanation more robust. Boopathy et
      al. (Boopathy et al., 2020) improved the performance by training with noisy labels. Tang et al. (Tang

      Relationship between Classification Robustness and Explanation Previous works have demon-
      strated that a good explanation is crucial for classification robustness (Simonyan et al., 2019; Wang
183
      et al., 2021), suggesting that a better saliency map correlates with improved classification robust-
184
      ness. Follow-up work by Boopathy et al. (Boopathy et al., 2020), Tang et al. (Tang et al., 2022)
185   and Huang et al. (Huang et al., 2023) further demonstrated when models are more robust to attacks
186   manipulating explanations, their robustness to classification attacks also improves and vice versa.
187   Therefore, increasing explanation robustness can benefit classification robustness.
188
      In our paper, we prove that improving explanation robustness indeed also boosts classification ro-
189
      bustness, specifically under adversarial training regimes using TRADES (Zhang et al., 2019). How-
190
      ever, through further analysis, we prove these two facets of robustness are not inherently the same
191   - they can be disconnected. Classification robustness is not fundamentally vital for explanation
192   robustness.

      5.2   SEPARATING E XPLANATION AND C LASSIFICATION ROBUSTNESS
408
409   We conducted a series of experiments involving multiple models and datasets on Gradient × Input
410
      and results are shown in Table 2 for ConvNet and ResNet18. We have the following observations:
411         • On one hand, SEPpos , SEPneg , and MAT have very similar adversarial accuracy, indicating
412           their classification robustness is similar in all datasets and models. On the other hand,
413           SEPpos shows the weakest explanation robustness by having the lowest explanation loss
414           at end. Similarly, SEPneg shows the strongest explanation robustness. These results show
415           that there is no inherent relationship between explanation robustness and classification
416
              robustness. The different performance w.r.t. explanation loss at end for SEPpos and SEPneg
              is mainly induced by the difference in explanation loss at start, which is influenced by our
417
              training method by setting different λ.
418
            • In the setting of CIFAR10 and ResNet18, increasing the explanation robustness by SEPneg
419
              hurts the clean accuracy while it still does not change classification robustness. This obser-
420           vation further validates our argument: classification robustness and explanation robustness
421           may not be strongly correlated. We provide the results for W-ResNet and MoblieNetV2
422           in the Appendix Table 6 and the results show a very similar conclusion to the results of
423           ConvNet and ResNet.

      6      C ONCLUSION
524   In summary, our study challenges the previous conclusion that explanation robustness and classifica-
525   tion robustness are strongly correlated. Using TRADES (Zhang et al., 2019) to control explanation
526   robustness by adjusting classification robustness, we found that increasing explanation robustness
527   does not necessarily lead to a flatter input loss landscape for explanation loss. This contrasts with
528   the observation that enhancing classification robustness results in a flatter input loss landscape for
529
      classification robustness. We introduce a novel algorithm to flatten the input loss landscape for
      explanation loss, addressing this contradiction. Our results show that our algorithm effectively im-
530
      proves explanation robustness without changing classification robustness, indicating a potential lack
531
      of strong correlation between the two. Our results reveal the importance of considering and opti-
532   mizing both aspects separately to ensure the overall reliability and trustworthiness of AI systems
533   in sensitive areas such as healthcare. For our future works, we hope to dive into two different ro-
534   bustness to understand why adversarial training can increase explanation robustness and what might
535   be the inner difference between two robustness to understand more about the inner mechanism of
536   adversarial attacks and explanations.

1001
       classification robustness still increases while the explanation robustness actually decreases.
