011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent work showed that retrieval based on embedding similarity (e.g., for
014
               retrieval-augmented generation) is vulnerable to poisoning: an adversary can craft
               malicious documents that are retrieved in response to broad classes of queries. We
015
               demonstrate that previous, HotFlip-based techniques produce documents that are
016
               very easy to detect using perplexity filtering. Even if generation is constrained to
017            produce low-perplexity text, the resulting documents are recognized as unnatural
018            by LLMs and can be automatically filtered from the retrieval corpus.
019            We design, implement, and evaluate a new controlled generation technique that
020            combines an adversarial objective (embedding similarity) with a “naturalness”
021            objective based on soft scores computed using an open-source, surrogate LLM.
022            The resulting adversarial documents (1) cannot be automatically detected using
023            perplexity filtering and/or other LLMs, except at the cost of significant false pos-
024            itives in the retrieval corpus, yet (2) achieve similar poisoning efficacy to easily-
025            detectable documents generated using HotFlip, and (3) are significantly more ef-
026            fective than prior methods for energy-guided generation, such as COLD.
027
028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Many modern retrieval systems use embeddings, i.e., dense vector representations, of documents
031   and queries to enable retrieval based on semantic similarity. Chaudhari et al. (2024) and Zhong
032   et al. (2023) recently demonstrated that an adversary can use HotFlip Ebrahimi et al. (2018) to
033   generate documents whose embeddings have high similarity to, and will thus be retrieved in response
034   to, broad classes of queries. An adversary can poison a retrieval corpus with such documents to
035   propagate spam and disinformation, influence retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), etc.
036   We first demonstrate that adversarial documents produced by HotFlip have much higher perplexity
037   than normal text and can be filtered out with negligible collateral damage (i.e., false positives).
038   Controlled generation with a perplexity constraint is a plausible evasion. It produces low-perplexity,
039   yet unnatural text. Our first contribution is a defense that uses multiple LLMs and prompts to filter
040   out unnatural adversarial texts without collateral damage to legitimate documents.
041
      The main question we investigate in the rest of this paper is whether it is possible to generate ad-
042   versarial texts whose embeddings are similar to broad classes of queries, yet evade both perplexity-
043   based and LLM-based detection of unnatural documents. Inspired by prior work on controllable
044   text generation Lu et al. (2021), we design, implement, and evaluate a new adversarial decoding
045   method for generating natural-looking adversarial documents. Our method maximizes cosine simi-
046   larity with the target embedding and minimizes perplexity in the decoding stage of generation. As
047   mentioned above, this is not enough. We also use the logits of a surrogate open-source LLM to
048   compute a soft “naturalness” score that helps guide generation towards natural sequences. Adver-
049   sarial decoding requires only black-box access to the embedding encoder models (and can thus be
050
      deployed against closed-source encoders) and no access to the LLMs used for detection or filtering.
051   We demonstrate that adversarial decoding is effective in the retrieval poisoning scenarios considered
052   in Chaudhari et al. (2024) and Zhong et al. (2023). It outperforms energy-guided decoding Qin
053   et al. (2022) and produces adversarial texts that are likely to be judged as natural by LLM-based
      detection, including LLMs significantly more advanced than the surrogate LLM used for guidance.

060   Zhong et al. (2023) and Xue et al. (2024) poison a retrieval corpus by first separating documents into
061
      clusters, then generating an adversarial document for each cluster using a gradient-based method
      similar to HotFlip (see Section 3). Shafran et al. (2024) use black-box optimization to generate
062
      adversarial documents that are retrieved for specific queries and cause the RAG system to refuse to
063
      answer these queries. Chaudhari et al. (2024) generate adversarial documents that are retrieved for
064   any query containing a particular trigger word, and also investigate how to influence LLM responses
065   generated from the retrieved documents (i.e., RAG poisoning).
066
067
      Adversarial documents produced by these attacks are unnatural and thus not stealthy. Morris et al.
      (2020) show that if adversarial examples are constrained to be grammatical and to preserve the
068
      semantics of documents from which they are generated, their success rate against several NLP tasks
069
      drops by over 70%. Jain et al. (2023) show that optimization-based attacks are unable to achieve
070   both low perplexity and jailbreaking (a different adversarial objective from the embedding similarity
071   objective in this paper). Cho et al. (2024) propose Genetic Attack on RAG to generate adversarial
072   typos in text to poison retrieval systems. Zhang et al. (2024) insert adversarial strings at locations
073   that are invisible to readers when rendered in rich text formats like HTML. These approaches do not
074   produce natural adversarial text but rather hide unnatural text where it is unlikely to be noticed.
075
      Xiang et al. (2024) propose a defense for RAG that generates the answer based on the majority of
076   retrieved documents. The answer is correct as long as adversarial documents are in the minority.
077
078   Retrieval poisoning is related to “hubness”: there exists a small set of embedding vectors that are
079
      similar to many queries. Liu et al. (2020) proposed a hubness-aware loss for text-image matching.

081   3   BACKGROUND : C ONTROLLED G ENERATION
082
083   There are several methods for generating natural text that satisfies non-adversarial constraints, e.g.,
084   LLM decoding. Lu et al. (2021) introduced a soft penalty term in the LLM beam-search decoding
085   objective to generate fluent text satisfying lexical constraints.
086   HotFlip is a white-box method for generating adversarial texts Ebrahimi et al. (2018). Starting with
087   a sequence of [PAD] tokens, it iteratively replaces individual tokens with the token t∗ from the
088   vocabulary that reduces the target loss function the most, t∗ = arg mint∈V −e⊤   t ∇et L(x), where
089   et is the token embedding, ∇et L(x) is the gradient of the loss. Because token space is discrete,
090   HotFlip uses beam search to explore multiple flip candidates. Chaudhari et al. (2024) and Zhong
091   et al. (2023) used HotFlip to produce adversarial documents for retrieval poisoning.
092   COLD uses an energy-based model (EBM) for constrained text generation Qin et al. (2022). Starting
093   with a “soft” sequence of token vectors in logit space, it applies an energy function E(y) to evaluate
094   how well the sequence meets constraints such as fluency or coherence, then iteratively updates it
095   using Langevin dynamics y (n+1) ← y (n) − η∇E(y (n) ) + ϵ(n) , where η is the step size and ϵ(n) is
096   noise. After several iterations, the soft sequence is converted into discrete text using top-k sampling.
097   Guo et al. (2024) applied COLD to generate adversarial text for jailbreak attacks. In our experi-
098   ments, the original COLD failed to produce useful text under an adversarial constraint (embedding
099   similarity). For comparisons in Section 7, we modify COLD following Song et al. (2020): always
100   set the noise level to 0 and use beam search for decoding instead of top-k sampling.

107   the retrieval system returns top K documents Pk∗ = TopK(q, Pn , k) whose embeddings are most
      similar to E(q): TopK(q, P, k) = {pi ∈ P : |{pj ∈ P : Sim(q, pj ) > Sim(q, pi )}| < k}

      Trigger attack. As in Chaudhari et al. (2024), we assume that the attacker controls a small fraction
109   of the retrieval corpus Pn . His goal is to generate documents that will be retrieved when some trigger
110   word occurs in the query. For example, the attacker may target queries about rival products by using
111   brands and product names as triggers (e.g., “spotify”). This attack may be used to decrease the
112   quality of retrieval results or to spread adversarial content. We assume that the attacker is restricted
113   to a single document per trigger, to keep the attack stealthy.
114
      Formally, the attacker’s goal is to generate text a′ that, when added to the corpus, will be retrieved
115   as a top-k result whenever trigger strg is prepended to query q from the set of queries Q: a′ ∈
116   TopK(strg ⊕ q, Pn ∪ {a′ }, k), ∀q ∈ Q, Generating such documents is non-trivial because there could
117   be millions of real documents in the corpus. a′ cannot be query-specific (e.g., simply repeating the
118   query will not work) because it should be retrieved for any query with the trigger.

      achieves this goal, the attacker can insert a set of adversarial documents A into the corpus Pn , where

      trained and the attacker has no control over it. Furthermore, the attacker has only black-box access
      to E and cannot observe its gradients when generating adversarial documents.

145   Decoding strategies are essential for generating coherent text. Beam search enhances quality of
146   generation by exploring multiple possible output sequences and focusing on the most probable ones.
147   Beam search maintains a fixed number of beams (hypotheses). At every time step, each beam
148   is extended by appending tokens with the highest probabilities, ensuring that the final sequence
149
      represents the most likely or plausible completion of the given input. At the end of the time step,
      all candidates are ranked by their log-likelihood and only top candidates are retained. In effect, this
150
      process optimizes for fluency and alignment with the model’s training data.
151

211   5.2    A DDING A NATURALNESS C ONSTRAINT
212
213   Because decoding under an embedding constraint does not produce natural text, we also need to
214   evaluate “naturalness” and impose the corresponding constraint on generation. We use an auxiliary
215   LLM as an evaluator by prompting it “Is this text unintelligible? {TEXT} Just answer Yes or No.”
      Empirically, this prompt has few false positives (see Section 6) and yields naturalness scores that


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      work well for optimization. Still, binary decisions do not change smoothly with small modifications
217   to the text and thus cannot serve as an optimization target to guide generation during beam search.
218
219   To overcome this limitation, we developed a new method to compute a soft score that can be
220
      smoothly incorporated into the beam search objective. LLMs output logits zt that indicate the next-
      token distribution conditioned on the input sequence. The outputs of our evaluator LLM are “Yes”
221
      and “No”. We use the ratio between the corresponding probabilities as a “naturalness” score:
222
223
                                                            zt (wno )
                                           snatural =
224                                                   zt (wno ) + zt (wyes )
225   We incorporate this score into the beam search process as an additional objective, alongside cosine
226   similarity, with parameter λ controlling the trade-off between the objectives:
227                                            s = scos sim + λsnatural
228
      Algorithm 1 shows our full AdversarialDecoding method. At each decoding step, beams are ranked
229   not only by their semantic alignment with the target queries (measured by cosine similarity), but
230   also by their naturalness. This multi-objective optimization helps generate sequences that are (1)
231   semantically relevant to the target queries, (2) have low perplexity, (3) are natural and readable, and
232   (4) cannot be filtered out by asking an LLM to evaluate their naturalness. Empirically, adversarial
233   decoding maintains both semantic and perceptual quality of generated text in a unified decoding
234   framework. This is a significant improvement over pure likelihood-based methods.

      7.3                                  NATURALNESS F ILTERING
366
367   Low perplexity is not enough for stealthiness. Table 2 shows that all texts generated by BasicAdver-
368   sarialDecoding can be detected by the LLM-based naturalness filter defined in Section 6 with a false
369   positive rate of 0. AdversarialDecoding, however, produces documents that are much more difficult
370   to detect and filter out.
371
      The defender’s threshold on the naturalness score determines the trade-off between the true and
372   false positives. If the threshold is low, the filter detects more adversarial documents (true positives)
373   but also incorrectly detects real documents (false positives) and drops them from the corpus. If
374   the threshold is high, both true and false positives decrease and more adversarial documents evade
375   detection. Table 2 shows that a threshold which filters only 20% of adversarial documents also incor-
376   rectly filters 6% of the real corpus. Filtering 70% of adversarial documents incorrectly filters 17%
377   of the corpus. Therefore, the naturalness filter cannot be deployed to detect documents generated by
      AdversarialDecoding except at the cost of substantial collateral damage to the real corpus.

453   8    D ISCUSSION
454
455   We proposed and evaluated adversarial decoding, a new controlled generation method that creates
456   low-perplexity adversarial documents whose embeddings are similar to broad classes of queries.
457   We also demonstrated that low perplexity is not sufficient for stealthiness, since even low-perplexity
458   adversarial documents can be detected with high accuracy by LLM evaluators.
459
      We then developed a new technique that converts logits of an LLM “naturalness” evaluator into a
460   soft score that can be used to guide generation. The resulting adversarial documents maintain their
461   efficacy for retrieval poisoning but can no longer be detected by LLM evaluators, except at the cost
462   of significant collateral damage to the corpus of real documents due to false positives.
463
      Defenses are an important topic for future work. Current techniques, including ours, produce ad-
464
      versarial documents that do not transfer well across encoders. This observation can potentially be a
465
      basis for a defense (but may be evaded with more sophisticated generation methods). Hubness-aware
466   encoders are another potential direction for developing defenses.
467
468
469   E THICS S TATEMENT
470
471   This paper describes techniques for generating adversarial documents that may evade detection by
472   the best currently known methods. The sole purpose of this research is to highlight the risks of
473
      adversarial inputs in modern retrieval systems and to motivate research on effective defenses.
