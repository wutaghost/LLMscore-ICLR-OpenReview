011                                                          A BSTRACT
012
013                A complete and accurate representation of Earth’s time-evolving ocean field is crucial
014                for understanding global warming as well as climate dynamics. However, the sparsity of
015                current in-situ ocean measurements presents a significant challenge in estimating values
016                in largely unobserved regions. Traditional methods, such as objective interpolation (OI),
017
                   struggle with accuracy due to their reliance on discrete grids and fixed spatial correlation
                   structures. In this paper, we propose a novel approach to reconstruct 4D ocean fields only
018
                   from raw observations using implicit neural representations (INRs). Our method improves
019
                   field representations by leveraging neural networks to capture continuous, complex, and
020                nonlinear patterns inherent in ocean data. To address uncertainties in ocean measurements
021                and the limited availability of daily observations, we incorporate uncertainty estimates
022                and a meta-learning strategy into existing INRs. These innovations enable our approach
023                to provide daily, resolution-free ocean temperature reconstructions, a significant improve-
024                ment over monthly averaged discrete fields. Experiments demonstrate the accuracy and
025                adaptability of our method compared with approaches, establishing our method as a trans-
026                formative solution for future ocean analysis and climate monitoring.

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Global warming is one of the most crucial challenges for us. With over 90% of Earth’s excess heat ac-
032   cumulating in the ocean, accurate ocean temperature measurements are essential to monitor this ongoing
033   climate change. (Von Schuckmann et al., 2013; Abraham et al., 2013; Allan et al., 2014). Despite various
034
      ocean observing programs (Levitus, 1982; Argo, 2000; Goni et al., 2019; Roemmich et al., 2019), we still
      have a limited number of daily ocean profiling floats (∼ 400 profiles). Such sparse observation impedes
035
      our understanding of global warming and its impact on climate dynamics (Lyman & Johnson, 2008; Gille,
036
      2008; Abraham et al., 2013; Cheng & Zhu, 2014a). To overcome the shortage of raw oceanic observa-
037   tions, researchers develop methods to approximate or reconstruct a complete 4D ocean temperature field
038   from in-situ observations 1 (Hosoda et al., 2008; Li et al., 2017a; Cheng et al., 2017). Known as objective
039   interpolation (OI), these methods typically rely upon an error covariance function to correct a first-guess
040   field (Meyers et al., 1991; Bonekamp et al., 2001). To improve the accuracy of ocean reconstruction, efforts
041   have been made to improve both the first-guess field (Smith & Murphy, 2007; Levitus et al., 2012) and error
042   covariance functions (Li et al., 2017a; Cheng & Zhu, 2016; Zhang et al., 2022).
043   However, the potential of using deep learning methods on ocean reconstruction remains under-explored. In
044   this work, we present MARIN, a new AI-powered method thats integrates MetA-leaRning with Implicit

      Neural representation network to reconstruct complete 4D ocean data from raw oceanic observations. Un-
048
      like traditional methods that represent the first-guess field as discrete grid values, MARIN uses a neural
049   network to model the field as a continuous function of geographical coordinates (latitude, longitude, depth).
050   Such an idea draws inspiration from Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) (Chen et al., 2021; Sun et al.,
051   2021; Sitzmann et al., 2020), which has shown better representation accuracy compared with discrete signal
052   representations (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Ramasinghe & Lucey, 2022; Saragadam et al., 2023). In addition,
053   our method corrects the first-guess field by optimizing the neural network’s parameters with in-situ obser-
054   vations. This data-driven method addresses a major limitation of covariance functions, which are linearly
055   modeled and often fail to account for varying spatial correlations.
056   Additionally, we modify and address two challenges when learning INRs to approximate ocean temperature
057   from observation. First, ocean measurements have errors due to instruments and geolocations (Gouretski
058   & Koltermann, 2007; Cheng et al., 2016), but INRs inherently lack uncertainty estimates. We address
059   this limitation by extending the output of neural network to predict both the mean and variance of ocean
060   temperature. Second, ocean observations are sparse and unevenly distributed (Gould et al., 2013; Boyer
061   et al., 2016; Meyssignac et al., 2019). In our scenario, ocean observations are located sparsely, (∼ 400 daily
062   profiles), and large data gaps remain if considering a 1◦ × 1◦ horizontal resolution (∼ 45,360 grid points),
063
      as most ocean products consider (Cheng et al., 2024). We address this issue by treating each day’s sparse
      ocean observations as a few-shot learning task. We incorporate the meta-learning framework (Hospedales
064
      et al., 2021) that learns to optimize model weights based on each day’s observations. This allows MARIN to
065
      adapt to new data by one gradient step, eliminating the need for extensive retraining. By doing so, our
066   method enables daily ocean temperature reconstructions, a significant improvement over current methods
067   that typically offer monthly averaged estimates (Hosoda et al., 2008; Zhang et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2024).

      tinuous representation of ocean temperature fields. Experiments show that MARIN uses only 1.79% of the
070
      parameters a traditional method requires, yet reduces reconstruction error by 5.5% on unseen data when
071
      compared to in-situ observations. Notably, MARIN demonstrates significant improvements in regions with
072   complex ocean dynamics, such as reducing RMSEs ∼ 0.2◦ C in the upper 100 meters of the ocean, ∼ 0.6◦ C
073   in costal zones and high-latitude areas. These results highlight MARIN’s ability to efficiently and accurately
074   model ocean temperature fields where traditional methods struggle, making it a highly reliable choice for
075   global ocean monitoring.

      Objective gridded products. Ocean gridded products are complete ocean temperature field estimates
      mostly produced by objective interpolation (OI) methods. So far, the most widely used products are at
081
      1◦ ×1◦ horizontal resolution and monthly temporal resolution from near-surface to about 2000 meter depths.
082
      There are amoutable ocean gridded temperature data created from various groups (Hosoda et al., 2008; von
083   Schuckmann & Le Traon, 2011; Li et al., 2017a; Zhang et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2024) with different data
084   sources (Johnson et al., 2022; Cabanes et al., 2012; Boyer et al., 2018). Instead of discrete-grid values, our
085   method produces a neural network that can represent ocean temperature at any location in the upper 2000 m
086   of the ocean.
087
      Implicit representations (INRs). Also known as coordinate-based representations, INRs offer an innovative
088
      approach to parameterizing multidimensional fields. The incorporation of Fourier features (Tancik et al.,
089   2020) or modifications in advanced continuous function (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Ramasinghe & Lucey, 2022;
090   Saragadam et al., 2023) further improve their representation capabilities. INRs have begun to be applied to
091   Earth data. For example, Rußwurm et al. (2023) used spherical harmonics to encode geospatial patterns;
092   Huang & Hoefler (2022) demonstrated over 300 times memory savings by compressing 3D weather data
093   with MLP layers. These two papers learn representations of Earth field from densely gridded data. In

251
      we used to approximate temperature field. Given that conventional mapping methods often use 12-month
252

257   since 2006 (Roemmich et al., 2019). Additionally, research indicates that using a shorter 15-year period is
258   preferable (Cheng & Zhu, 2015). Longer datasets can introduce inconsistencies in spatial baselines across
259   locations, which could violate the spatial structure of the temperature field (Cheng & Zhu, 2015; Li et al.,
260   2022). Further implementation details and hyperparameters are provided in Appendix B.

374   free both horizontally and vertically, but it also reduces parameters over 50 times, making our method
375   efficient to use.

455   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
456
457   In this paper, we introduced a novel method MARIN, to reconstruct dynamic 4D ocean fields using implicit
458   neural representations (INRs) enhanced with uncertainty estimates and meta-learning. Taking advantages
459   of the continuous, nonlinear modeling capabilities of neural networks, our method demonstrated superior
460   performance over traditional interpolation techniques, offering daily, resolution-free ocean temperature re-
461   constructions. As more ocean observation data becomes available, our approach is expected to yield even
462   more accurate temperature reconstructions. We hope the contributions of this paper will inspire further
463   advancements in AI-driven ocean field estimation. While our method shows substantial improvements, sev-
464
      eral promising directions remain for future exploration. Expanding the model to include additional ocean
      variables, such as salinity and currents, would enhance the comprehensiveness of the reconstructions. Ad-
465
      ditionally, improving the handling of extreme weather events and seasonal anomalies could make the model
466
      more resilient to abrupt environmental changes. Tackling these challenges will contribute to more complete
467   weather and climate analyses and benefit the society as a whole.

      B     IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
800
801
      B.1   DATA NORMALIZATION
802
803
      Input coordinates, including latitude, longitude, and ocean depth are each normalized to 0-1 range by min-
804   max normalization. For temperature values, following conventional settings (Cheng et al., 2017; 2024),
805   compute temperature anomalies by removing long-term monthly climatological means. We notice that the
806   quality of the climatology field is crucial for global ocean reconstruction (Cheng & Zhu, 2014b; Boyer et al.,
807   2016). In this paper, we use the 30-year (1991-2020) 1◦ × 1◦ monthly means from World Ocean Atlas 2023
808   (WOA23) (Boyer et al., 2018) as our climatological field. This is because WOA23 create monthly means
809   based on data from WOD, which is the same source as our training data and previous works have use world
810   ocean atlas as ground truth values for ocean climatology analysis (Rohling et al., 2015; Meyssignac et al.,
811   2019; Osman et al., 2021).
812
813   B.2   M ETA - LEARNING SETTINGS
814
815   The task here for meta-learning is to learn how to reconstruct a complete ocean field from ocean in-situ ob-
816   servations each day. Each task here contains daily observations on a single day, and is split into support/test
817   set in a 80/20 ratio. However, the number of tasks we have is efficient to train a meta-learner. For example,
818
      the 15-year training period (2006-2020) has 31 × 15 = 465 tasks if we want to train a monthly model for
      January. This is a heavy burden for meta-learning since we need to wait for 465 tasks and perform a single
819
      meta-optimizing step on model parameters θ and gradient step α. Therefore, for each step in the outer loop,
820
      we sample data from one day in each month and create a batch of data containing in-situ observations from
821   15 days (i.e., 15 tasks). The reduction from 465 tasks to 15 further accelerates our training process, making
822   our method easy to train.
823
824
      B.3   HYPERPARAMETERS
825
826
      We used Cosine-Annealing Learning Rate scheduler, with maximum learning rate 1.e-3 and minimum learn-
827   ing rate 1.e-6. We trained the outer-loop of our meta-learning for 2000 epochs.
