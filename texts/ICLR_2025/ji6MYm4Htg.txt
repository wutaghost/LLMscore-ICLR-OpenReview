009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Pruning is a highly effective approach for compressing large language models
012            (LLMs). By strategically reducing model size, pruning significantly decreases both
013            latency and GPU memory usage during inference, resulting in more efficient and
014            cost-effective deployment of these models. Despite their effectiveness, current
015            structured pruning algorithms have limitations. They still require extensive contin-
016
               ued pre-training on large datasets to achieve model compression. Moreover, most
               of these methods are unable to reduce the memory usage of the key-value cache
017
               during generation tasks. In this work, we propose a novel pruning algorithm that
018
               requires no additional training and targets specific parameters within LLMs. We
019            classify the model’s parameters into three categories: aggregation, transformation,
020            and normalization. Our method primarily focuses on pruning the aggregation
021            parameters in the higher layers of the model. To further improve the performance
022            of the pruned LLM, we also introduce a rescaling parameter that adjusts the output
023            of the pruned block. We conduct comprehensive experiments on a wide range of
024            LLMs, including LLaMA3.1-8B/70B, Qwen2-7B/72B, Gemma2-9B, and Mistral-
025            7B-v0.3. Our evaluation includes both generation and discriminative tasks across
026            various benchmarks. The results consistently demonstrate that our method outper-
027
               forms recent block pruning methods. This improvement is particularly notable in
               generation tasks, where our approach significantly outperforms existing baselines.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Yang
033   et al., 2024; Gemma2-Team et al., 2024), pre-trained on extensive text data from across the internet,
034   have achieved remarkable performance in downstream tasks such as information retrieval (Asai et al.,
035   2024), code generation (Guo et al., 2024a), and mathematical reasoning (Wang et al., 2023; Yang
036   et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). These LLMs, however, contain a huge number of parameters,
037   resulting in substantially slower inference speed compared to their smaller counterparts. To address
038   this issue in generation tasks, a common approach is to use key-value (KV) cache (Pope et al., 2023),
039
      which stores intermediate computation results. While this technique effectively trades space for time,
      speeding up inference, it also significantly increases GPU memory consumption. As reported in Zhou
040
      et al. (2024), the KV cache size can exceed the LLM model size during peak usage, and the inference
041
      latency increases as the KV cache size grows. As a result, one major bottleneck for LLM serving is
042   GPU memory consumption.
043
044   Recent strategies to improve LLM efficiency primarily fall into two categories. The first category
045
      focuses on the models themselves, aiming to reduce inference latency and GPU memory consumption
      through pruning (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Jaiswal et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024;
046
      Ashkboos et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Jaiswal et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024c; Dong et al., 2024c;
047
      Yin et al., 2024a;b; Zhao et al., 2024) or quantizing (Frantar et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023; Chee et al.,
048   2023; Lin et al., 2024). The second category targets the KV cache, specifically for generation tasks,
049   by either compressing (Dong et al., 2024b) or quantizing (Zhang et al., 2024d; Liu et al., 2024b) it to
050   decrease GPU memory usage during inference. Among these approaches, structured pruning (Xia
051   et al., 2024) searches for crucial substructures within the model while pruning other substructures
052   through continued pretraining on extensive text datasets. However, a significant limitation of most
053   current pruning algorithms is their inability to reduce the GPU memory consumption within the KV
      cache. To address this issue, KV cache compression algorithms like LESS (Dong et al., 2024b) have

      from accessing information from future positions in the sequence. For a given input sentence

191   as vanishing or exploding gradients. In this work, we propose a pruning algorithm that specifically
192   targets the aggregation parameters to improve the LLMs’ efficiency.

195
      Recent research (Men et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2024; Gromov et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Siddiqui
196
      et al., 2024) has revealed the presence of redundant parameters in the higher layers of LLMs. These
197
      studies demonstrate that selectively pruning certain blocks within these higher layers has little
198   performance degradation on downstream discriminative tasks. These pruning strategies can be
199   classified into three distinct approaches: Self-AttentionPruner, FFNPruner, and LayerPruner. Each
200   targets different components of the model:
201
202   • Self-AttentionPruner: This method bypasses the self-attention computation module (Step 1 in
203     Algorithm 1), removing parameters across all three categories: Aggregation, Transformation, and
204     Normalization.
205   • FFNPruner: By skipping the feed-forward network computation process (Step 2), this approach
206     primarily prunes Transformation and Normalization parameters.
207   • LayerPruner: This method skips an entire layer, resulting in the removal of all parameter types
208     within that layer.
209
210   These studies have introduced heuristic metrics to evaluate the importance of blocks within each
211   layer of LLMs. A consistent finding across these works is the greater significance of parameters in
212
      lower layers compared to those in higher layers. Consequently, pruning algorithms typically target
      parameters in higher layers while preserving those in lower layers.
213
214   This phenomenon can be intuitively explained through the lens of GNNs. The fundamental principle
215   of GNNs is to aggregate information from neighboring nodes to achieve smoother representations.

252   3.1     T HE B OTTLENECK IN LLM S ERVING
253
      In applications such as chatbots and content generation tools, which handle a high volume of daily
254   API requests, maintaining low latency is crucial. This is typically achieved by batching multiple
255   requests for inference, thereby reducing computational waste. Moreover, modern LLMs employ the
256   KV cache to accelerate inference by storing intermediate results. While effective, this approach leads
257   to increased memory consumption as the number of requests grows. To illustrate the scale of memory
258   consumption from the KV cache, we use an example from PagedAttention (Kwon et al., 2023). A
259   13B parameter OPT model (Zhang et al., 2022), capable of generating up to 2048 tokens, requires
260   approximately 800 KB of GPU memory per token. This can lead to a potential consumption of 1.6
261   GB per request. Given that LLM operations are primarily constrained by memory bandwidth (Dao
262
      et al., 2022), the amount of memory access becomes the primary factor in determining runtime.
      Consequently, understanding the mechanism by which LLMs generate and utilize the KV cache is
263
      essential for optimizing resource utilization.
264

288
      and memory complexity with respect to sequence length. To accelerate the generation of subsequent
      tokens, modern LLMs typically employ a KV cache mechanism as illustrated in Algorithm 1. This
289

290
      When generating a new token, the model only needs to compute the query, key, and value for the last
291   token in the sequence. It then combines the KV cache with the last token’s query and key to aggregate
292   information from previous tokens, integrating this context into the last token’s representation. By
293   avoiding the need to recompute keys for each token, this approach significantly accelerates the
294   calculation of attention scores. These scores are then used to aggregate contextual information from
295   the V cache and the last token’s value. This optimization strategy greatly enhances inference speed
296   by minimizing redundant computations, particularly for long sequence generation tasks.
297   While the KV cache significantly accelerates inference, it also introduces substantial GPU memory
298   consumption. As previously discussed, higher layers in LLMs typically contribute less unique
299   information to the model’s output. Leveraging this insight, our work focuses on pruning aggregation
300   parameters in these higher layers to reduce the size of the KV cache. This approach aims to balance
301   the trade-off between inference speed and memory efficiency, optimizing overall model performance.

303   3.2.2    T HE ROLE OF T RANSFORMATION PARAMETERS
304
305
      Transformation parameters in LLMs comprise two main components: Wv and Wo in the Self-
      Attention module, and Wgate , Wdown , Wup in the Feed-Forward Network (FFN). These parameters
306
      apply linear transformations on token embeddings and, as some research (Anderson, 1972; Kohonen,
307
      1972; Geva et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2023) suggests, serve as storage of compressed knowledge (Dele-
308   tang et al., 2024; Lester et al., 2024) derived from vast internet-scale text data.
309
310   Current block pruner methods risk discarding valuable stored knowledge when pruning these transfor-
311
      mation parameters. Furthermore, since pruning aggregation parameters already provides substantial
      memory savings, further pruning of transformation parameters results in diminishing returns. This
312
      additional pruning could also introduce potential issues, especially when it comes to maintaining
313
      performance across various downstream tasks.

      When modifying the higher layers of an LLM, we propose that the original residual connection
      coefficient of 1 may no longer be optimal. Inspired by GCNII, which uses a decreasing coefficient to
341
      address the diminishing unique information in higher layers caused by over-smoothing, we introduce
342

343   residual connection, as shown in Line 8 of Algorithm 2.
344
345   Determining the optimal value for α presents a challenge. Traditional retraining methods are not
346
      applicable due to α’s non-differentiable nature. While some recent works have employed Zeroth-
      Order Optimization (Guo et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024b) to estimate gradients during fine-tuning,
347
      we propose a simpler, more efficient approach: a greedy search strategy. Our method involves
348
      calculating the perplexity of the pruned LLM to identify the optimal α. To simplify the search
349   process, we adopt a top-down approach. We begin by determining α for the uppermost layer and then
350   use this value as a starting point for the subsequent layer. This cascading strategy significantly reduces
351   the search space. The entire process is implemented as a grid search as illustrated in Algorithm 3,
352   balancing efficiency with thoroughness. This approach allows us to fine-tune the rescaling parameter
353   across layers, optimizing the model’s performance post-pruning without the need for extensive
354   retraining. The development of more complex search strategies leaves room for future work. The
355   inference process, which incorporates AggregationPruner, is detailed in Algorithm 4.

      5     R ELATED W ORK
523
524
      Pruning. Pruning is a widely adopted and efficient technique in both Computer Vision and Large
525   Language Models. It can be categorized into two main types: Structured Pruning and Unstructured
526   Pruning. Structured Pruning (Lagunas et al., 2021; Xia et al., 2022; Kurtic et al., 2023; He & Xiao,
527   2023; Xia et al., 2024) involves removing entire filters from neural networks, making it particularly
528   conducive to model deployment. On the other hand, Unstructured Pruning (Chen et al., 2020b; Sanh
529   et al., 2020) focuses on removing individual neurons within the network. Some recent works (Men
530   et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2024; Gromov et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Siddiqui et al., 2024) have been
531   proposed to prune blocks in the higher layers of Large Language Models.
532
533   6     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we propose AggregationPruner, a novel approach that focuses on pruning query and
536   key parameters in the higher layers of LLMs. Our method can reduce GPU memory consumption
537   associated with the KV cache during generation tasks. Through extensive experimentation, we
538   demonstrate that our pruning algorithm consistently outperforms recent block pruning techniques,
539   offering a significant advancement in model efficiency without compromising performance. We hope
      our work will inspire future research on pruning strategies to reduce the KV cache in LLM serving.
