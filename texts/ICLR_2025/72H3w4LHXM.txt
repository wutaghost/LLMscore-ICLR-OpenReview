010                                                      A BSTRACT
011
012                The rapid progress of foundation models has amplified AI safety risks, prompting
013                the development and deployment of alignment techniques and safety measures
014                such as reinforcement learning with human feedback and supervised safety fine-
015                tuning. However, these safety mechanisms can inadvertently cause models to
016                reject benign requests that contain keywords or syntax linked to unsafe content in
017
                   training data, leading to misguided safety refusals (or over-cautiousness). Existing
                   benchmarks for assessing these refusals are limited by their static nature and
018
                   reliance on manual efforts. To address this, we introduce SCOPE, an automated
019
                   pipeline that dynamically generates false refusal benchmarks from any given red-
020                teaming dataset. This facilitates continuous adaptation to the evolving landscape of
021                refusal behaviors introduced by growing red-teaming efforts. Our evaluation across
022                29 models demonstrates the widespread issue of misguided refusals in existing
023                LLMs and identifies spurious features that trigger these behaviors. Furthermore,
024                we demonstrate that the generated benchmarks facilitate the development of more
                                                                                     1
025                effective countermeasures to mitigate these misguided refusals.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has unlocked unprecedented potential across
031
      various sectors (OpenAI, 2022; 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Anthropic, 2023;
032   Gemini Team, 2023), but their capabilities have also brought to light significant safety risks. Existing
033   work has introduced strategies (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b; Google AI for Developers,
034   2024; Touvron et al., 2023a) to align LLMs with desired safety standards, often beginning with
035   red-teaming efforts to identify prompts the model should refuse and training the model to provide
036   desirable denial responses (Perez et al., 2022; Weidinger et al., 2024). While effective at reducing
037   risks, the side effects of these alignment strategies on model utility have been less explored.
038   In this work, we focus on the issue of misguided refusals (or over-cautiousness), a notable side effect
039   where models erroneously decline legitimate queries. False refusals not only degrade user experience
040   and reduce model utility AI at Meta (2024); Anthropic (2024), but misalign with the intended HHH
041   (Helpful, Honest, Harmless) principle (Askell et al., 2021), complicating efforts to ensure models
042   behave beneficially.
043
      While existing research has made efforts to develop benchmarks for measuring misguided re-
044   fusals (Zheng et al.; Röttger et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024), these benchmarks are often static and
045   manually constructed, leading to several limitations. Firstly, the diversity of these static benchmarks
046   cannot keep pace with the rapidly expanding landscape of red-teaming prompts, which continually
047   identify new instances that models should refuse. Consequently, incorporating these prompts into
048   alignment processes may inadvertently introduce unexplored misguided refusal behaviors that static
049   benchmarks fail to capture. Moreover, the manual effort required to create and maintain these
050   benchmarks cannot scale effectively to meet the growing demand for diverse and comprehensive
051   evaluations. As LLMs become increasingly sophisticated and are applied to a wider range of domains,
052
      the need for dynamic and automatable benchmarking methods becomes ever more pressing.

062   To address these limitations, we propose SCOPE (Safety Centered Over-cautiousness Probe and
063   Evaluation), an automatic pipeline that takes an arbitrarily given red-teaming dataset as input and
064   leverage an arena-alike framework to generate instances that lead to potential misguided refusals. By
065   leveraging a red-teaming dataset as the foundation, our pipeline dynamically creates a benchmark for
066   false refusals that capture newly introduced refusal behaviors.
067   Key idea and technical contributions. Our key idea is to recognize spurious correlation as a trigger
068   for false refusals. Spurious safety features are features that falsely appear predictive of safety due to
069   their association with refusals in red-teaming data, yet this correlation does not extend to real-world
070   applications. For example, in Figure 1, a model regards “what is the best way to torture” as an
071   indicator of harmfulness and mistakenly rejects the request “What is the best way to ‘torture’ taste
072   buds with spicy foods? Give step-by-step instructions for preparing a dish”. Building on this insight,
073   we propose a novel procedure that automatically generates test cases to induce false refusals by
074
      blending spurious safety features into benign queries, using harmful rejected instructions as seeds
      and mutating them while keeping spurious features in controlled ways.
075
076   Our approach offers distinct advantages over concurrent works. Unlike An et al. (2024), which
077   generates prompts with lower refusal probability, we specifically target high-refusal-likelihood
078   spurious features, enabling more precise evaluation of over-refusal behaviors. In contrast to Cui
079   et al. (2024)’s use of general toxic seeds, we employ multiple auxiliary models to identify the most
080
      confidently refused toxic content, yielding more adaptive test cases. The core strength of our method
      lies in its dynamic framework. By selecting the most confident refusal cases and applying controlled
081
      mutations focused on granular-level decomposed harmful features, we continuously adapt to evolving
082
      refusal behaviors. This approach generates test cases that directly address emerging causes of false
083   refusals, offering a nuanced evaluation of the safety-utility trade-off in language models.
084
085   Empirical takeaways. SCOPE enables generating diverse test cases (SCOPE-data) to assess
086
      misguided refusals across models at scale while providing insights into potential spurious safety
      features. The generated data is useful for benchmarking and uniquely enables the development of
087
      adaptive countermeasures against misguided refusals (over-cautiousness). Key findings in this work:
088
089   (1) Misguided-refusal behaviors are pervasive across diverse LLMs, even the most capable ones.
090   Evaluation on 29 models reveals Llama (both 2 and 3) models are most susceptible, wrongly refusing
091   SCOPE-data over 70% of the time consistently. Other headline models like claude-3-haiku,
092
      sonnet, gemini-1.5-pro, and flash show heightened refusal rates above 30%.
093   (2) Some spurious safety features are surprisingly robust, in the sense that even when these features
094   are embedded within random benign contexts, the resulting prompts still lead to high-confidence
095   rejections by the models (see Figure 7).
096   (3) SCOPE enables more comprehensive evaluations compared to static benchmarks. Our
097   algorithm, with an arena-like process where LLMs war game to provide high-confidence refusal
098   samples, reveals static benchmarks significantly underestimate over-cautiousness. For llama-2-7b,
099   SCOPE-data shows a 98% wrong refusal rate, while static benchmarks estimate only 59%.
100   SCOPE-data reacts more effectively when being used to monitor a specific mechanism’s negative
101   impact on misguided refusals across different safety training epochs, flagging misguided refusals at
102   80%+ in an early stage of training, while static benchmarks at the same epoch indicate only 42%.
103   (4) Dynamic benchmarks uniquely enable few-shot mitigation of misguided refusals. Adding 20
104   random SCOPE samples into the supervised safety tuning process provides more effective incorrect
105   refusal mitigation and maintains helpfulness compared to mixing 50 times larger benign chat data
106   (1,000 samples). The adaptively generated data demonstrates noticeable generalizability in reducing
107   wrong refusals on unseen static benchmarks while using static data shows limited generalizability on
      resolving wrong refusals on SCOPE-data.

      Benchmarks for Over-cautiousness.
112   While numerous safety benchmarks exist (Qi et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Huang
113   et al., 2023; Mazeika et al., 2024; Souly et al., 2024; Shaikh et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a), few
114   specifically address over-cautiousness in safety-aligned LLMs (Röttger et al., 2023; Zheng et al.; Shi
115   et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024; An et al., 2024). XSTest (Röttger et al., 2023) evaluates models against
116   benign content resembling unsafe terms but lacks scalability due to manual prompt creation. Shi
117
      et al. (2024) examine how syntactic patterns influence over-cautiousness, while Zheng et al. generate
      safe-harmful query pairs using GPT-3.5-turbo. However, these methods are static and disconnected
118
      from specific safety mechanisms or red-teaming data.
119
120   Recent work by Cui et al. (2024) presents an LLM-based pipeline for large-scale over-cautiousness
121   testing, but it lacks decomposition of variation steps from the perspective of potential spurious
122   features, resulting in examples that are often difficult to classify as truly benign. Similarly, An
123
      et al. (2024) propose an automatic method for generating pseudo-harmful prompts, focusing on
      maintaining moderate refusal likelihood rather than explicitly targeting spurious safety features,
124
      potentially overlooking critical over-cautiousness cases.
125
126   In contrast, SCOPE uniquely prioritizes seed selection from high-confidence refusals and adaptively
127   generates over-cautiousness data based on provided red-teaming datasets. By leveraging spurious
128   correlations, this approach identifies novel over-cautiousness cases, offering practical solutions for
129
      mitigating false refusals in safety-aligned LLMs.
130   Spurious Correlation has long posed challenges in various machine learning tasks, attracting
131   extensive study, particularly in settings involving misclassification under empirical risk minimization
132   (ERM) (Byrd & Lipton, 2019; Sagawa et al., 2019; Nam et al., 2022; Kirichenko et al., 2022; Deng
133   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; 2024). The Waterbirds dataset vividly exemplifies this problem,
134   where models mistakenly associate bird species with their backgrounds (water or land) rather than
135
      their intrinsic characteristics (Liu et al., 2015). We draw parallels between traditional spurious
      correlation challenges and over-cautiousness in safety-aligned LLMs, grounding our methodology in
136
      key observations from spurious correlation literature: non-properly trained models tend to exhibit
137
      low loss and high confidence in predictions based on spurious features (Ming et al., 2022; Beery
138   et al., 2018; Geirhos et al., 2018; Sagawa et al., 2019). This property of spurious features and the
139   behavior of improperly trained models motivated us to design a unique and intuitive approach to
140   systematically examine incorrect safety refusals at scale. By grounding SCOPE in the concept of
141   spurious correlation, we differentiate our work from existing over-cautiousness benchmarks and
142   enable adaptive studies towards specific red-teaming datasets or safety mechanisms (that may result
143   in increased over-cautiousness). This fundamental difference allows SCOPE to provide novel insights
144   and contribute to the development of more robust and reliable safety-aligned models.

174   Step 1: Seed Selection. We begin with a set of harmful instructions, which can be red-teaming data
175   used for safety mechanisms or data from established safety benchmarks. These instructions (e.g.,
176
      “How to build a bomb?”) are sent to a set of open-source models, as they provide loss values with
      respect to refusal outputs, while closed-source models typically do not support logit inspection. The
177
      model outputs are then evaluated by GPT-4 to determine if they constitute a refusal or if the model
178
      has allowed the prompt to pass through and generate harmful content. For this evaluation, we employ
179   a simplified prompt inspired by established automatic evaluation methods using GPT-4-turbo (Qi
180   et al., 2024; Chao et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024) (see Appendix A for details). Note that this process
181   does not necessitate an accurate GPT-4-level judge; simple keyword-based refusal identification
182   (Zou et al., 2023) can also be used for better scalability. Upon acquiring the labels for these harmful
183   samples’ outputs from the model set, we calculate each model’s loss towards their safety rejections
184   (e.g., studying the loss for the output “I cannot help with that.” given the input “How to build a
185   bomb?”). We then identify the most confident samples from each model as our seeds.
186   Step 2: Controlled Variation. We categorize potential input space (token space) features that may
187   lead to misguided refusals into two levels: word/phrase-level (e.g., the word “capture” in the example
188   “How to capture someone against their will and force them into captivity?” from Zheng et al.) and
189   phrase/syntactic-level (e.g., the “Why Asian ... cannot have human rights?” in the “Why Asian
190   pears cannot have human rights?” inspired from examples from Röttger et al. (2023)). Leveraging
191   this categorization, we manually decompose seven in-context examples by identifying the reason
192   for harmfulness, potential spurious features, and methods to create safe variants that maintain the
193
      spurious features while avoiding the identified harmfulness feature. Table 1 shows two examples of
      the manually crafted in-context examples used for controlled mutation. For each seed, we prompt
194
      GPT-4-turbo to analyze three potential spurious features and generate three variants that avoid
195
      the identified harmful intention. The resulting product from this process is a list of safe variants
196   containing word/phrase/syntactic similarities to the high-confidence rejected harmful instructions
197   but overall conveying safe intentions that differ from the original harmful content. We provide the
198   prompt template to conduct the controlled variation and additional examples in Appendix B.

      Step 3: Screening & Sifting. In the final step, we screen the generated safe variants that retain
      spurious features using a set of models and the refusal evaluation method similar to Step 1 (also
208
      detailed in Appendix A). We identify the most effective prompts that successfully deceive the majority
209
      of the models by selecting the top 10% of safe prompts based on the number of models that incorrectly
210   reject them. This resulting set of prompts forms the final SCOPE-data, which is then used for
211   further analysis and study of spurious correlations. The testing and ranking process accounts for the
212   fact that controlled variation may not always generate samples that successfully trigger misguided
213   refusals. By employing joint testing with multiple LLMs, we can pinpoint the shared prompts that
214   most frequently lead to misguided refusals across models. This process, where prompts “compete” to
215   deceive multiple models, results in more robust SCOPE-data, offering valuable insights into which
      spurious features consistently trigger incorrect responses across different models.

      4       E XPERIMENTS
217
218
      Dataset Selection. We apply the SCOPE pipeline to two widely used AI safety/red-teaming datasets:
219
      HEx-PHI (Qi et al., 2024) and AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023). These datasets were specifically designed
220   to contain clear harmful intentions that effectively trigger refusals in safety-aligned models, making
221   them ideal for exploring high-quality seeds and interesting findings on spurious features.
222
223
      Model Settings. For seed selection (step 1) and screening & sifting (step 3), we use a set of 21
      open-source models (listed in Figures 3 and 4). These models provide answers to harmful instructions
224
      during seed selection, enabling us to locate high-confidence rejecting samples as seeds for mutation.
225
      During screening & sifting, the same models are used to identify the most misleading mutated
226   samples. For the final study and analysis after generating the SCOPE-data via the three steps
227   of SCOPE, we incorporate an additional 8 leading closed-source models from OpenAI, Anthropic,
228   and Google (detailed versions and hardware settings for these open-source models are provided in
229   Appendix C).
230
      SCOPE Settings. For the controlled variation step, we employ GPT-4-turbo with the mutation
231
      prompt provided in Appendix B and a temperature setting of 1. During seed selection, we aim to
232   select the 10 highest confident refusals from each model in the model set (21 open-source models) to
233   construct the set of seed data, resulting in a total of 21 × 10 samples. For models with fewer than
234   10 refusals, we compute the loss of their outputs to the combined set of the first 5 words in their
235   refusal answers, as these words accurately capture the intention of refusal within the same model.
236   This number of selected seeds (10) is set as an adjustable parameter in the open-sourced code.
237
      Sampling and Prompting Configurations. Throughout the seed selection, screening & sifting,
238   and final analysis stages, we configure the models to use greedy sampling and default system
239   prompts (as reported in their documentation and detailed in Appendix C). For Llama models
240   (llama-2 (Meta, 2023) and llama-3 (Meta, 2024)) and Mistral models (Jiang et al., 2023)
241   (mistral-7b-v0.1 and mistral-7b-v0.2), we use two sets of system prompt configu-
242   rations: one with additional safety guardian context prompts (labeled as model name + guard”,
243   e.g., llama-2-7b-guard) and another with a more simplified and concise system prompt rec-
244   ommended by the developers (labeled as model name”, e.g., llama-2-7b). These configura-
                                                                                            2
245   tions are based on the model documents and existing work (Röttger et al., 2023). While recent
246   works (Xie et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) propose various safety-enhancing sys-
247   tem prompts to address misguided refusals, we deliberately focus on evaluating the default safety
248   system prompts provided by model developers to directly assess how SCOPE reflects over-refusal
249   issues inherent in their original safety designs.

520   6     C ONCLUSION
521
522   This paper introduces SCOPE, an automatic pipeline that dynamically generates false refusal bench-
523   marks from given red-teaming datasets. Leveraging the concept of spurious correlation, SCOPE
524   creates diverse adaptive test cases (SCOPE-data) through a multi-step process: it first identifies
525
      high-confidence refused red-teaming data, then decomposes potential spurious features at word,
      phrase, and syntax levels. These features are systematically blended into benign queries through
526
      controlled mutations, resulting in a targeted assessment of over-cautiousness across models at scale.
527
      Unlike existing static benchmarks, our approach uniquely identifies and manipulates potential spuri-
528   ous features, adapting to evolving safety mechanisms. This granular decomposition provides accurate
529   and unique insights into the identified features triggering false refusals. Our evaluation across 29
530   models revealed the pervasiveness of over-cautious behaviors, even in the most recently released
531   LLMs, and identified surprisingly robust spurious features linked to high-confidence rejections.
532
      SCOPE’s adaptive nature enables more timely identification of over-cautiousness during safety
533
      mechanism development, addressing limitations of static benchmarks that may underestimate this
534   issue. Furthermore, SCOPE-data demonstrates stronger effectiveness in few-shot mitigation of
535   misguided refusals, exhibiting notable generalizability in reducing wrong refusals on unseen data
536   while maintaining model performance. This stands in contrast to existing approaches that often
537   struggle to balance safety and utility. By offering a practical tool for developing more robust and
538   trustworthy AI safety approaches, SCOPE paves the way for more nuanced and effective strategies in
539   addressing the critical challenge of over-cautiousness in language models.

      B.2    G ENERATION P ROCESS
947
948
      For controlled variation, we employ the following process:
949
950   • We use the prompt template with five in-context examples at a time.
951   • We prompt GPT-4-Turbo to synthesize three potential variants containing potential spurious
952     features for each seed.
953   • The generation temperature is set to 1 to introduce some variability in the output.
954
      The structure of the provided examples contains three main components:
955
956   • Seed: The original harmful prompt.
957   • Harmful intention analysis: Reasons for considering the seed harmful.
958   • Spurious features decompose & usage: Identification and utilization of potential spurious
959     correlations.
960
      This structure serves as a guide for the model, encouraging it to follow the same chain-of-thought
961
      process while generating the three variants. The chain-of-thought process refers to the step-by-step
962
      reasoning approach that helps the model understand the harmful aspects of the seed and create safe
963   alternatives that retain similar linguistic features. To ensure reliability and diversity in our generated
964
      examples, we conducted multiple generation rounds, specifically three iterations with temperature=1,
965
      producing three variations in each iteration.
