009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Fusing speech into pre-trained language model (SpeechLM) usually suffers from
012            inefficient encoding of long-form speech and catastrophic forgetting of pre-trained
013            text modality. We propose SSR-C ONNECTOR (Segmented Speech Representation
014            Connector) for better modality fusion. Leveraging speech-text alignments, our
015            approach segments and compresses speech features to match the granularity of
016            text embeddings. Additionally, we introduce a two-stage training pipeline that
017
               includes the distillation and fine-tuning phases to mitigate catastrophic forgetting.
               SSR-C ONNECTOR outperforms existing mechanism for speech-text modality
018
               fusion, consistently achieving better speech understanding (e.g., +10 accuracy on
019
               StoryCloze and +20 on Speech-MMLU) while preserving pre-trained text ability.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Large language models (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2022; Chiang et al., 2023; Anil et al.,
025   2023; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI et al., 2024, LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance
026   across various tasks and extending pre-trained abilities from LLMs to other modalities has sparked
027   interest in multimodal LLMs (Alayrac et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b; OpenAI et al., 2024; Tang et al.,
028   2024; Défossez et al., 2024). In this work, we focus on integrating speech into pre-trained language
029   models (SpeechLMs). A straightforward approach is to transcribe speech into text and use these
030
      transcriptions as prompts for large language models (Huang et al., 2023); however, such cascaded
      systems suffer from error propagation, higher latency, and cannot leverage raw speech information
031
      like emotion, speaker identity, and other paralinguistic cues (Faruqui & Hakkani-Tür, 2021; Lin et al.,
032
      2022; Kim et al., 2024). Consequently, developing end-to-end SpeechLMs that directly fuse speech or
033   audio input has gained popularity, where various approaches have been explored to encode speech and
034   align its representation with pre-trained language models (Zhang et al., 2023; Rubenstein et al., 2023;
035   Yu et al., 2023; Maiti et al., 2024; Hassid et al., 2024a; Tang et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024).
036
      Speech representations can be integrated into pre-trained language models mainly through two
037
      approaches. The first method involves using connector modules that align speech representations
038
      with the language model’s input space without modifying the model’s existing vocabulary. These
039   connector-based techniques typically incorporate a compression module to shorten the speech features,
040   enhancing efficiency. However, connectors are generally first trained for the speech recognition task
041   (with concatenated speech-to-text data) and lack the ability to support text or speech generation
042   unless further instruction-finetuned. The second approach, unit-based fusion, directly incorporates
043   discrete speech units—normally derived from self-supervised models like HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021),
044   XLS-R (Babu et al., 2021), or DinoSR (Liu et al., 2023a)—into the language model’s vocabulary. This
045   allows the language model to be fine-tuned with a combination of speech and text tokens, enabling it
046   to handle dual-modal inputs and outputs. Despite its versatility, unit-based fusion can lead to longer
047
      and less efficient training contexts due to the sparser nature of speech information. Regardless of the
      fusion approach, SpeechLMs often face the challenge of catastrophic forgetting, where the model
048
      loses its pre-trained text capabilities (Tang et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024; Défossez et al., 2024).
049
050   To tackle these challenges, we propose SSR-C ONNECTOR (Segmented Speech Representation
051   Connector), which grounds speech representations in the same semantic space as transcription token
052   embeddings. Different from prior work that concatenates speech with text (Fig. 1 (a,b)) for modality
053
      fusion, we leverage speech-text alignments to segment and compress speech features (Fig. 1 (c)),
      resulting in representations that match the length of text tokens.

080   Modality Fusion for Speech Language Models SpeechLM typically encodes audio waveforms into
081   high-dimensional features using pre-trained encoders and integrate these representations to pre-trained
082   LLMs via a connection (adapter) module (Wu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023;
083   Tang et al., 2024). To compress speech representations, Fathullah et al. (2023) apply stacking-based
084   fixed-rate compression on speech features extracted from the Conformer model (Gulati et al., 2020).
085   Inspired by the Q-former architecture (Li et al., 2023a), Yu et al. (2023) compress speech features
086
      using a fixed number of query tokens, while Tang et al. (2024) extend this approach to a window-level
      Q-former to support variable frame-rate reduction. Alternatively, Wu et al. (2023) utilize Connectionist
087
      Temporal Classification (CTC) (Graves et al., 2006) to compress representations.
088
089   Besides connector-based modality fusion, pre-processing other modalities—such as speech, vision,
090   and videos—into tokens (Lyu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Team, 2024; Kondratyuk et al., 2024)
091   has attracted attention for its scalability. Speech units are typically extracted from self-supervised
092
      representations (Hsu et al., 2021; Babu et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023a). For instance,
      AudioLM (Borsos et al., 2023) integrates semantic tokens from w2v-BERT (Chung et al., 2021)
093
      and acoustic tokens from SoundStream (Zeghidour et al., 2021), modeling them autoregressively
094
      for audio generation. Rubenstein et al. (2023) fine-tune the pre-trained LLM PaLM-2 (Anil et al.,
095   2023) with audio tokens processed by AudioLM, enabling both text and speech as input and output.
096   Similarly, VoxtLM (Maiti et al., 2024) performs multi-task training with speech units and text tokens,
097   achieving high-quality speech recognition and synthesis. To mitigate catastrophic forgetting, Nguyen
098   et al. (2024) propose an interleaved training mechanism to fuse speech tokens into LL A M A 2 model.
099
100
      Speech-text Alignment Extraction Various aligner tools are available for extracting speech-text
101
      alignments. For example, the Montreal Forced Aligner (MFA) (McAuliffe et al., 2017) is an easy-to-use
102   tool based on the Kaldi toolkit (Povey et al., 2011). Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC)
103   (Graves et al., 2006) is also widely used for speech-text alignment (Sainath et al., 2020; Huang et al.,
104   2024); since it is a by-product of speech recognition, it supports alignment without explicit text labels.
105   More recently, the UnitY2 aligner (Communication et al., 2023) and the ZMM-TTS aligner (Gong
106   et al., 2024) have shown excellent alignment performance across multiple languages. These aligners
107   rely on speech units extracted from pre-trained encoders (Baevski et al., 2020; Hsu et al., 2021; Babu
      et al., 2021) and use variants of RAD-TTS (Shih et al., 2021) as their alignment backbone.

120   3     M ETHODOLOGY
121
122   We develop an alignment-aware speech representation connector to foster modality fusion between
123   speech and pre-trained language model. We introduce our connector in §3.1, with detailed descriptions
124   of its aligners in §3.2. Lastly, we present the two-stage training pipeline for our connector in §3.3.
125
126   3.1   A LIGNMENT- AWARE S PEECH R EPRESENTATION C ONNECTOR
127
      Though previous connectors (Fathullah et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024)
128
      vary in their compressor designs, they do not explicitly leverage speech-text alignment information.
129   SSR-C ONNECTOR, in contrast, uses speech-text alignments to segment and compress speech features
130   into the same granularity as text tokens. As illustrated in Fig. 2, our connector consists of two
131   components: (1) a speech-text aligner and (2) a feature compressor.
132
      Given speech features x = (x1 , · · · , xn ) ∈ Rn×D extracted by pre-trained speech encoders (e.g.,
133
      WAV 2V EC 2.0, H U BERT, W HIPSER, etc.), the aligner produces a monotonic mapping (alignment
134
      path) between the speech features and their transcriptions y = (y1 , · · · , ym ) ∈ Rm×1 . This mapping
135   can be computed based on both speech features (or their units) and transcriptions (Communication
136   et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2024), or solely based on speech input (Sainath et al., 2020; Dong & Xu,
137   2020; Huang et al., 2024) (see §3.2 for details). Using the alignment mapping, we segment the input
138   into m chunks of speech features, where each chunk semantically corresponds to a transcription token.
139   For example, in Fig. 2, speech features are segmented at indices (2, 5, 7) according to the alignment
140   path. We refer to these indices as boundary indices.
141   Once the boundary indices are identified, we first apply a linear layer to transform the speech features
142   to match the embedding dimension H(H > D) of the pre-trained LLM, since LLMs typically have
143   a larger feature dimension than pre-trained speech encoders. We then use the boundary indices to
144   aggregate and compress the speech representations in each chunk through a Transformer Decoder
145   model (Vaswani et al., 2017). Specifically, we apply a causal decoder-only model to transform
146   the speech features into high-dimensional representations g = f (x; θdec ) ∈ Rn×H . Given that
147   features at later positions include information from prior positions, we employ a selection-based
148   compression method that takes the transformed features g at the boundary indices to form the
149
      compressed representation z ∈ Rm×H . Although our initial design included a block-wise attention
      mask to restrict information flow within each chunk (as shown in Fig. 2, where the middle segment’s
150
      features do not attend to previous segments), we found that removing these masks simplifies training
151
      and inference with minimal impact on performance, as detailed in §4.4.

      RAD-TTS model (Shih et al., 2021) but replacing the target mel-spectrogram with speech units. It first

      For distillation training, we use the aligned speech-to-text dataset MLS (Pratap et al., 2020), specifically
247
      the English portion, which consists of about 50,000 hours of speech. To evaluate our SpeechLMs, we
248   employ several datasets as shown in Table 1. To assess the model’s spoken language understanding
249   (SLU) capabilities, we follow Nguyen et al. (2024) and use sWUGGY, sBLIMP, and the StoryCloze
250   dataset. sWUGGY and sBLIMP are detailed in (Nguyen et al., 2020). Briefly, sWUGGY evaluates
251   whether a model can discriminate between real spoken words and non-words (e.g., “brick” vs. “blick”),
252   while sBLIMP assesses if the model can distinguish between a grammatically correct spoken sentence
253   and its ungrammatical variant (e.g., “cats are lazy” vs. “cats is lazy”). We evaluate our SpeechLMs on

256
      of a short story, which typically requires high-level semantic understanding and common sense

257

288   4.2   M ODEL S ETUP
289
      We instantiate our LLM using the pre-trained LL A M A 3 model (Touvron et al., 2023) and employ
290
      DinoSR (Liu et al., 2023a) as our pre-trained speech feature extractor. Our speech connector includes
291
      a linear layer that maps DinoSR’s extracted representations (D = 768) to the LLM’s embedding space
292   dimension (H = 4096). We then utilize a 4-layer Transformer Decoder to transform and compress the
293   speech representations based on alignments, as described in §3.1. The compressed representations
294   z and the embeddings of text tokens h are used to compute the distillation loss for updating the
295   connector’s parameters. We train our connector for 400,000 steps with a learning rate of 1 × 10−5 ,
296   using dynamic batching with a maximum of 4,096 tokens per device. We employ distributed data
297   parallelism (DDP) with 32 A100 GPUs.
298   To extract alignments, we experimented with different aligners listed in §3.2. For the UnitY aligner2 , we
299   used it off-the-shelf to construct alignment indices. Since the UnitY2 aligner provides alignments based
300   on character-level tokens, we merge the durations into subword level to ensure that the compressed
301   representations and text embeddings have the same granularity. For CTC-based aligners, we trained
302   them using a 4-layer Transformer Decoder followed by a linear projection. In the character-level
303   variant (C HAR -CTC), we deduplicate the sequence to obtain character-level durations and then merge
304   them into subword-level durations to segment the speech features, similar to the UnitY2 aligner. In the
305   subword-level variant (S UB -CTC), we directly use CTC’s blank token to segment the speech input.

446   5     S TAGE 2: S PEECH L ANGUAGE M ODEL F INE - TUNING
447
448   In Stage 1 (§4), we freeze the pre-trained LLM and distill its text embeddings into our alignment-aware
449   connector. In this section, we fine-tune SpeechLM by freezing the connector and updating the LLM.
450   This process enhances the model’s spoken language understanding (SLU) performance by fitting
451   SpeechLM on the aligned speech-text data, albeit at the expense of degrading its pre-trained text
452   capabilities. In the following sections, we compare various methods to mitigate catastrophic forgetting
453   and demonstrate their trade-offs between speech and text understanding.
454
455   5.1    M ITIGATE C ATASTROPHIC F ORGETTING
456
      Model and Dataset Setup We fine-tune SpeechLM using the next-token prediction objective
457
      described in §3.3. In this stage, we freeze the connector distilled in Stage 1 and unfreeze the LLM
458
      (LL A M A 3) parameters. Following Stage 1 (§4), we use the MLS dataset for training and evaluate the
459   model on the same speech and text understanding tasks. Beyond vanilla fine-tuning, we also explore
460   Low-rank Adaptation (Hu et al., 2021, LoRA) and multitask fine-tuning as they have been shown
461   effective for mitigating catastrophic forgetting in other tasks (Xue et al., 2021; Vu et al., 2022). Details
462   of our fine-tuning setup are shown below:
463
      • Vanilla Fine-tuning: We perform full fine-tuning on the aligned speech-text data with a learning
464
        rate of 1 × 10−6 and a maximum token size of 4096. Training is model-parallelized across 32 A100
465     GPUs using Fully Sharded Data Parallel (Zhao et al., 2023, FSDP).
466
      • LoRA Fine-tuning: We leverage the low-rank constraints from as regularization to prevent model
467     overfitting in MLS dataset. We configure LoRA layers with α = 512, r = 256, and a dropout
468     probability of 0.1.
469   • Multitask Fine-tuning: To preserve the LLM’s pre-trained text capabilities, we also fine-tune
470     SpeechLM on text-only data using the standard Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL) loss. The dataloader
471     is configured to sample from both speech-text and text-only datasets with equal probability. We
472     continue using the MLS dataset for speech-text training and utilize a subset of the LL A M A 2 training
473     datasets (Touvron et al., 2023) for text-only training.

509   5.2             C OMPARISON OF F INE - TUNING M ETHODS
510
      In Fig. 5, we compare different fine-tuning methods on StoryCloze (S) and MMLU. StoryCloze
511
      performance is indicative of how well model is fitted to the speech modality and MMLU measures the
512   degree of catastrophic forgetting in pre-trained text abilities. We observe that Vanilla Fine-tuning
513   quickly overfits to the speech domain, achieving improved performance on StoryCloze but drastically
514   decreasing MMLU accuracy. In contrast, LoRA Fine-tuning introduces strong regularization, resulting
515   in limited improvements in speech understanding. Although LoRA mitigates catastrophic forgetting to
516   some extent compared to vanilla fine-tuning, performance still steadily declines. Multitask fine-tuning
517   emerges as the most promising approach, enhancing speech understanding while largely mitigating
518   catastrophic forgetting, evidenced by the modest 2-point drop in MMLU.
519   Since model performance does not further improve with additional training steps (as shown in Fig. 5),
520   we utilize the checkpoint trained for 5,000 updates to compare with baseline models. The results are
521   presented in Table 6 and Table 7. Note that even with only 5,000 updates, the model has observed
522   all speech-text data due to our large effective batch size. Across SLU, MMLU, and ASR tasks,
523   the fine-tuned SpeechLM outperforms baseline methods on tasks primarily relying on speech-only
524   information (sWUGGY, sBLIMP, ASR), with multitask fine-tuning achieving the best performance
525   among all fine-tuning methods. However, we also observe a decline in performance on S → T tasks
526
      such as Speech-MMLU and StoryCloze, indicating that there is still unavoidable degradation of
      text capabilities which adversely affects SpeechLM’s cross-modal performance.
527
528   Overall, Stage 2 fine-tuning experiments highlight a trade-off between enhanced speech understanding
529   and degraded text abilities when unfreezing pre-trained LLM weights. Though such forgetting
530   phenomenon is unavoidable, our two-stage training pipeline has largely preserved SpeechLM’s text
531   ability and our experimental results underscore the importance of incorporating high-quality text data
532
      during cross-modal fine-tuning to balance performance across both modalities.
533
534   6              C ONCLUSION
535
536   We propose SSR-C ONNECTOR to inject speech representation into pre-trained LLMs. Through
537   explicitly leveraging speech-text alignment, our connector compresses long and sparse speech
538   information to the same granularity as text tokens. To mitigate catastrophic forgetting, we propose a
539   two-stage training pipeline for modality fusion. Compared to previous baselines, our SpeechLM
      achieves much better speech understanding ability while retaining its pre-trained text ability.
