011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is critical for ensuring the reliability and ro-
014            bustness of deep learning models in real-world applications. While normalizing
015            flows have demonstrated impressive performance for various task of image OOD
016
               detection, recent findings suggest that they still encounter limitations and severe
               biases when applied to datasets with different statistics. Specifically, it has been
017
               observed that normalizing flow models tend to assign higher likelihoods to OOD
018
               samples with low complexity, which undermines the effectiveness of likelihood
019            based OOD detection methods. In this paper, we explore the bias related to data
020            complexity linked to normalizing flow models in OOD detection. We propose a
021            novel method for bias correction by incorporating synthetic outliers during train-
022            ing, guiding the model to assign lower likelihoods to OOD samples. Additionally,
023            we introduce a specialized training objective that leverages the softplus function
024            for OOD data, ensuring a smooth and effective training process. Extensive exper-
025            iments on benchmark and high-dimensional real-world datasets, including both
026            images and texts, confirm that our proposed approach significantly enhances OOD
027
               detection accuracy, achieving performance comparable to models trained with a
               limited number of real outliers. Moreover, our method increases the Lipschitz
028
               constant, supporting the hypothesis presented in related literature.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is essential for ensuring the reliability and safety of deep learn-
034
      ing models, especially in real-world applications such as autonomous driving, medical diagnosis,
035
      and security systems (Chandola et al., 2009; Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Chalapathy & Chawla,
036   2019; Cao et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2021; Pang et al., 2021; Morteza & Li, 2022). These systems
037   often encounter data outside the training distribution, and the failure to accurately detect such OOD
038   data can lead to severe consequences. Therefore, OOD detection has become a critical area of re-
039   search, aimed at improving machine learning models’ ability to effectively distinguish between in
040   distribution (ID) and OOD data.
041
      Normalizing flows are recognized as a powerful tool in deep learning due to their ability to model
042   complex probability distributions and perform exact likelihood estimation (Dinh et al., 2014; 2016;
043   Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018; Papamakarios et al., 2021). By leveraging invertible transformations,
044   normalizing flows enable flexible, bijective mappings from data space to a latent space, making
045   them effective in extracting features from in-distribution data. The general principle is to train
046   normalizing flows mapping on high level features of ID data by maximizing the likelihood and
047   then use low likelihood as a score function for OOD detection(Nalisnick et al., 2018; Serrà et al.,
048   2020; Kirichenko et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021; Ahmadian & Lindsten, 2021; Osada et al., 2024).
049   They have already demonstrated strong performance in anomaly detection, covering time series,
050
      industrial imaging, and medical imaging (Yu et al., 2021; Rudolph et al., 2021; 2022; Dai & Chen,
      2022; Gudovskiy et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023).
051
052   However, recent literature report that there exhibits likelihood bias with normalizing flows based
053   model for OOD detection. Ren et al. (2019) observed that likelihood scores are significantly influ-
      enced by population-level background statistics and proposed using a likelihood ratio test for OOD


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      detection. Nalisnick et al. (2019) introduced the concept of the typical set to determine whether an
055   input is OOD. Additionally, Serrà et al. (2020) demonstrated that simpler images tend to yield higher
056   likelihoods, irrespective of whether the data is OOD, and proposed a new OOD detection score that
057   accounts for input complexity to mitigate this effect. Osada et al. (2024) presented a hypothesis
058   offering a theoretical explanation for the likelihood bias due to image complexity and the reason for
059   the failure of OOD detection.
060
      While normalizing flows are unsupervised models that learn exclusively from in-distribution data,
061   recent studies have explored the incorporation of outlier data to regularize the model and improve its
062   performance in OOD detection. For example, Outlier Exposure (OE), proposed by Hendrycks et al.
063   (2018), introduced auxiliary outliers during training to improve the model’s ability to differentiate
064   between ID and OOD data. Expanding on this, Virtual Outlier Synthesis (VOS), introduced by Du
065   et al. (2022), generated synthetic outliers through empirical Gaussian sampling in an unsupervised
066   setting, thereby improving the model’s generalization to unseen data. Moreover, Wang et al. (2023)
067   constructed an OOD distribution set that encompasses all distributions within a Wasserstein ball
068   centered on the auxiliary OOD distribution, while Zheng et al. (2023) proposed using generated
069
      data to design an auxiliary task for improved OOD detection. Additionally, SANFlow (Kim et al.,
      2023) incorporated synthetic outliers and trained normalizing flows across multiple distributions
070
      instead of a single distribution to enhance anomaly detection performance.
071
072   Based on the observations that bias occurs in normalizing flows based OOD detection methods,
073   where performance is robust when the ID data is less complex than the OOD data but declines when
074   the ID data is more complex, we aim to counteract this bias and enhance models OOD detection
075
      ability for a wide range of applications. Specially, we propose incorporating synthetic outliers into
      the training process to reduce the likelihood bias. For both image and text data, we propose a
076
      simple but effective synthetic method for outliers generation from ID data. An adverse likelihood
077
      objective, which simultaneously maximizes the likelihood of ID samples while minimizing that of
078   OOD samples is proposed for training normalizing flows. By incorporating the softplus function,
079   this objective ensures numerical stability and prevents gradient explosion, allowing for smoother
080   optimization. Furtheremore, our approach is also shown to ensure that the trained normalizing flow
081   models satisfy broader Lipschitz continuity conditions, a crucial assumption in prior work, which
082   validates the model’s ability to prevent the assignment of higher likelihoods to low-complexity OOD
083   inputs. Comprehensive experiments on widely-used benchmark datasets and high-dimensional real-
084   world datasets, including texts and images, demonstrating that our method significantly improves
085   OOD detection performance. Notably, our approach with synthetic outliers are comparable to the
086
      use of limited real labelled outlier data, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed bias
      correction method for normalizing flows using synthetic outlier data.
087
088   The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Section 2 describes our methodology, starting
089   with the hypothesis and motivation, followed by the generation of synthetic outliers for both images
090   and texts. We then discuss the learning objective and OOD scoring method. Section 3 presents
091   our experimental setup and results, covering benchmark image datasets, high-dimensional image
092
      datasets, and text datasets. Finally, Section 4 concludes the paper with a summary of key findings.

141   To address this limitation, we propose incorporating synthetic outliers during training, specifically
142   designed to correct the bias toward simpler OOD inputs. By introducing these synthetic outliers,
143   we aim to reduce the likelihood assigned to low-complexity OOD samples, thereby improving the
144
      model’s ability to differentiate between ID and OOD data.
145   Moreover, as highlighted in Remark 2 of Osada et al. (2024), the local Lipschitz constant LA in-
146   creases as the complexity of the input decreases. This implies that the mapping function f becomes
147   more sensitive to variations in the latent space when processing less complex inputs. To the best
148   of our knowledge, this relationship is model-dependent and has not been explicitly explored in the
149   context of OOD detection in the existing literature.
150   From this perspective, we hypothesize that the introduction of synthetic outliers for training has an
151   influence on the local Lipschitz constant LA , increasing its value and leading to more dispersed
152   latent representations for low-complexity OOD images. To validate the effectiveness of our method,
153   we will empirically measure changes in LA by assessing the gradient norms of the model. If our
154   method leads to both improved experimental results and an increase in LA , it would confirm the
155   utility of introducing synthetic outliers to correct the bias in normalizing flows.

      2.2   SYNTHETIC O UTLIERS
157
158   In order to regularize the normalizing flow model, we introduce synthetic OOD samples. These
159   synthetic OOD samples are crafted based on the empirical observation that normalizing flow models
160   tend to assign higher likelihoods to data with lower complexity. Our approach defines synthetic
161   outliers as low-complexity samples that are distinct from the ID data while still retaining certain
      structural elements. In the following, we present the method of synthesize outliers of low complexity


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      for both image and text data. Incorporating these outliers during training encourages the model to
163   assign lower likelihoods to OOD data and enhances its ability to distinguish between ID and OOD
164   samples. Let X denote the ID domain and Z the corresponding latent space.
165
166   2.2.1    I MAGE O UTLIERS S YNTHESIS
167
168   For a given ID sample x ∈ X , we generate a synthetic outlier x′ ∈ X . For images, we initially gen-
169   erate an augmented outlier xa ∈ X by randomly employing one of three techniques: CutPaste(Li
170   et al., 2021), CutMix(Yun et al., 2019), or MixUp(Zhang, 2017). This step enriches the semantic
171   diversity and broadens the range of the outliers. Then we synthesize an outlier by applying a Gaus-
172
      sian blur to the augmented outlier, which effectively reduces image complexity and introduces a
      controlled degree of randomness. The Gaussian blur is a widely used technique in image processing
173
      that smooths an image by replacing each pixel with the average of the pixels within a certain ra-
174
      dius. The augmentation and Gaussian blur processes are applied independently and randomly. The
175   mathematical formulation of the convolution operation used for generating the synthetic outlier in a
176   multi-channel image is defined as follows:
177

180
181   In this formulation, C is the number of channels in the image (e.g., C = 3 for RGB images). The
182   variable xac denotes the c-th channel of the augmented image xa , g is Gaussian blur kernel which
183   has dimensions of (2k + 1) × (2k + 1), and x′c (u, v) represents the output value at position (u, v)
184   in the c-th channel after applying convolution operation.
185
      By convolving the augmented image xa with the Gaussian kernel, we reduce its high-frequency
186   components, thereby generating a synthetic version that retains the global structure but reduces the
187   image complexity. It is crucial to carefully tune the blur degree to balance two competing objectives:
188   (1) ensuring that the synthetic outliers are sufficiently different from the ID samples to be effective
189   for training, and (2) avoiding excessive distortion that would render the outliers unrecognizable and
190   irrelevant for model training. Achieving this balance is critical for the model to robustly distinguish
191   between authentic data and artificially degraded samples, thereby enhancing its ability to detect
192   outliers.
193
194   2.2.2    T EXT O UTLIERS S YNTHESIS
195
      To generate synthetic text outliers, we employ a two-step process focusing on both sentence length
196
      and vocabulary complexity. First, we tokenize the input text into individual sentences and filter out
197   longer sentences by applying a maximum length constraint. Sentences exceeding this length are
198   removed, enhancing the overall readability and reducing complexity.
199
200
      Next, we simplify the vocabulary by substituting complex words with simpler synonyms. For each
      word in the remaining sentences, we identify its part of speech and attempt to find a synonym using
201
      WordNet (Miller, 1994). We replace the word with the first synonym that differs from the original
202
      word. If no suitable synonym is found, the original word is retained. This process preserves the
203   semantic content of the text while simplifying its vocabulary.
204
205   The simplified sentences are then concatenated to form a new text, creating a synthetic outlier that
206
      is less complex than the original text. This approach effectively generates synthetic text outliers that
      can be utilized in our OOD detection framework, helping the model distinguish between ID and
207
      OOD samples.

      2.3     L EARNING O BJECTIVE
210
211   Our learning objective of the normalizing flow model f : X → Z is designed to maximize the
212   likelihood of ID samples while simultaneously minimizing the likelihood assigned to synthetic
213   OOD samples. Here, X represents the input space (i.e., the observed data space), and Z repre-
214   sents the latent space, where the distribution is typically modeled as a standard normal distribution,
215   pZ (z) = N (0, I). Our adverse objective is formulated as a combination of the maximum likelihood
      estimation for the ID samples and a softplus-based penalty for the OOD samples.

      2.4   OOD S CORING
306
307   To determine whether a given sample is OOD or ID, we employ two approaches for computing OOD
308   scores:
309
310         1. Likelihood-based Scoring: This method leverages the negative log-likelihood derived
311            from a trained normalizing flow model to quantify how well a sample x fits the learned
312
               data distribution. The negative log-likelihood, Snll , serves as a measure for anomaly detec-
               tion, with lower values indicating better conformity to the distribution:
313
314
                                                  Snll (x) = − log pZ (z),                                  (4)
315
316            where z is the latent representation of x in the learned feature space and pZ (z) is the density
317
               under the model.
318         2. Complexity-adjusted Scoring: To account for the inherent complexity of the input data,
319            we extend the likelihood-based scoring by incorporating an image complexity term, as
320
               proposed by Serrà et al. (2020). The complexity-adjusted score, Scomp , is defined as:
321
322
                                               Scomp (x) = Snll (x) − C(x),                         (5)
323            where C(x) denotes the complexity of the image x. This adjustment ensures that the score
               better reflects images with varying levels of complexity.

      In our experiments, we aim to evaluate the effectiveness of training normalizing flow models us-
      ing synthetic outliers and the adverse likelihood loss function. For images, we employ JPEG2000
341
      compression to calculate the image complexity of the datasets. We utilize CIFAR-10, CIFAR-
342
      100(Krizhevsky et al., 2009), SVHN(Netzer et al., 2011), LSUN(Yu et al., 2015), iSUN (Xu et al.,
343   2015) and CelebA(Liu et al., 2015) for our experiments, and the details of the complexities can be
344   found in Appendix B. We employ the CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and iSUN datasets as ID datasets due
345   to their relatively high complexity. For each dataset, we calculate two commonly used metrics in
346   OOD detection: AUROC and FPR95.
347
      The normalizing flow model is implemented using the FrEIA library (Ardizzone et al., 2018-2022).
348
      Each coupling layer’s subnet is composed of two 3 × 3 convolutional layers with ReLU activations.
349   The model consists of 8 coupling layers, utilizing the AllInOneBlock module from the FrEIA
350   library (Ardizzone et al., 2018-2022). The model is trained for 500 epochs on the CIFAR-10 and
351   CIFAR-100 datasets, with synthetic outliers generated at a probability of 0.5. The hyperparameter
352   α for CutMix and MixUp is set to be 1.0. All input images are resized to 32 × 32. We employ
353   the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) with a learning rate of 1 × 10−3 , a weight decay of 1 × 10−5 ,
354   and a batch size of 128. For evaluation, we randomly select 1,000 ID and 1,000 OOD samples to
355   ensure a balanced evaluation dataset. The radius setting of the Gaussian filter is 1. The model’s
356   performance is evaluated on the OOD datasets using both likelihood-based scoring and complexity-
357
      adjusted scoring methods, aiming to quantify its efficacy in detecting OOD samples under various
      conditions.

      augmentaion (which combines CutPaste, CutMix, and MixUp), and a combination of both Gaussian

      In our experiments, we also estimate the Lipschitz constant of the trained normalizing flow model
      to validate the assumption made in Hypothesis 1 regarding the model’s effectiveness. We apply the
421
      following method for estimating the Lipschitz constant:
422
423         1. Sampling Input Samples: Randomly select 1, 000 samples xi from the input distribution
424            to represent the input space.
425         2. Computing Gradients: For each sample xi , we utilize PyTorch automatic differentiation
426            tool to compute the gradient ∇f (xi ) of the model’s output f (xi ) with respect to the input.
427
            3. Calculating Norms: Compute the L2 norm ∥∇f (xi )∥ of each gradient.
428
429
            4. Estimating the Lipschitz Constant: Approximate the Lipschitz constant LA by taking
               the maximum gradient norm: LA = maxi ∥∇f (xi )∥.

      4     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we propose a novel method to address the bias in normalizing flow models, which
533
      tend to assign higher likelihoods to low-complexity OOD samples, reducing their effectiveness in
534   OOD detection. To correct the bias, we incorporate synthetic outliers during training and introduce
535   a specialized objective using the softplus function, enhancing the model’s ability to differentiate be-
536   tween ID and OOD data. Extensive experiments on both image and text datasets confirm that our
537   method significantly boosts OOD detection accuracy, achieving performance comparable to mod-
538   els trained with real outliers, while demonstrating broad applicability across tasks. Our approach
539   also improves the model’s Lipschitz constant, aligning with the hypothesis that a higher Lipschitz
      constant enhances robustness in OOD detection.

867   We conducted an ablation experiment to investigate the effect of varying degrees of blurring on
868   synthetic outliers in images. We use CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets as ID data, and SVHN
869   as OOD. The blurring level was controlled by adjusting the Radius parameter, with larger values
870   corresponding to higher degrees of blurring. As illustrated in the Figure 6, the results show that as
871   the blurring increases, the AUROC scores initially improve, reaching a peak at moderate blurring
872   levels . However, at extreme levels of blurring, the scores fluctuate and even decrease, indicating that
873   both very low and very high levels of blurring negatively affect the performance of OOD detection
874   methods. This pattern highlights the nuanced impact of blurring on synthetic outliers, with moderate
875
      blurring yielding the best detection results.
