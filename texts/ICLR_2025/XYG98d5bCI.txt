009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Linear Autoencoders (LAEs) have shown strong performance in state-of-the-art
012            recommender systems. Some LAE models, like EASE, can be viewed as multi-
013            variate (multiple-output) linear regression models with a zero-diagonal constraint.
014            However, these impressive results are mainly based on experiments, with little
015            theoretical support. This paper investigates the generalizability – a theoretical
016            measure of model performance in statistical machine learning – of multivariate
017            linear regression and LAEs. We first propose a PAC-Bayes bound for multivari-
018            ate linear regression, which is generalized from an earlier PAC-Bayes bound for
019            single-output linear regression by Shalaeva et al., and outline sufficient conditions
020
               that ensure its theoretical convergence. We then apply this bound to EASE, a
               classic LAE model in recommender systems, and develop a practical method for
021
               minimizing the bound, addressing the calculation challenges posed by the zero-
022
               diagonal constraint. Experimental results show that our bound for EASE is non-
023            vacuous on real-world datasets, demonstrating its practical utility.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In recent years, simple (linear) recommendation models have consistently demonstrated impressive
030   performance, often rivaling deep learning models (Dacrema et al., 2019; Jin et al., 2021; Mao et al.,
031   2021), especially for the implicit setting, where interactions are inferred from user behavior (e.g.,
032   clicks or purchases). In particular, linear autoencoders (LAEs) such as EASE (Steck, 2019) and
033
      EDLAE (Steck, 2020) have shown a surprising edge over widely used matrix factorization (MF)
      methods such as ALS (Hu et al., 2008). The LAE architecture is remarkably simple: Let R ∈
034
      Rm×n be the data matrix and W ∈ Rn×n be the parameter matrix, the LAE model is defined as
035
      fW (R) = RW , where W is trained to satisfy fW (R) ≈ R. W is considered both an encoder and a
036   decoder. Typically, we add constraints such as diag(W ) = 0 to prevent W from overfitting towards
037   I (Steck, 2019).
038
039
      Despite their power and widespread use, linear autoencoders, particularly in the context of recom-
      mendation systems, remain theoretically underexplored. Recommendation research has understand-
040
      ably focused on performance evaluation to compare models, but issues such as weak baselines and
041
      unreliable sampled metrics often make these evaluations difficult to reproduce (Dacrema et al.,
042   2019; Cremonesi & Jannach, 2021). A recent study attempted to provide a theoretical comparison
043   between linear recommendation models, such as matrix factorization and LAE, using spectral anal-
044   ysis, showing that both approaches “reduce” the singular values of the original user-item data matrix
045   R, albeit in different ways (Jin et al., 2021). Another related study investigates the loss landscape of
046   low-rank LAEs, characterizing their critical points through the smooth submanifold theory (Kunin
047   et al., 2019).
048   In this work, we aim to advance the theoretical understanding of linear autoencoder (LAE) models’
049   generalizability using statistical learning theory. While generalization theory has been extensively
050   studied for various machine learning and deep learning models (Vapnik, 1991; Dziugaite & Roy,
051   2017), its application to LAE recommendation models remains largely unexplored. To address this
052   gap, we leverage PAC-Bayes theory (McAllester, 1998), which integrates the Probably Approxi-
053   mately Correct (PAC) framework with Bayesian inference. Our analysis produces a nonvacuous
      bound, offering practical insights into LAE performance on unseen data. It is worth noting that prior


                                                         1

054
      work (Srebro et al., 2004) theoretically analyzed the generalizability of linear matrix factorization
055   models, deriving a vacuous PAC bound based on covering numbers.
056
057   Our study to establish PAC-Bayes bounds for LAE models builds on the theoretical framework in-
058
      troduced by Shalaeva (Shalaeva et al., 2020), which provides a PAC-Bayes bound for multiple linear
      regression (a single dependent variable with multiple independent variables) under the assumption
059
      of Gaussian data. However, applying this framework to LAE models introduces several challenges:
060
061   1. Multivariate Linear Regression: The PAC-Bayes bound must be extended from the multiple
062      linear regression setting to the multivariate linear regression scenario, which involves multiple
063
         dependent variables. Notably, PAC-Bayes bounds for multivariate linear regression – an impor-
         tant method and topic in statistical learning and inference – remain unexplored in the existing
064
         literature.
065
066   2. Additional Convergence Requirements: Our analysis reveals the need for additional conver-
067
         gence conditions beyond those presented in (Shalaeva et al., 2020). These conditions are essential
         for ensuring theoretical convergence in the more complex multivariate setting.
068
069   3. Zero-Diagonal Constraint: LAE models, such as EASE and EDLAE, enforce a structural zero-
070
         diagonal constraint on the weight matrix. This introduces unique theoretical challenges in adapt-
         ing PAC-Bayes bounds from multivariate linear regression to LAE models.
071
072   This paper addresses the aforementioned challenges and makes the following key contributions:
073   • (Section 3) We develop a general theoretical PAC-Bayes bound for multivariate linear regression
074     (Theorem 1), of which Shalaeva’s bound (Shalaeva et al., 2020) for single-output multiple linear
075     regression is a special case. Additionally, we propose sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) that guar-
076     antee convergence for both the new bound (Theorem 1) and Shalaeva’s original bound (Shalaeva
077     et al., 2020).
078   • (Section 4) We apply the bound of Theorem 1 to a LAE model for recommendation, EASE (Steck,
079     2019) and develop a practical method for calculating the optimal parameters that minimize the
080     bound. Specifically, we incorporate the constraint diag(W ) = 0 into the bound and resolve the
081     calculation challenges that arise from it by presenting Theorem 3 and Theorem 4.
082   • (Section 5) We conduct experiments for the bound in Section 4 on real-world datasets, and the
083     results show that the bound does not exceed 3× of the test error on three out of four datasets we
084     used.
085   • (Section 6) We conclude and discuss the empirical implication and potential application of PAC-
086     Bayes bound for LAE models in recommendation setting.
087
088   All proofs of the theorems and lemmas presented in this paper are provided in Appendix A, while
089   related works are discussed in Appendix D.

478   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
479
480   This paper studies the generalizability of multivariate linear regression and LAE. We propose a new
481   PAC-Bayes bound for multivariate linear regression, which generalizes Shalaeva’s bound for multi-
482   ple linear regression (Shalaeva et al., 2020). We also present a convergence analysis and demonstrate
483   the sufficient conditions that ensure the bound’s convergence. To illustrate how the bound applies
484   to LAE, we use it with EASE, a simple yet very effective LAE recommendation model, and de-
485   velop a practical method to calculate the optimal parameters that minimize the bound. This method
      primarily addresses the calculation challenges introduced by the zero diagonal constraint of EASE.

      Extending to other LAE models: Another class of Linear Autoencoder (LAE) models employs
504   low-rank approximations to represent and constrain W . While our multivariate linear regression
505   approach can potentially be applied and generalized to these models (though special handling is
506   needed to model a low-rank W from a certain distribution, which is non-trivial), they are generally
507   less effective than the zero-diagonal constraint on W in recommendation settings. Consequently,
508   we chose not to explicitly discuss them in this paper, focusing instead on the more effective zero-
509   diagonal constraint, which better aligns with the practical demands of recommendation tasks.
510   Empirical Implication and Potential Applications of PAC-Bayes Bound for Recommendation
511   Setting: In implicit recommendation settings, the performance of recommendation models is typi-
512   cally evaluated using top-k metrics such as Recall@k or NDCG@k during offline evaluation. How-
513   ever, optimizing these metrics directly is challenging due to their non-differentiable nature. As a
514   result, recommendation models often rely on surrogate loss functions – for example, linear rec-
515   ommendation models commonly minimize the sum of squared element-wise errors. This reliance
516   creates a potential mismatch, as the loss function optimized during training does not directly align
517   with the metrics used for evaluation.
518   While PAC-Bayes bounds are derived for the surrogate loss (e.g., sum of squared errors), they can
519   be recast to indirectly relate to evaluation metrics by decomposing the bound into two components:
520   (1) a generalization bound on the surrogate loss, and (2) the empirical correlation between the sur-
521   rogate loss and top-k metrics. This decomposition provides a theoretical framework to quantify the
522   mismatch and understand how improving generalization on the surrogate loss translates to better
523   performance on top-k metrics.
524   Additionally, since recommendation models depend on surrogate loss functions, ensuring that these
525   functions generalize well to unseen data is critical. PAC-Bayes bounds offer guarantees on the
526   generalization of surrogate losses, which is a necessary condition for achieving strong downstream
527   performance. Thus, while surrogate losses do not directly align with top-k metrics like Recall@k or
528   NDCG@k, demonstrating low generalization error on the surrogate loss provides a strong theoretical
529   foundation for the model’s ability to perform well on these evaluation metrics.
530   For instance, if a model performs poorly on the top-k metrics, PAC-Bayes bounds can help identify
531   whether the poor results are likely due to model uncertainty (indicated by a large bound) or other
532   factors: 1) A large PAC-Bayes bound could indicate high model uncertainty, suggesting insufficient
533   training or data sparsity. 2) A small PAC-Bayes bound coupled with poor performance might point
534   to issues like suboptimal surrogate metrics, data distribution shifts, or model design.
535   Quantifying the Gap between model loss and top k metrics: Finally, we would like to point that
536   there is lack of formal analytical framework that links various loss functions to top-k recommenda-
537   tion metrics in implicit settings. Establishing such a connection would bridge the divide between
538   training objectives and evaluation metrics, potentially enabling the development of more effective
539   recommendation models. We argue that addressing this challenge is an important open problem for
      both the recommendation systems and machine learning communities.

1077   D        R ELATED W ORKS
1078
1079   The earliest PAC-Bayes bound is proposed by (McAllester, 1998). (Alquier et al., 2016) proposed
       an oracle PAC-Bayes bound based under Hoeffding assumption. (Germain et al., 2016) applied


                                                                        20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Alquier’s bound to linear regression problem under Gaussian data and parameter distribution as-
1081   sumptions, but the bound does not converge for being independent of the number of samples.
1082   (Shalaeva et al., 2020) improved Germain’s bound by proposing a bound related to the number
1083   of samples, and showed the bound converges as the number of samples increases. Most PAC-Bayes
1084   bounds are theoretical and difficult to calculate in practice, and some research is focused on mak-
1085   ing the bound more practical to compute. (Dziugaite & Roy, 2017) proposed a practical way to
1086   calculate Seeger’s bound (Langford & Seeger, 2001) for neural networks, and showed the bound is
1087   nonvacuous on MNIST dataset, where the bound is around 10× of the test error.
1088   Recent years LAEs gains popularity in recommendation systems (particularly on implicit settings)
1089   due to their simplicity and effectiveness. (Steck, 2019) proposed the EASE model and showed it
1090   surpasses the performance of deep neural network models on recommendation datasets under Recall
1091   and NDCG metrics. Later (Steck, 2020) proposed EDLAE which introduces a mask to the target
1092   function to avoid the parameter matrix overfitting towards identity. (Vančura et al., 2022) proposed
1093   ELSA which constructs the LAE with an item-item similarity matrix AAT − I with zero diagonal.
1094   Most LAE based recommender models constraints the diagonal of the weight matrix to zero. The
1095   zero diagonal constraint is closely related to the trace norm, which is considered as an effective
1096   tool for matrix completion. (Srebro & Salakhutdinov, 2010) applied the weighted traced norm in
1097   collaborative filtering. (Shamir & Shalev-Shwartz, 2014) proposed a sample complexity bound for
1098   the trace norm in matrix completion.
1099   Another type of linear recommendation model is based on matrix factorization, which can be viewed
1100   as a form of low-rank matrix completion (Candes & Tao, 2009; Recht, 2011; Chen et al., 2014; Sre-
1101   bro & Shraibman, 2005; Foygel et al., 2011; Shamir & Shalev-Shwartz, 2011). Matrix factorization
1102   methods have been shown to be highly effective in explicit settings (Koren et al., 2009), where user
1103   preferences are explicitly expressed (e.g., ratings). However, they have been found to be less ef-
1104   fective than LAE models in implicit settings (Cremonesi & Jannach, 2021; Jin et al., 2021), where
1105   interactions are inferred from user behavior (e.g., clicks or purchases).
1106   Some studies have investigated the generalizability of the matrix factorization models. (Srebro et al.,
1107   2004) proposed a PAC bound based on covering number for collaborative filtering. Other general-
1108   ization bounds include (Ledent et al., 2021) for inductive matrix completion and (Ledent & Alves,
1109   2024) for deep non-linear matrix completion.
