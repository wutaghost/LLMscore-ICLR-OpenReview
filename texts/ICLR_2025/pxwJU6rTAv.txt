011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent studies have increasingly demonstrated that large language models (LLMs)
014            possess significant theory of mind (ToM) capabilities, showing the potential for
015
               simulating the tracking of mental states in generative agents. In this study, we
               propose a novel paradigm called ToM-agent, designed to empower LLMs-based
016
               generative agents to simulate ToM in open-domain conversational interactions.
017
               ToM-agent disentangles the confidence from mental states, facilitating the emu-
018            lation of an agent’s perception of its counterpart’s mental states, such as beliefs,
019            desires, and intentions (BDIs). Using past conversation history and verbal re-
020            flections, ToM-Agent can dynamically adjust counterparts’ inferred BDIs, along
021            with related confidence levels. We further put forth a counterfactual intervention
022            method that reflects on the gap between the predicted responses of counterparts
023            and their real utterances, thereby enhancing the efficiency of reflection. Lever-
024            aging empathetic and persuasion dialogue datasets, we assess the advantages of
025            implementing the ToM-agent with downstream tasks, as well as its performance
026
               in both the first-order and the second-order ToM. Our findings indicate that the
               ToM-agent can grasp the underlying reasons for their counterpart’s behaviors be-
027
               yond mere semantic-emotional supporting or decision-making based on common
028
               sense, providing new insights for studying large-scale LLMs-based simulation of
029            human social behaviors. The codes of this project will be made publicly available
030            for camera ready.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Generative agents (Park et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023), which are computational interactive agents
036   with critical components such as memory, observation, planning, and reflection, have been pro-
037   posed to simulate believable human behavior during conversational interactions by fusing with
038   LLMs (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al.,
039
      2023). Nevertheless, the limitations of LLMs in generating extended, coherent dialogues are well-
      documented, particularly their proclivity for generating hallucinated or inconsistent content (Rawte
040
      et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). These shortcomings are especially problematic when the purpose
041
      of the conversation extends beyond simple information exchange to include emotive or persuasive
042   elements, such as in scenarios of emotional support, sales, or persuasive communication (Hu et al.,
043   2023; tse Huang et al., 2023; Remountakis et al., 2023). Such situations necessitate not merely
044   the exchange of factual information, but also the articulation of nuanced demands or emotional
045   appeals (Yakura, 2023), which current LLMs architectures struggle to maintain across natural and
046   prolonged conversational sequences (Zheng et al., 2023).
047
      Referring to psychology science, it is noticed that human normally do not only express their emo-
048   tions or demands during interaction but also care about their own or counterpart’s mental status, such
049   as beliefs, desires, and intentions (BDIs) has been understood or satisfied during the communica-
050   tions (Dvash & Shamay-Tsoory, 2014; Grazzani et al., 2018; Rusch et al., 2020). Theory of mind
051   (ToM), a cognitive skill that enables an individual to track the BDIs, emotions, and knowledge of
052   others, plays a crucial role in effective communication, self-consciousness, empathetic emotional
053   support, and decision-making when human beings interact with each other, as well as interactions
      between human beings and artificial intelligence (AI) (Georgeff et al., 1999; Rabinowitz et al., 2018;

      In this study, we extend the ToM to open-domain conversational interaction scenarios by propos-
109   ing generative agents leveraging a novel BDI tracking paradigm: ToM-agent, which is illustrated
110   in Figure 1. In contrast to existing models or paradigms that limit task-specific computable ToM
111   models to binary conditions (true belief or false belief), the proposed ToM-Agent can disentangle
112   the belief and confidence based on psychological research. This allows for the simulation of gen-
113   erative agents engaged in open-domain conversational interactions that consider varying degrees of
114   confidence in different mental states, such as BDIs, knowledge, and more. More specifically, unlike
115   the previous works that limited the ToM to studying confidence about certain situations or facts, our
116   work could extend the ToM study to a specific person’s BDIs, which are different from the common-
117
      sense knowledge with the highest priority in the generation process by LLMs. Further, when one
      ToM-equipped agent engages in a whispered conversation with the counterpart agent, it can generate
118
      utterances based on its BDIs. Additionally, it can infer the BDIs of the counterpart agent (first-order
119
      ToM), as well as the counterpart’s cognitive thinking about its own BDIs (second-order ToM). With
120   the processing of the conversation, agents will also update their confidence in the counterpart’s BDI
121   according to dialogue history and the reflection of confidence on the observations. Counterfactual
122   reflection mechanisms are also introduced to enhance the reflection performance minding the gap
123   between the predicted responses and the real observed responses.
124
      To evaluate the proposed paradigm, we conducted a simulation experiment between agents based on
125
      two downstream conversational tasks: empathetic dialogue and persuasion dialogue. Further, abla-
126   tion studies with different LLMs and prompting methods are conducted to confirm the performance
127   of the ToM-agent. The main contribution of this work could be concluded as follows:
128
129          • To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to apply ToM modeling to open-domain
130            conversational interactions for generative agents by disentangling the belief and confidence,
131            in contrast to previous studies that were confined to psychological narratives or specific
132            task-oriented cooperative conversation scenarios.
133          • By leveraging the zero-shot power of LLMs, generative agents are allowed to autonomously
134            produce utterances based on their BDIs and infer their counterparts’ BDIs based on the
135            conversation context without necessitating training on collected dialogue corpus with an-
136            notation.
137          • The counterfactual reflection method is introduced to reason about the discrepancy between
138            the predicted response and the real response, thus indirectly reflecting on the gap between
139            the real BDIs and the predicted BDIs, thereby enhancing the effectiveness of updating
140            confidence on inferred BDIs.
141          • In experiments conducted on downstream tasks related to conversational interactions, the
142            effectiveness of the proposed ToM tracking paradigm has been confirmed, and we posit
143            that the implications of our findings extend beyond the AI research community, potentially
144            offering valuable insights into the field of psychology and other scientific areas.

146   2     BACKGROUND
147
148   2.1   T HEORY OF MIND (T O M)
149
150   ”What is ToM?” and ”Why ToM is important for Artificial intelligence?” are two questions we
151   would like to stress at the very beginning. ToM has long been studied within cognitive science
152   and psychology, which is defined as an important social cognitive skill highly developed in hu-
153
      mans and a small number of animals that involves the ability to tack both oneself and counterparts’
      unobservable mental states, including but not limited to beliefs, desires, intentions, emotions, and
154
      knowledges (Premack & Woodruff, 1978; Cuzzolin et al., 2020). Humans naturally build rich in-
155
      ternal ToM models of others by observing others’ behaviors, conditioning their own behaviors, and
156   predicting the behaviors of others to forecast social interactions (Oguntola et al., 2023).
157
158   It has also long been argued that computational ToM, or machine ToM, is significant for AI systems
159
      and could be critical to realizing artificial general intelligence (AGI) (Bubeck et al., 2023; Mao
      et al., 2023; Rabinowitz et al., 2018). As an important well-established mental state model for
160
      the ToM, the Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) model consists of three critical components: beliefs
161
      that represent a virtual agent’s knowledge and understanding of the current state of the world or


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      relationships between objects and events, desires represent the agent’s goals and preferences, and
163   intentions represent the actions the agent plans to take to achieve its goals (Georgeff et al., 1999).
164   Many previous studies were also conducted to model the computational ToM (Nguyen & González,
165   2020; Liu et al., 2023), and most existing works about ToM interpret belief as a binary condition
166   as true belief and false belief (Zhang et al., 2024; Gandhi et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023). While
167   many classic psychological tests of ToM heavily rely on tasks such as false-belief tasks to assess
168   an individual’s belief about the world that contrasts with reality, some psychological studies suggest
169   that belief and confidence are distinct yet equally fundamental types of mental states. For example,
170   as a human, Alice may believe Bob is on the way to school or Alice may not believe so due to
171
      Bob’s poor credit. However, our confidence level, or credence, can be modeled as either a discrete
      or continuous variable, representing degrees such as fully confident, highly confident, somewhat
172
      confident, or not confident at all (Bricker, 2022). Further, there is also the argument that confidence
173
      in oneself and others is equally critical (Bang et al., 2022).
174
175   Additionally, regarding ToM, the term ”orders” pertains to the number of mental state attributions
176   needed to address a specific inquiry or contemplate a particular situation (Harré, 2022). For in-
177
      stance, first-order reasoning about an individual’s representation of the world is like ”Alice thinks
      that Bob likes football”, while, second-order reasoning is like ”Bob thinks that Alice believes that
178
      Bob likes football”. There could also be higher-order ToM such as third-order ToM, fourth-order
179
      ToM, etc. (Wu et al., 2023). In this study, only first-order ToM and second-order ToM are consid-
180   ered.

183
184   To streamline our analysis, we shift our attention away from expansive agent simulations, honing in
185   on the dynamics between two generative agents grounded in the LLMs framework instead of multi-
186   ple generative agents: agents A and B. These two agents participate in a dialogic exchange. Agent
187   A’s utterance, denoted as Ua , stems from its underlying beliefs, desires, and intentions. This triad
188   can be captured by R = (Br , Dr , Ir ), where Br , Dr and Ir represent agent A’s authentic beliefs,
189   desires, and intentions, respectively. In contrast, agent B’s utterance is represented as Ub . The be-
190
      liefs, desires, and intentions of agent A as inferred by agent B are encapsulated by I = (Bi , Di , Ii ).
      Agent B speculates on what is the real beliefs, desires, or intentions of agent A based on its re-
191
      sponses and updates the perceptions and confidence accordingly. Given that BDIs are unobservable
192
      inherently latent, and discernable only from the ongoing dialogue, our core challenge is to simulate
193   how agents might progressively recognize each other’s genuine BDIs throughout their conversation
194   and eventually benefit conversational communication.

      As illustrated in Figure 2, we propose a ToM aware generative agent consisting of three main mod-
217   ules: Self-BDI aware Module, Vanilla BDI Tracking Module, and Counterfactual Reflection-
218   based (CR-based) BDI Tracking Module. The self-BDI aware Module is used to generate utter-
219   ances based on the agent’s own beliefs, desires, and intentions. The Vanilla BDI Tracking Module
220   and the CR-based Module are designed to track the counterpart’s possible BDIs and update the
221   perceptions and confidence levels regarding these BDIs. The former serves as a baseline for bench-
222   marking, while the latter is a technique aimed at performance improvement. Ideally, an agent should
223   be capable of both generating conversations based on its own BDI and inferring and updating oth-
224   ers’ BIDs. However, for this study, we simplified the setup to include one agent equipped with a
225
      self-BDI-aware module and the other agent equipped with either a vanilla BDI tracking Module or
      a CR-based BDI tracking Module.

      Zero-shot BDIs Initialization. The initial BDIs of agent A are learned from a randomly selected
238   single episode of dialogue corpus, utilizing zero-shot prompting, without needing annotation or
239   additional learning. To increase randomness, we adopt an approach where the LLM generates the
240   top-k combinations of beliefs, desires, and intentions based on the conversation episode’s history.
241   From these combinations, one of the combinations R is randomly selected as the initial top-1 value
242   of BDI for the agent. In the prompt, we have also included hints about the concepts of beliefs,
243   desires, and intentions, emphasizing their relevance to ensure that the resulting BDI combination is
244   closer to reality.

246   Reverse BDIs Argumentation. Belief often reflects personal subjectivity and may not always
247
      be correct or even morally wrong, whereas common sense tends to align with the public’s general
      perception. In our experiments, we also discovered that expressing certain personalized beliefs is
248
      challenging because the conversation data tends to align more closely with common sense. Ulti-
249
      mately, the features learned by the LLMs are constrained by the available data, which often results
250   in a bias towards commonsense concepts that appear more frequently in the datasets. To address this
251   issue, we iteratively refine the resulting BDIs by inputting them back into the prompt and instructing
252   the LLM to generate BDIs with the opposite meaning or a counterfactual nature.

      3.3   CR- BASED BDI T RACKING M ODULE
271
272   In this study, rather than limiting belief to specific scenarios or tasks, we disentangle belief and
273   confidence to enable the ToM modeling in open-domain conversational interactions. This approach
274   allows agents to focus on personalized beliefs compared to mere common sense. In the initial phase,
275   top-k BDIs along with confidences are inferred by agent B based on the first utterance of agent A.
276   These BDIs and their associated confidence levels are then updated for ith turn of communicational
277   interaction, which is represented as TIi = ((Bi1 , Di1 , Ii1 ; C1 ), ..., (Bik , Dik , Iik ; Ck )), where C
278
      stands for the confidence for each set of BDIs. The agent can generate an update plan of the inferred
      BIDs and its corresponding confidence by prompting
279
280   During each round of interactions, agent B generates an updating plan Li+1 for the inferred BDIs
281   and confidence of agent A using the reflection mechanism, by prompting the LLMs with the cor-
282   responding reflection prompt Pr based on the dialogue history Hi . Then, the updated BDIs and
283   confidence levels combination TIi+1 are obtained by prompting the LLMs using the corresponding
284
      prompt Pr , the previous BIDs combination TIi , and the updating plan Li+1 , as is described in the
      Equation (3).

      set with the highest confidence along with the corresponding prompt, which is similar to Equation

293
      Reflection. Reflection is an effective reinforcement technique for LLMs-based agents, which can
294   be a reinforcement learning way via verbal feedback without tuning the parameters of LLMs or
295   devising a reword function (Shinn et al., 2023). It employs a persisting memory of self-refective
296   experiences, allowing an agent to revisit its errors and make improved decisions in subsequent iter-
297   ations. It consists of three distinct models: an actor model, an evaluator model, and a self-reflection
298   model, in which the evaluator model plays a crucial role in assessing the quality. We aim to update
299   the top-k BDIs and the related confidence level using the reflection of LLMs. However, since the
300   BDI is unobservable, it results in the established reflection cannot directly evaluate the similarity
301   between the inferred BDI and the actual BDI. To solve this problem, we propose a counterfactual
302
      reflection based on foresight and counterfactual thinking.
303
304
      Foresight. It is argued in previous studies that foresight and reflection are equally critical for
      machine ToM (Zhou et al., 2023). To solve the problem that BDI is unobservable, we compared the
305
      observable utterances with predicted utterances instead: in each interaction round, we let the agent
306
      B predict the agent A’s utterance Uap by prompting the LLM, utilizing inferred BDIs combination
307   with the highest confidence TI and conversation history H. After agent A’s real utterance Uar is
308   observed, agent B compares the Uap with Uar by scoring the two sentences with a decimal value S
309   between [0, 1] to evaluate their similarity.
310
311   Counterfactual Reflection. Inspired by the argument in the previous study that counterfactual
312   thinking may be critical for an individual to understand others by predicting what action they will
313   take in a similar situation (Cuzzolin et al., 2020), we propose a counterfactual reflection. The pro-
314   posed counterfactual reflection is conducted in the following steps: If the Si+1 increases compared
315   with Si , the agent B reflects on the previously inferred BDIs of agent A based on the evaluation
316   value S on the Uap and Uar by prompting the LLMs. Further, agent B reflects that ”what if my
317
      previously inferred BDI of agent A is not correct?”. Then agent B carries on the conversation with
      itself and generates a virtual response Uav instead of agent A and compares the Uav with the real
318
      one Uar to obtain a virtual score Sv . If the Sv > S, then update the BDI and generate the Ub for the
319
      next round, otherwise, update the BIDs and confidence level using the TI and H.

      4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
327
328   By conducting experiments, we try to answer the following research questions:
329
330          • RQ1: To what extent could the ToM-aware generative agent infer about other generative
331            agents’ unobservable beliefs, desires, and intentions during open-domain conversational
332            interactions? (First-order ToM)
333          • RQ2: To what extent could the ToM-aware generative agent infer about counterpart agents’
334            understanding of its own beliefs, desires, and intentions during open-domain conversational
335            interactions? (Second-order ToM)
336          • RQ3: To what extent could the ToM-aware generative agent benefit downstream tasks of
337            open-domain conversational interactions in short-term interactions?
338
339   We use these two downstream tasks to evaluate our method: empathetic dialogue and persuasive
340   dialogue, which are highly related to ToM mental status modeling during human beings’ interac-
341
      tions. We can abstract downstream tasks in the dialog domain into specific goals. For instance,
      in empathetic dialogue, one agent’s goal is to satisfy the other emotionally. While, in persuasive
342
      dialogue, one agent aims to persuade the other to make a decision, such as purchasing a certain
343
      good or service, or making a donation. Empathetic Dialogue(Rashkin et al., 2019) and Persuasion
344   Dialogue(Wang et al., 2019) are two dialogue datasets publicly available and the details about these
345   datasets can be found in the Appendix.
346
347
      In the simulation, one agent plays the role of either an Empathetic-needing NPC or a Persuadee
      NPC, taking actions as the previously introduced agent A. Meanwhile, another agent plays the role
348
      of either an Empathetic NPC or a Persuador NPC, taking actions as the agent B. The initialization of
349
      BDIs is conducted on 100 dialogue episodes randomly sampled from the above two datasets. Sim-
350   ilarly, for each experiment, the two agents interact with each other until agent A believes that agent
351   B understands its BDI, at which point the dialog is considered successfully concluded. However, if
352   the conditions for ending the dialog are not met within t rounds, the dialog is considered unsuccess-
353   ful. We mainly evaluated two versions of LLMs as generative agents: GPT-4 (gpt-4-0125-preview)
354   and GPT-3.5 (gpt-3.5-turbo-0125). OpenAI davinci model (text-similarity-davinci-001) is used for
355   scoring the similarity of the predicted utterance and the real utterance. In this study, the BDIs set
356   number top-k is set to 3 and the maximum number of turns in each dialogue episode t is set to 10.
357
358   4.2   BDI I NFERING E VALUATION (F IRST- ORDER T O M)

      5.3   E THICAL C ONCERNS
515   As the conversation examples illustrated in Figure 1, someone may think that the implementation
516   of this kind of agent may raise ethical concerns. However, we originally chose this nearly extreme
517   example because our research focuses on the broader context of generative agents, and we wanted to
518   demonstrate that ToM agents can take into account and imitate human beliefs in their interactions.
519   This distinction is crucial because human beliefs do not always follow common sense (may even
520   be morally incorrect), and such discrepancies can significantly impact agent behavior. Instead of
521   labeling the act of mimicking human beliefs as inherently unethical, we believe it is essential to
522   recognize this potential issue through our research. Our goal is to provide a foundation for the
523
      research community to discuss and study the implications of such behavior in ToM agents further.
      We hope our work stimulates constructive discourse and exploration into how generative agents can
524
      better understand and ethically interact with human beliefs.

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this study, we proposed a novel paradigm ToM-Agent that equips LLMs-based generative agents
530   with ToM reasoning, allowing them to emulate cognitive mental states such as beliefs, desires, and
531   intentions (BDIs) during open-domain conversational interactions. The counterfactual reflection
532
      method is also proposed to dynamically adjust confidence levels related to inferred BDIs of coun-
      terparts based on past conversation history, to reflect on the gap between predicted responses of
533
      counterparts and their real utterances, which enhances the confidence updating performances of in-
534
      ferred BDIs. Leveraging datasets from empathetic and persuasion dialogue research, we evaluate
535   the performance of our proposed agent architecture in downstream tasks. Finally, the results show
536   that equipping the generative agents with ToM is reasonable and will benefit the downstream task in
537   the long term.

727   A.1   L IMITATIONS
728
729   Challenges of Time and Cost Efficiency. The formidable cost associated with utilizing the Ope-
730
      nAI API posed significant constraints on our research endeavors, rendering us financially incapable
      of conducting extensive experiments. This issue is exacerbated by the practical challenges inherent
731
      in generating conversational content, as it necessitates using a prompt with a substantial number of
732
      tokens. For instance, the average expenditure per episode escalates to approximately 2 to 3 dollars
733   when employing GPT-4 for this purpose. Moreover, the generation of sentence-based conversations
734   entails multiple interactions with the API interface, thereby incurring substantial waiting times. Such
735   prolonged waiting periods may lead to user frustration and dwindling interest, adversely affecting
736   the viability of engaging in dialogues with the AI agent, should we prioritize technical practicality.
737
738   Limitation of Two Agents Simulations. While memory retrieval, and reflection all play pivotal
739   roles in conversational control of generative agents(Park et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023), our re-
740   search primarily emphasizes modeling the BDIs tracking paradigm between two agents for better
741   reflection. Consequently, we haven’t delved into matching conversations with analogous BDIs dur-
742   ing extended interactions. However, as we simulate interactions among a vast number of agents,
743
      conversations inevitably span a broader array of topics and knowledge domains. Hence, extracting
      short-term conversation content with the most pertinent BDIs from long-term memory emerges as
744
      a compelling research avenue, offering the potential for future exploration. Furthermore, in this
745
      article, we focus solely on interactions between one agent aware of its BDIs and the counterpart
746   attempting to infer that BDI. Yet, when scaled up in simulations, multi-agent interactions produce
747   more complex dynamics. Here, we must delve deeper, exploring scenarios where an agent is not
748   just self-aware of its BDI but also discerns the BDIs of its counterparts. Particularly when the agent
749   recognizes its differing BDI from the majority, it might either seek to influence others’ BDIs or,
750   conversely, be swayed by them to modify its stance.
751
752   Limitaions of Behavior Modality. To present the problem more comprehensively, we confined
753   our paradigm’s definition and validation strictly to the realm of conversational interactions. This
754   encompasses all actions and behaviors, ranging from the selection of empathetic responses to the
755   deployment of suitable persuasive tactics, and from choices involving donations to decisions against



                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      donating. Furthermore, the encapsulated semantic sentiments are exclusively tied to dialogic mani-
757   festations. However, it’s crucial to note that for applications such as in-game non-player characters
758   (NPCs), generative urban simulations, or robotic interfaces, our paradigm’s definition ought to be
759   expanded. This will allow it to encompass behaviors and actions beyond mere dialogue, potentially
760   extending to multimodal communications. ToM in Multi-modal interaction should be further studied
761   such as in VQA scenarios (Takmaz et al., 2023; Chandrasekaran et al., 2017).
762
763   A.2   F UTURE W ORKS
764
765   Large-scale Multi-agent Simulation. Although only the interactions between two agents are
766
      studied in this study, multiple Agents’ behaviors should be further studied to mimic human be-
      ings. And belief transition between agents or the rise and disappearance of BDIs should also be
767
      studied in future work. The other big premise is whether local LLMs can achieve the same perfor-
768
      mance as GPTs to support large-scale simulations. Also, some supporting measures such as memory
769   retention, simulation of forgetting, etc. need to be further researched.
770
771   Interaction in Multi-modal scenarios. ToM in Multi-modal interaction should be further studied
772   such as in VQA scenarios (Takmaz et al., 2023; Chandrasekaran et al., 2017). Agents based on LLMs
773   can be used to simulate not only dialogues but also human expressions, voices, etc. so that the agents
774   can better understand human inner emotions by analyzing multimodal information. Combining
775   image processing facial expression recognition with conversational context based on Agent LLM,
776   the machine can better estimate the person’s condition and improve the accuracy of facial expression
777   recognition as well as the performance of conversational communication for empathy or persuasion.
778
779   The relationship between belief, desire, and intention could be studied. The research here
780
      treats belief, desire, and intention as equal relationships and focuses primarily on the possibility
      of a new paradigm. In future research, some psychological models about equality can be applied
781
      to the paradigm to map the human heart state further. For example, there are various BDI models
782
      in psychology, and some of them are based on the causal relationship between belief, desire, and
783   intention. If such BDI models can be used for simulation, on the one hand, the reliability and
784   usefulness of these models can be verified, and on the other hand, the simulation can be facilitated,
785   which will enable the agent to better simulate human beings. or understand human beings.
786
787   Higher-oder ToM for the ToM-agent. We only studied the first-order ToM and the second-order
788   ToM, and higher-order ToM should be studied in the future. However, the difficulty of the research
789   is how to evaluate it, so the evaluation method in simulation is also an important research direction
790   in future research.

795
796   Empathetic Dialogue. Empathetic Dialogue is a novel dataset of 25k conversations grounded in
797   emotional situations. Each dialogue is based on a specific scenario where a speaker experiences a
798   particular emotion, and a listener responds accordingly. This resource, consisting of crowdsourced
799   one-on-one conversations, covers a wide range of emotions in a balanced manner. It is larger and
800
      includes a more extensive set of emotions than many existing emotion prediction datasets. The
      dataset contains 32 emotion labels, which were selected by aggregating labels from several emotion
801
      prediction datasets.
802
803   In each conversation, the person who wrote the situation description (the Speaker) initiates the di-
804   alogue by discussing it. The other participant (the Listener) learns about the underlying situation
805   through the Speaker’s words and responds. The Speaker and Listener then exchange up to six addi-
806
      tional turns. The resulting dataset comprises 24,850 conversations based on situation descriptions,
      gathered from 810 different participants. The final train/validation/test split is 19,533 / 2,770 / 2,547
807
      conversations, respectively.
808
809   Persuasion Dialogue. Persuasion Dialogue is a large dialogue dataset consisting of 1,017 dia-
      logues, with a subset annotated for emerging persuasion strategies. The dataset not only explores


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      how personal information influences persuasion outcomes but also examines which strategies are
811   most effective based on different user backgrounds and personalities. Before the conversation be-
812   gins, participants complete a pre-task survey to assess psychological profile variables using the Big
813   Five personality traits. The roles of persuader and persuadee are then assigned to the two partici-
814   pants, which helps eliminate any correlation between the persuader’s strategies and the persuadee’s
815   characteristics. Each participant is required to complete at least 10 conversational turns, with multi-
816   ple sentences allowed in a single turn. The dataset includes 4,313 instances of persuasion strategies
817   categorized into 10 types, such as logical appeal, emotional appeal, personal-related inquiry, and
818   non-strategy dialogue acts.
