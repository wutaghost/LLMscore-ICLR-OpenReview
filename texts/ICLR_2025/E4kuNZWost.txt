009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            A reliable uncertainty estimation method is the foundation of many modern out-
012            of-distribution (OOD) detectors, which are critical for safe deployments of deep
013            learning models in the open world. In this work, we propose TULiP, a theoretically-
014            driven post-hoc uncertainty estimator for OOD detection. Our approach considers
015            a hypothetical perturbation applied to the network before convergence. Based on
016
               linearized training dynamics, we bound the effect of such perturbation, resulting in
               an uncertainty score computable by perturbing model parameters. Ultimately, our
017
               approach computes uncertainty from a set of sampled predictions, thus not limited
018
               to classification problems. We visualize our bound on synthetic regression and
019            classification datasets. Furthermore, we demonstrate the effectiveness of TULiP
020            using large-scale OOD detection benchmarks for image classification. Our method
021            exhibits state-of-the-art performance, particularly for near-distribution samples.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   An important safety component for deep neural networks (NNs) in real-world environments is
027   the awareness of their uncertainty upon receiving unknown or corrupted inputs. Such capability
028
      enables systems to fall back to conservative decision-making or defer to human judgments when
      faced with unfamiliar scenarios, which is imperative in safety-critical domains, such as autonomous
029
      driving (Atakishiyev et al., 2024) and medical applications (Esteva et al., 2017). The problem is often
030
      framed as Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) detection, which has witnessed significant growth in recent
031   years (Yang et al., 2024).
032
033   Theoretically, this issue directly relates to quantifying epistemic uncertainty (Hora, 1996), which
034
      measures the lack of knowledge in a fitted model due to insufficient training data. The training process
      is typically modelled as a Bayesian optimization process (Wang & Yeung, 2020) with approximations
035
      for practical use (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016; Daxberger et al., 2021). More generally, epistemic
036
      uncertainty could be formalized by the variance of a trained ensemble of networks ϕ(x; θ):
037
038                                           VarθInit [ϕ(x; θTrain )] ,                                   (1)
039   where θTrain are parameters trained by some learning algorithm from random initialization θInit .
040   Intuitively, higher prediction variance corresponds to inputs x further from training set (OOD), as
041   there lack enough training data to eliminate model disagreements via training, hence epistemic.
042   Many works redesign the network or training process to be uncertainty-aware (DeVries & Taylor,
043   2018; Huang & Li, 2021). However, these are often impractical due to heavy computational costs,
044   especially for large datasets. Instead, post-hoc methods (Liang et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2020; Hendrycks
045   et al., 2022; Djurisic et al., 2023) are generally preferred. These approaches can be easily integrated
046   into pre-trained models without interfering with the trained backbones, significantly enhancing their
047   versatility (Yang et al., 2022). Nevertheless, they often lack a direct theoretical link to the training
048   process, which weakens their theoretical foundation and necessitates extensive empirical validation.
049   Therefore, it is desirable to develop a post-hoc OOD method with direct theoretical justifications
050   regarding the training process. Recent analysis of NN optimizations reveals that gradient descent
051   can be seen as its first-order approximations (Jacot et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019), termed lazy
052   training, under specific conditions (Geiger et al., 2020). This enabled direct (but costly) computation
053   of equation 1, as well as rigorous analysis (Kobayashi et al., 2022) and methods (He et al., 2020) on
      model uncertainty, even beyond the lazy regime (Chen et al., 2020).

      Inspired by this series of work, we present TULiP (Test-time Uncertainty by Linearized fluctuations
055   via weight Perturbation), a post-hoc uncertainty estimator for OOD detection. Our method considers
056   hypothetical fluctuations of the lazy training dynamics, which can be bounded under certain assump-
057   tions and efficiently estimated via weight perturbation. In practice, we found our method works well
058   even beyond the ideal regime. Our contribution is threefold:
059
060           (i) We provide a simple, versatile theoretical framework for analyzing epistemic uncertainty at
061
                  inference time in the lazy regime, which is empirically verified;
062          (ii) Based on our theory, we propose TULiP, an efficient and effective post-hoc OOD detector
063               that does not require access to original training data;
064         (iii) We test TULiP extensively using OpenOOD (Zhang et al., 2023), a large, transparent,
065               and unified OOD benchmark for image classifications. We show that TULiP consistently
066               improves previous state-of-the-art methods across various settings.
067
      The outline is as follows. Sec. 2 provides a summary of related works, Sec. 3 presents theoretical
068
      derivations, and Sec. 4 bridges theory to the implementation of TULiP. Sec. 5 reports the effectiveness
069   of TULiP via empirical studies.

072   2     R ELATED W ORKS
073
074
      Uncertainty Quantification (UQ) As being discussed in Sec. 1, theoretically-driven methods often
      estimates epistemic uncertainty from a Bayesian perspective. This includes, notably, Variational
075
      Inference (Blundell et al., 2015a). Gal & Ghahramani (2016) connects Bayesian inference and the
076
      usage of Dropout layers, led their method, Monte Carlo (MC) Dropout, widely adopted in practice
077   due to its simplicity and effectiveness. Moreover, Daxberger et al. (2021) approximates the posterior
078   via Taylor approximation and Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017) directly used independently trained
079   deep models as an ensemble.
080
081   Post-hoc OOD Detectors For post-hoc methods, the baseline method using maximum softmax
082   probability (MSP) was first introduced by Hendrycks & Gimpel (2017). ODIN (Liang et al., 2018)
083   applies input preprocessing on top of temperature scaling (Guo et al., 2017a) to enhance MSP. Liu
084   et al. (2020) proposes a simple score based on energy function (EBO). Hendrycks et al. (2022) uses
085   maximum logits (MLS) for efficient detection on large datasets. GEN (Liu et al., 2023) adopts
086
      the generalization of Shannon Entropy, while ASH (Djurisic et al., 2023) prunes away samples’
      activation at later layers and simplifies the rest. Some methods also access the training set for
087
      additional information, as MDS (Lee et al., 2018b) used Mahalanobis distance with class-conditional
088
      Gaussian distributions, and ViM (Wang et al., 2022b) computes the norm of the feature residual on
089   the principal subspace for OOD detection.
090
091   Due to the nature of post-hoc setting, most methods such as EBO, ODIN and MLS compute OOD
092
      score solely from trained models, overlooking the training process. In contrast, as previously stated,
      inspired by the more theoretically-aligned UQ methods, TULiP addresses the problem with regard
093
      to the training process from a theoretical aspect. In practice, TULiP works by a series of carefully
094
      constructed weight perturbations, ultimately yielding a set of model predictions, which can be seen
095   as surrogates to posterior samples for OOD detections. Our contribution is orthogonal to methods
096   working with logits and predictive probabilities, such as GEN, as they can work on top of TULiP
097   outputs. In such an aspect, TULiP shares the similar plug-and-play versatility as seen in recent works,
098   such as ReAct (Sun et al., 2021) and RankFeat (Song et al., 2022).

107   training process. Lee et al. (2019) extends the result by examining them in the parameter space, with
      a formal result equalizing linearized networks and empirical ones under mild assumptions.

      3.2   M ODELING U NCERTAINTY
134
      Under our problem setting, neither the distribution of initialized models nor the training process is
135   accessible, which renders a significant difficulty for the direct computation of the uncertainty shown
136   in equation 1. Instead, we choose to intuitively model it by considering a perturbation applied towards
137   the network function f (x), at a time t = ts before the training terminates at t = T . This perturbation
138   prior to convergence is hypothetical, as it is inaccessible in our post-hoc setting, and we will only use
139   it to establish our theoretical framework.
140
      Formally, consider a perturbation to fts at t = ts as fˆts (x) = fts (x)+∆f (x). After the perturbation,
141
      the perturbed network fˆ(x) will be trained following the same dynamics as equation 3:
142
143                                ∂t fˆt (x) = −ηEx′ [Θ(x, x′ )ℓ′ (fˆt (x′ ); y(x′ ))],                    (4)
144   until termination time T .
145
      Under such a perturb-then-train process, we model the epistemic uncertainty as the difference
146
147
      between converged networks, reads ∥fT (z) − fˆT (z)∥. It measures the fluctuation of the training
      process, capturing the sensitivity of training w.r.t. noise. Indeed, by applying a perturbation at
148
      t = 0, we essentially perturb fInit , which can be seen as a sampling process from some model
149
      prior (Appendix A.7). Therefore, fˆT (z) can be interpreted as samples from the trained ensemble as
150
      in equation 1, where their variance reflects epistemic uncertainty.
151
152   However, as stated above, in practice we only know the trained network fT at t = T . It would be
153   impractical to recover the full training trajectory, apply the perturbation at t = ts and then retrain the
154   network. Therefore, in the following, we will come up with a bound of ∥fT (z) − fˆT (z)∥ given the
155   strength of the perturbation ∆f , which can be evaluated at z without actually retrain the network.
156
      Thus, the perturbation is hypothetical, as it has never been applied in our practice.
157   We first present this bound, then we examine a method to estimate the bound without explicit access
158   to training data.

347   As stated in Sec. 3, the bound in equation 5 or 9 is insufficient to capture a full picture – for example,
348   a well-trained classifier can be certain that a test data belongs to neither class, in a sense that an
349   evaluation of equation 9 yields a small value, but their prediction (i.e., it belongs to neither classes)
350   fˆT (z) indicates OOD input. Informally yet intuitively, fˆT (z) can also be seen as predictions of
351   models sampled from some model posterior.
352
      To this end, we propose to approximate fˆT (z) by constructing f˜(z), via the process described in
353
      line 14-18 of Alg. 1. In short, we squeeze the perturbed predictions f˜iraw (z), producing f˜(z), so
354
      that their variance matches equation 10, which is an upper bound of the variance of true perturbed
355
      predictions Tr(Var∆f [fˆT (z)]).

413   In this subsection, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our method for OOD detection in real-world
414   scenarios by comparing TULiP with state-of-the-art OOD detectors.
415
416   Experiment Setup We evaluate the performance of TULiP with OOD detection tasks based on
417   manually defined ID-OOD dataset pairs (Zhang et√al., 2023). For TULiP, we use M = 10 surrogate
418   posterior samples with ϵ = 2.0, δ = 2 and λ = o where o is the number of output dimensions.
419   Only weights in the convolutional and fully connected layers are being perturbed, while biases are
420   ignored. Following Zhang et al. (2023), we conduct a hyper-parameter search on a small validation
421         √ √ possible, within a reasonable range of ϵ ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 1.5, 2.0}, δ ∈ {2, 5, 8} and
      set whenever
422   λ ∈ { o, 3 o}. We explain our choice for hyper-parameters in Sec. B.2. We consider two OOD
423
      scenarios, namely Semantic-Shift OOD (SS-OOD) and Covariate-Shift OOD (CS-OOD) (Yang et al.,
      2024). The fundamental difference between them is that SS-OOD considers distributional shift on
424
      both input x and label y, often with unseen classes. CS-OOD considers distributional shift solely on
425
      input x. Recently, Yang et al. (2021) raised concerns regarding the negligible covariate shifts between
426   ID and OOD data with same labels. Our setup does not contradict with this work as overlapping
427   classes have been removed from our SS-OOD experiments, following (Yang et al., 2022). Instead,
428   we believe the CS-OOD setting is also significant for practical use. For instance, one may wish to
429   distinguish real-world images from AI-generated ones (Zhang et al., 2024), or identify images that
430   are severely contaminated due to environmental factors or sensor malfunctions (Baek et al., 2024).
431   We present details of all datasets in Sec. B.1 and provide additional experimental results as well as
      details of reported results in Sec. C.

452   Semantic Shift OOD We report the performance of TULiP on OpenOOD v1.5 benchmark (Zhang

454

456

457   far OOD sets (Yang et al., 2022), where near is more similar to ID and therefore more difficult to

460   TULiP. TULiP achieves remarkable performance in near-OOD settings with either top-1 or top-2
461   AUROC scores across all datasets. Indeed, as suggested by equation 8, better performance on near-ID

463   methods significantly outperform TULiP on far-OOD either access the training dataset (ViM and

465
      despite being outperformed by ASH, TULiP still outperforms other baselines by a large margin, with

466
      does not significantly impact ID accuracy (Djurisic et al., 2023). ResNet-50, compared to ResNet-18
467
      used otherwise, is more likely to have redundant representations due to its increased expressive
468   power. In such cases, particularly in near-OOD scenarios, one may expect high performance for
469   ASH when pruning parameters are appropriately tuned. On the other hand, TULiP demonstrates
470   relatively consistent and high performance across all datasets. This indicates that properly evaluating
471   uncertainty is fundamentally important, and our method achieves this goal to a considerable extent.
472   Notably, ASH failed when using a different set of weights on ImageNet-1K (Appendix C.3). In
473   contrast, TULiP, without access to training information, performs consistently well with theoretical
474   foundations.

      6        C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this study, we present TULiP, an uncertainty estimator for OOD detection. Our method is driven
530   by the fluctuations under linearized training dynamics and excels in practical experiments. However,
531
      there are some limitations and future works remaining. Theoretically, our framework only considers
      functional perturbation. The perturbation on the NTK is also important (Kobayashi et al., 2022)
532
      and could be integrated into the estimator in the future. Furthermore, the layer-wise scaling scheme
533
      deserves more exploration as being discussed in Appendix C.2. Empirically, TULiP does not achieve
534   state-of-the-art performance when the OOD data is far from ID (far-OOD). Such tradeoff in Fig. 4
535   hints at the inconsistency of best hyper-parameters for different setups. Future works may improve
536   upon these aspects, covering a wider range of OOD data by examining the network parameters and
537   refining weight perturbations. As shown in Appendix C.4, It is also beneficial to further develop
538   TULiP for networks other than convolutional ones, such as transformers. In a broader aspect,
539   exploring TULiP in other learning paradigms, such as Active Learning (Wang et al., 2022a) or
      Reinforcement Learning (Szepesvari, 2010) will be valuable.
