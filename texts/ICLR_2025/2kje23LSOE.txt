009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011           This paper concerns the application of techniques from optimal transport (OT) to
012           mean field control, in which the probability measures of interest in OT correspond
013           to empirical distributions associated with a large collection of controlled agents.
014           The control objective of interest motivates a one-sided relaxation of OT, in which
015           the first marginal is fixed and the second marginal is constrained to a “moment
016           class”: a set of probability measures defined by generalized moment constraints.
017           This relaxation is particularly interesting for control problems as it enables the
018
              coordination of agents without the need to know the desired distribution before-
              hand. The inclusion of an entropic regularizer is motivated by both computational
019
              considerations, and also to impose hard constraints on agent behavior. A com-
020
              putational approach inspired by the Sinkhorn algorithm is proposed to solve this
021           problem. This new approach to distributed control is illustrated with an application
022           of charging a fleet of electric vehicles while satisfying grid constraints. An online
023           version is proposed and applied in a case study on the ElaadNL dataset containing
024           10,000 EV charging transactions in the Netherlands. This empirical validation
025           demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach to optimizing flexibility
026           while respecting grid constraints.

031   Optimal Transport Optimal Transport (OT) theory first emerged in the 18th century, and more
032   recently has become a significant tool in the machine learning toolbox (Villani, 2008; Peyré et al.,
033   2019). The goal is simply described: given two random variables X and Y , find a joint probability
034   measure π ∗ for the pair (X, Y ) that preserves the marginals, and minimizes some criterion. When X
035   and Y belong to a common state X , the Monge-Kantorovich formulation is expressed as follows.
036   Let U(µ1 , µ2 ) = {πR ∈ B(X × X ) : π1 = µ1 , π2 = µ2 } where πi denotes the ith marginal, for
037   example π1 (dx) = X π(dx, dy), and with B(X × X ), the set of Borel probability measures on
038   X × X . Given a cost function c : X × X → R+ , the optimal transport problem is formulated as the
039   minimum

043   Several authors have proposed relaxations of the OT problem, such as unbalanced OT where
044   an entropic penalization of the deviation from the marginals is introduced (Chizat et al., 2017).
045   Relaxations of marginals have been considered to improve numerical performance or to approximate
046
      the OT problem (Balaji et al., 2020; Le et al., 2021; Alfonsi et al., 2020) but, to the best of our
      knowledge, never as a natural representation of a Mean Field control (MFC) problem.

050   linearly-solvable Markov decision framework (Todorov, 2007). The area of mean field games begins
051   with a multi-objective control problem, but the final solution technique amounts to transporting
052   a probability measure on a high dimensional space in such a way as to minimize some objective
053   function. Similar to mean field games is the cooperative setting of mean field control or ensemble
      control, with applications (Hochberg et al., 2006; Chertkov & Chernyak, 2018) ranging from power

      Often in the Mean Field litterature, a Kullback-Leibler cost term is introduced as a regularizer
      (Chertkov & Chernyak, 2018; Todorov, 2007) and similar control objectives, but with the constraints
078
      on the functions f relaxed through a quadratic penalty have been addressed (Cammardella et al.,
079
      2020; Bušić & Meyn, 2018). Inspired by the similarities between the OT problem (1) and the Mean
080   Field Control applications such as (3), we want to build bridges between these fields and investigate
081   how computational techniques from OT theory might apply to the computation of optimal control
082   solutions.
083
084   Contributions We introduce Moment Constrained Optimal Transport for Control (MCOT-C) which
085   is a natural representation of a MFC problem designed to achieve three objectives:
086   • Coordination of an ensemble of agents to achieve a desired goal.
087
088   • Enforcement of physical constraints, both spatial and dynamics.
089
      • Enforcement of strict constraints on the distribution of exogenous variables.
090
091   Instead of considering the whole state space often very large or even infinite dimensional (e.g.
092   trajectories of agents), this approach focuses on a finite set of moments, relevant to the control
093   objective (e.g. signal tracking). This leads to a tractable algorithm: we modify the Sinkhorn algorithm
094   (Cuturi, 2013) by replacing the update on the second marginal by gradient descent on the dual. An
095
      MFC application on charging a fleet of electric vehicles (EVs) while satisfying grid constraints is
      used to illustrate this new approach. This MCOT-C setting is then extended in two ways: (i) by an
096
      online approach which allows to consider real datasets where the algorithm discovers at each step the
097
      state of the agents, as presented in section 4 with the ElaadNL dataset (OpenDataset, 2019) (ii) by
098   the use of Monte Carlo type methods, which allow tackling MFC problems where the state space is
099   infinite-dimensional, as in the case study on water heaters presented in appendix E.

220                      Consider a large fleet of electric vehicles (EVs) arriving to a charging station at random times and
221                      with random state of charge, according to an initial law ν0 . There is a central planner whose goal is
222                      to maintain constraints for the aggregate power consumption, as well as constraints for each vehicle
223                      owner. The vehicles arrive during the period [9am, 10 : 30am], and must be fully charged by 5pm.
224                      The goal is power tracking: total power consumption should follow a reference signal (rt ) over a
225                      time period [t1 , t2 ], with 9am ≤ t1 < t2 ≤ 5pm. This can be formulated as an MCOT-C problem
226                      over the space of distributions on X = S × W with S = [0, T ] × [0, 1] and W = [0, T ].
227
                         The two first coordinates of x ∈ X are the time and the battery state of charge at the arrival and the
228
                         third is the time when the EV will start charging, called the plugging time; so x ∈ X is of the form

                         x = (ta , b, tc ). At each iteration, a gradient is calculated on X × W, with complexity of Nt3 × Nb ,

      5                            CONCLUSIONS
524
525
      One-sided moment relaxation of OT problem provides a very natural representation setting for
526   tracking applications in control. In such applications, the OT problem is often infinite-dimensional
527   (e.g. trajectories of agents). Instead of using approximations techniques for OT, MCOT-C leads to a
528   tractable algorithm by directly considering only the distribution moments that are relevant for control.
529   Furthermore, KL-term has a dual role in MCOT-C: a relaxation term as in many other machine
530   learning algorithms, but it also enables to enforce the constraints on the dynamics via the choice of
531   µ2 and absolute continuity imposed by KL. There are many directions for future research:
532
      • The "Semi Sinkhorn" algorithm might be improved through the introduction of advanced optimiza-
533
      tion techniques (e.g., proximal methods or momentum).
534
535   • Obtain probabilistic error bounds for the stochastic gradient descent algorithms proposed in
536   appendix E, which is useful in cases where the size of the problem makes the use of Monte Carlo
537   methods attractive such as the water heaters problem presented in appendix E.2.
538
539
      • We believe that representing distributions by their moments to perform optimal transport has
      broader applications in machine learning and control. We aim to explore its potential in other contexts.
