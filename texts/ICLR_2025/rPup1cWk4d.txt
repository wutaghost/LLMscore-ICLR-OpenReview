009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose a novel and interpretable data augmentation method based on energy-
012            based modeling and principles from information geometry. Unlike black-box gen-
013            erative models, which rely on deep neural networks, our approach replaces these
014            non-interpretable transformations with explicit, theoretically grounded ones, en-
015            suring interpretability and strong guarantees such as energy minimization. Central
016            to our method is the introduction of the backward projection algorithm, which
017            reverses dimension reduction to generate new data. Empirical results demon-
018
               strate that our method achieves competitive performance with black-box gener-
               ative models while offering greater transparency and interpretability.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      Data augmentation has advanced significantly in recent years, primarily due to the increasing use
024   of generative models to meet the growing demand for large datasets (Feng et al., 2021; Wong et al.,
025   2016). Despite their success, these generative models often rely on modern deep neural networks,
026   which are typically treated as black boxes, raising concerns about their interpretability (Guidotti
027   et al., 2018). For instance, the popular autoencoder model encodes original data into a compact
028   latent representation and then decodes it back, with both processes usually handled by black-box
029   neural networks (Kingma & Welling, 2022). Consequently, even when these models perform well,
030   the lack of understanding of the underlying transformations makes it difficult to control the generated
031   outputs, forcing researchers to depend heavily on empirical heuristics.
032   A natural approach to developing a more interpretable data augmentation method is to replace black-
033   box transformations with more explicit ones (Rudin, 2019). In this work, we take inspiration from
034   the autoencoder model, which consists of encoder and decoder. Encoder, when viewed as a form
035   of dimension reduction (Wang et al., 2016), contributes to the model’s success by acting as a form
036   of regularization and potentially avoiding sparsity through encoding data into a low-dimensional la-
037   tent representation space. Indeed, various data augmentation methods adopt this philosophy, where
038
      compact representations are first learned via neural networks, incorporating dimension reduction
      as a key component of the pipeline (Maharana et al., 2022). However, while dimension reduction
039
      is a well-established field in data science, two main obstacles prevent its direct application to data
040
      augmentation. First, classical methods like Principal Component Analysis (Wold et al., 1987) and
041   Singular Value Decomposition (Stewart, 1993) inherently rely on linear projection in the ambient
042   space (e.g., Euclidean), making the straightforward application of these methods unsuitable for cer-
043   tain modality such as images. Second, the decoder—which aims to reverse the dimension reduction
044   to generate new data—is highly non-trivial to design even for these classical linear methods. This is
045   one of the main reasons why modern generative models rely on black-box transformations.
046
      We address both issues by proposing a new framework and a data-centric algorithm. The frame-
047   work introduces non-linearity through the well-known energy-based model (Xie et al., 2016), and is
048   built upon recent developments in the log-linear model on partially ordered sets (posets) (Sugiyama
049   et al., 2016; 2017) and information geometry (Amari, 2016; Amari & Nagaoka, 2000; Ay et al.,
050   2017): the log-linear model on posets embeds structured data (e.g., tensors) as discrete probability
051   distributions via an explicit mapping φ into a statistical manifold S, and subsequently, provides intri-
052   cate geometric structure of the data that enables efficient dimension reduction method via projection
053   in S. Building on this, the proposed algorithm, termed backward projection, aims to reverse this
054   forward projection process to generate new data via projection again. The core idea of backward
      projection is simple and general: given a new point in the low-dimensional latent representation

      space, we identify its k-nearest latent representations of the original data (obtained via forward pro-
056   jection) and use them to create a target subspace to project backward onto. A key insight of the

058   linearity arises from the divergence minimizing property when projecting onto flat low-dimensional

062   By combining backward projection with the log-linear model on posets, our approach benefits from
063   explicit, energy-based transformations: these non-linear projections are interpretable, fully white-
064   box, and energy-minimizing, while the framework offers the potential to capture intricate informa-
065   tion beyond the ambient space structure. Our contributions are summarized as follows:

081   2.1   DATA AUGMENTATION
082
083   In the Era of Deep Generative Models. Data augmentation has proven to be highly effective in
084   enhancing deep learning training by increasing dataset size, improving model robustness (Rebuffi
085
      et al., 2021), and introducing implicit regularization (Hernández-Garcı́a & König, 2018). These
      techniques have been applied across various modalities, including text (Shorten et al., 2021; Feng
086
      et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022a) and images (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019; Mumuni & Mumuni,
087
      2022; Wang et al., 2017). Much of the recent progress in data augmentation has been driven by
088   advancements in black-box generative models, such as autoencoders (Kingma & Welling, 2022;
089   Chadebec et al., 2022) and generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Antoniou, 2017).
090
091
      Interpretability. Although there are data augmentation methods that do not rely on generative
      models (Maharana et al., 2022), these often depend on the knowledge of the underlying data gen-
092
      eration mechanisms, which are typically unknown for complex datasets. As a result, creating inter-
093
      pretable augmented data involves interpreting black-box generative models, an area that remains an
094   active research focus. To date, there is no fully satisfactory solution to this challenge. For example,
095   the design of interpretable GANs is still evolving (Li et al., 2022b; She et al., 2021) and remains
096   largely limited to specific domains, such as image generation.
097
098   2.2   D IMENSION REDUCTION AND RELATION TO DATA AUGMENTATION
099
100   Linear Methods. Classical linear dimension reduction techniques, such as Principal Component
101   Analysis (PCA) (Wold et al., 1987) and Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) (Stewart, 1993), work
102   by identifying the optimal linear subspace that minimizes reconstruction error, typically through the
103   orthogonal projection of data onto this subspace. These methods are not only straightforward and
104
      explicit, but they also provide valuable geometric insights. For instance, PCA highlights the princi-
      pal directions that capture the most variance in the data, uncovering important structural patterns.
105
106   However, one of the challenges in applying linear dimension reduction methods to data augmen-
107   tation is the inverse problem, where reconstructing the original data from the space of reduced di-
108   mension is highly non-trivial. While some studies have explored indirect approaches to using linear
109   dimension reduction for data augmentation (Abayomi-Alli et al., 2020; Sirakov et al., 2024), they
      are often application-specific and hard to generalize, limiting their broader applicability.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


110
      Non-Linear Methods. The non-linear generalizations, often called manifold learning (Meilă
111   & Zhang, 2024), offer an alternative approach to dimension reduction. Popular methods like t-
112   SNE (Hinton & Roweis, 2002; Van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008), Isomap (Tenenbaum et al., 2000),
113   and UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) are based on the manifold hypothesis, which suggests that high-
114   dimensional data lie on a lower-dimensional manifold within the ambient space. The goal is to
115   uncover this manifold and develop a smooth embedding that captures the data’s intrinsic low-
116   dimensional structure. While classical manifold learning methods do not rely on black-box neural
117   networks, they are computationally complex, prone to overfitting, and require careful hyperparame-
118   ter tuning, making interpretation challenging (Han et al., 2022).
119   In theory, manifold learning avoids the inverse problem by aiming to recover the underlying low-
120   dimension manifold of the data with near-zero information loss, making it conceptually appealing
121   for data augmentation. However, this is rarely achieved in practice, hence solving the inverse prob-
122   lem is still necessary to generate realistic augmented data. Additionally, classical manifold learning
123   methods that do not rely on black-box neural networks are often limited to providing fixed embed-
124   dings for training data and cannot perform out-of-sample extensions (Duque et al., 2020), further
125
      limiting their ability to augment data. Recent approaches to address this limitation involve more
      complex algorithms (Coifman & Lafon, 2006; Williams & Seeger, 2000; Vladymyrov & Carreira-
126
      Perpinán, 2013) or the introduction of black-box generative models (Duque et al., 2020), which
127
      reintroduces the concern about interpretability.

132   3.1       D UALLY- FLATNESS IN INFORMATION GEOMETRY
133
134   Information geometry studies the structure of statistical manifolds S within the space of probabil-
135   ity distributions. In this paper, we are primarily concerned with the space of exponential families
136   {pθ (x) | θ ∈ RD }, where each pθ denotes a probability density function parameterized by θ. We
137   focus on the key concept in this field, dually-flatness, in this preliminary, while directing readers to
138
      Amari (2016) for more comprehensive details.1
139   The starting point is the observation that the log-partition function ψ(θ) (also known as the cumulant
140   generating function in statistics and free energy in physics) of an exponential family with density pθ
141   is convex in the natural parameter θ ∈ RD . This convexity induces a natural coordinate system,
142   θ, on S, defining both the Riemannian metric g = ∇2 ψ(θ) and the Bregman divergence (Bregman,
143   1967) Dψ (pθ , pθ′ ). With these structures, the manifold (S, g) is flat, meaning that any curve θ(t) =
144   at + b (where a, b ∈ RD are constants) is a geodesic and lies entirely within S. This flatness is
145
      known as e-flatness, and the geodesics are referred to as e-geodesics or primal-geodesics.
146   The dual structure arises from the Legendre transform (Legendre, 1787), which generates the dual
147   function ψ ∗ (η), where η ∈ RD is the expectation parameter. This dual function is also convex,
148   giving rise to the expectation coordinate system η, the dual Riemannian metric g ∗ , and also the
149   dual Bregman divergence Dψ∗ which is the well-known Kullback-Leibler divergence DKL . The
150   corresponding flatness is termed m-flatness, with m-geodesics or dual-geodesics as its geodesics.
151   Dually-flatness then emerges from the interplay between these two structures. Specifically, for any
152   point p in S, there is a unique point p∗ on an e-flat sub-manifold B ⊆ S that minimizes the dual
153   Bregman divergence Dψ∗ (p, q) = DKL (p, q) (Amari, 2016, Theorem 1.5). This process, known as
154   the m-projection, can be efficiently solved via convex optimization. The dual holds when switch-
155   ing e and m in this context. Projection is a central tool in information geometry with profound
156   implications for understanding the geometry of S, which we will utilize later.

230   4.2   F ORWARD AND BACKWARD PROJECTION
231
232   We now demonstrate how to incorporate projection theory to achieve data augmentation. As our
233
      algorithm is inspired from the architecture of autoencoders, we focus on two of the central building
      blocks: the encoder Enc(·) and the decoder Dec(·). First, for the encoding step, we formally explain
234
      how projection theory can be applied to perform dimension reduction within our framework. Next,
235
      for the decoding step, we introduce our proposed algorithm, termed backward projection, which
236   serves as the inverse of dimension reduction. While our explanation is tailored to our proposed
237   framework, i.e., the log-linear model on posets, the proposed backward projection algorithm itself
238   is general and may be of independent interest as well.
239
240   Dimension Reduction: Forward Projection. Given the log-linear model on posets framework
241   in Section 4.1, the embedding from ΩR to the statistical manifold S does not achieve dimension
242   reduction as dim(S) ≈ dim(ΩR ). To achieve dimension reduction, we leverage the projection
243
      theory: by projecting zi′ = φ(zi ) onto a low-dimensional flat base sub-manifold B ⊆ S with
      dim(B) ≪ dim(S), we obtain the desired encoding Enc := ProjB ◦φ : ΩR → B that maps the
244
      data to a low-dimensional latent representation manifold. The encoding Enc(·) is smooth and well-
245
      defined as the projection is unique when B is flat and minimizing either the primal or the dual Breg-
246   man divergence, depending on B. These theoretical guarantees provide rigor and support reasoning
247   through geometric intuition, which in turn offers interpretability.
248
249   Inverse Dimension Reduction: Backward Projection. As we have hinted at, one of the technical
250   burdens is that the encoding Enc(·) is not invertible, hence no natural decoding Dec(·) is available,
251   even when Enc(·) only involves traditional linear dimension reduction algorithm. While finding the
252
      exact inverse is mathematically impossible as the pre-image of the projection is not unique in any
      sense (even in Euclidean space), here, we propose a simple, geometrically intuitive, and data-centric
253
      solution that aims to find the inverse of the projection that is similar to the original data.
254
255   The high-level intuition is simple: if the result of the projection is close, then so is the original data,
256   i.e., its inverse. Hence, given a point in the low-dimensional latent representation space, we try to
257   “project it back” to approximate the original dataset by exploiting the fact that we have access to
258
      the inverse of the dataset’s projection, i.e., the dataset itself. Specifically, we can artificially create a
      local sub-manifold around a subset of the dataset, determined by the nearest neighbors of that given
259
      point in the latent representation space, and backward project onto it.
260
261   Formally, assuming that we have access to the embedded dataset {zi′ = φ(zi )}ni=1 and their
262   projected result {wi = ProjB (zi′ )}ni=1 for some base sub-manifold B. To find the inverse of
263   some given point w∗ ∈ B assuming it comes from the projection on B, we first find w∗ ’s k-
264
      nearest neighbors among wi ’s, obtaining a size k index set N . Then we create a local data sub-
      manifold D based on the pre-images zi′ ’s of these wi ’s, and project w∗ on D to obtain the inverse

355   4.4      C ONSTRUCTION OF SUB - MANIFOLDS
356
357   For any sub-manifold S ′ ⊆ S, as dim(S ′ ) increases, more information of the data is preserved after
358   forward projection onto S ′ . In the case of constructing the base sub-manifold B, the quality of the
359   backward projection Proj−1
                               B (·) (Algorithm 4.1) should increase along with dim(B) for the same rea-
360   son. However, in the extreme case where dim(B) ≈ dim(S), Algorithm 4.2 becomes less effective
361
      due to the sparsity of the data, resulting in an intrinsic trade-off for choosing dim(B) (see Ap-
      pendix A.3 for an empirical justification). In this section, we argue that by leveraging existing tools
362
      and understandings of the log-linear model on posets, such an intrinsic trade-off for constructing
363
      sub-manifolds (either B or D) provides an additional layer of interpretability and control compared
364   to black-box generative models like autoencoders.
365
366   To keep our presentation concise and concrete, we focus on positive tensors, although the argument
367
      and the high-level idea extend to more general cases. Firstly, the projection theory is well-explored
      for positive tensors within the log-linear model, where several established constructions for flat base
368
      sub-manifolds B ⊆ S (Sugiyama et al., 2018; Ghalamkari et al., 2024) have proven powerful in
369
      capturing the non-trivial structure of positive tensors after the projection. One of which is called
370   the many-body tensor approximation (Ghalamkari et al., 2024), which captures a hierarchy of mode
371   interactions with different dim(B). Specifically, the ℓ-body approximation considers projection on
372   the following sub-manifold
373
374                    Mℓ := {θ ∈ Rdim(S) | (θ)x = 0 for all non ℓ-body parameters x ∈ Ω},                          (1)
375
      where the ℓ-body parameter corresponds to ℓ non-one indices, acting as a generalization of one-
376
      body and two-body parameters (Ghalamkari & Sugiyama, 2022). Intuitively speaking, an ℓ-body
377   parameter captures the interaction among ℓ different modes, hence, when B = Mℓ , all interactions
378   between modes of orders higher than ℓ are neglected. This offers a practical design choice for
379   employing Algorithm 4.2. In particular, it allows us to leverage prior knowledge of the data to design
380   an appropriate base sub-manifold B and also the local data sub-manifold D that captures different
381   degrees of information with appropriate dimension. This approach provides a more principled way
382   of defining the latent space, compared to black-box models like autoencoders, where the latent space
383   dimensions are oftentimes tuned without a clear understanding of what those dimensions represent.

523   6   D ISCUSSION
524
525   Structural Limitation. While the log-linear model is flexible to represent structural data, it still
526   faces limitations. The key issue lies in the model’s reliance on a partial order of the index set, which
527   makes it impossible to ensure invariance under the permutation of indexes. For instance, modeling
528   graphical data usually requires non-invariance and non-equivariance of vertices (i.e., indexes), in
529
      this case, the log-linear model might not be the best model due to its structural limitations.
530   Meta-Perspective. Classical information geometry typically involves learning a single distribu-
531   tion by manipulating a single point in the statistical manifold S, as seen in tasks like learning the
532   Boltzmann machine or finding the maximum likelihood estimation (Amari, 2016). In our case, how-
533   ever, we treat data as probability distributions within S, offering a new perspective for applying the
534   information geometry framework. With multiple distributions in S, a natural extension would be to
535
      employ data-centric machine learning algorithms to learn the “data” distribution, i.e., the distribution
      of these distributions, thereby providing a meta-perspective.
536
537
538   7   C ONCLUSION
539
540   In this paper, we proposed a novel data augmentation method that leverages several information ge-
541   ometric algorithms, incorporating interpretability while maintaining competitive performance. Our
542   framework, built on the log-linear model on posets, equips data with information geometric struc-
543   tures, facilitating geometric reasoning and algorithm design. The proposed backward projection
544
      algorithm reverses the dimension reduction process in a geometrically intuitive and data-centric
      manner, which may be of independent interest.
545
546   We empirically demonstrated that our method achieves competitive performance compared to tra-
547   ditional autoencoder-based approaches on downstream tasks, even though the latter may possess
548   greater representational power but lack interpretability, which is a crucial requirement in many prac-
549   tical applications. Overall, our work paves the way for further exploration of information geometric
      algorithms in various domains, not limited to data augmentation.
