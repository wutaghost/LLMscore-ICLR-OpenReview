009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                A real-world object is expressed by composing distinctive characteristics that
012                distinguish it from others and some common properties shared with different
013                objects. Recent advances in generative modeling focus on identifying the shared
014                concepts within images of individual identities. However, it remains unclear
015                how to identify shared concepts beyond multiple identities while preserving the
016                unique concepts inherent to each. In this work, we address this new problem of
017                simultaneously discovering similarities and differences between two sets of images
018
                   and propose a two-stage framework coined DISCOD (DIstinct and Shared COncept
                   Discovery). In the first stage of DISCOD, we introduce information-regularized
019
                   textual inversion, focusing on separating representative concepts distinctive from
020
                   others while capturing the shared concepts among different objects. In the next
021                stage, we further optimize them to align composited concepts of those with the
022                corresponding objects, respectively. We demonstrate the effectiveness of DISCOD
023                by showing that DISCOD discovers the concepts better than baselines, as measured
024                by CLIPScore and success rate. The human study also validates the reasonable
025                discovery capability of DISCOD. Furthermore, we show the practical applicability
026                of our approach by applying to various applications: image editing, few-shot
027                personalization of diffusion models, and group bias mitigation in recognition.

030   1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Concepts (Smith & Medin, 1981) are essential notions that define and describe an object and range
033   from concrete notions of attributes like color and shape to abstract ones like functionality. Given a set
034   of visual objects, the objects are represented by a combination of common concepts shared across
035   them and distinct concepts for each individual object or each subset of the objects. Recognizing these
036   shared and distinct visual concepts across objects is beneficial in various fields, including taxonomy
037   definition, which improves our understanding of categories (Zhao et al., 2024), the creation of novel
038   objects,1 and the efficient learning of new concepts (Lake et al., 2015).
039   Despite the fundamental benefits of recognizing concepts, discovering visual concepts from images
040   remains challenging due to factors such as complex object composition in real-world scenes and
041   entanglement with various other concepts (Huang et al., 2023a). Recent works (Gal et al., 2023;
042   Vinker et al., 2023; Chefer et al., 2024; Avrahami et al., 2023) have proposed concept discovery
043   methods from the set of input images, where the images contain object instances sharing at least
044   one concept. These methods optimize a textual concept embedding that encodes a common concept
045   or multiple embeddings by decomposing shared sub-concepts recognizable by Vision Language
046
      Models (VLMs),2 such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or Diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022).
      Although these approaches have proven useful for many applications of image synthesis (Gal et al.,
047
      2023; Safaee et al., 2024; Ruiz et al., 2023; Sohn et al., 2023; Avrahami et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
048
      2023b; Kumari et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a), such as personalization, editing, and compositing, and
049   understanding sub-concepts of a given object in a human interpretable form (Gal et al., 2023; Chefer
050   et al., 2024), these approaches primarily focus on extracting commonalities and are not capable of
051   identifying differences.

069
070   In this work, we focus on a new discovery problem identifying both shared and distinct concepts
071
      within and between two groups of objects. To recognize the commonalities and differences, we
      need to analyze objects into disentangled concepts and then compare these objects comprehensively
072
      according to the concepts. We formulate such a comparative procedure into a novel information-
073
      regularized textual inversion. We observe that, due to the ambiguity of the comparative formulation,
074   optimizing a continuous concept vector does not converge to desirable solutions. To find a human
075   interpretable and meaningful concept, we parameterize the concept by discrete embeddings with
076   existing language tokens as prior. This reduces the risk of discovered concepts being optimized to
077   adversarial concepts 3 and makes them reside in a meaningful embedding space. Additionally, since
078   the discrete parameterization restricts the expressivity, we propose the second refinement stage, where
079   we learn the residual continuous concept vectors to better fit the discovered concepts by leveraging
080   the discovered ones. To better shape the residual concept, we generate the synthetic images from
081   each concept and optimize the residual concepts in the continuous embedding space. It helps the
082
      discovered concepts further align with given objects. This DISCOD (DIstinct and Shared COncept
      Discovery) procedure is illustrated in Fig. 1.
083
084   We validate our method of discovering commonalities and differences given respective image sets of
085   two objects and three tasks. In our systematic experiment, we demonstrate that DISCOD effectively
086   discovers the shared and distinct concepts. We show that DISCOD outperforms baselines in discovering
087   these concepts with respect to CLIPScore and discovery success rate. We also compute the alignment
088
      and agreement scores by human study, where the subjects agree that the discovered concepts represent
      the percieved concepts well. Additionally, we demonstrate the applicability of DISCOD in three
089
      applications: image editing, fine-tuning for personalization, and group bias mitigation. Our method
090
      can be integrated with the existing text-conditioned image editing. During fine-tuning diffusion
091   models given a few images, DISCOD reduces the undesirable entanglement. In a recognition scenario,
092   where the classes are correlated to their attributes, inducing short-cut learning, our method can find
093   the group bias and enables us to mitigate bias. The effectiveness of our bias mitigation is validated on
094   Waterbirds (Sagawa et al., 2020) and CelebA (Liu et al., 2015).

098
099   In this section, we first provide a background of the prior methods that discover concepts representing
100   a single object. Then, we introduce our goal to simultaneously discover shared and distinct concepts
101   given two objects and propose our method, DISCOD, to discover these concepts effectively.
102
      Background: Inversion-based concept discovery. Prior concept discovery methods with vision
103
      language models (VLMs) (Rombach et al., 2022; Radford et al., 2021) aim to identify or optimize
104   a concept given a few images containing a single object by applying model inversion techniques
105   to discover its textual concept embedding; we call it as inversion-based concept discovery. Textual
106   inversion (TI) (Gal et al., 2023) is a seminal work in this context; they optimize a few newly initialized
107

      tokens that correspond to the new concept while the original pre-trained tokens remain the same.
109   Once a concept representing the object is discovered, its textual token can be used for image synthesis
110   and style transfer. Some works (Safaee et al., 2024; Ruiz et al., 2023; Sohn et al., 2023; Avrahami
111   et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023b; Kumari et al., 2023) have further developed techniques for image
112   synthesis, including personalization and text-guided synthesis. Another important research direction
113   is to decompose a given object into sub-concepts (Vinker et al., 2023; Chefer et al., 2024). It is useful
114   to understand how VLMs recognize the object and can improve our lack of understanding of VLMs.
115   This direction provides a way to gain insights into VLMs’ representation mechanisms.
116   While it is useful to extract the shared concept given an object, it is even more crucial to discover both
117   the shared and distinct concepts; this discovery can further provide a richer understanding of how
118   VLMs recognize concepts between objects. Since the previous methods optimize for a single object,
119   their methods cannot be directly applied or extended to discover the shared and distinct concepts.
120   Their discovered textual token can be adversarial; certain textual tokens can encode a large coverage
121   of information, making other textual token concepts meaningless. In this work, we tackle this problem
122   by introducing information-regularized textual inversion.

151   In the first stage, we aim to discover the distinct concepts of yA\B and yB\A while maximizing the

160   language, this implicitly acts as a prior.

185
186   Although we find meaningful concepts from the first stage, it is not sufficient to align the concepts
187   with the given two objects, A and B, due to the limited expressivity of the discrete representation. To
188

      the learned discrete tokens in the first stage using text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models. We denote
189
190

      4    R ELATED W ORK
506
507
      Inversion-based concept discovery. With VLMs (Radford et al., 2021; Desai et al., 2023; Girdhar
508   et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2021; Kang et al.,
509   2023; Chen et al., 2024a), the concept discovery aims to identify the concept corresponding with a
510   few images containing a single object. Textual inversion (TI) (Gal et al., 2023) optimizes the textual
511   embedding to the given object using T2I diffusion models. Subsequently, recent works (Vinker et al.,
512   2023; Chefer et al., 2024) have proposed methods for decomposing a single object into sub-concepts.
513   Vinker et al. (2023) have introduced the tree-structure construction of token embedding, uncovering
514   the hidden sub-concepts of the single object. Chefer et al. (2024) also have decomposed a single
515   concept into sub-concepts to understand the internal representation of VLMs. In this work, we focus
516
      on discovering both the commonalities and differences between two image sets, in contrast to prior
      research, which focuses on discovering commonalities in an image set. We develop information-
517
      regularized methods tailored to our objective.
518
519   Explainable machine learning. Explainable machine learning aims to provide terms understandable
520   to humans about machine decision (Doshi-Velez & Kim, 2017). Prior works have proposed algorithms
521   to measure the score that affects the decision of models and provide saliency map (Kim et al., 2021;
522   Choe et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Selvaraju et al., 2017; Li et al., 2016; Arras et al., 2017).
523   Additionally, neural networks accumulate their knowledge into neurons referred to as knowledge
524   neuron (Dai et al., 2022). Some works (Liu et al., 2023b; Dai et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024b)
525
      have explored which neurons influence model decisions. Concept-based models (Koh et al., 2020;
      Yuksekgonul et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2018) are composed of the concept bottleneck layer, where each
526
      layer represents human interpretable concepts. In this work, DISCOD identifies the shared and distinct
527
      concepts recognized by VLMs and visualizes the concepts in the human interpretable medium.
528
529
530   5    C ONCLUSION
531
      We take a closer look at how VLMs recognize the commonalities and differences between two objects.
532
      Unlike the previous works that focus on single objects, DISCOD discovers shared and distinct concepts
533
      simultaneously. We formulate the task to maximize the information of sub-concepts for the given
534   objects and propose a two-stage framework. We validate DISCOD on pairs of real and synthetic
535   settings, observing that DISCOD identifies various concepts like color, category, and abstract concepts.
536   Both CLIPScore evaluations and human studies demonstrate the effectiveness of DISCOD. In addition,
537   we also validate the effectiveness of DISCOD on three tasks: image editing, fine-tuning diffusion
538   models on the DreamBooth dataset, and group-bias mitigation on WaterBirds and CelebA. We believe
539   that understanding how machines distinguish between objects will lead to a better understanding of
      machines, which in turn leads to better performance.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      DISCOD discovers the shared and distinct concepts between two sets of images and provides them
543   with a form that humans can understand. Thus, we can use it to investigate the two objects to help our
544   understanding of these objects. However, if we rely heavily on the discovered ones, it leads humans
545   to misunderstand these objects because the discovered ones are related to how VLMs perceive them;
546   it can have a negative societal impact. Thus, we should use it as a tool to help us. In terms of the
547   positive societal impact, we apply DISCOD to reduce the bias; bias mitigation is a crucial problem in
548   deploying AI, preventing social issues, and improving fairness.

      A.1    F IRST STAGE
759
760   The mutual information satisfies the following equation, I (x | y) = H (x) − H (x | y) where
761   H is the entropy. Since we optimize y, H (x | y) is related to the optimization goal. Thus, we
762   can maximize the mutual information by minimizing the conditional entropy, H (x | y). The
763   minimum of entropy is 0, and we can achieve it by making the probability 1. We maximize
764
      the conditional probability. The probability is proportional to the cosine similarity, p (x | y) ∼
                                           CLIPI (x)·CLIPT (y)
765   CosSim (CLIPI (x) , CLIPT (y)) = |CLIP   I (x)||CLIPT (y)|
                                                                 in CLIP models where CLIPI , CLIPT are
766   image and text encoders. The one of objective term L̂s , the approximation of Ls , is the following:
767
             L̂s (A, B, yA\B , yB\A , yA∩B ) = − I A | yA\B , yA∩B + I B | yB\A , yA∩B                  (6)
768
769           ≈ 1 − CosSim CLIPI (A) , CLIPT yA∩B ; yA\B                 +

770            1 − CosSim CLIPI (B) , CLIPT yA∩B ; yB\A               .                                 (7)
771
772   Further, we can express the mutual information with Kullback-Leibler divergence:
773                                   I (x | y) = E [DKL (p (y|x) || p (y))] ,                             (8)
774   where DKL is Kullback-Leibler divergence. We do not know p(y), but we want to infuse the
775   information bottleneck into p (y|x) not to contain unnecessary information; we set p(y) as uniform
776   distribution. As mentioned in the main paper, we compute p (y|x) by computing cosine similarity
777   between the pre-trained embedding of tokens and applying the softmax operation. The another term
778   L̂m is the following:

779      L̂m (E, yA\B , yB\A ) = E DKL p yA\B |A || U + E DKL p yB\A |B || U                               (9)
780
                                = CE P CosSim E, yA\B , U + CE P CosSim E, yB\A , U ,
781
                                                                                                          (10)
782
783   where P (·) denotes the softmax function, U is the uniform distribution, and E is the pre-trained token
784   embeddings. The final objective contains the projection on discrete embedding. Since the pre-trained
785
      text tokens embed vast prior knowledge of human interpretable language, this implicitly acts as a
      prior. Our objective is as follows:
786
787                      min           L̂s (A, B, yA\B , yB\A , yA∩B ) + λm L̂m (E, yA\B , yB\A )        (11)
                 yA\B ,yB\A ,yA∩B ∈E
788
789
      Since we optimize the above objective on discrete embedding, we adopt PEZ method (Wen et al.,
      2024).

      A.2    S ECOND STAGE
792
793   We use the diffusion model in the second stage. The diffusion model formulates the Gaussian
794   distribution with time schedule as p(x) ∼ N (αt x, σ 2t ). Since the Kullback-Leibler divergence
795   between two Gaussian distributions has the closed form solution, the maximization of mutual
796
      information is naturally described as the minimization of L2 loss, Ld (A, y) = ||ϵ − ϵθ (x, t, y)||22
      where x ∈ A. Thus, the proposed objective is the following:
797
798                  L̂s =Ld (A, [yA∩B ; yA\B ]) + Ld (B, [yA∩B ; yB\A ])
799                         + Ld (A \ B, yA\B ) + Ld (B \ A, yB\A ) + Ld (B ∩ A, yA∩B )                  (12)
800
801   where A and B are the given objects, and A \ B, B \ A, and B ∩ A are the synthetic images from
802
      the first stage. Unlike the first stage, we discard the information-regularized term because we use
      synthetic images. Our first stage and second stage maximize the information of two objects.
