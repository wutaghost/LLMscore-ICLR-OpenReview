009                                                 Abstract
010
011              Gradient-based Discrete Samplers (GDSs) are effective for sampling discrete en-
012              ergy landscapes. However, they often stagnate in complex, non-convex settings.
013              To improve exploration, we introduce the Discrete Replica EXchangE Langevin
014              (DREXEL) sampler and its variant with Adjusted Metropolis (DREAM). These
015              samplers use two GDSs (Zhang et al., 2022b) at different temperatures and step
016
                 sizes: one focuses on local exploitation, while the other explores broader energy
                 landscapes. When energy differences are significant, sample swaps occur, which
017
                 are determined by a mechanism tailored for discrete sampling to ensure detailed
018
                 balance. Theoretically, we prove both DREXEL and DREAM converge asymptoti-
019              cally to the target energy and exhibit faster mixing than a single GDS. Experiments
020              further confirm their efficiency in exploring non-convex discrete energy landscapes.

023   1     Introduction
024
025   Sampling from high-dimensional discrete distributions has been an important task for decades across
026   applications in texts (Mikolov et al., 2013; Devlin et al., 2019), images (Krizhevsky et al., 2012;
027
      Ronneberger et al., 2015), signal processing (Mallat, 1989; Donoho, 2006), genome sequences
      (Metzker, 2010; Macosko et al., 2015), etc. However, the exponential growth in the number of
028
      configurations makes sampling from π(θ) ∝ exp [U(θ)] computationally prohibitive. The computa-
029
      tional burden comes from evaluating the exact probabilities and normalizing constants, which makes
030   exact sampling impossible in practice. Algorithms such as rejection sampling (Neumann, 1951),
031   Swendsen-Wang (Swendsen & Wang, 1987), and Hamze-Freitas (Hamze & de Freitas, 2004) leverage
032   special structures within the problem to make global updates. In more general settings, these methods
033   may suffer from slow exploration, local dependencies, and poor convergence.

070   2   Related Work
071
072   Gradient-based Discrete Sampling becomes popular for complex discrete sampling tasks and its
073   original idea comes from LBPs. The concept of LBPs, as introduced by Zanella (2020), utilized local
074   information in the form of density ratios to improve the sample efficiency. Grathwohl et al. (2021)
075   expanded LBPs by the use of first-order Taylor approximation, which further ensures computational
076   feasibility. To improve sampling in high-dimensional discrete spaces, LBPs were extended to cover
077   larger neighborhoods by performing a sequence of small moves (Sun et al., 2021). Zhang et al.
078   (2022b) further developed GDSs, which adapt the continuous Langevin MCMC to discrete spaces
079   and allow parallel updates of all coordinates based on gradient information. Subsequently, GDSs
080
      were improved through the introduction of an adaptive mechanism by which the step size can be
      automatically adjusted (Sun et al., 2023a). Most recently, Pynadath et al. (2024) introduced an
081
      automatic cyclical scheduling approach in step sizes to better handle multi-modal distributions by
082
      alternating between exploration and exploitation phases. While these methods have shown promise,
083   sampling from highly non-convex discrete distributions remains challenging, particularly when
084   dealing with strongly correlated variables or energy-based deep learning models.
085
086
      Replica Exchange MCMC is a powerful method that enhances exploration in complex, multi-modal
      distributions, and a variety of related algorithms build on this. For instance, unadjusted Langevin
087
      MCMC (Durmus & Moulines, 2017) leverages gradient information to guide proposals but lacks
088
      the exchange mechanism. Importance sampling (Wang & Landau, 2001) adjusts for the discrepancy
089   between target and proposal distributions, which offers flexibility in sampling but without temperature-
090   based exchanges. Simulated tempering (Lee et al., 2018) further refined the temperature-scaling
091   strategy by dynamically adjusting the temperature of a single chain. Recently, Zhang et al. (2020)
092   proposed a cyclical step-size scheduler to maintain a balance between exploration and exploitation.
093   To enhance the exploration, reMCMC runs multiple chains at different temperatures and allows for
094   chain swaps between them. Dong & Tong (2022) analyzed its mixing by quantifying the spectral
095   gap, and Deng et al. (2020; 2022) validated its efficiency in large-scale deep learning tasks. Despite
096   its success in continuous sampling and high-dimensional settings, to the best of our knowledge, its
097
      potential has not been studied in sampling from discrete distributions.

296   5     Theoretical Analysis
297
298
      In the previous section, we introduced DREXEL and DREAM, which use factorization to allow
299
      parallel updates and employ swap mechanisms to improve non-convex exploration. While these
300
      features are beneficial, the overall performance heavily relies on their convergence properties and
301   theoretical guarantees. In this section, we provide asymptotic convergence guarantees for DREXEL
302   (i.e. the version without the MH correction).

305
      5.1     Asymptotic Convergence on Log-Quadratic Distributions
306
      Our focus is first on the asymptotic behaviors of DREXEL. The analysis aims to show that as step
307
      sizes approach zero, the algorithm exhibits zero asymptotic bias, which ensures accurate sampling
308   from the target distribution. Specifically, we focus on log-quadratic energy π(θ) ∝ exp (θ⊺ Jθ + b⊺ θ),
309   where J ∈ Rd×d is a symmetric matrix, and b ∈ Rd is a vector. If J is asymmetric, we apply spectral
310   decomposition to obtain a symmetric matrix, which enables an analytically tractable solution.
311
      Zhang et al. (2022b) showed that DLS with temperature 1 is reversible for log-quadratic energy
312
      distributions when the step size is sufficiently small. However, this result does not directly extend to
313
      the proposed algorithm, as the swap mechanism (3) introduces potential imbalances. This imbalance
314   is due to discontinuous transitions between neighboring states in discrete space, which makes the
315   swap acceptance rule insufficient to maintain a detailed balance. This further introduces bias during
316   the sampling process and leads to inaccurate modeling. To address this, we carefully control the swap
317   probability in (10) to regulate transitions between high- and low-temperature samplers.
318
319    Theorem 1. Let α1 and α2 be the step sizes for the low- and high-temperature samplers, and let
320    q(·|θ) be the Markov chain transition kernel. Suppose the target π(θ) is log-quadratic, then:
321       • The Markov chain induced by DREXEL is reversible with respect to an intermediate distribution
322         π̃, i.e., for all θ, θ′ ∈ Θ, π̃(θ)q(θ′ |θ) = π̃(θ′ )q(θ|θ′ ).
323       • As α1 , α2 → 0, the stationary distribution π′ converges weakly to the target distribution π.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      This analysis focuses on the state transition of the low-temperature sampler, as the high-temperature
325   sampler only facilitates exploration and does not produce final samples. Intuitively, with probability
326   ρS̃ , the next low-temperature sample is drawn from the high-temperature sampler, and with probability
327   ρ(1 − S̃ ), it selects from the low-temperature sampler. The transition simplifies to DLS without swaps
328   and directly maintains the detailed balance, but the swap probability becomes essential for preserving
329   this balance once swaps are considered in discrete sampling. Our designed swap function ensures
330   that the overall transition dynamics remain balanced, as demonstrated in Appendix D.1.

440   of probabilistic models where the energy function is parameterized by a ResNet (He et al., 2016).
441   Specifically, the probability of a data point x is given by Pθ (x) = exp [Eθ (x)] /Zθ , where Eθ (x) is the
442   energy function parameterized by θ, and Zθ = Eθ∼Θ exp [Eθ (x)] normalizes the distribution.
443   With DULA and DMALA as baselines, we evaluate DREXEL and DREAM2 by learning Deep EBMs.
444   During training, the intractable likelihood gradient of the model is approximated through Persistent
445   Contrastive Divergence (Tieleman & Hinton, 2009), while a replay buffer (Du & Mordatch, 2019)
446   containing 1,000 past samples is implemented to improve both the efficiency and stability of the
447   training process. Each sampler runs for 40 steps per iteration. Upon completing training, Annealed
448   Importance Sampling (Neal, 2001) is conducted with DULA to estimate the test log-likelihoods.

      7     Conclusion and Discussion
469
470
      In this work, we addressed the challenge of balancing global exploration and local exploitation
471
      in non-convex discrete energy landscapes by proposing DREXEL and DREAM. These samplers
472   integrate DLS with replica exchange to overcome the limitations of traditional samplers, which tend
473   to get trapped in local modes due to reliance on local gradients and small disturbances.
474
475
      We theoretically prove that the proposed samplers are reversible, which guarantees the accurate
      preservation of the target distribution. Moreover, these samplers achieve faster mixing than the naïve
476
      DLS. The empirical evidence suggests that the proposed samplers and swap mechanism significantly
477
      improve exploration and mixing in non-convex discrete spaces. Furthermore, while DREXEL main-
478   tains detailed balance throughout the process, MH corrections are critical for optimizing performance
479   in certain tasks.
480
481
      Our current work focuses on designing a single low-temperature and a single high-temperature
      sampler. Future research could extend this framework by introducing multiple parallel samplers to
482
      enhance exploration. We will also study theoretical guarantees to quantify the acceleration effect of
483
      the swap mechanism in the future.

      A    Futher Discussion on Related Work
703
704   Modeling Non-Convex Discrete Distributions is important in modern machine learning tasks.
705   Swendsen & Wang (1987) designed cluster updates in Monte Carlo simulations to efficiently navigate
706   complex energy landscapes by updating groups of variables simultaneously. Wolff (1989) further
707   improved sampling efficiency by flipping single large clusters of spins, thereby reducing autocorre-
708   lation times near critical points in non-convex distributions. Marinari & Parisi (1992) considered
709   temperature as a dynamic variable and allowed the system to overcome energy barriers and explore
710   multiple modes of a non-convex distribution effectively. While these models might work for specific
711   discrete structures, they rely on random walk or Gibbs sampling. Their extensions to gradient-based
712
      discrete sampling may significantly improve their efficiency in non-convex exploration.
713   Related Discrete Methods have been made in modeling and optimizing discrete distributions. Zhang
714   et al. (2022a) introduced energy-based generative flow networks to amortize expensive MCMC
715   exploration into a fixed number of actions. Discrete Diffusion Models (Sun et al., 2023b; Lou et al.,
716   2024) extended continuous-time diffusion models to discrete spaces with well-defined score functions
717   for discrete variables. Wen et al. (2024) proposed an efficient gradient-based discrete optimization
718
      method for generative models. These approaches to discretizing continuous methods and handling
      complex discrete data offer valuable insights for developing DLSs.
719
720   Stochastic Gradient Langevin MCMC (Welling & Teh, 2011) has become a favored MCMC
721   method in big data due to its effective transition from optimization to sampling. However, its lack
722   of adaptive step sizes to the energy curvature limits the use of this crucial information. To further
723   leverage curvature information, Quasi-Newton methods (Ahn et al., 2012; Simsekli et al., 2016)
724
      exploit curvature information by adjusting step sizes, while Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (Neal, 2012;
      Campbell et al., 2021) and higher-order approaches (Chen et al., 2015; Li et al., 2019) employ larger
725
      step sizes to improve stability. These approaches, however, still encounter difficulties in avoiding
726
      local traps, which is where advanced reMCMC methods help balance exploration and exploitation
727   when navigating non-convex energy landscapes.
728
729
730   B    DREXEL and DREAM with Binary Variables
731
732
      When the variable domain Θ is binary {0, 1}d , Algorithm 1 can be further simplified. In this binary
      setting, the Hadamard product, denoted by ⊙, simplifies several operations. This streamlined version
733
      demonstrates that both DREXEL and DREAM can be efficiently parallelized across CPUs and GPUs,
734
      which leads to reduced computational cost.
735
736   Binary variables are particularly advantageous in this context. The binary domain facilitates the use
737   of efficient bitwise operations, which not only speed up the computation but also enable the algorithm
738
      to scale better in high-dimensional spaces. Moreover, the simplicity of the binary domain reduces
      algorithmic complexity, which can facilitate computational efficiency.

1060   We further examine the influence of bias correction terms in DREXEL and DREAM with bias
1061   corrections (bDREXEL and bDREAM) on the experimental results of Ising models. For comparison,
1062   we also evaluate DREXEL and DREAM, which incorporate historical energy corrections. Specifically,
1063   we modify the swap function (10) by directly adding bias corrections and adjusting σ2 in (9). Each
1064   experiment is repeated 20 times with different random seeds, and the average and standard deviation
1065   of log RMSE are reported. The results are shown in Figure 6.
1066   The results demonstrate that DREAM achieves the lowest log RMSE values when corrections are
1067   small, which indicates the minimal influence of bias correction on its performance. In contrast, the
1068   performance of bDREXEL and bDREAM becomes better with increasing σ2 , which suggests its
1069   performance may heavily rely on a good selection of bias correction. bDREAM and DREAM show
1070   comparable log RMSEs, with log RMSE across different bias correction magnitudes. However,
1071   DREAM yields the lowest log RMSE with no correction, while bDREAM has the lowest one when
1072   the correction increases.

1113   E.4   Learning Deep Energy-Based Models
1114
1115   We trained Deep EBMs using a ResNet-64 backbone and optimized the model with the Adam
1116   optimizer at a fixed learning rate of 0.001 without gradient clipping. The model was trained for
1117   50,000 iterations with a batch size of 256. we employed Persistent Contrastive Divergence (PCD) to
1118
       approximate the intractable likelihood gradient, which builds upon standard contrastive divergence
       by maintaining persistent Markov chains throughout training. It allows for more stable and accurate
1119
       sampling. Specifically, the model’s log-likelihood gradient is given by:
1120
1121                              ∇θ log Pθ (x) = EPdata [∇θ log Pθ (x)] − EPθ [∇θ log Pθ (x)],
1122   where the second term (the model expectation) is intractable. PCD approximates this by updating
1123   samples across training iterations via Gibbs sampling, which ensures that the Markov chain does not
1124   restart after each parameter update. Additionally, a replay buffer containing 1,000 past samples is
1125   used to further stabilize training. The buffer stores past model samples and reuses them to reduce
1126   variance, thus improving both the efficiency and stability of the learning process.
1127   To evaluate Deep EBMs, we applied Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) with DULA to estimate the
1128   test log-likelihoods. AIS is a technique used to estimate partition functions by smoothly interpolating
1129   between a known distribution and the target distribution. This is achieved by introducing a sequence
1130   of intermediate distributions:
                                                       1
1131                                          Pt (x) = exp(−βt E(x)),
1132                                                   Zt
1133   where t denotes the current annealing step, E(x) is the energy function, Zt is the partition function,
       and βt is a temperature that gradually transitions between 0 and 1 over the course of the annealing

       process. When βt = 0, the intermediate distribution is identical to the proposal distribution (which we
1135   can sample from easily). When βt = 1, the intermediate distribution becomes the target distribution,
1136   which is more complex and generally intractable to sample directly. To adjust βt , we typically choose
1137   a monotonic schedule that increases smoothly from 0 to 1 over the course of the AIS process. A
1138   common choice is a linear interpolation (βt = t/T, t = 0, 1, 2, . . . , T ) or an exponential schedule
1139   (βt = (t/T )2 ), where βt increases evenly across T annealing steps. AIS computes an estimate of the
1140   partition function by sampling from these intermediate distributions and adjusting the importance
