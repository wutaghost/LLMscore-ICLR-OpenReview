075   since ∇F (x) itself depends on y∗ (x) (seen in Eq. (1)).

078
      & Wang (2024), which falls short in two main aspects. First, their algorithm only provides some

079
      Hessians at each step, which significantly limits scalability; see Section 1.2 for further discussion.

      1.2   R ELATED WORK
122
123   Bilevel optimization was introduced by Bracken & McGill (1973), and grew into a vast body of
124   work, with classical results focusing on asymptotic guarantees for specific problem structures (Anan-
125   dalingam & White, 1990; Ishizuka & Aiyoshi, 1992; White & Anandalingam, 1993; Vicente et al.,
126   1994; Zhu, 1995; Ye & Zhu, 1997). There exist multiple surveys and books covering various ap-
127   proaches for these problems (Vicente & Calamai, 1994; Dempe, 2002; Colson et al., 2007; Bard,
128   2013; Sinha et al., 2017).
129   Ghadimi & Wang (2018) observed Eq. (1) under strong-convexity of the inner problem using the
130   implicit function theorem, therefore asserting that the hypergradient can be computed via inverse
131   Hessians, which requires solving a linear system at each point. Many follow up works built upon this
132   second-order approach with additional techniques such as variance reduction, momentum, Hessian
133   sketches, projection-free updates, or incorporating external constraints (Amini & Yousefian, 2019;
134   Yang et al., 2021; Khanduri et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2021; Ji et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Akhtar
135
      et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Tsaknakis et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Abolfazli
      et al., 2023; Merchav & Sabach, 2023; Xu & Zhu, 2023; Cao et al., 2024; Dagréou et al., 2024).
136
137   Only recently, the groundbreaking result of Liu et al. (2022) proved finite-time convergence guar-
138   antees for a fully first-order method which is based on a penalty approach. This result was soon
139   extended to stochastic objectives (Kwon et al., 2023), with the convergence rate later improved by
140   (Yang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024), and also extended to constrained bilevel problems (Yao et al.,
141
      2024; Kornowski et al., 2024). The first-order penalty paradigm also shows promise for some bilevel
      problems in which the inner problem is not strongly-convex (Shen & Chen, 2023; Kwon et al., 2024;
142
      Lu & Mei, 2024), which is generally a highly challenging setting (Chen et al., 2024; Bolte et al.,
143
      2024). Moreover, Pan et al. (2024) provided an efficient implementation of this paradigm, showing
144   its effectiveness for large scale applications.
145
146   As to DP optimization, there is an extensive literature on optimization problems, both for ERM and
147
      for stochastic losses, which are either convex (Chaudhuri et al., 2011; Kifer et al., 2012; Bassily
      et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2017; Bassily et al., 2019; Feldman et al., 2020; Gopi et al., 2022; Carmon
148
      et al., 2023) or smooth and nonconvex (Wang et al., 2019; Tran & Cutkosky, 2022; Arora et al.,
149
      2023; Ganesh et al., 2024; Lowy et al., 2024).
150
151   To the best of our knowledge, the only existing result for DP bilevel optimization is the very recent
152   result of Chen & Wang (2024), which differs than ours in several aspect. Their proposed algorithm
153
      is second-order, requiring evaluating Hessians, and solving corresponding linear systems at each
      time step, which we avoid altogether. Moreover, Chen & Wang (2024) study the local DP model
154
      (Kasiviswanathan et al., 2011), in which each user (i.e. ξi ) does not reveal its individual information.
155
      Due to this more challenging setting, they can only derive guarantees for some finite privacy budget
156   ϵ < ∞, even as the dataset size grows n → ∞. We study the common central DP model, in which a
157   trusted curator acts on the collected data and releases a private solution, and thus are able to provide
158   any desired privacy and accuracy guarantees with sufficiently many samples. Our work is the first
159   to study bilevel optimization in this well-studied DP setting. We also note that Fioretto et al. (2021)
160   studied the related problem of DP in Stackelberg games, which are certain bilevel programs which
161   arise in game theory, aiming at designing coordination mechanisms that maintain the individual
      agents’ privacy.

      Assumption 2.5. For (BO) with either (ERM) or (Pop), we assume the following hold:
220
221             i. X ⊆ Rdx is a closed convex set.
222
223            ii. F (x0 ) − inf x∈X F (x) ≤ ∆F for some initial point x0 ∈ X .
224

226
227
               iv. For all ξ ∈ Ξ : f (·, · ; ξ) is Lf0 -Lipschitz (hence, so is f ).
228             v. g is Lg2 -Hessian-smooth, and for all x ∈ X : g(x, ·) is µg -strongly-convex.
229
230            vi. For all ξ ∈ Ξ : g(·, · ; ξ) is Lg1 -smooth (hence, so is g).

232   As mentioned, these assumptions are standard in the study of bilevel optimization problems and are
233
      shared by nearly all of the previous works we discussed. In particular, the strong convexity of g(x, ·)
      ensures that y∗ (x) is uniquely defined, which is generally required in establishing the regularity of
234
      the hyporobjective. Indeed, it is known that dropping this assumption, can, in general, lead to patho-
235
      logical behaviors not amenable for algorithmic guarantees (cf. Chen et al. 2024; Bolte et al. 2024
236   and discussions therein). For the purpose of differential privacy though, the strong convexity of
237   g(x, ·) raises a subtle issue. As the standard assumption in the DP optimization literature is that the
238   component functions are Lipschitz, which allows privatization of gradients using sensitivity argu-
239   ments, strongly-convex objectives cannot be Lipschitz over the entire Euclidean space.3 Therefore,
240   strongly-convex objectives are regularly analyzed in the DP setting under the additional assumption
241   that the domain of interest is bounded. For bilevel problems, the domain of interest for y is the lower
242   level solution set, thus we impose the following assumption.
243   Assumption 2.6. There exists a compact set Y ⊂ Rdy with {y∗ (x) : x ∈ X } ⊆ Y, such that for all
244   x ∈ X , ξ ∈ Ξ : g(x, · ; ξ) is Lg0 -Lipschitz over Y.
245   Remark 2.7. Note that diam(Y) ≤ 2Lg0 /µg . Indeed, fixing some x ∈ X , since g(x, · ; ξ) is Lg0 -
246   Lipschitz over Y for all ξ ∈ Ξ, then so is g(x, ·). Moreover, by µg -strong-convexity, we get that for
247   all y ∈ Y : µg ∥y − y∗ (x)∥ ≤ ∥∇y g(x, y)∥ ≤ Lg0 . Hence Y ⊆ B(y∗ (x), Lg0 /µg ), which is of
248   diameter 2Lg0 /µg .

472   6   D ISCUSSION
473
474   In this paper, we studied differentially-private bilevel optimization, and proposed the first algorithms
475   to solve this problem that enable any desired privacy guarantee, while also requiring only gradient
476
      queries. Our provided guarantees hold both for constrained and unconstrained settings, cover em-
      pirical and population losses alike, and account for mini-batched gradients.
477
478   Our work leaves open several directions for future research. First, it is likely that the rate derived
479   in this work can be improved. Specifically, for “single”-level DP nonconvex optimization, Arora
480   et al. (2023) showed that incorporating variance reduction leads to gradient bounds that decay faster
481   with the sample size. Applying this for DP bilevel optimization as the outer loop would require,
482
      according to our analysis, to evaluate the cost of inexact gradients in variance-reduced methods,
      which we leave for future work.
483
484   Another open direction is understanding whether mini-batch algorithms can avoid the additive
485   1/bout factor in the unconstrained case X = Rdx . As previously discussed, for constrained prob-
      lems, even single-level nonconvex algorithms suffer from this batch dependence (Ghadimi et al.,


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2016). Nonetheless, for unconstrained problems, Ghadimi & Lan (2013) showed that setting a
487   smaller stepsize, roughly on the order of α2 /σ 2 , converges to a point with gradient bounded by α
488   after O(α−4 ) steps, even for bout = 1. Applying this to DP bilevel unconstrained optimization,
489   would require analyzing SGD under biased gradients, and accounting for the larger privacy loss due
490   to the slower convergence rate (compared to O(α−2 ) in our case), both of which seem feasible.
491
      Lastly, an important direction is of course empirical validation of our proposed methods. At a high
492
      level, our methods are privatized variants of the first-order penalty approach for bilevel optimization,
493   which has been substantially scaled up following initial theoretically-focused works, confirming this
494   paradigm as highly effective in some large scale non-private applications (Pan et al., 2024). While
495   our analysis provides conservative (worst-case) estimates for the convergence rate under privatiza-
496   tion of both the upper and lower level problems, it would be interesting to explore the actual cost of
497   privatization seen in practice for these problems. As this work is a theoretically focused, we leave
498   this for future research.

1083   The goal of this appendix is to provide a self contained analysis of a DP algorithm for strongly-
1084   convex optimization which achieves the optimal convergence rate with a a high probability guaran-
1085   tee. Any such algorithm can be used as the inner loop in our DP bilevel algorithm.
1086   In particular, we analyze localized DP (S)GD. Although it would have been more natural to apply
1087   DP-SGD, this seems (at least according to our analysis) to yield an inferior rate with respect to
1088   the required high probability guarantee.6 Indeed, for DP-(S)GD, previous works (such as Bassily
1089   et al. 2014) typically provide bounds in expectation, and then convert them into high-probability
1090   bounds via a black-box reduction, which applies several runs and selects the best run via the private
1091   noisy-min (i.e. Laplace mechanism). The additional error incurred by this selection is of order n1 ,
1092   which translates to √1n in terms of distance to the optimum, thus spoiling the fast rate of n1 otherwise
1093   achieved in expectation for strongly-convex objectives. We therefore resort to localization (Feldman
1094   et al., 2020): by running projected-(S)GD over balls with shrinking radii, applying martingale
                                                                                                   q       con-

       centration bounds enables us to show that the distance to optimum shrinks as Rm+1 ≲              n + n,
1096

1097
