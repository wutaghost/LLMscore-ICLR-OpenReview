011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013            Decentralized federated learning (DFL) is a collaborative machine learning framework for
014            training a model across participants without a central server or raw data exchange. DFL
015            faces challenges due to statistical heterogeneity, as participants often possess different data
016            distributions reflecting local environments and user behaviors. Recent work has shown that
017            the neural tangent kernel (NTK) approach, when applied to federated learning in a central-
018            ized framework, can lead to improved performance. The NTK-based update mechanism is
019            more expressive than typical gradient descent methods, enabling more efficient convergence
020
               and better handling of data heterogeneity. We propose an approach leveraging the NTK
               to train client models in the decentralized setting, while introducing a synergy between
021
               NTK-based evolution and model averaging. This synergy exploits inter-model variance and
022
               improves both accuracy and convergence in heterogeneous settings. Our model averaging
023            technique significantly enhances performance, boosting accuracy by at least 10% compared
024            to the mean local model accuracy. Empirical results demonstrate that our approach con-
025            sistently achieves higher accuracy than baselines in highly heterogeneous settings, where
026            other approaches often underperform. Additionally, it reaches target performance in 4.6
027            times fewer communication rounds. We validate our approach across multiple datasets,
028            network topologies, and heterogeneity settings to ensure robustness and generalizability.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Federated learning (FL) is a machine learning paradigm in which multiple clients train a global model without
      the explicit communication of training data. In most FL scenarios, clients communicate with a central server
034
      that performs model aggregation. In the popular federated averaging (FedAvg) algorithm (McMahan et al.,
035
      2017), clients perform multiple rounds of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) on their own local data, then
036   send this new weight vector to a central server for aggregation. As FL gains popularity in both theoretical
037   studies and real-world applications, numerous improvements have been made to address challenges, including
038   communication efficiency, heterogeneous data distributions, and security concerns (Sattler et al., 2020; Li
039   et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2019). To handle the performance degradation caused by data heterogeneity, many
040   works have proposed mitigation for FedAvg (Karimireddy et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020). Notably, some
041   researchers have introduced the neural tangent kernel (NTK), replacing the commonly-used SGD in order to
042   improve the model convergence (Yu et al., 2022; Yue et al., 2022).
043   Despite these advancements, the centralized nature of traditional FL schemes introduces the possibility for
044   client data leakage, computational bottlenecks at the server, and high communication bandwidth demand
045   (Kairouz et al., 2021). Decentralized federated learning (DFL) has been proposed as a solution to these
046   issues (Martı́nez Beltrán et al., 2023). In DFL, clients may communicate with each other along an undirected

                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      graph, where each node represents a client and each edge represents a communication channel between
048
      clients. While DFL addresses some of the issues inherent to centralized FL, both frameworks grapple with the
049   challenge of statistical heterogeneity across clients. Although mixing data on a central server could readily
050   resolve this issue, privacy concerns and the burden of extensive communication make FL and DFL approaches
051   necessary to address this challenge. This paper focuses on the following research question: How can we
052   design a DFL approach that effectively addresses statistical heterogeneity?
053
      We propose a DFL method that exploits the NTK to evolve weights. We denote this paradigm NTK-DFL.
054
      Our approach combines the advantages of NTK-based optimization with the decentralized structure of DFL.
055   The NTK-DFL weight evolution scheme makes use of the communication of client Jacobians, allowing
056   for more expressive updates than traditional weight vector transmissions and improving performance under
057   heterogeneity. Complementing this NTK-based evolution, we utilize a model averaging step that exploits
058   inter-model variance among clients, creating a global model with much better generalization than any local
059   model. We demonstrate that NTK-DFL maintains high performance even under aggressive compression
060   measures. Through reconstruction attack studies, we also analyze how this compression affects data privacy.
061   The contributions of this paper are threefold.
062
063   1. The proposed NTK-DFL method achieves convergence with 4.6 times fewer communication rounds
064      than existing approaches in heterogeneous settings. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work
065
         leveraging NTK-based weight evolution for decentralized federated training.
066   2. The effective synergy between NTK-based evolution and DFL demonstrates superior resilience to data
067      heterogeneity with model averaging.
068
      3. The NTK-DFL aggregated model achieves at least 10% higher accuracy than the average accuracy of
069
         individual client models. This aggregated model exhibits robust performance across various network
070      topologies, datasets, data distributions, and compression measures.

073   2   R ELATED W ORK
074
075   Federated Learning (FL) FL was introduced by McMahan et al. (2017) as a machine learning approach
076   that enables training a model on distributed datasets without sharing raw data. It attempts to address key
077   issues such as data privacy, training on decentralized data, and data compliance for more heavily regulated
078   data (e.g., medical imaging) (Zhang et al., 2021). Despite its advantages, the centralized topology of FL
079   introduces several challenges. These include potential privacy risks at the central server, scalability issues
080
      due to computational bottlenecks, and high communication overhead from frequent model updates between
      clients and the server (Mothukuri et al., 2021).
081
082   Decentralized Federated Learning (DFL) DFL aims to eliminate the need for a central server by con-
083   necting clients in a fully decentralized topology. Sun et al. (2023) adapted the FedAvg approach of multiple
084   local SGD iterations to the decentralized setting, leveraging momentum to improve model convergence
085   and weight quantization to reduce total communication cost. Our NTK-DFL method may be viewed as
086
      building on this foundation, using the neural tangent kernel for more effective weight updates. Dai et al.
      (2022) proposed a method of DFL where each client possesses their own sparse mask personalized to their
087
      specific data distribution. Shi et al. (2023) employed the sharpness-aware minimization optimizer to reduce
088
      the inconsistency of local models, whereas we tackle this issue through per-round averaging and final model
089   aggregation. DFL approaches can aim to train one global model, such as the case of many hospitals training
090   a model for tumor classification with local, confidential images (Shiri et al., 2022). They may also aim
091   to train a personalized model for each client in order to perform better on the local data distribution. For
092   example, different groups of mobile phone users may use different words or emojis and would benefit from
093   a personalized model (Tan et al., 2023). Our method focuses on training a high-performing global model

                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


094
      that generalizes well across all clients, offering improved convergence and resilience to data heterogeneity
095
      compared to existing DFL approaches.
096
097   Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) NTK has primarily been used for the analysis of neural networks (Golikov
098   et al., 2022), though it has recently seen use in the training of neural networks for FL (Yue et al., 2022).
099
      Introduced by Jacot et al. (2018), it shows that the evolution of an infinitely wide neural network converges to
      a kernelized model. This approach has enabled the analytical study of models that are well approximated by
100
      this infinite width limit (Liu et al., 2020). The NTK has also been extended to other model types, such as
101
      the recurrent neural network (Alemohammad et al., 2021) and convolutional neural network (Arora et al.,
102   2019). We instead use the linearized model of the NTK approximation as a tool for weight evolution. Some
103   studies have explored the integration of NTKs with FL. For instance, Huang et al. (2021) applied the NTK
104   analysis framework to study the convergence properties of FedAvg, while Yu et al. (2022) extended NTK
105   applications beyond theoretical analysis by training a convex neural network. Moreover, Yue et al. (2022)
106   replaced traditional SGD-based optimization with NTK-based evolution in a federated setting, where clients
107   transmit Jacobian matrices to a central server that performs weight updates using the NTK.

204   topology, or in a secure, centralized manner. Clients may also connect in a denser topology than that of

210
      order for the aggregate model to benefit from improved convergence. We note a difference between model
211   consensus, often discussed in the DFL literature (Savazzi et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022), and the proposed
212   final model averaging approach. Model consensus refers to the gradual convergence of all client models to
213   a single, unified model over numerous communication rounds. In contrast, our approach implements final
214   model averaging as a distinct step performed after the completion of the training process.
215
      Lastly, while memory efficiency is not the primary focus of this paper, we briefly note a technique to address
216
      potential memory constraints in NTK-DFL implementations. For scenarios involving dense networks or
217   large datasets, we introduce Jacobian batching. This approach allows clients to process their local datasets in
218   smaller batches, reducing memory complexity from O(Ni d2 d) to O(Ni d2 d/m), where m is the number of
219   batches. Clients compute and transmit Jacobians for each batch separately, evolving their weights multiple
220   times per communication round. This complexity reduction allows clients to connect in a denser network for
221   the same memory cost. A thorough discussion of network overhead can be found in Appendix D.

387   5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
388
389   In this paper, we have introduced NTK-DFL, a novel approach to decentralized federated learning that
390   leverages the neural tangent kernel to address the challenges of statistical heterogeneity in decentralized
391   learning settings. Our work extends NTK-based training beyond centralized settings through novel studies in
392   Jacobian batching and datapoint subsampling, while discovering a unique synergy between NTK evolution and
393   decentralized model averaging that improves final model accuracy. Our method combines the expressiveness
394   of NTK-based weight evolution with a decentralized architecture, allowing for efficient, collaborative learning
395
      without a central server. We reduce the number of communication rounds needed for convergence, which
      may prove advantageous for high-latency settings or those with heavy encoding/decoding costs.
396
397   There are promising unexplored directions for NTK-DFL. For instance, extending the algorithm to training
398   models such as CNNs, ResNets (He et al., 2016), and transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017). Additionally, future
399   research could explore the application of NTK-DFL to cross-silo federated learning scenarios, particularly
400   in domains such as healthcare, where data privacy concerns and regulatory requirements often necessitate
401
      decentralized approaches. Lastly, NTK-DFL may serve as a useful paradigm for transfer learning applications
      in scenarios where a single, centralized source of both compute and data is not available.

      NTK-FL is the only centralized baseline that we compare with. We choose a part rate that ensures that the

684   setting as the table in Figure 2, NTK-FL converges to threshold accuracy in 73, 85, and 180 communication
685   rounds for heterogeneity settings IID, α = 0.5, and α = 0.1 respectively. D-PSGD is one of the first
686   decentralized, parallel algorithms for distributed machine learning that allows nodes to only communicate
687   with neighbors. DFedAvg adapts FedAvg to the decentralized setting, and DFedAvgM makes the use of
688   SGD-based momentum and extends DFedAvg. Both use multiple local epochs between communication
689   rounds, like vanilla FedAvg. DisPFL is a personalized federated learning approach that aims to train a global
690
      model and personalize it to each client with a local mask. In order to make the comparision fair, we report the
      accuracy of the global model on our test set.

772   Then, the best-performing weights are evolved using the left side of Equation 5 and selected for the next
773   communication round.
774
775   D     D ISCUSSION OF N ETWORK OVERHEAD
776
777   While analysis of memory and communication overhead are not a central theme of this paper, we include
778   strategies to mitigate both forms of overhead for practical deployment. A thorough analysis of optimization
779   and parallelization is out of the scope of this work and we leave it to future research.
780
781   D.1    JACOBIAN BATCHING
782
783   We introduce Jacobian batching to address potential memory constraints in NTK-DFL implementations.
784   For scenarios involving dense networks or large datasets, clients can process their local datasets in smaller
785   batches, reducing memory complexity from O(Ni d2 d) to O(Ni d2 d/m1 ), where m1 is the number of batches.
786   Clients compute and transmit Jacobians for each batch separately, evolving their weights multiple times per
787
      communication round. This approach effectively trades a single large NTK H ∈ RN ×N for m1 smaller
788
      NTKs Hm1 ∈ RN/m1 ×N/m1 that form block diagonals of H, where N represents the total number of data
      points between client i and its neighbors Ni . While some information is lost in the uncomputed off-diagonal
789
      entries of H, this is mitigated by the increased frequency of NTK evolution steps. Figure 13 demonstrates this
790
      phenomenon, where an increasing batch number m1 actually leads to improved convergence. This complexity
791   reduction enables clients to connect in a denser network for the same memory cost.
792
793
      D.2    C OMMUNICATION C OST
794
795   Compared to traditional weight-based approaches that communicate a client’s parameters wi each round,
796   NTK-DFL utilizes Jacobian matrices to enhance convergence speed and heterogeneity resilience. This tensor
797   has memory complexity O(Ni d2 d), where Ni denotes the number of data points between client i and its
798   neighbors Ni , d is the model parameter dimension, and d2 is the output dimension. We propose the following

                                                                  17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


799
      strategies to improve the communication efficiency of NTK-DFL while maintaining convergence properties
800
      in heterogeneous settings.
801
802   Data Subsampling We introduce an approach where clients sample a 1/m2 fraction of their data each
803   round for NTK evolution. Clients follow the protocol described in Section 3.2, but exchange Jacobian matrices
804
      of reduced size. As demonstrated in Figure 14, moderate values of m yield light performance degradation,
      validating this communication reduction strategy.
805
806   Jacobian Compression We employ several techniques to reduce Jacobian tensor dimensionality. First,
807   we apply top-k sparsification, zeroing out elements with the smallest magnitude (Alistarh et al., 2018). The
808   remaining non-zero values are quantized to b bits. Additionally, we introduce a shared random projection
                           ′
809   matrix P ∈ Rd1 ×d1 generated from a common seed, creating projections Zi = Xi P that reduce input
810   dimension from d1 to d′1 . This combination of techniques maintains convergence properties while significantly
811
      reducing communication costs. Note that similar compression schemes applied to weight-based approaches
      lead to significant degradation in performance (Yue et al., 2022). Figure 15 illustrates the relative differences
812
      in communication load for a different combinations of the techniques above, with a sparsification of 0.5,
813
      quantization to 6 bits, a sampling of m2 = 4, and a projection to d′1 = 200 for the full optimization curve.
814   In Figure 16, we see the communication comparison of NTK-DFL updates with less expressive, DFedAvg
815   weight updates. The communication-optimized NTK-DFL converges in 3.9 times fewer communication
816   rounds compared to DFedAvg (19 rounds for NTK-DFL vs. 75 rounds for DFedAvg). However, with more
817   expressive updates than DFedAvg, it uses 7.5 times as many bits (195 MB for NTK-DFL vs. 26 MB for
818   DFedAvg). This enforces the idea that NTK-DFL is especially useful in scenarios where convergence in few
819   communication rounds is important, such as those with non-negligible encoding and decoding delays.

821   E    R ECONSTRUCTION ATTACK
822
823   While privacy preservation is not the primary focus of this work, we conduct a brief analysis of data privacy
824   in NTK-DFL. Following the reconstruction attack method of Zhu et al. (2019), we evaluate the feasibility
825   of reconstructing client data from transmitted Jacobian matrices under varying compression levels. Our
826   experiments range from basic top-k sparsification with sparsity 0.25 to combined sparsification with random
827   projection to dimension d′1 = 200. Figure 17 illustrates that client data reconstruction becomes increasingly
828   difficult when a random projection is additionally applied to the Jacobian matrices.
