009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Out-of-distribution generalization capabilities of sequence-to-sequence models
012            can be studied from the lens of two crucial forms of generalization: length gen-
013            eralization – the ability to generalize to longer sequences than ones seen dur-
014            ing training, and compositional generalization: the ability to generalize to token
015            combinations not seen during training. In this work, we provide first provable
016            guarantees on length and compositional generalization for common sequence-to-
017            sequence models – deep sets, transformers, state space models, and recurrent neu-
018
               ral nets – trained to minimize the prediction error. Taking a first principles per-
               spective, we study the realizable case, i.e., the labeling function is realizable on
019
               the architecture. We show that simple limited capacity versions of these differ-
020
               ent architectures achieve both length and compositional generalization. In all our
021            results across different architectures, we find that the learned representations are
022            linearly related to the representations generated by the true labeling function.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large language models (LLMs), such as the GPT models (Achiam et al., 2023) and the Llama
028   models (Touvron et al., 2023), have led to a paradigm shift in the development of future artificial
029   intelligence (AI) systems. The accounts of their successes (Bubeck et al., 2023; Gunasekar et al.,
030   2023) as well as their failures, particularly in reasoning and planning (Bubeck et al., 2023; Stechly
031
      et al., 2023; Valmeekam et al., 2023), continue to rise. The successes and failures of these models
      have sparked a debate about whether they actually learn general algorithms or if their success is
032
      primarily due to memorization and a superficial form of generalization (Dziri et al., 2024).
033
034   A model’s ability to perform well across different distribution shifts highlights its ability to learn gen-
035   eral algorithms. For models with fixed-dimensional inputs, considerable efforts have led to methods
036   with provable out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization guarantees (Rojas-Carulla et al., 2018; Rame
037
      et al., 2022; Chaudhuri et al., 2023; Wiedemer et al., 2023b; Eastwood et al., 2024). For sequence-
      to-sequence models, a large body of empirical works have investigated OOD generalization (Anil
038
      et al., 2022; Jelassi et al., 2023) but we lack efforts that study provable OOD generalization guaran-
039
      tees for these models. These provable guarantees provide a stepping stone towards explaining the
040   success of the existing paradigm and also shine a light on where the existing paradigm fails.
041
042   OOD generalization capabilities of sequence-to-sequence models can be studied from the lens of
043
      two forms of generalization: length generalization – the ability to generalize to longer sequences
      than ones seen during training, and compositional generalization – the ability to generalize to token
044
      combinations not seen during training. While transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) are the go-to
045
      sequence-to-sequence models for many applications, recently, alternative architectures based on
046   state-space models, as noted by Gu et al. (2021), Orvieto et al. (2023b), and Gu & Dao (2023),
047   have shown a lot of promise. This motivates us to study a range of natural sequence-to-sequence
048   architectures, including deep sets (Zaheer et al., 2017), transformers, state space models (SSMs),
049   and recurrent neural networks (RNNs). We focus on the realizable case, i.e., the labeling function
050   is in the hypothesis class of the architecture. Further, in our theoretical analysis, we make certain
051   simplifications to permit tractable analysis, for instance, we study RNNs with a limit on hidden state
052   dimension. Our key contributions and insights are summarized below.

067   2     R ELATED W ORKS
068
069   Length generalization In the field of length generalization, many important empirical insights
070   have been synthesized over the last few years. Shaw et al. (2018) discovered the drawbacks of ab-
071   solute positional embeddings and suggested relative positional embeddings as an alternative. Sub-
072
      sequent empirical analyses, notably by Anil et al. (2022) and Jelassi et al. (2023), explored length
      generalization in different settings for transformer-based models. Key findings revealed that larger
073
      model sizes don’t necessarily enhance generalization, the utility of scratchpads varies, and the effec-
074
      tiveness of relative positional embeddings appeared task-dependent. In Kazemnejad et al. (2024),
075   the authors did a comprehensive study of different positional embeddings and provided evidence to
076   show that explicit use of positional encodings is perhaps not essential. In Delétang et al. (2022), the
077   authors conducted experiments on tasks divided based on their placement in the Chomsky hierarchy
078   and showed the importance of structured memory (stack, tape) in length generalization. In a recent
079   work, Zhou et al. (2023) proposed RASP conjecture, which delineates the tasks where transformers
080   excel or fall short in length generalization, emphasizing the necessity of task simplicity for the trans-
081   former and data diversity. Our work is inspired by the experimental findings of their work. While
082   Zhou et al. (2023) provide empirical evidence for the conjecture, our work formalizes and proves
083
      simpler versions of the conjecture for a range of architectures.
084   On the theoretical side of length generalization, in Abbe et al. (2023), the authors showed an implicit
085   bias of neural network training towards min-degree interpolators. This bias was used to explain the
086   failures of length generalization on the parity task from Anil et al. (2022). In Xiao & Liu (2023),
087   the authors leverage directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) to formulate the computation in reasoning tasks
088   and characterize conditions under which there exist functions that permit length generalization. Our
089
      results crucially differ, we show a range of conditions under which models learned via standard
      expected risk minimization achieve length and compositional generalization.
090

092   Compositional generalization Compositionality has long been seen as a key piece to the puzzle
093
      of human-level intelligence (Fodor & Pylyshyn, 1988; Hinton, 1990; Plate et al., 1991; Montague,
      1970). Compositionality is a large umbrella term associated with several aspects (Hupkes et al.,
094
      2020). In this work, we focus on systematicity, which evaluates a model’s capability to understand
095
      known parts and combine them in new contexts. The breadth of research on compositional general-
096   ization, encompassing studies like Lake & Baroni (2018); Loula et al. (2018); Gordon et al. (2019);
097   Hupkes et al. (2020); Kim & Linzen (2020); Xu et al. (2022); Arora & Goyal (2023); Zhang et al.
098   (2024), is too expansive to address comprehensively here, refer to these surveys (Lin et al., 2023;
099   Sinha et al., 2024) for more detail.
100
      In recent years, several works have taken first steps towards theoretical foundations of composition-
101
      ality. We leverage the mathematical definition of compositionality from Wiedemer et al. (2023b),
102   which focuses on generalization to the Cartesian product of the support of individual features. In
103   Dong & Ma (2022), the authors analyze the conditions that provably guarantee generalization to the
104   Cartesian product of the support of individual training features. Dong & Ma (2022) studied additive
105   models, i.e., labeling function is additive over individual features. In (Wiedemer et al., 2023a), the
106   authors focus on a more general model class than Dong & Ma (2022), where the labeling function
107   is of the form f (x1 , · · · , xn ) = C(ψ1 (x1 ), · · · , ψn (xn )). However, to guarantee compositional
      generalization, Wiedemer et al. (2023b) require that the learner needs to know the exact function C


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      that is used to generate the data. In our work, we do not make such an assumption, our data gen-
109   eration is dictated by the architecture in question, e.g., RNN, and we constrain the dimension of its
110   hidden state. Lachapelle et al. (2023); Brady et al. (2023) extend these precursor results from Dong
111   & Ma (2022) from the supervised setting to the unsupervised setting. In particular, Lachapelle et al.
112   (2023); Brady et al. (2023) are inspired by the success of object-centric models and show additive
113   decoder based autoencoders achieve compositional generalization.

187   A preview of the technical challenges Both notions of compositional generalization and length
188
      generalization introduced above involve testing on distributions whose support is not contained in
      the training distributions. The long line of work on distribution shifts (Sugiyama et al., 2007; David
189
      et al., 2010; Ben-David & Urner, 2014; Rojas-Carulla et al., 2018; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Ahuja
190
      et al., 2021) assume the support of test is contained in the support of train distribution. In recent
191   years, there has been development of theory for distribution shifts under support mismatch Dong &
192   Ma (2022); Abbe et al. (2023); Wiedemer et al. (2023b); Netanyahu et al. (2023); Shen & Mein-
193   shausen (2023). Our work is closer to the latter line of work but it comes with its own technical
194   challenges, which involve building new proofs different from the above line of work, as we study
195   a new family of models, i.e., sequence-to-sequence models, and a new form of generalization, i.e.,
196   length generalization.
197
198   RASP conjecture Zhou et al. (2023) propose a conjecture backed by empirical evidence, which
199   delineates the conditions that suffice for length generalization for transformers. The conjecture
200   places three requirements – a) realizability: the task of interest is realizable on the transformer, b)
201
      simplicity: the task can be expressed as a short program in RASP-L language, c) diversity: the
      training data is sufficiently diverse such that there is no shorter program that achieves in-distribution
202
      generalization but not OOD generalization. We leverage assumptions similar to a) and b). We
203
      assume realizability, which means labeling function f is in the hypothesis class H. As to simplicity,
204   we consider hypothesis class H with limited capacity, e.g., we study one block transformer, or RNNs
205   with a limit on hidden state dimension. We emphasize that the third assumption c) on diversity from
206   Zhou et al. (2023) is quite strong. In our setting, we do not invoke it and instead, we require that the
207   support of test distribution is not larger than the Cartesian product of the marginal distribution of the
208   tokens. We now move to proving simplified versions of this conjecture for different architectures.

      5     D ISCUSSION AND LIMITATIONS
527
528
529   Our work is a step towards theoretical foundations of successes and failures of length and composi-
530   tional generalization in sequence-to-sequence models. We prove simplified versions of the recently
531
      proposed RASP conjecture under weaker data diversity assumptions. In our analysis, we make cer-
      tain simplifications, e.g., on the architectures considered, which motivates some of the important
532
      conjectures for future work. The main conjectures go as follows – a) Conjecture 1: Theorem 2
533
      and 3 currently incorporate different non-linear attentions but not the softmax attention. We believe
534   these guarantees on transformers extend to softmax attention given the experimental evidence in
535   Section 4. b) Conjecture 2: Theorem 2 and 3 use one block of attention and one block of non-
536   linearity. We believe that it is possible to extend these results to more expressive H, e.g., with more
537   alternating blocks. c) Conjecture 3: Our results focus on the generalization properties of all the
538   possible solutions to risk minimization equation 1. However, in practice the optimization procedure
539   may be biased towards a subset of those. Does accounting for the bias of optimization procedure
      give way to explaining the success of generalization in even higher capacity architectures?

      We briefly discuss some other relevant works here, which could not be mentioned in the main body
842
      due to space constraints. In Schug et al. (2023), the authors exploit compositionality in the context
843   of meta learning, where each task parameter is specified via a linear combination of some basis
844   module parameters. They construct an approach that achieves provable compositional guarantees
845   and outperforms meta-learning approaches such as MAML and ANIL. In a concurrent work Hou
846   et al. (2024) propose an interesting scratch pad strategy inspired from the operation of Turing ma-
847   chines. They call this strategy Turing programs. The scratch pad emulates the operation of a Turing
848   machine. The authors argue that there exist short RASP program (O(n) length) that can simulate
849   the operation of a Turing machine for sufficiently long number of steps (O(exp(n))). Our current
850   framework does not incorporate scratchpad strategies into it, and it is a promising future work to
851
      investigate provable length generalization guarantees with scratchpad.

858   results that follow, we either work with continuous random variables for which the Radon-Nikodym
859   derivative of X≤t is absolutely continuous w.r.t Lebesgue measure ∀t or we work with discrete

      counting measure ∀t.

      f (x) = g(x), ∀x ∈ X and ∇f (x) = ∇g(x), ∀x ∈ X , where ∇ is the Jacobian w.r.t x.

2511   Discrete Tokens Evaluating compositional generalization with discrete tokens introduces addi-
2512   tional challenges. This is because we have to sample the training and test distribution according
2513   to Fig. 1 (and 15). There are multiple ways to achieve this but they become infeasible with long
2514   sequences of interest in practice:
2515
                                              • We could continuously sample from the regions in Fig. 1 and 15 and then round up or down
2516
                                                the components to one of the predefined set of values. However, with longer sequences
2517                                            this translates to sampling and then rounding values in a high-dimensional hyper-diamond
2518                                            where points are increasingly spread out toward the boundaries. Rounding up results in
2519                                            corners becoming part of the training samples, corrupting the test set. Rounding down will
2520                                            result in a training set in which the support of tokens no longer follows Fig. 1 (i.e., does not
2521                                            cover the discrete set of values predefined in [0, 1]).
2522                                          • We could instead sample continuously and then discretize based on finding the nearest
2523                                            neighbour of each point to the points in a discrete grid of values in RT . Having as few as
2524                                            5 discrete levels renders this sampling procedure impossible for long sequences due to the
2525                                            complexity of finding nearest neighbours.
2526                                          • Lastly, one could construct the set of discrete points in RT that satisfy the constraints in
2527                                            Fig. 1 and then sample from this set, however, this enumeration also proves infeasible as
2528                                            the search space grows exponentially.
