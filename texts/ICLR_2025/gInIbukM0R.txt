                                                   A BSTRACT
009
010            Emergence, where complex behaviors develop from the interactions of simpler
011            components within a network, plays a crucial role in enhancing neural network
012            capabilities. We introduce a quantitative framework to measure Emergence as the
013            structural nonlinearity in networks, study the dynamics of this measure during the
               training process, and examine its impact on network performance, particularly in
014
               relation to pruning and training dynamics. Our hypothesis posits that the degree
015            of Emergence — defined by the distribution and connectivity of active nodes —
016            can predict the development of emergent behaviors in the network. We demon-
017            strate that higher Emergence correlates with improved training performance. We
018            further explore the relationship between network complexity and the loss land-
               scape, suggesting that higher Emergence indicates a greater concentration of local
019
               minima and a more rugged loss landscape. We show that this framework can be
020            applied to explain the impact of pruning on the training dynamics. These findings
021            provide new insights into the interplay between Emergence, complexity, and per-
022            formance of neural networks, offering implications for designing and optimizing
023            architectures.

      1. I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Emergence is a phenomenon where complex behaviors arise from the interactions of simpler ele-
028   ments within the network. Understanding and leveraging Emergence is critical for further enhancing
029   the capabilities of artificial neural networks. However, a notable lack of work has been on defin-
030   ing Emergence and its practical applications for improving network architectures (Li et al., 2023a).
031
      Emergence in neural networks encapsulates the dynamics of information flow, where intricate pat-
      terns and functionalities arise from the interaction between active and inactive nodes. This emergent
032   behavior is not explicitly programmed but emerges through the network’s ability to propagate and
033   integrate information during the training process, leading to the network’s ability to solve complex
034   tasks. This paper delves into the theoretical aspects of Emergence in neural networks and validates
035   our findings through empirical studies.
036   Our primary objective is quantifying Emergence during neural network training and exploring its
037   correlation with performance. Understanding how Emergence evolves can help us identify when a
038   network begins to specialize and focus on relevant features. By tracking this process, we can predict
039
      when a model is nearing its optimal performance, allowing for earlier intervention or fine-tuning.
      Evaluating a network’s structures and weight values using our method provides valuable insights into
040   its potential performance during training. This approach can guide optimization, reducing training
041   time and computational resources.
042
      We measure Emergence as the structural nonlinearity in the neural network as it grows in scale, hy-
043   pothesizing that such quantity is a predictor of the development of emergent traits (Han et al., 2015).
044   The results of our experiments indicate a correlation between higher levels of Emergence and im-
045   proved training performance of the networks (Ruder, 2016). Networks exhibiting greater Emergence
046   tend to converge more efficiently and achieve higher accuracy. Additionally, we explore the concept
047
      of network complexity and its spatial representation within the loss landscape. Our observations
      indicate that Emergence might reflect the concentration of potential local minima, implying that
048   networks with higher Emergence could navigate the loss landscape more effectively.
049
050
      Pruning is also examined in the context of Emergence. While pruning leads to faster convergence
      and enhanced training efficiency(Han et al., 2015), it typically results in a reduction of final accuracy.
051   This trade-off highlights the importance of balancing the number of parameters and efficiency in the
052   design of neural network architectures (Nair and Hinton, 2010). By eliminating the weights in the
053   neural network and thus the activity of nodes, pruning affects our Emergence value. Good pruning
      algorithms that keep the network performance high also keep the Emergence value high; we validate
054
      the idea that our quantitative framework to measure Emergence correlates with performance and
055   training capabilities.
056
057
      Emergence Increases with scale: Emergence (E) is inherently tied to the network’s overall scale
      and connectivity, which increases with the number of parameters and layers. Higher Emergence
058   values in larger, more complex networks indicate a stronger potential to develop sophisticated be-
059   haviors and patterns during training. Intuitively, complexity in this context refers to the network’s
060   capacity to represent intricate functions and patterns, often a function of its architecture and the
061   number of parameters it possesses. As a measure of scale and connectivity, Emergence suggests that
062
      as networks become larger, they have a higher potential for exhibiting emergent traits, leading to
      more nuanced and sophisticated behaviors (Li et al., 2023a).
063
064   We have also defined relative Emergence, which investigates the Emergence of the model relative to
065
      the number of parameters in the model and gives us a way to find Emergence relative to the size of
      the network.
066
067   Relative Emergence Correlates with training performance: Relative Emergence (Ẽ) provides a
068
      metric for evaluating how efficiently a network’s scale contributes to its learning process. Trainabil-
      ity refers to the ease and efficiency with which a neural network can learn from data and improve its
069   performance during training. A higher relative Emergence in pruned networks suggests that these
070   networks, although simpler, are more adept at learning and adapting, leading to faster convergence
071   and improved performance during training. This suggests that some smaller models, despite having
072   fewer parameters, are better at leveraging their complexity for effective learning (Ruder, 2016).
073   Additionally, we discuss the interpretation of Emergence from the loss function landscape perspec-
074   tive. Models with strong Emergence can be trained on various tasks, which suggests the existence
075   of multiple local minima within a certain proximity to the network’s current placement in the loss
076
      landscape. If the Emergence value is low, it could indicate a flat region of the loss function land-
      scape. This insight allows us to understand the local geometry of the loss landscape and predict the
077   network’s training behavior.
078
079
      Our work builds on and is inspired by recent theoretical advancements, such as the framework pre-
      sented by (Li et al., 2023a). This work provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for understand-
080   ing Emergence in network structures, which we extend and apply to the training dynamics of neural
081   networks. By integrating these theoretical insights with empirical validation, our findings offer new
082   perspectives on the role of Emergence in neural network performance and complexity. Our findings
083   offer new insights into the theoretical underpinnings of Emergence and complexity in neural net-
084
      works. We can develop more efficient and effective neural network architectures by understanding
      how Emergence influences network performance and complexity.

087   2. R ELATED WORK
088
089   The concept of Emergence has been explored in various contexts within machine learning. In large
090   language models, Emergence refers to the phenomenon where new skills appear as the model’s
091
      scale, in terms of parameters and training data, increases, which can be linked to the model’s ability
      to perform tasks it was not explicitly trained for, such as in-context learning or zero-shot learning
092   (Arora and Goyal, 2023). In a similar framework, (Wei et al., 2022) defines Emergence in large lan-
093   guage models as the phenomenon where certain abilities are absent in smaller language models but
094   appear in larger ones, making these abilities unpredictable based on smaller-scale models. Another
095   perspective on Emergence focuses on emergent abilities through the lens of pre-training loss rather
096
      than model size or training compute, as discussed by (Du et al., 2024).
097   Emergence has also been explored in various contexts in network systems, similar to how we explore
098   Emergence in neural networks. (Siyari et al., 2019) classifies Emergence as the development of hi-
099
      erarchical modularity within complex systems, where smaller, function-specific modules combine
      to form larger, complex structures. This hierarchical structure is seen as a product of evolutionary
100   processes that optimize the system for efficiency and robustness, often resulting in an ”hourglass
101   architecture” where the system of interest produces many outputs from many inputs through a rel-
102   atively small number of highly central intermediate modules, these could be classified as the alive
103   nodes. Emergence is also seen in a similar framework in (O’Brien et al., 2021), which defines Emer-
104
      gence within the context of network structures, particularly focusing on the Emergence of leader
      nodes in non-normal networks; this gives room for some of the nodes to be dead by our defini-
105   tion while the leader nodes are alive. Here, Emergence is tied to the directedness and asymmetry
106   of the network, whereas the network becomes more non-normal, and leader nodes (nodes with no
107   out-degree) spontaneously emerge, driving the dynamics and hierarchical organization within the
      network.
108
      Emergence can also be defined in a mathematical sense, particularly concerning networks. (Li
109   et al., 2023a) provides a categorical framework for quantifying emergent effects in complex sys-
110   tems through the lens of network topology. Their framework introduces a computational measure of
111   Emergence that ties the phenomenon to the network’s topology and local structures. This approach
112   offers a novel method for quantifying Emergence, which could be applied to various systems, in-
113
      cluding machine learning models and biological networks. Emergence is also explored by (Li et al.,
      2024), who proposes a new neural network initialization scheme that enhances Emergence, which
114   refers to the complex behaviors arising from simpler system components by adjusting layer-wise
115   weight scaling, resulting in improved training speed and accuracy. This method demonstrates sig-
116   nificant performance gains across various architectures.
117   There are a few machine learning aspects that are related to Emergence, such as the robustness and
118   generalizability of neural networks. (Lei et al., 2019) looks at the relationship between the size
119   of training data and the resulting accuracy and robustness of neural networks. Robustness refers
120   to the ability of a neural network to maintain its performance in the presence of small, impercep-
121
      tible changes to the input data that can cause a well-trained model to make incorrect predictions.
      As networks become more specialized through training, they might lose some generalization capa-
122   bility (or overall robustness), similar to the decline or stabilization of robustness. (Mangal et al.,
123   2019), explore probabilistic robustness, which accounts for real-world input distributions and pro-
124   vides a practical approach for verification. Robustness ensures that the network performs reliably,
125   and studying Emergence explores the internal dynamics that contribute to this performance.

      3.1. A N OVERVIEW OF THE M ATHEMATICAL F RAMEWORK FOR E MERGENCE
130   Emergence fundamentally arises from the observation of a system from a higher scale. We build our
131   definition of Emergence on the notion of nonlinearity as the information is passed to higher scales.
132   Two key conceptual components are necessary to qualitatively describe emergent effects within the
      framework proposed by (Adam, 2017). The first is a notion of interaction or local computation
133
      among the components of a system. For example, the communication and propagation of informa-
134   tion among nodes or subnetworks in the neural network. The second is the notion of interactional
135   effects, which equips each system with an observable, for example, attaches network with its per-
136   formance or abilities. These kinds of interactional effects are almost always associated with partial
137   observations or a simplification and integration of lower — more foundational or granular —levels
      or scales in the system that result in a ’loss of information’ or pattern/ feature formation at a higher
138
      level.

234   3.2. P REDICTING E MERGENCE IN THE TRAINING DYNAMICS
235   Emergence has been widely studied in neural networks as the network grows and new properties
236   and abilities emerge. The natural question arises: What scale and structure would allow the network
237   to exhibit these emergent properties? To answer this question, it is important to approximate the
238   network size sufficient to train on a dataset. We start by building the measure of Emergence, which
239
      correlates with the model’s ability to train on a given dataset. The Emergence measure evaluates the
      pathways of information flow between active and inactive nodes, as reflected in node activations and
240   weight changes. By analyzing these pathways during early training, we capture the network’s abil-
241   ity to propagate information effectively, which underpins its capacity to form relevant features and
242   improve performance. Specifically, we calculate Emergence measures based on the distribution of
243   active nodes. The dichotomy classification of the active and inactive nodes is motivated by explor-
244
      ing the feature formation inside the network, where nodes with higher activations represent certain
      features (Simonyan et al., 2013; Bau et al., 2017; Yosinski et al., 2015). As the number of paths
245   is made by the weighted connection of the neural networks, from the inactive nodes to the active
246   nodes, it essentially captures the potential paths of information flow that form feature representation
247   in the network. As we show above, the pattern of information flow is related to model’s nonlinearity
248   in its response to input, this forms an evaluation of a model’s ability to produce emergent traits.

250   3.3. E MERGENCE , R ELATIVE E MERGENCE AND T RAINING DYNAMICS
251
252   The impact of Emergence on the training dynamics and loss-function landscape can be understood
      as follows. In the previous sections, we quantify Emergence as the number of paths from the inactive
253
      nodes to the active nodes; it essentially captures the potential paths of information flow that form
254   feature representation in the network. If the network has a higher value of Emergence, there are
255   more paths between base-nodes (inactive) to the feature representing nodes (active); the features are
256   formed by summarizing over a larger amount of inputs. When networks exhibit such characteristics,
257   they are more likely to form features (Shwartz-Ziv, 2022), corresponding to more local minimums
      (Li et al., 2018).

      Emergence elucidates how our theory correlates with network pruning and trainability.

      reduced number of parameters. However, when we normalize Emergence by the number of param-
      eters in the pre-pruned network, we define this normalized metric as relative Emergence, denoted as

      Ẽ, providing deeper insights into the network’s trainability. This relative Emergence is calculated as

      3.3.1. I NTERPRETING E MERGENCE FROM THE E NERGY L ANDSCAPE P ERSPECTIVE
303
304   Relative Emergence can be conceptualized as reflecting the density of local minima within a given
305   region of the loss landscape. Although pruning reduces the overall ’size’ of the region by decreasing
306
      the number of parameters, it effectively increases the density of local minima within this smaller
      region. This higher density of local minima implies that the pruned network, despite its reduced
307   scale, possesses a more intricate and rich structure of optimization paths, facilitating faster and more
308   efficient training convergence.
309
      In Figure 2, we explore how one would visualize Emergence in the loss landscape pre- and post-
310   pruning. The lighter pink region shows how the larger pre-pruned network covers a larger region
311   than the darker region. The blue region represents the local minima. This distinction is critical
312   because it highlights that while absolute Emergence is essential for developing complex behaviors,
313   the efficiency of this network (relative Emergence) is crucial for practical trainability. By pruning
314
      the network, we reduce unnecessary parameters, thereby enhancing the network’s ability to focus
      on only the most relevant features and pathways, which accelerates learning and convergence.
315
316   In the context of our methodology, this understanding aligns with our findings on the spatial repre-
317
      sentation of network complexity within the loss landscape. Emergence reflects the size of the region
      around the network’s current state, indicating potential local minima. Pruned networks, with their
318   higher relative Emergence, navigate this landscape more effectively, finding optimal solutions with
319   greater efficiency.
320
      Overall, the concept of relative Emergence not only corroborates our theoretical framework but also
321   provides actionable insights for optimizing neural network architectures, balancing the number of
322   parameters with trainability to achieve more efficient and effective learning models. We hypothesize
323   that the quantity of Emergence, represented by the number of paths between active and inactive
      nodes, is indicative of the potential for emergent traits to arise later in the training process.
324
      Furthermore, we propose that pruning the network will decrease the quantity of Emergence due to
325   its reduced scale. This reduction in Emergence is expected to correlate with faster convergence in
326   training and improvements in training efficiency and accuracy.

535   4.4. D ISCUSSION
536
      Our study highlights the critical role of Emergence measure in understanding and optimizing
537   neural network performance. Emergence, reflected as the connectivity and distribution of active
538   nodes, serves as a predictor of the network’s potential to develop complex, high-performing traits.
539   Our results suggest that higher levels of Emergence may correlate with improved training perfor-
      mance. This observation raises the possibility that networks with greater complexity could be better
540
      equipped to navigate the loss landscape effectively. Pruning significantly impacts both Emergence
541   and performance. By reducing network complexity, pruning decreases absolute Emergence, leading
542   to faster convergence. However, the relative Emergence—Emergence normalized by the network’s
543   size—increases in pruned networks, indicating a high concentration of local minima within their
544   loss landscape. While pruned networks converge more quickly, they achieve lower final accuracy
545
      compared to non-pruned networks, which maintain higher levels of absolute Emergence and, ulti-
      mately, higher performance. This trade-off underscores the importance of balancing complexity and
546   trainability in neural network design. The predictive capability of Emergence is another significant
547   finding. As training progresses, a decrease in Emergence correlates with the network’s approach
548   to convergence. When Emergence drops to zero, further significant improvements in accuracy are
549   unlikely, marking an optimal point to terminate training. This insight can optimize computational
550
      resources and streamline training processes. Understanding the role of Emergence allows for more
      informed decisions regarding network complexity and pruning strategies, ultimately contributing to
551   the development of more efficient and effective neural network architectures.
552
553
      Future research should focus on further validating these findings across different network archi-
      tectures and datasets. Additionally, exploring the theoretical underpinnings of Emergence in more
554   depth, including its mathematical modeling and spatial representation within the loss landscape, will
555   provide a stronger foundation for applying these concepts in practical scenarios. Investigating the
556   impact of various pruning techniques and their timing relative to the training process will also offer
557   valuable insights into optimizing neural network performance.
558
559   5. C ONCLUSION
560
561   In this paper, we have investigated the concept of Emergence in artificial neural networks, empha-
      sizing its theoretical foundations and empirical validation. Our findings suggest that Emergence,
562
      defined by the connectivity between active and inactive nodes, could serve as a useful metric for
563   understanding network performance. Our experiments validate that higher Emergence correlates
564   with improved training performance. We also explored the implications of network complexity
565   and its spatial representation within the loss landscape, revealing that higher Emergence indicates a
566   more effective navigation of the loss landscape. Furthermore, we examined the effects of pruning
      on Emergence and network performance, showing that while pruning enhances training efficiency,
567   it typically results in lower final accuracy. Our work, inspired by recent theoretical advancements,
568   provides new insights into the role of Emergence in neural network performance, offering significant
569   implications for the design and optimization of efficient neural network architectures.
