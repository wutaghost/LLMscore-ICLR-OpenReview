009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This study investigates the efficacy of Large Language Models (LLMs) in causal
012            discovery. Using newly available open-source LLMs, OLM O and BLOOM,
013            which provide access to their pre-training corpora, we explore three research ques-
014            tions aimed at understanding how LLMs process causal discovery. These ques-
015            tions focus on the impact of memorization versus generalization, the influence of
016
               incorrect causal relations in pre-training data, and the role of contexts of causal re-
               lations. Our findings indicate that while LLMs are effective in recognizing causal
017
               relations that occur frequently in pre-training data, their ability to generalize to
018
               new or rare causal relations is limited. Moreover, the presence of incorrect causal
019            relations significantly undermines the confidence of LLMs in corresponding cor-
020            rect causal relations, and the context of a causal relation markedly affects the
021            performance of LLMs to identify causal relations. This study shows that LLMs
022            possess a limited capacity to generalize novel causal relations. It also highlights
023            the importance of managing incorrect causal relations in pre-training data and inte-
024            grating contextual information to optimize LLM performance in causal discovery
025            tasks. 1

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Identification and understanding of causal relations hold fundamental importance in human cogni-
030   tion and science, as those relations form the basis of causal models, which are utilized to answer
031   observational, interventional, and counterfactual questions (Zanga et al., 2022; Wan et al., 2024).
032   The task of identifying causal relations among a set of random variables is known as causal dis-
033   covery, where a random variable may refer to an event in daily life, a medical treatment, or a drug
034   effect, etc. (Pearl, 2009; Peters et al., 2017; Nogueira et al., 2021). For decades, various statistical
035   methods have been developed to identify causal relations from observational or interventional data
036   Heckerman et al. (1995); Chickering (2002); Koivisto & Sood (2004); Mooij et al. (2016a). How-
037   ever, algorithms that can accurately recover true causal structures from observational data remain
038
      elusive. Neal (2020).
039   With the rise of Large Language Models (LLMs), recent studies exploit the potential of LLMs for
040   causal discovery by evaluating them on benchmark datasets Willig et al. (2022); Ban et al. (2023).
041   Closed-source LLMs, such as GPT-3 and GPT-4, surpass the state-of-the-art (SOTA) statistical
042   methods on several publicly available datasets (Kıcıman et al., 2023). However, Romanou et al.
043   (2023) notice both GPT-3 and GPT-4 have a performance drop on the causal relations involving
044
      real-world events occurring post-Jan 2022, compared to the ones before Jan 2022. Kıcıman et al.
      (2023) find out that given part of a data table in the Tübingen cause-effect pairs dataset (Mooij et al.,
045
      2016b), GPT-4 can recover 61% of the remaining part. Zečević et al. (2023) conjecture that LLMs
046
      may just recall causal knowledge in their large pre-training corpora by acting as ”causal parrots”.
047   However, there are no solid experiments to verify to what extent memorization and generalization
048   affect model performance in causal discovery tasks because the pre-training corpora of those LLMs
049   are not accessible and the high-performing LLMs are closed-source.
050
      The recently released open-source LLMs OLM O and BLOOM make their respective pre-training
051
      corpora Dolma and ROOTS publicly available Groeneveld et al. (2024); Workshop et al. (2023).

      This provides the opportunity for us to investigate the correlations between model outputs and the
055   frequency of relations mentioned in their pre-training corpora. In this work, we focus on three
056   research questions and try to conduct experiments to answer them. RQ 1) What is the difference in
057   performance on causal discovery tasks for LLMs when recognizing relations through memorization
058   compared to inferring them through generalization? RQ 2 How does the occurrence of incorrect
059   causal relations affect LLMs performance in causal discovery? and RQ 3 How does the context of a
060   causal relation influence LLM performance in causal discovery tasks?
061   Our experiments reveal the following findings.
062
063          • Although LLMs are proficient at recognizing causal relations through memorization, their
064            ability to generalize novel causal relations is highly limited. This limitation poses signif-
065            icant challenges for deploying LLM-based causal discovery methods in scenarios where
066            causal relations are rarely or not included in their pre-training data.
067          • The presence of incorrect causal relations, such as the reversal of correct causal relations,
068            adversely impacts LLMs’ confidence in identifying correct causal relations. This find-
069            ing highlights the necessity of minimizing conflicting causal information in pre-training
070            datasets to enhance the performance of LLMs.
071          • The validity and strength of causal relations can vary significantly across different contexts.
072            This variability suggests that LLM-based causal discovery methods should incorporate the
073            context of causal relations as input to ensure accuracy, particularly to avoid misleading
074            contexts that could substantially degrade performance.

      2    BACKGROUND
077
078
      Causal discovery aims to identify causal relations among a given set of random variables. For each
079   pair of variables X and Y , it identifies whether X ← Y , Y ← X, or there is no causal influence
080   between them, where ← denotes the direction of causality. The traditional algorithms for this task
081   are statistical methods that perform causal discovery on tabular data, which are capable of unveil-
082   ing previously unknown or uncertain causal relations that are not explicitly mentioned anywhere
083   in text (e.g., ”sea level pressure causally influences zonal wind at 10 m” Huang et al. (2021)). In
084   contrast, prior NLP methods focus on either extracting mentions of known causal relations from
085   documents Yang et al. (2022) or answering questions related to causality Oh et al. (2013). The gold
086   standard for causal discovery is experimental approaches such as randomized controlled trials and
087
      A/B testing Fisher (1935). However, such experiments are often not feasible due to ethical or fi-
      nancial constraints, which necessitates the use of alternative methods that rely solely on statistics
088
      collected from observational data.
089
090   The statistical causal discovery methods are conventionally categorized into constraint-based meth-
091   ods, such as Peter and Clark (PC) Spirtes et al. (2000) and inductive causation (IC) Pearl (2009),
092   and score-based methods Heckerman et al. (1995); Chickering (2002); Koivisto & Sood (2004);
093
      Mooij et al. (2016a). Those methods rely on statistics calculated from tabular data to infer causal
      graphs, in which random variables are depicted as nodes and their causal relations are represented as
094
      edges. However, a significant drawback of these approaches is their dependency on extensive data
095
      collection to construct reliable tabular data, a process that can be both time-consuming and costly.
096   Furthermore, a theoretical limitation of these statistical methods is their inability to precisely predict
097   ground-truth causal graphs, unless strong assumptions are made. Instead, they typically yield an
098   equivalence class of true causal graphs Spirtes et al. (2000); Pearl (2009).
099
      Recent advances of LLMs provide new opportunities to tackle the task without accessing tabular data
100
      by formulating it as a pairwise causal relation prediction task Kıcıman et al. (2023); Zečević et al.
101
      (2023); Long et al. (2022). Given a pair of variable names, an LLM is instructed to identify which
102   is the cause and which is the effect using prompts Kıcıman et al. (2023); Zečević et al. (2023),
103   by distilling such knowledge directly from the LLM. However, the reliability of such methods is
104   under scrutiny. Zečević et al. (2023) argue that LLMs are ”causal parrots”, which may depend
105   on memorization to recall the causal relations present in their training data. In other words, LLMs
106   may not generalize well to detect causal relations that seldom or never occur in pre-training data.
107   If this argument holds, LLMs may primarily excel at reproducing causal relations known in their
      training data rather than uncovering novel ones. However, there is no solid empirical justification

      of this argument because prior works employ either commercial LLMs or open-source LLMs that
109   have no access to their training data. The current techniques for understanding and investigating
110   memorization in LLMs are still in their infancy Speicher et al. (2024).

      3   M ETHODOLOGY
115
116
117
118
      We aim to investigate the key limitations of LLMs for causal discovery by answering three re-
      search questions. The first research question aims to collect stronger empirical evidence to verify
119
      the ”causal parrots” hypothesis. The second research question investigates to what extend the pres-
120
      ence of incorrect causal relations in the training data, which are oriented in the opposite direction
121   to their true counterparts, influence the performance of LLMs. Instead of only feeding two variable
122   names to LLMs, the last research question is concerned with the first quantitative study on how the
123   context of a causal relation impacts the predictive performance of LLMs.
124
      Unlike prior works, we collect evidence of memorization from the pre-training datasets of LLMs
125
      and investigate their statistical properties in relation to LLMs’ predictive performance. As it is al-
126
      most infeasible to collect all mentions of a causal relation from a dataset, we curate a synthetic
127   causal relation dataset to further investigate to what extent LLMs can generate to unseen causal
128   relations. Herein, we select OLMo-7b-Instruct and BLOOM-7b1, which are the LLMs that have
129   their pre-training data publicly available Groeneveld et al. (2024); Workshop et al. (2023).To deter-
130   mine whether LLMs primarily rely on memorization or generalization, we classify causal relations
131   into various occurrence intervals, ensuring that each interval contains a comparable number of rela-
132   tions. We then assess the LLMs’ performance in recognizing causal relations across these defined
133   intervals. Evaluations are performed by transforming causal relations into yes-no questions, such
134   as ”does smoking cause lung cancer?”. We employ accuracy and F1 score metrics to assess perfor-
135
      mance. If LLMs mainly utilize memorization to identify causal relations, we anticipate observing
      high accuracy and F1 scores for relations that frequently occur in the pre-training data, with a notable
136
      decline in performance for less frequently occurring relations. This experimental method aligns with
137
      the approaches stated in Razeghi et al. (2022).
138
139   In addition to examining the frequency of causal relations, we also investigate how the presence
140   of incorrect causal relations impacts LLMs’ confidence in corresponding correct causal relations.
141
      For example, we want to explore how the occurrence of ”lung cancer causes smoking” might affect
      an LLM’s confidence in the correct relation ”smoking causes lung cancer.” To this end, we have
142
      devised a novel experimental setup. We assess the confidence of LLMs in correct causal relations
143
      under varying frequencies of corresponding incorrect causal relations. We hypothesize that a higher
144   presence of incorrect causal relations diminishes the LLMs’ confidence in the correct causal rela-
145   tions. The confidence level of the LLMs is measured by the proportion of responses that affirm the
146   correct causal relation out of multiple generated responses for one query.
147
      Due to the impracticality of exhaustively retrieving all semantically equivalent mentions of target
148
      causal relations in pre-training data, we create new pre-training corpora including synthetic causal
149
      and incorrect causal relations. These relations, such as ”blaonge causes goloneke,” utilize terms that
150   do not exist in the original pre-training corpora. We then integrate these synthetic relations into the
151   pre-training data at various frequencies. This approach allows us to re-evaluate our experimental re-
152   sults on real-world causal relations, thereby validating the reliability of our findings under controlled
153   conditions.
154
      One distinction between causal discovery and numerical reasoning tasks Razeghi et al. (2022) is
155
      context dependency. Numerical reasoning, such as 3 + 4 = 7, exhibits consistency across various
156   contexts. However, causal relations might not have this consistency. For example, the causal relation
157   ”rain causes flooding” may be true during a heavy downpour in a city with poor drainage but may not
158   be true during light rain in areas with good drainage systems. Therefore, we assess the performance
159   of LLMs on causal discovery across varying contexts. For each selected causal relation from human-
160   annotated datasets, we employ GPT-4o to generate five positive contexts that affirm the relation and
161   five negative contexts that challenge it. The LLMs’ ability to recognize these causal relations is then
      evaluated in these contexts.

196   4.1              DATASETS
197
      Tasks. Following (Kıcıman et al., 2023), we consider the following two causal discovery tasks.
198   Causal Direction Identification. Given two causally related variables (X, Y ), the causal direction
199   identification task involves deciding whether X → Y or X ← Y is true. Full Causal Discovery.
200   Given a set of random variables X, for each possible pair of variables (Xi , Xj ), an LLM is in-
201   structed to identify whether: Xi → Xj , Xi ← Xj , or no causal relation between Xi and Xj . The
202   causal direction identification and full causal discovery tasks can be treated as classification tasks.
203   Therefore, we evaluate the results using F1 and accuracy.
204

209   tions by swapping the cause and effect, resulting in 3,800 causal and reverse-causal relations. From
210   CauseNet, we select 814 high-confidence causal relations and create an equal number of reverse-
211   causal relations, totaling 1,628 relations. These procedures are detailed in Appendix A.2.

      4.1.2                              S YNTHETIC DATA
251
252   Causal Direction Identification. We create a pre-training dataset including synthetic correct and
253   incorrect causal relations that are absent in the original corpora. This dataset includes 100,000 docu-
254   ments randomly sampled from Dolma, with incorrect causal relations that either swap the positions
255   of cause and effect or use negation templates such as ”X does not cause Y.” We generate 100 ar-
256   tificial causal relations using fictitious terms like ’blaonge’ and ’goloneke’. Utilizing predefined
257
      templates listed in Table 5 in Appendix A.5, we craft mentions for both correct and incorrect causal
      relations. Then we create positive documents containing correct causal relations and negative doc-
258
      uments containing incorrect causal relations by inserting these mentions between sentences within
259
      the documents. We adopt three approaches for the insertion of mentions. Correct Relation Scaling:
260   we vary the insertion of each correct causal relation from 0 to 1,000 occurrences. Reverse Relation
261   Scaling: we first insert 1000 occurrences of each correct causal relation followed by inserting the
262   corresponding reverse causal relations from 0 to 1,000 occurrences. Negated Relation Scaling:
263   After inserting 1,000 occurrences of each correct causal relation, we insert negations of these causal
264   relations, from 0 to 1,000 occurrences. We then fine-tune OLMo-7b-Instruct utilizing LoRA Hu
265   et al. (2022) on synthetic datasets, with details provided in Appendix A.6.

360   memorization, their capacity to generalize from less frequent or entirely novel data remains highly
361   constrained. This limitation highlights the challenges in deploying LLMs in scenarios where causal

363   of traditional statistical methods for causal discovery that rely solely on statistics to determine causal

483   Causality with LLMs Kıcıman et al. (2023); Zečević et al. (2023); Long et al. (2022); Feng et al.
484   (2023) explore the inference of causal relations by submitting pairwise queries about variable pairs
485   to LLMs. These queries are either structured as option selection questions Kıcıman et al. (2023) or
      yes-no questions Long et al. (2022); Zečević et al. (2023). Results from these experiments demon-


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      strate that the LLM-based approach surpasses traditional statistical algorithms in performance. Re-
487   markably, the LLM-based method requires only the names of the variables, without needing their
488   statistical data. However, the approach of pairwise queries may lead to inefficiencies in time and
489   computation, as identifying all possible relations among a set n of variables necessitates O(n2 )
490   queries. To address this, Jiralerspong et al. (2024) have proposed a breadth-first search strategy
491   that significantly reduces the number of queries to a linear scale. Additionally, beyond exploring
492   relationships among observable variables, Liu et al. (2024) has developed a framework capable of
493   uncovering high-level hidden variables from unstructured data using LLMs, and subsequently infer-
494   ring causal relationships.
495
496   Influence of Pre-training Data on Language Models. Research conducted by Kassner et al.
497   (2020) and Wei et al. (2021) involving controlled variations in pretraining data sheds light on its
498   impact on language models’ (LM) capabilities to memorize factual information and understand syn-
499   tactic rules. Their findings confirm that the frequency of data plays a crucial role in determining a
500   model’s ability to remember specific facts or grammatical structures about verb forms. Furthermore,
501   Sinha et al. (2021); Min et al. (2022) show that altering the word order during pretraining barely af-
502   fects the LMs’ performance in subsequent tasks, and mixing up labels in in-context learning scenar-
503   ios does not significantly affect the models’ few-shot learning accuracy. These studies collectively
504
      indicate that the efficacy of LMs predominantly hinges on their capacity to process complex word
      co-occurrence patterns. Additionally, Carlini et al. (2023; 2019); Song & Shmatikov (2019) have
505
      identified that LMs can retain sensitive information from their training datasets, even when such in-
506
      stances are infrequent. The experiments of Razeghi et al. (2022) demonstrate that models are more
507   accurate on numerical reasoning questions whose terms are more prevalent in pre-training data.

      In this study, we investigate the factors that impact the performance of LLMs in causal discovery
      tasks. Our results show that the frequency of causal relations within a model’s pre-training data has
514
      a positive correlation with LLM performance, while the presence of incorrect causal relations can
515
      negatively affect the models’ confidence in correct causal relations. Furthermore, our experiments
516   reveal that the context of causal relations significantly affects the validity of causal relations. To fa-
517   cilitate a deeper understanding of LLMs, we strongly advocate for the release of both model weights
518   and pre-training data by more LLM providers.
519

521   R EPRODUCIBILITY S TATEMENT
522
523
      We release our code and scripts at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/

      causality_llm-5FD3. Appendix A.1 presents the ground-truth causal graphs used in
525   our full causal discovery task. Appendix A.3 provides examples of in-context learning and the
526   prompts used during our experiments. Appendix A.4 outlines the queries utilized for searching
527   within the pre-training data. Appendix A.7 details the human evaluation process for assessing
528   causal relations under various contexts. These resources ensure transparency and facilitate the
529   replication of our research findings.

532   E THICS S TATEMENT
533
534   The ability of LLMs to identify and generalize causal relations could significantly impact various
535   fields, from healthcare to social sciences, where understanding causality is crucial. However, we
536   acknowledge that relying on LLMs for causal discovery may perpetuate existing biases present in
537   training data, potentially leading to misleading or harmful conclusions if deployed without proper
538   safeguards. The limitations we’ve identified, particularly regarding incorrect causal relations and
539   context dependency, underscore the need for careful human oversight when applying these models
      in real-world scenarios.

      A.2    C AUSAL D IRECTION I DENTIFICATION TASK

854   ConceptNet is a knowledge graph that connects natural language concepts via labeled edges. It
855   includes the ”[A, /r/Causes, B]” relation, indicating that event A causes event B. Each relation in
856   ConceptNet also has a weight attribute, reflecting the confidence level of the relation; a higher
857   weight suggests broader agreement across sources. From ConceptNet, we selected the top 1,900
858   causal relations by weight and generated an equal number of reverse-causal relations by swapping
859   the cause and effect. This process yielded a total of 3,800 causal and reverse-causal relations.
860   CauseNet is a large-scale knowledge base containing claimed causal relations between concepts.
861   We extract 814 high-confidence causal relations from CauseNet, each supported by at least 100 web
862   sources and 10 extraction patterns. By swapping the cause and effect, we generate an equivalent
863   number of reverse-causal relations. We then create a dataset containing 1,628 causal and reverse-
      causal relations.

946

947   nodes (A, B), we generate two questions: ”Does A cause B?” and ”Does B cause A?”.

949   In the causal direction identification task, the ground-truth instances are formatted as (A → B, true)
950
      and (A ← B, f alse). These yes-no questions are directly transformed into such instances, aligning
      perfectly with the binary nature of the task. In the causal discovery task, the ground-truth instances
951
      are structured as (A, B, l), where the label l can take one of four possible values: ←, →, ×, ↔.
952
      Here, × denotes no causal relation, and ↔ indicates a bi-directional causal relation. We include
953   bi-directional causal relation because it exists in some ground-truth causal graphs such as Arctic Sea
954   Ice. The conversion of yes-no responses to these four-way labels is handled as follows. If only one
955   of the questions receives a ’yes’ answer, it translates directly to the corresponding causal direction
956   (i.e., ← or →). If both questions are answered with ’no’, this indicates no causal relation (i.e., ×).
957   If both questions receive a ’yes’ response, this suggests a bi-directional relation (i.e., ↔).
958
      To determine the most confident answer, each LLM should generate ten distinct responses Chen &
959   Mueller (2023); Geng et al. (2024). We then extract ’yes’ or ’no’ from each output. If the count of
960   ’yes’ responses is greater than or equal to the count of ’no’ responses, the final answer is ’yes’. If
961   ’no’ responses predominate, the final answer is ’no’. This methodology ensures a robust approach
962   to determining causal relationships in both tasks.
963
      The decoding hyperparameters are configured as follows: the top-p sampling parameter is set to
964
      0.9, the repetition penalty is 1.25, the temperature is 0.8, and the maximum number of new tokens
965   generated does not exceed the maximum input length. We employ the Hugging Face library to load
966   LLMs and generate responses Wolf et al. (2020). All experiments were conducted on NVIDIA A100

1168
       nantly focus on specific closed-source models and offer a limited examination of open-source LLMs.
1169
       In this section, we employ closed-source and open-source LLMs to conduct causal relation identifi-
1170   cation and causal discovery tasks. We aim to compare and analyze the performance disparities when
1171   utilizing different models. Table 8, 9, 10, 11, 12 and 13 show the results of causal discovery ex-
1172   periments on the Arctic Sea Ice, Insurance, Alcohol, Cancer, Diabetes, and Obesity causal graphs.

1175
       We employ the Normalized Hamming Distance (NHD) as one metric for full causal discovery. A
1176   notable issue with NHD is that due to the typically sparse nature of causal graphs, models that predict
1177   no edges can still achieve a low NHD. This setup inadvertently penalizes models that predict a larger
1178   number of edges, even true edges may be predicted. To address this, following the methodologies
1179   outlined by Kıcıman et al. (2023) and Jiralerspong et al. (2024), we calculate the ratio between the
1180   NHD and the baseline NHD of a model that outputs the same number of edges but with all of them
1181   being incorrect. The lower the ratio, the better the model performs compared to the worst baseline
1182   that outputs the same number of edges. Therefore, we report NHD ratio (i.e., NHD / baseline
1183   NHD), along with the number of predicted edges, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of
1184
       model performance in the full causal discovery task.
1185   Due to the transparency of OLMo-7b-Instruct and the robust capabilities of its search tool, OLMo-
1186   7b-Instruct serves as our primary analysis model. Therefore, we explored various numbers of in-
1187   context learning examples to identify the optimal example number. In seven out of eight datasets,
       OLMo-7b-Instruct with three demonstration examples achieves the highest F1, compared to other

       Research Question 4. Do pre-training corpora contain more correct causal relations than incorrect
1266   ones?
1267
1268   Humans fundamentally rely on causal relations to understand and generate text. Therefore, it is
1269   reasonable that pre-training corpora, which are collected from human-generated texts, are likely to
1270   inherently contain a higher proportion of correct causal relations.
1271
       Observation We count the total occurrence of correct and incorrect causal relations in Dolma and
1272   ROOTS corpora. The results are shown in Table 16. We use exact matching to count correct and
1273   incorrect causal relations. We observe that the occurrence of causal relations is, on average, 12 times
1274   higher than that of incorrect causal relations in Dolma and ROOTS corpora. From our observation,
1275   most incorrect causal relations do not exist in an affirmation context. They are usually in a question
1276   or negation context. For example, ”Which option is correct? A. smoking causes cancer B. cancer
1277   causes smoking” or ”Which means that either smoking causes cancer or cancer causes smoking. ”
1278   Discussion In conclusion, these experimental results show that correct causal relations are more
1279   frequently represented than incorrect ones in pre-training corpora. This also explain why LLMs can
1280   identify many causal relations in causal discovery tasks.

1284   Research Question 5. Do larger models perform better on causal discovery tasks?
1285
1286   We assume that within the same architectural framework, increasing the model size (i.e., the number
1287   of parameters) leads to improved performance on causal discovery tasks. The rationale is that larger
1288   models can memorize more information from the pre-training data than their smaller models.
1289
       Observation We select models from the Llama2 and Llama3 series, each varying in size. These
1290   models are evaluated on causal discovery and causal direction identification tasks, with results doc-
1291   umented in Table 17 and 18. The findings indicate that for both the Llama2 and Llama3 models,
1292   there is a positive correlation between the number of parameters and performance. However, dis-
1293   crepancies arise when comparing across architectures. For example, a small Llama3 model (e.g.,
1294   Llama3-8b-Instruct) can outperform a significantly larger Llama3 model (e.g., Llama2-70b-chat).
1295   Notably, across most datasets, Llama3-70b-Instruct either matches or surpasses the performance of
       the currently leading closed-source LLM, GPT-4o.
