                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012            Existing studies of training state-of-the-art Contrastive Language-Image Pretrain-
013
               ing (CLIP) models on large-scale data involve hundreds of or even thousands of
               GPUs due to the requirement of a large batch size. However, such a large amount of
014
               resources is not accessible to most people. While advanced compositional optimiza-
015
               tion techniques for optimizing global contrastive losses have been demonstrated
016            effective for removing the requirement of a large batch size, their performance on
017            large-scale data remains underexplored and not optimized. To bridge the gap, this
018            paper explores several aspects of CLIP training with limited resources (e.g., up to
019            tens of GPUs). First, we introduce FastCLIP, a general CLIP training framework
020            built on advanced compositional optimization techniques while designed and op-
021            timized for the distributed setting. Our framework is equipped with an efficient
022            gradient reduction strategy to reduce communication overhead. Second, to further
023            boost training efficiency, we investigate three components of the framework from
024
               an optimization perspective: the schedule of the inner learning rate, the update rules
               of the temperature parameter and the model parameters, respectively. Experiments
025
               on different strategies for each component shed light on how to conduct CLIP
026
               training more efficiently. Finally, we evaluate the performance of FastCLIP and the
027            state-of-the-art training baseline (OpenCLIP) on different compute scales up to 32
028            GPUs on 8 nodes, and three data scales ranging from 2.7 million, 9.1 million to 315
029            million image-text pairs to demonstrate the significant improvement of FastCLIP
030            in the resource-limited setting.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034   Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) is a popular approach for
035   vision-language representation learning (Cherti et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023c; Li
036   et al., 2023a; Qiu et al., 2023). The method effectively embeds data from the image and language
037   modality into a joint embedding space by optimizing a contrastive loss in a self-supervised manner. It
038   has demonstrated strong performance on various downstream tasks (e.g., zero-shot classification and
039
      retrieval) and has been adopted in various applications, including text-to-image generation (Ramesh
      et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Crowson et al., 2022), image captioning (Yu et al., 2022; Mokady et al.,
040
      2021), and evaluation of image generation (Hessel et al., 2021). Its popularity is further fueled by
041
      releases of web-scale datasets (Schuhmann et al., 2021; 2022; Gadre et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2023).
042
043   However, vanilla mini-batch based methods for self-supervised contrastive learning are known to
044   require a large batch size to obtain satisfactory performance (Chen et al., 2023b; 2020). Theoretically,
045
      it has been shown that the optimization error of mini-batch based contrastive learning methods
      inversely depends on the batch size (Yuan et al., 2022). Empirically, state-of-the-art CLIP models
046
      are typically trained using a large batch size on a large number of GPUs (e.g., 84k batch size and
047
      1024 Nvidia A100 GPUs in OpenCLIP (Cherti et al., 2023)). Such a large amount of resources is
048   not accessible to most researchers and practitioners in academia and small companies. Recently,
049   Yuan et al. (2022) proposed an algorithm named SogCLR to address the large batch size issue, which
050   leverages finite-sum coupled compositional optimization (FCCO) techniques to optimize a global
051   contrastive loss (GCL) that contrasts each anchor data with all other data in a compositional structure.
052   A key feature of compositional optimization is the inner and outer steps where the inner steps
053   maintain and update a sequence of estimators to track the inner functions on the solution path, which
      can be interpreted as an SGD update with a learning rate called the inner learning rate (Wang & Yang,


                                                         1

054
      2022). Later, SogCLR has been leveraged by Qiu et al. (2023) to design the iSogCLR algorithm for
055   optimizing a robust global contrastive loss (RGCL) with individualized learnable temperatures for
056   training CLIP models. However, these algorithms are not fully optimized for large-scale training of
057   CLIP models since they were examined only on small-scale datasets.
058
      This paper aims to scale up the advanced optimization algorithms for optimizing global contrastive
059
      losses of CLIP training on large-scale data with limited compute resources. We introduce a distributed
060
      training framework named FastCLIP by employing data parallelism such that each worker computes
061   the gradient estimator using their respective data and then reduces (averages) them through com-
062   munication, based on which the model is updated. A novel gradient reduction strategy is designed,
063   which requires less communication than the existing distributed framework. This distributed training
064   framework lays the foundation for scaling up CLIP training with limited resources. To further boost
065   the efficiency of our framework, we investigate its three aspects from an optimization perspective:
066   the schedule of the inner learning rate (LR) of compositional optimization, the update rule of the
067   temperature parameter, and the update rule of the model parameters, respectively.
068
069
      • Previous studies (Yuan et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2023) set the inner LR to a constant value less
        than but close to one, which could slow down the training for large-scale data at earlier iterations.
070
        Inspired by the learning rate schedule of existing optimizers of Deep Learning (Loshchilov &
071
        Hutter, 2017), we examine a cosine decay schedule for the inner LR by comparing its performance
072     with the constant schedule.
073
      • For the update rule of the temperature parameter, we compare four different strategies in the
074
        FastCLIP framework, including a heuristic approach based on the gradient of GCL, a constant
075
        strategy as used in SogCLR, learning individualized temperatures as used in iSogCLR, and learning
076     global temperature by optimizing a new RGCL with a single learnable temperature.
077
078
      • For the update rule of the model parameters, we compare the performance of commonly-used
        optimizers for CLIP training in the FastCLIP framework, including AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter,
079
        2019), LAMB (You et al., 2020), Lion (Chen et al., 2023a) and SGD with momentum (Polyak,
080
        1964).
081
082   Moreover, in order to study the scaling capability of FastCLIP, we compare the performance of
083   FastCLIP and state-of-the-art baseline OpenCLIP (Ilharco et al., 2021) on three data scales and four
084   compute scales. The data scales include 2.7 million (CC3M (Sharma et al., 2018)), 9.1 million
085   (CC12M (Changpinyo et al., 2021)), and 315 million (LAION400M (Schuhmann et al., 2021))
086   image-text pairs1 . The compute scales include 1, 2, 4, and 8 nodes, with 4 GPUs on each node.
087   The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows: (1) We propose FastCLIP, an efficient
088   distributed framework to scale up CLIP training with limited computing resources. (2) We study
089   the performance of different strategies for three components of FastCLIP, providing insights on
090   how to conduct CLIP training more efficiently. (3) We compare the performance of FastCLIP on
091   different data scales and compute scales. The results show that FastCLIP consistently outperforms
092   state-of-the-art training baseline OpenCLIP by a large margin. A quick comparison between FastCLIP
093   and OpenCLIP on different data scales and compute scales are shown in Figure 1, with more detailed
094   results presented in Section 6.
095   Roadmap: In Section 2 we review the literature of CLIP training, in Section 3 we introduce the
096   objective of interest and provide background on the Global Contrastive Learning framework. We
097   propose our FastCLIP framework and explain its gradient reduction strategy in Section 4. Then in
098   Section 5 we compare different strategies for different components within the FastCLIP framework,
099   and we compare the scaling performance of FastCLIP and OpenCLIP under different settings in
100   Section 6. Finally, we conclude this paper in Sections 7 and 8.

      Improved CLIP training: Many works have studied efficient CLIP training with limited resources.
148
      Yuan et al. (2022) propose SogCLR to improve the performance of contrastive learning with small
149
      batch size. Our work scales up SogCLR in the distributed setting and incorporates several algorithmic
150   strategies to accelerate its training speed. Besides the algorithm, other directions are also explored for

156   CLIP training. Many techniques have been proposed to update or set the temperature parameter.

159   samples to independently control intra-anchor and inter-anchor hardness-awareness. Kukleva et al.
160   (2023) study a cosine decay schedule for setting the temperature. Huang et al. (2023b) propose
161   to set the temperature parameter proportional to the alignment between positive pairs. Qiu et al.
      (2023) propose a robust global contrastive loss (RGCL) with individualized temperatures inspired by

291   5    I MPROVEMENT OF O PTIMIZATION C OMPONENTS
292
293   In this section, we propose different strategies to improve three components of the FastCLIP frame-
294   work, i.e., the schedule for inner LR γt , the update rule of the temperature parameter, and the
295   optimizer for updating the model parameters.
296   The Inner LR Schedule: We first explore different schedules for γt in Equation (1), which is
297   interpreted as an SGD step with learning rate (LR) γt by Wang & Yang (2022). They showed in
298   theory that γt should be set to a very small value close to 0 in order to guarantee convergence.
299   However, in practice a large γt value close to 1 is adopted (Yuan et al., 2022). Ideally, γt should be
300   large to rely more on the current mini-batch at earlier iterations and be smaller to rely more on history
301   in later iterations. To achieve this, we consider a cosine schedule to decrease γt : Let t be the current
302   iteration, Ê be the number of iterations per epoch and E be the number of decay epochs, then we set
303   γt = 0.5 · (1 + cos(π⌊t/Ê⌋/E)) · (1 − γmin ) + γmin . With this schedule, γt will decrease from 1.0
304   to γmin . Note that ⌊t/Ê⌋ denotes the current epoch, which means the value of γt stays unchanged
305   within one epoch. Also, The number of decay epochs E is a hyperparameter, and it is not necessarily
306   equal to the total number of training epochs. If the current epoch exceeds E, γt will be set to γmin .
307   The Temperature Parameter Updates: At Line 12 of Algorithm 1, the temperature parameter τ
308   is updated. The update rule is not explicitly provided due to its variety. We consider four different
309   versions, named v0 to v3. Specifically, v1 sets τ to a constant as in SogCLR and the other three view
310   τ as a learnable parameter: v2 leverages the same τ update as iSogCLR, which maintains individual
311   temperature parameters for each data and updates them using gradient of (RGCL) w.r.t. τ . A potential
312   issue of maintaining and updating individualized temperature is that it may overfit the data and hence
313
      harm the generalization for large-scale data. To mitigate this issue, we also consider the following
      loss, which unifies the individual temperature in (RGCL) into a single global one:

      7     C ONCLUSION
525   In this paper, we have proposed a distributed training framework of CLIP models in a resource-
526   limited setting named FastCLIP. It leverages advanced compositional optimization with a novel
527   gradient computation strategy to reduce the communication cost. We have investigated different
528   optimization components, by proposing new techniques and benchmarking different techniques for
529   each component under different settings to provide valuable insights on which techniques to use.
530   Finally, leveraging the best-performant techniques from the experiment results, we compare the
531
      performance of FastCLIP with OpenCLIP on different data scales and compute scales, from 3 million
      to 315 million image-text pairs and from 1 node to 8 nodes. The results demonstrate that FastCLIP
532
      outperforms OpenCLIP by a large margin and achieves a significant speedup. This helps accelerate
533
      research in the areas of CLIP training and its various applications, as more researchers would be able
534   to contribute their ideas and train CLIP models without access to a large amount of resources.

1212   We found that in the xlarge-scale setting, the constant ε plays an important role in the performance of
1213   FastCLIP-v3. In particular, the gradient estimators Gtw,a,k and Gtw,b,k of (RGCL-g) in Equation (2)
1214   and (3) are scaled by two factors: 1/(ε + ut+1                      t+1
                                                       1,i ) and 1/(ε + u2,i ). Recall that u1,i and u2,i are
1215                             t t                      t t
       approximations of g1 (w , τ , i, Si− ) and g2 (w , τ , i, Si− ), respectively. Thus, in the later stage of
1216
       training many examples (those that are well-learned) will have very small ut+1            t+1
                                                                                       1,i and u2,i . Then with
1217   a very small ε the scaling factors in the estimated gradient for these samples will be very large, which
1218   may suffer from over-optimization for those examples and harm generalization. In the following
1219   figure, we plot the performance of FastCLIP-v3 with two schemes of ε along with the performance of
1220   OpenCLIP (in blue): i) ε= 1e-14 (in orange) and ii) ε= 1e-6 (in green). The value 1e-6 is not tuned
1221   due to limited compute resources and the three experiments were run for only 30 epochs (9.45B
1222   samples seen). From Figure 7 we can see that with larger ε, both the ImageNet-1k top 1 accuracy and
1223   Datacomp Average performance improve by a large margin.
