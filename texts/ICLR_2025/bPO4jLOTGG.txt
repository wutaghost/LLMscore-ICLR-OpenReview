009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reward models (RM) play a critical role in aligning generations of large language
012            models (LLM) to human expectations. However, prevailing RMs fail to capture
013            the stochasticity within human preferences and cannot effectively evaluate the re-
014            liability of reward predictions. To address these issues, we propose Uncertain-
015            aware RM (URM) and Uncertain-aware RM Ensemble (URME) to incorporate
016            and manage uncertainty in reward modeling. URM can model the distribution of
017            disentangled attributes within human preferences, while URME quantifies uncer-
018            tainty through discrepancies in the ensemble, thereby identifying potential lack
019
               of knowledge during reward evaluation. Experiment results indicate that the pro-
               posed URM achieves state-of-the-art performance compared to models with the
020
               same size, demonstrating the effectiveness of modeling uncertainty within human
021
               preferences. Furthermore, empirical results show that through uncertainty quan-
022            tification, URM and URME can identify unreliable predictions to improve the
023            quality of reward evaluations.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Large language models (LLM) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various domains
029
      (Singhal et al., 2023a; Cui et al., 2024; Kasneci et al., 2023). These powerful LLMs are trained
030   to align with human values and expectations to avoid harmful and toxic generations. To achieve
031   alignment, LLMs rely on feedbacks from reward models (RM), where the feedbacks are provided
032   in the form of rewards (Singhal et al., 2023a; Cui et al., 2024; Kasneci et al., 2023). These rewards
033   typically reflect the quality and users’ preferences of the responses provided, and hence reward
034   maximization will guide the LLM to more effectively satisfy user queries. In this paradigm, RMs
035   fundamentally decides the efficacy of alignment, as they primarily steer the LLMs through feedback.
036   Therefore, the reliability and accuracy of this feedback is essential in aligning LLMs with intended
037   human values and preferences.
038   However, current RMs fail to capture the stochastic nature of human preferences (Baylis, 1950)
039   and lack the ability to evaluate the reliability of the predicted rewards. In prevalent RMs, a value
040   head (usually a linear layer) is added to the pretrained base model and maps the hidden states to
041   reward scalars (Bai et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022) or attribute scores (Adler et al., 2024; Wang
042   et al., 2024a). This results in a deterministic reward modeling process, unable to accommodate the
043   variabilities of human preferences. Moreover, there is no other information to validate the reliability
044
      of these reward predictions.
045   Uncertainty is of major importance in machine learning (Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021) and an
046   appropriate representation for uncertainty is essential for developing trustworthy and reliable mod-
047   els (Yang et al., 2009; Varshney & Alemzadeh, 2017). Uncertainty originates from two different
048   sources: aleatoric and epistemic. Aleatoric uncertainty refers to the inherent variability and ran-
049   domness of data. As opposed to this, epistemic uncertainty is caused by lack of knowledge, i.e.
050
      ignorance of the model instead of any underlying randomness.
051   In the context of reward modeling for LLM, aleatoric uncertainty refers to the stochasticity of human
052   preferences, while epistemic uncertainty comes from the RMs’ lack of knowledge to make reliable
053   evaluations. Therefore, introducing uncertainty to reward modeling improves modeling capacity of
      RMs and enhance reliability of the reward predictions. Consequently, by identifying and filtering

054
      out out-of-distribution (OOD) data where RMs fail to generalize, rewards with better reliability pave
055   the way for more efficient alignments of LLMs.
056
057   In this paper, we propose Uncertain-aware RM (URM) and Uncertain-aware RM Ensemble (URME)
058
      to handle aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty in reward modeling respectively. URM is equipped
      with an uncertainty-aware value head to model the distributions of multiple attributes within hu-
059
      man preferences. We demonstrate that with the popular bradley-terry-model loss function (Bradley
060
      & Terry, 1952), RMs cannot quantify uncertainty of human preferences even with an uncertainty-
061   aware value head. Therefore, URMs are trained via maximum likelihood estimation and attribute
062   regression. URME quantifies epistemic uncertainty by the discrepancies among URMs in the en-
063   semble, identifying potential lack of knowledge. During reward evaluation, filtering strategy can
064   be applied to prompt-response pairs with high uncertainty, in case that LLMs learn unintended or
065   potentially harmful behaviors that URMs may not be able to accurately evaluate.
066
      Empirical results on a popular RM benchmark RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024) demonstrate
067   that URM with 8B model size achieves state-of-the-art performance among models with the same
068   size and outperforms a number of strong large models including Nemotron-4-340B (Adler et al.,
069   2024). And through uncertainty quantification, URM and URME are able to identify their level
070   of knowledge for the input data and make the reward evaluations more reliable through filtering
071   strategy. Furthermore, results of best-of-n sampling validates that URM and URME can effectively
072   enhance the generation quality of LLMs.
073   Contributions of this paper include:
074
075   (1) We introduce URM and URME to model the uncertainty within human preferences and reward
076
      models themselves.
077   (2) URM and URME are able to improve LLMs’ generation effectively. Notably, URM achieves
078   state-of-the-art performance on RewardBench compared with models of the same size (8B).
079
      (3) Empirical results demonstrate that URM and URME can successfully quantify uncertainty to
080   identify areas where the models lack sufficient knowledge to make accurate predictions, leading to
081   more reliable reward evaluations.

116   3.1   M ULTI - ATTRIBUTE R EWARD M ODELING
117
118
      To generate helpful, harmless and truthful responses (Askell et al., 2021), LLMs must be aligned
      with human expectations. Current methods fine-tune models based on human (Christiano et al.,
119
      2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022) or AI feedbacks (Bai et al., 2022b;
120
      Sun et al., 2024) to maximize preference-based rewards, which are provided by reward modeling.
121   Typically, a reward model is learned and LLMs will improve their generation quality according to
122   feedbacks from the reward model (Bai et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2024; Stiennon
123   et al., 2020).
124
      Recent studies show that human and LLM judges may introduce potential biases to annotations of
125
      preference (Zhang et al., 2023; Kotek et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024a). More-
126
      over, traditional RMs usually rely on single-dimensional feedback on general quality instead of
127   fine-grained multifaceted signals to indicate multiple attributes such as helpfulness, coherence and
128   verbosity (Dong et al., 2023b). Adler et al. (2024) discovered that multi-attribute RMs trained on
129   datasets with high-quality attribute-specific annotations (Cui et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024c) are
130   able to disentangle real helpfulness and other irrelevant aspects such as lengthy bias (Shen et al.,
131   2023; Singhal et al., 2023b). There are also alignment methods directly aimed at multi-attribute
132   alignment. Zhou et al. (2023) includes preference on multiple attributes in the Direct Preference
133   Optimization (DPO) loss function (Rafailov et al., 2024), trying to optimize preference rewards for
134   all attributes simultaneously. Lou et al. (2024) proposed to achieve multi-attribute alignment se-
135
      quentially, one attribute at a time, where LLMs learns to align with new attributes while staying
      aligned with previous dimensions.

138   3.2   RLHF, O FFLINE RL AND U NCERTAINTY
139
140   In RLHF, LLM policy is optimized via interactions with the RM, whose training data is pre-collected
141
      preference pairs (Bai et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022). In this setting, RLHF falls into the category
      of offline RL, where RL policies cannot interact with the environment and get feedbacks in real time,
142
      but instead can only be updated based on an offline dataset collected by some other policy (Levine
143
      et al., 2020). Offline RL is notoriously difficult due to the distributional shift issue (Lou et al., 2022;
144   Ma et al., 2021; Prudencio et al., 2023). Recent advancements in iterative LLM alignment methods
145   (Yuan et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2024) iterates between LLM fine-tuning and the
146   sampling and annotation of new training data, alleviating the distributional shift issue. Although
147   these iterative methods aim to transcend the constraints of the offline setting,, RLHF is still offline
148   within each iteration.
149
      An important topic in offline RL is uncertainty quantification, which has long underpinned many
150   critical roles (Abdar et al., 2021), such as trustworthy decision-making (Huang et al., 2019; Eriksson
151   & Dimitrakakis, 2019) and improving reliability of machine learning models (Wang et al., 2019). In
152   offline RL, uncertainty quantification enables out-of-distribution data detection and keep the policy
153   within the offline dataset’s support area through conservative updates to avoid distributional shift
154   (Yu et al., 2020; Kidambi et al., 2020; An et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2024). So it is natural to introduce
155   uncertainty to RLHF to make LLM alignment more reliable and effective.
156   Ensemble of RMs are discussed in previous works. However, we study the ensemble of uncertainty-
157   aware RMs to identify unreliable reward evaluations, while previous discussions are limited to using
158   RM ensembles to mitigate reward hacking Coste et al. (2023); Eisenstein et al. (2023) and using
159   value heads to disentangle length and quality in reward modeling Chen et al. (2024b). We notice a
160   concurrent work QRM Dorka (2024) which also models human preferences by distributions. QRM
161   only studies distributional RMs, while we also study the ensemble of such uncertainty-aware RMs.

206   Traditional RMs optimize the Bradley-Terry model loss (BT-loss) in Eq. 2,to enlarge the discrep-

209   mapping is deterministic and thus cannot catch any uncertainty (Chua et al., 2018) within the reward
210   modeling process.
211   However, at its core, human preferences exhibit a distinctly probabilistic nature, rather than being

213   sistencies introduced by human annotators (Sylolypavan et al., 2023; Sleeman & Gilhooly, 2023;
214   Chen et al., 2024a). Between individuals, preferences differ from person to person. This means
215   what’s preferable for one may not be for another. Even within individuals, preferences are not static.
      They can swing based on numerous factors such as mood and context. These stochastic natures of

358   5.1   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
359
360   In our experiment, URM is based on Llama3.1 with 8 billion parameters. Before adding the prob-
361   abilistic value head, we initialize URM’s base model with weights from Liu & Zeng (2024). The
362   gating layer consists of two fully-connected layers with hidden size 4096 activated by SELU (Klam-
363   bauer et al., 2017). More information on URM training and implementation is given in the appendix
364   A.1, C.1. URME have 3 URMs with different random seeds, probabilistic value head initialization
365   and mini-batches of training data.
366   We utilize HelpSteer 2 (Wang et al., 2024c) as the training dataset to train the base model and the
367   probabilistic value head for 1 epoch with learning rate 2 ⇥ 10 6 . After obtaining the attribute-
368   specific uncertain-aware probabilistic value head and base model, we keep them frozen and train the
369   gating layer on Skywork-reward-preference-80k (Liu & Zeng, 2024) for 4000 steps with batchsize
370   256. During training, we held out 4k data from the dataset as validation set to choose the checkpoint
371   with highest validation accuracy.
372   RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024) , our evaluation benchmark for RMs, has 2985 questions and
373   response pairs. For multi-attribute RMs and BT-model RMs, a prediction for a response pair is
374   correct if the RM gives a higher reward to the chosen response than the rejected response. For
375   generative models, RewardBench evaluates them via LLM-as-a-judge (Zheng et al., 2023). If the
376   generative model prioritizes the chosen response than the rejected response, the prediction is seen
377   as correct. To test URM and URME’s ability in improving LLMs’ generation quality, we evaluate

456   Now we study the uncertainty quantification of URM and URME and how they behave when dealing
457
      in-distribution and OOD data. Given the challenge inherent in identifying what precisely is OOD
      for LLMs, we adopt numeric calculations as simulated OOD data. This is because LLMs are known
458

463
      data. Therefore, this uncertainty quantified by the variance can serve as an informative tool for
      identifying and filtering out OOD data, where reward models exhibit a tendency towards making
464
      uniform guess than providing an accurate evaluation. This strategy ensures the evaluated outcomes

523   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525   In this paper, we study the uncertainty issue in reward modeling for LLMs. Uncertainty-aware Re-
526   ward Model (URM) and Uncertain-aware Reward Model Ensemble (URME) are proposed to model
527
      and quantify the uncertainty during reward modeling. Unlike previous methods that determinis-
      tically map hidden states to reward scalars, URM and URME model the distribution of rewards
528
      and evaluate prediction confidence by uncertainty quantification. Notably, among RMs with 8B
529
      or smaller model size, URM achieves state-of-the-art performance on RewardBench, surpassing a
530   number of larger models. Empirical evidence further validates that through uncertainty quantifica-
531   tion, URM and URME can effectively evaluate their level of knowledge for input data, leading to
532   more reliable reward predictions.
533
      The limitation of our paper is that our experiment for generation improvements is limited to best-of-
534
      n due to the limit of computation resources. In the future, we plan to introduce URM and URME to
535
      prevailing LLM alignment methods like RLHF Ouyang et al. (2022) and iterative DPO Yuan et al.
536   (2024). Another direction to look at is model merging for URMs in the weight space (Ramé et al.,
537   2024), which demonstrate competitive efficiency and robustness compared to ensembles. A simple
538   empirical study to RM merging with URMs is included in the appendix C.1.
