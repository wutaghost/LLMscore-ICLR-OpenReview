011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Frequency-domain data augmentation (DA) has shown strong performance in time-
014
               series prediction due to its ability to preserve data-label consistency. However,
               we observed that existing frequency-domain augmentations introduce excessive
015
               variability, leading to out-of-distribution samples that may be harmful to model
016
               performance. To address this, we introduced two simple modifications to frequency-
017            domain DA. First, we limit perturbations to dominant frequencies with larger mag-
018            nitudes, which capture the main periodicities and trends of the signal. Second,
019            instead of using complicated random perturbations, we simply shuffle the dominant
020            frequency components, which preserves the original structure while avoiding exter-
021            nal noise. With the two simple modifications, we proposed dominant shuffle—a
022            simple yet highly effective data augmentation technique for time-series prediction.
023            Our method is remarkably simple, requiring only a few lines of code, yet excep-
024            tionally effective, consistently and significantly improving model performance.
025
               Extensive experiments on short-term, long term, few-shot and cold-start prediction
               tasks with eight state-of-the-art models, nine existing augmentation methods and
026
               twelve datasets demonstrate that dominant shuffle consistently boosts model per-
027
               formance with substantial gains, outperforming existing augmentation techniques.
028            Our method is simple, practical, and effective. The code is available at https:
029            //anonymous.4open.science/r/dominant-shuffle-A70E.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Time-series prediction aims to forecast multivariate future values based on historical observations. It
035   is a long-standing problem with various applications in electricity pricing, weather forecast, traffic
036   prediction Lim & Zohren (2021); Zhou et al. (2021). Recently, impressive results have been achieved
037   by using various deep learning architectures, e.g. recurrent neural networks (RNNs) Rangapuram
038   et al. (2018); Salinas et al. (2020); Ma et al. (2020), Transformers Zhou et al. (2021); Wu et al.
039
      (2021); Zhou et al. (2022b); Liu et al. (2024), and temporal convolutional networks (TCNs) Wang
      et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2023). Neural networks require a large volume of training
040
      data to effectively fit their numerous parameters. Unfortunately, time-series data acquired from
041
      real-world sensors are often limited in many time-series applications. The patterns of the time series
042   heavily depend on specific dynamic system that generates the data and other data sources are not
043   applicable Chen et al. (2023a); Semenoglou et al. (2023).
044
045
      To mitigate the impact of insufficient data in time series analysis, several data augmentation techniques
      have been explored. Most of these data augmentation techniques in time series analysis focus on
046
      classification Qian et al. (2022); Um et al. (2017); Le Guennec et al. (2016); Steven Eyobu &
047
      Han (2018); Nam et al. (2020); Lim & Zohren (2021); Zhang et al. (2022b); Chen et al. (2023b)
048   and anomaly detection Lim et al. (2018); Lim & Zohren (2021); Gao et al. (2020). The data-label
049   coherence is a key factor to effective data augmentation Wen et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2023); Sun
050   et al. (2023). It measures the semantic connection between the augmented data and the label. These
051   augmentations designed for classification modify only the input time series (data) without affecting
052   the class labels, ensuring data-label coherence as long as the perturbed sample remains within
053   the original category. However, the prediction task requires more fine-grained historical temporal
      variation to accurately estimate future dynamics Zhang et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023a). Only

      Recently, to improve the data-label coherence for time-series prediction, Chen et al. (2023a) proposed
      to perturb the combined data (historical sequence) and labels (future sequences) in the frequency
075
      domain. This method first merges the data and label into a single sequence, then applies random
076
      perturbations in frequency domain, followed by conversion back to the time domain. However,
077   existing frequency-domain augmentations apply random perturbations across the full spectrum1 ,
078   which can lead to excessive changes and produce samples that fall outside the original data distribution.
079   Incorporating these out-of-distribution (OOD) samples into the training set creates a domain gap
080   between the training and testing sets, which negatively affects model performance, particularly
081   when a large number of augmented samples are used. In addition, random perturbations such as
082   FreqMix, FreqAdd Zhang et al. (2022b) could introduce external noise and further enlarge the
083   original-augmented gap.
084   In this paper, to reduce the domain gap between the augmented and original data, we propose to
085   limit the perturbation in data augmentation. First, we restrict the perturbation to specific frequencies
086   instead of full-spectrum perturbation. Several recent studies have pointed out that a few frequency
087   components are dominating the periodicity and main trends of the time series, and other Frequencies
088   correspond to minor trends or noise Wu et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2022b;a). Following Wu et al.
089   (2023), we perturb top-k frequencies with highest magnitudes. Second, to avoid excess external noise,
090   we use random shuffle for perturbation. Shuffle rearranges existing components without introducing
091
      any external randomness.
092   Extensive comparisons were made among eight state-of-the-art (SOTA) time series models, nine
093   different data augmentation methods on eight public datasets using. These comparisons demonstrate
094   that, despite its simplicity, our method significantly outperforms other competitors by a substantial
095   margin. As shown in Fig. 1, our method consistently improves the performance of iTransformer Liu
096   et al. (2024) model across various datasets, and outperforms other DA methods such as MSB Bandara
097
      et al. (2021), FreqPool Chen et al. (2023b), FreqMask Chen et al. (2023a), FreqMix Chen et al.
      (2023a), RobustASD Gao et al. (2020), and STAug Zhang et al. (2023) in most cases.
098
099   Comprehensive ablation studies demonstrate that perturbing dominant frequencies yields significantly
100   better performance than various full-spectrum perturbations. And shuffle is proven to be superior
101   to other randomization techniques. Besides, our augmentation demonstrates improved augmented-
102   original gap over other augmentations, confirmed by both qualitative visualizations and quantitative
103
      results.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110   In the last decade, deep learning has emerged as a powerful tool in time-series prediction and has
111   shown superior performance over traditional statistical methods such as ARIMA and Exponential
112   Smoothing McKenzie (1984). A rich line of studies has introduced various deep-learning architectures,
113   including recurrent neural networks (RNNs) Rangapuram et al. (2018); Salinas et al. (2020); Ma
114   et al. (2020), temporal convolution neural networks (TCNs) Wang et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2022); Wu
115   et al. (2023), and Transformers Wu et al. (2021); Ni et al. (2023); Nie et al. (2023a); Liu et al. (2024);
116   Zhou et al. (2022b). These models learn to predict the future from large volumes of historical data.
117   Various data augmentations have been proposed for time series data and many of these techniques
118   were proposed for the classification tasks Wen et al. (2021); Qian et al. (2022); Um et al. (2017);
119   Le Guennec et al. (2016); Steven Eyobu & Han (2018); Nam et al. (2020); Lim & Zohren (2021);
120   Zhang et al. (2022b); Chen et al. (2023b). Many of these methods regard time series data as one-
121   dimensional image and borrowed data augmentations, e.g. cropping Le Guennec et al. (2016); Cui
122   et al. (2016) flipping Wen et al. (2021), and noise injection Wen & Keyes (2019), from computer
123   vision. Window warping Wen et al. (2021) is a time series-specific data augmentation that upsamples
124
      (or downsamples) a random range of the time series while keeping other time ranges unchanged.
125   In addition to time-domain augmentations, there are also methods that perturb the original data in
126   the frequency domain. Gao Gao et al. (2020) proposed to add noise on both magnitude and phase
127   in the frequency domain. Zhang Zhang et al. (2022b) proposed to add single or multiple frequency
128   components in the first half of the frequency spectrum. Chen Chen et al. (2023b) proposed to perform
129   pooling or smoothing operations in the frequency domain.
130   While most of the augmentations focus on the classification tasks, a few methods for forecasting task
131   have also been explored. Bandara Bandara et al. (2021) introduces two DA methods for forecasting :
132   (i) Average selected with distance (ASD), which generates augmented time series using the weighted
133   sum of multiple time series, and the weights are determined by the dynamic time warping (DTW)
134   distance Forestier et al. (2017); (ii) Moving block bootstrapping (MBB) generates augmented data by
135   manipulating the residual part of the time series after STL Decomposition Semenoglou et al. (2023)
136
      and recombining it with the other series. Zhang Zhang et al. (2023) proposed to simultaneously
      augment in frequency and time domains. Recently, Chen et. al. Chen et al. (2023a) proposed to
137
      augment both the data (historical sequence) and the label (future sequence) in the frequency domain
138
      to improve the data-label coherence. Although this method generally achieves decent results, full-
139   spectrum randomization imposes a large domain gap between the augmented and the original data,
140   sometimes leading to degraded performance.

146   Time-series prediction is a sequence-to-sequence problem where the model estimates a future mul-
147   tivariate sequence based on a sequence of historical measurements. Let x = {x1 , x2 , ..., xL }L
                                                                                                     t=1 ∈
148   RL×D be the D-dimensional historical sequence, and y = {xL+1 , xL+2 , ..., xL+T }L+T

149   is the future sequence to be estimated. xt is the measurement at timestep t and D is the number of
150   variates. Next, we will use x ∈ RL×D and y ∈ RT ×D to denote the historical and future sequences.
151   x and y are the input and output of time-series prediction models, respectively.
152
      Deep neural networks learn the x → y mapping from large volume of (x, y) pairs. DA expands
153
      the training set by adding perturbations to existing pairs. Ensuring data-label coherence hinges on
154   applying consistent perturbations to both the data and label. Chen et al. (2023a) proposed to merge
155   historical and future sequences, and then perturb the merged sequence in the frequency domain. The

175   3.2        D OMINANT F REQUENCY S HUFFLE
176
177   Existing frequency-domain augmentations like FreqMix and FreqMask Chen et al. (2023a) may
178   perturb arbitrary frequency components, potentially causing significant deviations from the original
179   data and introducing out-of-distribution (OOD) issues. For instance, masking high-magnitude
180
      frequencies or mixing a high-magnitude frequency with a low-magnitude one can drastically alter the
      original signal. We analyzed the features of augmented and original samples to highlight the OOD
181
      problem. Fig. 2 presents the t-SNE Van der Maaten & Hinton (2008) visualization of features of
182
      different augmented (orange) samples and original (blue) samples. Obvious domain gap can be found
183   in full-spectrum perturbations such as FreqMask, FreqMix, and FreqAdd. Fig. 4 (c) illustrates the
184   results of swapping (mixing) a minor frequency and a dominant frequency, which generates samples
185   that are significantly away from the original data.
186
      To tackle the OOD issue and align the distributions between augmented and original samples, we
187
      introduced Dominant Shuffle, which reduces perturbations during data augmentation. Let F (ω) =
188
      FFT([x, y]) be the the complex frequency-domain representation of original time series, we perturb
189   in the frequency domain by shuffling dominant frequency components. Let Ωk = [ω1 , ω2 , ..., ωk ] be

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUPS
236
237
      Implementation details All the experiments were conducted with the PyTorch Paszke et al. (2019)
238
      framework on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU. We reproduced the results of other data augmenta-
239
      tions using their official code or following the original papers. Please refer to appendix A.2 for the
240   details about our reimplementations. We only changed the data augmentation for fair comparisons.
241   Following the practice of Chen et al. (2023a), we performed data augmentations to double the size of
242   the original training dataset unless otherwise specified.
243
      Evaluation protocols We tested our method with short-term and long-term prediction protocols. In
244
      the long-term protocol, the prediction period T ranges from 96 to 720, with variations at 96, 192,
245
      336, and 720. In contrast, the short-term protocol has prediction periods ranging from 12 to 48,
246   with variations at 12, 24, 36, and 48. Following the common practice of previous works Zhou et al.
247   (2021); Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2022b); Liu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023),
248   we quantified the performance of the prediction using the mean-squared error (MSE) between the
249   ground-truth and the prediction.
250
      Datasets For long-term prediction, we experimented on eight well-established benchmarks: the ETT
251
      datasets (ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2) Zhou et al. (2021), and the Weather, Electricity, Exchange,
252   and Traffic datasets Wu et al. (2021). For short-term prediction, following iTransformer Liu et al.
253   (2024), we used four public traffic network datasets (PEMS03, PEMS04, PEMS07, PEMS08) from
254   PEMS Chen et al. (2001). Each dataset is divided into training, testing, and evaluation subsets in
255   specific ratios. The training, testing, and evaluation ratio is 6:2:2 for ETT and PEMS datasets, and
256   the ratio is 7:1:2 for Electricity, Traffic, Weather, and Exchange-rate datasets. Detailed statistics of
257   these datasets are summarized in appendix A.1. For each setting (dataset+prediction length T ), we
258   tuned the optimal number of dominant frequencies k on the evaluation set. The optimal k on various
259   datasets can be found in appendix B.4.
260   Baseline Models We selected diverse models as the baseline in our experiments, including ,
261   Autoformer Wu et al. (2021), Lightts Zhang et al. (2022a), SCINet Liu et al. (2022), TiDE Das
262   et al. (2023), MICN Wang et al. (2023), PatchTST Nie et al. (2023b), iTransformer Liu et al. (2024),
263   PDF Dai et al. (2024), PathFormer Chen et al. (2024). For short-term prediction, we used the SOTA
264   iTransformer Liu et al. (2024) on PEMS dataset Chen et al. (2001) as the baseline model.
265   Other data augmentation methods We compared the proposed method with nine existing data
266   augmentation methods, including three time-domain augmentations (ASD Forestier et al. (2017),
267   MSB Bandara et al. (2021) Upsample Semenoglou et al. (2023)), five frequency-domain meth-
268   ods (FreqMix Chen et al. (2023a), FreqMask Chen et al. (2023a), FreqAdd Zhang et al. (2022b),
269   FreqPool Chen et al. (2023b), Robusttad Gao et al. (2020)), and a temporal-frequency method
      STAug Zhang et al. (2023).

531
532
      We proposed the dominant shuffle, a simple yet highly effective data augmentation technique for
533
      time series prediction. Our method mitigates the domain gap between augmented and original data
534   by limiting the perturbation to dominant frequencies, and uses shuffles to avoid external noises.
535   Although our method is straightforward and effective, it is primarily based on heuristics and lacks a
536   deep theoretical foundation. Instead of relying on theoretical justifications, we performed extensive
537   experiments across a diverse range of datasets, baseline models, and augmentation methods to validate
538   its consistent improvements under various configurations. Investigating the theoretical justifications
539   and principles of the proposed method presents a promising avenue for future research that could
      enhance our understanding of its mechanisms.
