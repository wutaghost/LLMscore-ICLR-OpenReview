010                                                    A BSTRACT
011
012            There is increasing interest in tracking the capabilities of general intelligence founda-
013            tion models. This study benchmarks leading large language models and vision language
014            models against human performance on the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale (WAIS-IV),
015            a comprehensive, population-normed assessment of underlying human cognition and in-
016
               tellectual abilities, with a focus on the domains of Verbal Comprehension (VCI), Work-
               ing Memory (WMI), and Perceptual Reasoning (PRI). Most models demonstrated excep-
017
               tional capabilities in the storage, retrieval, and manipulation of tokens such as arbitrary se-
018
               quences of letters and numbers, with performance on the Working Memory Index (WMI)
019            greater or equal to the 99.5th percentile when compared to human population normative
020            ability. Performance on the Verbal Comprehension Index (VCI) which measures retrieval
021            of acquired information, and linguistic understanding about the meaning of words and
022            their relationships to each other, also demonstrated consistent performance at or above the
023            98th percentile. Despite these broad strengths, we observed consistently poor performance
024            on the Perceptual Reasoning Index (PRI; range 0.1-10th percentile) from multimodal mod-
025            els indicating profound inability to interpret and reason on visual information. Smaller and
026            older model versions consistently performed worse, indicating that training data, parame-
027
               ter count, and advances in tuning are resulting in significant advances in cognitive ability.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) refers to a class of models capable of creating new content, whether
033
      it is text, images, music, or even code (Team et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Gardner et al., 2023; Ouyang et al.,
      2023). Unlike traditional AI systems designed for specific tasks, these models learn the underlying patterns
034
      and structures within vast datasets and then use this knowledge to generate novel outputs that often mimic
035
      human creativity (Zhao et al., 2023; Nath et al., 2024). The excitement surrounding GenAI stems from its
036   potential to revolutionize numerous fields by performing human-like cognitive functions (Wang et al., 2024;
037   Zhuang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). This ability to understand, learn, adapt, and create in a way that
038   mirrors our own thought processes opens doors to groundbreaking applications across industries like art,
039   design, research, and communication (Ko et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024).
040   However, human cognition encompasses a vast array of specialized abilities in the processing, storage,
041   interpretation, and generation of information across auditory and visual channels to accomplish these unique
042   human capabilities (Cao et al., 2024; Subramonyam et al., 2023).
043   Recent advances in generative AI have been driven by large parameter models (e.g., the GPT (Achiam et al.,
044   2023), Gemini (Team et al., 2023) families) trained with a broad range of textual content. Scaling exper-
045   iments have shown that testing losses often follow power law relationships with data, model size (number
046   of parameters) and compute (Kaplan et al., 2020). This progress has extended to multimodal models that

      To assess their potential and limitations as models of human cognition, it is crucial to characterize the cog-
081
      nitive capabilities of generative models and understand how they compare directly to human ability (Ilić &
082
      Gignac, 2024). This will facilitate an understanding of pragmatic capabilities, limitations, and an ability to
083   track progress just as is done with human normative cognitive tests of ability. It will also provide insight into
084   unique streams of cognitive development of generative artificial intelligence under the assumption that mod-
085   els, like biological organisms, will develop unique capabilities based on their underlying neural architecture
086   and environmental demands.
087
      General cognitive capabilities have been systematically researched and benchmarked in humans for over a
088
      century (Spearman, 1961; Sternberg et al., 1981; Weiss et al., 2013). Refined standardized tests of ability and
089   performance that are normed in large representative population-based samples allow for individual perfor-
090   mance scores to be compared directly to normative performance. Because tests such as the Wechsler Adult
091   Intelligence Scale–Fourth Edition (WAIS-IV; (Wechsler, 2008)) have population norms for comparison, in-
092   dividual scores can be used to make decisions about relative ability, and can be used to inform the allocation
093   of special resources for education, housing, care, or competency. They can also be utilized to identify excep-

      tional ability and gaps between areas of performance that are diagnostic of specific neurocognitive disorders
095
      (e.g., Autism Spectrum Disorder, Dyslexia; (Neisser et al., 1996)).
096
097   Beyond the focus on comparison to human performance, there may be value in the development of specific
098   tests of GenAI cognition across discernible domains that are analogous to human tests akin to the devel-
099
      opment of analogous tests of cognition in animal models. Indeed, models of cognitive constructs such as
      working memory, semantic understanding, and visual processing, have facilitated research to understand the
100
      underlying neural architecture and mechanisms of cognition. Similar to the relative capabilities of the visual
101
      cortex of an hawk compared to a human – both display impressive but highly specialized abilities – we may
102   find that GenAI models, by virtue of their architecture and context, develop novel patterns in cognitive func-
103   tioning that prove to be quite different from humans, where, for example, consistent non-negligible positive
104   correlations have been demonstrated between many cognitive ability test scores (Detterman & Daniel, 1989;
105   Walker et al., 2023). This phenomenon, which is known as the Positive Manifold (Jensen, 1998), has been
106   demonstrated to hold for LLMs using acceptable, yet non-validated, subtest proxies of the VCI and WMI
107   cognitive ability tests (Ilić & Gignac, 2024).
108   We compared the performance of GenAI models on tests of cognitive performance that are commonly used to
109   index human intellectual ability. We focused on tests of verbal ability including linguistic-based knowledge
110   and conceptual understanding, capabilities to temporarily store, retrieve and manipulate arbitrary informa-
111   tion, and capabilities to understand, reason, and manipulate visual information. Both individual test scores
112   and composite index scores provide nuanced and interpretable information about intellectual capabilities
113   and both were used to evaluate and better understand the capabilities of generative models.
114
115   2     M ETHODS
116
117   To facilitate the assessment of GenAI models using the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale, Fourth Edition
118   (WAIS-IV), we implemented a series of methodological adaptations to accommodate the unique input and
119   output modalities of these models. This involved converting traditional verbal and visual stimuli into text-
120   based prompts (see Appendix B) and interpreting model-generated text outputs as responses to test items.
121   Specific adaptations for each subtest, along with validation procedures, are detailed in 2.1. For this study, we
122   selected a representative set of state-of-the-art (SOTA) large language models (LLMs) and vision language
123   models (VLMs), encompassing a range of model sizes, architectures, and training datasets (See section 2.2).
124
125   2.1   T ESTING MATERIALS , ADMINISTRATION , AND SCORING
126
127
      Individual items from WAIS-IV subtests (Wechsler, 2008) were converted to prompts to be individually
      administered to the selected LLM (See Table 1 for a full list of tests with descriptions). Specifically, tasks
128
      comprising the verbal comprehension and working memory indices (VCI and WMI) were converted to
129
      language-based prompts and administered to all models including those with language-only capabilities
130   as well as those with multimodal language and visual capabilities. Tests from the perceptual reasoning
131   index (PRI) were only administered to multimodal models because they require both image recognition and
132   language capabilities. We were unable to administer the Block Design subtest from the PRI, because it
133   requires manual manipulation of real-world objects (cubes). However, valid PRI composite scores were
134   still calculable substituting the alternate PRI subtest Figure Weights (Wechsler, 2008). Subtests from the
135   Processing Speed Index (PSI) were not administered as there was no clear way to maintain fidelity to the
136   WAIS-IV testing procedures that are required for valid comparison to human performance norms. A full-
137   scale IQ score (FSIQ) could not be calculated for any model as PSI is a required component of FSIQ.
138   In each instance, the prompt presented to the model consisted of the instructions as outlined in the manual
139   (Wechsler, 2008), including the example provided as part of the administration process. However, certain
140   adaptations were necessary to accommodate the specific requirements of the model. Several tests involved


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


141
      reading the test content, while others utilized visual and physical aids (e.g., cards and boards). These tests
142
      were converted into prompts, and in some cases, the translation provided the GenAI models with an advan-
143   tage due to their ability to access the full context while generating responses. Additionally, phrases such as
144   ”You can only ask me to read the problem one more time” were omitted, as GenAI models are trained on in-
145   structions and often request repetition without responding to the actual assessment being conducted. Phrases
146   such as ”Just say what I say” or ”Listen” were not removed and were left as they were originally presented,
147   causing some models to mistakenly provide responses such as ”I am a text-based chat assistant and thus I
148   cannot hear or repeat the numbers.” and ”Okay, I’m listening!.”. No further instructions or inquiries were
149   provided. No additional instructions or queries were provided.
150   Each subtest consists of progressively challenging questions whose difficulty-level is normed against the
151   population. For example, the Digit Span test, in which subjects are requested to verbally repeat a sequence
152   of digits read aloud to them, begins with a sequence of three digits and progresses to a sequence of eight or
153   nine digits depending on the subtest. Primary WAIS-IV composite indices and related cognitive domains are
154   described below, along with brief descriptions of each subtest and administration procedures (See Table 1).
155
156
      Verbal Comprehension Index These tests assess verbal knowledge and reasoning capacity. Subtests
157
      include: Similarities, in which the subject must state how two words are alike (e.g., ”How are a kite and an
158   airplane alike?”); Vocabulary, in which the subject provides definitions of words; Information in which the
159   subject answers questions on a broad set of topics (e.g., ”Who was the first president of the United States?”);
160   and Comprehension in which subjects are asked to reason on common sense narrative scenarios.
161
162
163   Working Memory Index These subtests probe the capacity to store, manipulate, and reorder information,
164   including reasoning numerically. Digit Span requires subjects to repeat back random number strings accord-
165
      ing to varying rules (i.e., Forwards, Backwards, and Sequencing conditions). Arithmetic requires subjects
      to solve math word problems of increasing difficulty without pen and paper. Letter-Number Sequencing
166
      requires subjects to manipulate arbitrary letter-number strings into alphabetic and numerical order.
167
168
169   Perceptual Reasoning Index Involves tests of visuospatial skills and nonverbal abstract reasoning. Matrix
170   Reasoning (Ex: Fig. 2) requires subjects to choose the correct image from multiple options to successfully
171   complete a visual pattern. Visual Puzzles (Ex: Fig. 3) requires subjects to select the correct tile to complete
172   a picture, assessing mental rotation and spatial relations ability. In Figure Weights (Ex: Fig. 4), subjects
173   are presented with an analogue scale depicting shapes of relative weight (e.g., two blue squares equals one
174
      red triangle). The subject is presented with a balanced scale demonstrating the weight relationship between
      objects, and an incomplete scale with shapes on only one side. They must then select from multiple choices
175
      to balance the incomplete scale. Picture Completion is a test in which subjects are presented with an image
176
      that is missing some key characteristic (e.g., an image of a woman without a shadow standing next to a sign
177   that is casting a shadow). The subject must verbally indicate what is missing in the image.
178
179   All answers to prompts were scored by one of two clinical psychologists trained to administer and score
180
      the WAIS-IV. Any ambiguous results were reviewed by both psychologists to reach a consensus on scoring.
      Individual test scores were collated into raw index scores and then converted to age-normed scores with
181
      accompanying performance percentiles. As different norms exist for different age ranges, we selected the
182
      norms for age 25 years to 29 years and 11 months. In addition to the calculation of composite indices, we also
183   compared individual subtests to domain-specific averages and composite scores in a series of discrepancy
184   analyses, in order to probe the relative strengths and weaknesses of the models. The WAIS-IV provides
185   standardized scoring for a variety of such comparisons, to determine if performance on an individual subtest
186   might represent a statistically significant deviation from what would be expected in the population of those
187   with similar performance profiles.

240   3   R ESULTS
241
242   Index level results (VCI, WMI, PRI) demonstrate both exceptional capabilities and significant deficits when
243   compared to a normative population (See Table 2 for full results). All but one model demonstrated abilities
244   on the VCI in the Very Superior range, scoring within the top percentile of human normative ability with the
245
      exception of Gemini Flash which fell in the Superior range (82nd %ile) and Gemini Nano (23rd %ile) which
      demonstrated ability in the Borderline range. The majority of models also performed in the Very Superior
246
      range on the WMI, with the exception of Gemini Nano (37th %ile; Low Average). However, among the
247
      models with multimodal capabilities, performance on the PRI demonstrated a significant and consistent
248   deficit with all models demonstrating capabilities in the Extremely Low range (< 1st %ile) with the lone
249   exception of Claude Sonnet which demonstrated ability in the Low Average range (10th %tile).
250
251
      Next, we analyzed individual differences in index-level results to understand relative strengths and weak-
      nesses within domains of intellectual ability (See Table 3 for full Results). First, we observed a consistent
252
      relative strength in WMI compared to other indexes representing a statistically significant difference com-
253
      pared to the normative population (p<.15 or p<.05 level). Overall,this indicates that models’ generally
254   evidence stronger ability to process, store and manipulate information compared to any other ability, includ-
255   ing language. Further, all models with visual/multimodal capability to perform tests comprising the PRI
256   demonstrated significantly worse perceptual reasoning performance p<.05) when compared verbal compre-
257   hension and working memory indexes. This indicates a general relative weakness that is invariant across
258   distinct developers and models in the ability to understand and reason on visual information.
259
      Next, we examined performance on individual tests within each index (See Table 3 and Table 4). Within the
260   WMI, a consistent relative weakness in Arithmetic, was observed in comparison to Digit Span, indicating
261   a relative weakness in mathematical reasoning compared to the ability to encode, manipulate, and reorga-
262   nize numeric values. Gemini Nano demonstrated further relative weaknesses in the ability to sequence and
263   reverse numbers compared to the ability to encode and retrieve simple numeric lists. Analysis of the VCI
264   demonstrated consistent relative strengths across models from independent developers, on the Information
265   subtest compared to Similarities and Vocabulary (p<.05 and p<.15). This indicates that models are par-
266   ticularly strong in the storage and retrieval of natural language-encoded knowledge – often referred to as
267   “crystallized knowledge” in human subjects – in comparison to the cognitive capabilities required for verbal
268
      analogic and verbal abstract reasoning and semantic understanding. This difference in relative performance
      persisted across generations of models indicating that models are consistently stronger in predicting the
269
      write answer than understanding and reasoning with language concepts. Finally, within the PRI, relative
270
      differences in opposite directions between best-in class models were observed with GPT-4o demonstrating
271   stronger relative ability in decoding Visual Puzzles and Claude Sonnet demonstrating significantly stronger
272   relative performance in Matrix Reasoning.
273
274
      Wide variability in performance was observed on the VCI. While scores were highly skewed towards excep-
      tional performance (99.5th %ile to >99.9th %ile, models that are known to be small parameter counts demon-
275
      strated the worst relative and absolute performance (Gemini Flash = 82nd %ile; Gemini Nano = 23rd %ile).
276
      Similarly, most models performed exceptionally on the WMI (Range, 95th %ile - >99.9th %ile) with the
277   exception of Gemini Nano (37th %ile). In contrast, all models tested demonstrated poor performance on the
278   PRI (Range, <.01th %ile - 10th %ile), with skewed performance such that the majority of models fell below
279   the 2nd %ile. The exception is Claude Sonnet which demonstrates a significant increase over its predecessor
280   Claude Opus indicating that while performance overall is poor, there is measurable progress in the ability
281   for generative models to reason on visual concepts.

      The above results clearly demonstrate that for current SoTA models the Positive Manifold, which posits
      positive correlations between different cognitive capabilities, holds when VCI and WMI are considered (as
457
      demonstrated in Ilić & Gignac (2024), and fails to hold for when including PRI as well.
458
459   The current work presents with the significant limitations that the model parameters themselves (underlying
460   training data, parameter count, tuning approach) are all proprietary across all examples and thus not available
461   for comparative analysis. While this does not limit the validity of the tests as these parameters are not known
462
      with human subjects either, it does limit the ability to draw definitive conclusions about factors that influence
      performance. This hampers scientific inquiry into GenAI models as model organisms to understand the
463
      acquisition of cognitive capabilities. The study is further limited by the inherently non-standard approach to
464
      WAIS-IV administration. While we attempted to copy key testing conditions and excluded tests that could
465   not conform, there is an inherent limitation in the difference in testing setup from that which the scores were
466   normed on. Despite these limitations, the current work represents the first of its kind approach to benchmark
467   GenAI against human norms of intelligence. The results demonstrate the unique cognitive capabilities of
468   current generative AI models as well as relative weaknesses, while providing a path to advance discrete
469   domains of cognition that underlie wide sets of human cognitive abilities.
