009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The rise of large language models (LLMs) has increased concerns over source
012            tracing and copyright protection for AI-generated content (AIGC), highlighting
013            the need for advanced detection technologies. Passive detection methods usually
014            face high false positives, while active watermarking techniques using logits or
015            sampling manipulation offer more effective protection. Existing LLM watermark-
016
               ing methods, though effective on unaltered content, suffer significant performance
               drops when the text is modified and could introduce biases that degrade LLM per-
017
               formance in downstream tasks. These methods fail to achieve an optimal tradeoff
018
               between text quality and robustness, particularly due to the lack of end-to-end
019            optimization of the encoder and decoder. In this paper, we introduce the first
020            end-to-end logits perturbation method for watermarking LLM-generated text. By
021            jointly optimizing the encoder and decoder, our approach achieves a better bal-
022            ance between quality and robustness. To address non-differentiable operations in
023            the end-to-end training pipeline, we introduce an online prompting technique that
024            leverages the on-the-fly LLM as a differentiable surrogate. Our method demon-
025            strates superior detection robustness, consistently outperforming state-of-the-art
026            (SOTA) methods by 1.2%, 4.0%, and 5.5% across 3 LLMs, averaged over 6 types
027
               of text distortions. Simultaneously, our approach achieves exceptional text quality,
               as evidenced by reduced text perplexity and improved performance in the down-
028
               stream tasks with a margin of 19.2% and 3.03%. Our method can be easily gener-
029
               alized to different LLMs. The code is available in supplementary material.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023), Llama (Touvron et al., 2023;
035   Dubey et al., 2024), and OPT (Zhang et al., 2022) have greatly improved the quality of AI-generated
036   content (AIGC), broadening their applications in fields such as translation (Hendy et al., 2023), con-
037   tent creation (Ni et al., 2023), etc. However, such a rapid expansion has also raised concerns about
038   copyright infringement, academic dishonesty, and unethical practices (Augenstein et al., 2024).
039
      These issues highlight the urgent need for reliable methods to distinguish between human-written
      text and LLM-generated content, ensuring digital integrity and combating misinformation (Barrett
040
      et al., 2023).
041
042   Numerous approaches have been proposed to address LLM ethical concerns by detecting LLM-
043   generated content. Passive detection methods focus on identifying unique properties of generated
044   text, often through training binary classifiers (Bakhtin et al., 2019; Jawahar et al., 2020) or statis-
045
      tical techniques like DetectGPT (Mitchell et al., 2023), which compares the log probability value
      of a sentence with its perturbed version. However, as LLMs improve and the gap between gen-
046
      erated and human-written text narrows, the effectiveness of these methods declines dramatically.
047
      In contrast, active detection methods, like embedding watermarks in generated text, are proving
048   to be more robust alternatives. LLM watermarking methods fall into two main categories: logits-
049   based and sampling-based. Specifically, logits-based methods (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
050   2024b; Huo et al., 2024) randomly divide the vocabulary into “green” and “red” lists by hashing
051   preceding tokens as the seed. Then, perturbations are introduced to the logits that favor green list
052   tokens in the generated text, and the proportion of the “green” tokens is used to distinguish whether
053   a text is LLM-generated. Sampling-based methods (Kuditipudi et al., 2024; Christ et al., 2024) rely
      on random bitstream to guide token sampling, creating detectable correlations in the text. Despite

054
      advancements, current watermarking schemes experience significant performance degradation with
055   even slight text modifications. Additionally, existing algorithms introduce logit biases or guide sam-
056   pling through random bitstream, which would result in semantic differences between watermarked
057   and non-watermarked content, negatively impacting LLM performance on downstream tasks, and
058   limiting the practicality of these watermarking techniques. Among logits-based methods, there is
059   a growing trend to replace the hashing schemes with trainable networks for generating logit per-
060   turbations (Liu et al., 2024b; Huo et al., 2024), and to substitute statistical decoding with trainable
061   decoders (Liu et al., 2024a), leveraging the flexibility of neural network training to improve per-
062   formance. However, these existing approaches still fail to achieve an optimal trade-off between
063
      text quality and robustness, primarily due to the separate training of the encoder and decoder rather
      than a joint, end-to-end optimization. With this observation, we introduce the first logits-based end-
064
      to-end model, where lightweight encoder and decoder networks are jointly optimized to enhance
065
      both detecting robustness and text quality. Note that building such an end-to-end system is highly
066   non-trivial because many involved modules, such as the complex text modification and the seman-
067   tic loss computation, are inherently non-differentiable. To resolve these challenges brought by the
068   non-differentiability, we introduce a novel online prompting technique that utilizes the on-the-fly
069   LLM as a differentiable surrogate. This approach enables the model to effectively handle the above-
070   mentioned non-differentiable operations. Our framework is LLM-agnostic, allowing any LLM to be
071   used during training, and once trained, the model can be applied to other LLMs without retraining.
072   Our key contributions are as follows:
073
074          • For the first time, we present a logits-based end-to-end model for LLM watermarking, im-
075            proving detection robustness and text quality through encoder-decoder joint optimization.
076          • We introduce an online prompting technique that transforms non-differentiable operations,
077            such as semantic loss calculation and advanced online text modification, into differentiable
078            operations by dynamically prompting the on-the-fly LLM. This technique enables seamless
079            end-to-end training without external models.
080          • Extensive experiments show that our method surpasses SOTA methods by 1.2%, 4.0%, and
081            5.5% across 3 LLMs and 6 distortion types. It also achieves superior text quality, reducing
082            perplexity and improving downstream task performance by 19.2% and 3.03%. Notably,
083
               our method can be generalized across LLMs without additional training.
084   The paper is organized as follows: Sec. 2 reviews related works on LLM watermarking. Sec. 3
085   details our proposed method. Sec. 4 presents experimental results demonstrating the superior per-
086   formance of our method. Finally, Sec. 5 concludes the paper.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS ON LLM WATERMARKING
089
090
      Recent works on LLM watermarking can be classified into two main categories: logits-based and
091   sampling-based methods. Logits-based methods, as presented in KGW (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023),
092   split the vocabulary into “green” and “red” lists by hashing preceding tokens and biasing green list
093   logits to favor their selection, using the proportion of green tokens to detect watermarks. Building on
094   KGW, several methods aim to improve the robustness, quality, or unforgeability of the watermarked
095   text. KGW-R (Kirchenbauer et al., 2024) explores different hashing schemes, while Unigram (Zhao
096   et al., 2024) uses a fixed red-green separation to enhance editing resistance. SWEET (Lee et al.,
097   2023) selectively modifies logits at high-entropy tokens to improve quality in low-entropy scenarios
098   such as the code generation. UPV (Liu et al., 2024a) employs an encoder network to split lists and
099
      a detector network for classification, enabling public detection. SIR (Liu et al., 2024b) trains an
      encoder to apply context-aware biases for better robustness, and TSW (Huo et al., 2024) uses two
100
      networks to adaptively adjust watermark strength and list-splitting ratio for a better balance between
101
      detectability and text quality. Sampling-based methods, such as EXP (Kuditipudi et al., 2024), use
102   a pseudo-random bitstream to guide token selection through the exponential sampling rule. This
103   process produces watermarked text that aligns with the bit sequence and makes detection accurate.
104   EXP-Edit (Christ et al., 2024) builds on the EXP method by introducing edit distance to measure
105   sequence alignment, enhancing robustness against tampering. Nevertheless, as will be shown in the
106   experiments, current LLM watermarking methods suffer from significant drops in detection perfor-
107   mance when the text is modified and can introduce biases that impair LLM performance in common
      use cases, such as the translation and the code generation.

343   4.1.2    M ETRICS
344
      We benchmark LLM watermarking methods across five key aspects, following the tradition of Pan
345
      et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024): 1) detection effectiveness of the human-written and pristine wa-
346   termarked text, measured by the F1 scores at optimal thresholds; 2) detection robustness, evaluated
347   by subjecting the watermarked text to 6 types of text modifications; 3) text quality, assessed using
348   metrics including text perplexity and log diversity, as well as performance on downstream tasks
349   including translation and code generation; 4) undetectability, by visualize token distribution and
350   training BERT classifiers to distinguish between watermarked and non-watermarked samples; and
351   5) efficiency of the watermarking model, by measuring both the time and GPU memory overhead
352   for text generation and watermark detection.

533   5     C ONCLUSION
534
535   We introduce the first logits-based end-to-end model, where encoder and decoder networks are
536   jointly optimized to improve detection robustness and text quality. Constructing such a system
537   is challenging due to the non-differentiability of key modules, such as the online text editor and
538   semantic loss computation. To overcome these obstacles, we propose a novel online prompting
539   technique that leverages the on-the-fly LLM as a differentiable surrogate, effectively handling these
      non-differentiable operations. Our method can be easily generalized to different LLMs.

961   Post-generation watermarking methods, such as AWT (Abdelnabi & Fritz, 2021) and REMARK-
962   LLM (Zhang et al., 2024), embed watermarks after the text has been fully generated. These ap-
963
      proaches rely on a language model to rephrase the generated text, embedding a watermark signal
      while preserving the semantic meaning of the original sentences. However, post-generation meth-
964
      ods have notable limitations. They do not fully leverage the capabilities of the original LLM and
965
      are more susceptible to OOD issues. For instance, models trained on datasets like HC3, a natu-
966   ral language question-answering corpus, often struggle with OOD inputs, such as code, leading to
967   reduced performance on tasks like code generation (e.g., lower code passing scores). In contrast,
968   logits-based methods, including ours, embed watermarks during the generation process by sampling
969   tokens directly from a perturbed distribution. This approach minimally constrains the LLM, allow-
