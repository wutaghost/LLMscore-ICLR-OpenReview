009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) has become a crucial technique
012            for adapting pre-trained models to downstream tasks. However, the enormous
013            size of LLMs poses significant challenges in terms of computational complexity
014            and resource requirements. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) has emerged as a
015            promising solution. However, there exists a gap between the practical performance
016            of low-rank adaptations and its theoretical optimum. In this work, we propose
017            eXtreme Gradient Boosting LoRA (XGBLoRA), a novel framework that bridges
018            this gap by leveraging the power of ensemble learning. Inspired by gradient
019
               boosting, XGBLoRA iteratively learns and merges a sequence of LoRA adaptations
               to refine model predictions. It achieves better performance than the standard LoRA,
020
               while enjoying the computational efficiency of rank-1 adaptations. We provide
021
               theoretical analysis to show the convergence and optimality of our approach, and
022            conduct extensive experiments on a range of natural language processing tasks.
023            The results demonstrate that XGBLoRA consistently outperforms standard LoRA
024            and achieves performance comparable to full fine-tuning with significantly fewer
025            trainable parameters. This work advances parameter-efficient fine-tuning for LLMs,
026            and offers a promising solution for adapting LLMs to downstream tasks while
027            optimizing performance and efficiency.

102   2    R ELATED W ORKS
103
104   Fine-tuning LLMs has become a prevailing approach for adapting these models to specific down-
105   stream tasks (Houlsby et al., 2019b; Diao et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2022; Diao et al., 2023). The process
106   involves training the model on a task-specific dataset, usually with a smaller learning rate compared
107   to pre-training, to adapt its parameters to the target task. Fine-tuning has been successfully applied to
      a wide range of natural language processing tasks, including text classification, question answering,


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and natural language inference (Brown et al., 2020b; Kenton & Toutanova, 2019; Radford et al., 2018;
109   Touvron et al., 2023a). However, fine-tuning LLMs faces several challenges. A major challenge is
110   the computational complexity and memory requirements associated with updating billions of model
111   parameters, which can be prohibitively expensive and time-consuming (Strubell et al., 2019; Brown
112   et al., 2020a; Raffel et al., 2020b; Zhang et al., 2022; Scao et al., 2022; Almazrouei et al., 2023;
113   Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Chiang et al., 2023; Biderman et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024). Additionally,
114   the limited availability of labeled data for specific tasks poses challenges in terms of sample efficiency
115   and generalization ability (Zhang et al., 2020). To address these challenges, various approaches have
116   been proposed to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of LLM fine-tuning. One notable technique
117
      is LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) which freezes the pre-trained model’s weights and introduces a low-rank
      matrix to adapt the model to new tasks. LoRA reduces the number of trainable parameters and reduces
118
      the computational burden of LLM fine-tuning. Recently, (Zeng & Lee, 2024) provided theoretical
119
      results that characterize the expressive power of LoRA for Fully Connected Neural Networks (FCNN)
120   and Transformer Networks (TFN), which identify the necessary rank of LoRA for adapting a frozen
121   model to exactly match a target model. For Transformer networks, any model can be adapted to a
122   target model of the same size with LoRA adapters of rank_r=embedding_size/2.
123
      Gradient Boosting (Friedman, 2001; 2002) is a powerful ensemble learning technique that combines
124
      multiple weak learners to create a strong learner. The theoretical foundations of gradient boosting
125
      have been extensively studied, providing insights into its convergence properties, generalization
126   ability, and robustness to overfitting. A seminal work on gradient boosting theory by Zhang & Yu
127   (2005) proved that gradient boosting achieves the optimal convergence rate for a broad class of loss
128   functions, highlighting its theoretical optimality. Koltchinskii & Panchenko (2002) further investi-
129   gated theoretical properties of gradient boosting from the perspective of empirical risk minimization.
130   They derived bounds on the generalization error of gradient boosting and showed that the technique
131   is resilient to overfitting when the base learners are weak and the step size is appropriately chosen.
132   They include insights into its convergence behavior, generalization ability, and robustness, which are
133   relevant to the theoretical analysis of our proposed XGBLoRA framework.

140   form the foundation of our approach. Below, we provide an overview of gradient boosting and LoRA,
141   highlighting their principles, advantages, and relevance to LLM fine-tuning.

186   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) is a PEFT technique designed specifically for
187   adapting large language models to downstream tasks. The key idea behind LoRA is to freeze the
188   pre-trained model’s weights and inject trainable low-rank matrices into each layer of the model. By
189
      doing so, LoRA significantly reduces the number of trainable parameters while still letting the model
      adapt to specific tasks. Formally, let W ∈ Rd×k be the weight matrix of a fully connected layer in a
190
      pre-trained language model, where d is the input dimension and k is the output dimension. LoRA
191
      introduces a low-rank decomposition of the weight matrix:
192
193                                              W = W0 + AB,                                               (5)
194                                                                         d×r              r×k
      where W0 is the original pre-trained weight matrix, A ∈ R            and B ∈ R        are trainable
195
      matrices, and r is the rank of the adaptation. During fine-tuning, only the matrices A and B
196   are updated, while the original weights W0 remain frozen. This reduces the number of trainable
197   parameters from dk to (d + k)r, which is significantly smaller when r ≪ min(d, k). Compared
198   to traditional fine-tuning methods, LoRA offers computational efficiency, memory savings, and
199   efficient model storage, as it eliminates the need to store separate copies of fine-tuned models.
200   Remarkably, despite its parameter efficiency, LoRA has been shown to achieve comparable or even
201   better performance than full fine-tuning on various natural language processing tasks, making it a
202   promising technique for efficient and effective model adaptation. However, LoRA has a theoretical
203   limitation (Zeng & Lee, 2024). To fit any target matrix, the rank of the adaptation must satisfy
204
      (r ≥ embedding_size/2). In practice, much smaller ranks (e.g., r ∈ [1, 32]) are often used to
      trade-off performance with efficiency. The discrepancy between the theoretical optimum and practical
205
      usage leads to a performance gap. Increasing the rank to satisfy the above theoretical requirement
206
      increases memory usage and computational complexity, negating the benefits of LoRA by making it
207   as costly as the full fine-tuning strategy.

213   iteratively refines a Transformer’s predictions by learning a sequence of booster (LoRA adaptations)
214   and merging them into the model’s weight matrices. Let D = {(xi , yi )}N        i=1 be a dataset of N
215   examples, where xi is the input text and yi is the corresponding target output. Our goal is to fine-tune
      a pre-trained Transformer M0 on dataset D to optimize its performance on the downstream task.

      3.3     U NDERSTANDING E NSEMBLE OF W EAK L EARNERS .
263
      The crucial principle of Gradient Boosting is building a strong ensemble model with weak learn-
264   ers. For instance, a very successful gradient boosting method, i.e., Gradient Boost Decision Tree
265   (GBDT) (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) artificially limits the tree boosters to a very shallow depth (usually
266   only 1 split) to ensure that each booster is only slightly better than the random decision. Thus,
267   boosting algorithms are highly resilient against noisy data and overfitting (Friedman, 2002). Since
268   the individual booster is too simple to overfit, it is very hard to combine them in a way that the strong
269   ensemble would overfit to the whole training data. Adhering to this principle, below we design the
      following strategies for controlling the the expressiveness of each LoRA boosters.

280   The Rank-1 update. The rank r of the LoRA matrices is a hyperparameter that controls the
281   expressiveness and efficiency of the adaptation. A smaller rank leads to more parameter-efficient
282
      adaptations but may limit the ability to capture complex patterns in the residual errors. On the
      other hand, a larger rank allows for more expressive adaptations but increases the computational
283
      and memory requirements. In light of the gradient boosting theory, rank-1 updates provides the
284
      right balance between approximation power and regularization. They allow for a more fine-grained,
285   step-by-step approximation of the target function due to a larger number of weaker learners in
286   ensemble, leading to better generalization.

      Random Layer Selection. This strategy resembles the random column selection strategy in GBDT,
      which limits complexity but increases diversity among boosters. Instead of modifying all layers of the
289
      language model (LM), we randomly select Ls (Ls ≤ L) layers to add LoRAs for building the booster.
290
      By adapting only a subset of layers in each iteration, the booster’s ability to make drastic changes is
291   limited. This intentional constraint ensures that each booster remains ‘weak’ in its predictive power
292   (core principle of GB). Moreover, this strategy injects randomness into the final ensemble model,
293   creating diversity among the boosters. Each booster focuses on different parts of the model, capturing
294   distinct aspects of the data. This diversity is crucial for the success of ensemble methods.
295
296    XGBLoRA offers several advantages over other fine-tuning approaches and the standard LoRA.
297    1) Ensemble Learning: By iteratively learning and merging LoRAs, XGBLoRA can efficiently
298    adapt the pre-trained model to the downstream task. The iterative nature of the algorithm allows
299    for progressive refinement of model predictions, leading to better generalization performance
300    without explicitly adding regularization terms to the loss function.
301    2) The Weak Learner Principle: By rank-1 updates and random layer adaptation XGBLoRA
302    significantly reduces computational cost, achieving efficient LLM fine-tuning. XGBLoRA enables
303
       rapid, incremental adaptation incurring low memory overhead, making it particularly suitable for
       fine-tuning LLMs where full parameter updates are prohibitively costly.

353    the benefit diminishes as r increases. However, the theorem implies that increasing the number
354    of iterations T can compensate for a lower rank r. By using rank-1 updates and increasing
355    T , we can achieve a similar convergence rate to higher-rank methods while maintaining lower
356
       computational complexity per iteration. It supports that XGBLoRA using multiple simple (rank-1)
       weak learners in an ensemble, rather than fewer complex (higher-rank) learners, to efficiently
357
       approximate the target function.

       Remark 2 The theorem shows that the approximation error can be reduced by either increasing r
369
       or T . Let ϵ∗ = Ex∼D [(fT (x) − f ∗ (x))2 ] be the desired approximation that achieves the optimal
370    generalization. Theorem 2 explicitly reveals that to maintain ϵ∗ , one can trade off LoRA rank r
371    against iterations T . Original LoRA is the case where T = 1 and r ≫ 1. In contrast, XGBLoRA
372    enjoys the opposite setting r = 1 and T ≫ 1. Thus,XGBLoRA can maintain high expressiveness

533   5           C ONCLUSIONS
534   We have proposed XGBLoRA for fine-tuning LLMs in a parameter-efficient manner by posing fine-
535   tuning as a gradient boosting where LoRA matrices are used as weak learners to be iteratively
536   combined to form a strong ensemble model. We provide theoretical analysis establishing the
537   convergence and approximation error of XGBLoRA, highlighting the interplay between the LoRA
538   rank, expressiveness, and the number of boosting iterations. Extensive experiments demonstrate the
539   effectiveness of XGBLoRA, which consistently outperforms the standard LoRA while maintaining
      parameter/computational efficiency. Broader Impact & Limitations are in Appendices D & E.
