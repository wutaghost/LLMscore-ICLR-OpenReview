011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In offline reinforcement learning, most existing methods have focused primarily
014            on optimizing performance, often neglecting the promotion of diverse behaviors.
015            While some approaches generate diverse behaviors from well-constructed, hetero-
016
               geneous datasets, their effectiveness is significantly reduced when applied to less
               diverse data. To address this, we introduce a novel intrinsic reward mechanism
017
               that encourages behavioral diversity, irrespective of the dataset’s heterogeneity.
018
               By maximizing the mutual information between actions and policies under each
019            state, our approach enables agents to learn a variety of behaviors, including those
020            not explicitly represented in the data. Although performing out-of-distribution
021            actions can lead to risky outcomes, we mitigate this risk by incorporating the
022            ensemble-diversified actor-critic (EDAC) method to estimate Q-value uncertainty,
023            preventing agents from adopting suboptimal behaviors. Through experiments us-
024            ing the D4RL benchmarks on MuJoCo tasks, we demonstrate that our method
025            achieves behavioral diversity while maintaining performance across environments
026            constructed from both heterogeneous and homogeneous datasets.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Learning to perform tasks with a range of behaviors, often referred to as quality-diversity opti-
032   mization, is a growing area in reinforcement learning (RL) (Fontaine & Nikolaidis, 2021; Cully &
033
      Demiris, 2017; Pugh et al., 2016). Current methods for promoting diverse behaviors have primarily
      focused on online RL (Nilsson & Cully, 2021; Pierrot et al., 2022). These approaches, by encourag-
034
      ing policies to behave diversely, enhance environmental exploration (Hong et al., 2018) and facilitate
035
      skill discovery (Eysenbach et al., 2019; Sharma et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2024), ultimately leading to
036   improved performance. However, producing diverse behaviors in offline RL (Kostrikov et al., 2021;
037   Wu et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2019; Fujimoto & Gu, 2021; Wang et al., 2020b) remains relatively
038   unexplored and poses challenges due to the static nature of training datasets.
039
      Learning diverse behaviors from offline data is just as important as in online scenarios. As the saying
040
      goes, ”all roads lead to Rome,” meaning multiple strategies can lead to successful outcomes, with
041
      each unique behavior in the dataset reflecting a distinct style or preference. Recent advancements
042   in offline RL, such as SORL (Mao et al., 2024) and DIVEOff (Osa & Harada, 2024), have focused
043   on learning diverse behaviors from heterogeneous datasets. These methods employ expectation-
044   maximization (EM) algorithms to cluster data and train policies based on those clusters, fostering
045   diversity in actions. While they effectively leverage dataset diversity to develop varied agent strate-
046   gies, they often face challenges with non-heterogeneous datasets and tend to prioritize one aspect
047   over another in the balance between quality and diversity optimization.
048   In this study, we introduce a unique behavior (UB) objective function designed to enable offline RL
049   policies to learn diverse behaviors, even from homogeneous datasets. The objective is derived from
050   maximizing mutual information between actions and policies within each state. This dependence
051   between actions and policies naturally leads to distinct behaviors. Unlike baseline methods that
052   achieve diversity through data segmentation, our method encourages agents – through a UB reward
053   – to behave differently. This reward directly guides policy training, allowing agents to learn actions
      not present in the training data.

081   One major challenge in offline RL is handling out-of-distribution (OOD) state-action pairs, as re-
082   wards can only be estimated rather than obtained from the environment. Since our approach allows
083   policies to deviate from the training data, it risks distorting learning outcomes. To address this,
084   we adopt the Ensemble-Diversified Actor-Critic (EDAC) method (Kumar et al., 2020), which em-
085
      ploys an ensemble of networks to estimate Q-values. By selecting the minimum Q-value from the
      ensemble, EDAC provides a more conservative Bellman target estimate, reducing the risk of over-
086
      estimating Q-values for OOD state-action pairs. This ensures that while policies generate diverse
087
      actions, the quality of those actions is maintained.
088
089   We evaluated our approach against baseline methods using the D4RL (Fu et al., 2020) and diverse
090   D4RL (Osa & Harada, 2024) datasets, which are rigorous benchmarks for offline RL algorithms.
091
      The results in Figure 1 and Table 1 show that, while there is typically a trade-off between perfor-
      mance and action/state diversity, our method achieves high performance while ensuring significant
092
      diversity across both homogeneous and heterogeneous datasets.

097   2.1                                    D IVERSITY IN O FFLINE RL
098
099
      While multiple strategies can lead to successful outcomes, learning diverse behaviors from datasets
      contributed by multiple users can reflect their unique styles and preferences, which is beneficial in
100
      many real-world applications. The exploration of training diverse agents in offline settings has re-
101
      cently gained attention. For instance, CLUE (Liu et al., 2023) uses a Variational Autoencoder (VAE)
102   to learn latent representations of state and action, defining different partial datasets as behavioral
103   targets. It employs the latent distance from other data to the target behavior as an intrinsic reward,
104   enabling the learning of varied behaviors. Similarly, SORL (Mao et al., 2024) uses an expectation-
105   maximization (EM) algorithm, where the E-step estimates the posterior distribution of the latent
106   variable based on current policies, and the M-step updates policies to maximize a lower bound of the
107   posterior log-likelihood, achieving diverse latent behaviors. DIVEOFF (Osa & Harada, 2024) also
      applies the EM algorithm, augmented with a VAE for latent representation. In DIVEOFF’s E-step,


                                                                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the latent-conditioned policy is updated based on the posterior distribution of the latent variable,
109   while the M-step updates the posterior distribution of the latent variable given the latent-conditioned
110   policy. Unlike SORL, which uses policy probability density to compute the posterior, DIVEOFF
111   uses a VAE to learn the latent posterior distribution given a state and action, enhancing learning by
112   maximizing the mutual information between latent representations and state-action pairs.
113
      Existing methods promote diversity by encouraging agents to learn from data that are distant in
114
      latent space. However, a challenge arises when the dataset is homogeneous, or when distant latent
115   vectors are decoded into similar actions. To address this, we introduce an alternative approach
116   with a diversity objective function defined in the action space. Agents are rewarded for behaving
117   differently from each other during training, providing direct guidance that allows policies to learn
118   behaviors even beyond the dataset distribution.
119
120   2.2   M UTUAL I NFORMATION IN RL
121
122   Mutual Information (MI) has been widely used in online RL to enhance exploration through mech-
123   anisms such as empowerment (Klyubin et al., 2008; 2005; Mohamed & Jimenez Rezende, 2015;
124
      Leibfried et al., 2019) and information-theoretic curiosity (Still & Precup, 2012; Bai et al., 2021;
      Tao et al., 2020). These applications focus on leveraging MI between successive states to encour-
125
      age agents to interact with environments in various ways. Beyond exploration, MI has also been
126
      instrumental in advancing representation learning (Anand et al., 2019; Stooke et al., 2021; Nachum
127   et al., 2019; Schwarzer et al., 2021; Mazoure et al., 2020), where it measures dependencies between
128   states and their representations or between state-action pairs and their corresponding representa-
129   tions. While many of these methods utilize MI to improve an agent’s performance, others apply
130   MI in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), focusing on coordination (Jaques et al., 2019;
131   Konan et al., 2022) and promoting diversity among agents (Jiang & Lu, 2021; Li et al., 2021; Liu
132   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2020a).
133   Most existing works utilize MI to enhance the performance of a single agent or promote diversity
134   among multiple agents in online RL. In this study, we maximize the MI between actions and policies
135   within each state to achieve diverse behaviors in offline RL.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
      In this paper, we present a novel approach to enhancing diversity in offline RL environments, a
      field traditionally constrained by static and homogeneous training datasets. Unlike conventional
532
      methods that depend on heterogeneous datasets to cultivate diverse agent behaviors, our approach
533
      leverages mutual information to promote the development of unique and effective strategies across
534   agents. By introducing an intrinsic reward mechanism based on the distinctiveness of an agent’s
535   actions relative to the overall action distribution, our method encourages significant diversity without
536   compromising performance. Empirical evaluations consistently demonstrate that our framework
537   outperforms existing methods, offering a powerful solution for generating diverse behaviors even in
538   environments with limited data diversity. This work represents a significant advancement in offline
539   RL, broadening the potential for deploying these strategies in more dynamic and unpredictable real-
      world scenarios.
