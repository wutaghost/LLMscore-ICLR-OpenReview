009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite theoretical guarantees of existing dynamic pricing (DP) methods, their
012            strong model assumptions may not reflect real-world conditions and are often
013            unverifiable. This poses major challenges in practice since the performance of an
014            algorithm may significantly degrade if the assumptions are not satisfied. Moreover,
015            many DP algorithms show unfavorable empirical performance due to the lack of
016
               data efficiency. To address these challenges, we design a practical contextual DP
               algorithm that utilizes regression oracles. Our proposed algorithm assumes only
017
               Lipschitz continuity on the true conditional probability of purchase. We prove
018                  2              1
               Õ(T 3 RegretR (T ) 3 ) regret upper bound where T is the horizon and RegretR (T )
019
               is the regret of the oracle. The bound is nearly minimax optimal in the canonical
020
               case of finite function class, and our analysis generically applies to other function
021            approximators including neural networks. To the best of our knowledge, our
022            work is the first algorithm to utilize the powerful generalization capability of
023            neural networks with provable guarantees in dynamic pricing literature. Extensive
024            numerical experiments show that our algorithm outperforms existing state-of-
025            the-art dynamic pricing algorithms in various settings, which demonstrates both
026            provable efficiency and practicality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      The contextual dynamic pricing (DP) is an online decision making and learning task where the seller
031
      sets prices for products based on contexts containing customer characteristics or product information.
032
      The seller tries to maximize the revenue by balancing between exploration and exploitation. In
033   the widely studied customer valuation model setting, the binary choice of customer purchase yt is
034   associated with market valuation vt to given context xt ; if the price pt set by the seller is cheaper
035   than vt , the seller receives yt = 1 (purchase), otherwise yt = 0 (non-purchase). The seller’s goal
036   is to maximize the revenue E[pt yt | xt , pt ] = pt P(vt > pt ). For the customer valuation vt , the
037   existing literature have considered various structures including linear (Amin et al., 2014; Javanmard &
038   Nazerzadeh, 2019; Fan et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2022), log-linear (Shah et al., 2019), and proportional
039   hazard model (Choi et al., 2023). When the noise distribution of the valuation, denoted by F0 , is
040   assumed to be known (Javanmard & Nazerzadeh, 2019; Cohen et al., 2020; Xu & Wang, 2021), the
041
      algorithms proposed under such a setting are considered parametric. In contrast, semi-parametric
      algorithms (Shah et al., 2019; Fan et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2022; Xu & Wang, 2022; Choi et al., 2023)
042
      operate with unknown nonparametric F0 . The regret analysis of both parametric and semi-parametric
043
      methods often heavily rely on additional assumptions including log-concavity of F0 , smoothness of
044   the revenue function, and stochastic conditions on contexts.
045
046   For this rigorous sequential decision-making problem, which also has significant practical impact, we
047
      pose the following fundamental question:
048
       Question: Is there a practical contextual dynamic pricing method that works well across various
049                            instances while providing provable guarantees?
050
051   To the best of our knowledge (and as many practitioners would agree), there has not been a single
052   method that can confidently be said to perform robustly across various domains. As evident in
053   the numerical experiments in Section 6, existing DP methods often fall short in terms of practical
      performance despite having provable guarantees. This contrasts sharply with the field of contextual

054
      bandits. Although the two classes of problems appear similar, the practical effectiveness of contextual
055   DP is still far from that of contextual bandits, where both provably efficient and practically effective
056   methods are widely used (e.g., UCB (Li et al., 2010; Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011), TS (Chapelle & Li,
057   2011; Agrawal & Goyal, 2013), and IGW (Foster & Rakhlin, 2020)). Where do these discrepancies
058   come from?
059
      The reasons why the empirical performances of many of the DP methods with provable guarantees
060
      lack practical effectiveness are as follows: (i) Existing DP algorithms with provable guarantees do not
061   utilize powerful function approximations such as neural networks, which may significantly improve
062   practical performance. They are frequently restricted to strong assumptions about model or context
063   distributions (Javanmard & Nazerzadeh, 2019; Fan et al., 2022). However, these assumptions may
064   not be reflective of real-world conditions and, moreover, are often unverifiable in practical scenarios.
065   The discrepancy between theoretical assumptions and practice can lead to significant performance
066   degradation when these assumptions fail to hold true. This unreliable binary outcome—where
067   algorithms perform well under ideal conditions but falter otherwise—poses a substantial challenge
068   from a practitioner’s perspective. (ii) Even when assumptions are satisfied, many of the existing
069
      DP methods still suffer from degradation in practical performance due to a lack of data efficiency.
      This is in fact the aspect that makes many contextual DP algorithm not as robust as contextual
070
      bandit algorithms. Many DP algorithms are based on epoch-wise estimation strategies (Javanmard &
071
      Nazerzadeh, 2019; Fan et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2022; Choi et al., 2023), where only at most half of the
072   accumulated data can be utilized in any given epoch, throwing away at least half of the data. Moreover,
073   they rely on exploration strategies such as epoch-wise explore-then-commit (Fan et al., 2022; Luo
074   et al., 2022) or ε-greedy (Choi et al., 2023) (or some methods even never explore (Javanmard &
075   Nazerzadeh, 2019)). This may lead to inefficient exploration since their epoch-wise exploration
076   strategies are not fully adaptive to past history.
077
      In this work, we propose DP-IGW (Dynamic Pricing with Inverse Gap Weighting), a practical
078   dynamic pricing algorithm that leverages regression oracles including neural regression oracles, with
079   provable guarantees. Our method applies to more general problem settings than existing approaches
080   without sacrificing performance for theoretical rigor. We demonstrate that our algorithm significantly
081   outperforms existing methods across a range of environments, including both simulation and real-
082   world data, as demonstrated in Section 6. A key factor in its empirical success is the use of flexible
083   regression oracles, such as neural networks, enabling general function approximation in diverse
084   scenarios. Our analysis is based on the generic binary choice model instead of assuming customer
085   valuation. In this model, the probability of purchase is a Lipschitz function of context and price:
086
      yt | xt , pt ∼ Ber(f ⋆ (xt , pt )) for some f ⋆ (·, ·). This minimal assumption of Lipschitz continuity
      captures complex real-world demand structures, with the customer valuation model as a special case.
087
      Our DP-IGW algorithm explores the price space using inverse gap weighting (IGW), a state-of-the-art
088
      exploration technique in contextual bandits due to its flexibility and minimal assumptions. (Abe &
089   Long, 1999; Foster & Rakhlin, 2020; Foster & Krishnamurthy, 2021; Zhu & Mineiro, 2022). To
090   the best of our knowledge, our work is the first adaptation of the IGW technique to dynamic pricing
091   problems. The key difference between our algorithm and the IGW-based contextual bandit algorithms
092   is that we separate the regression target (purchase) and exploration target (revenue), which facilitates
093   efficient learning and exploration simultaneously in the DP setting. This efficiency, combined with
094   the model flexibility, leads to remarkable empirical performance in diverse settings.
095   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
096

236   The hyperparameter γt > 0 determines the degree of exploration, which is discussed in the next
237   paragraph. Given a sampling oracle for drawing samples from µ, the sampling from Pt can be
238   efficiently done by rejection sampling. The construction of Pt is based on the SmoothIGW algorithm
239   in Zhu & Mineiro (2022), which extends the inverse gap weighting (IGW) technique (Abe & Long,
240   1999; Foster & Rakhlin, 2020) to continuous spaces. Specifically, Pt is a mixture of the atomic
241   distribution Ip̂t and the distribution induced by the density mt (p) and the base measure µ. Note
242
      that mt (p) ≤ 1 for all p ∈ P, thus Mt (P) ≤ 1 and Pt is well-defined. The density mt (p) assigns

243
      and price p. Intuitively, the IGW smoothly balances exploration and exploitation by placing more
244
      weight on the region of high estimated revenue.

248

249   algorithm. If we fix the exploration parameter as γk = γ for T steps, γ must depend on the horizon

251   epoch length τk , thus the algorithm is executable without knowing the horizon beforehand.
252
253   Remark. The DP-IGW algorithm is fully sequential; we do not discard any past data, and fully
254   adaptive; we explore adaptively based on full data, as opposed to many existing epoch-based dynamic
255   pricing algorithms (Javanmard & Nazerzadeh, 2019; Fan et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2022; Choi et al.,
256   2023). Unlike the standard doubling trick (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006), our use of epoch is only for
257   scheduling the value of γk . The computational complexity of DP-IGW is determined by the argmax
258   operation (Line 6) and oracle update (Line 8), as the sampling of pt can be done by rejection sampling
259   at a constant cost per step. The argmax operation and the oracle update depend on function class
260
      F, and we note that almost every DP algorithm has the argmax operation for finding the greedy
      price, thus DP-IGW is no worse than existing DP algorithms with respect to the argmax operation.
261
      The oracle update also maintains feasible complexity if we use neural regression algorithms (Deb
262
      et al., 2024) which is based on standard gradient descent. Therefore, our algorithm is computationally
263   efficient.

352
353   We evaluate our DP-IGW algorithm via extensive numerical experiments including simulation environ-
354   ments and real data. We train a neural network oracle by minimizing logarithmic loss, as described in
355   Appendix E. We compare our method to existing DP algorithms and contextual bandit algorithms
356   (with proper modification), using cumulative regret as the performance metric.
357   Baseline Methods: Dynamic Pricing We first consider dynamic pricing methods with flexible model
358   assumptions as baselines, as algorithms with strong assumptions are impractical due to the potential
359   for model misspecification. Therefore, we select 5 semi-parametric or nonparametric dynamic
360   pricing methods as baseline methods: ExUCB (Luo et al., 2022), Fan et al. (2022), DEEP-C (Shah
361   et al., 2019), CoxCP (Choi et al., 2023), and ABE (Chen & Gallego, 2021). ExUCB and Fan et al.
362   (2022) assume the linear valuation model, DEEP-C works on the log-linear model, and CoxCP
363
      is based on the PH model. ABE is a nonparametric DP algorithm. We then consider dynamic
      pricing methods which has prior information on the model with strong assumptions as baselines to
364
      verify whether our algorithm performs well despite such prior information. ONSP (Xu & Wang,
365
      2021), RMLP (Javanmard & Nazerzadeh, 2019), and RMLP2 (Javanmard & Nazerzadeh, 2019)
366   are parametric methods which assume the linear valuation model with known noise distribution F0
367   and log-concavity of both F0 and 1 − F0 . We optimized hyperparameters for each method, see
368   Appendix E for the details.
369
      Baseline Methods: Neural Bandits Although contextual bandits are not equivalent to dynamic
370
      pricing problems in general, we can interpret dynamic pricing problems as contextual bandits with
371
      stochastic rewards. Considering the price pt as an action and the realized revenue pt yt as a reward, the
372   distribution of the reward is determined by the context xt and the action pt . From this viewpoint, we
373   can interpret the regret 1 as the pseudo-regret of the bandit problem. Therefore, we compare DP-IGW
374   with recent state-of-the-art contextual bandit algorithms leveraging neural networks: NeuralUCB
375   (Zhou et al., 2020), Neural Thompson sampling (NeuralTS), Zhang et al. (2020)), SquareCB (Foster
376   & Rakhlin, 2020), and SmoothIGW (Zhu & Mineiro, 2022). Since NeuralUCB, NeuralTS, and
377   SquareCB are finite-armed bandit algorithms, we evenly discretize the price with finite arms. The
      neural network structures for all algorithms are the same, as described in Appendix E.

520   6.2            R EAL - WORLD DATA
521
522   In the dynamic pricing literature, experiments with real-world data have been limited since com-
523   plex underlying structures of real-world data may violate model assumptions. However, DP-IGW
524   empowered by neural networks can successfully learn with real-world data, as we demonstrate in
525   this section. We experiment with six real-world datasets for regression tasks: Abalone (Nash &
526   Ford, 1994), Diamonds (Wickham, 2016), Appliance Energy Prediction (Energy) (Candanedo, 2017),
527   Estimation of Obesity Levels (Obesity) (Palechor & De la Hoz Manotas, 2019), California Housing
528   (Housing) (Pace & Barry, 1997), Wine Quality (Wine) (Cortez & Reis, 2009). The datasets contain
529   continuous and categorical features, with dimensions ranging from 10 to 26 after one-hot encoding
530
      of the categorical features. Therefore, we can investigate the performances of algorithms in various
      real-world scenarios. Refer to Appendix E.1 for the details on the datasets. We left out DEEP-C
531
      because its computational cost increases exponentially with d, and also excluded CoxCP as it can’t
532
      estimate model parameters when dealing with categorical features.
533
534   To simulate the online interaction of dynamic pricing problems, we treat the regression targets of
535   the datasets as valuations. At step t, one context vector xt and corresponding valuation (regression
536
      target) vt is sampled from the dataset. The algorithm sets the price pt based on xt , then yt is sampled
      from Ber(1 − F0 (pt − vt )). As in the simulation experiments, we consider two options for F0 .
537
538   Figure 2 shows that DP-IGW has the best performance among the baselines, in almost every dataset.
539   This shows that DP-IGW can efficiently learn complex real-world data. Also, it scales well with the
      dimension of contexts and the size of datasets.
