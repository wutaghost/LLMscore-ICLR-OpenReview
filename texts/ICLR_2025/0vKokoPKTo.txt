011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Few-shot prompting methods can significantly improve the ability of Large Lan-
014            guage Models (LLMs) in mathematical reasoning, including geometry prob-
015
               lem solving (GPS). GPT-4 Vision (GPT-4V), as a leading example of LLMs,
               also demonstrates significant improvements. This tremendous achievement is
016
               mainly attributed to prompting methods like ”Chain-of-Thought” and ”Program-
017
               of-Thought,” which leverage the in-context learning ability of the model com-
018            bined with few-shot prompting to solve new problems. Despite the success of
019            these prompting methods, it remains understood what the GPT-4V model learns
020            from the demonstrations that leads to improved performance. In this paper, we
021            evaluated the answering accuracy of GPT-4V with few-shot prompting on five
022            geometric problem datasets and conducted a series of detailed analyses. Firstly,
023            through ablation experiments with valid and invalid demonstrations, we found
024            that the model’s performance improvement is not due to the quality of the demon-
025            stration, but rather to the input format, output format, and logic and structure of
026
               the demonstration. Secondly, by analyzing the reasoning and computational re-
               quirements of GPS, and verifying experimental results, we found that GPS tasks
027
               emphasize reasoning ability more than computational power. Finally, our analysis
028
               of various prompt methods revealed that existing approaches are not effective at
029            improving model performance concerning problem length and geometric shape.
030            Therefore, specialized prompt methods could be designed to enhance the model’s
031            performance in these aspects, or fine-tuning the model by adding geometric prob-
032            lem data with longer lengths or mixed geometric shapes could optimize its per-
033            formance. Overall, developing an LLM that fully adapts to GPS tasks represents
034            a key research direction. The source code will be made available in a GitHub
035            repository.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      It is widely consensus that leveraging the reasoning and in-context learning capabilities of large
040
      language models (LLMs) Zhang et al. (2023a); Kandpal et al. (2023); Shi et al. (2023); Ye et al.
041
      (2023b), combined with few-shot prompting, can significantly improve their performance in math-
042   ematical reasoning Yin et al. (2023); Gao & Das (2024); Firdaus et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023);
043   Wu et al. (2023b). In mathematical reasoning research, geometry problem solving (GPS) Gao et al.
044   (2023a); Chang et al. (2022); Ning et al. (2023); Peng et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2024a) is crucial
045   as it demands higher levels of specialized knowledge and comprehensive skills Lu et al. (2024),
046   showcasing the potential of large language models more effectively Zhang et al. (2024). Therefore,
047   exploring methods to solve geometry problems using LLMs with few-shot prompting, along with
048   an in-depth analysis of key factors, can provide essential guidance and insights for future research
049   in the GPS field.
050   Currently, we know very little about what LLMs have learned from prompting methods Chen et al.
051   (2023b); Wu et al. (2023a); Wang et al. (2023a); Gao et al. (2023c); Hu et al. (2024). The most direct
052   way for LLMs to improve their ability to solve geometric problems is by providing a small number of
053   examples, which prompts the model to answer new questions based on these examples. The current
      prompt methods are mainly divided into two categories: ”Chain-of-Thought” Wei et al. (2022);

054
      Zhou et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023b); Mondal et al. (2024) and ”Program-of-
055   Thought” Chen et al. (2023a); Gao et al. (2023b); Ye et al. (2023a); Imani et al. (2023). The ”Chain-
056   of-Thought” methods utilize the model’s extensive pre-training parameters (’memory’) Li et al.
057   (2023); Zhong et al. (2024) combined with provided demonstrations to guide the model in reasoning
058   the answer step by step Gao et al. (2023b). In contrast, the ”Program-of-Thought” methods leverage
059   the model’s code generation ability to use external tools for performing complex computations Chen
060   et al. (2023a). While these methods have shown some success in enhancing the model’s problem-
061   solving ability, the key factors driving this improvement remain unknown. To deeply analyze the
062   auxiliary effect of prompting methods on LLMs, we research several key issues: Firstly, are valid
063
      demonstrations of ”Chain-of-Thought” reasoning or ”Program-of-Thought” computation important
      for guiding GPT-4V in GPS tasks? Secondly, are the ”Program-of-Thought” computation methods
064
      superior to the ”Chain-of-Thought” reasoning methods in GPS tasks? Finally, what other aspects
065
      need to be considered when using prompting methods to solve geometric problems, beyond the
066   reasoning process and computation methods?
067
068   We have conducted a series of detailed experimental designs and analyses to address the aforemen-
069
      tioned key issues Wang et al. (2023a); Gao et al. (2023c). First, to evaluate the impact of including
      only valid demonstrations on model performance, we conducted ablation experiments using eight
070
      different prompting methods. Meanwhile, each method included invalid demonstrations, allowing
071
      us to assess the model’s ability to learn between valid and invalid demonstrations. From the experi-
072   mental results, we found that the improvement in the model’s performance is not related to the value
073   of the demonstration, but rather to the input format, output format, and the logic and structure of the
074   demonstration. Second, to evaluate whether solving geometric problems is important for improving
075   reasoning or computational abilities, we analyzed the reasoning and computational requirements
076   from the domain knowledge and the complexity of the geometric problems themselves. By com-
077   paring the experimental results of two types of prompting methods, we found that solving most
078   geometric problems requires a stronger emphasis on reasoning ability, while only a few geomet-
079   ric problems require complex computational power. Therefore, in GPS tasks, ”Chain-of-Thought”
080
      reasoning methods are superior to ”Program-of-Thought” computation methods. Finally, we an-
      alyzed the effect of various prompting methods on the problem length and the geometric shapes
081
      involved. The analysis of experimental results showed that these prompting methods did not signif-
082
      icantly improve these aspects. This indicates that specialized prompting methods could be designed
083   to improve the model’s performance in these aspects, or fine-tuning the model can be achieved by
084   adding problem data with longer lengths or mixed geometric shapes to optimize its performance.
085
086
      Overall, our research and analysis of LLMs with few-shot prompting to solve geometric problems re-
      vealed that the model can learn the prompting framework, including the input format, output format,
087
      and answering ideas, from the demonstrations. However, the specific answer process still depends
088
      on the problem’s content and relies on ”memory” to generate an answer. Therefore, proposing a
089   large language model that fully adapts to GPS tasks is a key research direction.

094   2.1   C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT METHODS
095
096   The Chain-of-Thought methods involve a series of reasoning prompts that divide a problem into mul-
097   tiple intermediate steps, gradually solving each step to obtain the final answer ultimately. Among
098   these methods, chain-of-thought prompting (CoT) Wei et al. (2022), as shown in Figure 1 (a),
099
      has achieved remarkable results in solving general reasoning tasks. However, its performance di-
      minishes when tackling problems with higher reasoning difficulty. To address this issue, the least-
100
      to-most prompting (LtM) Zhou et al. (2023) method is proposed. LtM decomposes a complex
101
      problem into a series of easier subproblems and solves them sequentially, using the answers from
102   previous subproblems to assist in solving subsequent ones. To clarify the solution process, the
103   plan-and-solve prompting (PS) method Wang et al. (2023b) is proposed. PS solves new problems
104   through a series of simple trigger sentences (such as ’give’, ’plan’, ’calculation’, and ’answer’).
105   The standard CoT method follows a roughly linear reasoning approach but often forgets previous
106   intermediate results during the process. The residual connection prompting (RP) method Jiang
107   et al. (2023) mitigates this issue by reintroducing the results of previous steps as prerequisites for
      subsequent steps, thereby reducing the model’s tendency to forget.

      2.2   P ROGRAM - OF -T HOUGHT METHODS
131
132   The Program-of-Thought methods involve a series of computational prompts that utilize the pow-
133   erful code-generation capabilities of LLMs to solve complex computational problems. Although
134   LLMs excel at gradually decomposing problems using Chain-of-Thought methods, they often en-
135   counter logical and arithmetic errors when solving individual subproblems, even if the overall de-
136   composition is correct. To overcome these issues, two representative methods have been proposed:
137   program-of-thought (PoT) Chen et al. (2023a) and program-aided language (PAL) Gao et al.
138   (2023b), as shown in Figure 1 (b). Both methods separate reasoning from computation by placing
139
      each computational step into an external code executor (such as a Python interpreter) for execu-
      tion. The key difference is that PoT describes each step entirely in the programming language,
140
      while PAL integrates both natural language and programming language. To improve the effective-
141
      ness in solving search constraint problems, the satisfiability-aided language (SATLM) Ye et al.
142   (2023a) is proposed. This method converts natural language reasoning problems into satisfiability
143   (SAT) problems and then obtains answers by using an SAT solver. To enhance the credibility of
144   LLMs-generated answers, MathPromoter (MP) Imani et al. (2023) uses hint techniques to gener-
145   ate multiple algebraic expressions and Python functions, solving the same mathematical problem
146   with different approaches. The consensus among these solutions serves as the final answer, thereby
147   increasing the confidence in the output.

157   3.1   C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT R EASONING
158
159   In the realm of ultra-large-scale unsupervised deep learning, large models are often perceived as
160   black boxes Rai et al. (2023), with their reasoning and decision-making processes being difficult to
161   explain Wang et al. (2023c). This lack of transparency poses a challenge to the credibility of the
      model results. However, the introduction of the few-shot chain-of-thought method offers a potential

      solution Wei et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023b); Mondal et al.
163   (2024). This method breaks down logical reasoning problems into multiple steps, generating results
164   with a clear logical context. This approach improves the interpretability of the model, allowing
165   humans to understand the derivation process of the answer. Thus, the chain-of-thought reasoning
166   method marks a crucial milestone in enhancing the success of LLMs.

190
191   Even with chain-of-thought reasoning, LLMs do not truly understand mathematical logic or the
192   fundamental concepts of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division Zhong et al. (2024);

194   much like ”drawing a dipper with a gourd as a model” (a Chinese idiom meaning to imitate without

196   still necessary, leading to the development of program-of-thought computing Chen et al. (2023a);
197
      Gao et al. (2023b); Ye et al. (2023a); Imani et al. (2023).
198   Specifically, few-shot program-of-thought computation is a method where LLMs solve a new prob-

      6.1   S TATISTICAL REQUIREMENTS
373
374   Reasoning. The reasoning requirements for solving a geometric problem are positively correlated
375   with the domain knowledge involved in the problem, so, the more domain knowledge the problem
376   involves, the more reasoning steps are required. For example, the domain knowledge involved in
377   problem p in Figure 1 is only one - ”Isosceles (Equilateral) Triangle”, so the reasoning requirements
      for the problem are not significant. From the analysis of the average domain knowledge (Average

      knowledge) in Table 1, the average domain knowledge of all four datasets exceeded 1.5, indicating
379   the existence of geometric problems involving many domain knowledge, further proving that GPS
380   has a clear reasoning requirement. In Appendix E, we further refined the distribution of domain
381   knowledge involved in the problem. We analyzed and found that problems involving more than 2
382   domain knowledge accounts for a considerable proportion, with 71.4% in the GeoQA dataset and
383   40.9% in the GeoQA+ dataset. Specifically, in the GeoQA dataset, 9.4% of the questions involve
384   domain knowledge exceeding 4. This indicates that GPS tasks require complex reasoning to be
385   completed.
386   Computation. The computational requirements for solving geometric problems are related to multi-
387   digit arithmetic. Multi-digit arithmetic refers to arithmetic operations involving numbers. For ex-
388   ample, the numerical values involved in solving the problem p5 in Appendix F include 222, and
389   38707.567, where the largest number 38707.567 is a 5-digit number and the smallest number 222 is

391   arithmetic computation of decimals numerical values as 0-digit. For example, the numerical values
392   involved in solving the problem p0 in Appendix F include 74 , 57 , and 0.429, which are regarded as
393
      decimals. Therefore, we take 0 (the decimals) as the multi-digit. In Figure 3, we statistically an-

394
      that the biggest computational requirement in these datasets is also in the five-digit arithmetic, and
395
      the vast majority (over 98%) of problems are lower than 3-digit arithmetic. Therefore, the GPS task
396   requires a small amount of computation.

536   performance improvement is not due to the quality of the demonstration, but rather to the input
537   format, output format, and the logic and structure of the demonstration; (2) GPS tasks emphasize
538   reasoning ability more than computational power; (3) Specialized prompting methods could be de-
539   signed to enhance the model’s performance. Overall, developing an LLM that fully adapts to GPS
