009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual Information Extraction (VIE) focuses on extracting named entities and their
012            relationships from visually rich document images. Traditionally, VIE systems rely
013            on three separate models to handle three distinct subtasks, but the emerging trend
014            in research is to design a single model that can address all of these tasks simultane-
015            ously. However, current methods face quadratic computational complexity when
016
               extracting entity relationships, as they must iterate over all token pairs. To address
               this issue, this paper introduces a Unified VIE Diffusion Model (UniVIEDM) for
017
               all tasks within VIE. UniVIEDM generates entity labels and their relationships
018
               conditioned on their plane coordinates, greatly reducing the computational com-
019            plexity. UniVIEDM represents the layout of each visually rich document as a
020            plane graph and converts the three subtasks into plane graph generation problems.
021            During the pre-training stage, UniVIEDM leverages a jump-diffusion process to
022            learn to generate valid sets of bounding boxes for all words and line segments
023            connecting different boxes. During the fine-tuning stage, UniVIEDM employs a
024            continuous-time Markov chain diffusion model to learn to predict the labels of
025            boxes and line segments based on their coordinate features.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The visual information extraction task (VIE) involves extracting texts of multiple key segments from
030
      given document images and saving these texts to structured documents. With the acceleration of the
031   digitization process, the VIE task has been regarded as a crucial part of intelligent document process-
032   ing and is required by many real-world applications in various industries such as finance, medical
033   treatment, and insurance Cui (2021). A VIE task is often divided into three sub-tasks, namely line
034   grouping, semantic entity recognition (SER) and relation extraction (RE) Li et al. (2021b); Zhang
035   et al. (2021); Lin et al. (2024), the illustration of three sub-tasks can be found in Figure 1. Line
036   grouping aims to combine different words into text fields, SER aims to determine the semantic la-
037   bels of different text fields, and RE aims to identify the key-value pair relationships between text
038   fields. The past decades have witnessed the progress of VIE Gu et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2020;
039
      2021a); Huang et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2021b); Li et al. (2021a); Appalaraju et al. (2021). However,
      since these three subtasks are modeled separately, the VIE systems are overly complex. Therefore,
040
      one emerging trend in research is to design a single model that can address all of these tasks simulta-
041
      neously. In this paper, we aim to unify the three subtasks at the levels of task formulation, modeling
042   approach, and inference model. This unification allows the system to perform all tasks using a single
043   model, simplifying the overall structure and reducing computational complexity greatly.
044
045
      Current approaches for developing general models for VIE rely on pre-trained multimodal encoders
      to learn robust features for tokens and their pairs, and then determine labels based on these fea-
046
      tures. Notable systems include ESP Yang et al. (2023), PEneo Lin et al. (2024), and UniVIE Hu
047
      et al. (2024). For instance, the UniVIE model constructs an N × N token pair matrix, using the
048   diagonal elements to predict token labels and the off-diagonal elements to predict inter- or intra-
049   entity relationships. These approaches typically encounter two significant challenges. First, they
050   face scaling issues, as the size of the token pair matrix increases quadratically with the token length
051   N . Second, while they unify the task formulation across the three subtasks, they fail to unify the
052   underlying mathematical principles, resulting in substantial design differences between the neural
053   networks used for each subtask. In summary, the question of how to unify the three subtasks at the
      levels of task formulation, modeling approach, and inference model remains an open challenge.

071
072   To overcome these limitations, this paper introduces the concept of plane graphs to model layout
073   modality information, transforming the three subtasks (line clustering, SER, and RE) into plane
074   graph generation problems. As illustrated in Figure 2, each word’s bounding box is represented
075   as a ”node” in the graph, and the relationships (such as being on the same line or forming a key-
076   value pair) between pairs of words are represented as ”edges” in the graph. Plane graphs embed
077   ”nodes” and ”edges” in a plane, allowing them to be described using natural coordinates. This
078   enables connectivity between ”nodes” to be determined based on the natural coordinates of ”nodes”
079
      and ”edges”. Plane graphs differ from general graphs by using natural coordinates of ”nodes” and
      ”edges” (continuous random variables) to describe connectivity, rather than relying on an ”adjacency
080
      matrix” (discrete random variables). This approach provides a unified data format, allowing line
081
      grouping and RE to be solved based on the natural coordinates of ”edges”, just as SER is based on
082   the natural coordinates of ”nodes”. The introduction of the plane graph concept unifies the modeling
083   methods of the three subtasks, allowing them to be completed as an integrated whole.
084
085
      Meanwhile, it is non-trivial to learn a valid generative model for plane graphs. First, because the
      number of nodes (edges) is not fixed, the collection of coordinate data for all nodes (edges) forms
086
      ”trans-dimensional data,” meaning the data dimension varies across samples. Because the subtrac-
087
      tion between two trans-dimensional data is not well defined, the score function in diffusion models
088   that excel at modeling ”fixed-dimensional data,” such as DDPM Ho et al. (2020), is not applicable.
089   Second, because the probability of a plane graph does not depend on the order of nodes (or edges),
090   auto-regressive models like transformers are not well-suited for this task. Third, since the variables
091   in a plane graph include both continuous (coordinates) and discrete (labels) elements, the generative
092   model must be capable of handling mixed-type random variables.
093
      This paper proposes UniVIEDM, a unified layout model capable of addressing all tasks within VIE.
094   UniVIEDM incorporates two continuous-time diffusion processes: the first is based on a jump-
095   diffusion process, and the second on a Continuous Time Markov Chain (CTMC). In the jump-
096   diffusion process, UniVIEDM learns to generate valid coordinates for nodes (edges) and produces
097   coordinate features. In the CTMC process, UniVIEDM generates the labels for both nodes and
098   edges, conditioned on the coordinate features and text features, simultaneously completing the tasks
099   of line grouping, SER, and RE. During the jump-diffusion process, a new node (or edge) is added
100   to the existing plane graph with a small probability, which is controlled by a node (or edge) number
101   prediction network. The coordinates of the new node (or edge) are predicted by a transformer net-
102
      work. Throughout this process, a coordinate-denoising graph neural network continuously modifies
      the coordinates of existing nodes (or edges). During the CTMC process, a label-denoising graph
103
      neural network predicts the label of each node (or edge) based on the coordinate and text features.
104

129   2.1   U NIFIED VIE M ODELS
130
131   The challenge of unifying all three tasks within VIE has attracted significant attention from re-
132   searchers Zhang et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2021); Cheng et al. (2022); He et al. (2023); Yang et al.
133   (2023); Lin et al. (2024); Hu et al. (2024). Both generative and discriminative models are viable
134
      technical approaches. While the discriminative approach has been extensively researched, the gen-
      erative approach has not. In the early days, discriminative models like TRIE Zhang et al. (2020),
135
      VIES Wang et al. (2021), and TRIE++ Cheng et al. (2022) proposed to integrate the OCR stage with
136
      SER. However, their drawback lies in the poor generalization ability to different downstream tasks.
137   Later, ESP Yang et al. (2023) replaced the OCR stage with a ConvNeXt-FPN backbone to detect
138   text and output visual feature maps. The SER and RE modules are sequentially concatenated after
139   the ConvNeXt-FPN backbone. However, ESP overlooks the error accumulation problem because
140   the SER and RE modules are processed sequentially. PEneo Lin et al. (2024) and UniVIE Hu et al.
141   (2024) both constructed an N by N token pair matrix to perform the three subtasks. Inspired by the
142   TPLinker Wang et al. (2020) method, PEneo constructed five token pair matrixes and fed them into
143   three different layers to accomplish line extraction, line grouping, and entity linking. UniVIE used
144   the diagonal matrix elements to predict the labels of tokens and the off-diagonal elements to predict
145
      the inter- or intra-entity relationships. Both PEneo and UniVIE face scaling issues because the size
      of the token pair matrix grows quadratically with the token length N .
146
147   State-of-the-art generative models encompass autoregressive models, such as LLMs Yenduri et al.
148   (2024), and diffusion models, like DDPM Ho et al. (2020). ICL-D3IE He et al. (2023) first applies
149   OCR to extract the contents and coordinates of words. Then, it fine-tunes a large language model
150   (LLM) to predict entity labels and spatial relationships between entities. Rather than explicitly
151
      modeling the layout modality, ICL-D3IE requires the LLM to determine spatial relationships by
      directly comparing the coordinate values. In contrast, this paper proposes an explicit diffusion layout
152
      model that addresses the three subtasks from a unified perspective.

      2.2   L AYOUT M ODELS
155
156   A layout model is defined as a non-sequential collection of mixed-type random variables (coordi-
157   nates and labels) and their joint distribution. Modeling the layout modality is a challenging problem
158   that has captured the attention of many researchers Li et al. (2019); Jyothi et al. (2019); Zheng et al.
159   (2019); Lee et al. (2020); Patil et al. (2020); Gupta et al. (2021); Arroyo et al. (2021); Yamaguchi
160   (2021); Kikuchi et al. (2021); Jiang et al. (2022); Kong et al. (2022); Chai et al. (2023). Their prob-
161   lem setup has been generating sets of box coordinates given sets of labels. Researchers have focused
      on addressing the challenges of varying data dimensions and insensitivity to the generation order.

      Autoregressive models are effective at generating an arbitrary number of boxes. This approach
      converts the sets of coordinates into a sequence and then designs backbones that excel at model-
177
      ing sequential data. Typical models like Layout-Transformer Gupta et al. (2021), BLT Kong et al.
178
      (2022), and Layout-DM Chai et al. (2023) first use discrete tokens to replace the continuous coor-
179   dinates and then concatenate the coordinate and label tokens to form the target sequence. However,
180   autoregressive models struggle with the generation order problem due to their sequential nature.
181
182
      Autoencoder models are typically effective at addressing the problem of generation order. This
      approach usually first samples a certain number of random box coordinates and then iteratively
183
      modifies them. Models such as Layout-GAN Li et al. (2019), NDN Lee et al. (2020), and VTN Ar-
184
      royo et al. (2021) employ different neural network architectures, including transformers and GNNs.
185   However, autoencoders do not consider the impact of the number of boxes during generation.
186
187   A promising direction is to combine the strengths of variational autoencoders and autoregressive
188
      models. In the Layout-VAE Jyothi et al. (2019), a Count-VAE predicts the number of boxes for
      each label. Based on the label sets and the number of boxes, a Bbox-VAE generates the contin-
189
      uous coordinates of each box one-by-one. However, Layout-VAE suffers from a weak encoder-
190
      decoder problem because denoising diffusion models have been empirically shown to outperform
191   VAE encoder-decoder Chen et al. (2024).

237   3.2   T HE F RAMEWORK OF U NI VIEDM
238
      Inspired by equations (3)-(6), the framework of UniVIEDM consists of 4 modules. As shown in
239
      Figure 3, 4 modules are organized into three stages. In the first stage, UniVIEDM trains a node co-
240   ordinate diffusion module to fit the probability distribution of node coordinates on the plane. It then
241   encodes the observed node coordinates to obtain their feature representations. The edge coordinate
242   diffusion module generates the edge coordinates, conditioned on node coordinate features.
243
      The second and third stages correspond to the node and edge label generation modules, which oper-
244
      ate simultaneously. Each module uses the feature representations from the node and edge coordinate
245
      diffusion modules as conditions for label generation. Starting from random label sets, they employ
246   diffusion models designed for discrete variables to predict the labels, conditioned on the feature
247   representations of the corresponding spatial coordinates.

249   3.3   C OORDINATES D ENOISING BASED ON J UMP -D IFFUSION
250
251   This paper adopts the jump-diffusion process for the forward and backward processes. The math-
252   ematics of a jump-diffusion process can be found in Campbell et al. (2024). Over time, a jump-
253   diffusion process can involve two types of events: jump events and diffusion events. Jump events
254   occur at specific moments, changing the number of nodes (or edges) by either adding or removing
255   one (a node/edge is randomly deleted in the forward process, while a new node/edge is added in
256
      the reverse process). Diffusion events happen during the remaining time, continually adjusting the
      coordinates of each node (or edge) based on the current coordinates (Gaussian noise is added to each
257
      node/edge’s coordinates in the forward process, and a portion of this noise is removed in the reverse
258
      process). This approach enables the continuous coordinate diffusion algorithm to flexibly determine
259   and generate the appropriate number of edges and their coordinates, based on the observed node
260   coordinates.

314   3.5   T RAINING AND I NFERENCE
315
316   According to the research in Campbell et al. (2024), our training loss of coordinates diffusion in-
317   cludes 3 terms. The first term minimizes the score-matching loss of coordinates of existing nodes.
318   The second term minimizes the jumping-rate loss. The third term minimizes the cross-entropy loss
319   of predicted initial labels. The label diffusion loss is the continuous time elbo derived in Campbell
320
      et al. (2022).
321   During testing, UniVIEDM first uses the node coordinates denoising network to extract features.
322   Then the edge coordinates denoising network will generate the coordinates of edges in the line
323   grouping task and RE task. At last, the predictor-corrector Campbell et al. (2022) sampling method
      is used to determine the labels of both nodes and edges based on their coordinates features.

      a widely used public benchmark, comprising 800 training images, 100 validation images, and 100

      Evaluation metrics. In prior works such as LayoutLMv3, RE tasks are evaluated by inputting the
      ground truth from line clustering and SER directly into the RE module. This approach, however,
418
      neglects the issue of error propagation from earlier tasks, resulting in artificially higher performance
419
      metrics compared to the system’s true capabilities. To address this, studies like PEneo Lin et al.
420   (2024) take error accumulation into account, offering more realistic performance evaluations. In
421   this section, we adopt the testing methodology used in PEneo. To rigorously evaluate UniVIEDM’s
422   RE performance, we designed a post-processing step for the edge coordinate diffusion process. For
423   each generated edge, we first calculate the coordinates of its two endpoints and then identify the
424   nearest node to each. The edge is considered to predict a relationship between these two nodes.
425   We discard edges if they connect the same node or if the endpoints are too far from any nodes.
426   For the SIBR dataset, the text labeled as “answer” is always concatenated after the text labeled as
427   “question.” The concatenated texts are then used to compute the pair-level F1-score.
428   Quantitative and qualitative results. As shown in Table 1, our model achieves the highest SER F1
429   score of 96.86%, surpassing all baseline models, demonstrating its superior ability in recognizing
430   sequence entities. However, when comparing Pair F1 scores, which assess the accuracy of predicting
431   relationships between entities, we observe more variation across models. While UniVIE and other
      baselines perform well, our model remains competitive, particularly in the “LayoutLMv3 Chinese

472   4.5   C ONCLUSION
473
474   In this paper, we introduced UniVIEDM, a unified diffusion model for Visual Information Extraction
475   (VIE) tasks, addressing key challenges in extracting named entities and relationships from visually
476   rich documents. By leveraging plane graph structures and integrating layout and textual features,
477   our model unifies the traditionally separate subtasks of line grouping, semantic entity recognition
478   (SER), and relation extraction (RE) into a single framework. The introduction of jump-diffusion for
479   coordinate generation and continuous-time Markov chain (CTMC) processes for label prediction
480   allows UniVIEDM to effectively handle the complexities of trans-dimensional and mixed-type data.
481   Extensive experiments on public datasets, including SIBR and CORD, demonstrate that UniVIEDM
482   achieves state-of-the-art performance in SER F1 while maintaining competitive results in the more
483   stringent Pair F1 metric. Our model’s ability to integrate layout and text features proves crucial in
484   achieving robust and accurate VIE results, as shown by its superior performance across all tasks.
485   The results validate the effectiveness of our unified approach, suggesting that it can overcome the
      limitations of previous systems that treat these tasks separately.
