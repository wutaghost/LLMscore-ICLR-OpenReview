009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Adaptive Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is an effective strategy to alle-
012            viate hallucination of large language models (LLMs). It dynamically determines
013            whether LLMs need external knowledge for generation and invokes retrieval ac-
014            cordingly. This paper introduces Self-aware Knowledge Retrieval (S EA KR), a
015            novel adaptive RAG model that extracts self-aware uncertainty of LLMs from
016            their internal states. S EA KR activates retrieval when the LLMs present high self-
017            aware uncertainty for generation. To effectively integrate retrieved knowledge
018
               snippets, S EA KR re-ranks them based on LLM’s self-aware uncertainty to pre-
               serve the snippet that reduces their uncertainty to the utmost. To facilitate solving
019
               complex tasks that require multiple retrievals, S EA KR utilizes their self-aware un-
020
               certainty to choose among different reasoning strategies. Our experiments on both
021            complex and simple Question Answering datasets show that S EA KR outperforms
022            existing adaptive RAG methods.

      nature of LLMs can be utilized to determine when retrieval is needed and help with knowledge in-
055   tegration. Motivated by this, we propose SElf-Aware Knowledge Retrieval (S EA KR) for adaptive
056   RAG. To the best of our knowledge, S EA KR is the first to leverage self-awareness from the inter-

059
      To decide when to retrieve, existing adaptive RAG Wang et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2023); Su et al.
060
      (2024) judges the knowledge sufficiency of LLMs solely based on their outputs, which is prone to
061   ubiquitous self-bias of LLMs Xu et al. (2024). In contrast, S EA KR initiates retrieval self-aware
062   uncertainty from the internal states of LLMs, which more accurately determines the knowledge
063   demand. To be specific, the self-aware uncertainty of LLMs is extracted from the internal states
064   in the feed-forward network (FFN) of each layer corresponding to the last generated token. The
065   consistency measure across multiple generations to the same prompt is computed as the self-aware
066   uncertainty score of LLMs, subsequently used for the retrieval decision and knowledge integration.
067   To effectively integrate retrieved knowledge into the generation process, which is largely neglected
068   by previous adaptive RAG methods, S EA KR designs two adaptive integration strategies based on
069   the LLM self-awareness: 1) Self-aware re-ranking. S EA KR asks the LLM to read multiple recalled
070   snippets and selects the knowledge that reduces most of its uncertainty as the augmented context.
071   2) Self-aware reasoning. S EA KR supports iterative knowledge retrieval to gather multiple knowl-
072   edge for answering complex questions. With multiple retrieved knowledge, S EA KR integrates dif-
073   ferent reasoning strategies, including direct generation and comprehensive reasoning, to digest the
074   knowledge. It then selects the strategy that produces the least generation uncertainty.
075   We conduct experiments on complex question-answering (QA) and simple QA tasks. We find
076   that S EA KR brings substantial improvement over existing adaptive RAG methods on complex QA
077   benchmarks. Our ablation study shows that dynamically integrating retrieved knowledge brings
078   even more performance gain than self-aware retrieval, further highlighting the necessity of dynami-
079   cal integration for adaptive RAG.

086   2.1   R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTED G ENERATION
087
088   Retrieval augmented generation (RAG) system typically comprises a search engine for knowledge
089   retrieval and a Large Language Model (LLM) for answer generation Khandelwal et al. (2019); Guu
090   et al. (2020); Lewis et al. (2020); Borgeaud et al. (2022); Ram et al. (2023); Shi et al. (2023). Given
091   a user-posed question, RAG first searches for relevant knowledge snippets using the search engine
092
      and then generates the answer via machine reading comprehension Chen et al. (2017).
093   Adaptive retrieval augmented generation dynamically determines whether LLMs require re-
094   trieved knowledge, thereby reducing the adverse effect of inaccurately retrieved information.
095   FLARE Jiang et al. (2023) and DRAGIN Su et al. (2024) activate the search engine when LLMs
096   output tokens with low probability. Self-RAG Asai et al. (2023) and Wang et al. (2024) prompt
097   LLMs to decide on retrieval. Self-knowledge guided generation Wang et al. (2023) trains a classifi-
098
      cation model to judge the factuality of model generation.
099   Existing adaptive RAG methods mainly face two challenges. 1) To decide when to retrieve, it is
100   superficial to have the decision of retrieval solely on the output of LLM. However, the retrieval
101   decision made by LLMs is still at risk of hallucination, which potentially does not reliably indi-
102   cate the actual knowledge sufficiency Yona et al. (2024). Furthermore, LLMs have the tendency
103   to confidently produce incorrect contents even when correct knowledge is missing from their pa-
104
      rameters Huang et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024). 2) To integrate retrieved knowledge, these attempts
      rely on the correctness of search engine returned knowledge, neglecting to re-rank multiple retrieved
105
      knowledge and optimize the reasoning paths.
106
107   Retrieval augmented reasoning integrates the reasoning capabilities of LLMs into the RAG frame-
      work to solve complex questions. IRCoT Trivedi et al. (2022) implements retrieval augmentation

      2.2        S ELF - AWARENESS IN I NTERNAL S TATES OF LLM S
132
133   Most of the mainstream LLMs are stacks of Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017) decoders. To predict
134   the next token, without losing generality, the ith layer processes the hidden representation  H(l−1)
135                                                           (l)                 (l−1)

      from its previous layer according to the formula: H = FFN Attn H                     , where Attn(·)
136   denotes attention sub-layer, FFN(·) is the feed-forward sub-layer.
137
138   Many works Meng et al. (2022); Li et al. (2022); Gurnee & Tegmark (2023); Zou et al. (2023)
139   show that the hidden representations H(l) entail non-trivial information about the internal states
140
      of LLMs. These internal states are capable of being used to detect hallucinated generations from
      LLMs. One direct way is to train a factuality classifier with internal states as input Kadavath et al.
141
      (2022); Azaria & Mitchell (2023); Chen et al. (2023b); Zhang et al. (2024). Non-factual generation
142
      can also be detected as uncertainty of LLMs by internal state level consistency measuring among
143   multiple generations Chen et al. (2023a).
144
145   These works potentially pave the way for improving adaptive RAG via examining the self-awareness
146
      from internal states. Since model decoding breaks down continuous internal states into discrete to-
      kens, information loss during this process is inevitable. Compared with output-level self-awareness
147
      detection, internal states-level detection is more substantial and therefore better suited for adaptive
148
      RAG.

151   3        S ELF -AWARE K NOWLEDGE R ETRIEVAL
152
153   As shown in Figure 2, S EA KR has three key components. 1) a search engine S(·), which returns
154   ranked knowledge snippets according to the relevance to its input search query qry. 2) a large
155   language model, denoted as LLM(c), which takes a context c as input, outputs a continuation to the
156   context. Most importantly, (3) a self-aware uncertainty estimator U (c), to quantify the uncertainty
157   level of LLM to generate for input context c.
158   For each input natural language question q, S EA KR adopts a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Wei et al.
159   (2022) style iterative reasoning strategy. It maintains two buffers to collect retrieved knowledge
160   K = {ki } and generated rationales R = {ri } during the iteration. During the ith iteration, S EA KR
161   generates a rationale ri , before which it dynamically determines whether to augment the generation
      with external knowledge, i.e., self-aware retrieval (§3.1). If S EA KR decides to invoke retrieval, it


                                                                                   3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      adaptively selects knowledge ki with self-aware re-ranking (§3.2). Finally, S EA KR integrates all
163   previously gathered information, including K and R, into the final answer, with self-aware reasoning
164   (§3.3). At each stage, S EA KR utilizes the self-aware uncertainty estimator to measure the LLM
165   uncertainty level from its internal states (§3.4).
166
167   3.1   S ELF - AWARE R ETRIEVAL
168
169   Self-aware retrieval relies on the self-aware uncertainty estimator U (·) to decide whether to use
170   retrieved knowledge for rationale generation. In the following, we introduce our design to organize
171   the input context, to generate the search query, and to generate the rationale.
172   Input Context. We first prepare the input context to prompt LLMs to generate one step of rationale
173   without retrieval, and use U (·) to examine whether the LLM is uncertain so as to invoke retrieval
174   accordingly. We organize q and historical rationales R into the input context cr using the following
175   prompt template, we show details of the prompting template in Appendix D.1:
176
177      [In-Context Learning Examples]                     Rationale [r1] Rationale [r2]
178      Rationale ...... For question:                     [q] The Next Rationale is:
179
      Here, placeholders are denoted in square brackets. Retrieval is triggered if the self-aware uncertainty
180
      exceeds an empirical threshold U (cr ) > δ.
181
182   Query Generation. To generate a query for the search engine, the LLM performs a pseudo-
183   generation: rs = LLM(cr ). Tokens in r indicating high uncertainty due to their low probability
184   are identified and removed from the pseudo-generated rationale to form the search query Jiang et al.
185
      (2023). We expect the retrieved knowledge to contain information that directly provides information
      to fill in the uncertain tokens in rs .
186
187   Rationale Generation. Finally, S EA KR generates rationale to proceed on answering the question
188   q. If retrieval is invoked, then knowledge snippets k are added to the current input context cr .
189   Otherwise, the input context remains unchanged. The generated rationale r = LLM(c) is then
190   appended to the rationale buffer.
191
192   3.2   S ELF - AWARE R E - RANKING
193
      Traditional RAG ranks the retrieved knowledge according to its relevance to the posed query. This
194
      approach overlooks how the retrieved knowledge aligns with the intrinsic knowledge of LLMs, po-
195
      tentially leading to performance degradation when the retrieved information contradicts the model’s
196   internal knowledge. Unlike existing methods, S EA KR prioritizes the utility of the retrieved knowl-
197   edge in reducing the LLM’s self-aware uncertainty. It selects the knowledge that most effectively
198   reduces the LLM’s uncertainty.
199
      Specifically, S EA KR allows the search engine to retrieve multiple knowledge pieces. We preserve
200
      the top N results and organize them along with previously generated rationales using the following
201
      template (Detailed in Appendix D.2):
202
203    [In-Context Learning Examples] Rationale [r1] Rationale ......
204    Knowledge Evidence: [k] For question: [q] The Next Rationale is:
205
206   As the search engine recalls top N different knowledge snippets, S EA KR creates N input contexts
207   and evaluates their corresponding self-aware uncertainty from the LLM. The knowledge piece with
208   the least uncertainty evaluated by U (·) is selected.
209
210   3.3   S ELF - AWARE R EASONING
211
212
      The retrieval process within the S EA KR system halts under two conditions: 1) the LLM signals the
      end of generation with a prefatory statement, “So the final answer is”, terminating the iteration; 2)
213
      the retrieval activity reaches the maximum limit.
214
215   To effectively synthesize all previously retrieved knowledge, S EA KR employs two distinct rea-
      soning strategies: 1) Reasoning with generated rationales R. This approach prompts the LLM to


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      directly generate the final answer. It puts the instruction “So the final answer is” right after the last
217   generated rationale. 2) Reasoning with retrieved knowledge K. This strategy involves concatenating
218   all re-ranked retrieved knowledge, which is then prepended to the question to serve as a reference
219   context. S EA KR then requires the LLM to engage in CoT reasoning based on this augmented textual
220   context. We show detailed prompting templates in Appendix D.3. The final answer is generated us-
221   ing the strategy that promotes the lowest level of uncertainty evaluated by U (·) between the answers
222   generated with these two strategies.
223
224   3.4     S ELF - AWARE U NCERTAINTY E STIMATOR
225
226   For input context c = ⟨IN⟩1 · · · ⟨IN⟩n with n tokens, LLM works as a probabilistic distribution
227   conditioned on the input context. To generate, it outputs o with m tokens ending with an ⟨EOS⟩
228   token: LLM(c) = ⟨OUT⟩1 · · · ⟨OUT⟩m ⟨EOS⟩. We aim to extract how certain LLMs are that o is a
229   correct continuation for c. To this end, we follow INSIDE Chen et al. (2023a) and measure the
230
      uncertainty in the hidden space of the ⟨EOS⟩ token.
231   Specifically, for an input context c, we first sample generation and preserve the hidden representation
232                                         (l)
      for its ⟨EOS⟩ token, denoted as H⟨EOS⟩ . As ⟨EOS⟩ attends to all previous tokens, it compresses
233                                                                    (l)
      information on both the output and the input. Then, we treat H⟨EOS⟩ as a random variable, and sample
234
                                                                                       (l)
235   k different generations from the LLM for the same input context, whose H⟨EOS⟩ are subsequently
236   used to compute their Gram matrix Horn & Johnson (2012), which measures the correlation among
237   each pair of representations. Finally, the uncertainty of the LLM is evaluated as the determinant of
238   the regularized Gram matrix, a score of the consistency among a set of representations.
239   S EA KR uses the regularized Gram determinant as the self-aware uncertainty score for two reasons.
240   1) Pre-trained LLMs are proved to be well-calibrated probabilistic models, which behave less con-
241   sistently when producing incorrect contents Kadavath et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2023). 2) The Gram
242   determinant examines the consistency on the internal state level, free from the influence of natural
243   language where the same semantics can be expressed differently Qi et al. (2022).

      4.1.1    B ENCHMARK DATASETS
257   We use knowledge-intensive QA tasks, including both complex QA and simple QA.
258
259   Complex QA requires the model to perform multi-hop reasoning to answer the questions. Each
260
      question also needs multiple supporting knowledge. Specifically, for complex QA tasks, we test
      on 2WikiMultiHopQA (2Wiki, Ho et al., 2020), HotpotQA (HPQA, Yang et al., 2018), and the
261
      answerable subset of IIRC Ferguson et al. (2020).
262
263   Simple QA does not require multi-hop reasoning. These questions focus more on evaluating ac-
264   curate knowledge acquisition. We use NaturalQuestions (NQ Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), TriviaQA
265   (Joshi et al., 2017), and SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016) in the experiments.
266   We use them in the open-domain QA setting, where documents for machine reading comprehension
267   are discarded. For dataset splitting, S EA KR is tuning-free and thus does not need a training set. We
268   use a sampled subset from NQ’s training split to search for hyper-parameters, which are adopted by
269   all other datasets. We follow IRCoT Trivedi et al. (2022) to use their official development set and
      DPR Karpukhin et al. (2020) for simple QA.

      4.1.2    BASELINES
271
272   We mainly compare S EA KR with representative RAG models, which include:
273
      Non-adaptive RAG-based methods. • Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Wei et al. (2022) prompts an

      LLM to answer questions with multi-step explanations. We implement CoT with similar prompts as
275   S EA KR by removing the retrieval-related instructions. • IRCoT Trivedi et al. (2022) interweaves
276   CoT reasoning with retrieval augmented generation strategy. IRCoT retrieves for every reasoning
277   step by default and integrates the top-ranked knowledge.
278
      Adaptive RAG-based methods. • Self-RAG Asai et al. (2023) fine-tunes the LLM to generate a spe-
279
      cial token to indicate whether they need retrieval. The LLM is also trained to criticize the retrieved

      knowledge. The training data is generated by GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023) with seed questions from
281   NaturalQuestions Kwiatkowski et al. (2019). • FLARE Jiang et al. (2023) triggers retrieval when
282   the LLM generates tokens with low probability. If so, it retrieves knowledge and regenerates the
283   answer. The original FLARE does not support complex QA. We re-implement FLARE with IRCoT
284   strategy to support evaluation on complex QA. • DRAGIN Su et al. (2024) decides to retrieve when
285   low-probability tokens are generated and reformulates the query based on attention weights.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we propose self-aware knowledge retrieval (S EA KR) to perform adaptive RAG.
489   S EA KR extracts self-aware uncertainty of LLMs from their internal states, and uses this as an in-
490   dicator to invoke knowledge retrieval and dynamically integrate retrieved knowledge. Experiments
491   on both complex QA and simple QA tasks show that S EA KR outperforms existing adaptive base-
492   lines. It is intriguing to further explore whether we can further enhance the uncertainty awareness
493   via model training in the future.

      A     L IMITATIONS
757
758   We discuss the limitations of S EA KR.
759
760   (1) Scope of Usage. As S EA KR requires access to the internal state of LLMs, this limits the usability
761
      of S EA KR to open-sourced LLMs. However, the most powerful and widely adopted LLMs are still
      preserved by commercial companies, such as GPT series model. We still need to explore new
762
      ways to estimate the self-aware uncertainty from the output of the language model, rather than their
763
      internal states.
764
765   (2) Task Coverage. We mainly evaluate S EA KR on short-form question answering tasks, neglect-
766   ing a broad spectrum of natural language processing tasks, such as long-form question answering,
767
      creative writing, etc.
768   (3) Computation Issues. To compute Gram determinant, S EA KR requires the backbone to conduct
769   20 pseudo-generations, which is computationally costly. We explore the engineering trick to mitigate
770   this issue—by deploying the backbone LLM with vLLM Kwon et al. (2023), which implements
771   paged attention to support parallel inference in a single batch. Thus, the latency of 20 pseudo-
772   generation is roughly the same as a single pseudo-generation. All the experiments can be held on a
773
      single NVidia 3090 GPU with 24GiB GRAM.
774   (4) Model Scaling. Due to our limited computation resources, we are not able to deploy LLMs
775   larger than one with 8 billion parameters. As recent evidences suggest that model scaling is more
776   closely related to training FLOPS, rather than model scale. We thus compare between LLaMA-2
777   (7B) and LLaMA-3 (8B) to verify whether S EA KR is scalable to more powerful LLMs. This is
778   because although they have similar parameter scales, LLaMA-3 is trained on 10× more corpora,
779
      and thus 10× more FLOPS than LLaMA-2.
780   (5) Information Retrieval. The authors would like to mention that, with the development of in-
781   formation retrieval technology, the second part of S EA KR (i.e., Self-aware Re-ranking) could be
782   surpassed by advanced IR methods, in the future.
783
784   B     B ROAD I MPACT
785
786   We discuss the ethical considerations and broader impact of S EA KR.
787
788
      (1) Intended Usage. S EA KR falls into the category of retrieval augmented generation, which is
      intended to increase the factual correctness of LLMs. Thus, the intention of our work is to improve
789
      the trustworthiness of LLM.
790
791   (2) Potential Misuse. However, for detailed technology we adopted, it can be misused to create
792   misleading information. For example, the self-aware uncertainty estimator can be used as an adver-
793   sarial signal for model training, which could make models better at deceiving humans with uncertain
794
      information. Another issue is the increased integration of LLM and IR systems, which may be used
      to automate cyber manhunt.
795
796   (3) Risk Control. S EA KR is developed upon open-sourced LLMs. We will also release our code.
797   We hope that transparency helps to monitor and prevent its mis-usage.
798   (4) Intellectual Artifacts. We cite the creator of our used intellectual artifacts. Specifically, we use
799   6 question answering benchmark dataset in this paper, they are 2WikiMultiHopQA Ho et al. (2020),
800   HotpotQA Yang et al. (2018), IIRC Ferguson et al. (2020), NaturalQuestion Kwiatkowski et al.
801   (2019), TriviaQA Joshi et al. (2017), and SQuAD Rajpurkar et al. (2016). We would also like to
802   acknowledge creators of Self-RAG Asai et al. (2023), FLARE Jiang et al. (2023), and DRAGIN Su
803   et al. (2024) for sharing their codebases, which are used to reproduce their methods, along with
804   IRCoT. All the used intellectual artifacts’ license allows for academic usage.
