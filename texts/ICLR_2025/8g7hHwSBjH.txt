009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has shown its impressive capability of
012            providing reliable answer predictions and addressing severe hallucination prob-
013            lems. A typical RAG implementation adopts powerful retrieval models to ex-
014            tract external information and leverage large language models (LLMs) to gener-
015            ate corresponding answers. Different with that, recent LLM-based retrieval has
016            raised much attention because it brings substantial improvements in information
017            retrieval (IR) via LLMs’ vigorous semantic understanding capability. However,
018            directly applying LLM to RAG systems remains certain challenges. This may
019
               cause feature locality problems since massive parametric knowledge impedes the
               effective usage of the global information among all corpus, e.g., a LLM-based re-
020
               triever usually inputs the summary of documents instead of the whole documents.
021
               Moreover, various tasks pre-trained in LLMs induce severe variance, which fur-
022            ther weakens its performance as the retriever. To address these issues, we propose
023            a novel two-stage fine-tuning architecture called Invar-RAG. In the retrieval stage,
024            a LLM-based retriever is constructed by integrating a LoRA-based representation
025            learning to address the feature locality problem. To justify and consolidate this
026            retrieval’s performance, two patterns (i.e., invariant and variant patterns) and an
027            invariance loss are also developed to alleviate the variance in LLM. Moreover, in
028            the generation stage, a meticulously designed fine-tuning method is devised to im-
029            prove our LLM for accurate answer generation based on the retrieved information.
030
               Experimental results demonstrate that Invar-RAG significantly outperforms exist-
               ing baselines across three Open-domain Question Answering (ODQA) datasets.
031
               The code is available in Supplementary Material to ease reproducibility.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Over the past decade, large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated promising capability in pro-
038   cessing natural language Minaee et al. (2024). Owing to the vast amount of knowledge encoded in
039
      their internal parameters, LLMs such as GPT Achiam et al. (2023) and LLaMa Touvron et al. (2023)
      have demonstrated remarkable performance on various downstream tasks, including Open-domain
040
      Question Answering (ODQA) Zhu et al. (2021), Reading Comprehension Cheng et al. (2023). How-
041
      ever, the fixed parametric knowledge of LLMs has hindered the further applications of LLMs and
042   made them prone to errors (hallucination Rawte et al. (2023) and factual errors Wang et al. (2023)).
043
044   To overcome the limitations of parametric knowledge, one promising approach is Retrieval-
045
      Augmented Generation (RAG) Wang et al. (2023); Lewis et al. (2020). Compared to relying solely
      on parametric knowledge, RAG enables LLMs to use retrievers to access relevant information from
046
      external knowledge sources, enhancing their question-answering abilities. Among the two compo-
047
      nents of RAG, current methods primarily focus on optimizing the retriever to return more relevant
048   documents due to the high cost of fine-tuning and black-box LLM APIs. Previous retrievers lever-
049   aged deep learning technology (e.g., dense retrieval Zhao et al. (2024)) to encode the text represen-
050   tations from the lexical space into the high-dimensional latent space, allowing them to model more
051   complex semantic relationships between queries and corpora. However, the separation between the
052   retriever and generation model has hindered their full integration, limiting their compatibility in
053   downstream applications . Some advanced RAG systems, such as RA-DIT Lin et al. (2023b), have
      adopted joint training mechanisms that fine-tune both the retriever and the generation model for


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      better alignment. However, this approach is impractical due to the need for frequent fine-tuning and
055   fails to utilize the LLMs’ semantic understanding capabilities during the retrieval stage.
056
057   Consequently, generative retrieval (GR), also known as LLM-based retrieval, leverages the para-
058
      metric memory of generative models to directly generate document identifiers (DocIDs) Li et al.
      (2024), which has aroused much attention. By memorizing the documents as the parametric knowl-
059
      edge of LLM, this kind of method breaks the limitations of traditional IR in terms of document
060
      granularity and simple relevance matching Nguyen & Yates (2023), offering more flexibility and
061   creativity, thus better meeting practical needs. However, two severe problems hinder the current
062   LLM-based retrieval. 1)Feature Locality: LLM-based retrieval normally adopt language models
063   to learn the mapping from queries to the relevant document DocIDs. However, these DocIDs ac-
064   tually can not fully represent the global information of the passages. Meanwhile, directly feeding
065   the whole passages into LLM is costly and infeasible, causing a trade-off between effectiveness and
066   efficiency. 2)Retrieval Variance: Due to the inherent generative inconsistency property of large
067   language models , current LLM-based retrieval may generate unforeseeable variances, especially
068   when the input query or the size of context varies, directly results in undesirable and vulnerable
069
      performance which may not be preferred.
070   Considering the problems mentioned above and better leveraging the capability of LLMs, we pro-
071   pose a fully LLM-based architecture with a two-stage fine-tuning method called Invar-RAG, as
072   illustrated in Figure 1. In the retrieval stage, our approach initializes the pre-trained LLaMA Tou-
073   vron et al. (2023) as the backbone and follows the bi-encoder architecture in DPR Karpukhin et al.
074   (2020) to construct our retriever. Compared to normal GR methods which need an iterative pro-
075
      cess of encoding and decoding, we introduce a component called LLM-aligned Retrieval. It first
      represents the input query and corpora into high-dimension space using a small language model
076
      (MiniLM) Wang et al. (2020), then introduces a new loss function constructed by KL-divergence
077
      to align the coarse query-documents pairs representation to the LLM’s representation space. This
078   allows the retriever to leverage the rich prior knowledge of LLM, typically addressing the feature
079   locality caused by only feeding DocIDs to LLM. Moreover, based on the initial objective of our
080   LLM-aligned Retrieval, we introduce the invariance loss to overcome the variance in the retrieval
081   stage. By recognizing the invariant pattern that contributes the most to the performance and gradu-
082   ally forcing the model to rely on the invariant pattern, we can avoid the unforeseeable variances in
083   practice and enhance the robustness of our RAG system. Finally, in the generation stage, we freeze
084   the weights we fine-tuned before and optimize the generation function to allow the LLM to give
085   correct answers to the retrieved documents. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
086
             • We introduce Invar-RAG, a novel framework featuring a two-stage fine-tuning method on
087
               a single shared LLM, including the retrieval stage and generation stage.
088
089          • We introduce a novel LLM-based retrieval method containing representation learning and
090
               invariance loss, respectively addressing the issues of feature locality and retrieval variance.
091          • We validate Invar-RAG’s performance on three public ODQA datasets, no matter for re-
092            trieval performance or generation performance, demonstrating its superiority.

401   3.4     I NVARIANCE A NALYSIS
402
403   In this section, we leverage a special example in TQA to illustrate the effectiveness of our designed
404   invariance loss in two parts: (i) the importance of defining different patterns, (ii) the difference in
405
      retrieval performance that invariance loss brings.
406   As mentioned in Sec. 2.2, we return four different sets of retrieved documents and rerank them by
407   LSR score to identify the invariant pattern. There are two reasons to explain this:
408
409            • Rewriting the query and resizing the context window does affect the normal relevance score
410
                 computing by the dot product, leading to the variance in practice when we feed different
                 lengths or formats of questions to the RAG system to ask for the answer.
411
412            • Prior works Zhang et al. (2024) have shown that adding a suitable amount of irrelevant or
413              relatively ineffective documents does help improve the retrieval performance.
414
      To verify that, we present the normal relevance score and LSR score of each retrieved document in
415
      four different sets in Fig. 3.4. The darker color represents the change that happened in the Top-5
416   documents. We can see that, for the question: ‘Who was the man behind The Chipmunks’, the
417   relevance score for the top-5 documents in each set shows substantial changes while the LSR score
418   does not vary a lot, which means the variance caused by rewriting query or resizing context window
419   change the importance of documents, directly resulting in poor retrieval performance. Moreover,
420   as illustrated in Fig. 3.4, the variant without invariance loss gives the wrong answer to our selected
421   question example while our designed Invar-RAG system accurately predicts the result.

      Retrieval-augmented Language Model: Currently, retrieval-augmented language models have
      proven effective in answering questions by leveraging external information through the integration
462
      of novel retrievers and LLMs Zhu et al. (2021). However, the architectural gap between retrieval and
463
      generation continues to hinder unified optimization across the entire retrieval-augmented generation
464   system Wang et al. (2023). To address the isolation between retrieval and generation, a novel archi-
465   tecture called RA-DIT was introduced Lin et al. (2023b). By aligning retriever scoring with LSR
466   scoring Shi et al. (2023), it has been shown to deliver state-of-the-art performance across various
467   tasks. However, it still employs dense retrievers like DRAGON+ Lin et al. (2023a) in the retrieval
468   stage, which fails to eliminate the problem at its source and introduces inefficiencies throughout
469   the process. Correspondingly, we introduce a representation learning method and invariance loss in
470   our Invar-RAG architecture, which partially addresses these issues and explores a novel approach to
471   using a single LLM for multiple roles within the RAG system.

477
      In this paper, we analyze the challenges and problems of current methods to apply the large language
      model as a retriever in the RAG system and propose a novel framework, Invar-RAG, to address these
478
      challenges. We introduce an LLM-aligned retrieval method, incorporating a well-designed represen-
479
      tation learning approach to align coarse query-document pairs with the LLM’s representation space,
480   allowing our architecture to leverage the extensive parametric knowledge of the LLM to compute
481   relevance scores. Additionally, to address retrieval variance, we propose invariance loss, building
482   on our initial KL-divergence loss, during the retrieval stage to reduce the impact of irrelevant doc-
483   uments. Finally, we perform additional fine-tuning on the same LLM for the answer-generation
484   task, enabling our architecture to better utilize the retrieved information and provide more accu-
485   rate predictions. Extensive experiments on three open-domain question-answering datasets confirm
      Invar-RAG’s superiority and validate the effectiveness of each module.
