009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            With the widespread deployment of deep learning models, they influence their en-
012            vironment in various ways. The induced distribution shifts can lead to unexpected
013            performance degradation in deployed models. Existing methods to anticipate per-
014            formativity typically incorporate information about the deployed model into the
015            feature vector when predicting future outcomes. While enjoying appealing the-
016
               oretical properties, modifying the input dimension of the prediction task is often
               not practical. To address this, we propose a novel technique to adjust pretrained
017
               backbones for performativity in a modular way, achieving better sample efficiency
018
               and enabling the reuse of existing deep learning assets. Focusing on performative
019            label shift, the key idea is to train a shallow adapter module to perform a Bayes-
020            optimal label shift correction to the backbone’s logits given a sufficient statistic of
021            the model to be deployed. As such, our framework decouples the construction of
022            input-specific feature embeddings from the mechanism governing performativity.
023            Motivated by dynamic benchmarking as a use-case, we evaluate our approach
024            under adversarial sampling, for vision and language tasks. We show how it leads
025            to smaller loss along the retraining trajectory and enables us to effectively select
026            among candidate models to anticipate performance degradations. More broadly,
027
               our work provides a first baseline for addressing performativity in deep learning.
028
029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Machine learning models have been experiencing a growing adoption for automated decision-
032   making. High-stake applications necessitate models to generalize beyond the training distribution
033
      and perform robustly over distribution shifts. A prevalent but often neglected cause of distribution
      shift is the model deployment itself. When informing down-stream decisions, and actions, the pre-
034
      dictions of machine learning models can change future data. Such patterns are ubiquitous in social
035
      settings, where algorithmic predictions impact individual expectations, steer consumer choices, or
036   inform policy decisions. Similarly, standard community practices can lead to future data depending
037   on the deployment of past models; this can be through data feedback-loops (Taori & Hashimoto,
038   2023), active learning pipelines (Settles, 2009), and dynamic benchmarks (Nie et al., 2020). Per-
039   formative prediction (Perdomo et al., 2020) articulates how this causal link between predictions and
040   future data surfaces as distribution shift in machine learning pipelines.
041
      It is inevitable that repeatedly ad-hoc trained models become suboptimal after deployment under
042   performativity (Kumar et al., 2022; Recht et al., 2019; Taori et al., 2020). Thus, a natural question
043   to ask is—can we learn to foresee these shifts? Of course, in full generality performative shifts
044   can be arbitrarily complex. However, given a low-dimensional sufficient statistic for the shift (e.g.
045   class-wise accuracies, group accuracies etc.), Mendler-Dünner et al. (2022) show that a data-driven
046   approach can be successful in anticipating performativity. Once the relevant mechanism mapping
047   the model statistic to the induced data is learnt, shifts can be anticipated and the performative pre-
048   diction problem can be solved offline (Kim & Perdomo, 2023). While this approach is appealing
049   theoretically, a demonstration of the practical feasibility in the regime of deep learning was still
050
      missing.
051   In particular, existing approaches (e.g., Mendler-Dünner et al., 2022; Kim & Perdomo, 2023) learn
052   predictive models from scratch, assuming access to performativity-augmented datasets that contain
053   statistics about the deployed model, in addition to feature-label pairs. In large-scale deep learning,
      this approach to anticipating performativity has two fundamental practical limitations. First, large-

068   datasets are hard to gather and they are not yet widely available. Second, existing pre-trained models
069   only process raw features and they are not compatible with this paradigm, preventing the utilization
070   of valuable data resources and existing open source models. In this work we provide the first practi-
071   cal approach to building performativity-aware deep learning models around pre-trained backbones.
072
073   1.1   O UR WORK
074
075
      We propose a pipeline to adjust deep learning predictions for performative label shift. This refers
      to a setting where the deployment of a model changes the class proportions in future rounds. This
076
      setting is particularly relevant for vision and language tasks, where pretrained backbones prevail. For
077
      instance, adaptive data collection (Shirali et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2020) and performance-dependent
078   participation (Liu et al., 2018; Ensign et al., 2018) are instances of this problem which has been
079   under ongoing investigation (Lipton et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021a; Garg et al., 2020b).
080
081
      To anticipate performative label shift, we propose a modular framework to equip existing pre-trained
      models with a learnable adaptation module. The adaptation module takes the sufficient statistic for
082
      the shift, and the pretrained model’s intermediate representations as input and outputs adjusted pre-
083
      dictions. In the concrete instantiation of performative label shift, the adaptation module learns to
084   predict the label marginals and corrects for performativity post-hoc with a Bayes-optimal correction
085   to the model’s logits. More generally, our framework decouples the task of modeling the under-
086   lying concept from modeling performative effects. This has the crucial advantage that existing
087   pre-trained models can be used for the former, and only the parameters of the latter are learned from
088   performativity-augmented data, making it more practical and data-efficient.
089
      To evaluate our approach, we draw upon connections between performativity and dynamic bench-
090   marks (Nie et al., 2020) and simulate performative shifts over vision and language tasks through
091   adversarial sampling. Our main empirical findings can be summarized as follows:
092
093      • We demonstrate that our proposed adaptation module can learn the performative mechanism
094        effectively from a few performativity-augmented datasets collected along a natural retraining
095        trajectory.
096      • The module enables us to adapt the predictor to future data before deployment, significantly
097        reducing performance degradation due to performative distribution shifts, compared to state-
098        of-the art fine-tuning techniques.
099      • The readily trained adjustment module is flexible in that it can be combined with various pre-
100        trained backbones allowing zero-shot transfer during model updates, e.g., when more perfor-
101        mant backbones become available.
102      • We show that our trained adaptation module can anticipate a model’s brittleness to performative
103        shifts before deployment, enabling more informed model selection.
104
105   In a nutshell, we offer the first baseline to effectively adapt state-of-the art deep learning models
106   to performative distribution shifts. Along the way we highlight connections between performative
107   prediction, state-of-the art fine-tuning techniques and their application in dynamic benchmarking,
      as well as several interesting opportunities for future work.

      2   BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
109
110
111   Perdomo et al. (2020) introduce the framework of performative prediction to study performativity
112   in machine learning. We refer to Hardt & Mendler-Dünner (2023) for a comprehensive overview
113
      on related literature. The key conceptual component of the framework is to allow the data distri-
      bution to depend on the predictive model. A natural approach to deal with distribution shifts of all
114
      kind is to perform naive retraining. Interestingly, such heuristics can converge to equilibria under
115
      performativity (Perdomo et al., 2020; Mendler-Dünner et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Drusvyatskiy
116   & Xiao, 2023). However, it is known that retraining can lead to suboptimal solutions even after
117   convergence (Perdomo et al., 2020; Miller et al., 2021). Thus, a more ambitious goal is to antici-
118   pate performative shifts, instead of solely responding to them (Miller et al., 2021; Jagadeesan et al.,
119   2022). In particular, Mendler-Dünner et al. (2022) suggest treating predictions as features in a ma-
120   chine learning model, assuming that performativity is mediated by predictions. Kim & Perdomo
121   (2023) formalize requirements under which such a model allows for optimizing any downstream
122   loss under performativity, also referred to as an omnipredictor (Gopalan et al., 2022). Both of these
123   approaches require a dataset containing information about the deployed model large enough to train
124
      a performativity-aware predictor from scratch. Unfortunately, such data is rarely available in prac-
      tice, and the paradigm prevents the use of existing pre-trained models and benchmark datasets as
125
      they lack such information. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to offer a solution that
126
      allows to build on existing pretrained-backbones towards this goal.
127
128   We primarily focus on label shift in this work. Label shift refers to the shift of the marginal distri-
129   bution P (Y ), while the class conditionals P (X|Y ) remain fixed (Manski & Lerman, 1977; Storkey
130
      et al., 2008; Zhang et al., 2013; Lipton et al., 2018). In contrast to prior work on model-induced
      shifts, focusing predominantly on covariate shift (e.g., Hardt et al., 2016) and concept shift (Mendler-
131
      Dünner et al., 2022; Kim & Perdomo, 2023), our focus on deep learning applications puts forth this
132
      novel and important dimension of label shift. While concept shift would mean, e.g., a change in
133   the image labeling function, label shift means a change in the sampling procedure, making it much
134   more ubiquitous. Performance degradation due to label shift has been a long-standing problem for
135   computer vision tasks (Menon et al., 2021; Kang et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019; Cui et al., 2019; Shi
136   et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023), with a plethora of principled approaches to cor-
137   recting the label shift through unlabeled test data (Alexandari et al., 2020; Garg et al., 2023; 2020a).
138   However, existing efforts do not consider the dynamic interplay between model deployments and
139   induced shifts. Our work aims to address this issue and provide initial empirical baselines.
140   A practical setting where performative label shift surfaces are adaptive data collection settings.
141   Here performativity is a response to the predictive performance of the model. For example in active
142   learning Settles (2009) data samples are collected to obtain information in high uncertainty regimes
143   of the current model. Dynamic benchmarks Nie et al. (2020) suggest designing datasets adaptively
144   to challenge prior models. Approaches to mitigating fairness issues Abernethy et al. (2022); Globus-
145   Harris et al. (2022) suggest collecting data for groups on which the model performs poorly. In all
146   these settings, the shifts are mediated by model performance affecting future data collection. In
147
      contrast to tabular data, performative concept shift is less common in image and language settings,
      whereas covariate and label shift prevail.
148
149   Finally, there are various techniques to address distribution shifts in deep learning, independent
150   of their origin. Prominent example include full fine-tuning (Kornblith et al., 2019; Kolesnikov et al.,
151   2020; Zhai et al., 2020), partial adaptation (Chen et al., 2020; Houlsby et al., 2019), last-layer re-
152   training (Kirichenko et al., 2023; Rosenfeld et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2023),
153
      prefix-tuning Liu et al. (2022); Jia et al. (2022), unsupervised domain adaptation (Zhang et al.,
      2013; Ganin et al., 2016; Shu et al., 2018; Sun & Saenko, 2016; Courty et al., 2017) and test-time
154
      adaptation (Sun et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021b). Orthogonal
155
      to these, continual learning focuses on mitigating catastrophic forgetting (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017)
156   (i.e., knowledge accumulation) while dealing with a stream of data distributions (Castro et al., 2018;
157   Aljundi et al., 2018; Hou et al., 2019; Belouadah & Popescu, 2019; Wang et al., 2024; Mi et al.,
158   2020). None of these methods is designed to address shifts proactively. They all need to observe
159   the induced distribution before adaptation and, thus, inevitably suffer from performance degradation
160   due to performative shifts. By training the model to learn how to perform an adaptation before
161   a performative shift occurs, our work takes a first step into a widely unexplored new direction to
      improve predictive performance under distribution shifts of known cause.

      3     A NTICIPATING PERFORMATIVITY
163
164   Performative distribution shifts are caused by model deployment. Thus, having access to the right
165   statistic about the model is in principle, sufficient to foresee performative shifts. This is the core idea
166   making it possible to anticipate performativity, in contrast to arbitrary distribution shifts. Making
167   this more practical is the challenge we tackle in this work. Figure 1 illustrates our proposal.
168
      Problem setup. We consider discrete time steps, indicating the deployment of model updates. In
169   each step t ≥ 0, first, a dataset of feature label pairs (X, Y ) is collected. We consider a classification
170   setting with X ∈ Rd and Y taking on K discrete values. We use Pt to denote the distribution over
171   data points at time step t. Then, a new model ft is trained to predict Y from X. The model ft is de-
172   ployed and t is incremented. The new distribution Pt+1 is fully characterized by a sufficient statistic
173   St , which is a function of ft and Pt . This corresponds to a stateful extension of the framework by
174   Perdomo et al. (2020), using a Markovian assumption similar to Brown et al. (2022):
175
                          Pt+1 (X, Y ) = P (X, Y |S = St ) with         St = Stat(ft , Pt )                  (1)
176
177   An example of a sufficient statistic could be the model predictions over the previous data (Mendler-
178   Dünner et al., 2022; Kim & Perdomo, 2023), or model accuracy across subgroups (Nie et al., 2020).
179
      Such statistics are typically significantly lower-dimensional than the raw parameters of Pt and ft
      (and avoid explicit parametric assumptions for Pt ). In the following, we assume that, through expert
180
      and domain knowledge, we can specify such a statistic. This means we assume the model developer
181
      knows, for example, that predictions are causing the shift, rather than the specifics of the model
182   parameters themselves. We leave for future work the possibility of identifying such statistics from
183   data in settings where such knowledge can not be assumed. Following the notion of independent
184   causal mechanisms (Schölkopf et al., 2021; Peters et al., 2017), we assume that the mechanism
185   underlying the distribution shift is fixed and shifts only manifest through instantiations of S.
186
      Practical challenges. Given a statistic S, anticipating performativity means to predict Y from
187
      X taking the instantiation of S into account. This corresponds to learning a performativity-aware
188   predictor of the form
189                                          fperf : (X, S) → Y.
190
      Toward this goal, we highlight two important practical challenges:
191
192       Challenge 1: (Scarcity of performativity-augmented data). Curating a training dataset of
193       (X, S, Y ) pairs for learning fperf can be prohibitively expensive, as it necessitates exposing
194       the environment to models associated with different statistics S and pooling the obtained data
195       together for training. The complexity of gathering such datasets is insurmountable for high-
196
          dimensional data such as images and text and drastically increases training costs.
197
198       Challenge 2: (Compatibility with existing backbones). The function fperf processes
199       performativity-augmented data points (X, S) as its input. This forbids the direct application
200       of existing pre-trained deep learning models to learn fperf , as they typically do not include a
201       feature about the statistic S related to the dataset collection as their inputs.
202

204
205   Our goal is to develop an architecture to model fperf that uses fpre as a building block. That is, we
206

212   The adapter module reduces to a scalar function if it operates only on top of the pretrained model’s

304        We empirically investigate the performance of our adapter module under performative label shift
305        for vision and language classification tasks. For vision, we evaluate our model on CIFAR100
306        (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), ImageNet100 (Chun-Hsiao Yeh, 2022), and TerraIncognita (Beery et al.,

310        Baselines. We use three different baselines for adjusting a model to performative distribution shifts:
311        Oracle Fine-tuning, Oracle Distribution, and No Adaptation. All of them start with the deployment
312        of a pretrained model and then tackle performative shifts in their own way.
313
314        • Oracle Fine-tuning adapts the pretrained model by training it with complete information about
315
             current round’s (x, y) pairs for 25 epochs after observing the shift. While this approach ensures
             convergence on the available data and allows the model to continuously learn from an expanding
316
             number of samples across rounds, it may be computationally costly and potentially overfits to the
317
             current distribution, which increases its sensitivity to distribution shifts. In other words, Oracle
318          Fine-tuning updates ft in each step to fit the current distribution Pt (X, Y ).
319
320
           • Oracle Distribution uses the true label marginals to adjust the pretrained model’s predictions in-

321
322        • No Adaptation uses a fixed pretrained model without making any adjustments for the performative
323          distribution shifts over rounds. This baseline provides a reference point for evaluating the value
             of adaptation strategies in handling performative shifts.

      5   C ONCLUSION
516
517   This work investigates performative prediction in deep learning. We design the first practical algo-
518   rithm to adjust pre-trained models for performativity that is compatible with existing deep learning
519   assets. We motivate the use of modular architectures to increase data efficiency and evaluate our
520   approach under performative distribution shifts arising in typical dynamic benchmark settings. On
521   multiple vision and language datasets with different types of shifts, we observe consistent perfor-
522   mance gains along the retraining trajectory compared to standard baselines for the same adjustment
523   module applied to different backbones. Finally, we illustrate how the adapter can be used for model
524   selection under performativity to enable more informed model deployments and anticipate unwanted
525
      consequences.
526   Limitations and extensions. Overall, our work is the first tackling performativity in deep learning.
527   Thus, there are countless possible extensions of our method. We demonstrated the feasibility of
528   designing a modular architecture in the context of performative label shift by accessing the logits of
529   the pre-trained models. An interesting and natural direction could be to capture and adjust represen-
530   tations, closer to the input level. This would allow to account for more complex shifts, and offers a
531
      natural lever to trade off expressivity of the adapter and sample requirements. Further, our approach
      critically assumes known statistics, which are easily encoded in the label shift setting we consider,
532
      and easy to reconstruct with minimal knowledge about the paradigm. An interesting extension is
533
      to learn such statistics, perhaps leveraging causal representation learning tools (Schölkopf et al.,
534   2021). Besides being more general, it could also serve open vocabulary tasks (Radford et al., 2021),
535   where even labels shifts would be challenging to characterize with a finite dimensional vector, or
536   even generative modeling. Another unanswered question is to derive theoretical guarantees for
537   learning the underlying performative mechanism such as causal identification guarantee, similar
538   to Mendler-Dünner et al. (2022), as well as sample complexities. These results can potentially
539   guide more data-efficient algorithms, or more effective strategies to select the sequence of models
      to deploy during the training phase of the adapter module, akin to Jagadeesan et al. (2022).

      Training Details. We use a train-test-split with ratios 0.4, 0.3 and 0.3 respectively. Each dataset is
899
      treated as a data pool for sampling. To compute the initial performance of the pretrained model and
900
      generate the first statistic (class-level accuracies) we sample instances using a Dirichlet distribution.
901   Choice of parameter α for the distribution guides the skewness of the initial distribution for the
902   initial model. We set α = 100 to evaluate the initial model on a fairly balanced dataset. For each
903   round, we sample 1, 000 train and validation samples and 2, 000 test samples from the data pools to
904   simulate the round. Each iteration of the loop (rounds) follows: (1) evaluation of the existing model
905   on the current distribution, (2) updating the model using the current distribution, (3) computing
906   the statistics using the updated model on the current distribution. The computed statistics in the
907   final step determine the next distribution and these steps are repeated over 200 rounds. Baselines
908   differ based on their approach to step (2). Oracle Fine-tuning uses train and validation set to fit to
909
      the current distribution. No Adaptation skips that step and Performativity-aware Predictor adds
      previous round statistic, current label marginal pair to its memory buffer and make a pass over it to
910
      update the label marginal predictor. This memory buffer simulates epoch-like training for the label
911
      marginal predictor. Since it passes over the first pair it has added to the memory buffer many times,
912   we apply a scaling to balance sample importance exponentially with a decay factor 0.995. For
913   the vision experiments we use a cosine annealing learning rate scheduler, while for the language
914   datasets we use linear scheduling. Throughout all classification tasks we used a cross entropy loss
915   as the metric. To train the Performativity-aware Predictor, we used Adam optimizer with a learning
916   rate of 1e − 4 and KL-Divergence loss. For the model switching experiments, we switched models
917   at rounds 60 and 120 over the course of 200 rounds of simulation.
