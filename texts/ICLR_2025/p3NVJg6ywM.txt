011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated Learning (FL) is a decentralized machine learning paradigm that enables clients
014            to collaboratively train models while preserving data privacy. However, surmounting the
015            obstacles introduced by data heterogeneity in heterogeneous federated learning remains
016            a profound challenge, as it drives each client towards distinct convergence trajectories,
017
               impeding the global model’s convergence. To transcend these challenges, we propose
               DFED, a novel data-free ensemble knowledge distillation method designed to counteract
018
               the effects of data heterogeneity. DFED leverages multi-source Generative Adversarial
019
               Networks (GANs) to generate synthetic data that aligns with local distributions, ensur-
020            ing privacy while promoting diverse feature representations across clients. Additionally,
021            DFED aggregates client models into an ensemble based on their specialized knowledge,
022            and applies ensemble distillation to refine the global model, mitigating the issues caused
023            by disparities in data distributions. Across a variety of image classification benchmarks,
024            DFED demonstrates superior performance compared to several state-of-the-art (SOTA)
025            methods. The source code will be made publicly accessible once the paper has been ac-
026            cepted for publication.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a pivotal paradigm in the realm of machine learning, driven by
032   the increasing demand for privacy-preserving computational frameworks (Yang et al., 2019; Aledhari et al.,
033
      2020a). In contrast to traditional centralized learning, FL enables multiple clients, each possessing their
      own local datasets, to collaboratively train a global model without the need to exchange raw data (Li et al.,
034
      2023). This methodology not only facilitates effective collaboration but also strengthens privacy protection
035
      by eliminating the direct transfer of sensitive information, thereby significantly reducing the risks of data
036   leakage and unauthorized access(Matsuda et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2024).
037
038   However, the promise of Federated Learning does not come without significant challenges, chief among
039
      them being data heterogeneity (Li et al., 2020; Konecny et al., 2015; Ye et al., 2023; Mendieta et al., 2022).
      In practical scenarios, data possessed by different clients can vary significantly due to differences in user
040
      behavior, local environments, or underlying data-generating processes(Zhang et al., 2021). This variation
041
      in data, typically characterized as non-IID (Independent and Identically Distributed), further exacerbates
042   the difficulty in achieving a uniformly performing global model(Aledhari et al., 2020b; Zhao et al., 2018;
043   Zhu et al., 2021a). Specifically, when clients possess heterogeneous data, their local models tend to diverge
044   during training, adapting to the distinct characteristics of their respective datasets. This divergence, known
045   as client drift(Karimireddy et al., 2020), leads to models that reflect the disparities of private data rather
046   than contributing towards a unified global objective. As a result, the trained global model may perform well

      on some clients’ data but struggle to generalize effectively to others, causing inconsistent performance and
048
      reduced fairness across clients (Shang et al., 2022). Directly aggregating model parameters or updates in
049   such scenarios can further reduce the global model’s overall performance, leading to fairness concerns and
050   diminished transferability.

075   With the progression of Federated Learning (FL), Knowledge Distillation (KD)(Hinton et al., 2015) has
076   emerged as a pivotal technique for transferring knowledge from a large, complex model (teacher) to a
077   smaller, more efficient model (student)(Gou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021). Widely applied in tasks such as
078   model compression, transfer learning, and domain adaptation, KD enables the student model to assimilate
079   the teacher’s knowledge with minimal performance degradation(Park et al., 2019). This not only simplifies
080
      model complexity but also enhances adaptability and robustness, particularly in settings characterized by
      diverse data distributions. Unlike conventional FL approaches that aggregate model weights—often exacer-
081
      bating heterogeneity—KD facilitates learning from a distilled global representation, allowing client models
082
      to better align with their local data and architecture(Zhang et al., 2024b; Qiao et al., 2023). For instance, the
083   works of Jiang et al. (2020) and Ma et al. (2022) illustrate how knowledge distillation can enhance feder-
084   ated learning by efficiently transferring knowledge from local models and mitigating catastrophic forgetting,
085   thereby improving the global model’s performance across heterogeneous and continual learning scenarios.
086   Nevertheless, methods such as FedMD(Li & Wang, 2019), which depend on publicly available datasets
087   for distillation, present challenges in privacy-sensitive contexts due to the risk of exposing sensitive client
088   information.
089   To address these limitations, data-free knowledge distillation (DFKD) has emerged as a promising alter-
090   native(Lopes et al., 2017; Luo et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2024). By eliminating the dependency on public
091   datasets, DFKD ensures that sensitive client data remains protected while still allowing the global model to
092   leverage the knowledge of individual clients(Zhu et al., 2021b). Building upon the framework of DFKD,
093   we propose a novel approach called DFED to address data heterogeneity and privacy concerns in federated

      learning by integrating ensemble knowledge distillation with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) for
095
      synthetic data generation. Firstly, to safeguard data privacy, we deploy GANs on each client to generate
096   synthetic data reflective of their local distributions. These GANs are subsequently integrated into a unified
097   collection on the server, offering valuable and diverse samples for the knowledge distillation process. Sub-
098   sequently, to mitigate the inherent Non-IID nature of the data—which restricts local models to excel in only
099   distinct tasks—we aggregate the local models into a specialized ensemble, with each model focusing on
100   particular objectives, leading to a substantial improvement in predictive performance compared to the global
101   model alone. Lastly, we refine this integration through attention-based meta-learning, followed by knowl-
102   edge distillation, wherein the model ensemble serves as the teacher and the global model as the student. This
103   three-step methodology ensures iterative enhancement of the global model’s performance.
104   Our primary contributions are summarized as follows. First, we introduce an innovative federated learning
105   method that enhances the model’s effectiveness in heterogeneous environments. Second, we explore the
106   use of GANs in scenarios characterized by data imbalance, where each client trains its own GAN. The
107   collective deployment of these GANs generates diverse synthetic data, ensuring both distribution uniqueness
108   and privacy preservation. Moreover, we leverage a combination of model ensembles and attention-based
109   meta-learning to significantly elevate the performance of the ensemble beyond that of a conventional global
110
      model. Finally, we utilize knowledge distillation with the generated synthetic data alongside the high-
      performing model ensemble, resulting in further performance improvements. Our approach demonstrates
111
      significant superiority over several state-of-the-art methods on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

      3.2   T RAINING G ENERATOR
158
159   Leveraging generators for produce data knowledge distillation is not a novel concept. For instance, Zhang
160   et al. (2022b) introduced FedFTG, which uses a server-side GAN to simulate synthetic data based on
161   knowledge aggregated from multiple clients. While this approach effectively captures some unique char-
162   acteristics from each client’s data using hard samples, it falls short in fully harnessing diversity. Similarly,
163   DENSE (Zhang et al., 2022a) synthesizes data on the server using a GAN trained on ensemble models
164   uploaded from clients. Although this method strives to generate data that accurately represents the client
165   distributions, it faces limitations in fully capturing the nuanced diversity of each client’s local data. In con-
166   trast, our method avoids reliance on a single centralized generator by employing a group of GAN models,
167
      each specifically tailored to its client’s data. At this stage, well-behaved generators Gi are trained on each
      client i, capturing the data distribution Di over the feature space Xi . Instead of uploading compressed rep-
168
      resentations to the server, we upload the trained GAN models {Gi }N     i=1 , preserving the diversity D of each
169
      client’s local data. To validate our approach, we compare the performance of different generator training
170   strategies. Specifically, we assess a single generator G trained on a global dataset Dglobal against multiple
171   GANs {Gi }, each trained on highly skewed, non-IID datasets Di . The results in Fig. 2 demonstrate that
172   the data quality remains comparable across both methods, confirming the robustness of our distributed GAN
173   setup in addressing data heterogeneity.
174
      In our approach, the method for generating synthetic data is inspired by DeGAN (Addepalli et al., 2019), a
175
      data-free knowledge distillation framework. Building on DeGAN, we adopt a three-player adversarial game
176   between the generator Gi , a discriminator Ti , and a pre-trained classifier Ci on each client i. The generator
177   Gi produces samples from a latent space Z ∼ N (0, I), while the discriminator Ti ensures that the generated
178   samples align with the distribution of the proxy dataset on client i. The classifier Ci , a standard model
179   trained on the client, ensures that the generated samples are representative of the true data distribution Di by
180   minimizing classification entropy.
181
      The generator’s loss LG incorporates three key components. We consider y as the classifier output corre-
182   sponding to the generator input z, where z is sampled from a Gaussian distribution Z ∼ N (0, I). The
183   expectation over classifier outputs across a batch of samples from the latent space is denoted by w:
184

291   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
292
293   Datasets. In this study, we assess the performance of various methods using two image classification
294   datasets, CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 (Alex, 2009). To simulate the inherent data heterogeneity among
295   clients, we follow the approach adopted in previous works (Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2020; Yurochkin
296   et al., 2019), where the Dirichlet distribution Dir(ω) is applied to partition the training dataset for each client.
297   The concentration parameter ω controls the extent of data heterogeneity, with smaller values of ω resulting
298   in more non-uniform data distributions. The same partitioning process is employed for both CIFAR-10
299
      and CIFAR-100 datasets. This setup provides a suitable foundation for evaluating the effectiveness of our
      proposed methods under different levels of data non-IID conditions.
300
301   Baselines. We compare our method with the following baselines: FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2016), Fe-
302   dRS (Li & Zhan, 2021), Focal Loss (Lin et al., 2017), FedLF (Lu et al., 2024), DENSE (Zhang et al.,
303   2022a), DFRD (Luo et al., 2023), and FedFTG (Zhang et al., 2022c). The first four methods focus on ad-
304   dressing data heterogeneity, while the last three methods, similar to ours, are based on data-free knowledge
305
      distillation techniques. These methods extract knowledge from local models at the client side to synthesize
      data and perform knowledge distillation on the global model in a fine-tuning manner. We place particular
306
      emphasis on comparing the performance of these latter three approaches. Further configurations can be
307
      found in Appendix A.2.

354   5     C ONCLUSION
355
356   In this work, we present a novel federated learning framework designed to improve model performance in
357   heterogeneous environments. Our approach utilizes GANs at the client level to handle data imbalance, where
358   each client trains its own GAN, generating diverse synthetic data while maintaining privacy and ensuring
359   unique distribution characteristics. By integrating model ensembles with attention-based meta-learning, we
360   significantly enhance the ensemble’s performance, surpassing traditional global models. Furthermore, we
361   employ knowledge distillation using both the synthetic data generated by the GANs and the high-performing
362   ensemble, leading to further improvements in accuracy. Our method achieves superior results compared to
363
      several state-of-the-art baselines, as demonstrated on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets.

      A.1   R ELATED W ORK
574
575   Heterogeneous federated learning (HFL) has emerged as a crucial field of study, primarily due to the diverse
576   and decentralized nature of client environments and data distributions(Gao et al., 2022). One of the central
577   challenges in HFL is addressing data heterogeneity and ensuring robust performance across non-IID data
578   distributions. To address these issues, Xu et al. (2021) develop an adaptive federated averaging technique
579   that enhances communication efficiency and reduces convergence time by dynamically adjusting learning
580
      rates to better accommodate local data distributions. Additionally, Tan et al. (2023) propose FedICON,
      which uses contrastive learning to handle feature shifts by extracting invariant information across clients,
581
      enhancing robustness in non-IID federated learning scenarios. In parallel, Shen et al. (2023) propose a
582
      closed-form classifier framework that enhances cross-device learning by optimizing aggregation strategies,
583   resulting in faster convergence and more stable training dynamics. While these methods offer substantial
584   advancements, they often neglect the challenge of client drift, a phenomenon where the non-IID nature of
585   data causes divergence in client updates, leading to misaligned aggregation. This drift impairs the global
586   model’s ability to converge effectively. As a result, without adequately addressing client drift, existing
587   approaches may struggle to maintain stability and consistent performance as data heterogeneity increases in
588   federated learning environments.
589   Data-Free Knowledge Distillation (DFKD) has become a pivotal approach in scenarios where data privacy
590   and availability are constrained. In contrast to traditional distillation methods that require access to original
591   training data, DFKD facilitates knowledge transfer from teacher to student models by generating synthetic
592   data, ensuring the protection of sensitive information. Recent advancements in this domain have introduced
593   innovative techniques aimed at improving the quality and efficiency of synthetic data generation. For in-
594   stance, Yu et al. (2023) employ channel-wise feature exchange and spatial activation region constraints to
595   enhance data diversity, resulting in more robust student models without relying on real data. Similarly,
596   Tran et al. (2023) propose NAYER, a method that shifts the source of randomness to a noisy layer, paired
597
      with label-text embeddings to produce high-quality samples. This approach accelerates the training process
      while maintaining competitive accuracy. Another significant contribution comes from Shin & Choi (2024),
598
      who present the Teacher-Agnostic DFKD (TA-DFKD), which redefines the role of the teacher model as a
599
      lenient expert, allowing for more diverse sample generation by reducing class-prior restrictions. Despite
600   these innovations, DFKD still faces challenges in generating diverse, high-fidelity samples. Methods often
601   struggle to capture the full distribution of the original data, especially in imbalanced scenarios, which can
602   lead to biased student models. Nonetheless, DFKD continues to evolve, driven by the increasing demand for
603   privacy-preserving techniques in machine learning, establishing itself as a rapidly advancing field.
604
      Data-Free Knowledge Distillation (DFKD) in Federated Learning (FL) offers a privacy-preserving solution
605
      for knowledge transfer, eliminating the need for raw data exchanges between clients. By generating syn-
606   thetic data for distillation, DFKD ensures sensitive information remains protected while facilitating effective
607   knowledge transfer from global teacher models to local student models. This approach is particularly suitable
608   for handling data heterogeneity and non-IID distributions, as these issues often undermine model aggregation
609   in FL. Luo et al. (2023) introduce DFRD, a method that employs a conditional generator on the server to syn-
610   thesize training data, addressing distribution shifts and enhancing the diversity of synthetic samples. Yang

                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


611
      et al. (2023) propose FedFed, a framework designed to combat data heterogeneity through feature distilla-
612
      tion. In this method, clients retain robust features locally while sharing performance-sensitive features with
613   added noise, significantly improving model performance without compromising privacy. Similarly, Zhang
614   et al. (2024a) present FedKTL, a knowledge transfer method that leverages a server-side pre-trained gener-
615   ator, efficiently addressing both model and data heterogeneity while minimizing communication overhead.
616   While these methods excel in generating diverse synthetic data and have demonstrated impressive effective-
617   ness in addressing data heterogeneity through DFKD, they fall short in mitigating client drift, which can
618   lead to misaligned updates in non-IID settings. Our approach, by employing an ensemble learning strategy,
619   not only preserves data diversity but also effectively tackles client drift. This ensures greater stability and
620   enhanced performance in federated learning environments, offering a more comprehensive solution to both
621
      data diversity and alignment challenges.

626   N = 10 active clients. To simulate varying degrees of data heterogeneity, we use ω ∈ {0.01, 0.1, 1.0},

      codes for these methods. Regarding the meta-training process, we opt to update the meta model every ten
631
      communication rounds, setting the meta phase T to 20, with the number of selected clients per round ranging
632
      from 1 to 3, contingent upon the distribution setup.

636
      various FL methods using the original test set. To ensure reliability, we report the average results for each
      experiment over 5 different random seeds.

639   A.3   A NALYSIS IN OUR E XPERIMENTS
640
641   When ω = 1, the data distribution across clients is relatively uniform. Although data-free knowledge dis-
642   tillation methods can address data heterogeneity to some extent, they fail to exhibit a significant advantage
643   in this scenario, as the knowledge disparity between clients is not sufficiently pronounced. However, as ω
644
      decreases to 0.01, exacerbating the data heterogeneity, the advantages of data-free knowledge distillation
      become more pronounced. In this extreme scenario, DFED achieves the best overall performance, demon-
645
      strating its superior ability to handle highly heterogeneous data environments. Notably, both DENSE and
646
      DFED leverage ensemble methods in their respective frameworks. The results of the comparison are pre-
647   sented in Table 2. In our data partitioning experiments, we evaluated the performance of DENSE’s ensemble
648   strategy; however, its ensemble yielded lower accuracy compared to the global model. This outcome can
649   be attributed to DENSE’s simplistic approach of averaging the outputs of the client models, which does not
650   necessarily yield optimal results as it may fail to effectively account for the specialized strengths of indi-
651   vidual client models based on their specific expertise. In contrast, our ensemble method, applied under the
652   same partitioning scheme, achieved remarkable performance across a variety of configurations, significantly
653   surpassing the results of the DENSE ensemble.
654   Overall, our method achieved impressive outcomes in all experiments. Although it performed slightly below
655   the first four methods when data heterogeneity was less pronounced, it surpassed the three data-free knowl-
656   edge distillation methods. Furthermore, our approach yielded exceptional results under extreme partitioning
657   conditions.

730
731   In this subsection, we provide further analysis and discussions on the impact of the key hyperparameters
732   introduced in the main text. Building upon the DeGAN framework, we include the adversarial loss Ladv , en-
733
      tropy loss Lentropy , and diversity loss Ldiversity , with the additional inversion loss Linv incorporated to handle
      the challenges posed by non-IID data distributions. Our experiments reveal a certain degree of homogeneity
734
      between the inversion and diversity losses. The integration of global model features facilitates the GAN’s
735
      ability to generate diverse distributions. However, when the model is exposed to a dataset containing only a
736   single class, the diversity loss fails to assist the generator in synthesizing high-confidence images from other
737   classes, while the inversion loss can partially mitigate this limitation. It is important to highlight that the
738   inversion loss interferes with the discriminator T , affecting its confidence in generated samples. Although
739   the generator continues to produce images that exhibit favorable knowledge distillation effects, with most
740   generated samples closely approximating the local data, the discriminator assigns these samples an excep-
741   tionally low confidence score, interpreting them as significantly different from the real data. Consequently,
742   in scenarios where only a subset of active clients participate in the federated learning process, the inversion
743   loss aids the GAN group in capturing global information, enabling the generation of richer and more diverse
744
      samples. However, when the majority of clients are involved in the training process, the GAN group already
      possesses a broad range of sample knowledge, reducing the effectiveness of the inversion loss, which may
745
      even hinder the synthesis of high-quality samples. Another comparison arises when the inversion loss is
746
      set to a negative value, meaning that the generated images are more deviated from the global features and
747   may lean toward specific categories in the local dataset. Additionally, this approach introduces a level of
748   antagonism with the diversity loss. GAN training becomes highly unstable under these conditions, as global
749   features still encompass characteristics of the local data, and in the worst-case scenario, the generated images
750   tend to resemble noise. However, in some of our experiments, the GAN trained with a negative inversion
751   loss outperformed the one trained without inversion loss, particularly for clients that rarely participate in

      A.7   L IMITATIONS AND S HORTCOMINGS OF O UR M ETHOD
782
783   The foremost limitation of our method is the substantial communication overhead it generates, as well as the
784   high storage requirements for the clients. This is evident in several aspects: in terms of communication, both
785   the GANs and the local models are uploaded to the server, and during the meta-training phase, an ensemble
786   of models is distributed to clients for multiple rounds of communication. This results in a considerable
787   communication burden, which may not be feasible in practical applications. While this is still manageable
788   for the CIFAR-10 dataset, the ensemble for CIFAR-100 becomes too large. To mitigate this, we distribute a
789   subset of the ensemble models for weight updates in each round, rather than all 100 models at once, thereby
790
      reducing the communication load across more rounds. However, this also means that the typical federated
      learning training process will be paused for an extended period during these rounds.
791
792   In terms of storage, we assume that the server has unlimited storage capacity, but for clients, it is challenging
793   to store large-scale models and provide sufficient memory for training. This presents a significant limita-
794   tion of our method in practical applications. Our approach essentially trades off space and time for better
795   performance, which is a key aspect of our design philosophy.
796   Another shortcoming of our method lies in the DeGAN framework. We have not conducted in-depth research
797   on this data synthesis technique and have borrowed methods from other works, which may not be fully suited
798   to our use case. In both DeGAN and traditional data-free knowledge distillation methods, the teacher model

799
      typically has very high accuracy. For instance, 95% of the teacher models have excellent features, enabling
800
      the training of student models with up to 80% accuracy on the CIFAR datasets. However, when the accuracy
801   of the teacher model drops to around 60%-80%, the effectiveness of knowledge distillation is significantly
802   reduced. Our 60% model ensemble can only distill student models with around 40% accuracy, and the 80%
803   model ensemble can only distill student models with approximately 60% accuracy, which represents a major
804   loss in efficiency.
805
      If we had access to a public dataset, the accuracy of the student model post-distillation could even surpass
806

807   this hypothesis.
