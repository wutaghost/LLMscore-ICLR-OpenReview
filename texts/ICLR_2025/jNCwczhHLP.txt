009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Click-through rate (CTR) prediction tasks traditionally aim to model extensive user-
012            item feature interactions. Recent approaches fine-tune Large Language Models
013            (LLMs) using user-item features as input and click labels as output. However,
014            due to the sparsity of click labels, the attention mechanism may focus on a subset
015            of features rather than all features. This can hinder LLMs’ ability to accurately
016            match features to click labels, resulting in performance that does not consistently
017            exceed traditional state-of-the-art CTR approaches. To address this, we introduce
018
               a SLLM4CTR framework which uses adaptive temperature and label matching loss
               to improve fine-tuning and inference process of LLMs. The adaptive temperature
019
               serves as a confidence score to calibrate CTR predictions by quantifying the LLMs’
020
               attention to user-item features. The label matching loss clearly distinguish between
021            click-inducing and non-click-inducing features by constraining the representation
022            space of click labels. By combining these two designs, SLLM4CTR improves feature
023            utilization in LLMs and enhances the matching of user-item features to click labels.
024            Experimental results demonstrate that SLLM4CTR significantly outperforms state-of-
025            the-art baselines, including both traditional and LLM-based CTR approaches. The
026            code will be open-sourced.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Click-through rate (CTR) prediction uses user-item features, such as item rating, to estimate click
032   probabilities and rank candidate items. This task is crucial for recommendations across various
033
      domains, including social media and online advertising (Zhao et al., 2022). Accurate CTR prediction
      requires capturing extensive feature interactions, which involves different feature combinations (e.g.,
034
      item rating and price) to improve representation learning (Chen et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2021). For
035
      example, Meta’s CTR prediction model heavily relies on sparse and categorical feature interactions
036   to enhance performance (Zhang et al., 2022).
037
038   Recent approaches typically fine-tune LLMs using user and item features as the input prompt, with
039
      click labels as the target output (Geng et al., 2022; Hou et al., 2024b). These approaches take
      advantage of LLMs’ sophisticated attention mechanism to capture complicated semantic information,
040
      which allows them to highlight certain parts of users and items (Wu et al., 2023b). To further leverage
041
      the mechanism and inform LLMs’ decision-making process, recent approaches provide a broader
042   range of features for LLMs to attend. For example, LlamaRec (Yue et al., 2023) adds estimated
043   CTR probabilities from traditional CTR models to the prompt, while ClickPrompt (Lin et al., 2024b)
044   includes feature representations from traditional CTR models as additional tokens to the prompt.
045
      While the attention mechanism equips LLMs with powerful feature emphasis capabilities, it may not
046
      be sufficient on its own for CTR prediction tasks. This insufficiency arises due to the sparsity of click
047
      labels: click labels are typically absent from LLMs’ pre-training corpora, and the fine-tuned click
048   labels are usually sparse and limited. Therefore, attention may struggle with learning high-quality
049   feature interactions and might focus on only a subset of features rather than considering all relevant
050   ones. Consequently, LLMs may face difficulties in effectively matching user-item features with
051   click labels, which is more severe for items with few click labels (i.e., tail items), where accurate
052   predictions rely more heavily on intricate feature interactions. To investigate this hypothesis, we start
053   with the following research question: How well do fine-tuned LLMs utilize the features in click
      label prediction?

      2     P RELIMINARIES
109

      2.1    P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
111
112   We denote a CTR dataset D as (U, I, R). U = {U1 , U2 , ..., UU } represents U user profiles, where
113   each user profile Uu contains a list of textual and non-textual features, such as age and location.
114   Similarly, I = {I1 , I2 , ..., II } denotes I item profiles, where each item profile Ii includes features
115   like title and description. R ∈ RU ×I is the matrix, where each value rui ∈ R gives the label
116   justifying whether the user u clicks the item i or not. To leverage LLMs for CTR prediction, we
117   verbalize the template function with Uu and Ii and get a list of prompt tokens {x1 , x2 , . . . , xL } of
118
      length L, where xclick is the next click label token that needs to be predicted by LLM, i.e., “Yes” or
      “No”. We input the prompt tokens into LLM and get their hidden representation {e1 , e2 , . . . , eL }
119
      at the last transformer layer. And the two weight vectors wY es and wN o in the LLM’s head layer
120
      will project eL to click and non-click scores l(xY es |x1 , x2 , . . . , xL ) and l(xN o |x1 , x2 , . . . , xL ). Our
121   objective is to maximize the probability of predicting the correct click label token p(xclick ), where
122   probabilities p(xY es ) and p(xN o ) are obtained by applying the softmax function to lY es and lN o .
123
124   2.2    P ROMPTING AND F INE - TUNING S TRATEGIES
125
126   Prompt Templates. We formulate a prompt template for each dataset to encode user and item
127   features. Additionally, we include user ID and item ID, as the previous research has shown their
128   effectiveness in personalization (Yuan et al., 2023). For the Amazon Movies dataset, we employ the
129   following prompt template (templates for other datasets are detailed in the Appendix A.4):
130   Given the user’s and item’s attributes, identify whether the user will like the target movie by answering
131   Yes or No. Here is the information of the user [user id]. Here is the information of the movie
132   [movie id]: Its title is [title], and its price is [price]. Its description is: [description]. Its sales rank
133   among all movies is [sales rank].Response:[ ]
134   We fill the blank [·] with features Uu , Ii to obtain the prompt text. The LLM is expected to output
135   “Yes” or “No” for the last empty token, which corresponds to rui = 1 or rui = 0, respectively
136   following previous effort (Geng et al., 2022). To optimize the target token predicted probability, we
137   employ parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) for fine-tuning LLMs.

251

254   4      SLLM4CTR
255
256   In this section, we present SLLM4CTR which includes two plug-and-play designs, as illustrated in
257   Figure 3. Feature-wise, an adaptive temperature provides high confidence for LLMs’ predictions that
258   are highly attentive to user-item features and penalizes low-confidence predictions with large training
259
      loss (Section 4.1). Click-wise, a label matching loss is introduced to compact the representation
      space of click and non-click labels to assist LLMs in differentiating between click-inducing and
260
      non-click-inducing features (Section 4.2).
261
262
      4.1      ADAPTIVE TEMPERATURE
263
264   The key idea is to leverage the correlation between the feature and click embeddings as the confidence
265   score to dynamically refine the click probability pY es and click prediction loss Lclick , This refinement
266   guides LLMs to effectively associate predictions with features. The embeddings of prompt tokens
267   contain rich contextualized feature information and serve as indicators of feature modeling. When the
268   click embeddings of training samples are not correlated with the prompt token embeddings, the LLMs
269   may fail to fully capture the feature-click relationships and are assigned a low confidence. To address
      this, we introduce a self-monitoring mechanism that uses the correlation between these embeddings

270
      as learnable temperatures to calibrate the predicted click probability. This temperature modulates the
271   behavior of the LLM during both training and testing phases. During training, it increases the click
272   prediction loss, resulting in smaller gradients for low-confidence predictions. This has two effects
273   during optimization: (i) the small gradients have a limited impact on model parameter updates, and
274   (ii) to reduce the training loss, the LLM increases feature utilization for this sample. Together, these
275   two effects cause the LLM to focus on this sample with enhanced feature utilization. During testing,
276   the temperature results in a lower click probability for less correlated samples, thereby improving
277   the ranking order of items with better feature modeling. This approach ensures that the LLM more

307
308   To better match user-item features and click labels, especially for items with limited click labels,

342   associated with the non-click label when k = 0 and the click label when k = 1. By compressing the
343   volume of these spaces, LLMs could learn a more clear click decision boundary. But it may also take
344   the risk of collapsing the representation space gradually to a single point. To prevent this collapse
345   and maintain meaningful representations, we also widen the space between click and non-click labels
346   in the batch (Yu et al., 2020). The label matching loss Lmat is formally denoted as:

352
      matching loss is equivalent to compacting the representation space. (ii) No need for additional sample
      construction: CTR datasets suffer from label imbalance (Muhamed et al., 2021), where one label
353
      may have a large number of samples while another has a few. Directly pushing click embeddings
354
      belonging to the same label closer together to compact the representation space can be hindered by
355   this label sparsity issue. However, manually constructing more samples for the tail class may require
356   multiple forward propagations, which is inefficient. We combine the click prediction loss and label

      5.3                     A BLATION S TUDY, E FFICIENCY & G ENERALIZATION (RQ3 & RQ4)
466
467
      We perform an ablation study by analyzing each design individually to understand their contributions
468
      and the associated time cost. In addition, to examine the generalizability SLLM4CTR, we apply our
469   framework on (i) different LLM backbones and (ii) different LLM-based CTR backbones. We have
470   the following observations based on the results in Figure 4 and Figure 7. ④ Each design component
471   in SLLM4CTR enhances model performance. And the added computational overhead remains
472   minimal even expanding the dataset by 20 times. The base model’s performance is generally
473   unsatisfactory. Two main issues are identified: inconsistent feature attention, and unclear click
474   decision boundaries due to rarely appearing features in prompts. Both variants (without adaptive
475   temperature and without label matching loss) show improvement over the base model, with the latter
476   performing better. We infer that without temperature adjustment, the model may not prioritize features
477
      in click embeddings effectively. Notably, combining both designs leads to significant performance
      enhancement, suggesting their complementary nature. Our framework introduces no additional
478
      learnable parameters and relies solely on click embeddings, ensuring only marginal computational
479
      overhead compared to the base model. The well-trained SLLM4CTR serves as a data augmentor. It
480   simulates realistic user behaviors offline. This simulation generates new potential user interactions.
481   Such an approach avoids the high costs of direct online deployment. ⑤ SLLM4CTR enhances the
482   performance of the other LLM backbones and LLM-based CTR backbones. We observe that the
483   SLLM4CTR enhances the performance on Llama-2-7B and Llama-3-8B. This demonstrates that LLMs
484   may struggle with feature utilization due to the sparsity of click labels even for the advanced LLMs.
485   Our framework guides these LLMs to focus on informative user-item features, resulting in improved
      performance. Besides, SLLM4CTR unlock greater potential within other LLMs-based CTR predictors.

      5.4   H YPERPARAMETER S ENSITIVITY (RQ5)
505
506
507   To test the sensitivity of the hyper-parameters α, β, we vary one hyper-parameter value and fix the
508   other. The results on the Amazon Movies dataset are shown in Figure 5(a) and Figure 9 while the
509
      results on the other two datasets are shown in Figure 8 in Appendix. From the results, we observe: ⑥
      Generally, intermediate values of α and β tend to lead to better results. For hyper-parameter
510
      α, larger values expand the learned representation space for click and non-click labels. However,
511
      if too large, LLMs may struggle to differentiate click labels, significantly degrading performance.
512   Conversely, small α values could compress all click labels to a single point in the representation space,
513   severely impacting model performance. For hyper-parameter β, small values limit the benefits of
514   label matching loss in strengthening feature-label matching. Conversely, large β values may interfere
515   with normal click prediction, leading to performance degradation.
516
      Related Work: Pre-trained Language Models for CTR. Text embeddings from pre-trained lan-
517
      guage models have been explored to enhance traditional recommender systems (Lin et al., 2024a).
518   Recent advancements involve utilizing valuable semantic information from pre-trained language
519   models like BERT for exploration purposes (Wang et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2023b). For example,
520   Microsoft Bing Ads uses the embedding of TwinBERT to facilitate precise advertising Lu et al.
521   (2020). Furthermore, BST and BERT4CTR combine the outputs of language models and non-textual
522   features through one additional layer Chen et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2023a). Instead of using text
523   embeddings, we show that LLMs’ inherent reasoning abilities can conduct CTR prediction directly.

527   6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529
530   This study investigates how fine-tuned LLMs utilize user-item features and match features with click
531
      labels in CTR tasks. Our feature-wise and click-wise analyses reveal that purely fine-tuned LLMs
      inadequately attend to user-item features and struggle with feature-to-click matching. To address
532
      these limitations, we introduce SLLM4CTR, which incorporates two simple yet effective designs to
533
      enhance LLM fine-tuning and inference. The first, adaptive temperature, calibrates click probability
534   based on LLM attention to user-item features, increasing the click prediction loss when attention
535   is insufficient. The second, label matching loss, compacts representations of click and non-click
536   labels, helping LLMs distinguish click-inducing and non-click-inducing features for click prediction.
537   These designs jointly promote attention towards informative user-item features and feature-to-click
538   matching, resulting in significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art traditional CTR
539   and LLM-based CTR approaches. Future work will explore how different prompt strategies and
      temperature calibration methods affect LLM performance.

1109   A.8 R ELATED W ORK
1110    LLMs for Recommendation. Currently, there is a line of research focus that attempts to leverage
1111      the in-context learning ability of LLMs to enhance the performance (Wei et al., 2022; Lin et al.,
1112      2023). They aim to leverage retrieval-based methods by learning to access and utilize historical
1113   user-item interaction records (Salemi et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). Moreover, there is an emerging
1114        effort to fine-tune LLMs for different recommendation tasks (Yang et al., 2024), e.g., the job
1115         recommendation (Wu et al., 2023a), and the explainable recommendation (Li et al., 2023).
1116
       Transferring LLM semantic knowledge towards small language models to improve their performance
        is another popular topic explored not only in collaborative filtering (Zheng et al., 2023; Zhu et al.,
1117
       2024; Ren et al., 2024) but also in sequential recommendation (Lin et al., 2024c; Bao et al., 2023a;
1118
       Zheng et al., 2024) or session-based recommendation (Sun et al., 2024). Recently, the application of
1119   LLMs as agents has gained significant attention. These approaches leverage large language models
1120     as planners to decide on and execute subsequent steps (Shi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b). Our
1121        work instead focuses on fine-tuning LLMs for CTR prediction, a task with learning complex
1122   feature-click and feature-feature correlation. We demonstrate that fine-tuned LLM equipped with the
1123          proposed two simple designs can outperform traditional and LLM-based CTR predictors.
