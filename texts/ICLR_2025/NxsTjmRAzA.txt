009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is a major threat for deploying machine learn-
012            ing models in safety-critical scenarios. Data augmentations have been proven to
013            be beneficial to OOD detection by providing diverse features. However, previous
014            methods have only focused on the role of data augmentation in the training phase,
015            overlooking its impact on the testing phase. In this paper, we present the first
016
               comprehensive study of the impact of test-time augmentation (TTA) on OOD
               detection. We find aggressive TTAs can cause distribution shifts on OOD scores of
017
               In-distribution (InD) data, whereas mild TTAs do not, resulting in the effectiveness
018
               of mild TTAs on OOD Detection. Based on the above observations, we propose a
019            detection method that performs a K-nearest-neighbor (KNN) search on mild TTAs
020            instead of InD data. With only 25 TTAs, our method outperforms existing methods
021            using the entire training set (1.2 million images) on I MAGE N ET for OOD detection.
022            Moreover, our approach is compatible with various model architectures and robust
023            to adversarial examples.

072   Currently, numerous studies have demonstrated that data augmentation can enhance the performance
073   of OOD detection. Notable methods include Mixup (Zhang et al., 2017), CutMix (Yun et al., 2019),
074   and PixMix (Hendrycks et al., 2022). However, these methods are mainly applied in the training

076   dearth of comprehensive research on the impact of TTAs on OOD detection.
077   In this paper, we propose an InD-independent OOD detection method based on TTA. First, we
078   present a comprehensive exploration of the effect of TTA on OOD detection. We categorize TTA
079   into In-distribution Augmentation (IDA) and Out-of-distribution Augmentation (OODA) based on
080   their effects on image feature expression. Empirical results reveal that OODA leads to a shift in
081   the distribution of OOD scores, rendering it ineffective for OOD detection. In contrast, IDAs are
082   favorable for OOD detection. Based on these findings, we propose an OOD detection method that
083   boosts KNN with TTA. Specifically, we use the K-th nearest neighbor (KNN) distance between the
084
      embedding of the input sample and the generated TTAs to indicate ID-ness, instead of InD data as
      a reference set. As a non-parametric method, our method does not depend on the information of
085
      external OOD data and InD data, and does not modify the model, which is a model-agnostic method.
086
      Detection results for common OOD datasets on C IFAR -10 and I MAGE N ET show that our method
087   outperforms existing methods. Especially for concurrent KNN (Sun et al., 2022), our method only
088   generates 25 TTAs and outperforms the performance of KNN with 1.2 million images as the reference
089   set on I MAGE N ET (as shown in Figure 1). We summarize our contributions as follows:
090
091   1. Our study is the first to investigate the effect of TTA on OOD detection. We classify test-time
092      data augmentations into IDA and OODA and demonstrate that IDA can enhance OOD detection
093      performance. We believe our findings will encourage further research into TTA-based data-efficient
094
         OOD detection techniques.
095   2. We proposed an OOD detection method that employs TTA to improve KNN. Experimental results
096      show that generating as few as 25 TTA samples outperforms SOTA methods achieved by using a
097      reference set of 1.2 million images on I MAGE N ET.
098   3. Our method introduces the sequential mask as TTA, and comprehensive evaluations on various
099      OOD detection benchmarks across different model architectures show our method consistently
100      outperforms the SOTA methods. As a plug-and-play method, our method’s performance can be
101      further enhanced by incorporating high-quality embeddings. Moreover, our method is also robust
102
         to adversarial examples that cause OOD score shifts.

      The impact of IDA and OODA on the distribution of OOD scores. We find IDA and OODA have
138   distinct effects on the OOD score distribution. Figure 2 illustrates the shift in the distribution of OOD
139   scores (Liu et al., 2020) for both InD and OOD data resulting from IDA and OODA. Our observations
140   reveal that IDA has a negligible effect on the score distribution of InD data, while slightly modifying
141   the distribution of OOD data. In contrast, OODA induces a distribution shift in InD data, making it
142   resemble the distribution of OOD.
143
      The performance of IDA and OODA. Based on the above findings, we conduct a simple method
144   for OOD detection by comparing output consistency between input samples and their augmentations.
145   The results in Table 1 show that IDA can effectively detect OOD data. Moreover, using multiple
146   IDAs and selecting the one with the highest similarity can further improve the detection performance.
147   In contrast, OODA cannot be used for OOD detection, as it causes the score distribution of InD and
148   OOD data to become similar.
149
      How to identify IDA and OODA? According to the conclusions of Geiping et al. (2022), aug-
150   mentations that have a great impact on the expression of image features are considered aggressive
151   augmentations (OODA), and vice versa, moderate augmentations (IDA). To assess how various aug-
152   mentation techniques affect image representation, we computed the Learned Perceptual Image Patch
153   Similarity (LPIPS) distances, as shown in Table 2. LPIPS quantifies the perceptual difference between
154   two images, with lower scores indicating greater similarity and thus implying less impact from the
155   augmentation method. Our analysis reveals that IDA typically yields lower LPIPS scores than OODA.
156   Interestingly, augmentations with lower LPIPS scores tend to exhibit superior performance in Table 1.
157   This correlation suggests that LPIPS can serve as an effective metric for differentiating between IDA
158
      and OODA techniques. Notably, grayscale transformation is an exception to this pattern, possibly
      due to the LPIPS model’s learned sensitivity to color characteristics.

      Preliminary. Let X denote the input space and Y denote the label space. Given a pre-trained
188
      classifier f : X → Y, trained on InD data drawn from distribution Pin , the goal of OOD detection is
189
      to determine whether a test sample x ∈ X is drawn from Pin or from an unknown distribution Pout .
190   Formally, we seek a scoring function g : X → R and a threshold λ such that:

      Design Objective. This work considers the classifiers trained on InD data that may encounter OOD
196
      samples. Unlike previous methods (Sun et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2018), our goal is to design an
197
      effective OOD detection method that requires neither OOD data nor prior knowledge of InD data.
198   Our approach aims to explore the relationship between a sample and its TTAs, and exploit this
199   for OOD detection. Notably, our method does not alter any component of the classifier, including
200   the architecture and trained weights, making it a model-agnostic plug-and-play detector that can
201   seamlessly integrate with different model architectures.
202
      Core Idea. Our core idea is to construct a scoring function for OOD detection by utilizing the
203
      relationship between samples and their TTAs. Unlike KNN which performs a nearest neighbor
204   search in the feature space of the entire training data, our approach focuses on searching within the
205   local neighborhood of input samples provided by TTAs. Therefore, our method is data-efficient
206   and InD-independent. However, our method requires some IDA that is effective for OOD detection.
207   Therefore, selecting an appropriate TTA strategy becomes crucial for the success of our method.
208
      TTA Strategy. Our findings in Sec. 2 suggest that enhancing OOD detection performance hinges
209
      on identifying an array of effective IDAs. However, the repertoire of conventional IDAs is limited,
210   and combining multiple stylistically diverse augmentations doesn’t necessarily improve detection
211   performance. As shown in Table 1, where the combination of Hflip and Gray underperforms
212   compared to Hflip alone. Furthermore, Table 2 reveals that masking has the least impact on image
213   features among common augmentations. Leveraging this insight, we introduce a novel Test-Time
214   Augmentation (TTA) strategy called Sequential Mask. This approach applies masks to images in a
215   sequential manner, generating a substantial number of similar IDAs. Figure 5 shows the detection
      performance when utilizing varying numbers of masked images as a reference set. Note that the

      Performance. In Table 4, we compare our method with competitive methods on I MAGE N ET for six

350   Comparison with ID-dependent Methods. Vim Wang et al. (2022) and KNN (Sun et al., 2022)
351   need ID data to calculate OOD scores, so their performance is affected by ID data. In contrast, our
352   method computes the OOD score exclusively through TTA. For each detection, KNN searches the
353   k−th nearest neighbor within the reference set (usually the entire training set), while our method
354   only needs to perform distance calculations with generated TTAs, reducing the computational cost.
355   Only generating 25 TTAs, our method outperforms KNN with 1.2 million images as a reference set.
356   Moreover, ID-dependent methods are susceptible to unbalanced data (Mani & Zhang, 2003), while
357
      ours does not.

359   methods for detecting texture whether on C IFAR -10 or I MAGE N ET. We think the reason is that
360   texture images are not sensitive to masking. Hence, how to better detect OOD datasets that are not
361   sensitive to TTA is our future work.

471   5.1   OOD D ETECTION M ETHODS
472
      OOD Data Exposure Approach. Some works collect a bunch of external OOD samples to help
473
      OOD detectors better learn ID/OOD differences. Outlier Exposure (Hendrycks et al., 2018a) utilizes
474   an auxiliary OOD dataset to improve OOD detection. Lee et al. (2017) use GAN to generate OOD
475   samples that are located near ID samples. Several methods including MCD (Yu & Aizawa, 2019),
476   NGC (Wu et al., 2021), and UDG (Yang et al., 2021b) can leverage external unlabeled noisy data to
477   enhance OOD detection performance. Although using external OOD data is a simple and effective
478   approach, how to effectively select additional data and how to prevent the model to overfit the given
479   OOD is still an open problem.
480   InD-Dependent Approach. Some InD-dependent methods require InD data as a reference
481   set. Lee et al. (2018) measures the minimum Mahalanobis distance of class centroids, KL-
482   Matching (Hendrycks et al., 2019a) computes the minimum KL divergence between softmax and
483   the mean class conditional distribution, and KNN (Sun et al., 2022) performs a K-nearest neighbor
484   search on the reference set. VIM (Wang et al., 2022) uses InD data to estimate the covariance of
485   features to analyze the main space of features. Another part of the InD-dependent methods requires
      InD data for training. ConfBranch (DeVries & Taylor, 2018) builds an additional branch from the

503   et al., 2017) uses temperature scaling and gradient-based input perturbation. Energy Liu et al. (2020)
504   uses energy-based functions. GRAM (Sastry & Oore, 2020) computes the gram matrix within hidden
505   layers. DICE (Sun & Li, 2022) performs weight sparsification in the last layer. GradNorm (Huang
506   et al., 2021) focuses on gradient statistics. ReAct (Sun et al., 2021) uses rectified activations, and
507   ASH (Djurisic et al.) reshapes the activation by three shaping algorithms.
508
509   5.2    AUGMENTATION FOR OOD D ETECITON
510
511   Some works have observed that regularizing the model during the training phase using data augmen-
512
      tation will help to better estimate the uncertainty. Mixup Zhang et al. (2017) mixes samples by pair,
      and AugMix Hendrycks et al. (2019b) mixes samples with their augmentations. CutMix Yun et al.
513
      (2019) replaces cut regions in a sample with patches from another image, and PixMix Hendrycks et al.
514
      (2022) combines images through additive or multiplicative fusion with additional mixing datasets.
515   YOCO Han et al. (2022) crops images both vertically and horizontally, then mix them in pairs.
516   Mohseni et al. (2021) search for the optimal combination of augmentations through reinforcement
517   learning. Geiping et al. (2022) systematically studied the effect of data enhancement in the training
518   phase on OOD generalization. They found that aggressive augmentations result in more diverse
519   features, while mild augmentations lead to more consistent features. As a result, aggressive augmen-
520   tations provide a higher but unstable gain, whereas mild augmentations yield a lower but more stable
521   gain. However, the research on the impact of TTA on OOD detection remains elusive.
522
523   6     C ONCLUSION
524
525   This paper presents the first systematic study on the impact of TTA for OOD detection and demon-
526   strates that IDA at test time is beneficial and data-efficient for OOD detection. Furthermore, we
527   propose a new TTA-based OOD detection method, which conducts a K-nearest neighbor search on
528   TTAs. Our method only requires a handful of TTAs and spares the need for InD data as a reference
529   set and external OOD data. Extensive experiments show that our method outperforms the SOTA
530   methods on several OOD detection benchmarks. We hope that our work can inspire future research
531
      on data-efficient OOD detection using TTAs. We also do not see any immediate ethical concerns or
      negative societal impacts from this study.

818   E    V ISUALIZATION
819
820   Sec. 2 shows that horizontal flipping can cause a difference between the heatmaps of InD and OOD
821   data. To further demonstrate the impact of IDA and OODA on image features, we show the heat
822
      maps of common IDAs and OODAs on large-scale datasets in Figure 10. The visualization results of
      C IFAR -10 are not shown because its resolution is too low. It can be observed that OODA has a great
823
      influence on the features of both InD and OOD data. IDA will not change the high thermal area of
824
      InD, while OOD will be affected by IDA. Based on the observation of a large number of visualization
825   results, we have obtained the following empirical conclusions:
826
827          • OOD data has a larger proportion of high thermal regions than InD data, that is, the useful
828            features of OOD are more dispersed.
829          • IDAs do not change the high thermal region of InD, but they will change the high thermal
830            region of OOD. And OODAs have an impact on the features of both InD and OOD. Therefore,
831            IDA can be used for OOD detection, and OODA cannot be used for OOD detection.
832          • No single IDA was able to cause changes in the high thermal regions of all OOD data.
833            Horizontal flip is an effective TTA for OOD Detection, but the third row of Figure 10
834            (Places365) shows that horizontal flip does not have as much impact on the heatmap as other
835            TTAs.
836
837
      Based on the above conclusions, we designed Sequential Mask for OOD detection. First, masking is
      an IDA that can effectively detect OOD. Then, since the useful features of OOD are more dispersed
838
      than InD, the features of OOD are more likely to be changed in the masked samples produced by
839
      sequential mask. Finally, The sequential mask can generate multiple Masked samples to make up for
840   the inability of a single IDA to maintain high detection performance for all OODs.
841
842   Moreover, we visualise the samples with their masked augmentations in Fig. 11 (a), and it can
843
      be seen that there may be some kind of ”non-ideal” mask that causes the InD and OOD and their
      enhancements to be far apart. However, the use of multiple IDAs makes the distance between the InD
844
      and its nearest neighbour significantly smaller than that of the OOD.
845
846   We also show the distribution of embedding similarity between images from different datasets and
847   their 16 IDAs in Fig. 11 (b). It also shows that multiple IDAs will lead to a significant difference in
848   the distribution of embedding similarity between InD and OOD.
