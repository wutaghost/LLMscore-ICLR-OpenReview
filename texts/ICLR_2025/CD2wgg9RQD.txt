009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The scaling of inference-time computation in large language models (LLMs) has
012            emerged as a promising approach for enhancing reasoning capabilities by trad-
013            ing off inference-time and pre-training compute. The practice of how to enable
014            LLMs to utilize additional computation at test time to improve response accuracy
015            is crucial for both academia and industry. Proposer-Verifier, as a typical paradigm
016            of inference scaling, often fails to generalize to various scenarios. Specifically,
017            in tool use tasks, LLM face the risk of lacking effective verifiers, leading to er-
018            ror accumulation across multiple reasoning steps. In this work, we address these
019
               challenges by introducing InfCycle, a multi-stage data synthesis strategy that em-
               ploys LLMs as a data synthesizer and cycle consistency verification to ensure
020
               high-quality trajectory generation. This approach utilizes step-wise cycle consis-
021
               tency among synthesized trajectories for a given tool, providing effective process
022            supervision that has advantages over outcome supervision. Extensive experiments
023            on multiple tool use and reasoning tasks demonstrate that InfCycle efficiently en-
024            ables self-improvement. It outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on StableTool-
025            Bench, achieving a 75.4% pass rate and a 79.6% win rate using small models (7B),
026            without relying on external supervision or expert trajectories for warm-up.

029   1   INTRODUCTION
030
031   Tool use is a critical capability for LLMs, enabling them to perform complex reasoning tasks through
032   multi-step inference, and interact with real-world environments (Mallen et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
033
      2023b; Zeng et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2024). While many studies have focused on
      the challenges of tool use, they often rely heavily on imitation learning (Hussein et al., 2017), such as
034
      learning from teacher models (Yang et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2023a) (the OpenAI GPT series (Achiam
035
      et al., 2023)) or human-annotated execution trajectories. Moreover, tool invocation scenarios require
036   LLMs to perform very complex multi-step reasoning, posing a great challenge to ensure the accuracy
037   of intermediate steps, especially when lacking of reliable verification. Consequently, the high cost
038   of data synthesis in imitation learning and the requirements for reliable verifiers hinder the progress
039   of tool use by LLMs. This research focuses on exploring how LLMs can autonomously learn tool
040   use capabilities from scratch, without relying on any external supervision.
041
      Recently, leveraging inference-time compute (Brown et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
042   2024a) to enhance model capabilities has become an effective method for enhancing model capa-
043   bilities and facilitating self-improvement. The Proposer-Verifier (Snell et al., 2024) strategy is a
044   common method that increases the sampling cost of the LLM and selects the highest score accord-
045   ing to a verifier, thereby trading off inference cost for performance improvement. However, in most
046   tool use scenarios, the lack of a reliable verifier undermines the effectiveness of inference-time
047   scaling (Liu et al., 2024c; Mekala et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Ye et al., 2024). Additionally, in
048   multi-step reasoning scenarios, error accumulation may hinder small size models from identifying
049   the correct execution path, significantly reducing the effectiveness of data sampling.
050   To address these issues, we first decompose the tool use process into multi-step reasoning tasks,
051   enabling models with limited capabilities to be integrated into our data synthesis pipeline. Crucially,
052   we develop two modules for data synthesis: the Generator and Simulator, which generate interme-
053   diate steps for tool invocation as training data for the LLM. As illustrated in Figure 1, the principle
      of cycle consistency (Zhu et al., 2017) ensures that inconsistent intermediate steps are less likely to

078   correct execution trajectories, effectively unlocking the potential of inference-time computation.
079
      Our main contributions are as follows:
080
081          • In tool use scenarios, we investigate how to effectively leverage additional computation
082            at test time to enhance accuracy, enabling LLMs to improve their capability through self-
083            improvement from scratch.
084
             • We propose InfCycle, which employs a multi-stage trained model to act as a Proposer,
085            and utilizes a cycle consistency mechanism to serve as a Verifier. Overall, this approach
086            significantly boosts data sampling efficiency and model performance.
087
             • We validate our method using multiple models with varying capabilities on the StableTool-
088

089            a 79.6% Win Rate, as well as the Berkeley Function Calling Benchmark, where Meta-
090            LLaMA3-8B achieve an improvement of over 16.31 points. Additionally, extensive exper-
091            imental results and analyses confirm the effectiveness and scalability of our approach.

096   Tool Use As pioneers, Toolformer (Schick et al., 2024), Gorilla (Patil et al., 2023), and ToolAl-
097   paca (Tang et al., 2023) have explored the potential of LLMs in tool use. ToolLlama Qin et al.
098   (2023b) notably expanded the number of available tools, exceeding 10,000 APIs, and investigated
099
      the possibilities of data scaling. Many related works primarily seek improvements through two
      approaches: Inherent Abilities: This involves manipulating prompts or enhancing the execution
100
      framework. Xu et al. (2023b) utilize examples, in-context demonstrations, and generation styles to
101
      explore the potential of LLMs. AutoAct (Qiao et al., 2024) employed a multi-agent collaboration
102   framework to complete reasoning tasks. RestGPT (Song et al., 2023) introduced a coarse-to-fine
103   online planning mechanism by using three main modules (Planner, API Selector, and Executor).
104   Synthetic Data: This strategy empowers model capabilities through synthetic data. ToolVeri-
105   fier (Mekala et al., 2024) leveraged the LLaMA-2 70B model to verify the accuracy of synthetic
106   data. APIGen (Liu et al., 2024c) used a strong model to filter API calls based on rules and seman-
107   tics, ensuring data accuracy. In contrast, our approach combines the strengths of both methods, and
      our setups do not rely on any teacher LLM or external supervision.

125   that these methods often have limited effectiveness for complex tasks (Huang et al., 2023; Stechly

127   future, particularly in exploring the trade-offs between inference time and pre-training computing.

136   Problem Formulation In tool use scenarios, the LLM receives a user query Q along with a set
137   of candidate API functions, represented as A = {API0 , API1 , . . . , API|A| }. The goal of the LLM
138   is to fulfill the user’s intent by executing a specific sequence of API function calls. The decision
139   process can be described as y ∼ π(y|s0 , a1 , a2 , · · · ), where π(·) represents the policy, s0 denotes
140   the initial task state, and a represents the actions taken by the model, such as selecting or executing
141   a specific API function from A. Each action may update the state si , guiding subsequent decisions.
142
143   Preliminary Experiments We conduct investigations to evaluate the impact of increasing reason-
144   ing steps on the Berkeley Function-Calling benchmark. Specifically, we use the LLM as a Proposer
145   and combine it with two Verifiers 1 to explore inference-time scaling: the Correct Verifier Vc , which
146   checks if the model output matches the expected correct answer, and the Format Verifier Vf , which
147
      ensures that the output conforms to JSON format or the specified API parameter types, for example,
      distinguishing between numeric types such as floats and integers.
148
149   Specifically, we sample multiple outputs using the LLM as π(·) and select the results that pass the
150   verification process: y ∗ = arg maxyi ∈{y1 ,y2 ,...,ym } I[Vc (yi ) = 1]. Here, yi represents the generated
151   results obtained through sampling. For both LLaMA3-8B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, we
152   conduct experiments using their official inference frameworks 2 . As illustrated in Figure 2, the
153
      LLMs demonstrate significant performance improvements across different datasets, suggesting that
      better data trajectory samples exist within a limited search space for open-source LLMs. In the case
154
      of Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, the model struggles to generate JSON formatted outputs, making it
155
      nearly impossible to complete user queries on the benchmark. Therefore, we utilize the framework
156   proposed in Section 4.1 for inference. We observe conclusions similar to those previously noted.
157   When we introduce the Format Verifier using Vc (Vf (yi )), the model’s performance significantly
158   exceeds that of using only the Correct Verifier Vc (yi ) alone.

177   Critical Insight From the experiments, we can draw the following key conclusions: (1) Increasing
178   the inference cost leads to significant improvements in model performance for both single-step and

183
      models in certain tasks. These findings indicate that process verifiers are more effective than
      solely outcome verifiers in enhancing long-range reasoning capabilities and assisting models
184
      in identifying high-quality execution trajectories.

188
189   In this section, we first build a holistic data synthesis pipeline to generate training data of tool
190   invocation trajectories, as shown in Figure 3. Then, we discuss how to utilize cycle consistency
191   to filter the data synthesized by the Generator and Simulator. Finally, we introduce InfCycle, a
192   multi-stage synthesis strategy designed to help LLMs learn the ability of tool use from scratch.
193
194
      4.1   T HE PIPELINE OF DATA SYNTHESIZER
195
196   To validate data scalability in large-scale tool use, we select real-world APIs as our tool candi-
197   dates. To support this, we create and deploy an execution environment for these APIs, enabling type
198   checking and providing execution feedback. For more details, please refer to Section 5.
199
200   Collector The execution environment naturally acts as a verifier, ensuring that the API request can
201   be executed successfully while filtering out invalid requests. We collect the available APIs as tool
202   candidates from the environment and maintain input consistency through a unified format.
203
204
      Sampler Considering the complexity of user intent, multiple tools are often needed to achieve a
205   solution. Thus, we categorize the tool collection into three types: Simple: Users utilize a single tool.
206   Parallel: Users make multiple calls to the same tool. Multiple: Users employ multiple tools. The
207   sampled tool cases include ’API definitions’, ’request parameters’, and ’execution results’, which
208   we use to build tool-invocation trajectory data.
209
210   Generator Based on the sampled API execution parameters and results, we can use LLMs to gen-
211   erate potential user queries Q = {q1 , q2 , · · · , q|Q| }. To ensure accuracy, we use LLMs as semantic
212   checkers (Liu et al., 2024c). They filter out unsuitable examples by evaluating whether the user
213   query originates from the real world and whether the response adequately meets the user’s intent.
214   For each example, we construct execution paths for task planning based on the order of calls. Specif-
215   ically, we obtain synthesized action trajectories {q, âp , âs , âe }, where âp represents task planning,
      âs denotes the selected API and parameters, and âe indicates the execution results.

226   Simulator To adapt models with limited capabilities, we decompose the reasoning process of tool
227   invocation into five steps: 1) Task Planning: The model starts by analyzing the user query q and
228
      decomposing it into sub-tasks ap , similar to ReWoo (Xu et al., 2023a). 2) Tool Selection: Based
      on the planned sub-tasks, the model takes action as to choose the appropriate tools and parameters.
229
      3) Tool Execution: The model then interacts with the environment to gather results ae ; 4) Tool
230
      Reflection, Through action ar , the model evaluates execution feedback to determine if the sub-plan
231   is complete or if the API needs re-execution. 5) Task Reflection, Finally, the model uses atr to
232   assess if the task is completed and, if necessary, re-planed based on its execution history. Through
233   the aforementioned steps, we derive the sequential action trajectory {ap → as → ae → ar → atr },
234   respectively. For simplicity, we omit the explicit states on which the model’s predictions are based.
235   However, these states are implicitly derived from the outcomes of previous actions, such as as ∼
236   π(s0 , ap ) or ar ∼ π(s0 , ap , as , ae ). More inference details refer to Appendix A.5.
237   This multi-step execution process effectively addresses two key challenges in model reasoning:
238   First, it bridges the gap between natural language and JSON format. Many general models struggle
239   to handle JSON context (Tam et al., 2024), which can impair their performance on tasks that require
240   this format. Second, it simplifies decision-making processes. In methods like ReACT (Yao et al.,
241   2022), the model must choose the next action from multiple options, which can be challenging for
242   models with limited reasoning abilities.
243
244   4.2   T HE S TEP - WISE C YCLE C ONSISTENCY
245
246   Given candidate APIs, the Generator filters out semantically incorrect trajectories, while the Simu-
247   lator excludes user queries that cannot be executed successfully. Furthermore, we verify the cycle
248
      consistency of trajectories between {âp , âs , âe }Si ) and {ap , as , ae }Si , where S represents the total
      number of reasoning steps. We ensure that each action in the multi-step reasoning process is consis-
249
      tent and executed sequentially.
250
251
      Why is the cycle consistency verifier effective? The Generator may overlook numerous errors,
252
      even when selecting the prompt carefully. These issues are unpredictable, as illustrated in Table 1: 1)
253
      Internal logical issues within the user query: These issues can lead to synthesized queries that lack
254   executable trajectories. 2) Semantic problems in planned sub-tasks: Such problems can result in
255   synthesized trajectories that are fundamentally unreasonable. Unlike recent approaches (Qin et al.,
256   2023b; Liu et al., 2024c) that use an outcome verifier to filter samples, step-wise cycle consistency
257   as a process verifier ensures the accuracy of the reasoning process, thereby guaranteeing the high
258   quality of the synthesized data.

260   4.3   M ULTI - STAGE SYNTHESIS STRATEGY
261
262   A⋆ search We define the entire reasoning process starting from s0 as an expansion into a decision
263   tree T , with all actions being represented as nodes V(T ). The successors of each node are generated
264   by using temperature sampling. Specifically, we first select a node n from the frontiers of the tree
265   (denoted as F(T ) ⊆ V(T )) according to the cost function. Then, we expand node n using the LLM
266
      to generate k subsequent actions, which are used to update F(T ).
267   The A⋆ algorithm aims to search for an appropriate path that minimizes the cost function c(n) =
268   g(n) + h(n), where n is the current node, g(n) represents the cost of the path from the start node
269   to n, and h(n) is a heuristic function estimating the cost of the cheapest path from n to the goal. In
      practice, we define c(n) as the total number of reasoning steps required to complete the user query,
