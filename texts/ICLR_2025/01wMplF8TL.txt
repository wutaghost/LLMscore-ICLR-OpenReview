011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce Text-Informed Time Series Prediction (TITSP), an innovative
014            multimodal framework that integrates textual knowledge with temporal dynamics
015
               using Large Language Models (LLMs). TITSP employs a two-stage process that
               bridges numerical data with rich contextual information for enhanced forecasting
016
               accuracy and interpretability. In the first stage, we present AutoPrompter, which
017
               captures temporal dependencies from time series data and aligns them with se-
018            mantically meaningful text embeddings. In the second stage, these aligned embed-
019            dings are refined by incorporating task-specific textual instructions through LLM.
020            We evaluate TITSP on several multimodal time series prediction tasks, demon-
021            strating substantial improvements over state-of-the-art baselines. Quantitative re-
022            sults reveal significant gains in predictive performance, while qualitative analyses
023            show that textual context enhances interpretability and actionable insights. Our
024            findings indicate that integrating multimodal inputs not only improves prediction
025            accuracy but also fosters more intuitive, user-centered forecasting.

031   pipelines typically involve three key stages: data preprocessing, model selection, and performance

      Recent advancements in Transformer-based architectures Ilbert et al.; Zhou et al. (2021); Wen et al.
055   (2022) and Large Language Models (LLMs) (Gruver et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2023) have enhanced
056   the ability to capture long-range dependencies in time series data. Yet, these models also face

061   To address these challenges, we propose Text-Informed Time Series Prediction (TITSP), a novel

063   with the contextual richness of domain-specific textual inputs. TITSP first captures temporal depen-

066   to generate more accurate, context-aware, and interpretable predictions. Our extensive experiments
067   demonstrate that TITSP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art models, particularly in scenarios
068   where expert input is essential.

099   2.1            T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING A PPROACHES
100
101   Time series forecasting began with classical models like ARIMA (Box et al., 2015), which are
102   effective for linear patterns but struggle with non-linearities common in real-world data (Chatfield,
103   2000). Feature extraction methods like tsfresh (Christ et al., 2018) and machine learning algorithms
104
      such as Random Forests (Breiman, 2001) and Support Vector Machines (Vapnik, 1998) improved
      predictive accuracy by capturing non-linear dependencies.
105
106   The adoption of deep learning models, particularly RNNs (Rumelhart et al., 1986), LSTMs (Hochre-
107   iter & Schmidhuber, 1997), and Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), has further advanced the field
      by automatically learning complex temporal patterns (Wen et al., 2017). Following the sucess of

      transformer, works including TimeXer (Wang et al., 2024), itransformer (Liu et al., 2023), PatchTST
109   (Nie et al., 2022) and Informer (Zhou et al., 2021) are designed to address time-series prediction
110   problems. Despite these advancements, most approaches rely solely on time series data. Our work
111   diverges by integrating time series with textual context via Large Language Models (LLMs),
112   offering a richer, more contextual understanding of the data.

114   2.2   M ULTIMODAL L EARNING IN T IME S ERIES P REDICTION
115
116   Integrating time series data with unstructured text has become a key strategy for enhancing forecast-
117   ing accuracy through multimodal approaches that leverage diverse data types for richer context.
118   Advances in Model Architectures and Joint Prediction Zhang et al. (2024) introduced the Dual-
119   Adapter model to optimize time series representation by balancing textual and temporal information.
120   Building on this, Liu et al. (2024b) proposed UniTime, a unified model leveraging NLP for enhanced
121   time series forecasting across domains, while Liu et al. (2024a) explored LLMs for dynamical sys-
122   tems, showcasing their adaptability. Surveys such as (Liang et al., 2024; Deldari et al., 2022) offer
123   taxonomies of multimodal learning, identifying gaps in self-supervised methods for multimodal and
124   temporal data. The Multi-Modal Forecaster by Kim et al. (2024) jointly predicts time series and
125
      text, highlighting the potential of cohesive multimodal frameworks. Similarly, Cheng et al. (2024)
      introduced a prompt-based multimodal framework focused on classification. Recent advancements,
126
      such as (Jia et al., 2024) and (Jin et al., 2023), further integrate time series and text using LLMs,
127
      enhancing predictions through textual cues.
128
129   Existing Gaps and Novel Contributions. While recent works have advanced the integration of
130   time series and text, a key gap remains in instruction-based time series forecasting. Our method
131
      addresses this by introducing a novel framework for text instruction-based forecasting, leveraging
      the interplay between textual instructions and time series data for richer, interactive predictions.
132
      Additionally, we present a tailored methodology to benchmark such tasks, enabling applications in
133
      ”what-if” scenarios and dynamic, user-interactive forecasting.

250   Stage 1: AutoPrompter: Enhancing VQ-VAE for Unsupervised Alignment of Time Series and
251   Pre-trained Language Codebook
252
253
      In the first stage, we propose a novel mechanism, AutoPrompter, which serves as a bridge, trans-
      lating time series data into a compressed, semantically meaningful text embedding space. By in-
254
      troducing cross-attention and a pre-trained language codebook to the classical VQ-VAE (Vector
255
      Quantised-Variational AutoEncoder; (Van Den Oord et al., 2017)), AutoPrompter quantizes the time
256   series space, allowing it to align effectively with text embeddings. This translation helps capture the
257   underlying semantic patterns within the time series data, facilitating meaningful connections with
258   textual information. The key components are as follows:
259
260              1. Time Series Embedding: The time series data, X, is processed through a Convolutional
261                 Neural Network (CNN), producing a high-dimensional temporal embedding, zts , that cap-
262                 tures key temporal patterns.
263              2. Text Embedding with Pre-trained Codebook and Linear Compression: A pre-trained
264                 language model, equipped with a fixed vector quantization codebook, provides discrete text
265                 embeddings, ztxt . These embeddings are subsequently passed through a linear compression
266                 layer to obtain a compressed semantic representation, ztxt compressed . This compression re-
267                 duces the dimensionality of the text embeddings, facilitating efficient alignment with the
268
                    time series embeddings while preserving essential semantic information.
269              3. Cross-Attention Alignment: A cross-attention mechanism aligns the time series embed-
                    ding zts with the compressed text embedding ztxt compressed . In this setup, zts acts as the


                                                                                                                     5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
              query, and ztxt compressed serves as the key-value pair. This interaction facilitates the integra-
271           tion of temporal and semantic information, producing an aligned multimodal representa-
272           tion, zaligned .
273
           4. Self-Supervised Learning: Our model is trained using self-supervised learning to align
274
              time series embeddings with meaningful text embeddings while preserving key character-
275
              istics of the original time series data. The training minimizes two key losses:
276
              1. Reconstruction Loss (Lrecon ) 2. Vector Quantization Loss (Lvq )
277
278              • Vector Quantization Loss (Lvq ): The Vector Quantization Loss ensures that the en-
279                coder outputs are quantized by aligning them with the nearest vectors from a fixed,
280                pre-trained language codebook. This encourages the model to represent time series
281
                   data using a discrete set of embeddings from the codebook, facilitating alignment be-
                   tween the input data and text-based embeddings.
282
                   Unlike traditional VQ-VAE, where the codebook is updated during training, our model
283
                   employs a fixed codebook. However, because we apply a linear compression layer to
284
                   the text embeddings, we use two distinct terms to maintain effective alignment:
285
                    – Quantization Latent Loss (Lvq latent ) ensures that the quantized embeddings (ob-
286
                       tained from the codebook) closely match the encoder’s output.
287
                    – Commitment Loss (Lcommit ) encourages the encoder’s output to remain close to
288
                       the quantized embeddings. This ensures the model consistently uses the closest
289                    embedding in the codebook, preventing the encoder from drifting too far from the
290                    quantized space.
291                These losses are defined as:
292
                                                                                    2
293                                           Lvq latent = ∥zaligned − sg(zencoder )∥2
294                                                                                 2
                                               Lcommit = ∥zencoder − sg(zaligned )∥2

297                where:
298
                    – zencoder is the output of the encoder.
299
                    – zaligned refers to the quantized embeddings obtained from the vector quantizer.
300

301                    quantizer during optimization.
302

1672   The sharp drop in the GBP/USD exchange rate on October 11, 2008, can be traced to events on
1673   October 10, 2008, when global financial markets experienced a massive sell-off, exacerbating the
       effects of the ongoing financial crisis.

1674
       On October 10, 2008, the Dow Jones Industrial Average plunged 679 points (or about 7.3%) by
1675   the end of the trading day, after dipping as much as 800 points during intra-day trading. European
1676   markets saw even steeper declines, with the FTSE 100 (UK’s benchmark index) dropping around
1677   8.9% and Germany’s DAX falling 7%. In Asia, Japan’s Nikkei 225 dropped 9.6%, adding to the
1678   already severe financial instability.
1679
       Despite the UK government’s £500 billion bailout package announced on October 8 to rescue major
1680
       banks like Royal Bank of Scotland and Lloyds TSB, concerns over the health of the global financial
1681   system persisted. Investors began fleeing to safer assets, driving demand for the U.S. dollar as a safe
1682   haven, which in turn weakened the British pound.

1686
