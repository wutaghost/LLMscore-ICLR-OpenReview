009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The widespread deployment of machine learning systems in critical real-world
012
               decision-making applications has highlighted the urgent need for counterfactual
013            explainability methods that operate effectively. Global counterfactual explana-
014            tions, expressed as actions to offer recourse, aim to provide succinct explanations
015            and insights applicable to large population subgroups. Effectiveness is measured
016            by the fraction of the population that is provided recourse, ensuring that the ac-
017            tions benefit as many individuals as possible. Keeping the cost of actions low
018            ensures the proposed recourse actions remain practical and actionable. Limiting
019            the number of actions that provide global counterfactuals is essential to maxi-
020            mize interpretability. The primary challenge, therefore, is balancing these trade-
021
               offs—maximizing effectiveness, minimizing cost, while maintaining a small num-
               ber of actions. We introduce GLANCE, a versatile and adaptive framework, com-
022
               prising two algorithms, that allows the careful balancing of the trade-offs among
023
               the three key objectives, with the size objective functioning as a tunable parameter
024            to keep the actions few and easy to interpret. C-GLANCE employs a clustering
025            approach that considers both the feature space and the space of counterfactual ac-
026            tions, thereby accounting for the distribution of points in a way that aligns with the
027            structure of the model. T-GLANCE provides additional features to enhance flexi-
028            bility. It employs a tree-based approach, that allows users to specify split features,
029            to build a decision tree with a single counterfactual action at each node that can be
030            used as a subgroup policy. Our extensive experimental evaluation demonstrates
031            that our method consistently shows greater robustness and performance compared
032
               to existing methods across various datasets and models.

      Machine learning models are increasingly deployed in critical domains such as loan approvals, hir-
038
      ing, and healthcare. This widespread adoption has intensified the need for transparency and in-
039   terpretability in model decisions, requiring users to understand how their input features influence
040   the outcomes and how they might change them to achieve favorable outcomes, known as recourse
041   (Miller, 2019). Counterfactual explanations have gathered extensive attention for their suitability
042   for achieving algorithmic recourse (Karimi et al., 2020), their interpretability (Wachter et al., 2017),
043   actionability (Ustun et al., 2019), utility in fairness audits (Sharma et al., 2019; Kavouras et al.,
044   2023), etc. A counterfactual action, or simply an action, defines the specific feature changes needed
045   to convert an unfavorable decision into a favorable one.
046   Traditionally, counterfactual explanations refer to local explainability, being tied to a particular
047   negatively affected instance. However, in many real-world scenarios, global counterfactual explain-
048   ability is more useful, offering generalized explanations that apply across the affected population.
049   While a collection of all local counterfactuals could technically cover all affected individuals, this
050   approach sacrifices interpretability, which is central to global explainability.
051   Similar to past work (Rawal & Lakkaraju, 2020; Kanamori et al., 2022), we define Global Counter-
052   factual Explanations (GCE) as a small set of global actions that provide effective recourse for the
053   affected population. Any global counterfactual solution must meet three objectives as identified from
      previous research: (1) be composed of a small number of actions to ensure interpretability (size),

086   actions are generated for each instance and a small subset is chosen as the global actions, this may
087   still result in a suboptimal GCE. In our example, the optimal global action a3 is not an optimal local
088   action for either x1 or x2 ; in fact, a3 can be viewed as a compromise between a1 and a2 , the locally
089   optimal actions. Finding good GCEs requires a nuanced exploration of the action space.
090
      In this work, we distinguish between two variants of GCE that differ in how they assign actions to
091   individuals. In implicit GCE, each individual chooses the best (i.e., min-cost) global action in GCE
092   to achieve (if possible) recourse (as in e.g., Ley et al., 2023). Implicit GCE is helpful when one
093   wishes to obtain a model-level understanding of recourse, or to select a global (horizontal) policy
094   to apply to the entire affected population. In explicit GCE, individuals are partitioned according
095   to their features and a single action applies to each partition (as in e.g., Rawal & Lakkaraju, 2020;
096   Kanamori et al., 2022). Explicit GCE provides a more interpretable summary as it associates actions
097   to partitions of the feature space, and can also be used to implement targeted policies to bring
098   recourse to subpopulations.

103   and the model’s decision boundary, we propose a novel clustering approach for GCEs that consid-

105   counterfactuals (see e.g., Kanamori et al. (2022)), we are the first to propose the joint clustering in
106   feature and action spaces and demonstrate its effectiveness. We introduce GLANCE, a novel and
107   highly adaptable framework, that consists of two algorithms based on hierarchical clustering tech-
      niques, C-GLANCE and T-GLANCE. GLANCE bridges local and global explanations by combining

127   C-GLANCE. We present C-GLANCE, a clustering-based approach for the implicit GCE problem
128   that returns s global actions. At a high level, C-GLANCE consists of two phases: candidate action
129   generation, and action selection.
130
      To generate candidates, C-GLANCE first performs a fine-grained clustering of the individuals in the
131
      feature space. For each cluster, C-GLANCE then discovers a few diverse actions that bring recourse
132   to the cluster centroid. The intuition here is that highly similar individuals are expected to have a
133   similar perspective of the decision boundary, and can thus achieve recourse through similar cost-
134   efficient actions. Figure 2a shows an example with four fine-grained clusters, where three diverse
135   actions are discovered from each centroid. Each action represents an alternate way to bring recourse
136   to cluster members, giving C-GLANCE the flexibility to later select among them.
137
      At the end of the first phase, C-GLANCE has associated each cluster of individuals with a set of
138   actions. In the second phase, C-GLANCE iteratively merges such clusters until exactly s clusters
139   remain. Two clusters are merged if they either concern similar individuals or contain similar actions.
140   The intuition behind the former is similar to that of the candidate generation phase. The intuition
141   for the latter is that if similar actions bring recourse to them it should be easier to identify a common
142   low-cost, highly effective action, even though they are dissimilar feature-wise. Figure 2b shows an
143   example where the red individuals are grouped together either through affinity in the feature or in
144   the action space.
145   As discussed in (Kanamori et al., 2022), performing a coarse-grained clustering on feature space and
146   then assigning a single action per cluster leads to inadequate global actions, that suffer either in cost
147   or effectiveness. For example, in Figure 2c, individuals are clustered based on feature similarity and
148   a single action is selected per cluster. Because the blue cluster is associated with rather dissimilar
149   actions, choosing a single actions leads to a compromise in either effectiveness (as depicted) or cost.
150   T-GLANCE. Recognizing the need for explicit GCE, we present T-GLANCE, an algorithm em-
151   ploying a tree-based approach, similar to explainable clustering techniques (e.g., Moshkovitz et al.,
152   2020). This allows assigning actions to individuals while satisfying the transparency and consis-
153   tency properties required by Kanamori et al. (2022). T-GLANCE also allows users to select features
154   that define specific groups, to produce targeted GCEs. For the user’s facilitation, we also provide
155   suggestions based on the importance feature permutation score for selecting features to be used
156   in splits. T-GLANCE directly supports policymaking and audit scenarios—an application not ad-
157   dressed by existing methods. As shown in our experiments, T-GLANCE delivers competitive results,
158
      outperforming previous tree-based methods while maintaining efficiency.

      1.2   R ELATED W ORK
167
      Counterfactual Explanations. There has been a plethora of work with an emphasis on counter-
168
      factual explanations (Verma et al., 2020). An overview of methods on algorithmic recourse, which
169   provides explanations and recommendations to individuals impacted by automated decision-making
170   systems is presented by Karimi et al. (2020). These methods can be model-agnostic or model-
171   specific (e.g., for trees Carreira-Perpiñán & Hada, 2021), focus on properties like diversity (Mothilal
172   et al., 2020) or feasibility (Ustun et al., 2019)), global or local. While local counterfactuals are well
173   defined, global counterfactuals present challenges, as they must provide recourse for all individuals
174   within a specific group in the same manner while maintaining explainability and staying true to the
175   notion of local counterfactual, which focuses on minimal changes. Achieving this balance globally
176   presents significant difficulties.
177   Prior work does not make the distinction between explicit and implicit GCE. Regarding explicit
178   GCE, Rawal & Lakkaraju (2020) introduced AReS, a framework for global counterfactual expla-
179   nations, that jointly optimizes recourse correctness, coverage, and cost, providing an interpretable
180   summary of recourses, expressed in a two-level rule set. However, the AReS framework may fail to
181   cover the entire population. Ley et al. (2022) later improved the computational efficiency with Fast
182   AReS. In another direction, Kanamori et al. (2022) introduced CET, which partitions the space and
183
      assigns an action to each part transparently and consistently. Although effective, its computational
      complexity limits scalability.
184
185   Regarding implicit GCE, Warren et al. (2023) developed Group-CF, that generates counterfactuals
186   that seeks to maximize effectiveness, though it can result in higher costs. Ley et al. (2023) proposed
187   GLOBE-CE, where global counterfactual explainability is defined differently, as a small set of action
188   directions along which individuals can “move” to achieve recourse. Therefore, GLOBE-CE attacks
189
      a different optimization problem than the one we study here. For the toy example in Figure 1, the
      single direction that minimizes the total recourse cost for x1 and x2 is the direction d depicted in
190
      Figure 1b. This direction contains actions a4 and a5 that bring recourse to x2 and x1 , respectively,
191
      with minimum cost along d. It is important to note that neither a4 nor a5 is optimal as a GCE (or
192   for local explainability). Even the set {a4 , a5 } is a suboptimal GCE—the set {a1 , a2 } dominates it
193   with equal size and effectiveness but lower total cost. In general, (1) directly translating the output
194   of GLOBE-CE into a GCE leads to numerous micro-actions (two in our example, but potentially as
195   many as the number of individuals), which reduces interpretability, (2) choosing a few actions along
196   the optimal directions may result in suboptimal GCEs (in our example, any single action along d
197   is dominated by a), and (3) since directions lack clear endpoints, they fail to specify the magnitude
198   of change required, leading to uncertainty for individuals seeking recourse and limiting real-world
199   applicability.
200   Other works include Carrizosa et al. (2024a;b), who use mixed-integer quadratic models for group-
201   level explanations, and Koo et al. (2020), who employ Lagrangian methods. Research has also
202   extended to generating global counterfactuals for graphs (Huang et al., 2023) and auditing subgroup
203   fairness (Kavouras et al., 2023).
204   Global Explainability. Many works in global explainability are related to our approach. For in-
205   stance, Chowdhury et al. (2022) summarize model logic by dividing the desired region of explana-
206   tion features into subspaces based on similar logic, referred to as equi-explanation maps. Similarly,
207   Lakkaraju et al. (2019) provides explanations for different subspaces characterized by specific fea-
208   tures. Their method also allows users to customize the model explanations by selecting features
209   of interest, akin to the flexibility offered by T-GLANCE. While these works share a conceptual
210   focus on subspace-based explanations, they differ fundamentally from ours in their ultimate goals
211   and outcomes. Both primarily aim to summarize model behavior or provide faithful subspace-level
212
      explanations, whereas GLANCE seeks to generate actionable global counterfactual explanations tai-
      lored to decision-making processes. Consequently, the direct comparison of these approaches with
213
      T-GLANCE is not entirely applicable.

283   3     C-GLANCE
284
285   C-GLANCE (Algorithm 1) presents a clustering algorithm that follows an agglomerative approach.
286   The first step is to establish multiple small clusters and determine representative diverse actions
287   for each. The initial clusters will undergo merging, exploiting the notion of proximity in both the
288   original feature space and the action space, until no more than s clusters exist. Finally, a single
289   action will be extracted from each cluster with universal applicability across all instances. For the
290   time complexity analysis we refer the reader to Appendix C.
291   Algorithm Description. (1) Initial clusters and actions generation. The feature space is partitioned
292   into k clusters, by a clustering algorithm (e.g., k-means, line 1, alg. 1). We compute the centroid
293   of each cluster and generate m diverse counterfactual actions for each centroid (line 3, alg. 1),
294   employing any candidate counterfactual generation method (see Appendix G for the methods used
295   in the experimental evaluation). We aim to efficiently and effectively explore the action space by
296   generating actions from widely dispersed points within the feature space and guiding them in diverse
297   directions that cross the decision boundary.
298   (2) Merging. Similar to agglomerative clustering, our approach merges clusters with the goal of
299
      maximizing intra-cluster similarity and minimizing inter-cluster similarity with respect to both the
      feature and action spaces. Specifically, similarity is computed by a metric D, which is the sum
300
      of two distances: d1 : Xaff × Xaff → R, the distance between the centroids of each cluster, and
301
      d2 : A × A → R, the distance between the average counterfactual actions of each cluster; other
302   formulations for d2 are also possible, e.g., Wasserstein distance. Thus, clusters are combined based
303   on both their feature proximity and the proximity of their respective actions. Until the desired
304   number s of clusters is reached, we merge (line 7, alg. 1) the two clusters that minimize the total
305   distance d1 + d2 (line 6, alg. 1). Merging two clusters means that their action sets are combined,
306   ensuring that the most effective actions for each cluster are retained (line 8, alg. 1).
307   (3) GCE extraction and optimality. For each of the s clusters, we extract a single candidate action,
308   and return the set of these s actions (line 10, alg. 1). Assuming that the action generation and
309   subsequent merging has succesfully grouped together individuals that can achieve recourse through
310
      similar cost-efficient actions, in the final step we target effectiveness, selecting at each cluster the
      optimal action, in terms of effectiveness, among those associated with the cluster.
311
312   Approach Strengths. C-GLANCE supports various clustering methods, cost metrics, and action
313   generation techniques making it adaptable to different applications. In the App. Table 13, we present
314   the results using various action generation methods, all of which lead to near-optimal solutions in
315   terms of the effectiveness-cost tradeoff. Additionally, C-GLANCE demonstrates speed, robustness,
316
      and near-optimal solution quality across datasets, as shown in the experimental evaluation.

366   Algorithm Description. T-GLANCE selects a different direction for splitting at each iteration at
367   every cell in the current partition. Specifically, the algorithm begins with a single cluster containing
368   all affected instances and generates m diverse actions for the centroid, retaining the best action
369   according to the criterion in eq. (1) (lines 1–2, alg. 2). The root then is established as the first cell
370   A to be examined, and the possible splits are examined based on the actions that were generated for
371   each split (lines 8–9, alg. 2). For simplicity, we present the algorithm with binary splits (branching
372   factor of 2), though it can be generalized to accommodate any branching factor. We proceed with
373   the split that maximizes eq. (2), provided it yields a nonnegative value, and add the new cells as
374
      nodes of the tree to be examined in the next repetitions (lines 10–13, alg. 2); otherwise the node is
      not split. Importantly, the set of directions allowed for splitting is a subset of the features, provided
375
      by the user, allowing for controlled and interpretable partitioning of the data.
376
377   Termination Criteria. This process continues until one of the following termination criteria occurs:
      (1) a node has no remaining features to consider for splitting, (2) the maximum score of the actions

378
      from all remaining features is negative, or (3) the specified number of leaf nodes has been reached
379   (i.e., size constraints).
380
381   Approach Strengths. A key strength of T-GLANCE is its user-driven flexibility, allowing policy-
382
      makers or auditors to select the features that determine tree partitioning. By allowing users to guide
      the feature selection, T-GLANCE can adapt to specific policy needs or audit requirements, making
383
      it highly customizable. T-GLANCE provides multi-level solutions, assigning a single optimal coun-
384
      terfactual action to each tree node, ensuring transparency and consistency across decision-making
385   processes. This enables both high-level and granular solutions for additional user flexibility, in con-
386   tradiction with Kanamori et al. (2022), who optimize actions only at the leaf nodes. Additionally,
387   the method remains efficient, robust, and effective across various datasets and scenarios, as shown in
388   our evaluations, making it well-suited for real-world applications. Finally, unlike the stochastic local
389   search used by Kanamori et al. (2022), which may not converge (in about 20% of our experiments),
390   T-GLANCE guarantees a solution at every node.

      Models. We trained three different model types: XGBoost (XGB), Logistic Regression (LR), and

      A    H ARDNESS
649
650   To analyze the complexity of the s-GCE problem, we suppose that the set of all actions is finite by
651   discretizing continuous features.
652
      Theorem 3 (NP-hardness). The s-GCE problem is NP-hard.
653
654
      Proof. We establish the NP-hardness of the s-GCE problem by reducing the well-known Max k-
655   Coverage problem to it.
656
657   The Max k-Coverage problem is defined as follows: Given a universe U of elements and a collection
658
      of subsets S = {S1 , S2 , . . . , Sn } where each Si ⊆ U, along with an integer k, the goal is to select
      at most k subsets from S such that the total number of covered elements is maximized.
659
660   To perform the reduction, we proceed by mapping each subset Si to an action ai in the s-GCE
661   problem:
662
663          • Actions and Subsets Correspondence: Each action ai corresponds to a subset Si of ele-
664            ments in U.
665          • Coverage Mapping: The effectiveness of an action ai in the s-GCE problem is defined as
666            the elements in Si .
667
668          • Ignoring Costs: We ignore the cost associated with actions in the s-GCE problem, focus-
669            ing solely on coverage.
670
671
      Under this mapping, the objective of selecting at most k actions in the s-GCE problem to maximize
      the coverage of elements aligns exactly with the objective of the Max k-Coverage problem.
672
673
674
675   Given that even the relaxed version of the s-GCE problem that ignores costs is NP-hard, identify-
676   ing the optimal solution for the s-GCE is computationally infeasible. Moreover, the action space,
677   although finite, may be too large to process efficiently. Therefore, our approach involves generating
678
      a manageable subset A′ ⊆ A of actions before extracting the final s actions. In the next section, we
      will introduce C-GLANCE, an efficient heuristic designed to solve the s-GCE problem.

      B    S IZE -C OST AND S IZE -E FFECTIVENESS T RADE - OFFS
683
684   Let C be a set of actions. Let a new action a and the C′ = C ∪ {a}. We analyze how increasing the
685   size (size) of the solution, impacts the effectiveness (eff) and the average cost (avc).
686
687    Case 1 The new action does not flip any new instances.
688           In this scenario, the effectiveness remains the same.
689              • If the new action is cheaper than the current actions:
690                Some instances already receiving recourse from the current actions may now be ad-
691                dressed by the cheaper new action, reducing the average cost.
692              • If the new action is costlier than the current actions:
693                The current actions will continue to provide recourse for the instances they already
694                cover, leaving the average cost unchanged.
695    Case 2 The new action flips new instances.
696           In this scenario, the effectiveness increases, as more instances now receive recourse.
697              • If the cost of the new action is lower than the current average cost:
698                The new action provides recourse for additional instances at a lower cost, thereby
699                reducing the average cost.
700              • If the cost of the new action is higher than the current average cost:
701                The inclusion of the new action raises the average cost, as the costlier new instances
                   contribute positively to the total, increasing the average cost.


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      This analysis highlights the trade-offs between size, effectiveness, and cost, emphasizing the im-
703   portance of carefully selecting new actions to achieve a desirable balance. Notably, achieving near-
704   optimal effectiveness (close to 100%) often requires adding actions that address outliers, which may
705   have higher costs. However, one of the strengths of our method is its ability to achieve near-optimal
706   effectiveness with relatively low average costs across most datasets.

      G.1   D I CE
919
920   Detailed information on its methodology can be found in the corresponding paper by Mothilal et al.
921   (2020), and we encourage interested readers to refer to it for a deeper understanding.
922
      For the scope of this paper, it suffices to say that DiCE offers a number of alternative algorithms
923
      for finding local counterfactuals. In our experiments, we use the ‘random’ algorithm, which selects
924   features to change at random and tests whether the change results in a successful counterfactual.
925
926   The other algorithms were either unsuitable for a black-box setting (e.g., requiring gradients), com-
927
      putationally inefficient (e.g., genetic algorithm), or failed to generate cost-efficient counterfactual
      (e.g., KDTree) during our initial experimentation.
928
929
930   G.2   R ANDOM S AMPLING
931
      We developed a method called ‘Random Sampling’. To find counterfactuals for an affected instance,
932   this method iteratively modifies its features one at a time. The process begins by randomly altering
933   one feature at a time, generating multiple new candidate instances. Each modified instance is then
934   evaluated by querying the black-box model to determine whether it qualifies as a valid counterfac-
935   tual. The method proceeds to modify additional features, building a set of potential counterfactuals.
936
      Key differences between our method and DiCE’s random approach include:
937
938         1. We focus on modifying only the top kf most important features (using permutation feature
939            importance), set to 3 in all experiments.
940
            2. For categorical features, only the top kc most frequent categories among unaffected indi-
941
               viduals are considered replacement candidates (set to 10 for all experiments).
942
943
            3. We also introduce vectorization in certain operations, improving computational efficiency
               over DiCE’s implementation.
944
945
946   G.3   N EAREST N EIGHBORS
947
      This method is implemented by storing all unaffected individuals in memory. When queried to
948
      provide k counterfactuals for an affected individual, it retrieves the k nearest neighbors from the set
949   of unaffected instances based on their proximity to the affected individual. This approach ensures
950   that the generated counterfactuals are valid and closely aligned with the original instance, improving
951   the relevance of the recommendations.
952
953   G.4   N EAREST N EIGHBORS S CALED
954
955   This algorithm closely resembles the ‘Nearest Neighbors’ one but introduces a key enhancement.
956   Rather than returning the k nearest neighbors, it performs a localized search along the multidimen-
957   sional line connecting the affected individual and any neighbor. We sample points along this line
958   segment, and if any are classified as positive by the model, they are returned instead, as they are
959
      closer to the affected instance, potentially offering a more cost-efficient recourse.

1478
1479   Following the best practices outlined in Ley et al. (2023); Chowdhury et al. (2022), we recruited 40
1480   participants consisting of (a) master’s students in a data science program who attended lectures on
1481   machine learning explainability and (b) PhD students from various machine learning domains with
1482   extensive knowledge of explainability. After excluding six participants due to inconsistent responses
1483   to repeated questions and/or incomplete rankings, we analyzed the responses of the remaining 34
1484   participants.
1485   In the first part of the study, where participants assumed the role of individuals seeking recourse, we
1486   find that participants prefer information conveyed through concrete counterfactual actions (similar
1487   to GCEs produced by methods like ARES Rawal & Lakkaraju (2020) and this work) over counter-
1488   factual directions represented by average-cost actions (akin to GCEs generated by GLOBE-CE Ley
1489   et al. (2023)). Specifically, for Q1, 56% of participants preferred information tied to concrete actions
1490   (project B) over average-cost actions (project A). This preference increased to 71% in Q2 when un-
1491
       certainty rose (effectiveness decreased from 100% to 75%). When asked to rank their preference
       among three types of information in Q3, 62% of participants rated Info-1, which represents an opti-
1492
       mal solution to the 1-GCE problem (similar to action c in Figure 5), as the most helpful. Conversely,
1493
       41% ranked Info-2, which represents the average-cost action along the optimal direction as defined
1494   in Ley et al. (2023) (similar to action b in Figure 5), as the least helpful. Additionally, Info-3, an-
1495   other optimal solution to the 1-GCE problem (similar to action a in Figure 5), was considered more
1496   helpful than Info-2.
1497
       In the second part of the study, where participants assumed the role of system owners aiming to
1498
       provide recourse to adversely affected individuals, we again found that participants preferred in-
1499
       formation conveyed through concrete counterfactual actions rather than counterfactual directions.
1500   Specifically, when asked (Q1) to choose among three projects corresponding to directions A, B,
1501   and C in Figure 5, 65% favored projects/directions A or C (both optimal 1-GCE solutions) over
1502   project/direction B (aligned with the optimal direction as defined in Ley et al. (2023)), with C
1503   emerging as the clear favorite, selected 50% of the time. A similar pattern was observed in project
1504   placement decisions (Q2), involving actions a, b, and c in Figure 5, where 60% of participants
1505   preferred the optimal actions a and c over the average-cost action b.
1506   In the third part of the study, participants were asked to evaluate which method for global coun-
1507   terfactual explanations is superior based on performance in terms of average cost and effectiveness.

1533   J   A DDRESSING P OTENTIAL E DGE C ASES IN R ECOURSE A LGORITHMS
1534
1535
1536   While the proposed algorithms are designed to provide robust and actionable recourse, certain edge
1537
       cases may pose challenges:
1538   Highly nonlinear decision boundaries might cause the model’s decision boundary to vary signifi-
1539   cantly across small regions, leading to oversimplified recourse suggestions. We address this issue
1540   by using proximity-aware clustering and local counterfactual action generation to better approximate
1541   the decision boundary, ensuring more accurate and actionable recommendations.
1542   Sparse data regions or outlier points might result in unrealistic, infeasible, or overly tailored recourse
1543   actions. To mitigate these challenges, we generate many clusters and diverse counterfactuals, en-
1544   suring that feasible solutions are identified without being disproportionately influenced by outliers.
1545   This approach enhances robustness and broad applicability.
1546
       Ambiguity in selecting the “optimal” action can arise when multiple actions have similar costs and
1547   effectiveness, potentially leading to overly complex recourse suggestions. We resolve this by em-
1548   ploying predefined criteria that prioritize actionable and interpretable solutions while maintaining a
1549   balance between cost and effectiveness.
