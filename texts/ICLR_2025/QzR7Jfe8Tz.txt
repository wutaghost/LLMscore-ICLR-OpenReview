009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In settings where only a budgeted amount of labeled data can be afforded, active
012            learning seeks to devise query strategies for selecting the most informative data
013            points to be labeled, aiming to enhance learning algorithms’ efficiency and per-
014            formance. Numerous such query strategies have been proposed and compared in
015            the active learning literature. However, the community still lacks standardized
016
               benchmarks for comparing the performance of different query strategies. This
               particularly holds for the combination of query strategies with different learning
017
               algorithms into active learning pipelines and examining the impact of the learning
018
               algorithm choice. To close this gap, we propose ALPBench, which facilitates the
019            specification, execution, and performance monitoring of active learning pipelines.
020            It has built-in measures to ensure evaluations are done reproducibly, saving exact
021            dataset splits and hyperparameter settings of used algorithms. In total, ALPBench
022            consists of 86 real-world tabular classification datasets and 5 active learning set-
023            tings, yielding 430 active learning problems. To demonstrate its usefulness and
024            broad compatibility with various learning algorithms and query strategies, we
025            conduct an exemplary study evaluating 9 query strategies paired with 8 learning
026            algorithms in 2 different settings.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Supervised learning requires labeled data, i.e., a collection of data points labeled with regard to the
031   respective learning task. However, labeling data is usually time-consuming and expensive, e.g., if it
032   has to be done by human domain experts (Settles et al., 2008). Collecting unlabeled data is often
033   more affordable in terms of cost and easier to obtain, but not directly useful for supervised learning.
034
      For situations where only a limited budget is available for labeling data, the field of active learning
035
      (AL) (Settles, 2009) develops methods for selecting the most suitable data points from unlabeled data
036   to be labeled by a so-called oracle. The notion of “most suitable” here refers to data points that help
037   achieve the best possible generalization performance for a given learning algorithm.
038
039
      While AL is in principle applicable to different data modalities, such as images, text, video, or tabular
      data, each of these modalities presents unique challenges that affect not only the learning algorithm
040
      but also the active learning strategies (Werner et al., 2024). For instance, image data often involves
041
      high-dimensional, spatially correlated features, whereas tabular data requires handling mixed feature
042   types, missing features, etc. (Shwartz-Ziv and Armon, 2022). In this work, we specifically focus on
043   tabular data, which is widely used across various sectors, including medicine (Przystalski and Thanki,
044   2023), insurance (Hussain and Prieto, 2016), and manufacturing (Chen et al., 2023), and hence highly
045   relevant for many real-world machine learning applications (Chui et al., 2018).
046
      For tabular data, a diverse array of query strategies (QSs) are available in the literature that quantify
047
      the suitability of a data point in different ways, e.g., by Seung et al. (1992); Lewis and Gale (1994);
048   Scheffer et al. (2001); Houlsby et al. (2011); Kirsch et al. (2021), to name a few. However, the
049   performance of a QS depends on various factors, including the dataset, the budget constraints, and
050   the learning algorithm, among other things (Evans et al., 2013; Ramirez-Loaiza et al., 2017; Pereira-
051   Santos et al., 2019). Several empirical evaluations have already been conducted in the tabular data
052   domain (Yang et al., 2018; Zhan et al., 2021; Bahri et al., 2022a; Lu et al., 2023). Still, the community
053   lacks a benchmark for comparing the performances of different QSs that standardizes evaluation
      protocols and facilitates their comparison. Moreover, the existing evaluations are often limited in the

      Contributions. Thus far, a comprehensive benchmark to investigate the benefits of different query
075   strategies in combination with different learning algorithms remains absent. Moreover, the field lacks
076   a standardized evaluation framework to ensure fair comparisons and promote reproducible research.
077   In this work, we address these gaps by proposing ALPBench, a comprehensive benchmark for active
078   learning pipelines in the domain of tabular data classification tasks.
079
080   1. We propose ALPBench, the first tabular-data active learning benchmark that combines different
081
         learning algorithms and query strategies into active learning pipelines to execute and benchmark
         them against other pipelines across different settings and metrics.
082
083   2. We provide an implementation of ALPBench as an extensible Python package 1 , offering standard-
084      ized evaluation protocols to ensure consistent and reliable research outcomes. In an experimental
085
         study we showcase its usefulness by evaluating 72 different active learning pipelines on 86
         real-world classification datasets across 2 settings and 2 metrics.
086
087
088   Lessons learned. In the following, we present a summary of our key findings, including insights
089
      into the performance differences between different learners, binary and multi-class datasets, different
      metrics and the scalability across small and large settings.
090
091   1. Different learners: We confirm that MarginSampling is a highly effective query strategy, par-
092      ticularly when combined with tree-based models. For models like SVM, KNN, and TabNet,
093      representation-based approaches such as TypicalClustering prove to be better suited. FALCUN
094      performs exceptionally well with MLPs.
095   2. Different datasets: For binary datasets, uncertainty-based methods combined with strong learners
096      prove to be best, as these models provide reliable uncertainty estimates. However, as the number
097      of classes increases, the data distribution might become more challenging to learn, and the benefit
098      of incorporating representation or diversity-based approaches becomes more apparent.
099   3. Different metrics: When evaluating for accuracy, Margin Sampling is one of the most effective
100      query strategies. For AUC, methods incorporating diversity, such as, e.g., ClusterMargin or
101      PowerMargin, deliver the best performance. The importance of diversity might arise because
102      achieving a high AUC requires a well-balanced representation of all classes in the dataset.
103   4. Different settings: The dominance of MarginSampling in the large data setting is reduced in
104      the small setting, where methods that incorporate diversity excel. In these scenarios, having
105      representative samples becomes more crucial, whereas in the large data setting, the initial samples
106      may already provide sufficient information to cover different classes.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Various active learning benchmarks have been proposed in the literature, each focusing on different
111   domains such as image (Beck et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023), text (Vysogorets
112   and Gopal, 2024), or tabular data (Bahri et al., 2022a; Lu et al., 2023). Recently, Werner et al.
113   (2024) explored active learning across multiple domains. However, their analysis is limited to linear
114   models and deep neural networks. Considering that models like GBDTs and TabPFN excel on
115   tabular data (McElfresh et al., 2023), and our aim to also investigate the interplay between learning
116   algorithms and query strategies, we focus specifically on the tabular domain and integrate models
117   tailored for this data type.
118   In the tabular data domain, an early benchmark of AL demonstrated that margin sampling (MS)
119   often outperforms other QSs (Schein and Ungar, 2007) in combination with logistic regression
120   (LR) as a learning algorithm. The performance of combining varying learning algorithms and
121   QSs was investigated by Evans et al. (2013); Ramirez-Loaiza et al. (2017); Pereira-Santos et al.
122   (2019). However, the studies are outdated, i.e., there are stronger machine learning (ML) algorithms
123   nowadays (Grinsztajn et al., 2022; McElfresh et al., 2023), and many of the used datasets from the
124   UCI repository (Newman and Merz, 1998) are rather old.
125   More recent QSs were investigated by Yang et al. (2018); Zhan et al. (2021); Lu et al. (2023). Although
126   varying the strategy for instance selection, the learning algorithm is fixed, precisely a support vector
127   machine (SVM) (Zhan et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2023) or LR (Yang et al., 2018). However, as the
128   employed learner is crucial to the overall performance of AL (Ramirez-Loaiza et al., 2017), such
129   design choice raises the question of whether the findings generalize to other learners as well. Further,
130   their scope is limited to binary or only a handful of multi-class datasets.
131   All mentioned tabular benchmarks so far only considered one specific AL setting, i.e., the size of
132   the initially labeled pool and the budget. Yang et al. (2018) initially provided only one labeled
133   instance for each class, compared to, e.g., Lu et al. (2023), who randomly sampled 20 instances for
134   the labeled pool. These misalignments across different benchmarks complicate comparisons and
135   hinder the ability to draw general conclusions. Bahri et al. (2022a) were the first to address this
136   issue by investigating three different AL settings. They also considered very recent QSs and datasets
137
      from the OpenML-CC18 Benchmark Suite (Bischl et al., 2019). However, again, the authors chose
      only a single specific learner, in this case, a deep neural network. Motivated by recent works by
138
      Grinsztajn et al. (2022) and McElfresh et al. (2023), we believe that an up-to-date benchmark has
139
      to include multiple SOTA learning algorithms for tabular data such as GBDTs (e.g., Catboost) and
140   prior-fitted networks (PFNs) (e.g., TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023)) as well as recent QSs, e.g., power
141   margin sampling and power BALD (Kirsch et al., 2021). To the best of our knowledge, we are the
142   first to combine various SOTA learning algorithms with QSs and evaluate their performance on a
143   large amount of binary and multi-class real-world classification tasks for tabular data. To address
144   the challenges of evaluating active learning pipelines (Lüth et al., 2023), we provide standardized
145   evaluation protocols across multiple settings, and metrics.

      3     P OOL - BASED ACTIVE L EARNING
148
149
      In pool-based AL instances from the pool of unlabeled data are selected to be labeled by an oracle,
150
      which is done in an iterative procedure. Three different scenarios are commonly considered in
151   AL, namely, the membership-query synthesis, the stream-based, and pool-based scenario (Settles,
152   2009; Tharwat and Schenck, 2023). We focus on the pool-based scenario, being the preferred one in
153   real-world applications (Tharwat and Schenck, 2023). We first describe this scenario in Section 3.1
154   before elaborating on the implemented learning algorithms and QSs within ALPBench (Sections 3.2
155   and 3.3), and their combination into active learning pipelines (ALPs) (Section 3.4).
156
157   3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
158

160   Y = {1, ..., C}. A dataset (DS) is denoted as D = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 ⊂ X × Y, where each instance
161   xi = (x1i , . . . , xdi ) ∈ X is associated with an underlying true label yi ∈ Y. In AL, however, only a

171   3.2   L EARNING A LGORITHMS
172
173   The choice of the learning algorithm is quite important for the overall success of AL (Dos Santos
174   and Carvalho, 2016). However, existing benchmarks typically fix a single learning algorithm, such
175   as a deep neural network (DNN) (Bahri et al., 2022a) or an SVM (Lu et al., 2023), and recommend
176
      suitable QSs for this choice. To reveal insights for suitable QSs based on different learning algorithms,
      we investigate a variety of models. In particular, we choose the following models, covering a wide
177
      range of model types and including SOTA algorithms for tabular data (McElfresh et al., 2023):
178
      SVM, k-nearest neighbor (k-NN), random forest (RF), extremely randomized trees (ETC), LR, and
179   naïve Bayes (NB) represent the group of base learners. For each of them, we implement multiple
180   instantiations with different parameters. Further, we choose two GBDTs, namely XGBoost (XGB)
181   and Catboost. Finally, we include a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) and TabNet (Arik and Pfister,
182   2021) as representatives of DNNs, and TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023) representing PFNs.
183
184   3.3   Q UERY S TRATEGIES
185
186   Query strategies (QSs) can be classified into information-based (Info.), representation-based (Repr.),
187   and hybrid strategies (Hybr.) (Settles, 2009; Tharwat and Schenck, 2023). Info.-based strategies
188   leverage the predictions of the learning algorithm to select instances where the learner exhibits
189   uncertainty, as from these instances we expect the most informative insights. Repr.-based strategies
190   rely solely on the structure of the data to identify the most representative instances. Hybr. strategies
191
      combine both of the aforementioned strategies.
192   Formally, let zi ∈ Z either be a raw input instance, or its embedding of a neural network, pi ∈ P the
193   predicted class probabilities of a learning algorithm for that instance and {(xi )}R
                                                                                         i=1 ⊆ DU the pool of
194   instances that is queried by the QS in each iteration. Loosely speaking, info.-based approaches select
195   instances based on some uncertainty measure u(·) on the probability scores, repr.-based compute
196   representativeness r(·) leveraging the structure of Z; hybr. approaches combine both:

202   Information-based. Information or uncertainty-based approaches calculate the uncertainty for each
203   instance in the unlabeled pool, leveraging probability scores of the learning algorithm to subsequently
204   select the most uncertain instances. These approaches are quite fast, as the calculations are performed
205   in the (lower-dimensional) space of probabilities. However, they bear the risk of leading to a strong
206   shift in the data distribution. We implement various approaches, most of which were also considered
207
      by Bahri et al. (2022a). Amongst them are the well-known margin sampling (MS) (Scheffer et al.,
      2001), entropy sampling (ES) (Shannon, 1948) and least-confident sampling (LC) (Lewis and
208
      Gale, 1994), sampling instances which have the lowest margin, highest entropy, or where the
209
      learning algorithm is the least-confident about, respectively. The QSs variance reduction (VR) (Cohn,
210   1993) and expected error reduction (EER) (Roy and McCallum, 2001) select instances that are
211   expected to reduce the prediction error or output variance, respectively, and epistemic uncertainty
212   sampling (EU) (Nguyen et al., 2019) samples instances, where the model exhibits uncertainty due to a
213   lack of knowledge. Further, we also considered methods that compute uncertainty based on predicted
214   probabilities of an ensemble such as query-by-committee (QBC) (Seung et al., 1992) (disagreement
215   of the ensemble members), maximum entropy (MaxEnt) (Gal et al., 2017) (entropy of the averaged
      predictions) and BALD (Houlsby et al., 2011) (difference between MaxEnt and the averaged entropy

      Representation-based. QSs compute the representativeness of each instance in the raw input space
229
      or in some feature space. Both can potentially be high-dimensional, leading to high computational
230   costs. K-means sampling (k-means) (Kang et al., 2004) performs clustering of the instances in DU

234   which the closest neighbor in DL is the most distant.

236   (Citovsky et al., 2021) selects instances by first performing clustering on DU and then taking into
237   account the margin scores as well. Clustering uncertainty-weighted embeddings (CLUE) (Prabhu
238   et al., 2021) performs weighted k-means clustering on DU with the entropy of the learning algorithm as
239   sample weight. FALCUN (Gilhuber et al., 2024) computes a relevance score per instance, consisting
240   of the margin scores of the learning algorithm and a diversity score.

      3.4   ACTIVE L EARNING P IPELINES
243
244   We call the combination of a learning algorithm and a QS an active learning pipeline (ALP). Within an
245   ALP, the learning algorithm and QS are used in alternating order to (re-)fit a model for the labeled data
246   points and determine data points to be labeled by the oracle. In ALPBench, we explicitly account
247   for this interplay and therefore allow for constructing ALPs out of every possible combination of
248   learning algorithms and QS as long as they work with certain interfaces.
249
250
251   4     ACTIVE L EARNING P IPELINE B ENCHMARK
252
253   ALPBench is meant to provide an easy-to-use and easy-to-extend platform for investigating ALPs,
254   considering different combinations of learning algorithms and QSs, and evaluating new query
255   strategies to be tested and compared against already known strategies. To this end, in ALPBench,
256   we aim for high modularity with simple interfaces for the individual parts of an ALP, as well as for
257   applying the composed pipelines to different datasets and experiment setups.
258   To facilitate the usage of ALPBench, we subsequently explain how AL problems and ALPs are
259   specified, (Section 4.1 and 4.2, respectively), and what measures are taken for ensuring reproducibility
260   and therewith high-quality experimental studies (Section 4.3).

300   4.2       S PECIFICATION OF ACTIVE L EARNING P IPELINES
301
      To apply AL methods to AL problems, active learning pipelines (ALPs) are specified by a learner
302   and a query strategy (QS), as has been outlined in Section 3.4. They implement the main logic for the
303   interplay between the learner and QS and take care of the communication with the oracle.
304
305
      Learner. The learner is a learning algorithm that implements the scikit-learn classifier interface and
      is responsible for model induction. There are no restrictions on the type of learner as long as its
306
      interface matches that of a scikit-learn classifier. It is only provided with labeled data points.
307
308   Query Strategy. Provided with the learner, the already labeled and unlabeled data points, the QS
309   selects unlabeled data points to be labeled by the oracle. While we wrap and include random
310   sampling, BALD, QBC, EER and EU from the scikit-activeml library (Kottke et al., 2021),
311
      the remaining QSs are original implementations in ALPBench. In total, we include 19 QSs and a
      broad spectrum of 11 different learners that can be combined into more than 200 ALPs.

407
408   We firstly aim to investigate the interplay of the QS with different learners, to reveal which QSs are
409   particularly effective for each learner. Adhering to common evaluation procedures for comparing
410
      QSs (Bahri et al., 2022b;a; Lu et al., 2023), we compute budget curves and win-matrices.
411   Budget curves. Budget curves quantify the (test) performance of an ALP at each round of the AL
412   procedure. The area under the budget curve (AUBC) then offers a robust metric to compare different
413   ALPs over the whole AL procedure, given this (test) performance. Within our benchmark, we tracked
414   six different performance measures, as shown in Table 2, but in this evaluation study focused on
415   accuracy and AUC, as they are most widely used in the AL literature (Ramirez-Loaiza et al., 2017).
416
      We denote the AUBC given both metrics as AUBC (accuracy) and AUBC (AUC), respectively.
417   Win-matrices. For each learning algorithm, we compute a win-matrix W to compare the performances
418   of different QSs. Let D be the number of available datasets and assume M different QSs, this results
419   in a matrix of size M × M . To make the plots visually more appealing, we slightly modify the
420   definition of the entry of W at position (i, j) compared to Bahri et al. (2022b;a) as follows

451   5.3                    R ESULTS
452
453
      In this section, we present our main insights, aiming to answer the following research questions (RQ):
454
               RQ1: Which ALPs perform best and worst?
455
456
               RQ2: Given a specific choice of the learning algorithm, setting, metric and types of datasets, which
                    QS is particularly well suited?
457
458            RQ3: Are there datasets and/or settings where AL leads to a decrease in performance?
459
      RQ1. In Figure 2, we show heatmaps as described in Section 5.2 evaluated on AUBC (accuracy) and
460
      AUBC (AUC), separately for binary and multi-class datasets and for the small and large setting.
461
462   RF, Catboost, and TabPFN are quite dominant, as they constitute to many winning ALPs, especially
463   for the binary datasets. XGB also performs well overall, however, showing a preference for large
464   settings and multi-class datasets. TabNet, MLP, and k-NN are performing inferior, which, in the
465
      case of TabNet, might be due to limited training time. Overall, information-based strategies are quite
      dominant, especially for binary datasets regarding AUBC (accuracy). We hence confirm the finding
466
      that MS is a very competitive QS if evaluated for AUBC (accuracy) (Schein and Ungar, 2007; Bahri
467
      et al., 2022a) and extend it to other learners. However, when the AUBC (AUC) is considered, QSs
468   that incorporate also diversity are superior, especially for binary datasets. This particularly holds for
469   CluMS and TypClu. Also Rand is more competitive in this scenario, which extends findings of Lu
470   et al. (2023) for learners beyond a SVM. The QSs MS and power-set margin sampling (PowMS) are
471   quite strong for multi-class datasets regarding both metrics. Moreover, the learning algorithm seems
472   to be the crucial choice for the pipeline to achieve good performance.
473
      RQ2. In Figure 3, we present win-matrices for different learning algorithms in the large setting. We
474   choose SVM since it has been chosen as a learning algorithm in other AL studies, such as in Zhan
475   et al. (2021); Lu et al. (2023). Further, we choose Catboost and XGB, as they have shown strong
476   performance when combined into ALPs. We evaluate on all datasets and present results for the
477   AUBC (accuracy) in the first row and AUBC (AUC) and in the second row, where the last columns in
478   each Figure indicate the average win and loss percentages. Further win-matrices are provided in the
479   Appendix A.4.
480   The win-matrices clearly show that the suitability of different QSs varies, depending on the given
481   learning algorithm and metric. Regarding the AUBC (accuracy), MS is quite dominant, if the learner
482   is chosen to be Catboost or XGB. This can be also deduced from the high win percentage. If the
483   learner is an SVM, however, all other information-based QS and also TypClu outperform MS with
484   respect to the win to loose percentage ratio. For the AUBC (AUC), PowMS is very strong when
485   combined with Catboost and XGB. Regarding the SVM, both representation-based QSs outperform
      all other information-based strategies.

      RQ3. In Figure 4, we present budget curves for RF, k-NN, and XGB on two datasets (OpenML ID
487
      846 and 1053) in the small setting. For better visual clarity, we only combine the learners with Rand,
488   MS, CoreSet, and CluMS, each representing a different type of QSs.

490   Budget curves in AL are generally expected to show an upward trend, indicating improved per-
491
      formance with an increasing budget, as visual in the first subfigure. However, this pattern is not
      consistent across all learners, as for the combination of k-NN and CoreSet, the performance decreases.
492
      On a different dataset (third subfigure), this also holds for random sampling and slightly for MS and

      CluMS. Even for a learning algorithm that demonstrates a strong overall performance, the picture can
494   look quite similar, as shown in the fourth subfigure. To conclude, AL can deteriorate performance,
495   as has also been shown by Guo and Schuurmans (2007a) and Gasperin (2009). We again want to
496   emphasize the strong dependence of the performance of ALPs on the chosen learning algorithm,
497   dataset, setting, and potentially other properties, which still need to be understood. We hope that
498   ALPBench will serve as a tool to gain more insights into this.

      6    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
501
502   We proposed ALPBench, a benchmark for active learning pipelines (ALPs) on tabular data.
503   ALPBench allows for easily combining QSs and learning algorithms into ALPs and provides a
504   unified API to evaluate and benchmark them against each other. The open-source implementation of
505   our benchmark is available as a Python package.
506
      In the benchmark so far, we predefined five different settings, which were partly inspired by Bahri
507
      et al. (2022a). However, the exploration of more settings having different requirements for suitable
508   pairs of QSs and learning algorithms outlines an interesting avenue for future work. Further, it might
509   be appealing to incorporate other more recent trends, such as label noise, multiple annotators, etc.
510
511
      In our experimental evaluation, we find that most of the time, strong pipelines consist of learners
      such as RF, Catboost, or TabPFN and information-based query strategies. However, there is no
512
      clear SOTA QS, as the suitability of a QS heavily depends on the chosen learner, the metric to be
513
      evaluated and the specific dataset. For instance, we confirm that MS is highly competitive regarding
514   the AUBC (accuracy) (Schein and Ungar, 2007; Bahri et al., 2022a) and extend this finding to learners
515   like e.g., Catboost and XGB. However, when evaluating for AUBC (AUC), query strategies that
516   also incorporate diversity tend to perform better. Additionally, for learners such as SVM or k-NN,
517   representation-based approaches are more suitable.
518
      With this benchmark and library, we hope to foster further research to fairly evaluate new QS
519
      considering different datasets, settings, and learners. Moreover, it might be appealing to specifically
520   develop new QSs for certain settings and/or learners. Lastly, we would also like to study whether it
521   might be advantageous to devise hyperheuristics switching between different QSs within one active
522   learning procedure.
523
524
525
      7    L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER I MPACT S TATEMENT
526
      Both the benchmark and the evaluation study are limited to tabular classification problems and
527
      consider a specific set of active learning settings. Furthermore, in the empirical study, we restricted
528
      the training time to 180 seconds per iteration, which might limit generalizability for the large
529   settings. Nevertheless, we observe complementary performance for both learning algorithms and
530   query strategies, which underpins the need for a benchmark like ALPBench.
531
532
      Current active learning research often lacks consistency, as researchers choose different settings
      and learners to demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed QS. However, key factors such as,
533
      e.g., the choice of learner, batch size, and number of iterations can significantly impact performance.
534
      Therefore, establishing a standardized evaluation framework is needed to ensure fair comparisons
535   and encourage more consistent and comparable research in this area. ALPBench aims to serve as a
536   starting point to address this need.
537
538
      As this paper presents work that aims to advance the field of machine learning, there are many
      potential societal consequences of our work. However, we feel that none of these needs to be
539
      specifically highlighted here.

944
      In this section, we elaborate in more detail on the experiments that were conducted within our evaluation study.
945   Datasets. From the 90 datasets from the OpenML-CC18 Bischl et al. (2019) and the TabZilla Benchmark
946   Suite McElfresh et al. (2023) we filtered and excluded the datasets with OpenML IDs 1567, 1169, 41147, and
947
      1493. The first three were filtered out for all settings because they consist of more than 300,000 data points,
      which would result in a large amount of computing time for the non-info. based QSs. The last dataset with
948   OpenML ID 1493 was filtered out since it consists of 100 classes, which would result in a huge amount of the
949   per iteration budget R, limiting the number of iterations to a high degree. Further, for the large setting, we
950   wanted to guarantee that at least 10 iterations can be performed until all instances from DU are queried. This
951   led to the removal of OpenML IDs 11, 12, 14, 16, 18, 22, 25, 51, 54, 188, 307, 458, 469, 1468, 1501, 40966,
952
      and 40979 for this setting. For the preprocessing steps, we proceed as follows. Categorical features are one-hot
      encoded and missing values are imputed by the mean or mode of the corresponding feature.
953
954   Active Learning Setting. As mentioned, we investigate a small and a large setting. Explicitly, the small and large
                                   0                           0
955
      settings are specified by |DL  | = R = 5 · |Y| and |DL     | = R = 20 · |Y|, respectively, for the given dataset and a
      total amount of 20 iterations or until all instances from the unlabeled pool DU are queried. We choose the factor
956   5 for the small setting, since then R matches the one in the (static) small setting in Bahri et al. (2022a). For the
957   large setting, we should have chosen a factor of 100 to be again consistent with Bahri et al. (2022a). However,
958   this seemed unrealistic to us for real-world applications. For some (imbalanced) datasets, it may happen that not
                                                      0
959   every class is at least once represented in DL    . In these cases, we additionally randomly sample one instance
                                                                                       0
960
      from DU per missing class and add them with their corresponding label to DL        . We run each ALP ten times with
      different seeds, where the seed defines the 23 / 13 -split of the total dataset D into Dtrain and Dtest as well as the
961   split of Dtrain into DL and DU . Needless to say, the datasets we consider are originally (fully-)labeled datasets.
962   Tailored to the AL setting, we discard the labels for the instances in DU and assure that only the oracle O can
963   access them.
964   Configuration of Learning Algorithms. In general, we do not perform any hyperparameter optimization (HPO)
965   but rather stick to the default parameters. To contain computational costs, we limit the training time of the
966   learning algorithms. For XGB and Catboost, we reduce the training time by setting the tree method to hist
967   and limiting the amount of iterations, respectively. For Catboost and for TabNet, we implement a timeout of
968
      three minutes per iteration for the same purpose. This of course may decrease the performance of the learning
      algorithms and poses a limitation to the generalizability of our empirical study. Further, TabPFN (Hollmann
969   et al., 2023) can so far only be fitted on a maximum amount of 1,000 instances. Therefore, we uniformly sample
970   1,000 instances from the current dataset to be fitted on, in case this constraint is violated, similar to McElfresh
971   et al. (2023). For TabPFN and TabNet we modify the implementation for the representation-based and hybrid
      approaches. Concretely, we extract the output of the encoder from the TabPFN and the activations of the
