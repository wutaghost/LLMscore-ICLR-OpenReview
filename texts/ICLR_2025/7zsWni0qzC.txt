009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Modern high-throughput biological datasets with thousands of perturbations pro-
012            vide the opportunity for large-scale discovery of causal graphs that represent the
013            regulatory interactions between genes. Numerous methods have been proposed to
014            infer a directed acyclic graph (DAG) corresponding to the underlying gene regu-
015            latory network (GRN) that captures causal gene relationships. However, existing
016            models have restrictive assumptions (e.g. linearity, acyclicity), limited scalability,
017            and/or fail to address the dynamic nature of biological processes such as cellular
018
               differentiation. We propose PerturbODE, a novel framework that incorporates
               biologically informative neural ordinary differential equations (neural ODEs) to
019
               model cell state trajectories under perturbations and derive the causal GRN from
020
               the neural ODE’s parameters. We demonstrate PerturbODE’s efficacy in trajectory
021            prediction and GRN inference across simulated and real over-expression datasets.
022

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   GRNs capture the complex regulatory interactions between genes that dictate cell function, develop-
027   ment, and responses to environmental changes. High-throughput perturbation assays with single-cell
028   RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) readouts, such as Perturb-seq, enable precise measurement of gene
029   expression changes across cell types resulting from genetic perturbations. However, inferring GRNs
030   from scRNA-seq experiments remains challenging due to the problem’s exponential search space. To
031   overcome the inherent combinatorial complexity of GRN discovery, recent causal graphical modeling
032   approaches relax the problem into a continuous, albeit non-convex, optimization program that learns
033
      a directed acyclic graph (DAG) corresponding to the underlying GRN (Zheng et al., 2018; Fang et al.,
      2023; Brouillard et al., 2020; Lopez et al., 2022).
034
035   While causal graphical models have predominantly focused on learning structure from gene
036   knockdown-based perturbations, new interventional single-cell experiments offer insights into previ-
037   ously unexplored aspects of gene regulation at an unprecedented scale. In particular, the Transcription
038   Factor (TF) Atlas applied single-cell resolution assays to systematically study the effects of overex-
039
      pression of 1,836 TFs on cell differentiation, generating over 1.1 million cell profiles measured 7
      days following TF perturbation (Joung et al., 2023). TFs, proteins that bind to the genome to regulate
040
      gene expression, play a crucial role in defining cell states. Their overexpression can induce significant
041
      changes in cell fate, directing the differentiation of stem cells into various cell types such as myocytes
042   and neurons. Since gene regulation during differentiation is inherently dynamic, accurately capturing
043   these dynamics is essential for models aiming to uncover the underlying regulatory network. Previous
044   experiments in yeast and E. coli have demonstrated that gene regulatory dynamics can be effectively
045   modeled by complex non-linear dynamical systems (Alon, 2006; Setty et al., 2003; Kalir and Alon,
046   2004). Their experimentally validated GRNs contain negative self-loops, which contradicts the
047   assumption of graph acyclicity imposed by most structure learning methods.
048   Causal structure learning methods are limited in their ability to model the full complexity of inter-
049   ventional data generated by emerging single-cell assays. To address these limitations, we propose
050   PerturbODE, a novel neural ODE-based framework that 1) implicitly encodes the GRN in its pa-
051   rameters, enabling simultaneous trajectory inference and GRN discovery, 2) maps cell states into
052   a lower dimensional “gene module” space analogously to causal representation learning (CRL), 3)
053   allows explicit input of which gene(s) were perturbed, a feature uncommon in CRL approaches,
      4) can model cycles and non-linear gene interactions, and 5) incorporates novel diffusion model-

      inspired regularization of the system’s dynamics. Trained on the TF Atlas scRNA-seq data that
055   captures the differentiation pathways of cells perturbed by over-expression of over a thousand TFs,
056   PerturbODE enables scalable and interpretable discovery of the gene dependencies that drive cellular
057   differentiation.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
061
      Causal graph discovery from genetic perturbations. Structure learning of causal graphs has
062
      recently been applied to Perturb-seq interventional experiments to infer an underlying GRN. The
063   nodes in the encoded causal graph correspond to genes and the directed edges ideally correspond
064   to direct causal regulatory relationships between genes. Since the number of possible DAGs grows
065   exponentially with the number of nodes, classical causal graph discovery approaches are unable
066   to scale beyond a modest number of genes (typically 50-200). NO-TEARS (Zheng et al., 2018)
067   introduced a continuous optimization objective via the trace exponential acyclicity constraint, signifi-
068   cantly simplifying the problem complexity and enabling gradient descent-based structure learning.
069   Extensions have further improved scalability. NO-TEARS-LR (Fang et al., 2024) adds a low-rank
070   assumption to NO-TEARS to efficiently infer large and dense DAGs. DCDI (Brouillard et al., 2020)
071
      extends the continuous optimization formulation to interventional data but can only scale up to 50
      dimensions in their original implementation with the trace exponential acyclicity constraint. DCDFG
072
      (Lopez et al., 2022) addresses DCDI’s limited scalability by employing a low-rank factor graph
073
      structure and spectral radius acyclicity constraint.
074
075   Neural ODEs for cell trajectory inference and modeling gene regulation. Differential equation-
076   based models have long been the preferred framework for describing dynamical systems in biology
077
      due to their interpretability and flexibility in incorporating known properties of the system. Neural
      ODEs extend this framework by leveraging neural networks to learn the dynamics directly from data,
078
      making them particularly suited for modeling complex, high-dimensional systems without explicit
079
      formulations. Neural ODEs and their stochastic variants have been applied to trajectory inference,
080   where the continuous development of cellular states is mapped over time. Some authors fit a discrete
081   ODE specified by a gene regulatory function to temporal pairs of cells sampled from the optimal
082   transport plan (Schiebinger et al., 2019). The gene regulatory function encodes information about
083   the cell-autonomous regulatory networks. Jackson et al. (2023) parameterizes ODEs with recurrent
084   neural networks (RNNs) to model dynamics before obtaining the coefficient of partial determination
085   to represent the contribution of each TF.
086   Causal Graph learning through stationary diffusion. The recently proposed method BICYCLE
087   (Rohbeck et al., 2024) parameterizes the GRN adjacency matrix as the linear drift of a stable Olstein-
088   Uhlenbeck (OU) process, approximating the steady state distribution under each intervention induced
089   by the OU process by solving the Lyapunov equation. Despite the novelty in methodologies, it can
090   currently only handle tens of observed genes.
091
      Key Limitations. Despite recent improvements to structure learning, causal GRN inference methods
092   remain difficult to scale and are limited in their modeling capabilities. While they can learn some
093   causal regulatory relationships from knockdown data, they lack the expressivity to capture how gene
094   regulation affects cellular dynamics across time. Schiebinger et al. (2019) and Jackson et al. (2023)
095   applied neural ODE-based methods for learning GRNs from a single perturbation or reprogramming
096   trajectory, but provide no framework for leveraging datasets with multiple known genetic perturba-
097   tions. PerturbODE combines ideas from causal structure learning and trajectory inference to provide
098   a flexible and scalable framework that accurately captures cell dynamics and learns gene regulation
099   from thousands of perturbations.

147   This system encapsulates the interaction between genes through a Multi-layer Perceptron (MLP) with
148   a single hidden layer. Each neuron in the hidden layer is analogous to a gene module, similar to Segal
149   et al. (2005). Such regulatory structure is known in the biology, such as production of flagella in E.
150   coli (Macnab, 2003; Alon, 2006). For more details, appendix 6.10 illustrates the representation of the
151   regulatory circuit of E. coli’s flagella as a two-layer MLP.
152   The matrix B ∈ Rl×d represents a linear transformation from the d-dimensional gene expression Yt
153   to a lower l-dimensional latent (“module”) space (l ≪ d). Bjm is the signed effect of j-th gene’s
154   expression on the the m-th module.
155
      The gene module signals are then non-linearly transformed after shift and scaling to give module
156   activations. The non-linear activation function σ(·) : Rl → Rl models the activation of the gene
157   modules, with the logistic sigmoid function used as the default choice for σ(·). This activation function
158   is chosen because of its relationship to the Hill function, which is well-studied and biophysically
159   motivated for representing the effect of TF concentration on target gene transcription rate (Alon,
160   2006). The vector β ∈ Rl is a strictly positive bias that shifts the activation threshold of the function
161   σ. The vector α ∈ Rl is a scaling factor that modulates the rate of activation through a Hadamard
      (i.e., elementwise) product ( ◦ ) with the gene modules.

      The module activations regulate downstream genes by combining linearly with those from other
163   modules. The matrix A ∈ Rd×l maps the l-dimensional latent vector back to the d-dimensional gene

166
      Most importantly, the interaction between genes mediated by modules encodes the GRN matrix,
      which we compute as W = A diag(α ◦ 1N )B. Conveniently, working with the lower-dimensional
167
      module space reduces our task from learning the full gene-to-gene matrix of size d × d (i.e., d2
168
      parameters) to learning two factorized graphs of size d × l (i.e. 2dl parameters).
169
170   The matrix W ∈ Rd×d is diagonal with strictly positive entries, such that Wii > 0 is the decay rate
171   for gene i. The decay component −W Yt represents cellular RNA levels decreasing over time due
172
      to molecular decay and concentration dilution as the cells grow and divide. Decay is biologically
      well-motivated and encourages stability in the ODE system to prevent extreme expression by creating
173
      a trapping region.
174
175   Interventions on the system are captured by shifts, where δj = ej ∈ Rd is a standard basis vector
176   corresponding to the induced over-expression of gene j. Also, s = (s1 , s2 , ..., sd )⊤ scales the
177   strength of intervention on each gene. We generally fix s to be a vector of ones. If needed, s can
178   become a model parameter updated during training. The vector encodes a 1 in the j th entry and 0
179
      in all other entries, enabling variable dynamics between cells with over-expression of different TFs.
      Importantly, δj is the only parameter manually set according to the perturbed genes to reflect each
180
      intervention, while all other model parameters (A, B, W , α, β, and s if needed) are shared across all
181
      interventions.

226   3.4   D IFFUSION - BASED REGULARIZATION OF NEURAL DYNAMICS
227
228
229
      PerturbODE can optionally augment the primary training objective by using diffused target samples
      as alternative initial states. This additional regularization encodes our prior expectation that the final
230
      cell states should be locally stable, helping to form a local contraction map that implies a locally
231
      stable fixed point, as ensured by the Contraction Mapping Theorem (Hunter and Nachtergaele, 2000).
232   Further, the stable fixed points establish the theoretical equivalence between PerturbODE and a
233   deterministic structural causal model (SCM), endowing it with its causal mechanism (Mooij et al.,
234   2013; Schölkopf et al., 2021).
235
      The augmentation involves diffusing Yi using Brownian motion with a time step ∆t to generate
236
      diffused targets Ỹi . Across a reduced time span t ≤ T , Ỹi is pushed forward through ϕit to obtain the
237                          ′                                                                   ′
238   predicted targets Ybi , and we backpropagate against the augmented loss L̃i = W2 (Ybi , Yi ) + λ|B|.
239   During training, we alternate between using control samples Y0 and diffused targets Ỹi for each
240   intervention. Information on the exact training hyperparameters can be found in Appendix 6.2.2.

245
246
247   We compare PerturbODE to the causal graph discovery methods DCDFG (Lopez et al., 2022), DCDI
248
      (Brouillard et al., 2020), NO-TEARS (Zheng et al., 2018), and NO-TEARS-LR (Fang et al., 2023)
      through extensive experiments on both simulated and large-scale perturbational scRNA-seq datasets.
249
      These methods are good comparisons since, similar to PerturbODE, they also embed GRNs as
250
      matrices either in neural networks or directly in linear models.
251
252   PerturbODE not only infers cycles but also detects both positive and negative edges, whereas the
253   DCDI and DCDFG only identify edge existence under the DAG constraint. To enable benchmarking,
254
      the ground truth GRNs used in simulated data are DAGs with positive edges, and we validate solely
      against positive edges in the reference GRNs in the scRNA-seq dataset. As ground truth negative
255
      edges are unavailable for evaluation, we classify any negative edge inferred by PerturbODE as the
256
      absence of an edge. This setup gives PerturbODE a more difficult task in predicting the correct edge
257   sign and prevents it from leveraging its full range of capabilities. Therefore, we provide further
258   downstream analysis on PerturbODE’s model parameters when trained on real datasets, showcasing
259   its strengths in uncovering network structures through its biologically faithful and interpretable
260   modeling approach.

      We extend SERGIO to simulate data with over-expression genetic perturbations. We implement
325   interventions by masking the protein production induced by TF interactions (analogously to M in
326   Equation 2) of the intervened genes and adding a scalar over-expression to the intervened gene’s
327   transcription rate. We select an experimentally validated GRN identified for yeast cells with dimension
328   400 as the input to SERGIO for simulation. The GRN is pruned to fit the required DAG constraints.
329   The output synthetic dataset from SERGIO consists of 10,100 cells generated from 100 intervention
330   schemes each targeting 5 genes and one non-intervention scheme. Each regime contains 100 cells.
331   Similarly, ten random DAGs with dimension 100 are generated with data simulated in the same
332   manner.
333   For evaluation, we threshold the weights of the output GRNs to obtain classification metrics based
334   on edge detection (details in Appendix 6.2.1). The recall, and consequently the F1 score, can be
335   strongly influenced by the number of edges returned by the model. If the model consistently predicts
336   full graphs, the recall may be artificially inflated. Therefore, we evaluate the AUPRC across models.
337   DCDI achieves a higher AUPRC than PerturbODE, and PerturbODE outperforms all other models.
338   To further address the discrepancies between graph sparsity and predictive performance, we employed
339
      random graphs to generate an empirical null for each test statistic for random graphs with the same
      edge density. We compare the precision-recall test statistics of the predicted GRN against those from
340
      10, 000 Erdős-Rényi random networks, yielding empirical p-values (for details, see 6.3). Note the
341
      Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) would strongly favor predictions of empty graphs since the
342   ground truth GRNs are extremely sparse and high dimensional, so we decided not to use SHD for
343   evaluating model performance.
344
345
      There is considerable variation in recall scores for PerturbODE especially in the simulated yeast
      dataset. This is likely due to the high sparsity in the ground truth GRN, which leads to weak signals in
346
      the simulated dataset. This results in false negatives. Further, L1 penalty is enforced on the individual
347
      matrix. As multiplication of sparse matrices is not always sparse, the number of predicted edges tend
348   to fluctuate. Denser predictions would have higher recall scores.
349
350   PerturbODE demonstrates significantly higher precision, recall, F1, and AUPRC scores compared
351
      to DCDFG, NO-TEARS, and NO-TEARS-LR, while performing comparably to DCDI in these
      metrics (Fig. 3, Fig. 2). DCDI is the state-of-the-art method that outperforms PerturbODE in lower
352
      dimensional simulated datasets (100 − 400 genes), but it lacks scalability. In fact, for dimensions
353
      greater than 400, DCDI simply fails to execute, even with the more computationally feasible spectral
354   radius acyclicity constraint. Details of the performance across all models with varying numbers of
355   modules are provided in 6.5.4. PerturbODE’s main contribution is its ability to train on real datasets
356   with thousands of genes, while maintaining competitive predicative performance.

      4.2.2    N EGATIVE AUTOREGULATION IN P ERTURB ODE INFERRED GRN S
442
443   PerturbODE’s unique ability to learn cyclic GRNs sets it apart from other causal methods that assume
444   acyclicity. Cycles, especially negative autoregulation, are known to be a prevalent network motif in
445   gene regulation. Negative autoregulation accelerates response times by enabling quicker adjustments
446   to input signals and enhances robustness by stabilizing gene expression levels against fluctuations in
447
      production rates (Alon, 2006). Approximately 40% of known E. coli TFs exhibit negative feedback
      regulation (Rosenfeld et al., 2002).
448
449   When trained on the TF Atlas, PerturbODE naturally incorporate this network motif (pattern) without
450   the need for explicit priors. The model predicts that 26.4% of modeled genes are subject to negative
451   autoregulation, which aligns with the expected prevalence of the motif according to prior studies.
452   To assess the statistical significance, we numerically compute the frequency of negative self-loops
453
      in random graphs with the same graph density, yielding a highly significant p-value of less than
      0.001. The result underscores both the statistical significance and biological realism of PerturbODE’s
454
      predictions. By inferring the GRN from interventional dynamics, PerturbODE could learn network
455
      structures that can not be captured by strictly acyclic approaches.

651   6.1     P REPROCESSING
652
653   The scRNA-seq gene expression matrix is normalized per cell by 104 and log(1 + X) transformed.
654   The total gene expression vector comprises RNA counts for N genes consisting of all the TF
655   over-expression genes j and the top k = 817 variable genes.
656   For each TF gene j, we perform a Mann-Whitney U test on differential gene expression of TF j
657   between the unperturbed control samples in X0 and over-expressed samples in Xj consisting of nj
658   cells. The returned p-value pj from the U test determines whether over-expression of the targeted TF
659   gene j is sufficiently induced in the experiments. The dataset is then filtered based on the criteria
660   D = {Xj | pj < 0.1 and nj ≥ 10, ∀j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , M }}.
661   Over-expression distributions of the genes encoding the GRNs of interest are added to the training and
662   validation dataset. In addition, when training for GRN inference only without trajectory prediction,
663   distributions of TF over-expression encoded by the marker genes of the cell types or the developmental
664   role targeted by the genes in the GRNs are included in the joint train, test, and validation dataset.
665
      We design a train-test split based on TF over-expression genes to select Dtrain,val and Dtest .For each
666   Xj ∈ Dtrain,val where nj ≥ 100, we apply a 80% to 20% training-validation split of the over-
667   expression samples. If nj < 100, we would use all the samples in Xj for Dtrain due to an insufficient
668   number of training samples.
669
      Furthermore, we apply the log1p transformation to prevent negative predictions of gene expression
670
      and mitigate length biases in expression counts (Gorin and Pachter, 2023). This transformation results
671
      in a substantial improvement in model performance.
672
673
674   6.2     M ODEL S PECIFICATIONS
675
676   PerturbODE utilizes adaptive Runge-Kutta of order 5 of Dormand-Prince-Shampine which provides
677   an exceptionally high order of accuracy and leverages its adaptive step size for efficient ODE solving.
678   The adaptive step size also detects and handles a wide range of stiff ODEs. Differentiable numerical
679   solution is computed via the adjoint method implemented in PyTorch by Chen (2021), available
680   at https://github.com/rtqichen/torchdiffeq. The Sinkhorn-based W2 distance is
681   differentiable through the GeomLoss implementation in PyTorch (Feydy et al., 2019).
682   For the baseline methods, the authors of DCDFG have implemented DCDI, DCDFG, NO-TEARS,
683   and NO-TEARS-LR in the repository Lopez (2024), available at https://github.com/
684   Genentech/dcdfg.
685
686
687   6.2.1    T HRESHOLDS
688
689   We apply a threshold ϵ to the GRN matrix W, where any edge with a weight below ϵ is set to 0 and
690   any edge whose weight exceeds ϵ is set to 1.
691   PerturbODE’s ϵ threshold is determined using the formula ϵ = c · σ, where σ represents the standard
692   deviation of the inferred GRN matrix W across all entries, and c is a positive scalar. For SERGIO
693   simulated data with 400 genes, c = 0.1, while for SERGIO simulated data with 100 genes and TF
694   Atlas, c = 0.01. c is chosen so that the PerturbODE predicts a reasonable number of edges (no more
695   than 30% of possible edges). A lower threshold is chosen for the clarity of presentation by getting
696   similar number of edges as DCDI.
697   As recommended by their authors, DCDFG determines the threshold ϵ through binary search, using
698   depth of 20 evaluations of an exact acyclicity test to find the largest possible DAG for each method.
699   NO-TEARS and NO-TEARS-LR’s ϵ are chosen to be 0.3 while DCDI’s is set to 0.5 as recommended
700   by the respective authors. For DCDI, NO-TEARS and NO-TEARS-LR different thresholdings such
701   as binary search are attempted without meaningful change to the result. Different fixed values for ϵ
      were also experimented for DCDFG without improvements.

      6.3     C OMPARISON TO E RD ŐS -R ÉNYI R ANDOM G RAPHS
742
      We generate 10, 000 random graphs with the same density as our inferred GRN to numerically
743   simulate the test statistics under Erdős-Rényi random matrices. The p-value is calculated using the
744   equation,
745
746
                                                      1 + #{τ ∗ ≥ τ }
747                                       p-value =                                                      (6)
                                                          1+Π
748
749   where τ is the test statistic, Π indicates the total number of random graphs, and τ ∗ denotes the test
750   statistics computed from each graph. The p-value quantifies how often a test statistic is observed (or
751   a more extreme one) purely by chance.
752   When evaluating SERGIO simulated data, the test statistics used is the F1 score, whereas recall score
753   is used for TF Atlas due to availability of only positive benchmark edges. To identify gene modules,
754   we use test statistics based on the count of incoming edges to the module and outgoing edges from
755   the module that are consistent with known regulatory relationships. Further, to identify the network
      motif of negative auto-regulation, test statistics is the number of negative self-loops.

      6.4     S AMPLING FROM L INEAR SCM S FOR TF ATLAS
757
758   For a learned GRN represented by W (ensured to be a DAG, or thresholded to enforce acyclicity),
759   we sample from linear structural causal models (SCMs) using the following procedure. First, for
760   each parent gene i (master regulator) in the GRN, if not over-expressed, its expression level Xi is
761   sampled from a normal distribution, Xi ∼ N (µ, σ), where µ and σ represent the mean and standard
762   deviation of gene expression levels across all genes and cells in the TF Atlas, respectively. If Xi
763   is over-expressed, it is instead sampled from Xi ∼ N (µγ , σγ ) where µγ and σγ are the mean and
764
      standard deviation of gene expression levels in over-expression genes across all over-expressed cells.
765   Downstream genes are realized in Equation 7:
766
767                 X
768     Xi =                    Wj,i Xj                                     if Xi is not over-expressed,
769             Xj ∈pa(Xi ,W)
                    X                                                                                          (7)
770
        Xi =                    Wj,i Xj + γi ,   γi ∼ N (µγ − µ, σ∆γ )      if Xi is over-expressed,
771
                Xj ∈pa(Xi ,W)
772
773   where σ∆γ is the standard deviation of the differences between over-expressed genes and mean
774   expression levels (average over genes) across all over-expressed cells. Further, pa(Xi , W) denotes
775   all the parent genes (regulators) of gene i in the GRN W.

1271
1272   It is well established that the regulatory circuit responsible for the production of E. coli follows the
1273
       network motif of multiple-output Feedforward Loop (Alon, 2006, pp. 64-68). Its circuit is shown on
       the left of Figure 20, where FlhDC and FliA regulate Z1 , Z2 , and Z3 , which are operons encoding
1274
       the proteins that make up the flagella of E. coli. (In fact, there are in total 6 operons for this process.)
1275
       Each operon consists of a group of genes, and it is regulated by a weighted sum of non-linearly
1276   activated signals from FlhDC and FliA through Hill functions.
1277
1278   The order in which the operons are activated matches the order of proteins needed to assemble the
1279
       flagella. The timing of activation is achieved by different activation thresholds in the Hill functions.
       If Z1 is activated before Z2 , which is activated before Z3 , then K2 < K3 < K4 . In other words, Z1
1280
       needs a lower concentration of FliA to be switched on. For example, Z1 would include the group of
1281
       genes encoding the proteins for MS ring (base of flagella) and Z3 would be for the filament (tail of
1282   flagella). In PerturbODE, the activation threshold is tuned by the bias term, β, to the hidden neurons.
1283
1284   This structure can be represented in a two-layer MLP shown on the right of Figure 20. Each operon
1285
       Zi is regulated by the weighted sum of signals from two modules Mi and Mi′ . The signals from
       FliA and FlhDC are first activated by Hill functions with different activation thresholds before being
1286
       transferred to modules Mi and Mi′ respectively.
1287
1288   To represent this gene regulatory circuit with an adjacency matrix W = A diag(α ◦ 1N )B, we
1289   multiply the two coefficient weight matrices of the MLP with an additional scaling to account for the
1290   rate of activation controlled by α.
