009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Training data attribution (TDA) methods aim to quantify the influence of individual
012            training data points on the model predictions, with broad applications in data-centric
013            AI, such as mislabel detection, data selection, and copyright compensation. How-
014            ever, existing methods in this field, which can be categorized as retraining-based
015            and gradient-based, have struggled with the trade-off between computational effi-
016            ciency and attribution efficacy. Retraining-based methods can accurately attribute
017            complex non-convex models but are computationally prohibitive, while gradient-
018            based methods are efficient but often fail for non-convex models. Recent research
019
               has shown that augmenting gradient-based methods with ensembles of multiple
               independently trained models can achieve significantly better attribution efficacy.
020
               However, this approach remains impractical for very large-scale applications.
021
               In this work, we discover that expensive, fully independent training is unnecessary
022
               for ensembling the gradient-based methods, and we propose two efficient ensemble
023
               strategies, D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE and L O RA E NSEMBLE, alternative to naive inde-
024            pendent ensemble. These strategies significantly reduce training time (up to 80%),
025            serving time (up to 60%), and space cost (up to 80%) while maintaining similar
026            attribution efficacy to the naive independent ensemble. Our extensive experimental
027            results demonstrate that the proposed strategies are effective across multiple TDA
028            methods on diverse datasets and models, including generative settings, significantly
029            advancing the Pareto frontier of TDA methods with better computational efficiency
030            and attribution efficacy. We conduct a theoretical analysis that provides insights
031            into the success of our empirical findings.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Training data plays an increasingly crucial role in modern artificial intelligence (AI) models (Kaplan
037   et al., 2020). Consequently, data-centric AI emerges as a vital paradigm, emphasizing the collection,
038   curation, and understanding of training data. Training Data Attribution (TDA) is a family of
039
      methods that assess the influence of each training sample on a model’s output. Numerous TDA
      methods have been developed and applied to a wide range of data-centric AI applications, such
040
      as mislabel detection (Koh & Liang, 2017), data selection (Engstrom et al., 2024), and copyright
041
      compensation (Deng & Ma, 2023), thereby gaining increasing popularity.
042
043   However, accurately attributing the training data influence on very large-scale AI applications remains
044   an open challenge. Existing TDA methods can be generally categorized into two groups: retraining-
045
      based methods and gradient-based methods (Hammoudeh & Lowd, 2024). Retraining-based methods
      involve the systematic retraining of the model with and without specific training samples to observe
046
      changes in the model’s output (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Jia et al., 2019; Feldman & Zhang, 2020;
047
      Ilyas et al., 2022; Wang & Jia, 2023). Such methods often require thousands of model retraining,
048   sometimes even growing with the size of the training data, to achieve satisfactory performance, which
049   makes them infeasible for moderately large models. Gradient-based methods, on the other hand,
050   estimate the influence by tracking the gradients of data samples (Koh & Liang, 2017; Yeh et al., 2018;
051   Pruthi et al., 2020). These methods are typically computationally more efficient as they do not require
052   the training of multiple models. But empirically they can be brittle in handling complex non-convex
053   models (Basu et al., 2020; Bae et al., 2022) and be sensitive to the randomness associated with model
      initialization and training dynamics (Søgaard et al., 2021).

054
      Recent studies have shown that gradient-based TDA methods can be significantly improved by
055   ensembling tens of models independently trained with different random seeds, leading to state-of-the-
056   art attribution efficacy on modern neural network models (Søgaard et al., 2021; Park et al., 2023).
057   Aggregating these independently trained models helps to mitigate the randomness introduced by
058   training dynamics, such as random initializations and stochastic optimization. However, despite
059   its impressive performance, scaling such a naive independent ensemble approach further for very
060   large models remains challenging due to the significant computational costs associated with training
061   multiple models.
062   In this work, we hypothesize that training models independently is unnecessary for the purpose of
063   TDA ensemble, and we propose two efficient ensemble strategies alternative to the naive independent
064   ensemble approach. Our first strategy, D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE, is motivated by dropout (Srivastava
065   et al., 2014), a common deep learning module initially designed to efficiently approximate ensemble.
066   D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE reduces the computational costs by replacing the independently trained models
067   in the naive ensemble approach with multiple dropout-masked models using the same original model.
068   Our second strategy, L O RA E NSEMBLE, is motivated by an efficient fine-tuning technique, LoRA (Hu
069
      et al., 2021). Similar to D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE, L O RA E NSEMBLE reduces the computational costs
      by replacing the independently trained models with LoRA fine-tuned models from the same original
070
      model, which is particularly suitable for generative Transformer models (Vaswani et al., 2017).
071
072   We evaluate the proposed D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE and L O RA E NSEMBLE with extensive experiments.
073   Our experiments span various datasets (MNIST (LeCun et al., 1998), CIFAR (Krizhevsky et al.,
074   2009), and MAESTRO (Hawthorne et al., 2018)), model architectures (Multi-Layer Perceptrons
075
      (MLP), Residual Neural Networks (ResNet) (He et al., 2016), and Transformers (Vaswani et al.,
      2017)), and TDA methods (TRAK (Park et al., 2023), Influence Function (Koh & Liang, 2017), and
076
      Grad-Dot/Grad-Cos (Charpiat et al., 2019)). Compared to the naive independent ensemble approach,
077
      D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE and L O RA E NSEMBLE can significantly reduce the training time, serving
078   time, and space costs by respectively up to 80%, 60%, and 80% while maintaining similar TDA
079   efficacy.
080
081
      Additionally, we present a theoretical analysis to demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed
      efficient ensemble strategies over the naive independent ensembling method. Specifically, we
082
      first introduce a general ensemble scheme: two-step ensemble estimator, which includes both
083
      D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE and L O RA E NSEMBLE. Two-step ensemble estimator utilizes a number of
084   base estimators and generates several variants on top of each of these base estimators. Based on mild
085   assumptions, our theory shows that compared to the naive ensemble estimator, two-step ensemble
086   estimator can achieve smaller squared error for estimating the optimal attribution score obtained from
087   any given TDA method.
088
      We summarize the contributions of this work as follows:
089
090   • We show that fully independent training is not a strict requirement for effective TDA with ensembles,
091     which opens up a promising direction for developing more efficient and effective TDA methods.
092   • We provide theoretical analysis that compares the TDA method with naive independent ensemble
093
        and the two novel efficient ensemble strategies: D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE and L O RA E NSEMBLE.
      • Our proposed D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE and L O RA E NSEMBLE achieve significant efficiency improve-
094
        ment across a diverse range of machine learning models, datasets, and TDA methods, advancing
095
        the state-of-the-art of TDA.

097   2 R ELATED W ORK
098   TDA methods quantify the influence of each training sample on the model predictions by assigning
099   a TDA score to the sample. These methods can be categorized into retraining-based ones and
100   gradient-based ones (Hammoudeh & Lowd, 2024), and our work focuses on the latter. Please refer to
101   Appendix A for more detailed related works.
102   Ensembling for gradient-based TDA methods. Recent studies have shown the effectiveness of
103   ensembling for improving TDA scores computed with gradient-based methods (Søgaard et al., 2021;
104   Park et al., 2023), which mitigates their typical issues associated with non-convexity and sensitivity
105   to randomness. Ensembling normally applies the TDA method to many independently trained models.
106   Either averaging the final TDA scores (Søgaard et al., 2021) or aggregating some intermediate terms
107   for score calculation (Park et al., 2023). Besides independently trained models, Park et al. (2023)
      suggest that model checkpoints at different stages of a single training can be used for ensembling as

120   3.1       P RELIMINARIES
121
122   We start by formalizing the TDA problem and the naive independent ensemble method for TDA.
123   The TDA problem. We have a training set S = {x1 , . . . , xn } where each xi ∈ Xtrain , a test
124   set T = {x1 , . . . , xm } where each xi ∈ Xtest , and a trained model output function fΘ that is
125   parameterized by Θ. Here Xtrain and Xtest are the space of the training and test data respectively. We
126   consider both supervised learning and generative modeling settings. For the supervised learning
127   setting, Xtrain and Xtest are typically identical, and each element of them corresponds to a data point
128   with both the feature and label. For the generative modeling setting, Xtrain refers to the space of
129   training data (e.g., text sequence segments for autoregressive language models) while Xtest refers to the
130   space of model generation. For the model output function fΘ , typically we will use theP model learned
131
      from the training set S (i.e., the model parameters will be chosen as Θ∗ = argminΘ xi ∈S L(xi ; fΘ )
      for some loss function L). However, this is not always the case in practice (Pruthi et al., 2020). For a
132
      training set S and any test sample x ∈ T , a TDA method τ derives the TDA scores τ (x, S; fΘ ) ∈ Rn
133
      to quantify the influence of each training data point in S on the model learned from S. More
134   specifically, the i-th element, τ (x, S; fΘ )i ∈ R, is a real-valued score indicating the importance of
135   xi on the model output on the test sample x. In the supervised classification setting, the “model
136   output on x” usually refers to the loss, (log-)likelihood, or logit for the model predicting the correct
137   class of x (Koh & Liang, 2017; Park et al., 2023); in the generative setting, it typically refers to the
138   (log-)likelihood for the model generating x (Deng & Ma, 2023).
139
      The naive independent ensemble method for TDA. To mitigate the randomness led by the
140   training dynamics of non-convex deep learning models, the naive independent ensemble method
141   first trains a set of I independent models, {Θ(i) }Ii=1 , and then derives ensembled TDA scores
142   τens (x, S; {fΘ(i) }Ii=1 ) ∈ Rn based on the set of models.
143
144   The ensembled TDA scores could be simply obtained by averaging over the TDA scores for individual
145
      models, i.e.,

      3.2   C OMPUTATIONAL COSTS OF TDA METHODS
180
181   While recent research has shown that ensembling gradient-based TDA methods can achieve decent
182   attribution efficacy with tens of independently trained models (as opposed to hundreds or even
183   thousands of model retraining in retraining-based TDA methods) (Park et al., 2023), it is still
184   impractical for very large-scale or edge-device applications due to the increased time and space costs.
185   To better quantify the time and space costs associated with TDA methods, we categorize the costs into
186   three parts: training time cost, serving time cost, and storage cost. We provide a detailed explanation
187
      for these costs as follows:
188   • Training time cost: This refers to the computational time incurred by processing and training
189     models to be used by TDA ensembles. This is the major computational challenge for ensembling
190     TDA methods.
191   • Serving time cost: This refers to the remaining computational time to deploy TDA ensembles
192     after model training. Typically, ensembling gradient-based TDA methods derives different TDA
193
        scores using multiple trained models and then aggregates them. The serving time cost will include
        all computational costs incurred during this process.
194
      • Space cost: This refers to the parameters of the trained models that are stored by TDA ensembles.
195
        These model parameters are required to run forward and backward passes on top of the models for
196     TDA score calculation. The space costs are generally proportional to the model size.

         Evaluation metric for TDA efficacy. We utilize the linear datamodeling score (LDS) (Park et al.,
342      2023) to evaluate the efficacy of the TDA methods augmented by different ensembling methods.
343      Intuitively, LDS measures the rank correlation between the TDA scores among training samples and
344      the change of model outputs by removing a subset of training samples and retraining the model from
345      scratch. A higher LDS value corresponds to a better alignment between the TDA scores and the
346      influence of the training samples on the model outputs, thus better TDA quality. We refer the reader
347      to Appendix E for the exact definition of LDS.
348      Evaluation metrics for TDA efficiency. As introduced in Section 3.2, we measure the TDA
349      efficiency in terms of the training time cost, serving time cost, and space cost. For both training
350      and serving time costs, we measure the wall-clock time on a single A40 GPU. For the space cost,
351      although ideally one would measure it by memory usage, this can be challenging because memory
352      usage is sensitive to the specific implementation and parallelization. Therefore, we measure the space
353      cost by the total parameter count instead. For D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE, we will also use the number of
354      independently trained models (I) as a measure of the training time cost and space cost, as both of the
355      two costs are proportional to I in this case. More details about the measurements are provided in
356
         Appendix F.

361      Improvement over the training time cost. To illustrate the improvement over training time cost
362      comparing D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE to the naive ensemble, we first report the results on the TRAK
363
         method across four experiment settings. As can be seen in Figure 3, across all four experiment
         settings, increasing the number of dropout-masked passes (D) results in significant LDS improvement
364
         for a fixed number of independently trained models (I). Recall that D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE does
365
         not incur any additional training time cost for a fixed I. This implies that D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE
366      can significantly reduce the training time cost for achieving the same level of attribution efficacy

531
      We present D ROPOUT E NSEMBLE and L O RA E NSEMBLE as efficient alternatives to the naive
532
      independent ensemble approach for improving gradient-based TDA methods. The proposed strategies
533
      significantly reduce training time (up to 80%), serving time (up to 60%), and space cost (up to 80%),
534   while maintaining similar attribution efficacy in comparison to the naive ensemble. Empirical results
535   from our extensive experiments show that the proposed efficient ensembles can remarkably advance
536   the Pareto frontier of TDA methods with better computational efficiency and TDA efficacy. Notably,
537   we demonstrate the proposed methods work well on a generative modeling setting. Our theoretical
538   analysis shows our ensemble method has a smaller squared error for estimating the optimal scores
539   compared to naive ensembles. In the future, we will utilize our methods in more real-world generative
      settings that were blocked by high computational costs or low effectiveness of TDA methods.

      A    M ORE RELATED WORK
649
650   Retraining-based TDA methods. Retraining-based methods compute TDA scores by systematically
651   retraining the model with and without specific training samples to quantify their influence on the
652   model’s output. These methods are computationally expensive due to the requirement of the large
653   amount of model retrainings. For example, Leave-One-Out (LOO) influence Tukey (1958) measures
654   the prediction difference between the model trained on the full training dataset and models trained
655   on subsets with only one specific sample dropped. Data Shapley (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Jia et al.,
656   2019), Beta-Shapley (Kwon & Zou, 2022), and Data Banzhaf (Wang & Jia, 2023) extends the LOO
657   idea to consider data interactions for more equitable TDA scores, but they require retraining models
658
      on all possible subsets of the training dataset. Similarly, DataModels (Ilyas et al., 2022) tries to learn
      the model predictions when the model is trained on each subset of the training dataset, which requires
659
      a nontrivial amount of model retraining. While, in practice, sampling or approximation will be used
660
      to reduce the number of model retrainings, these methods typically still require thousands of or more
661   retrainings to achieve satisfactory attribution efficacy. In summary, the high demand for retraining
662   makes these TDA methods computationally inefficient and limits their practical applicability to even
663   moderately large models.
664
      Gradient-based TDA methods. Gradient-based methods are another group of TDA methods
665
      that usually provide closed-form TDA scores using gradients. Since the seminal work of influence
666
      function by Koh & Liang (2017), gradient-based methods have become increasingly popular due
667   to their scalability. The influence function (Koh & Liang, 2017) and subsequent studies (Guo
668   et al., 2021; Barshan et al., 2020; Schioppa et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2023) obtain TDA scores
669   by approximating the effect of upweighting a training sample on the loss function. Moreover,
670   Representer Point Selection (Yeh et al., 2018) decomposes the pre-activation of a neural network
671   as a linear combination of training samples. TracIn (Pruthi et al., 2020) traces the loss changes on
672   the test points during the training process. TRAK (Park et al., 2023) uses the neural tangent kernel
673   with random projection to assess influence. These gradient-based methods significantly reduced the
674   computational cost compared to retraining-based methods. The downside is that they typically rely
675
      on the convexity assumption and Taylor approximation to calculate TDA scores. These requirements
      lead to performance degradation on non-convex neural networks and sensitivity to the randomness
676
      inherent in model initialization and training.

      E     L INEAR DATAMODELING SCORE (LDS)
776
777   Park et al. (2023) proposed the linear datamodeling score (LDS), aiming at probing the TDA method’s
778   ability to make counterfactual predictions based on the attribution score derived from the learned
779   model output function fΘ and the corresponding dataset to train fΘ . Because most TDA methods are
780   assumed to be additive2 , the TDA scores can be used to predict the model output function learned
781   from a subset of training data in a summation form. Formally, the attribution-based output predictions
782   of the model output function fΘS ′ is defined as follows:
783                                                       X
                                        gτ (x, S ′ ; S) ≜     τ (x, S; fΘ )i ,                            (5)
784
                                                            i:xi ∈S ′
785
786   where S is the training set, S ′ ⊆ S is a subset of S and fΘS ′ is the model output function with
787   ΘS ′ learned from S ′ . Intuitively, gτ (x, S ′ ; S) computes the overall attribution of the subset S ′ on
788   example x, which should be a powerful indicator of the model prediction on x (i.e., fΘS ′ (x)) if the
789
      TDA method works well. The linear datamodeling score (LDS) is defined to measure the predictive
      power of gτ (x, S ′ ; S) and can be formalized as follows:
790
791   Definition E.1 (Linear datamodeling score). Given a training set S, a model output function fΘ , and
792
      a corresponding TDA method τ . Let {S1 , . . . , Sm : Sj ⊆ S} be m randomly sampled subsets of S,
      each of size α × n for some fixed α ∈ (0, 1). The linear datamodeling score (LDS) of τ for a specific
793
      example x is defined as
794
795                      LDS(τ, x) ≜ ρ({fΘSj (x) : j ∈ [m]}, {gτ (x, Sj ; S) : j ∈ [m]}),
796
      where ρ is the Spearman rank correlation (Spearman, 1904), fΘSj is the model output function with
797
      ΘSj learned from Sj and gτ (x, Sj ; S) is defined in Eq (5).
798
799   To compute LDS for our experiment settings, we use 50 models that are independently trained on
800   random subsets with size half of the full dataset (i.e., we set m = 50 and α = 0.5 in Definition E.1).
