009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Fraudulent transactions have been on the rise, leading to significant financial
012            losses annually. In credit card fraud detection (CCFD), various predictive mod-
013            els aim to mitigate these losses by assessing transaction risk. While GNN-based
014            methods have been employed to capture spatio-temporal transaction features, they
015            often suffer from oversmoothing as graph layers increase, causing fraudulent and
016            legitimate transactions to become indistinguishable. Existing semi-supervised
017            methods that mask some labels have not fully resolved this issue. To address
018
               this, we propose the Multi-head Attention Conditional Variational Autoencoder
               (Ma-CVAE), which leverages weight distributions from imbalanced datasets and
019
               the Gumbel softmax distribution to construct more diverse reconstructed features,
020
               reducing feature homogenization. Then, we utilize Temporal Graph Attention
021            Networks (TGAT) with a Multi-Attention mechanism to model risk propagation
022            among transactions. Finally, classification probabilities are mapped to risk scores
023            via a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP). Our approach achieves state-of-the-art per-
024            formance, improving AUC scores by 1.45%, 3.05%, and 0.83% on three semi-
025            supervised datasets: FFSD, YelpChi, and Amazon, respectively.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The rise of online commerce has transformed commodity trading, with over 2.28 billion credit cards
031   issued in a single quarter Bin Sulaiman et al. (2022). Credit cards have become a widely preferred
032   payment method Wu et al. (2019); Zhu et al. (2023), significantly boosting transaction efficiency.
033
      Consequently, many predictive models have been developed to detect fraud and generate risk scores,
      functioning as binary classifiers. These models assist financial experts in prioritizing high-risk trans-
034
      actions and refining model performance.
035
036   Recently, deep learning models such as LSTM Jurgovsky et al. (2018), CNN Chen & Lai (2021);
037   Muppalaneni et al. (2019), Transformer Benchaji et al. (2021), and generative models such as GAN
038   Fiore et al. (2019); Ibrahim et al. (2020) have gained traction in CCFD tasks. However, these meth-
039
      ods often overlook relational dependencies between transactions, leading to a disjointed learning
      process for different label types. This limitation underscores the need for models that can effec-
040
      tively capture these dependencies and improve the accuracy of fraud detection.
041
042   To address this, GNN-based models have been proposed to leverage relational information, showing
043   promising results Zhang et al. (2020); Qiao et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2021a); You et al. (2024).
044   Deeper layers in GNNs have the potential to capture complex and subtle features more effectively,
045
      as they allow the model to aggregate information from a larger neighborhood, leading to a richer
      understanding of the underlying data structure. However, with increasing depth, these models often
046
      suffer from over-smoothing, where node representations become overly similar, resulting in feature
047
      homogeneity. This causes performance to peak at a certain shallow depth before degrading with
048   further layer stacking Rusch et al. (2023); Giraldo et al. (2023); Liang et al. (2024); Shen et al.
049   (2024). Even state-of-the-art (SOTA) models like TGAT Xiang et al. (2023) are not immune to
050   this issue, as the aggregation process diminishes the distinction between fraudulent and legitimate
051   features when excessive layers are used. To qualitatively assess feature diversity and differentiation,

053   original features are projected into a 2D space using PCA and clustered with DBSCAN Ester et al.

143   Problem Formalization Given a series of credit card transactions defined as R =
144   {R1 , R2 , . . . , Rn }, each transaction Rn comprises various attributes Rn = {S, L, Ta, Ty}. Here,
145   S denotes the source of the transaction initiator, L indicates the transaction location, Ta represents
146
      the target party receiving the payment, and Ty refers to the transaction type, such as online shopping
      or cash withdrawals. The subset D ⊆ R consists of target transaction events for fraud detection,
147
      aiming to predict the probability of credit card fraud in the target transaction ti+1 based on historical
148
      records t1 , . . . , ti .

151   3.1.1    F EATURE P REPROCESSING AND ATTRIBUTE E MBEDDING
152
153
      Following Xiang et al. (2023), all user transaction records are retained, including those with few
      authorized transactions, to avoid overlooking potential fraud cases. Time-series features are con-
154
      structed by sorting all transactions in the dataset R chronologically by timestamp. For each time
155
      window, features are extracted from historical transactions t1 , . . . , ti and the target transaction ti+1 ,
156   with the target’s label serving as the time window label. Inspired by Fu et al. (2016), metrics such
157   as average, total, standard deviation of transaction amounts, and the difference between current and
158   average amounts are calculated. Following Cheng et al. (2020), the number of transactions, distinct
159   targets (merchants), transaction locations, and types of transactions within the time window are also
160   counted. These computed features, combined with original transaction attributes (source S, loca-
161   tion L, target Ta, type Ty), create a comprehensive feature set for the CCFD model, denoted as
      U ∈ RN ×d , where N is the number of transaction records and d is the feature dimension.
