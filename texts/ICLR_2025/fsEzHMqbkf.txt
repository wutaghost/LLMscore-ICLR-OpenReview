009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The ability to accurately identify out-of-distribution (OOD) samples is essential not
012            only as a stand-alone machine learning task but also for maintaining the reliability
013            and safety of machine learning systems. Within this domain, post hoc density
014            estimators like the energy score are popular ways for detecting OOD samples.
015            However, most of the existing post hoc density estimation have mainly focused on
016
               marginalizing the conditional distributions over all possible classes. In this paper,
               we introduce the Conditional Density Ratio (CDR) score, a principled post hoc den-
017
               sity estimator that leverages both a class-conditional generative model in the latent
018
               space and a discriminative classifier model, allowing us to estimate the marginal
019            densities of the latent representation without marginalization. We demonstrate that
020            a key component to the success of the CDR score lies in correctly calibrating the
021            two models and propose a simple yet effective method to automatically tune the
022            temperature parameter without the need for out-of-distribution samples. We illus-
023            trate the general compatibility of the proposed method with two popular density
024            estimators, the kernel density estimator and the Mahalanobis estimator. Through
025            experiments on a wide range of OOD benchmark tasks, we verify the effectiveness
026            of the proposed method and advocate it as an easy-to-implement baseline that can
027
               achieve competitive performance in most tested scenarios.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   As the field of machine learning rapidly advances, ensuring the integrity and safety of predictive
032   models becomes crucial, particularly when deployed in dynamic and uncertain environments. A
033   fundamental aspect of ensuring model reliability is the effective detection of outlier/out-of-distribution
034   (OOD) samples, which is pivotal not only for maintaining the robustness of ML systems but also for
035   their capability to perform standalone outlier detection tasks. Traditional OOD detection techniques
036   often fall short in complex and high-dimensional data scenarios. Following recent advancements
037   in neural networks, a plethora of neural network-based OOD detection methods have been pro-
038   posed (Yang et al., 2022; Han et al., 2022). Among these methods, post-hoc detection methods
039
      like the maximum softmax probability (MSP) score (Liu et al., 2020; Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016;
      Liang et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2018) stand out as favorable in practice due to their
040
      plug-and-play simplicity with pre-trained neural networks. This is especially beneficial in the era of
041
      large-scale models where fine-tuning can be challenging without access to latest compute resources.
042
043   Recently, post-hoc density estimation based methods like the energy score (Liu et al., 2020) and the
044   Gaussian mixture based energy measurement (GEM) score (Morteza & Li, 2022) have also been
045
      proposed for OOD detection. However, most of the existing post hoc density estimation have mainly
      focused on marginalizing the conditional distributions over all possible classes. In this paper, we
046
      explore an alternative approach, and propose a simple yet principled method termed the Conditional
047
      Density Ratio (CDR) score for post hoc density estimation. At a high level, the proposed CDR score
048   leverages both a class-conditional generative model in the latent space and a discriminative classifier
049   model to estimate the densities for outlier detection without the need for marginalization with respect
050   to different classes. Importantly, the CDR score is a general framework that applied to a wide range
051   of density estimators. In this paper, we showcase the effectiveness of the proposed method with the
052   Mahalanobis estimator and the kernel density estimation. Like all post-hoc detection methods, the
053   CDR score builds upon trained models without requiring additional training, enabling the utilization
      of both models with minimal computational overhead. The additional conditional generative models

077   Problem Setup To simulate a challenging real-world scenario, we consider a setup where we
078   have access to the pretrained model weights but not the training data. Additionally, we have no
079   access to any OOD samples and only a small number of in-distribution (ID) data for validation.
080   Such a scenario is frequently encountered in real-world applications, yet it is under-explored in the
081
      context of outlier detection. We evaluate the effectiveness of the CDR score on a wide range of OOD
      detection benchmarks, demonstrating superior performance. With the temperature scaling technique,
082
      our approach is hyperparameter-free and easily adoptable for any OOD model, serving as a strong
083
      baseline in the domain of outlier detection.

085   Summary of Contributions Firstly, we advocate for a previously under-emphasized post-hoc OOD
086   detection setup where only the model weights and a small number of inlier validation samples are
087
      available. Secondly, we propose the Conditional Density Ratio (CDR) score, a general framework
      for post hoc OOD detection. Thirdly, we emphasize temperature scaling as a key component of the
088
      proposed method and devise a novel methodology to automatically tune the temperature parameters
089
      with a small number of inlier samples. Lastly, we showcase the wide applicability of the proposed
090   method and empirically demonstrate its effectiveness.

094
095   Post-Hoc Methods Post-hoc OOD detection is a widely adopted setup for detecting out-
096   of-distribution samples. A popular approach is the maximum softmax probability (MSP)
097   score (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016). Building upon this approach, temperature scaling and ad-
098   versarial perturbation have been found useful for MSP score-based OOD detection (Liang et al.,
099
      2017). Adversarial perturbation can be seen as implicitly leveraging gradient information for OOD
      detection, while another line of research focuses on explicitly using gradients as the score for de-
100
      tection (Huang et al., 2021; Behpour et al., 2024; Igoe et al., 2022). It has also been found that
101
      using cosine distance on the latent representation yields better OOD detection (Noh et al., 2023;
102   Techapanurak et al., 2019). More recently, Liu & Qin (2023) has proposed a method to construct
103   decision boundaries for OOD detection given a trained classifier. Alternatively, Shannon entropy can
104   also be used as the score for post-hoc OOD detection (Liu et al., 2023). Most of the aforementioned
105   methods use the trained classifier model for OOD detection. Instead, applying Mahalanobis distance
106   measure in the latent representation space has also achieved success for OOD detection (Lee et al.,
107   2018; Chen et al., 2023). However, none of these methods model the underlying p(x) distribution
      for OOD, which can be considered a fundamental flaw, under reasonable assumptions. In this work,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      we propose a unified approach that leverages both the discriminative classifier and the Mahalanobis
109   distance in a principled way to approximate the underlying p(x) for OOD detection. Most similar
110   to our work are (Morteza & Li, 2022; Peng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2020), which also highlight the
111   limitations of MSP and Mahalanobis distance due to their underlying assumptions. However, instead
112   of using conditional probability ratios to mitigate the issue, these methods propose to aggregate over
113   all possible classes to approximate p(x) instead. We empirically show that the performance of these
114   methods falls short. Recently, the non-parametric KNN algorithm has also been demonstrated to
115   work well for OOD detection (Sun et al., 2022). Lastly, we emphasize that, unlike many previously
116   proposed methods which contain hyperparameters that are hard to tune without access to OOD data,
117
      our proposed method only includes a temperature parameter that we demonstrate can be effectively
      tuned with only a small number of inlier samples.
118
119   Training-Time Adjustments Aside from post-hoc OOD methods, many train-time adjustments
120   have been proposed to tackle the problem of OOD detection. For instance, it was recently discovered
121   that the norm of logits can be harmful for OOD detection, and a simple fix was proposed to normalize
122   logits during the training of neural networks (Wei et al., 2022). Similarly, research has proposed
123
      normalizing the latent representation space before logits (Haas et al., 2023). Most of these methods
      require customized training of models, which can be a significant limitation when models are large
124
      and training data is not available. Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that outlier exposure during
125
      training can significantly enhance the performance of OOD detection (Hendrycks et al., 2018). In
126   light of this observation, numerous methods have been proposed to exploit different types of outlier
127   samples during training (Zheng et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Du et al., 2021; 2023; Tao et al., 2023).
128   However, incorporating OOD samples during training can lead to bias in the model. In this work, we
129   consider a setup where no labeled OOD samples are available for training nor inference.
130
      Unsupervised/Self-Supervised Methods OOD detection has also been considered in the context of
131
      unsupervised learning where no label information is available. This line of research typically overlaps
132   with the literature on anomaly detection (Salehi et al., 2021). Traditionally, such problems are
133   tackled using support vector machine models (Fumera & Roli, 2002; Li et al., 2003). These models
134   have recently been combined with neural networks (Ruff et al., 2018). Aside from discriminative
135   approaches, generative unsupervised methods like normalizing flows (Chali et al., 2023), VAE (Xiao
136   et al., 2020; An & Cho, 2015), and GANs (Di Mattia et al., 2019) have also been proposed for
137   OOD detection. However, these generative models have generally shown poor OOD detection
138   performance (Kirichenko et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021; Nalisnick et al., 2019). Instead of modeling
139   the density function explicitly, another interesting line of work considers self-supervised learning for
140
      OOD detection. For instance, contrastive learning has been demonstrated to be an effective approach
      to enhance OOD detection performance (Ming et al., 2022; Sehwag et al., 2021). Transformation-
141
      based self-supervised methods have also shown improved performance compared to explicitly
142
      modeling the density (Shenkar & Wolf, 2021; Qiu et al., 2021; Golan & El-Yaniv, 2018; Bergman
143   & Hoshen, 2020). In this work, we demonstrate that post-hoc density estimation methods can be
144   integrated into these pseudo-label-based self-supervised learning methods at test time.

245   On Marginalization Versus Conditional Density Ratio While the CDR score does not offer a
246   computational advantage over marginalization-based methods such as the energy score or GEM
247   score, we empirically demonstrate in the experimental section below that the proposed CDR score
248
      performs competitively against its marginalization-based counterparts. We attribute this to the fact
      that the CDR score leverages a combination of two distributions. Specifically, the CDR score can
249
      be interpreted as a principled approach to combining p(y|z) and p(z|y), both of which have been
250
      independently used for OOD detection, to achieve better performance when used together.

252   From a theoretical perspective, the energy score is only a valid density estimator if the partition

      potentially leading to inaccurate estimates. In sharp contrast, while the same classifier p̂ψ (y|z) is
      used, the CDR score avoids marginalization, allowing it to serve as a valid density estimator even in

      scenarios where the partition function for normalization is not constant.

333   Benchmark Methods We compare the CDR score with MSP score (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016),
334   Energy score (Liu et al., 2020), Mahalanobis distance score (Lee et al., 2018), GEM score (Morteza &
335   Li, 2022) and GradNorm score (Huang et al., 2021). We have selected these methods for benchmark
336   comparison as all of these are post-hoc OOD detection methods that do not require additional training
337   nor auxilliary data (Yang et al., 2022). To compare with the baseline methods in their original
338
      configurations, we set temperatures to 1 for all the baseline methods. In Appendix E, we evaluate
      the impact of temperatures on these benchmark methods. To further illustrate the effectiveness of
339
      the proposed method, we also benchmark against the KNN score (Sun et al., 2022), a competitive
340
      baseline for post-hoc OOD detection with a hyper-parameter k. We use the k = 50 for all our
341   experiments, a good hyper-parameter that worked well for a range of tasks in the original paper, We
342   compute KNN scores using a small validation set for fair comparison. The goal of the experiments
343   is to demonstrate that the proposed method can serve as an off-the-shelf competitive method that
344   works on a wide range of datasets. As such, we have excluded comparisons against other relevant, but
345   more complicated methods that require additional model training, finetuning and/or hyperparameter
346   tuning (Liang et al., 2017; Hsu et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2024). We compare different methods with
347   the false positive rate of OOD samples when true positive rate of ID samples is at 95% (FPR95) and
348   the area under the ROC curve (AUROC) (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016).
349   Datasets We use common OOD benchmark datasets to demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed
350   method (Yang et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2022). Specifically, we use the CIFAR (Krizhevsky et al.,
351   2009) datasets and the ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009) as the ID data. For CIFARs, we use
352   Textures (Cimpoi et al., 2014), SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), LSUN-resize (Yu et al., 2015) and
353   iSUN (Xu et al., 2015) as the OOD datasets. To further validate the effectiveness of the proposed
354   method, we also use the hard-OOD datasets comprising of ImageNet-Resize, ImageNet-Fix and
355
      LSUN-fix and CIFAR10/CIFAR100 depending on the ID data (Tack et al., 2020). For ImageNet,
      following prior literature (Sun et al., 2022), we use Places365 (Zhou et al., 2017), Textures (Cimpoi
356
      et al., 2014), iNaturalist (Van Horn et al., 2018) and SUN (Xiao et al., 2010) as OOD datasets.
357
358   Model Details For CIFARs, we train our own classifier model because we need an additional
359   validation set for our experiments. No hyperparameter tuning is done in training the classifier model.
360   Specifically, we use a 121 layer DenseNet (Huang et al., 2017) as the classifier model for training.
361
      During training, we withhold 10% of the training dataset as the validation set. The same validation
      set is used at test time to compute temperatures and the means and variance for the Mahalanobis
362
      distance. We train the models using SGD with momentum 0.9, and weight decay 5 × 10−4 for 200
363
      epochs1 . For ImageNet, we use the pretrained WideResNet50 (Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2016)
364   model provided by PyTorch. We use 25 samples per class to estimate the means and the covariance
365   matrix, and for temperature tuning.
366

      Additional Experiments To test the applicability of the proposed method on other models, we
      have conducted additional experiments using the ResNet model for CIFARs in Appendix F. We also
368
      benchmark the OOD detection performance of the proposed method on a wide range of models
369
      trained with the ImageNet dataset in Appendix D. Lastly, experiments with NLP datasets can be
370   found in Appendix C. In general, similar conclusions hold across all experiments conducted.

      6     D ISCUSSIONS
522
523
524   We have proposed the conditional density ratio (CDR) for OOD detection. We have illustrated the
525   importance of temperature scaling for our proposed method and devised a simple yet effective method
526   to automatically tune the temperatures. Of the two variants of CDR scores tested, we have found
527   CDR_KDE to be the more robust method across different models and tasks. Collectively, CDR
528   provides us with an easy-to-implement, hyper-parameter-free method for outlier detection.
529   Limitations There are several potential limitations to the proposed method. Firstly, while the CDR
530   score works well in most cases tested, the method can perform unsatisfactorily when either one of
531   the conditional density approximations performs poorly. Secondly, we have made the simplistic
532   assumption that the marginal distribution p(z) is a good approximation to the true data distribution
533   p(x). This might not be the case in reality. Lastly, despite its effectiveness, the proposed method for
534   temperature scaling may not be optimal in practice.
535   Future Directions We have just scratched the surface on density estimation-based approaches
536   for OOD detection, and there are a lot of future directions for exploration. For instance, it can be
537   beneficial to use a more expressive distribution like an exponential family (Peng et al., 2024) or even a
538   non-parametric one to approximate p(z|y). To take a step further, instead of modeling the distribution
539   in latent representation space, we can instead leverage a generative model like normalizing flow to
      directly approximate p(x) instead.

      A    D ERIVATIONS ON U SING ID D ENSITY F UNCTIONS FOR O UTLIER

           D ETECTION
759
      In this section, we formalize the intuition that, under the assumption that outlier samples have low
760
      likelihood under the inlier density function, a scoring function for outlier detection is optimal when
761
      based on the inlier density function. Derivations are adapted from (Sun et al., 2022).
762
763   Outlier detection can be formulated as a binary classification task of OOD and ID samples, where the
764   OOD class is only available at test time. Suppose the test set {(zi , gi )} is drawn i.i.d. from PZ,G ,
765
      where zi ∈ Z is the feature embedding and gi ∈ G = {0 (OOD), 1 (ID)} is the corresponding label.
766   Let P denote the marginal distribution of Z. At test time, P can be represented as:
767
                                             P = ϵPood + (1 − ϵ)Pid ,
768
769   where Pood and Pid are the underlying distribution of Z for OOD and ID data, respectively, and ϵ
770   represents the proportion of OOD samples at test time. The correspinding probability density function
771   can be represented as pood (zi ) = p(zi |gi = 0) and pid (zi ) = p(zi |gi = 1).
772   In general, OOD detection can be an ill-posed problem without assumptions on which out-distributions
773   are relevant (Zhang et al., 2021). In this work, we make a reasonable assumption that OOD samples
774   live in low density regions with respect to the inlier density function, and that OOD samples are
775   uniformly distributed outside of the high-density region of ID data:
776
                                          pood (z) = c0 1{pid (z) < c1 },
777
778   for some constants c0 and c1 .
779
      Now, given the two distributions, the Bayes Optimal Classifier is a probabilistic model that makes the
780
      most probable prediction for a new example (Devroye et al., 2013). It is described using the Bayes
781   Theorem, which provides a principled way for calculating a conditional probability, assuming the
782   underlying density function is available.
783
784                                     hBay (zi ) = 1{p(gi = 1|zi ) ≥ β}
785   From the above, the empirical estimation of the probability of z being ID data p̂(gi = 1|zi ) can be
786   expressed as:
787
788
789                                         p(zi |gi = 1) · p(gi = 1)
                           p(gi = 1|zi ) =
790                                                     p(zi )
791                                                       pid (zi ) · p(gi = 1)
792                                       =
                                            pid (zi ) · p(gi = 1) + pood (zi ) · p(gi = 0)
793
                                                    (1 − ϵ) p̂id (zi )
794                        p̂(gi = 1|zi ) =
                                            (1 − ϵ) p̂id (zi ) + ϵ p̂ood (zi )
795
796                                                       (1 − ϵ) p̂id (zi )
                                          =                                              ,
797                                         (1 − ϵ)p̂id (zi ) + ϵ ĉ0 1{p̂id (z) < ĉ1 }
798
      where p̂id (z) and p̂ood (z) denote estimates of the underlying densities pid (z) and pood (z). Observe
799   that, under our assumptions, the OOD detection problem boils down to accurately deriving the
800   empirical estimation of p̂id (z).
