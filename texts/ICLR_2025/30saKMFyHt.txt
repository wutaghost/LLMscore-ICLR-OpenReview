011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            One-Shot Federated Learning (OSFL), a special decentralized machine learning
014            paradigm, has recently gained significant attention. OSFL requires only a sin-
015
               gle round of client data or model upload, which reduces communication costs
               and mitigates privacy threats compared to traditional FL. Despite these promising
016
               prospects, existing methods face challenges due to client data heterogeneity and
017
               limited data quantity when applied to real-world OSFL systems. Recently, Latent
018            Diffusion Models (LDM) have shown remarkable advancements in synthesizing
019            high-quality images through pretraining on large-scale datasets, thereby present-
020            ing a potential solution to overcome these issues. However, directly applying
021            pretrained LDM to heterogeneous OSFL results in significant distribution shifts
022            in synthetic data, leading to performance degradation in classification models
023            trained on such data. This issue is particularly pronounced in rare domains, such
024            as medical imaging, which are underrepresented in LDM’s pretraining data. To
025            address this challenge, we propose Federated Bi-Level Personalization (FedBiP),
026
               which personalizes the pretrained LDM at both instance-level and concept-level.
               Hereby, FedBiP synthesizes images following the client’s local data distribution
027
               without compromising the privacy regulations. FedBiP is also the first approach
028
               to simultaneously address feature space heterogeneity and client data scarcity in
029            OSFL. Our method is validated through extensive experiments on three OSFL
030            benchmarks with feature space heterogeneity, as well as on challenging medical
031            and satellite image datasets with label heterogeneity. The results demonstrate the
032            effectiveness of FedBiP, which substantially outperforms other OSFL methods.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Federated Learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) is a decentralized machine learning paradigm,
038   in which multiple clients collaboratively train neural networks without centralizing their local data.
039
      However, traditional FL frameworks require frequent communication between a server and clients to
      transmit model weights, which would lead to significant communication overheads (Kairouz et al.,
040
      2021). Additionally, such frequent communication increases system susceptibility to privacy threats,
041
      as transmitted data can be intercepted by attackers who may then execute membership inference
042   attacks (Lyu et al., 2020). In contrast, a special variant of FL, One-Shot Federated Learning (OSFL)
043   (Guha et al., 2019), serves as a promising solution. OSFL requires only single-round server-client
044   communication, thereby enhancing communication efficiency and significantly reducing the risk of
045   interception by malicious attackers. Therefore, we focus on OSFL given its promising properties.
046
      Despite these promising prospects, existing methods for OSFL face significant challenges when ap-
047
      plied to real-world scenarios. Previous works (Guha et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020) require additional
048   public datasets, presenting challenges in privacy-critical domains such as medical data (Liu et al.,
049   2021), where acquiring data that conforms to client-specific distributions is often impractical. Al-
050   ternatively, they can involve the transmission of entire model weights (Zhang et al., 2022) or local
051   training data (Zhou et al., 2020), which are inefficient and increase the risk of privacy leakage. More-
052   over, these approaches overlook the issue of feature space heterogeneity, wherein the data features
053   across different clients exhibit non-identically distributed properties. This presents an important and
      prevalent challenge as emphasized in (Li et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023). Another vital challenge in

097   2     R ELATED W ORKS
098
099   2.1    O NE -S HOT F EDERATED L EARNING
100
      A variety of efforts have been made to address One-Shot Federated Learning (OSFL), primarily
101
      from two complementary perspectives: one focuses on model aggregation through techniques such
102   as model prediction averaging (Guha et al., 2019), majority voting (Li et al., 2020), conformal pre-
103   diction method (Humbert et al., 2023), loss surface adaptation (Su et al., 2023), or Bayesian methods
104   (Yurochkin et al., 2019; Chen & Chao, 2020; Hasan et al., 2024). These approaches may not fully
105   exploit the underlying knowledge across different client data distributions. Another aims to transmit
106   training data instead of model weights: data distribution (Kasturi et al., 2020; Beitollahi et al., 2024;
107   Shin et al., 2020), Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2020; Zhang et al.,
      2022; Kasturi & Hota, 2023; Kang et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024), or distilled dataset (Zhou et al.,


                                                              2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2020; Song et al., 2023) are optimized and transmitted to the central server for subsequent model
109   training. Given the success of diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), (Zhang et al., 2023; Yang
110   et al., 2024b) suggests transmitting image captions to reproduce training data at the server, while
111   (Yang et al., 2024a) focuses on one-shot semi-supervised FL. However, these approaches are either
112   inefficient or pose risks of client information leakage. In contrast, FedBiP functions as an OSFL
113   algorithm, offering enhanced efficiency and robust privacy-preserving capabilities.
114
115   2.2   D IFFUSION M ODELS F OR I MAGE S YNTHESIS
116
117   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020), especially Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) (Rombach et al.,
118   2022), have attracted significant attention due to their capability to generate high-resolution natural
119   images. They have demonstrated effectiveness in various applications, including image stylization
120
      (Guo et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2021; Kawar et al., 2023) and training data generation (Yuan et al.,
      2023; Sarıyıldız et al., 2023; Azizi et al., 2023). We refer readers to (Croitoru et al., 2023; Yang
121
      et al., 2023b) for a comprehensive overview of recent progress on diffusion models. Pretrained
122
      LDM has been adopted to address client data scarcity in OSFL (Zhang et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
123   2024b). However, these methods often overlook the feature distribution shift between the LDM
124   pretraining dataset and the clients’ local data. This challenge is particularly pronounced in complex
125   domains such as medical and satellite imaging. To address this issue, we propose FedBiP, which
126   personalizes the pretrained LDM to synthesize data that is aligned with the clients’ data distributions.

      3.1   H ETEROGENEOUS O NE - SHOT F EDERATED L EARNING
131
132   In this section, we introduce our problem setting, i.e., heterogeneous One-Shot Federated Learning
133   (OSFL). Following (Zhang et al., 2023), we focus on image classification tasks with the goal of
134   optimizing a C-way classification model ϕ utilizing the client local data, where C ∈ N denotes the
135   number of categories. We assume there are K ∈ N clients joining the collaborative training. Each
                                                                                                  k
136   client k owns its private dataset Dk containing N k ∈ N (image, label) pairs: {xki , yik }N
                                                                                                i=1 . Only
137   one-shot data upload from the clients to the central server is allowed.
138
      As described in (Kairouz et al., 2021), OSFL with data heterogeneity is characterized by distribution
139
      shifts in local datasets: PXk1Y ̸= PXk2Y with k1 ̸= k2 , where PXk Y defines the joint distribution of input
140
      space X and label space Y on Dk . Data heterogeneity can be decomposed into two types: (1) label
141
      space heterogeneity, where PY varies across clients, while PX |Y remains the same, and (2) feature
142
      space heterogeneity, where PX or PX |Y varies across clients, while PY|X or PY remains the same.

186   4.1     M OTIVATIONAL C ASE S TUDY
187
188
      To substantiate the necessity of the proposed method, we present an empirical analysis to address the
      following research question: Can pretrained Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) generate images that
189
      are infrequently represented in the pretraining dataset using solely textual conditioning? Specifi-
190
      cally, we adopt two datasets, namely DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019) and DermaMNIST (Yang et al.,
191   2023a), which contain images indicating different styles and images from challenging medical do-
192   mains, respectively. We prompt LDM with ”A quickdraw style of an airplane.” to generate airplane
193   images in quickdraw style for DomainNet dataset, and ”A dermatoscopic image of a dermatofi-
194   broma, a type of pigmented skin lesions.” for DermaMNIST. We synthesize 100 images for each
195   setting and adopt a pretrained ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) to acquire the feature embeddings of real
196   and synthetic images. Finally, we visualize them using UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018).
197   As shown in Figure 1, we observe markedly different visual characteristics between synthetic and
198   real images. Specifically, for DomainNet, there exist significant discrepancies between the ”quick-
199   draw” concept demonstration in the original dataset and the pretrained LDM. For DermaMNIST, the
200   pretrained LDM is only able to perceive the general concepts of ”dermatoscopic” and ”skin lesion”,
201   failing to capture category-specific information. This further highlights the difficulties in reproduc-
202   ing medical domain data via LDM. Additionally, there is a substantial gap in the extracted feature
203   embeddings between real and synthetic images. Most importantly, despite the high visual quality of
204   the synthetic images, they may not contribute to the final performance of the classification model.
205
      As demonstrated by our experimental results (Table 5.3), directly applying such prompts to generate
      images for server-side training sometimes yields worse results than baseline methods. Therefore,
206
      it is essential to design a more sophisticated method to effectively personalize the pretrained LDM
207
      to the specific domains of client local datasets. These observations motivate our proposed method
208   FedBiP, which mitigates the distribution shifts between pretrained LDM and the client local data.
209   We introduce FedBiP in the following.

      4.2.1     I NSTANCE - LEVEL P ERSONALIZATION
240
241   While the traditional Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) employs a Gaussian distribution to initialize
242   the latent vector z(T ) ∼ N (0, I), we directly compute z(T ) from the local training set Dk of each
243   client. Specifically, we leverage the VAE encoder E from pretrained LDM to obtain zi (T ) for each
244   specific real sample xi . We first extract the low-dimensional latent representation by feeding the
245   training image into VAE encoder: zi (0) ← E(xi ). We implement additional measures to enhance
246   client privacy. First, we interpolate zi (0) with another latent representation, zi′ (0), from the same
247
      class, thereby reducing the risk of exact sample reconstruction. Second, we add T -steps of random
      noise to obtain zi (T ), which corresponds to the maximum noise intensity in LDM. A comprehensive
248
      privacy analysis is provided in Section 5.5 and 5.6. The overall process can be formalized as
249
250                         zi (T ) ← δ(T, γzi (0) + (1 − γ)zi′ (0)), s.t., i ̸= i′ , yi = yi′ ,             (3)
251
252   where γ ∼ N (0.5, 0.12 ) and clipped to [0, 1]. After the computation, we store zi (T ) and its corre-
253   sponding ground truth label yi for all training images in the k th client as the instance-level personal-
254   ization. We emphasize that this level of personalization does not require any additional optimization,
255   making the process computationally efficient.

      4.2.2     C ONCEPT- LEVEL P ERSONALIZATION
257
258   Solely applying instance-level personalization results in reduced diversity in image generation. To
259   mitigate this limitation, we enhance personalization by incorporating domain and category concepts
260   into the LDM generation process. Specifically, ”domain” denotes the feature distribution within a
261   client’s local dataset, such as an image style in the DomainNet dataset. To avoid the costly finetuning
262   of the LDM weights θ, we finetune only the textual guidance. Specifically, we randomly initialize
263   the domain concept vector S ∈ Rns ×dw and category concept vector V ∈ RC×nv ×dw , where ns
264   and nv are the number of tokens for domain concept and category concept, respectively, and dw is
265   the token embedding dimension of the textual conditioning model τθ . Subsequently, specific tokens
266
      in the textual template P are substituted with the concept vectors S and Vy corresponding to a
      specific category y. For instance, this could result in textual prompts like ”A [S] style of a [Vy ]” for
267
      DomainNet dataset. Following this, τθ encodes these modified prompts, transforming the textual
268
      embeddings into intermediate representation for the denoising UNet ϵθ .

307
308   We conduct extensive empirical analyses to investigate the proposed method. Firstly, we compare
309   FedBiP with other baseline methods on three One-Shot Federated Learning (OSFL) benchmarks
310   with feature space heterogeneity. Next, we evaluate FedBiP using a medical dataset and a satellite
311   image dataset adapted for OSFL setting with label space heterogeneity, illustrating its effectiveness
312   under challenging real-world scenarios. Finally, we perform an ablation study on FedBiP and

      further analyze its promising privacy-preserving capability.

315   5.1       B ENCHMARK E XPERIMENTS
316
      Datasets Description: We adapt three common image classification benchmarks with feature dis-
317
      tribution shift for our OSFL setting: (1) DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019), which contains six domains:
318   Clipart (C), Infograph (I), Painting (P), Quickdraw (Q), Real (R), and Sketch (S). We select 10 cate-
319   gories following (Zhang et al., 2023). (2) PACS (Li et al., 2017), which includes images that belong
320   to 7 classes from four domains: Art (A), Cartoon (C), Photo (P), and Sketch (S). (3) OfficeHome
321   (Venkateswara et al., 2017) comprises images of daily objects from four domains: Art (A), Clipart
322   (C), Product (P), and Real (R). Each client is assigned a specific domain. To simulate local data
323   scarcity described in previous sections, we adopt 16-shot per class (8-shot for OfficeHome) for each
      client, following previous works (Li et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023).

324
      Baseline Methods: We compare FedBiP with several baseline methods, including FedAvg and
325   Central, i.e., aggregating the training data from all clients. We note that Central is an oracle method
326   as it infringes on privacy requirements, while FedAvg requires multi-round communication and is
327   not applicable to OSFL. Besides, we validate concurrent generation-based methods for OSFL: (1)
328   FedD3 (Song et al., 2023), where distilled instances from the clients are uploaded. (2) DENSE
329   (Zhang et al., 2022), where client local models are uploaded and distilled into one model using
330   synthetic images. (3) FedDEO (Yang et al., 2024b), where the optimized category descriptions are
331   uploaded and guide pretrained diffusion models. (4) FGL (Zhang et al., 2023), where captions of
332   client local images, extracted by BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023), are uploaded and guide pretrained LDM.
333   Implementation Details: We adopt the HuggingFace open-sourced ”CompVis/stable-diffusion-v1-
334   4” as the pretrained Latent Diffusion Model, and use ResNet-18 pretrained on ImageNet (Deng et al.,
335   2009) as the initialization for the classification model. We investigate three variants of FedBiP,
336   namely ”S”, ”M”, and ”L”, which corresponds to generating 2×, 5×, 10× the number of images
337   in the original client local dataset, respectively. Note that synthesizing more images does not affect
338   the client’s local optimization costs. We optimize the concept vectors for 50 epochs at each client.
339
      For FGL, 3500 samples per class per domain are generated. For FedDEO, the total number of
      synthetic images is identical to FedBiP-L for a fair comparison. Further details about training
340
      hyperparameters are provided in the Appendix.

342   Results and Analyses: We report the validation results in Table 1, where we observe FedBiP-L
343   outperforms all baseline methods in average performance, indicating an average performance im-
344   provement of up to 5.96%. Notably, FedBiP-S achieves comparable performance to FGL by
345
      generating only 16 images for DomainNet per class and domain, while FGL requires 3500 images.
      This further highlights the efficiency of our proposed method. Additionally, FedBiP excels in
346
      challenging domains, such as Quickdraw (Q) of DomainNet and Sketch (S) of PACS, showcasing
347
      its effectiveness in generating images that are rare in the Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) pretraining
348   dataset. However, FedBiP slightly underperforms in certain domains, e.g., Real (R) in Domain-
349   Net. We attribute this to the overlap between these domains and the LDM pretraining dataset, where
350   adapting LDM with the client local datasets reduces its generation diversity. Nevertheless, FedBiP
351   narrows the gap between the generation-based methods and oracle Central method.

531
532
      In this work, we propose the first framework to address feature space heterogeneity in One-Shot Fed-
533
      erated Learning (OSFL) using generative foundation models, specifically Latent Diffusion Model
534   (LDM). The proposed method, named FedBiP, personalizes the pretrained LDM at both instance-
535   level and concept-level. This design enables LDM to synthesize images that adhere to the local data
536   distribution of each client, exhibiting significant deviations compared to its pretraining dataset. The
537   experimental results indicate its effectiveness under OSFL systems with both feature and label space
538   heterogeneity, surpassing the baseline and multiple concurrent methods. Additional experiments
539   with medical or satellite images demonstrate its maturity for challenging real-world applications.
      Moreover, additional analysis highlights its promising scalability and privacy-preserving capability.

796
      We use 1 NVIDIA RTX A5000 with 24GB RAM to run the experiments. We use PyTorch (Paszke
797
      et al., 2019) to implement our algorithm. For the baseline FedAvg, the total communication round
798   is set to 50. For FGL (Zhang et al., 2023), we generate 3500 images per class per domain. For
799   the optimization of the classification model, we use SGD with momentum as the optimizer, where
800   the learning rate is set to 0.01 and the momentum is 0.9. The optimization epoch is set to 50. The
801   training image resolution is set to 512 × 512 for all datasets.
802
      For FedD3 (Song et al., 2023), we adopt Kernel Inducing Points (KIP) to distill the original dataset
803
      into 1 image per class per domain and transmit them to the central server. For DENSE (Zhang
804   et al., 2022), we first finetune the pretrained ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) at each client and then
805   optimize a Generator to conduct model distillation at central server. The hyperparameters used in
806   these methods are following their original papers. For FedMLA, we use Adam optimizer to optimize
807   the concept vectors. The learning rate is set to 0.1 and beta is set to (0.9, 0.999). The total training
808   epochs is set to 30. We adopt the Pseudo Numerical Diffusion Model (PNDM) (Liu et al., 2022) in
809   the Latent Diffusion Model. The perturbation intensity for domain concept vector σµ is set to 0.1
      for all dataset. More dataset specific hyperparameters are provided in Table 5.
