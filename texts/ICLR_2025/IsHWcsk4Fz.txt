009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               In federated learning, there are two critical challenges: 1) the data on distributed
012
               learners is heterogeneous; and 2) communication resources within the network are
013
               limited. In this work, we propose a framework, Federated Adaptive Dissimilarity
014            Measure (FedADM), which can be regarded as an adaptively enhanced version
015            of the Federated Proximal (FedProx) algorithm. This adaptiveness is primarily
016            manifested in two aspects: (i) how it adaptively adjusts the proximity between the
017            local models on different learners and the global model; and (ii) how it adaptively
018            aggregates local model parameters. Building on the FedProx model, FedADM
019            incorporates the concept of the Lagrangian multiplier to control the proximal co-
020            efﬁcients of different learners, using “parameter dissimilarity” to address data
021            heterogeneity. It explicitly captures the essence of using “loss dissimilarity” to
022
               adaptively adjust the aggregation frequency on distributed learners, thereby reduc-
               ing communication overhead. Theoretically, we provide the performance upper
023
               bounds and convergence analysis of our proposed FedADM. Experiment results
024
               demonstrate that FedADM allows for higher accuracy and lower communication
025            overhead compared to the baselines across a suite of realistic datasets.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Data privacy and security are of paramount importance, especially in highly sensitive sectors such as
032   healthcare, ﬁnance, and smart manufacturing. Typically, data in different institutions or departments
033   is stored independently, making it challenging to effectively integrate and utilize this dispersed data
034   architecture. Federated learning technology (Kairouz et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
035   2024b) offers a potent solution to this “data island” issue, enabling the collaborative use of multi-
036   source data across institutions for distributed model training. This approach allows for the resolution
037
      without the direct exchange of any sensitive information, thus ensuring the security of the data (Yang
      et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023).
038
039   Federated learning represents a promising method of distributed machine learning training, partic-
040   ularly showing distinct advantages over other traditional distributed optimization methods in het-
041   erogeneous data settings (Yang et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021). The Federated Proximal (FedProx)
042   algorithm is a classic federated learning approach tailored for heterogeneous data distributions (Li
043
      et al., 2020). It incorporates a proximal term in local model training, which helps the local mod-
      els converge towards the global aggregated model, thereby accelerating learning in heterogeneous
044
      data and promoting model convergence. However, it lacks in-depth exploration and utilization of
045
      heterogeneous data and does not adequately consider resource consumption. Although there has
046   been signiﬁcant work in federated learning optimizing participant selection (Cho et al., 2022; Tang
047   et al., 2022), local update frequency (Singhal et al., 2021; Ruan & Joe-Wong, 2022), and aggregation
048   count (Pillutla et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b; Lee et al., 2023) to
049   reduce overheads, achieving global optimum remains challenging. A pivotal question of federated
050   learning regarding the data heterogeneity and limited communication resources that emerge in our
051   research is:
052
053   Question 1 How can we deeply excavate and precisely harness the heterogeneity inherent in data
      to guide the local updates and global aggregation processes in federated learning?

                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In this work, we introduce the Federated Adaptive Dissimilarity Measure (FedADM), a federated
055   learning approach that addresses this question. The effective utilization of heterogeneous informa-
056   tion directly impacts the performance and speed of model learning. Heterogeneity in distributed
057   data can be measured through network parameters, loss functions, and gradient information. The
058   FedADM approach ﬁrstly utilizes a variety of heterogeneity metrics, including parameter dissim-
059   ilarity and loss dissimilarity, to improve the training efﬁciency and accuracy of the model. Our
060   contributions to this work are as follows:
061
062          • Parameter Dissimilarity for Local Network: We construct a proximal term in the local loss
063            functions by utilizing the parameters dissimilarity between local and global aggregation.
064            Note that this approach dynamic controls the inﬂuence of parameter dissimilarity on the
065            local loss function by adjusting the proximal coefﬁcient, thus promoting the convergence
066            of the local model.
067
068          • Loss Dissimilarity for Surrogate Function: The framework utilizes local loss dissimilarity
069            to construct a surrogate for the optimization objective function. This effectively captures
070            the variations in local models and theoretically achieves a suboptimal solution for the num-
071
               ber of local updates. This strategy aids in optimizing the frequency of local updates and
               global aggregations.
072
073
074          • Experiment Results: Extensive experiments verify the effectiveness and convergence of the
075
               FedADM under limited communication resources using three real datasets, four cases with
               heterogeneous data, various neural network models, and different system conﬁgurations.

      plify optimization problems, we use surrogate functions to prove the models’ convergence.

      A federated learning model is considered, operating within a constrained resource budget R. This
111   model consists of a central controller and n local learners. Each learner maintains its own network
112   parameters and performs local updates during the training process. After every τ local update, learn-
113   ers transmit their model updates to the central controller. Upon receiving these updates, the central
114   controller performs a global aggregation and redistributes the results back to the local learners. De-
115   ﬁne T as the total number of iterations conducted by each learner, with each iteration denoted by t,
116   where t ∈ {1, 2, . . . , T }. Let K represent the number of aggregations, calculated as T /τ , with each
117   aggregation indexed by k, where k ∈ {1, 2, . . . , K}. Both local updates and global aggregations

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
412
413
      Datasets and Models: We conduct experiments using three popular datasets: MNIST (LeCun et al.,
414   1998), Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), and CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009). For the MNIST
415   and Fashion-MNIST datasets, a simple Support Vector Machine (SVM) Cortes (1995) serves as the
416   backbone for training and testing, while for CIFAR-10, we use a Convolutional Neural Network
417   (CNN) (He et al., 2016). For detailed information about the datasets, refer to Appendix D.1.
418
      Baselines and Cases: Our method is compared with existing similar approaches, including FedAvg
419
      (McMahan et al., 2017), FedProx (Li et al., 2020), and centralized learning. The experiments con-
420   sider four distinct cases with varying data distributions across learners. In Case 1, each learner
421   performs random sampling of uniformly informative data. In Case 2, each learner contains different
422   types of labels, indicating heterogeneous data across learners. In Case 3, each learner possesses the
423   complete dataset. In Case 4, the ﬁrst half of the learners contain only data samples from Case 1,
424   while the second half contains only data samples from Case 2.
425
      Implementation Details: In the simulation, we conﬁgure the number of local learners to vary be-
426   tween 5 and 100, with learners uniformly sampled from the dataset. We assume that the resource
427   consumed is time, with a total time resource of 60 seconds unless otherwise speciﬁed. For more
428   information regarding training and control parameters, please refer to Appendix D.1. Experiments
429   are implemented using TensorFlow. Locally, experiments are conducted on CPU machines equipped
430   with a 2.3 GHz Intel Core i7 processor and an NVIDIA 3070Ti GPU. For more resource-intensive
431   tasks, we use a remote server equipped with a 16 vCPU Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold 6430 processor, 120
      GB of memory, and two RTX 4090 GPUs along with six RTX 2080Ti GPUs.

530   6   C ONCLUSION
531
532   This work proposes FedADM, which utilizes parameter dissimilarity and loss dissimilarity to ad-
533   dress data heterogeneity and reduce communication overhead in federated learning. Parameter dis-
534   similarity is embedded into the local objective functions to guide local models towards approxi-
535
      mating the global model, while loss dissimilarity is integrated into the surrogate function to ﬁnely
      control local updates and aggregation. We derive the convergence bounds for FedADM by con-
536
      sidering the Lipschitz continuity and smoothness properties. Our experiments achieved superior
537
      performance compared to the baselines across three datasets, four cases, and two neural network
538   models, demonstrating the convergence behavior of FedADM in resource-limited heterogeneous
539   networks.
