011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013              This paper introduces Defense through Perturbing Privacy Neurons (DPPN), a
014              novel approach to protect text embeddings against inversion attacks. Unlike ex-
015              isting methods that add noise to all embedding dimensions for general protection,
016              DPPN identifies and perturbs only a small portion of privacy-sensitive neurons.
017              We present a differentiable neuron mask learning framework to detect these neu-
018
                 rons and a neuron-suppressing perturbation function for targeted noise injection.
                 Experiments across six datasets show DPPN achieves superior privacy-utility trade-
019
                 offs. Compared to baseline methods, DPPN reduces more privacy leakage by
020
                 5-78% while improving downstream task performance by 14-40%. Tests on real-
021              world sensitive datasets demonstrate DPPN’s effectiveness in mitigating sensitive
022              information leakage to 17%, while baseline methods reduce it only to 43%.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Text embeddings are general representations of textual data, allowing users to conduct various
029
      downstream learning without having to access or reveal the raw text data. Advancements in pre-
      trained models like Sentence-T5 Ni et al. (2022a) and Sentence-BERT Reimers & Gurevych (2019)
030
      allow users to leverage these models for generating high-quality embeddings. These embeddings
031
      power a wide range of NLP applications. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems Lewis et al.
032   (2020) are a prime example that has fueled the adoption of online embedding database services like
033   Chroma1 and Faiss Johnson et al. (2019). In these databases, only the text embeddings are shared with
034   third-party services, not the actual text. Since only encoded data (i.e., embeddings) is shared, there
035   is a common misconception that privacy is well-preserved through this mechanism. Nevertheless,
036   research has shown that attackers can infer sensitive information by conducting embedding inversion
037   attacks with a reasonable success rate Li et al. (2023); Pan et al. (2020); Song & Raghunathan (2020).
038   Recent work Vec2text Morris et al. (2023) further reveals that an adversary can recover 92% of a
039   32-token text input given embeddings from a T5-based pre-trained transformer. Such vulnerabilities
040
      are particularly concerning in scenarios where sensitive information like medical records or financial
      data is embedded.
041
042   To defend against embedding inversion attacks, perturbing text embeddings by injecting random
043   noises is a widely used approach. For instance, previous works Pan et al. (2020); Morris et al. (2023)
044   often add Laplace noises in text embeddings to counteract embedding inversion attacks. Existing
045   noisy embedding methods often add random noise to all embedding dimensions for general protection.
046
      The drawbacks of this approach are twofold. First, although adding noise to all dimensions can
      protect sensitive information, it can also alter non-sensitive information embedded within the text,
047
      thereby degrading the performance of downstream tasks. Second, adding random noise uniformly
048
      across all dimensions might not be ideal, as some parts of the embeddings might require larger
049   perturbations while others do not. Therefore, this work aims to address a key research question:
050
051    Research Question: Is it possible to manipulate as few embedding dimensions as possible to protect
052
      sensitive information while minimizing perturbation to the non-sensitive parts?

070   In essence, we aim to identify a set of so-called privacy neurons (i.e., embedding dimensions) within
071   the representations that correlate with the private information to be protected. Take Figure 1 as
072   an example: (a) demonstrates an example of privacy leakage caused by an embedding inversion
073   attack. (b) represents previous models that add random noise to all dimensions. If the noise is large

075   of seriously altering the original meaning of the data. (c) illustrates our solution that identifies the
076
      privacy neurons associated with the term depression and selectively perturbs these dimensions to
      obfuscate the attack model. The ultimate goal is to not only protect the sensitive information but also
077
      ensure the non-sensitive information is still correctly encoded in the embeddings.
078
079   To achieve the above goal, this work focuses on addressing two follow-up research questions. First,
080   how to identify a subset of neurons associated with a given sensitive concept; and second, after
081   identifying such neurons, how to manipulate their values to defend the attack. In this work, we present
082
      the Defense through Perturbing Privacy Neurons (DPPN) framework for a better privacy-preserving
      text embedding. Specifically, we first leverage a differentiable neuron mask learning framework
083
      to identify the top-k privacy neurons associated with a target token t to be protected. Given the
084
      detected neurons, we introduce a neuron-suppressing perturbation function to obfuscate the privacy
085   information through directional noise injection. To fully evaluate the effectiveness of DPPN, we
086   conduct comprehensive experiments and summarize the findings as follows:
087
088   • Better privacy-utility tradeoffs. We evaluated DPPN on six datasets across various perturbation
089     levels. Compared to baseline methods, DPPN reduced relative privacy leakage by 5% to 78% while
090     improving downstream utility by 14% to 40%.
091   • DPPN achieves comparable performance to white-box defense. Our black-box neuron detection
092     method performs comparably to a white-box method. On the STS12 dataset, DPPN shows only
093     a 3–6% absolute difference in privacy leakage metrics and less than a 5% relative difference in
094     downstream task performance.
095   • Effectiveness against real-world privacy threats. We test DPPN on two real-world privacy-
096     sensitive datasets: PII-masking 300K and MIMIC-III clinical notes. The results show that DPPN
097     can significantly mitigate the leakage of sensitive information (e.g., sex, disease name) to 17%
098     while baseline methods reduce it only to 43%.

101
102   2.1      ATTACK S CENARIO
103
104   Text embeddings, which are dense vector representations of textual data, pose significant privacy
105   risks due to their ability to inadvertently encode sensitive information Li et al. (2023); Morris et al.
106   (2023). One primary concern is that these embeddings can reveal personal or confidential details
107   present in the input text. In this work, we focus on a specific embedding inversion attack where
      the adversary aims to reconstruct the input text from the corresponding text embedding. Formally,


                                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      given a sequence of text tokens x and the text embedding model Φ : x → Rd , where d denotes the
109   embedding dimension, the attacker seeks to find a function f to approximate the inversion function
110   of Φ as: f (Φ(x)) ≈ Φ−1 (Φ(x)) = x. These inversion attacks can be classified into two categories
111   based on their target: (i) token-level inversion Pan et al. (2020); Song & Raghunathan (2020), which
112   focuses on retrieving individual tokens from the original text, and (ii) sentence-level inversion Li
113   et al. (2023); Morris et al. (2023), which attempts to reconstruct the entire ordered sequence of
114   text. Regardless of the attack model employed, our study prioritizes understanding whether private
115   information (e.g., names, diseases) within the original text is revealed.
116
117   2.2   P RIVACY-P RESERVING T EXT E MBEDDING
118
119   Privacy Definition. Preserving privacy is crucial with the rise of powerful pretrained language
120   models. The first step is defining the scope of what constitutes private information. While the concept
121
      of privacy can be broad and context-dependent Brown et al. (2022), for practical purposes, a narrower
      definition is often adopted Sousa & Kern (2023). This definition focuses on personal identifiable
122
      information (PII) as privacy concerns, including names, ID numbers, phone numbers, and other
123   similar entities. This definition can extend to named entities in text, such as locations or organizations,
124   depending on the specific privacy requirements of the task.
125
126   Goals. To clarify the scope of this work, our privacy-preserving text embedding aims to achieve the
      following two goals:
127
128
      • Goal 1 (Defending against sensitive token inference attack): For the threat model A and text
129
        embedding Φ(x), where x is a sentence that contains sensitive information and Φ is the embedding
130     model. The data owner defines a set of sensitive tokens T = {t1 , t2 , . . . , t|T | } that require to be
131     protected. The objective is to generate an obfuscated embedding Φ′ (x) that prevents the threat
132     model A from accurately reconstructing or identifying the tokens in T .
133
      • Goal 2 (Maintaining downstream utility): The secondary objective is to ensure that the protective
134     measures, while securing the embeddings from inversion attacks, do not compromise the utility of
135     the embeddings in downstream tasks.
136
137   Defender’s Knowledge. Our work primarily addresses a black-box setting, where the defender lacks
138   prior knowledge of the specific attack model employed by adversaries. We focus on developing
139   a robust noise injection mechanism capable of defending against a broad spectrum of inversion
140   attacks without requiring insight into adversarial strategies. However, to comprehensively evaluate
141   our approach, we also explore a white-box scenario in Section 5.1.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
146
147   We present DPPN, a novel defense framework against embedding inversion attacks. The core concept
148   of our approach is twofold. Identify privacy neurons: We employ a differentiable neuron masking
149   learning method to assess the importance of each embedding dimension in carrying token-specific
150   information. The top-k dimensions with the highest importance scores are selected as privacy neurons.
151   Obfuscate privacy-sensitive information: We introduce a neuron suppressing perturbation function
152   that adds directional noise to the identified privacy neurons. In constrat to conventional isotropic
153
      noise, we show that this perturbation enhances the indistinguishability of embeddings and thus leads
      to better defense performance. Next, we define the concept of privacy neurons and the associated
154
      perturbation framework.
155
156   Definition 1 (Privacy Neurons). Consider an input text x and an embedding model Φ : x → Rd .
157
      We assume there is a subset of dimensions Nt ⊆ V = {1, . . . , d} that encapsulates the sensitive
      information associated with a token t. Consequently, the embedding Φ(x) can be expressed as:
158
159                                        Φ(x) = (ΦNt (x), ΦV\Nt (x)),
160
161   where ΦNt (x) represents the privacy-sensitive neuron activations and ΦV\Nt (x) the privacy-
      invariant neuron activations.

      For simplicity, we assume the number of privacy neurons (i.e., |Nt |) is a constant k across all tokens.
163   Given the privacy neurons Nt , the data owner shares the perturbed text embedding with:
164
                                     M(x; Nt ) = F(ΦNt (x))∥ΦV\Nt (x),                                     (1)
165
166   where F is a randomized perturbation function on selected dimensions Nt , and ∥ is the concatenation
167   operation.
168   Preliminary analysis on privacy-sensitive dimensions. As described in Eq. 1, embeddings can be
169   decomposed into privacy-sensitive and privacy-invariant activations. To verify the hypothesis, we cal-
170   culate the dimension-wise sensitivity as: ∆i = max ({|Φ(x+ )i − Φ(x− )i | ; x+ ∈ D+ , x− ∈ D− }).
171   Φ(·)i represents the activation of the i-th dimension of the embedding. The sensitivity captures
172   the largest change observed in dimension i, with a higher value indicating greater responsiveness
173   to the presence of token t. We present a pilot study of the sensitivity distribution of the top and
174
      bottom 10% of privacy neurons detected by our approach in Figure 2. Empirically, we found that
      top privacy neurons exhibit significantly higher sensitivity (average 0.04) compared to tail neurons,
175
      whose sensitivity is close to zero. Given this observation, we believe it possible to manipulate only a
176
      small portion of dimensions to defend against inversion attacks.

      3.2   P RIVACY N EURON D ETECTION THROUGH N EURON M ASKING L EANING
179
180   To detect the privacy neurons associated with a sensitive token t, we propose a neuron mask learning
181   framework to assess the importance of each neuron. Our objective aims to determine a binary neuron
182   mask, m ∈ {0, 1}d , which filters irrelevant dimensions and retains the most informative neurons
183   responsible for token t when applied to an embedding e. The masked embedding is represented as
184   e ⊙ m, where ⊙ denotes the Hadamard product operator. Ideally, a perfect mask would have values of
185   0 for privacy-irrelevant dimensions and 1 for privacy-related dimensions. However, since the training
186
      loss is not differentiable for binary masks, we first introduce a differentiable neuron mask learning
      framework, followed by a description of the optimization process.
187

189   token t, however, the training loss is not differentiable for binary masks. Therefore, we resort to a
190   practical method that employs a smoothing approximation of the discrete Bernoulli distribution Mad-
191   dison et al. (2017). In our method, we assume each mask mi follows a hard concrete distribution

239     3.3          E MBEDDING P ERTURBATION FOR S UPPRESSING P RIVACY I NFORMATION
240
241     After the privacy neurons are identified, the next step is to perturb these neurons for obfuscating
242     privacy information against the adversary. A common approach is to inject isotropic noise from
243     the Laplace distribution as follows: e′ = e + ν. Here, ν ∈ Rd is a noise vector with elements
244
        ν ∼ Lap(0, 1/ϵ) sampled from the Laplace distribution. However, we found that adding isotropic
        noise fails to effectively obfuscate private information as it perturbs data points towards all directions.
245
        Instead of employing the typical DP approach, we propose a novel neuron-suppressing perturbation
246
        function that adds random noise that pushes each embedding dimension toward its negative direction.
247     Formally, this can be expressed as:
248
249
                                               F(e) = e − sign(e) ⊙ ν ′ .                                                      (6)
250     The perturbation function in Eq. 6 samples one-sided Laplace noise where νi′ = |νi | and multiplied
251     by the negative sign of the embedding e. To elucidate the distinction between isotropic noise and
252     neuron-suppressing perturbation, Figure 3 illustrates two distributions represented by red and green
253     data points, along with their perturbed counterparts (in lighter shades) under different perturbation
254
        functions. The red and green dots can be conceptualized as the text embeddings sampled from the
        D+ and D− datasets in R2 . We also include kernel density estimation (KDE) plots of the angles
255
        (i.e., arctan2(y, x)) below each scatter plot for visualization. As depicted in Figure 3, the isotropic
256
        noise applies perturbations in all directions, which is ineffective in obfuscating the data points.
257     In contrast, our proposed neuron-suppressing perturbation introduces noise predominantly in the
258     negative direction that makes the data more indistinguishable. Finally, we apply the perturbation
259     function F in Eq. 6 and the detected neuron Nt with Eq. 1 to release the perturbed text embedding.

        labels. To meet the second objective, we select six widely used datasets with downstream labels,

         5.1            E VALUATION ON NEURON DETECTION METHODS
346
347     Our work rests on the fundamental assumption that privacy neurons can be detected and perturbed to
348     defend against inversion attacks. This premise raises two critical questions: (i) How effectively can
349     we defend against inversion attacks using ground truth privacy neurons? and (ii) To what extent can
350     our black-box detection model approach this ideal defense? To address this, we examine a white-box
351     defense scenario where the defender possesses complete knowledge of the attack model’s parameters.
352     White-box privacy neuron detection. Under white-box access to the attack model, we employ the
353     Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) Goodfellow et al. (2014) to identify the most influential neurons
354     for privacy protection. Our approach involves computing the gradient of the attack model’s loss with
355     respect to the input text embedding for a specific sensitive token. Neurons with the highest average
356     gradient magnitudes are identified as privacy neurons. This white-box defense method is referred to
357     as DPPN-Oracle in subsequent experiments.
358     Comparison of neuron detection methods. Figure 4 presents experimental results evaluating
359     various privacy neuron detection methods, including our black-box method (DPPN), the white-box
360     approach (DPPN-Oracle), and a random selection method (DPPN-Rand), alongside LapMech and
361     non-protected baselines for reference. The white-box method consistently achieves the best privacy-
362     utility tradeoff, confirming that perturbing the most informative neurons significantly reduces privacy
363     leakage. Notably, our black-box method performs comparably to the white-box approach; at ϵ = 2, it
364     exhibits an absolute Leakage difference of only 3% to 6%, with less than a 5% relative difference
365     in downstream metrics. In contrast, the random selection method is significantly less effective.
366
        Furthermore, Figure 5 depicts the top-r% overlap ratio between the black-box and white-box neurons.
        The results indicate that our black-box detection methods successfully identify neurons with 32%
367
        and 51% accuracy for the top 10% and 20% of neurons, respectively. To conclude, these results
368
        demonstrate the effectiveness of DPPN in approximating the ideal white-box scenario.

510   8    R ELATED W ORK
511
512
      Inversion attacks on embeddings. Embedding inversion attacks pose significant privacy risks in
513   both computer vision and natural language processing (NLP). These attacks exploit the unintended
514   memorization capabilities of neural models, allowing adversaries to reconstruct original data from
515   embeddings. In computer vision, high-fidelity reconstructions of images from embeddings have been
516   demonstrated Bordes et al. (2022); Dosovitskiy & Brox (2016); Teterwak et al. (2021). Similarly, in
517   NLP, embeddings can reveal sensitive text data and even demographic information about authors Pan
518   et al. (2020); Song & Shmatikov (2019); Lyu et al. (2020); Coavoux et al. (2018). The recent
519   work Morris et al. (2023) shows that embeddings from services like OpenAI’s can be accurately
520   inverted to recover the original text.
521   Privacy-preserving text embeddings. Two lines of work were explored for generating privacy-
522   preserving text embeddings: adversarial training and noisy embedding. The noisy embedding
523   approach defends against inversion attacks by adding random noise to the embeddings. For instance,
524   Laplace noise has been widely used to defend against inversion attacks Morris et al. (2023), member-
525   ship inference Song & Raghunathan (2020), and attribute inference attacks Coavoux et al. (2018).
526   Advanced techniques like the Purkayastha mechanism Du et al. (2023) further enhances the Laplace
527
      method for superior defense performance against inference attacks. On the other hand, adversarial
      training Coavoux et al. (2018); Elazar & Goldberg (2018); Li et al. (2018) involves creating a simu-
528
      lated adversary that tries to infer sensitive information while the main model is optimized to confuse
529
      this adversary. However, this approach’s success largely depends on the quality of the simulated
530   adversary Zhang et al. (2018).

533   9    C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we addressed the privacy risks of text embeddings, particularly against embedding
536   inversion attacks. By identifying and suppressing privacy neurons, our method enhances defense with
537   minimal impact on downstream tasks. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of DPPN
538   across various attack models, embedding models, and real-world privacy threats. As privacy risks
539   grow and attack models advance, we aim for our work to establish a robust framework for protecting
      sensitive information in text embeddings.

      A    L IMITATIONS
703
704
      While DPPN demonstrates significant effectiveness in protecting sensitive information within text
705   embeddings, it lacks the theoretical guarantees provided by differential privacy (DP). DP offers
706   a formal framework to quantify privacy, ensuring that the inclusion or exclusion of a single data
707   point does not significantly affect an algorithm’s output. This gap between DPPN and DP is
708   particularly relevant for applications involving highly sensitive data, such as medical records or
709   financial information.
710
      The primary challenge in bridging this gap lies in adapting DPPN’s targeted perturbation approach to
711   meet DP’s rigorous standards. DP requires that all released data be perturbed to provide a strong,
712   provable privacy guarantee. However, DPPN only perturb a subset of dimensions while keeping the
713   remaining dimensions intact. To bridge this gap, future research could explore developing a hybrid
714   approach that applies DP-compliant noise to all dimensions while concentrating higher magnitude
715   perturbations on privacy-sensitive neurons such as Mahalanobis mechanism Xu et al. (2020) or Rényi
716   differential privacy Mironov (2017).
717
718   B    C OMPLETE D EFENSE P ERFORMANCE ACROSS A LL DATASETS
719
720   In addition to the STS12 Agirre et al. (2012) and FIQA Maia et al. (2018) datasets used in the
721   main experiment, Table 8 also presents statistics of other datasets, including STSB Cer et al. (2017),
722   STS14 Agirre et al. (2014), Quora Bondarenko et al. (2020), and NFCorpus Boteva et al. (2016).
723   Figure 7 and Figure 8 show the complete defense performance on all datasets. Besides using Leakage,
724   we also utilize Confidence to assess the defense performance. This metric reflects the certainty of the
725   attack model’s predictions. A higher Confidence score indicates that the model is more confident
726   in its prediction of the sensitive token. For the semantic textual similarity (STS) task, downstream
727   performance is measured using the Pearson correlation of Cosine Similarity (Pearson corr.). In
728
      the context of information retrieval, we employ the ranking metric NDCG@10. As described in
      Section 4.2, DPPN consistently demonstrates superior performance over LapMech and PurMech
729
      across all levels of perturbation and datasets, both in defense and downstream task metrics.
