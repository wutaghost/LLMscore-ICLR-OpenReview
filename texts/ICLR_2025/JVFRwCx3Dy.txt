011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive performance in
014            various tasks, including In-Context Learning (ICL), where the model performs
015            new tasks by conditioning solely on the examples provided in the context, with-
016
               out updating the model’s weights. While prior research has explored the roles of
               pretraining data and model architecture, the key mechanism behind ICL remains
017
               unclear. In this work, we systematically uncover properties present in LLMs that
018
               support the emergence of ICL. To disambiguate these factors, we conduct a study
019            with a controlled dataset and data sequences using a deep autoregressive model.
020            We show that conceptual repetitions in the data sequences are crucial for ICL,
021            more so than previously indicated training data properties like burstiness or long-
022            tail distribution. Conceptual repetitions could refer to n-gram repetitions in tex-
023            tual data or exact image copies in image sequence data. Such repetitions also
024            offer other previously overlooked benefits such as reduced transiency in ICL per-
025            formance. Furthermore, we show that the emergence of ICL depends on balancing
026            the in-weight learning objective with the in-context solving ability during training.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In-context learning (ICL) is a remarkable feature of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Radford et al.,
032   2019; Brown et al., 2020) since it enables the model to adapt and solve tasks never seen during
033
      training, conditioned solely on the context provided during inference (Brown et al., 2020) without
      demanding any retraining or task-specific fine-tuning. ICL contrasts with standard in-weight learn-
034
      ing (IWL), where the knowledge needed for inference tasks is embedded within the model weights
035
      during training. Models showing ICL are trained autoregressively to predict the next token as the
036   in-weight learning objective. However, since no explicit training objective is tailored for ICL, it is
037   challenging to identify the underlying factors for its emergence.
038
039
      Previous research (Han et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2022) has attributed the emergence of ICL to pre-
      training data properties such as long-tail token distribution and high burstiness in data sequences.
040
      Here, burstiness refers to clustered occurrences of data points within a sequence. Following up on
041
      the idea of reoccurring concepts in data sequences, Chen et al. (2024) analyzed the impact of parallel
042   structures in the pretraining data of LLMs, showing that the pairs of phrases following similar tem-
043   plates in the pretraining corpora lead to ICL. This paper shows that while these properties improve
044   ICL performance, they are not the predominant factors for ICL emergence.
045
      We distinguish key driving factors of ICL operating at the data sequence level and the in-weight
046
      learning objective level. At the data sequence level, we demonstrate that it is important for the
047
      training data to provide opportunities to solve training tasks in context, which we refer to as an in-
048   context look-up mechanism. Specifically, in this work, we show that the look-up mechanism can
049   be strongly driven by conceptual repetitions in data sequences, which are also commonly present in
050   the training sequences of LLMs. In Figure 1, we illustrate that the pretraining text corpora used to
051   train LLMs naturally contain a high frequency of repetitive n-grams in the context, indicating high
052   concept-level repetitions. It shows that an input sequence of the context window of 2048 tokens
053   contains more than ten 10-gram repetitions across different corpora on average. In this work, we
      conduct a controlled study to showcase the contribution of similar repetitions for ICL. At the in-


                                                        1

054
      weight learning objective level, we hypothesize that the high complexity of the in-weight learning
055   objective is crucial for consistent ICL performance throughout the training process. In contrast,
056   a simple IWL objective can result in subdued or transient ICL performance. This aspect is often
057   overlooked because the training objective in LLMs is naturally quite complex, which demonstrate
058   strong and stable ICL performance. To support this argument, we study the impact of different IWL
059   objectives on ICL performance.

082   We present a controlled study to show the contribution of each responsible factor. We train an
083   autoregressive GPT-2 model for the image classification task where the data sequence contains input-
084   output pairs. This data format allows us to study different data properties and training objectives.
085   We test the impact of high and low burstiness in data, repetitions, and skewed distribution on ICL
086   performance. We further examine the influence of different in-weight learning objectives on ICL
087   performance by modifying the number of classes, using skewed distribution, introducing noise in the
088
      supervision signal, and shifting the in-weight learning objective to a much more complex instance
      discrimination task.
089
090   Our analysis shows that even a single repetition in the context sequence enables the ICL emergence
091   without other properties like high burstiness or skewed label distribution. In our setup, we obtain
092   strong ICL ability without significantly impacting IWL performance. Similar behavior is reported
093   for LLMs (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2024) and Vision-Language Models (Alayrac et al.,
094
      2022; Huang et al., 2023), where both ICL and IWL performances are reported even at the end of the
      long training period. Prior controlled studies observe a transient nature of ICL, showing diminished
095
      ICL performance as IWL training progresses (Chan et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2023) while our setup
096
      shows a much more stable ICL performance. Therefore, being a more representative model for
097   studying ICL in large models. In our setup, ICL transiency is eliminated by using repetitions in the
098   data sequences and using a complex in-weight learning objective.
099
100
      To summarize, we contend that the strong look-up mechanism created by repetitions in the training
      data sequences along with a complex in-weight learning task, enable non-transient and stable ICL
101
      performance. We speculate the same mechanisms enable ICL in LLMs, where the ICL performance
102
      is stable and non-transient even after the long training phase.

105   2                                        R ELATED WORK
106
107   ICL research directions. Plenty of research has been dedicated to understanding and optimizing
      the model response to obtain the best ICL performance. Prior works have analyzed the important


                                                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      concepts that enable ICL in LLMs such as pretraining data (Liu et al. (2022); Rubin et al. (2022);
109   Levine et al. (2022)), in-context prompt design (Voronov et al., 2024), impact of the in-context
110   samples and length (Agarwal et al., 2024), and more. Studies (Liu et al., 2022; Rubin et al., 2022)
111   have also focused on designing demonstrations, also known as prompt engineering, to get better
112   responses from pretrained language models. Other works (Akyürek et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023;
113   Wu et al., 2024) tested the ability of ICL to solve novel tasks and its robustness (Raventós et al.,
114   2024; Min et al., 2022) by evaluating on out-of-distribution tasks. Furthermore, (Xing et al., 2024;
115   Chan et al., 2022) aimed to understand the underlying components driving ICL, such as model
116   architecture, training data distribution, and optimization objectives.
117   Simultaneously, ICL has been studied in the context of specific tasks. Many works have attempted
118   to explain the emergence of ICL by considering it as a regression task. (Akyürek et al., 2023; Dai
119   et al., 2023; Von Oswald et al., 2023) showed that self-attention architectures with linear atten-
120   tion implement a gradient descent with in-context examples, while Dai et al. (2023) theoretically
121   identified that the transformer attention mechanism has a dual form of SGD, where in-context learn-
122   ers implicitly perform fine-tuning. Other works showed that transformers can implement simple
123
      functions in-context like least squares, ridge regression, and gradient descent in two-layer neural
      networks (Bai et al., 2023; Garg et al., 2022). Alternatively, ICL for classification tasks relies on
124
      an in-context solving ability to match and retrieve the label from the context. This demonstrates a
125
      clear difference in the underlying mechanism between these two tasks. Our work focuses on under-
126   standing the relationship between different components for the classification task setup. Given the
127   different underlying ICL mechanisms, our findings may not directly transfer to regression tasks.
128
129
130   Mechanistic perspective on ICL. Prior work has studied ICL from different angles through sim-
131   plified experiments and model probing. Many works have analyzed the transition phases during the
132   training process, discovering the sequence of operations and circuitry leading to ICL. Olsson et al.
133   (2022) provided the initial evidence that induction heads may be pivotal for in-context learning in
134   transformer-based models. Singh et al. (2024) conducted another mechanistic study using a causal
135   approach to understand the abrupt emergence of induction heads and identified three interacting sub-
136   circuits leading to their formation. Reddy (2024) demonstrated with a simple two-parameter model
137   that ICL is driven by the formation of an induction head, which emerges due to nested non-linearities
138
      in a multi-layer attention network. Building on previous work in mechanistic interpretability, we an-
      alyze the emergence of induction heads alongside ICL in our setup using a small GPT-2 model with
139
      three layers and one head. Singh et al. (2023) found that ICL becomes transient as training pro-
140
      gresses and discussed different ways to reduce transiency. In this work, we also observe similar
141   transiency and analyze how induction heads appear and disappear as ICL becomes transient.
142
143
144
      Training data properties for ICL. Numerous works indicate that the training data distribution
145   both in the corpus and sequences plays a role in the emergence of ICL. Shin et al. (2022) investi-
146   gated the ICL behaviour w.r.t. different pretraining data source and size, confirming that the source
147   data properties can make or break ICL. Han et al. (2023) conducted an empirical study to show that
148   challenging examples and long-tail tokens promote ICL by making the long-range information gain
149   difficult. On the same lines, Chan et al. (2022) demonstrated that a large number of rarely occurring
150   classes facilitate ICL. These works align with our generalized argument that a complex enough IWL
151   task promotes ICL. Similarly, Razeghi et al. (2022) found a correlation between the term frequency
152   of the input data in the pretraining corpus and ICL performance, further underscoring the influence
153
      of data characteristics. For local data patterns, studies by Olsson et al. (2022), and Chan et al.
      (2022) demonstrated that burstiness and repetitive structures within training sequences are vital for
154
      ICL. In a similar direction, Chen et al. (2024) have argued that parallel structures in the pretrain-
155
      ing textual data, which follow a similar semantic or syntactic template, facilitate ICL in language
156   models. They include repetitions in the parallel structures however their independent significance
157   is not well disambiguated. Shi et al. (2024) studied the impact of combining the documents within
158   the context window for language modeling and different textual in-context tasks. They showed a
159   clear improvement in the ICL performance once the model was trained with a sequence of related
160   documents showing the importance of data patterns in the pretraining. Our work studies local data
161   patterns present in the training sequences, showing that simple copy-based data repetition, which
      can be seen as a special case of parallel structures is a major factor for stable ICL performance.

162
      Meta-learning vs ICL. Meta-learning and In-context learning aim to enhance rapid adaptation to
163   new tasks but operate through distinct mechanisms. Meta-learning often utilizes episodic pretraining
164   where the model is pretrained with randomly sampled classes and label mappings in each episode,
165   which enables fast adaptation to new unseen tasks during the testing phase with only a few parame-
166   ters updates (Finn et al., 2017; Santoro et al., 2016; Snell et al., 2017). In this way, the model learns
167   how to learn the task but does not learn the input label mapping directly due to the episodic permu-
168   tations, which present an almost impossible learning task. On the other hand, there is no explicit
169   pretraining objective for in-context learning. The input label mapping is crucial for the in-weight
170   learning task and must not be harmed by the ICL abilities. Thus, while meta-learning explicitly
171
      optimizes for adaptability and robustness during training, ICL leverages pretraining knowledge like
      the original input-label mapping for adaption through information provided by input context. In
172
      this paper, we focus solely on concepts relevant to in-context learning but draw connections to the
173
      meta-learning pretraining via episodic pretraining and task difficulty when analyzing the choice of
174   the in-weight learning task.

178
179   We conducted an analysis to show how different properties impact ICL. We train an autoregressive
180
      model GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) on different sequences containing image-label pairs from image
      classification datasets widely popular in the FSL literature (Lake et al., 2015; Bertinetto et al., 2019;
181
      Fei-Fei et al., 2004; Cimpoi et al., 2014).

      3.1   DATA SEQUENCING
184
185   The autoregressive model in this work is trained with a sequence length of 2L + 1 with L image-
186   label pairs in the context followed by a query image, as shown in Figure 2. The in-weight learning
187   objective is to predict the label of the last image, which is the (2L + 1)-th token. Training sequences
188   consist of a mixture of (1) standard sequences, in which sample-label pairs are randomly selected
189   from the training dataset, and (2) in-context sequences, where the query image-label information is
190   enforced to be present in the sequence by using a pair similar to the query image-label pair. The
191   proportion of each sequence type in the total amount of training sequences is treated as a hyper-
192
      parameter. Using in-context sequences, the model is implicitly regularized to attend to the similar
      context tokens without completely relying on the model weights.

225   3.2   T RAINING AND EVALUATION DETAILS
226
227   We train a causal GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) transformer to predict the label yq of the query
228   image xq given a sequence of L interleaved image-label pairs: (x1 , y1 , x2 , y2 , ..., xL , yL , xq , ?) as
229
      illustrated in Figure 2. Each image-label pair is converted into token embeddings separately. The
      model is trained to maximize the likelihood of the next token, with the loss applied to the final query
230
      output, thus using last-token prediction as the IWL training objective.
231
232
      Dataset construction. We conduct our controlled experiments and analysis on the Omniglot
233
      dataset (Lake et al., 2015) and scale to more realistic visual datasets. Omniglot contains 1623
234
      classes with 20 images each. Following previous work (Chan et al., 2022), we use 1600 classes for
235   training and the remaining 23 as novel classes for ICL evaluation. More experiments (in Section 4)
236   are performed on datasets including CIFAR-100 (Bertinetto et al., 2019) , Caltech-101 (Fei-Fei
237   et al., 2004), and DTD texture datasets (Cimpoi et al., 2014). ICL evaluation is performed using
238   20, 10, and 10 novel classes for CIFAR-100, Caltech-101, and DTD datasets, respectively. More
239   experiment details are included in the Appendix B.
240
241   Data sequencing details. In all supervised image classification experiments, the models are
242   trained with a mix of in-context and standard sequences. Models with instance discrimination
243   (self-supervised learning) tasks are trained with 100% in-context sequences. ICL is evaluated in
244   a few-shot classification setting for 2-way-4-shot and 4-way-2-shot tasks. We show results in the
245   main paper for the more challenging 4-way-2-shot setting, while other results are included in the
246
      Appendix B.1 and Appendix C.3. This evaluation is performed on held-out novel classes. The
      trained classifier output is used for the few-shot evaluation using label mapping from 0-1 or 0-3
247
      corresponding to both evaluation tasks. IWL is evaluated for the multi-class classification task on
248
      the held-out samples from the trained classes. The standard sequences with (A-B-C-D-E-F-G-H)
249   format are used for IWL evaluation (see Figure 2). The same pre-set 10K and 3.2K sequences are
250   used for ICL and IWL evaluation, respectively.

      3.3   BASELINE
253
254   Our proposed baseline model is trained on the Omniglot dataset with a mix of standard and in-
255   context sequences with 10% and 90% probability respectively. The standard sequences follow
256   (A-B-C-D-E-F-G-H) format, and in-context sequences have a high burstiness with (3xQ-3xA-B-C)
257   format. The baseline model with in-context sequence format (3xQ-3xA-B-C), achieves strong
258   ICL and IWL performance (shown in Figure 3). Prior work observed poor IWL performance
259   with strong ICL performance. We think this happens because they utilize standard sequences
260   in (Q-A-B-C-D-E-F-G) format with at least one repetition of the query in context, which undermines
261
      the in-weight learning task. However, similar to prior work, we observe that the ICL performance is
      transient as training proceeds for this baseline model.

264   4     W HAT P ROMOTES I N -C ONTEXT S OLVING A BILITY ?
265
266   The term “in-context solving ability” or “look-up mechanism” refers to the ability of the transformer-
267   based model, where the model can prioritize the usage of information present within its current input
268   context to generate responses. The model is not explicitly trained to learn this mechanism, however,
269   it is crucial for the model to solve the task based on the in-context information. This work examines
      a previously proposed in-context sequence strategy, which are motivated by the trends observed


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      in the pretraining corpora of LLMs and further analyzes the impact of repetitions in training data
271   sequences.
272
273   Burstiness. Burstiness is an inherent feature of natural sequential data. Chan et al. (2022) showed
274   that the burstiness property in training sequences is crucial for ICL and demonstrates its effective-
275   ness. Burstiness, in a classification setup like ours, is defined as the number of occurrence of samples
276   from the same class as the query sample in the context. Models with this strategy are trained with a
277   mix of highly bursty (3xQ-3xA-B-C) and non-bursty (A-B-C-D-E-F-G-H) sequences to obtain both
278   ICL and IWL. High-bursty and non-bursty sequences are types of in-context and standard sequences,
279   respectively.
280
281   Repetitions. Although burstiness is typically a natural feature of sequential data, repetitions are
282
      also a specific aspect of burstiness. As motivated in the introduction, the analysis of pretraining
      corpora reveals frequent repetitions of n-grams in different pretraining corpora, indicating that pre-
283
      training text exhibits burstiness not only via clustered phrases of synonyms or similar topics but also
284
      through exact repetitions of different lengths. Therefore, we conducted a study to examine the im-
285   pact of repetitions on ICL. We find that simply copying the query-label pair into the input sequence
286   during training develops a strong look-up mechanism.
287
288   iCopy. In light of previously introduced ways of fostering stronger in-context learning, we refer to
289   the sequences with conceptual repetitions for image classification tasks as iCopy. iCopy utilizes an
290   instance-based copying mechanism where the query-label pair is copy-pasted within the sequence
291   of defined burstiness. This simplifies the matching process between a query token and its duplicates
292   across different token positions in the context. Since the positions of the copied token are shuffled
293   and sequences with repetitions are mixed with standard sequences, the model learns a generalized
294
      look-up mechanism across the whole context. The sequences with repetitions can also be considered
      as a special case of an in-context sequence with low-burstiness, denoted as (Q-A-B-C-D-E-F-G
295
      iCopy) in this work. This copied version in the context can be an exact copy or an augmented
296
      version of the original query sample-label pair.
297
298   In the previously proposed high-burstiness strategy, samples from the same class as the query appear
299   multiple times, whereas the sequences with repetitions may only contain one occurrence of the same
300
      instance as the query sample. However, repetitions show enhanced results when combined with the
      high-burstiness strategy (3xQ-3xA-B-C iCopy), comprising multiple instance copies in the context.
301
302   We conducted ablations and analysis to study how repetitions perform against previously introduced
303   data distributional properties on ICL and IWL tasks.
304

      Repetitions are sufficient for ICL. We compare in-context sequences with high-
      burstiness (3xQ-3xA-B-C) and sequences with one repetition (Q-A-B-C-D-E-F-G iCopy),
308
      observing similar ICL peak performance for both types of in-context sequences but with reduced
309
      transiency for the sequences with repetition. Combining the repetitions and high-burstiness
310   strategies (3xQ-3xA-B-C iCopy) further reduces ICL transiency. Using low-burstiness without
311   iCopy (Q-A-B-C-D-E-F-G) shows no ICL ability. ICL and IWL performance curves are shown in
312   Figure 3 a,b, where we can also observe that the repetitions in iCopy sequences do not harm the
313   IWL performance.

      Skewness is not necessary. We compare models trained with skewed and uniform label distribu-
315
      tion, using 7200 samples randomly selected from 992 classes. The sequences with repetitions and
316   uniform distribution outperform the high-burstiness strategy with Zipfian distribution (Figure 3c).
317   Thus showing that a skewed distribution improves ICL over the baseline, it is not necessary for ICL.

349   training: language modeling is a complex IWL task due to the complexity of natural language,

372   4.3                                   I C OPY PROMOTES THE I NDUCTION H EADS
373
374     Prior works show that the formation of an induction head leads to in-context learning (Olsson
375   et al., 2022; Reddy, 2024). Induction heads are a result of the two-layer circuit that performs match
376   and copy operations from the context. For a bigram sequence containing image-label pairs, first, the
377   label tokens attend to the previous image tokens and copy the information into their representation.
      Then the query image token attends to the matching label tokens and copies the label information.

      Since the repetitions accompanied by high burstiness create a strong look-up mechanism, we can
379   obtain ICL with a GPT-2 model using only 3-layer with 1-head per layer. We observe that it is not
380   possible to obtain a clear ICL performance with the same architecture using only the high-burstiness
381   strategy.
382

      pendently and track the induction head formation throughout the training. As shown in Figure 5(c,

426   5     D OES IWL OBJECTIVE MATTER FOR ICL?
427
428   As described in Section 4.2, we believe the look-up mechanism on its own is not sufficient for a
429   stable ICL performance and a choice of the appropriate IWL objective plays an important role. In
430   particular, we hypothesize that if the in-weight task is too simplistic, the look-up mechanism does not
431   emerge or results in a transient ICL performance since the model can optimize for the IWL objective
      without needing to attend to the context. Therefore, the in-weight task must have a minimum level


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      of complexity to give rise to the look-up mechanism. A related study (Chan et al., 2022) showed
433   that a long-tail distribution and an increase in the number of classes improve ICL, connecting these
434   properties to natural language data. In this work, we additionally provide an explanation of why
435   these properties enhance ICL - by increasing the complexity of the IWL task.
436
      We propose four different ways of regulating the IWL task difficulty - changing the number of
437
      training classes, changing the number of samples used for training, training with noisy labels, and
438
      switching to the instance discrimination task. Here, we show how each of the proposed techniques
439   influences the ICL and IWL performance.
440
441   Number of classes: We observe that increasing the number of classes monotonically improves
442   ICL performance, as illustrated in Figure 6a. This finding follows similar insights in prior
443   work (Chan et al., 2022; Reddy, 2024). However, these works explain the improved ICL capa-
444   bilities due to the large number of rarely occurring classes. We interpret it as just one out of many
445   ways to make the IWL task harder. Please refer to Appendix D for more details.
446
447   Skewed distribution: Keeping the total number of samples the same, we compare the ICL per-
448   formance of a model trained with balanced and imbalanced (Zipfian) distributions. We observe
449   improved ICL performance with skewed distribution, as illustrated in Figure 6b. This experiment is
450   a confirmation of prior work (Chan et al., 2022), which also shows improved ICL with the increased
451
      long-tail distribution. Imbalancing the label distribution is a known way to make the in-weight
      learning task harder. Please refer to Appendix D for more details.

503   6                                 D ISCUSSION
504
505
506   Key insights. We identify that the balance between training data that provides opportunities to
507
      use the in-context look-up mechanism and the complexity of the model training objective influences
      the emergence and stability of ICL. We show that the conceptual repetitions, which are also com-
508
      monly present in textual data sequences, induce a strong in-context look-up mechanism. Repetitions
509
      combined with high-burstiness in the training sequences result in peak ICL performance. Models
510   with such in-context look-up mechanisms ensure strong ICL performance with no harm to the IWL
511   component, while also demonstrating reduced transiency in ICL performance. This supports our hy-
512   pothesis that the conceptual repetitions in the data are strong drivers for the ICL ability. As shown in
513   Section 5, training with a complex in-weight learning objective leads to improved and non-transient
514   ICL performance. This conclusion aligns with the complex training objective and observed nature
515   of ICL performance in LLMs.
516
517
518
      Limitations. Although the ranking between different compared methods is clear, we observed a
      large variance in the ICL performance curves w.r.t. random seeds where the IWL task is simple. We
519
      believe one of the reasons for this is the sensitivity of ICL towards training sequences (Press et al.,
520
      2023). While our approach transfers well to other visual datasets and different training objectives,
521   showing a similar analysis on real-world sequential data is out of scope of this work. We also
522   found that ICL is sensitive to certain model design choices such as model weight initialization and
523   image embedding architecture. For instance, using a truncated normal distribution for initialization
524   improves the stability of ICL performance across different seeds. Addressing the robustness of ICL
525   is a goal for future work.
526
527
528   Broader implications. Models with in-context learning ability allow users to leverage large mod-
529   els for various downstream tasks without the need to adapt the model weights by simply defining
530
      the task during inference. We provide an analysis of the training sequences showing that conceptual
      repetitions are influential and they reduce the need for specific properties in the data distribution,
531
      giving more flexibility to obtain ICL for different domains and applications. From a research per-
532
      spective, our findings allow for analyzing internal workings in a controlled setup, facilitating a better
533   understanding of ICL. Our work is based on mainly synthetic training sequences and thus has no
534   direct societal impact.
535
536
537   Future work. We believe that our findings about the essential properties of the data sequence and
538   design of in-weight learning objectives could be crucial for future large model training, where in-
539   context solving ability is important. Our findings are agnostic of the data domain and should be
      applicable to LLMs, VLMs or other modalities.

      B       E XPERIMENT SETUP DETAILS
795
796
      Training details. The model is trained with a mix of two types of sequences with different bursti-
797
      ness forms: in-context sequences and standard sequences. In-context sequences can have multiple
798   reoccurring samples from the same class as the query, whereas standard sequences have all samples
799   selected from random classes. The high-burstiness strategy uses in-context sequences with three
800   re-occurrences and iCopy strategy uses only one re-occurrence. All the supervised models in this
801   work are trained with a burstiness probability of 90%, which means 90% in-context sequences and
802   10% standard sequences. All self-supervised models use 100% in-context sequences.
803
      Evaluation details. We evaluate separately for both ICL and IWL. ICL evaluation is performed in
804   a few-shot manner with 2-way 4-shots and 4-way 2-shots tasks. The evaluation is performed using
805   the pretrained softmax classifier without any model update. We use label mappings 0 to k with k
806   being the number of classes in the few-shot setting. We notice that the ICL results are agnostic to
807   label mappings. ICL is always performed on the hold-out classes not seen during training. ICL is
808   performed over 10K presampled sequences to ensure a fair comparison. IWL is evaluated on the
809   hold-out samples from the training classes where the sequence format must be (A-B-C-D-E-F-G-H)
      so that the model does not perform look-up, but relies only on the model weights.

       As described earlier, we believe the look-up mechanism on its own is not sufficient for a stable
1165   ICL performance and a choice of the appropriate IWL task plays an important role. We proposed
1166   4 different ways of regulating the IWL task difficulty - changing the number of training classes,
1167   changing the number of samples used for training, training with noisy labels, and switching to the
1168   self-supervised training regime. Here, we show how each of the proposed techniques influences the
1169   ICL and IWL performance.
1170
1171   Number of classes As already mentioned before, we observe that increasing the number of classes
1172   monotonically improves ICL performance. This finding follows similar insights in prior work (Chan
1173   et al., 2022; Reddy, 2024). However, these works explain the improved ICL capabilities by the large
1174   number of rarely occurring classes. We interpret it as just one out of many ways to make the IWL
1175   task harder.
1176   To simulate harder IWL scenarios, we gradually increase the number of training classes from 200
1177   to 1600. We show quite bad and unstable ICL performance until 600 classes. ICL performance
1178   significantly improves for 1000 classes, but it is still unstable, with occasional ICL failure cases.
1179   However, we observe strong and stable ICL performance for a high number of classes as shown in
1180   Figure 17 where we report the strong ICL accuracies for the easier (2-way 4-shot) and harder (4-way
1181   2-shot) setup. Impaired IWL accuracy for a higher number of classes shows the IWL task is now
1182   more difficult.
1183
1184   Skewed distribution Keeping the total number of samples the same, we compare the ICL per-
1185   formance of a model trained with balanced and imbalanced (Zipfian) distributions. We observe
1186   improved ICL performance with skewed distribution, as illustrated in Figure 18. This experiment is
1187   a confirmation of prior work (Chan et al., 2022), which also shows improved ICL with the increased
       long-tail distribution.
