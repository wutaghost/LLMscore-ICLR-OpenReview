009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Long-term point tracking is essential to understanding non-rigid motion in the
012            physical world better. Deep learning approaches have recently been incorporated
013            into long-term point tracking, but most prior work predominantly functions in 2D.
014            Although these methods benefit from the well-established backbones and match-
015            ing frameworks, the motions they produce do not always make sense in the 3D
016            physical world. In this paper, we propose the first deep learning framework for
017            long-term point tracking in 3D that generalizes to new points and videos with-
018
               out requiring test-time fine-tuning. Our model is a coarse-to-fine approach that
               contains a cost volume fusion module at each level, which effectively integrates
019
               multiple past appearances and motion information via a transformer architecture,
020
               significantly enhancing overall tracking performance. Our explicit occlusion rea-
021            soning also allowed tracking through long occlusions. In terms of 3D tracking
022            performance, our model significantly outperforms simple scene flow chaining and
023            previous 2D point tracking methods, even if one uses ground truth depth and cam-
024            era pose to backproject 2D point tracks in a synthetic scenario.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Motion estimation is a task that has existed since the beginning of computer vision. Short-term dense
030   motion estimation problems, such as optical flow in 2D and scene flow in 3D, have been extensively
031   studied. However, the utility of these tasks is limited because many points are featureless, making
032   their motion estimation within two frames fundamentally ambiguous. Besides, chaining 2-frame
033
      motions to derive point trajectories is susceptible to significant cumulative errors and ineffective
      for handling long occlusions. However, spatio-temporal tracking of keypoints (Laptev, 2005) have
034
      always been interesting because it offers the capability to track long-term non-rigid object motions.
035
      Such knowledge would be greatly helpful for augmented reality and robotics applications, as well
036   as providing supervision for generative models that generate dynamic videos with arbitrary non-
037   rigid motions. Recently, Harley et al. (2022) reinvigorated the long-term pixel tracking problem
038   and proposed a framework inspired by previous state-of-the-art optical flow and object tracking
039   work. Doersch et al. (2022) released datasets specifically designed to address point tracking. These
040   datasets, including 2D and 3D data, have boosted research on this task.
041
      Most existing methods predominantly address the problem of 2D long-term point tracking. But in
042   the 3D world we live in, such 2D tracking, however accurate it might be, might still miss the 3D
043   motion of the points. Even the state-of-the-art video generator SORA has significant trouble under-
044   standing long-term 3D point motion (Bupe, 2024), which may limit its usage for downstream tasks
045   such as augmented reality and robot manipulation. As we will show in the experiments, backpro-
046   jecting 2D long-term point tracks into 3D, even with known camera poses and pixel depths, is still
047   prone to errors especially at object boundaries, where a difference of 1-2 pixels may lead to a large
048   difference in 3D.
049   The ability to track any point long-term in 3D would significantly enhance our understanding of
050   the scene dynamics. Recently, Luiten et al. (2023) proposed a test-time optimization method to
051   model dynamic scenes using a set of Gaussians, thereby enabling long-term point tracking. While
052   surpassing the performance of prior 2D methods, this approach relies on test-time optimization for
053   each scene and struggles to track new points entering the scene. Test-time optimization is quite
      computationally expensive, especially for longer videos, making it unsuitable for online tracking.

054
      In this paper, we propose an efficient and generalizable method for long-term online tracking of
055   keypoints in a dynamic 3D point cloud. To our knowledge our approach is the first 3D point-based
056   approach that directly attacks long-term 3D point tracking in a generalizable manner that can work
057   on new points and videos without test-time fine-tuning. Our online tracking framework takes as
058   input a sequence of point clouds representing the dynamic scene. The model predicts a position for
059   each query point by combining multiple past appearance information with motion information from
060   the past point trajectory with a transformer-based framework. Occlusions are predicted explicitly
061   to filter out noisy appearance features. We propose an adaptive decoding module that selectively
062   decodes around the query point, enabling the network to process denser point clouds and gener-
063
      ate more precise motion for each point. Experiment results show that our approach significantly
      outperforms baselines such as linking scene flow results or backprojection from 2D point tracks.
064
065   In summary, our contributions include:
066
             • We propose the first online 3D point-based tracking framework that can track any point in
067
               3D point clouds without test-time optimization.
068
             • We devise a novel Cost Volume Fusion module that effectively takes into account the long-
069
               term appearances of each point and its past motion trajectory.
070
             • We propose an adaptive decoding module that significantly reduces memory consumption
071
               when training on denser point clouds.

076   2.1   P OINT TRACKING
077
      Tracking any pixel or point in 2D long-term has recently gained significant attention. MFT (Schmidt
078
      et al., 2023) presents an extension for optical flow by constructing optical flows not only between
079   consecutive frames but also between distant frames. These flows are chained together guided by
080   the predicted occlusion and uncertainty scores obtained from pre-trained networks, to derive the
081   most reliable sequence of flows for each tracked pixel. PIPs (Harley et al., 2022) presents a novel
082   framework designed for multi-frame point tracking which simultaneously estimates the target point
083   positions in multiple frames. Therefore, it can handle short occlusion events. To improve tracking
084   performance, Cotracker (Karaev et al., 2023) utilizes self-attention layers to track target points and
085   their local and global contextual points together. The self-attention layers enable information ex-
086   change among these points, leading to better tracking quality. Recently, SpatialTracker (Xiao et al.,
087
      2024) and SceneTracker (Wang et al., 2024) utilizes a 2.5D approach which collects 3D neighbor-
      hoods of 2D pixels using a triplane approach. However, their final output is still within each camera
088
      frame and hence predicts a mixture of camera motion and pixel motion.
089
090   Tap-Vid (Doersch et al., 2022) offers valuable datasets along with a straightforward baseline method.
091   It achieves this by predicting the position of a point in each frame based on the cost volume derived
092   from the query feature and the corresponding feature map. Meanwhile, TAPIR (Doersch et al.,
093
      2023) introduces a two-stage network comprising a matching stage and a refinement stage. This
      framework also incorporates the prediction of uncertainty to suppress ambiguous or unreliable pre-
094
      dictions, enhancing point tracking accuracy. Bozic et al. (2020) introduces a differentiable non-rigid
095
      approach that achieves superior performance in reconstructing non-rigidly moving objects. How-
096   ever, this approach can only focus on a single object in the video. In contrast, we focus on modeling
097   entire dynamic scenes by tracking any point within them.
098
099
      A different line of work utilizes test-time optimization techniques to model the scene, resulting in
      superior tracking performance. Additionally, these methods directly track points in 3D. Therefore,
100
      they can utilize useful 3D priors for tracking purposes. Specifically, OmniMotion (Wang et al.,
101
      2023) represents video content by employing a canonical 3D volume. It learns a set of bijections to
102   map points between any frame and the canonical one, thereby enabling tracking capability. Note that
103   the 3D canonical volume here is only used for finding 2D point correspondences between frames
104   and does not represent the real-world coordinate system, limiting OmniMotion to 2D point tracking.
105   Similarly, Luiten et al. (2023) represents the scene with a set of Gaussians. While the number of
106   Gaussians, their colors, and opacity remain fixed throughout the video, these Gaussians can move
107   and rotate freely to model the dynamic scene. Therefore, the tracking capability emerges from
      persistently modeling the dynamic scene under these constraints.

131   2.2         S CENE FLOW ESTIMATION
132
133   Scene flow estimation predicts the 3D motion field of points, with the input being either 2D images
134   or 3D point clouds. We focus on the approaches that has 3D point clouds as input as they are
135   more relevant to our work. Approaches can usually be categorized into two types: supervised and
136
      self-supervised. However, many supervised methods can utilize self-supervised losses to adapt a
      pre-trained model to a new dataset without ground truth.
137
138   FlowNet3D (Liu et al., 2019) is among the first to use PointNet++ (Qi et al., 2017), a deep network
139   for point clouds, to predict scene flow from point clouds directly and proposes important basic mod-
140   ules such as flow embedding, set conv, and set upconv layers that are commonly used in subsequent
141   works. FLOT (Puy et al., 2020) reformalizes scene flow estimation as an optimal transport prob-
142
      lem to initially compute scene flow and subsequently refine it using a deep network. PointPWC (Wu
      et al., 2020) introduces novel cost volumes, upsampling, and warping layers and utilizes a point con-
143
      volution network (Wu et al., 2019) to handle 3D point cloud data. Scene flow is then constructed in
144
      a coarse-to-fine fashion. The authors also propose a novel self-supervised loss that has been adopted
145   in subsequent works (Zhang et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023).
146   PV-Raft (Wei et al., 2021) introduces a point-voxel correlation field to handle both long-range and
147   local interactions between point pairs. NSFP (Li et al., 2021b) represents scene flow implicitly with a
148   neural network trained directly on test scenes with self-supervised losses. Fast neural scene flow (Li
149   et al., 2023) improves upon NSFP by utilizing the distance transform (Breu et al., 1995; Danielsson,
150   1980) and a correspondence-free loss, significantly reducing processing time. Finally, with the rise
151   of diffusion models, Zhang et al. (2024b); Liu et al. (2024) incorporate diffusion processes into the
152   scene flow estimation pipeline, achieving millimeter-level end-point error.

      3.4        F USION OF A PPEARANCE AND M OTION C UES
254
255   A major benefit of long-term point tracking over simply chaining 2-frame scene flows is the capa-
256   bility to incorporate motion cues. Motion cues can help produce motion estimates during occlusion
257   or a blurry frame. However, due to our goal of non-rigid point tracking, points can move in sur-
258   prising and different ways that cannot be easily captured by a motion prior. In those cases, a good
259   appearance tracking module should take over and predict more precise locations.
260   To properly consider multi-frame appearance and motion jointly, we devise a novel data-driven Cost
261   Volume Fusion module that softly combines motion-based and appearance-matching-based features.
262   The output of this combination is then used to predict the actual motion and occlusion status for each
263   point in the target frame. Below, we detail the specific components of the module.
264
265   3.4.1           C OST VOLUME F USION M ODULE
266
267   To provide the motion prior for the network, the last M predicted motions of the query point,
268   v(M,t),i = [vt−(M −1),i , . . . , vt,i ], are concatenated and encoded with an MLP followed by a group
269   normalization layer (Wu & He, 2018) to obtain a motion prior vector ϕt,i = M LP (v(M,t),i ). Note
      that, at the beginning of the video, the list of past motions v(M,t),i is initialized with zeros. Addi-

271   past motions as input.

274
      ume Ctk ,i (a simplified notation for Ctk (qt,i )) using Eq. (3). Intuitively, the cost volume contains
      the appearance matching information the query point in the current timestep and its corresponding

      neighboring points in the next timestep - Fig. 2. Therefore, the cost volume provides appearance
276
      matching information to predict the potential location of the query point in the next timestep. More
277   details about cost volume can be found at Sec. 3.2 and Eq. 3. In the experiment, to estimate the mo-
278   tion of a query point from T to T +1, we extract the appearance of the query point from the following
279   frames, {0, T − 6, T − 2, T } to reduce the appearance redundancy from consecutive frames.

281
      rent frame at the sparse level l, our network jointly uses the motion and appearance information

310   3.5   M ODEL T RAINING
311
      Our model relies on the estimated frame-to-frame motion of each point to construct the long-term
312
      point trajectory. Therefore, instead of directly training the model on the long-term tracking data, we
313   split the training process into two stages:
314
315          • Scene flow pretraining. The whole model is pre-trained with the scene flow datasets. Each
316            training sample includes two consecutive frames randomly sampled from a video. After the
317            training, the model can achieve competitive performance in the scene flow estimation task.
318          • Long-term tracking. In this stage, the Cost Volume Fusion Module is added to handle
319            multiple appearances of the query point and its past trajectory. The network is trained with
320            randomly sampled longer videos.
321
322   By following this two-stage training pipeline, we can utilize synthetic scene flow datasets to improve
323   the overall tracking performance and stabilize the training process. This has been important for the
      model to achieve good performance.

345                   L = λ1 · Lsf + λ2 · Ltrack + λ3 · Lsmooth + λ4 · Lrigid + λ5 · Liso
346
      We use grid-search to find the optimal values for these hyper parameters. During the scene flow
347   pretraining stage, we only use Lsf and Ltrack . λ1 and λ2 are set to 2 and 1 respectively. During the
348   second stage, we set λ1 = 2, λ2 = 1, λ3 = 0.3, and λ4 = λ5 = 0.2 for all datasets.

      4.1   DATASET AND T RAINING D ETAILS
353
354   We use the FlyingThings (Mayer et al., 2016) dataset to pre-train the scene flow model, and then train
355   and test on two separate datasets, TAPVid-Kubric (Doersch et al., 2022) and PointOdyssey (Zheng
356   et al., 2023).TAPVid-Kubric is a synthetic dataset with 9, 760 training videos and 250 testing videos.
357   Each video has 24 frames with resolution 256 × 256. In each validation video, 256 query points
358   are randomly sampled from all frames. The model is required to track these points in the rest of the
359   video. In the training set, we can generate an arbitrary number of query points for training. Because
360   the dataset is synthetic, we can generate ground truth depth for all points.
361   We first build a scene flow task based on the point tracking ground truth on TapVid-Kubric’s training
362   split and fine-tune the pretrained model on this task. Then, with the encoder and decoder backbone
363   frozen to save GPU memory, the full model is fine-tuned on 16-frame videos from the point tracking
364   dataset constructed from TapVid-Kubric (Doersch et al., 2022). For data augmentation, we use
365   random horizontal flipping, random scaling of the point-cloud coordinates, and random temporal
366   flipping. We use a batch size of 16 during the scene flow pre-training stages and a batch size of 8
367   during the training of the full model.
368   PointOdyssey provides much longer synthetic videos (over 1,000 frames and up to 4,000 frames)
369   for training and testing. The dataset includes 131/15/13 videos in the training/validation/testing split.
370   Because Point Odyssey does not provide scene flow ground truth, we augment a single frame with
371   random translations and rotations to simulate scene flow data. The model is first fine-tuned on these
372   simulated scene flow data before being trained on the entire training set. We observe that the model
373   supervised with the simulated scene flow data tends to converge faster in the second phase than the
374
      one trained with self-supervised loss.
375   We utilized a U-Net-based PointConvFormer (Wu et al., 2023) backbone. This is similar to PCF-
376   PWC in Table 1 but with our simplied cost volume (Eq. (3)) instead of theirs (Eq. (1)). During the
377   inference stage, we have query points that can appear in any place in the video. Hence, we run the
      model twice (forward and backward) for each video to track the query points in both directions.

390   4.2   M ETRICS
391
392   We extend previous 2D point tracking metrics to 3D:
393          • Occlusion accuracy (OA) is the accuracy of the occlusion prediction for each query point
394            on each frame.
395          • δ x measures the position accuracy of the predicted point on each frame where the point is
396            visible. A predicted point is considered correct if it is within x centimeters (cm) from the
397            ground truth position.
398          • δ avg is the average of δ x with x ∈ [1, 2, 4, 8, 16] (cm).
399
      For PointOdyssey where the videos are longer, we also adopt the survival rate metric (Luiten et al.,
400
      2023) (SR) which is the average number of frames before each tracked point drifts T cm away from
401
      the ground truth position, divided by the number of frames in the video, with T = 50.
402
403   We also report results with 2D metrics in order to compare with other 2D methods, but those are
404   secondary results because the primary goal of this paper is 3D tracking.

516   4.5   A BLATION E XPERIMENTS
517
518   We analyze the contribution of using multiple appearance features and the motion prior. Results
519   are presented in Table 5. We demonstrate that by employing multiple appearances and the mo-
520   tion prior, the performance across all 2D and 3D metrics consistently improves over using a single
521   appearance. Additionally, the performance improvement under occlusion surpasses that in normal
522
      conditions, showing that utilizing multiple appearances of the query point makes the model more
      resilient to occlusion. By integrating appearances from the past, the model can recover from a few
523
      bad appearances during or right before/after occlusion and achieve better results.
524
525   We also conduct ablation on the regularization terms. Results in Table 6 show that our smoothness
526   term is very useful in terms of improving the 3D point tracking results. The rigidity and isometry
527   loss from Luiten et al. (2023) provide marginal improvements in the 2D results.

      5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we proposed a 3D long-term point tracking approach based on fusing multiple cost
532
      volumes and motion information with a transformer model, which, to the best of our knowledge,
533
      is the first generalizable online long-term 3D point tracking approach using deep learning. By
534   selective decoding, we significantly increased the size of the input point cloud that fits into GPU
535   memory, which improves the performance of scene flow and 3D long-term point tracking. In terms
536   of 3D point tracking performance, our approach significantly outperforms scene flow chaining and
537   2D long-term point tracking approaches even if they are backprojected to 3D with ground truth
538   depths and camera poses, showing the benefits of tracking in 3D. We hope this paper could increase
539   the interest of the community in 3D long-term point tracking. In future work, we plan to utilize our
      3D point tracking framework in downstream tasks.
