009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce FedSOM, a clustering and interpolation module based on the Self-
012            organizing Map (SOM), which can be appended to any encoder and which can be
013            trained in a federated way either in tandem with the encoder or post training on the
014            resulting representations. The result is a discrete moduli space of representations
015            that provides for cluster or sample-level interpolation, hierarchical clustering, and
016
               can be leveraged as a function to cluster new vectors at test time. This moduli
               space can either be created from data alone or by glueing pre-existing clusters
017
               along regions of commonality, although we do not explore the latter in this work.
018
               Interpolation is accomplished by considering the n-dimensional tensor underly-
019            ing the SOM as a weighted undirected graph, where the weights are computed as
020            a function of the dispersion of the two clusters corresponding to the nodes bound-
021            ing the given edge. Any two clusters or samples may then be interpolated by com-
022            puting the lowest-cost path between their associated graph nodes via Dijkstra’s
023            algorithm. The method is validated on MNIST-like and parsed-binary malware
024            datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Self-supervised learning has been successfully applied to clustering and interpolation tasks in do-
029
      mains such as vision, text, and speech, in which datasets are large and homogeneous. However,
030
      recent advancements such as VIME [27] have facilitated the application of self-supervised learning
031   to clustering and interpolation on tabular data. The typical approach to creating representations for
032   downstream tasks is to minimize a contrastive loss, in which positive pairs are constructed by condi-
033   tionally sampling from column marginals. Non-contrastive methods such as VICReg [2] have also
034   shown great promise on downstream image classification tasks. Approaches to deep clustering vary
035   widely not only in terms of preferred data, but in terms of classical cluster approaches, i.e., centroid
036   based, density based, and online vs batch. In [44] they explore clustering utterance data with BERT
037   embeddings with a centroid-based approach. Works such as [8] and [29] rely on a softmax function
038   to assign cluster labels, meaning that the number of clusters must be fixed at train time. The number
039
      of clusters are also fixed at train time in [17]. While the vast majority of deep clustering methods are
      batch trained, online methods such as [45] also exist, in which the number of clusters need not be
040
      set at train time. In [48] the authors describe a GMM-type approach in which the number of clusters
041
      is cast as a hidden variable in an EM-type approach. Similar to multi-modal learning on tabular
042   data, multi-view learning has successfully been applied to both tabular and feature-homogeneous
043   datasets as in [46] and [47], in which multiple views take the place of tabular perturbations. The
044   latter seeks to discover correlations between multiple views of the same object. This differs from
045   our work in that perturbations are created for the sake of creating tightly-grouped representations
046   for downstream clustering tasks.
047
      Self-organizing map structures have long been leveraged both for clustering and interpolation. Train-
048   ing methodologies vary widely, but the main idea is that SOM tensor nodes correspond to cluster
049   labels. In [9], the authors construct SOM-VAE in which they combine the structure of a SOM with
050   a differentiable learning objective to learn the underlying discrete structure. Learning such a dis-
051   crete structure is an example of vector quantisation. See [23] in which a vector quantised-variational
052   autoencoder is constructed for homogeneous (images, video, speech) rather than tabular data. Inter-
053   polation is one of the primary applications of self-organizing map structures and there are many such
      approaches. Leveraging weighted-averages between weight vectors attached to SOM tensor nodes is

      a popular approach taken in [10], in which they leverage topological information contained in local
055   neighborhoods within the SOM to learn nonlinear relationships between samples in the input data.
056   A additional layer may also be added as in [11] that computes interpolations between two given

      1.1.1    L IMITATIONS
072
073   The number of nodes in the SOM tensor τ is exponential in the dimension of the SOM, so training
074   becomes prohibitive for dim τ ≥ 4 when the number of samples exceeds 1e5. Because train-
075   ing is performed in an unsupervised way, the contrastive learning objective can lead to label-
076   inhomogeneous clusters and over-partitioning of coherent clusters. SOM training is performed
077   classically, meaning that the method does not benefit from being structured as a loss minimiza-
078
      tion problem. This also represents a benefit, however, in that the method can be applied to existing
      representations.
079
080   Finally, malware representations result from feeding parsed binaries through the encoder, which
081   means information available to FedSOM is limited by the parser itself. Future work should include
082   representations created from raw bytes.
083
084   2     R ELATED W ORK
085
086   In this work we define interpolation between two points as identifying a finite sequence of samples
087   that are successively similar and represent a transformation from the first sample to the second. We
088   do not refer to interpolative points as in [23,25] in which a point is said to be interpolative if it
089   lies within the convex hull of the given dataset. Most prior art leveraging self organizing maps for
090   interpolation involves the construction a sequence of self organizing maps that functions as a discrete
091   set of intermediate steps between two given samples. This differs dramatically from our approach
092   in which interpolation takes place within a fixed SOM. The purpose of leveraging a self-organizing
093
      map is to create a discrete topological space to represent a typically continuous latent space, or to
      learning relationships between classes, as in [5]. As in the case of [14], the SOM clustering during
094
      training encourages convex clusters. As in [14], we considered training the encoder in tandem
095
      with the clustering algorithm, but settled on training the SOM on trained representations for wider
096   method applicability. There is no effort devoted to learning illusory underlying manifolds as in
097   [28] and instead focus on learning proximity-based cluster assignments via the SOM. In [7], they
098   construct smooth interpolations in latent space to construct interpolations via GANs in the space of
099   architectural design. Our notion of interpolation is akin to the Category Morphing process described
100   in [15]. Self-organizing maps have been used previously for interpolation via stepping successively
101   between neighboring nodes. This method was leveraged in [9] for interpolating between steps in
102   a time series. Interpolation between SOM nodes is performed in [9] by computing a weighted
103   average between weight vectors, where the weight is a function of the given input vectors. This
104
      is essentially a generative method in that representation vectors which do not correspond to any
      samples in the original dataset are leveraged for interpolation. In contrast, all interpolations in this
105
      work are performed using only samples that exist in the data.
106
107   The application of these methods to cybersecurity data is non-existent. Our self-organizing map
      approach requires the user to specify only an upper bound on the number of clusters by specifying

      the dimensions of the underlying tensor. Small perturbations in the encoder will change the inferred
109   metric on representation space, and therefore alter the geodesic interpolative path connecting two
110   test points. We avoid this sensitivity by creating a discrete moduli space of the input data optimized
111   for clustering and interpolation. Current SOM-based interpolation approaches involve either the dis-
112   cretization of each sample by training a SOM and considering the sequence of such, or by computing
113   a weighted average of weight vectors attached to neighboring nodes.

      3       P RELIMINARIES
117
118
      For the purpose of creating representations on which FedSOM is trained, we train both UMAP
      and neural network encoders, the latter by minimizing some form of self-supervised loss. We take
119
      the term interpolation to refer to a transformational sequence of samples that connects two given
120
      samples, rather than referring to the well-known mode of overfitting, as described in [3].

123
      3.1       S ELF -O RGANIZING M APS
124
      A self-organizing map is a collection of learnable weight vectors indexed by the coordinates of
125   a tensor, along with an update rule that encourages the weight vectors to closely match the input
126   vectors. This update rule includes a neighborhood function that determines the extent to which
127   weight vectors attached to neighbor nodes are updated. See 1 for a visual representation. The key
128   SOM hyperparameter is σ, the variance of the Gaussian kernel which determines the reach of the
129   neighborhood function. The result is a map of the input data such that weight vector similarity
130   recedes with distance within the indexing tensor.
131
132   3.2       DATA
133
134   Interpolation and clustering of cybersecurity data is the focus of this work, but the method itself can
135   be applied to a variety of sparse tabular datasets. In order to validate this claim, we train our models
136   not only on parsed binaries and network data, but on MNIST-like datasets. This latter family was
137   desirable for its sparsity and its well-defined structural and ontological groupings.
138
139   3.2.1       C YBERSECURITY
140
      Parsed portable executable datasets based on a parser are not amenable to smooth interpolation
141
      within representation space. It is highly unlikely that a given region in latent space could give rise to
142   feature vectors that could have been produced by parsing executable binaries. For this reason, inter-
143   polation is performed by considering only representations corresponding to samples in the corpus.
144
145
      In the case of Ember and Sorel20M, we considered only feature vectors corresponding to malicious
      binaries. This was done to focus our experiments, but future work should include clustering and
146
      interpolation with clean files included. In this way one may be able to discover and understand
147
      parasitic file infectors from the point of view of the leveraged parser.

150   4       M ETHOD
151
152   FedSOM is a federated self-organizing map with an underlying weighted graph structure that fa-
153   cilitates clustering and interpolation between any two clusters or any two samples. Interpolation
154   between clusters of parsed binaries can be leveraged to understand the parser by inspecting feature
155   distribution changes through the interpolation path.
156   We consider two approaches to embedding creation for the sake of FedSOM clustering and interpo-
157   lation. The first is an encoder with a self-supervised learning objective. This approach benefits from
158   the scalability and flexibility of batch training. The second is UMAP, which offers the advantage of
159   considering all data at once and allows for a globally optimized topological embedding for datasets
160   that satisfy the manifold hypothesis. See 1 for code and data.

177   4.1     N EURAL N ETWORK E NCODER
178
179   The encoder is a vanilla residual network that separately handles continuous and categorical values.
180   The categorical variables are discovered by an initial sweep through the data, based on a set of
181   heuristics. We cast a column as categorical if any one of the following holds: (1) contains only
182   bools, (2) contains only integers, (3) contains at least one string.
183   Categorical columns are encoded by an integer label encoder, and then embedded into a low-
184   dimensional Euclidean space via an embedding layer E. These embeddings are concatenated and
185   passed through two separate fully-connected layers. The output of the first passes through a sigmoid
186   and is combined with the latter via a Hadamard product. The result then passes through a third fully
187   connected layer after which we apply layer norm.
188   Replacing the encoder and embedding layer with batchnorm, the numerical columns pass through
189   an identical network. The numerical and categorical embeddings are then concatenated and passed
190   through two fully-connected + leaky relu layers, the latter function as the SimCLR projection head.
191
      All loss functions, with the exception of VICReg, are contrastive in nature. This means that for
192
      a given batch x, the loss is a function of the form L(x, x̃), where x̃ is a perturbed version of x
193
      and (x, x̃) is termed a positive pair. For the sake of constructing positive pairs, we leverage a
194   perturbations module.
195
196
197   4.1.1    TABULAR P ERTURBATIONS M ODULE
198
199
      Perturbation is performed by conditionally drawing from the marginal distribution of each column,
      which is a uniform over [min, max] if the column is numerical and a uniform over unique observa-
200
      tions if the column is categorical. The uniform distribution was chosen for the sake of maximizing
201
      sample speed.
202
203   We first perform a distribution learning step in which the sparsity of each column is measured via
204   Welford’s algorithm applied counts of x.is null() | (x == 0). Columns with sparsity above a set
205
      threshold are discarded before being passed to the encoder. An example of such a column would be
      the upper left pixel of the MNIST dataset.
206
207   We perturb batches according to the following recipe:
208
209           • remove columns above the upper sparsity threshold ψu
210           • for each column c, draw a Bernoulli random variable ρc ∼ B(1, p) for p fixed across
211             columns
212
213
              • if ρc = 1, draw batch size samples from the marginal distribution P (c) of column c
214           • if the sparsity of c is above the lower sparsity threshold ψb , then draw batch size random
215             variables ρr ∼ B(1, q) and for each ρr = 1, sample from U (Ωc ) if c is categorical and
                from U ([min(Ωc ), max(Ωc )]) if c is numerical.

231   4.2     UMAP E NCODER
232
233   For the sake of testing FedSOM on embeddings created independently of any sort of SOM structure,
234   we leverage UMAP on smaller datasets. We train separate UMAP encoders for categorical and
235   numerical columns. A third UMAP encoder is then trained on the concatenation of the output
236   from the first two. HDBSCAN is leveraged for optimization by maximizing normalized mutual
237   information between the given cluster labels and the labels learned by HDBSCAN.
238   Datasets are preprocessed by Z-score normalization applied to the nonzero rows of each numerical
239   column to preserve the sparsity of the original dataset.

242   4.3     S ELF O RGANIZING M AP
243
244
      The Self-Organizing Map update rule is given by wis+1 = wis + θ(β, i)α(v − wis ), where v is the
245   sample, θ is the neighborhood kernel function, β is the index of the best matching unit (node) in
246   the som grid, i is the index of the node being updated, s is the current iteration, α is the learning
247   rate, wis , wis+1 are and the old and new weight vectors for node i, respectively. The neighborhood
248   function is given by θ(β, i) = exp(−∥τ [i]−τ [β]∥2 /2σ 2 ), where τ is the tensor of node coordinates.
249   See Algorithm 2 for details.
250
      The update rule for FedSOM is similar, but differs in that the training corpus consists of the weights
251   of the several SOMs.

      4.3.1    T ENSOR D IMENSIONALITY
255
256
      The ability of the SOM to partition a neighborhood of representation space into distinct clusters is a
257
      function of the number of nodes neighboring a given node in the tensor. The maximum number of
258
      distinct clusters neighboring a given cluster is equal to 3dim(τ ) − 1, making train time exponential
259   in the number of tensor dimensions. We thus limited our experiments to dimensions two and three.
260

316   4.5     I NTERPOLATION
317
318   Samples x(i) , x(j) are mapped via the encoder E to representation vectors v (i) , v (j) =
319   E(x(i) ), E(x(j) ), and finally to nodes ni and nj in the SOM tensor via cluster assignment. The
320
      optimal interpolative path is discovered via Dijkstra’s algorithm. See Figure 2 for a visual represen-
      tation.
321
322   The smoothness of the interpolation is a function of the σ parameter in the self-organizing map,
323   as this parameter determines how finely the map partitions the set of encodings into clusters. The
      self-organizing map can be retrained with a smaller σ if smoother interpolations are desired.

345   5   E XPERIMENTS
346
347   Training FedSOM on a given dataset is a multi-step process. Hyperparameter search is performed
348   for embedding creation (NN and UMAP) and separately for SOM and FedSOM training via the tree-
349
      structured Parzen estimator in Optuna. Full hyperparameter ranges for every variable and configs
      containing optimal hyperparameters for both encoders, SOM, and FedSOM models are available in
350
      the code. We experiment on a variety of tabular datasets of widely varying type, with a focus on
351
      security datasets, including Ember and Sorel20M. Every FedSOM model consists of at least two
352   constituent SOMs. We compare SOM and FedSOM unsupervised clustering performance on both
353   UMAP and encoder-generated representations by computing normalized mutual information (NMI)
354   and adjusted rand score (ARS). Each MNIST-like image contains a caption giving label as well as
355   graph coordinates. Malicious binary file moduli spaces for three malware datasets show how various
356   malware families relate to each other from the point of view of the leveraged parser.
