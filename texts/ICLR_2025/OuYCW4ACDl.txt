009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper studies the problem that a learner aims to learn the reward function of
012            the expert from the interactions with the expert and how to interact with the ex-
013            pert. We formulate the problem as a stochastic bi-level optimization problem and
014            develop a double-loop algorithm ”general-sum interactive inverse reinforcement
015            learning” (GSIIRL). In the GSIIRL, the learner first learns the reward function of
016            the expert in the inner loop and then learns how to interact with the expert in the
017            outer loop. We theoretically prove the convergence of our algorithm and validate
018            our algorithm through simulations.

021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) has been widely applied to various domains including robotics
024   (Ziebart et al., 2008; Okal & Arras, 2016) and cyber security (Zhang et al., 2019; Elnaggar & Bezzo,
025   2018). In IRL, a learner aims to learn a reward function and an associated policy that are consistent
026   with the demonstrations of an expert. IRL focuses on learning the reward function since “the reward
027   function is the most succinct, robust, and transferable definition of the task” (Ng & Russell, 2000).
028
      When the reward function is learned, methods like reinforcement learning (RL) can be used to learn
      the corresponding policy.
029
030   In this paper, we consider a variant problem of IRL called interactive IRL. In the classic IRL, the
031   learner only passively observes the experts and learns from demonstrations in isolation from the
032   experts, so that the learner does not affect the expert’s demonstrated trajectories. In interactive IRL,
033   the learner actively interacts with the expert and thus can influence the trajectories of the expert.
034
      That is, the learner is not only an observer but also a participant. Consider a scenario where a human
      (i.e., expert) and a ground mobile robot (i.e., learner) navigate in a 2D plane while their destinations
035
      are distinct. The human usually has a higher priority so the robot needs to bypass or slow down for
036
      the human to cross. Meanwhile, the robot also wants to reach its destination as soon as possible.
037   Therefore, the interaction between the robot and the human is a general-sum game. Notice that the
038   robot has a human-independent goal (i.e., its own destination) and a human-dependent goal (i.e.,
039   bypassing the human). The reward function of the robot can be decomposed into two parts and each
040   part captures one of the goals. For example, the reward function in (El-Shamouty et al., 2020) is
041   decomposed into the distance of the robot to its goal location and the fact whether a collision between
042   the robot and the human happens. Motivated by the above example, this paper considers the general-
043   sum game where the learner’s reward function is decomposed into the expert-dependent and the
044   expert-independent parts. The learner knows the expert-independent reward function and is unaware
045
      of the expert-dependent reward function and the expert’s reward function. The learner aims to learn
      the expert reward from expert demonstrations during the interactions. The benefits of learning the
046
      expert’s reward include facilitating the learner’s policy learning and understanding a transferable
047
      expert model. When the transition probabilities of a Markov game (MG) substantially change,
048   the policy needs to be re-trained, but the reward function could be the same. Fully cooperative
049   (Palaniappan et al., 2017; Büning et al., 2022; Kamalaruban et al., 2019; Hadfield-Menell et al.,
050   2016) and fully competitive (Zhang et al., 2019; Wang & Klabjan, 2018; Goktas et al.) interactive
051   IRL problems are the special cases of our interactive IRL problem. The fully cooperative interactive
052   IRL problems assume the learner and the expert cooperate with each other and the reward functions
053   of the learner and the expert are identical. The fully competitive interactive IRL solves a zero-sum
      game where the reward functions of the learner and the expert are opposite.

054
      Our interactive IRL problem is relevant to classic IRL, multi-agent IRL (MA-IRL) (Lin et al., 2019;
055   Yu et al., 2019; Liu & Zhu, 2022), and multi-agent RL (MARL) (Lowe et al., 2017; Yu et al.,
056   2022; Kuba et al., 2021). In the classic IRL and MA-IRL, the learners are isolated from the experts.
057   However, the learner actively influences the trajectories of the expert in our interactive IRL problem.
058   As a result, classic IRL and MA-IRL can not be directly applied to our interactive IRL problem.
059   MARL is usually utilized to learn the policies with the given rewards. it can not used to learn the
060   reward function of the expert. Although the model-based MARL (Moerland et al., 2023) can learn
061   the reward function of the learner, it still can not learn the reward function of the expert. Therefore,
062   MARL can not solve our interactive IRL problem.
063   Contribution. The contributions of this paper are four-fold. First, we study the general-sum interac-
064   tive IRL problem, which includes fully cooperative and fully competitive IRL as special cases. We
065   formulate the problem as a stochastic bi-level optimization problem where the lower level learns a
066   reward function of the expert through IRL and the upper level learns a reward function for the learner
067   and a corresponding policy through RL. Second, we develop a double-loop algorithm ”general-sum
068   interactive inverse reinforcement learning” (GSIIRL) to solve the bi-level optimization problem.
069
      The outer loop solves the upper-level optimization problem and the inner loop solves the lower-level
      optimization problem. In both the inner loop and the outer loop, we use the projected stochastic
070
      gradient descent (SGD) to update the decision variables. In particular, we leverage the simultaneous
071
      perturbation stochastic approximation (SPSA) (Spall, 1992) to approximate the hypergradients in
072   the outer loop since the hypergradients include the Hessian of the lower-level objective function.
073   With the SPSA, the computational complexity per iteration of the outer loop reduces from O(T 3 )
074   to O(T 2 ) where T is the maximum length of the trajectories. Third, we show that the expected
075   hypergradients decrease at the rate of O( √1K ) where K is the iteration number of the outer loop.
076   Fourth, we validate our algorithm through three experiments.

      2   R ELATED WORK
080
081
082   IRL and Multiagent IRL. As (Ng & Russell, 2000) mentioned, IRL faces the challenge that one
083   demonstration can be explained by multiple reward functions. A number of methods have been
084   developed to address the challenge. The maximum margin method in (Ng & Russell, 2000) aims to
085   maximize the differences between the reward of the optimal action and that of the second-best action.
086   Feature expectation matching in Abbeel & Ng (2004) aims to minimize the difference between the
087
      feature expectation of a learned policy and the empirical feature of demonstrations. Based on the
      previous idea, maximum entropy IRL (ME-IRL) (Ziebart et al., 2008) finds the reward function by
088
      maximizing the entropy while imposing the feature expectation matching as a constraint.
089
090   The aforementioned works are only focused on a single expert. When multiple experts interact with
091   each other in the environment, it becomes MA-IRL. To learn the reward functions of all experts,
092   papers (Lin et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2019; Liu & Zhu, 2022) extend the above single-expert IRL to
093
      multi-expert IRL where a learner or a group of learners recover the reward functions of all experts
      from their demonstrations. Consider a MG which is controlled by two experts. Each expert knows
094
      its reward function which depends on the MG state and/or the joint action of the experts. Since they
095
      know the reward functions, the experts can execute the policies that maximize the reward functions
096   and provide the induced trajectories of the MG as demonstrations. In these papers, the learners
097   only passively observe the experts and learn from demonstrations in isolation from the experts. The
098   learner cannot influence the MG but only observes the demonstrations.
099
      Fully cooperative and fully competitive interactive IRL. Recent papers (Palaniappan et al., 2017;
100
      Büning et al., 2022; Kamalaruban et al., 2019; Hadfield-Menell et al., 2016) study a fully cooperative
101
      setting where the learner has the same reward function as the expert. The learner learns this shared
102   reward function. The papers formulate the interactive IRL problem as a Stackelberg game where
103   the lower level learns the policy of the expert and the upper level finds the reward function of the
104   expert. Papers (Zhang et al., 2019; Wang & Klabjan, 2018; Goktas et al.) study a fully competitive
105   setting where the reward function of the learner is the opposite of that of the expert and formulate
106   the problem as a zero-sum game. The learner learns the opposite of the expert’s reward function.
107   These interactive IRL methods impose strong assumptions on the relationship between the expert’s
      reward function and the learner’s reward function.

108
      In this paper, the relationship between the reward functions of the learner and the expert is arbitrary.
109   The fully cooperative and fully competitive cases are special cases of the above scenario. Let us
110   ignore the expert-independent reward function. If the expert-dependent reward function is identical
111   to the expert’s reward function, it reduces to the fully cooperative case. If the expert-dependent
112   reward function and the expert’s reward function are opposite, it reduces to the fully competitive
113   case.
114
      Bi-level optimization. Bi-level optimization has been applied to many machine learning prob-
115   lems, including meta-learning (Lee et al., 2019; Xu & Zhu, 2022), hyperparameter optimization
116   (Pedregosa, 2016; Xu & Zhu, 2023), and IRL (Liu & Zhu, 2022). The descent method (Kolstad &
117   Lasdon, 1990; Xu & Zhu, 2023) is a classic approach for solving bi-level optimization problems.
118   This method requires to calculate the second-order Hessian of the lower-level objective function.
119   To avoid computing the Hessian, a common way is to use the finite difference method (Strikw-
120   erda, 2004) to approximate the Hessian. This paper adopts SPSA to further reduce the number of
121   measurements by disturbing all directions of the decision variable at one time, so it only requires 2
122   measurements of the objective function.

124   3    M OTIVATING EXAMPLE
125
126   Consider the scenario of a human and a robot moving and coordinating in a 2D plane.
127
128   Human model. The human moves in the 2D plane by changing his/her velocity vh and wants
129
      to reach his/her destination gh as soon as possible. This goal is captured by the reward function
      −∥gh −ph ∥ where ph is the position of the human. The human can measure ph and assess the reward
130
      function. When interacting with the robot, the human exhibits different emotions. For example, the
131
      human is angry when the robot is close or he/she is slow, the human is happy when he/she moves
132   fast (Bera et al., 2019).
133
134   Robot model. Analogous to the human, the robot decides the velocity vr to reach its destination gr
135
      as soon as possible. The robot can measure its current position pr and access the reward function
      −∥gr − pr ∥2 . In addition, the robot aims to keep the human with positive emotions like happiness
136
      during the interactions. This goal of the robot is captured by the reward function h(pr , ph , vr , vh ).
137
      The function h is unknown to the robot but the robot can measure the value of h(pr , ph , vr , vh ) given
138   any locations pr , ph and velocities vr , vh . For example, the robot captures the facial expressions of
139   the human through a camera and recognizes his/her emotions using a deep neural network-based
140   classifier. Positive emotions like happiness are interpreted as positive values and negative emotions
141   like anger are interpreted as negative values (Arakawa et al., 2018). These are the measured values of
142   h(pr , ph , vr , vh ). The overall reward function of the robot is given by −∥gr −pr ∥2 +h(pr , ph , vr , vh )
143   where the second term is dependent on the human but the first term is not.
144   Robot learning. The robot aims to learn the expert’s reward function −∥gh − ph ∥ from observed
145   positions and velocities of the human.

148   4    M ODEL AND PROBLEM STATEMENT
149
150   In this section, we will introduce the interactive IRL problem, which generalizes the example in
151   Section 3.
152   Markov Game model. We model the interactions between a learner and an expert based on a finite
153   horizon MG (S, A, P, T, rld + rli , re , γ). The elements of the MG are defined as follows.
154         • S ≜ Sl × Se is a non-empty Borel state space where Sl and Se are associated with the
155           learner and the expert, respectively; s ∈ S, sl ∈ Sl , se ∈ Se .
156         • A ≜ Al × Ae where Al and Ae are non-empty Borel spaces of actions associated with the
157           learner and the expert, respectively; a ∈ A, al ∈ Al , ae ∈ Ae .
158         • P (s′ |s, a) is the probability density for the state transition from s to s′ by taking joint action
159           a.
160         • T is the finite time horizon.
161         • rld + rli is the reward function of the learner. It includes two parts: the expert-dependent
              reward function rld (s, a) and the expert-independent reward function rli (sl , al ). The func-

               tion rld maps the state-action pairs (s, a) to bounded rewards, and the function rli maps the
163            learner’s state-action pairs (sl , al ) to bounded rewards.
164          • re is the reward function of the expert which maps the state-action pairs (s, a) to bounded
165            rewards.
166          • γ ∈ (0, 1) is the discount factor.
167   Referring to the motivating example in Section 3, the robot is the learner and the human is the ex-
168   pert. The human-dependent reward function rld is h(pr , ph , vr , vh ), the human-independent reward
169   function rli is −∥gr − pr ∥2 and re is the reward function −∥gh − ph ∥2 of the human .

188   Goal of the learner. Using the trajectories of the MG, the learner aims to learn re .

      Related problems. The aforementioned MG is a general-sum game between the learner and the
190
      expert. For the special case with rld (s, a) = re (s, a), ∀(s, a) ∈ S×A and rli (sl , al ) = 0, ∀(sl , al ) ∈
191
      Sl × Al , the reward functions of the learner and the expert are identical and this case reduces to the
192   fully cooperative interactive IRL in (Palaniappan et al., 2017; Büning et al., 2022; Kamalaruban
193   et al., 2019; Hadfield-Menell et al., 2016). When rld (s, a) = −re (s, a) , ∀(s, a) ∈ S × A and
194   rli (sl , al ) = 0, ∀(sl , al ) ∈ Sl × Al ,, the MG reduces to the fully competitive interactive IRL in
195   (Zhang et al., 2019; Wang & Klabjan, 2018). In classic IRL (Ziebart et al., 2008; Abbeel & Ng,
196   2004; Ziebart et al., 2010; Lin et al., 2019; Liu & Zhu, 2022), the learner only passively observes the
197   demonstrations of the expert and does not influence the expert. In contrast, the learner in our MG
198   actively interacts with the expert. Consider the case where the MG is independent of the learner,
199   i.e., the state transitions and the reward function of the expert are independent of the action of the
200
      learner. The case reduces to the classic IRL.
201   As πe is influenced by πl , the learner actively influences the trajectories of the expert. We can not
202   directly utilize classic IRL and MA-IRL for our interactive IRL problem. MARL is unable to solve
203   our interactive IRL problem because the learner learns π ∗ instead of re through MARL.

534   9   C ONCLUSION
535
536   This paper develops a general-sum interactive inverse reinforcement learning framework to learn the
537   reward functions and policies of the learner and the expert during the interactions. This framework
538   includes the fully cooperative framework and the fully competitive framework as special cases.
539   Then we propose the GSIIRL algorithm and theoretically quantify its convergence rate. From the
      experiments, we show the effectiveness of the GSIIRL in both continuous and discrete environments.

       A.10.2    H UMAN - ROBOT INTERACTION
1377
1378   In the simulation, the policies for the human and the robot are calculated through Multi-Agent Deep
1379   Deterministic Policy Gradient (MADDPG) (Lowe et al., 2017). During the training process, noises
1380   are added to the action to increase the exploration. Once the policies are generated, the further
1381   calculations use deterministic policies.
1382
       The state of the robot is its location sr = (xr , yr ) ∈ R2 , the action of the robot includes the
1383   horizontal and vertical velocities and defined as ar = (vrx , vry ), where vrx ∈ [−0.1, 0.1], vry ∈
1384   [−0.1, 0.1]. Similarly, the state of the human is sh = (xh , yh ) ∈ R2 , the action of the human is
1385   ah = (vhx , vhy ), where vhx ∈ [−0.1, 0.1], vhy ∈ [−0.1, 0.1]. At each time step t, the robot chooses
1386   the action ar (t) based on the current joint state (sr (t), sh (t)) and moves to the next state xr (t+1) =
1387   xr (t) + vrx (t), yr (t + 1) = yr (t) + vry (t). Analogously, the motion dynamics of the human is given
1388   by xh (t + 1) = xh (t) + vhx (t), yh (t + 1) = yh (t) + vhy (t). In the experiment, the robot starts
1389   from an initial state sr (0) ∈ [−0.25, 0.25] × [−0.4, 0.5] and aims to reach a circle goal region whose
1390   center is at (0, 0.5) with radius 0.05. The human starts from sh (0) ∈ [0.4, 0.5] × [−0.25, 0.25] and
1391   aims to reach a circle goal region whose center is at (−0.5, 0) with radius 0.05. Both the robot and
1392
       the human are penalized when a collision happens.

1395
       There are 8 nodes and 10 edges. Each node represents a machine and each edge represents an exploit
1396
       between two nodes. The decision-making of the defender and the attacker is modeled as an MG.
1397
       The state s ∈ {0, 1}8 , represents the condition of each node where the value 1 means the current
1398   node is compromised by the attacker, and the value 0 is vice versa. In each action pair, the attacker
1399   chooses one edge to attack and the defender chooses one edge to block. Suppose the attack chooses
1400   to attack the edge {i, j}. If the node i is already compromised and the defender does not block this
1401   edge, there is a probability for node j to be compromised. For other situations, the node j keeps
1402   clean. Each edge has a cost for the attacker to utilize and a cost for the defender to block. The attack
1403   receives a reward when it successfully compromises a new node. The net reward of the attacker for
       each state-action pair is the sum of the reward and the cost. For the defender, the expert-dependent
