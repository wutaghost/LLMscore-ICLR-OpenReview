009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Due to the need of interacting with the world, embodied agents are required to
012            possess comprehensive task-relevant knowledge, long-horizon planning capabil-
013            ity, and a swift response speed. Large language models (LLMs), owing to their
014            rich general knowledge, recently achieve promising results in open-world em-
015            bodied tasks, like the world exploration in Minecraft. However, the outputs of
016
               LLMs are descriptive sentences or code, which are slow to generate and not end-
               to-end, as a translator is required to translate the LLM outputs into actions to per-
017
               form. To address these limitations, we introduce the large auto-regressive model
018
               (LARM). LARM leverages environment observations as input and predicts subse-
019            quent actions in an auto-regressive manner. Compared with LLM based methods,
020            LARM directly predicts the next skill for execution according to the current ob-
021            servation. In addition, considering that the commonly adopted training paradigms
022            do not reflect the mutual influence and dependency between actions and obser-
023            vations, we develop a novel data format named auto-regressive node transmis-
024            sion structure and assemble a corresponding dataset to train LARM. Combining
025            these techniques, LARM successfully harvests enchanted equipment in Minecraft,
026            which demands significantly more complex decision-making chains than the high-
027
               est achievements of prior best methods. Besides, the speed of LARM is 6.8×
               faster than LLMs with similar parameter volume.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, remarkable progress has been achieved in various artificial intelligence (AI) fields
033   LeCun et al. (2015) like computer vision He et al. (2016) and natural language processing Kenton
034   & Toutanova (2019), but most of them lack the capacity to physically interact with the real world.
035   To address this disconnect, the concept of embodied AI is introduced Chrisley (2003). Early em-
036   bodied agents are predominantly developed on simulation platforms for specific tasks such as object
037   grasping and indoor navigation Savva et al. (2019). While notable advancements are achieved, these
038   agents tend to be specialist models confined to isolated tasks Huang et al. (2023). To overcome
039
      this limitation, recent studies, including this work, employ Minecraft Baker et al. (2022); Fan et al.
      (2022); Guss et al. (2019) as a benchmark to explore embodied agents with open-ended objectives
040
      and long-horizon reasoning chains.
041
042   The early methods for developing such agents primarily rely on reinforcement learning, the explo-
043   ration of which is inefficient and results in limited performance Yuan et al. (2023). Recent works
044   begin to investigate the use of large language models (LLMs) Brown et al. (2020). Owing to the
045
      extensive general knowledge and formidable reasoning capabilities of LLMs, these methods demon-
      strate promising results with significantly reduced domain-specific engineering efforts Wang et al.
046
      (2023a). Nevertheless, LLMs continue to exhibit a number of limitations, rendering them ill-suited
047
      for embodied AI tasks. First of all, the outputs of LLMs are usually sentences or code Zhao et al.
048   (2023), which are generated through iterative token prediction. This prediction mode necessitates N
049   inference operations for N tokens, resulting in sluggish response speed, which is critical for embod-
050   ied applications. Secondly, recent research suggests that a huge model size is important for an LLM
051   to master the ability to generate correct descriptions or code Achiam et al. (2023). Nevertheless, the
052   computing resource for deploying embodied agents is usually very limited. Moreover, LLMs are
053   commonly trained through web data based text completion, which does not reflect how the actions
      affect the environment and how the environment update alters the optimal action.

074   To address the limitations of LLM, we propose Large Auto-Regressive Model (LARM). Taking both
075   text information and vision observation as input, LARM engages in real-time interaction with the
076   environment and predicts subsequent actions in an auto-regressive manner. Instead of generating
077
      a descriptive sentence composed of multiple tokens, LARM directly produces a single token to
      determine the next action. In addition, LARM shares a similar basic network architecture with
078
      existing LLMs, enabling us to utilize LLM weights to initialize the parameters of LARM. In this
079
      way, LARM is equipped with both rich general knowledge and high inference efficiency.
080
081   For training, we first employ a 34G multi-modal dataset Fan et al. (2022), sourced from Wiki
082   Minecraft webpages, to provide LARM with general knowledge about the Minecraft world. How-
083
      ever, this web dataset does not sufficiently describe the mutual dependency relations among the
      environment, observations, and actions. To bridge this gap, we introduce a unique data organization
084
      structure termed as Auto-Regressive Node Transmission Structure (ARNTS). Within ARNTS, each
085
      data sample is conceptualized as a node, encompassing text information, multi-view images, and the
086   expected subsequent action. A chronological dependency exists among these nodes. The process of
087   accomplishing a complex target can be interpreted as transmissions between different nodes. Ad-
088   hering to this data structure, we manually collect an ARNTS dataset comprising 2,589 data samples,
089   which cover diversified targets, weathers, and biomes. Leveraging this dataset to train the LARM
090   agent, the agent can effectively learn the dependency knowledge among various information types.
091
      Through integrating the aforementioned efforts, we develop an agent capable of crafting enchanted
092   diamond tools. As shown in Fig. 1, crafting enchanted diamond tools requires a far more intricate
093   step chain compared to existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods Wang et al. (2023a); Zhao et al.
094   (2023), which are only capable of crafting standard diamond tools. Besides, the inference speed of
095   LARM is 6.8× faster on average compared to an LLM with comparable parameter volume. This
096   work reveals a new path of developing data-driven embodied intelligence.

099
100
      Embodied benchmarks. Embodied AI recently garners significant attention due to its potential
101
      to bridge the gap between virtual algorithms and physical interactions Bredeche et al. (2018). The
102   works in embodied AI primarily focus on enhancing the interaction of agents with the environment,
103   and many benchmarks are established Duan et al. (2022). Existing benchmarks can be catego-
104   rized into two classes, simulator-based and game-based. Simulator-based benchmarks are estab-
105   lished upon popular simulation platforms, such as MuJoCo Todorov et al. (2012) and Isaac Gym
106   Makoviychuk et al. (2021). Through complex engineering efforts on dynamics modeling, these sim-
107   ulation platforms mimic the interaction kinematics between agents and manipulated objects in the
      real world well. Some recent benchmarks designed based on these simulation platforms include


                                                          2
108
      RLbench James et al. (2020), HumanoidBench Meser et al. (2024), etc. Nevertheless, running these
109   simulation platforms could take much computing resources even though only simulating a very lim-
110   ited range of the environment. Due to this problem, existing works conducted on these benchmarks
111   usually only focus on low-level motion control Zhuang et al. (2023). By contrast, we mainly study
112   the high-level and long-horizon embodied tasks in this work and assume the low-level policies have
113   been well developed. Another line of embodied AI benchmarks is the game-based ones. Different
114   from simulator-based benchmarks, game-based benchmarks do not pay much attention to simulating
115   motion dynamics Anderson et al. (2018). Alternatively, they mainly focus on providing environ-
116   ments for performing high-level actions Savva et al. (2019). Hence, this category of benchmarks is
117
      more suitable for exploring how to develop agents with complex decision-making capabilities.
118   Minecraft agents. Minecraft is an open-ended platform suitable for exploring building agents with
119   long-horizon planning capabilities Fan et al. (2022). It simulates diverse weathers, biomes, and
120   mobs in an unlimited 3D virtual world and provides well-designed low-level APIs of basic actions
121   PrismarineJS (2013). According to the output formats of models, we classify existing works into two
122   categories, i.e., atom-based and skill-based. Early methods mostly adopt the atom-based paradigm,
123
      which means the output of the model is directly a low-level atom action, e.g., a short movement,
      a mouse click, or a keyboard press Frazier & Riedl (2019). The advantage of this paradigm is
124
      its control flexibility. However, due to the huge potential decision space, such atom-based agents
125
      are quite challenging for optimization, and thus these works pay their main attention to devising
126   strategies for alleviating the optimization complexity Scheller et al. (2020).
127
128   Instead of using atom actions, the output of methods based on the skill-based paradigm is directly
129
      the skill, such as chopping down a tree or crafting a table. The skill could be a well-trained policy
      based on reinforcement learning or provided APIs. As the available skills help agents avoid the need
130
      to learn low-level skills, the training difficulty is greatly alleviated. Therefore, the works based on
131
      this paradigm concentrate better on how to boost the long-term scheduling ability of agents. Voyager
132   Wang et al. (2023a) is representative among the skill-based methods. It successfully crafts diamond
133   tools using a training-free system based on GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023). Similarly, DEPS Wang et al.
134   (2023b) develops a more comprehensive LLM system, yielding satisfactory results. However, these
135   methods rely heavily on existing knowledge of LLMs. If an LLM does not have accurate knowledge
136   about this task, the agents cannot complete it successfully. Besides, LLMs solely support text input,
137   which is inadequate for encapsulating environmental information. To surmount this hurdle, certain
138   studies train large vision-language models through fine-tuning LLMs Zhao et al. (2023); Feng et al.
139   (2024). Despite these advancements, the output of these models remains descriptive text. In this
140
      work, we aim to explore how to develop agents with long-term scheduling intelligence.
141   Large language models. LLMs draw broad attention from the research and industrial communities
142   due to their rich general knowledge and the ability to generate the answers to diverse kinds of
143   questions Chang et al. (2024). Early LLMs mainly serve as pre-training weights for downstream
144   tasks Devlin et al. (2019). GPT-3 emerges as a milestone in the evolution of LLMs, as it takes
145   the next token prediction problem as the pre-training task and showcases remarkable open-world
146
      generalization capabilities across diverse tasks Brown et al. (2020). Subsequently, the fine-tuning of
      GPT-3 using reinforcement learning with human feedback leads to the creation of ChatGPT, a model
147
      that displays an impressive breadth of general knowledge OpenAI (2023). Further advancements
148
      result in the creation of GPT-4, reinforcing the advantages of increasing model size and training
149   data Achiam et al. (2023). Although these LLMs present remarkable performance, their model
150   weights are mostly inaccessible to the research community. To bridge this gap, some notable open-
151   source works are released. These works train models with fewer parameters while still achieving
152   promising results, such as LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023). However, a significant limitation of LLMs
153   is their inability to interpret information in images, which are vital for humans to perceive the world.
154
      To overcome this problem, researchers devise strategies that inject vision information into LLMs and
155   enable LLMs to perceive images. The common method is fine-tuning a small amount of network
156   parameters using numerous language-image data pairs to bridge the representation gap between text
157   and images Ding et al. (2023). In this way, some large vision-language models like LLaVA Liu
158   et al. (2024) and Flamingo Alayrac et al. (2022) are derived. While these models can describe the
159   content in provided images, they lack the capability to interact with the real world, which needs the
160   models to predict the correct actions to execute according to the environment status. To address this
161   limitation, some works like EmbodiedGPT boost the performance of LLMs on scheduling actions
      through fine-tuning models using chain of thought data Mu et al. (2024). The fine-tuning process is

190   3.1    P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
191
192   What we study in this work can be conceptualized as an auto-regressive prediction problem in-
193   volving long sequences, and is effectively framed as a Markov Decision Process, symbolized by
194   E = (S, A, P). In this formulation, S is the space encompassing all possible states of the envi-
195   ronment, A is the set of feasible actions that the policy can take, and P represents the probability
196
      distribution that governs state transitions given a current state and action. At any discrete time step
      t, the environment resides in a state st ∈ S, and the corresponding observation ot by a policy π is
197
      a function of this state, expressed as ot = f (st ). This observation ot is then utilized to select the
198
      subsequent action according to at ∼ π(ot ), where at ∈ A. After the action at is executed, the envi-
199   ronment transitions to the next state following the probability distribution st+1 ∼ P(st+1 | st , at ).
200
201   In tackling the long-horizon prediction problem, the objective is to navigate through a sequence of
202
      intermediate states s1 , s2 , . . . , sT −1 to ultimately reach the target state sT at the final time step T .
      This requires the policy to generate a series of actions a0 , a1 , . . . , aT −1 such that each action at
203
      transitions the environment from state st to the next state st+1 , adhering to the dynamics prescribed
204
      by the transition probability distribution P. Formally, the trajectory through the state sequence is
205   constructed iteratively. Starting from an initial state s0 , the policy selects an action a0 based on
206   the initial observation o0 = f (s0 ) leading to a transition s1 ∼ P(s1 | s0 , a0 ). This process is
207   repeated iteratively such that at any time step t, the action at selected from the policy π depends on
208   the observation ot = f (st ), thereby driving the state evolution st+1 ∼ P(st+1 | st , at ). It is crucial
209   that each intermediate state st is accurately predicted and achieved in sequence to ensure the policy
210   attains the target state sT by the final time step T .

      The real-time observation ot of LARM at the timestamp t consists of both text and image informa-
234   tion. The text information includes the target task description, agent information, and environment
235   feedback. Specifically, the agent information contains the last skill performed by the agent, the
236   current 3D position coordinate of the agent in the Minecraft world, and the inventory list (a list of
237   the resources that the agent owns). For the environment feedback, two kinds of information are
238   included, i.e., whether the last skill performed by the agent is executed successfully and the game
239   response message to that skill. All the text information is tokenized into text tokens by a frozen
240   tokenizer, the weight of which is initialized from BERT Kenton & Toutanova (2019).
241   Different from previous Minecraft agents that take a monocular image as input Zhao et al. (2023),
242   LARM adopts N views of images in different directions, providing a more comprehensive descrip-
243   tion of the surrounding environment. The N multi-view images are tokenized as N × N I image
244   tokens by a frozen CLIP encoder. However, since there are N images, the number of obtained to-
245   kens is increased by N times and would result in a significant computational burden if using all these
246   tokens as input to the subsequent decoders. To resolve this problem, we utilize a trainable Q-Former
247   Li et al. (2023) module to reduce the number of image tokens from N × N I to N I , as shown in
248
      Fig. 2. Besides reducing image tokens, this Q-Former module is also helpful for fusing the feature
      existing in multi-view images. After the Q-Former, the image tokens are further transformed by a
249
      trainable projector linking the vision branch with decoders. The decoders take both text and image
250
      tokens as input to conduct feature interaction. Given that the decoders contain numerous parameters
251   and are challenging to train, we initialize the parameters from the LLaVA weight Liu et al. (2023)
252   and freeze them during training. Additionally, a trainable LoRA Hu et al. (2021) is applied to assist
253   the model in understanding Minecraft knowledge.
254
      The output of the decoders is multiple tokens and we compress them into a single token with a
255
      trainable projector. We name the token produced by this projector as skill token, which contains
256
      the information about which skill should be executed according to the observation. By conducting
257   cosine distance Nguyen & Bai (2010) based matching between the skill token and the many pre-
258   generated skill embeddings, the most similar skill is selected for the agent to perform. The chosen
259   skill alters both the status of the agent and the environment. LARM repeats the above process in an
260   auto-regressive way until an End token is matched, marking the end of this skill execution chain.

263
264   We develop a two-stage protocol to train LARM, including Wiki pre-training and ARTNS fine-
265   tuning. Although the parameters of LARM are initialized from LLaVA, its initial knowledge about
266   the Minecraft world is very limited. Therefore, we first employ 34G webpage data Fan et al. (2022)
267   crawled from Wiki to pre-train LARM. Specifically, we remove the text comprising fewer than 30
268   letters in the webpage data. In this way, 63,666 sentences are obtained. The sentences are matched
269   with the closest images on webpages and provided to the model. In Wiki pre-training, we mask a
      part of these sentences and train the model to predict the masked words. This helps the pre-trained

290   During ARTNS fine-tuning, we fine-tune the LARM policy with our defined ARNTS data. In
291   ARTNS, we represent the process of performing a multi-step task as a sequential chain, which
292   includes nodes and edges that link these nodes. As illustrated in Fig. 3, the nodes denote the agent
293   and environment status {st }Tt=0 , and edges represent the corresponding selected skills {at }Tt=0 . At
294   timestamp t, when a skill at is performed, the status transitions from a node st to the next node
295   st+1 . Notably, for i < j, reaching sj does not necessarily require first arriving in si . There could be
296
      multiple viable transition paths, reflecting the huge decision-making challenges in Minecraft. For
      example, as shown in Fig. 3, making a crafting table requires planks but not sticks, which means
297
      Node C depends on the completion of the target in Node A but not Node B. In addition, a node could
298
      depend on multiple previous nodes, like arriving in Node D relies on first reaching Node A, Node
299   B, and Node C, simultaneously. In accordance with the ARTNS format, we manually operate an
300   agent to complete various tasks in Minecraft and record the data. Through collecting data in diverse
301   biomes and weather, we obtain a dataset consisting of 2,589 data pairs. Each pair contains the explo-
302   ration trajectory ID, data pair ID, time, weather, biome name, target, agent position, agent inventory
303   list, the name of the last performed skill and its corresponding execution result, the next skill, and
304   multi-view images. Some multi-view image examples are visualized in Fig. 4. Then, we annotate
305   the dependency relations among nodes, which are represented as a graph like the blue dotted lines
306   illustrated in Fig. 3. This graph describes the skills in some nodes are prerequisites for other nodes,
307
      and the agent should learn this knowledge to understand how to schedule future skills.

410   Inference efficiency. Besides the tech tree mastery achievement, inference efficiency is also impor-
411
      tant. However, many of the aforementioned methods are implemented based on LLM APIs. The
      inference speed of these methods is significantly affected by network latency, while LARM is a
412
      model deployed in local machines. Additionally, the GPUs used by these methods may vary and are
413
      uncontrollable. Therefore, directly comparing their inference speeds is meaningless. Thankfully, the
414   methods built upon LLM share a similar Transformer architecture. Thus, it is reasonable to compare
415   LARM with an LLM of the same parameter volume to validate inference efficiency. In line with
416   this thought, we compare the inference time costs of LARM and the LLaVA-1.6 model with both
417   7B parameters. They are tested in 100 cases to compute the average inference time, with each case
418   corresponding to a node in Fig. 3. The output of LLaVA is prompted to be as concise as possible.
419   This experiment is performed using one RTX3090 GPU. Through this experiment, we find that the
420   average inference time of LLaVA on the 100 cases is 5.78 seconds while LARM is 0.85 seconds,
421   which indicates that LARM is 6.8× more efficient. The high efficiency of LARM stems from its
422
      distinctive design, which enables the LLM to generate a single token for every skill.

      In this work, we have focused on strategies of how to design and implement long-horizon embod-
479
      ied intelligence in an efficient manner. We have introduced the concept of the large auto-regressive
480   model and implemented a corresponding model LARM, an embodied agent predicting subsequent
481   skills in an auto-regressive manner. To train LARM, a new data organization structure has been pro-
482   posed and a corresponding dataset has been collected. Extensive experiments have been conducted
483   to validate the effectiveness of LARM. Compared with previous methods, LARM has presented
484   stronger long-horizon scheduling ability and faster inference speed. LARM is the first method capa-
485   ble of crafting enchanted diamond tools in Minecraft. We hope this work to significantly contribute
      to the development of high-level embodied intelligence.
