009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              LLM-as-a-Judge and reward models are widely used alternatives of multiple-
012              choice questions or human annotators for large language model (LLM) evalua-
013              tion. Their efficacy shines in evaluating long-form responses, serving a critical
014              role as evaluators of leaderboards and as proxies to align LLMs via reinforcement
015              learning. However, despite their popularity, their effectiveness in diverse contexts,
016              such as non-English prompts, factual verification, or challenging questions, re-
017              mains unexplored. In this paper, we conduct a comprehensive analysis of auto-
018              mated evaluators, reporting several key findings on their behavior. First, we dis-
019
                 cover that English evaluation capabilities significantly influence language-specific
                 evaluation capabilities, often more than the language proficiency itself, enabling
020
                 evaluators trained in English to easily transfer their skills to other languages. Sec-
021
                 ond, we identify critical shortcomings, where LLMs fail to detect and penalize er-
022              rors, such as factual inaccuracies, cultural misrepresentations, and the presence of
023              unwanted language. Finally, we find that state-of-the-art evaluators struggle with
024              challenging prompts in either English or Korean, underscoring their limitations in
025              assessing or generating complex reasoning questions. We release the dataset and
026              codes used in this research.1

      Figure 1 summarizes the key findings of this research. First, LLM-as-Judges and RMs demonstrate
055   equal proficiency in Korean, a language they have not been trained in. Interestingly, the capability
056   of models to evaluate Korean question and answer (QA) pairs can be predicted by their performance
057   on R EWARD B ENCH (Lambert et al., 2024), an English meta-evaluation dataset. This predictive abil-
058   ity surpasses that of Korean language benchmarks (Son et al., 2024; 2023; Kim, 2024; Kim et al.,
059   2022), challenging the expectation that proficient Korean speakers would naturally excel at evaluat-
060   ing Korean text. We conjecture that a significant portion of evaluation ability is language-agnostic,
061   which explains this positive correlation. Secondly, however, we identify weaknesses in the trans-
062   ferability of proprietary and fine-tuned LLMs. These models fail to detect and penalize instances
063
      containing false information or cultural misrepresentations. This limitation indicates that they may
      not serve effectively as factual verifiers in unfamiliar languages or cultural contexts. Finally, auto-
064
      mated evaluators struggle to assess QA pairs that include questions requiring complex reasoning in
065
      either language, underscoring the need for better judge models.
066
067   The main contributions of this work are as follows:
068
069         1. We empirically analyze the circumstances under which automated evaluators are effective
070            in new languages and when not.
071         2. We identify the shortcomings of state-of-the-art (SOTA) judge models in evaluating chal-
072            lenging prompts, even in English, highlighting the need for improved evaluators.
073
074
            3. We release K UDGE, a bilingual meta-evaluation dataset—the first of its kind in Korean. It
               also includes a challenge subset designed to enhance the evaluation of English models.

077   2   R ELATED W ORKS
078
079
      Traditional metrics such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (Lin, 2004), which measure
080   lexical overlap between texts, have long been the standard for evaluating generated text. However, as
081   LLMs advance, they are now capable of producing semantically equivalent yet syntactically varied
082   expressions that these metrics cannot accurately assess (Chung et al., 2023). Although human evalu-
083   ation may be ideal, it is often impractical due to resource constraints (Li et al., 2023). Consequently,
084   model-based evaluation (i.e., LLM-as-a-Judges and RMs) has emerged as an alternative, employing
085   LLMs in a peer-review-like setup.
086
087   Automated Evaluators Initially, the LLM-as-a-Judge approach involved prompting LLMs to
088   evaluate outputs (Zheng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Verga et al., 2024). However, recent efforts
089   have shifted towards specifically training LLMs to enhance their evaluative accuracy (Kim et al.,
090   2024c; Vu et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024e). LLM-as-a-Judge models offer high
091   flexibility, employing customizable rubrics (Ye et al., 2023) and scoring ranges (Dong et al., 2024).
092   Typically, these models are prompted to provide feedback (Wang et al., 2024e;d) before their evalu-
093
      ations, which boosts performance and enhances interpretability for users. However, relying on gen-
      eration for every evaluation step may be resource-intensive. Instead, reward models (RMs) attach
094
      classification heads to LLMs to directly output continuous scores (Munos et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
095
      2024c; Swamy et al., 2024). These models may be easily integrated into training systems, serving
096   as proxies for human preferences and facilitating the training of aligned LLMs (Lee et al., 2023;
097   Dong et al., 2023; Aksitov et al., 2023). Recent efforts also include merging the two approaches by
098   training RMs with a next-token prediction objective (Zhang et al., 2024).
099
100
      Meta-Evaluation As automated evaluators gain traction, meta-evaluation tools have been devel-
101
      oped to assess their reliability (Zeng et al., 2023; Lambert et al., 2024). These benchmarks evaluate
102   how closely LLMs mirror human judgments and determine their efficacy as reliable proxies for hu-
103   man annotators. Such tools are crucial in guaranteeing the significance of LLM-based evaluations.
104   However, despite their widespread use in multilingual settings (Aryabumi et al., 2024; Chiang et al.,
105   2024), the full capabilities of these evaluators remain largely unexamined. This work presents a
106   comprehensive analysis of automated evaluators across diverse settings, including non-English con-
107   texts, factual verification, and complex reasoning tasks. We assess their reliability, determine when
      they can be trusted, and identify key limitations in handling such scenarios.

      In this section, we introduce K UDGE, a dataset designed to assess the performance of automated
      judges. The original subset primarily focuses on Korean to complement existing English datasets
127
      of similar difficulty (Lambert et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024). The challenge subset is created in both
128
      English and Korean. Sections 3.1 to 3.3 detail the creation of the original subset, while Section 3.4
129   describes the development of the challenge subset. The dataset is made publicly available 2 .
130
131
132
      3.1       DATASET C REATION
133
        Existing Korean benchmarks for long-form question answering (Research, 2024; Park, 2024) pri-
134
      marily translate or replicate MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024), omitting aspects of Korean culture. To
135   address this issue, we handcraft 90 unique instructions. We classify Korean knowledge into nine dis-
136   tinct categories and map seven unique reasoning skills to ensure comprehensive coverage of topics
137   and reasoning abilities, distributing the instructions evenly across these categories. We also incorpo-
138   rate personalized evaluation rubrics and gold-standard responses for each question. The concise size
139   of the instruction set aims to keep evaluation and annotation feasible within budget constraints. Fol-
140   lowing this, to collect model responses of a wide variety, we generate answers for each instruction
141   using 31 LLMs, ranging in size from 1.3 billion to over 100 billion parameters. For an overview of
142   all models utilized in generating responses, see Table 1. Finally, fifteen human annotators, including
143
      five authors and ten hired experts compensated at ten US dollars per hour, evaluate the responses.
      Annotators were provided the above-mentioned reference answers and scoring rubrics to rate model
144
      generations on a Likert scale from 1 to 5. To prevent negligence, they were required to justify their
145
      scores. For quality control, each instance undergoes evaluation by two different annotators. For ad-
146   ditional details on the creation process, see Appendix A.
147
148
149   3.2       Q UALITY A NALYSIS
150
       To assess the quality of the collected annotations, we analyze the evaluation times and agreement
151
      rates between authors and hired annotators. The average evaluation time is 146 seconds for authors
152
      and 150 seconds for hired annotators, showing no significant difference. In 83.85% of cases, anno-
153   tations by the two annotators are identical or within a 1-point margin, likely due to the subjective
154   nature of the task. However, in 8.36% of cases, disagreements exceed a 2-point margin, indicating
155   significant discrepancies. Such discrepancies often stem from annotators assigning identical scores
156   to multiple responses with very brief evaluation times or from unusually long annotation times ex-
157   ceeding 1000 seconds, which may indicate negligence or loss of concentration. In our experiments,
158   we average scores for instances where the discrepancy between annotators is 1 point or less. Fur-
159   thermore, we exclude instances with larger margins or those with only one annotation. The missing
160   annotations are likely due to platform failures or human errors in curation.

      3.3   P OINTWISE & PAIRWISE S UBSETS
163
164      LLM-as-a-Judge applications typically include two evaluation methods: pointwise and pair-
165   wise (Vu et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024c). Pointwise evaluation involves assigning integer scores
166   to individual responses, whereas pairwise evaluation contrasts two responses to determine a pref-
167   erence. Although initially intended for pointwise assessment with a Likert scale, we have adapted
168   K UDGE for pairwise evaluation by pairing responses from distinct models into pairs. This approach
169   involves selecting a “chosen” response with a score exceeding three and a “rejected” response scor-
170   ing two or less. Accordingly, the original subset of K UDGE features a pointwise subset comprising
171
      2506 instances and a pairwise subset totaling 818 instances.

197   4.1   P ROMPT C ONFIGURATION
198
199   For the evaluation of LLM-as-a-Judges, we employ a generative setting in which models are
200   prompted to first generate an analysis and append their final decisions in a standardized format.
201
      In pointwise evaluation, models are instructed to generate the “[RESULT]” token followed by an
      integer from 1 to 5. In pairwise evaluation, models select the superior response as either “[[A]]” or
202
      “[[B]]”, which is subsequently parsed. If a model fails to generate the correct format, we attempt up
203
      to three retries. For further details on generation configuration and prompts, refer to Appendix B.
204   For RMs, we adopt the codebase provided from Lambert et al. (2024).

293
294
       Verga et al. (2024) suggests that aggregating judgments from multiple LLMs could enhance correla-
      tion with human evaluations. We test this hypothesis using K UDGE. In Figure 3, we sample N mod-
295
      els from proprietary and open-source LLMs with over 70B parameters, excluding the top-performing
296
      GPT-4o, and average their accuracy across five trials. Similar to prior findings, we observe aggregat-
297   ing models yield improvement; however, the margins are minimal and still underperform compared
298   to GPT-4o alone. We find that the underperformance of aggregated models compared to GPT-4o
299   alone could be attributed to multicollinearity among the models, as indicated in Figure 3. We observe
300   that the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) increases from 10.02 to 16.91 as more models are included
301   in the ensemble, suggesting that these models contribute similar, rather than diverse, perspectives.
302   This lack of diversity means the ensemble is limited in its ability to rectify incorrect assessments,
303   undermining the effectiveness of model aggregation.

505   8    C AN LLM S EVALUATE CHALLENGING PROMPTS ?
506
507    Intuitively, evaluating a problem necessitates solving it, as the evaluator must determine the cor-
508   rectness of an answer. We posit that models may find it difficult to assess challenging questions that
509   they themselves cannot solve. In this section, we use the K UDGE C HALLENGE subset to explore
510
      how question difficulty influences judgability.
511   In Table 9, we observe that model performance significantly correlates with the difficulty of the
512   prompts. All models manage reasonable success on simpler MMLU questions but encounter sub-
513   stantial difficulties with the more demanding GPQA subset. No model surpasses a 70% accuracy
514   rate on these harder questions, a concerning performance given that random guessing would result
515   in 50% accuracy. Reward models, although competitive on easier sets, underperform on tougher
516
      questions, likely due to their relatively smaller size (the largest being 27B parameters) compared to
      the LLMs-as-judges, all of which exceed 70B parameters. This disparity suggests that the complex-
517
      ity of GPQA questions particularly challenges smaller models. Overall, the findings indicate that
518
      models struggle with complex questions, revealing a significant limitation in employing state-of-
519   the-art, open-source judges for training LLMs (OpenAI, 2024) on advanced reasoning tasks.

522   9    C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we introduce K UDGE, a bilingual meta-evaluation dataset that assesses meta-evaluation
525   capability in both Korean and English. Our findings show that LLM-as-a-Judges and RMs perform
526   comparably in Korean, effectively evaluating questions and answers beyond their English training.
527
      This observation challenges the assumption that native Korean proficiency is essential for high-
      quality text evaluation. However, we also uncover significant deficiencies in proprietary and finely-
528
      tuned models’ ability to detect cultural misrepresentations or false information. This underscores
529
      a substantial gap in their effectiveness as reliable fact-checkers across varied languages and con-
530   texts. Furthermore, both model types struggle with complex reasoning tasks in our Challenge subset,
531   pointing to a widespread limitation in current automated evaluators.

      A.1   K2-E VAL
759
760   Given the absence of long-form question datasets in Korean at the time of this research, we create
761   K2-E VAL, a curated set of 90 prompts encompassing various aspects of Korean knowledge. The
762   concise dataset size is intended to keep evaluation and annotation manageable within budgetary
763   limits. Despite its small size, we ensure broad coverage by categorizing Korean knowledge into nine
764   distinct areas and identifying seven unique reasoning skills, with each task in the dataset pairing
765   one knowledge category with one reasoning skill. See Table 10 for an overview of the knowledge
766   and reasoning types. The gold answer for each instruction is crafted through a three-step process.
767
      Initially, we generate answers for each prompt using GPT-4, enhanced with a browsing-augmented
      chain of thought (CoT) technique (Yao et al., 2022). Subsequently, two authors independently review
768
      and amend any problematic responses. Finally, one author consolidates these revisions to finalize
769
      the gold standard answers. We also establish specific scoring rubrics and evaluation metrics for each
770   task, linked to the paired knowledge and reasoning categories. These assessment tools measure the
771   effectiveness of responses, their cultural accuracy, and the use of unique Korean linguistic elements,
772   including honorifics. The scoring rubric and evaluation criteria are not unique to each instruction
773   instead, they are shared within each combination of knowledge and reasoning types.
