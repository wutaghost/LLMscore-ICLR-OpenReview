011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in AI have significantly enhanced molecular representation
014            learning, which is crucial for predicting molecule properties and designing new
015            molecules. Despite these advances, effectively utilizing the vast amount of molec-
016
               ular data available in textual form from databases and scholarly articles remains
               a challenge. Recently, a large body of research has focused on utilizing Large
017
               Language Models (LLMs) and multi-modal architectures to interpret textual in-
018
               formation and link it with molecular structures. Nevertheless, existing datasets
019            often lack specificity in evaluation, as well as direct comparisons and compre-
020            hensive benchmarking across different models and model classes. In this work,
021            we construct a dataset specifically designed for evaluating models on structure-
022            directed questions and textual description-based molecule retrieval, featuring about
023            500,000 question-answer pairs related to approximately 240,000 molecules from
024            PubChem. Its structure enhances evaluation specificity and precision through the
025            use of multiple-choice answers. Moreover, we benchmark various architectural
026            classes fine-tuned using this dataset, including multi-modal architectures, and large
027
               language models, uncovering several insights. Our experiments indicate that the
               BioT5 and MoleculeSTM models are the top performers in Molecule QA and
028
               Molecule Retrieval tasks respectively, achieving about 70% accuracy. We have
029
               made both the dataset and the fine-tuned models publicly available.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Drug discovery is an extensive and laborious process, costing billions of dollars due to extensive
035
      property validations, lab tests, animal studies, and human trials (Dickson and Gagnon, 2009). In
036   the field, much of the valuable information about molecules is documented in text form, accessible
037   through an extensive array of public databases. This textual information includes a wide range of
038   data, from drug details (Wishart et al., 2018) and toxicity reports (Fonger et al., 2014) to the methods
039   of molecule extraction, as well as their physical characteristics (Kim et al., 2019), information
040   available in patents, chemical reactions (Lowe, 2017), and applications in producing various goods
041   like materials, fertilizers, perfumes, and insecticides (Dionisio et al., 2018).
042   While deep learning methods have significantly impacted drug discovery by predicting specific
043   molecular properties such as solubility or energy, these approaches face challenges when deciphering
044   the information encoded in textual form. For example, identifying the potential side effects of a drug
045   might depend heavily on analyzing narrative case studies, and patient reports documented in FDA
046   reports (U.S. FDA, 2024), clinical-trial documents(CTGov, 2024), etc which are rich in textual data
047   but not easily quantifiable through traditional regression or classification methods. This necessitates
048   the need for methods capable of interpreting molecular structures and their relationship with textual
049   information and performing inference based on text. By bridging this gap, we can significantly
050
      enhance our ability to understand molecules and unlock a broader spectrum of textual knowledge that
      remains largely untapped. This improved understanding, in turn, enables literature-based discovery
051
      of novel materials and drugs with potentially significant benefits for science and medicine.
052
053   Very recently, there has been a surge in the development of models to decipher the complex relation-
      ships between molecular structures and textual descriptions (Liu et al. (2023a;b); Li et al. (2024);

073

075   which hinder the assessment of a model’s ability to infer specific molecular properties. These datasets
076   typically use generic prompts like “Describe the molecule,” which fail to extract precise information.
077   A more effective approach would involve using targeted questions, such as “What is the physical
078   state of the molecule at room temperature?”. Additionally, widely used evaluation metrics, such as
079   the BLEU score in molecule captioning tasks, are inadequate for this context. Since answers to vague
080   prompts like “Describe the molecule” can vary significantly, ranging from physical properties to
081   industrial applications, BLEU’s reliance on semantic similarity makes it unreliable. Further details
082   on these methodological shortcomings are provided in section 2.2.
083   In this work, we have developed a comprehensive dataset consisting of over 500,000 question-and-
084   answer (QA) pairs and small molecules represented by SMILES (Simplified Molecular Input Line
085   Entry System) (Weininger, 1988) sequences. These QAs are crafted from a rich base of textual
086   data sourced from PubChem (Kim et al., 2019), encompassing a wide array of information such as
087   chemical structures, physical properties, applications, and uses of molecules in drugs and biological
088   pathways, as well as manufacturing details. We believe this dataset will significantly enhance the
089
      ability to infer information from molecular structures aid in the design of new molecules and improve
      the capability to evaluate these processes more effectively (See Figure 1). Our main contributions are:
090
091   1. We created a comprehensive dataset with about 500,000 QA pairs for 240,000 distinct molecule
092      SMILES sequences across various categories, including multiple-choice answers to improve
093      evaluation precision on specific areas including chemical information, biological information,
094      physical properties, and more.
095
      2. To ensure the reliability of our dataset, we implemented a comprehensive validation process that
         includes human annotation of a small subset to evaluate data accuracy.
096
      3. We conduct an extensive evaluation of this dataset using state-of-the-art (SoTA) molecule-text mul-
097
         timodal models and recent advancements in large language models. Our analysis provides valuable
098      insights into the advantages and limitations of current models, highlighting their performance in
099      understanding and interpreting molecular data.
100
101
      To the best of our knowledge, this work represents the first large-scale dataset and benchmarking
      effort dedicated to diverse question-answering methodologies for small molecules.

      2.1   M ODELS A RCHITECTURES IN M OLECULE -T EXT LEARNING
109
110   Molecule-Text learning with a Single Model: Initial models in molecule-text modeling combined
111   molecules and texts using a unified encoder-only framework. Key works include KV-PLM (Zeng
112   et al., 2022) and GPT-MolBERTa (Balaji et al., 2023), built on the foundations of SciBERT (Beltagy
113   et al., 2019) and RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), respectively. KV-PLM fine-tunes SciBERT with
114   SMILES sequences from 15,000 PubChem (Kim et al., 2019) descriptions, employing masked
115   language modeling for representation learning. This model acts as a dual encoder for text and
116   molecule representations. GPT-MolBERTa, using RoBERTa as its base, integrates descriptions from
117
      ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2022), which may introduce non-factual information, affecting reliability.
118   Molecule-Text with Separate Encoders: Recent and more effective models used multi-modal
119   strategies by integrating separate encoders for molecules and text. For instance, Text2Mol (Edwards
120   et al., 2021) employs SciBERT, akin to KV-PLM, for text processing, while molecule representation
121   benefits from Mol2Vec (Jaeger et al., 2018) tokens as initial inputs, utilizing contrastive training
122   to synchronize molecule and text embeddings, mirroring CLIP’s (Radford et al., 2021) approach.
123
      Conversely, MoMu (Su et al., 2022) introduces molecules as graphs, leveraging a Graph Isomorphism
      Network (GIN) (Xu et al., 2018) for molecule encoding and uses SciBERT for text processing.
124
      MoleculeSTM (Liu et al., 2023a) enhances its methodology by training with both SMILES strings
125
      and molecular graphs, initializing SMILES encoder weights from MegaMolBART (NVIDIA, 2021)
126   and adopting a GIN model for graph representation, with text decoding also relying on SciBERT. All
127   these methodologies use of contrastive learning to align text and molecule representations, gaining
128   advantages from expansive datasets and diverse molecular encoding techniques. Alternatively, MolT5
129   (Edwards et al., 2022) has fine-tuned a T5 model(Raffel et al., 2020) to train separate SMILES and
130   text encoders and decoders. Recently, MolCA (Liu et al., 2023b) utilize the BLIP model (Li et al.,
131   2023), employing a GINE model (Brossard et al., 2020) for graph encoding and SciBERT for text,
132   and text generation fine-tuned by LoRA optimization of the Galactica model (Taylor et al., 2022).
133   3DMolLM(Li et al., 2024) extended this framework to include question prompts for more directed
134
      QA.
135   Decoder-based Scientific Large Language Models: The Galactica model (Taylor et al., 2022),
136   trained explicitly on scientific data, supports a variety of scientific tasks such as generating molecular
137   and protein sequences, question answering, code generation, and mathematical problem-solving.
138   We also evaluate popular large language models (LLMs) like GPT-3.5 (OpenAI, 2022) and LLaMA
139   (Touvron et al., 2023) in our work, noting their strong performance across many tasks, including
140
      those in the scientific domain.

142   2.2   C ORRESPONDING B ENCHMARK DATASETS FOR M OLECULE -T EXT LEARNING
143
144   All the methods discussed so far draw upon datasets sampled from various public sources. For
145
      example, KV-PLM introduced PCdes, utilizing PubChem to compile a dataset of 15,000 samples.
      Additionally, KV-PLM incorporated a set of multiple-choice questions (MCQs) akin to those in this
146
      work, albeit with a smaller scope of approximately 1,500 entries, which, in context, was considered a
147
      significantly large dataset. Text2Mol developed the CheBI-20 dataset by sampling from the Chemical
148   Entities of Biological Interest (ChEBI) (Degtyarenko et al., 2007) database, resulting in a collection
149   of approximately 20,000 molecular descriptions. MoMu extracted 50,000 captions from PubChem,
150   while MoleculeSTM significantly expanded this approach by sampling over 300,000 captions from
151   PubChem to create the PubChemSTM dataset. 3DMolLM (Li et al., 2024) has curated a set of
152   Question-answering pairs from these captions using ChatGPT.
153
      Limitations in existing datasets: We identify several limitations in previous dataset curation
154   methodologies that justify the development of our current dataset and benchmarking efforts. Detailed
155   discussions of these issues, with specific examples, are presented in Appendix.
156

160      captions are free-form, containing diverse information about various tasks without categorization.
161      Other datasets like InstructMol(Cao et al., 2023) and MolInstructions(Fang et al., 2024), which
         also derive captions from these sources, feature queries such as “Describe the molecule.” Such

      3.2    DATASET OVERVIEW
243
      The dataset comprises about 500,000 questions related to more than 240,000 molecules, categorized
244
      into five distinct areas:
245
246   1. Chemical Information covers the chemical structure, functional groups, and chemical properties.
247   2. Physical Properties addresses the properties such as solubility, physical state, and odor.
248
      3. Biological Information contains the molecules’ role in biological pathways, drug applications,
         and drug toxicity.
249
      4. Source details the molecules’ origin and manufacturing processes.
250
      5. Application describes application areas such as perfumes, fertilizers, and insecticides.
251
252   We provide detailed dataset statistics across these QA categories in Table 2. Further, in Table 3,
253   we depict a sample data point indicating different attributes. Each data point includes a question,
254
      options, a SMILES sequence, a set of candidate answers, a question category, the correct choice, and
      options in sentence form. Each datapoint also includes a set of candidate SMILES randomly sampled
255
      from the data and useful for the Molecule Retrieval task. A Tanimoto similarity threshold of 0.2
256
      was applied during sampling to ensure that the selected molecules are sufficiently distinct from one
257   another. The dataset is divided into test, valid, train and pretrain sets for different stages of model
258   development and evaluation.

      3.3    DATA CONSTRUCTION
262
      In this work, we employed different LLMs, specifically Llama3-70B, Llama3-8B, and ChatGPT3.5,
263
      for constructing a question-and-answer dataset. The methodology involves passing molecular de-
264   scriptions to the LLMs and prompting them to generate a set of questions with multiple-choice
265   options that are semantically related to ensure challenging and informative questions. To prevent the
266   possibility of inferring information solely from the molecule’s common name, we specifically prompt
267   the LLM to anonymize the common name of the molecule in the resulting QAs. This ensures that
268   downstream models must rely on molecule structure alone for inference. Due to the expensive nature
269   of the Llama3-70B API, the pretrain split was generated with Llama3-7B, while the other splits are
      generated using the Llama3-70B.

294   3.4   DATASET VALIDATION
295
296   To enhance the reliability of the generated question-and-answer content and mitigate the risk of
297   fabricated information ("hallucinations") or uninformative, nonsensical questions, we implemented a
298
      two-pass validation process. First, following a methodology similar to that outlined by Es et al., we
      employed an alternative language model (GPT-3.5) to verify the accuracy of the QA pairs. In this step,
299
      the caption, question, and multiple-choice options were provided to the model, which was tasked
300
      with selecting the correct answer based on the given context. QA pairs where the model either failed
301   to choose the correct answer or could not deduce it from the provided information were excluded
302   from our dataset, ensuring that the content is grounded in the factual information from the captions.
303   In the second stage, the remaining questions were passed to the LLM for an additional verification
304   step. This phase focused on filtering out questions that were unanswerable, uninformative, or not
305   useful for evaluating molecular characteristics and applications. Detailed descriptions of the prompts
306   used in both stages can be found in the Appendix. Figure 2 depicts the data generation process.

308   3.5   B ENCHMARKING DATASET EFFICACY
309
310   To assess the accuracy of language models in generating question-and-answer pairs, we manually
311   reviewed a random sample of 400 QA pairs from the test set. Our evaluation focused on three key
312   criteria: whether the question could be logically derived from the provided caption, the unambiguous
313
      correctness of the answer, and the relevance of the question—specifically, ensuring it avoids uninfor-
      mative queries and contributes to meaningful chemical or biological insights based on the structure.
314
      We have found that 391 of the 400 samples satisfy this criteria. This corresponds to a greater than
315
      96.13 percent accuracy on the entire dataset with a p-value of <0.05 under a hypergeometric test,
316   indicating statistically significant performance. We elaborate the specifics of the hypothesis test and
317   human verification in Appendix. We also include these samples with the supplementary material.

328   4.1   B ENCHMARKED M ODELS IN E XPERIMENTS
329
330
      1. Single Encoder Architectures:
331      SciBERT (Beltagy et al., 2019) leverages a BERT base, fine-tuned on scientific papers, especially
332      from biomedical fields. It has superior performance on several scientific tasks like entity recognition
333
         and text classification.
334      KV-PLM (Zeng et al., 2022) builds on SciBERT, trained further with molecule-text pairs from
335      PubChem. It incorporates pre-training with SMILES and fine-tuning for text retrieval, employing a
336      max hinge loss for improved prediction and retrieval.
337      MolT5 (Edwards et al., 2022) is a T5 model fine-tuned for molecule captioning. It features an
338      encoders and decoder architecture for both SMILES and captions.
339      BioT5 Pei et al. (2023) is a T5 model similar to MolT5. However, the training dataset includes a
340      larger training set and modalities including small molecules and proteins.
341      BioT5 Plus Pei et al. (2024) is an updated BioT5 model including additional data sources and
342      multi-task instruction tuning.
343
      2. Separate Encoder Architectures
344
345
         MoleculeSTM (Liu et al., 2023a) employs a dual-encoder combining SciBERT (Beltagy et al.,
         2019) and a Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) (Xu et al., 2018) for encoding text and chemical
346
         structures, respectively. It trains on PubChem data, using InfoNCE loss to refine molecule-text
347
         alignment.
348
349
         MoMu (Su et al., 2022) is similar to MoleculeSTM, yet introduces an extra contrastive loss
         between molecules in addition to the molecule-text contrastive loss.
350
351   3. Large Language Models (Decoder-Only)
352      Llama (Touvron et al., 2023) includes autoregressive transformer models, fine-tuned for instruction
353      adherence showing versatility in several general tasks from QA to code generation. We experiment
354      with 4 model from the Llama2 and Llama3 series - Llama2-7B, Llama2-70B, Llama3-8B, Llama3-
355      70B.
356      GPT-3.5 Turbo (OpenAI, 2022), part of the GPT series, is optimized for human alignment, with
357      broad application across numerous tasks.
358      Galactica (Taylor et al., 2022) features autoregressive, decoder-only models trained on scientific
359      content, effective in specialized biomedical datasets. We experimented with multiple sizes of the
360      model - 125M, 1.3B, and 6.7B.

      4.2   TASKS AND O BJECTIVES
363
364   1. Molecule QA task: The first task, "Molecule QA", entails choosing the right answer from multiple
365      options, where models are given a SMILES string or molecular graph of a molecule and a related
366      question. The goal is to select the correct option, testing the model’s capability to recognize and
367      deduce molecular properties or characteristics from the molecule’s structure.
368      While large decoder-only models can be directly prompted to obtain answers to such queries, this
369      approach is not feasible for the encoder-based models (e.g. KV-PLM, MoleculeSTM, MoMU)
370      since they cannot generate text. For these models, this is framed as a sentence retrieval task from
371      SMILES input, where each sentence is a "question + answer choice.“
372   2. Molecule Retrieval task: The second task, known as the "Molecule Retrieval Task," reverses
373      the domains. The model is given a sentence description of a molecule and must identify the
374      correct SMILES string from among a list of candidates. This task evaluates the model’s ability
375      to accurately retrieve structures, useful for generating new molecules with target properties. For
376      decoder-only LLMs, the task involves presenting the model with a sentence and a set of SMILES
377      options, from which the model is prompted to retrieve the correct SMILES string. For encoder
         models, the task remains similar: retrieving the appropriate SMILES strings based on a sentence.

483   scaling could further enhance performance. The results overall indicate that LLMs can be effective
484   in infering molecular properties. On the other hand, multimodal architectures also demonstrated a
485   considereable improvement across categories, increasing performance by 20%. However, the MolT5

487   advantages of BioT5’s multi-modal training approach.

491
      these architectures showed superior performance in chemical properties in zero-shot settings, fine-
492
      tuning enabled them to excel across categories. Despite significant gains in tasks like Physical
493   properties (by over 10%), this has also led to a slight decline in performance for Chemical properties
494   and consequently, overall. This performance dip suggests that embeddings may not fully capture
495   diverse types of information, leading to trade-offs. We also observe a similar trend between BioT5
496   and BioT5-plus, where BioT5-plus demonstrates improved performance in the "Sources" and "Uses"
497   categories but shows a decline in accuracy for "Physical" and "Chemical Properties." This highlights
498   the need for improved modeling strategies to achieve robust performance across all tasks.
499   It is important to note that these fine-tuning efforts utilized only a small portion of the full dataset,
500   raising questions about potential outcomes if the entire dataset were employed. Given the success
501   of LLMs in Molecule QA tasks and multimodal architectures in Molecule Retrieval tasks, we
502   speculate that an architecture fine-tuned on QA tasks, which integrates elements from both types
503   of architectures and employs strategies to capture diverse sorts of information, could excel in both
504   scenarios. Exploring this possibility will be a focus of our future research.

506   5    C ONCLUSION
507
508   Our work introduces MolTextQA, a novel dataset featuring over 500,000 QA pairs related to small
509   molecule structures, covering a broad spectrum of molecular properties and applications. We
510   have benchmarked MolTextQA against a diverse array of large language models and state-of-the-
511
      art multimodal architectures, analyzing their strengths and weaknesses. We also see potential in
      extending our approach to include other scientific modalities, like proteins, to widen the dataset’s
512
      applicability. We aspire for MolTextQA to become a foundational resource for the development
513
      of more efficient molecule-text foundation models. We anticipate its application in drug discovery,
514   materials science, and other fields.
515
516   We acknowledge a few limitations in the dataset creation process. First, the dataset exhibits minor
517
      inaccuracies, as reported in Section 3.5, which could potentially complicate model training. Future
      work will focus on implementing more rigorous validation strategies to enhance data precision.
518
      Additionally, the dataset includes a broad spectrum of questions, a small fraction of which may
519
      appear straightforward, such as identifying the polarity of a molecule—an aspect that only requires
520   a basic knowledge of chemistry. While this diversity of questions benefits model training, it also
521   suggests we could refine our selection process to ensure each question more effectively serves the
522   dataset’s purpose. Furthermore, the dataset covers data across a wide range of general categories.
523   The exploration of data acquisition and fine-tuning of specialized datasets for niche applications is an
524   area that requires further investigation.

      are free-form and include diverse information across various tasks without specific categorization.
818
      For instance, consider the following captions from PubChem:
819

             • Caption B: It is an N-glycosyl compound, a ribose triphosphate, a pyrimidone, and an

824   Datasets such as InstructMol (Cao et al., 2023) and MolInstructions (Fang et al., 2024), which also
825   source their captions from similar databases, pose queries like ‘Describe the molecule.’ Given the
826   broad range of information in the captions—from molecule manufacturing, to physical properties,
827   to chemical structures, to drug toxicity, to industrial applications—the queries remain insufficiently
828   structured for detailed inference. In contrast, our proposed dataset includes specific queries such as Is
829   this molecule denser than water?, Does this molecule contain a mannose ring?, or Is this molecule
830   an antibiotic or an analgesic?
831   This issue also affects retrieval models such as MoleculeSTM or MoMu, where the challenge is
832   compounded by the necessity for a single molecule embedding to retrieve both Caption A and Caption
833   B. This task is difficult as these captions semantically reside in distinct spaces.
834
      Factual Correctness: Datasets such as 3DMolLM(Li et al., 2024) employ data augmentation from
835
      LLMs in their data generation procedure, raising concerns about the overall accuracy of the dataset.
836   Moreover, these datasets generate five questions for each data point, irrespective of whether sufficient
837   information exists. There is no validation process to determine the reliability of the data, leading to
838   the generation of numerous unreliable questions. Consider the following example from PubChem:
839   https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/compound/10008613

      These questions illustrate the challenge of relying on LLM-generated content without appropriate

863   In contrast, our current work employs a two-stage procedure to validate and filter data generated
      with LLMs. We also manually evaluate the dataset and estimate its overall accuracy at less than 0.05.

864
      Since not much information is available on most molecules, our dataset averages about two questions
865   per molecule, enhancing factual reliability.
866
867   Information Leakage: The 3DMolLM dataset does not anonymize molecule names, resulting in
868
      many questions that include either the common or chemical name of the molecule, thereby providing
      unintended hints. For example, a question in the dataset - “What is the main component of Lobaplatin
869
      that gives it its anticancer properties?” offers additional clues that may influence the evaluation
870
      process, complicating assessing a model’s ability to learn from the molecule sequence or structure
871   alone.
872
873   Evaluation Limited to Samples with Available Data: Datasets like MoMu, 3DMolLM, and MolCA
874
      limit their test sets to molecules accompanied by captions of more than 20 words. This approach
      inherently biases the evaluation towards well-known and extensively studied molecules. Conversely,
875
      the dataset presented in this work, while including these well-documented data points, also augments
876
      the test set with molecule captions chosen randomly, not by length. This strategy helps to provide
877   a more balanced and representative evaluation of the model’s capabilities across a wider range of
878   molecular data.
879
880
      Benchmarking Across Several Model Classes: Existing datasets often exhibit limitations in their
      benchmarking scope. For instance, MolInstructions has been evaluated solely using LLMs, while
881
      3DMolLM employs only Llama as an additional baseline. Other models like KV-PLM and MoMu
882
      are benchmarked against only a subset of available models. Similarly, MoleculeSTM and MolT5 lack
883   direct comparisons in their evaluations. In contrast, our approach aims to extensively benchmark and
884   compare models across different architectural classes. This broader evaluation is facilitated by the
885   structure of our dataset, which includes questions and multiple-choice options, allowing for a more
886   comprehensive assessment of model performance.

       E     DATASET EFFICACY EVALUATION
1027
1028
       To assess the overall reliability of our dataset, we conducted a benchmark by randomly sampling
1029
       400 data points from the test split. The data points were evaluated on three criteria: whether the
1030
       question could be logically derived from the provided caption, the unambiguous correctness of the
1031   answer, and the relevance of the question—specifically, ensuring it avoids uninformative queries and
1032   contributes to meaningful chemical or biological insights based on the structure. Out of the sampled
1033   data, 391 points met these criteria. To understand the implications of this result for the entire dataset,
1034   we calculated the p-value using a hypergeometric distribution (sci). A hypergeometric test is used
1035   to measure the probability of obtaining a specific number of successes in a given number of draws
1036   from a finite population containing a certain amount of successes. With parameters k=400, n=391,
1037   N=11,922 (i.e the size of all test split), and K=0.961*11,922, we found a p-value of <0.05. This
1038   result suggests that the dataset is over 96.1 percent accurate, demonstrating a high level of reliability.
1039
       Details of the random test samples used for this evaluation can be accessed through the project’s
       repository.

       In this section, we expand on the specific details of different models used to evaluate the MolQA

1141   F.1.1    S INGLE E NCODER A RCHITECTURES :
1142
1143
       Scibert: SciBERT (Beltagy et al., 2019) is an encoder model, that leverages a pre-trained BERT
       framework, subsequently fine-tuned on a substantial corpus of scientific papers, predominantly from
1144
       the biomedical domain (constituting 85% of its training data). This specialization makes SciBERT a
1145
       useful baseline for our study. It has a robust performance across various scientific tasks, including
1146   named entity recognition and text classification.
1147
1148   KV-PLM: KV-PLM(Zeng et al., 2022) is a single encoder model derived from SciBERT, further
1149
       trained on molecule-text pairs sourced from PubChem. The training process begins with pre-training,
       during which SMILES sequences are appended to molecular descriptions to form the training data.
1150
       The model employs a masking strategy where certain tokens representing both molecular structures
1151
       and biomedical text are masked at random. The model’s task is to predict these masked tokens
1152   based on the surrounding context. Following pre-training, KV-PLM undergoes fine-tuning for text
1153   retrieval tasks. In this phase, the model learns to accurately retrieve specific text descriptions based
1154   on SMILES sequence inputs, utilizing a max hinge loss function. This loss is given by:
1155
1156
1157                                LMH = max
                                           ′
                                              [α + s (m, d′ ) − s(m, d)]
                                              d

1159                                            ′ m
1160   here LMH represents the max hinge loss, m and d denotes the molecule (SMILES sequence) and
1161   its corresponding text description, respectively. The terms d′ and m′ refer to a negative text and
1162   molecule that do not match the original pairing and the function s(m, d) calculates the similarity
1163   score between a molecule and a document.
1164
       MolT5: MolT5 (Edwards et al., 2022) is an encoder-decoder model, built by fine-tuning a T5 (Raffel
1165   et al., 2020) model. The model is trained in two stages. First, the the model is trained with masked
1166   language modeling objective, to encode and decode SMILES string and molecule captions. Next, the
1167   model is fine-tuned to generate SMILES strings or captions from the captions or the SMILES strings
1168   input respectively. The dataset used for fine-tuning is CheBI-20 (Edwards et al., 2021)).

       MoleculeSTM: The paper (Liu et al., 2023a) introduces a framework that uses a dual-encoder to
1172
       extract and align representations of text and molecules. The framework employs a Graph Isomorphism
1173
       Network (GIN) (Xu et al., 2018) to encode chemical data, represented by fc , and SciBert to encode
1174   textual data, denoted as ft . The GIN model is initialized from GraphMVP (Liu et al., 2021), which
1175   does multi-view pretraining between the 2D topologies and 3D geometries from the GEOM dataset
1176   (Axelrod and Gomez-Bombarelli, 2022). The components are trained end-to-end on a dataset that
1177   contains molecules and their descriptions sourced from PubChem. The model’s learning process is
1178   governed by the InfoNCE loss:

       MoMu: MoMu (Su et al., 2022) is another dual-encoder model similar to MoleculeSTM, leveraging

       including common sense reasoning, closed-book QA, mathematical reasoning, and code generation.
