009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               As AI models grow larger, the demand for accountability and interpretability has
012            become increasingly critical for understanding their decision-making processes.
013            Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) have gained attention for enhancing inter-
014            pretability by mapping inputs to intermediate concepts before making final predic-
015            tions. However, CBMs often suffer from information leakage, where additional
016            input data, not captured by the concepts, is used to improve task performance,
017            complicating the interpretation of downstream predictions. In this paper, we in-
018            troduce a novel approach for training both joint and sequential CBMs that allows
019            us to identify and control leakage using decision trees. Our method quantifies
020
               leakage by comparing the decision paths of hard CBMs with their soft, leaky
               counterparts. Specifically, we show that soft leaky CBMs extend the decision
021
               paths of hard CBMs, particularly in cases where concept information is incom-
022
               plete. Using this insight, we develop a technique to better inspect and manage
023            leakage, isolating the subsets of data most affected by this. Through synthetic
024            and real-world experiments, we demonstrate that controlling leakage in this way
025            not only improves task accuracy but also yields more informative and transparent
026            explanations.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Deep learning models have demonstrated significant capabilities and been widely adopted in appli-
033
      cations such as image recognition, natural language processing, and disease prediction. However,
      their use in high-stakes fields like healthcare, pollution monitoring, credit risk, and criminal justice
034
      has raised concerns about transparency and flawed predictions (Hu et al., 2019). While numerous
035
      methods provide post-hoc analysis of trained neural networks (e.g., Ghorbani et al. (2019); Zhou
036   et al. (2018)), these explanations do not always align with human understanding (Rudin, 2019).
037
038   Several recent studies suggest explicitly aligning intermediate outputs of neural network models with
039
      predefined expert concepts during supervised training processes (e.g Koh et al. (2020); Chen et al.
      (2020); Kumar et al. (2009); Lampert et al. (2009)) through the use of Concept Bottleneck Models
040
      (CBMs). Given a high-dimensional input of features (such as the raw pixels of an image), CBMs
041
      first predict a set of human-understandable concepts, which are then used to predict the final task
042   labels with the help of an interpretable label predictor. Thus, a user is able to follow the decision-
043   making process of the label predictor, while the task performance can remain close to that of the
044   black-box model (Koh et al., 2020).
045
      Despite these characteristics, CBMs frequently suffer from information leakage (Mahinpei et al.,
046
      2021; Margeloiu et al., 2021). This occurs where unintentional signal from the data not captured
047
      by the concepts is used for predicting the label and potentially increases its accuracy. If the label
048   predictions rely on this information, explanations derived from the concepts may be inaccurate and
049   potentially misleading. Crucially, leakage compromises our ability to intervene on concepts – a key
050   benefit of CBMs. Recent works have attempted to mitigate leakage in CBMs by allowing a residual
051   layer or side channel to capture a set of unknown, latent concepts (e.g. Havasi et al. (2022a); Shang
052   et al. (2024); Zabounidis et al. (2023); Heidemann et al. (2023); Vandenhirtz et al. (2024)). Yet,
053   while leakage is reduced, the information captured by these latent concepts is difficult to interpret
      and not necessarily disentangled from the known concepts, only partially resolving the issue.

      Given the current limitations, the purpose of this work is provide an interpretable method for inspect-
080
      ing where leakage occurs in CBMs and controlling this leakage using decision trees. We develop a
081
      method called Mixed CBM Training with Trees (MCBM) for better inspecting and managing leak-
082   age in those subsets, and introduce both sequential (MCBM-Seq) and joint training (MCBM-Joint)
083   variants of this method for different scenarios of incomplete concept sets. An overview of our ar-
084   chitecture can be found in Figure 1. Due to their hierarchical nature, trees allow us to first identify
085   leaf nodes with subsets that are missing concept information, and subsequently control information
086   leakage to only specialise those decision paths. This is achieved through a 3-step process, where
087   a Hard CBM (global tree) is first trained, then an individual sub-tree is trained to extend each leaf
088   node only if it can take advantage of leakage, and finally all trees are merged for global inspection.
089   Our contributions are as follows: We introduce a tree-based method for inspecting and control-
090   ling leakage in CBMs. We show that our method enables more interpretable decision-making with
091   explanations that have higher accuracy and are guaranteed to make faithful predictions (in terms
092   of fidelity) when concept sets are incomplete. We also demonstrate that our method allows us to
093   quantify leakage for specific data subsets associated with tree regions where concept information
094   may be incomplete, while identifying those decision rules most affected by leakage. Finally, we
095   show that the derived group-based explanations can be very meaningful in real-life decision-making
096
      scenarios, and provide practical recommendations for selecting the appropriate method for training
      a CBM, based on the context of a problem and preferences of the user.

      Concept Bottleneck Models and Information Leakage. Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs)
102   (Koh et al., 2020; Lampert et al., 2009; Kumar et al., 2009) are trained on data with covariates
103   x ∈ X, target y ∈ Y , and annotated binary concepts c ∈ C. These models use a neural network
104   fθ , parameterized by θ and structured as ⟨gψ , hϕ ⟩ (Leino et al., 2018), to enforce a concept bottle-
105   neck ĉ = hϕ (x). The final output depends solely on the predicted concepts ĉ. Soft CBMs (Chen
106   et al., 2020; Koh et al., 2020) improve prediction by using probabilistic concept values but are prone
107   to information leakage from the concept predictor to the label predictor (Margeloiu et al., 2021;
      Mahinpei et al., 2021). Most CBM research focuses on extending concept representations in the


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      embedding space to enhance predictive power (Zarlenga et al., 2024; Oikarinen et al., 2023; Kim
109   et al., 2023; Semenov et al., 2024), while neglecting information leakage. Some works address leak-
110   age by allowing missing concept information to bypass concept representations through a Residual
111   Layer (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023; Shang et al., 2024). Havasi et al. (2022b) tackle missing infor-
112   mation with a side channel and an auto-regressive concept predictor, but these approaches struggle
113   with interpretability and disentanglement of residual information (Zabounidis et al., 2023). Another
114   approach by Marconato et al. (2022) rejects test samples prone to leakage, though it relies on as-
115   sumptions about the prior distribution. Unlike these approaches, the work we present here enables
116   both interpretability and leakage inspection, without any prohibitive assumptions on the distribution
117
      that may not hold in practice.
118
      Explainable Label Predictors. According to CBM definitions (Koh et al., 2020), any inter-
119
      pretable machine learning model can serve as a label predictor, such as Logistic Regression (McK-
120
      elvey & Zavoina, 1975), Generalised Additive Models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1985), or Decision
121   Trees (Breiman et al., 1984; Kass, 1980). Recent work integrates neural networks with interpretable
122   decision-making. Wu et al. (2018; 2020) introduce tree-regularization to approximate neural net-
123   work boundaries with decision trees. Ciravegna et al. (2021) propose the ψ network for logic-based
124   concept explanations using L1-regularization and pruning to extract interpretable First Order Logic
125   formulas. Barbiero et al. (2022) enhance this with the Entropy-Net, using Entropy Loss for more
126   concise logic formulas. Ghosh et al. (2023) further introduce a mixture of Entropy-Net experts
127   for specialized explanations while maintaining performance. Yet, these concept-based explanations
128   assume concept probabilities without evaluating potential label information leakage as we propose.

      In what follows, we present the core contribution of our work. Specifically, given both a trained hard
      CBM and a trained (either sequentially or jointly) soft CBM, we would like to determine whether it
182
      is possible to inspect any information leakage that the soft model exploits to make its predictions,
183
      and understand how this information was used. Based on Definition 3.1, we know that any additional
184   information used to predict y that is not contained in concepts c, should be captured by the difference
185   of conditional mutual information terms in Eq. 4. A naive attempt to answer this question is thus to
186   first train the same concept predictor for both hard and soft models, and then use separate decision
187   tree classifiers for each CBM as label predictors and subsequently inspect their trees to observe how
188   they differ. However, inspecting the corresponding decision tree label predictors for both hard and
189   soft CBMs is non-trivial as the trees be incomparable and contain very different splits to distinguish
190   samples (an example is given in Appendix A.3).
191   Motivated by this challenge, we present a new training method with mixed concept representations.
192   Our key insight is that any subset of data that cannot be further split by a hard CBM’s decision tree
193   but can be split by a soft leaky CBM’s tree is vulnerable to information leakage. The method has
194   three steps, shown in Fig. 1. First, we train a hard, leakage-free CBM with a decision tree as the
195   global label predictor. This tree is decomposed into decision paths with some concepts, while other
196   concepts are predicted independently with calibrated probabilities. In the second step, the decision
197   paths are used to train a mixed CBM, combining hard and soft concepts with a new tree-based label
198
      predictor. Finally, the global tree and sub-trees are merged for complete inspection.
199

315
      Joint CBMs tend to achieve higher task performance compared to the other forms of CBM Training,
316
      due to the end-to-end optimization procedure of Eq. 3 (Koh et al., 2020). However, this comes at the
317
      expense of interpretability. According to (Mahinpei et al., 2021), they are more prone to Information
318   Leakage compared to Sequential CBMs because the label predictor can ”shape” the concept proba-
319   bilities in an unexplainable way in order to improve the task performance while decreasing concept
320   accuracy. In practice, this trade-off is controlled by the λC parameter of Eq. 3. Unlike Sequential
321   CBMs, these probabilities do not correspond to the true confidence of the concept predictor, and thus
322   concept-based explanations based on these probabilities are even less human-intuitive. However, we
323   could control Information Leakage to only specialise the main decision rules, in order to achieve
      better interpretations when higher performance is a priority.

379   We also train all CBM methods using the state-of-the-art Entropy-Net Barbiero et al. (2022) as the
380   label-predictor, which achieves higher task accuracy compared to linear label predictors while also
381   providing interpretable logic explanations.
382
      Quantitative Metrics. In terms of performance, we evaluate our CBM methods using the a) Task
383
      and b) Concept Accuracy (Koh et al., 2020). To compare the interpretability and reliability of
384
      our methods compared to existing work, we use the following metrics defined by Barbiero et al.
385   (2022): a) The Explanation Accuracy as the task performance of a method when using its extracted
386   explanation formulas, and b) the Fidelity of an Explanation which measures how well each formula
387   matches the model’s predictions. Finally, we provide an estimate of Information Leakage as the
388   Information Gain of leaky splits according Eq. 5, which is unique to our methods.

      5.1   DATASETS AND M ODELS
391
392   Morpho-MNIST (Castro et al., 2018): The dataset describes MNIST digits in terms of measurable
393   shape attributes which we use as concepts. These are the thickness, area, length, width, height,
394   and slant of digits. We categorise each real-valued concept in one of three equally spaced bins,
395   indicating a ”small”, ”medium”, ”large” or value, resulting in mutually exclusive concept groups
396   such as ”length::small”, ”length::medium” and ”length::large”. We use a LeNet model Lecun et al.
397   (1998) as the concept predictor. Hyper-parameter details are given in Appendix A.7.
398   The CUB Dataset (Koh et al., 2020): The dataset consists of n = 11, 788 pictures of 200 different
399   bird species. There are 312 annotated binary concepts available. Similarly to Koh et al. (2020);
400   Yeh et al. (2020), we only select those concepts that appear in at least 10% of the dataset, and thus
401   we form a reduced set of 112 binary concepts. The data is denoised by converting instance-specific
402   concepts to class-specific concepts via majority voting. To simulate a scenario of an incomplete
403   concept set, we choose 45 concepts and denote the rest as missing. We train a ResNet-18 model (He
404   et al., 2016) as the independently trained concept predictor (see Appendix A.6 and A.7 for pre-
405
      processing and hyperparameter tuning details).
406   MIMIC: MIMIC-IV (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care IV) (Johnson et al., 2023) is a
407   large, freely accessible dataset consisting of de-identified electronic health records from over 70,000
408   critical care patients. It includes data such as demographics, vital signs, laboratory results, medi-
409   cations, and clinical notes. Our binary classification task is to identify recovering or dying patients
410   after ICU admission. Since the dataset does not have annotated concepts, we calculate the six Se-
411
      quential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) scores (Lambden et al., 2019) and categorise them into 3
      levels of severity, for a total of 18 concepts. We use a 3-layer MLP as the concept predictor. Details
412
      about the concept selection and hyper-parameters are given in Appendix A.6 and A.7.

      CUB dataset(Wah et al., 2011) is shown in Fig. 2. We consider two bird classes: Red Bellied Wood-
509   pecker and Red Headed Woodpecker. At test time, the method traverses the decision path in the
510   global tree and identifies the two classes as indistinguishable based on available concepts. After
511   training a Mixed-Sequential CBM, the label predictor separates the classes using the concept pre-
512   dictor’s calibrated probability for “has-breast-pattern-solid.” The predictor finds that Red Bellied
513   Woodpeckers are less than 70% likely to have a solid breast pattern, while Red Headed Woodpeck-

516   prediction using majority voting, b) extend the decision process with MCBM-Seq for more intuitive
517
      and higher-performance results, or c) use MCBM-Joint’s less intuitive probabilities for maximum
      accuracy. Importantly, unlike a purely soft CBM, leakage will not impact all decision-making paths
518
      in a mixed CBM and will be isolated to only some leaf nodes that can be extended.

520
      6    C ONCLUSION
521
522
      In this work, we introduce MCBM-Seq and MCBM-Joint methods that use decision trees to inspect
523   and control information leakage in CBMs. These tree-based approaches maintain high fidelity in the
524   explanations and achieve better accuracy on datasets with incomplete concept information. Unlike
525   purely Soft CBMs, the mixed concept representations limit information leakage in data subsets with
526   insufficient concept information. They also quantify leakage per decision path and rule, producing
527   more meaningful group-based explanations.
528
      A limitation of our work is that the final, merged trees can be difficult to visualise and inspect for very
529   large datasets. Yet, we show that a leakage analysis for specific decision paths of interest can also
530   be meaningful. Also, mixed CBMs allow us to derive more interpretable and reliable explanations
531   but typically do not match the model accuracy of purely Soft CBMs. A promising avenue for future
532   work is to further address the problem of missing concept information by combining our approach
533   with concept discovery strategies. While information leakage makes up for some of the missing
534   concept information, these discovery strategies could be optimised per decision path in our tree-
535   structure, to further distinguish groups of samples that cannot take advantage of leakage. Moreover,
536   our method does not pose any constraint on the architecture of the concept encoder, thus it could
537
      also integrate a more expressive Auto-Regressive Concept encoder or a Stochastic Concept Encoder
      to further assist the concept predictions.

      As in section 4.2, consider again the subset of samples s that end up in one of the leaf nodes of the
715   global tree, and a soft concept ĉk based on which we perform the first split in the Mixed Sequential
716   CBM. Consider also the two subsets after the split s1 and s2 , and the target distributions ys , ys1 and
717   ys2 respectively. According to Breiman et al. (1984), the Information Gain of a split in a decision
718   tree can be defined as the difference of the entropy of the subset s before the split and the weighted
719   sum of the entropy’s of the two nodes after the split using the concept ĉk :
720
      If cs represents the set of hard concepts that appear in the decision path of the global tree up to the
721   leaf node of the subset s, then we assume that

725   construct the target distribution of this path ys from the initial target distribution of all samples y
726   when building the global tree. Similarly, given we have the information from the hard concepts of
727
      the path cs and the new soft concept ĉk , we assume that:

728                                                   |s1 |           |s2 |

729                                                    |s|             |s|
730   Thus, we can approximate the Information Leakage given to the label encoder by the soft concept
731   ĉk according to Eq. 4:

      only valid because of the way we constructed our tree, by first using the hard concepts to fit the
740

      A.3    L EAKAGE I NSPECTION : A NAIVE SOLUTION
743
744   A naive attempt to inspect any Information Leakage that the Soft Model exploited compared to a
745   Hard CBM is to first train the same concept predictor for both models, and then use a separate
746   Decision Tree classifier for each CBM as label predictor. In the first case, the inputs of the Tree
747   are binary, ground truth concepts, whereas in the second case the inputs are the predicted concept
748   probabilities of the concept encoder. To answer the question, we then need to compare two Trees.
749   For reference, we visualise the Decision Trees trained for the Morpho-MNIST example Castro et al.
750   (2018) with a constraint on the minimum samples per lead of msl = 150. The trees are shown
751   side-by-side in Fig. 6.
752   We immediately observe that the task is not trivial because the structure of the two trees can be very
753   different. Even if the root node uses the same concept in both trees to perform the primary split (e.g.
754   ”length xlarge”), the different threshold value used in each case may cause the resulting subsets
755   to be completely different. Thus, the two trees may be incomparable after the first split, since the
      respective children nodes may use a completely different structure to distinguish their samples.

788   A.4    C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS FOR THE LABEL PREDICTOR OF MCBM-S EQ
789
      An advantage of our approach is the limited computational overhead compared to training a standard
790
      independent CBM, because the concept encoder is trained only once. The individual Sequential
791
      CBMs all share the same concept predictor. Thus, the only overhead is that of training a sub-tree for
792   each leaf of the global tree. In practice, this added computational cost is minimal because each tree
793                                                                        Pd
      only has access to a small subset ni of the total samples n, where i ni = n and d is the number
794   of leaf nodes in the global tree.
795
796   More specifically, the time complexity of a Decision Tree is O(mn log2 n) according to Sani et al.
797
      (2018), where n is the total number of samples and m is the number of attributes. This holds because
      the computational cost of performing a split based on one attribute follows the recursion formula of
798
      the divide and conquer algorithm O(n log2 n), and this process is repeated for all m attributes to
799
      find the best split. Thus, the total cost J of training the label predictor in the Leakage Inspection
800   algorithm can be written as the cost of training the global tree and all its sub-trees:
801
                                                    d
802                                                 X
803
                                    J = Jglobal +       Ji −→ O(mn log2 n)                            (11)
                                                    i
804
805
      The complexity is thus equivalent to that of a purely soft Sequential CBM that uses a single decision
806
      tree as label predictor, since the msl constraint of the global and sub-trees is the same.

951   A.8.2    M IMIC -IV
952
953   In this section we demonstrate that the msl constraint does not only affect the Task Accuracy and
954   the total size of the MCBM-Seq or MCBM-Joint tree, but also the meaning of our explanations in
955   terms of the Information Leakage inspected by either method.
956   In the following figures, the MIMIC-IV trees are shown after the application of the MCBM-Seq
957   algorithm for low, medium and high msl values. The nodes annotated dark grey show the splits
958   of the global tree, and the nodes with light grey show those of a leaky extension, if found. When
959   msl = 150, we observe that the tree is too restricted for this small-sized dataset. Yet, the MCBM-
960   Seq is able to find a leaky split under this constraint for patients whose renal system is in moderate
961   condition (SOFA score 2-3). If the concept predictor is more than 80% confident that the condition
962   of a given patient is indeed moderate, the label predictor is more confident that this patient may live.
963
      When the constraint is slightly relaxed at msl = 70, we observe that the corresponding tree can
      split the same group of patients with a second hard concept (condition of the cardiovascular system)
964
      and does not need to use Information Leakage. Thus, this constraint fits better for this dataset. For
965
      medium-sized groups, using an msl = 50, we observe that the predictor can fit the data even better,
966   without the presence of leakage. However, when msl = 30, the two discovered leaky rules are
967   very non-intuitive: When the concept predictor is more than 90% confident that the renal system is
968   in severe condition, it is more likely that a patient may live. When a rule is very non-intuitive, it
969   indicates a high noise in concept annotations. On the contrary, the leaky rule described in the first
970   tree aligns with human intuition. This case-study shows that tuning the msl constraint is critical
971   for the performance and interpretability of our methods. Yet, this is the only single hyper-parameter
      that our label-predictor has.
