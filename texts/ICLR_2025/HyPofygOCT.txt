010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012
               In this paper, we introduce a new post-training compression paradigm for Large
013
               Language Models (LLMs) to facilitate their wider adoption. We delve into LLM
014            weight low-rank decomposition, and find that the challenges of this task stem from
015
               ❶ the distribution variance in the LLM activations and ❷ the sensitivity difference
016            among various kinds of layers. To address these issues, we propose a training-free
017            approach called Activation-aware Singular Value Decomposition (ASVD). Specif-
018            ically, ❶ ASVD manages activation outliers by transforming the weight matrix
019            based on the activation distribution. This transformation allows the outliers in
020            the activation matrix to be absorbed into the transformed weight matrix, thereby
021            enhancing decomposition accuracy. ❷ Additionally, we propose an efficient iter-
022
               ative calibration process to optimize layer-specific decomposition by addressing
               the varying sensitivity of different LLM layers. In this way, ASVD can compress
023
               a network by 10%-30%. Based on the success of the low-rank decomposition of
024
               projection matrices in the self-attention module, we further introduce ASVD to
025            compress the KV cache. By reducing the channel dimension of KV activations,
026            memory requirements for KV cache can be largely reduced. ASVD can further
027            achieve 50% KV cache reductions without performance drop in a training-free
028            manner. Code is anonymously available in supplementary materials.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In the realm of Large Language Models (LLMs) compression, various techniques have been exten-
034
      sively explored, including weight quantization [Dettmers et al., 2022], network pruning [Frantar &
035
      Alistarh, 2023], and knowledge distillation [Agarwal et al., 2023]. Distinct from these approaches, the
036   paradigm of low-rank matrix decomposition is less explored in LLMs but holds significant promise.
037   Decomposition involves approximating the weight matrices in neural networks with matrices of lower
038   rank, effectively reducing the model size. Given the massive number of parameters in LLMs, low-rank
039   decomposition offers significant potential for memory reduction. Furthermore, low-rank decompo-
040   sition can complement existing LLM compression techniques by further compressing quantized or
041   pruned models, enhancing overall efficiency [Cheng et al., 2017].
042   From the perspective of network compression, traditional low-rank decomposition methods typically
043   adhere to a straightforward process: initially training the original model and subsequently fine-tuning
044   the decomposed model [Jaderberg et al., 2014, Khodak et al., 2021, Wang et al., 2021, Hsu et al.,
045   2022]. While this approach is effective, it is resource-intensive and requires the entire training dataset
046   and substantial computational power for end-to-end backpropagation. Applying this method to LLMs
047   would encounter major challenges. Firstly, the training data for LLMs may not always be readily
048   available, often restricted by privacy and commercial considerations. Secondly, the training process
049   for these models is notoriously expensive, both in terms of time and computational resources.
050   Given these constraints, the concept of “training-free” compression emerges as a more viable
051   approach for LLMs [Zhu et al., 2023]. This approach includes methods like LLM post-training
052   quantization [Dettmers et al., 2022, Yuan et al., 2023] and LLM post-training pruning [Frantar &
053   Alistarh, 2023], which compress LLMs without the need for extensive retraining. These training-free
      (i.e., post-training) methods offer a more practical solution for efficiently compressing LLMs.

      To realize LLM low-rank decomposition in a training-free manner, we conduct an extensive
067
      analysis of the baseline methods for LLM decomposition. We first observe that straightforward
068   application of existing low-rank decomposition techniques, which typically necessitate training, turns
069   out ineffective for LLMs [Denton et al., 2014, Lebedev et al., 2014, Sainath et al., 2013, Moczulski
070   et al., 2015, Jaderberg et al., 2014, Khodak et al., 2021, Wang et al., 2021].
071
      Digging into the failures, we reveal two challenges to post-training decomposition for LLMs. ❶
072
      Managing activation distribution in LLMs: This challenge involves addressing outliers in the acti-
073
      vations, which can intensify the decomposition error. The importance of handling such outliers in
074   LLMs echoes findings in recent quantization research [Lin et al., 2023, Kim et al., 2023]. These
075   outliers can disproportionately affect the accuracy of matrix approximations, leading to suboptimal
076   compression results. ❷ Balancing layer’s decomposition sensitivity: Some layers are more sensitive
077   to the decompostion than others, and decomposing them uniformly can lead to significant perfor-
078   mance degradation. The key challenge is to balance the sensitivity of each layer with the efficiency
079   of the whole network’s decomposition.
080   Targeting challenge ❶, we propose the activation-aware decomposition method, where the distribution
081   of activations are considered into the weight decomposition process. Specifically, we transform the
082   values in the weight matrix column-wisely via a scaling matrix. The scaling matrix is designed
083   based on the distribution patterns observed across input activation channels. This adjustment proves
084   particularly beneficial for activation with outliers, allowing the decomposition to allocate enhanced
085   focus to these specific weights. Targeting challenge ❷, we further investigate the varying sensitivity
086   of different LLM layers to decomposition. We find that weights in Multi-Head Attention layers
087   [Vaswani et al., 2017] tend to be more resilient to decomposition compared to those in Multi-Layer
088
      Perceptron layers. This sensitivity variability across layers prompts us to develop a method to assign
      the compression ratio for each layer. ASVD assesses each layer’s sensitivity to decomposition at
089
      different ranks, enabling us to assign a suitable rank for optimal decomposition. Note that this probing
090
      assess is very efficient, requiring only a limited sample set for evaluation.
091
092   Our experiments reveal that ASVD can reduce the rank of the weight matrix by 10% to 90% in
093   different layers, and it can achieve compression of model size 10%-30% in LLaMA models [Touvron
094
      et al., 2023a;b]. We also validate ASVD is compatible with 4/8-bit weight quantization, which is
      described in Sect. 4.4.
095
096   Importantly, leveraging the successful low-rank decomposition of projection matrices in the self-
097   attention module, we can integrate ASVD with KV cache compression. Specifically, by applying
098   ASVD to decompose the Key/Value projection matrices, we can derive low-rank intermediate acti-
099   vations that serve as replacements for the KV cache stored in a high-dimension space, as shown in
100
      Fig. 1b. This substitution significantly reduces the memory usage of the KV cache, enabling support
      for larger batch sizes or longer sequence lengths, which are essential for real-world applications [Yuan
101
      et al., 2024]. In practice, by replacing the KV cache with intermediate low-rank activations, we can
102
      reduce up to 50% of the memory consumption of the KV cache.

106   Large Language Model Compression. The field of model compression for Large Language Models
107   (LLMs) has seen a surge of innovative techniques aimed at mitigating the substantial computation and
      memory requirements these models demand [Zhu et al., 2023, Yuan et al., 2024]. Various methods


                                                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      have emerged to address this challenge, each taking a unique approach to reduce the memory footprint
109   of LLMs. These methods primarily fall into three categories: weight quantization [Courbariaux et al.,
110   2015, Dettmers et al., 2022], network pruning [LeCun et al., 1989, Frantar & Alistarh, 2023], and
111   knowledge distillation [Hinton et al., 2015, Agarwal et al., 2023]. For the wide body of research
112   on LLM compression, please refer to [Zhu et al., 2023] for the comprehensive survey. Among
113   these methods, weight quantization has gained significant traction in the context of LLMs due to its
114   effectiveness. However, despite its popularity as a neural network compression technique, low-rank
115   factorization has not been extensively explored in the realm of LLMs. Recognizing this gap, we
116   introduce a novel low-rank decomposition method tailored specifically for decomposing the weight
117
      matrices of LLMs in a training-free manner.
118   Low-rank Decomposition. In the realm of low-rank decomposition [Schotthöfer et al., 2022] for
119   neural network compression, existing methods can be broadly classified into two categories: fixed low
120   rank and variable low rank approaches. Fixed rank methods typically involve decomposing weight
121   matrices of pre-trained networks using techniques like Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) or tensor
122   decomposition, followed by fine-tuning the factorized network [Denton et al., 2014, Lebedev et al.,
123
      2014, Sainath et al., 2013, Moczulski et al., 2015]. They also involve constraining weight matrices to
      maintain a fixed low rank during training [Jaderberg et al., 2014, Khodak et al., 2021, Wang et al.,
124
      2021], or constructing layers as linear combinations of layers with varying ranks [Ioannou et al.,
125
      2015]. A notable limitation of these methods is the introduction of matrix decomposition rank as
126   a hyperparameter requiring fine-tuning. In contrast, rank-adaptive methods address this limitation
127   by automatically determining and adjusting the low-rank structure. In particular, Kim et al. [2015;
128   2019] apply heuristics search to pre-determine the decomposition rank, while Wen et al. [2017] learn
129   low-rank weights through a loss function penalizing approximated matrix ranks. Li et al. [2023] use
130   low-rank approximation plus a sparse matrix to compress the weight matrix in transformers.
131
      However, none of these methods have worked in the era of LLMs due to their training-require nature.
132   We propose ASVD, a post-training LLM decomposition approach enabling the adaptive determination
133   of SVD ranks to optimize the matrix approximations based on feature activations. To our knowledge,
134   ASVD represents the first attempt to compress the weights of LLMs through decomposition in a
135   training-free manner. Since the introduction of ASVD, there have been subsequent works on training-
136   free LLM decomposition, such as SVD-LLM [Wang et al., 2024] and Palu [Chang et al., 2024].
137   These follow-up studies underscore the significance and potential of our approach. We hope that
138   our proposed post-training LLM decomposition method can establish a new paradigm for LLM
139   compression, opening up avenues for more efficient and accessible deployment of LLMs. Recently,
140
      Lin et al. [2024] highlight a key issue of SVD-based LLM compression methods including ASVD: the
      full-rank decomposition initially doubles the parameter count of the original model. Consequently,
141
      achieving a 90% compression ratio of the original model’s parameters requires approximately 55%
142
      rank reduction in the decomposed matrices. They observe that more efficient compression can be
143   achieved in layers without intermediate non-linear activation functions, where a 50% rank reduction
144   directly corresponds to a 50% parameter reduction. This paradigm shows more potential of low-rank
145   decomposition for LLM compression.

150   3.1   NA ÏVE SVD FOR C OMPRESSING W EIGHT M ATRIX
151
152   Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) can be used to decompose the weights of linear layers, which
153   involves decomposing a weight matrix W ∈ Rm×n into three matrices: U, Σ, and VT , such that
154   W ≈ UΣVT ), where Σ is an m × n diagonal matrix, the diagonal values in Σ are the singular
155   values of W, and U ∈ Rm×m and V ∈ Rn×n are corresponding right and left singular vector
156
      matrices, respectively [Demmel, 1997].
157   The SVD compression process for a weight matrix can be summarized in three steps: Decomposition:
158   Factorize W using SVD. Truncation: Retain the top k singular values and their corresponding right
159   and left singular vectors. This results in approximated matrices Uk , Σk , and VkT , where the right
160   singular vector matrix Uk is m × k, singular Σk is k × k, and left singular vector matrix VkT is
161   k × n. The choice of k is critical in balancing the compression ratio and the compressed model’s
      performance. Reconstruction: Reconstruct an approximated weight matrix: Wk = Uk Σk VkT .

181   3.2   C HALLENGES OF C OMPRESSING LLM S VIA SVD
182
183
      Decomposing the large matrices in LLMs (e.g., 4096 × 4096 matrices ubiquitous in LLaMA-
      7b [Touvron et al., 2023a]) into lower ranks presents a viable pathway for model compression.
184
      However, straightforward application of existing low-rank decomposition techniques [Denton et al.,
185
      2014, Lebedev et al., 2014, Moczulski et al., 2015, Khodak et al., 2021, Wang et al., 2021, Li et al.,
186   2023], which typically necessitate training, proves ineffective for LLMs.
187
188   Challenge 1: Influence of Activation: This perspective shifts the focus from solely relying on the
189
      truncation error Lt , which depends only on the model’s weights, to also accounting for the activations.
      The rationale behind this is the critical role of outliers in activations within LLMs [Lin et al., 2023,
190
      Wei et al., 2022, Kim et al., 2023]. Thus, for effective LLM decomposition, our objective optimization
191
      becomes:
192                                    Wk⋆ = arg min ∥Wk X − WX∥2F .                                       (1)
193                                                 W   k

194   Here, X represents the input activations, which are cached from a small calibration set. This set is
195   derived from the pre-training dataset to avoid overfitting to a specific task. Essentially, our objective
196   is to ensure that the output of the decomposed LLM closely mimics the output of the original LLM,
197
      rather than merely aligning their weights. This approach prioritizes functional equivalence over
      structural similarity, recognizing that accurate output replication is more critical for maintaining
198
      the model’s post-decomposition performance. We define the variation in activations between the
199
      compressed matrix Wk and the original matrix W as:
200
201                                               ∆Y = (Wk − W)X.                                             (2)
202
      To illustrate this concept, we visualize an example of W, Wk (decomposed by simply SVD), X, and
203
      the resulting variation in activations ∆Y in Fig. 2 (Top line). This visualization reveals a critical
204   insight: even when the variation in weights ∆W = W − Wk is relatively minor, the corresponding
205   variation in activations ∆Y can be huge. This significant variation in activations is a key factor in
206   why a straightforward SVD-based decomposition approach falls short in effectively decomposing
207   LLMs. The activation variations, despite being derived from input activations of large magnitude
208   (not the weight variations), can lead to considerable changes in the whole model’s output, thereby
209   undermining the decomposition’s efficacy.
210   Challenge 2: Singular Values Variations among Layers: The distribution of singular values
211   within a matrix is indicative of its sparsity and, by extension, its sensitivity to certain types of
212   information [Kim et al., 2015; 2019, Wen et al., 2017]. In LLMs, there is a notable variation in
213   singular values across different layers. Specifically, some layers exhibit a concentration of large
214   singular values, signifying less sensitivity to weight variation. This characteristic often correlates with
215   these layers being easy to compress. Conversely, other layers in the LLMs display a more uniform
      distribution of smaller singular values. Such a pattern suggests a balanced contribution from various

216
      singular vector pairs. This variability in the distribution of singular values among layers presents a
217   unique challenge, as it implies that each layer may require a tailored approach to decompose and
218   maintain the overall functionality of the LLM.
219
220
      These challenges underscore the necessity for innovative approaches specifically designed for the
      LLM decomposition. Our objective is to achieve efficient compression while circumventing the
221
      substantial computational and data demands associated with training-based methods. To address the
222
      first challenge, we introduce an Activation-aware SVD mechanism, which is detailed in Section 3.3.
223   This method is designed to mitigate the impact of weight variation on activations. For the second
224   challenge, we propose a Sensitivity-based Truncation Rank Searching mechanism, elaborated in
225   Section 3.4, which adapts to the varying singular value distributions among different layers.

      The second challenge arises from the fact that different layers in LLMs exhibit varying degrees
      of sensitivity to information compression, which is reflected in the distribution of their singular
298
      values. Targeting this challenge, we propose the Sensitivity-based Truncation Rank Searching (STRS)
299
      method. STRS evaluates the layer sensitivity and decides the best truncation of singular values. In
300   the realm of NLP, perplexity is a key metric for assessing how effectively a language model predicts
301   a sequence of tokens [Brown et al., 2020]. Therefore, we use the reduction in perplexity on the
302   calibration dataset to evaluate the sensitivity of each layer. Similar to post-training compression
303   methods [Dettmers et al., 2022, Frantar et al., 2022, Frantar & Alistarh, 2023], we collect a small
304   number of input token sequences as calibration dataset. Concurrent work by Gao et al. [2024]
305   addresses rank optimization through a differentiable binary masking mechanism. Their method
306   employs regularization to ensure masked ranks maintain consistency with SVD’s inherent property of
307   sorted singular values.
308   The core of the sensitivity evaluation process involves an in-depth exploration of how the neural
309   network reacts to varying levels of truncation. We define a set of potential truncation ratios, denoted
310   as R = {0.1, 0.2, · · · , 0.9}. These ratios r = km+kn
                                                        mn   determine the fraction of the rank k preserved
311   during the SVD truncation for a weight matrix with dimensions m × n. For each linear layer in the
312   LLM, we iterate through these candidate ratios. At each ratio, truncated SVD is applied to the layer’s
313   weight matrix, temporarily replacing the original layer in the model with this decomposed version.
314
      The model’s perplexity is then evaluated on the calibration dataset.
315   This detailed exploration of sensitivity across various truncation levels provides essential insights
316   into each layer’s performance dynamics, informing the optimization and decision-making processes
317   in model compression. As illustrated in Fig. 3, there are noticeable variations in sensitivity among
318   the different layers. Three key observations emerge from this analysis: 1. Inversely Proportional
319   Relationship: lower parameter ratios tend to result in higher perplexity scores. 2. Higher Sensitivity in
320
      MLP Layers: MLP layers demonstrate higher sensitivity, indicating where more cautious truncation
      is necessary. 3. Variable Sensitivity Among Layers: Some layers exhibit relatively lower sensitivity,
321
      indicating potential for more aggressive compression.
322

343
      LLM inference with large context lengths can be incredibly resource-intensive, requiring high-end
344
      GPUs and, for the largest models, costly multi-GPU setups. Analysis of generative inference with
345
      LLMs reveals that, for relatively small batch sizes, the computation is primarily memory-bound
346   [Hooper et al., 2024, Liu et al., 2024]. Given the growing gap between computational speeds and
347   memory speeds, this issue is expected to worsen over time, making it crucial to address the memory
348   bottleneck. Further analysis indicates that the memory bottleneck is strongly correlated with context
349   size. For long sequence lengths, the main contributor to memory consumption is the KV cache storing,
350   so minimizing the KV cache can reduce both memory consumption and bandwidth requirements
351   [Yuan et al., 2024].
352   As we discussed in Sec.3.3, ASVD decomposes the key and value projection weight matrix W ∈
353   RN ×N into two low-rank matrices, U ∈ RN ×r and V ∈ RN ×r , in a training-free manner, where
354   N is the dimension of K/V embedding space. As shown in Fig.4, replacing the high-rank matrix
355   with two low-rank matrices via ASVD allows us to obtain intermediate activations in low-rank form.
356   These intermediate activations can be stored as a replacement for the original KV cache. In other
357   words, the original KV cache requires storing two L × N matrices. With ASVD, the new KV cache
358   only needs to store two L × r matrices. In summary, ASVD can compress the KV cache Nr times.
359   This significant reduction in memory usage for the KV cache enables larger batch sizes or longer
360
      sequence lengths, which are critical for real-world applications.

534   This study presents a training-free approach to compressing Large Language Models (LLMs). We
535   propose Activation-aware Singular Value Decomposition (ASVD) and Sensitivity-based Truncation
536   Rank Searching (STRS), effectively address the challenges posed by activation outliers and varying
537   layer sensitivities. These techniques enable more accurate and efficient decomposition, reducing
538   memory usage and computational demands while maintaining model performance. The successful in-
539   tegration of ASVD into KV cache compression further underscores its potential for broad applicability
      and substantial impact in real-world scenarios.

      A.1    I MPACT S TATEMENTS AND L IMITATIONS
705
706   In this study, we propose a technique that improves the efficiency of Large Language Models (LLMs),
707   making them more accessible. This approach helps to democratize LLMs by lowering deployment
708   costs and hardware barriers, facilitating their use in edge computing. However, it does not mitigate
709   the potential misuse of LLMs by malicious actors.
710   Despite the remarkable achievements of the ASVD method in compressing large language models
711   (LLMs), several limitations persist. One limitation arises when ASVD is combined with quantization
712   techniques, which can lead to a decline in model performance. While 8-bit weight quantization has
713   minimal effects on both original and ASVD-compressed networks, switching to 4-bit quantization can
714   result in a slight decrease in predictive accuracy. Additionally, ASVD faces difficulties in compressing
715   multi-layer perceptron (MLP) in LLMs, as these layers typically contain more parameters than self-
716   attention mechanisms, resulting in increased computational burdens due to their high-dimensional
717
      feature mappings. Although ASVD effectively compresses the weights in multi-head attention (MHA)
      with fewer parameters, it struggles with MLP. Furthermore, the need to evaluate the sensitivity of each
718
      layer requires a forward propagation step to calculate perplexity, demanding significant computational
719
      resources.
