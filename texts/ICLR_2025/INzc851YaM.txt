009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-objective reinforcement learning (MORL) is crucial for real-world applica-
012            tions where multiple conflicting goals must be optimized, such as in healthcare or
013            autonomous systems. Offline MORL extends these benefits by using pre-collected
014            datasets, allowing for effective learning without continuous interaction with the
015            environment. However, existing offline MORL algorithms often struggle with
016            scaling across large preference spaces and handling unknown preferences dur-
017            ing evaluation. To address these challenges, we propose the Preference-Attended
018            Multi-Objective Decision Transformer (PA-MODT), a novel architecture that in-
019
               tegrates a preference-attention block with a modular transformer structure. This
               design enables effective generalization over different preferences and trajectories,
020
               providing a more robust approach to generating optimal Pareto fronts. We tested
021
               PA-MODT on five D4MORL datasets with millions of trajectories representing
022            various objectives and found that it consistently outperforms existing models,
023            achieving Pareto fronts that align closely with behavioral policy. This demon-
024            strates PA-MODT’s potential to effectively manage complex multi-objective rein-
025            forcement learning tasks.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Offline reinforcement learning (OfflineRL) has seen a surge in popularity due to its capability to
031   effectively utilize pre-existing datasets to train optimal policies without the need for direct inter-
032   action with the environment. This approach offers a data-driven pathway to learning, allowing for
033   sample-efficient policy optimization Prudencio et al. (2023). After training, the policies can either
034   be fine-tuned through environmental interactions or deployed for immediate use, making OfflineRL
035   a versatile tool in applications where real-time interaction is expensive or risky, such as autonomous
036   driving, robotics manipulation, and dialog generation Levine et al. (2020). Various OfflineRL al-
037   gorithms have emerged, encompassing both model-based approaches from Kidambi et al. (2020);
038   Yu et al. (2020; 2021), and model-free strategies from Fujimoto & Gu (2021); Kumar et al. (2019);
039
      Wu et al. (2019), typically leveraging temporal difference learning Sutton & Barto (2018) or value
      function estimation. An alternative route has also been explored with Decision Transformer Chen
040
      et al. (2021) and Reinforcement Learning via Supervised Learning (RvS) Emmons et al. (2021),
041
      which rely on autoregressive generative modeling. This shift towards OfflineRL is transforming
042   the landscape of reinforcement learning by reducing the need for costly environmental interactions
043   while offering flexibility for real-world applications.
044
045
      In RL, the primary goal is to derive a policy that maximizes the return for a specific objective func-
      tion. However, many real-world applications demand the optimization of multiple objectives simul-
046
      taneously. Consider wind turbine control Hayes et al. (2022), which must balance power output with
047
      reducing component fatigue to extend turbine lifespan, or medical treatment, which involves opti-
048   mizing effectiveness while minimizing side effects. Multi-objective reinforcement learning (MORL)
049   addresses such scenarios by accommodating conflicting goals within a single framework. MORL
050   algorithms in online settings generally focus on predefined preferences, either targeting a single opti-
051   mal policy Van Moffaert et al. (2013); Roijers et al. (2013) or generating multiple policies Mossalam
052   et al. (2016); Roijers et al. (2014) to cover a range of desired outcomes. This versatility makes
053   MORL a robust approach for complex, real-world optimization problems where balancing multiple
      conflicting objectives is essential.

075   preferences in the dataset (Evaluation Phase). The Pareto front ultimately represents the ant’s movement for
076   various preferences, highlighting the trade-offs and optimal solutions achieved.

079   Offline MORL extends the advantages of OfflineRL by using previously collected datasets to op-
080   timize learning across multiple objectives, eliminating the need for real-time interaction with the
081   environment. An overview of the offline MORL process is shown in Figure 1. Similar to online
082   MORL, offline MORL algorithms Wu et al. (2021); Thomas et al. (2021) aims to create either a
083   single optimal policy or an ensemble of policies based on predefined target preferences. However,
084   offline MORL often struggles with scalability across a large preference space 1 and managing un-
085   known preferences a priori. To address these issues, the Pareto Efficient Decision Agent (PEDA)
086
      framework Zhu et al. (2023) integrates preference information into conventional OfflineRL inputs
      (i.e., states, actions, reward), creating preference-conditioned trajectories by concatenating prefer-
087
      ences with other inputs. This approach allows policies to generalize across both trajectories and
088
      preferences. The PEDA framework introduced large-scale datasets derived from the MuJoCo envi-
089   ronment Xu et al. (2020), containing millions of pre-recorded trajectories, demonstrating effective
090   methods for building multi-objective decision transformers and multi-objective RvS models to tackle
091   complex MORL tasks.
092
      Preferences, which are fixed within a single trajectory example in a dataset, are time-independent
093
      features. In contrast, states, actions, and returns are time-dependent features that vary with each
094
      time step. MODT uses a transformer architecture Vaswani et al. (2017) to predict future actions by
095   training on a mix of these time-dependent and time-independent features. Prior studies indicate that
096   simply adding preferences as an extra token along with time-dependent features in the transformer
097   can create a weak correlation between preferences and predictions Zhu et al. (2023). However,
098   concatenating these two types of features has proven to be a more effective method for autoregressive
099   training, known as preference conditioning. On the other hand, the authors in Ghanem et al. (2023)
100   point out that the decision transformer in online settings Zheng et al. (2022) may not fully leverage
101   the transformer model’s potential for future action prediction, as distinct attention blocks tend to
102   learn uniform patterns. This challenge indicates that integrating preferences directly with trajectory-
103
      based time-dependent features could result in inefficient model utilization and limit the flexibility
      of preference prediction. By contrast, modifications to transformer architecture targeting specific

124

      Offline RL The key challenge of OfflineRL is handling the out-of-distribution behavior, referred
126
      to as distribution shift Levine et al. (2020); Prudencio et al. (2023). A class of algorithms applies
127   behavior policy regularization to avoid distribution shift so that the learned policy stays close to the
128   behavior policy. Batch Constraint Deep Q-learning (BCQ) Fujimoto et al. (2019) applied off-policy
129   learning, combining the Q-network with a state-conditioned variational auto-encoder to model the
130   behavior policy distribution. Bootstrapping Error Accumulation Reduction (BEAR) Kumar et al.
131   (2019) is an actor-critic algorithm that uses maximum mean discrepancy between samples from the
132   learned policy and pre-modeled behavior policy as a policy regularization method. Behavior regu-
133   larized actor-critic (BRAC) Wu et al. (2019) introduced a general policy regularization framework
134   by evaluating previous works extensively.
135   Some algorithms learn a conservative Q-function by learning a lower bound of the true value func-
136   tion to handle distribution shifts. Conservative Q-learning (CQL) Kumar et al. (2020) and Con-
137   servative Offline Model-Based Policy Optimization (COMBO) Yu et al. (2021) prevent the over-
138   estimation of value function due to out-of-distribution actions using the above method. A few algo-
139   rithms do not explicitly handle distribution shift by applying any restriction but still handle the issue
140   using the single step of policy evaluation and improvement Kostrikov et al. (2021); Brandfonbrener
141
      et al. (2021), i.e., without off-policy evaluation.
142   MORL In online settings, a group of MORL strategies involves training a single policy corre-
143   sponding to a single preference vector by transforming the MORL problem into a single objective
144   RL problem through techniques such as scalarization Agarwal et al. (2022), combining objectives in
145   distributional space Abdolmaleki et al. (2020). A few single policy algorithms Abels et al. (2019);
146   Basaklar et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2019) aim to approximate the Pareto front with a single policy
147
      that generalizes over preference space and resolves the scalability issues in previously stated meth-
      ods. The other group of strategies involves obtaining an ensemble of multiple policies. A method of
148
      obtaining multiple policies is to apply single policy algorithms for multiple preferences Mossalam
149
      et al. (2016); Roijers et al. (2014). Xu et al. (2020) uses a prediction-guided evolutionary learn-
150   ing algorithm to obtain a set of disjoint policies corresponding to different segments in the Pareto
151   front space. Handa (2009) extends the Estimation of Distribution Algorithms approach to estimate
152   multiple policies in the MORL problem.
153
      Offline MORL MORL in offline settings has gotten attention recently, and a limited number of
154
      works exist in this domain. Pessimistic Dual Iteration (PEDI) Wu et al. (2021) employs dual gradient
155
      descent with pessimism while formulating the constraint problem (non-interactions with the envi-
156   ronment) as a primal-dual problem to find an optimal policy for a fixed preference vector. Thomas
157   et al. (2021) extends the work of Laroche et al. (2019) in multi-objective settings by adapting the
158   Seldonian framework for safe policy improvement for predefined preferences. Zhu et al. (2023) uti-
159   lizes decision models to find a single policy for all preferences by providing the information as input
160   to the models they evaluated on the proposed dataset D4MORL. Lin et al. (2024) extends existing
161   offline policy regularization method for single objectives into multi-objective settings, which is then
      evaluated on the D4MORL and proposed MOSB datasets.

      Setup and Notation A MORL environment is generally formulated as multiobjective markov
165
      decision process, represented by the tuple < S, A, P, R, γ, F, Ω >. The MOMDP tuple consists of
166                                                                            ′
      states (s ∈ S), actions (a ∈ A), a transition distribution P (s |s, a), a reward function (R), a discount
167
      factor (γ), a preference-reward mapping function (F ), and preferences (p ∈ Ω). At any timestep
168
      t, the next state of the agent is obtained using the transition function: st+t ∼ P (st+1 |st , at ).
169   The reward function generates the vector reward based on state and action as r = R(s, a) =
170   [R1 (s, a), R2 (s, a), ..., Rn (s, a)], where n is the total number of objectives and Ri (s, a) is the re-
171   ward obtained for ith objective. A trajectory contains the transitions taken by the agent, which
172   is represented as (s0 , a0 , r0 , s1 , a1 , r1 , ..., sT , aT , rT ) where T denotes the length of the trajectory.
173   The vector-valued return at any timestep t in a trajectory   Pt         is the discounted sum of the reward ob-
174   tained till the current timestep, given by Γt = i=0 γ i · ri . On the other hand, the return-to-go
175   at any timestep t represents the future return of the trajectory from the current timestep, given by
              PT      i−t
176   gt =      i=t γ     · ri . A preference-reward mapping function maps a vector-valued reward to a
177   scalar utility value. In this paper, we have used a linear preference-reward mapping function i.e.
178   F (r, p) = r · pT , where p is a preference vector such that p ∈ Ω.
179   Decision Transformer A family of OfflineRL algorithms tries to find an optimal policy that
180   maximizes the expected return on any given state in an MDP by applying supervised learning on
                                                      ′
181   some prerecorded RL dataset D = {(s, a, s , r)}. Decision Transformer (DT) is an OfflineRL
182   algorithm that formulates the RL problem as a sequence modeling task. DT applies autoregressive
183   training and generation on the GPT model to predict future action tokens by feeding return-to-go,
184   states, and actions as input. One input token contains a combination of return-to-go, state, and action
185   embeddings given by (g, s, a). A total of K such input tokens are used where K is called the context
186   length, and therefore, the final input is denoted as τ = (g1 , s1 , a1 , g2 , s2 , a2 , ..., gK , sK , aK ). For
187   the evaluation, the desired return, along with initial state is used to generate the required/optimal
188
      trajectory.
189   Pareto Optimality The goal of MORL is to obtain a policy (π(a|s, p)) that maximizes the ex-
190   pected return of the induced trajectory for any given preference vector p. A policy is evaluated for
191   a set of preferences containing a finite number of preference vectors, E = [p1 , p2 ...pm ]. A solution
192   set of returns (∆) is constructed containing returns corresponding to each preference vector. A point
193   β in the feasible solution set is considered Pareto efficient if there exists no other point α in the
194   feasible solution set such that Riβ < Riα for at least one objective function i ∈ {1, 2, ..., n}, where
195   Riβ and Riα denote the values of objective function i at points β and α, respectively. Mathematically,
196   this can be expressed as: ∄α ∈ ∆, α ̸= β : Riβ < Riα , ∃i ∈ {1, 2, ..., n}. The curve traced by all
197   Pareto-efficient points is called the Pareto front, as shown in Figure 1. The goodness of the policy is
198   measured by evaluating the obtained Pareto front on Hypervolume and Sparsity matrices.

203   MORL poses significant challenges, as it requires balancing time-dependent features like states,
204
      actions, and return-to-go with time-independent features such as preferences. To achieve robust
      generalization and effective decision-making in complex environments, finding an optimal archi-
205
      tecture that integrates these diverse features is crucial. We address this challenge by focusing on
206
      transformer-based models for their capacity to handle complex data and explore various approaches
207   to effectively integrate preference-based information. We experiment with different architectural
208   configurations to determine the most effective strategy for multi-objective RL:

474   We presented an empirical analysis of how utilizing the input preferences through structural ad-

483   per, our focus has primarily been on predicting future actions, yet we acknowledge the potential
484   benefits of including preferences, states, and return-to-go in these predictions. This prompts impor-

      and the potential improvement of prediction abilities by adding future state, return-to-go, and prefer-
