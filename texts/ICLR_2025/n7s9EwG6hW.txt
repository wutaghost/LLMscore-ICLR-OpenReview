009                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011             Open-source tools have actively propelled advancements in physical electronic
012             design, yet the deployment still requires substantial expertise. Recent progress in
013             large language model (LLM)-based agents offer potential for automating physical
014             design, but challenges remain in imparting domain-specific expertise and extract-
015             ing case-specific design objectives to meet complex requirements. To address
016
                these issues, we introduce LLM4Floorplan, a multi-agent Floorplanner powered
                by LLMs. Unlike flow-level approaches that design workflows for multiple tasks,
017
                LLM4Floorplan is the first task-level agent specifically dedicated to a single phys-
018
                ical design task. Specifically, we propose a simple yet effective search-cluster-
019             based retriever that extracts the most relevant and diverse solutions from prior
020             knowledge, drawing on essential domain-specific knowledge to ensure robust de-
021             sign performance. Building on the retriever, LLM4Floorplan integrates a novel
022             Dynamic Retrieval-Augmented Thought (DRAT) prompting technique in which
023             the LLM generation interacts with the retrieval system to precisely capture case-
024             specific design objectives. With these innovations, LLM4Floorplan simulates the
025             workflow of human engineers by facilitating task comprehension, model selection,
026             hyperparameter tuning, code revisions, and performance evaluation. Extensive
027
                evaluations on public circuits with seven different LLM backbones demonstrate
                that LLM4Floorplan exhibits strong task comprehension and decision-making ca-
028
                pabilities. Remarkably, for the strict requirement, LLM4Floorplan boosts the suc-
029
                cess rate from 0.250 to 0.875.

048   guage models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Tou- rameter tuning, code revisions, and perfor-
049   vron et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024), especially the mance evaluation.

051   ous scenarios like video game (Tan et al., 2024), smartphone users (Yang et al., 2023b), software
052   development (Qian et al., 2024), the potential of EDA agents has garnered significant attention from
053   both industry and academia (Wang et al., 2024c). However, in contrast to other agent systems,
      EDA agents face two critical challenges: 1) imparting sufficient domain-specific expertise to handle

      the intricacies of physical design, and 2) extracting case-specific design objectives to meet diverse
055   and complex requirements. Domain-specific expertise provides the foundation for understanding
056   and tackling general EDA challenges (e.g., floorplanning principles), while case-specific objectives
057   focus on how this knowledge is applied to meet the unique needs of an individual design.
058
      To address these, we target floorplan, a critical stage in physical design, and devise LLM4Floorplan,
059
      a multi-agent floorplanner powered by LLMs, to perform domain- and case-specific guidance, which
060
      designs floorplan layouts that successfully meet diverse requirements.
061
062   Specifically, inspired by RAT (Wang et al., 2024d), we develop a retrieval system (retriever) to ex-
063   tract domain-specific prior knowledge. Unlike RAT, which retrieves information via web searches,
064
      this approach is impractical for EDA design due to the closed-source nature of the community.
      To address this, we construct a database that stores historical design experience and apply a sim-
065
      ple search and clustering technique to obtain relevant, diverse instances as domain-specific prior
066
      knowledge. Building on the retriever, we propose a novel Dynamic Retrieval-Augmented Thought
067   (DRAT) prompting technique, enabling LLMs to interact with the retriever. With this technique,
068   LLM4Floorplan captures case-specific design objectives, facilitating the design of floorplan layouts
069   under complex requirements. These innovations allow LLM4Floorplan to reduce the labor-intensive
070   efforts of human engineers across various design tasks, including task comprehension, model selec-
071   tion, hyperparameter tuning, code revisions, and performance evaluation.
072
      To assess the efficacy of these techniques, we propose a benchmark comprising six distinct floorplan-
073   ning requirements. Empirical results on public datasets using seven LLM backbones demonstrate
074   improved performance and successful design outcomes. Notably, LLM4Floorplan achieves success-
075   ful designs even under complex and unseen requirements, showcasing its strength in generality and
076   potential for more personalized design solutions. The highlights of this work are:
077
      1) Pioneering Task-Level Multi-Agent for Physical Design. We introduce LLM4Floorplan, to the
078
      best of our knowledge, the first implementation of a multi-agent system dedicated to a specific phys-
079   ical design task. It builds on large language models (LLMs) to automate complex design processes,
080   providing a novel framework for floorplanning and reducing manual effort in design processes.
081
082
      2) Novel Retrieval System to Integrate Domain-Specific Design Guidance. We introduce a novel
      retrieval system featuring a search and clustering module that extracts the most relevant and diverse
083
      instances as domain-specific prior knowledge.
084
085   3) DRAT Prompting for Case-Specific Design Guidance. Building on the retrieval system, we
086   propose a novel DRAT prompting technique, which enables LLM4Floorplan to dynamically inte-
087   grate the retriever and LLM generation. DRAT enhances the system’s ability to capture case-specific
088
      design objectives, addressing diverse and complex design challenges effectively.
089   4) Benchmark and Significant Empirical Performance. We validate our approach by introducing
090   a new benchmark with six distinct design requirements and testing LLM4Floorplan on floorplan-
091   ning tasks using public circuits from the MCNC and GSRC datasets with seven LLMs. Our results
092   demonstrate that LLM4Floorplan consistently outperforms existing floorplanning baselines, partic-
093   ularly in strict design requirements, where the success rate improves from 0.250 to 0.875.

098   LLM prompting. Since the emergence of pretrained LLMs as foundation models, prompting engi-
099
      neering has been explored to improve answer quality, serving as an alternative to fine-tuning (Cobbe
      et al., 2021). Among various prompting techniques, in-text learning (Dong et al., 2022) enables
100
      LLMs to learn tasks from only a few examples provided as demonstrations. To enhance LLM ca-
101
      pabilities in solving math word problems, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) incorporates
102   intermediate reasoning steps, while Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020)
103   retrieves information from an external knowledge base. Combining the strengths of both CoT and
104   RAG, Retrieval-Augmented Thoughts (RAT) (Wang et al., 2024d) addresses long-horizon genera-
105   tion and improves rating scores. However, RAT lacks an external knowledge base when applied
106   directly as an EDA agent due to the closed-source nature of the EDA community. To overcome this
107   limitation, we propose a novel dynamic RAT prompting technique, along with a search-clustering-
      based retriever, to extract both domain- and case-specific guidance.

109   coming more popular to address entertainments or industrial problems. For example, Cicero (FAIR
110   et al.,2022), CRADLE (Tan et al., 2024), Park et al. (2023), and Xu et al. (2023) regard LLM as
111   game characters or players to interact with gaming environments. ToolLLM (Qin et al., 2023), Tool-
112   former (Schick et al., 2023), GPT4Tools (Yang et al., 2023a), and ToolkenGPT (Hao et al., 2023)

114   et al., 2024a) use LLM to simulate smartphones users. Additionally, Codex (Chen et al., 2021), App-
115   World (Trivedi et al., 2024) Chatdev (Qian et al., 2024) use LLMs to develop software. These agents
116   are generally powered by frequent interactions with environments and abundant datasets, which are
117
      intractable to be applied to EDA scenarios.

119   tention. Among these efforts, most of the LLM-based approaches are devised to generate Hardware
120   Description Language (HDL) code such as Verilog (Blocklove et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Lu
121   et al., 2024) or Register Transfer Level (RTL) code (Fu et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2024), which mainly

123
      et al., 2024) and DeLorenzo et al. (2024) are proposed for Verilog design optimization while Analog-
      Coder (Lai et al., 2024) uses LLMs to generate codes to design analog circuits. The most related
124
      works to our paper are ChipNeMo (Liu et al., 2023a) and ChatEDA (Wu et al., 2024), which design
125
      agents to interact with EDA tools. However, these agents operate at the flow level, designing high-
126   level workflows without significantly contributing to the enhancement of specific tasks, whereas
127   our task-level agent aims to improve a specific task. Our LLM4Floorplan is a task-level agent that
128   emphasizes floorplanning, which is a prior and critical task in physical design.

186   two challenges for EDA agents: 1) impart domain-specific expertise, and 2) extract case-specific
187   design objectives. Specifically, we respectively introduce an effective retriever in Sec. 4.1 and a
188   novel prompting technique in Sec. 4.2, and give a specific process of LLM4Floorplan in Sec. 4.3.
189   We show the pipeline in Fig. 3, where LLM4Floorplan contains four aspects, including datasets,
190   retrieval system, decisions, and dynamic information state process.

      4.1      S EARCH -C LUSTER - BASED R ETRIEVER
193
194   To address the first challenge, we initially construct a standard database D = (S, B, C) that contains
195   the set of executed instances S, the set of circuit information B, and the set of model selections C.
196   Specifically, we design an instance collection system. Each time an experiment is conducted, the
197   corresponding log is recorded as an instance S j ∈ S. These records serve as an experience pool,
198   allowing LLMs for retrieving domain-specific knowledge for improved decision.
199
      The retrievers in RAG (Lewis et al., 2020) and RAT (Wang et al., 2024d) are quite important in the
200   system. Unlike RAG, which fine-tunes a pretrained retriever and generator, LLM4Floorplan has lim-
201   ited instances from which to learn parametric knowledge. Therefore, to acquire better knowledge,
202   we develop a search-cluster-based retriever that selects instances based on relevance and diversity.
203
      Specifically, in the retriever, we employ a text encoder gϕ to obtain the embeddings gϕ (S j ) of
204
      each instance S j ∈ S and the embedding gϕ (B j ) of its circuit information B j . Then we utilize
205
      these embeddings to retrieve corresponding instances and circuits, respectively. The retriever is
206
      performed in two steps:
207
208   Relevance: We first identify the most relevant circuits based on their circuit information using
209   K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) search (Cover & Hart, 1967), a classical search technique. For a task
210
      involving a new circuit B i , we compute the cosine similarity between gϕ (B i ) and all previously seen
211   circuits B j ∈ B, selecting the top-k1 relevant circuits. To better align with real-world scenarios, note
212
      that typically B i ∈
                         / B.
213   Diversity: Among the top-k1 relevant circuits, diverse instances, such as excellent, poor, and failed
214   designs, are all valuable for improving the new design. Excellent instances provide insights for
215   optimal designs, while poor or failed instances help the model avoid ineffective model choices or
      hyperparameters. To capture this diversity, we apply spectral clustering (Ng et al., 2001), a technique

      Metrics. Following previous floorplan methods (Li et al., 2022), we utilize the total HPWL, as
      defined in Eq. 1, calculated across all nets. To evaluate the agents’ comprehension of design re-
306
      quirements, we introduce the Success Rate (SR), which quantifies the proportion of cases that meet
307
      the Overlapping Ratio (OR) criteria. For a comprehensive performance assessment, we propose the
308   Rank metric, primarily based on SR. A higher SR guarantees a better Rank, and when SR values are
309   identical, a lower HPWL results in an improved Rank. Further details on the total HPWL and OR
310   calculations are provided in Appendix D.2.

523   6                              C ONCLUSION AND O UTLOOK
524
525   In this paper, we presented LLM4Floorplan, the first task-level multi-agent system for physical de-
526   sign, particularly targeting the floorplanning stage in EDA. By introducing a search-cluster-based re-
527
      triever and the Dynamic Retrieval-Augmented Thought (DRAT) prompting technique, we addressed
      the challenges of domain-specific expertise integration and case-specific design guidance. Through
528
      extensive experiments on public datasets, we demonstrated the efficacy of LLM4Floorplan across
529
      diverse and complex requirements. The system achieved significant improvements in task compre-
530   hension, model selection, hyperparameter tuning, and performance evaluation. Our findings indicate
531   that LLM4Floorplan successfully simulates the workflow of human engineers and provides strong
532   generalization capabilities, paving the way for further research in applying LLM-powered agents to
533   other stages of physical design.
534
      This paper also has some limitations that suggest avenues for future work: 1) LLM4Floorplan faces
535
      challenges in handling highly novel designs due to its reliance on a predefined design database. 2)
536   The system has yet to be evaluated on expanded benchmarks or integrated into other physical design
537   stages, such as placement and routing.

826   The vanilla fixed-outline floorplanning problem, described in Sec. 3, can be formulated as
827                                        X

                                        Θ
829                                           ei ∈E

830                                    s.t. no overlapping area among blocks,                                 (4b)

834
      Although the optimization variables Θ include the width wi , height hi , and coordinates (xi , yi ) of
835   each block bi ∈ Vb , it is important to note that some parameters are fixed depending on the type of
836   block:
837
838           • For soft blocks, width wi , height hi , and coordinates (xi , yi ) are all learnable.
839
              • For hard blocks, width wi and height hi are fixed while coordinates (xi , yi ) are learnable.
840
841           • For pre-placed blocks, width wi , height hi , and coordinates (xi , yi ) are all fixed.
842
843   The first constraint in Eq. 4b ensures that there is no overlapping area among the blocks. The second
844   constraint in Eq. 4c ensures that each block maintains an appropriate aspect ratio that satisfies the
845
      given criterion. For example, it may require the aspect ratio to lie between 1/3 and 3, meaning the
      width-to-height ratio must be within the range [1/3, 3]. The third constraint in Eq. 4d ensures that
846
      all blocks remain within the fixed outline.
