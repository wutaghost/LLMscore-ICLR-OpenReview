009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Human visual experience is markedly different from the large-scale computer vi-
012            sion datasets consisting of internet images. Babies densely sample a few 3D
013            scenes with diverse variations such as object viewpoints or illuminations, while
014            datasets like ImageNet contain one single snapshot from millions of 3D scenes.
015            We investigated how these differences in input data composition (i.e., visual diet)
016            impact the Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) generalization capabilities of a visual sys-
017            tem. Training models on a dataset mimicking attributes of the human-like visual
018
               diet improved generalization to OOD lighting, material, and viewpoint changes
               by up to 18%. This observation held despite the fact that the models were trained
019
               on 1, 000-fold less training data. Furthermore, when trained on purely synthetic
020
               data and tested on natural images, incorporating these visual diet attributes in the
021            training dataset improved OOD generalization by 17%. These experiments are en-
022            abled by our newly proposed benchmark—the Human Visual Diet (HVD) dataset,
023            and a new model (Human Diet Network) designed to leverage the attributes of a
024            human-like visual diet. These findings highlight a critical problem in modern day
025            Artificial Intelligence—building better datasets requires thinking beyond dataset
026            size and rather focus on improving data composition. All data and source code
027            will be made available upon publication.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The development of the visual system is intricately tied to the visual experiences encountered from
033
      infancy (Kandel et al. (2000); Kreiman (2021); Arcaro et al. (2017); Hubel & Wiesel (1964); Daw
      & Wyatt (1976); Wood & Wood (2018; 2022); Bambach et al. (2018); Lee et al. (2021)). Growing
034
      evidence highlights the importance of visual experience (Smith & Slone (2017); Wood & Wood
035
      (2018; 2022); Sheybani et al. (2023; 2024); Tsotsos (1992); Tsotsos et al. (2019)). These visual
036   experiences are constrained by the statistics of natural scenes (Simoncelli & Olshausen (2001)),
037   resulting in data significantly different from large-scale datasets used in computer vision.
038
039
      Fig. 1a illustrates two such differences. First, children learn from the physical space they occupy—a
      few 3D scenes and objects viewed under diverse real-world transformations including viewpoints,
040
      lighting, object textures, and occlusions. Second, children always view objects in the context of
041
      their surroundings. We refer to these as real-world transformational diversity (RWTD) and scene
042   context, respectively. Here we investigate how these differences in input data composition impact
043   Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) generalization performance.
044
045
      Here we show that incorporating these visual diet attributes improves generalization. Models trained
      with a human-like visual diet achieve up to 18% improvement on OOD lighting, materials, and view-
046
      point changes. Training with such data outperforms training models on 1,000-fold larger internet
047
      datasets. Furthermore, when trained on synthetic images and tested on natural images, incorporating
048   attributes of the human visual diet improved OOD generalization performance by up to 17%. These
049   experiments are enabled by two key technical contributions. First, we introduce the Human Visual
050   Diet (HVD) dataset, which mimics the input data during visual development and contains both trans-
051   formational diversity and scene context (Sheybani et al. (2023); Smith & Slone (2017)) as shown
052   in Fig. 2. Second, we propose the Human Diet Network (HDNet)—a model designed to leverage
053   the attributes present in HVD (Fig. 1c). HDNet exploits transformational diversity by employing
      a contrastive loss over real-world transformations (lighting, material, 3D viewpoint changes), and

      2   R ELATED W ORK
087
      Out-of-Distribution (OOD) generalization continues to be the Achilles heel of modern AI (Engstrom
088
      et al. (2018); Chaman & Dokmanic (2021); Zhang (2019)). Failure modes include OOD rotations
089
      and translations (Engstrom et al. (2018); Chaman & Dokmanic (2021); Zhang (2019)), real-world
090   transformations including 3D viewpoints (Barbu et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2018); Zeng et al. (2019);
091   Madan et al. (2021c); Sakai et al. (2022); Zheng et al. (2023)), changes in lighting (Madan et al.
092   (2021c); Beery et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2021)), and color changes (Joshi et al. (2019); Shamsabadi
093   et al. (2020)), among other transformations.
094
      Existing approaches to counter this generalization gap include—specialized architectures (Shah-
095
      talebi et al. (2021); Sun & Saenko (2016); Arjovsky et al. (2019); Kim et al. (2021); Vedantam
096   et al. (2021); Krueger et al. (2021); Blanchard et al. (2017a)), novel pre-processing and data aug-
097   mentation strategies (Yun et al. (2019); Hendrycks et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2017); Madan et al.
098   (2021b;a)), and generative modeling (Ilse et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2020)), among others. Lately,
099   investigators introduced billion scale datasets like LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al. (2022)) and IG-1B
100   Targeted (Yalniz et al. (2019)) hoping that they will leave little out of the distribution. However,
101   despite progress, OOD samples remain an unsolved problem (Radford et al. (2021); Wortsman et al.
102   (2022); Pham et al.). We introduce a new dataset and model inspired by the human visual diet.
103   Some recent work has emphasized the importance of training with more human-like data ( Bambach
104   et al. (2018); Lee et al. (2021); Wood & Wood (2018; 2022)). These efforts include incorporating
105   scene context (Zhang et al. (2020)), temporal structure (Sheybani et al. (2024)), binocular vision
106   (Orhan et al. (2020); Orhan & Lake (2024)), and goal-directed/active sampling (Tsotsos (1992);
107   Tsotsos et al. (2019); Bajcsy et al. (2018); Bajcsy (1988); Pelgrim et al. (2024)), among others. Our
      work extends these efforts to Out-of-Distribution generalization.

      3.1   H UMAN VISUAL DIET (HVD) DATASET
163
164   3D scenes from ScanNet (n=1,288) (Dai et al. (2017)) were reconstructed using the OpenRooms
165   framework (Li et al. (2020a;b)), and 15 photo-realistic domains were constructed with these scenes
166   by introducing 3 real-world transformations—lighting, material, and viewpoint changes. For each
167   domain, 19, 800 images were rendered resulting in a total of 300, 000 images containing 1 million
168   object instances with controlled variations in lighting, materials, and viewpoints (Fig. 2a).
169   Light shift domains: Outdoor lighting was controlled using 250 High Dynamic Range (HDR) envi-
170   ronment maps from the Laval Outdoor HDR Dataset ( Hold-Geoffroy et al. (2019)) and OpenRooms.
171   These were split into 5 sets of 50 each to create 5 light shift domains. We split the HSV color space
172   into chunks of disjoint hue values. Each domain sampled indoor light color and intensity from one
173   chunk. One domain was held out for testing (OOD Light), and never used for training (see sample
174   images in Fig. S1).
175   Material shift domains: 250 procedural materials from Adobe Substances were used, including
176   different types of wood, fabrics, floor and wall tiles, and metals, among others. These were split
177   into sets of 50 materials each to create 5 different material domains. For each material domain, one
178   of these 50 materials were randomly assigned to each scene object. One domain was held out for
179   testing (OOD Materials), and never used for training (see sample images in Fig. S2).
180   Viewpoint shift domains: Disjoint viewpoint domains were constructed by changing the height at
181   which the camera focuses, i.e., the zenith angle. Five viewpoint domains were constructed, and one
182   was held out for testing (OOD Viewpoints, see sample images in Fig. S3).
183
184
185
186   3.2   S EMANTIC - I L AB DATASET
187
      Images from iLab (Borji et al. (2016)) were modified to create a natural image dataset with variations
188
      in lighting, material and viewpoints (Fig. 2b). iLab contains objects from 15 categories placed on a
189
      turntable and photographed from varied viewpoints. Fist, a foreground detector was used to extract
190   the object. Then, material variations were implemented using AdaIN-based style transfer (Huang
191   & Belongie (2017b)) on these object masks and the style transferred object was overlaid onto the
192   original background. Lighting changes were simulated by modifying the white balance. Unlike
193   HVD, this dataset does not contain scene context (see Sec. B for more details).
194
195   3.3   S YN 2R EAL DATASET: NATURAL IMAGE TEST SET FROM S CAN N ET
196
197   The Syn2Real dataset is composed of a test set of natural images from the ScanNet dataset, and a
198   training set of only synthetic images from HVD. The natural image test set was created by annotating
199   images from ScanNet ( Dai et al. (2017)). To capture distinct images, one frame was sampled every
200   100 frames from ScanNet’s raw video footage. These frames were then annotated using LabelMe
201
      (see Sec. H for further details).

203   4     H UMAN D IET N ETWORK (HDN ET )
204
205   A schematic of HDNet is shown in Fig 3. There are two main components to this model. First,
206   a two-stream network inspired by the eccentricity dependence of human vision that jointly reasons
207   over target object and context. Second, a contrastive loss over real world transformations.
208
      Two-stream network inspired by human vision: Given the training dataset D = {xi , yi }ni=1 ,
209
      HDNet is presented with an image xi with multiple objects and the bounding box for a single target
210   object location. The target (Ii,t ) is obtained by cropping the input image xi to the bounding box
211   whereas Ii,c covers the entire contextual area of the image xi . yi is the ground truth class label
212   for Ii,t . The first stream processes only the target object (It , 224 ⇥ 224) and outputs yt , while the
213   second stream processes the periphery (Ic , 224 ⇥ 224) and outputs yt,c . Based on the confidence in
214   the prediction yt (denoted p), HDNet computes a confidence-weighted average of yt and yt,c to get
215   the final prediction yp . If the model makes a confident prediction with the object only, it overrules
      the context reasoning stage.

296   5     R ESULTS
297
298   We present findings demonstrating the effectiveness of training models with two key attributes of the
299
      human visual diet:—Real-World Transformational Diversity (RWTD) and Scene Context. To begin,
      Sec. 5.1 establishes a lower baseline by benchmarking generalization capabilities of conventional
300
      vision models trained with low RWTD and minimal scene context. Building further, Sec. 5.2 and
301
      Sec. 5.3 respectively show that incorporating real-world transformation diversity and scene context
302   into the training data improves generalization significantly. Sec. 5.4 presents an extreme test of our
303   hypothesis, showing that models trained with a human-like visual data outperform models trained
304   on a 1000x-fold larger internet-scraped dataset. Finally, as a real-world litmus test, Sec. 5.5 shows
305   that a human-like visual diet leads to significant improvement in generalizing from synthetic images
306   from HVD to natural images from ScanNet.
307
      For all experiments, one domain per transformation (light, material, viewpoint) was held out as the
308   OOD test set and never used for training. As Real-World Transformational Diversity (RWTD) was
309   increased from 1 to 4 domains (20% to 80% RWTD), the number of images sampled per domain
310   were reduced. This ensured a fixed training dataset size. All models were pre-trained on ImageNet.
311   Additional details on hyperparameters and baseline models are provided in Sec. I and Sec. J.

      5.2     R EAL - WORD TRANSFORMATIONAL DIVERSITY (RWTD) IMPROVES GENERALIZATION
357
              FOR ALL OOD TRANSFORMATIONS
358
359
      For each transformation, we increased the amount of real-world transformational diversity (RWTD)
360   that the models were exposed to by training them using samples from more domains from 1 domain
361   (20%) to 4 domains (80%). Fig. 4 reports results for our proposed Human Diet Network (HDNet)
362   trained with the HVD dataset, and for a ResNet model trained with the Semantic-iLab dataset as
363   transformational diversity is increased. As described in Sec. 3 and Sec. 4, HDNet was designed
364   specifically to leverage the transformational diversity present in the HVD dataset.
365
      OOD generalization improved approximately monotonically with transformational diversity for all
366   three transformations in the HVD dataset (Fig. 4c). This improvement was significantly greater for
367   OOD materials than for OOD lighting (p < 10 4 ) and OOD viewpoints (p < 10 4 ). Increased
368   diversity improved generalization for a ResNet model trained on the Semantic-iLab dataset as well
369   (Fig. 4d). As with HVD, improvement in generalization across the Semantic-iLab dataset was higher
370   for unseen materials than for unseen lighting (p < 10 3 ) and unseen viewpoints (p < 10 6 ). Thus,
371   with sufficient diversity, generalization to OOD lighting and materials reached almost ceiling levels.
372   However, despite improvement, OOD viewpoints remained a challenge.
373   RWTD outperforms data augmentation: We compared the impact of training with real world
374   transformations versus training with traditional data augmentation (Crops, Rotations, Contrast, and
375   Solarize operations). Fig. 5a compares HDNet trained on HVD with 80% RWTD, and the same
376   architecture trained with 20% RWTD+traditional data augmentation. RWTD outperformed data
377   augmentation for all three transformations (two-sided t test, p < 10 4 ). The same was true for a
      ResNet model trained with Semantic-iLab dataset (Fig. 5(b)). For additional details, see Sec. G.

522   6     C ONCLUSIONS
523
524
525
      We investigated the impact of data composition on the out-of-distribution generalization capabilities
      of visual recognition models. Specifically, we demonstrated that incorporating two key components
526
      of the human visual diet—transformational diversity and scene context—improves generalization to
527
      OOD viewpoints, lighting, and material changes. Our contributions include three new benchmarks,
528   and a novel architecture that models and leverages these human-like visual attributes. This work
529   provides an approach complementary to existing directions on data augmentation and specialized
530   domain generalization architectures.
531
      While our results are promising, the human visual diet is complex and multifaceted, with several
532
      additional features like temporal information, egocentric views, embodiment, and goal-driven/active
533
      sampling that warrant further investigation. We hope that future datasets extending the Human
534   Visual Diet (HVD) dataset introduced here can address these. Similarly, the Human Diet Network
535   (HDNet) introduced here represents a promising first step in integrating human-like scene context,
536   but is currently limited to only spatial context. We hope that future work can build architectures
537   incorporating temporal context—such as motion and sequential dependency. In summary, this work
538   opens new avenues for aligning biological and artificial vision systems, and advancing generalization
539   in AI. Out-of-distribution generalization remains the Achilles’ heel of modern AI, and we hope
      future research in these directions will lead to models that generalize as effortlessly as human vision.

1046   E.2   R EAL - WORLD TRANSFORMATIONS OUTPERFORM AUGMENTATION WITH GENERATIVE
1047         AI.
1048
1049   Several existing works rely on increasing data diversity using AdaIn-based methods Huang & Be-
1050   longie (2017a); Zhou et al. (2021). These style transfer methods change the colors in the image while
1051   retaining object boundaries, but do not modify materials explicitly as done in our HVD dataset. We
1052   evaluated how well models perform if diversity is increased using style transfer as opposed to mate-
1053   rial diversity. We started with one material domain, and created four additional domains using style
1054   transfer. Sample images of style transfer domains are shown in Fig. 5(c). Corresponding images
1055   from the HVD dataset with real-world transformation in materials can be seen in Fig. 2(a). The
1056
       total number of domains (and images) created using style transfer was kept the same as the material
       domains in HVD. The only difference in the training data was that instead of four additional material
1057
       domains, we have four additional style transfer domains. We compared models trained with these
1058
       two different visual diets—one consisting of four material domains, and the other consisting of four
1059   style transfer domains. All models were then tested on the same held-out OOD Materials domain.
1060   Style transfer domains did not enable models to generalize to new materials as well as the material
1061   shift domains presented in HVD (Fig. 5(d)).
1062
       These experiments support the notion that in order to build visual recognition models that can gen-
1063
       eralize to unseen materials, it is important to explicitly increase diversity using additional materials
1064
       at the time of training data collection. The impact of diverse materials cannot be replicated by using
1065   style transfer to augment the dataset after data collection.

       E.3   E ACH INDIVIDUAL REAL - WORLD TRANSFORMATION IS HELPFUL
1069
1070
1071
       Some real-world transformations are easier to capture than others. For instance, capturing light
       changes during data collection might be significantly easier than collecting multiple possible room
1072
       layouts, or object viewpoints. Thus, it would be beneficial if training with one transforma-
1073
       tion (e.g., light changes) can improve performance on a different transformation (e.g., viewpoint
1074   changes). We refer to such a regime as assymetric diversity—as models are trained with one kind of
1075   diversity, and tested on a different kind of diversity (Fig. 5(e),(f)). In all cases, the best generaliza-
1076   tion performance was obtained when training and testing with the same real-world transformation
1077   for both HVD (Fig. 5(e)) and Semantic-iLab datasets (Fig. 5(f)). In most cases, there was a drop
1078   in performance of 10% or more when training in one transformation and testing with a different
1079   (assymetric) transformation. These experiments imply that to build models that generalize well, it
       is important to collect training data with multiple real-world transformations.

1083   Given the success of HDNet, we asked whether implementing a two-stream separation of target and
1084   context would also improve performance for other architectures. We modified ResNet18 He et al.

1086   For ResNet, a two-stream version was made where each stream is a ResNet backbone. One stream
1087   operates on the target, and the other one on the scene context. Output features from each stream were
1088
       concatenated, and passed through a fully connected layer for classification as shown in Fig. 1(c).
       The two-stream architecture for ViT was analogous. In contrast, the one-stream architecture did not
1089
       use scene context and operated on the target object alone (see methods for additional details). The
1090
       two-stream architectures consistently led to improved performance (two-sided t test, p < 0.05), as
1091   shown in Table 3.

1094
       additional experiments. Firstly, we evaluated the impact of reducing scene context information by
       blurring it using a Gaussian Blur. As shown in Table. 2, performance dropped consistently for all
1095
       three transformations as contextual information is reduced. Secondly, we confirmed that the increase
1096
       in performance is due to the addition of contextual information and not due to the two-stream ar-
1097   chitecture per se by training HDNet with both streams receiving only the target information. This
1098   removal of context led to a drop in performance, as reported in Table. S1 (see Sec. F for details).

       J.2   E VALUATION OF COMPUTATIONAL MODELS
1218
1219   Performance for all models is evaluated as the Top-1 classification accuracy. Error bars reported
1220   on all figures refer to the variance of per-class accuracies of different models. For statistical test-
1221   ing, p-values were calculated using a two-sample paired t-test on the per-category accuracies for
1222   different models. The t-test checks for the null hypothesis that these two independent samples have
1223   identical average (expected) values. For ScanNet, a t-test is not optimal due to the smaller number
1224   of samples, and thus a Wilcoxon rank-sum test was employed for hypothesis testing as suggested in
1225   past works De Winter (2019); Posten (1982). All statistical testing was conducting using the python
1226
       package scipy, and the threshold for statistical significance was set at 0.05.
