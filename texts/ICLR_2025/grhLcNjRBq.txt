009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This work focuses on building a task planner for Embodied Instruction Following
012            (EIF) using Large Language Models (LLMs). Previous works typically train a
013            planner to imitate expert trajectories, treating this as a supervised task. While these
014            methods achieve competitive performance, they often lack sufficient robustness.
015            When encountering a suboptimal action, the planner may encounter an out-of-
016            distribution state, which can lead to task failure. In contrast, we frame the task as
017            a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) and aim to develop
018
               a robust planner under a few-shot assumption. Thus, we propose a closed-loop
               planner with an adaptation module and a novel hindsight method, aiming to use
019
               as much information as possible to assist the planner. Our experiments on the
020
               ALFRED dataset indicate that our planner achieves competitive performance under
021            a few-shot assumption. For the first time, our few-shot agent’s performance
022            approaches and even surpasses that of the full-shot supervised agent.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   With the development of AI and robotics, many previous works have combined them to handle Em-
028   bodied Instruction Following (EIF). Among them, the Action Learning From Realistic Environments
029   and Directives (ALFRED) benchmark (Shridhar et al., 2020) is particularly challenging because it
030   requires an agent to learn a long-horizon policy that maps egocentric images and language instructions
031   into a sequence of actions. In each task, the agent will be given a natural instruction (e.g. “Put a
032   heated mug down on a table”) and an egocentric visual observation at each step. The agent is required
033
      to output low-level actions (e.g. MoveAhead, RotateRight, etc.) based on the observation to complete
      the task. These tasks are usually challenging due to the sparse reward settings. For such a reason,
034
      many works have adopted a hierarchical structure to deal with it (Song et al., 2023; Min et al., 2021;
035
      Blukis et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2024). The high-level module decomposes the whole task into several
036   sub-tasks, the low-level module outputs actions to finish each sub-task. Previously, sub-goal planners
037   are trained on human-annotated dataset through supervised learning. However, they require large
038   amounts of data and often lack robustness (Min et al., 2021; Blukis et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2024).
039
      With recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs), many studies have explored using
040
      LLMs as sub-goal planners, utilizing their in-context learning abilities (Song et al., 2023; Shin et al.,
041
      2024; Ahn et al., 2022). Although these methods have achieved competitive performance under
042   the few-shot assumption, a critical limitation is that these approaches all study the problem from a
043   supervised learning perspective. They merely attempt to imitate the ground truth trajectories, which
044   results in a lack of robustness within their agents. EIF benchmarks, on the other hand, require
045   long-horizon planning ability. For example, the task “Put a warmed apple in the fridge” requires
046   12-step planning. Assuming that after applying in-context learning, the distribution of the agent’s
047   output actions becomes closer to that of the Oracle, with an accuracy of 0.9, the overall accuracy of
048   the entire planning task decreases to 0.912 = 0.28. Traditionally, a large amount of data is required to
049   mitigate such an issue (Blukis et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2024). However, under the few-shot assumption,
050
      in-context learning methods rely heavily on the reasoning ability of pretrained LLMs (Brown et al.,
      2020; Dong et al., 2024). The hallucination problem of LLMs (Zhang et al., 2023) suggests that
051
      supervised methods through in-context learning are limited.
052
053   To address this issue, we approach the ALFRED task (Shridhar et al., 2020) as a Partially Observable
      Markov Decision Process (POMDP), where the planner makes decisions based on its current state.

054
      Each task begins with a natural language description. At each step, the planner receives an egocentric
055   RGB image and returns a high-level sub-goal. The planner can only receive reward signals (Success
056   or Fail) at the end of the task. There are three major challenges in building a robust planner: (1)
057   The sparse reward settings make it difficult for the planner to learn and make accurate decisions. (2)
058   The planner can only receive an egocentric picture and cannot detect the whole state. (3) Under the
059   few-shot assumption, the planner cannot obtain enough information from trajectories.
060
      For the first problem, we adopt an actor-critic framework (Liu et al., 2024) which consists of two
061   actors, one critic, and one generator. At each step, the planner receives a new state and performs a
062   tree search with the actors and generator to plan future trajectories, rather than directly outputting a
063   sub-goal. The critic is then used to select the best rollout and return its initial action. Thus, the planner
064   can optimize the output over the long horizon to address the issue of sparse reward. For the second
065   difficulty, we design an adaptation module instantiated by LLMs. Upon receiving an egocentric
066   image, the adaptation module aims to predict the invisible latent PDDL variables of the task, which
067   could help the planner better understand the environment. For the third challenge, we propose a novel
068   hindsight method. It collects suboptimal trajectories from the agent in the training environment and
069
      relabels them to complete the task. This approach provides the planner with additional information.
      During the deployment phase, the relabeled trajectories can guide the planner in adjusting its policy
070
      when incorrect actions are proposed and executed.

091

103   2.1    L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL (LLM) AND I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING (ICL)
104
105   Large language models (LLMs) have shown incredible reasoning ability (Vaswani et al., 2023; Wei
106   et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI et al., 2024) across a wide range of tasks. A crucial way
107   to enhance this reasoning ability is through in-context learning (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020; Dong
      et al., 2024), which allows LLMs to solve complex tasks with only a few samples. Furthermore,

      this approach removes the need for fine-tuning, which can be time-consuming and computationally
109   expensive. To utilize the ICL ability better, many studies propose certain frameworks aimed at
110   enhancing the reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Yao et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024).
111   Among them, Liu et al. (2024) proposes a novel perspective by bridging RL and LLM, which inspires
112   us to study ICL from an RL aspect. Xie et al. (2022) interprets ICL as Implicit Bayesian Inference,
113   while Dai et al. (2023) believes that ICL is performing implicit Gradient Descent. All of these imply
114   the importance of the content in ICL, an area that remains relatively understudied. To this end, we
115   propose Hindsight Planner as an exploration.

      2.2   A DAPTATION M ODULE IN POMDP
118
119   In a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP), planners are presented with observ-
120   able states, while the latent states are invisible to the planner. Making decisions with incomplete
121   information is challenging; therefore, a component to map the observable state into the latent space is
122   crucial (Lee et al., 2023). Adaptation modules have been proven effective in legged robots (Kumar
123   et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2019; Peng et al., 2020). These modules aim to bridge the gap between
124   the simulator and the real world. They are often trained to predict crucial information that a robot
125   can sense in the simulator but not through its sensors in the actual world, such as surface friction or
126
      payload of the robot. The base policy then makes decisions based on the observed information and
      the invisible latent information predicted by adaptation modules. Inspired by this, we propose an
127
      adaptation model that maps the visible object list to the latent, invisible Planning Domain Definition
128
      Language (PDDL) (Chapman, 1987) of ALFRED (Shridhar et al., 2020).
129
130   Previous work such as Min et al. (2021), trains a BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) to predict the PDDL
131   arguments and decompose high-level instructions into templated sub-goals. However, our approach
132
      differs from these in two aspects: (1) Previous works predict the arguments at the beginning of a
      task, which is equivalent to predicting the latent variables based on the initial observed state. In
133
      contrast, our method predicts the latent arguments at each time before reasoning, allowing predictions
134
      to be adjusted through exploration, which makes our planner more robust. (2) We do not apply the
135   templated approach directly. The adaptation module is used to reveal the latent information for the
136   planner and assist the planner in making better decisions. Experiments show that our method achieves
137   competitive performance even without the assistance of the adaptation model, as demonstrated in
138   Table 4.

      2.3   H INDSIGHT IN LLM S
142
      Hindsight algorithms (Andrychowicz et al., 2018; Li et al., 2020; Pong et al., 2020) are widely
143
      adopted in the reinforcement learning (RL) area. Generally, the hindsight method aims to reveal
144
      future information after collecting a trajectory and relabel the trajectory to make it more informative
145   during training process (Furuta et al., 2022; Andrychowicz et al., 2018). Furuta et al. (2022) applies
146   the hindsight method in training a Transformer model and achieves competitive performance on
147   several baselines. However, training a model from scratch usually requires a large amount of
148   data. In contrast, in-context learning, leveraging the reasoning ability of LLMs, allows an agent to
149   complete complex tasks with only a few samples. Dai et al. (2023) has shown that ICL executes
150   an implicit parameter update. As a result, we utilize ICL in our proposed method. Intuitively, we
151   hope hindsight prompts can provide guidance when an out-of-distribution state is encountered. For
152   example, “Wash a pan and put it away” requires the agent to wash a Pan and put it on the DiningTable.
153
      The trajectory from a planner could be: {(PickupObject, Pan), (PutObject, Sink), (ToggleObjectOn,
      Faucet), (PickupObject, Pan), (PutObject, CoffeeMachine)}. Note that in this example, the agent
154
      fails to place the pan in the correct location, does not turn off the faucet, and thus the trajectory
155
      from the planner is suboptimal. Our hindsight method proposes a novel relabeling process that
156   appends actions to the suboptimal trajectory, aiming to complete the task. In the above example, the
157   corrected trajectory should be: {(PickupObject, Pan), (PutObject, Sink), (ToggleObjectOn, Faucet),
158   (PickupObject, Pan), (PutObject, CoffeeMachine), (ToggleObjectOff, Faucet), (PickupObject,Pan),
159   (PutObject, DiningTable)}. This approach enables us to guide the planner in addressing unknown
160   states resulting from incorrect actions. Consequently, during the deployment phase, when the planner
161   encounters a similar state, it can learn from suboptimal trajectories and subsequently take correct
      actions to correct previous mistakes.

      4.4   A DAPTATION M ODULE
346
347   In a POMDP, the adaptation module is used to predict the latent variables from the observed environ-
348   ment yt (Lee et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2021) and construct the whole state xt = (Adapter(yt ), yt ).
349   In practice, we utilize an LLM as the adaptation module and set PDDL arguments as the prediction
350   target for it. The input prompt for the adaptation module begins with an intuitive explanation of
351   ALFRED, followed by several in-context samples. At the end of the prompt is the current task and
352   the object list. At each step, the object list is updated as the agent explores the environment.
353   The output from the adaptation module varies depending on the task description. Inspired by PDDL
354   (Chapman, 1987; Silver et al., 2023) of ALFRED, the adaptation module needs to predict the
355   following arguments at each step: (1) object target: The specific object to be interacted with during
356   the task. (2) parent target: The final place for the object in the task. (3) mrecep target: The container
357   or vessel necessary for the task. (4) toggle target: The device that needs to be toggled in the task.
358   (5) object state: Indicates whether the target object needs to be cleaned, heated, or cooled. (6)
359   object sliced: Determines if the object must be sliced. (7) two object: Specifies whether the task
360
      involves handling and placing two objects. The adaptation module predicts these arguments at each
      time before reasoning. Then, the arguments are processed into a specific format to assist the task
361
      planner to sense the environment better.

      4.5   TASK P LANNER
364
365   We adopt an actor-critic planner (Liu et al., 2024). At each time step t, the planner receives xt from
366   the environment and the adaptation module. We initiate two Actors: Actorgt and Actorhind , with
367   different samples from the sample pool D. For each state, we prompt each Actor to generate W           2
368   actions. The Critic then selects the top B actions. A generator ψ generates the next state based on
369   each action. In this way, we map Actors and Critic to B future trajectories and select the best
370   future trajectory (xt , a∗t , . . .) through Critic. a∗t is then returned as the sub-goal for Low-PL. The

406   We then give a detailed discussion of the relabeling process. Directly applying the task description
407   from ALFRED may lead to unsatisfactory results, as the task description is often vague. For example,
408   the task “Put a chilled potato on the small black table” requires the planner to put the potato on a
409
      SideTable. If the task description is applied directly, LLMs might focus incorrectly on the Black
      Table and return an incorrect action “PutObject BlackTable”. If the task description is not included
410
      in the prompt, it could lead LLMs to imitate the ground truth trajectory. However, planners usually
411
      have multiple ways to complete a certain task. For instance, in a task requiring the planner to slice
412   an apple, after slicing the apple, the planner could put the Knife on the DiningTable or CounterTop.
413   To address this issue, we relabel the task based on the latent PDDL arguments. The task description
414   “Put a chilled potato on the small black table” becomes “Pick up one cooled potato and put it on
415   the SideTable”. This approach helps clarify the task for the planner and reduces the ambiguity in
416   instructions.
417
      For the kNN retriever, we use a frozen BERT from Wolf et al. (2020). We employ GPT-4 Turbo as
418   the target LLM and set temperature to 0. For the Adapter, 5 in-context examples are retrieved from
419   the sample pool through the kNN retriever. For the Actors and Critic modules, 2 in-context
420   examples are retrieved. The task planner uses beam search with a depth and width of 2. To preserve
421   the few-shot assumption and ensure a fair comparison, we directly adopt the pretrained modules for
422   navigation, perception, and low-level control from HLSM (Blukis et al., 2021).

481   This paper explores an effective few-shot framework for Embodied Instruction Following. We
482   approach the task as a POMDP and design a closed-loop Hindsight Planner equipped with an
483   adaptation module to enhance the agent’s environmental sensing capabilities. Compared to previous
484   open-loop, supervised methods, our approach is more robust and performs better. Furthermore, the
485   planner incorporates a novel hindsight method that enables it to learn from suboptimal trajectories.
      we hope our work inspires future research in this area.
