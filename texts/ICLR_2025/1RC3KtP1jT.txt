009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011               To address privacy concerns with large language models, industrial users request
012               local fine-tuning and deployment, but unencrypted models risk theft. Hardware-
013               based security provides protection but is constrained by secure memory, leading to
014               semi-open configurations. Semi-open models balance security and customization
015               by keeping key layers closed-source within a secure environment while allowing
016               others to be fine-tuned, but closed-source layers are susceptible to recovery attacks.
017
                  In this paper, we explore the design of semi-open models with fewer closed-source
                  layers, aiming to increase customizability while ensuring resilience to recovery
018
                  attacks. We analyze the contribution of closed-source layer to the overall resilience
019
                  and theoretically prove that in a deep transformer-based model, there exists a
020               transition layer such that even small recovery errors in layers before this layer
021               can lead to recovery failure. Building on this, we propose SCARA1 , a novel
022               approach that keeps only a few bottom layers as closed-source. SCARA employs
023               a fine-tuning-free metric to estimate the maximum number of layers that can be
024               publicly accessible for customization. We apply it to five models (1.3B to 70B
025               parameters) to construct semi-open models, validating their customizability on six
026               downstream tasks and assessing their resilience against various recovery attacks on
027               sixteen benchmarks. We compare SCARA to baselines and observe that it generally
028
                  improves downstream customization performance and offers similar resilience with
                  over 10 times fewer closed-source parameters. We empirically investigate the
029
                  transition phenomenon and analyze the effectiveness and limitations of our scheme.

      vulnerability to model recovery attacks (Zanella-Beguelin et al., 2021), where attackers query the
055   secured module and train a new module to imitate its functionality (Solaiman, 2023). While the
056   community has extensively studied recovery attacks against closed-source models (Chen et al., 2023;
057   Jiang et al., 2023b), defending against recovery attacks in the semi-open setting is an uncharted area.
058   Recovery attackers targeting fully closed-source models seek to fine-tune a new model that precisely
059   replicates the closed-source model (Tamber et al., 2024; Dubiński et al., 2024). In contrast, attackers
060   in semi-open settings are not required to exactly replicate the closed-source module. Instead, they can
061   fine-tune the concealed module alongside the exposed module to reconstruct the overall functionality.
062   Beyond the closed-source amount, the specific sections concealed are vital for defending against
063   recovery attacks. Shen et al. (2023) suggested concealing several top layers and keeping the bottom
064   public. However, the benefits of hiding bottom layers (near the input) versus top layers (near the
065   output) are still unclear. Therefore, we examine the impact of each layer on resilience and theoretically
066   identify a transition layer. Any recovery error in bottom layers before this transition layer leads
067   to a high probability of recovery failure. In contrast, errors in later layers have limited impact.
068   This finding suggests that keeping the bottom layers closed-sourced offers better protection against
069
      recovery attacks than the top layers, even when the same number of layers is hidden.
070   In this paper, we introduce SCARA, a selective closed-sourcing method for designing semi-open
071   models that balances customizability with resilience against recovery attacks. Building on our
072   theoretical findings, SCARA selectively hides a few bottom layers. It determines the minimal number
073   of closed-source layers using a recovery difficulty score, a metric that estimates recovery performance
074   without requiring fine-tuning. This score is based on the initial average recovery loss during the
075
      attack. SCARA identifies the optimal closed-source strategy by selecting the layers with metric
      corresponding to the worst recovery performance. Consequently, models designed by SCARA retain
076
      most layers as publicly accessible, achieving customizability comparable to fully open-source models
077
      while remaining resilient to recovery attacks. Our main contributions are as follows:
078
079   • We theoretically demonstrate the existence of a transition layer in LLMs. We prove that small
080     recovery errors in bottom decoder layers before this layer can lead to recovery failure with high
081     probability, whereas errors in later layers have a limited impact. (see Section 4.1)
082
      • We introduce SCARA, a selective closed-source approach that conceals a few bottom decoder
083     layers to enhance customizability while maintaining resilience. Specifically, we propose a metric
084     that does not require fine-tuning, but correlates with the recovered performance under attacks,
085     enabling us to approximately find the minimal number of hidden layers. (see Section 4.2)
086
      • We compare our approach with two baselines across five models (1.3B to 70B parameters), as-
087     sessing customizability on six tasks and resilience against three recovery strategies across sixteen
088     benchmarks in six domains. Experiments show that our method significantly improves downstream
089     performance while maintaining comparable resilience against recovery attacks, with over 10 times
090     fewer closed-source parameters than the baselines. For example, the semi-open Llama2-70B
091     produced by our method hides only 2.5% of the parameters but achieves a 30% higher downstream
092     performance score than the baselines in the Financial domain. We also observe a performance
093     improvement of over 40% on Mistral-7B. Additionally, our method maintains similar resilience
094     against recovery attacks compared to both baselines. (see Section 5.2)
095   • We empirically investigate the presence of transition layers and the correlation between our metric
096     and the recovered performance of each closesd-source combination. We conclude by analyzing the
097     hyper-parameter sensitivity and discuss the limitations of our approach. (see Section 5.4)

099   2    R ELATED W ORKS
100
101
      Model Customization. Vendors have introduced three main strategies for model customization,
102   each with distinct trade-offs. First, fine-tuning APIs allow customization of fully closed-source
103   models (e.g., La Plateforme, Azure AI Services) while restricting access (Finlayson et al., 2024).
104   Second, embedding models offer richer customization by enabling users to select and modify
105   subsequent structures (Sarıtaş et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024), but lack of joint pre-training may degrade
106   performance (Nussbaum et al., 2024) and increase vulnerability to recovery attacks (Caspari et al.,
107   2024; Tamber et al., 2024). Third, open-source models offer full customization flexibility yet pose
      challenges to model control and usage supervision (Bommasani et al., 2022; Roumeliotis et al., 2023).

108
      Model Recovery Attacks. Prior attacks (Tramèr et al., 2016; Krishna et al., 2020; Dziedzic et al.,
109   2023a) attempt to recover the functionality of fully closed-source models through API queries.
110   Carlini et al. (2024) advanced these by entirely extracting the embedding projection layer and hidden
111   dimension size. Recently, various defenses against fully closed-source model recovery attacks
112   have been proposed (Jiang et al., 2024), including malicious queries detection (Shang et al., 2024),
113   watermarking (Zhang et al., 2021), fingerprinting (Guan et al., 2022), etc. These methods do not
114   directly apply to the semi-open settings with only partial model information.
115   Semi-Open Model. Previous studies (Lin et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Qiao & Zhou, 2023) explore
116   opening bottom layers of models for user customization, while keeping later layers closed-sourced
117   to maintain vendor control. For example, Shen et al. (2023) introduced SAP, which open-sources
118   the first six transformer layers but limits customization options. Meanwhile, Dubiński et al. (2024);
119   Dziedzic et al. (2023b) proposed a semi-open approach where encoder models are offered as APIs,
120   allowing users to customize task-specific subsequent modules. However, (Liu et al., 2022) and Sha
121   et al. (2023) showed that these encoder models are still vulnerable to recovery attacks.

      3.2   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
155
156   In this paper, we consider the performance of a large language model within a defined distribution,
157   denoted as PX×Y , representing the relationship between the input matrix X and corresponding label
158   Y . We assume that the victim LLM f (X; θ) performs well within this distribution. Additionally, we
159   presume the attack set D consists of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) samples drawn
160   from PX×Y . To assess the alignment between the outputs of LLM and the ground-truth labels, we
161   use a scoring function, denoted as s : Y × Y → R+ . For any closed-source index set I ⊆ [L], we
      introduce the concept of a “Recovery Ratio” R(I). This ratio measures the extent to which the

                                       R(I) =                                .                                   (1)

166   Here, E in the numerator reflects the expectation computed over random samples (X, Y ) drawn from
167   PX×Y , the random attack set D, and the random initialization of parameters within the closed-source
168   layers during fine-tuning. Conversely, the term E in the denominator solely considers the expectation
169   over random samples. With this definition, the term R([L]) denotes the recovery ratio of the recovered

            Given ε > 0, what is the smallest closed-source set I for which R(I) ≤ (1 + ε)R([L])?
172
173   This question essentially asks whether it is feasible to identify a minimal closed-source index set I,
174   such that, under this closed-source strategy, the resulting recovered model exhibits similarity to the
175
      model recovered under fully-closed approach. In other words, the recovery score does not surpass
      that of fully-closed approach by more than a factor of (1 + ε).

255   4.2   SCARA: S ELECTIVE C LOSED - SOURCING A PPROACH A GAINST R ECOVERY A TTACK
256
      We propose a method to approximately find the smallest bottom layer index set I that satisfies
257
      R(I) ≤ (1 + ε)R([L]). A simple approach is to start with Il = {1, . . . , l} for each l beginning from
258   1, then evaluate the recovery ratio R(Il ) after the attack, and identify the smallest l that meets the
259   inequality. This extensive fine-tuning process is time-consuming, prompting the critical question:
260   Can we create a fine-tuning-free metric that predicts LLM performance under semi-open model
261   recovery attacks? Hence, our goal is to establish a metric directly correlated with the recovery ratio.
262
      In the recovery ratio R(I), each I has the same denominator, so our focus is on a metric related to the
263
      numerator, specifically E[s(f (X; θFT (I, D)), Y )], which measures the average performance score of
264   the recovered model. This average performance score generally inversely correlates with the average
265   testing loss E[ℓ(f (X; θFT (I, D)), Y )], where ℓ denotes the cross-entropy loss employed by LLM.
266   Therefore, our goal becomes finding the l such that
267                 E[ℓ(f (X; θFT ({1, ..., l}, D)), Y )] ≥ (1 − ε)E[ℓ(f (X; θFT ([L], D)), Y )].
268
269   However, calculating both sides of this inequality requires knowing the recovered parameters from the
      fine-tuning process. To bypass this, we aim for an approximate solution. The recovered parameters

270
      are generated through gradient descent, starting from the initial parameters θ0 (I), with the hidden
271   layers being randomly initialized. Using the Taylor Expansion, we find
272
273           E [ℓ (f (X; θFT (I, D)), Y )] = E [ℓ (f (X; θ0 (I)), Y )] + O(∥θFT (I, D) − θ0 (I)∥2 ).
274   Previous research (Choi et al., 2024; Bailly et al., 2022) suggests the difference ∥θFT (I, D) − θ0 (I)∥2
275   is minor for large networks compared to the dataset size |D|. In models like a single-layer ReLU
276   network
                (Anthony et al., 1999; Zou et al., 2020), the difference ∥θFT (I, D) − θ0 (I)∥2 is of order
277        |D|
      O √N (Jacot et al., 2018; Wei et al., 2019), where N , the number of model parameters, which
278
      is much larger than the dataset size in LLMs (Dubey et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Hence, the first
279   term that does not require fine-tuning dominates, suggesting it as a viable metric for predicting the
280   recovery ratio. Thus, we define the first term as “Recovery Difficulty” (RD(I)) with the expression:
281
282                                 RD(I) = EX,Y,θ0 (I) [ℓ (f (X; θ0 (I), Y )] .
283   This score, which can be estimated using a sample average, represents the recovered performance
284   of the model when specific layers I are closed-sourced. A higher RD(I) suggests worse recovery
285   performance, indicating a lower recovery ratio R(I). Therefore, our SCARA operates in the following
286   way. SCARA begins by sampling evaluation data targeting general capabilities from the underlying
287   distribution, and then computes RD(Il ) for each set of closed-sourced layers Il = {1, ..., l} for
288   l = 1, ..., L. SCARA stops at the smallest l∗ that satisfies RD(Il∗ ) ≥ (1 − ε)RD([L]).

292   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
293
294   In this subsection, we introduce the experimental setups. Details can be found in Appendix B.
295   Models. We consider five open-source, decoder-only structured LLMs with various architec-
296   tures. Specifically, we select Llama2-70B-chat, Llama2-7B-chat (Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral-7B-
297   v0.1 (Jiang et al., 2023a), Phi-2 (Abdin et al., 2024), and Phi-1.5 (Li et al., 2023). We designate these
298   pre-trained models as the base models for customization and victims in semi-open model recovery
299   attacks.
300   Attack Methods. We recover models produced by different closed-source approaches using three
301   attack methods: FT-all, FT-closed and SEM. Following (He et al., 2021), a diverse attack set is
302   required for full recovery. Therefore, we merge data evenly form two general datasets, MMLU
303   benchmark (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and Alpaca 52k (Wang et al., 2022), resulting in a 51k combined
304   set. Moreover, we also construct four larger general datasets (100k–500k) to strengthen the attack.
305
      Baselines. We compare SCARA with the other two baselines: SAP-DP and the fully-closed (Eiras
306   et al., 2024) approach. The SAP (Shen et al., 2023) framework keeps the first six decoder layers open
307   and the rest closed-source. SAP-DP extends SAP by adding Laplace noise to the model outputs, a
308   common strategy for model protection (Lee et al., 2018). The fully-closed approach represents the
309   extreme, where all layers are closed-sourced.
310
      Implementation Details of SCARA. We apply the SCARA algorithm to identify the smallest
311
      closed-source set I such that R(I) ≤ (1 + ε)R([L]). To calculate recovery difficulty (RD), we use
312   cross-entropy loss and approximate the expectation over samples distributed on the general domain
313   and randomly initialized closed-source parameters. This is done using a 1,500-sample evaluation
314   set randomly sampled from the MMLU benchmark and Alpaca 52k, with closed-source parameters
315   initialized via Xavier initialization and averaged over three random seeds (20, 42, 1234). For models
316   up to 7B parameters, we use four RTX 4090 GPUs, while for Llama2-70B, we use four A100 GPUs.
317   We find that ε = 0.05 yields optimal performance. For ε sensitivity, see Section 5.3.
318   Evaluation Benchmarks We assess customizability on six downstream tasks: Code (Zheng et al.,
319   2024b), Math (Yue et al., 2023), Medical (Zhang et al., 2023), Finance (Wang et al., 2023b),
320   Law (Guha et al., 2024), and Alignment (Meng et al., 2024). To fully evaluate recovered functionali-
321   ties, we focus on six capabilities domains following Llama2 report (Touvron et al., 2023). Specifically,
322   we assess the recovered model across sixteen benchmarks grouped into (1) Commonsense Reasoning
323   (Rsn.); (2) Reading Comprehension (Read.); (3) World Knowledge (Knl.); (4) Code; (5) Math; and
      (6) General Ability (Gen.).

448   5.3          A NALYSIS OF THE C USTOMIZABILITY-R ESILIENCE T RADE - OFF IN SCARA
449
450
      Closed-source Module Placement vs. Trade-off. Theorem 1 demonstrates that the bottom layers
      before a transition layer provide stronger resilience against model recovery attacks. However, it
451
      remains unclear how hiding these layers might impact model customizability. To investigate this,
452
      we designed semi-open models with closed-source layer sets of equal size using Llama2-7B and
453   Phi-2. These models were customized for the math domain and evaluated under FT-all recovery
454   attacks. Figure 6(a) shows that while the placement of the closed-source set has minimal impact on
455   customizability, it significantly affects resilience, consistent with Theorem 1. For Llama2-7B, the
456   resilience transition occurs at the eighth layer set, where ∆ARR remains close to zero for earlier sets,
457   indicating that hiding layers before this point ensures strong resilience. Importantly, customization
458   accuracy remains stable regardless of placement, further supporting the effectiveness of hiding layers
459   before the transition. In contrast, Phi-2 exhibits an earlier transition at the first layer set, where only
460   the first layer achieves a balance between customization and resilience, with subsequent sets resulting
461
      in diminished resilience. These results suggest that placing the closed-source set before the transition
      layer optimizes the trade-off between customization and resilience against recovery attacks. Further
462
      analysis on Mistral-7B and Phi-1.5 is provided in Appendix B.8.
463
464   Closed-source Module Size vs. Trade-off. We investigate how the size of the closed-source
465   module impacts the trade-off between customizability and resilience to recovery attacks. Semi-open
466   models based on Llama2-7B and Phi-2 are created by incrementally increasing the number of hidden
467
      layers starting from the first. These models are customized on the math domain and evaluated for
      resilience under the FT-all attack, with results shown in Figure 6(b). For Llama2-7B, the results
468
      reveal a clear transition in customizability, while resilience remains largely unaffected by module size.
469
      Customization accuracy drops from 29% to 21% as the closed-source module grows from one to five
470   layers, while ∆ARR stays near zero, indicating strong resilience regardless of closed-sourced size.
471   Further, as shown in Figure 6(c), resilience emerges when at least 3% of parameters—equivalent to a
472   single decoder layer—are closed-sourced. This suggests that hiding the first layer alone provides the
473   best trade-off between customization and resilience.
474
      In contrast, Phi-2 shows a different pattern: as the closed-source module size increases, customization
475
      accuracy declines, but resilience improves significantly. This is evident from a marked decrease in
476   ∆ARR as the module size grows from one to five layers, suggesting enhanced resilience to recovery
477   attacks. These findings indicate that larger models like Llama2-7B achieve an optimal balance with
478   fewer closed-source layers, while smaller models like Phi-2 require more layers to maintain resilience
479   against recovery attacks. Further analyses are provided in Appendix B.9.

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we explored finding minimal closed-sourced sets to enhance LLM customizability while
535   preserving their resilience against semi-open model recovery attacks. We theoretically prove that
536   minor errors in bottom decoder layers prior to a transition layer greatly reduce recovery attack success.
537   We introduced SCARA, which selectively closed-sources a small set of layers, achieving superior
538   customizability and comparable resilience to SAP-DP and fully-closed. We empirically investigated
539   the existence of customization and resilience transitions, showed the impact of closed-source size on
      model resilience, analyzed the effectiveness of our approach, and finally discussed its limitations.

       B.3   BASELINES .
1243
1244   In this section, we provide further details on the baselines used in our comparisons: SAP-DP and
1245   fully-closed. These schemes represent different strategies, each with distinct trade-offs in terms of
1246   customizability and resilience against model recovery attacks.
1247
       SAP. The Split-and-Privatize (SAP) framework (Shen et al., 2023) offers an approach to balance
1248
       between protecting model privacy and data privacy while maintaining competitive performance.
1249   Specifically, the SAP framework keeps the bottom six encoder layers open, allowing user access and
1250   fine-tuning while closing the deeper layers on the vendor.
1251
1252
       SAP-DP. To further strengthen protection while maintaining competitive performance, we extend
       SAP by incorporating differential privacy techniques by adding Laplace noise to perturb the logits
1253
       during the fine-tuning process (Lee et al., 2018). The Laplace Distribution with mean µ and scale b is
1254
       the distribution with probability density function:
1255
                                                                     |x − µ|

1256                                                       1
1257
                                      Laplace(x|µ, b) =      exp −
                                                          2b            b
1258
1259   Specifically, in SAP-DP, the noise n is sampled: n ∼ Laplace(0, 0.5) and added to the output logits
1260   of the model to balance privacy protection and model performance.
1261   Fully-closed. Following (Eiras et al., 2024), we use the fully-closed approach as a baseline. This
1262   assumes the adversary has no access to internal model parameters, treating the model as a black-
1263   box, where only output data can be collected. We slightly broaden this setup by assuming the
1264   adversary knows the model’s architecture but no other details. Thus, recovering the fully-closed
1265   model involves using the collected data to retrain a model with the same architecture to restore its
1266   general functionality.
1267

1558   B.11        A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
1559
1560   In this section, we provide a detailed comparison of SCARA and SAP-DP in their effectiveness against
1561   three types of black-box adversarial attacks on the Llama2-7B model. The attacks considered include
1562
       Membership Inference Attacks (MIA), Attribute Inference Attacks (AIA), and Prompt Injection
       Attacks (PIA).
1563
1564   Membership Inference Attack (MIA): This attack aims to determine whether a specific data point
1565   was included in the training dataset of the model. Attackers utilize model outputs to infer membership
       status, potentially exposing sensitive information about the training data (Fu et al., 2023; Chen &


                                                                                29
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       Pattabiraman, 2024). We conducted our experiment following SPV_MIA 10 , which provides a robust
1567   framework for assessing model vulnerabilities. We focus on the AUC scores for SPV-MIA against
1568   semi-open models across Ag News datasets (Zhang et al., 2016).
1569
1570
       Attribute Inference Attack (AIA): In this scenario, the adversary attempts to infer specific attributes
       of training data based on the model’s outputs. This can lead to privacy breaches, particularly when
1571
       sensitive attributes are involved (Staab et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). We conducted our experiments
1572
       following the methodology outlined in Staab et al. (2023) 11 and evaluated the top-3 accuracy on the
1573   PersonalReddit (PR) Dataset.
1574
1575   Prompt Injection Attack (PIA): This attack manipulates input prompts to coerce the model into
1576
       producing desired outputs that may compromise the integrity or security of the system (Zhao et al.,
       2024; Xu et al., 2024). In our experiment, we follow AutoDAN 12 , which can automatically generate
1577
       stealthy jailbreak prompts by the carefully designed hierarchical genetic algorithm.We evaluate the
1578
       effectiveness of these prompts using the keyword-based attack success rate (ASR), which measures
1579   the presence of predefined keywords in responses generated LLMs. For gold standard, LED 13 ,
1580   significantly enhances the resilience of LLMs against prompt injection attacks (PIA), reducing the
1581   ASR to 0.
