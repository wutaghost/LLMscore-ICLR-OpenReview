                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009            Neural surrogates for PDE solvers suffer from an inability to model the spectrum
010            of solutions adequately, especially in the medium to high frequency bands. This
011            impacts not only correct spectral shapes, but also stability and long-term rollout
012            accuracy. We identify three convergent factors that exacerbate this phenomenon,
013            namely: distribution shift over unrolls, spectral bias of the MSE loss, and spu-
014            rious high frequency noise, or spectral junk, introduced by the use of pointwise
015
               nonlinearities. We find that spectral shaping, filtering the spectrum of activations
               after every layer of pointwise nonlinearities, is enough to reduce spectral junk and
016
               improve long-term rollout accuracy. We show spectral shaping not only fixes the
017
               learned spectrum (down to machine precision in some cases), but also leads to
018            very stable neural surrogates. We validate these findings on a suite of challenging
019            fluid dynamics problems in the field of neural PDE surrogacy, promoting a clear
020            need for more careful attention to surrogate architecture design and adding a new
021            and simple trick to the practitioner toolbox.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025
      The simulation of physical phenomena is one of the cornerstones of modern science and engineer-
      ing, enabling the emulation of physics-based systems and the development of digital twin technolo-
026
      gies (Ames, 2014; Wagg et al., 2020). Due to the commonplace analytical intractability of physical
027
      models, we have seen the development of computational science and numerical methods. Growing
028   interest in building fast surrogate models to partial differential equation solvers, modeling vast areas
029   of the physical sciences, has fueled a boom in the development of neural methods for approximat-
030   ing PDE solutions (Brunton & Kutz, 2023). Anecdotally speaking, however, building such neural
031   surrogates is difficult and, like many other areas of deep learning, is more an art than a science.
032
      For time-dependent PDEs, which we focus on, the standard procedure is to train autoregressive
033
      models, such as in Bar-Sinai et al. (2019) or Brandstetter et al. (2022). They require some degree of
034
      autoregressive training via backpropagation-through-time (Werbos, 1990), which is slow, memory
035   intensive, and high variance (Metz et al., 2021). Additionally, rollouts are often unstable during test
036   time (Brandstetter et al., 2022) and there are no principled ways to control the error of the rollout.
037   Specifically, there is a lack of rigor in establishing the ideal training setup regarding loss function,
038   architecture, and other tricks for stabilization.
039
      In this paper, we investigate the spectrum of solutions output by such autoregressive surrogate mod-
040
      els and demonstrate that correct spectral modeling is important for stable and accurate long-term
041   rollouts. We also note that it is very difficult to model the spectrum correctly with standard neural
042   surrogate baseline architectures. We pinpoint three factors at the root of this poor modeling capa-
043   bility: unroll distribution shift (Brandstetter et al., 2022), spectral bias of the MSE loss (Rahaman
044   et al., 2019), and our key insight, spurious high frequency noise, or spectral junk, introduced by the
045   use of pointwise nonlinearities.
046
      Based on these insights, we find out that the simplest remedy, a simple low pass filtering after every
047
      nonlinearity, is sufficient to shape the spectrum enough for stable rollouts with increased accuracy. In
048   the rest of this work, we explain our reasoning, with key investigations that connect rollout instability
049   to high frequency signals, and then isolate the source of these high frequency signals as spectral
050   junk introduced by pointwise nonlinearities. We then propose a simple patch, carefully tuned low
051   pass filtering, and demonstrate that this is enough to clean up the spectrum and improve both unroll
052   stability and long-term accuracy. We conduct our experiments with various benchmark-style models
053   on a suite of challenging fluid dynamics problems: Kuramoto-Shivashinsky, Kolmogorov flow, and
      Rayleigh-Bénard convection, showcasing the efficacy of this one simple trick.

      2    BACKGROUND
055
056   Partial Differential Equations We focus on a class of dynamical systems described by time-
057   dependent partial differential equations. Such systems are ubiquitous in nature. They describe how
058   a solution u(t, x) ∈ Rd evolves in continuous time t ∈ R≥0 . These solutions are defined on spatial
059
      domain x ∈ Ω ⊆ Rn beginning from initial conditions u(0, x) := u0 (x), and subject to boundary
      conditions such as periodicity or B[u](t, x) = 0 for x ∈ ∂Ω, evolves in continuous time t ∈ R≥0 . In
060
      this work we only consider solutions whose snapshots in time u(t, ·) are spatially smooth functions.
061
      We also only consider autonomous systems of the form
062
063                                ∂t u(t, x) = F (x, u, ∂x u, ∂xx u, ...)|u=u(t,·) .                       (1)

065   Discretization For computational reasons, in silico solutions are discretized in space and time. In
066
      space they may be discretized on to a set of points X = {xm } ∈ Ω with gridded snapshots u(t) :=
      {u(t, x)|x ∈ X}. We may also project against a set of linearly independent basis functions B =
067
      {e1 , ..., eN } obtaining spectral coefficients ûn (t) := ⟨u(t, ·), en ⟩ for appropriately defined inner
068
      product and spectrum û(t) := {û1 (t), ..., ûN (t)}. For example, we would choose a Fourier basis
069   en (x) = e−iπnx for Ω = [−1, 1] and periodic boundary conditions, and a Chebyshev polynomial
070   basis en (x) = cos(k arccos x) for non-periodic boundary conditions.
071
072
      Similarly, we further discretize in time on to a uniform grid tj = j∆t, for j = 0, 1, ... and timestep
073   ∆t > 0, writing uj := u(tj ) and ûj := û(tj ). Successive snapshots are related as
074                                               uj+1 = Φ∆t (uj )                                          (2)

076
077   Neural Operator Learning The field of neural operator learning (Raissi, 2018; Sirignano &
078   Spiliopoulos, 2018; Rahman et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021; Liu-Schiaffini et al., 2024) is an attempt to
079   learn the flow map Φ∆t associated with a given PDE or family of PDEs, using a neural network N .
080   Neural operators specifically learn to regress the complete snapshot u extending over all Ω, rather
081   than the object u discretized on to X. For practical purposes, however, it seems that for surrogate
082
      models it is acceptable to stick to a fixed resolution.
083   In this paper, we focus on autoregressive methods (Bar-Sinai et al., 2019; Greenfeld et al., 2019;
084   Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Brandstetter et al., 2022; List et al., 2024; Schnell & Thuerey, 2024)
085   that map initial conditions u0 to any future state at time tj via j-times repeated application N j :=
086   N ◦ ... ◦ N . These allow us to roll out to any future point in time, or if ∆t is fixed, we can at least
087   roll close to it. The problem with autoregressive methods stems in how difficult they are to train.
088
      Test time generalization is usually poor when we roll out beyond the longest time unrolled to during
      training; unrolling at training time is computationally costly; and for chaotic systems it can also be
089
      that longer training unrolls provide poorer gradients (Metz et al., 2021; List et al., 2024).
090
091   Stability and Accuracy The famous Lax-Richtmeyr equivalence theorem (Lax & Richtmyer,
092   1956) for classical finite difference solvers states that a solver is convergent if and only if it is sta-
093   ble and accurate. Similar extensions exist for broader classes of solvers (Quarteroni & Quarteroni,
094
      2009, Theorem 1.1). Accuracy refers to polynomial accuracy constraints on a single step; that is,
095   ∥u(t + ∆t) − Φ̃∆t (u(t))∥ = O(∆tp+1 ) for small ∆t, integer p > 0, and solver flow map Φ̃∆t .
096
      Furthermore, the solver must be stable, meaning that given the underlying dynamics are stable, the
097
      solver never diverges; that is, limN →∞ ∥Φ̃N T /N (u(t))∥ < ∞ for all T > 0 and N = Z>0 . The term
098   Φ̃N
        T /N refers to the N times repeated application of solver Φ̃T /N with timestep T /N . These require-
099   ments we generally do not satisfy in machine learned systems, but what we lose in guarantees, we
100   gain in speed and ease of deployment.
101
      Long Term Accuracy and the Distribution Shift Problem The major difficulty in training au-
102   toregressive models is that good short-term rollout performance does not always translate into good
103   long-term rollout performance. This is due to the distribution shift problem (Brandstetter et al.,
104   2022), where one-step errors accumulate over a roll out, shifting the distribution of inputs at timestep
105   k away from the training distribution. This distribution shift pushes the model into a region of data
106   space where it performs poorly. As we shall see, these distribution shifting errors tend to be high
107   frequency signals, emanating from the use of pointwise nonlinearities. Our remedy is to smooth out
      these errors, projecting model outputs closer to the solution manifold.

      Below we introduce the phenomenon of spectral junk through a step-by-step investigation into the
      eigenvalue spectrum of the learned flow N . We target its source—pointwise nonlinearities—and
134
      its effect on stability and accuracy of long term rollouts. In the following section we then propose
135
      a few methods to mitigate spectral junk, essentially smoothing the spectrum to improve long term
136   behavior. In the results section, we find that improving the spectrum provides an ancillary benefit of
137   improving unroll accuracy and stability.
138
139   Learned flow spectrum One technique to predict flow in/stability is to analyze the eigenvalue
140
      spectrum ΛN of the linearized flow map in the complex plane; that is, the eigenvalues of ∂u N (u).
      For linear, constant-coefficient PDEs the spectrum is constant, but for all other PDEs it is a function
141
      of the current solution. Eigenvalues with magnitude greater than unity correspond to directions in
142
      solution space that cause instability. Eigenvalues with magnitude less than unity correspond to stable
143   directions. Importantly, the spectrum of the learned flow should match that of the true flow.
144
145   Figure 1a compares the spectrum of a learned flow against ground-truth. The ground-truth is ob-
146
      tained by exponentiating the spectrum of the linearized flow as exp (∆t · ΛF ). We can also compare
      this with the linearized flow map of a differentiable off-the-shelf integrator, Exponential Time Differ-
147
      encing fourth-order Runge-Kutta (ETDRK4) (Kassam & Trefethen, 2005). A unit locus is marked
148
      by a dashed line. The learned model is green, groundtruth blue, and classical integrator red. We
149   see the underlying flow has many stable eigenvalues on the real axis (close inspection would reveal
150   they are actually complex pairs with extremely small imaginary component), implying convergence
151   in those directions to a low dimensional solution/inertial manifold. Meanwhile, the learned map has
152   many large amplitude eigenvalues, in this case up to 100× larger than unity. The presence of these
153   large eigenvalues explains why sometimes models are seen to rapidly diverge during test time as
154   documented in Brandstetter et al. (2022). Probing these eigenvalues, we see that most of them are
155   high frequency modes (see Figure 2 for visualization). This begs a number of questions: 1) Why is
156   the model unstable in the very the directions the ground-truth flow map assigns as stable?; 2) Does it
157
      matter that the learned flow is unstable in these high frequency directions, if we perhaps are unlikely
      to visit them?; and 3) Why are error vectors typically high frequency?
158

160   argue no, in fact, without special attention, stable directions will generally be very difficult to learn.
161   Since we gather training examples from the ground-truth (or close to the ground-truth) solution, the
      one-step model only ever sees inputs and targets that are trapped close to the solution manifold—

      Related work on stabilization strategies Autoregressive training, also known as Backpropaga-
243   tion Through Time (BPTT) (Werbos, 1990), is the simplest training technique. During training, the
244   network is unrolled for as many timesteps as there are in the training sequence, with the time and
245   memory complexity scaling linearly with sequence length. For increased efficiency, it is common
246   to truncate trajectories into shorter snippets (e.g., (Brandstetter et al., 2022)). But unroll too little
247   and the model still goes unstable, unroll too much and the signal-to-noise ratio of backpropagated
248   errors is too small and slows down learning (List et al., 2024). DySLIM (Schiff et al., 2024) is a
249   recently proposed approach, which is autoregressive in nature, but uses a different loss. It minimizes
250   the measure-theoretic distance/divergence of a bundle of prediction trajectories to the training data.
251
      One angle on DySLIM is that it tackles the distribution shift problem directly. Another is that it
      circumvents spectral bias, by switching to a different loss function.
252
253   There is also a broad class of projection-based techniques: Denoising corrupts the input to the
254   network such that we minimize the loss Eϵ L(u(t1 ), f (u(t0 ) + ϵ)). Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020)
255   choose Gaussian ϵ and later Mayr et al. (2023) choose Brownian motion noise for ϵ. This noise
256   mimics the high frequency perturbations off the solution manifold, unseen in one-step training but
257   introduced in rollouts. Choice of this noise distribution is empirical and requires hand-tuning and so
258   the pushforward trick (Brandstetter et al., 2022), a special case of Truncated BPTT (e.g., (Williams
259   & Zipser, 1995)) was introduced to sample ϵ directly from the distribution of errors. For this, a
260
      model is unrolled 2 steps in training, but gradients are only backpropagated through 1 step. It is a
      simple and effective trick. Lippe et al. (2023) takes the one-step denoising step to the extreme with
261
      PDE-Refiner, designing an iterative denoising procedure. They learn two kinds of map. A one-step
262
      map to push a solution one step forward in time u(t0 ) 7→ û1 (t1 ), where û1 (t1 ) is a first candidate for
263   u(t1 ). They then learn a series of denoising maps, which successively refine û1 (t1 ) 7→ û2 (t1 ) 7→
264   · · · 7→ ûJ (t1 ), for J steps. Each successive denoising map is trained as a denoising auto-encoder
265   on a exponentially decreasing noise schedule. While PDE-refiner is effective, it requires multiple
266   iterations per time step during inference. It is also not clear how PDE-Refiner fares when combined
267   with autoregressive training, since this was never shown in the original paper.

      All the above methods work to stabilize unrolled dynamics. Sometimes they can be combined (e.g.
281
      denoising and autoregressive training). The effectiveness of each method varies per system. In
282
      this paper, we introduce a new, simple technique for improving the spectrum that can can also aid
283   stabilization. It too can be combined with the aforementioned techniques. Crucially, it adds a new
284   operation that remedies poor spectral modeling, important for modeling of PDEs.
285
286
      We observe for all the systems we consider, the perturbations are all high frequency. Therefore, in-
      stead of learning a projection operator back on to the solution manifold, we can directly build one by
287
      filtering out the noise. This turns out to be both remarkably simple, but also cheap and effective. Fur-
288
      thermore, we identify the pointwise nonlinearity as the source of spurious high frequency. Placing a
289   simple blurring operation after each pointwise nonlinearity, we can improve the spectrum, stabilize
290   roll-outs, improve rollout decorrelation time, and reduce the need for autoregressive training.

294
      alternatives, we find that convolution with a discrete Gaussian filter (Lindeberg, 1990) works well.

311   5        E XPERIMENTS
312   Systems We demonstrate the utility of spectral shaping on a range of PDE benchmarks. In 1D,
313   we focus on the Kuramoto-Shivashinsky (KS) equation, a fourth-order chaotic 1D equation, on a
314   periodic domain. The power spectral density exhibits strong roll-off, spanning 16 orders of magni-
315   tude, as exhibited in Figures 1a & 3b. In 2D we focus on Kolmogorov flow (KF) as per Kochkov
316   et al. (2021b), defined on a 2D periodic domain with velocity field u. We choose to model at
317   Reynolds number Re = 500, which exhibits strong roll-off in the Fourier domain. We also intro-
318   duce Rayleigh-Bénard convection (RBC), a model of turbulent thermal convection, between two
319
      plates, heated below and cooled from above. It contains non-periodic boundary conditions at the
      two plates, turbulent decay, non-stationarity, and extreme sensitivity to initial conditions. Complete
320

       Learning to minimize spectral gap Another technique to improve spectral shape is to use a spec-
464
       tral gap minimizing loss. We explore two candidates: the MELR metric, and the DySLIM loss
465    (Schiff et al., 2024). The DySLIM loss is a sum of two maximum mean discrepancy (Gretton et al.,
466    2012) losses, one between a minibatch of predictions and a minibatch of targets, and another be-
467    tween the predictions and the initial conditions. We add each loss as a regularizer to the MSE loss,
468    sweeping over hyperparameters to find a good combination. Results are shown in Figure 9. A
469    combination of spectral shaping and a spectral loss always improves both spectrum and decorrela-
470    tion window. This makes sense, because spectral shaping endows models the capacity to achieve
471    sufficient dynamic range in the spectrum, while the spectral loss enforces the correct final shape.

         6            C ONCLUSIONS
487
488      We have conducted an analysis into the role and importance of the spectrum on long-term temporal
489      rollouts of deterministic autoregressive PDE surrogates. As corroborated by other works (Lippe
490      et al., 2023), we found poor modeling of the mid- to high-frequency modes limits the extent of rollout
491
         accuracy. We identified three convergent factors accountable for this poor mid-to-high frequency
         modeling: statistical bias of training data versus test time rollouts (the distribution shift problem
492
         (Brandstetter et al., 2022)), learning dynamics bias encouraging low frequencies to be learned first
493
         (spectral bias of the L2 loss (Rahaman et al., 2019)), and architectural bias injecting high amounts of
494      spectral junk (pointwise nonlinearities). We found that inserting a simple low pass filter after every
495      pointwise nonlinearity in the model was enough to address the poor spectrum and improve rollout
496      accuracy. Thus a simple modification to training can significantly improve PDE surrogate modeling
497      and rollout capabilities.
498
         We demonstrated this on three systems in 1D and 2D, exhibiting a range of behaviors, including
499
         chaos, non-stationarity, and non-periodic boundary conditions. We also showed that low pass filter-
500      ing cannot easily be learned by the addition of a high frequency regularizer to the loss function: a
501      (potentially learnable) low pass filtering operation has to be placed strategically after every pointwise
502      nonlinearity.

      A.3 R AYLEIGH -B ÉNARD C ONVECTION
865
866
      Equation Rayleigh-Bénard convection (RBC) is a model of turbulent thermal convection, where
      a fluid is confined between two plates, heated below and cooled from above. This phenomenon is
867
      tricky to model, because it contains non-periodic boundary conditions at the two plates, turbulent
868
      decay, non-stationarity, and extreme sensitivity to initial conditions. The equations are
869                                                      r
870
                                                            P 2
                                     ut = −(u · ∇)u +          ∇ u − ∇p + θez                          (11)
871                                                         R
872                                                         1
                                     θt = −(u · ∇)θ + √          ∇2 θ                                  (12)
873                                                         PR
874                               ∇·u=0                                                                (13)
875                             2
      for domain [x, z] ∈ [0, 1] . The boundary conditions at the upper and lower plates are
876
                           θ(z = 0) = 1, θ(z = 1) = 0, u(z = 0) = 0, u(z = 1) = 0.                     (14)
877

878
      ber P (= 1 in all our experiments), and Rayleigh number R ≫ 1. The boundary conditions represent
879
      the constant temperature plates and no-slip condition on the fluid velocity. To make this even harder,
880   the equations are parametrized by Rayleigh number Ra, which we sample from a log-uniform distri-
881   bution and pass as side-information to our models. The Rayleigh number is a dimensionless number
882   representing the balance between buoyancy-driven flow and viscous/thermal diffusion. The higher
883   it is the more mixing we expect to see.
