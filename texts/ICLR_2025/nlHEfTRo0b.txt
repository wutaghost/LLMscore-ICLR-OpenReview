009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            While physics-informed neural networks have achieved remarkable progress in
012            modeling dynamical systems governed by partial differential equations (PDEs),
013            their ability to generalize across different scenarios remains restricted. To address
014            this limitation, we present PIDO, a novel physics-informed neural PDE solver that
015            demonstrates robust generalization across various aspects of PDE configurations,
016            including initial conditions, PDE coefficients, and training time horizons. PIDO
017            leverages the shared intrinsic structure inherent to dynamical systems with varying
018
               properties by projecting the PDE solutions into a latent space via auto-decoding
               and subsequently learning the dynamics of these latent embeddings conditioned on
019
               the PDE coefficients. However, the inherent optimization challenges associated
020
               with physics-informed loss present substantial obstacles to integrating such latent
021            dynamics models. To tackle this issue, we adopt a novel perspective by diagnosing
022            these challenges within the latent space. This approach enables us to enhance both
023            temporal extrapolation ability and training stability of PIDO via simple yet effective
024            regularization techniques, ultimately leading to superior generalization performance
025            compared to its data-driven counterpart. The effectiveness of PIDO is validated
026            on diverse benchmarks, including 1D combined equations and 2D Navier-Stokes
027            equations. Moreover, we investigate the transferability of its learned representations
028            to downstream tasks like long-term integration and inverse problems.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Partial differential equations (PDEs) constitute the cornerstone of comprehending complex sys-
      tems and forecasting their behavior. Recent years have witnessed a surge in the effectiveness of
034
      deep learning methods for solving PDEs (Yu et al., 2018; Kovachki et al., 2021; Brandstetter et al.,
035
      2021). Among these, Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) have emerged as a burgeoning
036   paradigm (Raissi et al., 2019). PINNs leverage Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) to parameter-
037   ize PDE solutions, enabling them to effectively bridge data with mathematical models and tackle
038   high-dimensional problems. This unique characteristic has led to their widespread adoption in
039   a variety of applications , including computational fluid dynamics (Raissi et al., 2020), photonic
040   structures design (Ma et al., 2021) and material science simulations (Zhang et al., 2022).
041
      A key advantage of PINNs lies in their ability to be trained by enforcing PDE-based constraints even
042   in the absence of exact solutions. This flexibility proves valuable in real-world settings where perfect
043   data might not be available. However, this very benefit comes at a cost. Each instance of PINNs
044   is trained tailored to a specific configuration of initial and boundary conditions, PDE coefficients,
045   geometries, and forcing terms. Modifying any of these elements necessitates retraining, resulting in
046   significant computational inefficiency. In addressing this obstacle, Neural Operators (NOs) have been
047   proposed as a promising solution (Li et al., 2020b; Lu et al., 2021a). NOs aim to tackle the so-called
048   parametric PDEs by learning to map variable condition entities to corresponding PDE solutions.
049   Despite their success, several limitations hinder the generalization ability of NOs. First, some NO
050   architectures restrict the choice of grids. This limitation leads to challenges when encountering input
051   (Lu et al., 2021a; Wang et al., 2021b) or output (Li et al., 2020b) grids that differ from those used in
052   training. Secondly, for time-dependent PDEs, NOs are typically trained to provide predictions within
053   a fixed time horizon, limiting their applicability in scenarios requiring broader temporal coverage.
      Finally, while NOs may generalize well to specific types of variable conditions, their ability to handle

080
      Explicit Dynamics Modeling (EDM) (Yin et al., 2022; Wan et al., 2022), the temporal dynamics
081
      model then learns the coefficient-aware evolution of latent embeddings using Neural ODEs (Chen
082   et al., 2018), which are known for their exceptional ability to extrapolate beyond the training horizon.
083
084   While previous EDM methods are typically trained in a data-driven manner, their performance relies
085
      heavily on dataset size. In contrast, we focus on a physics-informed setting, where PIDO is optimized
      to satisfy governing PDEs without relying on real data. However, the physics-informed loss is known
086
      to face optimization challenges (Krishnapriyan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021a), leading to two key
087
      issues in its integration with EDM: instability during training and degradation in time extrapolation.
088   To address these challenges, we adopt a novel perspective by diagnosing and tackling them within the
089   latent space. By projecting high-dimensional data into low-dimensional representations, we identify
090   two problematic latent behaviors: overly complex dynamics and latent embedding drift. Based on
091   these insights, we propose two regularization techniques-Latent Dynamics Smoothing and Latent
092   Dynamics Alignment-to improve training stability and extrapolation ability, respectively. Overall,
093   these strategies enhance PIDO’s generalization ability compared to its data-driven counterparts. Our
094   contributions can be summarized as follows:
095
                  • We propose a novel physics-informed learning framework that achieves robust generalization
096
                    across diverse variables in PDE configurations.
097
098
                  • We diagnose the learning difficulties of physics-informed dynamics models within the latent
                    space and address them using latent dynamics smoothing and latent dynamics alignment,
099
                    resulting in improved generalization performance compared to the data-driven counterpart.
100
101
                  • We demonstrate the effectiveness of PIDO on extensive benchmarks including 1D combined
                    equation and 2D Navier-Stokes equations, and explore the transferability of PIDO’s learned
102
                    representations to downstream tasks including long-term integration and inverse problems.

      3.4     D IAGNOSING P HYSICS - INFORMED O PTIMIZATION IN THE L ATENT S PACE
294
295   While physics-informed training eliminates the need for exact PDE solutions, it suffers from optimiza-
296   tion difficulties (Krishnapriyan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021a), presenting two key challenges in
297   integration with EDM: instability during training and degradation in time extrapolation. Nevertheless,
298   the latent space introduced by EDM offers a novel perspective for diagnosing and addressing these
299   issues. By capturing essential information in low-dimensional representations, it facilitates a more
300   straightforward analysis. Notably, this framework allows us to identify latent behaviors responsible
301
      for each challenge and mitigate them using simple but effective regularization in latent space.
302
303
      3.4.1    S TABILIZING THE T RAINING P ROCESS THROUGH L ATENT DYNAMICS S MOOTHING
304   In contrast to data-driven methods, physics-informed loss imposes stricter constraints on the time step
305   size to ensure stable training. If the time step is too large, training often collapses into trivial solutions
306   as shown in Figure 2(a), where the loss is minimized on training steps but remains high on unseen
307   time points close to the initial conditions. Consequently, despite small training losses, the predictions
308   deviate from the ground truth because information fails to propagate from the initial conditions to
309   subsequent steps (Wang et al., 2022a). To resolve this, a smaller time step size is often required,
310   which, in turn, significantly increase computational costs and exacerbate the training difficulty.
311   As an alternative, we explore this issue within the latent space. Our findings, as illustrated in Fig-
312   ure 2(a), reveal that the model tends to learn excessively fluctuating latent dynamics from all possible
313   dynamics that minimize training loss. This leads to a complex loss distribution along the time axis,
314   which exhibits poor generalization to unseen points. As a result, the step size constraint becomes
315   even stricter for our method compared to other physics-informed approaches. We attribute this to
316   the increased flexibility introduced by the latent dynamics model, which is trained without direct
317
      supervision in latent space. While data-driven EDM methods utilize a similar framework, they do
      not encounter this issue, as they leverage embeddings from pretrained encoder-decoder networks as
318
      ground truth for training the dynamics model (Yin et al., 2022). In contrast, our method provides the
319
      dynamics model with only indirect supervision through the PDE loss in data space.
320

322   the model to favor simpler dynamics. Inspired by Finlay et al. (2020), this regularization mitigates

500
501   Long-term Integration. Training a physics-informed neural PDE solver for long temporal horizons
502   presents a significant challenge. We adopt the training setting outlined in Wang & Perdikaris (2023),
503   which reformulates the long-term integration problem as a series of initial value problems solved
504
      within a shorter horizon (Ttr ). Data snapshots are uniformly sampled across the entire test horizon
      (Tts ) to serve as training initial conditions. The model, trained on the shorter interval Ttr , can
505
      iteratively predict future states by using previous prediction at the end of Ttr as new initial condition,
506
      extending its effective horizon without direct long-term training. While this approach can be sensitive
507   to the number of training initial conditions, PIDO’s pre-training knowledge mitigate this limitation.
508
509   We consider a challenging setting where the test horizon Tts is ten times longer than the training
510
      horizon Ttr . We set Ttr to 5s. The data is generated with a Reynolds number α = 950, which
      is unseen by PIDO during the pre-training stage. Ten data snapshots are uniformly sampled from
511
      the test horizon as training initial conditions. We compare PIDO against PI-DeepONet. When
512
      inference, PIDO can directly predict the entire horizon, while PI-DeepONet relies on the iterative
513   scheme. As shown in Table 5a, both PI-DeepONet and PIDO struggle to achieve satisfactory long-
514   term performances when trained from scratch. In contrast, the pre-trained PIDO exhibits significant
515   improvement (77% error reduction) through fine-tuning the dynamics model. This showcases its
516   effectiveness in enhancing long-term predictions with insufficient data. Furthermore, fine-tuning all
517   components of the pre-trained PIDO can lead to further performance gains.
518
519   Inverse Problem. PINNs have demonstrated efficacy in solving inverse problems (Raissi et al.,
520   2019; 2020), aiming to recover the PDE coefficients α from a limited set of observations (Pakravan
521   et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022; Nair et al., 2023). We then showcase the effectiveness of PIDO’s
522
      pretrained knowledge in this context by comparing it against PINN. We follow Raissi et al. (2019) to
      treat coefficients α as learnable parameters and optimize them with a neural network to simultaneously
523
      fit the observed data and satisfy the PDE constraints. We focus on a case with Reynolds number
524
      α = 950 and a training horizon of Ttr = 10s. Training data consists of N solution snapshots
525   uniformly sampled across the entire horizon. For each snapshot, 5% of spatial locations are randomly
526   selected as observed data points. Table 5b shows that PIDO consistently outperforms its from-scratch
527   counterpart when only finetuning the dynamics model. Notably, even with only two snapshots, the
528   pretrained PIDO achieves accurate predictions. This remarkable performance highlights the capability
529   of PIDO’s transferable representations in alleviating the data scarcity burden in inverse problems.

      5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose PIDO, a novel physics-informed neural PDE solver demonstrating ex-
534   ceptional generalization across diverse PDE configurations. PIDO effectively leverages the shared
535   structure of dynamical systems by projecting solutions into a latent space and learning their dynamics
536   conditioned on PDE coefficients. To tackle the challenges of physics-informed dynamics modeling,
537   we adopt an innovative perspective by diagnosing and mitigating them in latent space, resulting in
538   a significant improvement in the model’s temporal extrapolation and training stability. Extensive
539   experiments on 1D and 2D benchmarks demonstrate PIDO’s generalization ability to initial conditions,
      PDE coefficients and training time horizons, along with transferability to downstream tasks.

      B     AUTO -D ECODER VERSUS AUTO -E NCODER FOR P HYSICS -I NFORMED EDM
876
      Since physics-informed training strategies for auto-encoders differ significantly from those used
877
      for auto-decoders (our method), this section discusses these key differences and details the specific
878   training settings employed in auto-encoder methods.
879
880   Previous work PINODE (Sholokhov et al., 2023) utilizes an auto-encoder framework for physics-
881
      informed training. Unlike our auto-decoder, which takes spatial coordinates x and embeddings c as
      input data and output predictions of states u, PINODE’s encoder E operates in the opposite direction.
882
      It takes u as input and output c = E(u). This allows PINODE to calculate the derivative of c w.r.t u
883
      using Auto-Differentiation (AD). Consequently, PINODE can derive the temporal derivative of its
884   embedding c, assuming the input data u follows the PDE ∂u ∂t = L(u):
885
886
                                         ∂c     ∂c ∂u        ∂c
                                             =      ·     =      · (−L(u)).                              (17)
887                                      ∂t     ∂u ∂t       ∂u
888   This derivative then serves as labels to train the dynamics model F by the following loss:
889                                          ∂c                 ∂c
                         lPDE (θE , θF ) = ∥    − F(c)∥22 = ∥       · (−L(u)) − F(c)∥22 ,                (18)
890                                          ∂t                ∂u
891   where θE and θF denote parameters of E and F, respectively.
892
      However, this approach has three limitations. First, PINODE’s encoder and decoder adheres to a
893   fixed grid for both input data and output predictions, limiting its flexibility. Second, PINODE relies
894   on an analytical representation of the input data u to compute its spatial derivatives involved in L(u)
895   in Equation (18). This is achieved by pre-defining an analytical distribution from which the input
896   data is sampled. Finally, PINODE assumes the input data distribution accurately reflects the true
897   PDE solutions. However, finding such a representative distribution in real-world scenarios can be
898   challenging.
899   Deviations from this assumption can significantly degrade PINODE’s performance. Our experiments
900   (Table 10) demonstrate this sensitivity. In these experiments, we sample PINODE’s input data
901   from exact PDE solutions (ideal scenario) or the distribution of initial conditions (the only data we
902   can access in the data-constrained scenario). The results show that PINODE’s time extrapolation
903   performance suffers significantly when the input data deviates from the true distribution of PDE
904   solutions. Additionally, increasing the number of initial conditions offered little improvement, further
905   highlighting PINODE’s dependence on a suitable input data distribution. Given the data-constrained
906   setting of our experiments in Section 4, we employ the distribution of initial conditions as input data
907
      for PINODE to ensure a fair comparison with other methods.
908   In contrast, PIDO adopts the auto-decoder framework, which is grid-independent and enables the
909   calculation of spatial derivatives of predicted solutions through AD. Moreover, the training of PIDO
910   does not require any prior knowledge about data distribution, making it more robust for real-world
911   scenarios.

       D     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1017
       In this work, we mainly focus on the coefficient-aware dynamics modeling, concerning the general-
1018
       ization w.r.t initial conditions, PDE coefficients and time horizons. However, we acknowledge two
1019
       key limitations that motivate future research directions: First, while we employ periodic boundary
1020   conditions in all our experiments, a crucial future direction is to extend the generalization study to
1021   handle diverse boundary conditions and geometries. A promising approach might involve utilizing
1022   multiple decoders, each tailored to specific boundary conditions. Second, the smoothing regulariza-
1023   tion used for stability training can potentially penalize high-frequency physics information, which
1024   is crucial for accurate modeling. Currently, we achieve a trade-off between stability and accuracy
1025   with a suitable regularization weight. It is a promising avenue to investigate alternative regularization
       techniques that improve stability without compromising the capture of high-frequency details.
