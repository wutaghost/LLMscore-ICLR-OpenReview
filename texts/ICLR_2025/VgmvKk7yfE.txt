010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            We present first experimental results from the Turing Game, a modern implementa-
013            tion of the original imitation game as proposed by Alan Turing in 1950. The Turing
014            Game is a gamified interaction between two human players and one AI chatbot
015            powered by state-of-the-art Large Language Models (LLMs). The game is designed
016            to explore whether humans can distinguish between their peers and machines in
017            chat-based conversations, with human players striving to identify fellow humans
018
               and machines striving to blend in as one of them. To this end, we implemented
               a comprehensive framework that connects human players over the Internet with
019
               chatbot implementations. We detail the experimental results after a public launch
020
               at the Ars Electronica Festival in September 2024. While the experiment is still
021            ongoing, in this paper we present our initial findings from the hitherto gathered data.
022            Our long term vision of the project is to deepen the understanding of human-AI
023            interactions and eventually contribute to improving LLMs and language-based user
024            interfaces.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   AI systems are built with the goal of performing activities that were traditionally reserved to humans,
030   from playing strategy games, like chess (Campbell et al., 2002), Go (et al., 2016) or Dota-2 (et al.,
031   2019), to generating artistic imagery (Mid) and written texts (OpenAI, 2023; Jones and Bergen,
032   2023). They became better and better up until the point where some have already surpassed human
033
      performances in fields that have traditionally been believed to require human abstract thinking and
      strategic planning. In the field of content generation, we have arrived at the point where we find it
034
      hard to discern whether images or clips are generated or represent real footage or whether texts stem
035
      from a human or a machine.
036
037   Alan Turing, a founding figure in computer science, posed the question of whether machines can
038   think (Turing, 1950; Saygin et al., 2000). Drawing from the theoretical Turing Machines - capable of
039
      computing anything computable - and emerging insights into brain function, Turing hypothesized that
      human thought processes could be computationally replicated. To address the ambiguous concept
040
      of “thinking”, he proposed the Turing Test (also called “Imitation Game”), where an interrogator
041
      communicates with a machine and a human through a text interface, aiming to distinguish between
042   them. The machine passes if it convinces the interrogator it is human, suggesting it simulates aspects
043   of human thought. However, the test’s outcomes depend heavily on the participants’ motivations and
044   susceptibility to deception, issues unaddressed in Turing’s original formulation or later implementa-
045   tions like the Löbner Prize (Shieber, 1994; Epstein et al., 2008) and “Human or Not?” (Jannai et al.,
046   2023).
047
      In this paper, we propose to extend the Imitation Game by symmetrizing the roles of the original two
048   human participants, see Fig. 1. This seemingly slight redesign of the test shifts the focus away from
049   the simple question-answering to the collaboration between the humans and the inference of their
050   mutual intentions, a characteristic feature of human communications (Tomasello et al., 2005; Zhang
051   et al., 2024). Due to that, the question comes down to which of the interlocutors understands the
052   intentions better, a human or a machine. Note that in this way we avoid being explicit about what
053   behavior is human-like, allowing participants to decide what behavior is human-like, and what is not.
      Just like Alan Turing, we leave the kind and length of the conversation fully up to the humans.

      Humans often express their verbalized thoughts in a non-explicit and incomplete way (Clark and
      Brennan, 1991). In order for a machine to correctly understand human desires and needs, it needs to
073
      understand our thoughts on a large enough joint context (common knowledge), and thus behave as
074
      human-like as possible (Christian, 2020; Amodei et al., 2016). Our contributions are as follows:
075
076             • We propose a generalization of the Turing Test, the Turing Game, which is symmetric with
077               respect to the role of the two humans. We also develop a tailored matching algorithm to pair
078               human players according to their playing performance and average time to make decisions.
079             • We have developed and installed the Turing Game as a platform and made it publicly
080               available.1 Our platform serves as a sandbox for testing various LLMs and chatbot imple-
081               mentations designed to mimic human-like thinking, evaluated by an open community. We
082               have designed the ratings of the bots such that the most qualified humans contribute the
083               most to those ratings.
084             • We present the preliminary experimental results from the hitherto gathered data, mainly
085               from a public exposition and public installation at the Ars Electronica Festival.
086
087   The paper is organized as follows: Sec. 2 details the related work and shortcomings of hitherto
088   implementations of Turing-like tests; Sec. 3 describes the proposed Turing Game; Sec. 4 presents
089   results and their analysis from the already gathered data; Sec. 5 concludes and reflects on our
090   contributions. In the Appendix, Sec.A discusses potential ethical consequences, Sec.B complements
091   the presented scores, Sec.D describes our platform, Sec.E supplements the results from Sec.4, and

      Sec.F details our installation at the Ars Electronica Festival.

096   Turing(-like) tests before LLMs. Levesque proposed the Winograd Scheme Challenge (WSC),
097   as a possible alternative to the Turing Test (see also Levesque et al. (2012)). The challenge consists
098   in a set of cleverly constructed pairs of sentences that differ by only one or two words. Correct
099   interpretation of these sentences relies on resolving pronoun ambiguities, a task that seemingly
100   requires common-sense reasoning (Kocijan et al., 2023). In addition to the Turing Test, numerous
101
      other tests have been proposed. Examples include The Marcus Test that evaluates AI system’s ability
      to understand the meaning behind video content, such as plot, humor and sarcasm. To pass, an AI
102
      system needs to describe the video content like a human would (Marcus et al., 2016). The Lovelace
103
      Test, which examines whether AI can generate original ideas that exceed its training data (Bringsjord
104   et al., 2001). The Reverse Turing Test, in which the AI acts as the interrogator and must determine if
105   the human participant is actually a machine. The human passes the test if the AI misidentifies them as
106   a machine (Sejnowski, 2022). The Visual Turing Test, designed to assess computer vision systems

      by asking binary questions about an image. An operator answers or dismisses each question for
109   ambiguity. The system asks one question at a time, focusing solely on visual understanding without
110   natural language processing. The test aims to evaluate the system’s ability to interpret complex visual
111   narratives and relationships between objects (Geman et al., 2015). The Löbner Prize (Shieber,
112   1994), established in 1990 by Hugh Löbner, was an annual competition based on the Turing Test
113   that challenged AI programs to mimic human conversation. Judges would determine if responses
114   came from humans or machines. The contest aimed to advance AI but was criticized for encouraging
115   superficial techniques. The competition continued until 2019, without ever awarding its prize for a
116   fully indistinguishable AI.
117
118   Turing(-like) tests and LLMs. Jannai et al. presented “Human or Not”, an online game aimed to
119
      measure the capability of AI chatbots to mimic humans in conversation, as well as humans’ ability to
      tell bots from other humans. Over 1.5 million unique users participated, engaging in two-minute chat
120
      sessions with either another human or an AI language model simulating human behavior. We observe
121
      the following shortcomings in the above work: the authors impose a 2-minute time constraint, which
122   may push participants toward System 1 type reasoning (Suter and Hertwig, 2011), and they do not
123   address the issue of asymmetry in the original Imitation Game (what we do by adding more players).
124
125
      Relatively big-scale and multimodal experiments were performed by Zhang et al.. The results revealed
      that current AIs are not far from being able to impersonate humans across different ages, genders,
126
      and educational levels in complex visual and language challenges. Jones and Bergen evaluated
127
      GPT-4 in a public online Turing Test to find out that familiarity with LLMs did increase the detection
128   rate. Zheng et al. examined the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) as evaluators (“judge”) of
129   chatbot performance, an approach called “LLM-as-a-judge.” They developed Chatbot Arena,2 a
130   crowdsourced platform featuring anonymous battles between chatbots in real-world scenarios – users
131   engage in conversations with two chatbots at the same time and rate their responses based on personal
132   preferences. The system ranks AI bots through pairwise comparisons. However, the analysis reflects
133   the subjective preferences of an average human, without setting a specific goal or scale on which
134   performance should be rated.
135
136   Shortcomings. Levesque identified several major issues related to Turing’s original question,
137
      summarized as follows. Deception: The machine is forced to construct a false identity, which is not
      part of intelligence. Conversation: A lot of interaction may qualify as “legitimate conversation” —
138
      jokes, clever asides, points of order — without requiring intelligent reasoning. Evaluation: Humans
139
      make mistakes and judges might disagree on the results. In addition to those issues, and shortcomings
140   of the Turing Test discussed in the literature (for a comprehensive overview, see French (2000)), we
141   identify problems related to the role of the judge: to the best of our knowledge, all previous work
142   assumes an “average” judge, and bases their analysis on this assumption. In contrast, we propose
143   employing highly skilled judges who have specifically demonstrated proficiency in distinguishing
144   between machines and humans. To identify these top-performing judges, we propose dividing
145   the experiment into two phases: the phase designed to assess which humans excel as judges, and
146   the phase where we evaluate how the bots perform against highly skilled judges. Note that this
147   approach encourages a more rigorous test, not an easier one. Additionally, we do not enforce any
148
      time constraints and allow for deliberate decision-making, encouraging System 2 reasoning rather
      than impulsive System 1 judgements (Suter and Hertwig, 2011).

151   3       T HE T URING G AME
152
153   Motivated by the reported shortcomings of the original implementation, we symmetrize the interaction
154   between the two human participants by allowing the three participants (two humans and one machine)
155   to interact with each other, and we removed the predetermined role of the interrogator (see Fig. 1).
156
      That gives rise to a gamified interaction between players, called the Turing Game. At any point during
      the game, the players may decide to cast their vote and try to identify the machine. The game finishes
157
      as soon as the both humans have cast their vote. The humans win the game only if both of them have
158
      correctly identified the machine. If at least one of them misidentifies his fellow human as a machine,
159   then both humans lose. This redesign introduces the following changes to the test’s dynamics: (i)
160   already with three participants we may observe an effect of siding between any two players, absent in
161

              https://chat.lmsys.org/

      one-on-one interactions (Tajfel and Turner, 1979); (ii) the presence of two players further mitigates
163   the reverse effect of the Turing Test as the machine’s responses do not get influenced solely by one
164   player (Sejnowski, 2022); (iii) the participants benefit from forming collaborations within the group,
165   a typically human feature (Tomasello et al., 2005). Their interaction’s style may range from fully
166   collaborative, to fully interrogative, or anything in between. Lastly, as participants interact using
167   written language without additional cues such as body language or facial expressions, they rely more
168   on deliberate reasoning rather than intuitive judgment (Kurzban, 2001).

172   Ranking in games has been explored in the context of feedback systems and has been shown to have a
173
      positive effect on the motivation of players (Przybylski et al., 2010; Deci et al., 1999). The frequently
      used ELO rating (Elo, 1978) is not applicable to our case, and its generalizations such as Herbrich
174
      et al. (2006) are not robust against players who may deliberately misidentify fellow humans. Hence,
175

176   identify the most proficient players and match them based on their game strength, as an experienced
177   player may underperform when paired with an inexperienced one. Observe that by pairing humans
178   who performed well we ensure that (a) the players were able to correctly identify the machine, (b)
179   they managed to convince other human players about the machine’s identity, in this way eliminating
180   those who are more likely to deliberately misidentify fellow humans.

      4.2   G OOD J UDGMENT N EEDS T IME
379
380   Next, we investigate the influence of the game’s duration on the probability that the humans win.
381   Specifically, we look at the ratio of games won by humans as a function of the game duration
382   once measured in minutes, and once measured in the total number of the exchanged messages. We
383   observe that initially the ratio of human-won games increases with time, up until around 3 minutes
384   of interaction, when it plateaus (Fig. 6). It indicates that: (1) for the current bots it is necessary to
385   interact for about 3 minutes in order to make a deliberate guess about the bot’s identity, (2) if we were
386   to impose a strict time limit on the duration of the interaction, we would have forced humans to rely
387
      more on the quick intuitive judgement rather than a well-reflected deductive one. That further explains
      why Jannai et al. achieve lower identification rates of the machine by a human. We hypothesize, that
388
      questioning and a reasonably informed decision making process needs significantly more than two
389
      minutes. Note that more that half of the games lasted 3 minutes or significantly more, the longest
390   game even took 25 minutes. We assume that the players in those games needed this time in order
391   to gather the information necessary for their final decision. Hence, with an enforced time limit this
392   same number of games would rely essentially on a random guess, making humans prone to an error
393   in judgement.

      4.3   AUTOMATED JUDGING
423
424
      We further assess how well a trained classifier can differentiate between bot and human writing
425
      patterns in dialog. We train a simple classifier c : M → {0, 1} that predicts whether a single
426   message m is machine-generated or human-written. The classifier’s domain, M, is the space of
427   all text messages. c(m) = 1 indicates a human-generated message and c(m) = 0 indicates a
428   machine-generated message. The classification accuracy of such a trained classifier reaches 81%
429   (see Appendix C for details on the classifier architecture and the train-validation split). Already a
430   single message from a game allows us to conclude correctly in more than 4 of 5 cases if the message
431   originates from a bot or a human. This analysis will serve as a benchmark to compare with more
      established methods, e.g., DetectGPT (Mitchell et al., 2023).

468   5    C ONCLUSIONS
469
470   We have proposed a framework designed to understand how proficient people are in telling their
471
      kind from machines in a direct, text-based, interaction. In our extended version of the Turing Test,
      involving two humans and one machine without predetermined roles, we aim to engage the System 2
472
      cognitive processes of the participants. This setup requires players to employ analytical reasoning and
473
      critical thinking to meticulously evaluate responses and discern subtle cues indicative of non-human
474   behavior (Yu et al., 2024; Kahneman, 2011). The nature of the interaction fosters strategic dialogue
475   and collaboration, where players must formulate insightful questions and share their observations to
476   collectively identify the machine. This collaborative effort invokes meta-cognition and theory of mind,
477   as players reflect on their own thought processes and anticipate the reasoning of others (Frith and
478   Frith, 2006). By consciously overcoming cognitive biases and avoiding snap judgments, participants
479   engage in deliberate decision-making characteristic of System 2 thinking (Stanovich and West, 2000).
480   The game’s complex problem-solving environment not only enhances cognitive engagement but also
481   provides deeper insights into differentiating human intelligence from artificial intelligence.
482   Moreover, with the proposed framework we have started to gather a dataset which contains thousands
483   of deductive-interactions human-AI, to be released shortly. We will compare the detection rate of
484   machine-generated text by humans with recent approaches designed to automatically detect text
485   generated by LLMs (Mitchell et al., 2023; Christ et al., 2024). This comparison will establish a
      human benchmark for the detection of LLM-generated text.

      A    E THICAL C ONSEQUENCES
649
650
      The development of AI systems capable of convincingly mimicking human behavior, including
651   those that might get close to passing the Turing Test, raises profound ethical concerns, particularly
652   regarding the alignment problem and the need for AI certification. The alignment problem entails
653   ensuring that the actions of AI systems are consistent with human values and intentions — an issue
654   of growing importance as these systems increasingly engage in decision-making processes. However,
655   passing tests such as the Turing Test does not inherently demonstrate that an AI system is aligned
656   with ethical norms, nor does it guarantee its (functional) trustworthiness. This underscores the need
657   for certification processes of AI systems that extend beyond evaluating their ability to simulate human
658   behavior, ensuring that AI systems remain trustworthy and beneficial to humanity.
659   Nevertheless, the Turing Test plays a significant role in discussions about transparency and awareness
660   with regards to modern-day AI systems, especially LLMs, by highlighting how easily these systems
661   can imitate human conversations. As LLMs become more adept at passing this test, it raises ethical
662   concerns about users potentially being unaware that they are interacting with an AI. This lack of
663   transparency can lead to confusion, misplaced trust, or manipulation, as users may assume they are
664   conversing with a sentient being or a human expert. The Turing Test underscores the need for clear
665
      disclosure when AI systems are in use, ensuring that people are aware they are engaging with a
      machine, not a person. Without such transparency, the increasing sophistication of LLMs could blur
666
      the line between human and AI interaction, eroding trust and ethical standards in communication.
