011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013
                   Scaling neural networks in image-based deep reinforcement learning often fails
014                to improve performance. While it was shown that unstructured pruning of scaled
015                networks can unlock performance gains, we find that refining the architecture of
016                the scaled network yields even greater improvements. However, scaled networks
017                in deep reinforcement learning present a practical challenge: the increased com-
018                putational demands can hinder deployment on embedded devices, as commonly
019                encountered in robotics applications. To address this, we propose a novel gradual
020                group-structured pruning framework that allows performance gains through scal-
021                ing while maintaining computational efficiency. Our method preserves the net-
022
                   work’s functional integrity of inter-layer dependencies in groups, such as residual
                   connections, while seamlessly integrating with standard deep reinforcement learn-
023
                   ing algorithms. Experiments with PPO and DQN show that our approach sustains
024
                   performance while significantly reducing inference time, making it the preferred
025                approach for resource-limited deployment.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recent works on deep reinforcement learning (DRL) have revealed that apart from algorithmic im-
031   provements, considerable performance increases can come from the network architecture and train-
032   ing approach of the used deep neural networks (DNNs) themselves. Notably, Cobbe et al. (2020);
033   Schwarzer et al. (2023); Obando-Ceron et al. (2024a) have shown that the Impala-CNN model (Es-
034   peholt et al., 2018), a 15-layer ResNet, outperforms the widely used convolutional neural network
035
      (CNN) model from Mnih et al. (2015) substantially. However, raising the parameter count of DNNs
      in DRL does not necessarily lead to improved performance (Schwarzer et al., 2023), as opposed
036
      to other areas in deep learning. Obando-Ceron et al. (2024a) provide a new perspective on scal-
037
      ing1 DNNs in DRL by using unstructured magnitude pruning to increase sparsity gradually during
038   training, which leads to a performance boost for Q-network-based DRL in Atari games.
039
040   Network pruning is a widely used technique in other deep learning fields , e.g., image classification
041
      (Vadera & Ameen, 2022), originally aimed at reducing DNNs’ memory footprint and inference time
      but also known to frequently enhance robustness and generalization (Bartoldson et al., 2020). Its
042
      use in DRL may introduce advantageous regularization (Obando-Ceron et al., 2024a) but poses a
043
      unique challenge due to its dynamic training, requiring methods that maintain training stability over
044   time. Unstructured pruning zeros out individual weight entries without considering their structural
045   arrangements, such as filters and channels. This is in contrast to structured pruning, where such
046   structures are entirely removed, directly reducing computational operations (Luo et al., 2017; He &
047   Xiao, 2023) but leading potentially to high training instability.
048
      We show in the following preliminary experiment that the benefits of network scaling in image-
049
      based DRL can also be unlocked by simple architectural refinements of the Impala-CNN, rendering
050   the use of unstructured pruning from Obando-Ceron et al. (2024a) for performance increase obsolete.
051   However, it opens the question of leveraging pruning in image-based DRL for the original motivation
052   of lowering computational requirements, which is of high practical appeal for scaled DNNs.

068       Preliminary Experiment on Network Scaling: We base this preliminary experiment on the Proc-
069       gen benchmark (Cobbe et al., 2020), which is considered to challenge generalization better than
070       Atari games. Figure 1 illustrates that scaling the width of the original Impala-CNN architecture to
071       τ = 3 does not result in performance improvements. Consistent with the findings of Obando-Ceron
072       et al. (2024a), we observe that unstructured pruning of scaled Impala-CNNs enhances performance
073
          also for proximal policy optimization (PPO) within the Procgen environment. However, we demon-
          strate that scaling gains can also be realized by simply adding a Pooling layer before the Flatten layer
074
          of the Impala-CNN in combination with learning rate scheduling and weight decay–we name this
075
          architecture Impoola-CNN. Note that classical ResNet models (He et al., 2016) also have this Pool-
076       ing layer; the ablation in Figure B.4 shows that using one is crucial. The Impoola-CNN achieves
077       significantly greater improvements than the use of unstructured pruning for scaled Impala-CNNs.
078       Most notably, when using the scaled Impoola-CNN, the benefits of unstructured pruning vanish,
079       even decreasing performance in training levels. Further results in Appendix B exhibit similar trends
080       for deep Q-networks (DQNs) and include an additional supervised learning example. Thus, we en-
081       courage using the Impoola-CNN model for image-based DRL as it unlocks performance gains by
082       network scaling directly, without the need for unstructured pruning.
083       Structured Pruning for Efficient Scaling: However, the use of scaled network architectures ren-
084       ders new practical problems in the form of increased memory footprint and computational require-
085       ments. This has particular implications for many DRL applications, e.g., robotics (Funk et al.,
086       2022) or autonomous driving (Trumpp et al., 2023), as such applications are eventually deployed to
087       resource-limited embedded devices with high control frequency requirements. This situation brings
088       us back to the original notion of pruning to reduce computation requirements. As unstructured prun-
089       ing only sets weights to zero, it often does not translate to a reduction in real-world inference times
090
          (Cheng et al., 2024). Structured pruning can be seen as a remedy since complete structures are re-
          moved from the DNN, thus reducing run times straight away (Luo et al., 2017). The feasibility of
091
          structured pruning in image-based DRL has been unexplored yet despite its practical appeal.
092
093       This paper establishes a framework for gradual group-structured pruning in image-based DRL, de-
094       signed to reduce the computational requirements of scaled DNNs while closely matching the per-
095       formance of dense baselines. To this end, we center our work on the Impoola-CNN since it not
096
          only outperforms the Impala architecture but also achieves greater efficiency with a reduced pa-
          rameter count. Our study is based on the Procgen Benchmark (Cobbe et al., 2020) as this is the
097
          ideal evaluation platform to assess generalization, but we provide a supplementary experiment for
098
          Atari games. We discuss practical aspects such as fine-tuning capabilities and noise robustness and
099       measure single-sample inference time, an aspect often overlooked by other works. Our main analy-
100       sis uses PPO as this is the common baseline algorithm for the Procgen Benchmark but we provide
101       additional results for DQN to cover a Q-network-based method.
102
          Our main contributions are the following:
103
104                                 • We identify architectural limitations in the original Impala-CNN and propose the improved
105                                   Impoola-CNN model that unlocks performance gains through network scaling.
106
                                    • Our gradual group-structured pruning framework accounts for inter-layer dependencies and
107
                                      enables performance gains through scaling while maintaining computational efficiency.

116
117   Neural Network Pruning: Neural network pruning is a technique to reduce compute time and/or
118   memory size of a DNN by removing its weights, ideally without a substantial loss of accuracy
119   (Han et al., 2016). Networks can be pruned in single or multiple steps with subsequent fine-tuning
120
      after training or gradually over its course (Cheng et al., 2024). Structured pruning (He & Xiao,
      2023) leads to universal speed-ups as complete structures, e.g., filters or neurons, are removed. As
121
      single weights are set to zero in unstructured pruning, inference speed is not necessarily reduced but
122
      only theoretical FLOPS (Luo et al., 2017). Specialized hard- and software, which may improve the
123   computation of such sparse kernels, e.g., semi-structured (2:4) patterns (Mishra et al., 2021), makes
124   it slowly into the mainstream. Modern network architectures, e.g., ResNets of Transformers, pose
125   complex structural dependencies that must be captured for correct pruning (Fang et al., 2023).
126
      Sparsity in Deep Reinforcement Learning: Compared to computer vision (CV), exploring sparsity
127
      and pruning in DRL is a relatively recent effort. Livne & Cohen (2020) demonstrated that it is
128
      feasible to sparsify DRL agents during training without performance degradation. Various methods
129   that sparsify agents during training are discussed by Yu et al. (2020); Tan et al. (2023); Sokar et al.
130   (2021); Su et al. (2024). However, it was revealed by Graesser et al. (2022) that magnitude pruning
131   during training of DRL agent with a gradually increasing target sparsity outperforms such methods.
132
      Scaling in Deep Reinforcement Learning: The subsequent work of Obando-Ceron et al. (2024a)
133
      further investigates the effect of gradual unstructured pruning. When pruning a scaled Impala-CNN,
134
      they discover magnified performance in Atari for Q-network-based DRL. However, performance
135   stays mostly the same for soft actor-critic (SAC) and PPO with dense networks in Mujoco, assum-
136   ably because Mujoco is not image-based. It was shown that training can also be stabilized by using
137   mixtures of experts to scale the Dense layer of the Impala-CNN (Obando-Ceron et al., 2024b), or by
138   incorporating auxiliary tasks during training (Farebrother et al., 2023).
139
      Generalization in Deep Reinforcement Learning: Zhang et al. (2018) reveal that DRL agents can
140
      memorize a non-trivial number of training levels, even with completely random rewards. Similar
141   experiments in (Cobbe et al., 2019) quantify that the use of the same environment for both training
142   and testing results in high overfitting of DRL agents. They show that well-known techniques from
143   supervised learning, e.g., L2 regularization, batch normalization, and data augmentation, reduce
144   overfitting. However, only slightly better test performance is achieved when combining them than
145   using them individually. Overfitting in DRL may be associated with a loss of network plasticity
146   (Nikishin et al., 2022; Sokar et al., 2023). (Cobbe et al., 2020) introduces the Procgen Benchmark
147   with various procedurally generated environments to measure sample efficiency and generalization.

      Assume an initial DNN fθ with parameters θ = {w1 , w2 , . . . } of a parameter space H and weight
190
      tensors w ∈ RN ×M ×... . Let c(f ) be a counting function that counts the number of parameters in a
191   DNN. We then define an arbitrary pruning operation as a function
192
193                                p : fθ → fθ′ with c(fθ ) ≥ c(fθ′ ) and θ′ ⊆ θ;                             (1)

196   Importance Score: The selection of which parameters to prune is based on a score function that
197   estimates the importance of each parameter ι : H → R. This score defines the order of parameters
198   to be pruned, i.e., w1,1 will be pruned first when ι(w1,2 ) > ι(w1,1 ). Various criteria are discussed in
199   the literature (Cheng et al., 2024), e.g., weight magnitude, saliency, and Taylor expansions; random
200   weights selection often meets their performance (Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022).
201   Unstructured Pruning: Specific entries in w are set to zero, but the overall tensor shape is kept.
202   dim(w′ ) = dim(w). Inference time is not necessarily reduced.
203
204
      Structured Pruning: Full structures of the weight matrix w are removed, reducing the size of the
      weight matrix. For example, pruning of a single output channel of a Conv2D layer with a weight
205
      tensor shape2 of {48, 32, 3, 3} leads to a new tensor with shape {47, 32, 3, 3}.
206
207   Gradual Pruning: Gradual pruning in DRL (Graesser et al., 2022) involves progressively removing
208   parameters from the network throughout the training process by applying a series of pruning steps
209   pF (. . . (p2 (p1 (f )))). Typically, this process follows a predefined schedule with a target sparsity ζt,T
210
      at each pruning step t to define the number of parameters to be pruned. The pruning schedule begins
      after a warmup training phase at step tstart and concludes with the final pruning operation at step tend .
211
      The final target sparsity ζF represents the fraction of the remaining parameters. A commonly used
212
      gradual pruning schedule is a third-order polynomial with tstart and tend set to 20% and 80% of the
213   total training steps, respectively (Graesser et al., 2022; Obando-Ceron et al., 2024a).

      3.3   D EPENDENCY G RAPH
233
234   Weight tensors w in DNNs exhibit inter-layer dependencies, e.g., the output dimension of one layer
235   defines the input dimension of the next. Consequently, pruning parameters in one layer may neces-
236   sitate further pruning in the dependent layers. These group-structured parameters form a graph that
237   models the dependencies between network layers (Fang et al., 2023). While building such a depen-
238   dency graph for networks composed solely of linear layers is straightforward, modern architectures
239   with residual connections or attention layers introduce additional complexity, requiring automated
240   methods. Fang et al. (2023) propose DepGraph, a generic framework that uses graph traversal to
241   identify the dependency graph D and its dependencies. A parameter group g = {w1 , w2 , ·} is a
242
      subgraph of D and must be pruned simultaneously to maintain the network’s functional integrity.
243
244   4     M ETHODOLOGY
245
246   As outlined in Section 1, our preliminary experiments reveal that our proposed Impoola-CNN model
247   leads to an overall performance boost, but gains attributed to unstructured pruning vanish. Thus,
248   we introduce an approach using structured pruning instead with the motivation to reduce compute
249   time but without degrading performance. Our gradual group-structured pruning framework can be
250   plugged into existing DRL algorithms easily; we show this for PPO and DQN agents. Our method
251   accounts for dependencies of the Impoola/Impala-CNN encoders, which is crucial for performance.
252
253   4.1   I MPOOLA -CNN
254
255   In contrast to the Impala-CNN (Espeholt et al., 2018), the Impoola-CNN simply adds an Average-
256   Pooling layer after the last Con2D layer as listed in Table E.5. The overview in Appendix E shows
257
      that for the Impala model, 64.19 % of the weights are located in the encoder’s last Linear layer,
      while weights in the Impoola-CNN are equally distributed over the network with 10.1 % in the last
258
      layer. We speculate that this balanced distribution, specifically reducing the number of Linear layer
259
      weights, contributes to the significant performance improvements of the Impoola-CNN.

      Dependency Graph: We use a dependency graph (Fang et al., 2023) to correctly identify parameter
264   groups {g1 , g2 , . . . } in the DNN that should be pruned simultaneously. Figure 2 visualizes the need
265   to correctly account for dependency introduced by the residual connection in the Impala/Impoola-
266   CNN model. Opposed to our used group-structured pruning method, unstructured and naive struc-
267   tured pruning does not account for such dependencies, altering the nature of the DNN’s residual
268   connections. We define our pruning approach as the function p(g, N ) per group g with N as the
269   number of structures to be pruned. The pruning operation p process groups g = {w1 , w2 , . . . } and
      assigns importance scores ι(w1 , w2 , . . . ) for the common tensor dimension of the group.

      Group Importance Scoring: Following other works (Graesser et al., 2022; Obando-Ceron et al.,
271   2024a), we define the weight magnitude , i.e., L1-norm, as scoring function ι(w) = ||w||1 to be
272   used along the common tensor dimension independently for each group. The final importance score
273   of each structure in g is obtained by normalizing ι(w) first for each structure’s weight tensor. Next,
274   we take the mean of these local scores in group g as a reduction function to obtain the final scores,
275   which creates an aggregated score vector for the group’s common tensor dimension. This reduction
276   means that our method takes a neuron’s intra-layer dependencies into account for its score.
277   Gradual Pruning Schedule: We utilize the same third-order polynomial pruning scheme with
278   tstart = 20% and tend = 80% as done in other works (Graesser et al., 2022; Obando-Ceron et al.,
279   2024a). At each time step during training, the current sparsity of all layers li is measured as ζli . The
280   number of structures to be pruned is then calculated as

      4.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
292   Deep Reinforcement Learning Agents: We use PPO and DQN agents in this work. Our im-
293   plementations are derived from CleanRL (Huang et al., 2022) for PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2017).
294   Hyperparameters are listed in Appendix D. The used DQN agent is extended by double q-learning
295   (Van Hasselt et al., 2016), multi-step rewards (Sutton, 1988), and prioritized replay buffer (Schaul
296   et al., 2015). We use the framework from Fang et al. (2023) to derive the dependency graph D,
297   allowing us to deploy the correct structured pruning of inter-layer dependencies. The unstructured
298   and naive structured pruning methods use weight masks from PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2017).
299   Network Architecture: We deploy the Impala/Impoola-CNN encoders with an output feature di-
300   mension of 256 in all experiments. We set τ = 3 for all experiments unless otherwise specified,
301   as suggested by other works (Obando-Ceron et al., 2024a). The CNN encoder is shared between
302   the actor and critic for PPO. Given an image input of 64x64 pixels, the Impala and Impoola-CNN
303   consists of 2,450,640 and 976,080 trainable parameters for PPO, respectively.
304   Regularization: Our Impoola-CNN model uses a weight decay of 1e−5 . Linear learning rate an-
305   nealing rate is used for the PPO agent, which greatly improves performance. It was shown by Li
306   et al. (2019) that as learning rate annealing may allow for higher initial learning rates, generalization
307   can be improved. We provide an ablation study on this in Section 5.4. No learning rate schedule is
308   used for DQN as this reduced performance in environments with sparser reward; see Appendix B.2.

      5     E XPERIMENTS
311
312
      Unless otherwise stated, the results presented are based on the Impoola-CNN model, as we showed
313   its superiority against the Impala-CNN. The experiments are conducted for a subset of four environ-
314   ments for the Procgen Benchmark (Cobbe et al., 2020); see Appendix A for their description. Our
315   evaluation strongly focuses on measuring the generalization of DRL agents, for which Atari games
316   are unsuitable. To keep compute requirements reasonable, results are based on the easy game setting
317   with the configuration as recommended by Cobbe et al. (2020). The presented scores are median
318   results and 95-% confidence intervals, using 5 seeds for each environment per experiment and 2,500
319   evaluation episodes. We report collected returns as normalized scores S according to Equation A.1,
320   where 1.0 corresponds to an optimal policy and 0.0 is equivalent to a random one.
321   Evaluation Tracks: We provide an extensive evaluation by introducing the following tracks:
322

           5.2                 R ESULTS FOR DQN
414
415       We provide another study for DQN. As shown in Figure 4, we observe a similar trend to PPO,
416       where the Dense Impoola-CNN constitutes the consistent performance across environments. No-
417       tably, group-structured pruning is slightly outperformed by unstructured pruning. This may be at-
418       tributed to the higher frequency of gradient updates in DQN, which favors the smoother pruning
419       schedule of unstructured pruning, where single weights are removed incrementally. In contrast,
420       group-structured pruning involves fewer but larger pruning steps, resulting in a more step-like prun-
421
          ing scheme. Further analysis of Figure 4 reveals that the pruning methods underperform primarily
          in the Dodgeball and Bossfight environments. In the Bossfight environment, the DQN agent appears
422
          not to have learned a strong policy by the time pruning starts at 5M steps. We conclude that initiating
423
          pruning when the Dense agent’s performance is still unstable can induce training instability, leading
424       to further deterioration in performance. Appendix C contains the results on training levels for DQN.

      5.4      A BLATIONS
468
469   Learning Rate Annealing: We examine the influence of linear learning rate annealing on the perfor-
470   mance of PPO agents. Li et al. (2019) indicate that learning rate annealing improves generalization
471   performance. However, this could interfere with pruning, as the network may adapt more slowly af-
472   ter pruning when using a lower learning rate. As shown in Figure 5, learning rate annealing plays a
473   crucial role in generalization also for pruning methods with PPO. Notably, its use appears to reduce
474   performance variance across the pruning methods.
475   Pruning Ratio and Width Reduction: Figure 6 visualizes performance for different target pruning
476   rates ζF . With a high target ratio ζF = 0.9, both unstructured and group-structured pruning result
477   in reduced performance. Group-structured pruning seems to be more sensitive to high pruning rates,
478   positioning ζF = 0.8 as a favorable compromise between maintaining performance and reducing
479   computation time. As presented in Figure 7, reducing the width scale to τ = 1 decreases overall
480   performance, but group-structured pruning seems to be more sensitive than unstructured pruning,
481   i.e., structured grouping appears to require a minimum amount of filters to work well.
482   Importance Score: We use the L1 -norm as importance score function ι to allow for easier compar-
483   ison with other works (Obando-Ceron et al., 2024a). We ablate using instead L2 -norm, a first-order
484   Taylor expansion of the loss (Molchanov et al., 2019), and random scoring in Figure 8. It can be
485   seen that the Taylor-based score function may improve performance slightly. However, the overall
      influence of the importance score seems minor, as even random selection yields good performance.

521   6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
522
523   Following preliminary experiments, this work introduces the scaled Impoola-CNN encoder, which
524   significantly boosts image-based DRL performance compared to the widely used Impala-CNN. We
525
      present a group-structured pruning framework for the Impoola-CNN that unlocks performance gains
      through scaling while maintaining computational efficiency. Our results on the Procgen Benchmark
526
      for PPO and DQN show that this pruning method for image-based DRL maintains performance
527
      comparable to that of networks with unstructured pruning, even outperforming dense DNNs for
528   generalization in long-term training. Moreover, we show that while pruned networks do not adapt to
529   fine-tuning levels as high as the dense Impoola-CNN, pruned DNNs show strong noise robustness
530   instead. Additional results for Atari games demonstrate the broad applicability of our approach.
531   A final analysis highlights the efficiency of our group-structured pruning method, with significant
532   reductions in computing time, eventually making the case for group-structured pruning as the pre-
533   ferred approach for real-world image-based DRL applications with scaled networks.
534   For future work, evaluation for real-world image-based DRL applications could provide valuable
535   insights, particularly given the demonstrated noise robustness of group-structured pruning. The fact
536   that pruning methods seem to not outperform the Dense networks for the Impoola-CNN requires
537   further analysis but can be related to the finding of (Cobbe et al., 2019) that if some regularization is
538   already applied, the combination with further regularization technique does not necessarily improve
539   performance further. Additionally, we see potential for exploring adaptive gradual pruning schedules
      and the use of global pruning methods.

      A    P ROC G EN E NVIRONMENTS
713
714
      The ProcGen environments were developed by Cobbe et al. (2020) to test sample efficiency and
715
      generalization of DRL agents. Unless otherwise stated, our results are based on the easy setting.
716   For the generalization track, 200 levels are used for training, while all procedurally generated levels
717   are used for evaluation. Our experiments have shown that the initial set of 200 levels can influence
718   the agent’s performance. Thus, we fix the level generation to the first 200 levels for all experiments
719   but always report results for independent runs with different seeds for the training. When the hard
720   setting is used, 1000 training levels are used. The action space of the ProcGen environments consists
721   of 15 discrete actions. Observations are RGB images with 3x64x64 pixels. No stacking of images
722   is required, as we utilize the environments without the setting that requires memory.
723   Game Selection: We chose Bigfish, Starpilot, Dodgeball, and Bossfight as environments for our
724   main evaluation using the easy setting. They constitute a set of various game dynamics. While
725   high performance in Bigfish and Starpilot is commonly achieved, Dodgeball and Bossfight are more
726   challenging. Especially agents trained for Bossfight experience limited reward signals early in train-
727   ing. We provide an additional experiment that uses Coinrun and the hard setting; the normalization
728   constants remain the same.
