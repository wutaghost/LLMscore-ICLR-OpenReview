009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Watermarking techniques have emerged as pivotal safeguards to defend the intel-
012            lectual property of deep neural networks against model extraction attacks. Most
013            existing watermarking methods rely on the identification of samples within ran-
014            domly selected trigger sets. However, this paradigm is inevitably disrupted by the
015            ambiguous points that exhibit poor discriminability, thus leading to the misiden-
016            tification between benign and stolen models. To tackle this issue, in this paper,
017            we propose a boundary-based watermarking method that enhances the discerni-
018
               bility of trigger set, further improving the ability in distinguish benign and stolen
               models. Specifically, we select trigger samples on the decision boundary of base
019
               model and assigned them labels with the least probabilities, while providing a
020
               tight bound based on the Lovász Local Lemma. This approach ensures the wa-
021            termark’s reliability in identifying stolen models by improving discriminability
022            of trigger samples. Meanwhile, we provide theoretical proof to demonstrate that
023            the watermark can be effectively guaranteed under the constraints guided by the
024            Lovász Local Lemma. Experimental results demonstrate that our method outper-
025            forms the state-of-the-art watermarking methods on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 and
026            ImageNet datasets. Code and data will be released publicly upon the paper accep-
027            tance.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep learning methods have demonstrated remarkable success in innumerable industrial contexts,
033
      such as computer vision (He et al., 2016; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and natural language process-
      ing (Brown et al., 2020; Vaswani et al., 2017). However, deep neural networks (DNNs) entail
034
      substantial costs, primarily due to two critical factors: the heavy reliance on extensive training
035
      data (Halevy et al., 2009) and substantial computational resources (Floridi & Chiriatti, 2020). Train-
036   ing and deploying high-performing DNNs is both time-consuming and resource-intensive. Conse-
037   quently, it is crucial to protect the intellectual property of model providers, especially when users are
038   permitted to query and access the outputs of these valuable models deployed on the cloud platforms.
039
      While model providers can protect their intellectual property by keeping model parameters confi-
040
      dential, they are obligated to provide APIs for user access as service providers. This exposes the
041
      model to potential threats from adversaries who can extract the functionality of DNN models using
042   black-box model extraction attacks (Tramèr et al., 2016; Orekondy et al., 2019). In this scenario, ad-
043   versaries engage in extensive querying of the victim model to acquire labels for a surrogate dataset,
044   which is then utilized to train their replica model, effortlessly creating a stolen copy of the original
045   model (Pal et al., 2019; Papernot et al., 2017). Since adversaries’ inputs are common and similar
046   to those of benign users, model providers struggle to differentiate between them. As a result, all
047   outputs are exposed to adversaries, making it challenging to prevent model extraction attacks solely
048   through the API interaction process.
049   To tackle model extraction attacks, researchers have introduced model watermarking tech-
050   niques (Kahng et al., 1998). Early works followed the parameter-embedding watermarking
051   paradigm (Uchida et al., 2017; Darvish Rouhani et al., 2019; Kuribayashi et al., 2021; Mehta et al.,
052   2022), where the watermark is embedded into the model parameters. However, these approaches
053   are limited to white-box scenarios, and it is impossible to verify the watermark if suspicious models
      disclose their parameters. To this end, recent studies have shifted their focus to backdoor-based

      watermarking techniques (Adi et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019; Namba & Sakuma,
055   2019; Zhang et al., 2020a;b; Chen et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Maini et al., 2021;
056   Li et al., 2022; Bansal et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Pautov et al.,

058   fenders select specific input-output pairs (x̃, ỹ) as the trigger set and train the model to overfit to this
059   trigger set. To claim ownership of the model, defenders query the suspicious model with these spe-
060   cific inputs x̃ and verify whether the returned results match the predefined set ỹ. By using different
061   backdoor algorithms, these studies successfully improve the ability of stolen models to recognize
062   trigger samples. Moreover, several certifiable watermarking techniques (Bansal et al., 2022; Jiang
063
      et al., 2023; 2024) provide theoretical guarantees. These methods perturb the model parameters to
      increase robustness, and the watermark is guaranteed to be effective unless adversaries change the
064
      model parameters beyond a certain ℓ2 distance.
065
066   However, previous works predominantly rely on “random” configurations for trigger sets: they either
067   randomly sample instances (Kim et al., 2023), assign random labels to triggers (Zhang et al., 2018;
068   Yu et al., 2023), or combine both strategies (Zhang et al., 2018; Jia et al., 2021; Bansal et al., 2022;
069
      Li et al., 2022). This approach may lead to the selection of low-quality points and makes it difficult
      to accurately distinguish between benign models and stolen models. Specifically, randomly chosen
070
      samples that cluster around central points, a common occurrence in dense clusters, can adversely
071
      impact neighboring samples and consequently deteriorate the model’s performance. When the ran-
072   domly selected label aligns with the output of the benign models, it presents a hurdle in identifying
073   a stolen model. Additionally, in terms of theoretical guarantees, existing random smoothing-based
074   methods (Bansal et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023; 2024) impose constraints on all model parameters,
075   introducing calculations involving a multitude of extraneous parameters, consequently diminishing
076   the theoretical boundaries that can be assured. Furthermore, these assurances are tailored to random
077   smoothing techniques and do not offer a universal guarantee for alternative methodologies.
078   To address the above issues, we propose a boundary-based watermarking method that meticulously
079   selects boundary samples and their corresponding labels. Specifically, we dissect the identification
080   of whether a suspicious model is benign or stolen into two pivotal components: the discriminability
081   of the trigger set and the probability of (x̃, ỹ) in distinguishing between benign and stolen models.
082   Correspondingly, we devise a trigger set selection strategy based on this analysis to enhance both
083   components simultaneously. This strategy comprises two processes: Initially, we train a base model
084   and select samples near the decision boundary as trigger set. Subsequently, we perturb the base
085   model to simulate multiple independently trained models, then query these perturbed models and
086
      select the least frequently occurring label for each trigger sample. This process ensures that the
      resulting trigger set can tolerantly identify benign and stolen models. Furthermore, we provide a
087
      tighter bound by constraining watermark-related parameters guided by the Lovász Local Lemma.
088
      Under such constraints, the effectiveness of the watermark can be guaranteed by incorporating mul-
089   tiple watermarking pairs.
090

104   2.1   WATERMARKS
105
106   Watermarking techniques aim to protect intellectual property against the theft of elaborate mod-
107   els (Kahng et al., 1998). Traditional deep learning based methods embedded the watermarks into
      the parameters (Uchida et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2019; Darvish Rouhani et al., 2019), necessitating


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      parameter access for watermark verification. However, given that the owners of suspicious mod-
109   els may not grant access to the parameters, hindering the use of white-box methods for ownership
110   identification, black-box scenarios emerge as a more desirable approach.
111
112
      Due to the remarkable performance in ownership verification of stolen models, recent works fo-
      cusing on backdoor-based watermarking have drawn much attention. These methods operate by
113
      training the model to fit outlier input-output pairs exclusively known to the defender, which can
114
      subsequently be utilized to assert ownership of the model. The backdoor-based work can be roughly
115   divided into two categories. One paradigm involves selecting random trigger sets and introducing
116   novel loss functions to enhance the robustness of classification on trigger samples, such as expand-
117   ing the trigger’s neighborhood (Kim et al., 2023) and entangling normal data with trigger data (Jia
118   et al., 2021). The other approaches focus on the selection of meticulously chosen triggers, primarily
119   derived from out-of-distribution (OOD) data (Zhang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023).
120   Furthermore, MAT (Li et al., 2024) selects trigger sets from the decision boundary and assigns the
121   second probable class as classification labels. However, this selection approach exhibits a low toler-
122   ance for classification errors. Specifically, under various training settings (e.g. random initialization
123
      or different optimization methods), the models can easily misclassify these samples as the category
      that should originally have the second highest confidence, thus may be erroneously classified as
124
      stolen models. Conversely, our method improves distinguishability of trigger set by selecting sam-
125
      ples from the decision boundary and assigning labels that rarely appear in the predictions of benign
126   models. When a suspicious model exposes a small number of watermarked input-output pairs, the
127   defender can confidently assert ownership of this model.
128
129
      In terms of theoretical proofs, various watermarking methods have been proposed to prove their cer-
      tificates, with the majority relying on random smoothing technique (Goldberger et al., 2020; Bansal
130
      et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023; 2024). Nevertheless, these methods necessitate the adherence of the
131
      stolen model’s parameters to a fixed ℓ2 threshold, which can be easily circumvented by adversaries
132   through diverse tricks such as selecting random seeds, adapting optimizers, or even fine-tuning hy-
133   perparameters. In this paper, we alleviate this restriction by adapting Lovász Local Lemma into the
134   selection of trigger sets. And our watermark is guaranteed to be unremovable when the watermark-
135   related neurons satisfy the conditions of the Lovász Local Lemma and the size of the trigger set is
136   not small.

      2.2   M ODEL E XTRACTION ATTACK
140
141
142   Model extraction attack (Tramèr et al., 2016), as a functionality stealing method (Orekondy et al.,
143   2019), is currently considered the most powerful black-box attack (Lukas et al., 2022). In these
144
      attacks, adversaries typically start by collecting or synthesizing an initially unlabeled surrogate
      dataset. For example, Papernot et al. (2017) utilized Jacobian-based dataset augmentation to in-
145
      crease the potency of attacks. Tramèr et al. (2016) proposed three techniques for uniform data
146
      sampling. They assign labels for these samples by querying the original model and then use this
147   surrogate dataset to train a stolen copy of the victim model.
148
149   Watermarking techniques designed to combat such attacks intertwine the training of both the dataset
150
      and the trigger set, facilitating the surrogate model in comprehending the watermarked decision
      boundary. However, the presence of watermarked decision boundaries may impact the model’s per-
151
      formance, leading to a trade-off in all approaches between the model’s practical utility for legitimate
152
      users and the success of watermark transfer (Alabdulmohsin et al., 2014; Shukla, 2020; Lee et al.,
153   2019; Tramèr et al., 2016). Our approach mitigates this dilemma while ensuring the effective water-
154   marking by meticulously selecting boundary samples and assigning them permissive labels.

531
532
      In this paper, we present a boundary-based watermarking method designed to defend against model
533
      extraction attacks by carefully selecting boundary samples and assigning permissive labels. Our
534   approach dissects the identification of stolen models into two key components: trigger set discrim-
535   inability and the probability of distinguishing benign and stolen models. Accordingly, we propose
536   a trigger sample selection strategy to enhance both aspects. Furthermore, we establish a tight theo-
537   retical bound on watermarking models by constraining parameters using the Lovász Local Lemma,
538   ensuring reliable watermark detection in stolen models through the incorporation of multiple pairs
539   of watermarks. Extensive experiments on three public datasets demonstrate the superiority of our
      method on ownership verification of stolen models.
