009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Identifying critical nodes in networks is a classical combinatorial optimization task,
012            and many methods struggle to strike a balance between adaptability and utility.
013            Therefore, we propose an approach that empowers Evolutionary Algorithm (EA)
014            with Large Language Models (LLMs), to generate a function called "score_nodes"
015            which can further be used to identify crucial nodes based on their assigned scores.
016            Our model consists of three main components: Manual Initialization, Population
017            Management, and LLMs-based Evolution, and it evolves from initial populations
018
               with a set of designed node scoring functions created manually. LLMs leverage
               their strong contextual understanding and rich programming techniques to perform
019
               crossover and mutation operations on the individuals, generating new functions.
020
               These functions are then categorized, ranked, and eliminated to ensure the stable
021            development of the populations while preserving diversity. Extensive experiments
022            demonstrate the excellent performance of our method compared to other state-of-
023            the-art algorithms. It can generate diverse and efficient node scoring functions to
024            identify critical nodes in the network.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Various networks widely exist in the real world, such as social networks, biological networks,
030   etc West et al. (2001). For the network, identifying an optimal set of critical nodes for which the
031   activation/removal has the maximized impact is a basic research problem with broad applications in
032   epidemic control, influence maximization, etc Lalou et al. (2018); Arulselvan et al. (2009). From the
033
      perspective of combinational optimization, this problem could be considered as a kind of optimal
      search task, in which the computational complexity increases exponentially along with the size of the
034
      network Taniguchi et al. (2006). Existing approaches to this problem can be categorized as follows:
035
      from heuristic methods to optimization-based methods, and finally, to learning-based methods.
036
037   The first kind of work is the basic methods based on heuristics, such as using degree centrality
038   Zhang and Luo (2017) to judge whether the node is critical or not, which is simple yet very effective
039
      and efficient, showing superior performance in many kinds of networks Arulselvan et al. (2009);
      Mao et al. (2023). The second kind of work further tries to re-formulate the problem with approx-
040
      imated optimization problems. Some other algorithms like CoreHD Zdeborová et al. (2016) and
041
      WeakNeighbors Schmidt et al. (2019) utilize k-core decomposition, in which the k-core of a network
042   is defined as the maximal subgraph with every node having a degree of at least k. These methods
043   progressively eliminate nodes with the highest degrees within their respective k-cores to identify key
044   nodes. Furthermore, Min-Sum Braunstein et al. (2016) focuses on creating an acyclic network by
045   removing nodes that form loops, followed by utilizing greedy tree-breaking algorithms to decompose
046   the remaining forest into smaller disconnected components. Another work, GND Ren et al. (2019),
047   adopts a spectral approach to iteratively partition the network into two components. These methods
048   have the advantage of fast computation, but they may lead to sub-optimal solutions, or even lack a
049   suitable solution altogether. Recently, with the advancement of deep learning, some works Zhang
050
      and Wang (2022); Grassia et al. (2021) approach the problem by using neural networks to learn the
      node importance or ranking nodes adaptively. That is, they utilize neural networks to assign scores to
051
      network nodes, determining their importance based on these scores.
052
053   Despite these advances, there still exists a key challenge of adaptability-utility dilemma, which
      largely affects the real application values of these methods. Specifically, the heuristic or optimization

      methods provide the same solution for all kinds of networks, but ignoring the different characteristics,
055   which leads to poor performance in those unpopular networks. The learning-based methods, on the
056   other hand, try to search the critical nodes adaptively, but are faced with high search costs in such a

      Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown a human-like ability in many tasks, from basic
059

064   operations, continuously iterating to generate new solutions.

      • We propose a general framework integrating LLMs and evolutional learning, and a generation-

        update mechanism for LLMs and the crossover-mutation method for evolutional learning.

076   2     P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
077
078   Given a network G = (V, E), where V represents the set of nodes {v1 , v2 , ..., v|V| } and E repre-
079   sents the set of edges {e1 , e2 , ..., e|E| }, our objective is to identify a sequence of critical nodes
080   (v1 , v2 , · · · , vN ), whose deletion makes the network disconnected Lalou et al. (2018). These critical
081   nodes will be obtained through a function f generated by our method, evaluating the importance of
082   the nodes for the network.
083
      For the sake of facilitating function processing, we represent the network G as an adjacency matrix
084
      A = {0, 1}|V|×|V| , where Ajk = 1 indicates that there is an edge between node vj and node vk ;
085
      otherwise, Ajk = 0. Many existing methods rely on a crucial parameter, the number of target nodes
086
      N , directly impacting the algorithm’s performance. To ensure the efficiency and generalization of the
087   function learned by our approach, our objective node scoring function f does not include the variable
088   N . Instead, it will take the network’s adjacency matrix A as input and produce scores for each node
089   S = {sv1 , sv2 , ..., sv|V| } as output. In other words, the formulation S = f (A) enables us to rank
090   nodes based on their scores and obtain any desired quantity of critical nodes.

165   related to our specific task: identifying critical nodes in the network through the learned function.
166   Therefore, we define the node scoring function’s fitness evaluation as follows:
167                                     E(f ) = ϕ (A, SORT (f (A)) [: L]) ,                                   (1)

169   where SORT represents a function that sorts nodes in descending order based on their scores, [: L]
170   indicates selecting the top L elements from the sorted list, and ϕ stands for a function that evaluates
171   the network connectivity after removing given nodes. Through the evaluation function above, each
172   executable node scoring function obtains a suitable fitness score cf = E(f ), which assists us in
173
      better-performing selection and managing the function populations.

      3.2.3   S ELECTION
200   We implemented two designs for selection of parent functions within the populations, namely,
201   intra-population selection and inter-population selection.
202
203   • Intra-population Selection: Considering the diversity of functions, our model selects one individ-
204     ual from each population to serve as intra-population parent functions Fintra in each selection. At
205
        the same time, to maintain elite individuals, the highest-fitness function is also added to Fintra . Then,
        pairs of functions within Fintra are combined for subsequent crossover operations. Intra-population
206
        selection can integrate different types of functions to assist LLMs discover the characteristics of
207
        node scoring of given parent functions, thereby generating new offspring functions.
208
209
      • Inter-population Selection: Taking into account the fitness of functions, two functions are chosen
        from each same population to serve as inter-population parent functions Finter in each selection,
210
        allowing subsequent crossover operations to discover differences between functions of the same
211
        population but with different fitness scores. In other words, inter-population selection aids LLMs
212     in considering which programming steps lead to different fitnesses of two functions from the same
213     population, thereby focusing more on these key steps to enhance function performance.
214
215   In both selection methods, the fitness scores of each function serve as selection weights, encouraging
      the selection of more excellent individuals. For convenience, we use Fparent to represent Fintra ∪ Finter .

442
443   5.1     S YNERGY BETWEEN LLM S AND EA S
444
445   Recent research underscores the effectiveness of leveraging LLMs for optimization tasks via prompt
446   engineering Yang et al. (2023); Guo et al. (2023a). This method proves particularly advantageous
447
      for EAs, attributed to the advanced text comprehension and generation capabilities of LLMs Chao
      et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2024). For example, recent investigations into discrete prompt optimization
448
      have shown how LLMs can mimic evolutionary operators, generating innovative candidate prompts
449
      through crossover and mutation based on fixed prompts Guo et al. (2023b); Li and Wu (2023).
450   Furthermore, studies have explored the use of LLMs in tackling combinatorial optimization problems
451   (COPs) by developing evolved heuristic solutions Pluhacek et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023; 2024);
452   Ye et al. (2024). EvoPrompting Chen et al. (2023) utilizes LLMs for Neural Architecture Search
453   (NAS), performing crossover and mutation at the code level to discover new architectures. Likewise,
454   FunSearch Romera-Paredes et al. (2023) implements an LLM-powered evolutionary method to
455   determine solution functions for mathematical challenges. In addition, Eureka Ma et al. (2023)
456   focuses on the design of reinforcement learning reward functions using LLM-based evolutionary
457   algorithms, while Fuzz4All Xia et al. (2024) applies LLMs in fuzz testing to generate and mutate
458
      inputs.
459   Distinct from previous studies, our research emphasizes the use of LLMs that perform mutation and
460   crossover processes on node scoring functions, aiming to identify critical nodes in complex networks.
461
462
      5.2     C RITICAL N ODES I DENTIFICATION
463
464
      Identifying critical nodes is of significant importance across various practical domains. Basic methods
465   such as degree centrality entail removing nodes with the highest degrees. Algorithms like CoreHD
466   Zdeborová et al. (2016) and WeakNeighbors Schmidt et al. (2019) utilize k-core decomposition, in
467   which the k-core of a network is defined as the maximal subgraph with every node having a degree
468   of at least k. These methods progressively eliminate nodes with the highest degrees within their
469   respective k-cores to identify key nodes. On the other hand, Min-Sum Braunstein et al. (2016) focuses
470   on creating an acyclic network by removing nodes that form loops, followed by utilizing greedy
471   tree-breaking algorithms to decompose the remaining forest into smaller disconnected components.
472   Additionally, GND Ren et al. (2019) adopts a spectral approach to iteratively partition the network
473
      into two components. Learning-based methods like NIRM Zhang and Wang (2022) and GDM Grassia
      et al. (2021) utilize neural networks to assign scores to network nodes, determining their importance
474
      based on these scores.
475
476   Our research is not limited to identifying specific algorithms. Rather, it leverages LLMs-empowered
477   EAs to investigate a broad spectrum of potential algorithms in all kinds of networks.
478

482   In this study, we employ an evolutionary algorithm enhanced by LLMs for generating node scoring
483   functions, to identify critical nodes within networks. Experimental results across various network
484   datasets reveal that our method effectively balances adaptability and utility in comparison to other
485   state-of-the-art algorithms. Moreover, it is capable of generating a variety of both reasonable and
      efficient node scoring functions for identifying critical nodes.
