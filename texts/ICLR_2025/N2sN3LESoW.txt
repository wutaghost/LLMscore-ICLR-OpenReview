009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) approaches are widely
012            used for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) to align with instructional
013            preferences. However, traditional RLHF methods often rely on binary labels,
014            which fail to capture the pairwise differences in human perception, leading to
015            potential performance degradation. To address this limitation, we introduce Gap-
016            Aware Preference Optimization (GaPO), a novel approach that integrates the
017            degree of semantic gaps into preference optimization. By modifying the exist-
018            ing margin term in the loss function and replacing it with an estimated gap com-
019
               puted using general metrics, GaPO provides a new supervisory signal that explic-
               itly highlights the nuances between preference pairs. This new signal helps the
020
               model allocate gradients more rationally during optimization, facilitating more
021
               effective learning from the preference data. Experiments conducted with a strong
022            base model, Llama-3-8B-Instruct, demonstrate that GaPO surpasses State-of-the-
023            Art methods on widely used benchmarks. Our best-performing model, GaPO-
024            ROUGE L, achieves a win rate of 52.8% on AlpacaEval 2.0, exceeding the base-
025            line methods by 5.3 points.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF, Christiano et al. (2017); Ouyang et al.
031   (2022)) has proven to be an effective and promising method for fine-tuning large-scale language
032   models (LLMs, Achiam et al. (2023); Bai et al. (2023); Dubey et al. (2024)), aligning their gen-
033   erative preferences with human-established standards. This alignment extends beyond content ac-
034   curacy to encompass attributes such as helpfulness, harmlessness, and logical coherence, which
035   are fundamental to human linguistic norms (Wang et al., 2024a). Furthermore, RLHF enables the
036   customization of generative behaviors to meet the specific demands of particular tasks. Notably,
037   RLHF-optimized models with fewer parameters can achieve generation quality that is comparable
038
      to, or even rivals, that of larger models. This significantly enhances the practical utility of smaller
      models in real-world applications.
039
040   The RLHF dataset consists of winning and losing response pairs, assigned binary labels of 1 and 0,
041   respectively. This binary labeling scheme does not reflect the nuanced quality differences between
042   the preference pairs. During training, this results in uniform treatment of all data pairs, potentially
043   causing a sub-optimal optimization trajectory. Specifically, the model might disproportionately fo-
044
      cus on complex examples at the expense of adequately learning from simpler, yet informative data
      points, thus undermining its general fitting performance.
045
046   Alternatively, the model might expand its search space excessively to accommodate challenging
047   cases, resulting in a potential decrease in the log probabilities of positive examples and creating
048   unmanageable contamination of the base model.
049   From a human perspective, some winning cases may only be marginally better than losing cases,
050   while others can be substantially superior. Therefore, during optimization, it is essential to allocate
051   more gradient updates to the latter. Although the reward functions in previous Preference Optimiza-
052   tion approaches assess the overall sentence generation probability by cumulatively summing the
053   generation probabilities of each token, they still fail to fully consider the sentence as a whole and do
      not effectively compute the margin at the sentence level.

069   We first seek to quantify and simulate the perceived differences between pairs of training data with
070   minimal complexity. To achieve this, we tested various traditional machine translation evalua-
071
      tion metrics, such as Jaccard Score (Costa, 2021), ROUGE (Lin (2004); Lin & Och (2004)), and
      BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019).
072
073   Secondly, to incorporate these degree metrics into the training phase and reduce the model’s fitting
074   difficulty, we propose a straightforward transformation of these metrics into a ”gap” score, which
075   represents the reward difference between winning and losing examples in the loss function. Empiri-
076   cally, we experimented with several mappings to ensure compatibility with the reward space.
077   From another perspective, our novel loss function can also be viewed as a replacement for the reward
078   function in DPO (See Figure 1). In this framework, the reward function for winning examples is as-
079   signed a denominator of 1, while the denominator for losing examples is dynamically determined by
080   our evaluation factor (EF ). This approach not only circumvents potential conflicts between reward
081   optimization and the actual log probability optimization objective, but also alleviates inconsistencies
082   in the gaps between training pairs.
083   In conclusion, our contributions are highlighted as follows:
084
085          • Introduction of GaPO: We propose GaPO, a novel method that introduces the concept of
086            human preference intensity to provide additional preference information. This approach not
087            only aligns with human preferences but also ensures that the model reflects the strength of
088            these preferences. Consequently, it enhances the model’s ability to accurately capture and
089            reflect the subtleties of human preference intensity. Additionally, this approach ensures that
090            the log probability of generating a good response does not experience a significant decrease
091            during the training phase.
092          • Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods: We compare our GaPO method with state-
093            of-the-art approaches, including DPO and SimPO. Our results demonstrate that the GaPO
094            loss function effectively utilizes pairwise gap instruction signals to achieve superior perfor-
095            mance in downstream tasks.
096
             • Explore Different Gap forms: We empirically evaluate the performance of GaPO by ex-
097
               perimenting with different EF function forms and normalization techniques. Our model
098            trained by ROUGE L as estimated gap values achieves 52.8% win rate on the AlpacaEval
099            2.0 test set.

131   Recent research has explored other methods aimed at directly optimizing LLMs strategies based
132   on human preferences, without relying on a scalar reward signal (Ouyang et al., 2022). The goal
133   of these methods is to simplify the alignment process, reduce computational overhead, and achieve
134   more robust optimization by directly utilizing preference data. The most well-known approach is
135
      DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024), which employs a method called the Bradley-Terry model. It directly
      optimizes preference data pairs by leveraging an analytical mapping from the reward function to
136
      the optimal policy. However, DPO is highly sensitive to the parameter beta, making it prone to
137
      overfit to preference data (Feng et al., 2024). This may reduce the probability of generating good
138   responses, leading to sub-optimal training outcomes. Additionally, the use of a reference model
139   causes a inconsistency between the win-loss reward ranking in the training objective and the model
140   win-loss output ranking.
141
      Currently, many works have focused on optimization from different perspectives, which can be
142
      mainly divided into two directions:
143
144   Retain the Reference Model. β-DPO (Wu et al., 2024) concentrates on data quality and trains
145   using batch-level dynamic β adjustments. R-DPO (Park et al., 2024) and DPOP (Pal et al., 2024)
146   both introduce new normalization terms, represented by the difference in sentence length and the
147
      difference in generation probability from the model of optimization and reference, respectively.
      RSO (Liu et al., 2023) computes gradients only for data pairs where there is a discrepancy between
148
      the model-generated objectives and human preferences, and it employs a rejection sampling method
149
      to acquire preference data. WPO (Zhou et al., 2024) adjusts the weights of data pairs based on the
150   preference output information provided by the current model policy. IPO (Azar et al., 2024) and
151   KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) use KL divergence to guide model updates and IPO ensures that the
152   KL regularisation remains effective.
153
      Remove the Reference Model. RRHF (Yuan et al., 2023) trains by using rank loss robustly without
154
      complex hyperparameter tuning. SLiC-HF (Zhao et al., 2023) calculates contrastive loss through
155
      Sequence Likelihood Calibration and introduces a regularization term to increase the margin dis-
156   tance. ORPO (Hong et al., 2024) propose a reference-free loss function that enables simultaneous
157   supervised fine-tuning and preference alignment within a single training session. CPO (Xu et al.,
158   2024) directly uses log likelihood as a reward function and the SFT objective as a regulation. SimPO
159   (Meng et al., 2024) introduces a target reward margin γ, which helps to separate winning and losing
160   responses. Additionally, most methods incorporate SFT loss to ensure the probability of generating
161   high-quality responses, and they all use sequence length as a normalization factor. On a higher level,
      GPO (Tang et al., 2024) summarizes preference optimization from a unified perspective.

190   Direct preference optimization (DPO, Rafailov et al. (2024)) is currently one of the most widely
191

192

193

      While SimPO effectively establishes a discrepancy between reward and generation for preference
217   optimization, it still potentially sacrifices pairwise optimization by employing a fixed margin γ com-
218   pared to DPO. Some training pairs exhibit a clear distinction from a human perception perspective,

223   3.2   G A PO O BJECTIVE
224
225   Accurate preference training in models necessitates a nuanced understanding of human perception.
226   To address this issue, it is crucial to develop methods for computing and stimulating the degrees of
227
      human perception. By quantifying perception, we can introduce a spectrum of preference intensities
      that provide additional layers of information beyond binary labels. This enriched data allows models
228
      to differentiate between varying degrees of preference quality, leading to a more refined and accurate
229
      optimization process.

398   4.2   E XPERIMENT R ESULTS AND A BLATION
399
400   GaPO significantly improves performance on AlpacaEval 2.0. As shown in Table 3.2, our GaPO
401   method achieves the best performance on the AlpacaEval 2.0 evaluation dataset. Specifically, GaPO
402   attains a notable win rate (WR) of 52.8% and a length-controlled win rate (LC) of 56.1%, out-
403   performing the best baseline, SimPO, by 5.3 and 2.4 percentage points, respectively. In the more
404
      challenging benchmark Arena-Hard, our GaPO method performs comparably to the baseline SimPO
      in terms of win rate. This suggests that GaPO does not demonstrate an enhancement over SimPO
405
      in addressing complex problems, potentially owing to constraints in the quality of the dataset and
406
      the capacity of the model. In the MT-Bench benchmark, our GaPO method attains a score of 7.1,
407   marginally outperforming the SimPO score of 7.0. However, the MT-Bench benchmark demon-
408   strates limited discriminatory capacity when assessing diverse responses across the three datasets.
409   This limitation could stem from the assessment model’s dependence on single-score evaluations,
410   which tend to be less sensitive at discerning fine-grained distinctions than pairwise comparison
411   methods.
412
      Different EF metrics show different improvements. We explored various functions to compute
413   the evaluation factor (EF) for assessing human perception of the gap between pairs, including Jac-

      4.3   Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS
433
434   In preference optimization, our goal is to distinguish between preference pair rewards while main-
435   taining a high log probability for the winning response. This approach helps prevent significant
436   alterations compared to the base model during fine-tuning, which could result in unexpected out-
437   comes during inference. Often, a decline in log probability occurs because, during the fine-tuning
438   process, the model explores a larger search space to optimize the reward difference between the
439   winning response and the losing response, aiming to meet the preset gap in the loss function. The
440
      goal of GaPO is to optimize this preset gap for each pair of training data, fitting the data better and
      achieving higher win rates in downstream tasks without substantial adjustments to the base model
441
      parameters. In the figure 1, we observe that the GaPO method achieves better performance in down-
442
      stream tasks compared to SimPO, while maintaining a log probability similar to or even higher than
443   that of SimPO, highlighting the superiority of the GaPO method.

      5     D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
447
      Conclusion. In this work, we propose a method called GaPO, which introduces semantic gap infor-
448
      mation into the loss function. This enables the model not only to differentiate between good and bad
449
      responses but also to develop a more nuanced understanding of the degree of quality at the semantic
450   level. This improvement aids in optimizing the gradient update process, thereby enhancing the ef-
451   fectiveness of RLHF. By incorporating sentence-level gap information, the model is able to reduce
452   the log probability for the chosen response to a lesser degree while achieving a higher win rate.
453   Additionally, our GaPO approach is designed to be interoperable with all margin-based preference
454   optimization techniques to further improve performance.
455
      Future work. Firstly, the test datasets used in this study are all derived from the question-answering
456   (QA) domain. Given the widespread application of AI agents and the increasing demand for such
457   technologies, future work will explore how the GAPO method can be applied within AI agent con-
458   texts, particularly in scenarios where gaps in API calls are easier to quantify. Secondly, the cur-
459   rent semantic gap primarily relies on machine translation evaluation metrics such as ROUGE and
460   BERTScore. Future research will explore more appropriate evaluation functions and consider using
461   the reward model employed during DPO dataset generation to replace the existing semantic gap
462   calculation method. Lastly, drawing from the inference generation strategy of OpenAI-o1, we plan
463   to use Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in future work to generate datasets, which will be com-
464
      bined with GAPO training to further optimize model performance and its ability to capture human
      preferences.
