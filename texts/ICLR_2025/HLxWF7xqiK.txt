011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We address the challenging problem of dynamically pricing complementary items
014            that are sequentially displayed to customers. An illustrative example is the online
015            sale of flight tickets, where customers navigate through multiple web pages. Ini-
016            tially, they view the ticket cost, followed by ancillary expenses such as insurance
017            and additional luggage fees. Coherent pricing policies for complementary items
018
               are essential because optimizing the pricing of each item individually is ineffec-
               tive. Our scenario also involves a sales constraint, which specifies a minimum
019
               number of items to sell, and uncertainty regarding customer demand curves. To
020
               tackle this problem, we originally formulate it as a Markov decision process with
021            constraints. Leveraging online learning tools, we design a primal-dual online op-
022            timization algorithm. We empirically evaluate our approach using synthetic set-
023            tings randomly generated from real-world data, covering various configurations
024            from stationary to non-stationary, and compare its performance in terms of con-
025            straints violation and regret against well-known baselines optimizing each state
026            singularly.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Dynamic Pricing (DP) aims to determine the ideal pricing for a product or service in real-time
032   employing revenue optimization strategies (Rothschild, 1974; Kleinberg & Leighton, 2003; Trovò
033   et al., 2018). This practice is widely prevalent in various sectors, including airlines, ride-sharing,
034   and retail, owing to its capability to adapt to variables such as demand, competition, and time con-
035   straints. Undoubtedly, dynamic pricing is garnering considerable attention from both the industry
036   and the scientific community due to its profound economic impact on businesses. From a scientific
037   standpoint, while early research in this field assumed knowledge of the underlying demand func-
038   tions, the imperative for real-world AI applications has prompted the scientific community to shift
039
      their focus towards uncharted demand scenarios and exploration-exploitation algorithms, as under-
      scored by seminal works (Aviv & Pazgal, 2005; Besbes & Zeevi, 2009)). Moreover, research on
040
      non-stationary demand functions has made a substantial impact on the field. Specifically, recent
041
      studies have concentrated on external non-stationarity factors, such as internal ones driven by the
042   seller’s actions (Cui et al., 2023), as well as seasonality (Besbes & Saure, 2014). From an industrial
043   standpoint, the proliferation of e-commerce platforms and online marketplaces has led to a signifi-
044   cant acceleration in data collection and availability, thereby fostering the development of data-driven
045   dynamic pricing algorithms. It is not an exaggeration to regard these two categories of retailers as
046   the primary beneficiaries of dynamic pricing strategies.
047
      The use of reinforcement learning (RL) (Sutton & Barto, 2018) and Markov decision processes
048   (MDP) in dynamic pricing contexts is widely recognized. RL methodologies, such as Q-learning,
049   are extensively employed to address discrete pricing issues within the MDP framework. This is
050   largely due to their effectiveness in navigating the uncertainties associated with demand and pricing
051   results (e.g., (Kim et al., 2015; Lu et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021)). The same reasoning holds for
052   simpler single-state dynamic pricing settings, where Multi-Armed Bandits algorithms (Auer et al.,
053   2002; Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006) and online learning techniques (Hazan, 2019; Orabona, 2019)
      are used to learn the optimal price, while keeping small the losses that the seller may incur during


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the learning dynamic (see (Ganti et al., 2018; Misra et al., 2019; Trovò et al., 2018)). Due to space
055   constraints, we refer to Appendix A for a complete discussion on related works.
056
057   Our study centers on the pricing of complementary items that are sequentially presented to cus-
058
      tomers. To illustrate, consider the online sale of flight tickets, where customers navigate through
      multiple web pages. Initially, they view the base ticket cost, followed by ancillary expenses such as
059
      insurance and additional luggage fees. Coherent pricing policies play a pivotal role in optimizing the
060
      overall value of these complementary items, as individually optimizing the price of each item may
061   lead to arbitrarily inefficient solutions. We delve into two additional features commonly encountered
062   in real-world applications. First, we address the uncertainty surrounding customer demand curves,
063   emphasizing the need for online machine learning tools. In particular, we explore both stationary
064   and non-stationary settings. Second, we examine constraints related to pricing optimization. Specif-
065   ically, we impose a requirement that the number of sales should not fall below a certain threshold.
066   Interestingly, managing these constraints when learning online poses significant technical challenges
067   that are still open in the scientific literature.
068   We first formulate the problem by adopting the Markov decision process (MDP) framework with
069   constraints (CMDP) (Altman, 1999), where the sale constraint is encoded within the violation defini-
070   tion. In each state of the CMDP, the seller must choose the price for an item, subsequently observing
071   the associated reward and any incurred violations. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work
072   to employ the online CMDP mathematical framework to solve dynamic pricing scenarios. Thus, to
073   address the problem, we introduce a primal-dual online learning algorithm that optimizes the seller’s
074   expected reward while minimizing the number of violations. Notably, our algorithm is tailored to
075
      handle non-stationary demand curves, eliminating the need for stationarity assumptions regarding
      reward and constraint functions. Finally, we empirically evaluate the performance of our algorithm.
076
      To do so, we generate an online simulator from real-world data from one of the world’s leading
077
      online travel agencies. This empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of our approach in
078   maximizing the expected rewards while minimizing the violations of constraints.

081   2     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
082
083   In our study, we examine a scenario where an e-commerce platform aims to sell a primary product
084
      i1 and a related ancillary item i2 (for instance, a flight ticket and corresponding baggage). It is
      crucial to price these items by considering their interdependence. Indeed, the pricing of the primary
085
      product (and similarly, the ancillary item) can influence the sales of the other. This is because
086
      potential customers might base their decision to purchase either or both items on the price of just
087   one. For instance, if a customer needs to carry an extra bag on a flight and finds the baggage fee
088   excessively high, they might buy his tickets from a competitor. Therefore, it’s imperative to strike a
089   balanced trade-off between the two prices to maximize total revenue. Additionally, the platform has
090   a requirement that a specific portion of the revenue and a certain number of sold tickets are ensured
091   by the set prices.
092
      We formalize the aforementioned scenario employing the following mathematical model, known in
093   the literature as an online Constrained Markov Decision Process (CMDP, (Altman, 1999)).
094
      Remark 1. Please notice that, even if the paper focuses on the pricing of two products (the main
095
      one and the related ancillary), both the model and the proposed algorithm can be easily extended to
096
      take into account more than two items.

123           denote by P (x′ |x, a) the probability of going from state x ∈ X to x′ ∈ X by taking action

125           x ∈ Xk for some k ∈ [0 . . . L − 1]. Transitions from state x0 represent the probability of a
126           user clicking on the primary product and either progressing to states x1 or x2 or opting to
127           leave the website, which corresponds to transitioning to state x3 . Similarly, transitions from
128
              state x2 depict the likelihood of the user engaging with the ancillary product, advancing to
              the final payment state x4 (but without generating the final conversion), or deciding to exit
129
              the website, leading to the transition to the exit state x5 .

            • {rt }t=1 is a sequence of vectors describing the rewards at each episode t ∈ [T ], namely
131
132
              rt ∈ [0, 1]|X×A| . We refer to the reward of a specific state-action pair x ∈ X, a ∈ A for
              a specific episode t ∈ [T ] as rt (x, a). Rewards may be stochastic, in that case, rt is a
133
              random variable distributed according to a fixed probability distribution R for every t ∈
134
              [T ], but also highly non-stationary, namely rt ∼ Rt where Rt is the rewards distribution
135           at episode t ∈ [T ]. Reward at exit states x3 , x5 and x6 is always 0. Reward r(x0 , a)
136           measures the profit from selling the main product at price a. Similarly, the reward at state
137           x1 (namely, r(x1 , a)) is the profit from selling the ancillary product, plus a constant bonus
138           for clicking on the main product. The same constant bonus is applied in r(x2 , ·) and r(x4 , ·)
139           for engagement with the primary and ancillary product, respectively. Note that rewards
140           affect transitions:
141             1. If a user buys the primary product in state x0 , i.e., the reward is not equal to zero, they
142                 move to state x1 . Here, regardless of whether they purchase an ancillary product or
143
                    not, they will eventually reach the final payment state.
                2. Conversely, a zero reward in state x0 implies no primary product purchase. This leads
144
                    to two possibilities: the user either exits or moves to state x2 . In state x2 , since
145
                    the primary product was not purchased, the user can’t buy the ancillary product but
146                 receives the predefined bonus. The user will then either exit or go to the payment state
147                 without any purchase.
148                 T
            • {gt }t=1 is a sequence of constraint functions describing the constraint violations at each
149
              episode t ∈ [T ], namely gt ∈ [−1, 1]|X×A| , where non-positive violation values stand for
150           satisfaction of the constraints. We refer to the violation of the i-th constraint for a specific
151           state-action pair x ∈ X, a ∈ A at episode t ∈ [T ] as gt (x, a). Constraint violations could
152           be stochastic (in that scenario, gt is a random variable distributed according to a probability
153           distribution G for every t ∈ [T ]) or highly non-stationary, namely, gt ∼ Gt . The objective
154           of the constraints is to maintain a minimum ratio of product sales to website views, thus
155           preventing overly aggressive pricing tactics. For states other than x0 , the constraint value
156           is set to zero. In x0 , the constraint function is expressed as gt (x0 , a) = τ − I{rt (x0 ,a)̸=0} ,
157           where τ is the target ratio of sales to views, and the second term is the indicator function
158
              on the sale of the primary product. The constraint value is positive if the primary product is
              not purchased and negative otherwise. Therefore, the constraint is only met if the average
159
              proportion of sales exceeds τ .

245
      separate optimization processes. From a game-theoretic standpoint, these are identified as the primal
      and dual players in the Lagrangian game, which is defined as follows:

249   for any reward r and constraint g vectors. This concept aligns with the framework described in
250   (Stradi et al., 2024b) for adversarial CMDPs. Formally, the primal player focuses on optimizing
251   within the primal variable space of the Lagrangian game, specifically, the set of valid occupancy
252   measures, denoted as ∆(M ) (see Section 2.2 for a formal definition of valid occupancy measures).
253   Nevertheless, since the transition functions are unknown to the learner, ∆(M ) is not known a-priori
254   and must be estimated in an online fashion. On the contrary, the dual player optimizes over the dual
255   variable space, defined as [0, +∞). Specifically, as a primal optimizer, we employ the state-of-the-
256   art algorithm for unconstrained adversarial MDPs proposed in (Jin et al., 2020). Instead, the dual
257
      optimizer consists of a projected gradient descent on the Lagrangian variable λ.

      A    R ELATED W ORKS
668
      Our work is strongly related to the reinforcement learning (RL) literature applied to dynamic pricing
669
      and online learning in Markov decision processes theory. Below, we underscore the most cutting-
670   edge contributions in these specified fields of study.
671
672   Reinforcement Learning for Dynamic Pricing The use of RL and Markov Decision Processes
673   (MDP) in dynamic pricing contexts is widely recognized. MDPs provide a solid structure for mod-
674   eling complex dynamic pricing challenges, particularly when pricing multiple items. In a similar
675   vein, RL methodologies, such as Q-learning, are extensively employed to address discrete pricing
676   issues within the MDP framework. This is largely due to their effectiveness in navigating the un-
677   certainties associated with demand and pricing results (e.g., (Kim et al., 2015; Lu et al., 2018; Liu
678   et al., 2021)). Modeling dynamic pricing scenarios with RL frameworks allowed the development
679   of provably optimal algorithms with good empirical performances. The same reasoning holds for
680
      simpler single-state dynamic pricing settings, where Multi-Armed Bandits algorithms (see (Ganti
      et al., 2018; Misra et al., 2019; Trovò et al., 2018)) are used to learn the optimal price, while keeping
681
      small the losses that the seller may incur during the learning dynamic.
682
683
      Online Learning in Markov Decision Processes The literature related to online learning prob-
684
      lems (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006) in MDPs is wide (see (Auer et al., 2008; Even-Dar et al.,
685   2009; Neu et al., 2010; Rosenberg & Mansour, 2019b; Jin et al., 2020; Bacchiocchi et al., 2023;
686   Maran et al., 2024)). In such contexts, two primary forms of feedback are commonly examined: the
687   full-information feedback model, where the entire loss function is revealed following the learner’s
688   decision, and the bandit feedback model, in which the learner observes only the incurred loss. Over
689   recent years, online learning in MDPs with constraints has garnered considerable attention. Specifi-
690   cally, the vast majority of prior research in this area focuses on scenarios where constraints are im-
691   posed in a stochastic manner (Zheng & Ratliff (2020)). Wei et al. (2018) address adversarial losses
692   and stochastic constraints under the assumption that transition probabilities are known, and the feed-
693
      back provided is of the full-information type. Bai et al. (2020) introduce the first algorithm able to
      achieve sub-linear regret in situations where transition probabilities are unknown. This assumes that
694
      the rewards are deterministic and the constraints are stochastic, possessing a specific structure. Mo-
695
      rover, Efroni et al. (2020) suggest two strategies for navigating the exploration-exploitation trade-off
696   in episodic Constrained Markov Decision Processes (CMDPs). These methods ensure sub-linear re-
697   gret and constraint violation in scenarios where transition probabilities, rewards, and constraints are
698   both unknown and stochastic, and the feedback received is of the bandit type. Qiu et al. (2020)
699   provide a primal-dual approach based on optimism in the face of uncertainty. This research demon-
700   strates the efficacy of this approach in handling episodic CMDPs characterized by adversarial losses
701   and stochastic constraints. It achieves sub-linear regret and constraint violation while utilizing full-
      information feedback. Stradi et al. (2024b) present a ’best-of-both-worlds’ algorithm for CMDPs


                                                         13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      operating with full-information feedback. (Stradi et al., 2024a) is the first work to tackle CMDPs
703   with adversarial losses and bandit feedback, while assuming that the constraints are stochastic. Bac-
704   chiocchi et al. (2024) study a generalization of stochastic CMDPs with partial observability on the
705   constraints. Finally, Stradi et al. (2024c) study CMDPs with non-stationary rewards and constraints,
706   assuming that the non-stationarity is bounded.
