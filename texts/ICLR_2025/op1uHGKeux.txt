009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion Policy has attracted attention for its ability to achieve significant accu-
012            racy gains in a variety of imitation learning tasks. However, since Diffusion Policy
013            relies on the Diffusion Model, it requires multiple denoising steps to generate a
014            single action leading to long generation times. To address this issue, methods like
015            DDIM and Consistency Models have been introduced to speed up the process.
016
               While these methods reduce computation time, this often comes at the cost of ac-
               curacy. In this paper, we propose RAGDP, a technique designed to improve the ef-
017
               ficiency of learned Diffusion Policies without sacrificing accuracy. RAGDP builds
018
               upon the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) technique, which is commonly
019            used in large language models to store and retrieve data from a vector database
020            based on encoded embeddings. In RAGDP, pairs of expert observation and ac-
021            tions data are stored in a vector database. The system then searches the database
022            using encoded observation data to retrieve expert action data with high similar-
023            ity. This retrieved expert data is subsequently used by the RAGDP algorithm to
024            generate actions tailored to the current environment. We introduce two action gen-
025            eration algorithms, RAGDP-VP and RAGDP-VE, which correspond to different
026            types of Diffusion Models. Our results demonstrate that RAGDP can significantly
027
               improve the speed of Diffusion Policy without compromising accuracy. Further-
               more, RAGDP can be integrated with existing speed-up methods to enhance their
028
               performance.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In the effort to teach behaviors to intelligent agents, imitation learning has been utilized to solve
034   various tasks (Schaal, 1999; Osa et al., 2018). With the success of Diffusion Models in other fields,
035   researchers has been experimenting using these models for imitation learning showing excellent
036   results (Team et al., 2024; Chi et al., 2023; Ze et al., 2024; Reuss et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024),
037   with Diffusion Policy (Chi et al., 2023) achieving state-of-the-art performance in Behavior Cloning.
038   Despite these advancements, Diffusion Policy’s reliance on Diffusion Models introduces a signifi-
039   cant computational cost. The core challenge stems from the need to perform sequential denoising
040   of full Gaussian noise to generate a single sample, which greatly increases inference time. For
041   instance, Diffusion Policy operates using Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) (Ho
042   et al., 2020), which require approximately 100 iterations of denoising to generate an action from
043   Gaussian noise. While reducing the number of denoising steps can improve speed, it often leads to
044
      a trade-off in accuracy, as noise cannot be fully eliminated in fewer steps.
045   To further enhance speed, methods that reduce the number of required steps have been ex-
046   plored (Song et al., 2022; Salimans & Ho, 2022; Song et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024). These ap-
047   proaches show an increase in generation speed, but involve inherent trade-offs in quality, for ex-
048   ample in multi-stage tasks. A reduction in accuracy, even if small, can have compounding effect
049   in imitation learning due to the covariant shift leading to sub-optimal policies (Ross et al., 2011;
050
      Rajaraman et al., 2020). This limitation may further restrict applicability in domains that require
      high-precision movements, such as robotics (Ke et al., 2021).
051
052   Recent advances in retrieval-augmented diffusion models, such as Retrieval-Enhanced Asymmet-
053   ric Diffusion (READ) (Oba et al., 2024) for motion planning, Retrieve-Augmented Generation
      (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2021) for text generation, and ReDi (Zhang et al., 2023) for efficient image

079
      action generating methods, RAGDP-VP and RAGDP-VE used to accelerate the denoising process
080
      in diffusion models for imitation learning. Analogical to RAG (Lewis et al., 2021), we store expert
081   observations and action data in a vector database. When denoising the current action, the model
082   can search the available database based on its encoded observation data to retrieve actions with high
083   similarity to generate more accurate actions more aligned with the expert data. Once expert actions
084   have been retrieved we can speed up the sampling process in two distinct ways explained in item 1.
085   (1) RAGDP-VP lets us skip the initial denoising stages and start later in the process depending on
086   hyperparameter r. (2) RAGDP-VE instead simply reduces the amount of steps taken. We present
087   the following contributions:

      Fast sampling methods for Diffusion Models
102   Several approaches have been proposed to accelerate Diffusion Models. For example, Denoising
103   Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM) (Song et al., 2022) is a method that improves the sampling
104   method of DDPM and can be adapted to trained models. Progressive Distillation (Salimans & Ho,
105   2022) is proposed as a method that uses knowledge distillation to reduce the sampling steps of the
106   teacher model. Consistency Model (Song et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024) is based on the Score-based
107   Generative Models (Song et al., 2020), which is formulated as a stochastic differential equation for
      the Diffusion Models. Among these methods, since Progressive Distillation has a relatively high


                                                        2

108
      learning cost, we design a model based on DDIM and the Consistency Models. In this paper, we
109   show how these existing methods can be enhanced with RAGDP to improve the sampling efficiency
110   further during the inference.
111
112
      Retrieval-Augmented Methods
      While the most widely application of RAG (Lewis et al., 2021) is Large Language Models (Naveed
113
      et al., 2024), there are several methods leveraging data retrieval in Diffusion Models. For instance,
114
      ReDi (Zhang et al., 2023) achieves speed-up by retrieving noisy data paired with data that has some
115   noise removed. The Retrieval-Augmented Diffusion Model (Blattmann et al., 2022) retrieves both
116   during training and inference, with retrieval during training to augment the data and retrieval during
117   inference to search for appropriate conditional input.
118
      In the context of robot learning, There are two lines of work leveraging trajectory retrieval. The
119
      first one is methods that retrieve trajectories during model training. Some methods aim to improve
120
      the dataset by retrieving data that is close to the expert during training. Nasiriany et al. (2022); Du
121   et al. (2023) utilize Varial Auto Encoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2022) to embed observations
122   and the corresponding actions, and the trajectory retrievals are performed within the embedding
123   space. The other is to retrieve during both training and inference; ReMoDiffuse (Zou et al., 2024)
124   proposes to create a database of Text-Motion pairs and to make major architectural modifications to
125   input the retrieved data into the model. READ (Oba et al., 2024) proposes a model that works with
126   Image-Motion pairs, which retrieves trajectories during training and the image during inference.
127   However, these models primarily focus on refining trajectories or improving text and image genera-
128   tion, lacking application in action policy generation for imitation learning. RAGDP fills this gap by
129   integrating retrieval-based expert trajectories into a diffusion policy framework for efficient action
130   policy generation. By retrieving relevant expert demonstrations, RAGDP accelerates policy learn-
131   ing while maintaining high-quality action generation, making it particularly effective for real-time
132   robotic tasks.
133   Diffusion Model-based Data Editing
134   Diffusion Models are powerful tools for image editing tasks, and their methods can be classified
135   into three categories: training-based, testing-time finetuning, and training & finetuning free. While
136   most methods target image and text modalities, SDEdit (Meng et al., 2022) is a method that can be
137   utilized in the action space. SDEdit can obtain output in line with the input by reverse diffusion
138   process from the input data with noise added at a specific step. Other methods that potentially can
139   be applied in the action space include InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) and Denoising Diffusion
140   Bridge Models (DDBM) (Zhou et al., 2023); however, these methods are not suited to improving
141
      the speed of generation. In this study, we focus on Training & Finetuning Free to consider methods
      that deal with the action space (Huang et al., 2024). This let us use RAGDP without any additional
142
      training of the diffusion model.

      4.1   R ETRIEVAL
247
      Implementing Vector Database
248   The knowledge-base in RAGDP is a vector database consisting of pairs of observation data and
249   corresponding expert trajectory data. When performing a search, the key vector is the observed data
250   of the training data, and the value vector retrieved during the search corresponds to the trajectory
251   data At of the training data. The query vector during retrieval is the observed data during inference.
252   In most of the previous studies, the embedding space is created by VAE for retrieval on observed
253   data before retrieval. In this study, the vectors obtained from the encoders of the observed data
254   implemented in Diffusion Policy are stored in the knowledge-base; the encoders in Diffusion Policy
255   are CNN models in the case of images and identity functions in the case of states data. It has been
256
      shown that this encoder is better trained simultaneously with Diffusion Policy than pre-trained alone
      on a large data set.
257
258   The vector database is implemented in Facebook AI Similarity Search (FAISS) (Johnson et al.,
259   2017), which uses a search method that indexes from L2 distances in Euclidean space.
260   Retrieving Strategy
261   In searching the vector database, the following issues are considered: when to search for a time step
262   t, how many samples with the highest search similarity should be obtained, and whether to use a
263   threshold for the search. RAGDP simply searches at every time step, does not use a threshold when
264   searching, and works to obtain the top one similarity sample.

291   RAGDP-VP
292   In VP-SDE, the parameters in Equation 3 are constrained by α(τ )2 +σ(τ )2 = 1. This means that the

294   the database is used to calculate the final output from the ratio of action and noise corresponding

      to the starting diffusion step τ0 . RAGDP-VP introduces a hyperparameter r, which determines the

296
      start generating is τ0 , then r = τ0 /T . Since the parameter takes the range 0 < r < 1, the number
297

302
      on the size of the action and noise. Therefore, the action taken from the database adds noise of a
303
      magnitude corresponding to the starting diffusion step τ0 , and the output is obtained where this noise
304   becomes smaller. Therefore, RAGDP-VE always adds σmax without changing the amount of initial

307   for VE-SDE based Diffusion Model only and Consistency Models. The EDM-based RAGDP-VE is

499   5.5   C HOICE OF HYPERPARAMETER
500
      Consider the choice of hyperparameter r. In image-based diffusion models, there is a known trade-
501
      off between faithfulness to the input and realism. If the number of steps to denoise with respect to the
502
      input is small, realistic samples cannot be generated, and conversely, if the number of denoise steps
503   with respect to the input is large, the faithfulness to the output to be obtained is reduced. Therefore,
504   in the image-based case, the weightspot is chosen, which is expressed in r as r ∈ [0.4, 0.7]. The
505   proposed method also measured the hyper-parameters with the EDM model, as shown in Figure 8.
506   From the figure, it can be seen that for each task, performance tends to decrease when r = 0.75
507   or higher. Therefore, it is considered optimal to determine parameters around this point where a
508   trade-off between task accuracy and speed can be made.

      6     C ONCLUSION
511
512
      In this study, speed and accuracy benchmarks were created by utilising Diffusion Policy in DDPM,
513   EDM and CTM models. Then, using a vector database as a knowledge base, we proposed RAGDP,
514   a method that can improve sample speed without requiring additional training and without reducing
515   the accuracy of Diffusion Policy. in RAGDP, sample speed is determined by the parameter r and can
516   generate behaviour for VP-SDE and VE-SDE based Diffusion Models using the RAGDP-VP and
517   RAGDP-VE sampling methods. The proposed method is shown to be robust to a reduced number
518   of steps. And RAGDP-VP was shown to improve the performance of existing models by adjusting
519   the noise magnitude when utilising DDPM and when utilising CTM; RAGDP-VE was shown to be
520   effective for EDM-based models when speeding up the process.
521   However, several points need to be improved in the future. First, RAGDP is sensitive concerning
522   existing models and hyperparameters. Therefore, it is necessary to select hyperparameters in line
523   with specific tasks. This is because the initial values of the generation process vary. For example,
524   as Align Your Steps (Sabour et al., 2024) improves accuracy by compensating for the discretization
525   error of the scheduler, it may be necessary to consider a scheduler that follows the initial values.
526   Second, this study does not discuss the creation of a semantic space when searching with observed
527
      data. Future work should investigate improving the embedded space to be searched for in behavioral
      data as well.
