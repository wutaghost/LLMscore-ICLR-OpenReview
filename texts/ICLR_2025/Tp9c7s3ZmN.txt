009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Portrait animation aims to transfer the facial expressions and movements of a tar-
012            get character onto a reference character. This task presents two main challenges:
013            accurately transferring motion and expressions while fully preserving the identity
014            features of the reference portrait. We introduce Vividportraits, a diffusion-based
015            model designed to effectively meet these objectives. In contrast to existing meth-
016            ods that rely on sparse representations such as facial landmarks, our approach
017            leverages facial parsing maps for motion guidance, enabling a more precise con-
018
               veyance of subtle expressions. A random scaling technique is applied during
               training to prevent the model from internalizing identity-specific features from
019
               the driving images. Furthermore, we perform foreground-background segmen-
020
               tation on the reference portrait to reduce data redundancy. The long-video gen-
021            eration process is refined to improve consistency across sequences. Our model,
022            exclusively trained on public datasets, demonstrates superior performance rela-
023            tive to current state-of-the-art methods, achieving a notable 8% improvement in
024            expression metric. More visual results are available on the anonymous website
025            https://www.vividportraits.cn.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The objective of the portrait animation task is to transfer facial expressions and movements from a
031   target video to a reference image, allowing the reference character to accurately replicate the facial
032   dynamics of the target character (Ma et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024). Typically, these two characters
033
      are distinct individuals. This field presents two primary challenges: the full preservation of the iden-
      tity features of the reference portrait, including crucial aspects such as facial structure, texture, and
034
      individual characteristics, and the accurate transfer of motion expressions. The latter involves not
035
      only the replication of larger, more noticeable movements but also the precise conveyance of subtle
036   facial expressions, such as minor changes in the eyes or mouth, which are essential for generating
037   natural and believable animations. Portrait animation technology has been extensively utilized in
038   various domains, including video conferencing (Khakhulin et al., 2022), live streaming (Qu et al.,
039   2023), and e-commerce (Sun et al., 2023). This task is typically achieved using GAN-based models
040   or diffusion-based models.
041
      GAN-based models (Siarohin et al., 2019b; Drobyshev et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
042   2021; Siarohin et al., 2019a) initially derive a motion “flow field” to simulate the movement of fea-
043   tures within the feature map of the reference image. The feature map, distorted by this flow field,
044   is subsequently refined via a decoder and further optimized through GAN training. However, these
045   models exhibit two fundamental limitations: (1) The motion “flow field” is capable of simulating
046   alterations in the existing features of the reference image but has difficulty representing absent or
047   occluded features. (2) GANs suffer from mode collapse issues, preventing them from guiding the de-
048   coder to achieve flawless image generation. Consequently, GAN-based models frequently produce
049   outputs characterized by blurriness, unrealistic artifacts, and implausible content.
050   Diffusion models have been effectively employed in portrait animation tasks (Chang et al., 2023;
051   He et al., 2023; Hu, 2024; Xu et al., 2023; 2024b; Zeng et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Ma et al.,
052   2024; Xu et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2023a). Some approaches (Hu, 2024; Ma et al., 2024) employ
053   landmarks to represent facial poses and expressions; however, this sparse representation inevitably
      results in the loss of crucial motion information, such as the actions of the eyebrows and mouth,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      failing to adequately capture subtle movements. Other techniques (Xie et al., 2024; He et al., 2024;
055   Yang et al., 2024) circumvent intermediate motion representations, directly using the target image to
056   guide the generation process. RGB inputs introduces an excess of identity features from the target
057   portrait, causing a blend of reference and target portraits and introducing unrelated background
058   noise, which leads to noticeable artifacts in the generated output. Additionally, some methods (He
059   et al., 2023; Qu et al., 2023) utilize facial parameters to construct motion representations, but these
060   approaches overly depend on the accuracy of the facial encoder.
061   In this paper, we introduce Vividportraits, an innovative portrait animation method grounded in dif-
062   fusion models. To address the issues encountered in prior models, a suite of effective techniques is
063   proposed to enhance the quality of portrait animation: (1) To achieve a more precise and accurate
064   extraction of motion information, we propose facial parsing maps, a region-based motion represen-
065   tation. Compared to traditional point-based facial landmark methods, facial parsing maps preserve
066   the complete structure of facial expressions, allowing for more accurate conveyance of subtle facial
067   expressions. (2) A “random scaling” technique was introduced for the facial parsing maps to pre-
068   vent the incorporation of identity features from the target portrait. The processed parsing maps retain
069
      motion information while modifying identity features, thereby enabling the model to be trained ef-
      fectively under cross-id scenarios. (3) A foreground-background separation strategy is introduced
070
      to ensure each model component receives the necessary information, avoiding interference from
071
      redundant noise. (4) We strengthened the long-video generation method by integrating the “cyclic
072   overlap” technique, enhancing consistency between video frames by increasing the focus on each
073   individual frame.
074
075
      In summary, our contributions are as follows:
076
             • We propose a novel diffusion-based, zero-shot portrait animation framework that enables
077
               precise control over facial poses and expressions while preserving the character’s identity.
078
079          • A new motion representation is introduced, coupled with a cross-id training method,
080
               which accurately captures expression and motion features while mitigating the impact of
               appearance-related characteristics.
081
082          • A foreground-background separation method is presented, enhancing the extraction of rel-
083            evant information, reducing redundancy, and improving the interpretability of the frame-
084            work.
085          • The long-video generation strategy is optimized, further enhancing temporal consistency
086            between video frames.

      2.1   GAN-BASED M ETHODS
091
092   Portrait animation is a specialized image generation task that has evolved in tandem with the ad-
093   vancements in generative models, particularly GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2020) and diffusion mod-
094   els (Song et al., 2020a; Ho et al., 2020). Numerous GAN-based approaches address portrait ani-
095   mation by decomposing the task into three distinct stages (Xie et al., 2024): the first stage involves
096   encoding the reference image to obtain its feature map, thereby capturing the identity characteristics
097   of the reference; the second stage generates a flow field from the reference and target images to
098
      simulate the displacement of feature points within the reference image; and the third stage utilizes
      this flow field to manipulate the feature map and decodes it to produce the final animated result.
099
100   Various optimizations have been introduced in the generation of the flow field. Some methods utilize
101   sparse keypoints (Siarohin et al., 2019b; Hong & Xu, 2023; Wang et al., 2021; Zhao & Zhang, 2022;
102   Geng et al., 2018) to model the flow field, as exemplified by FOMM (Siarohin et al., 2019b), which
103   trains a keypoint detector to capture the relative movement of facial features. Utilizing keypoints and
104
      relative displacement as the flow field can mitigate identity leakage between different individuals;
      however, it necessitates that the pose of the person in the first frame of the target video closely
105
      resembles that of the reference.
106
107   Furthermore, some approaches forgo keypoints altogether and adopt alternative strategies for gener-
      ating the flow field, such as leveraging depth maps (Hong et al., 2022), pose and expression parame-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ters (Ren et al., 2021; Qu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Drobyshev et al., 2022; Qu et al., 2023), and
109   tri-planes (Siarohin et al., 2021). These methods endeavor to optimize flow field generation from
110   various perspectives, thereby enhancing the quality of portrait animation. However, they remain
111   constrained by the flow field, which continues to impose stringent requirements on the initial pose
112   of the target character. If there is a significant discrepancy between the pose of the reference portrait
113   and the initial pose of the target portrait, the generated results are likely to be subpar.
114
115   2.2   D IFFUSION -BASED M ETHODS
116
117   Diffusion models (Song et al., 2020b; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020a) have achieved significant
118   success in various tasks, including image generation (Gu et al., 2024; Song et al., 2020b; Saharia
119
      et al., 2022; Ruiz et al., 2023), image editing (Ye et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023), and video editing (Liu
      et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023) applications. Consequently, many studies have begun
120
      applying diffusion models to portrait animation. Mainstream diffusion-based portrait animation
121
      frameworks harness the robust generative capabilities of Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022)
122   and integrate temporal modules (Guo et al., 2023) to enhance inter-frame consistency.
123
124   There are multiple approaches for representing facial expression movements, including the direct
125
      use of portrait images (Xie et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024), facial encoding (He et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
      2023), and facial landmarks (Hu, 2024; Ma et al., 2024).
126
127   Utilizing portrait images introduces background elements and other information unrelated to motion,
128   while also transmitting redundant identity information—such as facial structure and size—from the
129   target portrait to the model, thereby complicating the training process.
130   Facial encoding employs an encoder to represent facial movements, with the accuracy of the en-
131   coder being a critical determinant of the method’s effectiveness. However, as a simplified feature
132   representation, facial encoding often fails to capture subtle variations and complex expressions of
133   the face, resulting in information loss. Additionally, this approach is sensitive to noise, which can
134   lead to instability in the generated output.
135   Facial landmarks are another commonly employed method for motion representation. However, the
136   number of keypoints significantly influences the precision of expression representation, with com-
137   mon counts being 5, 68, 81, and 98. These sparse, point-based representations overlook substantial
138   facial detail, resulting in incomplete expression transfer and hindering the accurate conveyance of
139   subtle facial movements.

144   zT ∼ N (0, 1) over T steps. The Latent Diffusion Model (Blattmann et al., 2023), a crucial com-

148   from the RGB space to a lower-dimensional latent space.

150   influence of text conditions. This is facilitated through the use of self-attention and cross-attention

152   via cross-attention, thereby steering the denoising process. The training objective of the LDM is
153   defined as follows:

188   4.1   R ANDOM S CALING OF FACIAL PARSING M APS
189
      The critical elements of the portrait animation task are expression transfer and identity preservation,
190
      with the representation of facial expressions being a pivotal step in achieving effective expression
191
      transfer. An optimal motion representation can accurately convey the demeanor, expressions, and
192   even the emotional nuances of the target portrait, resulting in more realistic and expressive generated
193   outcomes.
194
195
      To overcome the limitations of the aforementioned methods, we propose adopting facial parsing
      maps as a motion representation. We employ FARL (Zheng et al., 2022) to extract the facial parsing
196
      map from the target portrait, removing expression-irrelevant components and augmenting it with
197
      the missing ocular motion information. This approach effectively eliminates background elements
198   unrelated to motion, retaining solely the components pertinent to facial expressions and thereby cir-
199   cumventing the introduction of extraneous information. Moreover, the facial parsing map preserves
200   the complete shape of each facial component, ensuring the full transmission of expression and mo-
201   tion information, avoiding the information loss associated with sparse point-based representations.
202
      As mentioned above, the current facial parsing maps lack representation of the eyeball region, and
203
      relying on them alone would lead to the omission of essential eye movement information. To mit-
204   igate this limitation, we utilize the keypoint detection technique from FARL (Zheng et al., 2022)
205   to delineate the keypoints of the eye region, including the eyeball position, in a manner consistent
206   with the facial parsing map. These keypoints are then incorporated into the final target parsing map.
207   This approach ensures that the model captures the essential expression information related to the
208   relative position of the eyeball. Given that both the facial parsing map and landmarks are derived
209   using methods from FARL, their alignment is inherently maintained.
210   To attenuate the influence of identity-related attributes such as size and spacing in cross-id scenar-
211   ios, we introduce a random scaling technique for the facial parsing maps. Initially, we eliminate
212   the contours in the facial parsing map, preserving only elements such as the eyes and nose, thereby
213   removing facial shape information from the map. In the subsequent step, each component is ran-
214   domly scaled individually within a range of 0.8 to 1.2, modifying the size of each element within
215   the parsing map. In the final step, the entire face is scaled within a range of 0.7 to 1.3, adjusting the
      spacing between the components in the parsing map.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Throughout the overall scaling process, we maintain the position of the face within the complete
217   image and preserve the relative positions of each facial component within the face. This method-
218   ology allows us to generate target facial parsing maps that exhibit varied identity characteristics
219   while keeping the expressions and poses unchanged. By utilizing these parsing maps, which alter
220   identity features while retaining motion features, cross-id training is facilitated with precise ground
221   truth from the outset. This ensures that the model is dedicated to learning expression and motion
222   information from the parsing maps, effectively disentangling identity-related specifics.

266   4.2   F OREGROUND -BACKGROUND S EPARATION M ECHANISM
267
268   In previous works, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) is predominantly used to process the entire reference
269   portrait image, serving as the prompt for both ReferenceNet and DenoisingUNet. Consequently,
      both networks receive information pertaining to the identity features of the reference portrait as


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      well as the background. We posit that ReferenceNet should primarily focus on extracting identity
271   features. Therefore, the facial segmentation map of the reference image is employed to partition it
272   into a foreground, containing identity features, and a background that is unrelated to identity.
273
274
      ReferenceNet is provided exclusively with the foreground identity information encoded by CLIP,
      which enables the Transformer blocks within it to place greater emphasis on identity features during
275
      the attention operation. Given that the prompt contains only identity information without interfer-
276
      ence from background elements, ReferenceNet can concentrate on encoding identity features while
277   allocating minimal attention to background information.
278
279   The reduction in background information is compensated for by DenoisingUNet. We use CLIP to
280
      encode the background portion of the reference image and pass this encoding to DenoisingUNet
      instead of the entire image. This approach allows DenoisingUNet to receive a background en-
281
      coding from CLIP that is devoid of identity information contamination, thereby supplementing the
282
      background information that is reduced by our foreground-background separation mechanism in
283   ReferenceNet.
284
285   As demonstrated in the Appendix, our foreground-background separation mechanism facilitates im-
286
      proved preservation of both identity and background features in the generated results.

288   4.3   L ONG V IDEO G ENERATION S TRATEGY
289
      Typically, diffusion-based portrait animation methods generate a fixed number of frames in a single
290
      iteration, such as 16, 20, or 24 frames, with temporal modules ensuring consistency across these
291   frames. To generate longer videos while preserving overall consistency, many methods employ the
292   “overlapping video segments” technique. This approach involves segmenting the target video into
293   segments of length x and creating overlaps between consecutive segments. During each denoising
294   process, the overlapping regions between video segments are averaged to maintain consistency,
295   ensuring that each segment aligns with its overlapping regions with others, thereby sustaining overall
296   video coherence.
297   However, this method has an inherent limitation: the overlapping frames between video segments
298   remain static during each denoising iteration. The frames in the middle of each segment that are not
299   included in the overlap do not directly influence the generation of adjacent segments; they merely
300   exert an indirect effect on other frames through “consistency propagation”. This indirect guidance is
301   less effective than the direct guidance provided by the temporal module. The direct influence range
302   for these frames is confined to the generation frame count x, whereas the overlapping sections have
303   a direct influence range of 2x.
304   To fully harness the “guidance” potential of each frame, we optimize the conventional “overlapping
305   video segments” method by extending the direct guidance range of the middle frames to 2x. Specif-
306   ically, a cyclic overlap strategy is adopted instead of using fixed overlapping frames. Following
307   each denoising process for all frames, the frame index of each video segment is incremented by one,
308   keeping them within the proper range. In the subsequent denoising process, the indexes of the over-
309   lapping parts between video segments are also incremented by one, resulting in the overlap shifting
310   to the right by one frame. Since the middle frames, excluding the overlap, are typically fewer than
311
      the denoising steps, each middle frame participates in the overlap for at least one denoising process,
      thereby extending its direct influence range from x to 2x.
312
313   As shown in the Appendix, the cyclic overlap generation method enhances video consistency.

      5.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
318
319   We utilized the public VFHQ (Xie et al., 2022) dataset for our experiments, which comprises over
320   16,000 high-fidelity clips from a variety of interview scenes. The training procedure was divided into
321   two stages. In the initial stage, the Temporal layer was excluded, and both the reference network
322   and the denoising network were initialized using SD1.5. The pose encoder was initialized with
323   Gaussian noise, and a zero convolution was applied at its final layer. During training, two different
      frames from the same video were sampled to train the reference network, denoising network, and


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      pose encoder, with the objective of enhancing the model’s single-image generation capability. In
325   the second stage, the Temporal layer was incorporated, initialized with AnimateDiff, similar to
326   AnimateAnyone. During this phase, the weights of all other components were frozen, and only the
327   Temporal layer was trained to improve the temporal consistency of video generation.
328
      Throughout both stages, the weights of the VAE encoder, decoder, and CLIP image encoder were
329
      kept frozen. We employed the Adam optimizer to train the entire model on an NVIDIA 4090 GPU.
330
      In the first stage, the batch size was set to 1, and the model was fine-tuned for 120,000 steps. In the
331   second stage, the batch size remained at 1, with the video length set to 24 frames, and the model was
332   fine-tuned for an additional 30,000 steps. The learning rate for both stages was maintained at 1e-5.
