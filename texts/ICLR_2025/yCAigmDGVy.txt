011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Estimating the global Lipschitz constant of neural networks is crucial for under-
014            standing and improving their robustness and generalization capabilities. How-
015
               ever, precise calculations are NP-hard, and current semidefinite programming
               (SDP) methods face challenges such as high memory usage and slow processing
016
               speeds. In this paper, we propose HiQ-Lip, a hybrid quantum-classical hierarchi-
017
               cal method that leverages Coherent Ising Machines (CIMs) to estimate the global
018            Lipschitz constant. We tackle the estimation by converting it into a Quadratic Un-
019            constrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) problem and implement a multilevel
020            graph coarsening and refinement strategy to adapt to the constraints of contempo-
021            rary quantum hardware. Our experimental evaluations on fully connected neural
022            networks demonstrate that HiQ-Lip not only provides estimates comparable to
023            state-of-the-art methods but also significantly accelerates the computation pro-
024            cess. In specific tests involving two-layer neural networks with 256 hidden neu-
025            rons, HiQ-Lip doubles the solving speed and offers more accurate upper bounds
026
               than the existing best method, LiPopt. These findings highlight the promising util-
               ity of small-scale quantum devices in advancing the estimation of neural network
027
               robustness.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Neural networks have achieved remarkable success in various fields such as computer vision, natural
033   language processing, and autonomous driving, establishing themselves as core technologies in mod-
034   ern artificial intelligence (Zhao et al., 2024; Forner & Ozcan, 2023; Paniego et al., 2023). Despite
035   these advancements, the robustness and generalization capabilities of neural networks remain active
036   areas of research (Djolonga et al., 2021; Bennouna et al., 2023). The global Lipschitz constant is
037   a critical metric for measuring the robustness of a neural network’s output to input perturbations,
038
      playing a significant role in understanding and enhancing model robustness (Leino et al., 2021a).
039   However, accurately computing the global Lipschitz constant is an NP-hard problem, particularly for
040   deep networks with nonlinear activation functions like ReLU (Jordan & Dimakis, 2020). Due to this
041   computational challenge, researchers have developed various approximation methods to estimate
042   upper bounds of the global Lipschitz constant. Among these, the Formal Global Lipschitz constant
043   (FGL) assumes that all activation patterns in the hidden layers are independent and possible, thereby
044
      providing an upper bound for the exact global Lipschitz constant (Szegedy et al., 2014; Virmaux &
      Scaman, 2018).
045
046   Existing methods often rely on relaxations and semidefinite programming (SDP) to estimate the up-
047   per bound of the Lipschitz constant (Chen et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2022). Although these methods
048   have a solid theoretical foundation, they suffer from high memory consumption and slow computa-
049   tion speeds. Additionally, simpler approaches such as matrix norm approximations, while compu-
050
      tationally efficient, tend to provide overly conservative bounds that fail to reflect the network’s true
      robustness accurately. These limitations drive the need for more efficient and precise methods to
051
      estimate the global Lipschitz constant of neural networks.
052
053   In recent years, advancements in quantum computing have offered new avenues for tackling NP-
      hard problems (Khumalo et al., 2022; Choi, 2008; Chatterjee et al., 2024). Given the computational

054
      complexity of global Lipschitz constant estimation, quantum computation is seen as a potential so-
055   lution. Specifically, quantum devices based on the Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization
056   (QUBO) model, such as Coherent Ising Machines (CIMs) or quantum annealer, have demonstrated
057   unique potential in solving complex combinatorial optimization problems (Inagaki et al., 2016;
058   Date et al., 2019). However, the limited number of qubits in current quantum computers poses sig-
059   nificant challenges when directly applying them to neural network robustness evaluations. Although
060   numerous works have attempted to address large-scale QUBO problems by employing strategies
061   like divide-and-conquer and QUBO formula simplification to decompose them into smaller QUBO
062   subproblems for quantum solving (Pelofske et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023), applications in neural
063
      network robustness assessment remain largely unexplored.
064   To bridge this gap and harness the acceleration capabilities of quantum computing for FGL estima-
065   tion of neural network, we propose a quantum-classical hybrid hierarchical solving method named
066   HiQ-Lip. HiQ means multilevel solution method for quantum classical mixing, while Lip stands
067   for global Lipschitz constant. By reformulating the global Lipschitz constant estimation problem as
068   a cut-norm problem, we transform it into a QUBO form. Employing a multi-level graph coarsening
069
      and refinement approach, we reduce the problem size to a range manageable by quantum computers,
      successfully utilizing CIMs to solve for the neural network’s global Lipschitz constant.
070
071   In this study, we focus on the FGL under ℓ∞ perturbations, which approximates the maximum norm
072   of the gradient operator and assumes that all activation patterns in the hidden layers are independent
073   and possible. FGL serves as an upper bound for the exact global Lipschitz constant and has been
074   utilized in previous works (Raghunathan et al., 2018; Fazlyab et al., 2019; Latorre et al., 2020;
075
      Wang et al., 2022).
076   By exploiting the equivalence relation between FGL and the cut norm problem, our proposed HiQ-
077   Lip method targets the estimation of the FGL which is the upper bound of ℓ∞ global Lipschitz
078   constant for two-layer fully connected neural networks. Initially, we convert the Lipschitz constant
079   estimation of the neural network into a cut-norm problem. Subsequently, we iteratively coarsen the
080   graph structure until obtaining the coarsest graph, solve the cut-norm problem on this graph using
081
      CIMs, and finally refine the solution by mapping it back to the original graph. This process results
      in an accurate estimation of the neural network’s global Lipschitz constant.
082
083   Simulation experiments on fully connected neural networks demonstrate that HiQ-Lip achieves per-
084   formance comparable to existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods in estimating the ℓ∞ global Lips-
085   chitz constant for two-layer networks, while exhibiting faster computation speeds. When extended to
086   multi-layer fully connected neural networks, applying our approach to every two consecutive layers
087
      yields reasonable estimates within a valid range. The extension of HiQ-Lip provides tighter esti-
      mates compared to the naive upper bound approach (Weight-Matrix-Norm-Product, MP method),
088
      particularly excelling in shallower networks. Comparing SOTA methods, especially in terms of
089
      running time, HiQ-Lip and HiQ-Lip extensions show up to two times speedup on two-layer neural
090   networks and up to one hundred times speedup on multi-layer neural networks.
091
092   Our method is heuristic in nature and aims to obtain very approximate solutions for FGL estimation,
093
      and in the experiments, we obtain very close results to the SOTA method Geolip, which validates
      the effectiveness of HiQ-Lip.
