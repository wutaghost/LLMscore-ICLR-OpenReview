009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Convolutional neural networks (CNNs), including CORnet, have become essential
012            models for predicting neural activity and behavior in visual tasks. However, their
013            ability to capture complex cognitive functions, such as numerosity discrimina-
014            tion, remains a topic of debate. Numerosity—the ability to perceive and estimate
015            the number of items in a visual scene—is believed to be represented by special-
016            ized ‘number-detector’ units within CNNs. In this study, we utilize CORnet, a
017            specialized type of CNN inspired by brain anatomy, which also effectively cap-
018
               tures the variance in human behavioral data, to address the limitations of classical
               representational similarity analysis (RSA), which assumes equal importance for
019
               all features. We apply pruning, a feature selection technique that identifies and
020
               retains the most behaviorally relevant units. Our results demonstrate that number-
021            detector units are not critical for population-level representations of numerosity,
022            challenging their proposed significance in previous studies. These results can have
023            implications for both machine learning and neuroscience.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Early breakthroughs in the study of biological vision served as the foundation for convolutional neu-
029   ral networks (CNNs; Lindsay, 2021). Like the brain, these hierarchical models consist of several
030   feedforward layers, with each layer comprising numerous artificial units that mimic neurons. Since
031   then, CNNs have evolved into state-of-the-art models for predicting neural activity and behavior in
032   visual tasks (Khaligh-Razavi & Kriegeskorte, 2014; Yamins et al., 2014; Yamins & DiCarlo, 2016;
033
      Cichy et al., 2016). For example, it has been demonstrated that CNNs trained on an object classi-
      fication task can account for the brain responses of both humans’ and monkeys’ inferior temporal
034
      cortex (IT), a key region for object recognition (Khaligh-Razavi & Kriegeskorte, 2014).
035
036   But what happens when the images contain multiple objects? Perceiving and representing the num-
037   ber of items in a set—known as numerosity—without counting is considered a core and ancient
038   cognitive ability shared by humans and many animal species, often referred to as ‘number sense’
039
      (Dehaene, 2011). Specialized neurons, or ‘number neurons’, that are tuned to the number of items in
      a visual display have been identified in numerically naive monkeys (Viswanathan & Nieder, 2013),
040
      crows (Wagener et al., 2018), and untrained 10-day-old domestic chicks (Kobylkov et al., 2022),
041
      suggesting that numerosity is automatically represented in the brain. Brain imaging studies have
042   also pinpointed regions in the parietal cortex that are responsible for representing numerosity in both
043   adults (Piazza et al., 2004; Castaldi et al., 2019; Karami et al., 2023) and preverbal infants (Izard
044   et al., 2008; Hyde et al., 2010; Edwards et al., 2015), demonstrating this ability at the population
045   level. Additionally, fMRI decoding in the parietal regions of adults has been linked to behavioral nu-
046   merosity discrimination acuity (Lasne et al., 2019). Collectively, these findings highlight the critical
047   role of parietal brain activity in human numerosity discrimination.
048   Recently, it has been shown that number-detector units, analogous to number neurons recorded in
049   the prefrontal and parietal cortices of monkeys, can emerge in the final layers of CNNs trained for
050   visual object recognition (Nasr et al., 2019) and even in completely untrained CNNs (Kim et al.,
051   2021). However, Karami et al. (2023), using representational similarity analysis (RSA; Kriegesko-
052   rte, 2008), demonstrated that CNNs fall short of explaining the variance in numerosity representation
053   observed in fMRI data from the human parietal cortex. Further analysis using multidimensional scal-
      ing (MDS; Kruskal, 1964) revealed significant differences in the geometric structure of numerosity

054
      representations between human parietal regions and CNNs (Karami et al., 2023). In the classical
055   RSA framework used by Karami (2024), all features contribute equally to the final dissimilarity es-
056   timate. However, this ‘equal weights’ assumption conflicts with the notion that, when comparing
057   representational dissimilarity matrices (RDMs), certain features may carry more informative content
058   than others. As a result, this approach can underestimate the true correspondence between the model
059   and a specific brain region or behavior (Kaniuth & Hebart, 2022; Tarigopula et al., 2023). More-
060   over, the classical RSA approach may overemphasize non-relevant units by treating them as equally
061   important as units that carry behaviorally relevant information, such as number-detector units in our
062   case.
063   To assess the relevance of number-detector units in representing numerosity at the population level
064   within CNNs, we employed a pruning approach. Pruning is a feature selection technique used to
065   identify and retain the most relevant parts of a model, such as specific weights or activations, that best
066   align with the behavior data and improve predictions (Flechas Manrique et al., 2023; Bao & Hasson,
067   2024; Truong et al., 2024). This approach is based on the observation that pretrained models often
068   contain redundant information (Cheng et al., 2015; Frankle & Carbin, 2018). Therefore, using the
069
      entire model may not be necessary for a specific task, such as numerosity discrimination in our case.
      Specifically, we pruned different CNN architectures based on the number RDM, which captures the
070
      behavioral signature of numerosity perception in humans. This matrix serves as a benchmark for
071
      comparing the alignment of CNN representations with human numerosity processing. Our results
072   revealed that number-detector units are not critical for representing numerosity at the population
073   level within these networks. Furthermore, we demonstrated that the semantic embeddings of written
074   numbers extracted from GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014)—a simple distributional semantic model,
075   which has been shown to predict human performance in numerosity comparison tasks (Rinaldi et al.,
076   2022; Ren & Libertus, 2024)—better explain the variance in activity from pruned units compared
077   to number-detector units. Together these findings suggest that while number-detector units may
078   emerge in specific layers of CNNs, they do not play a significant role in capturing the broader,
079   population-level representation of numerosity, as reflected in human behavioral data.

081   2     M ETHODS AND EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
082
083   2.1   S TIMULI AND T RAINING THE CNN
084
085   To investigate number-detector units in CNNs we used CORnet-Z and CORnet-S, models with four
086   anatomically mapped areas (V1, V2, V4, and IT) followed by a decoder layer. CORnet-Z is the
087
      simplest network in the CORnet family and a lightweight alternative to AlexNet. CORnet-S also
      has recurrent connectivity and is designed to maximize Brain-Score, which measures the alignment
088
      of artificial neural networks with human brain activity and behavior (Schrimpf et al., 2018). Each
089
      anatomically mapped area in the CORnet consists of a single convolution, followed by a ReLU
090   nonlinearity, max pooling and the decoder is a 1000-way linear classifier (Kubilius et al., 2018;
091   2019).
092
093
      We chose CORnet-Z and CORnet-S because it balanced the resemblance to the architectures used
      by previous studies on numerosity and because it well fit the visual system. We used three versions
094
      of the CORnet:
095
096   1. the completely untrained version with randomly initialized weights to reveal the effect of archi-
097      tecture alone (Cichy et al., 2016),
098   2. a version trained on object recognition using the ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009), which
099      contained 1.2 million images of objects over 1000 categories, as it has been used in a previous
100      study by Nasr et al. (2019), and
101
      3. a version of the network was specifically trained to discriminate between ten numerosity values:
102      6, 7, 9, 10, 12, 14, 17, 20, 24, and 29. We specifically trained the networks to discriminate
103      between numbers because Mistry et al. (2023) demonstrated that training a CNN for numerosity
104      discrimination significantly reorganizes the number-detector units. To avoid flawed stimulus
105      design (Park, 2022), where low-level visual features like size or dot density correlate with the
106      number of dots, we used the method introduced by DeWind et al. (2015) to generate the dot sets.
107      A sample of the generated stimuli used for training the network is shown in Figure 1A. Following
         the approach of Mistry et al. (2023), we first initialized the network with weights pre-trained on

      2.2   I DENTIFICATION OF N UMBER -D ETECTOR U NITS
140
      Following Nasr et al. (2019) and Kim et al. (2021), we used ANOVA to find number-detector units.
141   Specifically, a three-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with three factors - numerosity, total field
142   area, and average item area - was applied to select number-detector units. The goal was to identify
143   units that had a significant change in response across different numerosities while maintaining an
144   invariant response across variations in total field area and average item area, as well as across the
145   three interactions among pairs of factors, and the interaction among all of the three. A unit is marked
146   as a number-detector if it shows a significant change for numerosity (p < 0.01) but no significant
147   change for the other two factors or any of the interactions. Units that did not meet these criteria
148   were classified as non-selective. This method of selecting number-detector units is analogous to the
149
      method that has been used to detect numerosity-sensitive neurons in monkeys and humans.
150
151   2.3   R EPRESENTATIONAL S IMILARITY A NALYSIS BETWEEN RDM S
152
153
      To create the CNNs’ RDMs, we used the 32 activity vectors obtained by averaging the 100 instances
      per condition. We chose 100 instances to address concerns about the limited number of sample
154
      images used in previous studies, such as Nasr et al. (2019), which were criticized for this limitation
155
      (Zhang & Wu, 2020). The CNNs’ RDMs were constructed using 1 – Pearson correlation between
156   the activations of each layer for each pair of conditions. The number RDM (Figure 1C) was based on
157   the logarithmic distance between the pairs of conditions in terms of numerosity. RDMs from GloVe
158   embeddings were created using 1 – Pearson correlation of 300-dimensional embeddings between
159   the written forms of four numbers: ‘6’, ‘10’, ‘17’, and ‘29’. To achieve the final RSA scores,
160   we extracted the lower triangular portion of each RDM, excluding the diagonal, and vectorized
161   them. These vectors, referred to as representational dissimilarity vectors (RDVs), were then used to
      compute the Pearson correlation between each CNN’s RDV and the number RDV.

      2.3.1     P RUNING THE L AYERS OF M ODELS
163
164   The pruning algorithm, which is adapted from Tarigopula et al. (2023), involves three steps. First,
165   the importance of each unit is assessed by removing it from the full set of units. Each time a unit
166   is removed, a new RDM is computed, and its score is compared to the number RDM. A significant
167   drop in the score compared to the full set RDM indicates that the unit is important for matching
168
      the number RDM, while a smaller drop or an increase in score suggests the unit is unimportant
      or possibly encoding noise. Second, all units are ranked based on their importance scores, from
169
      highest to lowest. Third, starting with an empty activation vector, units are sequentially added back
170
      in the order of their ranking. After each addition, the fit between the RDM derived from the new
171   embedding and the number RDM is re-evaluated. We truncate and select the set of neurons when
172   the highest RSA score is achieved, and refer these units as the ‘retained units’ after pruning.
173
174
      It is important to note that the pruning procedure was guided by the number RDM (figure 1C), not the
      GloVe RDM, as our goal when introducing GloVe RDM is to assess how well pruning generalizes
175
      from the vision domain to the language domain. The GloVe RDM serves as an evaluation rather
176
      than a supervised signal for selecting units.

      4   D ISCUSSION
282
283
      It has been emphasized that cognitive and behavioral functions emerge from the collective dynamics
      of neural populations, rather than isolated neuronal activity (Yuste, 2015), underscoring the impor-
284
      tance of population-level analysis for understanding complex behaviors like numerosity discrim-
285
      ination. In this context, RSA has become a widely used method for comparing representational
286   spaces from human brain activity, behavioral data, and computational models. However, classical
287   RSA assumes that each feature is equally important, making it difficult to interpret the contribution
288   of individual features. In our case, classical RSA does not provide insights into the importance of
289   number-detector units identified in CNNs. To address this limitation, we applied pruning, a feature
290   selection technique used to identify and retain the most relevant components of a model—such as
291   specific weights or activations—that best align with behavioral data.
292   Pruning revealed that almost none of the number-detector units contribute to the representation of
293   numerosity at the population level, casting doubt on their significance for numerosity discrimination.
294   These findings are also consistent with previous work by Mistry et al. (2023), which demonstrated
295   that training a CNN for numerosity discrimination significantly reorganizes the number-detector
296   units, and by Chapalain et al. (2024), which showed that dot-pattern-tuned units do not general-
297   ize to object-number information in photorealistic stimuli. Furthermore, we demonstrated that the
298   pruned units more effectively explain the variance in the GloVe embeddings of the written form of
299   numbers. This result revealed that pruning can better align the visual and written forms of number
300
      representation.
