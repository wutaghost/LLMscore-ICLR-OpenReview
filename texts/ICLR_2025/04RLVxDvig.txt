011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success, but their grow-
014            ing size leads to significant challenges in efficiency and cost. This work explores
015            parameter-efficient deep learning, aiming to achieve comparable performance with
016            fewer parameters and floating-point operations (FLOPs). We introduce NanoMoE,
017            a novel family of parameter-efficient building blocks inspired by the Mixture of Ex-
018            perts (MoE) framework. NanoMoE offers a modular and efficient replacement for
019
               fully connected layers within traditional neural networks. We instantiate NanoMoE
               with three variants of increasing complexity and theoretically demonstrate its su-
020
               perior expressivity compared to low-rank factorization with minimal parameter
021
               increase. Empirical results validate that NanoMoE achieves superior model quality
022            compared to low-rank factorization under the same parameter or FLOP budget,
023            confirming its enhanced efficiency.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional performance (Brown et al., 2020;
030   Devlin et al., 2018), yet they still exhibit limitations in factual accuracy (Ji et al., 2023), logical
031   reasoning (Teng et al., 2023), and mathematical proficiency (Collins et al., 2024). The pursuit of
032   ever-increasing model size to overcome these limitations, as seen in the progression from GPT-3
033
      (175B parameters) (Brown et al., 2020) to PaLM (540B parameters) (Chowdhery et al., 2023) and
      GPT-4 (estimated at 1.8 trillion parameters) (Achiam et al., 2023), leads to significant challenges in
034
      parameter efficiency, training efficiency, and inference costs. These challenges are further amplified
035
      in multimodal models (Baltrušaitis et al., 2018), where diverse application scenarios demand complex
036   and computationally expensive architectures.
037
038   This trend, however, raises a crucial question: can we achieve comparable performance and learn-
039
      ing capacity with a significant reduction in parameters and floating-point operations (FLOPs)?
      The pursuit of parameter and FLOP efficiency is paramount due to several critical factors. Firstly,
040
      reducing the number of parameters directly translates to lower memory requirements, enabling the
041
      deployment of LLMs on resource-constrained devices and reducing the financial burden of model
042   storage (Xu et al., 2024). Secondly, minimizing FLOPs lowers computational costs, leading to faster
043   inference times, decreased energy consumption, and a reduced carbon footprint (Strubell et al., 2020).
044   This efficiency is essential for real-time applications, accessibility, and environmental sustainability.
045   Finally, by optimizing model size and computational complexity, we can promote wider accessibility,
046   enabling researchers and developers with limited resources to leverage the power of LLMs, fostering
047   innovation and broader participation in the field.
048   Addressing the escalating computational demands of LLMs necessitates the design of parameter-
049   efficient building blocks. While parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) (Ding et al., 2023; Han et al.,
050   2024) has garnered considerable attention for adapting pre-trained models by optimizing injected
051   adapters (Rebuffi et al., 2018; Houlsby et al., 2019), such as the low-rank adaptation (LoRA) method
052   that injects low-rank factorized adapters into dense layers (Hu et al., 2022), the need for increased
053   efficiency extends to the entire training process, including pre-training. Rather than solely focusing
      on parameter-efficient adapters injected alongside existing layers, we propose exploring parameter

054
      efficiency within the original layers of the pre-trained model, enabling enhanced learning capacity
055   during the pre-training stage. We formally define this problem as parameter-efficient deep learning.
056
057   To address this problem, we introduce NanoMoE, a novel neural network structure inspired by the
058
      Mixture of Experts (MoE) framework. MoE draws inspiration from real-world problem-solving,
      where complex issues often necessitate specialized expertise. MoE models utilize "experts," special-
059
      ized sub-models focusing on specific knowledge areas, with a gating network intelligently routing
060
      input queries to the most relevant experts. This facilitates efficient model capacity utilization and
061   adaptability across diverse tasks. While the MoE concept originated in the early 1990s (Jacobs
062   et al., 1991), recent advancements, such as the sparse MoE layer introduced by Shazeer et al. (2016),
063   have revitalized its application in large-scale models. As LLMs continue to grow and application
064   scenarios become more specialized, MoE offers a compelling pathway to address both general and
065   domain-specific tasks within a unified framework, proving particularly valuable for multimodal mod-
066   els handling diverse data and feature relationships. The success of models like Mistral 8x7B (Jiang
067   et al., 2024), which outperforms the larger Llama 2 (Touvron et al., 2023) with fewer parameters,
068   underscores the potential of MoE in achieving comparable or superior performance with reduced
069
      computational resources. While recent LLMs like Mistral 8x7B employ MoE to combine large
      sub-models, NanoMoE is designed as a modular and efficient replacement for fully connected layers
070
      within traditional neural networks. This granular approach allows for the integration of multiple
071
      NanoMoE blocks within a single model, potentially yielding significant gains in performance and
072   flexibility without a dramatic increase in parameter count.
073
074   Our work makes the following contributions:
075
             • We propose NanoMoE, a novel family of parameter-efficient building blocks for neural
076
               networks inspired by the MoE framework. We instantiate NanoMoE with three variants:
077
               NanoMoE-I, NanoMoE-II, and NanoMoE-III, offering increasing levels of complexity and
078            computational cost.
079
             • We theoretically demonstrate that NanoMoE offers strictly greater expressivity compared to
080
               low-rank factorization while requiring only a minimal increase in parameters.
081
082          • We empirically validate that NanoMoE achieves superior model quality compared to low-
083
               rank factorization. Given a budget of parameters or FLOPs, we compare the train and test
               loss of NanoMoE against low-rank factorization and observe that NanoMoE consistently
084
               demonstrates superior performance, confirming its enhanced parameter and FLOP efficiency.

094
      exponential number of experts as a function of the network depth. Lepikhin et al. (2021) replace
095

096   MoE layer. The Switch Transformer (Fedus et al., 2022) integrates the MoE design into the T5 model

      Low-Rank Factorization Revisited To motivate our proposed method, we first revisit the well-
133
      established low-rank factorization technique for enhancing parameter efficiency in neural networks.
134   Consider a fully connected layer with weight matrix W ∈ Rd2 ×d1 , bias vector b ∈ Rd2 , and
135   activation function σ, where d1 and d2 denote the input and output dimensions, respectively. Let xin
136   and xout denote the input and output of this layer. The standard forward pass is given by
137
                                             xout = σ(W xin + b).
138
139   The dense weight matrix W contains d1 d2 parameters.
140
      Low-rank factorization replaces W with the product of two matrices U ∈ Rd2 ×r and V ∈ Rr×d1 ,
141   where r < min(d1 , d2 ) is the chosen rank. This yields the modified forward pass:
142
143                                          xout = σ(U V xin + b).
144   This factorization reduces the number of parameters to (d1 + d2 )r, which is significantly less than
145   d1 d2 (the number of parameters in the dense weight matrix) when the rank r is small enough.
146

148   row-wise and column-wise blocks, respectively:

217   represent. We show that this space is strictly larger than that of low-rank factorization and compute
218   the maximum rank attainable by NanoMoE. Recall the parameter counts for low-rank factorization
219   and NanoMoE summarized in Table 1. For example, compared to low-rank factorization, NanoMoE-I
220   introduces an additional K 2 parameters, but achieves a maximum rank K times that of low-rank
221   factorization, as shown in Theorem 1.

405   5   C ONCLUSION
406
407   This work introduces NanoMoE, a novel parameter-efficient building block designed to replace fully-
408   connected layers and low-rank factorization layers in neural networks. We theoretically demonstrate
409   that NanoMoE offers strictly greater expressivity compared to low-rank factorization, while requiring
410   only a minimal increase in parameters. Furthermore, our empirical results consistently validate that
411
      NanoMoE achieves superior performance in terms of both training and test loss across various FLOPs
      budgets and parameter constraints. These findings suggest that NanoMoE presents a promising
412
      avenue for developing more efficient and effective neural network architectures.
413
414
415   6   F UTURE W ORK
416
417   Our study presents several opportunities for future work. First, while our experiments showcase the
418   parameter efficiency of NanoMoE, exploring principled methods for selecting the optimal hyperpa-
419   rameters K (number of experts) and r is crucial to maximize this efficiency. Second, we haven’t
420
      investigated the performance of NanoMoE within the context of LoRA-type fine-tuning (Hu et al.,
      2022). Additionally, exploring NanoMoE’s potential in pre-training large language models and
421
      employing stacked NanoMoE architectures (e.g., replacing all fully-connected layers with NanoMoE
422
      layers) are promising avenues for future research.
