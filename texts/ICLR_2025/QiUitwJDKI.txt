009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Outside of transfer learning settings, reinforcement learning agents start their learn-
012            ing process from a clean slate. As a result, such agents have to go through a
013            slow process to learn even the most obvious skills required to solve a problem.
014            In this paper, we present I NNATE C ODER, a system that leverages human knowl-
015            edge encoded in foundation models to provide programmatic policies that encode
016            “innate skills” in the form of temporally extended actions, or options. In contrast
017            to existing approaches to learning options, I NNATE C ODER learns them from the
018
               general human knowledge encoded in foundation models in a zero-shot setting, and
               not from the knowledge the agent gains by interacting with the environment. Then,
019
               I NNATE C ODER searches for a programmatic policy by combining the programs en-
020
               coding these options into larger and more complex programs. We hypothesized that
021            I NNATE C ODER’s way of learning and using options could improve the sampling
022            efficiency of current methods for learning programmatic policies. We evaluated our
023            hypothesis in MicroRTS and Karel the Robot, two challenging domains. Empirical
024            results support our hypothesis, since they show that I NNATE C ODER is more sample
025            efficient than versions of the system that do not use options or learn the options
026            from experience. The policies I NNATE C ODER learns are competitive and often
027            outperform current state-of-the-art agents in both domains.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Outside of transfer learning settings, deep reinforcement learning (DRL) agents begin their learning
033
      process with randomly initialized neural networks. As a result, DRL agents must learn from scratch
      even the most basic skills required to solve a problem. In this paper, we harness the general human
034
      knowledge encoded in foundation models to endow agents with helpful skills before they even
035
      start interacting with the environment. This is achieved by using programmatic representations of
036   policies (Trivedi et al., 2021)—programs written in a domain-specific language encoding policies—
037   and the foundation models’ ability to write computer programs. Depending on the language used,
038   programmatic policies were shown to generalize better to unseen scenarios (Inala et al., 2020) and to
039   be human-interpretable (Verma et al., 2018; Bastani et al., 2018). In addition to these advantages and
040   loosely inspired by the innate abilities of animals (Tinbergen, 1951), we show that programmatic
041   representations of policies allow us to harness helpful “innate skills” from foundation models.
042   Given a natural-language description of the problem that the agent needs to learn to solve, our system,
043   which we call I NNATE C ODER, queries a foundation model for programs that encode policies to solve
044   the problem. Although the programs the model generates are unlikely to encode policies that solve
045   the problem, we hypothesize that the set of sub-programs we obtain from these programs can encode
046   helpful temporally extended actions, or options (Sutton et al., 1999). We consider options as functions
047   the agent can call and that will tell it how to act for a number of steps (Precup et al., 1998).
048   Options can ease the agent’s learning process in different ways. For example, options can allow
049   the agent to better explore the problem space (Machado et al., 2017; Bellemare et al., 2020) or can
050   transfer knowledge between different tasks (Konidaris & Barto, 2007). In this paper, we present
051   a novel way of learning options with foundation models. We also present a novel way of using
052   the learned options, which is inspired by recent work on learning semantic spaces of programming
053   languages (Moraes & Lelis, 2024). We leverage the compositional nature of the programmatic options
      we harness from a foundation model to learn the underlying semantic space of the programming

      I NNATE C ODER’s approach to harnessing options from foundation models contrasts with previous
      approaches to automatically learning them, e.g., (Tessler et al., 2017; Bacon et al., 2017; Igl et al.,
077
      2020; Klissarov & Machado, 2023). This is because options are harnessed from the general knowledge
078
      encoded in a foundation model, as opposed to the knowledge the agent gains by interacting with the
079   environment. This zero-shot approach to learning options is enabled by the use of a domain-specific
080   language to bridge the gap between the high-level knowledge encoded in foundation models and the
081   low-level knowledge required at the sensorimotor control level of the agent (Klissarov et al., 2024).
082   For example, foundation models trained on Internet data likely encode the knowledge that, to win a
083   match of a real-time strategy game, the player must collect resources and build structures, which will
084   allow for the training of the units needed to win the game. However, the model cannot issue low-level
085   actions in real time to control dozens of units to accomplish this plan. I NNATE C ODER bridges this
086   gap by distilling the knowledge of the model into options that can be executed in real time.
087   We evaluated our hypothesis that foundation models can generate helpful programmatic options in
088   the domains of MicroRTS, a challenging real-time strategy game (Ontañón, 2017), and Karel the
089   Robot (Pattis, 1994), which has been used as a benchmark for program synthesis and reinforcement
090   learning algorithms (Bunel et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Shin et al., 2018; Trivedi et al., 2021). The
091   results in both domains support our hypothesis, since I NNATE C ODER was more sample-efficient than
092   versions of the system that do not use options or learn options from experience. We also show that
093
      the policies I NNATE C ODER learns are competitive and often outperform the current state-of-the-art
      algorithms. I NNATE C ODER is inexpensive because it uses the foundation model a small number of
094
      times as a pre-processing step, making it an accessible system to smaller labs and companies.

159   Figure 2 shows a schematic view of I NNATE C ODER, which receives an MDP and a DSL and returns a
160   programmatic policy, denoted n∗ . I NNATE C ODER is composed of three components: one for learning

      3.1   L EARNING O PTIONS
163
164   An option is a program encoding a policy that the agent can invoke at specific states. Once invoked,
165   the program tells the agent what to do for a number of steps. Once completed, the option “returns”
166   the control back to the agent. An option ω is defined with a tuple (Iω , πω , Tω ), where Iω is the set of
167   states in which the option can be initiated; πω is the policy the agent follows once ω starts; Tω is a
168   function that returns the probability in which ω terminates at a given state st . I NNATE C ODER uses a
169   foundation model to learn programs, written in a given DSL, encoding options. The programs receive
170   a state of the MDP and return the action the agent should take at that state, thus encoding πω .
171   We assume that the set Iω is the set S of all states of the MDP, which means that the program can be
172   invoked in any state. However, note that the program may not return any actions in a given state s,
173   which is equivalent to s not being in Iω . For example, “if b1 then c1 ” returns the action given in c1
174   only if condition b1 is satisfied in the state in which the option was queried; it returns no action for
175   states that do not satisfy b1 . The option termination criterion Tω is also determined by the program;
176   the option terminates when the program terminates. This termination criterion means that, depending
177   on the DSL, options have an internal state, representing the line in the program in which the execution
178
      will continue the next time the agent interacts with the environment. For example, if the option “c1 c2 ”
      is invoked for state st and c1 returns an action, then the agent’s action in st+1 is determined by c2 .
179
180   Programmatic options are harnessed from a foundation model as follows. We provide a natural
181   language description of the MDP and the Backus-Naur form of the DSL to the model. The model then
182   provides a set of m programs written in the DSL encoding policies for the MDP. While it is unlikely
183   that the model can provide policies that can maximize the expected return for any MDP of interest,
184
      we hypothesize that these programmatic policies can be broken up into sub-programs that can encode
      helpful agent behavior. Each program p is broken up into one sub-program for each sub-tree rooted
185
      at a non-terminal symbol in the AST of p. For example, for the program “if b1 then c1 c2 ” we obtain
186
      the sub-programs “if b1 then c1 c2 ”, “b1 ”, “c1 ”, “c2 ”, and “c1 c2 ”. These sub-programs form a set of
187   options O, which I NNATE C ODER uses to approximate the semantic space of the DSL. Note that this
188   set of options is generated zero-shot, before the agent starts interacting with the environment.
189

191   3.2   A PPROXIMATING THE S EMANTIC S PACE WITH O PTIONS
192
193
      Methods for searching for programmatic policies traditionally search in the space of programs defined
      by the context-free grammar of the DSL (Koza, 1992; Verma et al., 2018; Carvalho et al., 2024). We
194
      refer to this type of space as the syntax space, since it is based on the syntax of the language.
195
196
197
      Definition 1 (Syntax Space) The syntax space of a DSL D is defined by (D, Nkx , I, E). With JDK
      defining the set of candidate programs, or solutions, Nkx (x is for “syntax”) is the syntax neighborhood
198
      function that receives a candidate and returns k candidates from JDK. I is the distribution of initial
199
      candidates. Finally, E is the evaluation function, which receives a candidate in JDK and returns a
200   value in R.
201
202
      A common way of defining the distribution of initial candidates I is through a procedure that starts
203
      with a string that is the initial symbol of the grammar and iteratively, and uniformly at random,
204   samples a production rule to replace a non-terminal symbol in the string. In the example of Figure 1,
205   we replace the initial symbol I with “if(B) then C” with probability 1.0, since this is the only rule
206   available; then, B is replaced with either “b1 ” or “b2 ” with probability 0.5 each. This iterative process
207   stops once the string contains only terminal symbols. If a probabilistic context-free grammar is
208   available, then the distribution I can be defined through the same process, but using the probabilities
209   from the grammar as opposed to a uniform distribution over production rules (Trivedi et al., 2021).
210   The syntax neighborhood function Nkx defines the structure of the search space, as it determines the
211   set of candidate solutions (programs that encode a policy) that the search procedure can evaluate from
212   a given candidate n. Given a candidate n, Nkx (n) returns a set of k neighbors of n. These candidates
213   are generated by selecting uniformly at random a node that represents a non-terminal symbol in the
214   AST of n. Then, the sub-tree rooted at the selected node is replaced by another sub-tree generated
215   using the process described for I, but starting at the non-terminal symbol the node represents. This
      process of replacing a sub-tree in n is repeated k times, to generate k possibly different neighbors of


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      n. Finally, E is an approximation of the value function of the policy encoded in n from a set of initial
217
      states s0 , V̂ n (s0 ); we obtain V̂ n (s0 ) by averaging the returns after rolling n out from states s0 .
218
219   Moraes & Lelis (2024) showed that searching in the syntax space can be inefficient because often the
220   neighbors n′ of a candidate n encode policies that are semantically identical to n; the programs differ
221   in terms of syntax, but encode exactly the same agent behavior. As a result, the search process wastes
222
      time evaluating the same agent behavior. Their solution is to approximate the underlying semantic
      space of the language, where neighbor programs are similar in terms of syntax, but are likely to differ
223
      in terms of behavior. In their setting, the agent learns programs for a set of tasks, which are used
224
      to induce the semantic space, and the induced space is used in downstream tasks. I NNATE C ODER
225   overcomes the requirement to operate on a stream of problems by using a foundation model to learn
226   the options in a zero-shot setting. A semantic space is defined as follows.
227
228   Definition 2 (Semantic Space) The semantic space of a DSL D is defined by (D, Nkm , I, E), where
229   I and E are identical to the syntax space (Definition 1). The function Nkm (m is for “semantics”) is
230   a semantic neighborhood function that also receives a candidate and returns k candidates from JDK.
231
232   We define the function Nkm with a set of options Ω, where each option ω in Ω represents a different
233   agent behavior. A neighbor of candidate n is then obtained by selecting, uniformly at random, a node
234   c in the AST of n that represents a non-terminal symbol. Then, we replace the sub-tree rooted at c
235   with the AST of an option ω in Ω. The option ω is selected, uniformly at random, among those in Ω
236   whose AST root represents the same non-terminal symbol c represents. By matching the non-terminal
237   symbols when selecting ω, we match ω with the type of the sub-tree that is removed from n. Similarly
238   to Nkx , Nkm generates k possibly different neighbors by repeating this process k times.
239   The intuition for requiring the options in Ω to encode different agent behaviors is to increase the
240   chance of seeing neighbors with different behaviors. For example, if many of the options in Ω
241   encoded exactly the same behavior, then the chances of all neighbors of a program also encoding the
242   same behavior would be higher, which is wasteful from a sample efficiency perspective. Next, we
243   describe how we obtain Ω from the set of options O we harnessed from a foundation model.
244   We filter the set of options O harnessed from the foundation model into a set Ω of behaviorally differ-
245   ent options by having the agent interact with the environment, as described in previous work (Moraes
246   & Lelis, 2024). Assuming an MDP with discrete actions, each option in O is evaluated in an ordered
247   set of states S of the MDP. This set S is obtained by rolling out all options o ∈ O once, from an
248   initial state s0 sampled from µ. The states s observed in this process form S. Then, every option is
249   invoked for each state in S, thus forming an action signature Ao for each o. An action signature is
250   a vector with one action for each state in S, where the i-th entry of Ao corresponds to the action o
251
      returns to the i-th state in S. The set of options Ω is given by one option for each observed Ao . If
      multiple options have the same signature, we arbitrarily select one of them.
252
253   The number of samples required to filter the set of options into a set of options with different behaviors
254   is negligible: it uses less than 1% of the computation in our experiments. Programs that cannot be
255   rolled out (e.g., Boolean expressions and do not issue actions) are not included in the set of options.
256
257   3.3   S EARCHING IN S EMANTIC S PACE
258
259   I NNATE C ODER uses stochastic hill-climbing (SHC) to search in the semantic space of a given DSL
260   D for a policy that maximizes the agent’s return. SHC starts its search by sampling a candidate
261   program n from I. In every iteration, SHC evaluates all k neighbors of n in terms of their E-value.
262   The search then moves on to the best neighbor of n in terms of E, and this process is repeated from
263   there. SHC stops if none of the neighbors has an E-value that is better than the current candidate, that
264   is, it reaches a local optimum. SHC uses a restarting strategy: once SHC reaches a local optimum, if
265   SHC has not yet exhausted is search budget, it restarts from another initial candidate sampled from I.
266
      SHC returns the best solution, denoted n∗ , encountered in all restarts of the search.
267   I NNATE C ODER does not search solely in the semantic space, but mixes both syntax and semantic
268   spaces in the search. This is because the set of options might cover only a part of the space of
269   programs the DSL induces. To guarantee that I NNATE C ODER can access all programs in JDK, with
      probability ϵ, SHC uses the syntax neighborhood function in the search, and with probability 1 − ϵ, it


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      uses the semantic one. We use ϵ = 0.4 in our experiments. We chose this value because it performed
271   better in preliminary experiments than the value of 0.2 used in previous work (Moraes & Lelis, 2024).
272
273   Although other local search algorithms could be used with I NNATE C ODER, such as Simulated
274
      Annealing (Kirkpatrick et al., 1983; Husien & Schewe, 2016), we use SHC because previous work
      showed that it performs well in our test domains (Moraes et al., 2023; Carvalho et al., 2024).

277   4       E MPIRICAL E VALUATION
278
279   Although foundation models are unlikely to generate programs that encode policies for fully solving
280   MDPs, we hypothesize that the programs they generate can be broken up into smaller programs that
281   serve as helpful options. We evaluated the usefulness of these programmatic options by measuring
282   the sampling efficiency of search algorithms searching in the semantic spaces induced by them. We
283   evaluate I NNATE C ODER on MicroRTS (Ontañón, 2017) and Karel the Robot (Pattis, 1994).
284
285   MicroRTS MicroRTS is a real-time strategy game that requires the agent to control dozens of
286   units in real-time, thus making it impractical to use foundation models to decide on agent actions
287   directly. We use the following maps from the MicroRTS repository,1 with the map size in brackets:
288
      NoWhereToRun (9×8), basesWorkers (24×24), and BWDistantResources (32×32), and BloodBath
      (64 × 64). We use these maps because they differ in size and structure. Since MicroRTS is a multi-
289
      agent problem, we use 2L, a self-play algorithm, to learn programmatic policies (Moraes et al., 2023).
290
      MicroRTS is not a symmetric game and the outcome of the game depends on the starting location of
291   the players. To ensure fairness, each pair of policies plays two matches on each map, so that each
292   player can start at each of the two initial locations; the results are then averaged out of these two
293   runs. In the context of 2L, I NNATE C ODER is required to solve an MDP in every iteration of self-play
294   (see Appendix M). We use a new version of the MicroLanguage as the DSL (Mariño et al., 2021).
295   The language offers specialized functions and an action-prioritization scheme through for-loops,
296   where nested for-loops allow for higher priority of actions. We provide a detailed explanation of the
297   MicroLanguage, as well as images of the maps used, in Appendices H.2 and K, respectively.
298
299   Karel Karel the Robot is an environment originally created for teaching people how to write
300   computer programs, which has later been used as a benchmark domain for reinforcement learning
301   algorithms (Trivedi et al., 2021). Karel is a robot interacting with a grid-world, where it can collect
302
      markers and place markers. We use the following Karel problems, which were designed in previous
      works (Trivedi et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023b): StairClimber, FourCorners, TopOff, Maze, CleanHouse,
303
      Harvester, DoorKey, OneStroke, Seeder, and Snake. The problems differ in terms of structure of the
304
      grids (e.g., where walls are located) and in terms of the task that Karel needs to accomplish. For
305   example, in CleanHouse, Karel needs to collect all markers placed in the grid, while in TopOff it has
306   to place a marker on top of all existing markers. The problem are described in Appendix I. We use
307   the more difficult version of the environment known as “crashable” (Carvalho et al., 2024), where an
308   episode terminates with a negative reward if Karel bumps into a wall. We use the same DSL used in
309   previous work (Trivedi et al., 2021), which we describe in Appendix H.1.
310
311   Baselines The current state-of-the-art methods for both MicroRTS and Karel use programmatic rep-
312   resentations of policies, where the policies are written in the DSLs we use in our experiments (Moraes
313   et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2021). Therefore, we focus on methods that use programmatic representa-
314   tions as baselines. However, we provide comparisons of I NNATE C ODER with deep reinforcement
315
      learning baselines in Appendices B.1 (MicroRTS) and B.2 (Karel). For both MicroRTS and Karel we
      use SHC searching in the syntax space as a baseline, as it represents state-of-the-art performance
316
      in both domains (SHC). We also use two variants of I NNATE C ODER where the options are learned
317
      without the help of a foundation model. These variants can be seen as implementations of the
318   Library-Induced Semantic Spaces (LISS) (Moraes & Lelis, 2024) for a non-transfer learning setting.
319   In the first variant, LISS learns the options as it learns how to solve the problem. In terms of the
320   scheme shown in Figure 2, we skip the “Learning Options” step and build the set of options from the
321   programs returned in every complete search of SHC. That is, when we reach the box “Restart?”, we
322   use the sub-programs of the best program encountered in that search to augment the set of options.

342   Other Specifications All experiments were run on 2.6 GHz CPUs with 12 GB of RAM. We use
343   k = 1, 000 in the neighborhood function. In MicroRTS, SHC is run with a restarting time limit of
344   2, 000 seconds for each self-play iteration. In Karel, since we are solving a single MDP, SHC restarts
345   as many times as possible within the computational budget. For MicroRTS, we query the foundation
346   models 120 times to generate the same number of programs; for Karel, we use 100 programs. We use
347   the same number of programs as the LISS-r baseline. We perform 30 independent runs (seeds) of

      5    R ELATED WORK
491
492
493   Programmatic Policies One of the key challenges in generating programmatic policies is that the
494
      search space is discontinuous and gradient-based optimization cannot be used. Some previous work
      relied on imitation learning to guide the search for policies (Verma et al., 2018; 2019; Bastani et al.,
495
      2018; Milani et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023d). The issue of this imitation learning approach is known
496
      as representation gap (Qiu & Zhu, 2022; Medeiros et al., 2022), where the space of programmatic
497   policies does not include the oracle policy that the system tries to imitate. As a result, the oracle
498   might guide the search to unpromising parts of the space. Previous work tried to learn latent spaces
499   of programming languages that are conducive to search (Trivedi et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023b), which
500   was shown to be outperformed by the syntax space with SHC (Carvalho et al., 2024). Semantic
501   spaces were shown to be more conducive to search than syntax spaces, but required a sequence of
502   tasks, where the agent learns the space in one task and reuses it in others (Moraes & Lelis, 2024).
503   Our work does not require an oracle agent nor a sequence of tasks to learn the semantic space.
504
505
      Options Options were shown to improve the sampling efficiency of learning agents through faster
506
      credit assignment (Mann & Mannor, 2014; Solway et al., 2014), better exploration (Baranes &
507   Oudeyer, 2013; Bellemare et al., 2020), and transfer of knowledge across tasks (Konidaris & Barto,
508   2007; Alikhasi & Lelis, 2024). However, previous methods for learning options require the user to
509   design them before learning starts (Sutton et al., 1999) or to provide considerable information as
510   input to the process, such as the option duration (Frans et al., 2017; Tessler et al., 2017) or the number
511   of options learned (Bacon et al., 2017; Igl et al., 2020). Other methods rely on the agent interaction
512   with the current environment (Achiam et al., 2018; Machado et al., 2018; Jinnai et al., 2020) or with
513   other earlier environments, as in transfer learning approaches (Konidaris & Barto, 2007; Alikhasi &
514   Lelis, 2024). We present a novel way of learning options as they are not learned from the agent’s
515
      experience nor designed by the user, but harnessed from foundation models. While we use options to
      define a search space, future work will explore their use as functions neural policies can call.
516
517   We provide additional related works on “foundation models as policies”, “foundation models for
518   planning”, and “foundation models as search guidance” in Appendix A.
519
520
521   6    C ONCLUSIONS
522
523   If given a single problem to solve, reinforcement learning agents start their learning process from
524   scratch. They have to learn by interacting with the environment even the most basic skills to solve
525   the problem. In this paper, we introduced I NNATE C ODER, a system that equips learning agents with
526   skills, in the form of programmatic options, before the agent starts to interact with the environment.
527   This is achieved by extracting programmatic options from foundation models. We hypothesized that
528   even if the model is unable to write programs encoding strong policies for a problem, sub-programs
529   of the generated program could encode helpful options. We tested our hypothesis in MicroRTS and
530   Karel, two domains in which programmatic policies represent the current state of the art. The policies
531
      I NNATE C ODER generated outperformed, often by a large margin, a baseline that did not attempt to
      learn options; a baseline that learned the options while learning how to solve the problem; a baseline
532
      that learned the options from programs sampled directly from the domain-specific language; and the
533
      foundation model that attempted to generate programmatic policies directly. We also showed that
534   some of the policies I NNATE C ODER generated were competitive or outperformed the winners of
535   previous MicroRTS competitions, including programmatic policies written by human programmers
536   and a deep reinforcement learning agent that used a larger computational budget than we allowed
537   I NNATE C ODER to use. These results place I NNATE C ODER as the current state-of-the-art in both Karel
538   and MicroRTS. Our experiments also showed that I NNATE C ODER’s scheme of using programmatic
539   options to induce semantic spaces allows it to benefit from thousands of options, while most previous
      work can benefit only from dozens or at most hundreds of options (Eysenbach et al., 2019).

751   Foundation Models as Policies Foundation models have been used to perceive, plan, and act
752   (Park et al., 2023), often decomposing long-horizon goals into subtasks (Wang et al., 2023b), and/or
753   integrating additional agent features such as memory (Zhu et al., 2023) and/or automatic learning
754   curricula (Wang et al., 2023a). By contrast, I NNATE C ODER uses the model as a pre-processing
755   step to generate programmatic options, which make it more accessible due to the limited number of
      model calls. Foundation models have also been used to learn reward functions that are later used to


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      train agents (Klissarov et al., 2024). Similarly to our work, the model is used in a pre-processing
757   step. However, in contrast with our work, it learns reward functions, while we learn programmatic
758   options. Moreover, it needs a “diverse” set of states, which are generated by existing and proficient
759   agents; I NNATE C ODER learns in a zero-shot setting and only uses the generated options themselves
760   to generate a set of states used to filter out the options encoding non-novel behaviors.
761
762
763
      Foundation Models for Planning Previous work has used foundation models for generating
      programmatic policies in the context of planning. For example, in generalized planning (GP), the goal
764
      is to synthesize programs that solve classical planning problems (Celorrio et al., 2019); foundation
765
      models have shown promise for GP (Silver et al., 2023). Foundation models has also been used
766   successfully in the context of code generation for decision making in robotics (Liang et al., 2023;
767   Singh et al., 2023). In contrast to these works, we do not attempt to use the model’s generated
768   program as a policy, but we extract options from them and use these options to induce a search space.
769
770
771   Foundation Models as Search Guidance Foundation models have also been used to guide search
772
      algorithms. This includes methods for solving optimization problems (Yang et al., 2023; Guo et al.,
      2023) and to guide Monte Carlo tree search (Zhao et al., 2023). Foundation models were also used in
773
      genetic operators (Lehman et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023c; Meyerson et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023),
774
      including multi-objective (Liu et al., 2023a) and quality-diversity algorithms (Nasir et al., 2023).
775   These works are resource-intensive due to calling the model during the search (Liu et al., 2023a).
776   This contrasts with our work, which uses the model a small number of times in a pre-processing step.

       The K AREL problem sets (Trivedi et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023b) are divided into two parts— K AREL
1112
       and K AREL -H ARD. K AREL consists of six different tasks, while K AREL -H ARD includes four
1113   additional tasks that are comparatively more difficult to solve. In this section, we describe the initial
1114   state and the return function of each task in both K AREL and K AREL -H ARD problem sets.
1115
1116   I.1    K AREL
1117
1118   StairClimber. In this task, the agent operates within a 12 × 12 grid containing stairs formed by
1119   walls. The goal for the agent is to reach a marker located above its position on the stairs. The initial
1120   positions of the agent and the marker are randomly initialized on the stairs. The agent receives an
1121   episodic return of 1 if it successfully reaches the marker, and 0 otherwise. Moving to a position
1122
       outside the contour of the stairs results in a return of −1.
1123
       FourCorners. In this task, the goal for the agent is to place a marker in the four corners of a 12 × 12
1124
       grid. The initial position of the agent is randomly initialized near the wall. The return is calculated as
1125
       the number of corners with one marker divided by four.
1126
1127   TopOff. In this task, the agent is always initialized on the bottom left of a 12 × 12 grid, and the
1128   markers are placed randomly on the bottom row of the grid. The goal for the agent to place markers
1129   on top of the markers present in that row. The return is calculated as the number of markers topped
1130   off divided by the number of markers present in the grid.
1131
1132   Maze. A maze, formed by walls, is randomly configured on a 12 × 12 grid, and a marker and an
1133   agent are randomly placed within it. The goal for the agent is to reach the marker, that yields an
       episodic return of 1. Otherwise, the episodic return is 0.


                                                          21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       CleanHouse. Markers and walls are randomly placed in a 22 × 14 grid, referred to as the apartment.
1135   The position of the agent is also initialized randomly. Its goal is to pick all the markers inside the
1136   apartment. The return is calculated as the number of picked markers divided by the total number of
1137   markers present.
1138
1139
1140
       Harvester. In this task, the agent operates within an 8 × 8 grid filled with markers. The agent is
       placed randomly on the bottom row, with the goal of collecting all markers in the grid. The return is
1141
       calculated as the number of collected markers divided by the total number of cells in the grid.

1695   Self-play algorithms attempt to approximate an optimal policy for two-player games. Iterated Best
1696   Response (IBR) (Lanctot et al., 2017) is perhaps the simplest self-play algorithm that we could use.
1697   IBR starts with an arbitrary policy π0 in JDK for one of the players and approximates a best response
1698   π1 to π0 for the other player. Then it approximates a best response π2 to π1 for the first player. This
1699   process is repeated a number of times m, which is normally determined by a computational budget.
1700   The last resulting policy πm is returned as IBR’s approximate optimal policy for the game.
1701   The self-play process IBR follows generates a sequence of policies for the players, but IBR only
1702   considers the latest policy while computing a best response. Other algorithms, such as Fictitious Play
1703   (FP) (Brown, 1951), compute best responses to a policy that mixes the best responses computed in
1704   all previous iterations. The use of more policies allows the method to find optimal policies even in
1705   games with cyclic dynamics such as Rock, Paper, and Scissors.
1706
       The algorithm we use in our experiments, Local Learner (2L) (Moraes et al., 2023), also considers
1707   multiple policies in its self-play loop. This allows 2L to use more information than IBR. However,
1708   instead of including all policies seen in the process, like FP does, it selects a subset of the policies
1709   seen in the process. Using all policies can be computationally wasteful, as many of the policies are
1710   “redundant”. 2L selects which policies to use based on the information collected during the search
1711   for programmatic policies. We refer the reader to the work of Moraes et al. (2023) for a detailed
1712   explanation of 2L.
