011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent developments in diffusion models have significantly advanced the field
014            of video generation. However, technical challenges still exist in terms of spa-
015
               tiotemporal continuity and content consistency in long video generation. In this
               paper, we propose Motion-Catcher, a diffusion model-based method for multi-
016
               sequence video generation that aims to address the issues of motion inconsistency
017
               and content degradation. By incorporating a motion capture module, the model
018            leverages optical flow information from video sequences to capture both local
019            and global movements, enhancing the motion consistency of the videos. Further-
020            more, a dynamic content prior module is proposed to monitor regions prone to
021            degradation, which helps maintain content consistency throughout the generated
022            videos. Extensive experiments have validated that the proposed Motion-Catcher
023            can generate videos with higher quality in terms of motion continuity and con-
024            sistency. The source code and additional experimental results are available at
025            https://github.com/YuukiGong/Motion-Catcher.

      In recent years, advancements in diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Guo
030
      et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2023; Li et al., 2018) have led to significant improvements
031   in video generation models, particularly in terms of generation efficiency, content quality, and con-
032   sistency. The open-sourcing of many video generation models, pre-trained on large datasets (Chen
033   et al., 2024b; Blattmann et al., 2023b; Rombach et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a;c;
034   Chen et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2023), has drawn more attention from researchers. After substantial
035   training, the generated videos show considerable improvements in stability and diversity. Although
036   current diffusion models have achieved good results in video generation, they still face certain limi-
037   tations when generating longer videos, such as motion inconsistency and content degradation. Given
038   that most video generation models are trained within a limited frame count range, the network strug-
039
      gles to maintain consistency over longer durations. Additionally, hardware limitations often con-
      strain training and inference on longer video streams due to memory restrictions. Considering the
040
      limited hardware, videos can be generated sequentially using an autoregressive approach to mitigate
041
      content degradation. However, final videos assembled from these sequences often exhibit varying
042   speeds and directions of motion in each sequence, leading to temporal inconsistencies in the final
043   output. Fine-tuning methods have been developed in the image generation domain (Hu et al., 2021;
044   Ruiz et al., 2023; Gal et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b) to stabilize the production of desired content
045   and have been successfully adapted to video generation, demonstrating strong performance in short
046   video contexts. However, these methods encounter significant challenges in maintaining motion and
047   content consistency in longer videos. This difficulty primarily stems from the fragmentary nature of
048   autoregressive generation, which frequently results in abrupt transitions. Thus, resolving the issues
049   of motion inconsistency and content degradation in multi-sequence video generation still remains
050
      underexplored comprehensively.

      works (Hu, 2024; Chen et al., 2023c; Ling et al., 2024; Materzynska et al., 2023) to compensate

083   To tackle the challenges of inconsistent motion and the degradation of primary subjects in autore-
084   gressive multi-sequence video generation, we propose Motion-Catcher, a novel method designed to
085   regulate the diffusion model for the stable generation of multi-sequence video content. This method
086   effectively captures both motion dynamics and the integrity of the main subject content, as illus-
087
      trated in Figure 1, which presents examples of the generated videos. Specifically, the model first
      generates an initial sequence based on given content and then captures the motion information of
088
      the scene and camera, as well as the content information of moving objects within the sequence, to
089
      guide the generation of subsequent sequences. Finally, the generated video can be obtained through
090   the concatenation of multiple continuous video sequences.
091
092   Motion-Catcher employs a motion capture module that utilizes local and global optical flow infor-
093
      mation from video sequences which indicates the movement of objects and the camera. This motion
      information is then integrated into the generation process of the following video sequence to provide
094
      motion compensation, enhancing the consistency of video motion. However, research has found that
095
      merely introducing motion information does not fully resolve the degradation of the video content’s
096   main subject that occurs through continuous iterations, specifically manifested by changes in the
097   appearance and shape of objects within the video. To alleviate this degradation, we also introduce
098   a dynamic content prior module. This module uses global absolute optical flow and calculations
099   between image frames to identify areas prone to degradation during the generation process, thereby
100   incorporating prior information about dynamic objects to maintain content consistency. In generat-
101   ing the initial sequence, zero optical flow information is used to produce videos with stable motion.
102   The subsequent video sequence is generated using a conditional diffusion process. This process in-
103   corporates the final frame of the current video sequence, along with the captured motion information
104
      and content priors, to ensure consistency.
105   Experimental results have demonstrated that by incorporating the motion capture module and dy-
106   namic content prior module, the proposed Motion-Catcher successfully maintains both video motion
107   and content consistency while generating each sequence. In the AIGCBench benchmark (Fan et al.,
      2024), the proposed method shows motion consistency and content preservation improvements, sur-

123   2   R ELATED W ORK
124
125   Video Diffusion Models. Recent work on video generation has predominantly utilized diffusion
126   models, extending the concept of diffusion to higher dimensions and more complex video data,
127   achieving progressive generation from random noise to high-quality video sequences. Leveraging
128   the success of diffusion models in image generation and guided by text or image conditions, video
129   diffusion models pre-trained on large datasets have been capable of producing high-quality video
130
      sequences while maintaining diversity and stability. Among these, unlike operating directly in the
      pixel space, Yu et al. (2023) operate in latent space, using latent variables to represent high-level
131
      features in video data that encapsulate dynamic changes, object relations, and scene structures. He
132
      et al. (2022) have introduced lightweight video diffusion models that utilize a low-dimensional la-
133   tent space to generate smooth video results within a limited computational budget, demonstrating
134   significant potential and broad application prospects. This improves the quality and diversity of
135   generated videos and optimizes computational efficiency. Most related work incorporates various
136   forms of temporal mixing layers into the training architecture to learn connections between video
137   sequences, with researchers like Ge et al. (2023) focusing on temporally correlated noise to enhance
138   temporal consistency and simplify the learning task. Blattmann et al. (2023b) introduce temporal
139   convolution and attention layers after each spatial convolution and attention layer to more broadly
140   capture the motion relationships between video frames. Recent video generation models (Blattmann
141
      et al., 2023a) have shown promising results by screening and filtering large datasets during the train-
      ing and fine-tuning stages of video diffusion generation models, further fueling users’ enthusiasm
142
      for creating imaginative videos.
143
144   Model Fine-tuning for Customization. Customizing pre-trained large foundational models can
145   better adapt to downstream tasks and user preferences while maintaining strong generative knowl-
146   edge without the need to train from scratch. Several mature customization methods have been de-
147
      veloped for generating images with specific subjects and styles in the image generation domain and
      have been successfully transferred to video generation. In video generation, fine-tuning methods
148
      must consider not only style and content consistency but also the consistency of inter-frame mo-
149
      tion. Methods such as Dreambooth (Wei et al., 2024) and Lora (Hu et al., 2021) have demonstrated
150   effective results in image fine-tuning and have successfully adapted to video generation, produc-
151   ing videos that reflect specific styles based on reference sequences. However, these approaches
152   are overly dependent on the fitted videos and exhibit challenges in capturing motion information
153   effectively, resulting in difficulties in consistently generating long video content.
154

161   smooth, continuous video sequences. However, these learned instructions concerning motion are
      often simple and coarse-grained. The Control a Video method (Chen et al., 2023c) trains additional

191   3       M ETHOD
192
193   The proposed Motion-Catcher captures both motion trends and dynamic content from the initial
194   scene and perpetuates them throughout the generation of subsequent video sequences. As depicted
195   in Figure 2, the video generation process commences with the creation of an initial video sequence
196   followed by subsequent sequences. Initially, a reference image and zero optical flow are input
197   into the module. The reference image dictates the content and semantics of the video, while the
198
      optical flow is fed into the motion capture module to extract motion information. In scenarios
      with zero optical flow input, the module struggles to capture precise motion information, resulting
199
      in a sequence characterized by random movements that inadvertently enhance output diversity. The
200
      generation of subsequent video sequences leverages the motion and content information from earlier
201   sequences. This method ensures that each sequence aligns with the motion patterns and semantic
202   context of preceding sequences, thereby effectively preserving consistency in motion and content
203   across the video.
204
      In the proposed method, two novel key modules are designed, which are the motion capture module
205
      and the dynamic content prior module. The motion capture module receives image frames and
206
      a series of optical flow sequences as inputs, which are utilized to extract the motion information
207   crucial for video generation. The dynamic content prior module uses accumulated absolute optical
208   flow to identify regions within the video frames susceptible to degradation. A mask is applied to
209   these frames before they are processed by the module. This preprocessing step allows for targeted
210   adjustments in subsequent outputs, aimed at preserving the shape and semantic integrity of objects
211   within the video frames, thereby ensuring content consistency of the output video sequences.

214
215   The relationship between motion and optical flow can be conceptualized as a mapping, where mo-
      tion refers to the physical displacement of objects in the real world, and optical flow represents the

216
      projection of this motion onto the imaging plane. Based on this relationship, we design a motion cap-
217   ture module, aiming to derive the video’s motion state from optical flow while maintaining motion
218   consistency across newly generated videos. This process is divided into two stages: the generation
219   of the initial video sequence and the subsequent sequences. In the initial video generation stage,
220   it is essential to establish the overall visual content, semantic information, and the direction and
221   speed of motion, which serve as prior information for subsequent sequences. For visual content, the
222   model takes a reference image as input, which is then embedded into a latent space and replicated
223   N times, where N corresponds to the length of the video sequence being generated. To initiate the
224   diffusion model, random Gaussian noise is concatenated along the channel dimension, providing an
225
      initial distribution. In the generation of subsequent video sequences, the final frame of the previous
      sequence is extracted as the starting image and processed in the similar manner.

275   3.2   DYNAMIC CONTENT PRIOR MODULE
276
277   The proposed Motion-Catcher method utilizes images to provide reference content and semantic
278   information for the generated videos. This approach, which diverges from text-conditioned video
279   generation models, uses images as conditional inputs to directly influence the distribution of the
280
      output. During generation, the input image is merged with Gaussian noise in latent space, form-
      ing the initial input distribution for the diffusion model. This method ensures continuity in content
281
      and semantics across the generated video sequences. However, without additional constraints, the
282
      model struggles to maintain long-term global content consistency. Over time, the background and
283   main subjects in the generated videos undergo significant alterations, eventually becoming unrecog-
284   nizable, as depicted in Figure 4. Studies have indicated that dynamic scenes tend to progressively
285   lose their original appearance and contour shape under sustained motion, resulting in noticeable
286   degradation.

456   In terms of control-video alignment, AIGCBech utilizes image fidelity metrics to evaluate how sim-
457
      ilar the generated video frames are to the initial image inputted into the model (particularly the first

458
      employed to gauge the retention of the first frame. For overall video frames, the image-to-generated
459
      video CLIP similarity is calculated for each frame. These evaluations are represented by the metrics
460   MSE (First), SSIM (First), and Image-Generated Video CLIP. Regarding motion effects, the primary
461   evaluation concerns whether the motion in the generated videos is significant and reasonable. The
462   motion effect’s magnitude is gauged by calculating the optical flow scores between adjacent frames,
463   with the average value representing the extent of motion. The squared mean of predicted values be-
464   tween adjacent frames describes the video’s dynamic motion, where higher values indicate stronger
465   motion effects. In accordance with the strategy outlined in AIGCBench, we establish a threshold:
466   the Flow-Square-Mean must be less than 10 to filter out undesirable scenarios. In the aspect of
467   temporal consistency, we mainly measure whether the generated video frames are consistent and
468
      coherent. The CLIP similarity between every two adjacent frames in the generated video is calcu-
      lated, with the average value serving as the metric for the video’s temporal consistency, denoted as
469
      GenVideo Clip (Adjacent frames).

472   1, our method achieves optimal levels in MSE and SSIM for the first generated frame compared
473   to the given content, indicating that our approach preserves more information from the initial im-
474
      age. Despite a higher frame count, the Flow-Square-Mean value remains elevated, demonstrating
      significant motion in each frame and richer motion information with increasing frame numbers. Ad-
475
      ditionally, in the Temporal Consistency column, our results surpass other methods, illustrating that
476
      our model can maintain the camera’s motion and content across each video sequence and overall
477   semantic consistency. It shows our method’s capability to sustain intrinsic motion states during
478   complex transformations while managing video semantic coherence. By integrating motion infor-
479   mation and dynamic content priors, our approach captures and perpetuates the initial scene’s motion
480   trends and dynamics, ensuring natural and physically plausible motion trajectories even in video
481   sequences involving significant action changes or scene transitions.

531
      In this paper, we present a diffusion model based method for multi-sequence video generation. It
      can capture and sustain the motion trends and dynamics of the initial scene throughout the video
532
      frame generation process. This method ensures that even sequences with significant action changes
533
      or scene transitions exhibit natural, fluid, and physically plausible motion trajectories. By utiliz-
534   ing the proposed motion capture module to regulate the direction and frequency of motion, along-
535   side integrating a dynamic content prior module to enhance content consistency, our method yields
536   better coherent and visually reasonable videos. However, there are still shortcomings in the diffu-
537   sion model based methods. Current image-to-video diffusion model approaches often experience
538   style drift over extended temporal dimensions, leading to gradual alterations in visual style. Fu-
539   ture research is needed to explore strategies for enhancing style stability and controllability in video
      generation models, thereby addressing these ongoing challenges.
