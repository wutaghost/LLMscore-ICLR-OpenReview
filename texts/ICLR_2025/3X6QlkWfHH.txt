011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Quantifying responsiveness of pain to opioid administration is a clinically impor-
014            tant, yet technically challenging problem. Pain is a subjective phenomenon that
015            is difficult to assess by means other than infrequent and low-resolution patient
016            self-reporting. We tackle this problem using a continuous-time state space mod-
017            eling approach that incorporates mechanistic models of opioid effect site concen-
018
               tration as well as information from covariates using black-box models iteratively
               trained to predict the distributions of partially observed variables. We evaluated
019
               our method in simulation, and applied it in a real-world observational study of
020
               21,652 surgical cases, where our method is able to recapitulate the known po-
021            tencies of different opioids, and stratify patients by pain and opioid use related
022            outcomes.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Pain is an inherently subjective phenomenon, affected not only by physiological condition but also
028
      psychosocial factors (Lumley et al., 2011). For most practical purposes, pain can only be assessed
029
      through patient self-report, which yields infrequent, irregular, and low-resolution measurements
030   (Williamson & Hoggart, 2005). Yet the assessment and management of pain is of critical impor-
031   tance to public health. Opioids are essential medicines for pain management, but growing rates of
032   opioid dependence have led to a crisis (Rummans et al., 2018; Chisholm-Burns et al., 2019) that has
033   expanded greatly over the past decades and is now responsible for over 80,000 deaths per year in the
034   United States (Abuse, 2024). Tools to identify patients for whom opioids may be less effective for
035   pain relief and that may have greater risk for dependence are greatly needed.
036   While reliance upon opioids for pain management has come under scrutiny (Hah et al., 2017), in-
037   adequately managed acute pain often becomes chronic and unresponsive to treatment (Glare et al.,
038   2019), and non-opioid interventions do not provide the same degree of pain relief (Munir et al.,
039   2007). Significant variability exists in responsiveness to opioid therapy due to difficult to assess
040   factors such as individual variation in opioid receptor expression (Smith, 2008). We seek to quan-
041   tify opioid responsiveness from observational data, to allow more objective and consistent clinical
042   decision making.
043   Despite the myriad challenges posed both by the complexities of the underlying system and the
044   available data, we propose a principled continuous-time state-space method for modeling the re-
045   sponsiveness of post-surgical pain to opioid therapy. We leverage known opioid pharmacokinetics
046   and pharmacodynamics (Shafer et al., 1990; Shafer, 2014; Lamminsalo et al., 2019), as well as
047   domain knowledge of the dependency structure between patient history and postoperative pain tra-
048   jectories (Liu et al., 2023a), and provide a generalizable framework for training black-box models
049   to predict latent state dynamics, enhancing our state space models with information from covariates.
050   We evaluated our method and its sensitivity to model misspecification in simulation, and applied it
051   to real-world observational data from the first 24 hours after 21,652 surgical cases. Our method,
052   with only an average of 16 pain observations across 24 hours per patient, is able to recapitulate
053   known differences in potency between different opioids (Nielsen et al., 2016), and stratifies patients
      by outcomes associated with opioid responsiveness (Glare et al., 2019).

      2     R ELATED W ORK
055
056
      The task of quantifying response to intervention is often approached as a problem of causal inference
057   (Pearl et al., 2016; Imbens & Rubin, 2015) and estimating individual treatment effects (Louizos
058   et al., 2017). One of the most common approaches for estimating both average and individual
059   treatment effects is by rebalancing covariates through back-door adjustment (Shalit et al., 2017).
060
      However, achieving and verifying adequate covariate balance (Markoulidakis et al., 2021), as well
061
      as other conditions necessary for back-door adjustment, becomes increasingly challenging for time-
062
      varying exposures. For example, the overlap assumption (also referred to as positivity), which states
063   that all covariate values have a nonzero probability of assignment to all exposure values, seems in-
064   creasingly unlikely to hold as the dimensionality of covariates and exposures increases (D’Amour
065   et al., 2021). In our specific problem of estimating opioid responsiveness, not all known con-
066   founders which influence pain and opioid administration, such as psychosocial factors, are observed.
067   Nonetheless, others including Liu et al. (2023b) and Bica et al. (2020) have taken this approach by
068   learning time-evolving covariate-balanced representations, and report good performance in terms of
069   counterfactual prediction error within their modeling assumptions.
070   Zou et al. (2024) note that when modeling time-evolving systems, black-box models such as neural
071   ODEs (Chen et al., 2018; Kidger, 2022) achieve better predictive performance than theory-based
072   mechanistic models, but are more susceptible to confounding. A wide range of gray-box modeling
073   (also referred to as hybrid model) approaches attempt to combine mechanistic and deep learning
074   components (Wang & Fox, 2023; Hussain et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2021). Zou et al. (2024) provide a
075   gray-box modeling framework which maintains the dependency relationships between states defined
076   by a mechanistic model, and encodes knowledge about the expected direction of effect. Without
077
      constraints, a data-driven black-box model can simply cancel out a theory-informed model; Takeishi
      & Kalousis (2023) provide a regularization-based solution to this problem when fitting gray-box
078
      models.
079
080   Our approach is overall heavily constrained: we specify our state space models in parametric form,
081   constrain our learned parameters by specifying the expected direction of effect, but incorporate
082   information from covariates by using black-box model predictions of posterior distributions of pa-
083
      rameters as priors.

151   3.2   C ONTINUOUS - TIME STATE SPACE MODELING WITH ORDINAL OBSERVATIONS
152
153   Typically, expectation-maximization procedures are used to fit state space models (McLachlan & Kr-
154   ishnan, 2007). When dynamics are linear and observations are Gaussian, Kalman filtering and fixed-
155   interval smoothing can be used to estimate the posterior expectation E[xj | aj , yj , uj (t)] (Shumway
156   et al., 2000). However, because our observations yj are non-Gaussian, there is no convenient ana-
157   lytic solution to the posterior.
158   Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods can be used instead to sample from the posterior
159   distribution of aj , xj | yj , uj (t). We can first derive a proposal function for a Metropolis-Hastings
160   sampling scheme, which is proportional to the likelihood function of the distribution we are trying
161   to sample, then use the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) to efficiently sample from a Markov chain with
      an equivalent limiting distribution (Hoffman et al., 2014; Carpenter et al., 2017).

277   3.4   S IMULATION STUDY
278
      We generated simulated data as numerical realizations of Equation 2 for varying values of σ. Co-
279   variates c were generated as realizations of independent Gaussian random variables such that a
280   prespecified proportion r2 of the variance in xj0 and a were explained by their sum.
281
282
      Wherever possible, parameters were chosen to match distributions found in our observational
      dataset, including the frequency of observations and opioid administration, as well as patient de-
283
      mographics (further details in Appendix D). yj was observed according to Equation 3 with expo-
284
      nentially distributed intervals between observations.
285
286   We applied the same data exclusion criteria to our simulated data as in our observational study; we
287   rejected data for which model identification was impossible, such as patients with only 0 or 10 pain
288
      scores, or patients who did not receive opioids.
289   We considered cases in which varying fractions of variance r2 in xj (tj0 ) and aj are explained by cj ,
290   comparing performance metrics with and without covariate-informed priors as an ablation study. We
291   compute concordance probability (also referred to as c-index, and equal to AUC when one variable
292   is binary) between true aj and sampled posterior mode, as well as Kendall’s rank correlation (τ )
293   in order to assess the quality of our estimated rankings. Confidence intervals for our performance
294
      metrics were computed using bootstrap.

296   3.5   R EAL - WORLD APPLICATION ON OBSERVATIONAL DATA
297
      We applied our method to electronic health record data from 21,652 adult surgical patients at a
298
      quaternary care academic medical center in the United States, computing responsiveness of post-
299   operative pain to fentanyl, hydromorphone, and oxycodone. Our protocol was approved by the
300   Institutional Review Board with a waiver of informed consent. Covariates in this dataset consisted
301   of routinely collected preoperative data, including patient demographics, surgical service of their
302   scheduled procedure, surgery urgency (elective, urgent, emergent), preoperative pain score, opioid
303   naivety, and other elements of patient medical and surgical history. We included only patients who
304   had at least 10 pain score observations in the first 24 hours after surgery, and were administered
305   hydromorphone or oxycodone at least once after surgery. Carry-over from intraoperative opioid
306   administration was factored into our PK/PD calculations; fentanyl was only administered intraoper-
307
      atively or immediately after surgery in the PACU.
308   Patients were stratified into high and low responsiveness cohorts for hydromorphone and oxycodone
309   based on whether their estimated responsiveness was greater or less than the median in our study
310   cohort; the distribution of postoperative outcomes was evaluated within these cohorts.

424   5    D ISCUSSION
425
426   We present a method for estimating responsiveness of postsurgical pain to opioid therapy using ob-
427   servations of patient-reported pain scores over time, medication records, and preoperative covariates.
428   We evaluated some of the performance characteristics of this method in simulation, and applied it
429   to observational data from real patients. Our method was able to recapitulate the known relative
430   potency of different opioids, and was able to stratify patients by postoperative outcomes related to
431   pain and opioid usage based on their estimated responsiveness to hydromorphone and oxycodone.
      Moreover, our model-estimated responsiveness is interpretable in terms of physical quantities: for


                                                               8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      each opioid, we have the log-odds of reduction in observed pain score per minute of exposure to a
433   given effect site concentration.
434
435   Another strength of this method is that it is uncertainty-aware; rather than simply producing a point
436
      estimate of responsiveness for each patient, we estimate a posterior distribution. The uncertainty
      in the distribution of a patient’s latent pain state increases with the duration of the interval between
437
      observations. Patients with sparse data will have posteriors with greater variance. Our method is
438
      also able to take into account information from covariates that purely mechanistic models cannot,
439   without explicitly specifying the relationship between those variables and the dynamical system,
440   while preserving causal relationships and mechanistic structures between variables.
441
442
      While our model was designed with the specific application of estimating postoperative opioid
      responsiveness in mind, and consequently is able to leverage known opioid PK/PD and depen-
443
      dency structures, our general concept of using black-box models to augment state space models
444
      with covariate-informed priors is potentially applicable to other gray-box modeling problems, data
445   modalities, and network structures.
446
447
      5.1   L IMITATIONS
448
449   While using MCMC to estimate posterior distributions affords us flexibility in the types of observa-
450   tions we are able to use in our state space models and makes it easy to incorporate priors, a conse-
451   quence of this approach is a relatively long run time. In our dataset, on a 16-core AMD 5955WX
452   CPU, it takes about 2 seconds per patient to run one E-step, which equates to about 72 hours to
453   analyze our dataset of over 20,000 patients. Overall, these computations remain tractable because
454   we are sampling a relatively small number of parameters; however, that also means that GPU-based
455
      MCMC implementations do not provide any performance increase. Moreover, because our E-step
      is not analytic and contains sampling noise, our EM procedure is not guaranteed to converge.
456
457   Furthermore, we assume that opioid responsiveness is time-invariant. However, this is not neces-
458   sarily true even within the 24 hour time window which we consider, as tolerance can develop on
459   the order of hours (Dumas & Pollack, 2008), and there are also other mechanisms of sensitization
460   that could modulate opioid responsiveness over time (Woolf, 2011). This could contribute to the
461
      discrepancy we observe between our estimated hydromorphone and oxycodone responsiveness and
      literature-reported ratios of equivalent doses, as oxycodone tends to be administered later in periop-
462
      erative care than hydromorphone and fentanyl.
463
464   Finally, we have a single set of intercepts β for our observations generated by Equation 3. In order
465   for us to compare responsiveness of pain to opioid administration between patients, it is necessary
466   that their pain states evolve in a common latent space. We treat the 0-10 numerical pain scale as
467
      fixed across all patients, even though in reality, different patients may report different scores after
      experiencing the same degree of painful stimulus. However, violation of this assumption is not
468
      necessarily fatal, so long as we shift our interpretation of estimated opioid responsiveness to a kind
469
      of average odds ratio across the different levels of the pain scale (Harrell, 2020).
