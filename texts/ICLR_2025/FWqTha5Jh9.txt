011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (DA) consists of adapting a model trained on
014            a labeled source domain to perform well on an unlabeled target domain with
015            some data distribution shift. While many methods have been proposed in the
016            literature, fair and realistic evaluation remains an open question, particularly due to
017            methodological difficulties in selecting hyperparameters in the unsupervised setting.
018            With DA-Bench, we propose a framework to evaluate DA methods on diverse
019            modalities, beyond computer vision task that have been largely explored in the
020
               literature. We present a complete and fair evaluation of existing shallow algorithms,
               including reweighting, mapping, and subspace alignment. Realistic hyperparameter
021
               selection is performed with nested cross-validation and various unsupervised model
022
               selection scores, on both simulated datasets with controlled shifts and real-world
023            datasets across diverse modalities, such as images, text, biomedical, and tabular
024            data. Our benchmark highlights the importance of realistic validation and provides
025            practical guidance for real-life applications, with key insights into the choice and
026            impact of model selection approaches. DA-Bench is open-source, reproducible,
027            and can be easily extended with novel DA methods, datasets, and model selection
028            criteria without requiring re-evaluating competitors.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Given some training –or source– data, supervised learning consists in estimating a function that makes
035   good predictions on target data. However, performance often drops when the source distribution used
036   for training differs from the target distribution used for testing. This shift can be due, for instance,
037   to the collection process or non-stationarity in the data, and is ubiquitous in real-life settings. It has
038   been observed in various application fields, including tabular data (Gardner et al., 2023), clinical
039
      data (Harutyunyan et al., 2019), or computer vision (Ganin et al., 2016b).
040   Domain adaptation. Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (DA) addresses this problem by adapting a
041   model trained on a labeled source dataset –or domain– so that it performs well on an unlabeled target
042   domain, assuming some distribution shifts between the two (Ben-David et al., 2006; Quinonero-
043   Candela et al., 2008; Redko et al., 2022). As illustrated in Figure 1, source and target distributions
044   can exhibit various types of shifts (Moreno-Torres et al., 2012): changes in feature distributions
045
      (covariate shift), class proportions (target shift), conditional distributions (conditional shift), or in
      distributions in particular subspaces (subspace shift). Depending on the type of shift, existing DA
046
      methods attempt to align the source distribution closer to the target using reweighting (Sugiyama
047
      & Müller, 2005; Shimodaira, 2000), mapping (Sun et al., 2017; Courty et al., 2017b), or dimension
048   reduction (Pan et al., 2011; Fernando et al., 2013) methods. More recently, it has been proposed to
049   mitigate shifts in a feature space learned by deep learning (Ganin et al., 2016b; Sun & Saenko, 2016;
050   Long et al., 2015a; Damodaran et al., 2018b), primarily focusing on computer vision applications.
051   Regardless of the core algorithm used to address the domain shift, hyperparameters must be tuned
052   for optimal performance. Indeed, a critical challenge in applying DA methods to real-world cases is
053   selecting the appropriate method and tuning its hyperparameters, especially given the unknown shift
      type and the absence of labels in the target domain.

      Model selection in DA settings. Without distribution shifts, classical model selection strategies
065
      –including hyperparameter optimization– rely on evaluating the generalization error with an indepen-
066
      dent labeled validation set. However, in DA, validating the hyperparameters in a supervised manner
067   on the target domains is impossible due to the lack of labels. While it is possible to validate the
068   hyperparameters on the source domain, it generally leads to a suboptimal model selection because of
069   the distribution shift. In the literature, this problem is often raised but not always addressed. Some
070   papers choose not to validate the parameters (Pan et al., 2011), while others validate on the source
071   domain (Sun et al., 2017) or propose custom cross-validation methods (Sugiyama et al., 2007b). Few
072   papers focus specifically on DA model selection criteria, which we will call scorers in this paper.
073   These scorers are used to select the methods’ hyperparameters, and mainly consists of reweighting
074   methods on source (Sugiyama et al., 2007a; You et al., 2019), prediction entropy (Morerio et al.,
075
      2017; Saito et al., 2021) or circular validation (Bruzzone & Marconcini, 2010a). One of the goals of
      our benchmark is to evaluate these approaches in diverse and realistic scenarios.
076
077   Benchmarks of DA. As machine learning continues to flourish, new methods constantly emerge,
078   making it essential to develop benchmarks that facilitate fair comparisons (Hutson, 2018; Pineau
079   et al., 2019; Mattson et al., 2020; Moreau et al., 2022). In DA and related fields, several benchmarks
080   have been proposed. Numerous papers focus on Out-of-distribution (OOD) datasets for different
081
      modalities: computer vision, text, graphs (Koh et al., 2021; Sagawa et al., 2022), time-series (Gagnon-
      Audet et al., 2023), AI-aided drug discovery (Ji et al., 2023) or tabular dataset (Gardner et al., 2023).
082
      Due to the type of data considered, existing benchmarks are mainly focused on Deep DA methods
083
      (Musgrave et al., 2021; Wang, 2018; Jiang et al., 2022; Fawaz et al., 2023), offering an incomplete
084   evaluation of DA literature. Moreover, only a few benchmarks propose a comparison of Deep
085   unsupervised DA methods with realistic parameters selection, on computer vision (Hu et al., 2023;
086   Musgrave et al., 2021) and time series (Fawaz et al., 2023) data. Those benchmarks have shown the
087   importance of validating with unsupervised scores and reveal that Deep DA methods achieve much
088   lower performance in realistic scenarios.
089
      Contributions. In the following, we propose DA-Bench, an ambitious and fully reproducible
090   benchmark with the following features: 1. A set of 4 simulated and 8 real-life datasets with different
091   modalities (computer vision, NLP, tabular, biomedical) totaling 51 realistic shift scenarios, 2. A wide
092   range of 20 Shallow DA methods designed to handle different types of shifts, 3. An evaluation of 3
093   deep DA methods on 4 real-world datasets from the computer vision and biomedical modalities, 4. A
094   realistic model selection procedure using 5 different unsupervised scorers with nested cross-validation
095   for hyperparameter selection, 5. An open-source implementation and publicly available datasets, easy
096   to extend for new DA methods and datasets without the need to re-run the whole experiment.
097   In addition, we provide a detailed analysis of the results and derive guidelines for practitioners to
098
      select the best methods depending on the type of shifts, and the best scorer to perform unsupervised
      model selection. In particular, the effects of model selection and the scorer’s choice on the final
099
      performances are highlighted, showing a clear gap between the unsupervised realistic scorers versus
100
      using target labels for supervised validation.

      2.1   DATA SHIFTS AND DA STRATEGIES
109
110   Domain Adaptation problem and theory. The theoretical framework of DA is well established
111   (Ben-David et al., 2006; Quinonero-Candela et al., 2008; Redko et al., 2022). The main results
112   highlight that the performance discrepancy of an estimator between the source and target domains is
113   linked to the divergence between both distributions. This has motivated the majority of DA methods
114   to search for a universal (or domain invariant) predictor by minimizing the divergence between the
115   two domains through the adaptation of the distributions. This is done in practice by modeling and
116   estimating the shift between the source and target distributions and then compensating for this shift
117
      before training a predictor.
118   Data Shifts and DA methods. A wide variety of shifts between the source and target dis-
119   tributions are possible. They are usually expressed as a relation between the joint distribu-
120   tions P s (x, y) = P s (x|y)PYs (y) = P s (y|x)PXs (x) in the source domain and P t (x, y) =
121   P t (x|y)PYt (y) = P t (y|x)PXt (x) in the target domain. We now discuss the main types of shifts and
122   the strategies proposed in the literature to mitigate them. Figure 1 illustrates these shifts.
123   In Covariate shift the conditionals probabilities are equal (i.e., P s (y|x) = P t (y|x)), but the feature
124
      marginals change (i.e., PXs (x) ̸= PXt (x)). Target shift is similar, but the label marginals change
      PYs (y) ̸= PYt (y) while the conditionals are preserved. For classification problems, it corresponds
125
      to a change in the proportion of the classes between the two domains. Both of those shifts can be
126
      compensated by reweighting methods that assign different weights to the samples of the source
127   domain to make it closer to the target domain (Sugiyama & Müller, 2005; Shimodaira, 2000).
128   In Conditional shift, conditional probabilities differ between domain (i.e., P s (x|y) ̸= P t (x|y) or
129   P s (y|x) ̸= P t (y|x)). This shift is typically harder to compensate for, necessitating explicit modeling
130   to address it effectively. For instance, several approaches model the shift as a mapping m between the
131   source and target domain such that P s (y|m(x)) = P t (y|x) (Sun et al., 2017; Courty et al., 2017b).
132   The estimated mapping is then applied to the source data before training a predictor.
133   Subspace shift assumes that while probabilities are different between the domains (PXs (x) ̸= PXt (x)
134   and P s (x|y) ̸= P t (x|y)), there exists a subspace Z and a function ϕ : X → Z such that
135
      PZs (ϕ(x)) = PZt (ϕ(x)) and P s (y|ϕ(x)) = P t (y|ϕ(x)). Note that this means the shift occurs
      in the orthogonal complement of Z. This implies that a classifier trained on Z will perform well
136
      across both domains. Subspace methods are specifically designed towards identifying the subspace
137
      Z and the function ϕ, as developed in Pan et al. (2011); Fernando et al. (2013). Note that, as discussed
138   in the introduction, a natural extension of this idea is to learn an invariant feature space using Deep
139   learning (Ganin et al., 2016b; Sun & Saenko, 2016).

      2.2   DA MODEL SELECTION STRATEGIES
142
143
      As seen above, DA methods are typically designed to correct a specific type of shift. However, in
144   real-world scenarios, the nature of the shift is often unknown. This presents a challenge in selecting
145   the appropriate method and tuning its parameters when facing a new problem. In this section, we
146   discuss the validation strategies proposed in the literature to compare DA methods, focusing on
147   realistic scorers that do not use target labels.
148
      Realistic DA scorers. In the literature, few papers propose realistic DA scorers to validate the
149
      parameters of the methods, i.e., unsupervised scorers that do not require target labels. The
150   Importance Weighted (IW) scorer (Sugiyama et al., 2007a) computes the score as a reweighted
151   accuracy on labeled sources data. The Deep Embedded Validation (DEV) (You et al., 2019) can be
152   seen as an IW in the latent space with a variance reduction strategy. DEV was originally proposed
153   for Deep learning models but can be used on shallow DA methods that compute features from the
154   data (mapping/subspaces). The Prediction Entropy (PE) scorer (Morerio et al., 2017) measures
155   the uncertainty associated with model predictions on the target data. Soft Neighborhood Density
156   (SND) (Saito et al., 2021) also computes an entropy but on a normalized pairwise similarity matrix
157   between probabilistic predictions on target. The Circular Validation (CircV) scorer (Bruzzone &
158
      Marconcini, 2010a) performs DA by first adapting the model from the source to the target domain and
      predicting the target labels. Next, it adapts back from the target to the source using these estimated
159
      labels. Performance is measured as the accuracy between the recovered and true source labels.
160
161   The MixVal scorer (Hu et al., 2023) also performs domain adaptation by first adapting the model
      from the source to the target domain and predicting the target labels. Then, it generates mixed target

180   DA validation in the literature. The model selection problem in DA has been widely discussed in
181   the literature. Yet, this literature constitutes a subfield of DA and has seldom been used to validate
182   new DA methods. Indeed, there is no consensus on the best validation strategy and many papers do
183   not properly validate their methods, leading to over-estimated performances. Some authors do not
184   discuss the validation procedure (Sugiyama & Müller, 2005; Shimodaira, 2000) or consider fixed
185   hyperparameters (Huang et al., 2006). While some methods rely on custom validation techniques
186   (Sugiyama et al., 2007b), others use cross-validation, either on the source or the target (Sun et al., 2017;
187
      Courty et al., 2017b), or alternatively other validation strategies proposed in the literature (Courty
      et al., 2017a; Bruzzone & Marconcini, 2010a). A complete picture of the model selection procedures
188
      used to validate the methods considered in DA-Bench in their original papers is presented in Table 4
189
      in Appendix A. The goal of DA-Bench is therefore to constitute a dedicated benchmark to compare
190   scorers from the literature and report performances that can be expected in real use cases for the
191   considered methods.

      strategies. Then, we present the compared DA methods followed by a description of the datasets used

200   3.1   N ESTED CROSS - VALIDATION LOOP AND IMPLEMENTATION
201
      We discuss below the nested cross-validation and the implementation details of the benchmark.
202
203   Hyperparameter validation loop. We propose a nested loop cross-validation procedure, depicted in
204   Figure 2. First, the source and target data are split into multiple outer test and train sets (outer loop in
205   Figure 2). The test sets are kept to compute the final accuracy for both the source and target domains.
206   For each split in the outer loop, we use a nested loop to select the DA methods’ parameters. Here,
207
      the training sets are further divided into nested train and validation sets (nested loop in Figure 2).
      Note that no labels are available for the target nested train and validation sets in this loop. The target
208
      training set is used to train the DA method, while the target validation set allows to compute the
209
      unsupervised score and select the best model.
210   For both loops, the data is split randomly 5 times using stratified sampling with an 80%/20% train/test
211   split, except for Deep DA methods, where only one split is computed for the outer loop due to
212   computation time. For one given method, we evaluate all the unsupervised scorers discussed earlier,
213   as well as a supervised scorer that uses target labels, over all the nested splits. After averaging, the
214   scores over the splits, the best hyperparameters are selected according to each scorer and then used to
215   train a final classifier on the outer training sets. Although the supervised scorer cannot be used in
      practice, it is included in our results to actually evaluate the performance drop due to the absence of


                                                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      target labels. To limit complexity and perform a fair comparison of the methods, we set a timeout of
217   4 hours for performing the nested loop. Additionally, we chose not to use the CircV scorer for Deep
218   DA methods, as training neural networks twice is computationally expensive.
219
220
      Base estimators and neural networks. Existing shallow domain adaptation methods typically rely
      on either a base estimator trained on the adapted data or an iterative estimation process to adapt this
221
      estimator to the target data. The choice of the base estimator is crucial, as it significantly impacts the
222
      final performance. Before validating the hyperparameters of the DA methods, we determined the best
223   estimator for each dataset using a grid-search on the source data. We tested multiple hyperparameters
224   for Logistic Regression, SVM with RBF kernel, and XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016), selecting
225   the ones that maximize the average accuracy on the source test sets. Note that for some methods that
226   specifically require an SVM estimator (i.e., JDOT and DASVM), we only validate SVM as the base
227   estimator. We validated the base estimator separately from the DA methods parameters to reduce
228   computational complexity and avoid too complex hyperparameter grids that can compromise the
229   reliability of DA scorers. For Deep DA methods, we similarly select an appropriate architecture and
230   experimental setup for training on the source data for each dataset: a two-layer convolutional neural
231
      network for MNIST/USPS, a ResNet50 (He et al., 2016) pretrained on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009)
      for Office31 and Office Home, and a ShallowFBCSPNet (Schirrmeister et al., 2017) for BCI.
232
      These architectures are widely used and well-supported in the literature of computer vision (Musgrave
233
      et al., 2021) and BCI (Schirrmeister et al., 2017). During the nested loop, only the DA parameters for
234   each method are validated.
235
236   Best scorer selection and statistical test. For all methods, we select the best validation scorer as the
237
      one that maximizes the averaged accuracy on the target domains for all real datasets. This provides a
      reasonable and actionable choice of scorer for each DA method for practitioners. For all methods and
238
      datasets, we perform a paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test at the 0.05 level to detect significant gain or
239
      drop in performance with respect to the no DA approach, denoted by “Train Src” in the following.
240   The test is done using the accuracy measures of the DA method with the selected scorer and the Train
241   Src for all shifts and outer splits, ensuring between 10 and 60 values depending on the dataset. Note
242   that these statistical tests are not performed on Deep DA methods, as the number of splits is too
243   limited for meaningful testing.
244
      Python implementation. The benchmark code will be made available on GitHub upon publication
245
      of the paper.1 Our benchmark is implemented following the benchopt framework (Moreau et al.,
246   2022), which provides standardized ways of organizing and running benchmarks for ML in Python.
247   This framework facilitates reproducing the benchmark’s results, with tools to install the dependencies,
248   run the methods in parallel, or cache the results to prevent redundant computations. It also makes it
249   easy to extend the benchmark with additional datasets and methods, enabling it to evolve to account
250   for the advances in the field. In the supplementary materials, we provide examples demonstrating how
251   to add DA methods or datasets to the benchmark. Using this framework, we aim to make DA-Bench
252   a reference benchmark to evaluate new DA methods in realistic scenarios with valid performance
253   estimations.

255   3.2      C OMPARED DA METHODS
256
257
      In this section, we present the different families of domain adaptation methods that we compare in our
      benchmark. The shallow methods are grouped into four categories: reweighting methods, mapping
258
      methods, subspace methods, and others. For Deep DA methods, we consider three domain invariant
259
      feature methods. We provide a brief description of each method and the corresponding references.
260
261   Reweighting methods. These methods aim to reweight the source data to make it closer to the
262   target data. The weights are estimated using different methods such as kernel density estimation
263
      (Dens. RW) (Sugiyama & Müller, 2005), Gaussian estimation (Gauss. RW) (Shimodaira, 2000),
      discriminative estimation (Discr. RW) (Shimodaira, 2000), or nearest-neighbors (NN RW) (Loog,
264
      2012). Other reweighting estimate weights by minimizing a divergence between the source and
265
      target distributions such as Kullback-Leibler Importance Estimation Procedure (KLIEP) (Sugiyama
266   et al., 2007b) or Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) (Huang et al., 2006). Finally, we also include the
267   MMDTarS method (Zhang et al., 2013) that uses a Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to estimate
268   the weights under the target shift hypothesis.
269
         1

270
      Mapping methods. These methods aim to find a mapping between the source and target data that
271   minimizes the distribution shift. The Correlation Alignment method (CORAL) (Sun et al., 2017)
272   aligns the second-order statistics of source and target distributions. The Maximum Mean Discrepancy
273   (MMD-LS) method (Zhang et al., 2013) minimizes the MMD to estimate an affine Location-Scale
274   mapping. Finally, the Optimal Transport (OT) mapping methods (Courty et al., 2017b) use the
275   optimal transport plan to align with a non-linear mapping of the source and target distributions with
276   exact OT (MapOT), entropic regularization (EntOT), or class-based regularization (ClassRegOT).
277   Finally, the Linear OT method (Flamary et al., 2020) uses a linear mapping to align the source and
278   target distributions, assuming Gaussian distributions.
279   Subspace methods. These methods aim to learn a subspace where the source and target data have
280   the same distribution. The Transfer Component Analysis (TCA) method (Pan et al., 2011) searches
281   for a kernel embedding that minimizes the MMD divergence between the domains while preserving
282   the variance. The Subspace Alignment (SA) method (Fernando et al., 2013) aims to learn a subspace
283   where the source and target have their covariance matrices aligned. The Transfer Subspace Learning
284   (TSL) method (Si et al., 2010) aims to learn a subspace using classical supervised loss functions on
285
      the source (e.g., PCA, Fisher LDA) but regularized so that the source and target data have the same
      distribution once projected on the subspace. Finally, the Joint Principal Component Analysis (JPCA)
286
      method is a simple baseline that concatenates source and target data before applying a PCA.
287
288   Others. We also include other methods that do not fit into the previous categories. The Domain
289   Adaptation SVM (DASVM) method (Bruzzone & Marconcini, 2010a) is a self-labeling method that
290   iteratively updates SVM estimators by adding new target samples with predicted labels and removing
291
      source samples. The Joint Distribution Optimal Transport (JDOT) method (Courty et al., 2017a) aims
      to learn a target predictor that minimizes an OT loss between the joint source and target distributions.
292
      The Optimal Transport Label Propagation (OTLabelProp) method (Solomon et al., 2014) uses the
293
      optimal transport plan to propagate labels from the source to the target domain.
294
295   Deep DA methods. These methods aim to reduce the divergence between the source and target data
296   distributions within the learned feature space while simultaneously learning a classifier on source data.
297
      The training loss consists in a traditional supervised loss on labeled source data and a second term
      measuring the discrepancy between the source and target distributions. The methods implemented in
298
      the Deep DA Benchmark use different discrepancies, such as covariance distance (Sun & Saenko,
299
      2016) for DeepCORAL, adversarial loss (Ganin et al., 2016a) for DANN and optimal transport
300   distance (Damodaran et al., 2018a) for DeepJDOT. Note that these approaches are not part of the
301   main shallow DA benchmark but have been added to provide an interesting comparison of DA
302   performances between shallow and Deep methods on computer vision and biomedical data.

346   et al., 2023a) before applying a PCA. The tabular data are one-hot encoded to transform categorical
347   data into numerical data. The biosignals from Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) data are embedded
348   using the state-of-the-art tangent space representation proposed in Barachant et al. (2012). For Deep
349   DA methods, only 4 datasets are used: Office31, Office Home, MNIST/USPS and BCI. Since
350   these methods focus on learning feature representations, the data are used in their raw form. The
351   datasets are split into pairs of source and target domains totaling 51 adaptation tasks in the benchmark.
352
      More details about the datasets and pre-processing are available in Appendix B.

413   which is crucial for reweighting to work effectively (Segovia-Martín et al., 2023) which is likely true

420   It is notable that simple transformations are the best in average across all modalities. Indeed most
421   methods that significantly outperform Train Src in ranking average across all modalities are LinOT,
422   CORAL, JPCA, and SA, which all rely on linear transformations such as scaling, linear projection or
423   rotations. These methods are robust across datasets and modalities, offering effective alignment with
424   minimal risk of negative DA.
425
      Take-away for DA users. Reweighting methods are best suited for scenarios where the same-support
426   assumption holds and perform particularly well when paired with regularized hypotheses like linear
427   models. Even when assumptions are not fully satisfied, reweighting tends to be robust to negative
428   transfer. Mapping methods are highly effective under moderate numbers of classes and in the absence
429   of target shift but carry a significant risk of negative transfer if target shift is present. When the type
430   of distribution shift is uncertain, simpler transformation-based methods like LinOT, CoRAL, JPCA,
431   and SA provide modest performance improvements while minimizing risks of negative DA, making
      them reliable and safe default options.

464   4.2             S TUDY OF VALIDATION SCORERS
465
466   We now investigate the performance of the various scorers to select hyperparameters of the DA
467   method. First, we consider the relationship between the cross-val score and the accuracy for each
468   inner split. In Figure 3, we plot for each scorer the cross-val score as a function of the accuracy
469   computed on the test set and report the Pearson correlation coefficient ρ. As expected, the supervised
470   scorer is highly correlated with the accuracy (ρ = 0.98), as it has access to the target labels. We
471
      observe that SND, DEV, and PE do not provide a good proxy to select hyperparameters that give the
      best-performing models (ρ ≤ 0.06). On the contrary, MixVal, IW and CircV are correlated with the
472
      accuracy, ρ = 0.34, ρ = 0.56 and ρ = 0.71 respectively. This is coherent with their selection as the
473
      best scorer in most scenarios in Table 2. Still, while those scorers are well correlated with the target
474   accuracy, it is important to note that they have a large variance. For instance, a score close to 1 in IW
475   or CircV corresponds to an accuracy between 0.5 and 1.0.
476   Furthermore, we provide in Figures 8 and 9, from Appendix D, several visualizations that illustrate the
477   relationship between the accuracy achieved when using a supervised scorer and the accuracy obtained
478   when using different unsupervised scorers. We also visualize in Figure 7 the drop in performance
479   when using the best-unsupervised scorer instead of the supervised scorer. Interestingly some methods
480   such as KMM, EntOT, and ClassRegOT can lose up to 10% accuracy when using realistic scorers,
481   which might come from their higher number of parameters or their sensitivity to them.
482   Our results thus show that most scorers have poor results when evaluated on many datasets. Of the
483   five methods under consideration, only two achieve satisfactory performance, although incurring large
484   variance in their results. This shows that proper hyperparameter selection is still an open question,
485   that needs attention from the research comunity to guide practitioners toward real life applications of
      unsupervised DA technics.

519   shallow methods (i.e., LinOT for example). Finally, a method like DANN, which is often considered

      whereas shallow DA methods can achieve similar or superior performances with less effort and fewer
523

526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we introduced DA-Bench, a extensive benchmark for unsupervised domain adaptation,
529   carefully evaluating the impact of the model selection criteria and covering diverse modalities:
530
      computer vision, natural language processing, tabular data and biosignals. While being quite
      comprehenvise on shallow methods, our results also provide a comparison of three common deep
531
      DA baselines on computer vision and biosignals. Importantly DA-Bench can be easily extended
532
      with new datasets and methods to push further the state-of-the-art. Our findings reveal that few
533   shallow DA methods consistently perform well across diverse datasets and that model selection
534   scorers significantly influence their effectiveness. While deep DA methods show similar trends,
535   they often require more extensive hyperparameter tuning and architectures tailored to each modality.
536   Notably, they tend to perform significantly better than shallow methods on some modalities, such
537   as computer vision, while facing challenges on others such as biosignals. For each DA method, we
538   provide the optimal model selection scorer for unsupervised hyperparameter tuning based on our
539   experiments.

956   In Table 4, we provide additional information on the validation procedures used in the original papers
957   that proposed the different domain adaptation methods implemented in DA-Bench. The first column
958   is the name of the method, the second column contains the procedure used to select hyperparameters
959   and the last column provides additional details. What is striking is that many methods do not conduct
960   or specify a validation procedure to select the hyperparameters, which limits the performance of
961   the proposed method on a novel dataset. Several others rely on cross-validation using target data.
962   However, since target labels are typically unavailable in practical scenarios, this validation approach
963
      is unrealistic. Overall, many methods have been evaluated with unrealistic or not reproducible
      validation procedures, making the performance of the proposed methods appear over-optimistic.

      A key contribution of our work is the extensive comparison of realistic, unsupervised scorers for
965
      selecting optimal hyperparameters and base estimators in DA methods.

968   B        DATASETS DESCRIPTION AND PREPROCESSING
969
970   The simulated dataset proves that DA methods can work well under the proper shift (see Table 2).
971   However, in real-world applications, we do not have prior knowledge of the type of data shift. Hence,
      finding the appropriate domain-adaptation method between reweighting, mapping, and subspace


                                                                18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       methods is a challenging task. In this section, we introduce 8 real-world datasets coming from
973    different fields. Table 1 summarizes the 8 classification datasets used in this benchmark with the
974    corresponding data modality, preprocessing, number of source-target pairs (# adapt), number of
975    classes, samples, and feature dimensions.
976
977    Computer Vision. First, three computer vision datasets are proposed: Office31 (Koniusz et al.,
978    2017), Office Home (Venkateswara et al., 2017), and MNIST/USPS (Liao & Carneiro, 2015). We
979    create embeddings for Office31 using the Decaff preprocessing method (Donahue et al., 2014) and for
980    Office Home using a pre-trained ResNet50 (He et al., 2016). These embeddings, as well as vectorized
981    MNIST/USPS, are dimensionally reduced with a Principal Component Analysis (PCA). These three
982    datasets encompass 3, 4, and 3 domains, respectively and all pairs of adaptations are used as DA
983
       problems. MNIST/USPS contain clear and blurry images digits, Office31 differentiates between
       images captured by various devices, while for OfficeHome, its by image style.
984
985
       NLP. The second task is Natural Language Processing (NLP). Two datasets are studied: 20News-
986
       group (Lang, 1995) and Amazon Review (McAuley et al., 2015). The 20Newsgroup dataset contains
987    20.000 documents categorized into 4 categories: talk, rec, comp, and sci. The learning task is to clas-
988    sify documents across categories. First, the documents are embedded using a Large Language Model
989    (LLM) (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019; Xiao et al., 2023a), and then PCA is applied for dimensionality
990    reduction.
991
       For the Amazon Review dataset, the task is to classify comment ratings. This dataset spans four
992
       domains (Books, DVDs, Kitchen, Electronics), and the domain shift results from these varying types
993    of objects. Similar to the 20Newsgroup dataset, comments are embedded using the same LLM and
994    then reduced in dimensionality using a PCA.
995
996    Tabular data. We propose two tabular datasets. The first one is the Mushroom dataset (Dai et al.,
997    2007), where the task is to classify whether a mushroom is poisonous or not. The two domains are
998    separated according to the mushroom’s stalk shape (enlarging vs. tapering). The tabular data are
999    one-hot-encoded to transform categorical data into numerical data. The second dataset is Phishing
1000   (Mohammad et al., 2012). The classification problem involves determining whether a webpage is
1001   a phishing or a legitimate one. The domains are separated according to the availability of the IP
1002   address. Since the data are already numerical, no preprocessing is done on this dataset.
1003
1004
       Biosignals. The last task is BCI Motor Imagery. The dataset used is BCI Competition IV (Tanger-
       mann et al., 2012), often used in the literature (Barachant et al., 2012) and availaoble in MOABB
1005
       (Aristimunha et al., 2023). The task is to classify four kinds of motor imagery (right hand, left
1006
       hand, feet, and tongue) from EEG data. In this dataset, nine subjects are available. The domains are
1007   separated based on session number. For each subject, session 1 is considered as the source domain
1008   and session 2 is considered as the target domain. The data are multivariate signals. To embed the data,
1009   we first compute the covariance and then project this covariance on the Tangent Space as proposed in
1010   Barachant et al. (2012).

       D.2             I MPACT OF THE BASE ESTIMATORS ON THE SIMULATED DATASETS
1688   As mentioned in the main paper, it is possible to partly compensate for the shift by choosing the
1689   right base estimator. In this part, we provide the results on the Simulated dataset for three different
1690   base estimators: Logistic Regression (LR) in Table 17, SVM in Table 18, and XGBoost in Table 19.
1691   Observing the two first rows for covariate shift, we see that with LR (Table 17), there is a significant
1692   drop in performance between training on the source v.s. training on the target (∼ 10%), while using
1693   SVC (Table 18) only leads to a drop (∼ 3%). Finally, using XGBoost (Table 19) maintains the
1694   performance. The reweighting DA methods help compensate for the shift when using a simpler LR
1695   estimator. However when using an SVC, as shown in the main paper, the reweighting does not help to
1696
       compensate for the covariate shift. If we look at the other shifts, the problem is harder. The subspace
       methods help with subspace shift, and the mapping methods help with the conditional shift.
1697
1698   These Tables show the importance of choosing the right base estimator. It is clear that choosing an
1699   appropriate base estimator can partially compensate for some shifts.
