009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In the context of face recognition models, different facial features contribute un-
012            evenly to a model’s ability to correctly identify individuals, making some features
013            more critical and, therefore, more susceptible to attacks. Deep Gradient Leakage
014            (DGL) is a highly effective attack that recovers private training images from gra-
015            dient vectors, posing significant privacy challenges in distributed learning systems
016            where clients share gradients. Data augmentation, a technique for artificially ma-
017
               nipulating the training set by creating modified copies of existing data, plays a
               crucial role in improving the accuracy of deep learning models.
018
               In this paper, we explore various data augmentation methods to protect original
019
               training images, in test time thereby enhancing security in distributed learning
020            systems as well as increasing accuracy during training. Our experiments demon-
021            strate that augmentation methods improve model performance during training on
022            augmented images, and we can use the same methods during testing as pertur-
023            bation methods to preserve some features of the image and have safety against
024            DGL.
025            This project has four primary objectives: first, to develop a vision transformer
026            face validation model that trains on distributed devices to ensure privacy; sec-
027            ond, to utilize augmentation methods to perturb private images and increase neu-
028            ral network safety; and third, to provide protection against attacks, ensuring that
029            reconstructing attacks cannot extract sensitive information from gradients at any
030
               point in the system. and lastly we introduce a new novel perturbation method for
               a multi biometric authentication, system which offers accuracy for identification
031
               and guarantees safety and anonymity of entities.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Federated learning (Rodrı́guez-Barroso et al., 2023) proposes letting participants train on their own
037   data in a distributed fashion and share only model updates —i.e., gradients with the central server.
038   The server aggregates these updates (typically by averaging) to improve a global model and then
039   sends updates to participants. This process runs iterative until the global model converges. Merging
040   information from individual data points into aggregated gradients intuitively preserves privacy to
041   some degree.
042   While this setting might seem safe at first glance, a few recent works have begun to question the
043   central premise of federated learning - is it possible for gradients to leak private information of the
044   training data? Effectively serving as a “proxy” of the training data, the link between gradients to
045   the data in fact offers potential for retrieving information: from revealing the positional distribution
046   of original data (Melis et al., 2019; Shokri et al., 2017), to even enabling pixel-level detailed image
047   reconstruction from gradients (Geiping et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2019).
048   Building on the findings in Wei et al. (2022), we draw two key insights: (1) different features
049   contribute unequally to the task, with some being so insignificant that they can be masked without
050   significantly affecting the model’s performance. In their work, classification tasks were completed
051   with high accuracy even with certain masked features, a concept we apply to our authentication task;
052   and (2) There is an inherent trade-off between the model’s security, robustness, and accuracy; it is
053   essential to carefully balance these aspects in designing effective defenses.
      Generalizability refers to a model’s ability to maintain consistent performance when applied to new,

      Vision Transformer (ViT) Alexey (2020) is an architecture inherited from Natural Language Pro-
067   cessing (Vaswani, 2017) while applied to image classification with taking raw image patches as
068   inputs. Different from classical Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), the architectures of ViTs
069   are based on self-attention modules (Vaswani, 2017), which aim at modeling global interactions of
070   all pixels in feature maps. More precisely, ViTs take sequential image patches as inputs, and the
071   attention mechanism enables interaction and aggregation directly among patch information. There-
072   fore, compared to CNNs where image features are progressively learnt from local to global context
073   via reducing spatial resolution, ViTs enjoy obtaining global information from the very beginning.
074   Up till now, such convolution-free networks have been achieving great success on various computer
075
      vision tasks, including image classification (Touvron et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021a;
      Li et al., 2022; Mao et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023), object detection (Liu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022;
076
      Yao et al., 2023), semantic segmentation (Strudel et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2023) and
077
      image generation (Chen et al., 2021b).

080   Resnet face recognition models to achieve superior image mapping during training. This approach
081
      aims to enhance performance on an unseen dataset that significantly differs from the training set.
      Distributed training and collaborative learning have been widely used in large scale machine learning
082
      tasks. In most scenarios, people assume that gradients are safe to share and will not expose the
083
      training data. Some recent studies show that gradients reveal some properties of the training data,
084   for example, property classifier Melis et al. (2019) (whether a sample with certain property is in the
085   batch) and using generative adversarial networks to generate pictures that look similar to the training
086   images (Gentry, 2009; Hitaj et al., 2017; Melis et al., 2019).

      In the work Zhu et al. (2019), present an optimization algorithm that can obtain both the training
088
      inputs and the labels in just few iterations. The method deep leakage is an optimization process

      and does not depend on any generative models; therefore, DLG Zhu et al. (2019) does not require
090   any other extra prior about the training set, instead, it can infer the label from shared gradients; the

092   of synthetic look-alike alternatives(Yin et al., 2021). We propose a new VIT model with multiple

097   (Kim et al., 2020) we propose to mix two biometrics to have better accuracy in authentication by
098
      having dynamic weight for each and try to find the optimal mask of the two inputs.

103
104   There is a rich literature for defense strategies against gradient attacks in distributed models. Most
105   techniques focus on protecting the image as a whole ratter than focusing on the features. Each of
106   these techniques has its own flaws. In this work we study, how separating features helps preserve
107   accuracy in face authentication task, and also protect the original image. In order to guarantee
      privacy, it is necessary to introduce randomness to the learning algorithm.

      Gradient perturbation Zhu et al. (2019); Sun et al. (2020), directly prunes the shared gradients in
109   federated learning to defeat gradient leakage attacks; However, recent contributions Huang et al.
110   (2021) found that it is usually required to prune too much gradient information to fully defeat gra-
111   dient leakage attacks, which will greatly hurt the model accuracy.
112   Input data encryption Huang et al. (2020a;b) encrypts the data and hides private information; How-
113   ever, current state-of-the-art encryption methods Huang et al. (2020b) can also be evaded by adaptive
114   attack methods (Carlini et al., 2021). Therefore, an effective defense method which can reliably pro-
115   tect the privacy of clients while preserving model accuracy is still highly demanded.
116   Zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) (Bonawitz et al., 2016; McMahan et al., 2017) are widely recognized
117   cryptography tools that enable secure and private computations while safeguarding the underlying
118   data. In essence, ZKPs empower a proof to convince a verifier of a specific fact without revealing
119   any information beyond that fact itself. By verifying these proofs, the users can ensure the aggre-
120   gator’s actions are transparent and verifiable, installing confidence that the aggregation process is
121   conducted with utmost honesty. ZKP (Geiping et al., 2020) can ensure the server is protected from
122   aggregating malicious updates and constantly prove the transparency of users and server’s action
123
      but unfortunately it is necessary to share the private raw information with server and trust the infras-
      tructure and system as entity. Additionally, generating the proofs can be computationally expensive,
124
      impacting performance due to the time required for processing.
125
126   Input mixup creates virtual training examples by linearly interpolating two input data and the corre-
127   sponding one- hot labels (Melis et al., 2019). The method induces models to have smoother decision
128   boundaries and reduces overfitting to the training data. Manifold mixup extends this concept from
129
      input space to feature space (Verma et al., 2019). Also, Guo et al. (2019) proposed an adaptive
      mixup method, which improves input mixup by preventing the generation of improper data
130
131   Yun et al. (2019) proposed CutMix which inserts a random rectangular region of the input into
132   another. However, these methods can generate improper examples by randomly removing important
133   regions of the data; this may mislead the neural network.
134   recent work Pang et al. (2020) proposed a method, which aims to defend against adversarial at-
135   tacks by leveraging the mixup augmentation technique. While Mixup (Zhang, 2017) can effectively
136   obfuscate the visual features of images, its reliance solely on blending may not provide sufficient
137   security against sophisticated attacks targeting federated learning systems(e.g.,model inversion at-
138   tacks or data reconstruction techniques).Therefore, relying solely on mixup inference may not be a
139   robust solution for enhancing security in federated learning environments.
140   Promising results in mixup methods led to the idea of using augmentation methods in the purpose of
141
      security. Kim et al. (2020) proposes Puzzle mix to explicitly leveraging the saliency information and
      the underlying local statistics of natural examples. their work, proved that there is an optimal mask
142
      region to transport to another image in order to maximize the exposed saliency under the mask.

168   3.1   S ALIENCY-AWARE M ASKING AND S ECURITY E NHANCEMENT
169
170   Given the fact that facial images consist of both low-level (e.g., texture and edge details) and high-
171   level (e.g., eyes, nose, mouth) features, we aim to mask low-saliency regions without compromising
172
      the task of face authentication. The intuition is that low-level features contribute less to identity
      recognition, so masking these features (or replacing them with face, or fingerprint data) maintains
173
      authentication performance. The mask z ∈ [0, 1] is defined to control the amount of each biometric
174
      (face vs. fingerprint) exposed:
175
                                        h(x0 , x1 ) = (1 − z) ⊙ x0 + z ⊙ x1                              (4)
176
177
      where z ∈ Rn×n represents a spatial mask across n × n blocks of the image, and ⊙ is the element-
      wise product. The mask optimally determines which portions of the face can be concealed and
178
      replaced by corresponding blocks from the fingerprint.

      3.2   C OMPARATIVE L EARNING AND B LOCK -W ISE T RANSPORT
181
182   Based on Puzzle MixKim et al. (2020), we apply an optimal transport strategy to rearrange parts
183   of the image during the mixup. Our method divides both face and fingerprint images into grids of
184   blocks and computes an optimal transportation plan Π0 and Π1 , representing the pixel-wise align-
185   ment of features from face x0 and fingerprint x1 :
186                                  h(x0 , x1 ) = (1 − z) ⊙ Π0 x0 + z ⊙ Π1 x1                          (5)
187   The matrices Π0 and Π1 encode how much mass is moved from one part of the face to another or
188   from fingerprint regions into face regions. By optimizing the block-wise rearrangement, we ensure
189   that the most salient facial features are preserved while maximizing the safety of the biometric data
190   by mixing in fingerprint blocks.

205   4     M ETHODS
206
207   The core idea of our approach is to leverage the difference in importance between high-level and
208   low-level facial features for biometric authentication. High-level features such as the eyes, nose,
209   and mouth are critical for recognition, while low-level features like texture and edge details are less
210   important. By selectively obscuring low-level features, we maintain accuracy while significantly
211   improving security. Additionally, by introducing a novel use of the Jigsaw Vision Transformer
212
      (ViT) for image shuffling, we strengthen robustness against gradient inversion attacks. Finally, we
      integrate face images with fingerprint data in a multi-biometric system to further enhance security.
213
214   The proposed obscuring methods are implemented using ResNet, Vision Transformer (ViT), and
215   Jigsaw Vision Transformer (Jigsaw ViT) architectures, which allow for effective feature extraction
      and learning across both modalities.

229   as the eyes, nose, and mouth) remain intact while less significant regions are replaced to enhance

      5.1   DATA AUGMENTATION AND P ERTURBATION
325
326   We trained the models on originals images and images with multiple augmentation techniques tested
327   on images with augmentation to improve the safety of the models. These techniques include:
328
329         • Mixup: Two biometric images (face and fingerprint) are linearly combined using a mixup
330           ratio λ sampled from Beta(0.4, 0.4). This helps the model generalize better by exposing it
331           to blended inputs and ensuring that it learns shared features between the two modalities.
332         • Random Erasing: Portions of the face images are randomly obscured, simulating the oc-
333           clusion of certain facial features to see how well the model performs under such conditions.
334         • Gaussian Noise: Noise was added to the input images, ensuring the model learned to
335           ignore irrelevant noise and focus on key identifying features.
336
            • Block-Wise Masking: We divided face and fingerprint images into grids of blocks, and
337           certain blocks from the face were replaced with selected blocks from the fingerprint. The
338           mask z controlled which blocks were replaced, allowing us to fine-tune the balance between
339           security and recognition accuracy.
340
            • Random Block Swapping (Same Person): Next, we divided each face image into 3 × 3
341
              grids and performed random block swapping, between two different images of the same
342           person. Swapping low-level blocks had minimal impact on accuracy, but swapping high-
343           level blocks resulted in a slight drop in performance. The model, however, remained re-
344           silient and was able to generalize across different views of the same individual, showing
345           that high-level features are essential but have some tolerance for variability within the same
346           identity.
347         • Random Block Swapping (Different People): We extended the swapping experiments by
348           swapping blocks between images of different individuals. When low-level feature blocks
349           were swapped, accuracy remained stable. However, when high-level feature blocks were
350           swapped between different people, the model’s performance dropped significantly, con-
351           firming the critical role of high-level features in distinguishing between identities.
352         • Targeted Swapping of High-Level Features (Same Person): To further validate the role
353           of high-level features, we performed targeted block swapping, focusing specifically on
354           high-level features (eyes, nose, and mouth). Swapping these features between images of the
355           same person resulted in a slight drop in accuracy, but the model was still able to correctly
356           identify the individual, demonstrating robustness to minor alterations in high-level features
357           within the same person’s images.
358         • Targeted Swapping of High-Level Features (Different People) : When high-level fea-
359           tures such as the eyes, nose, and mouth were swapped between images of different individ-
360           uals, the model’s accuracy dropped drastically, reflecting the significant role these features
361           play in distinguishing between identities. However, the accuracy did not plummet to 50%,
362           which would indicate random guessing, because the low-level features such as skin texture
363
              and facial shape still provided some distinguishing information. This outcome suggests
              that while high-level features are critical for identity recognition, low-level features are not
364
              entirely irrelevant and still contribute meaningfully to the model’s ability to differentiate be-
365
              tween people. The drop in accuracy emphasizes the sensitivity of the model to high-level
366           feature alterations, particularly when comparing different individuals.

441
442   Following the black masking experiments, we explored the impact of random block swapping

444   better by learning from different facial representations of the same individual.
445
      We divided the face images into grids of 3 × 3 blocks and randomly swapped blocks between the

447   However, when high-level features (e.g., the eyes or nose) were swapped, we observed that the
448   model’s accuracy slightly decreased but still remained robust. This indicates that even partial swaps
449   of high-level features allow the model to preserve identity recognition.

460   indicating that it could still identify individuals even when key high-level features were swapped
461   between different views of the same person.
462   By selectively obscuring non-essential regions and manipulating critical features, we confirmed that
463   the model could maintain accuracy while forming more robust representations of individuals.

465   6.5   M ULTI -B IOMETRIC I NTEGRATION : FACE AND F INGERPRINT S WAPPING
466
467   Building on the success of face-swapping experiments, we introduced a multi-biometric system
468   where face images were combined with fingerprint images. This was achieved by swapping blocks
469   between a face image x0 and a fingerprint image x1 , effectively creating a multi-modal biometric
470
      representation.
471   Using the same 3 × 3 block grid, we selectively replaced low-saliency regions of the face with
472   fingerprint blocks, while retaining high-saliency facial features. The mixed image h(x0 , x1 ) was
473   then generated as follows:
474                                    h(x0 , x1 ) = (1 − z) ⊙ x0 + z ⊙ x1
475   Here, z is a mask that dictates which regions of the face are replaced with fingerprint data. This com-
476   bination of face and fingerprint data allowed us to obscure key areas of the face while maintaining
477   enough high-level facial features for accurate authentication.
478   By integrating the fingerprint biometric, we observed a significant boost in both accuracy and secu-
479   rity. The combination provided a dual-layer of protection, making it more difficult for adversaries to
480   recover the original face from gradients, while simultaneously improving the model’s generalization.

513   7   C ONCLUSION
514
515   n this paper, we investigated how various data augmentation techniques can enhance both the accu-
516   racy and security of face recognition models in distributed learning environments. By strategically
517   manipulating low- and high-saliency facial features, we demonstrated that targeted augmentation can
518   protect sensitive biometric data while maintaining high performance. Our approach leverages meth-
519
      ods like random masking, mixup, and block-wise swapping to obscure low-level features without
      compromising key identity-related regions. Additionally, the integration of fingerprint data along-
520
      side face images in a multi-biometric system further strengthened model robustness, making it sig-
521
      nificantly harder for adversaries to reconstruct the original images through Deep Gradient Leakage
522   (DGL) attacks.
523
524   Through the use of Jigsaw Vit and our novel perturbation methods, we showed that augmentations
525
      not only improve training performance but also serve as effective perturbation techniques during
      testing. The proposed multi-biometric system offers a dual-layer of security while ensuring accu-
526
      racy remains uncompromised. Overall, our results highlight the importance of data augmentation
527
      in safeguarding privacy in federated learning and present a scalable solution for secure biometric
528   authentication that resists sophisticated gradient-based attacks.
