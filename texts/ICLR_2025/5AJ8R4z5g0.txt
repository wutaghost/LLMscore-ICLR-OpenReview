009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            One of the major challenges in estimating conditional potential outcomes and con-
012            ditional average treatment effects (CATE) is the presence of hidden confounders.
013            Since testing for hidden confounders cannot be accomplished only with observa-
014            tional data, conditional unconfoundedness is commonly assumed in the literature
015            of CATE estimation. Nevertheless, under this assumption, CATE estimation can
016
               be significantly biased due to the effects of unobserved confounders. In this work,
               we consider the case where in addition to a potentially large observational dataset,
017
               a small dataset from a randomized controlled trial (RCT) is available. Notably,
018
               we make no assumptions on the existence of any covariate information for the
019            RCT dataset, we only require the outcomes to be observed. We propose a CATE
020            estimation method based on a pseudo-confounder generator and a CATE model
021            that aligns the learned potential outcomes from the observational data with those
022            observed from the RCT. Our method is applicable to many practical scenarios of
023            interest, particularly those where privacy is a concern (e.g., medical applications).
024            Extensive numerical experiments are provided demonstrating the effectiveness of
025            our approach for both synthetic and real-world datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Estimating treatment effects is of significant interest to various scientific communities, such as
030
      in medicine (Glass et al., 2013; Feuerriegel et al., 2024) and social sciences (Imbens & Rubin,
031   2015; Imbens, 2024) for assessing the efficacy of a policy. Recently, various methods have been
032   developed using machine learning to estimate individual-level treatment effects, also known as
033   the conditional average treatment effects (CATE) (Shalit et al., 2017; Alaa & Van Der Schaar,
034   2017; Wager & Athey, 2018; Shi et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2023; Schweisthal et al., 2024; Fang
035   & Liang, 2024). While these methods have proven successful, their effectiveness in estimating
036   treatment effects can be significantly compromised in real-world applications due to the confounding
037   problem(Kallus et al., 2019; Chor et al., 2024). Confounders are variables that influence both the
038   treatment and the outcome. If not properly controlled for, they can severely bias the potential outcome
039
      and treatment effect estimations (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983). While it is well-established that
      treatment effects are identifiable under the assumption of conditional unconfoundedness (that is, no
040
      hidden confounders), estimating conditional treatment effects becomes much more challenging under
041
      unobserved confounders (Imbens & Rubin, 2015; Kallus & Zhou, 2018). In some ideal scenarios
042   like Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs), conditional unconfoundedness might be achieved by
043   design. However, these experiments often require an expensive data collection process. Furthermore,
044   the conditional unconfoundedness assumption is inherently not falsifiable from observational data
045   alone (Popper, 2005). For instance, passively collected healthcare databases often lack essential
046   clinical details that can influence treatment decisions made by both doctors and patients, such as
047   subjective evaluations of the severity of a condition or personal lifestyle factors. Consequently,
048   when applying causal inference models to observational data, it is common to assume conditional
049   unconfoundedness, which may fail to hold in practice and cannot be tested. This can cause significant
050
      bias in potential outcome estimation.
051   Problem Setting. In this work, we propose a novel approach to mitigate the bias in estimating CATE
052   under hidden confounders. Our analysis begins by considering a scenario in which both observational
053   data and RCT data are present – a common situation in many fields, such as in healthcare, where large
      observational datasets with rich features (e.g., electronic health records) are readily available, but

      Related Works Several recent works address the challenge of estimating treatment effects under
109   unobserved confounding by combining randomized controlled trials (RCTs) with observational data.
110   Some approaches leverage the internal validity of RCTs and how representative observational data is
111   using techniques such as weighting and doubly robust estimators (Colnet et al., 2024). Other methods
112   propose a linear correction term to adjust for confounding bias (Kallus et al., 2018). Methods have
113   also been developed for estimating heterogeneous treatment effects, requiring covariate-level data
114   for improved accuracy and balancing the representation of different observed features (Hatt et al.,
115   2022a). Kallus et al. (2019) introduce interval estimation for CATE under unobserved confounders
116   and the marginal sensitivity model (Rosenbaum, 2002). It is important to note that all of these
117
      methods assume that both individual covariates and outcomes from the RCTs are accessible, which
      differs from the assumptions of our approach, as we assume that only the outcomes of the RCT
118
      are observed. Other methods have explored specific scenarios for estimating CATE from multiple
119
      datasets, such as in recommendation systems (Li et al., 2024) or sequential observational data (Hatt &
120   Feuerriegel, 2024). Moreover, recent works have addressed the confounding introduced by applying
121   representation learning approaches to CATE estimation (Melnychuk et al., 2024).

      3   P ROPOSED A PPROACH
207
208
      In this section, we present two models designed to address the challenge of estimating conditional
209
      potential outcomes and the CATE in the presence of hidden confounders. To help understand
210   the challenge of hidden confounders, we first discuss in Section 3.1 with a case study about the
211   issue that arises on the baseline factual learner which relies solely on the observational data in the
212   presence of hidden confounders. Next, we introduce our two approaches: Marginals Balancing
213   (MB) in Section 3.2 and Projections Balancing (PB) in Section 3.3. Both approaches are designed
214   to mitigate bias, though they are based on distinct principles. Finally, in Section 3.4, we describe
215   our combined model, MB+PB, which integrates both approaches to improve CATE estimation under
      hidden confounding.

533
534   In this work, we introduced two approaches, Marginals Balancing (MB) and Projections Balancing
535   (PB), to address the challenge of CATE estimation under hidden confounders. By leveraging outcome-
536   only RCT data, we demonstrated how these models mitigate bias from unobserved confounders,
537   outperforming benchmark methods. The combination of MB and PB (MB+PB) leads to further
538   enhanced performance across synthetic and real-world datasets. While our methods show promising
539   empirical results, we aim to pursue a deeper theoretical understanding of the proposed methods in
      future works.

894
895   In line with the method used by Kallus et al. (2018); Hatt et al. (2022a) we generate a large
896
      observational dataset with confounding and a smaller unconfounded randomized dataset consisting
      solely of the outcomes, both derived from the real-world data described in Section A.2.1. Importantly,
897
      the randomized dataset is drawn from a different population than the observational one, reflecting
898
      the limitations of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) in generalizing to the broader population of
899   interest.
900
901   To do this, we follow the same procedure for the STAR, ACTG, and NSW datasets. First, we generate
902
      a small, unconfounded randomized dataset by sampling a small fraction of the RCT data points
      128, 50, 50. instances from the original dataset. We introduce a distributional discrepancy between the
903
      randomized and observational datasets by selecting individuals for the randomized dataset based on a
904
      covariate (“birthday” for STAR, “gender” for ACTG, and “age” for NSW), see (Hatt et al., 2022a) for
905   further details. Second, we create the observational dataset by introducing unobserved confounding,
906   ensuring that the treatment and control groups differ systematically in their potential outcomes.
907   Following Kallus et al. (2018), we select subjects from those who were not included in the randomized
908   dataset: controls (T = 0) with especially low outcomes (i.e., yi < E[Y | T = 0] − c · σY |T =0 , where
909   σY |T =0 is the standard deviation of the outcomes in the control group) and treated subjects (T = 1)
910   with notably high outcomes (i.e., yi > E[Y | T = 1] + c · σY |T =1 , where σY |T =1 is the standard
911   deviation of the outcomes in the treatment group).
912   The constant c is adjusted according to the size of the original dataset (with c = 1 for STAR, c = 0
913   for ACTG, and c = 0.25 for NSW) to control the number of subjects in the observational dataset,
914   ensuring that it remains large. This process introduces confounding by selectively including control
915   subjects with lower outcomes and treated subjects with higher outcomes into the observational
916   treatment and control groups. As a result, a naïve estimator relying solely on the observational
917   data will be biased. Moreover, because this selection is based on the outcome variable, it becomes
      impossible to control for this confounding.

      In this section, we provide the implementation details of our proposed algorithm MB+PB. Specifi-
      cally, we describe the neural network architectures used for the different modules in our algorithm.
957
      Additionally, we present a detailed pseudo-code for the training procedure.
958
959
      The Neural Networks Architectures. As detailed in Section 3.4, MB+PB consists of three
960   components: a generator ψ(η), a CATE learner µt (X, Ũ ), a marginals balancing module g̃, and a
961   projections balancing module g.
962
963           • Pseudo-Confounder Generator: The generator ψ(η) is a neural network designed to
964             generate pseudo-confounders from the input variables, which consist of standard Gaussian
965             noise. The network architecture consists of two fully connected layers with 16 hidden units
966             and ELU activation functions.
967           • CATE Learner: The CATE learner is modeled as an S-Learner µt (X, Ũ ) and is imple-
968             mented using a neural network with three fully connected layers. The first two layers have
969             32 hidden units, each followed by an ELU activation function. The final layer outputs a
970
                scalar, representing the estimated potential outcome.
971           • MB Module: The marginals balancing module g̃ is modeled as a neural network with two
                hidden layers, each containing 8 hidden units. ReLU activation functions are applied to the


                                                            18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
                 hidden layers, and the output is constrained between −1 and 1 or 0 and 1, using either a
973              tanh or a sigmoid activation function, respectively.
974
               • PB Module: The projections balancing module g is also modeled as a neural network with
975
                 two hidden layers, each containing 8 hidden units. ReLU activation functions are applied to
976
                 the hidden layers, and the output is constrained between −1 and 1 or 0 and 1, using either a
977              tanh or a sigmoid activation function, respectively.
978
979    We use the same neural network architectures for all of our results presented in the Experiments
980    Section 4.
981
982    The Algorithm. We present the full pseudo-code for MB+PB in Algorithm 1. The code consists of
983    the training loop of the proposed model and the loss functions computation.
984
985    Hyperparameters. For the regularization parameter α is set dynamically, following the heuristic
986    described below. We initially start with a small value for α, and as the observed factual loss
987    optimization stabilizes, we gradually increase the importance of the regularization term. In all of
988
       our experiments, we train for 2000 epochs. Specifically, we set α = 0.01 for the first 1230 epochs,
       then linearly increase α from 0.01 to 100 between epochs 1230 and 1430. From epoch 1430 to
989
       2000, we train the model with the high regularization term α = 100. Additionally, as described
990
       in Algorithm 1, there are multiple balancing steps involved in training the MB+PB constraint. To
991    increase the efficiency of our training process, we begin with a small number of balancing iterations
992    (5) when α is small, and increase this number to 50 as α becomes large. Note that we use the same
993    training strategy across all the datasets to avoid fine-tuning the hyperparameter and to have a better
994    assessment of the presented algorithm. For the learning rates of the different neural networks they are
995    all set at 0.001 and we use Adam as an optimizer. Finally, for the batch sizes, we use a batch size of
996    256, 200, and 200 for STAR, ACTG, and NSW respectively.
