009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper proposes a variable selection method based on maximum mean dis-
012            crepancy (MMD) under sparsity. Our approach assigns weights to each variable
013            and optimizes them within a regularized MMD framework, where some weights
014            are pushed to zero, corresponding to variables that are not important. These op-
015            timized weights serve as an importance measure for variables contributing to the
016            difference between two distributions. We propose an object-oriented variable se-
017            lection approach, where the selected variables via the optimized weights also mini-
018
               mize a specified loss function associated with particular objects or tasks. We focus
               on two common scenarios–two-sample tests and classification–aiming to improve
019
               the power of the MMD test and enhance the classification accuracy of classifiers.
020
               Theoretical results on the consistency of the estimated weights and the conver-
021            gence of the accelerated algorithms are established. Simulations and real-data
022            analysis validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Two-sample tests, which aim to determine whether two samples are drawn from the same distribu-
028   tion, have been extensively studied. Classical tests for the equality of two distributions, including
029   the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (Smirnov, 1939) and the Cramér–von Mises test (Anderson, 1962),
030   are only effective for low-dimensional data. Recently, the literature has witnessed the development
031   of many non-parametric testing methods (Rosenbaum, 2005; Székely & Rizzo, 2013; Gretton et al.,
032   2012; Wang et al., 2021) for multi-dimensional or even high-dimensional data. Among these, the
033
      maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) test (Gretton et al., 2012), which leverages kernel mean em-
      beddings to quantify the discrepancy between two distributions, has gained significant attention.
034
      The MMD test has found applications in various fields, such as generative models (Sutherland et al.,
035
      2017; Binkowski et al., 2018), transfer learning (Long et al., 2017; Wei et al., 2019), and change-
036   point detection (Cheng & Xie, 2021). With the rising popularity of the MMD method, researchers
037   have proposed several refinements and enhancements (Sutherland et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020;
038   Biggs et al., 2023) aimed at improving its test power in high-dimensional and complex data set-
039   tings, and some researchers have considered variants of the MMD statistic (Zaremba et al., 2013;
040   Chwialkowski et al., 2015; Ramdas et al., 2015a; Shekhar et al., 2022) to accelerate computation.
041
      Motivation. In multi-dimensional or high-dimensional data, sparsity is a common phenomenon
042   where only a small proportion of important variables contribute to the differences between two dis-
043   tributions. Non-important variables, often referred to as noise or irrelevant features, can adversely
044   affect the performance of two-sample tests. As shown in Mueller & Jaakkola (2015), an excessive
045   number of non-important variables may obscure the signals of important variables, making the dis-
046   tributions of the two samples become more similar and, consequently, reducing the power of the
047   MMD test. In addition, the binary classification also faces the issue of a sharp decline in classifi-
048   cation accuracy due to a large number of non-important variables. This motivates the great need to
049   identify important variables, also referred to as variable selection under sparsity.
050   Our approach. In this paper, we propose a variable selection method for two-sample problems un-
051   der the MMD framework. We first assign a non-negative weight to each variable and obtain the op-
052   timal weights by maximizing the MMD statistic. These optimal weights can serve as a reference for
053   variable importance. To prevent overemphasis on variables with strong signals, an ℓ2 -regularization
      term is incorporated into the weighted MMD for variable selection. Variables with weights signif-

      icantly greater than 0 are then identified as important. Considering that variable selection is often
055   a preliminary task for other objectives, we propose an object-oriented approach, which selects vari-
056   ables via the optimized weights that minimize a specified loss function tied to specific objects or
057   tasks. Moreover, we introduce an algorithm to accelerate the computation of the optimal weights.
058
      Related work. Yamada et al. (2018); Lim et al. (2020) propose performing marginal MMD tests
059
      on individual variables to identify important ones, but this approach overlooks interactions and de-
060
      pendencies, failing to capture differences in joint distributions. Similarly, Adamer et al. (2024)
061   ranks variable importance by optimizing feature weights but focus on marginal differences. Wang
062   et al. (2023) select variables by maximizing the MMD estimator to boost test power. However, this
063   method requires specifying a fixed number of variables, d, which risks including irrelevant variables
064   or excluding crucial ones if chosen improperly.
065
      The variable selection problem for binary classification has also attracted significant attention. Lo-
066
      gistic regression with ℓ1 -regularization Hastie et al. (2009) is widely used but assumes a specific
067   data model, limiting its applicability when the data deviates from this model. van Reenen et al.
068   (2016); Bénard et al. (2024) propose model-free variable selection methods for binary classification,
069   but these also focus on marginal differences, overlooking dependence in joint distributions.
070
      The selection of an appropriate regularization parameter in the regularization term is crucial for
071
      achieving better model fitting. Consequently, methods for choosing the regularization parameter
072
      have been extensively studied. A commonly used and effective method for selecting regularization
073   parameters is the cross-validation approach (Stone, 1974; Arlot & Celisse, 2010). In addition, infor-
074   mation criterion methods, such as AIC (Akaike, 1974), BIC (Schwarz, 1978) and GIC (Konishi &
075   Kitagawa, 1996) have also been widely applied. In addition, some researchers treat the regularization
076   parameter as a hyperparameter within the model and optimize it dynamically using gradient-based
077   methods (Bengio, 2000; Luketina et al., 2016; Franceschi et al., 2017).
078
      Contributions. The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows: (a) We pro-
079   pose a novel method for identifying important variables responsible for distributional differences in
080   sparse settings. Our approach leverages the advantages of the MMD test, which is non-parametric,
081   model-free, and accounts for dependence among variables. This allows our method to outperform
082   marginal approaches. (b) We propose an object-oriented variable selection method tailored to spe-
083   cific objectives, with applications in two-sample tests and classification. This approach significantly
084   enhances the performance of both two-sample tests and classification accuracy. (c) Given that the
085   weighted MMD statistic is a U-statistic, maximizing it over the weights using gradient-based itera-
086   tive algorithms often demands significant computational resources and time. To mitigate this chal-
087
      lenge, we employ the first-order Taylor expansion of the weighted MMD statistic as a new objective
      function, which accelerates the optimization process. Furthermore, we present the convergence of
088
      this accelerated method and the convergence rate of the associated solving algorithm.

      2.2   W EIGHTED MMD
121
122   When the null hypothesis in equation 1 is rejected, indicating a significant difference between the
123   two distributions F and G, a natural and important follow-up question is determining where these
124   differences occur. In sparse scenarios (Tibshirani, 1996), this reduces to identifying the variables
125   that contribute to the distributional discrepancy, which often involves a small subset of the total
126   dimensions. In addition, inspired by the fact that variable selection is often a preliminary task for
127   other objectives, in this paper, we consider the case where variable selection is guided by specific
128   objects or tasks, a problem we refer to as object-oriented variable selection.
129   One common scenario occurs in the two-sample testing problem. It is discovered in Ramdas et al.
130   (2015b) that the power of the MMD test diminishes as the number of non-informative variables
131   increases but the signal remains constant. A promising solution is to identify a subset of variables for
132   which the MMD test achieves optimal power (Mueller & Jaakkola, 2015). Similarly, in classification
133   tasks, the goal is to select the subset of variables that maximizes classification accuracy, as the
134   inclusion of non-important variables can degrade the performance of many classifiers (Andrews &
135
      McNicholas, 2013; Chen & Lee, 2020).
136   In the original versions of the MMD test and many distance-based classifiers (e.g., k-nearest neigh-
137   bors and support vector machines), all variables are treated equally, with the same weight. However,
138   assigning too much weight to non-important variables can lead to issues such as reduced test power
139   and decreased classification accuracy. By contrast, if we assign higher weights to important vari-
140   ables and near-zero weights to non-informative ones, the negative impact of non-important variables
141
      can be mitigated, potentially leading to increased test power and improved classification accuracy.
142   Figure 1 illustrates the impact of variable weighting on the power of the MMD test and the classi-
143   fication accuracy of a feedforward neural network with one hidden layer. In this toy example, the
144   data are drawn from X ∼ N (µ1 , Σ1 ) and Y ∼ N (µ2 , σ2 ) with µ1 , µ2 , Σ1 and Σ specified under
145   different scenarios. The results clearly show that increasing the weights for the important variables
146   (the first three dimensions) while simultaneously decreasing the weights for other variables leads to
147
      improvements in both MMD test power and classification accuracy. However, the optimal weighting
      scheme may vary across different scenarios, even when aiming for the same target.
148

190   We now turn to identifying important variables using the assigned weights. Intuitively, increasing
191   the weight of important variables should result in a significant rise in the MMD, while increasing
192   the weight of non-important variables should have the opposite effect, diminishing the MMD. This
193   observation motivates us to maximize the weighted MMD with respect to the assigned weights.

195   2.3       R EGULARIZATION ON WEIGHTED MMD
196
      To obtain the optimal weight vector that maximizes MMD2f,w , it is crucial to address the challenges
197
      posed by significant differences in signal strength among important variables. When these differ-
198
      ences exist, the process of maximizing the MMD tends to favor variables with higher signal strength,
199   resulting in an imbalance in weight allocation. Specifically, high-signal-strength variables receive
200   disproportionately large weights, while low-signal-strength variables are often overlooked. This dy-
201   namic can lead to the weights of less influential variables approaching zero, effectively rendering
202   them unselectable. To counter this bias, we propose incorporating a regularization term into the
203   objective function. This term mitigates the risk of excessively large weights for high-signal-strength
204   variables.
205   In this paper, we utilize ℓ2 -regularization (ridge penalty) for its computational flexibility, although
206   other types of regularization could also be employed. Additionally, the weights are further con-
207   strained to be non-negative and sum to p, as required under undisturbed conditions. In summary, the

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      This paper introduces a variable selection method based on the MMD framework. Our approach
534   assigns a weight to each variable, and the importance of variables is measured by solving an opti-
535   mization problem to obtain optimal weights. We then propose an object-oriented algorithm to select
536   variables for specific tasks, and the proposed accelerated algorithm significantly improves com-
537   putational efficiency. Numerical experiments demonstrate the reliability of the proposed method.
538   Although our approach is developed within the MMD framework, its underlying principles may
539   extend to other frameworks, such as the Wasserstein distance. Future research will explore these
      possibilities.
