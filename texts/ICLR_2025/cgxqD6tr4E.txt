009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The proliferation of creative video content has driven demand for textual descrip-
012            tions or summaries that allow users to recall key plot points or get an overview
013            without watching. The volume of movies available for viewing and speed of
014            turnover motivate automatic summarisation, which is challenging, requiring the
015            identification of character intentions and long-range temporal dependencies. The
016            few existing methods attempting this task rely heavily on textual screenplays as
017            input, greatly limiting their applicability. In this work, we propose the task of au-
018            tomatic screenplay generation, and a method, ScreenWriter, that operates only on
019
               video and produces output which includes dialogue, speaker names, scene breaks,
               and visual descriptions. ScreenWriter introduces a novel algorithm to segment the
020
               video into scenes based on the sequence of visual vectors, and a novel method for
021
               the challenging problem of determining character names, based on a database of
022            actors’ faces. We further demonstrate how automatically generated screenplays
023            can be used to create plot synopses with a hierarchical summarisation method
024            based on scene breaks. We test the quality of the final summaries on the recent
025            MovieSum dataset, which we augment with videos, and show that they are supe-
026            rior to several comparison models that assume access to goldstandard screenplays.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Thanks to the proliferation of streaming services and digital content providers, a large number of
032   movies are being released and made available every year. Automatic approaches to understanding
033   and summarising their content are paramount to enabling users to browse or skim through them,
034   and quickly recall key plot points, characters, and events without the need to re-watch from the
035   beginning. Aside from practical utility, movie summarisation is an ideal testbed for real-world
036   video and natural language understanding. Movies are often based on elaborate stories, with non-
037   linear structure and multiple characters whose emotions and actions are often not accompanied with
038   verbal cues. The summarisation task requires identifying beliefs and intentions of characters as well
039
      as reasoning over very long narratives (there are usually hundreds of pages in a transcript and tens
      of thousands of frames in a video), involving multiple modalities.
040
041   Most previous work on movie summarisation has focused primarily on the textual modality, un-
042   der the assumption that screenplays (or at least human-authored transcripts) are readily available
043   (Gorinski & Lapata, 2015; Chen et al., 2022; Agarwal et al., 2022). Movie summarisation is com-
044   monly viewed as a type of long-form summarisation, a task which has improved dramatically in
045
      recent years. This is partly due to large models with longer context windows, and the design of
      methods specifically for this task, such as dividing the input into chunks and forming a hierarchical
046
      summarisation structure (Chen et al., 2023a; Pang et al., 2023) or summarising chunks iteratively
047
      (Chang et al., 2023). Video understanding through generating descriptions for videos, has also re-
048   ceived much attention, largely independently from long-form summarisation, focussing instead on
049   short video clips lasting a few minutes (Tapaswi et al., 2016; Lei et al., 2018; Rafiq et al., 2023).
050   Two notable exceptions are Papalampidi & Lapata (2023) and Mahon & Lapata (2024) who con-
051   sider textual and visual modalities, aiming to summarise movies and television shows, respectively.
052   However, they both require a textual transcript with information about scene breaks, and charac-
053   ter names prefacing the lines actors speak. The assumption of having available written transcripts
      and/or screenplays is unrealistic, as most video providers do not have access to screenplays un-

054
      less they have produced the content themselves. Additionally, there are often differences between
055   original screenplays, and what is acted and shown in the movie.
056
057   In this paper, we address the challenging tasks of generating automatic screenplays and plot sum-
058
      maries of movies by leveraging only video and audio input, without relying on transcripts or text.
      As a result, our method, ScreenWriter is applicable to the large quantity of movie videos which exist
059
      without accompanying screenplays or human-annotated transcripts. A naive solution is to apply a
060
      speech-to-text model from which we could then build a rudimentary screenplay automatically. Us-
061   ing speech-to-text off the shelf for movie understanding is problematic for several reasons. Firstly, it
062   does not contain scene breaks, which can provide useful structural and semantic information (Gorin-
063   ski & Lapata, 2015; Papalampidi et al., 2019). Secondly, it does not contain descriptions of events
064   outside of dialogue such as characters fighting or kissing. And perhaps most importantly, it lacks
065   character names. Speaker diarization aims to determine whether different utterances are spoken by
066   the same speaker, but this speaker is still indicated with an arbitrary ID, rather than a character name.
067   ScreenWriter attempts to reconstruct screenplays and their sctructure as accurately as possible. It es-
068   chews the above mentioned pitfalls by segmenting the video into scenes using a novel parameter-free
069   method based on the minimum description length principle coupled with a dynamic programming
070   search algorithm. It associates speaker IDs to character names through a novel method which ex-
071   ploits a database of actor face images, coupled with the name of the character they played (which
072   is readily available online). By computing distances between face feature vectors in the same scene
073   as the utterance, we formulate the task as an instance of the linear sum assignment problem, which
074   can then be solved efficiently by known algorithms. Additionally, we add event descriptions (as an
075
      approximation to action lines which describe what the camera sees) using an image-to-text model on
      keyframes detected in each scene. We further demonstrate that automatically generated screenplays
076
      are good enough to produce textual summaries: we condense each scene independently and then
077
      fuse the information from all scenes into a final synopsis using zero-shot prompting.
078
079   ScreenWriter, and the accompanying summarisation method, is therefore modular, based on a num-
080   ber of interacting components that separately solve different subtasks. This design differs from
081
      recent work Song et al. (2024) proposing to modify transformer memory in order to handle longer
      video sequences. Scaling such end-to-end models to full-length movies remains a significant chal-
082
      lenge due to memory constraints and the complexity of extracting useful information from large
083
      inputs.1 Our approach, which breaks the task into subproblems which are then solved separately,
084   mitigates this issue and allows for more efficient processing and summarisation. In summary, our
085   contributions include:
086
087          • Proposing the new task of screenplay generation from video (and audio), and a modular
088            framework, ScreenWriter, to address this task;
089          • A novel, parameter free method for detecting scene boundaries in video;
090
             • A novel algorithm for detecting character names based on a database of actors’ faces;
091
092          • A-proof-of-concept summariser which takes automatic screenplays from ScreenWriter
093
               as input and produces textual synopses following a hierarchical method with zero-shot
               prompting.

098   Video Understanding The problem of generating descriptions for videos has received significant
099   attention in the literature. Traditional video description approaches often extract features from in-
100   dividual frames and fuse them into a single feature vector to generate a textual description (Zhang
101   et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2022). SwinBERT (Lin et al., 2022) introduces an end-
102
      to-end video network that samples frames densely, avoiding the need for image-based encoders.
      Similarly, Lei et al. (2020) use a memory-augmented vision transformer to generate descriptions for
103
      short videos. In the area of video segmentation, Souček & Lokoč (2020) introduce TransNet v2, a
104
      deep learning model for shot transition detection. However, shot boundaries are easier to detect than
105   scene boundaries because there is a more striking pixel-level discontinuity.

      Despite this progress, many popular video description datasets (Chen & Dolan, 2011; Xu et al.,
109   2016) contain videos that are only ∼10s in length, which are much shorter than full-length movies.

113   activity and behavior, but this too is far shorter and more constrained than full movie summarisation.
114   Some methods have started to tackle movie content, but in the form of much shorter clips. Wu &
115   Krahenbuhl (2021) use YouTube movie clips for small tasks like classifying movie year or genre, but
116   lack a comprehensive approach for understanding/summarising an entire movie. Chen et al. (2023b)
117
      propose Movies2Scenes, a method that uses movie metadata to learn scene representations, though it
      relies on predefined scenes based on shot transitions rather than semantically meaningful boundaries.
118
      Han et al. (2024) address the problem of assigning names to characters within a local movie clip,
119
      using a ”character bank” of images which is then used to prompt a LLM for character names. This
120   differs from our method in the use of a LLM for name assignment, and in that we are assigning names
121   to speaker IDs that arise from speaker diarization, rather than to dialogue directly. Moreover, our
122   assignment concerns the entire movie, rather than a short snippet. Some works aim to summarise
123   short videos, a task referred to as video captioning. Sridevi & Kharde (2020) summarizes short
124   videos using a two-stream CNN. Seo et al. (2022) use a bidirectional model that uses both video and
125   audio to produce video captions. Zhou et al. (2018b) propose a single masked transformer objective
126   to detect and then caption all events in a moderate length ( 3min) video Unsupervised pertaining has
127   also been explored, e.g. by Yang et al. (2023), who train a video-captioning model using transcribed
128
      utterances as pseudo-captions. Some systems based on large proprietary models have been proposed
      for longer videos. Zhang et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2023), include multiple modules, including visual
129
      GPT-4, and use PysceneDetect for scene breaks. Wu et al. (2024) prompts an LLM to predict scene
130
      breaks from transcribed speech and captions, which is then used for video question-answering.
131
132
      Long-form summarisation Recent work has started to address the task of summarising much
133
      longer videos. Papalampidi et al. (2021) describe a method for summarising full-length movies by
134   creating shorter videos containing their most informative scenes which they assume to be ‘turning
135   points’ (i.e., key events in a movie). Papalampidi & Lapata (2023) produce text summaries of TV
136   show episodes, by converting visual features into embeddings alongside word embeddings from the
137   transcript. Mahon & Lapata (2024) also summarise TV show episodes from video and text input,
138   by converting the video to text, and then treating it as a text-only problem. Our method differs from
139   all of these approaches in that it only requires video as input, tackling the more challenging task
140   of understanding who is speaking and what they are doing (this information is taken for granted in
141   screenplays and human-authored transcripts).
142
143   Movie Datasets Datasets containing videos for full-length feature movies are few and far be-
144   tween possibly due to copyright restrictions and the computational overhead of storing and process-
145
      ing lengthy videos. Papalampidi et al. (2024) introduce TRIPOD, a dataset consisting of screen-
      plays, their movies, and corresponding trailers, however no summaries or synopses are provided.
146
      MovieNet (Huang et al., 2020) contains summaries and other annotations for movies, but not the
147
      videos themselves, which are specifically the focus of the present work.2 Papalampidi & Lapata
148   (2023) release SummScreen3D a dataset consisting of soap opera episodes, their (crowd-sourced)
149   transcripts, and summaries. However, these episodes are not self-contained, as they typically refer
150   to events or characters from previous episodes. In addition, they tend to be relatively short, and
151   dialogue-heavy, with recurring scene locations. In this work, we wish to explore longer videos of
152   70+ minutes, and move away from the niche domain of soap operas. In experiments, we use the
153   recent MovieSum dataset (Saxena & Keller, 2024) which contains screenplays and their Wikipedia
154   plot summaries. We augment this dataset with videos that we privately procure for the test set.

      3.1   T RANSCRIPT G ENERATION
187
      The first step in ScreenWriter is to extract the audio from the movie, using an automatic transcription
188
      model with speaker diarization (Bain et al., 2023). This produces a transcript with each utterance
189   marked by a speaker ID. The speaker ID is the same for the same character throughout the movie
190   (up to the accuracy of the diarization), but the character names are lacking. We also extract key
191   frames from the entire video, and store the timestamps of these keyframes. These will be used to
192   compute speaker names and visual information, which we will add to the diarized transcript to form
193   a screenplay. It is also on this sequence of keyframes that the scene breaks are computed. There is
194   an average of around 1,000 detected keyframes for a feature-length movie.
195
196   3.2   S CENE D ETECTION
197
198   ScreenWriter extracts visual features from each keyframe, and applies a novel algorithm to segment
199   the resulting sequence of visual feature vectors. There are two parts to our algorithm: the definition
200   of a cost for a particular partition into scenes, and the search for the partition that minimizes this
201
      cost. The first part, the cost definition, is formulated using the minimum description length principle,
      which claims the correct representation of the data is the one using the fewest bits. We assume that
202
      the vectors for each scene are encoded with respect to their collective mean. That is, for each scene
203
      in the given partition, we calculate the mean of all vectors in that scene, and hence, the probability of
204   each vector, p(v), under the multivariate normal distribution with this mean. To reduce run time, we
205   use a single fixed covariance calculated from the entire sequence of vectors. The Kraft-McMillan
206   inequality (Kraft, 1949; McMillan, 1956) then determines that under the optimal encoding, the num-
207   ber of bits needed to represent v is − log2 p(v). The sum of this value across all N vectors v in the
208   video, plus the number of bits to represent the mean vectors themselves, gives the total bitcost for
209   a given partition. The mean vectors require dm bits, where d is the dimensionality, and m is the
210   floating point precision, for which we use the standard of 32. Partitions with more scenes require
211   more bits for the mean vectors, but also have mean vectors that better cover the keyframe features,
212
      leading to decreased − log2 p(v) on average. This trade-off encourages a partition with neither too
      few nor too many scene breaks.
213
214   The second part, the search for the minimizer of the above cost, can be solved exactly using dynamic
215   programming. Let B(i, j) be the cost of having a single scene that runs from keyframes i to j, and
      let C(i, j) be the minimum cost of all keyframes from i to j, under all possible partitions. Then we

254   3.3     C HARACTER NAME I DENTIFICATION
255
256   To replace the arbitrary speaker IDs with character names, we first create a database of images of
257   actors’ faces paired with the name of the character they played from the IMDB movie page. As some
258
      of these images may contain multiple faces, or no faces, or even an entirely different character, we
      filter them to ensure a higher proportion contain only the face of the correct character, keeping only
259
      images with exactly one detected face, and for which the detected gender matches the name gender.
260
      Finally, we verify the faces in all pairs of remaining images against each other, using the DeepFace3
261   library, to create a graph where images are connected if and only if they are verified as being the
262   same person, and then exclude all images that are not part of the largest clique. In total, we filter out
263   about 40% of images on average. This produces a name bank of character names paired with a set
264   of images of the face of that character.
265
      For each scene, and for each character in our name bank, we define the cost of putting that character
266
      name in that scene as the minimum distance between an image of that character‘s face, and a face
267   detected in any keyframe from the scene. The distance is the Euclidean distance of the DeepFace
268   feature vectors. This avoids the incorrect assumption that the character speaking must be in shot, and

299
      Specifically, we define a matrix S whose i, jth entry is the cost of assigning speaker j to name
      i. Let m, n, and k be the numbers of character names in the database, scenes in the movie, and
300
      unique speaker IDs in the transcript. Using matrix notation, we can then write S = AB, where A
301
      is the m × n speaker ID-scene cost matrix, whose i, jth entry is the cost of assigning speaker j to
302   scene i, and B is a n × k matrix whose i, jth entry is 1/a if speaker ID j appears in scene i, where
303   a is the number of scenes speaker j appears in, and 0 otherwise. Because speaker diarization is
304   imperfect and often mistakenly splits the same character into multiple IDs, we duplicate each matrix
305   column three times, which allows up the three different speaker IDS assigned to the same character
306   name. We also define a cost of leaving a SpeakerID unassigned as expected value of the cost of
307   assigning a random speaker ID to a random character, which means that an ID remains unassigned
308   if it is no closer to any character than a random speaker ID and character are to each other. The full
309   name-assignment method is shown in Algorithm 2 in Appendix A.

      3.4   S CREENPLAY G ENERATION
313
314   After running the assignment algorithm from Section 3.3, we can replace the common speaker IDs
315
      in the automated, diarized transcript with the corresponding character names. Also, by matching
      the utterance times with the keyframe timestamps, we can insert the scene breaks from Section 3.2
316
      into the transcript. Finally, we include descriptions of the visual contents of the scene by selecting
317
      three evenly spaced keyframes from that screen, applying an image captioning model (Peng et al.,
318   2023), and inserting the output to the corresponding timestamped location of the transcript. In the
319   caption, we replace occurrences of the nouns ‘person’, ‘woman’, ‘man’, ‘girl’, and ‘boy’ (and their
320   determiners) with a character name, if this can be inferred from gender matching with the speaker
321   names. For example, if the only non-male name in the script is ‘Clarice Starling’, then the occurrence
322   of ‘a woman’ in the caption is replaced with ‘Clarice Starling’. The result of adding names, scene
323   breaks and visual descriptions to the transcript is an automatically generated screenplay. We show
      an example of ScreenWriter output in Figure 3.

348   3.5   M OVIE S UMMARY G ENERATION
349
350   We experimentally show that the generated screenplay can be used as a basis for movide summari-
351   sation. We adopt a hierarchical summarisation approach (Pang et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2023),
352   as it has been shown to be particularly suited to long inputs that exceed the context window size
353
      of large language models, and in our case can leverage the organization of the content into scenes.
      We this first summarise the transcript dialogue of each scene, and then fuse the resulting sequence
354
      of summaries, along with the visual information for each scene into a single summary for the en-
355
      tire movie (see Figure 1). Our summariser is implemented using a widely-used open-source LLM
356   library (Dubey et al., 2024) with zero-shot prompting.

381   Evaluation Metrics Automated evaluation metrics are crucial for our task and for related long-
382   form applications where human evaluation is extremely labor-intensive, costly, and difficult to de-
383   sign Krishna et al. (2023). As there is no single agreed-upon metric for automatically evaluating
384   summarisation, we report several complementary metrics aimed at assessing different aspects of
385   summary quality. Rouge (Lin, 2004) assesses informativeness against the reference summaries;
386   PRISMA (Mahon & Lapata, 2024) measures factual precision and recall with respect to the gold
387   summary; we use GPT4-turbo for both fact the extraction and evaluation stages; SummaC (Laban
388   et al., 2022) uses NLI to measure consistency between the input document (gold screenplay) and
389
      generated summary; we use the SummaCConv version with 50 evenly-spaced bins; AlignScore
      (Zha et al., 2023) scores the ‘informational alignment’ between the source (gold screenplay) and
390
      the generated summary; we use the base-model checkpoint provided by the authors, and the rec-
391
      ommended ‘nli’ setting with sentence chunk splitting. For both Alignscore and PRISMA we score
392   duplicated information as incorrect, to penalize LLM outputs that repeat the same sentences over
393   and over. To measure the accuracy of our scene detection method, we use standard partition quality
394   metrics: cluster accuracy, adjusted Rand index and normalized mutual information, as defined in
395   Mahon & Lukasiewicz (2024).

      6      C ONCLUSION
518
519
      In this work, we proposed the task of generating automatic screenplays for movies from only video
520
      and audio input. Our model, Screenwriter, produces screenplays automatically (including dialogue,
521
      speaker names, scene breaks and visual descriptions) based on two novel algorithms: one for seg-
522   menting the video into scenes, based on the minimum description length principle and dynamic-
523   programing for search, and one for assigning character names to dialogue utterances using a database
524   of names and actor faces. Experimental results show that the output of ScreenWriter together with
525   a hierarchical summarisation method can be used to generate movie plot synopses from only video
526   and audio input. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt to address this task. In the
527   future, we would like to extend ScreenWriter’s capabilities to other types of long videos, including
528   documentaries, current affaires television programmes, and sports games, and explore personalised
529   summary generation.
