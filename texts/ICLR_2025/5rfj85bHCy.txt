                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
015            Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) are an increasingly popular class of
016
               techniques for the numerical solution of partial differential equations (PDEs),
               where neural networks are trained using loss functions regularized by relevant PDE
017
               terms to enforce physical constraints. We present a new class of PINNs called
018
               HyResPINNs, which augment traditional PINNs with adaptive hybrid residual
019            blocks that combine the outputs of a standard neural network and a radial basis
020            function (RBF) network. A key feature of our method is the inclusion of adaptive
021            combination parameters within each residual block, which dynamically learn to
022            weigh the contributions of the neural network and RBF network outputs. Addition-
023            ally, adaptive connections between residual blocks allow for flexible information
024            flow throughout the network. We show that HyResPINNs are more robust to
025            training point locations and neural network architectures than traditional PINNs.
026            Moreover, HyResPINNs offer orders of magnitude greater accuracy than compet-
027
               ing methods on certain problems, with only modest increases in training costs.
               We demonstrate the strengths of our approach on challenging PDEs, including
028
               the Allen-Cahn equation and the Darcy-Flow equation. Our results suggest that
029
               HyResPINNs effectively bridge the gap between traditional numerical methods
030            and modern machine learning-based solvers.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Partial differential equations (PDEs) model a variety of phenomena across science and engineering and
037   are traditionally solved using numerical methods such as finite difference methods (LeVeque, 2007)
038   and finite elements (Strang et al., 1974). Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) are meshless
039
      methods (Raissi et al., 2019; Raissi, 2018) that solve PDEs by training deep feedforward neural net-
      works (DNNs) using PDEs as soft constraints. This traditional PINN training method poses challenges
040
      due to complicated loss landscapes arising from the PDE-based soft constraints (Krishnapriyan et al.,
041
      2021). Recent approaches to ameliorating issues include curriculum learning (Krishnapriyan et al.,
042   2021), novel optimization techniques (Cyr et al., 2020), domain decomposition (X-PINNs) (Jagtap &
043   Karniadakis, 2020), gradient-enhanced training (G-PINNs) (Yu et al., 2022a), or discretely-trained
044   PINNs using RBF-FD approximations in place of automatic differentiation (DT-PINNs) (Sharma &
045   Shankar, 2022). Further significant strides have been made in developing new DNN architectures
046   to enhance PINNs’ representational capacity, including adaptive activation functions Jagtap et al.
047   (2020b;a), positional embeddings Liu et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2021b), and innovative architec-
048   tures Wang et al. (2021a); Sitzmann et al. (2020); Gao et al. (2021); Fathony et al. (2020); Moseley
049   et al. (2023); Kang et al. (2023).
050   In addition to architectural choices, certain training pathologies exist including, spectral bias (Ra-
051   haman et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021b), unbalanced back-propagated gradients (Wang et al., 2021a;
052   2022b), and causality violations (Wang et al., 2022a; 2024b). Efforts focused on improving PINNs’
053   training performance include loss re-weighting schemes (Wang et al., 2021a; 2022b; McClenny &
      Braga-Neto, 2020; 2023; Maddu et al., 2022) and adaptive resampling of collocation points, such as


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      importance sampling (Nabian et al., 2021), evolutionary sampling (Daw et al.), and residual-based
055   adaptive sampling (Wu et al., 2023).
056
057   Combining traditional methods with DNNs is a new line of research which focuses on leveraging the
058
      advantages of both. For example, incorporating a Fourier feature layer to preprocess DNN inputs
      enhances their capacity to capture high-frequency functions (Tancik et al., 2020), while also reducing
059
      eigenvector bias in PINNs (Wang et al., 2021b; Raynaud et al., 2022). Fourier neural operators (Li
060
      et al., 2020) utilize Fourier layers in the network architecture and have become a popular approach for
061   inverse problems. Similarly, physics-informed radial basis networks (PIRBNs) have been proposed
062   as efficient local approximators that integrate domain knowledge during training, performing well in
063   solving PDEs with high-frequency features or ill-posed domains (Bai et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024).
064   Chrysos et al. (2022; 2020) augment polynomial neural networks with DNNs, with a direct focus
065   on classification and discriminative problems. Further explorations encompass alternative objective
066   functions, such as those employing numerical differentiation techniques Huang & Alkhalifah (2024)
067   and variational formulations inspired by Finite Element Methods (FEM) Kharazmi et al. (2019);
068   Berrone et al. (2022); Patel et al. (2022), along with additional regularization terms to expedite
069
      PINNs’ convergence Yu et al. (2022b); Son et al. (2021).
070   Recent work has investigated deep architectures for solving PDEs through methods such as stacked
071   DNNs (Howard et al., 2023) and residual blocks with adaptive gating parameters (Wang et al., 2024a).
072   Wang et al. (2024a) incorporate residual blocks with adaptive skip connections, which dynamically
073   balance the input and learned residuals at each layer. Specifically, they incorporate adaptive residual
074   blocks which dynamically balance the contributions of both the input and residual function at each
075
      layer, while the stacked approach of Howard et al. (2023) utilizes transfer learning to improves the
      ability of PINNs to handle complex, multi-scale PDEs.
076
077   While these deep residual based approaches show much promise, the increased architectural complex-
078   ity, leads to higher computational costs both in terms of memory and training time—requiring careful
079   selection of training routines to prevent instabilities or poor convergence. Finally, while Fourier
080   features help in capturing high-frequency components, they can still struggle with discontinuous
081
      solutions or sharp interfaces, which are challenging for neural networks to approximate due to the
      smoothness of typical activation functions.
082
083   To address these challenges, we propose a novel class of network architectures termed HyResPINNs,
084   that combines a standard DNN with a Radial Basis Function (RBF) network within bi-level adaptive
085   residual blocks, such that the relative contribution of both the DNN and RBF networks are adaptively
086   learned during training, along with the full residual block outputs. The proposed architecture leverages
087
      the strengths of both smooth and non-smooth function approximators to model complex physical
      systems. The standard DNN using smooth activation functions effectively captures the continuous,
088
      global behaviors of the solution, which are common in many physical phenomena. While the RBF
089
      network then captures the localized, discontinuous or sharp features in the target solution. This
090   representational division ensures that the model can accurately capture both smooth and non-smooth
091   components of the solution, forming a robust network capable of modeling many types of physical
092   systems.
093
      Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
094
095
096          • Propose a novel adaptive residual architecture: We introduce a new class of physics-
097            informed neural networks, HyResPINN, that combine standard neural networks with Radial
098
               Basis Function (RBF) networks within bi-level adaptive residual blocks.
099
100          • Demonstrate the superiority of the block structure: We show that our residual block
101            architecture provides significant improvements over standard approaches in capturing both
102            smooth and non-smooth features, leading to more accurate modeling of complex physical
103            systems.
104
105          • Highlight the benefits of adaptivity between residual blocks: We demonstrate that the
106            adaptive learning of contributions between the NN and RBF networks, as well as between
107            residual blocks, results in superior performance and stability compared to non-adaptive
               methods.

             • Thorough empirical evaluation: Show our method HyResPINN outperforms standard

233   Adaptive Hybrid Contribution Coefficients The trainable parameter α(l) controls the relative
234   contributions of the DNN and RBF components to the output—where optimizing α finds the best
235   balance between these two elements. Since the sigmoid function φ constrains its output to the
236   range [0, 1], the resulting output is a convex combination of the RBF and DNN components. We
237   further incorporate adaptable residual connection parameters between each hybrid block, denoted as
238   β (l) —similar to the approaches in (Howard et al., 2023) and (Wang et al., 2024a)
239   However, unlike (Wang et al., 2024a), where their adaptable parameters are initialized to zero to force
240   the network to learn non-linearities from scratch, we initialize each φ(α(l) ) = 0.5, ensuring equal
241   contributions from both components at the start of training, and each β (l) = 1. When φ(α) = 1,
242   only the RBF network contributes; when φ(α) = 0, only the DNN contributes. The optimal choice
243   of α depends on the problem characteristics. For problems with large regions of smoothness, the
244   model might favor a lower α, assigning more weight to the DNN components in each residual block.
245   Conversely, for problems involving sharp transitions or discontinuities, a higher α (favoring the
246   RBF) may be preferable. In hybrid problems with smooth and non-smooth regions, α will likely fall
247   between 0.4 and 0.6, providing a balanced combination of the two networks.
248   To encourage smoother solutions and prevent the network from introducing excessive non-linearity,
249   we add a regularization term to each block’s trainable parameter α. This regularization penalizes
250   large values of α, effectively controlling the contribution of non-linearity from the RBF components.
