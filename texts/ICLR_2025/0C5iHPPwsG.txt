009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in representation learning have successfully leveraged the under-
012            lying domain-specific structure of data across various fields. However, represent-
013            ing diverse and complex entities stored in tabular format within a latent space
014            remains challenging. In this paper, we introduce D EEP CAE, a novel method
015            for calculating the regularization term for multi-layer contractive autoencoders
016            (CAEs). Additionally, we formalize a general-purpose entity embedding frame-
017            work and use it to empirically show that D EEP CAE outperforms all other tested
018
               autoencoder variants in both reconstruction performance and downstream predic-
               tion performance. Notably, when compared to a stacked CAE across 13 datasets,
019
               D EEP CAE achieves a 34% improvement in reconstruction error.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   The underlying structure of data can be defined by its organization and relationships, encompassing
025   semantic meaning, spatial positioning, and temporal sequencing across a range of domains. Lately,
026   this structure has been leveraged to build use case-agnostic data representations in a self-supervised,
027   auto-regressive, or augmentative manner (Pennington et al., 2014; Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al.,
028   2019; Conneau et al., 2019; Caron et al., 2020; Oord et al., 2018; He et al., 2020; Higgins et al.,
029   2016), including the prediction of next tokens given the previous ones in GPTs (OpenAI, 2023), and
030   image rotation (Gidaris et al., 2018).
031   Real world entities, such as products and customers of a company, generally stored in tabular for-
032   mat, can also be represented as multi-dimensional vectors, or embeddings. The demand for reusable
033   entity embeddings is growing across research and business units, as a universally applicable repre-
034   sentation for each entity can drastically reduce pre-processing efforts for a wide range of predictive
035   models. This, in turn, can shorten development cycles and potentially enhance predictive perfor-
036   mance. For example, customer embeddings could greatly improve sales and marketing analyses in
037   large enterprises.
038   In industry settings, data pre-processing and feature engineering steps can constitute a large portion
039   of a project’s lifespan and result in duplicated efforts: Munson (2012) and Press (2016) showed that
040   the average estimated percentage of time spent by data scientists for retrieving, pre-processing and
041   feature-engineering data is between 50% and 70% of a data science project’s lifespan. Furthermore,
042   scientists may encounter challenges in feature selection and in identifying relationships within the
043   data (e.g., correlation and causality). Complexity - frequently arising from high-dimensionality -
044
      can result in both overfitting and underfitting. Holistically, projects with different objectives relying
      on similar data could benefit from a unified shared pre-processing and feature engineering process,
045
      which would produce pre-processed data for a general purpose.
046
047   While text and images are structurally organized (syntax and semantics in text, spatial structure
048   in images), tabular data does not necessarily exhibit such relations between features that could be
049   leveraged for modeling purposes. Although many recent modality-specific representation learning
050
      methods (e.g. Transformers for text (Vaswani et al., 2017)) leverage the increase in computational
      capabilities, classical representation learning applicable to tabular data has yet to be advanced.
051
052   In this work, (1) we propose D EEP CAE, which extends the contractive autoencoder (CAE) frame-
053   work (Rifai et al., 2011b) to the multi-layer setting while preserving the original regularization
      design, unlike stacked CAE approaches; (2) we outline a general-purpose end-to-end entity embed-


                                                         1

054
      ding framework applicable to a variety of domains and different embedding models. We use the
055   proposed framework to evaluate different representation learning methods across 13 publicly avail-
056   able classification and regression datasets, covering a multitude of entities (A.1). We measure both
057   the reconstruction error as well as the downstream performance on the dataset’s respective classifi-
058   cation or regression task when using the embeddings as input. We show that D EEP CAE performs
059   especially well in entity embedding settings (see Figure 2).
060
      Our original contribution is the extension of the mathematical simplification introduced by Rifai
061   et al. (2011b) for the calculation of the Jacobian of the entire encoder in the contractive loss from
062   single-layer to multi-layer settings (see Section 4). This extension makes training the entire multi-
063   layer encoder computationally feasible, allowing for increased degrees of freedom, while maintain-
064   ing the benefits of regularization.

      2     P RELIMINARIES
067
068
      Autoencoders are a specific type of neural network designed for unsupervised learning tasks, whose
069   main purpose is to encode inputs into a condensed representation, and are often used for dimen-
070   sionality reduction and feature learning. The usually lower-dimensional space in which the input
071   is projected is referred to as latent space, or embedding space. An autoencoder is comprised of
072   two parts: an encoder, which transforms the input into its latent representation (embeddings), and a
073   decoder that reconstructs the input from the obtained embeddings during training (Rumelhart et al.,
074   1986). The encoder and the decoder are trained simultaneously with the objective of minimizing the
075   reconstruction loss, i.e., a measure of how well the decoder can reconstruct the original input from
076   the encoder’s output.
077   Methods like Principal Component Analysis (PCA) dominated the field before autoencoders were
078   introduced. Baldi & Hornik (1989) showed that a single layer encoder without a non-linear activa-
079   tion function converges to a global minimum that represents a subspace of the corresponding PCA.
080   Thanks to their non-linearity, autoencoders allow for a more sophisticated and effective feature ex-
081   traction, which motivates the use of autoencoders compared to PCA for many applications.
082   When using autoencoders for dimensionality reduction, the information bottleneck represented by
083   the embedding layer (which is smaller than the input) prevents the encoder from learning the identity
084   function. However, some applications can benefit from over-complete representations, i.e. represen-
085   tations that have a higher latent dimension than their original dimension (e.g. in image denoising
086   Xie et al. (2012) or sparse coding Ranzato et al. (2006)), which work with other methods of regular-
087   ization. Nonetheless, research has shown that even for applications with an information bottleneck,
088   some forms of regularization can lead to more robust latent representations (Rifai et al., 2011b). One
089
      of these methods are contractive autoencoders (CAE).
090   Moreover, unlike traditional autoencoders, which directly output a latent representation, Variational
091   Autoencoders (VAE) follow a stochastic approach by producing a multivariate distribution parame-
092   terized by µ, σ in the latent space Kingma & Welling (2013).
093
094   2.1   C ONTRACTIVE AUTOENCODERS
095
096   Contractive Autoencoders (CAE) contract the input into a lower-dimensional non-linear manifold
097
      in a deterministic and analytical way (Rifai et al., 2011b). Being able to learn very stable and
      robust representations, CAE were proven to be superior to Denoising Autoencoders (DAE) (see
098
      Rifai et al. (2011b)) - where the autoencoder is trained to remove noise from the input for robust
099
      reconstructions. In order to achieve the contractive effect, CAE regularize by adding a term to
100   the objective function J (·, ·) alongside the reconstruction loss d(·, ·): the squared Frobenius norm
101   ∥ · ∥2F of the Jacobian Jf (x) of the encoder w.r.t. the input x. This encourages the encoder to learn
102   representations that are comparatively insensitive to the input (see Sec. 2 Rifai et al. (2011b)). The
103   objective function can be formally expressed as:
104                                         X
                               J (θ, ϕ) :=      (d(x, Dθ (Eϕ (x))) + λ||Jf (x)||2F )                      (1)
105
                                           x∈D
106
107   where λ is used to factorize the strength of the contractive effect, Dθ (·) is the decoder parameterized
      by θ and Eϕ (·) is the encoder parametrized by ϕ. As shown in Section 5.3 of Rifai et al. (2011b),


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      CAE effectively learns to be invariant to dimensions orthogonal to the lower-dimensional manifold,
109   while maintaining the necessary variations needed to reconstruct the input as local dimensions along
110   the manifold. Geometrically, the contraction of the input space in a certain direction of the input
111   space is indicated by the corresponding singular value of the Jacobian. Rifai et al. (2011b) show that
112   the number of large singular values is much smaller when using the CAE penalty term, indicating
113   that it helps in characterizing a lower-dimensional manifold near the data points.
114   This property of CAE improves the robustness of the model to irrelevant variations in the input
115   data, such as noise or slight alterations, ensuring that the learned representations are both stable and
116   meaningful in capturing the essential features of the data. Additionally, Rifai et al. (2011b) showed
117
      the ability to contract in the vicinity of the input data using the contraction ratio defined as the ratio
      of distances between the two points in the input space in comparison to the distance of the encod-
118
      ings in the feature space. This ratio approaches the Frobenius norm of the encoder’s Jacobian for
119
      infinitesimal variations in the input space.
120   Rifai et al. (2011a) also proposed higher order regularization, which leads to flatter manifolds and
121   more stable representations. However, the additional computational cost comes with a limited posi-
122   tive effect, which leads us to use the standard version of first order.
123
      Moreover, Rifai et al. (2011b) show that stacking CAEs can improve performance. A stacked CAE
124
      is a series of autoencoders where the embeddings of the first autoencoder are further embedded and
125
      reconstructed using the second autoencoder, and so on. Thereby, the contractive penalty term of the
126   encoder is calculated in isolation with respect to the other autoencoders.

132   While research dedicated to embedding generic or entity-based tabular data is relatively scarce,
133   substantial work has been conducted on embedding data from various other domains including text
134   (Muennighoff et al., 2022; Devlin et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2023), images, and videos (Kingma &

      3.1   TABULAR DATA EMBEDDING
138
139
      Ucar et al. (2021) propose to learn representations from tabular data by training an autoencoder
140
      with feature subsets, while reconstructing the entire input from the representation of the subset dur-
141   ing training as a measure of regularization. Using this methodology, they obtained state-of-the-art
142   results when using the representation for classification. However, they did not perform any dimen-
143   sionality reduction and hence did not make a comparison on reconstruction quality. Huang et al.
144   (2020) applied a Transformer to encode categorical features obtaining only minor improvements
145   over standard autoencoders despite using a significantly more complex architecture when compared
146   to a simple multilayer perceptron (MLP). Gorishniy et al. (2022) found that complex architectures
147   such as Transformers or ResNets (see He et al. (2015)) are not necessary for effective tabular repre-
148   sentation learning, which is in line with our results (see Figure 5).

      3.2   E NTITY EMBEDDING
151
152
153
      As outlined in Section 1, our work aims to embed entities stored in tabular data, especially in in-
      dustrial settings, where entities are usually platform users, customers, or products. While several
154
      approaches have been taken to solve this problem, they usually represent only parts of entities.
155
      These include Fey et al. (2023) with an attempt to build a framework for deep learning on relational
156   data and various works on embedding proprietary user/customer interaction data for downstream
157   predictions (Wang et al., 2023; Chitsazan et al., 2021; Chamberlain et al., 2017; Mikolov et al.,
158   2013). Fazelnia et al. (2024) propose a high-level framework for embedding all available informa-
159   tion of an entity, including using modality-specific encoders before using a standard autoencoder
160   to produce a unified representation that can then be used for a variety of downstream applications.
161   They also observe a general improvement of downstream prediction performance, but limit their
      work to their specific proprietary use-case in the audio industry.

229   CAEs is O(k × dx × dh ), as the Jacobian is calculated for each layer. Instead, in D EEP CAE the
230   computation of the contractive regularization term is driven by the calculation of the Jacobian of
231   the entire encoder with respect to the input (Equation 6): the overall complexity of D EEP CAE is
232   O(k × d3x ), due to the multiplication of Jacobian matrices2 . As such, the complexity of both stacked
233   CAEs and D EEP CAE scales linearly with the number of layers, but scales cubically with the input
234   size for D EEP CAE, making it less efficient than stacked CAEs, which have a quadratic complexity
235   instead.
236   Finally, we call our proposed method D EEP CAE, i.e., a multi-layer contractive autoencoder where
237   the contractive loss calculation is based on the Jacobian of the entire encoder, in line with the orig-
238   inal design by Rifai et al. (2011b). We compare D EEP CAE to a stacked CAE, and observe that
239   D EEP CAE outperforms it (see Section 5.2).

241   4.2       E NTITY E MBEDDING F RAMEWORK
242
243   We outline a general-purpose entity embedding framework (see Figure 1) to generate embeddings
244   and evaluate their quality, and use it to compare D EEP CAE to other embedding methods. This
245
      framework can serve as inspiration for practitioners to serve multiple downstream applications with
      the same entity representation, possibly creating different variants by parameterizing input features
246
      and embedding models used.
247
248   Starting from the raw data characterizing an entity (e.g. customer metadata, metrics, third-party
249   information), we combine and pre-process it to obtain a tabular dataset. In some cases there may
250   exist additional data structures such as text (e.g. with a natural language description) and time
251
      series (e.g. customers purchase patterns per month). Those additional modalities are then embedded
      through specific encoders such as a pre-trained BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) for textual data, and
252
      TS2Vec (Yue et al., 2021) for time-series data. The dataset with all combined features is then fed
253
      into an autoencoder model to produce the entity embeddings, which are optionally concatenated
254   with the labels of the corresponding problems, and finally used in downstream applications. This
255   worked for us in a proprietary industrial setting. The datasets in the benchmark do not include text
256   or time series data.
257
      To find the embedding model that is best suited on average for general-purpose entity embedding,
258
      we benchmark a variety of autoencoders, including the standard linear autoencoder architecture, a
259
      contractive autoencoder, a variational autoencoder and a Transformer-based autoencoder. Beyond
260   the mentioned autoencoder variants, we also employ KernelPCA by Schölkopf et al. (1997) as a
261   non-linear baseline.

468   6     D ISCUSSION
469
470   6.1   D IFFERENCES IN RECONSTRUCTION AND DOWNSTREAM PERFORMANCE
471
472   We observe that the reconstruction performance of the various autoencoders is in line with the down-
473   stream performance when using the corresponding embeddings (see Figure 2 and Figure 3). Notably,
474   the performance of K ERNEL PCA surpasses expectations based on the reconstruction benchmark in
475   Figure 2, as it achieves the highest performance in both downstream regression (see Figure 4) and
476
      classification benchmarks (see Figure 3).
477   We hypothesize this difference to be caused by the different incentive of K ERNEL PCA compared
478   to an autoencoder: K ERNEL PCA identifies principal components, i.e., the directions of maximum
479   variance in the high-dimensional feature space to which the dataset is mapped using a non-linear
480   kernel. By prioritizing principal components, the maximum possible amount of information is re-
481   tained. While this may not lead to the the best reconstruction in terms of MSE, it explains why
482
      K ERNEL PCA informs downstream predictions so well.
483   In contrast, the autoencoder’s primary incentive for maximizing the information stored in its em-
484   bedding space is the information bottleneck imposed by the reduced dimensionality and the need
485   to minimize reconstruction loss. However, this approach introduces some slack, as it may deprior-
      itize features with low average magnitude but high variance, which are less penalized by the MSE


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      reconstruction loss compared to high-magnitude, low-variance features. Future research could ex-
487   plore new loss functions or incorporate feature normalization to address this limitation and improve
488   information retention.
489
490   6.2   I NVERSE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN MODEL SIZE AND PERFORMANCE
491
492   We observe that autoencoder variants with smaller parameter sets exhibit superior performance in
493   both reconstruction and downstream tasks. With reference to the detailed results (see Appendix A.3),
494   we observe that both T RANSFORMER AE and VAE perform comparatively well on larger datasets,
495   but still show lower performance than simpler embedding architectures. Therefore, we hypothesize
496   that these models require large amounts of data for effective training - as is often the case with mod-
497
      els that have complex architectures or large parameter sets. While a simple single-layer autoencoder
      is known to produce transformations similar to those of PCA (Baldi & Hornik, 1989), Transformers
498
      and convolutional neural networks (CNNs) augment the input for the extraction of features and rep-
499
      resentations that are usually of higher dimensionality. While they succeed at representing the input
500   well in a numerical format (e.g. using attention in Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017)), this may be
501   counterproductive when the objective is to produce a compact, lower-dimensional representation of
502   the input.
503
504   6.3   S UPERIORITY OF D EEP CAE
505
506   Considering all of our benchmarks, and in particular reconstruction performance in Figure 2, we
507   observe D EEP CAE outperforms all of the other embedding models thanks to the regularization
508   introduced by the contractive loss applied across the entire encoder in a multi-layer setting. As
509   explained in detail in Rifai et al. (2011b), this approach helps the model focus on the most relevant
510
      aspects of the input while becoming invariant to noise, thereby reducing the risk of overfitting.
511
512   7     C ONCLUSIONS
513
514   In this paper, addressing the need for a general-purpose entity embedding model, we proposed
515   D EEP CAE as an extension to the contractive autoencoder CAE in multi-layer settings. We showed
516   that D EEP CAE outperforms all the other tested embedding methods, including a stacked CAE, in
517   terms of both reconstruction quality and downstream performance of classification and regression
518
      tasks using the resulting embeddings. The improvement of D EEP CAE over a stacked CAE in terms
      of reconstruction quality is 34%.
519
520   Moreover, we outlined a general-purpose entity embedding framework to (a) produce embeddings
521   with different modalities and embedding models, (b) use such embeddings in downstream tasks
522   substituting or augmenting pre-processed data, and (c) evaluate the best embeddings in terms of
523   both reconstruction loss for a task-agnostic purpose and downstream task performance.
524   Through our experiments, we observed that simpler autoencoders with nuanced regularization, in-
525   cluding D EEP CAE, outperform more complex ones, and conclude that these are best suited for
526   robust dimensionality reduction yielding rich embedddings. Furthermore, we argue that the aug-
527   mentative capabilities of more complex architectures like Transformers and CNNs are not neces-
528   sarily useful in the production of a compact representation of an entity. Finally, we found that
529   while K ERNEL PCA is outperformed in terms of reconstruction performance by most of the tested
530
      autoencoders, it achieves the best performance in informing downstream predictions with its repre-
      sentations. We concluded that this is due to the inherent limitation of autoencoders, where the focus
531
      on minimizing reconstruction loss can introduce slack, leading to suboptimal retention of variance
532
      compared to methods explicitly designed to maximize it.
533
534   Despite the promising results of D EEP CAE and the successful internal testing on large datasets,
535   the computational complexity introduced by extending the contractive loss to multi-layer settings
536
      across the entire encoder may pose scalability challenges for large datasets and high-dimensional
      data. Future research could focus on addressing these limitations by optimizing the computational
537
      complexity of D EEP CAE for scalability. Exploring the integration of new loss functions, including
538
      variance maximization, or incorporating more robust feature engineering techniques could further
539   improve the model’s ability to retain information.
