011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In drug discovery, researchers make sequential decisions to schedule experiments,
014            aiming to maximize probability of success towards drug candidates while simul-
015            taneously minimizing expected costs. However, such tasks pose significant chal-
016            lenges due to complex trade-offs between uncertainty reduction and allocation
017            of constrained resources in a high-dimensional state-action space. Traditional
018            methods based on simple rule-based heuristics or domain expertise often result
019
               in either inefficient resource utilization due to risk aversion or missed opportuni-
               ties arising from reckless decisions. To address these challenges, we developed a
020
               Implicit Bayesian Markov Decision Process (IB-MDP) algorithm that constructs
021
               an implicit MDP model of the environment’s dynamics by integrating historical
022            data through a similarity-based metric, and enables effective planning by simulat-
023            ing future states and actions. To enhance the robustness of the decision-making
024            process, the IB-MDP also incorporates an ensemble approach that recommends
025            maximum likelihood actions to effectively balance the dual objectives of reducing
026            state uncertainty and optimizing expected costs. Our experimental results demon-
027            strate that the IB-MDP algorithm offers significant improvements over traditional
028            rule-based methods by identifying optimal decisions that ensure more efficient use
029            of resources in drug discovery.

034
      In drug discovery, strategic planning and selection of experiments play a pivotal role in impacting
035
      the pace and expenses of R&D activities. The identification of potential drug candidates requires
036   conducting numerous assays at various stages of preclinical studies. The process often begins with
037   limited information, creating significant challenges for achieving optimized outcomes due to time
038   and budget constraints. Optimizing the use of resources to achieve targeted goals within these lim-
039   itations is among the most demanding tasks in creating effective Research Operation Plans (ROP).
040   Conventional appoarches, often relying on simple rule-based heuristics or domain expertise, struggle
041   to adapt as new data emerges and typically fail to address state, model, and parameter uncertainties
042   effectively. Consequently, this results in suboptimal decision-making and inefficient allocation of
043   resources Puterman (2014).
044   To address these challenges, we propose the Implicit Bayesian Markov Decision Process (IB-
045   MDP) algorithm, a model-based approach that constructs an implicit model of the environment’s
046   dynamics by integrating historical data through a distance-based similarity metric. Unlike traditional
047   MDP methods that require explicit modeling of transition probabilities, the IB-MDP leverages his-
048   torical data to build a flexible model of the environment without the need for precise parameteriza-
049   tion Rainforth et al. (2024); Bellet et al. (2013). This implicit model captures complex, nonlinear
050
      relationships within the data manifold, enabling efficient planning by simulating future states and
      actions Alagoz et al. (2010).
051
052   Moreover, to improve the robustness and reliability of decision-making, we incorporate an ensemble
053   approach into the IB-MDP. Ensemble methods aggregate multiple policies derived from independent
      algorithm runs, reducing variance and mitigating bias in policy estimation Dietterich (2000); Osband

055   larly in high-dimensional and resource-constrained environments Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017).

057   Our algorithm is demonstrated in the context of assay scheduling and ROP optimization, where it
058

075   2   R ELATED W ORK
076
077   The optimization of decision-making under uncertainty has been a central focus in various domains,
078   including drug discovery.
079   Markov Decision Processes and Model-Based Reinforcement Learning: Markov Decision Pro-
080   cesses (MDPs) provide a mathematical framework for modeling sequential decision-making where
081   outcomes are partly random and partly under the control of a decision-maker (Puterman, 1994;
082   2014). In drug discovery, MDPs have been applied to tasks such as clinical trial optimization (Ben-
083   nett & Hauser, 2013; Eghbali-Zarch et al., 2019; Abbas et al., 2007; Fard et al., 2018). However,
084   applying MDPs to experimental scheduling has remained limited due to the difficulty of accurately
085   specifying transition probabilities and reward functions. Model-based reinforcement learning (RL)
086   offers an alternative by learning models of the environment to improve sample efficiency and plan-
087
      ning accuracy (Sutton, 2018; Kaiser et al., 2019). In the drug discovery field, model-based RL
      has been used for molecule generation (Wang et al., 2021; Bengio et al., 2021; You et al., 2018;
088
      Zhou et al., 2019), synthesis planning (Segler et al., 2018), and experimental design (Schneider
089
      et al., 2020). These methods typically require accurate environment models, a challenge in high-
090   dimensional and complex biological systems such as those found in preclinical studies.
091
092   Incorporating Historical Data and Similarity Metrics: Leveraging historical data is crucial for
093
      improving decision-making in contexts with limited experimental data. Bayesian approaches, in-
      cluding Bayesian reinforcement learning and optimization, maintain a posterior distribution over pa-
094
      rameters or value functions, updating beliefs based on new data (Ghavamzadeh et al., 2015; Shahriari
095
      et al., 2015). In drug discovery, Bayesian optimization has been applied to optimize molecular prop-
096   erties (Griffiths & Hernández-Lobato, 2020; Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018), but such methods are
097   often less effective in sequential decision-making scenarios. Using similarity metrics within MDPs
098   can further enhance the integration of historical data. Kernel-based methods, which use similar-
099   ity functions to generalize across states, have been explored in reinforcement learning to estimate
100   transition dynamics more accurately (Ormoneit & Sen, 2002; Kveton & Theocharous, 2012; Xu
101   et al., 2007). Our approach extends this by incorporating a variance-normalized distance metric to
102   dynamically integrate historical data into the MDP transition function.
103   Ensemble Methods in Reinforcement Learning (RL): Ensemble methods have gained popularity
104   for improving the robustness and reliability of decision-making. By aggregating multiple models
105   or policies, ensemble techniques reduce the variance and bias inherent in individual estimates (Di-
106   etterich, 2000; Osband et al., 2016; Wiering & Van Hasselt, 2008; Zhou, 2012). In RL, ensemble
107   methods are particularly effective in improving exploration and generalization, as demonstrated by
      their successful application in model-based RL (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). Our IB-MDP

108
      framework incorporates ensemble methods to enhance decision robustness, where multiple policies
109   derived from independent algorithm runs are aggregated to produce more reliable decision paths.
110
111   Applications in ADME Studies and Comparison to Existing Methods: ADME studies focus
112
      on the absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) of drugs to understand their
      pharmacokinetic properties and impact on effectiveness and safety (Hoffman, 1998; Hoffman et al.,
113
      2004; Hughes et al., 2011). Decision-making in ADME studies requires balancing information gain
114
      with constrained resources. Although RL has been applied to clinical trial optimization (Coronato
115   et al., 2020; Escandell-Montero et al., 2014; Martı́n et al., 2020), its application to preclinical ex-
116   perimental scheduling is underexplored. Our IB-MDP framework addresses this gap by providing
117   a flexible and scalable decision-making approach that integrates historical data and real-time ex-
118   perimental results. Unlike traditional methods, the IB-MDP does not require manual specification
119   of transition probabilities, instead leveraging a similarity-based metric to model the environment’s
120   dynamics implicitly. This, combined with the ensemble approach, distinguishes our method from
121   existing techniques, ensuring both adaptability and robustness in decision-making.

      4.1   F RAMEWORK D ESCRIPTION
157
158   The IB-MDP algorithm is designed to optimize experimental scheduling in resource-constrained
159   settings by leveraging historical data through a distance-based similarity metric. This framework
160   aims to strategically select assays to minimize costs and maximize information gain, particularly
161   in high-dimensional decision spaces, such as assays scheduling in preclinical pharmacokinetics and
      pharmacodynamics (PKPD) space.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      The algorithm starts with a partially known initial state and a collection of potential experimental
163   configurations (i.e., action sets in an MDP framework). As it explores the state-action space, the
164   IB-MDP dynamically adjusts its strategy based on emerging evidence, ensuring that the policies
165   remain optimal under given constraints. By constructing an implicit model of the environment’s
166   dynamics, IB-MDP eliminates the need for a parameterized transition probabilities. Instead, the
167   transition dynamics are inferred from historical data using a variance-normalized similarity metric.
168   This method significantly reduces computational complexity while retaining the flexibility to refine
169   decisions as new data become available.
170   A key feature of IB-MDP is its use of Monte Carlo Tree Search with Double Progressive Widen-
171   ing (MCTS-DPW), which enables efficient navigation through large state spaces without exhaustive
172   data collection. This approach is particularly suited for experimental planning, where the goal is to
173   balance exploration and exploitation in a computationally efficient manner. Furthermore, the frame-
174   work incorporates a Bayesian sampling method, continuously refining the policy to incorporate new
175   information, thus ensuring that the decision-making process adapts to changes in state uncertainty
176   and target feature values over time.
177   To further enhance robustness and accuracy, IB-MDP integrates an ensemble method. By aggre-
178   gating multiple policies generated from independent runs, the ensemble method mitigates inference
179   bias and reduces variance, ensuring that the decision-making process is both reliable and adaptive.
180   This combination of implicit modeling, dynamic policy adjustment, and ensemble learning offers a
181   powerful tool for optimizing resource usage in complex experimental designs.

301
      After solving the IB-MDP problem and obtaining a set of optimal policies πj∗ from the MCTS-DPW
302
      runs, the next step is to generate the Pareto front. This front helps to discern optimal trade-offs
303   between competing objectives, such as minimizing cost and reducing state uncertainty.
304
305   The Pareto front consists of non-dominated points in the objective space, where each point repre-
306
      sents a policy that is optimal under certain constraints. Mathematically, this can be formulated as:
      minimize {(C(s), H(s)) | s ∈ S} where C(s) is the cost associated with state s and H(s) represents
307
      state uncertainty. A state s′ dominates state s if:H(s′ ) < H(s) and C(s′ ) < C(s) indicating that
308
      s′ has lower uncertainty and lower cost. The Pareto front is the set of states that are not dominated
309   by any other state, ensuring that each point on the front represents a trade-off between cost and
310   uncertainty.

      4.4     E NSEMBLE M ETHOD FOR IB-MDP
313
314   The ensemble method addresses the inherent variability in single runs of the IB-MDP algorithm,
315   especially when using Monte Carlo Tree Search with Double Progressive Widening (MCTS-DPW).
316   Stochasticity and sensitivity to initial conditions can lead to different Pareto fronts and optimal
317   policies. By executing the IB-MDP algorithm N times, each run generates an optimal policy πj∗ and
318   a corresponding Pareto front Pj . Aggregating the results across multiple runs improves robustness,
319   reduces bias, and enhances the reliability of decision-making.
320   Advantages of Ensemble IB-MDP: The ensemble IB-MDP methodology provides several advan-
321   tages: Improved Robustness: By aggregating results from multiple simulations, the ensemble ap-
322   proach reduces the impact of variability and randomness in individual runs, enhancing the stability
323   of decision outcomes. Bias Reduction: Exploring diverse decision trajectories across runs mini-
      mizes inference bias and yields more accurate estimates of optimal policies. Predictive Power: The

325   forms the construction of a Maximum Likelihood Action Sets Path (MLASP), providing a candidate

411   lel assays, allowing simultaneous experimental operations. This setup helps reduce state uncer-
412   tainty more efficiently while maximizing information gain, both of which are essential for effective
413   decision-making. A computational threshold of 10 is applied to assess state uncertainty, ensuring
414   that the algorithm captures meaningful differences in uncertainty levels.
415   We employ the IB-MDP algorithm, integrated with a Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) solver using
416   Double Progressive Widening (DPW). This solver runs for 20,000 iterations, with an exploration
417   constant of 5.0, 50 ensembles, providing a balance between exploring new actions and exploiting
418   known outcomes.
419   The primary goal is to identify the actions that achieve the greatest reduction in state uncertainty,
420   comparable to performing the final target assay (kpuu), while minimizing both costs and resource
421   use throughout the decision-making process.

504   6     C ONCLUSIONS
505
506   In this study, we present the Implicit Bayesian Markov Decision Process (IB-MDP), a framework
507   designed to improve decision making under uncertainty in resource-constrained environments. By
508   dynamically integrating historical data using a similarity-based metric, the IB-MDP refines beliefs
509   about the current state in relation to target features. This refinement is achieved through implicit
510
      Bayesian updates with a sampling approach, ensuring the policy search maximizes information gain,
      minimizes costs, and achieves key experimental objectives within an optimal range.
511
512   A key advantage of the IB-MDP is its ability to significantly reduce state uncertainty without the
513   need to perform the most expensive and resource-intensive assays, such as the target assay (kpuu).
514   This not only enhances cost-efficiency but also accelerates decision-making by bypassing less criti-
515   cal, high-cost experiments.
516   Furthermore, IB-MDP benefits from an ensemble approach that aggregates policies across multiple
517   runs, reducing variance, and improving robustness. By aligning maximum likelihood action sets
518   with predefined probability bounds for target features, the methodology ensures consistent decision
519   quality. Through its comprehensive and data-driven approach, the IB-MDP outperforms traditional
520   heuristic methods, offering enhanced adaptability, precision, and resource optimization in experi-
521   mental planning. It demonstrates significant potential in streamlining decision-making tasks in drug
522
      discovery and other fields requiring strategic resource management under uncertainty.
523
524   7     B ROADER I MPACTS
525
526   The IB-MDP framework provides a versatile approach to adaptive decision making, offering benefits
527   beyond preclinical assay scheduling. By integrating historical data, dynamic Bayesian updates, and
528   ensemble methods, the framework enhances decision-making efficiency in various fields such as
529   healthcare, logistics, and financial risk management. Its ability to handle uncertainty and resource
530   constraints ensures robust, data-driven decisions, making it a valuable tool to improve outcomes in
531   diverse industries.
532
533   8     L IMITATIONS
534
535   Increasing the number of runs N enhances the accuracy and robustness of the optimal action set
536   estimation but also increases computational costs. While the ensemble method helps reduce variance
537   and bias, its efficiency decreases as N grows, with diminishing returns typical in ensemble-based
538   decision-making frameworks. The optimal value of N depends on the problem’s complexity and the
539   available computational resources, as larger ensembles may be necessary to fully explore complex
      state-action spaces. In particularly intricate environments, more iterations may be required to ensure


                                                              10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      reliable convergence, although the ensemble approach generally stabilizes after a sufficient number
541   of runs.
542
543   Another important limitation lies in the framework’s reliance on historical data. Although leveraging
544
      historical data helps to integrate similarity-based metrics for decision-making, it may not sufficiently
      account for novel scenarios in real-world experiments. Future extensions of the IB-MDP framework
545
      could incorporate more flexible strategies, such as adaptive kernel-based methods or deep learning
546
      approaches, to extrapolate to states not represented in the existing dataset.
547
548   Moreover, while thresholds for decision-making tend to converge toward a maximum likelihood ac-
549   tion path, further exploration of how state uncertainty reduction influences the likelihood of achiev-
550
      ing desired outcomes could offer additional insights. This might enable more effective ensemble
      adjustments, ultimately improving policy reliability and performance in dynamic environments.
