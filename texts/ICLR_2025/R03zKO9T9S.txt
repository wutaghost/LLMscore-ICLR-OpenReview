009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual anomaly detection aims to identify anomalous regions in images through
012            unsupervised learning paradigms, with increasing application demand and value in
013            fields such as industrial inspection and medical lesion detection. Despite significant
014            progress in recent years, there is a lack of comprehensive benchmarks to adequately
015            evaluate the performance of various mainstream methods across different datasets
016
               under the practical multi-class setting. The absence of standardized experimen-
               tal setups can lead to potential biases in training epochs, resolution, and metric
017
               results, resulting in erroneous conclusions. This paper addresses this issue by
018
               proposing a comprehensive visual anomaly detection benchmark, ADer, which is
019            a modular framework that is highly extensible for new methods. The benchmark
020            includes multiple datasets from industrial and medical domains, implementing
021            fifteen state-of-the-art methods and nine comprehensive metrics. Additionally,
022            we have proposed the GPU-assisted ADEval package to address the slow eval-
023            uation problem of metrics like time-consuming mAU-PRO on large-scale data,
024            significantly reducing evaluation time by more than 1000-fold. Through extensive
025            experimental results, we objectively reveal the strengths and weaknesses of dif-
026            ferent methods and provide insights into the challenges and future directions of
027
               multi-class visual anomaly detection. We hope that ADer will become a valuable
               resource for researchers and practitioners in the field, promoting the development
028
               of more robust and generalizable anomaly detection systems. Full codes have been
029
               attached in Appendix and will be open-sourced.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In recent years, with the rapid advancement in model iteration and computational power, Visual
035   Anomaly Detection (VAD) has made significant progress across various fields Liu et al. (2024); Cao
036   et al. (2024). It has become a crucial component in key tasks such as industrial quality inspection and
037   medical lesion detection. Due to its unsupervised experimental setup, VAD demonstrates immense
038   application value in real-world scenarios where the yield rate is high, defect samples are difficult to
039
      obtain, and potential defect patterns are diverse. However, the field faces challenges such as small
      dataset sizes and insufficient evaluation metrics, resulting in potentially unfair comparison outcomes
040
      due to differing training recipes among methods. Moreover, most methods have not been compared
041
      on the latest large-scale datasets (e.g., Real-IAD Wang et al. (2024) and COCO-AD Zhang et al.
042   (2024a)) and new evaluation metrics (e.g., mAD Zhang et al. (2023a) and mIoU-max Zhang et al.
043   (2024a)). The fundamental issue lies in the absence of standardized training strategies, akin to those
044   in object detection, to evaluate different algorithms. Factors such as training epoch and resolution
045   can potentially affect evaluation results, leading to erroneous conclusions.
046
      To address this pressing issue, we believe that establishing a comprehensive and fair benchmark
047
      is crucial for the sustained and healthy development of this field. Therefore, we have constructed
048   an integrated ADer library, benchmarking state-of-the-art methods by utilizing a unified evaluation
049   interface under the more practical multi-class setting. This library is designed as a highly extensible
050   modular framework (see Sec. 3), allowing for the easy implementation of new methods. Specifically,
051   the framework integrates multiple datasets from industrial, medical, and general-purpose domains (see
052   Sec. 3.2), and implements fifteen state-of-the-art methods (including augmentation-based, embedding-
053   based, reconstruction-based, and hybrid methods, see Sec. 3.1) and nine comprehensive evaluation
      metrics (see Sec. 3.3), ensuring thorough and unbiased performance evaluation for each method.

094   2.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION AND O BJECTIVE
095
096   Visual anomaly detection (VAD) is a critical task in computer vision, aiming at identifying patterns
097   or instances in visual data that deviate significantly from the norm. These anomalies can manifest as
098   industrial defects, medical lesion, or rare objects that are not typically present in the training data.
099
      The primary objective of VAD is to develop algorithms capable of discerning these irregularities with
      high accuracy and reliability. This task is particularly challenging due to the inherent variability and
100
      complexity of visual data, the scarcity of anomalous examples, and the need for robust generalization
101
      across diverse scenarios. In a formal context, multi-class VAD can be defined as follows: Given a
102   training dataset Dtrain = {x1 , x2 , ..., xn } with C categories and each visual image xi belonging to
103   a specific category, the goal is to learn a unified AD model M that can predict an anomaly score
104   si = M (xi ) for each image. This score reflects the likelihood of each pixel in xi being an anomaly.
105   The model M is typically trained on Dtrain that predominantly contains normal instances, with the
106   assumption that anomalies are rare and not well-represented in the training set. Inevitably, there are
107   some mislabeled or inaccurately labeled noisy samples, which constitute inherent biases within the
      dataset. These are typically disregarded under standard settings. The performance of the model is

125   2.2     C HALLENGES IN M ULTI - CLASS VAD
126
127   The complexity of VAD arises from several factors: 1) Data Imbalance: Anomalies are rare, leading
128   to highly imbalanced datasets where normal instances and region areas vastly outnumber anomalous
129   ones. 2) Variability of Anomalies: Anomalies can vary widely in appearance, making it difficult
130   to capture all possible variations during training. 3) Context Sensitivity: The definition of what
131
      constitutes an anomaly can be context-dependent, requiring models to understand the broader context
      in which the visual data is situated. 4) Efficiency Requirements: Many applications of VAD require
132
      real-time processing and limited GPU memory, necessitating efficient and scalable algorithms. 5)
133
      Comprehensive and Fair Evaluation: Current methods exhibit significant differences in training
134   hyperparameters and insufficient evaluation of performance metrics, so it is necessary to benchmark
135   them using fair and standardized criteria. In this benchmark study, we systematically evaluate a range
136   of state-of-the-art VAD methods (Sec. 3.1) across multiple datasets (Sec. 3.2) and comprehensive
137   metrics (Sec. 3.3). Our goal is to provide a comprehensive assessment of current capabilities, identify
138   key challenges, and suggest directions for future research in visual anomaly detection.
139
140   2.3     V ISUAL A NOMALY D ETECTION
141
142   Visual anomaly detection methods can generally be categorized into three types: 1) Augmentation-
143   based methods generate pseudo-supervised information for anomalies by creating abnormal regions Li
144   et al. (2021); Zavrtanik et al. (2021), constructing anomalous data Zhang et al. (2021a); Hu et al.
145   (2024), or adding feature perturbations Liu et al. (2023); Tien et al. (2023). This enables the model
146   to learn the differences between normal and abnormal distributions. 2) Embedding-based methods
147
      leverage pretrained models to extract powerful feature representations and judge anomalies in high-
      dimensional space. Typical approaches include distribution-map-based methods Gudovskiy et al.
148
      (2022); Lei et al. (2023), teacher-student frameworks Bergmann et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2021a),
149
      and memory-bank techniques Roth et al. (2022); Gu et al. (2023). 3) Reconstruction-based methods
150   use encoder-decoder architectures to locate anomalies by analyzing the reconstruction error. They
151   typically include both image-level Akçay et al. (2019); Liang et al. (2023); He et al. (2024b) and
152   feature-level approaches Deng & Li (2022); Zhang et al. (2023a; 2024a). There are also some hybrid
153   methods You et al. (2022); Tien et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023c) that attempt to integrate multiple
154   techniques to further enhance model performance.
155
      Basic network structures of VAD. Early visual anomaly detection methods typically employ UNet-
156   based autoencoder architectures Akcay et al. (2019); Liang et al. (2023); Zavrtanik et al. (2021).
157   With advancements in foundational visual model structures He et al. (2016); Wang et al. (2021b);
158   Liu et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021b; 2024b; 2023b) and pretraining techniques He et al. (2022);
159   Caron et al. (2021), more recent methods often utilize models pretrained on ImageNet-1K Deng et al.
160   (2009) as feature extractors, such as the ResNet He et al. (2016) series, Wide ResNet-50 Zagoruyko
161   & Komodakis (2016), and EfficientNet-b4 Tan & Le (2019). Recently, benefiting from the dynamic
      modeling capabilities of Vision Transformers (ViT) Dosovitskiy et al. (2021), some studies De Nardin

246
247   Following the ViTAD Zhang et al. (2023a) setting, we select image-level mean Area Under the
248   Receiver Operating Curve (mAU-ROC) Zavrtanik et al. (2021), mean Average Precision (mAP) Zavr-

253
      Union (mIoU-max) proposed in InvAD Zhang et al. (2024a).

      3.6         ADE VAL : FAST AND M EMORY- EFFICIENT ROUTINES FOR M AU-ROC/ M AP/ M AU-PRO
314
315
      The speed of metric evaluation is crucial for the iterative process of model algorithms. When
316
      the number of test images increases and the resolution becomes higher, the pixel-level evaluation
317
      algorithms implemented naively using sklearn and skimage packages become time-consuming. This
318   is particularly evident with large-scale datasets such as Real-IAD Wang et al. (2024) and COCO-
319   AD Zhang et al. (2024a), where evaluation times can exceed one hour. To address this issue, we
320   have released the GPU-assisted ADEval library, which employs an iterative-accumulating algorithm
321   with optional CUDA acceleration. For instance, in the case of the most time-consuming mAU-PRO
322   metric on the multi-class MVTec AD dataset, as demonstrated by UniAD You et al. (2022), the naive
323   implementation requires 242.7 seconds, whereas the optimized version reduces this to less than 0.1
      second, achieving a more than 1000-fold speedup.

358   Different methods potentially introduce various factors that can impact model performance. To ensure
359
      a fair and comprehensive evaluation of the effectiveness and convergence of different methods, we

360
      The reason lies in the fact that tasks such as classification, detection, and segmentation typically set

      specific resolutions and standard training epochs. We observe that for most methods, 100 epochs

365   and data augmentation. We report the evaluation results corresponding to the final epoch at the end of
366   training to ensure fairness. All experiments are conducted on one L40S GPU.

508   4.3   C HALLENGES FOR C URRENT VAD
509
510   Immature method. For challenging anomaly detection datasets such as MVTecLOCO, pose-agnostic
511   MAD, and general-purpose COCO-AD, current methods perform poorly in a multi-class setting.
512
      Future research should focus on designing more robust methods to address this issue.
      Efficiency. Most methods do not consider model complexity during design, resulting in high
513
      FLOPs. This issue becomes more pronounced when applied to real-world high-resolution scenarios.
514
      Incorporating lightweight characteristics in model design could be a potential solution.
515   Dataset scale. Mainstream datasets in the VAD field, such as MVTec AD and Real-IAD, are
516   relatively small compared to those in detection and segmentation fields and are tailored to specific
517   industrial scenarios. This limitation could hinder technological development. Collecting larger-scale,
518   general-scene AD datasets is crucial for the advancement of the VAD field.
519   VAD-specific metric. Metrics like mAU-ROC and mAP are not uniquely designed for the BAD field.
520   Developing more reliable evaluation methods to better meet practical application needs is essential.
521   Augmentation and tricks. In fields such as classification, detection, and segmentation, data
522   augmentation and tricks are extremely important for model training. However, few studies explore
523
      their role in the AD field, potentially limiting model performance.
      Model interpretability. In many applications, understanding why a model detects a particular
524
      anomaly is crucial. Providing effective visualization tools to display detection results and the model’s
525
      decision-making process remains a challenge.
526

528
529   5     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
530
531   This paper addresses the urgent need for a comprehensive and fair benchmark in the field of visual
532   anomaly detection. We introduce a modular and scalable ADer library designed to fairly facilitate
533   the evaluation of fifteen advanced VAD methods across multiple mainstream datasets. ensuring a
534   thorough and unbiased assessment of each method’s performance. Our extensive experiments reveal
535   the strengths and weaknesses of different methods, providing valuable insights into their efficiency
536
      and training resource consumption. We also develop and open-source a GPU-assisted ADEval
      package to reduce the evaluation time, enabling extensive assessments. Experimental results highlight
537
      the challenges of various VAD methods and offer valuable insights for future research directions.
538
539   Broader Impacts. The open-sourcing ADer can accelerate the development of new VAD technology
      for the open-source community and become a valuable resource for practitioners in the field.
