009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In an NBA game scenario, consider the challenge of locating and analyzing the
012            3D poses of players performing a user-specified action, such as attempting a
013            shot. Traditional 3D human pose estimation (3DHPE) methods often fall short
014            in such complex, multi-person scenes due to their lack of semantic integration
015            and reliance on isolated pose data. To address these limitations, we introduce
016            Language-Driven 3D Human Pose Estimation (L3DHPE), a novel approach that ex-
017            tends 3DHPE to general multi-person contexts by incorporating detailed language
018            descriptions. We present Panoptic-L3D, the first dataset designed for L3DHPE,
019
               featuring 3,838 linguistic annotations for 1,476 individuals across 588 videos,
               with 6,035 masks and 91k frame-level 3D skeleton annotations. Additionally, we
020
               propose Cascaded Pose Perception (CPP), a benchmarking method that simulta-
021
               neously performs language-driven mask segmentation and 3D pose estimation
022            within a unified model. CPP first learns 2D pose information, utilizes a body
023            fusion module to aid in mask segmentation, and employs a mask fusion module to
024            mitigate mask noise before outputting 3D poses. Our extensive evaluation of CPP
025            and existing benchmarks on the Panoptic-L3D dataset demonstrates the necessity
026            of this novel task and dataset for advancing 3DHPE. Our dataset can be accessed
027            at https://languagedriven3dposeestimation.github.io/.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   3D human pose estimation (3DHPE) Pavlakos et al. (2017); Pavllo et al. (2019); Sun et al. (2017);
033   Zheng et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2022); Su et al. (2022) seeks to accurately localize joints and
034   reconstruct the body’s representation within a 3D coordinate system based on input images or
035   videos. This capability to derive detailed motion and geometric information about the human body
036   underpins a plethora of applications, including video understanding Wang et al. (2021), sports
037   analytics Bridgeman et al. (2019), and human-robot interaction Xu et al. (2020). However, traditional
038   approaches have primarily concentrated on identifying poses without integrating high-level semantic
039
      knowledge, which is crucial for meaningful human communication. Despite recent efforts Zhang
      et al. (2020); Mazzia et al. (2022); Chi et al. (2022); Delmas et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2024); Feng
040
      et al. (2024) to link human pose with semantic information, these methods still face significant
041
      limitations. The actions they consider are typically simplistic, which results in ordinary and limited
042   semantic descriptions. Moreover, the input videos are usually restricted to scenarios involving a
043   single individual engaged in sports activities, thereby limiting the practical applicability of 3DHPE. In
044   reality, human motion is characterized by diverse and detailed descriptions, highlighting an emerging
045   need for 3D human pose estimation to address more complex, real-world, and multi-person scenarios.
046   This necessitates a shift towards incorporating motion grounding, a concept that connects dynamic
047   human movements with rich semantic context, enabling a more comprehensive understanding of
048   human activities in natural and multifaceted environments.
049   In response to these challenges, we introduce a novel task: Language-Driven 3D Human Pose
050   Estimation (L3DHPE), which can also be referred to as 3D Motion Grounding. It extends the
051   original 3DHPE problem to encompass more general multi-person scenes and explores the semantic
052   interplay between human poses and language expressions. L3DHPE addresses a more demanding
053   yet practical problem, aiming to reconstruct 3D pose sequences for individuals based on detailed
      language descriptions that capture aspects such as appearance, behavior, and body movements.

      We present two notable real-world examples of L3DHPE applications. In the first example (Fig. 1 (a)),
071
      a coach can focus on specific players and view them from multiple angles using language descriptions.
072   By integrating language understanding with 3D pose reconstruction, L3DHPE enables real-time
073   analysis of player movements, significantly enhancing feedback and performance. The second
074   example (Fig. 1 (b)) involves sports officiating. A referee can use language input to quickly isolate the
075   relevant players’ skeletons, simplifying decisions like offside calls by filtering out irrelevant players.
076
      Given the scarcity of pose estimation datasets with precise and richly contextual linguistic annotations,
077
      we developed Panoptic-L3D, the first L3DHPE dataset, to propel advancements in this field. Panoptic-
078   L3D includes 588 videos featuring 1,476 targeted individuals, 3,838 language descriptions, 6035
079   mask images, and corresponding 91k frame-level 3D skeleton annotations for all individuals. Each
080   video is meticulously selected to ensure the presence of at least two individuals, with clearly visible
081   upper bodies and significant movements by at least one individual. Annotators provide detailed, long-
082   form descriptions of individuals based on their appearance, behavior, and body movements, ensuring
083   specificity to eliminate ambiguity. In addition to 3D skeleton annotations for pose information,
084   we provide individual object masks using the pretrained SAM model Kirillov et al. (2023) for
085   coarse-level pose guidance. Fig. 2 showcases examples from our Panoptic-L3D dataset, showing its
086
      comprehensive and detailed annotations.
087   Building upon the proposed Panoptic-L3D dataset, a naive solution for our task is to apply referring
088   video object segmentation methods to videos. This approach takes both language and video informa-
089   tion as input to generate masks of the described objects, which are then used in single-person 3D
090   human pose estimation methods. However, this method has several significant drawbacks: Firstly,
091   referring video object segmentation methods do not leverage pose information, leading to a poor
092
      understanding of poses and incorrect segmentation of individuals, thus reducing the accuracy of 3D
      human pose estimation. Secondly, in multi-person interactions, occlusions between individuals often
093
      occur, and masks may be incomplete, introducing noise that is amplified during 3D pose estimation,
094
      further degrading accuracy. Finally, the workflows of both the reference video object segmentation
095   and single-person 3D human pose estimation methods are complex. Simply combining them results
096   in an excessively cumbersome processing flow, limiting their practicality in real-life applications.
097
098
      To address these issues, we propose Cascaded Pose Perception (CPP), a benchmarking method for
      the task we introduce. CPP jointly performs language-driven mask segmentation and 3D pose output
099
      within a single model. Initially, the model is trained to learn 2D pose information. We then design
100
      a body fusion module that utilizes pose information to assist the network in segmenting masks for
101   specified individuals. Unlike traditional 3DHPE methods, we do not rely solely on the predicted
102   human root nodes to output 3D poses. Instead, a mask fusion module is incorporated to help the
103   model identify individuals from human masks while minimizing the impact of mask noise.
104

        interaction, which holds great potential in several real-life applications.

135   3D Human Pose Estimation Datasets. We summarize various public 3D Human Pose Estimation
136   datasets in Tab. 1. HumanEva-I Sigal et al. (2010) contains 7 calibrated video sequences synchronized
137   with 3D body poses obtained from a motion capture system. Human3.6M Ionescu et al. (2013)
138   includes 3.6 million human poses with corresponding frames, offering precise 3D human joint
139   positions and high-resolution videos captured at 50 Hz by a motion capture system. CMU Panoptic Joo
140   et al. (2015) features extensive social interactions with up to eight characters per video, making it a
141
      significant choice for our annotations. 3DPW (3D Poses in the Wild) Von Marcard et al. (2018) is the
      first dataset with accurate 3D poses for evaluation in the wild, using the Video Inertial Poser (VIP)
142
      method to combine images and IMU readings. EgoBody Zhang et al. (2022) and EgoPW Wang et al.
143
      (2022) focus on egocentric human pose estimation from a first-person perspective. As discussed in
144   Sec. 3.2, these datasets focus on 3D human gestures without mask assistance or language guidance.
145   Our Panoptic-L3D dataset provides expressive linguistic descriptions and segmentation masks,
146   promoting close interactions between dynamic human poses and rich semantic context.
147
      Monocular 3D Human Pose Estimation Methods. Monocular 3D Human Pose Estimation (3DHPE)
148
      aims to reconstruct the 3D coordinates of individuals from a single camera view. One challenging
149
      aspect is depth estimation from a single view. Generally, multiple camera views are required to infer
150   the exact depth of individuals. However, the size of individuals and camera parameters can be used to
151   approximate their depth, as explored in studies like Lee & Kim (2019); Li et al. (2019); Li & Snavely
152   (2018). There are two approaches to monocular 3D pose estimation: single-stage and two-stage
153   approaches. Single-stage methods Pavlakos et al. (2017); Mehta et al. (2017); Sun et al. (2017);
154   Kanazawa et al. (2018); Sun et al. (2018) directly localize 3D human joints from input images or
155   videos. Two-stage methods Akhter & Black (2015); Park et al. (2016); Moreno-Noguer (2017); Yang
156   et al. (2018); Su et al. (2022); Park et al. (2023) first estimate 2D poses or utilize pre-trained 2D pose
157   estimators, then lift them to 3D space. Since estimating 2D poses is easier than 3D poses, models can
158
      benefit from the reliable results of 2D pose estimation. Due to its good performance, we also utilize a
      two-stage approach in our model. However, traditional methods lack the guidance of language, which
159
      our approach incorporates to enhance 3D pose estimation and interaction.

187   3     PANOPTIC -L3D DATASET
188
189   Our objective is to enable diverse and fine-grained 3D human pose estimation for in-the-wild scenes.
190   To achieve this, we introduce Panoptic-L3D, the first publicly available benchmark designed for
191   language-driven 3D human pose estimation. Panoptic-L3D incorporates multi-modal information,
192   including videos, language descriptions, and segmentation masks. The annotation pipeline of the
193   Panoptic-L3D dataset is summarized in Fig. 3.
194
195   3.1   DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
196
197
      Video and 3D Skeleton Collection. To construct the language-driven 3D human pose estimation
      dataset, we utilize videos from the Panoptic Joo et al. (2015) dataset, a large-scale collection of
198
      indoor human videos that provides 1.5 million 3D skeleton annotations for individuals engaged in
199
      social activities. To simulate complex multi-person interactions found in real life, we select fourteen
200   activity entries that feature more than one person and span five social interaction categories: Haggling,
201   Ultimatum, Toddler, Musical Instruments, and Special Events. For each activity entry, we obtain two
202   variants from different camera viewpoints (cameras No.16 and No.30) to ensure the robustness of 3D
203   human pose estimation.
204
      Subsequently, these videos are separated into clips and filtered according to the following rules:
205
206   • R1: The length of the video clips is standardized to ensure an average length of 5 seconds. This
207     duration allows individuals to perform a series of actions without being overly complex, making it
208     suitable for describing the behavior of characters using sentences.
209
      • R2: To ensure the accuracy of language descriptions referring to the target individuals, each
        video clip must include at least two participants with fully exposed upper bodies, and at least one
210
        individual must be undergoing significant movements. This requirement focuses on perceptible
211
        limb movements, providing necessary motion information that matches the descriptions in 3D pose
212     recognition and estimation.
213
214   Fig. 4 shows some invalid and valid samples. By rigorously separating and filtering the videos based
215
      on the above rules, we obtained 588 candidate videos with their corresponding 3D skeletons from the
      original Panoptic dataset, with an average duration of 5.17 seconds.

243
      For validation, we follow an interactive game-like approach inspired by ReferIt Kazemzadeh et al.
      (2014), involving two participants in an alternating validation process. Initially, the original video
244
      and its corresponding descriptions are presented to the first validator, who must identify the target
245
      person referred to by the statements. If the chosen target matches the annotator’s target, the annotated
246   video is retained. If there is a discrepancy, the sample is forwarded to a second validator for further
247   review. If the second validator finds any ambiguity, the description is revised. If ambiguity persists,
248   the sample is discarded to maintain the accuracy of descriptions.
249
      By adhering to these validation criteria, we ensure the unique referential integrity and high quality of
250
      language statements in our dataset, thereby enabling more robust evaluations and comparisons of
251
      different methods.
252
253   Annotation and Validation for Individual Masks. The mask annotations for each referred individual
254   in our video data are generated using the advanced 2D segmentation model SAM Kirillov et al. (2023).
255
      We utilize the projected joint points of the upper body (i.e., neck and head top) from the 3D skeleton
      provided by the original Panoptic dataset as coarse point prompts for SAM. For each video, we
256
      generate a coarse mask every 15 consecutive frames.
257
258   However, some misprojected 3D joints may occur during mask generation due to overlapping between
259   individuals. Additionally, SAM may focus only on the upper part of the referred person since the
260   joints are mostly located in the upper body. To validate and correct the mask annotations, we perform
261
      a multi-round check and re-annotation process, ensuring completeness and consistency between the
      masked individual and the corresponding description.
262
263   Dataset Split. The dataset is divided into training, validation, and test sets, consisting of 3,005, 312,
264   and 521 sentences, respectively, along with their corresponding videos, masks, and 3D skeletons.

351   To address these limitations, we propose a baseline, Cascaded Pose Perception (CPP), for language-
352   driven 3D human pose estimation. CPP integrates pose features with video features in a two-stage
353   manner. It first learns the 2D pose information and generates multiple levels of information, including
354   root depth maps, 2D joints maps, and box detection maps, to support 3D human pose estimation. We
355   then design a body fusion module to utilize this pose information to generate masks for specified
356
      individuals. Based on the generated masks, we further incorporate a 3D root estimator and a mask
      fusion block to improve the precision of 3D human pose estimation and reduce the impact of mask
357

359   Method. Initially, following Voxelpose Tu et al. (2020), we employ a backbone of 2D pose estimation
360   to extract 2D pose information from the relevant frame of the input video. This procedure yields
361   box detection maps Mb ∈ RW ×H×4 , root (waist) depth maps Mr ∈ RW ×H , and 2D joints maps
362
      Mj ∈ RW ×H×Nj for all individuals in the frame. Here, Nj indicates the number of human joints,
      and W , H are the width and height of the output maps.
363
364   Next, we extract both video and textual features and design a body fusion block to integrate them
365   with the 2D pose information, specifically the 2D joints maps Mj . The body fusion block blends
366   the video features with pose information, enhancing the understanding of pose and the precision of
367   individual segmentation.
368   The integrated features are then processed by a transformer-based auto-encoder to capture com-
369   prehensive information about all individuals present. Specifically, the human box detection maps
370   Mb are utilized to create object queries Oq ∈ RNq ×D that encapsulate each person’s information,
371   where Nq is the number of object queries and D is the dimension of object queries. By providing
372   precise locational data, our method efficiently eliminates ambiguity in masks, achieving more precise
373   identification and extraction of specified human features. These object queries are further transformed
374
      into bounding boxes, masks, and categories for all individuals, unified for outputting the candidate
      masks and corresponding confidence scores. The mask with the highest confidence is then selected
375
      as the final mask M ∈ RW ×H for the referred person, which is also a side output of our CPP.
376

410
411
      Implementation Details. The architectures of text encoder, video encoder, and pose encoder in our
412
      framework are pretrained RoBERTa Liu et al. (2019), Tiny Swin-Transformer Liu et al. (2022), and
413
      ResNet-152 He et al. (2016), respectively. The number of object queries is set to 10 by default. During
414   training and testing, we feed the model windows of w = 6 frames. The models are trained using a
415   24GB NVIDIA 4090 GPU. Both stage 1 and stage 2 are trained for 10 epochs with a learning rate of
416   1e-4; each epoch for stage 1 and stage 2 takes approximately 1.5 hours and 2 hours, respectively. The
417   input frames are resized to have a minimum size of 512 pixels on the shorter side and a maximum
418   length of 960 pixels on the longer side to ensure efficient memory usage on the GPU.
419
      Evaluation Metrics. The metrics employed in our proposed L3DHPE task are twofold: the Percent-
420   age of Individuals Correctly Identified (PICI) and the Mean Per Joint Position Error (MPJPE-L) in
421   millimeters. PICI calculates the proportion of correct cases in referring to individual identification.
422   MPJPE-L measures the accuracy of 3D skeleton estimation by calculating the mean Euclidean
423   distance between the predicted and ground truth joint positions across all joints. The calculation
424   approach for MPJPE-L is the same as that for MPJPE (usually used in 3DHPE task). The added
425   ’L’ indicates that this evaluation protocol simultaneously considers the tasks of text-referred person
426   identification and minimizing the predicted pose’s deviation. Note that when the identified person is
427   not the actual person referred to in the text description, the predicted joints exhibit significantly larger
428
      variations compared to the ground truth, leading to a larger value of MPJPE-L than the normal MPJPE.
      We believe MPJPE-L is a more straightforward and comprehensive metric for evaluating methods in
429
      our L3DHPE task. A detailed discussion of these metrics is provided in the supplementary.

527   6   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
528
529   Imagine a future where machines understand human actions and intentions through language. This
530   paper introduces Language-Driven 3D Human Pose Estimation and the Panoptic-L3D dataset, blend-
531   ing video data with linguistic annotations. The Cascaded Pose Perception baseline demonstrates the
532   power of integrating textual descriptions with visual data. While acknowledging the manual nature
533   of our annotation process as a challenge for future work, we see L3DHPE enhancing fields from
534   sports analytics to advanced robotics. By releasing Panoptic-L3D and CPP, we invite the research
535   community to build on our foundation, exploring and improving language-guided 3D pose estimation.
536   Limitations and Future Work. There are many interesting research directions and remaining
537   challenges to be addressed with the Panoptic-L3D dataset and L3DHPE task. These include but are
538   not limited to: (i) designing more efficient models to increase running speed, (ii) designing more
539   accurate and elegant models to recognize and understand complex motions, (iii). designing robust
      cross-modal fusion methods to better leverage motion information and language information.
