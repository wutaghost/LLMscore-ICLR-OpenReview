                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Generative models have achieved remarkable success in image, video, and text
               domains. Inspired by this, researchers have explored utilizing generative models to
011
               generate neural network parameters. However, these efforts have been limited by
012
               the parameter size and the practicality of generating high-performance parameters.
013            In this paper, we propose C OND P-D IFF, a novel approach that demonstrates
014            the feasibility of controllable high-performance parameter generation, particu-
015            larly for LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) weights, during the fine-tuning process.
016            Specifically, we employ an autoencoder to extract efficient latent representations
017            for parameters. We then train a conditional latent diffusion model to synthesize
018            high-performing model parameters from random noise based on specific task
019            conditions. Experimental results in both computer vision and natural language
020            processing domains consistently demonstrate that C OND P-D IFF can generate
021
               high-performance parameters conditioned on the given task. Moreover, we observe
               that the parameter distribution generated by C OND P-D IFF exhibits differences
022
               compared to the distribution obtained through normal optimization methods, in-
023
               dicating a certain level of generalization capability. Our work paves the way for
024            further exploration of condition-driven parameter generation, offering a promising
025            direction for task-specific adaptation of neural networks.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recent advancements in generative models Rombach et al. (2022); Ramesh et al. (2022); Saharia
031   et al. (2022); Brown et al. (2020) have marked substantial progress across several domains of artificial
032   intelligence. In the computer vision domain, generative adversarial networks Goodfellow et al.
033   (2014), diffusion models Ho et al. (2020), and other approaches Dinh et al. (2014); Rezende et al.
034   (2014) have shown impressive results in image synthesis and manipulation. Notably, models such
035   as Stable Diffusion Rombach et al. (2022), DALL-E 2 Ramesh et al. (2022), and Imagen Saharia
036   et al. (2022) have set new benchmarks in the quality and resolution of generated images. Moreover,
037   video generation models like Sora OpenAI (2024) have shown promising results in producing
038
      coherent and high-quality video sequences, opening new avenues for applications in entertainment
      and media. In the natural language processing domain Radford et al. (2019); Kaplan et al. (2020);
039
      Wei et al. (2022), autoregressive models like GPT Brown et al. (2020) and Llama Touvron et al.
040
      (2023) have demonstrated promising generation capabilities and alignment with human preference Jin
041   et al. (2024); Ouyang et al. (2022); Rafailov et al. (2024); Kadavath et al. (2022), which underscore
042   the potential of generative models.
043
044
      Inspired by these achievements, recent studies Peebles et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2024) have begun
      to explore the application of generative models in novel areas, generating high-performing model
045
      parameters. These studies focus on directly generating novel model parameters to accelerate the
046
      training process, uncovering parameters that achieve comparable performance with those obtained
047   through conventional optimization methods.
048
049   By harnessing the power of generative models, it is possible to substantially reduce the computational
050
      cost and time required for model optimization Peebles et al. (2022); Ruder (2016); Kingma & Ba
      (2014). Besides, examining the latent relationships between model parameters and performance
051
      provides valuable insights into the behavior and characteristics of neural networks Ha et al. (2016).
052
053   However, previous works on parameter generation Wang et al. (2024); Peebles et al. (2022); Soro
      et al. (2024); Schürholt et al. (2022); Knyazev et al. (2021) face several limitations. On the one hand,

082   Specifically, LoRA Hu et al. (2021) is a parameter-efficient fine-tuning technique that adapts
083   pre-trained models to specific tasks by learning low-rank matrices that modify the model’s weights.
084   To achieve high-performance controllable conditional parameter generation, we propose Conditional
085   Parameter Diffusion, named C OND P-D IFF, which utilizes a standard latent diffusion model to
086   perform conditional generation, synthesizing a new set of parameters tailored to specific conditions.
087   Specifically, we use an autoencoder and a conditional latent diffusion model to capture the distribution
088   of network weights. First, the autoencoder is trained on a selected set of parameters from models
089   optimized with normal optimization methods, e.g., SGD Ruder (2016), on different datasets, creating
090   latent representations of these parameters. Second, we utilize a domain-specific condition, e.g., text,
091   style image, projector to encode the condition information and train a conditional diffusion model
092
      to reconstruct latent representations. Finally, as shown in Figure 1, the trained conditional latent
      diffusion model C OND P-D IFF generates latent representations from random noise in the inference
093
      process based on specific task conditions. Then, the decoder of the trained autoencoder processes
094
      these generated representations to produce new, high-performing model parameters.
095
096   Our method has the following characteristics: i) It demonstrates comparable or superior performance
097   relative to models trained with conventional methods, spanning various datasets and architectures.
098
      ii) The parameters generated by our approach significantly differ from the parameters obtained
      during normal training, highlighting its capability to synthesize novel parameters rather than merely
099
      replicating the training examples. iii) Extensive evaluations demonstarte the robustness of our
100
      approach. Our method C OND P-D IFF also shows generalizability in generated high-performance
101   model weights space. We hope that our findings will provide new insights into the potential of
102   applying conditional diffusion models to parameter generation and highlight a promising direction
103   for task-specific parameter generation of neural networks.

      2.1   P RELIMINARIES OF L O RA
111
112   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2021) enhances the efficiency of fine-tuning large pre-trained
113   language models by minimizing the computational demands usually required for full model retraining.
114   LoRA introduces two trainable matrices, B ∈ Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k , to each transformer layer. These
115   matrices, where r is much smaller than hidden layer dimension d and task-specific dimension k,
116   perform a low-rank approximation of the typical updates made during fine-tuning. The core idea is
117
      that the necessary adjustments for task-specific adaptation have a low "intrinsic dimension," allowing
      significant reductions in trainable parameters while maintaining performance. The pretrained weight
118
      matrix W0 remains unchanged, with only B and A being optimized, thus speeding up training and
119
      decreasing memory and computational needs. The modified forward pass in LoRA is represented as:
120
121                                      W0 x + ∆W x = W0 x + B(Ax)                                      (1)
122   where ∆W = BA is the update. Initially, B is zero, ensuring no changes to W0 , and A starts with a
123   small random Gaussian distribution. In deployment, the learned low-rank matrices B and A can be
124   integrated into W0 . In this work, we aim to synthesize LoRA parameters because of the practicality
125   and effective LoRA fusion that show the continuous distribution in LoRA parameter space.
126

      Conditional diffusion models Ho et al. (2020); Rombach et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023) extend the
      standard diffusion model by incorporating conditions into both the forward and reverse processes.
130

131
      attributes or requirements.

      3.3   C ONDITIONAL PARAMETER GENERATION
217
218   We utilize a conditional latent diffusion model to synthesize high-performance parameters based
219   on conditions y such as text and image. To handle different tasks and modalities, we adopt the
220   domain-specific encoder, which is denoted as τdomain (y; ρ), where y represents the input condition
221   and ρ denotes the encoder parameters. For example, in the NLP experiments of this work, we employ
222   the text decoder in CLIPRadford et al. (2021). Inspired by in-context learning, the input condition
223   y consists of a task description and two-shot examples to capture the task information. Besides,
224
      we utilize stylized images as conditions in style transfer tasks and adopt ResNet He et al. (2016)
      to extract style latent representations as the condition vector. More details about the condition are
225
      shown in Appendix 6.1. Regarding the U-Net architecture, we apply one-dimensional convolutions
226
      in denoising autoencoders because the weight matrix parameters do not show strong positional

241   4.1   E XPERIMENT SETUP
242
243
      Datasets and metrics. We evaluate our method across various domains. Specifically, in NLP
      experiments, we test on the language understanding GLUE benchmark Wang et al. (2018). In CV
244
      experiments, we focus on the style-transfer tasks. We use the SemArt and WikiArt datasets Garcia
245
      & Vogiatzis (2018); Saleh & Elgammal (2015), which contain diverse artistic images, and evaluate
246   them using the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID, Heusel et al. (2017), as employed by StyleGAN
247   Karras et al. (2019), with lower scores indicating better performance.
248
249
      Dataset collecting and training procedures. In NLP experiments, we collect 150 training samples
      for models, including BERT, Roberta, GPT-2 by default. For instance, in the case of BERT, we
250
      fixed pre-trained parameters and fine-tuned the network using LoRA. Specifically, we conduct the
251
      hyperparameter search for fixed values of r and α and select the fine-tuning hyperparameters that
252   yield the best average performance. During the fine-tuning process, we save the checkpoints of the
253   last 150 steps as the training dataset, which includes the LoRA learnable matrix weights. In the
254   framework of C OND P-D IFF, the autoencoder includes 1D CNN-based encoders and decoders. We
255   utilize the text encoder from CLIP as the condition text encoder. In image style transfer tasks, we
256   fine-tune attention modules of a popular text-to-image model, P IX A RT-α model Chen et al. (2024)
257   using LoRA and collected the last 64 LoRA checkpoints of the training process once in 10 steps. In
258   the framework of C OND P-D IFF, we used pre-trained ResNet18 to extract style latent as the condition
259   vector. All experiments were conducted on the Linux server with four NVIDIA A100 GPUs. The
260
      noise ξz is Gaussian noise with an amplitude of 0.001 by default. Detailed training hyperparameters
      for LoRA fine-tuning and C OND P-D IFF framework are provided in Appendix B.
261
262   Inference procedures. In NLP tasks, we generate 20 LoRA parameters for each task using a
263   conditional diffusion model through random noise and merge these generated parameters into the
264   pre-trained model. We select the model that exhibits the best performance on the training dataset
265   and report its performance on the validation dataset. In style-transfer tasks, we synthesize LoRA
266
      parameters of the corresponding styles by feeding the conditional diffusion model with images in
      various styles as conditions. We then merge parameters with P IX A RT-α’s and utilize them to generate
267
      images using a set of prompts. Finally, we compute the FID score of the generated images.

406   5    R ELATED WORK
407
408   Diffusion models Diffusion models Ho et al. (2020); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Peebles & Xie (2023)
409   have recently emerged as a powerful class of generative models, enabling high-fidelity synthesis
410   of complex data distributions. The research on the diffusion model can be generally classified into
411
      four categories. The first category aims to enhance image synthesis quality Rombach et al. (2022);
      Ramesh et al. (2022); Saharia et al. (2022) Second, researchers focus on accelerating the sampling
412
      process Song et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2022). Third, recent research has also focused on reevaluating
413
      diffusion models through the lens of continuous analysis like score-based generative modeling Feng
414   et al. (2023). Fourth, the success of diffusion models has sparked their application in various domains,
415   Kong et al. (2021); Luo & Hu (2021); Wolleb et al. (2022). In this work, we explore the conditional
416   diffusion model in the parameter generation domain.
417
      Conditional generation Conditional generation has gained significant attention in computer vision
418
      and natural language processing. Three prominent frameworks have emerged: conditional GANs
419
      Mirza & Osindero (2014); Isola et al. (2018); Zhu et al. (2020), conditional VAEs Sohn et al. (2015);
420   Yan et al. (2016), and conditional diffusion models xwRombach et al. (2022); Ho et al. (2020), which
421   incorporate conditions to guide the generation process, enabling the creation of visually coherent and
422   semantically meaningful data samples. Conditional GANs incorporate condition information into
423   GAN to generate images conditioned on specific attributes or labels. Conditional diffusion models
424   take this further by generating visually coherent and semantically meaningful images from the textual
425   description, demonstrating superior image synthesis quality compared to GANs. Building upon the
426   success of conditional diffusion models, we propose to extend this approach to generating neural
427   network parameters based on specific conditions.
428   Parameter generation The field of parameter generation has seen significant progress in recent
429   years, with HyperNetworks ((Ha et al., 2016) and generative models of neural network checkpoints
430   Peebles et al. (2022) emerging as promising approaches. Ha et al. (2016) introduced HyperNetworks,
431   which uses a hypernetwork to learn the parameters for another neural network. Finn et al. (2017)
      proposes Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning, which learns an initialization for efficient fine-tuning.


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Peebles et al. (2022) introduce the model G.pt to predict the distribution over parameter updates
433   given an initial input parameter vector and a prompted loss or error. Schürholt et al. (2022) trained
434   autoencoder on a model zoo to learn a hyper-representation for generative use to sample new model
435   weights Knyazev et al. (2021) use a GNN-based model to sample network parameters. Erkoç et al.
436   (2023) directly leverages MLP weights and generates neural implicit fields encoded by synthesized
437   MLP weights. Wang et al. (2024) uses a diffusion model to generate high-performing neural network
438   parameters across various architectures and datasets. Different from the previous works, we focus
439   on conditional parameter generation to generate high-performing weights based on specific task
440   conditions practically.
441
442   6     C ONCLUSION
443
444   In this work, we proposed an approach C OND P-D IFF for high-performance controllable parameter
445   generation, specially for LoRA parameters. We utilize an autoencoder and a conditional latent
446   diffusion model to capture the distribution of high-performing parameters and perform conditional
447   generation, synthesizing a new set of parameters tailored to specific conditions. We show that
448   our method can efficiently synthesize novel and high-quality model parameters. The parameter
449   distribution generated by C OND P-D IFF exhibits differences compared to the distribution obtained
450
      through conventional optimization methods, indicating a certain level of generalization capability.
451
452   6.1   L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORK
453
      Nonetheless, it is essential to recognize that diffusion in parameter generation is still largely unex-
454
      plored despite the significant advances in the realm of image and video synthesis. In this work, we
455   present a preliminary methodology for conditional parameter diffusion. However, several challenges
456   remain unresolved, including reducing memory demands for large model architectures, enhancing
457   the generalizability of generation techniques, and improving the representation of dataset conditions.
458   Furthermore, integrating knowledge graphs with conditional diffusion offers promising directions for
459   controlling conditional generation.
