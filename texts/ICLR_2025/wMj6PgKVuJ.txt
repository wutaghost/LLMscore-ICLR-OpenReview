                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011            A key property of reasoning systems is the ability to make sharp decisions on their
012            input data. For contemporary AI systems, a key carrier of sharp behaviour is the
013            softmax function, with its capability to perform differentiable query-key lookups.
014            It is a common belief that the predictive power of networks leveraging softmax
015            arises from “circuits” which sharply perform certain kinds of computations con-
016            sistently across many diverse inputs. However, for these circuits to be robust, they
017            would need to generalise well to arbitrary valid inputs. In this paper, we dispel
018            this myth: even for tasks as simple as finding the maximum key, any learned cir-
019
               cuitry must disperse as the number of items grows at test time. We attribute this to
               a fundamental limitation of the softmax function to robustly approximate sharp
020
               functions, prove this phenomenon theoretically, and propose adaptive temperature
021
               as an ad-hoc technique for improving the sharpness of softmax at inference time.

030   is one of the most fundamental functions in contemporary artificial intelligence systems.
031   The role of softmax in deep learning is to convert any vector of logits, e ∈ Rn , into a probability
032   distribution, in a form that is part of the exponential family. Further, softmax allows for application
033   of a temperature parameter, θ ∈ R, to adjust the amount of probability mass attached to the highest
034   logit—a concept borrowed from the Boltzmann distribution in statistical mechanics.
035   Initially, the primary utilisation of softmax in deep learning was within the final layer of classifiers.
036   Its influence in this domain vastly expanded after it saw use in the internal layers—as a differentiable
037   key-value store (Graves et al., 2014) or a mechanism for attending over the most relevant parts of the
038   input (Bahdanau et al., 2015). This attentional framing of softmax was critical in defining important
039   models for sequences (Vaswani et al., 2017, Transformers), images (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021, ViTs)
040   and graphs (Veličković et al., 2018, GATs).
041   Several efforts attribute the success of softmax to its capability of modelling computations relevant
042   to reasoning. This can be related to the concept of circuits in theoretical computer science (Arora &
043   Barak, 2009). Several interpretable pieces of “circuitry” (Olah et al., 2020) have already been dis-
044   covered in large Transformers, primarily under the umbrella of mechanistic interpretability (Elhage
045   et al., 2021; Olsson et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022).
046
      Here we study the robustness of such circuitry, especially when going beyond the distribution the
047   models are trained on—a critical regime for reasoning engines. We find that, in spite of its many
048   successes, softmax does not have a chance to robustly generalise such circuits out of distribution,
049   especially as it provably cannot approximate sharpness with increasing problem size (Figure 1).

077   Key theoretical result We define sharp functions by their behaviour as their number of inputs
078   varies. This directly motivates the out-of-distribution setting we study: generalising to different
079   amounts of inputs. Specifically, when we analyse neural networks that learn sharp functions, we
080   assume that they are trained on problem instances containing no more than n input items, and we
081
      take a particular interest in their sharpness on instances with n′ > n items; these are considered
      out-of-distribution instances because they go beyond the maximal number of inputs the model had
082
      been prepared for. In language modelling, this setting is also known as length generalisation (Anil
083
      et al., 2022); in graph machine learning, it is known as size generalisation (Yehudai et al., 2021).
084
085   Through one of our key theoretical results (Theorem 2.2), we demonstrate that modern deep learning
086   architectures, operating over a fixed vocabulary of input tokens and leveraging the softmax function,
087
      are fundamentally incapable of learning functions that remain sharp under such out-of-distribution
      instances. This is due to the fact that the coefficients emitted by the softmax function must disperse
088
      as we increase the number of input items. Here by dispersing we mean that, as the number of input
089
      items grows, the coefficient attached to each individual item must decay towards zero. This makes it
090   impossible to robustly compute functions that depend on any particular finite amount of input values,
091   such as the aforementioned max, as we show in Appendix B (Corollary B.1 and Remark B.2).
092
093
      We hope that our results will encourage future study of alternative attentional functions, in light of
      the problems we identify, especially for building reasoning engines of the future. That being said,
094
      we also believe our findings indicate ways to modify the softmax function to support sharpness for
095
      longer—as one simple example, we propose an adaptive temperature mechanism for softmax.

098   Background The analysis of attentional coefficients and attempting to attribute interpretable op-
099   erations to them dates back to the earliest deployments of internal softmax layers at scale; examples
100   include (Graves et al., 2014, Figure 6), (Bahdanau et al., 2015, Figure 3), (Vaswani et al., 2017, Fig-
101   ures 3–5) and (Qiu et al., 2018, Figure 5). A strong current in this space analyses the self-attentional
102   heads of Transformers (Voita et al., 2019; Jain & Wallace, 2019).
103   With the rise of large language models, mechanistic interpretability has taken charge in detecting
104   and elucidating various circuits in Transformers (Elhage et al., 2021). Some prominent discoveries
105   include induction heads (Olsson et al., 2022), indirect object identification (Wang et al., 2022),
106   multiple-choice heads (Lieberum et al., 2023), successor heads (Gould et al., 2023), attentional
107   sinks (Darcet et al., 2023), comparator heads (Hanna et al., 2024) and retrieval heads (Wu et al.,
      2024). Most recently, these efforts have relied on sparse autoencoders (Kissane et al., 2024).

      The skills above are quite impressive and span many rules one might hope a robust reasoning sys-
109   tem would have, and the discovered heads always appear sharp when inspected on in-distribution
110   samples. However, it is also known that many easy tasks requiring sharp attention—such as find-
111   ing minima—are hard to do reliably with LLMs out-of-distribution (Markeeva et al., 2024, Fig-
112   ure 6). More challenging sharp order statistic tasks, such as finding the second minimum (Ong &
113   Veličković, 2022) may even be hard to learn in-distribution. The discrepancy of such results with
114   the previous paragraph motivate our study, and formalisation of softmax dispersion.
115   Certain dispersion effects in softmax—e.g. as an effect of increasing temperature—are already
116   well-understood in thermodynamics. A core contribution of our work is understanding dispersion in
117   a setting where the amount of logits can vary, which is relevant for generalisation in Transformers.
118   We are not the first to observe dispersion in this setting empirically; prior works studying the capa-
119   bility of Transformers to execute algorithms (Yan et al., 2020) and perform random-access lookups
120   (Ebrahimi et al., 2024) also note dispersion patterns. Our work is the first to rigorously prove these
121   effects, directly attribute them to the softmax operator, as well as propose ways to improve sharp-
122   ness empirically within softmax. The proof technique we will use to demonstrate this is inspired
123
      by Barbero et al. (2024), though unlike their work, our key results apply regardless of whether the
      computational graph is bottlenecked or not.

      Since we now know dispersion is inevitable, are there any ways we can leverage our theory’s findings
256
      to make softmax sharper? One obvious constraint our theory rests on is the assumption that θ > 0,
257
      i.e. that our temperature is nonzero. While zero temperature—also known as hard attention (Denil
258   et al., 2012; Ranzato, 2014; Mnih et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2015)—guarantees sharpness, training

335   The JAX (Bradbury et al., 2018) implementation of our adaptive-θ softmax is provided below, and
336
      we use it as a drop-in replacement for jax.nn.softmax in all of our experiments.
337
      def adaptive_temperature_softmax(logits):
338     original_probs = jax.nn.softmax(logits)
339
340       poly_fit = jnp.array([-0.037, 0.481, -2.3, 4.917, -1.791]) # see Figure 5
341       entropy = jnp.sum(-original_probs * jnp.log(original_probs + 1e-9),
342                         axis=-1, keepdims=True) # compute the Shannon entropy
343       beta = jnp.where( # beta = 1 / theta
344           entropy > 0.5, # don't overcorrect low-entropy heads
345           jnp.maximum(jnp.polyval(poly_fit, entropy), 1.0), # never increase entropy
346
              1.0)
347
          return jax.nn.softmax(logits * beta)

350   several additional intermediate tensors, we are able to implement it in a way that allows the en-
351   tropy correction computation to be fully streamed, and hence compatible with efficient, scalable
352   approaches like Flash Attention (Dao et al., 2022) that uses O(n) rather than O(n2 ) memory to
353   compute attention. We provide the derivation of our streamed algorithm in Appendix F.

      Note we are not the first to propose dynamically adapting temperature—Neumann et al. (2018);
355   Radford et al. (2021) do this in the classification layer (and hence do not have to handle an ever-
356   increasing amount of items), whereas Chiang & Cholak (2022); Cao et al. (2024) perform it over
357   intermediate attentional heads, but in a way that only depends on problem size (e.g. multiplying
358   logits by log n), hence not taking into account initial logit sharpness. It is important to also call out
359   AERO (Jha & Reagen, 2024), a method which introduces learnable temperature, and Entropix (xjdr
360   & doomslide, 2024), a notable library for (var)entropy-based LLM sampling.

413

414   inputs are often textual representations of floating-point numbers and therefore individual numbers
415   often span multiple tokens. It is therefore insufficient and inappropriate to aim for entropy levels
416   where all the focus would be on one token only, as was desirable in the max retrieval task.
417

418

419

420   now dealing with a system spanning many attentional heads across many layers, it is not easy to
421   even discover relevant attentional heads’ behaviours, and even less so to ascertain that the model’s
422   robustness depends on those specific heads in those ways. As briefly discussed before, any such
423   individual endeavour typically leads to a brand-new research project in mechanistic interpretability,
424   and we do not find this to be in-scope of our paper.
425
      That being said, there is an alternate route to make the Gemma model still benefit from our adap-
426   tive temperature module exactly as-is (i.e., with exactly the same polynomial fit as in Figure 5);
427   it just has to directly learn how to leverage it. As such, in our CLRS-Text ablation we apply
428   adaptive temperature both during fine-tuning and at inference time. What this means is, we re-
429   place all instances of jax.nn.softmax within all the attentional heads of Gemma 2B with our
430   adaptive temperature softmax function, both during fine-tuning of the model and during infer-
431   ence. This allows the model to learn how to compute key/query embeddings that can maximally
      exploit the temperature adaptation.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
494
      “Energy continuously flows from being concentrated
495
      To becoming dispersed, spread out, wasted and useless.”—The 2nd Law: Unsustainable, by Muse
496
497   In this paper, we have provided extensive theoretical and empirical evidence that the softmax—a key
498   function in the design of modern frontier architectures—is fundamentally unable to sustain robust
499   reasoning behaviours across all possible inputs, as its output coefficients are necessarily dispersing
500
      provided sufficient input elements.
501   Beyond illustrating and proving these dispersion effects, we also attempted to use our theoretical
502   framework to propose an adaptive temperature approach that is able—at least to a certain extent—to
503   hold the dispersion effect at bay. It is our opinion that the favourable results we observe with adaptive
504   temperature warrant further investigation, and indicate that such adaptive layers are a strategy worth
505   dedicating further attention to in future work.
506   We conclude by remarking, once again, that adaptive temperature is merely an ad-hoc method and
507   it does not escape the conclusions of our theory! The key takeaway of our paper is not the adaptive
508   temperature proposal; it is the fact that we find it worthwhile to more seriously invest in research of
509   hybrid architectures that will not fully rely on the softmax function, at least within the confines of
510   the assumptions of our theory. To name a few possibilities:
511
512
             • Any kind of unnormalised attention, such as linear (Schmidhuber, 1992), sigmoidal (Rama-
               puram et al., 2024) or stick-breaking attention (Tan et al., 2024) does not have the dispersion
513
               issues presented here. That being said, it becomes substantially harder to meaningfully rank
514
               items using them, see e.g. the GATv2 paper (Brody et al., 2022).
515
516
             • Similarly, forcing the attention to be hard or local (Martins & Astudillo, 2016; Correia
               et al., 2019; Peters et al., 2019) would also escape the confines of our theory. We already
517
               briefly discussed the challenges of learning with hard attention—local attention provides a
518
               very interesting alternative, but it must be stressed that “out-of-distribution” behaviours for
519            certain heads may appear even at highly “local” scales; OOD here refers to going outside
520            the largest problem size the head saw at training time, not the largest context deployed at
521            training time.
522
             • Lastly, our key Theorem relies on the model being built out of continuous building blocks.
523
               Inserting discontinuities in the feedforward layers—perhaps using approaches like Dudzik
524            et al. (2024) as inspiration—would also break the assumptions of our theory, though it
525            comes with obvious challenges to learning at scale.
526
527   While such approaches haven’t seen as much success at scale as the “vanilla” Transformer, we
528   hope our results inspire future work into making them stable, especially for constructing reasoning
529   systems.

723   A     E XPERIMENTAL DETAILS FOR THE MAXIMUM ENTRY RETRIEVAL TASK
724
725   As briefly described in the main paper, we leverage the max retrieval task over a single attention head
726   as a way to empirically validate our theory, as well as assess the benefits of adaptive temperature in
727   a controlled setting. In this section, we describe the various aspects of our experimental setup, for
728
      the purposes of clarity and reproducibility.
729
730   A.1    M OTIVATION
731
      We deliberately focus on a single attention head environment and a simple selection function (max)
732
      to remove any confounders from our observations.
733
734   Since we are using exactly one attention head, whatever coefficients it outputs can be directly related
735   to the network’s belief in which items are most important for the downstream prediction. This allows
736
      us to, e.g., correlate the coefficients with the ground-truth magnitude of the items.
737   Since we are looking for the maximal element’s property, we are not requiring any complicated
738   behaviour from the coefficients: when our target task is to approximate max, the softmax coefficients
739   need to approximate argmax—which is exactly what they are designed to be a smooth approximation
740   for. As such, this choice of target task exhibits high algorithmic alignment (Xu et al., 2020).
741
742   A.2    DATA GENERATION
743
744   Let n be the number of items in the set that we wish to classify. For each item, 1 ≤ i ≤ n, we need
745
      to define a priority value, which is used to select the maximal entry. We sample these values from a
      uniform distribution; ρi ∼ U(0, 1).
746
747   We would also wish our task to be a classification rather than regression task, in order to leverage a
748   more robust accuracy metric. As such, let C be the desired number of classes. We can now attach
749   to each item a class, κi ∼ U{1, . . . , C}, sampled uniformly at random. We assume C = 10 fixed.
750   Then, for each input item, 1 ≤ i ≤ n, we consider its features to be xi ∈ RC+1 to be defined
751   as xi = ρi ∥onehot(κi , C), i.e. the concatenation of these two sampled pieces of data where κi is
752   represented as a one-hot vector.
753
      Lastly, since we will leverage dot-product attention, we also need a query vector. In this particular
754
      task, the query is irrelevant, and we initialise it to a random uniformly-sampled value, q ∼ U(0, 1).
755
      Our task is to predict, given {xi }1≤i≤n and q, the class of the maximal item, i.e., κarg maxi ρi .
