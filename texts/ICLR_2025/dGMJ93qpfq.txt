                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Prompt tuning is an efficient way to adapt large foundation models. It introduces
               learnable prompts with the input data tokens, offering a practical alternative to
011
               full model finetuning. However, when prompts are trained on base/target tasks,
012
               they often overfit, leading to reduced performance on novel, unseen tasks. To
013            address this limitation, various techniques leverage global image semantics to
014            improve accuracy on unseen tasks while maintaining performance on base tasks.
015            However, they often overlook the rich fine-grained local information that could be
016            crucial for capturing finer semantics and improving generalisation. In this work,
017            we propose a modular approach to prompt tuning that leverages local semantics
018            by incorporating patch-level information, representing the first integration of such
019            semantics in this context. Specifically, we integrate patch-level information across
020            vision, text, and predictions through three consistency mechanisms: 1) Patch-based
021
               consistency loss that aligns patches from the prompted input image with those
               from the same image processed by a frozen model, while also enforcing inter-view
022
               consistency by applying the loss across different views, capturing fine-grained
023
               regional dependencies and improving vision representation quality; 2) Text prompt
024            consistency loss, where view-specific text prompts are tailored and regularised to
025            maintain coherence across views; 3) Vision features for each view, enriched with
026            patch-level information, are used to generate predictions based on view-tailored text
027            features. These predictions are then regularised across views, complementing the
028            earlier consistency mechanisms and contributing to a cohesive overall framework.
029            Our approach outperforms existing methods across multiple benchmarks, including
030            base-to-novel generalisation, domain generalisation, and cross-dataset evaluation.
031            These results underscore the potential of integrating fine-grained details for more
032
               robust and adaptable prompts, marking a step forward in foundation model tuning.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Foundation models, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), have demonstrated impressive zero-shot
038   performance across a wide range of tasks, showcasing their adaptability and potential for general
039
      applications. However, when it comes to task-specific performance, these models often fall short,
      compared to specialised models. Fine-tuning foundation models to obtain task-specific models can
040
      address this gap, but it is computationally expensive, time-consuming, and impractical when we have
041
      only limited data. To overcome these limitations, prompt tuning has emerged as an efficient alternative
042   for adapting large foundation models like CLIP (Zhou et al., 2021). This method introduces learnable
043   prompts to make minor adjustments, while keeping the rest of the model frozen, drastically reducing
044   the computational overhead (Zhou et al., 2021; 2022; Zhu et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023; Khattak et al.,
045   2022; Derakhshani et al., 2022; Khattak et al., 2023; Roy & Etemad, 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).
046
      Despite its efficiency, prompt tuning tends to overfit on base classification task which includes a
047
      subset of base classes, and diminishes generalisation to novel, unseen tasks. Models fine-tuned with
048   prompts can underperform on unseen tasks, compared to CLIP’s original zero-shot capabilities (Zhou
049   et al., 2022). This overfitting is due to a loss of generalisation, where the model becomes too tailored
050   to the specific training data, limiting its broader applicability. To address this issue, various strategies
051   have been proposed, such as generating text prompts conditioned on image features (Zhou et al., 2022;
052   Zang et al., 2022), treating prompt learning as a Bayesian inference problem (Derakhshani et al.,
053   2022), aligning prompt updates with gradients from non-prompted predictions (Zhu et al., 2022),
      and applying consistency-based regularisation to the model’s output features and logits (Khattak


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2023; Roy & Etemad, 2023). Among these, multi-modal frameworks that regularise output
055   features and logits with zero-shot CLIP, using ℓ1-norm or cosine similarity (Khattak et al., 2023; Roy
056   & Etemad, 2023), have shown notable success in improving generalisation.
057
058
      Existing methods, such as PromptSRC and CoPrompt, focus primarily on global text and vision fea-
      tures, overlooking the rich local information in image patches. Self-supervised frameworks (Yun et al.,
059
      2022; Li et al., 2022; Atito et al., 2022) incorporate patches but primarily optimize patch-level losses
060
      without addressing cross-modality consistency. Adapter based methods, such as CoPrompt (Roy &
061   Etemad, 2023), also fail to capture rich patch-level details for vision feature generation. In contrast,
062   our approach integrates patch-level features across multiple components, leveraging inter-view and
063   intra-patch-level consistency to address finer-grained variability beyond global consistency. By using
064   patches for both vision feature, text and logit regularization and conditioning text prompts, our
065   method improves feature generation and class predictions, enhancing generalization.
066
      Specifically, we introduce a novel patch-level consistency loss that enforces both intra-view and
067   inter-view consistency, aligning patch representations across different views. While prior works focus
068   on global consistency, we apply intra-view regularisation to the prompted patch features from the
069   anchor view with zero-shot anchor patches. In addition to promote further consistency and to design
070   a non-trivial regularisation, we enforce inter-view consistency which maintains patch consistency
071   across different views of an image by adding an extra guidance for generalisable prompts. An extra
072   augmented view is generated and patch consistency loss is applied with most similar zero-shot anchor
073   patches. Here, both the anchor and augmented prompted patch features are projected through a
074   convolutional layer to obtain better feature representations. This combined intra-view and inter-view
075
      consistency ensures robust prompt tuning and significantly enhances generalisation.
076   In addition to regularizing the vision branch, we also focus on the text branch by leveraging local
077   information and inter-view variations. To achieve this, we generate view-tailored text prompts based
078   on view-specific clustered patch features. Unlike CoCoop (Zhou et al., 2022), which relies on global
079   semantics for text prompt generation, our approach incorporates local patch features for a more
080   refined prompt. Finally, view-specific text features are generated from each of the view-tailored
081
      prompts. To further regularize the view-tailored text prompts, we apply an ℓ1-loss between the
      anchor-view and augmented-view text features, with a stop-gradient applied to the anchor-view
082
      output. This ensures inter-view consistency in the text branch and promotes the generalizability of
083
      the learned text prompts.
084
085   Lastly, patch-level information is integrated with vision features by averaging projected patch tokens
086   and combining them with class token features to produce enhanced vision outputs. These outputs are
087
      used to compute logits via a dot product with view-specific text features, further regularized with
      KL-Divergence between anchor and augmented view logits, applying a stop-gradient to the anchor
088
      view.
089
090   Our Patch-Aware Prompting (PAP) framework achieves superior performance over previous methods
091   across diverse tasks, including base-to-novel generalization, domain generalization, and cross-dataset
092   evaluation. By seamlessly integrating patch-level information and addressing both intra-view and inter-
093
      view variations, PAP enhances generalization and robustness in prompt tuning. While PAP includes
      multiple components, each is specifically designed to address distinct challenges in leveraging patch-
094
      level information, with their collective effectiveness validated through extensive ablation studies.
095
      This ensures a reliable and efficient pipeline for adapting foundation models to downstream tasks.

098   2    R ELATED W ORK
099
100
      Foundation models based on image-text alignment have demonstrated strong zero-shot performance,
101
      but finetuning them is computationally expensive and often leads to reduced generalisation (Radford
102   et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2021). As a result, many
103   applications leverage these models as feature extractors with task-specific components (Vidit et al.,
104   2023; Yu et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Yi et al., 2023; Yun et al., 2023; Bousselham et al., 2023).
105   Prompt learning offers an efficient alternative by adapting vision-language models with task-specific
106   prompts while keeping the base model frozen, thus avoiding the challenges of full finetuning (Zhou
107   et al., 2021). However, prompts trained on base classes tend to generalise poorly to unseen classes.
      To address this, several methods apply regularisation techniques—such as loss constraints, input


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      conditioning, or different strategies/components (Zhou et al., 2022; Khattak et al., 2023; Roy &
109   Etemad, 2023; Derakhshani et al., 2022)—to improve generalisation and ensure better adaptation
110   across both base and novel classes.
111
112
      CoCoop (Zhou et al., 2022), UniVL (Zang et al., 2022), and MaPLe (Khattak et al., 2022) regularise
      prompts by conditioning inputs with global information. CoCoop uses vision features to condition
113
      text prompts, UniVL jointly learns vision and text prompts via a small neural network, and MaPLe
114
      synchronises vision and text prompts across layers. In contrast, our method conditions text prompts
115   at the input layer using rich patch information and regularises output features with a loss function.
116   PromptSRC (Khattak et al., 2023) and CoPrompt (Roy & Etemad, 2023) focus on feature and predic-
117   tion regularisation with zero-shot CLIP knowledge. PromptSRC regularises global embeddings and
118   logits while aggregating prompts across epochs, whereas CoPrompt enhances feature learning with
119   an adapter (Gao et al., 2021). Our approach emphasises finer patch-level feature regularisation and
120   inter-view consistency. Independently developed, Long et al. (2024) uses clustered patch tokens for
121   text prompts but lacks inter-view consistency and patch integration into predictions, underperforming
122   compared to PromptSRC. Prograd (Zhu et al., 2022) and Bayesian Prompt (Derakhshani et al., 2022)
123
      propose different strategies for prompt regularisation. Prograd (Zhu et al., 2022) updates prompts
      only when their gradients align with zero-shot knowledge, while ProDA (Lu et al., 2022) enforces a
124
      Gaussian distribution on prompts to reduce bias. Bayesian Prompt (Derakhshani et al., 2022) views
125
      prompt tuning through a Bayesian lens to improve generalisation.
126
127   In contrast to these regularisation methods, recent studies focus on improving base class perfor-
128   mance (Zhang et al., 2023) or distilling knowledge from a larger teacher model (Li et al., 2024).
129
      DePT (Zhang et al., 2023) decouples the learning of base and novel classes by adding a classification
      head specifically for base classes, improving base class performance while maintaining results on
130
      novel classes. PromptKD (Li et al., 2024) distills a larger CLIP-L/14 model into a smaller CLIP-B/16
131
      model, enhancing overall performance. CasPL Wu et al. (2024), utilizing the larger CLIP-L/14 model
132   for distilling adaptable prompts, follows a two-stage process: learning adaptable prompts via unsu-
133   pervised learning and training boosting prompts using prior prompt-tuning methods. Orthogonally,
134   MetaPrompt (Park et al., 2024) applies meta-learning for prompt tuning by learning a regulariser.
135   Unlike these methods, we maintain the existing training pipeline, avoid distilling from larger models,
136   and focus on improving performance through loss functions and by exploiting fine-grained details.
137
      Our Patch-Aware Prompting (PAP) differs from existing regularisation techniques by incorporating
138   patch-level information at multiple stages. We regularise vision embeddings at the patch level
139   across both similar and different views for more effective regularisation. Additionally, we use
140   patch information to generate view-specific text prompts, which are further regularised to maintain
141   consistency across views. Finally, we enhance the vision embeddings with patch-level information
142   while also regularising logits at the inter-view level. This design allows our model to perform
143   competitively across several evaluation benchmarks.

      3   M ETHODOLOGY
146
147
      We propose a novel prompt tuning framework that leverages rich, local contextual information to
148
      maintain the generalisability of CLIP to novel tasks, while improving performance on base tasks.
149
      Our framework is a modular component that can be applied to previous global prompt consistency
150   methods (Khattak et al., 2023; Roy & Etemad, 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). By incorporating patch-
151   level information, we design components that further regularize the prompt learning process. Unlike
152   earlier approaches that rely on global feature embeddings (Khattak et al., 2023; Roy & Etemad,
153   2023) or architectural modifications (Zhou et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2022) to enhance generalisability,
154   our method focuses on local information. Fig. 1 depicts the overall architecture of the proposed
155   Patch-Aware Prompting framework.
156   First, we introduce a novel vision patch regularisation loss. This loss regularizes the patch outputs
157   generated by our framework with zero-shot predictions from CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), ensuring
158   consistency at the patch level. To handle inter-view variance among different crops typically sampled
159   during training, we add another view called the augmented view of the image and apply a patch loss
160   using zero-shot predictions from the anchor view. This ensures consistent representation with CLIP,
161   both within similar views and across different views of an image. To maintain inter-view consistency
      at the textual level and incorporate rich local context, we generate view-specific text prompts for both

231   We incorporate patch information in multiple ways in both the text and vision branches of our
232   model. Our framework generates two views: a main view, called the anchor view Xan , and an
233   augmented view Xaug . Let V̄an ∈ Rd and P̄an ∈ RM ×d represent the zero-shot class and patch
234   features for the anchor view, and let V̄aug ∈ Rd and P̄aug ∈ RM ×d represent the class and patch
235   features for the augmented view. The prompted class and patch features for the anchor view are
236   Van ∈ Rd and Pan ∈ RM ×d , respectively, while Vaug ∈ Rd and Paug ∈ RM ×d correspond to the
237
      augmented view. We further project these prompted patch features through a convolution projection
238
      block to obtain final patch features: P̂an = ConvProj(Pan ) and P̂aug = ConvProj(Paug ). This

      View-tailored text prompts: Regularizing text prompts with global vision semantics is crucial for
271   strong performance on novel tasks across domains (Khattak et al., 2023; Roy & Etemad, 2023).
272   We enhance this by incorporating rich patch-level details. Unique text prompts are generated for
273   both the anchor and augmented views. Using class tokens to generate prompts, as in Zhou et al.
274   (2022), creates overly similar prompts due to shared information in the tokens and fails to capture
275   patch-level details. To address this, we cluster patches as cues for patch-level semantic reasoning in
276   the prompts. Unlike the quantized bias in Bhardwaj et al. (2022), we derive prompts from patch-level
277   details for more flexible and expressive representations. Instead of a single bias vector for all prompt
278   vectors Gt = {gt1 , gt2 , . . . , gtnt }, we generate individual bias vectors B = {b1 , b2 , . . . , bnt }. These
279
      are derived by clustering the vision zero-shot patch features P̄ using the Voronoi algorithm (Voronoi,
      1908) to create nt clusters, each serving as a bias vector for a prompt vector:
280
281                                  {b1 , b2 , . . . , bnt } = Voronoi_Clustering(P̄ ).                           (9)
282
      These bias vectors are then added to the prompt vectors to generate the final prompt vectors Ĝ:

304   Improved vision features and logits regularisation: After regularizing the text and vision branches
305   using finer details from patches, we propose leveraging this information to enhance the vision features.
306   The class embeddings from the vision encoder, typically used to generate class probabilities, focus
307
      on a single dominant semantic object. However, patches contain local information that can improve
      the overall feature representation. To achieve this, we propose averaging the patch projections to
308
      obtain aggregated patch-level information, which is then added to the class-specific vision features to
309
      generate the final vision features V̂ :
310
311                                                V̂ = V + α ⋆ Avg(P̂ ),                                        (12)

313
      prompted inputs are computed by taking the dot product of the vision and text output features. These
314

315   views, which is defined as follows:

358   The evaluation is done on several different tasks including base-to-novel generalization, cross-dataset
359   evaluation and domain generalisation, with CLIP-B/16 as the frozen backbone. The protocol followed
360   is similar to previous works like CoCoop (Zhou et al., 2022), PromptSRC (Khattak et al., 2023),
361   CoPrompt (Roy & Etemad, 2023). For base to novel generalisation, cross dataset evaluation, we
362   use 11 datasets namely ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), Caltech101 (Fei-Fei et al., 2004), Oxford-
363
      Pets (Parkhi et al., 2012), StanfordCars (Krause et al., 2013), Flowers102 (Nilsback & Zisserman,
      2008), Food101 (Bossard et al., 2014), FGVCAircraft (Maji et al., 2013), SUN397 (Xiao et al., 2010),
364
      UCF101 (Soomro et al., 2012), DTD (Cimpoi et al., 2014), EuroSAT (Helber et al., 2019). For
365
      domain generalisation, we evaluate on the ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), ImageNet-A (Hendrycks
366   et al., 2021b), ImageNet-R (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), ImageNet-Sketch (Wang et al., 2019), Ima-
367   geNetV2 (Recht et al., 2019) datasets. For training we use CLIP with ViT-B/16, we set the learning
368   rate to 0.0025 and train for a maximum of 20 epochs on base-to-novel generalisation, while max-
369   imum of 5 epochs on cross-dataset generalisation and domain generalisation. We set λp , λt , λl to
370   1.0, 0.1, 1.0 respectively as default but modify it for individual dataset when required. The global
371   loss scaling factors mostly follow PromptSRC. Further details are provided in supplementary. All the
372   ablations are conducted with our model based on PromptSRC.

      4.4       A BLATIONS
461
      Effect of different components introduced: We studied the effects of different components we
462   introduced, mainly patch loss, view-tailored text, and enhanced vision features—on performance,
463   as shown in Table 4. Each component improves performance, particularly on novel tasks. However,
464   combining enhanced vision features, patch loss, and view-tailored text improves base, novel, and HM
465   performance. We also evaluated the effect of different conditioning inputs to generate view-tailored
466   text prompts, as shown in Table 7, and found that initializing prompts using patch clusters works
467   best. CoCoop (Zhou et al., 2022), which uses only the class token, performs poorly compared to
468   both our method and attention-based conditioning. Cross-attention from text prompts to patches
469   performs slightly worse than our method but still better than CoCoop, highlighting the importance of
470
      patch-level conditioning.
471   We also studied different clustering techniques in Table 8. KMeans performed comparably to our
472   Voronoi-based clustering on base classes but dropped significantly on novel classes, while EM was
473   inferior to both. Voronoi clustering generates more generalizable clusters, improving performance
474   across base and novel classes. In Table 9, we examined the effects of convolution projection and
475   the use of adapters. We found that a text adapter is required when using convolution projection on
476
      patches, while using adapters on either text or vision alone improves performance. Our default setup,
      which includes both text adapters and convolution projection, performs best.
477
478   We analyzed the effects of adding small crops (scale 0.05 to 0.4) and varying the number of large
479   crops (scale 0.05 to 1) in Table 10. Using two large crops without small crops provided the best
480   performance, while adding small crops or more large crops led to a slight decline. In Table 12,
481   simple augmentations for both anchor and augment views performed best on novel classes, while
482
      complex augmentations slightly decreased overall performance. The combination of simple anchor
      and complex augmentations remained competitive but slightly underperformed on both base and
483
      novel classes.
484
485   Effect of different losses: We study the effects of different losses introduced in our framework, as
      shown in Table 5. We find that each of the losses improves performance compared to cases where no

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We propose a novel prompting framework for vision-language models that incorporates patch-level
533
      information across multiple components, improving both task-specific performance and generalization
534   to novel scenarios. By utilizing patch-level loss for intra- and inter-view consistency, generating
535   regularized view-specific text prompts, and maintaining inter-view consistency of logits, our method
536   addresses the overfitting challenges typical of traditional prompt-tuning approaches. Extensive
537   experiments on tasks such as base-to-novel generalization, domain adaptation, and cross-dataset
538   evaluation demonstrate superior performance, surpassing prior methods. Additionally, ablation
539   studies confirm the effectiveness of each component and explore alternative designs. We believe this
      approach will significantly advance the tuning of foundation models across diverse applications.
