009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In dynamical system modeling, traditional numerical methods have a solid theoreti-
012            cal foundation but are limited by high computational costs and sensitivity to initial
013            conditions. Current data-driven approaches use deep learning models to capture
014            complex spatiotemporal features, but they rely heavily on large amounts of data
015            and assume a stable data distribution, making them ineffective against data scarcity
016            and distribution shifts. To address these challenges, we propose SPARK, a physics-
017            guided quantized augmentation plugin. SPARK integrates boundary information
018
               and physical parameters, using a reconstruction autoencoder to build a physics-rich
               discrete memory bank for data compression. It then enhances selected samples for
019
               downstream tasks with this pre-trained memory bank. SPARK then utilizes an at-
020
               tention mechanism to model historical observations and combines fourier-enhanced
021            graph ODE to efficiently predict long-term dynamical systems, enhancing robust-
022            ness and adaptability to complex physical environments. Extensive experiments
023            on benchmark datasets show that our approach significantly outperforms various
024            baseline methods in handling distribution shifts and data scarcity.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Modeling dynamical systems has long been a critical challenge across numerous scientific fields,
030   including fluid dynamics (Li et al., 2023; Janny et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023), molecular dynam-
031   ics (Brown & Yamada, 2013; Yang et al., 2022), and atmospheric science (Pathak et al., 2022; Bi
032   et al., 2023), et al. Conventional numerical methods (Odibat & Baleanu, 2020), often rooted in
033
      rigorous partial differential equations (PDEs) (Long et al., 2018; Takamoto et al., 2022) and physical
      theory formula (Lippe et al., 2023), offer a robust foundation for modeling the dynamical evolution
034
      of complex systems. However, these methods are notoriously limited by their computational cost and
035
      sensitivity to different initial conditions and physical parameters.
036
037   Recently, numerous data-driven methods leveraging various neural network architectures have
038   been proposed to solve this problem. They are committed to design delicate spatial and temporal
039
      components (Lippe et al., 2023; Raonic et al., 2024) to capture high-dimensional non-linear dynamical
      patterns and latent data distribution. This paper focuses exclusively on scenarios with fixed data
040
      observation points. Mainstream approaches select different model architectures based on whether the
041
      data is arranged in a regular pattern. Specifically, for irregular grids or complex geometric boundaries,
042   graph neural networks (Kipf & Welling, 2017) are employed to capture intricate interactions between
043   nodes and even along the boundaries (Wang et al., 2024a).
044
045
      Despite their promising performance, the majority of these data-driven approaches considerably rely
      on a substantial volume of data and the assumption of distribution invariance (Wang et al., 2021;
046
      Yang et al., 2022). Formally, this dynamical system modeling task is still highly challenging in: (i)
047
      Lack of Physical Guidance. Some existing methods simply attach an external parameter embedding
048   module (Lakshmikantham, 2019; Gao et al., 2021) to the neural networks. However, such methods
049   struggle to capture higher-order correlations between physical parameters and data itself, and are
050   difficult to generalize to unseen parameter configurations (Rame et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024b). (ii)
051   Data Sparsity. Data acquisition is limited due to the high computational cost of traditional numerical
052   simulations (Schober et al., 2019) or the practical constraints on sensor usage in several real-world
053   scenarios. (iii) Out-of-distribution Generalization.Within the dynamical systems, there usually exist
      two types of distribution shifts, namely environmental distribution shift (Li et al., 2022; Song et al.,

086   Dynamical System Modeling. Deep neural networks have recently emerged as powerful tools for
087   tackling the challenges associated with dynamics forecasting (Gao et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2022),
088   showcasing their capabilities to efficiently model intricate, high-dimensional systems. To handle
089   complex spatiotemporal dependencies, various advanced models based on convolutional neural
090   networks (CNN) (Ren et al., 2022; Raonic et al., 2024), recurrent neural networks (RNN) (Mohajerin
091   & Waslander, 2019; Maulik et al., 2021), Transformer (Wu et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024), or
092   exquisite hybrid architectures (Shi et al., 2015; Wu et al., 2023b) have been proposed. Recently,
093
      Neural Operators (Li et al., 2021; Rahman et al., 2022; Tran et al., 2023) become a popular data-driven
      approach by learning to approximate the past-future infinite-dimensional function space mappings.
094
      Additionally, Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINN) (Raissi et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020)
095
      integrate prior physical knowledge as additional regularizers into the training process for physical
096   constraints, particularly in systems governed by partial differential equations. Moreover, most of
097   these approaches are limited to structured grids and lack the ability to handle irregular grids or varying
098   connectivity. Thus researchers have turned to graph neural networks (Fan et al., 2019) as a promising
099   alternative to accommodate a broader range of scenarios. GNNs inherit key physical properties from
100   geometric deep learning, including permutation invariance and spatial equivariance (Li et al., 2020;
101   Wu et al., 2022), which offer distinct advantages for modeling dynamical systems.
102   Out-of-distribution Generalization. Out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization (Liu et al., 2021a;
103   Hendrycks et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2023) has emerged as a critical challenge in machine learning,
104   especially for models that encounter distribution shifts between training and testing data. Signifi-
105   cant advancements have been made in OOD generalization techniques, containing invariant causal
106   inference (Gui et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024), data augmentation (Wang et al., 2024b), and domain
107   adaptation (Kundu et al., 2020; Garg et al., 2022), et al. Most existing work focuses on static scenar-
      ios (Li et al., 2022; Gui et al., 2023), with limited research on dynamical systems. These dynamic

136   Problem Definition. Given a dynamical system governed by physical laws such as PDEs, we
137   aim to enhance prediction using autoencoder reconstruction and discrete quantization. We have
138
      N observation points in the domain Ω, located at s = {s1 , · · · , sN }, where si ∈ Rds . At time
      step t, the observations are X t = {X1t , · · · , XNt }, where Xit ∈ Rd and d represents the number of
139
      observation channels. Boundary information and physical parameters affect the dynamical system,
140
      leading to different conditions and distribution shifts. We first employ reconstruction model and
141   construct a discrete memory bank to compress and store physical prior information. Then, given
142
      historical observation sequences {Xi−T0 +1:0 }N    i=1 , our goal is to use the pre-trained memory bank
143
      for data augmentation and predict future observations {Yi1:T }N      i=1 at each observation point.

      3.1       F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
147
      In this section, we systematically introduce our SPARK framework, as shown in Figure 2. We
148   first introduce a coupled reconstruction autoencoder, which incorporates boundary information and
149   physical parameters, to generate a discrete, physics-rich memory bank for compression. Subsequently,
150   in downstream task, we employ the pre-trained memory bank to augment the training samples based
151   on the fusion mechanism. We then utilize the attention mechanism to model historical observations
152   and propose a fourier-based graph ODE to realize downstream dynamical system prediction.
153

160   observations, it is important to construct the graph G = {V, E} with the finite discrete internal node.
161   Similar to other methods within spatial domian (Fan et al., 2019), we propose to select the K-nearest
      nodes for each node to construct the edge set E.

526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we propose SPARK, a novel physics-guided quantitative augmentation framework
529
      aimed at improving out-of-distribution generalization on dynamical system modeling. SPARK first
      construct a discrete memorybank for efficient augmentation by incorporating boundary information
530
      and physical parameters through a vector quantization-based compression mechanism. Additionally,
531
      SPARK integrates a fourier-enhanced graph ODE for efficient and precise system prediction, further
532   addressing temporal distribution shifts. Experimental results on both real-world and synthetic datasets
533   demonstrate that SPARK outperforms existing methods under distribution shifts.
