009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We explore the internal mechanisms of how bias emerges in large language models
012            (LLMs) when provided with ambiguous comparative prompts: inputs that com-
013            pare or enforce choosing between two or more entities without providing clear
014            context for preference. Most approaches for bias mitigation focus on either post-
015            hoc analysis or data augmentation. However, these are transient solutions, without
016            addressing the root cause: the model itself. Numerous prior works show the influ-
017            ence of the attention module towards steering generations. We believe that analyz-
018
               ing attention is also crucial for understanding bias, as it provides insight into how
               the LLM distributes its focus across different entities and how this contributes to
019
               biased decisions. To this end, we first introduce a metric to quantify the LLM’s
020
               preference for one entity over another. We then propose ATLAS (Attention-based
021            Targeted Layer Analysis and Scaling), a technique to localize bias to specific lay-
022            ers of the LLM by analyzing attention scores and then reduce bias by scaling
023            attention in these biased layers. To evaluate our method, we conduct experiments
024            across 3 datasets (BBQ, Crows-Pairs, and WinoGender) using GPT-2 XL (1.5B),
025            GPT-J (6B), LLaMA-2 (7B) and LLaMA-3 (8B). Our experiments demonstrate
026            that bias is concentrated in the later layers, typically around the last third. We also
027            show how ATLAS effectively mitigates bias through targeted interventions without
028            compromising downstream performance and an average increase of only 0.34%
029
               in perplexity when the intervention is applied. We see an average improvement of
               0.28 points in the bias score across all the datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has enabled AI to perform increas-
035
      ingly complex tasks (Brown et al., 2020). Despite these advancements, LLMs often generate bi-
036   ased content, particularly when confronted with ambiguous prompts that require nuanced decision-
037   making (Gallegos et al., 2024). Bias in models can manifest in various forms which do not always
038   involve harmful language: reinforcing societal stereotypes (Caliskan et al., 2017b), displaying gen-
039   der bias (Bolukbasi et al., 2016), or demonstrating preferential treatment towards specific demo-
040   graphic groups (Gupta et al., 2023). This has led to growing concerns about the ethical implications
041   of deploying such LLMs, especially when their outputs affect sensitive domains like hiring, legal
042   advice, or healthcare (An et al., 2024). These manifestations of bias, where explicit harmful lan-
043   guage is not part of the picture, are arguably also most difficult to mitigate because commonly used
044
      mitigations such as post-inference content filters and guards (Inan et al., 2023) are not applicable.
045   To enable more reliable deployment, one must localize and minimize bias in these LLMs. However,
046   this is non-trivial. First, if one is to believe that data is the “only” cause, naively sanitizing data
047   may not only be difficult to execute, but could also inadvertently degrade downstream model perfor-
048   mance. Second, bias in LLMs is highly context-dependent (Sclar et al., 2024); it varies based on the
049   input prompt, which makes the mitigation process more complex, necessitating a prompt-dependent
050
      approach for mitigation. Third, bias is model-dependent: it is entangled within the multi-layered
      structure of the model, and training algorithms used will influence how bias manifests.
051
052   The attention module (Vaswani et al., 2017) governs how most modern LLMs assign importance
053   to different parts of the input. We conjecture that attention can also shed light on how bias
      is embedded in LLMs, in the way models internally distributes attention between competing

144   3   B IAS IN A C OMPARATIVE P ROMPT F RAMEWORK
145
146   What is the bias we are referring to? Bias in LLMs manifests when they demonstrate preferen-
147   tial implicit treatment or assumptions towards certain groups or entities, often reinforcing societal
148   stereotypes or exhibiting disparate performance across different demographic sub-groups (Faisal &
149   Anastasopoulos, 2022; Gupta et al., 2023).
150   How have we minimized/mitigated bias thus far? Some methods often focus on classifying out-
151   puts as either biased or unbiased, but such a binary view overlooks the complexity and subtleties in
152   LLM decision-making and typically requires a post-hoc classifier (which requires additional over-
153   heads to train) (Ruggeri et al., 2023). To capture nuances associated with bias, it is necessary to go
154   beyond this. Although one could attempt to probe the LLM’s outputs to evaluate bias, such probing
155   fails to faithfully represent the internal decision-making mechanisms at play (Turpin et al., 2024).
156   To better understand and address bias, we need to investigate the internal mechanisms and processes
157   of the LLM. The attention weights are particularly important (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023), as they
158
      serve as measurable signals for how much importance the model assigns to different entities, which
      can play a critical role in bias formation during generation.
159
160   In what setting are we going to focus on? We focus on comparative prompts (Parrish et al., 2022;
161   Nangia et al., 2020; Rudinger et al., 2018) where models are required to make a choice or express
      preference towards a decision that may favor or otherwise stereotype specific groups. To elaborate,


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      these prompts involve a situation or context that mentions two entities, followed by a question that
163   asks the LLM to choose between them. We believe this setting is both interesting and natural to
164   study. It is natural as it occurs in many real-world applications, where ambiguity is present due to
165   limited context, making it challenging to determine the “right” response. It is interesting, as this type
166   of bias does not result in harmful outputs generated by the model. By studying these comparative
167   decisions, we can gain insight into the internal biases of the LLM as it makes nuanced choices
168   between competing entities. More formally:
169   Definition 1 [Comparative Prompt Framework]: A comparative prompt is characterized by a situ-
170   ation or context (C) involving two entities. Following this context is a query (Q) that prompts the
171   LLM to make a decision between these two entities. The prompt (P) is defined as the concatenation
172   of the context and the question, represented as P = C ⊕ Q; P contains T tokens spanning the entire
173   prompt (including the context C and the query Q). This allows the LLM to understand and evaluate
174   both the situation and the entities in question. The two entities involved in the context are referred to
175   as candidate entities2 (C1 , C2 ), and the LLM (M) is expected to choose between these candidates
176   as the answer to the question. An example is provided below, where the candidates are underlined.
177
            Example Prompt:
178
179         Context (C): I saw a grandson and their grandfather last week outside the Walmart trying
180         to book a cab on Uber.
181         Question (Q): Who was not comfortable using the phone?
182
183   In the context of the comparative prompt framework, we introduce a metric called the bias ratio
184   to quantify how strongly the model prefers one entity over the other. Since the prompt explicitly
185   mentions two entities, the model can generate responses which select either one. Ideally, an unbiased
186   model should generate responses with equal likelihood for both, but biases in training data or internal
187   model behavior may lead to skewed probabilities favoring one entity disproportionately. The bias
188   ratio captures this imbalance by comparing the probabilities assigned to each entity, helping to
189   measure how far the model’s output deviates from a neutral decision.
190   Definition 2 [Bias Ratio]: The bias ratio (b) measures the relative probabilities (refer to Equation 1)
191   assigned to the two candidate entities in the LLM’s output. Formally, it is defined as:
192
                                                          PrM (C1 | P)
193                                                  b=                >1
194                                                       PrM (C2 | P)
195   where PrM (Cs | P) is the probability of selecting entity Cs given the prompt P. Note that b > 1
196   as we assume that candidate C1 is generated by the model (i.e., the higher probability candidate).
197
      An ideal, debiased model in this framework would yield b ≈ 1, indicating that the LLM assigns
198
      (near) equal probabilities to both candidates where decisions are being made purely based on context
199
      and question without favoring one entity over the other due to underlying biases.

326   Determining the Scaling Factor: The scaling factor λ is crucial for adjusting the attention scores
327   without over-penalizing the model’s focus on the higher-probability entity. For each layer, we de-
328
      termine λ by testing values within the range λ ∈ (0, 1], decreasing λ from 1 to 0.01 (at intervals of

329

330
      We stop the greedy search when b starts increasing with respect to the scaling factor applied in the
331   previous iteration. Please refer to Figure 6 in Appendix C.3 to visualize this effect.

333   Since ATLAS requires applying the scaling intervention “layer by layer” across the top-k biased
334
      layers (k = 3 in our experiments), we starting with the layer that exhibits the highest degree of bias.

335
      scaling factor is identified (and applied) for the most biased layer, we recompute the top-(k − 1)
336
      layers (by excluding the layer just edited), and repeat this process. This allows us to decrease the
337   search space from 11k values to k × 11 values.
338

340
      rather than being globally fixed. This prevents overfitting to a specific prompt distribution and allows

      7   R ELATED W ORK
499
500   Localization: Causal methods have been used to analyze model internals and address biases by in-
501   tervening directly on model processing components. Techniques such as neuron ablations (Lakretz
502   et al., 2019; Mohebbi et al., 2023) and replacing activations with baseline or alternative activa-
503   tions (Vaswani et al., 2017; Geiger et al., 2024) offer insights into the causal mechanisms behind
504
      model behavior. However, Meng et al. (2024) and Hase et al. (2024) show that localization methods
      should be carefully validated, as causal interventions may not always lead to predictive success.
505
506   Mitigation Strategies via Representation Editing: While hard-debias techniques (Bolukbasi et al.,
507   2016; Ravfogel et al., 2020) aimed to remove biases by modifying embedding spaces, more recent
508   approaches such as LEACE (Belrose et al., 2024) and DiffMask (De Cao et al., 2020) focus on run-
509   time activation changes. These methods effectively reduce only gender bias by making alterations to
510
      the model’s internal representations. Mitigations in word embeddings has also been a major focus,
      given their prevalence in NLP tasks (Caliskan et al., 2017a; Manzini et al., 2019). In contrast, our
511
      work addresses biases in transformer models, specifically targeting attention layers that contribute
512
      to biased decision-making rather than modifying static embeddings.
513
514   Activation Steering: Recent work on activation steering aims to dynamically influence model be-
515   havior during runtime by steering the activation space of LLMs. For instance, Turner et al. (2024)
516
      introduced the concept of “activation addition”, which steers model outputs by adding specific ac-
      tivation vectors. Arditi et al. (2024) demonstrated that specific directions in the activation space
517
      mediate refusal behaviors in LLMs, providing a potential avenue for bias mitigation. Similarly, Pan-
518
      ickssery et al. (2024) uses contrastive activation addition to steer models like Llama 2 by adjusting
519   internal activations post-hoc.
520
521   Sparse Autoencoders: Cunningham et al. (2023) has demonstrated that sparse autoencoders can
522
      capture interpretable features in LLMs, providing a pathway for targeting specific biases. Work
      on principled evaluation of these sparse autoencoders for interpretability (Makelov et al., 2024)
523
      further highlights their potential for gaining control over model behaviour. These autoencoders
524
      could potentially be used for interpretable mitigation of bias in future work.
525
526   8   C ONCLUSIONS
527
      In this paper, we provide a two-step approach, ATLAS, for identifying and mitigating bias in LLMs
528   when responding to ambiguous comparative prompts. To capture bias in this framework, we first
529   define the bias ratio (and the exponential bias score) metric. By analyzing attention distributions,
530   ATLAS can localize biased entity information to specific layers of the model. ATLAS systematically
531   reduces bias by scaling attention scores in these layers without degrading model performance. Ex-
532   perimental results highlight the efficacy of this approach. However, it is not without limitations.
533   ATLAS is designed for the comparative prompting framework with two entities. Determining the
534   scaling factor requires many inference calls, proportional to the number of layers being edited.
535   Given the computational costs associated with the experiments, we are unable to perform every
536
      experiment discussed with all models.
537   Disclaimer: Each of the datasets we use have their own framework for measuring bias and these
538   measures do not perfectly align with our end goal of reducing the bias ratio (especially since we
539   perform edits to the prompt formats in these datasets before utilizing them). Thus we proposed the
      above metric to unify and compare scores across all these different datasets for various models.

      C.1    M ORE DETAILS ABOUT ATTENTION L OCALIZATION
848
849   Cost of the Approach: This method of localizing bias by analyzing attention scores involves one
850   inference pass. During this pass, the generation is used to identify the higher probability candidate
851   Ci∗ while also collecting the attention scores at every layer. This allows us to calculate ᾱ(ℓ) (Ci∗ ),
852   and identify the top-k bias-contributing layers without requiring any additional forward passes.
853
      Another popular method to localize information in LLMs is using causal-tracing (Meng et al., 2024).
854   This approach involves several runs with corrupted and restored activations across each node in each
855   layer of the model (one inference pass is needed per node during restoration phase) to first localize
856   information. In contrast, our methodology only requires access to the attention scores at the last
857   token, thus making the computational costs substantially lower compared to causal-tracing methods.
858
859   C.2    M ORE DETAILS ABOUT THE APPLIED INTERVENTION
860
861   Why Scaling? We chose to scale attention scores rather than introducing random perturbations or
862   other (drastic) modifications to preserve the model’s internal decision-making integrity (evaluated
863   in § 6.3). This intervention is straightforward, and works by reducing the representation of the can-
      didate that is over-represented or assigned a higher probability by the model and doing so directly


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      reduces the bias ratio. Another key advantage of scaling is that it does not require access to the
865   model’s weights, specifically the Q, K, and V matrices. Instead, we only need access to the atten-
866   tion scores matrix A(ℓ,h) , making ATLAS easier to implement and less intrusive (in terms of model
867   modifications). Finally, scaling also has the advantage of being computationally inexpensive.
