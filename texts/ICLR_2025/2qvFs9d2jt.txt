011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Non-linear activation functions are well known to improve the expressivity of
014            neural networks, which is the main reason of their wide implementation in neural
015            networks. In this work, we showcase a new and interesting property of certain
016            non-linear activations, focusing on the most popular example of its kind – Rectified
017            Linear Unit (ReLU). By comparing the cases with and without this non-linear
018            activation, we show that the ReLU has the following effects: (a) better data
019            separation, i.e., a larger angle separation for similar data in the feature space of
020
               model gradient, and (b) better NTK conditioning, i.e., a smaller condition number
               of neural tangent kernel (NTK). Furthermore, we show that the ReLU network
021
               depth (i.e., with more ReLU activation operations) further magnifies these effects.
022
               Note that, without the non-linear activation, i.e., in a linear neural network, the
023            data separation and NTK condition number always remain the same as in the case
024            of a linear model, regardless of the network depth. Our results imply that ReLU
025            activation, as well as the depth of ReLU network, helps improve the worst-case
026            convergence rate of GD, which is closely related to the NTK condition number.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Non-linear activation functions, such as rectified linear unit (ReLU), are well known for their ability to
033   increase the expressivity of neural networks. A non-linearly activated neural network can approximate
034   any continuous function to arbitrary precision, as long as there are enough neurons in the hidden
035   layers (Hornik et al., 1989; Cybenko, 1989; Hanin & Sellke, 2017), while its linear counterpart –
036   linear neural network, which has no non-linear activation functions applied, can only represent linear
037   functions of the input. In addition, deeper neural networks, which have more non-linearly activated
038
      layers, have exponentially greater expressivity than shallower ones (Telgarsky, 2015; Poole et al.,
      2016; Raghu et al., 2017; Montufar et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2018), indicating that the network depth
039
      promotes the power of non-linear activation functions.
040
041   A natural question is: Does the non-linear activation have other beneficial effects (especially on
042   optimization), in addition to increasing the expressivity? Our answer is yes!
043   In this paper, we showcase a new and interesting property of certain non-linear activations, focusing
044   on the ReLU instance: the ReLU non-linearity improves data separation in the feature space of model
045   gradient, and helps to decrease the condition number of neural tangent kernel (NTK). We also show
046   that the depth of the ReLU network further magnifies these effects, namely, a deeper ReLU activated
047   neural network has a better data separation and a smaller NTK condition number, than a shallower
048   one.
049   Specifically, we first show the better separation phenomenon, i.e., the improved data separation for
050   similar data in the model gradient feature space. We prove that, for an infinitely wide ReLU network
051   f at its random initialization, any pair of data input vectors x and z that have similar directions (i.e.,
052   small but non-zero angle θin between x and z) become more directionally separated in the model
053   gradient space (i.e., model gradient angle ϕ between ∇f (x) and ∇f (z) is larger than θin ). We also
      find that deeper ReLU networks result in even better data separation, i.e., larger ϕ.

054
      We further show the better NTK conditioning property of ReLU, i.e., smaller NTK condition number.
055   First, we prove that, as a consequence of the better data separation, the NTK condition number of a
056   infinitely wide ReLU network is strictly smaller than that of the Gram matrix, if the dataset contains
057   two non-degenerate samples. Moreover, as the ReLU network depth increases, the NTK condition
058   number monotonically decreases. Then, we remove this data size assumption on two-layer ReLU
059   networks, and prove the same better NTK conditioning, regardless of the data size as long as the
060   dataset is not degenerated. The intuition is that, if there exists a pair of similar inputs x and z in the
061   training set (i.e., the angle between x and z is small), which is usually the case for large datasets, then
062   the Gram matrix and NTK of linear neural networks must have close-to-zero smallest eigenvalues,
063
      resulting in extremely large NTK condition numbers. The ReLU activation make these similar data
      more separated (enlarges the small angles between data), hence it helps to increase the smallest
064
      eigenvalues of NTK, which in turn leads to a smaller NTK condition number.
065
066   Note that, when the non-linear activation is absent, as in an infinitely wide linear neural network f¯
067   of any finite depth, the model gradient angle ϕ̄ is always equivalent to the input angle θin , and the
068   NTK condition number κ̄ also remains identical to κ0 of the Gram matrix. With this comparison, we
069
      conclude that the better separation phenomenon, i.e., ϕ > θin , and the better NTK conditioning, i.e.,
      κ < κ0 , observed for ReLU networks, are attributed to the non-linear activation.
070
071   We experimentally verify these findings on finite but wide neural networks. It also suggests that these
072   results hold for finite networks.
073   Condition number and optimization theory. Recent optimization theories showed that the NTK
074   condition number, or the smallest eigenvalue of NTK, controls the theoretical convergence rate
075   of gradient descent algorithms on wide neural networks (Du et al., 2018; 2019; Liu et al., 2022).
076   Combined with these theories, our findings imply that: (a), the ReLU activation function helps
077   improving the worst-case convergence rate of gradient descent, and (b), deeper wide ReLU networks
078   have faster convergence rate than shallower ones. Experimentally, we indeed find that deeper ReLU
079   networks converges faster than shallower ones.
080   In this paper, we focus on the special case of ReLU, the most commonly used non-linear activation
081   function. It remains theoretically an open question what are the effects of other non-linear activations
082   on NTK conditioning and theoretical convergence rates. While it need different analysis techniques
083   and we would like to leave it as a future work, we provide some preliminary numerical results in
084   Appendix F. It suggests that the non-linear activation effect on the NTK conditioning can be positive
085   (decreasing κ, as for tanh) or negative (increasing κ, as for sigmoid). It is worth to note that, in either
086   case, a larger network depth, where more non-linear activation are operated, magnifies the effect.
087
088   Contributions. We summarize our contributions below. We find that:
089
      • the ReLU non-linearity induces better separation between similar data in the feature space of model
090
        gradient. A larger depth of the ReLU network magnifies this better separation phenomenon.
091
092   • ReLU non-linearity has the effect of decreasing the condition number of the NTK matrix. A larger
093
        depth of the ReLU network further enhances this better NTK conditioning property.
094   • This better NTK conditioning property leads to faster convergence rate of gradient descent. We
095     empirically verify this on various real world datasets.

      1.1   R ELATED WORK
103
104   Studying a specific type of non-linear activation function, especially ReLU, is a common setting in
105   the literature. This is largely due to the fact that ReLU has emerged to be the dominant choice of
106   activation functions in neural networks used in practice, since Nair & Hinton (2010); Krizhevsky
107   et al. (2012). ReLU activated neural networks have received wide research attention, ranging from
      optimization (Li & Yuan, 2017; Du et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2020), expressivity (Hanin & Sellke, 2017;


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Yarotsky, 2017; Wang et al., 2018), generalization (Zheng et al., 2019; Ji & Telgarsky, 2019; Cao &
109   Gu, 2020), etc.
110
111   NTK and its spectrum have been extensively studied (Lee et al., 2019; Bietti & Mairal, 2019; Liu
112
      et al., 2020; Fan & Wang, 2020; Geifman et al., 2020; Xiao et al., 2020; Nguyen et al., 2021; Belfer
      et al., 2021; Chen & Xu, 2021), since the discovery of constant NTK for infinitely wide neural
113
      networks (Jacot et al., 2018). Velikanov & Yarotsky (2021) shows that the NTK spectrum of an
114
      infinitely wide ReLU network asymptotically exhibits a power law. Its distribution is further shown
115   to be similar to that of Laplace kernel (Geifman et al., 2020; Chen & Xu, 2021), and can be computed
116   (Fan & Wang, 2020). Nguyen et al. (2021) analyzed the upper and lower bounds for the smallest NTK
117   eigenvalue in O() and Ω(), respectively. With the assumption of spherically uniformly distributed
118   data where the spectrum of (elementary-wise) power of the Gram matrix becomes simplified, Murray
119   et al. (2023), utilizing Hermite polynomials and power series expansion of NTK, provides the order
120   of the smallest eigenvalue of the NTK of two-layer ReLU network in the infinite width limit. Under
121   the same data setting, Basri et al. (2019) computed the NTK eigenvalues for the two-layer ReLU
122   network. Relying on the values of off-diagonal entries of the NTK matrix in the infinite depth limit,
123
      another work Xiao et al. (2020) analyzed the asymptotic dependence of the NTK condition number on
      the network depth L for ReLU networks, which shows a decreasing trend as L increases, consistent
124
      with our result.
125
126   In contrast to prior works, we are able to distill the effect of ReLU activation function via a sharp
127   comparison between scenarios with and without ReLU, at any finite depth without data distribution
128   assumption. Note that, without an assumption on data distribution, NTK spectral analysis becomes
129
      much harder and many data-distribution-dependent results may not hold any more. Moreover, at finite
      depth, off-diagonal entries of the NTK matrix has not converged and are typically quite different
130
      from its infinite depth limit, which makes analysis even harder.
131
132   We are aware of a prior work (Arora et al., 2018) which has results of similar flavor. It shows that the
133   depth of a linear neural network may help to accelerate optimization via an implicit pre-conditioning
134   of gradient descent. We note that this prior work is in an orthogonal direction, as its analysis is based
135
      on the linear neural network, which is activation-free, while our work focus on the better-conditioning
      effect of ReLU activation function.

      2.1   L INEAR NEURAL NETWORK : THE BASELINE FOR COMPARISON
217
218   To distill the effect of the non-linear activation function, we need a activation-free case as the baseline
219   for comparison. This baseline is the linear neural network f¯, with the same width and depth as f .
220   Theorem 2.2. Consider the linear neural network f¯ as defined in Eq.(5). In the limit of infinite
221   network width m → ∞ and at network initialization w0 , the following relations hold:
222
223          • for any input x ∈ Rd : ∥ᾱ(l) (x)∥ = ∥x∥, ∀l ∈ [L]; and ∥∇f (w0 ; x)∥ = (L + 1)∥x∥.
224
225
             • for any inputs x, z ∈ Rd : θ̄(l) (x, z) = θin (x, z), ∀l ∈ [L]; and ϕ̄(x, z) = θin (x, z).
226
      This theorem states that, without a non-linear activation function, both the feature embedding maps
227
      α(l) : x 7→ α(l) (x) and the model gradient map ∇f : x 7→ ∇f (x) fail to change the geometrical
228
      relationship between any data samples. For any input pairs, the embedding angles θ̄(l) and ϕ̄ remain
229
      the same as the input angle θin . Therefore, it is not surprising that the NTK of a linear network is the
230   same as the Gram matrix (up to a constant factor), as formally stated in the following corollary.
231
      Corollary 2.3 (NTK condition number of linear networks). Consider a linear neural network f¯ as
232
      defined in Eq.(5). In the limit of infinite network width m → ∞ and at network initialization, the
233
      NTK matrix K̄ = (L + 1)2 G. Moreover, κ̄ = κ0 .
234
235   This corollary tells that, for a linear neural network, regardless of its depth L, the NTK condition
236   number κ̄ is always equal to the condition number κ0 of the Gram matrix G. Therefore, any non-zero
237   deviations, δϕ ≜ ϕ − θin from the input angle θin , and δκ ≜ κ − κ0 from the Gram condition
238   number κ0 , observed for a non-linearly activated network f , should be attributed to the corresponding
239   non-linear activation.

343   4    R E LU INDUCES SMALLER NTK CONDITION NUMBER OF NTK
344
345   In this section, we show both theoretically and experimentally that, the ReLU non-linearity induces a
346   decrease in the NTK condition number κ. Moreover, a ReLU network with larger depth L, which
347
      means more non-linear activations in operation, the NTK condition number κ is generically smaller.
348
349   Connection between condition number and model gradient angle. The smallest eigen-
350   value and condition number of NTK are closely related to the smallest model gradient angle
351
      mini,j∈[n] ϕ(xi , xj ), through the gradient feature matrix F . Think about the case if ϕ(xi , xj ) = 0
352
      (i.e., ∇f (xi ) is parallel to ∇f (xj )) for some i, j ∈ [n], then F , hence NTK K, is not full rank and
      the smallest eigenvalue λmin (K) is zero, leading to an infinite condition number κ. Similarly, if
353
      mini,j∈[n] ϕ(xi , xj ) is small, the smallest eigenvalue λmin (K) is also small, and condition number
354
      κ is large, as stated in the following proposition (see proof in Appendix B).

356   Proposition 4.1. Consider a n × n positive definite matrix A = BB T , where matrix B ∈ Rn×d ,
357
      with d > n, is of full row rank. Suppose that there exist i, j ∈ [n] such that the angle ϕ between
      vectors Bi· and Bj· is small, i.e., ϕ ≪ 1, and that there exist constant C > c > 0 such that
358
      c ≤ ∥Bk· ∥ ≤ C for all k ∈ [n]. Then, the smallest eigenvalue λmin (A) = O(ϕ2 ), and the condition
359
      number κ = Ω(1/ϕ2 ).
360
361   Therefore, a good data angle separation in the model gradient features, i.e., mini,j∈[n] ϕ(xi , xj ) not
362   too small, is a necessary condition such that the condition number κ is not too large. As is shown in
363   the last section, the ReLU non-linearity makes the samples more separated when mapped from the
364   input data space to the model gradient feature space. Hence, it is expected that the NTK condition
365   number will decrease in the presence of the ReLU non-linearity.
366
367   Smaller NTK condition number. Theoretically, we consider the infinite width limit. We require
368   that the dataset is not degenerated, i.e., xi ∦ xj for all i, j. This is a mild and commonly used setting
369   in the literature, see for example Du et al. (2018). We require that the first layer weights W (1) be
370   trainable and fix the other layers in the following theorem. This is also a common setting in literature
371   to simplify the analysis Du et al. (2018).
372   Theorem 4.2. Consider the ReLU network in Eq.(38) in the limit m → ∞ and at initialization. Let

377   scenario (b): κa < κb . Moreover, for two ReLU neural networks f1 of depth L1 and f2 of depth L2
