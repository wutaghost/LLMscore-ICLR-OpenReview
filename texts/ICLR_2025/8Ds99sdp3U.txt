009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Existing transformer-based video-moment retrieval models achieve sub-optimal
012
               performance when using the pretrain-finetuning learning paradigm – a pretrained
013
               multimodal encoder is finetuned using the target training data. While current work
014            has explored different model architectures and training paradigms to explore this
015            problem, the problem of data dilemma has been under addressed. Specifically,
016            there exists high diversity of how semantic is captured in textual query and the
017            training dataset only consist of limited moment-query pairs for the highly di-
018            verse moments. This work addresses this problem with a novel nested adaptor
019            and a LLM-driven semantic data generation pipeline. First, a LLM-driven data
020            augmentation generates queries that are semantically similar to the ground truth,
021            which enrich the semantic boundary captured by textual query. We empirically
022
               analyze the effectiveness of data augmentation, and proposed a simple yet effec-
               tive quality measure to retain high quality samples. Second, we propose a novel
023
               nested adapter that utilises both augmented queries and human annotated queries
024
               for model coarse-tuning and fine-tuning, respectively. By combining semantic
025            perturbation with domain adaptation, our approach addresses the variability in
026            video content while capturing nuanced features more effectively. Experimental
027            results on various baseline models show the efficacy of our proposed approach.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The proliferation of digital devices and platforms has led to a significant increase in the quantity
034   and variety of videos. Due to its inherent complexity and richer semantic information compared to
035   images and text, the demand for video understanding is on the rise Zhang et al. (2023). Recently, the
036   novel and challenging tasks of video moment retrieval (MR) and highlight detection (HD) Lei et al.
037   (2021) have attracted significant attention Ma et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2023); Lin
038   et al. (2023a); Jang et al. (2023); Moon et al. (2023b;a). These tasks involve localizing specific mo-
039
      ments within videos and determining the corresponding importance scores based on user-provided
      queries. An inherent challenge in these tasks is the need to efficiently retrieve desired moments from
040
      a vast array of videos and to navigate videos according to user preferences effectively. Additionally,
041
      it requires not only capturing temporal intra-modal context but also aligned cross-modal interaction
042   of visual and textual features which makes it more challenging and non-trivial.
043
044   Recent studies Yan et al. (2020); Ling et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2022) in image retrieval tasks
045
      have achieved efficient and effective retrieval of specific items from a large dataset. These approach
      generally utilizes a pretrain-finetune paradigm Liu et al. (2021a); Tang et al. (2024) – pre-training
046
      models on large datasets followed by fine-tuning on task-specific data – which has become a bench-
047
      mark in training high-performance models. Initially popularized by architectures like ResNet He
048   et al. (2016), this approach has continued to yield impressive results with the advent of visual trans-
049   formers, such as ViT Liu et al. (2023); Khan et al. (2022); Tan et al. (2021); Bazi et al. (2021). The
050   visual transformers leverage self-attention mechanisms to capture global dependencies and relation-
051   ships within images, allowing them to effectively encode complex visual information. Notably, even
052   with limited training data on target task, visual transformers have shown strong performance for im-
053   age classification and retrieval tasks Lanchantin et al. (2021); Song et al. (2023), indicating their
      robustness and generalization ability across different datasets and tasks.

054
      However, applying the pretrain-finetune paradigm for video-based moment retrieval tasks faces sig-
055   nificant challenges Selva et al. (2023); Radford et al. (2021). The complexity of video data, as well
056   as the rich and diverse natural language query, makes it difficult to train a discriminative model using
057   conventional methods. We argue that one underestimated factor in training model for video MR and
058   HD lies in the data dilemma. First, there’s a substantial domain gap between the pre-training dataset
059   and the target dataset. For example, the performance of a CLIP pretrained model is 70.97% lower
060   than current state-of-the-art Moon et al. (2023b) for mAP@0.5. Second, the existing training dataset
061   of video MR tasks is limited, where the available videos are sparse w.r.t. the potential diversity of
062   unseen videos. This can lead to a reduction in the discriminative power of the pretrained model
063
      during fine-tuning stage Jaiswal et al. (2024). Third, each moment is labelled with a single natural
      language query, yet, there exists multiple variations of how semantic is captured in textual query.
064
065   To address the challenges inherent in video MR, we propose a novel approach that leverages both
066   data augmentation and adaptive learning techniques to develop better aligned encoders. A central
067   difficulty in this task arises from the rich and diverse content in videos, where a single segment
068   can be described in multiple ways, depending on the focus of the caption. A single query often
069
      fails to capture the full semantic diversity of the content. To overcome this limitation, we employ
      data augmentation with large language models (LLMs) to generate varied, semantically coherent
070
      rephrasings of queries. This process expands the diversity of the training set and enables the model
071
      to generalize more effectively across different formulations of the same query, enhancing retrieval
072   performance.
073
074   In conjunction with data augmentation, we utilize learnable adapters to enhance both fine-tuning
075
      and coarse-tuning. The standard fine-tuning approach adapts the model to the target dataset based
      on the original queries. In contrast, coarse-tuning involves using Nested Adapters (Sec. 3.4), which
076
      are trained with augmented queries, allowing the model to better capture the broad semantic space
077
      of queries while mitigating the effect of noisy augmentations. Our approach is model-agnostic,
078   i.e., it can be applied across various architectures without being tied to a specific model, further
079   enhancing its adaptability. This tuning strategy enables the model to retain its discriminative power
080   while making it more adaptable to the diverse characteristics of the video data. By integrating
081   both data augmentation and adapter-based learning, we aim to enhance the model’s generalization
082   capability, making it more effective in real-world moment retrieval applications. We identify that
083   the core challenge of the MR task lies in the data dilemma, specifically (1) the domain gap between
084   the pretrained model and the target dataset, (2) the sparse and limited availability of fine-tuning
085   samples, and (3) the reliance on a single natural language query, which limits the generalizability of
086
      the fine-tuned model.
087   Our key contributions in this work are as follows:
088
089          • To address the aforementioned challenges, we introduce a novel integration of LLM-based
090            data augmentation and learnable adapters for domain adaptation. Our approach combines
091
               LLM-driven query diversity with cross-modal refinement, using adapters to bridge the do-
               main gap and enrich video representations.
092
093          • Specifically, we utilize data augmentation to diversify the training set and improve gener-
094            alization capabilities of the model. In addition, we propose a Nested Adapter that uses se-
095            mantic data augmentation for additional model coarse-tuning during the model fine-tuning
096
               process.
097          • Through extensive experiments and ablation studies, we empirically demonstrate the effec-
098            tiveness of our model-agnostic approach, which can be applied across a variety of baseline
099            architectures, resulting in improved performance across multiple MR baselines without be-
100            ing tied to any specific model.

104   2.1   V ISUAL UNDERSTANDING OF F OUNDATION M ODELS
105
106   The evolution of foundation models, as outlined in studies such as Li et al. (2022), has indeed led to
107   significant advancements in various tasks within computer vision and natural language processing.
      However, while these models have demonstrated remarkable performance on single modalities like

108
      text or images, they often struggle with understanding videos and handling multiple modalities ef-
109   fectively, as noted in studies such as Bazi et al. (2021); Song et al. (2023); Radford et al. (2021); Li
110   et al. (2023).
111
112
      Foundation large language models (LLMs) such as GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer), as
      highlighted in Shen et al. (2024), are primarily designed for tasks related to language generation,
113
      comprehension, and text classification. However, MR involves retrieving specific segments or mo-
114
      ments from multimedia data, particularly videos, based on natural language queries. This task re-
115   quires understanding both the textual queries and the visual content of the videos, which may be
116   beyond the capabilities of LLMs. One of the challenges in adapting LLMs for MR tasks lies in the
117   differences in data format and task requirements.
118
      Moment retrieval tasks Lei et al. (2021) often deal with sparse training data, and the nature of the data
119
      may be different from the corpora on which LLMs were originally trained. Simply fine-tuning LLMs
120
      Liu et al. (2024) on moment retrieval data may not be sufficient to achieve optimal performance,
121   as these models may not capture the nuances of video understanding and cross-modal reasoning.
122   Therefore, there is a need for specialized approaches that can effectively leverage both textual and
123   visual information for moment retrieval tasks. These approaches may involve combining LLMs with
124   specialized models for video understanding, employing techniques such as multi-modal fusion Li
125   et al. (2023), cross-modal attention mechanisms He et al. (2021a), and task-specific adaptations Liu
126   et al. (2021b). By integrating these approaches, it may be possible to enhance the performance of
127   LLMs in tasks involving videos and multiple modalities.

130   2.2   V IDEO M OMENT R ETRIEVAL
131
132   MR is the task of localizing the moment relevant to the given text description. Different from the
133   MR, HD aims to measure the clipwise importance level of the given video.
134   Traditional approaches like Moment context network (MCN) Anne Hendricks et al. (2017) em-
135   ployed a two-stage ”proposal-rank” pipeline, which involved handcrafted predefined proposals.
136   However, this approach often resulted in redundant computations due to dense proposals and a
137   large number of negative samples. In contrast to traditional approaches, proposal-free methods like
138   LPNet Xiao et al. (2021) directly regress start and end boundaries or probabilities without relying
139   on predefined proposals. This direct approach typically results in faster processing times since it
140   avoids the need for proposal generation.
141   Another category of methods, known as proposal-learnable methods, utilizes networks like adaptive
142   proposal generative network (APGN) Liu et al. (2021b) to adaptively predict video segments. These
143   methods demonstrate improved performance compared to proposal-free methods by dynamically
144   generating proposals based on the input data. However, they may suffer from location bias which
145   can limit their generalization ability to diverse datasets or scenarios.
146   Due to the similarity between MR and HD tasks based on queries, as well as the commonality in their
147   methods involving multi-modal feature extraction and feature interaction, some studies have focused
148   on designing various multi-task networks for joint MR and HD. In Moment-DETR Lei et al. (2021)
149   introduced the QVHighlights dataset and modifies detection transformer (DETR) based model to
150   handle the MR and HD jointly. Various research efforts Anne Hendricks et al. (2017); Ma et al.
151   (2023) were put into the search for the requested moments in the video and summarizing the video
152   highlights. However, these models still struggle with semantic of text query and relevance matching
153
      between text query and video content. They rely on separate modeling of visual and textual features,
      lacking deep integration between these two modalities. Recent approaches Moon et al. (2023b) have
154
      shown that deploying the cross-attention mechanism of transformer architecture is more effective to
155
      fuse the text query into the video representation. UMT Liu et al. (2022) proposed transformer based
156   architectures to take multimodal sources, e.g., video and audio. However, it removed the moment
157   decoder and bipartite matching, resulting in inferior performance on MR. Additionally, the potential
158   of LLMs, a naturally powerful textual transformer decoder, remains unexplored in the MR task.
159   MH-DETR Xu et al. (2023) incorporates a pooling operation into the encoder and integrates a cross-
160   modality interaction module to fuse visual and query features. Despite these advancements, the
161   existing methods still suffers from the limited data availability in the target dataset and multi-modal
      feature extraction and feature interaction.

      2.3   M ULTI - MODAL A LIGNMENT
163
164   Recently, there has been increasing interest in the multimodal computing field in developing con-
165   trastive losses to capture the interdependence across different modalities Wang et al. (2016). Most
166   of these multimodal strategies Wu et al. (2013) do not explicitly align semantic information from
167   different modalities before facilitating modality interaction. This limitation can lead to insufficient
168   discrimination of joint features.
169   Adapter learning Kim et al. (2021); He et al. (2021b) for cross-modality is a specialized technique
170   employed in machine learning to address the challenges associated with integrating information
171   from diverse modalities. It focuses on building adaptable modules or adapters that enable a model to
172   effectively handle information from multiple modalities. Houlsby et al. (2019) extensively studied
173   the choices of adapter architectures and concluded that a stack of networks works well which only
174   introduces a small amount of extra parameters to the model.
175   Cross-modality Yan et al. (2019) tasks involve processing and understanding data that originates
176   from different sources or modalities, such as combining text and images. In the context of cross-
177   modality, Lin et al. (2023b) aims to create modality-specific modules that can be easily plugged
178   into a unified model via adapter learning. The model becomes more versatile in handling distinct
179   types of data, allowing for improved performance on tasks that involve multiple modalities. adapter
180   learning for cross-modality is particularly valuable in applications such as segmentation He et al.
181   (2021a), multimodal sentiment analysis Ryumina et al. (2023).
182   This work is inspired from the utilization of adapter learning to enhance the flexibility and robustness
183   of our proposed model in addressing the intricacies associated with diverse data modalities.

187   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
188
189   Given a query Q and a video V with temporal length of L key frames, our main objective is to
190   localize a target moment M in the given video, starting at timestamp TS and ending at timestamp
191   TE , which semantically matches the query Q and rank the highlight score for each clip. Our solution
192   usually involves: 1) Data creation which leverages the ability of large language models (LLMs) to
193   create a diverse synthetic text queries. 2) To exploit multi-modal information, we learn query and
194
      video representations so that foundation models are fine-tuned over moment-retrieval tasks via an
      adapter learning to model the cross-modal relationship.
195
196
      3.2   OVERVIEW
197
198   The proposed architecture is shown in Figure 1. We augment text queries in the training set by
199   utilizing LLMs (Sec. 3.3). Then, the framework extracts representations for videos and queries using
200   frozen foundation models, namely SlowFast Feichtenhofer et al. (2019) and CLIP Radford et al.
201   (2021) for videos, and CLIP for queries. Furthermore, we incorporate nested adapter to facilitate
202   domain specific video-query alignment. The baseline MR pipeline involves initial steps of utilizing
203   pretrained models to extract features from both the input video and query. Subsequently, cross-
204   modal interaction is employed based on these features to derive the query relevance score for the
205   video moment.
206   We adopt the transformer-based architecture from previous works, including QD DETR Moon et al.
207   (2023b), Moment DeTR Lei et al. (2021), and CG DETR Moon et al. (2023a), for the encoder-
208   decoder framework. Each encoder layer consists of a multi-head self-attention mechanism and a
209   feed-forward network (FFN). In the context of Moment Retrieval and Highlight Detection (MR/HD),
210   the encoder generates clip-wise representations enriched with query-relevant information.
211   To improve the diversity and quality of query formulations, we employ GPT-3.5-turbo and GPT-4-o-
212   mini, leveraging their ability to generate varied yet semantically coherent queries in order enhance
213   the model’s ability to generalize and retrieve relevant moments more effectively. Cross-attention
214   between video and query modalities is applied early in the encoder to ensure query-dependent video
215   representations. These features are then used for tasks such as retrieving relevant moments (MR)
      and predicting clip-wise saliency scores (HD).

310
311   (1) Data Diversity: Augmenting queries through paraphrasing increases the dataset’s lexical diver-

340   improve performance on tasks requiring multi-modal understanding.

367   The QV-Highlights dataset contains 10,310 natural language queries associated with 18,367 mo-
368   ments in 10,148 YouTube videos. Each video averages around 150 seconds in duration, with ap-
369   proximately 1.8 distinct moments per query, and each moment lasting about 24.6 seconds. The
370   videos are categorized into three main types: daily vlogs, travel vlogs, and news events. Each

373   ‘Very Bad’, for all query-relevant clips. These comprehensive annotations support the development
374
      and evaluation of systems aimed at detecting relevant moments and identifying salient highlights
      across diverse user queries.
375
376   AS QV-Highlights final test dataset is not publicly released, we use the validation set as a proxy for
377   test set. The dataset we utilize consists of 7,218 video-query pairs for training and 1,550 video-query

438   number of randomly selected augmentations compared to the other two baselines (see Table 2).

441
      didn’t steadily rise always with increasing randomly selected augmentations.

531
      In this paper, we presented a novel approach to video moment retrieval by leveraging data augmen-
      tation and adaptive learning with learnable adapters. Our method addresses key challenges in video
532
      MR, including the domain gap, limited fine-tuning data, and the reliance on a single natural language
533
      query. By introducing diverse and semantically coherent query augmentations with LLMs, we en-
534   hanced the model’s ability to generalize across varied query formulations. Furthermore, our use
535   of distance-based augmentation selection mitigates the noise introduced by random augmentations,
536   as shown in our ablation studies and supported by t-SNE visualizations. The integration of learn-
537   able adapters for both coarse and fine-tuning further improved the model’s alignment with the target
538   dataset, enabling it to retain discriminative power while being adaptable to diverse video content.
539   Our proposed approach is model-agnostic and demonstrated consistent performance improvements
      across multiple baselines, proving its effectiveness in real-world moment retrieval tasks.
