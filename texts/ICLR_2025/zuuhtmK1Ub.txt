011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Partial differential equations (PDEs) on unstructured grids can be solved us-
014            ing message passing on a graph neural network (GNN). Implicit time-stepping
015            schemes are often favored, especially for parabolic PDEs, due to their stability
016            properties. In this work, we develop a fully differentiable implicit solver for un-
017
               structured grids. We evaluate its performance across four key tasks: a) forward
               modeling of stiff evolutionary and static problems; b) the inverse problem of esti-
018
               mating equation coefficients; c) the inverse problem of estimating the right-hand
019
               side; and d) graph coarsening to accelerate forward modeling. The increased sta-
020            bility and differentiability of our solver enable excellent results in reducing the
021            complexity of forward modeling and efficiently solving related inverse problems.
022            This makes it a promising tool for geoscience and other physics-based applica-
023            tions.

049   1   I NTRODUCTION
050
051   In recent years, graph neural networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful tool for handling data on
052   unstructured grids (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Veličković et al., 2018; Hamilton et al., 2017; Xu et al.,
053   2019). By leveraging message-passing mechanisms, GNNs are highly effective at capturing local
      dependencies and updating node features through their connections. This capability has sparked


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      increasing interest in applying GNNs to PDE solvers, particularly in problems where the data is
055   naturally represented as a graph, such as Voronoi grids and finite-volume discretizations.
056
057   Implicit deep learning, El Ghaoui et al. (2021), also referred as deep equilibrium models (DEQ), sub-
058
      stitutes conventional activation functions at a particular layers with a non-linear equilibrium equation
      completed with a prediction equation. DEQ combined with GNNs are known as implicit GNNs, Gu
059
      et al. (2021). Their benefit is that data propagates instantly within the graph, rather than with finite
060
      speed as in case of conventional deep learning.
061
062   The finite-volume (FV) method is a well-established numerical technique for solving PDEs, espe-
063   cially on unstructured grids, as it ensures local conservation properties. Integrating FV methods
064
      with GNN-based solvers provides a structured, grid-free representation of the domain, while pre-
      serving a rigorous framework for solving governing equations. In this context, the development of
065
      differentiable solvers is an especially promising research direction.
066
067   One key advancement explored in this work is the application of automatic differentiation (AD)
068   Naumann (2012) within GNN-based solvers, particularly for tasks such as graph coarsening and
069   solving inverse problems. AD has proven to be an invaluable tool for efficient gradient-based opti-
070
      mization, especially when combined with deep learning models, enabling end-to-end differentiable
      pipelines. The use of AD in inverse problems involving hyperbolic equations was demonstrated in
071
      Zhu et al. (2021).
072
073   In our work, we focus on parabolic partial-differential equations, which are crucial in various ap-
074   plications, including engineering and the oil and gas industry. However, the high stiffness of the
075   resulting evolutionary problems necessitates the use of implicit time-stepping schemes due to their
076
      stability properties. Additionally, we address elliptic partial-differential equations, where a distinc-
      tive challenge lies in solving large, sparse linear systems.
077
078   Although some automatic differentiation (AD) libraries support implicit relations between variables
079   (e.g., JAX), their capabilities for handling sparse linear solvers are currently quite limited. To address
080   this, we developed custom autograd functions tailored to our needs.
081   Building on this foundation, we propose an integrated approach that combines GNNs, finite-
082   volume methods, implicit time-stepping, and automatic differentiation to create a fully differentiable
083   pipeline for both forward and inverse modeling. Our approach ensures differentiability with respect
084   to various components, including grid cell locations, PDE coefficients, and the right-hand side, pro-
085   viding a flexible and robust framework for tackling both forward modeling and inverse problems.
086   Specifically, we focus on the following key applications:
087
088          • Forward modeling of stiff evolutionary and static problems;
089          • Solving inverse problems to estimate equation coefficients;
090
             • Solving inverse problems to estimate unknown right-hand side terms;
091
092          • Graph coarsening to accelerate forward modeling.
093
094   We demonstrate the capability of the proposed approach to effectively address all these problem
095
      types, showcasing its flexibility and ease of implementation for geoscience and other PDE-based
      applications.

206   3.2   F ORWARD M ODELLING AND ITS ACCELERATION
207
208   Let a polygonal domain be filled with heterogeneous porous media. We assume that a slightly
209   compressible fluid is injected at one point and pressure is measured at some other points. With
210   minor simplifications (Chen, 2007) the fluid flow is described by the parabolic equation, (1).
211   In this experiment, the coarsening process is driven by a graph neural network (GNN), which learns
212   soft cluster assignments to map nodes from the fine grid to coarser clusters. The GNN is composed
213   of graph convolutions followed by an MLP, and it represents a learnable aggregation function θ.
214   The aggregation function θ learns how to combine the node features from the fine grid into coarse
215   representations, ensuring that key physical properties are preserved during the coarsening process.
      The feature aggregation is mathematically represented as:

419   4   R ELATED W ORK
420
421   A large number works of recent works can be characterized as surrogate modelling. For forward
422   problems, the idea idea it approximate the equation Green’s function, e.g., Alet et al. (2019) or Nas-
423   torg et al. (2024) involving implicit GNN. For inverse problems, the idea is approximate the inverse
424   to the possibly non-linear forward operator using a given dataset. For example, Zhao et al. (2022)
425   involved GNN for this task. In Jessica et al. (2023), the author enriched GNN with novel geometric
426   features for better prediction accuracy. The work of Horie & Mitsume (2024) suggested a learnable
427   approach to compute fluxes in the FVM, motivated by convection-dominated problems. However,
428
      these approach typically requires massive datasets (GBs and TBs) and quite computationally de-
      manding learning (100-1000s of GPU hours).
429
430   In the traditional approach to inverse problems, Zhdanov (2002), optimization on performed every
431   new data. But this algorithms are implemented though derivation of the Jacobians. This derivations
      a tedious and prone-to-bugs task. Availability of AD pipeines can help to avoid it. The work of Zhu


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      et al. (2021) presented AD-based acoustic data inversion combined with explicit Euler temporal dis-
433   cretization of the wave equation. In our work, we followed a similar direction and showed how the
434   implicit schemes can be incorporated into an AD pipeline and further combined with a GNN. Our
435   work is close to PINNs, Raissi et al. (2019), Lu et al. (2021), and their extensions, graph convolu-
436   tional PINN, Gao et al. (2022), since both of the approaches can be attributed as self-supervised.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
439
440
      In this work, we integrated graph neural networks, the finite-volume method, and implicit time-
441   stepping to develop a fully differentiable modeling pipeline, applying it to both forward and inverse
442   problems in geoscience. Our PyTorch-based pipeline is flexible and easy to implement, enabling the
443   integration of new, fast solvers for large sparse linear systems.
444
      For comparison, Shumilin et al. presented results on accelerating forward modeling using graph
445
      coarsening, which is somewhat similar to our approach in Section 3.2. However, their method
446
      encountered significant time-step limitations due to the use of an explicit scheme.
447
448   Our pipeline is highly efficient. In future work, we plan to address large-scale 3D problems and more
449   complex equations, such as multi-phase fluid flow. Large-scale 3D modeling and inversion typically
450
      rely on custom preconditioned iterative solvers, as they offer both memory efficiency and fast con-
      vergence. Our pipeline’s ability to easily integrate new solvers makes it particularly appealing for
451
      industrial applications.
452
453   In this work, we discussed coarsening within the context of forward modeling and data inversion.
454   An interesting future research direction would be to combine coarsening with inversion, allowing
455   data inversion to be performed on an optimal coarse grid.
