009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural Cellular Automata (NCA) are computational models inspired by cellular
012            growth, capable of learning complex behaviors through local interactions. While
013            NCAs have been applied to various tasks like image restoration and synthesis,
014            their potential for image compression remains largely unexplored. This paper
015            aims to unravel the capabilities of NCAs for lightweight image compression by
016
               introducing a Grid Neural Cellular Automata (GNCA) training strategy. Unlike
               traditional methods that depend on large deep learning models, NCAs offer a
017
               low-cost compact and highly parallelizable alternative with intrinsic robustness
018
               to noise. Through experiments on the COCO 2017 dataset, we compare the com-
019            pression performance of NCAs against JPEG, JPEG-2000 and WebP, using the
020            metrics PSNR, SSIM, and MSE and Compression Rate. Our results demonstrate
021            that NCAs achieve competitive compression rates and image quality reconstruc-
022            tion, highlighting their potential as a lightweight solution for efficient image com-
023            pression. The code will be available upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Cellular Automata (CA) (Von Neumann, 2017) are computational models inspired by the process
028
      of cellular growth and differentiation, where these cells evolve over discrete time steps according
029
      to predefined rules and neighbouring cell states. Originating from the pioneering work of John von
030   Neumann and Stephen Wolfram, with Conway’s Game of Life (Adamatzky, 2010), Cellular Au-
031   tomata have found applications across various fields, including physics, biology, computer science,
032   and social sciences (Hoekstra et al., 2010), due to their ability to capture and simulate a wide range
033   of phenomena with simple yet powerful computational principles.
034
      Building on the foundational concepts of traditional Cellular Automata, Neural Cellular Automata
035
      (NCA) introduces an advanced approach by integrating Deep Neural Network architectures to define
036   their transition functions. Unlike CAs, the updated rules are not predefined but learned through
037   training. Applications of NCA (Mordvintsev et al., 2020; Palm et al., 2022) have demonstrated
038   the ability to reconstruct an image from a pixel seed by learning local neighbour interactions in a
039   self-organized manner. Although many promising applications have been proposed, ranging from
040   content generation (Sudhakaran et al., 2021) to self-organized control (Variengien et al., 2021),
041   NCAs have not been explored in the context of data representation for image compression.
042   State-of-the-art (SOTA) methods for image compression typically rely on large deep neural net-
043   works (Zhu et al., 2022; Sadeeq et al., 2021). While autoencoders have been successfully used for
044   image compression (Liu et al., 2022), these models often come with significant storage demands due
045   to their size, which is frequently overlooked when calculating the storage size for compressed repre-
046   sentations. By contrast, NCAs present a lightweight alternative with several compelling properties,
047   such as asynchronous operation with local interactions, high parallelization, and noise tolerance due
048   to their self-organized reconstruction capabilities.
049   This work proposes a novel approach to image compression using Neural Cellular Automata
050   (NCAs). The key advantages of this approach include its parallelization capability, robustness to
051   data corruption, and model compactness. Unlike traditional neural network-based solutions where
052   the compressed representation is a latent vector, the proposed solution based on NCAs use the model
053   weights themselves as the compressed data, reconstructing the image from a single pixel seed. The
      central research question we address is: : How efficient are Neural Cellular Automata for Image

075   2   BACKGROUND
076
077
      Neural net-based formulations of CAs can be traced back to the early work of Wulff & Hertz (1992).
078   Recent formulations of NCAs have shown their ability to learn complex desired behaviour, such as
079   semantic segmentation (Sandler et al., 2020), common reinforcement learning tasks (Variengien
080   et al., 2021), 3D locomotion (Najarro et al., 2022), Atari game playing (Najarro et al., 2022), and
081   image synthesis (Palm et al., 2022).
082
      Works by Mordvintsev et al. (2020) and Palm et al. (2022) show the capability of NCAs to recon-
083
      struct an image from a seed through learned neighbour interaction. Menta et al. (2024) uses NCA
084   for resource-efficient image restoration, showing its potential for noise tolerance. In the context of
085   image compression, the early work of Paul et al. (1999) proposed using Cellular Automata transfor-
086   mations to compress images. However, CAs have not been investigated as an efficient compression
087   method since then.
088
      Recent advancements in image compression have been driven by deep learning techniques (Mishra
089
      et al., 2022). Various compression algorithms based on convolutional neural networks (CNN) (Li
090   et al., 2021), recurrent neural networks (RNN) (Medsker & Jain, 1999), autoencoders (Liu et al.,
091   2022), and generative adversarial networks (GAN) (Gui et al., 2021) have shown significant im-
092   provements over traditional codecs like JPEG and WebP, particularly in terms of perceptual quality
093   and rate-distortion performance (Sadeeq et al., 2021).
094
      Using autoencoders and deep neural networks for image compression has some disadvantages de-
095
      spite their potential for high compression ratios and improved perceptual quality. These drawbacks
096   include the need for large amounts of training data, large models, and significant processing power
097   and memory resources for training. The SOTA MLIC++ (Jiang & Wang, 2023) uses a model with
098   83.5 million parameters, and the model size is not considered when calculating the compressed stor-
099   age size, although these parameters are necessary to reconstruct the image. Large architectures like
100   these demand extensive computational resources to train, encode and decode images (Mishra et al.,
101   2022), making them unsuitable for lightweight devices.
102   This work proposes the use of NCAs to provide a lightweight solution with high parallelization
103   capabilities, learning to compress and reconstruct images as self-organizing structures. Unlike au-
104   toencoders, which generate a latent compressed representation, we propose that NCAs use the model
105   weights as encrypted, compressed representation form. This approach enables the image to be re-
106   constructed from a pixel seed using the trained model, positioning the model itself as the compressed
107   representation. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to explore NCAs for image com-
      pression.

110   3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
111
112   Formally, image compression can be defined as a function C : I → C(I) that maps an original
113   image I to a compressed representation C(I), where C(I) contains fewer bits than the original
114   image while preserving important perceptual or structural features.
115   The goal of image compression is to minimize the distortion introduced during the compression
116   process while significantly reducing data size. Image compression algorithms aim to minimize some
117   distortion metric D under a constraint on the size of C(I). This can be formulated as an optimization
118   problem minC D(I, C(I)) subject to a size constrain on C(I), where D(I, C(I)) represents the
119   distortion between the original and compressed images.
120

      transition function. Let sti ∈ S indicate the state of cell I at time step t and Ωti = sti : j ∈ η(i) the
125

126

128   Neural Cellular Automata (NCAs) extend the CA framework by replacing the pre-defined transi-
129   tion function ϕ with a learnable function represented by a neural network ϕθ with parameters θ,
130
      preserving the essential feature of the locality. NCA can be trained to reconstruct images starting

131

132

133   2020), which is a lightweight model by design. Although alternative methods have been proposed to
134   train one model for multiple images, they often require larger models or external support (Hernandez
135   et al., 2021), which we do not explore in this work.

      3.3   NCA FOR I MAGE C OMPRESSION
169
170   We propose using NCA as a compression model, as they can reconstruct an image from a pixel
171   seed using a lightweight model. Different from Autoencoders, which extract a compressed latent
172   representation of an image, in the proposed application of NCA, the compressed representation is
173   the model itself since it can reconstruct the image from a pixel seed. As proposed by Mordvintsev
174   et al. (2020), the NCA requires a different model for each input image to generate the compressed
175   representation. Although some NCA variations enable one model to build many images (Menta
176
      et al., 2024), they require larger models and are not considered in this analysis. As the NCA proposed
      by Mordvintsev et al. (2020) is a low-resource model, the size of the required weights to reconstruct
177
      the image is lower than the input image size. Therefore, it is capable of generating a compressed
178
      and efficient representation.
179
180   When defining an NCA architecture for image compression, the objective is to define a model with
181   the lowest number of parameters but the highest reconstruction quality. From a compression per-
182
      spective, the compression rate is based on the size of the model weights compared to the size of the
      input image. The total number of parameters np used in this model is defined as follows:
183
184
185                                          np = hs · (4 · nc + 1),                                     (4)
186   where hs is the hidden size (i.e. the number of neurons in the hidden layer) and nc is the number of
187   channels or states for each cell.
188
      Reconstruction quality depends mainly on the parameters hs and nc , as will be shown in section 5.1.
189
      The main problem is that the number of steps necessary to reconstruct the image increases as the
190   image size increases. Thus, backpropagation through time will be longer, implying more training
191   time and a tendency for vanishing gradients. Also, larger images will require a larger number of
192   parameters as the number of reconstruction patterns increases.
193
      To address this problem, we propose the Grid Neural Cellular Automata (GNCA) training strategy
194
      for image compression. In this approach, we divide the input images into patches with size Pw ×Ph ,
195
      where Pw and Ph are the width and height of each patch. For each patch, we train
                                                                                     Pn a different NCA
196   to reconstruct that patch. The compressed representation is defined by R =         size(θn ), where
197   θn are the parameters of the NCA model trained at patch n. Figure 2 shows the encoding stage to
198   obtain the compressed representation. The decoding stage uses the trained model to rebuild each
199   patch through inference. The merged patches reconstruct the entire image.
200
      The GNCA approach addresses the scaling issue by using smaller models for each patch, which can
201
      be trained and reconstructed in parallel. This allows for efficient compression of large images while
202   maintaining high reconstruction quality. With this, no matter the size of the original input image, a
203   set of NCAs is able to learn to compress the representation of each image patch and then reconstruct
204   it. The process of reconstructing an image starts by providing an empty image with a pixel seed to
205   the trained model, as shown in Figure 1.

459   6     D ISCUSSION
460
461   Results demonstrate that NCAs can be used for data compression by treating the trained model
462   weights as the compressed representation. The architecture of the model plays a crucial role in
463   determining the compression efficiency, especially in balancing the tradeoff between the number
464   of parameters and the quality of the reconstructed image. Our findings indicate that this approach
465   is more efficient for low-resolution images, but the compression rate tends to decrease as image
466   size increases, especially when compared to JPEG, JPEG-2000, and WebP. Despite this, the GNCA
467
      method shows competitive performance against these classical methods, particularly in scenarios
      that benefit from high parallelization.
468
469   Compared to SOTA neural network compression methods, the solution based on NCA proposes a
470   much lighter approach. Our largest model (GNCA-large) uses only 2,600 parameters, compared to
471   state-of-the-art deep learning-based compression methods, such as MLIC++ (Jiang & Wang, 2023),
472   which requires 83 million parameters. This lightweight characteristic makes GNCA an attractive
473
      option for devices with limited computational resources, as it supports highly parallelized and asyn-
      chronous operations. However, due to the training time required for each image, NCAs are better
474
      suited for offline compression rather than real-time applications.
475
476   Some new approaches have been proposed to train a single model for many images (Hernandez et al.,
477   2021), but they require larger models. Otimizing NCA architecture in future work can lead to more
478   efficient compression. In terms of reconstruction quality, NCAs can generate images with higher
479
      quality than JPEG and WebP, given larger models. Below, we discuss the benefits and drawbacks of
      using NCA for image compression.
480
481
482   6.1   A DVANTAGES
483
      The main advantages of using NCAs for compression are:
484
485   Noise Robustness: NCA models are naturally robust to noise and are often used in denoising appli-
      cations. This analysis has been done by the work of Menta et al. (2024), where NCAs successfully


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      reconstructed images even when parts of the data were corrupted. The ability to reconstruct images
487   from minimal information, such as a single seed pixel, further enhances this robustness
488
489   Parallelization: NCAs can easily be parallelized due to their localized operations. Each cell inter-
490
      acts only with its immediate neighbors, allowing for asynchronous updates across different parts
      of the image. This makes NCAs particularly well-suited for large-scale or distributed computing
491
      environments where parallel processing is essential.
492
493   Low Computational Resources: Compared to deep learning-based methods, NCAs require fewer
494   parameters, making them highly efficient in terms of memory and computational power. This effi-
495   ciency enables NCAs to be deployed on devices with limited resources, such as mobile or embedded
496
      systems, which are often unable to support large and complex neural networks.
497   Predictable Compression Size: Another advantage of NCAs is the ability to predict the size of the
498   compressed representation before the training or encoding process begins. Since the compressed
499   size corresponds directly to the number of model parameters, it is possible to determine the storage
500   requirements beforehand, offering a level of control in memory-constrained environments.
501   Furthermore, the compact representation through model weights can serve as an encrypted compres-
502   sion.
503
504
505   6.2   L IMITATIONS
506
507
      Despite the advantages, there are some limitations to the current NCA-based approach:
508
509   Training Time: NCAs require a significant amount of training time for each individual image, which
510   limits their applicability in real-time compression scenarios. This makes the current approach more
511
      suitable for offline compression rather than dynamic, real-time environments.
512   Model-per-Image Limitation: In this work, we used NCA based on Mordvintsev et al. (2020), which
513   trains one model per image. We chose this method because it provided higher-quality reconstruction
514   with a low number of parameters. However, strategies to train one model to many images (Hernan-
515   dez et al., 2021) can be explored in future works.
516
517
518   7     C ONCLUSION
519
520
521   This work investigates the potential of Neural Cellular Automata for image compression. NCAs
522   present compelling properties compared to SOTA approaches, such as low computational cost, asyn-
523   chronous operation with local interactions, high parallelization, and robustness to noise. Our exper-
524
      iments compared NCA-based compression to traditional methods such as JPEG, JPEG-2000, and
      WebP, showing that NCAs can provide competitive image quality with far fewer parameters. We
525
      also introduce a grid strategy called GNCA to train NCAs in higher-resolution images.
526
527   Experiments show that NCA can store a compressed image representation with a high reconstruction
528   quality. In this approach, the model weights store the compressed representation, whose size is
529   smaller than the input image. NCA demonstrates competitive results compared to JPEG, JPEG-
530
      2000 and WebP, and it is capable of compressing and reconstructing images while maintaining a
      high level of similarity with the original image.
531
532   The key benefits of the proposed method include its lightweight model architecture, high paralleliza-
533   tion capability, and robustness to noise. These properties make NCAs well-suited for use in devices
534   with limited computational resources or environments that require asynchronous processing.
535   Although the current approach has some limitations—such as the need for long training times and
536   separate models for each image—there is potential for optimization and improvement. Future work
537   may focus on reducing the model size for higher-resolution images and developing methods to train
538   NCAs for multiple images without significantly increasing model complexity. With these advance-
539   ments, NCAs could emerge as a viable alternative to current state-of-the-art compression techniques,
      offering both efficiency and flexibility.
