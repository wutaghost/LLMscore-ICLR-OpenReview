009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph autoencoders (GAEs) are self-supervised learning models that can learn
012            meaningful representations of graph-structured data by reconstructing the input
013            graph from a low-dimensional latent space. Over the past few years, GAEs have
014            gained significant attention in academia and industry. In particular, the recent
015            advent of GAEs with masked autoencoding schemes marks a significant advance-
016            ment in graph self-supervised learning research. While numerous GAEs have
017            been proposed, the underlying mechanisms of GAEs are not well understood,
018
               and a comprehensive benchmark for GAEs is still lacking. In this work, we
               bridge the gap between GAEs and contrastive learning by establishing concep-
019
               tual and methodological connections. We revisit the GAEs studied in previous
020
               works and demonstrate how contrastive learning principles can be applied to
021            GAEs. Motivated by these insights, we introduce lrGAE (left-right GAE), a gen-
022            eral and powerful GAE framework that leverages contrastive learning principles
023            to learn meaningful representations. Our proposed lrGAE not only facilitates a
024            deeper understanding of GAEs but also sets a new benchmark for GAEs across
025            diverse graph-based learning tasks. The source code for lrGAE, including the
026            baselines and all the code for reproducing the results, is publicly available at
027            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/lrGAE/.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In the last years, self-supervised learning (SSL) has emerged as a powerful learning paradigm for
033
      learning graph representations, approaching, and sometimes even surpassing, the performance of
      supervised counterparts on many downstream tasks Hjelm et al. (2019); van den Oord et al. (2018).
034
      Compared with supervised learning, self-supervised learning gets equal or even better performance
035
      with limited or no-labeled data which saves much annotation time and plenty of resources. In a
036   nutshell, SSL purely makes use of rich unlabeled data via well-designed pretext tasks that exploit
037   the underlying structure and patterns in the data. Most recent approaches are shaped by the design
038   of pretext tasks and architectural design, which has led to two lines of research: contrastive and
039   non-contrastive learning Garrido et al. (2023); Balestriero & LeCun (2022).
040
      As one of the most successful and widespread SSL strategies, contrastive learning has first shown
041
      promising performance in vision representation learning Chen et al. (2020); Gao et al. (2021). It
042   brings together embeddings of different views of the same image while pushing away the embeddings
043   from different ones. Contrastive learning develops rapidly and has recently been applied to the graph
044   learning domain because of the scarcity of graph datasets with labels. Contrastive learning on graphs
045   (i.e., GCL) You et al. (2020) follows a similar framework to its counterparts in the vision domain,
046   with the objective of maximizing the agreement between different graph augmentation views Wu et al.
047   (2021); Li et al. (2024). Basically, contrastive views are designed as nodes, subgraphs, or mixtures of
048   the two that are either similar (positive pairs) or dissimilar (negative pairs) Velickovic et al. (2019);
049   Suresh et al. (2021); You et al. (2020); Xu et al. (2021).
050   On the other hand, non-contrastive learning approaches focus on capturing the generative aspects
051   of graph data, with a promising line of research being graph autoencoders (GAEs) Kipf & Welling
052   (2016a). Naïve GAEs adopt the edge-reconstruction principle to train the encoder, where edges of
053   the input graph are expected to be reconstructed from hidden representations, thereby preserving
      the topological proximity and facilitating representation learning. Compared to contrastive methods,

      self-supervised learning with GAEs is relatively less explored. This is particularly due to the fact that
069
      GAEs have been criticized for their limitations in capturing complex graph structures and potentially
070   overemphasizing proximity information Velickovic et al. (2019); Li et al. (2023b). As a result, the
071   rise of graph contrastive learning has led to a shift away from traditional GAEs, as researchers seek
072   more effective approaches for graph self-supervised learning. So far, contrastive methods have long
073   become a dominant SSL paradigm on graphs.
074
      Until recently, GAEs based on masked autoencoding have renewed interest in the field of graph
075
      SSL. Masked autoencoding He et al. (2021) is a technique where a subset of the components (e.g.,
076   graph topology or node features) in the graph is randomly masked or corrupted Hou et al. (2022);
077   Li et al. (2023b). By learning to reconstruct the graph from the partially masked input, GAEs can
078   better capture the underlying structure and semantic information encoded in the graph. Masked
079   GAEs offer improved performance and have the potential to capture the underlying graph properties,
080   making them a preferred choice in many advanced graph learning tasks. Building on this momentum,
081   research on (masked) GAEs has explored and become the new mainstream in graph self-supervised
082   learning Shi et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024); Tan et al. (2023); Hou et al. (2023).
083   While (masked) GAEs and contrastive approaches seem very different and have been described as
084   such, we propose to take a closer look at the similarities between the two, both from a theoretical and
085   empirical point of view. We argue that there exists a close relationship between GAEs and GCL. In
086   this work, we revisit the GAEs studied in previous works and and closely look into GAEs from a
087   contrastive learning perspective. To be specific, we demonstrate that
088
      GAEs, whether employing structure or feature reconstruction, with or without masked autoencoding,
089
                implicitly perform graph contrastive learning on two paired subgraph views.
090
091   The equivalence between structure-based GAEs and graph contrastive learning was initially demon-
092   strated by Li et al. (2023b) and further extended in our work by additionally taking feature-based
093   GAEs into consideration. To support our claim, we provide an illustrative comparison of three repre-
094   sentative GAEs from a contrastive learning perspective in Figure 1. Essentially, vanilla GAE Kipf &
095   Welling (2016a) and MaskGAE Li et al. (2023b) are structure-based approaches that contrast a pair of
096   connected nodes from their original and augmented structural views, respectively. On the other hand,
097   GraphMAE Hou et al. (2022) is a feature-based approach that performs asymmetric graph contrastive
098
      learning by contrasting a node itself with its original and augmented subgraph views.
099   The above findings bridge the gap between graph contrastive and generative SSL methods, and further
100   motivate us to unify GAEs into a contrastive architecture. In particular, we propose lrGAE, which
101   formulates GAEs with five components: augmentation, contrastive views, encoder/decoder networks,
102   contrastive loss, and negative samples. Among these, contrastive views are the core component
103   that contributes to different aspects of the GAE architectures. We then relate popular GAEs to
104
      our architecture, highlighting the connections between them and further motivating the design of
      more effective contrastive-based GAE architectures. In this work, lrGAE is not only proposed as a
105
      general and powerful GAE framework but also a comprehensive GAE benchmark across different
106
      graph-based learning tasks.

133   2    R ELATED W ORK
134
135   To situate GAEs in a broader context, we discuss recent advances in graph self-supervised learning,
136   including graph contrastive learning, graph autoencoders, and their recent masked variants.
137
      Graph contrastive learning. Graph contrastive learning (GCL) is a general self-supervised learning
138
      paradigm excelling at capturing invariant information from diverse graph augmentation views. GCL
139
      has taken over the mainstream of self-supervised learning on graphs for years. Many works in this
140   direction have recently flourished, with promising examples including DGI Velickovic et al. (2019),
141   MVGRL Hassani & Ahmadi (2020), and GRACE Zhu et al. (2020). While most GCLs potentially
142   suffer from scalability issues due to complex augmentation and sampling strategies, several efforts
143   have been made to scale up GCL through augmentation-free paradigms (e.g., AFGRL Lee et al.
144   (2022)), architecture simplification (e.g., BGRL Thakoor et al. (2021) and SGCL Sun et al. (2024))
145   or in-batch feature decorrelation (e.g., CCA-SSG Zhang et al. (2021)).
146
      Graph autoencoders. Graph autoencoders (GAEs) are one such non-contrastive-based method
147
      that aim to learn meaningful representations by leveraging the graph reconstruction as the pretext
148   task, i.e., reconstruct certain inputs within a given context. The pioneering works on GAEs can be
149   traced back to GAE and VGAE Kipf & Welling (2016a), which utilize GNNs as the encoder and
150   employ dot-product for link prediction decoding. Follow-up GAEs Li et al. (2023c); Pan et al. (2018);
151   Wang et al. (2017); Hasanzadeh et al. (2019) mostly share a similar architecture, employing structure
152   reconstruction or integrating both structure and feature reconstruction as their objectives. While
153   GAEs typically excel in link-level tasks, they have been criticized for over-emphasizing proximity
154   information at the expense of structural information and naturally underperform on node- and graph-
155   level tasks pretrained on the graph reconstruction task Li et al. (2023b); Hou et al. (2022). This makes
156
      GAEs less preferable as a choice of graph self-supervised methods when compared to GCLs.
157   Masked graph autoencoders. Masked graph autoencoders are advanced GAEs that learn represen-
158   tations by reconstructing randomly masked patches from a graph input. There are two major lines of
159   masked GAEs, with each line primarily focusing on reconstruction at either the feature level Hou
160   et al. (2022; 2023); Shi et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024) or the structure level Li et al. (2023b); Tan
161   et al. (2023). Two seminal works include GraphMAE Hou et al. (2022) and MaskGAE Li et al.
      (2023b). GraphMAE introduces masked feature reconstruction on graphs as self-supervisions, while


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      MaskGAE focuses on graph structure reconstruction. The success of masked GAEs has spurred a
163   surge of research in this area after the presentation. Later works seek to improve masked GAEs
164   through the utilization of powerful encoders Zhang et al. (2022b), advanced masking strategies Shi
165   et al. (2023), and regularization techniques Wang et al. (2024); Hou et al. (2023). However, a unified
166   framework for GAEs and their masked alternatives is lacking, which motivates us to address this gap
167   in our work.

269   Remark I: caveats of vanilla structure-based and feature-based GAEs. Taking the perspective
      of (approximate) contrastive learning of GAEs would allow us to gain insights regarding their

      deficiency. In particular, for structure-based GAEs, directly applying Eq. 3 would implicitly encode
271   the information of a potentially large overlapped subgraph, thereby over-emphasizing proximity
272   as elaborated in Li et al. (2023b). Alternatively, in feature-based GAEs, the vanilla objective
273   Eq. 5 contains no components that account for uniformity regularization as in standard contrastive
274   paradigms. Consequently, the GAE loss admits trivial shortcuts as optimal solutions (the constant
275   map) which may deteriorate learning.

      Remark II: on expositions of theoretical connections and potential implications. The expositions
277
      of theoretical connections between GAEs and GCLs were largely inspired by previously established
278
      theoretical insights. To the best of our knowledge, theoretical explorations of contrastive paradigms
279   often rely on a given augmentation distribution and derive learnability or generalization results
280   thereafter HaoChen et al. (2021); Parulekar et al. (2023). However, in lrGAE we are more focused
281   on the design aspects of the augmentations, with preliminary theory work presented in Huang et al.
282   (2021) but an extension to the field of graph learning remains highly non-trivial. Moreover, directly
283   analyzing downstream generalization based on emerging self-supervised learning theories like Lee
284   et al. (2021) is also challenging, as they typically rely on simple statistical models like linear models
285   or topic models, which are not yet widely applicable in practical graph learning scenarios. Therefore,
286   currently we believe a more detailed analysis tailored to the lrGAE framework is still beyond the
287
      scope of this paper but warrants extensive further studies.

289   4    L R GAE: DESIGN SPACE FOR GAE S FROM CONTRASTIVE LEARNING
290        PERSPECTIVE
291
292   We have demonstrated that GAEs are such generative yet contrastive models, here we introduce
293   lrGAE (left-right GAE), the first contrastive-based GAE architecture designed with the general
294   purpose of learning powerful representations. Following the works in GCLs, we decompose the
295   design space of lrGAE from five key dimensions: (1) augmentations, (2) contrastive views, (3)
296   encoder/decoder networks, (4) contrastive loss, and dispensable (5) negative samples.
297
      Augmentations. Graph augmentation is the first step in GCL, which generates multiple graph views
298
      from the input graph without affecting the semantic meaning You et al. (2020). These views are
299   typically created by applying certain transformations, and the goal is to help the model learn robust
300   and generalizable representations. In GCLs, the most prevalent augmentation techniques include node
301   dropping, edge perturbation, and attribute/feature corruption You et al. (2020); Rong et al. (2020);
302   Velickovic et al. (2019). However, as pointed out in § 3, the contrastive viewpoint of GAEs reveals
303   the deficiency of augmentation mechanisms like information redundancy and collapsed solutions. To
304   mitigate these issues, a recent line of work Li et al. (2023b); Hou et al. (2022) has been using a simple
305   idea of masking to improve learning performance. Specifically, in structure-based GAEs, masking a
306   certain proportion of edges effectively reduces information redundancy Li et al. (2023b). Meanwhile,
307
      masking the root node in feature-based GAEs Hou et al. (2022) can prevent trivial solutions (Zhang
      et al., 2022a, Theorem 3.6). Following the design space of GCL, we mainly consider lrGAE with
308
      node/edge/attribute masking as augmentations.
309
310   Encoder/decoder networks. An encoder maps the input graph into low-dimensional representations
311   and is typically defined as a GNN network. Basically, the receptive fields of the encoder are
312   determined by the depth of the GNN network. Meanwhile, the decoder network is regarded as a
313   task-specific ‘adapter‘, which maps augmented representations to another latent space where the
314   contrastive loss is calculated for different pretext tasks, such as graph reconstruction. In most cases,
315
      GAEs employ an asymmetric design, where the decoder network is implemented as an MLP, although
      a GNN can also be used to enhance decoding and expand the receptive fields. However, using a GNN
316
      as the decoder may additionally introduce the oversmoothing issue. Multiple encoder and decoder
317
      can be adopted to learn diverse representations in different ways. Similar to GCLs, lrGAE do not
318   apply any constraint on the encoder/decoder architecture.
319
320   Contrastive views. General GCLs typically follow a dual-branch architecture, which maximizes
321   the agreement between two graph views (e.g., a graph and its augmentation). In contrastive learning,
322   the choice of ‘view’ controls the information that the representation captures and influences the
323
      performance of the model. As depicted in Eq. 2, the contrastive views in GCLs can be simply denoted
           (k)        (k)
      as GA [v] ↔ GB [v]. In a nutshell, in GCLs, the goal is to maximize the correspondence of the


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      k-hop neighborhood representations of a node v and itself in two graph views, GA and GB , where
325   k represents the receptive fields of the encoder network (e.g., a k-layer GNN). When it comes to
326   GAEs, the same spirit could be followed where contrastive views are inherently determined by the
327   graph structure. For example, in GAEs, adjacent nodes that have an explicit connection in the graph
328   are considered positive pairs while non-connected nodes are treated as negative pairs. Following
329   the practice of GCLs, we highlight three key components in the contrastive views of lrGAE,
330   including graph views, receptive fields, and node pairs. Each of these components independently
331   contributes to the performance of lrGAE. Therefore, we have 23 cases of contrastive views by
332   exhaustively enumerating all the possible combinations, as summarized in Table 1 with corresponding
333
      implementations in current works. Note that case 1 is not applicable since it simply compares two
      original and identical samples as contrastive views, resulting in the loss becoming zero. Given that
334
      cases 2 3 4 5 have been extensively explored and implemented in current works, cases 6 7 8

349   Contrastive loss. Contrastive loss or objective is designed to train models to distinguish between
350   similar (positive) and dissimilar (negative) pairs of graph views according to different pretext tasks.
351   The main idea in vanilla GCLs is to bring the representations of augmented views of the graph
352   closer together while pushing the representations of different graphs further apart. By effectively
353
      contrasting positive and negative pairs, the model learns to capture the essential structural and feature
      information of the graphs, leading to improved performance on various downstream tasks. For GAEs,
354
      whose core idea is to perform reconstruction over graph structure or node features, the learning
355
      objectives involve binary cross-entropy (BCE) loss or regression loss mean squared error (MSE),
356   respectively. As discussed in § 3, both losses follow the same contrastive learning philosophy. As we
357   have established a strong connection between GAEs and GCLs, we can further advance GAEs with
358   various well-established contrastive learning objectives beyond simple BCE or MSE in lrGAE.
359
360   Negative samples. Negative samples are instances that are dissimilar to the positive samples in
361
      contrastive learning, which act as a reference for the learning model to distinguish between meaningful
      patterns and random noise. Conventional GCLs largely rely on negative samples to learn meaningful
362
      representations and capture important patterns in the data. However, this approach comes with a
363
      trade-off in learning efficiency, and the quality of negative samples is not always guaranteed. Since
364   graph structure reconstruction in GAEs is inherently tackled as a binary link classification task,
365   negative links or edges are necessary to prevent model collapse and encourage learning meaningful
366   representations. In contrast, feature reconstruction focuses on recovering node-level or graph-level
367   features without the need for negative samples since the task revolves around reproducing the original
368   feature representations. Recent advances have shown that GCLs can also be effective even without
369   explicit negative samples Thakoor et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2024). This opens up
370   the possibility of eliminating negative samples in lrGAE by properly incorporating several techniques
371   from GCL such as stop-gradient or asymmetric networks.

476   6   C ONCLUSION
477
      In this work, we first show that GAEs are not only generative but also contrastive self-supervised
478
      models that contrast two paired subgraph views. Built upon the equivalence between GAEs and
479
      GCLs, we present lrGAE, a comprehensive GAE benchmark that leverages the contrastive learning
480   principles to unify existing GAE approaches. Our extensive benchmarking experiments across
481   diverse graph datasets and tasks, coupled with detailed ablation studies, have provided valuable
482   insights into the effectiveness of contrastive views and the contributions of core components within
483   lrGAE. Our work is setting the foundation for a unified architecture of graph self-supervised learning,
484   with modular and scalable GAEs and a broader understanding of the role of GAEs with different
485   contrastive designs. So far, since GAEs and their masked variants are a promising research direction,
      lrGAE should be easily extended to be compatible with newly proposed approaches in the future.

      A    L R GAE FRAMEWORK AND IMPLEMENTATIONS
649
650
      lrGAE is introduced as a versatile and comprehensive framework that offers flexibility in implement-
651   ing powerful GAEs through customization of augmentations, contrastive views, encoder/decoder
652   networks, and contrastive loss. In particular, the design of contrastive views and the corresponding
653   learning loss play a crucial role in addressing various graph-based learning tasks efficiently and
654   effectively. For example, different tasks may require different types of contrastive views, such as
655   node-level views, link-level views, or even more complex views that incorporate both local and
656   global graph information. Here we provide the PyTorch Paszke et al. (2019) style pseudocode for the
657   implementation of lrGAE in Algorithm 1.
658   Algorithm 1 PyTorch Paszke et al. (2019) style pseudocode for lrGAE.
659

661     # l, r: receptive fields
662     # v, u: node pairs
663
664       # graph augmentation
665       gA, gB = augmentation(g)
666
667       # encoding
668
          left = encoder(gA)[l]
          right = encoder(gB)[r]

          # decoding
671       viewV = decoder(left[v])
672       viewU = decoder(right[u])

      To further showcase the versatility and broad applicability of lrGAE, we list seven variants of lrGAE
677
      with different contrastive views below:
678
679          • lrGAE 2 -ABllvv: This variant can be simply implemented with the same architecture as
680            naïve GCLs Zhu et al. (2020); You et al. (2020), which contrast two augmentation views
681            (GA and GB ) for each node (v) in its l-hop neighborhood (i.e., receptive field).
682          • lrGAE 3 -AAlrvv: This is a variant of lrGAE 2 -ABllvv that contrasts the node represen-
683            tations from different layers of the encoder, i.e., the receptive fields (l and r), which shares
684            similar philosophy of local-to-global graph contrastive learning Velickovic et al. (2019).
685            The most typical example is GAE Kipf & Welling (2016a) with feature reconstruction as its
686            learning objective (i.e., GAEf ).
687          • lrGAE 4 -ABlrvv: This is the core idea of GraphMAE Hou et al. (2022). By combining
688            the success of GCLs and GAEs, we are able to develop this variant that incorporates different
689            graph views (GA and GB ) and receptive fields (l and r) of a node v.
690          • lrGAE 5 -AAllvu: This is the learning paradigm of vanilla GAE and MaskGAE Li et al.
691            (2023b), which contrasts the two subgraph pairs of nodes associated with an edge (v, u).
692            Note that the graph view GA can be the original graph G or the augmented/masked graph,
693            leading to the implementations of GAE or MaskGAE.
694
             • lrGAE 6 -AAlrvu, lrGAE 7 -ABllvu, and lrGAE 8 -ABlrvu: The variants that do not
695
               have the exact implementation yet. Following the architecture of lrGAE 5 -AAllvu, we
696
               can vary the receptive fields (l and r), augmentation views (GA and GB ), or even both to

742   Time and space complexity. We briefly discuss the time and space complexity of our proposed
743   lrGAE framework. lrGAE is a standard GAE framework consisting of one encoder and one decoder
744   network. As decoder networks are typically simple feed-forward networks (e.g., MLPs) involved with
745   dense matrix computation, the major bottleneck arises from the message passing and aggregation
746   of encoder networks (e.g., GCN Kipf & Welling (2016b)). In particular, for an L layer encoder
747   network, the time complexity is related to the graph size and the dimension of features and hidden
748   representations, about O(L|E|d + L|V|d2 ). By incorporating mini-batch training Hamilton et al.
749   (2017), the time complexity for each sampled subgraph can be reduced to O(rL |V|d2 ), where r
750
      represents the neighborhood size shared by each hop. As for the space complexity, the major
      bottleneck also lies in the graph encoder network, which is O(L|V|d + Ld2 ) and O(BrL d + Ld2 )
751
      for full-batch and mini-batch training, respectively. Here, B denotes the batch size. Overall, the time
752
      and space complexity of lrGAE framework are guaranteed and can easily scale to larger graphs,
753   showcasing its scalability and generality.

755   Limitations and outlook. This work primarily presents initial benchmarks and baselines for GAEs,
      with a main focus on masked autoencoding based methods. Our work might potentially suffer

756
      from some limitations: (i) Graph augmentation, such as masking, is a core operation of lrGAE.
757   However, similar to existing augmentation-based GCL methods, performing masking on the graph
758   structure could harm the semantic meaning of some graphs, such as biochemical molecules. Zhao et al.
759   (2021). (ii) The theoretical results of lrGAE are mainly based on the homophily assumption, a basic
760   assumption in the literature of graph-based representation learning. However, such an assumption
761   may not always hold in heterophilic graphs, where the labels of linked nodes are likely to differ. This
762   could potentially limit the applicability of lrGAE on heterophilic graphs. Our current analysis is
763   based solely on existing datasets; hence, we plan to enhance the scope of lrGAE by acquiring and
764   incorporating more diverse datasets and domains. Furthermore, while this paper primarily conducts
765
      an empirical investigation on GAEs, advancing the theoretical framework of lrGAE will be crucial
      for its development and understanding.
766
767   Broader impact. In this work, we introduce lrGAE, a general GAE framework that integrates and
768   extends GAEs with powerful graph contrastive learning principles. lrGAE is also proposed as a
769   comprehensive benchmark with the establishment of a standardized evaluation protocol to ensure
770   fair and consistent comparisons in this line of research. The lrGAE benchmark encourages the
771   development of new algorithms and techniques for graph representation learning. This can lead to
772   improved methods for analyzing and understanding complex network structures, which can have
773
      wide-ranging positive impacts in various fields such as social network analysis, bioinformatics, and
      recommendation systems. While a standardized benchmark can promote comparability, it may
774
      also inadvertently discourage diversity in research approaches and lead to an overemphasis on
775
      performance metrics. The focus on benchmark performance metrics may inadvertently discourage
776   the exploration of novel or unconventional methods that may offer unique insights but perform less
777   well on standardized metrics. This could limit the potential for innovative approaches that could have
778   a significant social impact but may not fit within the benchmark’s evaluation framework.

      All of lrGAE ’s experimental results are highly reproducible. We provide more detailed information
813   on the following aspects to ensure the reproducibility of the experiments.
814
815   Datasets. We conduct experiments on seven graph benchmark datasets, including three citation
816   networks, i.e., Cora, CiteSeer, and PubMed Sen et al. (2008), two Amazon co-purchase graphs,
817   i.e., Photo and Computer Shchur et al. (2018), two co-author graphs, i.e., CS and Physics Shchur
818
      et al. (2018). The above datasets are used for the tasks of node classification, link prediction, and
      graph clustering. For the graph classification task, we perform experiments on the following seven
819
      datasets: IMDB-B, IMDB-M, PROTEINS, COLLAB, MUTAG, REDDIT-B, and NCI1 Yanardag
820
      & Vishwanathan (2015). Each dataset consists of a set of graphs, with each graph associated with
821   a corresponding label. We also incorporate three heterogeneous graph datasets, i.e., DBLP, ACM,
822   and IMDB Lv et al. (2021); Li et al. (2023a), for experiments on heterogeneous node classification
823   to showcase the generality and versatility of our lrGAE framework. All datasets used throughout
824   experiments are publicly available at PyTorch Geometric Fey & Lenssen (2019). Detailed information
825   about datasets are summarized in Table 6, Table 7 and Table 8.
826
      Baselines. In line with the focus of this work, we benchmark several GAEs in different graph
827
      learning tasks, including vanilla GAE Kipf & Welling (2016a) and its variant GAEf , as well as
828
      masked GAEs, i.e., MaskGAE Li et al. (2023b), S2GAE Tan et al. (2023), GraphMAE Hou et al.
829   (2022), GraphMAE2 Hou et al. (2023), and AUG-MAE Wang et al. (2024). Among the baselines,
830   GAE, MaskGAE, and S2GAE adopt structure reconstruction as their learning objective, while
831   GraphMAE, GraphMAE2, and AUG-MAE adopt feature reconstruction as their learning objective.
832   We implement baselines with PyTorch Paszke et al. (2019) and PyTorch Geometric Fey & Lenssen
833   (2019), which are open-source software released under BSD-style 1 and MIT 2 license, respectively.
834   For feature-based GAEs (GAEf , GraphMAE, GraphMAE2, AUG-MAE, GiGaMAE), we employ
835   the GAT Veličković et al. (2018) architecture and scaled cosine error (SCE) as the encoder network
836   and learning objective. On the other hand, for structure-based GAEs (GAE, MaskGAE, S2GAE),
837
      we utilize the GCN Kipf & Welling (2016b) architecture and binary cross-entropy as the encoder
      network and learning objective, respectively.
838
839   Implementation details. To align with the baseline implementations, we abstract the contrastive
840   learning principles of GAEs and implement lrGAE with PyTorch and PyTorch Geometric as well.
841   Specifically, we have seven basic variants of lrGAE with different contrastive views. Specifically,
842   we refer lrGAE 2 3 4 as feature-based variants while lrGAE 5 6 7 8 as structure-based ones.
843   Since lrGAE 2 3 4 5 have implementations proposed in previous works Zhu et al. (2020); You
844   et al. (2020); Kipf & Welling (2016a); Hou et al. (2022); Li et al. (2023b), we mainly provide the
845
      empirical results of lrGAE 6 7 8 in our experiments. The hyperparameters of all the baselines
      were configured according to the experimental settings officially reported by the authors and were
846
      then carefully tuned in our experiments to achieve their best results across all tasks. We also tune the
847
      hyperparameters of lrGAE variants for a fair comparison.
848
849   Evaluation. To provide a comprehensive benchmark, we conduct experiments on five graph learning
850   tasks from node, link, subgraph, and graph levels, across homogeneous and heterogeneous graphs,
851   i.e., node classification, link prediction, graph clustering, graph classification, and heterogeneous
852   node classification.
853
                • Node classification (N). Node classification is the most popular graph learning task, with
854
                  the goal of assigning a class label to each node. In the graph self-supervised learning
855               setting Li et al. (2023b), the GNN encoder is pretrained based on the pretext tasks to obtain
856               the node embeddings. The final evaluation is done by fitting a linear classifier (i.e., a logistic
857               regression model) on top of the frozen learned embeddings. We adopt the public splits
858               for Cora, CiteSeer, and PubMed, and 8:1:1 training/validation/test splits for the remaining
859               datasets. Classification accuracy is employed as the evaluation metric.
860             • Link prediction (L). For link prediction, the goal is to predict the existence of edges between
861               pairs of nodes. For structure-based GAEs, we directly use the output of the structure decoder
862
          1
863           https://github.com/pytorch/pytorch/blob/master/LICENSE
          2
              https://github.com/pyg-team/pytorch_geometric/blob/master/LICENSE

864
                  as the prediction, while for feature-based GAEs, we use the dot product of the learned
865               embeddings of the node pairs as the prediction. We adopt the 85/5/10 training/validation/test
866               splits for Cora, CiteSeer, and PubMed, as advocated by Kipf & Welling (2016a). The Area
867               Under the Curve (AUC) and Average Precision (AP) are employed for evaluation.
868
                • Graph clustering (C). Graph clustering involves grouping nodes in a graph such that nodes
869               in the same group are more similar to each other compared to those in different groups. We
870               perform K-means clustering on the learned embeddings to produce cluster assignments for
871               each node and employ Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) as the evaluation metric.
872
                • Graph classification (G). The graph classification task is similar to node classification
873               but differs in that the class labels are assigned to the entire graph. Therefore, we perform
874               graph pooling on the node embeddings to obtain the graph embedding. We follow the
875               evaluation protocol of Hou et al. (2022; 2023), which uses a 10-fold cross-validation setting
876               for train-test splits and reports the averaged results. Classification accuracy is employed as
877               the evaluation metric.
878             • Heterogeneous node classification (HN). We also extend our experiments from common
879               homogeneous graphs to heterogeneous ones, with heterogeneous node classification as the
880               downstream task. In this task, we extend GAEs to heterogeneous graphs by reconstructing
881               each graph view in a manner similar to homogeneous graphs and accumulating the loss from
882               all views to learn the representations of each node type. We adopt the public train/valid/test
883               splits as outlined in Lv et al. (2021) for all datasets. We use classification accuracy for the
884               evaluation metric for the target node type in each dataset, i.e., ‘author’ (DBLP), ‘paper’
885
                  (ACM), and ‘movie’ (IMDB).

1005
1006   In this section, we perform ablation studies on the key components of lrGAE, i.e., augmentation,
1007   encoders, contrastive loss, and negative sampling strategies. We opt for lrGAE 6 7 8 , three
1008   advanced GAEs that incorporate different contrastive schemes as the comparison methods. We
1009   conduct experiments on node classification tasks using the Cora dataset. The experimental results
1010   were averaged over 10 runs.
1011
       Augmentations. We first conduct ablation studies on the augmentation techniques, which include
1012   edge masking Rong et al. (2020), path masking Li et al. (2023b), and node masking You et al. (2020).
1013   Figure 3(a) presents the ablation results of lrGAE 6 7 8 using various masking strategies. As
1014   observed, the performance of lrGAE 6 7 8 varies significantly with different masking strategies.
1015   Basically, edge masking and path masking are better choices than node masking. The observation
1016   meets our intuition. This highlights the effectiveness of edge and path masking techniques in
1017   enhancing model performance, likely due to their ability to capture more nuanced relationships within
1018   the data compared to node masking.
1019
       Encoder networks. Given that most of the decoder networks in GAEs are MLPs, we will focus
1020   the ablation experiments only on the encoder networks. The encoder plays a crucial role in mapping
1021   graphs into low-dimensional representations. To investigate the potential of designing effective GAEs,
1022   we perform ablation studies on Cora using three different GNN encoders, including GCN Kipf &
1023   Welling (2016b), GraphSAGE Hamilton et al. (2017), and GAT Veličković et al. (2018). The results,
1024   shown in Figure 3(b), demonstrate that GCN is the most effective encoder architecture across all
1025   three lrGAE variants. lrGAE 6 7 8 with GCN as the encoder consistently outperforms GAT and
       GraphSAGE in all cases by large margins. This observation is consistent with the conclusions of


                                                                   19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       previous studies Li et al. (2023b); Velickovic et al. (2019); Thakoor et al. (2021); Zhu et al. (2021);
1027   Zhang et al. (2021).
1028
1029   Contrastive loss. Next, we conduct ablation studies on the contrastive loss in GAEs, using binary
1030   cross-entropy (BCE), SimCSE Gao et al. (2021), and InfoNCE van den Oord et al. (2018). SimCSE
1031   and InfoNCE are two effective losses utilized in contrastive learning literature. SimCSE focuses
1032
       on maximizing the similarity between augmented views of the same instance, whereas InfoNCE
       involves distinguishing positive samples from a set of negative samples. We present the results in
1033
       Figure 3(c). The results reveal that although BCE is the most widely used loss function in traditional
1034
       GAEs, SimCSE shows potential as a better alternative, outperforming BCE and InfoNCE in 2 out of
1035   3 lrGAE variants. This finding points to SimCSE as a promising direction for future research and
1036   optimization in contrastive learning within GAEs.
1037
1038   Negative samples. We explore different negative sampling strategies for lrGAE, which are detailed
1039   below:
1040
              • Random uniform sampling. Negative edges are sampled uniformly from the set of all
1041
                possible non-existent edges. This is the simplest approach but may not always yield the best
1042
                results.
1043
1044
              • Degree-based sampling. Negative edges are sampled based on the degree of nodes. The core
                idea is that nodes with higher degrees are more likely to be involved in many connections,
1045
                making them more likely to be sampled as negative examples.
1046
1047          • Similarity-based sampling. Negative edges are sampled based on the similarity or distance
1048
                between nodes in the graph. For instance, edges between nodes that are far apart are more
                likely to be sampled as negative. In this case, we use cosine similarity as the distance
1049
                measure.
1050
1051   The ablation results are shown in Figure 3(d). We observe that random negative sampling generally
1052   outperforms other strategies in most cases. Additionally, degree-based sampling shows promise as
1053   an effective negative sampling strategy. While similarity-based sampling was expected to improve
1054   performance by distinguishing nodes that are more dissimilar, it did not perform as well as the random
1055   or degree-based approaches. The possible reason could be that GAEs necessitate more hard negative
1056   samples, whereas similarity-based sampling might only furnish easy ones.
1057
1058   Remarks. We leave two additional remarks for our ablation studies. Firstly, the ablation exper-
1059   iments were only conducted on the node classification task and one dataset, which may limit the
1060   generalizability of the findings to other tasks and datasets. Secondly, we adopted three new lrGAE
1061
       variants as examples, which provide a representative but not an exhaustive exploration of the possible
       configurations of GAEs. Further studies with more tasks, datasets, and variants would be beneficial
1062
       to fully assess the robustness of the negative sampling strategies and the performance of lrGAE.
