011                                                       A BSTRACT
012
013                Recent multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have shown promising instruc-
014                tion following capabilities on vision-language tasks. In this work, we introduce
015                V ISUAL M ODALITY I NSTRUCTION (V IM)1 , and investigate how well multimodal
016
                   models can understand textual instructions provided in pixels, despite not being
                   explicitly trained on such data during pretraining or fine-tuning. We adapt V IM to
017
                   eight benchmarks, including OKVQA, MM-Vet, MathVista, MMMU, and probe di-
018
                   verse MLLMs in both the text-modality instruction (T EM) setting and V IM setting.
019                Notably, we observe a significant performance disparity between the original T EM
020                and V IM settings for open-source MLLMs, indicating that open-source MLLMs
021                face greater challenges when text instruction is presented solely in image form.
022                To address this issue, we train V-MLLM2 , a generalizable model that is capable
023                to conduct robust instruction following in both text-modality and visual-modality
024                instructions.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Interleaved image-text data has been increasingly prevalent, ranging from web pages with images
030   and tables, to user interfaces with instructions and forms, in which different modalities interact and
031   blend together. For instance, to perform online shopping, an agent needs to understand the images,
032   instructions and forms. Comprehensive understanding of this multi-modal data demands a range of
033
      skills, including recognizing text, understanding images, and also figuring out their interactions.
034   Inspired by the success of Large Language Models (LLMs), the recent research on Multimodal
035   Large Language Models (MLLMs) Liu et al. (2023b); Dai et al. (2023); Awadalla et al. (2023);
036   Ye et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023a); Su et al. (2023) may pave a way to understand this kind of
037   vision-language data, and show promising results on a number of newly proposed benchmarks Fu
038   et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023c); Li et al. (2023); Bitton et al. (2023); Wu et al.
039
      (2023), demonstrating superb visual understanding, reasoning and generation capabilities. Despite
      their impressive performance, they still remain poorly understood, and also these MLLMs are not
040
      as impressive as their LLM counterparts, let alone their landing business applications. The current
041
      MLLMs are built on top of the pretrained LLMs, and visual instruction tuning follows the recipe
042   from its LLM counterparts, specifically, the instruction data is synthesized by the LLMs (mostly from
043   GPT-4 or GPT-4V) in the text format. As illustrated in the left part of Figure 1, the instruction and
044   image are expressed in two modalities, we denote this kind of visual instruction data as Text-Modality
045   Instruction (T EM). Under this setting, a pure LLM, for example, Llama 2 or Vicuna in Figure 1 can
046   still make a plausible or correct prediction, even without accessing the image input. All the current
047   benchmarks Fu et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023c); Li et al. (2023); Bitton et al. (2023)
048   follow the same format.
049   This raises a question - how proficiently these MLLMs can follow instructions if we embed the text
050   instruction into visual format? As shown in the right part of Figure 1, we name it as V ISUAL
051   M ODALITY I NSTRUCTION, where the image and instruction are in the visual modality. Under the

150   Instruction following, is viewed as one key capability of high-performing MLLMs. In this section,

155   2.1     V IM
156
      2.1.1     V ISUAL -M ODALITY I NSTRUCTION
157
158   As illustrated in the left part of Figure 1, the current evaluation norm of MLLMs takes two modalities
159   as input: image and text (as instruction). The existing MLLMs are built on top of the LLMs, benefiting
160   from its strong text understanding capability. For the current MLLM evaluation paradigm, instruction
161   is presented in the text modality, which can utilize the strong language priors from the LLMs for
      understanding. As shown in Table 3, even a pure LLM model (GPT-4, Llama 2 or Vicuna) can get


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      some success without accessing to the images. Interestingly, on most of eight tasks, Llama 2 shows
163   better numbers over GPT-4 (gpt-4-1106-preview). We manually check some response, and
164   find that the output from GPT-4 are more reasonable than Llama 2. This might rise several interesting
165   issues, we leave a discussion in Section C.
166
      V IM challenges the MLLMs by rendering the textual instruction into the visual pixel space (image),
167
      this enhancement demands not just textual but also strong visual comprehension for instruction
168
      understanding. It asks for the strong visual interpretation capability to recognize and follow the
169   embedded instruction in the image.

225   2.2.1    V IM C ORPUS
226   One key ingredient of high-performing MLLMs is high-quality instruction tuning data. There are
227   two categories of visual instruction tuning data, one is the synthetic data by LLMs (i.e. GPT-
228   4), like LLaVA Liu et al. (2023b); the other one is the synthetic data generated by GPT-4V, like
229   LVIS-Instruct4V Wang et al. (2023) and ShareGPT-4V Chen et al. (2023). Here, we use the LVIS-

232   consider the first turn for the multiple turn conversation data. In total, we get 846k V IM training data
233   DV after filtering the unavailable image links.

      3.1   V IM -B ENCH
301
      Benchmarks To assess the generalization capability of MLLMs, we adapt V IM to eight represen-
302   tative benchmarks, including MME Fu et al. (2023), MM-Vet Yu et al. (2023), OKVQA Marino
303   et al. (2019), VizWiz Bigham et al. (2010), TextVQA Singh et al. (2019), MathVista Lu et al. (2023),
304   ChartQA Masry et al. (2022), and MMMU Yue et al. (2023). The details of source datasets, data
305   processing pipeline, and evaluations can be found in Appendix A.
306
307   Data Reformatting Given the above mentioned benchmarks, we try to do minimal changes (i.e.,
308   keeping the image resolution) for evaluation. This process involves reformatting text instruction into
309   visual-modality instruction by moving the text instruction into the image modality. In reformatting, we
310   retain the original task’s goal while maintaining the original images with text instructions rendering
311   at the bottom of the image, see the example in Figure 1. These repurposed benchmarks are integrated
312
      into our V IM-Bench. Theoretically, V IM can be applied to any existing benchmarks, even for pure
      NLP tasks. We choose eight representative MLLM benchmarks, although our selections are not
313
      exhaustive, they provide a broad basis for MLLM evaluation.

533
534   In this work, we review the existing MLLMs from a visual perspective, and present V IM, a challenging
535   setting to assess the visual instruction following ability of Multimodal Large Language Models. We
536   adapt V IM to eight benchmarks, leading to V IM-Bench. Through in-depth probing under zero-shot
537   setting, we observed a common issue for the existing open-source MLLMs: all fall short in the V IM
538   setting, in most cases performing not as good as those in the original T EM setting. Furthermore, we

      modality instruction settings on all the tasks.

723
724   For each source dataset, we start by building up zero shot by embedding instructions into the input

728   A.3     E VALUATION D ETAILS
729
730   Metrics We follow the evaluation pipeline for each benchmark. We use parsing and accuracy for
731   MathVista and MMMU with a more strict protocol. For example, when the model is outputting an
732   empty or random string for a multiple-choice question, in MathVista, the original evaluation protocol
733
      will use Levenshtein distance to map to a most similar prediction option, and in MMMU, a random
      choice from the candidate list will be applied. This will introduce noise and randomness for the
734
      evaluation, may not correctly reflect the model performance. In our strict evaluation protocol, we
735
