009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The widespread application of Large Language Models (LLMs) across various
012            tasks and fields has necessitated the alignment of these models with human val-
013            ues and preferences. Given various approaches of human value alignment, there
014            is an urgent need to understand the scope and nature of human values injected
015            into these LLMs before their deployment and adoption. We propose UniVaR,
016            a high-dimensional neural representation of symbolic human value distributions
017            in LLMs, orthogonal to model architecture and training data. This is a contin-
018
               uous and scalable representation, self-supervised from the value-relevant output
               of 8 LLMs and evaluated on 15 open-source and commercial LLMs. Through
019
               UniVaR, we visualize and explore how LLMs prioritize different values in 25
020
               languages and cultures, shedding light on the complex interplay between human
021            values and language modeling.
022

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The remarkable capabilities of large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized general-purpose
027   AI assistants leading to their widespread adoption in many tasks and fields (Bommasani et al., 2021;
028   Xi et al., 2023; Bang et al., 2023b; Qin et al., 2023). The ability of LLMs to adhere to ethical
029   and societal values has become a critical factor in development, just as important as the quality
030   and generalization of task performance (Durmus et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). Numerous efforts
031   have been made to imbue AI systems with ethical principles and moral values, from designing robust
032   frameworks for value alignment (e.g., RLHF, RLAIF, Constitutional AI, etc.) (Ouyang et al., 2022;
033
      Lee et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022a;b) to incorporating diverse perspectives into training data (Yao
      et al., 2023; Scheurer et al., 2023; Köpf et al., 2024; Glaese et al., 2022; Ganguli et al., 2022). These
034
      methods aim to make LLMs more performant, fairer, less toxic, and align better with human values.
035
036   Human values and preferences encompass a wide range, from universal ethical principles to cul-
037   turally specific values, social etiquette, to industry and domain-specific preferences (§2.1). These
038   values often become the foundation of AI regulations and guidelines. While LLMs are trained to in-
039
      corporate these values, differences may emerge due to the crowd-sourced annotations and variations
      in RLHF efforts across different languages (Arora et al., 2023; Ramezani & Xu, 2023; Hosking
040
      et al., 2024). For example, whereas the majority of English language LLMs produced by North
041
      American institutions tend to manifest American coastal liberal values (Hartmann et al., 2023), and
042   those from Chinese institutions might incorporate additional Chinese values (Du et al., 2022; Zeng
043   et al., 2022; Si et al., 2023; AI et al., 2024). The values pre-trained in LLMs are not always clear,
044   and it is uncertain if different models reflect consistent values within a language or culture.
045
      To better understand the human values of LLMs, one can use surveys of human values to query
046
      LLMs (Durmus et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Brown et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023a). While
047
      the surveys are useful, they capture an incomplete picture of LLM value distributions, as they only
048   explore constrained subspace with a limited number of dimensions. For instance, the cultural val-
049   ues (Hofstede, 2001; Hofstede et al., 2005) only uses 6 dimensions to represent a vast variability in
050   human cultures, while the theory of basic values (Schwartz, 1999; 2017; Schwartz & Cieciuch, 2022)
051   and the World Value Survey (WVS) (Inglehart et al., 2000; Inglehart, 2006; Haerpfer et al., 2022b),
052   each represented 19 and 10 dimensions of values, respectively. We argue that such a low-dimension
053   semantic representation will likely fail to give a full picture of human values in LLMs. Instead, we
      aim a high dimension representation of human value distribution to reflect the complexity of the

083   In this paper, we propose Universal Value Representation (UniVaR), the first high-dimensional
084   representation of human values in LLMs. We formulate the value embedding learning problem
085   and adopt a Siamese network structure to enable the model to capture values while filtering out
086   irrelevant information. To train UniVar, we generate 21k value-eliciting questions based on 87 core

088   set of 1M QA pairs. Since research suggests that LLMs express distinct values across languages (Lin
089   et al., 2022; Durmus et al., 2023; AlKhamissi et al., 2024), we treat language variations as distinct
090
      value representations within each model. We evaluate UniVaR through a value identification task
      using k-NN and linear probing on existing value datasets. More importantly, we visualized UniVaR
091
      representations and show that they reflect the distances and similarities between different cultures
092
      regarding human values in LLMs as illustrated in Figure 1. s UniVaR offers a systematic and
093   statistical approach to understanding the value systems of LLMs. It facilitates the exploration of
094   how LLMs learn and prioritize values in different languages, and is ultimately a powerful tool for
095   more transparent and accountable LLMs. We will release the UniVaR models and code to support
096   further research in this important area.

134   2.2     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
135
136   We assume that some factors in LLMs contribute towards aligning with certain human values while
137   others towards value-agnostic behaviors (e.g., wording, syntax, or style). Let an LLM parameterized
138
      by θ be fθ , our assumption can be formalized as θ = ϕ(ϑvalue , ϑother ) with some function ϕ, where
      ϑvalue is the value-decisive factors and ϑother is the value-agnostic factors. Our goal is to extract the
139
      value-decisive factors ϑvalue such that we can analyze similarities of values from different LLMs or
140
      transfer values across LLMs.
141
142   If we know LLM parameters θ and we are able to derive the inverse function ϕ−1 , we can di-
143   rectly recover value factors from by [ϑvalue , ϑother ] = ϕ−1 (θ). However, this cannot be applied to
144
      closed-source LLMs where θ is not accessible, and also there is no clue how to estimate ϕ−1 . The
      relationship and interactions between ϑvalue and ϑother are unknown, and locating value-decisive
145
      parameters from billions of LLM parameters is also difficult.
146
147   To overcome the difficulty of explicitly extracting ϑvalue , we consider a surrogate task of learning a
148   value embedding: a compact representation Z that contains maximized correlation with ϑvalue of
149   LLMs while discarding other confounding factors as much as possible. Following the information
150
      bottleneck principle of representation learning (Saxe et al., 2018; Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015; Tsai
      et al., 2021), the objective of value embedding learning can be written as:
151
152
153                                         max I(ϑvalue ; Z) − H(Z) ,                                                        (1)
                                             Z

                                                    maximizing           minimizing

159   2.3     VALUE E LICITING Q UESTION A NSWERING
160
161   The core challenge of value embedding learning lies in the fact that ϑvalue exists as a latent vari-
      able (Kügelgen et al., 2021; Zimmermann et al., 2021). Thus, we utilize value eliciting question


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      answering pairs, which are the observable input queries and output responses that are driven by
163   ϑvalue , to build value embedding.
164
165   Depending on the input question Q, LLM’s ϑvalue may or may not be involved when generating the
166
      answers A. For instance, a question asking for an arithmetic operation would be solely dependent
      on the reasoning capabilities represented by the value-agnostic ϑother , while ϑvalue hardly matters.
167
      On the other hand, a question that involves an ethical dilemma such as the trolley problem should
168
      be highly dependent on ϑvalue . Since our interest lies in values, we consider a set of value eliciting
169   questions Qvalue where the corresponding answers are dependent on ϑvalue . Thanks to this depen-
170   dency, if Q ∈ Qvalue , we know that one QA pair ⟨Q, A⟩ gives I(ϑvalue ; ⟨Q, A⟩) > 0 for the first
171   term in our objective (Eq. 1).
172
      A single QA pair is not representative enough for ϑvalue since it is impossible to extrapolate the
173
      entirety of human values from a single QA. For instance, even a broad question such as “What is
174
      the meaning of life?” or “What is the ideal society?” can only elicit values that are related to
175   terminal values (Rokeach, 1968; 1973) and cultural values (Hofstede, 2001; Hofstede et al., 2005),
176   while neglecting other aspects of human values. Therefore, we consider using a wide array of
177   value-eliciting questions to elicit and represent LLM’s values. We prepare a set of λ value eliciting
178   questions {Qj }λj=1 , and get the corresponding answers from each LLM. We denote a set of QA pairs
179   as X = {⟨Qj , Aj ⟩}λj=1 .
180
181
182   2.4   M ULTI - VIEW VALUE E MBEDDING L EARNING
183
184   With a large X, there is sufficient guidance to maximize its dependency to ϑvalue . However, this X
185   might share value-irrelevant information such as wording and syntax, which makes the second term,
186   i.e., minimizing superfluity, not satisfied.
187   To eliminate these irrelevant information, we compress X by applying multi-view learning (Tsai
188   et al., 2021; Shwartz Ziv & LeCun, 2024). Such strategy has already shown its effectiveness in learn-
189   ing compressed representation for various applications, such as for sentence semantics (Reimers &
190   Gurevych, 2019), facial identity (Taigman et al., 2014), object category in images (Chen et al.,
191   2020a), etc. As shown in Figure 2 (Right), we sample two views X1 , X2 (two sets of value-eliciting
192   questions and answers) that share the same values. We adopt a Siamese network with shared encoder
193
      g and takes two views as input producing representations ZX1 = g(X1 ) and ZX2 = g(X2 ). We
      then optimize g towards maximizing the mutual information across two views:
194
195
196
197                                                max I(ZX1 ; ZX2 ).                                              (2)
                                                     g
198
199
200   The principle of constructing views X1 , X2 is to ensure that these QA pairs share the same human
201   value while not having other superfluous correlations. If two views share ϑvalue (i.e., satisfying
202   the multi-view assumption), maximizing mutual information between views will enforce g to capture
203
      the shared values information (Shwartz Ziv & LeCun, 2024). Conversely, g will compress X but
      retain some superfluous information I(X1 ; X2 |ϑvalue ) that is shared by X1 , X2 but irrelevant to
204
      ϑvalue (Tsai et al., 2021). Ensuring both requirements optimizes the objective in Eq. 1.
205
206   As LLM in each language has a distinct ϑvalue (Lin et al., 2022; Durmus et al., 2023; AlKhamissi
207   et al., 2024), we treat different language in an LLM as a different ϑvalue (i.e., LLM values of Chat-
208   GPT English and of ChatGPT Chinese are distinct). Using prompts in various languages leads to
209
      diverse responses (Lin et al., 2022) and prompts in a culture’s dominant language typically align
      more with that culture (AlKhamissi et al., 2024) 1 . To ensure minimal sharing of linguistics as-
210
      pect across views, we preprocess the X by translating all the value-eliciting QAs to English and
211
      paraphrasing the QAs to increase the diversity.

      3.1        T RAINING
236
237   Preparing Value-Eliciting QA Figure 3 outlines our value-eliciting QA pipeline. We start by
238   compiling 87 reference human values from multiple human value studies including World Value
239   Survey (WVS) (Inglehart et al., 2000; Inglehart, 2004; 2006), cultural dimensions theory (Hofst-
240   ede, 2001; Hofstede et al., 2005; House et al., 2004; Hofstede, 2011), theory of basic human val-
241   ues (Schwartz, 1994; 1999; 2004; 2008; 2012; Schmidt et al., 2007; Beierlein et al., 2012), the
242   refined theory of values (Schwartz & Cieciuch, 2022) and Rokeach Value Survey (Rokeach, 1968;
243   1973; 1979; 2008). For each reference value (e.g., Individualism vs Collectivism), we use LLMs to
244
      generate 50 relevant value-eliciting questions Q ∈ Qvalue (see §D.2 for examples). After manually
      verifying and filtering our irrelevant questions, we retain 4,296 questions. To enhance robustness,
245
      we paraphrase each question 4 times, resulting in a total data size of 21,480 (4,296 × 5) questions.
246
      These questions are then translated into 25 languages to better understand the values expressed by
247   LLMs across different languages. The details of prompts for constructing value-eliciting questions
248   are in Appendix D.1.
249
250
      To obtain the corresponding answers, the value-eliciting questions in different languages are fed
      into LLMs. In order to minimize linguistic variations across different languages, all non-English
251
      question-answer pairs are then machine-translated into English. This translation step eliminates lan-
252
      guage information from becoming a confounding factor when training UniVaR since it is irrelevant
253   to human values. Overall, we collected ∼1M QA pairs for training. For translation, we employ the
254   widely used NLLB-200 (3.3B) model (Team et al., 2022). 2
255
256   Model and Language Coverage We incorporate 15 off-the-shelf chat or instruction-following
257   LLMs (Sanh et al., 2022; Muennighoff et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; Longpre et al., 2023) to ensure
258   their ability to answer the given query. We prioritize LLMs that have undergone human value and
259   preference tuning such as safety tuning (Zhang et al., 2023b; Meade et al., 2023; Bianchi et al.,
260   2024), RLHF (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022), or DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024). Out of
261   15 LLMs, we incorporate QAs from 8 LLMs for training and leave the other 7 as unseen LLMs for
262   validation and evaluation. We support 25 languages which are considered high-resource languages
263
      within LLMs under study. In total, we have 127 distinct LLM-language pairs. The list of LLMs and
      languages is shown in Appendix §C.

283   3.2      E VALUATION
284
285   Task We want to evaluate whether UniVaR embeddings can distinguish similar and different
286   human values in LLMs. We introduce an LLM value identification task, where we measure the
287   accuracy of classifying a given QA pair to the value ID. The value ID refers to LLM-language pairs,
288   e.g., GPT-4 English and GPT-4 Chinese are considered to be distinct, while two QA pairs that both
289   come from GPT-4 English share the same value (Lin et al., 2022; Durmus et al., 2023; AlKhamissi
290   et al., 2024). To measure the identification accuracy, we follow the standard practice of k-Nearest-
291
      Neighbour(kNN) classification and linear probing with frozen features.
292
293   Data We construct an evaluation dataset based on 4 sources, covering 3 well-established value
294   questionnaires in the field of social science and psychology – i.e., PVQ-RR (Schwartz, 2017;
295
      Schwartz & Cieciuch, 2022), WVS (Inglehart et al., 2000; Inglehart, 2004), and GLOBE sur-
      vey (House et al., 2004; Javidan & Dastmalchian, 2009) – and ValuePrism (Sorensen et al., 2024)
296
      – a large-scale value dataset for endowing AI with pluralistic human values, rights, and duties. 4
297
      These data sources do not originally provide natural questions for LLMs, hence we employ Mixtral
298   8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024) to generate value-eliciting questions based on the context provided in the
299   data sources (See §B.2 for details). We then translate the questions into 25 languages as detailed
300   in Appendix C. Using the multilingual questions, we generate the answers using all LLMs under
301   study on the languages that are supported by each of the LLMs, and then translated the QA back to
302   English. The resulting English-only value-eliciting QAs data is used for evaluating the effectiveness
303   of UniVaR.
304
305   Baselines The existing embedding focuses on semantic embeddings which may not capture human
306   value space at all or combined with other features. We want to highlight existing semantic embed-
307   ding has limitations for capturing human values. We compare UniVaR to word embedding model,
308   i.e., GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014a) and various sentence embedding models, i.e., RoBERTa (Liu
309   et al., 2019), XLM-R (Conneau et al., 2020), MPNet (Song et al., 2020), Nomic Embed v1 (Nuss-
310   baum et al., 2024), and LaBSE (Feng et al., 2022).

405   4.2   M AP OF U N I V A R R EPRESENTATIONS
406
407   Inspired by human value maps such as Hofstede’s Globe (Hofstede, 2001; Hofstede et al., 2005;
408   Hofstede, 2011; Hofstede & Minkov, 2013) and World Cultural Map (Inglehart et al., 2000; Ingle-
409
      hart, 2004; 2006) , we introduce a value map of LLMs to visualize the human values embedded
      in LLMs. To create the value map independent from the training data, we utilized the QAs from
410
      four value-eliciting question sources described in § 3.2. We encode each QA using UniVaR and
411
      we visualize the map of LLM values by projecting the value embeddings into a 2D plane using
412   UMAP (McInnes & Healy, 2018). The result of the value distributions are shown as a “world map”
413   in Figure 1. In general, we observe that value QA pairs in the same language from different LLMs
414   are clustered together, which show that the values embedded in LLMs largely come from the culture
415   of the language they are trained in. In this case, language acts as a proxy for culture (AlKhamissi
416   et al., 2024).
417
418   Relation between LLM Values and Human Cultures There is also a separation of value distri-
419   bution between LLMs in different languages as shown in Figure 5. The distance of values across
420   different languages also signifies the similarities and differences of human values between different
421   cultures. For instance, ”Chinese-Japanese-Korean”, ”German-French-Spanish”, and ”Indonesian-
422   Arabic-Malaysian” are closer in value distribution compared to the other language pairs with a
423
      relatively distant culture. German, French, and Spanish share similar European values. Chinese,
      Japanese, and Korean share similar Confucian and Buddhist values. Indonesian, Malaysian, and
424
      Arabic cultures share Islamic values, despite the linguistic difference between Indonesia/Malay and
425
      Arabic. Interestingly, English value distribution is relatively far from that of French, German, Italian,
426   and Spanish, despite originating from countries with Western values. This agrees with the human
427   value map in World Value Survey (Inglehart et al., 2000; Inglehart, 2004; 2006) (see Figure 5 (right)),
428   where English-speaking societies are categorized into their own group due to the impact of coloniza-
429   tion and massive immigration from the colonial society (Crystal, 2003; Tardy, 2004; Smokotin et al.,
430   2014; Suzina, 2020). As shown in Figure 7, this pattern is also consistent across four different value
431   corpora indicating that the value representation in UniVaR is robust to the variability of questions.

481   The adoption of LLMs across various fields necessitates understanding how these models represent
482   human values. Our paper introduces UniVaR, a high-dimensional, language- and model-invariant
483   representation, that enables a better understanding of the human value aspect in LLMs. UniVaR
484   allows us to examine how different LLMs prioritize values across languages and cultures, shed-
485   ding light on the complex interplay between human values and AI systems. Our approach enables
      us to statistically analyze the value systems embedded in LLMs, providing transparency and ac-

       Value Alignment in LLMs LLMs are aligned to human values for enhanced service and reduced
1083   risks (Liu et al., 2023b) with three major goals (Yao et al., 2023): teaching LLMs to follow hu-
1084   man instructions (Ouyang et al., 2022), aligning LLMs to implicit human preferences (Christiano
1085   et al., 2017), and conforming LLMs to pre-defined principles (Bai et al., 2022b). Value alignment
1086   typically involves Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and RLHF/RLAIF. In SFT, models are fine-tuned
1087   using well-curated conversation data data (Köpf et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Nakano et al., 2021;
1088   Shen et al., 2023) following human desirable features (Yao et al., 2023; Scheurer et al., 2023;
1089   Köpf et al., 2024; Glaese et al., 2022; Ganguli et al., 2022) through various training paradigms
1090   such as contrastive learning (Adolphs et al., 2023; Khalatbari et al., 2023) and distillation (Hong
1091
       et al., 2023). RLHF, commonly used by recent LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023;
       AI@Meta, 2024), adjusts models’ policies through RL by receiving feedback from a reward model
1092
       aligned with human preferences as in Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017).
1093
       Unlike PPO , Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), eliminates reliance on
1094   a reward model. Similarly, RLAIF (Lee et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Honovich et al., 2023; Liu
1095   et al., 2023a) generates feedback from the model itself to avoid costly human annotations. While
1096   RLHF implicitly elicits preferences from ranking data, Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022b) estab-
1097   lishes principles for AI to enhance model alignment to explicitly-defined human values through
1098   self-critique and response modification.
1099
1100   Surveying Human Values in LLMs Early studies on understanding human values in language
1101   models, such as the ETHICS dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2020), cover various ethical frameworks
1102   including justice, deontology, virtue ethics, and utilitarianism. Zhang et al. (2023a) further analyzed
1103   how language models categorize and reason about different values. Related research includes exam-
1104
       ining alignment with diverse societal views and stances, referencing global opinion surveys like the
       Pew Global Attitudes (PEW) and World Values Surveys (WVS) (Inglehart et al., 2000; Inglehart,
1105
       2006; Haerpfer et al., 2022a). Studies such as Durmus et al. (2023) and AlKhamissi et al. (2024)
1106
       specifically focus on cultural and social value alignment in language models, using data from these
1107   surveys. Zhang et al. (2024) employ social value orientation (SVO) measures to assess the alignment
1108   of language models with human values. Our work aims to develop methods for capturing complex
1109   human values in high-dimensional spaces to enhance understanding and verification of language
1110   models’ alignment with human values.
1111
1112   High-Dimension Embedding Representation Distributed representations of entities (Hinton,
1113   1984) underpinned the advancement of embedding representation, enabling algorithms to capture
1114   nuanced semantic relationships and enhance generalization capabilities. Seminal works in NLP laid
1115   the groundwork for word embeddings (Hinton et al., 1986; Rumelhart et al., 1986; Elman, 1990;
1116   Mikolov et al., 2013b). This progress was further accelerated by Mikolov et al. (2013a); Penning-
1117
       ton et al. (2014b), who refined methods to generate word vectors, subsequently enriching research
       on sub-word and sentence-level embeddings (Britz et al., 2017; Kudo & Richardson, 2018; Reimers
1118
       & Gurevych, 2019). In parallel, computer vision benefited from embedding techniques to capture
1119
       object representations (Gui et al., 2016; Mettes & Snoek, 2017; He et al., 2017), with recent ex-
1120   pansions into sub-object representations (Chen et al., 2024) demonstrating the versatility of this
1121   approach. Embedding has also been applied in healthcare and recommendation systems to model
1122   complex behaviors (Choi et al., 2016; Covington et al., 2016; Cahyawijaya et al., 2022). Our work
1123   extends the embedding paradigm to abstract value representations elicited by LLMs, advancing the
1124   applicability of embedding representations in understanding LLM preferences.

1719
1720   Coverage of Values We used a combination of existing value taxonomies as a starting point for
1721   the value-eliciting QAs resulting in 87 core values. Human value taxonomy is not a fixed entity
1722   and some philosophers think that we can never have a comprehensive human value taxonomy. The
1723   research on human values in philosophy, social science, and psychology is ongoing; and there are
1724   more crowd-sourcing efforts for collective value datasets. Our approach is agnostic to taxonomy
1725   development and can be updated with future taxonomies of human values and preferences.
1726
1727   Coverage of LLMs Our work underscores the significant finding that values encoded in LLMs
       vary across languages, reflecting the similarities and differences in human values between diverse


                                                       32
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1728
       cultures. While our study provides valuable insights, it only studied 15 LLMs, with 7 unseen LLMs
1729   in 25 languages across 4 value-eliciting question sources. Our current result does not cover the full
1730   diversity of LLMs, languages, or taxonomy sources. We will release the tool and invite the makers
1731   of LLMs to extend the coverage to build a more comprehensive and holistic value coverage across
1732   more LLMs, languages, and taxonomies in future work.
