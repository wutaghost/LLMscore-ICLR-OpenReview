009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Entropic optimal transport (OT) and the Sinkhorn algorithm have made it practi-
012            cal for machine learning practitioners to perform the fundamental task of calcu-
013            lating transport distance between statistical distributions. In this work, we focus
014            on a general class of OT problems under a combination of equality and inequality
015            constraints. We derive the corresponding entropy regularization formulation and
016
               introduce a Sinkhorn-type algorithm for such constrained OT problems supported
               by theoretical guarantees. We first bound the approximation error when solving
017
               the problem through entropic regularization, which reduces exponentially with
018
               the increase of the regularization parameter. Furthermore, we prove a sublinear
019            first-order convergence rate of the proposed Sinkhorn-type algorithm in the dual
020            space by characterizing the optimization procedure with a Lyapunov function.
021            To achieve fast and higher-order convergence under weak entropy regularization,
022            we augment the Sinkhorn-type algorithm with dynamic regularization schedul-
023            ing and second-order acceleration. Overall, this work systematically combines
024            recent theoretical and numerical advances in entropic optimal transport with the
025            constrained case, allowing practitioners to derive approximate transport plans in
026            complex scenarios. In addition, we extend the formulation of this work to partial
027
               optimal transport and propose a fast algorithm with practical super-exponential
               convergence.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Obtaining the optimal transport (OT) (Villani et al., 2009; Linial et al., 1998; Peyré et al., 2019)
033   plan between statistical distributions is an important subroutine in machine learning (Sandler and
034   Lindenbaum, 2011; Jitkrittum et al., 2016; Arjovsky et al., 2017; Salimans et al., 2018; Genevay
035   et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020; Fatras et al., 2021). In this work, we focus on an optimal transportation
036   problem with a combination of inequality and equality constraints. A typical example is an OT
037   problem with one inequality constraint:
038                                       min             C · P, such that D · P ≥ t,                       (1)
039                               P :P 1=r,P ⊤ 1=c,P ≥0

040   where · stand for entry-wise inner product, C ∈ Rn×n is the cost matrix, t ∈ R, D ∈ Rn×n
041   encode the inequality constraint, and c, r ∈ Rn are respectively the source and target density. As
042   an illustration, in Figure 1, we plot the optimal transport plan between 1D distributions when the
043   main cost is the transport cost induced by the l1 Manhattan distance, but an inequality constraint
044   is placed on the transport cost induced by Euclidean distance. One can see that solving inequality-
045
      constrained OT problems in equation 1 allows one to obtain transport maps under more complex
      geometric structures.
046
047   For the unconstrained OT problem, the most important recent breakthrough is the introduction of
048   entropic regularization and the resultant Sinkhorn algorithm (Yule, 1912; Sinkhorn, 1964; Cuturi,
049   2013). With simple matrix scaling steps, the Sinkhorn algorithm gives an approximate OT solution
050   in near-linear time (Altschuler et al., 2017), which fuels the wide adoption in the machine learning
051
      community. This prompts the following natural question:
052
053       Is there an extension of the Sinkhorn algorithm to constrained optimal transport problems?

083   In addition to the introduction of a novel Sinkhorn-type algorithm for constrained OT, this paper
084   systematically generalizes existing theoretical results as well as practical acceleration techniques
085   from the existing entropic OT literature. In particular,

088       strained case is exponentially close to the optimal solution.

      1.2      R ELATED LITERATURE
103
104   Constrained optimal transport The numerical treatment for constrained OT focuses on special
105   cases such as capacity constraints (Korman and McCann, 2013; 2015), multi-marginal transport
106   (Gangbo and Swiech, 1998; Buttazzo et al., 2012; Benamou et al., 2015; Pass, 2015; Khoo et al.,
107   2020), martingale optimal transport (Tan and Touzi, 2013; Beiglböck et al., 2013; Galichon et al.,
      2014; Dolinsky and Soner, 2014; Guo and Obłój, 2019), and partial optimal transport (Chapel et al.,


                                                                     2
108
      2020; Le et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2022; 2024). In terms of numerical methods for constrained
109   OT, Benamou et al. (2015) defines an iterative Bregman projection approach for equality constraints
110   and a Bregman–Dykstra iteration for general constraints. We show in Appendix I that the iterative
111   Bregman projection is equivalent to our proposed update step in the equality case. Our contribution
112   in relation to Benamou et al. (2015) is threefold. First, for equality constraints, we propose a gener-
113   alized Sinkhorn-type algorithm that implements the iterative Bregman projection, whereas previous
114   work only defines the projection step in special cases. Second, for inequality constraint, our ap-
115   proach is novel and enjoys theoretical guarantee as seen in our Theorem 1 and Theorem 2. Finally,
116   the Bregman–Dykstra iteration in (Benamou et al., 2015) requires a simple closed-form solution for
117
      each projection step, whereas our proposed method applies to general cases.
118
119
      OT in machine learning A substantial amount of research literature exists on the use of optimal
      transport in different areas of machine learning. Notable applications include statistical learning
120
      (Kolouri et al., 2017; Vayer et al., 2018; Genevay et al., 2019; Luise et al., 2018; Oneto et al., 2020;
121
      Huynh et al., 2020), domain adaptation (Fernando et al., 2013; Redko et al., 2017; Courty et al.,
122   2017; Alvarez-Melis et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2022; 2021; Xu et al., 2022; Turrisi et al., 2022),
123   and using optimal transport distance in designing training targets (Genevay et al., 2017; Bousquet
124   et al., 2017; Sanjabi et al., 2018; Deshpande et al., 2019; Lei et al., 2019; Patrini et al., 2020; Onken
125   et al., 2021). The usage of entropic optimal transport in the constrained case allows practitioners to
126   query transport plans which satisfy more complex structure than in the unconstrained case, which
127   are beneficial across all the applications above.
128
129   Acceleration for Sinkhorn There is a considerable body of work in speeding up the runtime of the
130   Sinkhorn algorithm, and a significant portion of our numerical treatment is devoted to the extension
131   of these algorithms to the constrained case. In addition to (Chen et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024) to
132
      be discussed in Section 3, we mention a few noteworthy methods for acceleration. Randomized or
      greedy row/column scaling (Genevay et al., 2016; Altschuler et al., 2017) can be directly extended
133
      to the constrained case by the inclusion of the dual variables for the linear constraints. Likewise,
134
      methods based on the variational form of entropic OT, such as Nesterov acceleration (Dvurechensky
135   et al., 2018) and mirror descent (Lin et al., 2019; Kemertas et al., 2023), can be used in the con-
136   strained case by considering the Lyapunov function introduced in Section 2. While the majority of
137   existing techniques can be directly extended to the constrained case, one notable exception is the
138   class of methods based on approximation to the kernel K = exp(−Cη) (Deriche, 1993; Solomon
139   et al., 2015; Bonneel et al., 2016; Altschuler et al., 2019; Scetbon and Cuturi, 2020; Lakshmanan
140   et al., 2022; Huguet et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). The main obstruction from performing kernel
141   approximation in the constrained case is that the kernel is changing throughout the optimization
142   process due to update in the constraint associated dual variables. As the kernel compression step is
143
      costly and often done in the offline stage, it poses a significant challenge if one has to update the
      kernel approximation with each dual variable update dynamically.

308   3.1   ACCELERATION TECHNIQUES
309
      Entropy regularization scheduling An important feature of the Sinkhorn algorithm is that the
310
      iteration complexity heavily depends on the entropy regularization term η, the tuning of which
311   plays a significant part in practical performance. To that end, one can aid acceleration by using
312   the doubling entropy regularization scheduling technique introduced in (Chen et al., 2023). For
313   a desired entropy regularization value ηfinal , we take an initial regularization strength ηinit and
314   take Nη = ⌈log2 (ηfinal /ηinit )⌉. Then, one defines successively doubling regularization levels
315   η0 , . . . , ηNη so that ηinit = η0 < . . . < ηNη = ηfinal and ηi = 2ηi−1 for i = 1, . . . , Nη − 1.
316   For each step i, one runs the subroutine in Algorithm 1 at ηi−1 , and the obtained dual variable is
317   used as the initialization when calling the subroutine for ηi .
318   Second-order acceleration through sparsity We further accelerate the Sinkhorn-type algorithm
319   with Sinkhorn-Newton-Sparse (SNS) (Tang et al., 2024), a second-order method originally devel-
320   oped for the unconstrained case. Instead of alternating maximization as in Algorithm 1, one can
321   instead introduce the combined variable z = (x, y, a), where a naive strategy would be to optimize
322   directly f through a full Newton update of the type z = z − α(∇2z f )−1 ∇z f , where α can be ob-
323   tained through backtracking line search. However, a full Newton step would require an impractical
      O((n + K + L)3 ) scaling.

326   key observation of SNS is that the Hessian submatrix corresponding to variable x, y has a special
327   structure:

340   We propose an extended SNS algorithm by sparsifying the ∇2xy f block in which one keeps only

      6    C ONCLUSION
523
524   We introduce an entropic formulation of optimal transport with a combination of additional equality
525
      and inequality constraints. We propose a Sinkhorn-type algorithm that has a novel constraint dual
      variable update step. We provide preliminary results on the approximation error of the entropic for-
526
      mulation and the convergence of the Sinkhorn-type algorithm. A future direction is a more refined
527
      analysis on the convergence property of Algorithm 1 to the optimal entropic solution Pη⋆ . More-
528   over, we provide a detailed discussion on improving the proposed Sinkhorn-type algorithm with
529   acceleration techniques, especially Sinkhorn-Newton-Sparse and entropy regularization scheduling.
530   The proposed work enables one to obtain approximately optimal solutions in more complicated OT
531   instances efficiently. We contend that this work has the potential to be a vital tool in the field of opti-
532   mal transport under constraint and in attracting the use of constrained optimal transport in machine
533   learning. While the entropic barrier function for the constrained optimal transport has shown robust
534   performance, the use of other barrier functions such as the log barrier might lend the formulation
535   added flexibility and may enjoy good practical performance.

1759   This section presents the result of quasi-Newton algorithms (Nocedal and Wright, 1999) applied to
1760   the variational form of constrained optimal transport problems. We show that, while being a rea-
1761

1762

1763

1764   of sparse approximation, a quasi-Newton method approximates the Hessian matrix through the his-
1765   tory of gradient information. In particular, we consider the Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno
1766   (BFGS) algorithm and the limited-memory Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno (L-BFGS) algo-
