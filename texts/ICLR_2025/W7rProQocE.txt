009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Spectral Embedding (SE) is a popular method for dimensionality reduction, ap-
012            plicable across diverse domains. Nevertheless, its current implementations face
013            three prominent drawbacks which curtail its broader applicability: generalizabil-
014            ity (i.e., out-of-sample extension), scalability, and eigenvectors separation. In this
015            paper, we introduce GrEASE: Generalizable and Efficient Approximate Spectral
016            Embedding, a novel deep-learning approach designed to address these limitations.
017            GrEASE incorporates an efficient post-processing step to achieve eigenvectors
018
               separation, while ensuring both generalizability and scalability, allowing for the
               computation of the Laplacian’s eigenvectors on unseen data. This method expands
019
               the applicability of SE to a wider range of tasks and can enhance its performance
020
               in existing applications. We empirically demonstrate GrEASE’s ability to consis-
021            tently approximate and generalize SE, while ensuring scalability. Additionally, we
022            show how GrEASE can be leveraged to enhance existing methods. Specifically,
023            we focus on UMAP, a leading visualization technique, and introduce NUMAP, a
024            generalizable version of UMAP powered by GrEASE. Our code will be publicly
025            available upon acceptance.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Spectral Embedding (SE) is a popular non-linear dimensionality reduction method (Belkin &
031   Niyogi, 2003; Coifman & Lafon, 2006b), finding extensive utilization across diverse domains in
032   recent literature. Notable applications include UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) (the current state-of-
033
      the-art visualization method), Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Zhang et al., 2021; Beaini et al.,
      2021) and Graph Convolutional Neural Networks (GCNs) (Defferrard et al., 2016), positional en-
034
      coding for Graph Transformers (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020; Kreuzer et al., 2021) and analysis of
035
      proteins (Campbell et al., 2015; Kundu et al., 2004; Shepherd et al., 2007; Zhu & Schlick, 2021).
036   The core of SE involves a projection of the samples into the space spanned by the leading eigenvec-
037   tors of the Laplacian matrix (i.e., those corresponding to the smallest eigenvalues), derived from the
038   pairwise similarities between the samples. SE is an expressive method which is able to preserve the
039   global structure of high-dimensional input data, underpinned by robust mathematical foundations
040   (Belkin & Niyogi, 2003; Katz et al., 2019; Lederman & Talmon, 2018; Ortega et al., 2018).
041
      Despite the popularity and significance of SE, current implementations suffer from three main draw-
042   backs: (1) Generalizability - the ability to directly embed a new set of test points after completing the
043   computation on a training set (i.e., out-of-sample extension); (2) Scalability - the ability to handle
044   a large number of samples within a reasonable timeframe; (3) Eigenvectors separation - the ability
045   to output the basis of the leading eigenvectors (v2 , . . . , vk+1 ), rather than only the space spanned
046   by them. These three properties are crucial for modern applications of SE in machine learning. No-
047   tably, the last property has attracted considerable attention in recent years (Pfau et al., 2018; Gemp
048   et al., 2020; Deng et al., 2022; Lim et al., 2022). While most SE implementations address two of
049   these three limitations, they often fall short in addressing the remaining one (see Sec. 2).
050   This paper extends the work by Shaham et al. (2018), known as SpectralNet. SpectralNet tack-
051   les the scalability and generalizability limitations of Spectral Clustering (SC), a key application of
052   SE. However, due to a rotation and reflection ambiguity in its loss function, SpectralNet cannot di-
053   rectly be adapted for SE (i.e., it cannot separate the eigenvectors). In this paper, we first present a
      post-processing procedure to resolve the eigenvectors separation issue in SpectralNet, thereby, cre-

101
102   Current SE implementations typically address two out of its three primary limitations: generalizabil-
103   ity, scalability, and eigenvector separation. Below, we outline key implementations that tackle each
104   pair of these challenges. Following this, we discuss recent works related to eigenvectors separation
105   and generalizable visualizations techniques.
106
107   Scalable with eigenvectors separation. Popular implementations of SE are mostly based on
      sparse matrix decomposition techniques (e.g., ARPACK (Lehoucq et al., 1998), AMG (Brandt et al.,


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      1984), LOBPCG (Benner & Mach, 2011)). These methods are relatively scalable, as they are al-
109   most linear in the number of samples. Nevertheless, their out-of-sample extension is far from trivial.
110   Usually, it is done by out-of-sample extension (OOSE) methods such as Nyström extension method
111   (Nyström, 1930) or Geometric Harmonics (Coifman & Lafon, 2006a; Lafon et al., 2006). However,
112   these methods provide only local extension (i.e., near existing training points), and are both compu-
113   tationally and memory restrictive, as they rely on computing the distances between every new test
114   point and all training points.
115
116   Scalable and generalizable. Several approaches to SC approximate the space spanned by the first
117   eigenvectors of the Laplacian matrix, which is sufficient for clustering purposes, and can also benefit
118   other specific applications. For example, SpectralNet (Shaham et al., 2018) leverages deep neural
119
      networks to approximate the first eigenfunctions of the Laplace-Beltrami operator in a scalable man-
      ner, thus also enabling fast inference of new unseen samples. BASiS (Streicher et al., 2023) achieves
120
      these goals using affine registration techniques to align batches. However, these methods’ inability
121
      to separate the eigenvectors prevents their use in many modern applications.
122
123
      Generalizable with eigenvectors separation. Another proposed approach to SE is Diffusion-
124   Net (Mishne et al., 2019), a deep-learning framework for generalizable Diffusion Maps embedding
125   (Coifman & Lafon, 2006b), which is similar to SE. However, the training procedure of the network
126   is computationally expensive, therefore restricting its usage for large datasets.
127
      In contrast, we introduce GrEASE, which generalizes the separated eigenvectors to unseen points
128
      with a single feed-forward operation, while maintaining scalability.
129
130
      Eigenvectors separation. Extensive research has been conducted on the eigenvectors separation
131
      problem, both within and beyond the spectral domain (Lim et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2024). How-
132   ever, recent suggestions are constrained computationally, both by extensive run-time and memory
133   consumption. For example, Pfau et al. (2018) proposed a solution to this issue by masking the gra-
134   dient information from the loss function. However, this approach necessitates the computation of
135   full Jacobians at each time step, which is highly computationally intensive. Gemp et al. (2020) em-
136   ploys an iterative method to learn each eigenvector sequentially. Namely, they learn an eigenvector
137   while keeping the others frozen. This process has to be repeated k times (where k is the embed-
138   ding dimension), which makes this approach also computationally expensive. Deng et al. (2022)
139   proposed an improvement of the latter, by parallel training of k NNs. However, as discussed in their
140
      paper, this approach becomes costly for large values of k. Furthermore, it necessitates retaining k
      trained networks in memory, which leads to significant memory consumption. Chen et al. (2022)
141
      proposed a post-processing solution to this problem using the Rayleigh-Ritz method. However, this
142
      approach involves the storage and multiplication of very large dense matrices, rendering it imprac-
143   tical for large datasets. In contrast, GrEASE offers an efficient one-shot post-processing solution to
144   the eigenvectors separation problem.
145
146   Generalizable visualization. Several works have attempted to develop parametric approximations
147   for non-parametric visualization methods, in addition to Parametric UMAP (P. UMAP) (Sainburg
148   et al., 2021). Notable examples include (Van Der Maaten, 2009) and (Kawase et al., 2022), which
149   use NNs to make t-SNE generalizable, and (Schofield & Lensen, 2021), which aims to make UMAP
150   more interpretable. However, P. UMAP has demonstrated superior performance. NUMAP presents
151   a method to surpass P. UMAP in terms of global structure preservation.

      using deep neural networks, for example SpectralNet (Shaham et al., 2018). SpectralNet follows a
179

180
      vectors to an optimization problem, through minimization of the Rayleigh Quotient (RQ).

194   3.3   UMAP AND PARAMETRIC UMAP
195
196   UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) is the current state-of-the-art visualization method. UMAP presents a
197   significant advancement over previous methods, primarily due to its enhanced scalability and supe-
198   rior ability to preserve global structure. This approach involves the construction of a graph from the
199   input high-dimensional data and the learning of a low-dimensional representation. The objective is
200
      to minimize the KL-divergence between the input data graph and the representation graph.
201   However, as discussed in (Kobak & Linderman, 2021), UMAP primarily derives its global preserva-
202   tion abilities, as well as its consistency, from initializing the representations using SE. Therefore, the
203   SE initialization serves as a critical step for UMAP to uphold the global structure (see demonstration
204   in Fig. 1). Global preservation, in this context, refers to the separation of different classes, and avoid-
205   ing the separation of existing classes. We refer the reader to (Kobak & Linderman, 2021) for a more
206
      comprehensive discussion about the effects of informative initialization on UMAP’s performance.
207   UMAP method can be divided into three components (summarized in Fig. 3): (1) constructing a
208   graph which best captures the global structure of the input data; (2) initializing the representations
209   via SE; (3) Learning the representations, via SGD, which best capture the original graph. This setup
210   does not facilitate generalization, as both steps (2) and (3) lack generalizability.
211   Recently, a generalizable version of UMAP, known as Parametric UMAP (P. UMAP), was intro-
212   duced (Sainburg et al., 2021). P. UMAP replaces step (3) with the training of a neural network.
213   Importantly, it overlooks step (2), the SE initialization. Consequently, P. UMAP struggles to pre-
214   serve global structure, particularly when dealing with non-linear structures. Fig. 1 illustrates this
215   phenomenon with several non-linear yet simple structures. Noticeably, P. UMAP fails to preserve
      global structure (e.g., it does not separate different clusters).

      4.1    M OTIVATION
219
220   It is well known that the matrix V , containing the first k eigenvectors of L (i.e., those corresponding
221   to the k smallest eigenvalues) as its columns, minimizes RL (A) under orthogonality constraint (i.e.
222   AT A = I) (Li, 2015).
223   However, a rotation and reflection ambiguity of the RQ prohibits a trivial adaptation of this concept
224   to SE. Basic properties of trace imply that for any orthogonal matrix Q ∈ Rk×k the matrix U := V Q
225   satisfies
226
            RL (U ) = Tr(U T LU ) = Tr(QT V T LV Q) = Tr(QQT V T LV ) = Tr(V T LV ) = RL (V )
227
228   Thus, every such U also minimizes RL under the orthogonality constraint, and therefore this kind
229   of minimization solely is missing eigenvectors separation, which is crucial for many applications.
230
231   4.2    G R EASE
232
233
      In fact, we prove that the aforementioned form V Q is the only form of a minimizer of RL under the
      orthogonality constraint.
234
235   Lemma 1. Every minimizer of RL under the orthogonality constraint, is of the form V Q, where V
236   is the first k eigenvectors matrix of L and Q is an arbitrary squared orthogonal matrix.
237
      The proof of Lemma 1 appears in Appendix A.
238
239   Lemma 1 implies that SpectralNet’s method, using a deep neural network for RQ minimization
240   (while enforcing orthogonality), does not lead to the SE. However, it only leads to the space spanned
241   by the constant vector and the leading k−1 eigenvectors of L, with different rotations and reflections
242
      for each run. Therefore, each time the RQ is minimized, it results in a different linear combination
      of the smallest eigenvectors. Although this is sufficient for clustering purposes, as we search for
243
      reproducibility, consistency, and separation of the eigenvectors, the RQ cannot solely provide the
244
      SE, necessitating the development of new techniques in GrEASE.
245
246   Setup. Here we present the two key components of GrEASE, a scalable and generalizable SE
247   method. We consider the following setup: Given a training set X ⊆ Rd and a target dimension k,
248   we construct an affinity matrix W , and compute an approximation of the leading eigenvectors of its
249   corresponding Laplacian. In practice, we first utilize SpectralNet (Shaham et al., 2018) to approx-
250   imate the space spanned by the first k + 1 eigenfunctions of the corresponding Laplace-Beltrami
251   operator, and then find each of the k leading eigenfunctions within this space (i.e. the SE). Namely,
252   GrEASE computes a map Fθ : Rd → Rk , which approximates the map f¯ = (f2 , . . . , fk+1 ), where
253   fi is the ith eigenfunction of the Laplace-Beltrami operator on the underlying manifold D.
254
255   Eigenspace approximation. As empirically showed in (Shaham et al., 2018), and motivated from
256
      Lemma 1, SpectralNet loss is minimized when Fθ = T ◦ (f1 , . . . , fk+1 ), where T : Rk+1 → Rk+1
      is an arbitrary isometry. Namely, Fθ approximates the space spanned by the first k + 1 eigenfunc-
257
      tions. However, the SE (i.e. each of the leading eigenfunctions) is poorly approximated. Each time
258
      the RQ is minimized, the eigenfunctions are approximated up to a different isometry T . Fig. 2a
259   demonstrates this phenomenon on the toy moon dataset - a noisy half circle linearly embedded into
260   10-dimension input space (see Sec. 5.1). Employing SpectralNet approach indeed enables us to
261   consistently achieve a perfect approximation of the space (i.e., the errors are accumulated around 0).
262   However, when comparing vector to vector, it becomes apparent that the SE was seldom attained.
263

      4.4   A DDITIONAL A PPLICATIONS
353
354   In this section we seek to highlight GrEASE’s potential impact on important tasks and applica-
355   tions (besides UMAP), as it integrates generalizability, scalability and eigenvectors separation. As
356   discussed in Sec. 1, SE is applied across various domains, many of which can benefit generaliz-
357   ability capabilities by simply replacing the current SE implementation with GrEASE. We therefore
358   elaborate herein the significance of SE in selected applications, and discuss how GrEASE, as a
359   generalizable approximation of it, can enhance their effectiveness and applicability.
360
361   Fiedler vector and value. A special case of SE is the Fiedler vector and value (Fiedler, 1973;
362
      1975). The Fiedler value, also known as algebraic connectivity, refers to the second eigenvalue of the
      Laplacian matrix, while the Fiedler vector refers to the associated eigenvector. This value quantifies
363
      the connectivity of a graph, increasing as the graph becomes more connected. Specifically, if a graph
364
      is not connected, its Fiedler value is 0. The Fiedler vector and value are a main topic of many works
365   (Andersen et al., 2006; Barnard et al., 1993; Kundu et al., 2004; Shepherd et al., 2007; Cai et al.,
366   2018; Zhu & Schlick, 2021; Tam & Dunson, 2020).
367
368
      As GrEASE is able to distinguish between the eigenvectors and approximate the eigenvalues, it has
      the capability to approximate both the Fiedler vector and value, while also generalizing the vector
369
      to unseen samples (see Sec. 5.1).
370
371

372   Laplacian matrix, is Diffusion Maps (Coifman & Lafon, 2006b). Diffusion Maps embeds a graph
373   (or a manifold) into a space where the pairwise Euclidean distances are equivalent to the pairwise
374   Diffusion distances on the graph.

398
      Common evaluation methods for dimensionality reduction, particularly for visualization, are pre-
399
      dominantly focused on local structures. For instance, McInnes et al. (2018); Kawase et al. (2022)
400
      use KNN accuracy and Trustworthiness, which only account for the local neighborhoods of each
401   point while overlooking global structures such as cluster separation. One global evaluation method
402   is the Silhouette score, which measures the clustering quality of the classes within the embedding
403   space. However, this score does not capture the preservation of the overall global structure.
404
      To address this gap, we propose a new evaluation method, specifically appropriate for assessing
405
      global structure preservation in graph-based dimensionality reduction methods (e.g., UMAP, t-SNE).
406
      The leading eigenvectors of the Laplacian matrix are known to encode crucial global information
407   about the graph (Belkin & Niyogi, 2003). Thus, we measure the distance between the global struc-
408   tures of the original and embedding manifolds using the Grassmann distance between the first eigen-
409   vectors of their respective Laplacian matrices. We refer to this method as the Grassmann Score (GS).
410
      It is important to note that GS includes a hyper-parameter - the number of eigenvectors considered.
411
      Increasing the number of eigenvectors incorporates more local structure into the evaluation. A nat-
412
      ural choice for this hyperparameter is 2, which corresponds to comparing the Fiedler vectors (i.e.,
413   the second eigenvectors of the Laplacian). The Fiedler vector is well known for encapsulating the
414   global information of a graph (Fiedler, 1973; 1975). Unless stated otherwise, we use two eigen-
415   vectors for computing the GS. Fig. 4 demonstrates GS (alongside Silhouette and KNN scores for
416   comparison) on a few embeddings of two tangent spheres, independently to the embedding methods.
417   Notably, the embedding on the right appears to best preserve the global structure, as indicated by the
418   smallest GS value. In contrast, the KNN scores are comparable across all embeddings (e.g., KNN
419   ignores separation of an existing class), and the Silhouette score even favors other embeddings. In
420   App. D we mathematically formalize GS and provide additional examples of embeddings and their
421
      corresponding GS. These examples further support the intuition that GS effectively captures global
      structure preservation better than previous measures.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
529
530   We first introduced GrEASE, a deep-learning approach for approximate SE. GrEASE addresses the
531   three primary drawbacks of current SE implementation: generalizability, scalability and eigenvec-
532   tors separation. By incorporating a post-processing diagonalization step, GrEASE enables eigen-
533   vectors separation without compromising generalizability or scalability. Remarkably, this one-shoot
534   post-processing operation lays the groundwork for a wide range of new applications of SE, which
535   would not have been possible without its scalable and generalizable implementation. It also presents
536
      a promising pathway to enhance current applications of SE.
537   In particular, we presented NUMAP, a novel application of GrEASE for generalizable UMAP vi-
538   sualization. We believe the integration of SE with deep learning can have a significant impact on
539   unsupervised learning methods. Further research should delve into exploring the applications of SE
      across various fields.

774   C.1    T IME AND S PACE C OMPLEXITY
775
776   Specifying the exact complexity of the method is difficult, As this is a non-convex optimization
777
      problem, However, we can discuss the following approximate complexity analysis. Assuming con-
      stant input and output dimensions and a given network architecture, we can take a general view
778
      on the complexity of each iteration by the batch size m. The heaviest computational operations at
779
      each iteration are the nearest-neighbors search, the QR decomposition and the loss computation (i.e.,
780   computation of the Rayleigh Quotient). For the nearest-neighbor search, we can use approximation
781   techniques (e.g, LSH Gionis et al. (1999)) which work in almost linear complexity by m. A naive
782   implementation of the QR decomposition would lead to an O(m2 ) time complexity. The loss com-
783   putation also takes O(m2 ) due to the required matrix multiplication. Thereby, the complexity of
784   each iteration is quadratic by the batch size. This is comparable to other approximation techniques
785   such as LOBPCG Benner & Mach (2011) (which also utilizes sparse matrix operations techniques
786   for faster implementation). However, GrEASE leverages stochastic training, allowing each iteration
787   to consider only a batch of the data, rather than the entire dataset.
788   Assessing the complexity of each epoch is now straightforward, and results a time complexity of
789   O(nm), where n, the number of samples, satisfies n ≫ m. This indicates an almost-linear com-
790   plexity.

815   In this section, we provide the formulation for the Grassmann Score (GS) evaluation method, and
816   present simple examples to visualize its meaning.

818   D.1    F ORMALIZATION OF GS
819
820   First, we remind Grassmann distance (see Def. 1). Grassmann distance is a metric function between
821   equidimensional linear subspaces, where each is represented by an orthogonal matrix containing the
822   basis as its columns. In other words, this is a metric which is invariant under multiplication by an
823   orthogonal matrix.
824   Definition 1. Given two orthogonal matrices A, B ∈ Rn×k , the Grassmann Distance between them
825   is defined as:
826                                                    Xk
827                                      dGr (A, B) =     sin2 θi
828                                                           i=1
829                            T
      where θi = arccos σi (A B) is the ith principal angle between A and B, and σi is the ith smallest
830   singular value of AT B.
831
832   Assuming we are given a dataset X = {x1 , . . . , xn } ⊆ Rd and a corresponding low-dimensional
833   representation Y = {y1 , . . . , yn } ⊆ Rk . We want to evaluate the dissimilarity between the global
834   structures of X and Y. We build graphs from X and Y, saved as affinity matrices WX and WY ,
835   respectively. We construct the corresponding Unnormalized Laplacians (see Sec. 3.1) LX and LY .
836   We define the matrices VX , VY ∈ Rn×t so that their columns are the first t eigenvectors of LX , LY ,
837   respectively.
838   Finally, we define the GS of Y (w.r.t X ) as follows:
839
      Definition 2. GSX (Y) = dGr (VX , VY )
840
841   t is a hyper-parameter of GS. A reasonable choice would be to take t = 2, which is equivalent to
842   measure the Grassmann distance between the Fiedler vectors of the Laplacians. The Fiedler vector
843   is known for its hold of the most important global properties. The larger t, the more complicated
844   structures are taken into consideration in the GS computation (which is not necceray desired).
845
      Note that for the construction of the affinity matrices WX , WY we use the same construction scheme
846   detailed in App. C.2. This construction method is similar to the one presented by McInnes et al.
847   (2018), and proved to capture the local topology of the underlying manifold.
848
849
      It is important to note that GS might ignore the local structures, while concentrating on the global
      structures (especially for smaller values of t). The ultimate goal in visualization is to find a balance
850
      between the global and local structure.
