009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Symmetries in representations within generative models play essential roles in
012            predicting unobserved combinations of semantic changes, known as combinato-
013            rial generalization tasks. However, existing methods primarily focus on learning
014            symmetries from training data, leaving the extension of trained symmetries to un-
015            seen samples unaddressed. A promising approach to addressing this limitation
016            is leveraging geometric information on manifolds containing semantic structures
017            for unseen data, though this remains insufficient for robust symmetry learning. In
018
               this paper, we tackle the problem of symmetry generalization by enforcing sym-
               metric space on the latent space, leveraging semantic structures from both sym-
019
               metry and manifold perspectives. We identify an equivariance-based constraint
020
               that restricts symmetry generalization and prove that: 1) enforcing the homoge-
021            neous space property of symmetric space onto the data manifold resolves this
022            constraint, 2) a homogeneous latent manifold induces a homogeneous data mani-
023            fold through diffeomorphic mappings, and 3) the isometry property of symmetric
024            space extends local symmetries across the space. To implement this, we propose a
025            method to align sampled points from symmetric space with their explicitly trained
026            geodesics. We validate our approach through a detailed analysis on a toy dataset
027            and demonstrate its effectiveness in enhancing combinatorial generalization on
028            common benchmarks. This work represents the first effort to integrate symmetric
029
               space learning into generative models for combinatorial generalization.

      This paper addresses the challenge of symmetry generalization, which involves extending learned
061   symmetries from observed data to unseen data in combinatorial generalization tasks. To tackle this,
062   we propose a framework that enforces the latent vector space manifold to exhibit properties of a
063   symmetric space. We propose enforcing the latent vector space as a symmetric space and provide
064   theoretical insights into its effectiveness: 1) enforcing homogeneity on the data manifold addresses
065   constraints that limit symmetry learning, 2) equivariant diffeomorphic mappings ensure consistency
066   between the latent and data manifolds, and 3) symmetries in the latent space can propagate to unseen
067   regions through isometric properties. These theoretical foundations are not stand-alone but serve as
068   guiding principles for the practical implementation of our framework.
069   To make symmetry generalization computationally feasible, we introduce a sampling-based ap-
070   proach to induce symmetric space structures. This approach involves: 1) Generating explicit curves
071   that connect sampled latent vectors to shared anchor, 2) approximating these curves as geodesics,
072   and 3) aligning extrapolated points from reflection symmetries with the geodesic approximations.
073   This method avoids the computational intractability of processing the entire space and enables the
074   practical application of symmetric space learning to complex datasets.
075   We verify our proofs and method in Morpho-MNIST (Castro et al., 2019) and analyze its impact
076   quantitatively and qualitatively on dSprites (Matthey et al., 2017) and 3D shapes (Burgess & Kim,
077   2018) datasets.

092   2    BACKGROUND
093
094   A manifold, a mathematical structure locally equivalent to Euclidean space, is a foundational con-
095   cept in machine learning due to the manifold hypothesis (Narayanan & Mitter, 2010), which posits
096   that high-dimensional data lies on a low-dimensional manifold. To apply differentiable func-
097   tions—integral to most machine learning methods—to the manifold must possess a differentiable
098   structure. In our discussion, we focus on Riemannian manifolds, defined as follows (do Carmo,
099   1992):
100   Definition 1 (Riemannian manifold). A Riemannian metric on a differentiable manifold M is an
101   inner product ⟨·, ·⟩p (a symmetric, bilinear, positive-definite form) on the tangent space Tp M at
102   each point p ∈ M. A differentiable manifold M equipped with a Riemannian metric is called a
103   Riemannian manifold.
104
105
      To accurately represent the latent manifold while preserving the structure of the data manifold, we
      require a mapping that preserves geometric properties, termed a diffeomorphism:
106
107   Definition 2 (Diffeomorphism). Let M and N be smooth manifolds. A map f : M → N is called a
      diffeomorphism if it is bijective, continuous, differentiable, and its inverse f −1 is also differentiable.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Symmetries play a central role in our method, and a homogeneous space provides a formal way to
109   describe them on a manifold:
110
      Definition 3 (Homogeneous space). A Riemannian manifold M is called a homogeneous space if
111
      a group G acts transitively on M
112
113   On a homogeneous space, every point corresponds to an element of the group G acting on the
114   manifold (do Carmo, 1992). This property allows us to analyze the structure of the manifold through
115   symmetries independent of specific coordinate systems.
116
      To further refine the concept of symmetries on manifolds, we consider symmetric spaces, a special
117   case of homogeneous spaces with additional symmetry properties:
118
      Definition 4 (Symmetric space). Let M be a manifold, and let p ∈ M be an arbitrary point.
119
      Assume that a curve γ : I → M, where I ⊂ R is a real interval, is a geodesic with γ(0) = p. A
120
      geodesic symmetry is a map f : U → U , where U is a neighborhood of p, such that f (γ(t)) =
121   γ(−t) for all t ∈ I. If geodesic symmetries are isometries and extend globally across the manifold,
122   then M is called a symmetric space (Helgason, 2001).

      3.1   L IMITED E XTENSION OF T RAINED S YMMETRIES TO U NSEEN R EGION
128   Symmetry-based machine learning methods aim to train models that capture variations in data
129   through symmetries. Group actions, supported by well-established mathematical and physical the-
130   ories, provide a concrete framework for representing these symmetries. In particular, equivariant
131   group actions have become central to embedding symmetry properties into latent space representa-
132   tions. For instance, Hwang et al. (2023) proposed a method to generate novel samples by applying
133   symmetries learned from observed data. However, this approach relies on group actions derived
134   solely from the training data, limiting the generalization of symmetries to unseen data. We term this
135   challenge symmetry generalization, which is formalized in the following proposition.
136   Proposition 1 (Limit of Symmetry Generalization). Let X be a dataset and X ′ ⊂ X be a subset of
137   partially observed samples. If G is a symmetry group that acts transitively on X, then there exists
138   no g ∈ G such that g · x = x′ for x ∈ X \ X ′ and x′ ∈ X ′ .
139
      While this limitation follows directly from the definition of group actions, it highlights a critical gap
140
      in existing approaches to symmetry learning: the inability to extend symmetries beyond the training
141   dataset. This constraint underscores the need for methods capable of generalizing symmetries to
142   unseen data, a focus of our work.
143
144   3.2   S YMMETRIC S PACE I NDUCTION
145
146   Motivation: Homogeneity on Entire Data Manifold via Symmetric Space on Latent Manifold
147   From a geometric perspective, the data manifold encodes semantic structures for both observed
148   (trained) samples and potential unseen samples that share the same marginal distribution as the
149   training data. Aligning symmetries to the manifold enables the propagation of learned symmetries
150   to unseen data, addressing the limitations of symmetry generalization highlighted in Proposition 1.
151
      We propose enforcing the homogeneous space property on the data manifold to achieve this align-
      ment. The transitivity of homogeneous spaces provides a systematic solution to Proposition 1 by
152
      ensuring that symmetries learned from training data can be extended to the entire manifold. How-
153
      ever, directly enforcing a homogeneous structure on the data manifold is computationally intractable
154   due to its unknown structure. To overcome this challenge, we adopt a two-step approach: 1) inducing
155   homogeneity on the data manifold via its latent representation, and 2) extending trained local sym-
156   metries to unseen regions by leveraging the symmetric space property. In the subsequent sections,
157   we formalize these steps and provide the necessary propositions to support our approach.
158
159   Conditions for Homogeneity Transfer from Latent to Data Manifold Existing methods in
160   symmetry-based machine learning focus primarily on group structures, often overlooking the role
161   of geometric structures. We need the modified condition, equivariant mapping with group action, to
      fit the given situation, defining a homogeneous data manifold from a latent homogeneous manifold.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      In particular, the mapping between manifolds must be equivariant while preserving the geometric
163   structure of the data manifold.
164
165   To formalize this, we establish the following conditions for a model to enable the homogeneous
166
      space framework: Let D be the data space and H be the latent space. Let M ⊂ D be the data man-
      ifold and N ⊂ H be the latent manifold. The model must satisfy:
167
168
            1. Homogeneity of the Data Manifold: A group G acts transitively on M, i.e., M is a ho-
169            mogeneous space under G.
170
171         2. Equivariant Diffeomorphism: A map ϕ : M → N is an equivariant diffeomorphism
172            with respect to the group G.
173
      The first condition extends the group action from the dataset to the entire data manifold, while the
174
      second ensures that the equivariance mapping preserves the geometric structure of the manifolds.
175
      With these conditions satisfied, we establish the following proposition to enable the induction of
176   homogeneity on the data manifold via the latent manifold:
177
178   Proposition 2 (Homogeneity Transfer via Equivariance). Let M and N be manifolds, and let ϕ :
179
      M → N be an equivariant diffeomorphism with respect to the group G. If M is a homogeneous
      space under the action of G, then N is also a homogeneous space under G, and vice versa.
180
181
182   Proof. If M is homogeneous, then for any p, q ∈ M, there exists g ∈ G such that g · p = q.
183   By equivariance, g · ϕ(p) = ϕ(g · p) = ϕ(q), so G acts transitively on N . Conversely, if N is
184   homogeneous, then for any p, q ∈ M, we have g · ϕ(p) = ϕ(q). Since ϕ is a diffeomorphism,
185   g · p = q. Hence, G acts transitively on M.
186
187
188
      This proposition highlights that homogeneity on the latent manifold can be transferred to the data
      manifold via an equivariant diffeomorphism. Although the result is straightforward, it provides a
189
      foundational step for identifying group structures that are homogeneous with the data manifold by
190
      constructing a homogeneous latent manifold.
191
192
193
      Homogeneity Extension via Symmetric Space Induction Proposition 2 resolves the symmetry
      generalization limitation identified in Proposition 1 by enforcing homogeneity in the latent space.
194
      However, directly identifying a homogeneous group for the entire data space without additional
195
      information is impractical. To address this, we propose leveraging the symmetric space structure,
196   which extends homogeneity by incorporating isometric geodesic symmetries across all point pairs
197   on the manifold. This extension ensures symmetry generalization, as shown in the following propo-
198   sition:
199
      Proposition 3 (Latent Space Symmetry Generalization). Let ϕ : M → N be an equivariant dif-
200
      feomorphism from the data manifold M to the latent manifold N . Suppose M is partitioned
201   into observed data X and unseen data X ′ , with corresponding latent partitions Z = ϕ(X) and
202   Z ′ = ϕ(X ′ ). If N is a symmetric space G/Ha , and the model learns Aut(Z), the automorphisms
203   of Z, then for any x′ ∈ X ′ , there exist g ∈ G and α ∈ Aut(Z) such that
204
205                                          x′ = ϕ−1 (g · α(a)),
206
207   where a is the origin point of N .
208
209
      Proof. By Proposition 2, M is homogeneous under G, so for any x ∈ X and x′ ∈ X ′ , there exists
210   g ∈ G such that g · x = x′ . Let p = ϕ(x) ∈ Z. Since N is a symmetric space G/Ha , and the
211   model learns Aut(Z), there exists α ∈ Aut(Z) such that α(a) = p. Combining these, g · p ∈ Z ′ ,
212   so x′ = ϕ−1 (g · α(a)), completing the proof.
213
214
215   This proposition establishes that trained local symmetries can be extended to unseen regions through
      the symmetric space structure of the latent manifold.

245   Overview In this section, we propose a practical method to implement symmetric space learn-
246   ing. This method addresses how to satisfy the equivariant diffeomorphism condition in a machine
247   learning framework and how to effectively learn geodesic symmetries.
248   Figure 2 illustrates the key components of our approach, which include: 1) a network module that
249   generates a curve function for a latent vector from a training sample, 2) a loss function designed
250   to approximate this curve to a geodesic connecting the latent vector and a learnable anchor, and 3)
251   a loss function for inducing geodesic symmetries by aligning extrapolated unseen samples through
252   reflection. Detailed explanations of each component follow.
253
254   4.1   F RAMEWORK FOR C ONDITIONS OF H OMOGENEITY T RANSFER
255
256   Base Model: MAGANet Our base model must exhibit equivariance and diffeomorphic properties
257   to satisfy the conditions discussed in Section 3.2. We adopt MAGANet (Hwang et al., 2023) as
258   the baseline model, which meets these requirements through its design. MAGANet combines a
259   Variational Autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2013) with a flow-based generative model,
260   GLOW (Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018), to achieve equivariant and differentiable mapping.
261   The GLOW module is particularly suitable for this task because it comprises invertible and differ-
262   entiable layers, ensuring bijective mappings between spaces. As demonstrated in Zhen et al. (2021),
263   flow-based generative models can implicitly capture manifold structures, making them apt for mod-
264   eling the equivariant and diffeomorphic mapping from the data manifold to the latent manifold.
265   In our approach, the GLOW module is trained to encode the manifold structure implicitly while
266   satisfying homogeneous group action.
267
268   Addressing the Limitation of Local Symmetry While the VAE encoder in MAGANet effec-
269   tively models local symmetries, it does not ensure their extension to unseen regions, as noted in
      Proposition 1. The modeled symmetries correspond to points on the latent manifold via group ac-

      tion, with the pivot acting as the manifold’s origin point. However, extending this symmetry across
271   the entire latent space requires additional mechanisms.

273   To complete the homogeneity of the latent manifold, we incorporate a module that enforces global

      structure through geodesic symmetry learning, as described in the subsequent sections.

276   4.2   I MPLEMENTATION FOR H OMOGENEITY E XTENSION
277
      Building on the MAGANet model (Hwang et al., 2023), we establish an equivariant diffeomorphism
278
      between the data manifold and the latent manifold, as outlined in Proposition 3. This map ensures
279
      that the latent manifold can serve as a symmetric space to generalize symmetries learned from
280   observed data to unseen data.
281
282   To implement symmetric space properties on the latent manifold, we design a novel module con-
283
      sisting of two key components: the curve function maker and the anchor. The curve function maker
      generates a curve parameterized by multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs), connecting a given latent vector
284
      to the anchor. Its objective is to approximate the curve to a geodesic, ensuring minimal distortion of
285
      the manifold structure.
286
287   Geodesic symmetries are modeled as linear extrapolations, with the additional objective of aligning
288   the extrapolated points with the extended geodesic. In this context, the encoder of MAGANet in-
289
      terprets group actions as vector operations (e.g., vector subtraction). By enforcing that the linearly
      extrapolated points lie on the latent manifold, our method effectively extends symmetries to unseen
290
      regions, enabling symmetry generalization across the entire latent space.
291
292

293

294

295   simplicity, all geodesics are constructed to start from z and end at the shared endpoint a. The anchor
296   a is implemented using a learnable parameter class from the PyTorch library.

358   Symmetry Representation Symmetry and its group representation play a foundational role in
359   representation learning (Higgins et al., 2022). Many approaches emphasize the connection between
360   disentangled representations and group structures to enhance performance (Higgins et al., 2018), im-
361   proving disentanglement results (Cha & Thiyagalingam, 2023; Yang et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021a).
362   However, these methods primarily learn symmetries from observed data, limiting their applicability
363   to unseen cases. In contrast, our method extends symmetry learning to unobserved data, addressing
364   a critical gap in existing literature.
365
366   Geometry in Generative Models A geometric perspective has been shown to improve represen-
367
      tation learning in generative models. For instance, Fumero et al. (2021) demonstrated that manifold
      learning enhances disentanglement in latent spaces, while Falorsi et al. (2018) explored manifold
368
      learning integrated with group actions. These studies suggest that combining geometry and symme-
369
      try benefits representation learning. Manifold learning has also been used for generalization tasks,
370   such as Out-of-Distribution robustness in NLP (Ng et al., 2020) and improved classification per-
371   formance (Vural & Guillemot, 2016). Building on these insights, we propose leveraging manifold
372   structures to enhance generative generalization, particularly for unseen combinations of semantic
373   factors.

452

453

454   selection.
455
456   The dSprites dataset consists of binary images with a white shape on a black background, uniquely
457
      defined by five factors: shape, size, orientation, x-position, and y-position. The 3D Shapes dataset
      comprises images of objects within a scene and is uniquely determined by six factors: object shape,
458
      object scale, object orientation, object hue, wall hue, and floor hue.
459
460   In the R2E setting, we exclude three combinations of specific values or small ranges for each fac-
461   tor in both datasets. In the R2R setting, we exclude three combinations of a specific value and a
462   range for each factor. Further details on dataset settings and excluded combinations are provided in
463
      Appendix D.2.
464
465   Model and Training Settings We evaluate our method using MAGANet (Hwang et al., 2023), as
466   described in 4.1. For comparisons, we include VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013) and β-VAE (Hig-
467   gins et al., 2016), which do not explicitly learn symmetries, and Commutative Lie Group VAE
468   (CLGVAE) (Zhu et al., 2021b), which does explicitly learn symmetries.
469   All models use a latent dimension of 10. Training is conducted with a batch size of 128, a learning
470   rate of 0.001, and 100 epochs. For the dSprites dataset, models are trained using binary cross-
471   entropy loss, while mean-squared error loss is used for the 3D Shapes dataset. The energy function
472   interval T in Equation 5 is set to 16, enabling efficient geodesic approximation.
473   For β-VAEs, we test three values of β (2, 4, and 8). For MAGANet and the GS-equipped version,
474   hyperparameters βkl and βlr are fixed at 1, based on optimal values determined through grid search.
475   The scaling factor ζ for our proposed loss is set to 1 across both datasets. Additionally, MAGANet-
476   based models require a pivot for inference; we select the median sample from the training dataset as
477   the pivot for both datasets.

      In this paper, we addressed the problem of trained symmetries being limited in their applicability
      to unobserved data for combinatorial generalization. We demonstrated that structuring the latent
532
      vector space as a symmetric space enables the generalization of trained symmetries and proposed
533
      a novel method to induce symmetric space by generating specific samples and aligning them to the
534   approximated geodesic. The effectiveness of our approach was validated through in-depth analysis
535   of tests on the toy Morpho-MNIST dataset and further corroborated by experiments on widely used
536   benchmarks, including dSprites and 3D Shapes, for combinatorial generalization tasks. Our work
537   is the first to establish the utility of integrating manifold and symmetry learning to enhance com-
538   binatorial generalization. This contribution opens up promising directions for future research, such
539   as exploring diverse sampling strategies tailored to specific data characteristics and extending the
      approach to tackle a broader range of generalization tasks beyond combinatorial generalization.

      A.2   L IMITATION
670
671   The proposed method is based on the manifold hypothesis; thus, if the given dataset does not con-
672   form to this hypothesis, our method may be less effective. Additionally, for practical implementa-
673   tion, we assume that the base model does not sample outlier variances in the context of variational
674   inference. Therefore, if such outliers are present, our model cannot transform the latent manifold
675   into a fully symmetric space but only into a locally symmetric one.
676   Since geodesics on the manifolds are based on variance-based metric as the Mahalanobis met-
677   ric (Chadebec & Allassonniere, 2022), the geodesic symmetry can induce isometry when involved
678   points have the small difference of variance values. However, more direct methods to inject the
679   isometry may be more effective to induce symmetric space to wider cases.

      Group Action Group (G, ∗) is a mathematical structure which is tuple of a set G and binary
      operation ∗ closed on the set. Group should satisfy following axioms:
685
686
            1. (associativity) a ∗ (b ∗ c) = (a ∗ b) ∗ c
687
688         2. (identity element) there exists e ∈ G such that a ∗ e = e ∗ a
689         3. (inverse element) there exists a−1 ∈ G such that a ∗ a−1 = a−1 ∗ a = e
690
691   for every a, b, c ∈ G. Group play as an atom of representing symmetry. The group action on a set X
692   of a group G is a map f : G × X → X which satisfy following axioms:
693
694
            1. (identity) f (e, x) = x
695         2. (compatibility) f (g, f (h, x)) = f (gh, x)
696
697   for an identity e ∈ G and every g, h ∈ G and every x ∈ X. We can decompose natural phenomena
698   into objects and symmetries of those via group action.
699   Definition 5. Let G be a group and X be a G-space. The action is said to be a transitive if there
700   exists g ∈ G such that g ∗ x = y for any x1 , x2 ∈ X.
701
      This means that every point x ∈ X can be translated into any point in X with an action g ∈ G.
