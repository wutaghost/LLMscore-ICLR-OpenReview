011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in artificial intelligence (AI) have led to the development of
014            AI vision systems that closely resemble biological vision in terms of both behav-
015            ior and neural recordings. While prior research in modeling biological vision has
016            largely concentrated on comparing individual AI systems to a biological counter-
017            part, our study instead investigates the collective behavior of model populations.
018            We focus on inputs that generate the most divergent responses among a diverse
019
               population of AI vision systems, as measured by their aggregate disagreement.
               We would expect that the factors driving disagreement among AI systems are also
020
               causes of misalignment between AI systems and human perception. We challenge
021
               this expectation by demonstrating alignment between AI systems and humans at
022            the population level, even for images that generate divergent responses among AI
023            systems. This unexpected finding challenges our understanding of the relation-
024            ship between the limitations of AI systems and human perception, suggesting that
025            even the most challenging stimuli for AI systems are reflective of human percep-
026            tual difficulties.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   As artificial intelligence (AI) systems scale in complexity, they tend to exhibit increasingly simi-
033
      lar behavior and representations (Li et al., 2015; Geirhos et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2021; Sorscher
      et al., 2022), making it challenging to differentiate the unique computational properties of individ-
034
      ual systems (Geirhos et al., 2018; Maheswaranathan et al., 2019; Han et al., 2023). Representational
035
      convergence or universality is a natural consequence of standard machine learning training because
036   optimization of overparameterized systems on vast datasets leads to similar solutions despite imple-
037   mentational differences (Cao & Yamins, 2021; Huh et al., 2024; van Rossem & Saxe, 2024), with
038   behavioral convergence as a downstream consequence. However, despite convergence in the aggre-
039   gate, AI systems can disagree in their predictions for specific visual inputs, especially for artificial
040   stimuli produced from corruptions poorly represented in their training diet (Geirhos et al., 2020).
041
      One might expect that the stimuli driving the most disagreement among AI systems are those that
042   cause these systems to deviate the most from human perception, with the intuition that these “dis-
043   agreeable” images are challenging cases in which AI systems struggle to reach a consensus due to
044   limitations in their training, architecture, or underlying assumptions about visual processing. This
045   expectation aligns with standard approaches for comparing internal representations of AI and biolog-
046   ical vision, such as representational similarity analysis (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008), which prescribe
047   computing similarities between model and biological activations aggregated over a large-scale and
048   often undifferentiated stimulus set. Moreover, this expectation is explicit in behavioral extrapolation
049   tests, which exploit intrinsic properties of AI systems to degrade their alignment to human behavior,
050
      for example, translation invariance or sensitivity to adversarial perturbations (Geirhos et al., 2019;
      Madan et al., 2020; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Ollikka et al., 2024).
051
052   In this work, we challenge the assumption that disagreement among AI systems is intrinsic to these
053   systems and unrelated to aspects of human visual processing. Instead, we aim to demonstrate that
      disagreement among populations offers a valuable opportunity for comparative analysis between

104   velopment of more robust and human-aligned vision systems, a problem of significant current inter-
105   est (Wichmann & Geirhos, 2023; Sucholutsky et al., 2023). Moreover, our study, and the converging
106   evidence for behavioral and representational convergence in the field at large, call for a rethinking
107   of model comparison away from the paradigm of one-to-many towards many-to-many comparisons
      between artificial and biological vision.

133   2.1   V ISUAL STIMULUS SETS
134
135   We conduct our analyses on a diverse set of images drawn from ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) and
136   ObjectNet (Barbu et al., 2019), widely used benchmarks in computer vision research. ImageNet
137   provides a dataset of labeled natural images across thousands of object categories, while ObjectNet
138
      introduces additional challenges by presenting objects in real-world scenarios with varied viewpoints
      and occlusions and has a “no training” policy to preserve its status as a test set. These datasets were
139
      chosen for their ability to test model performance across a wide range of object categories and
140
      difficulty levels, allowing us to capture a spectrum of disagreement. We use the 50 000 images
141   among 1000 classes from the ILSVRC 2012–2017 validation set (Russakovsky et al., 2015) and the
142   50 000 images among 313 classes from the ObjectNet test set, for a total of 100 000 visual stimuli
143   among 1200 classes due to the partial class overlap of ImageNet and ObjectNet. We will henceforth
144   refer to these sets simply as “ImageNet” and “ObjectNet.”
145
146   2.2   A POPULATION OF AI SYSTEMS
147
148   We examined the behavior of 1032 AI vision systems, spanning different architectures, including
149   convolutional neural networks (CNNs), vision transformers (ViTs), and hybrid architectures; see
150   Fig. (2) and Fig. (10). These systems varied in complexity, dataset size, and pre-training and fine-
151   tuning protocols, representing a comprehensive cross-section of early and state-of-the-art visual
152   recognition systems; see Appendix (A.1). We obtained the class label predictions of each system on
153   ImageNet and ObjectNet, enabling us to analyze patterns of agreement and disagreement at scale.
154

213   2.4   G ROUNDING IN HUMAN BEHAVIORAL MEASURES
214
215   Mayo et al. (2023) collected a large dataset of human object recognition judgments consisting of
      200 382 human responses from 2647 human participants for 4771 images, of which 2415 are from

216
      ObjectNet and 2356 from ImageNet. Images were presented at 6 different durations between 17ms
217   and 10s, with 42 responses collected per image (7 different subjects seeing each image at one of six
218   timings). In the next section, we compare population-level agreement with two human perceptual
219   measures derived from this dataset. The minimum viewing time is defined as the shortest duration
220   at which the majority of human participants (more than half) can correctly recognize an object in
221   an image. This metric serves as a proxy for image difficulty as longer times suggest that an image
222   is more challenging for humans to interpret quickly. The difficulty score is the total number of
223   incorrect responses out of the 42 presentations of each image. A higher difficulty score indicates
224   that more humans struggled with the image.
225   ImageNet-X (Idrissi et al., 2022) provides annotations for almost all images in the ImageNet dataset
226   (46 110 out of 50 000 images) across 16 factors of variation that capture how each image differs
227   from prototypical examples of its class. Annotations were made by comparing each image to three
228   class-prototypical images, with human inspection to categorize the differences into the 16 factors.
229   The annotations cover factors of variation like pose, background, color, texture, size, lighting, and
230   occlusion. While multiple factors could be selected, a top factor was also determined for each
231
      image to identify the main factor of deviation from the class prototypes. We take the intersection
      of images evaluated over models, humans and ImageNet-X annotated images which results in 2194
232
      with agreement scores and annotations for both models and humans.

      seen in both the low-agreement images and high-agreement images in Fig. (8).

495   4   R ELATED W ORK
496
497   Error analysis. Geirhos et al. (2020) introduced the concept of error consistency to vision model-
498   ing in order to better quantify the behavioral alignment of artificial vision systems to human vision.
499
      Complementary to accuracy, their method quantifies whether two decision-making systems make
      the same errors on the same visual stimuli, which provides further insights into the similarity of
500
      processing strategies between different models or between models and humans. Rather than con-
501
      centrating on error consistency between two systems, we turn our focus towards population-level
502   consistency by analyzing the inputs that generate the most divergent responses across a diverse pop-
503   ulation of AI vision systems. This approach allows us to explore the nature of model disagreement
504   and its relationship to human perception, even in cases in which individual models may not match
505   the errors of individual humans. Like a complete treatment of accuracy and error-consistency, our
506   analysis does not require any knowledge of the true label or the existence of one for a given stimuli,
507   making it suitable for unannotated data which may be more readily available.
508
509   Synthetic stimuli. In contrast to the approach of Golan et al. (2020), which synthesizes “con-
510   troversial” stimuli through an optimization procedure involving backpropagation through a vision
511   system, our work focuses on analyzing disagreement between models on naturalistic samples that
512
      either already exist or are generated without optimization. This approach allows us to assess models’
      inductive biases and generalization abilities under more realistic conditions, providing insights into
513
      how the models might behave when deployed in the wild, where they may encounter novel stimuli
514
      that are not purposefully crafted to expose their differences. Furthermore, many artificial pertur-
515   bations to stimuli impact the original labeling and features, which can no longer be used without
516   making assumptions about the nature of the perturbation (Ilyas et al., 2019).
517
518   Metamers. Metamers are distinct visual stimuli that are perceptually indistinguishable to a spe-
519   cific perceptual, often visual, system (Freeman & Simoncelli, 2011). In contrast, our study focuses
520   on identifying single stimuli that evoke divergent responses across a diverse population of AI vi-
521   sion models. While metamers explore how different inputs can be perceived uniformly by a single
522   system, our work examines how the same input can be interpreted variably by multiple systems.
523   This fundamental difference highlights two distinct dimensions of visual representation: invariance
524
      within a system versus variability across systems.
525
526   5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   Our study of disagreement among model and human populations in vision challenges the assumption
529   present in prior work that disagreement among AI systems is unrelated to human visual processing.
530   We demonstrate a correspondence between AI and human disagreement on naturalistic stimuli, re-
531   vealing that image difficulty drives disagreement in both populations, and we provide evidence that
532   the dominant factors driving disagreement in AI systems also cause disagreement in human visual
533
      perception. Our results suggest that individual differences in AI vision systems may overlap with
      those in human visual processing rather than being unique artificial limitations, which prescribes
534
      more population-level comparisons between AI and human vision systems rather than model-to-
535
      individual comparisons.
